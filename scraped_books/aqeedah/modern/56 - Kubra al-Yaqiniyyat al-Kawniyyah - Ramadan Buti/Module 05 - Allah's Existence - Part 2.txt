The Two Kinds of Existence and a Warning
Buti classifies existence and warns against speculative errors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the distinction between essential divine existence and dependent contingent existence, and why excessive speculation about God's nature leads to error.

Core Idea
God's existence is self-sufficient; all else is dependent and perishable.

Buti begins by clarifying a fundamental division in reality: existence itself has two modes. Only Allah’s existence is complete and self-standing. Everything else exists by Him and is surrounded by non-existence. This distinction safeguards divine transcendence. Buti then issues a stern warning: don't venture beyond this basic truth without proper tools, or you risk falling into philosophical madness or pantheistic delusion.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing, then read the translation. Let the author's classification and warning come through.
Notice the clear division and the strong warning language.
2
Identify the two kinds of existence
Find the terms 'complete/incomplete' and 'essential/dependent'. Note the characteristics of each: essential existence does not admit nonexistence; dependent existence is between two nonexistences.
This is the core doctrinal point.
3
Understand the warning
Buti warns against going beyond this basic distinction. Without proper tools (certain knowledge, empirical evidence, rational proofs), one falls into error.
He mentions two specific errors: existentialist abstraction and pantheistic identification.
4
Reflect on the errors
Consider the two erroneous views: existentialists posit an abstract existence stripped of quiddity; some Sufis identify God's existence with the world. Both violate the distinction drawn.

But what you should know here is that existence is divided into two parts: complete existence and incomplete existence. In other words, we say: essential existence and dependent existence. The existence of God Almighty is complete and essential, meaning He exists for Himself, not due to an efficient cause. As for these, their delusion ended with them claiming that the reality of God is the existence of the world itself, so these universes that you see around you are in reality nothing more than the existence of God Almighty incarnated in these forms and shapes!

Commentary
This passage is a classification of existence and a warning. It divides existence into two types: complete (kāmil) and incomplete (nāqiṣ), also called essential (dhātī) and dependent (tabaʿī). God's existence is complete/essential: He exists by Himself without a cause, and it does not admit nonexistence. The existence of everything else is incomplete/dependent: derived from another, dependent on a creator, and surrounded by two nonexistences—before and after. Thus, only God's existence is absolute and self-subsisting; all others are contingent. The author then warns against excessive speculation about the nature of God's existence versus contingent existence, or about the distinction between essence and existence, because without proper scientific tools (certain scriptural knowledge, empirical evidence, rational proofs), one can only rely on intuition and imagination, leading to error. Specifically, the author points out two erroneous views: the existentialist philosophers who claim God's existence is abstracted from all quiddity—i.e., it is the existence of nothing else, which reduces to a meaningless abstraction—and some Sufis who mistakenly identify God's existence with the existence of the world (pantheism). The passage thus safeguards the transcendence of God and the contingency of creation.

The Division of Existence into Perfect and Imperfect
Buti defines perfect and imperfect existence and warns against exceeding limits in speculation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Buti's classification of existence into perfect (self-subsistent) and imperfect (dependent) and why he warns against metaphysical speculation beyond this limit.

Core Idea
Only Allah's existence is perfect and self-subsistent; all else is dependent and bounded by non-existence.

Buti begins by clarifying a fundamental division: existence is either perfect or imperfect. This distinction is not merely academic; it sets the boundary for proper theological reflection. He explains the characteristics of each type and then cautions against overstepping human epistemological limits. Let's examine the passage carefully.

Note that the perfect existence is identified with Allah alone.
2
Note the characteristics

3
Appreciate the methodological warning
Buti explicitly forbids going beyond this classification into speculation about essence vs existence, because we lack the necessary tools (certain report, empirical evidence, logical proofs).
He warns that intuition and imagination lead only to confusion.

The First Rational Proof: God Cannot Be Composed of Parts
Buti presents the first rational proof against God being a composite whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that God cannot be a whole composed of parts, because that would imply dependence and resemblance to contingent things.

Core Idea
If God were made of parts, He would need them — and need is imperfection.

Buti begins his rational proofs for God's oneness by addressing the possibility that God could be a composite whole. This would imply need and resemblance to creation, which is impossible for the Creator. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic and translation to grasp the conditional structure: 'if... then...'.
Notice the logical flow: supposition leads to implications.
2
Identify the consequences
The passage lists two consequences of God being composed of parts. Identify them and reflect on why each is impossible for God.
3
Connect to oneness
Ask: How does this proof establish that God is one? Understand that composite implies need, which contradicts divinity.
This is a form of reductio ad absurdum.

وأما الدليل العقلي على نفيهما ، أي أنه سبحانه ليس كلاً قابلاً للتجزء ولاكلياً يندرج فيه أفراد وأعداد ، فهو ما يلي : أولاً - لو صح أنه سبحانه وتعالى كلّ مركب من أجزاء ، لاستلزم ذلك أن يكون عاجزاً بنفسه محتاجاً إلى غيره ، وللزم من ذلك أن يكون مشابهاً للحوادث ، وذلك باطل في حق الله كما قد علمت ثانياً ـ لو صح أنه سبحانه وتعالى كلي مكون من أفراد ، لأمكن أن يقوم بينهم تمانع في الإرادة والخلق ؛ وذلك بأن يريد واحد منهم إيجاد شيء ويريد الآخر إعدامه . وحينئذ إما أن يحصل الأمران فيجتمع النقيضان وهو محال ، أو يحصل أحدهما فيظهر عجز الآخر وهو مناف للألوهية ، أو يتصادمـا فلا هذا ولا ذاك فيظهر عجزهما معاً ؛ ومادام وقوع هذا التمانع ممكناً فإن صفة الكمال لهما تصبح غير ضرورية . وقد ذكر الله هذا البرهان بأسلوب مبسط في قوله : لَوْ كَانَ فِيهما آلِهَةٌ إلا اللهُ لَفَسَدَتا ... ﴾ [ الأنبياء : ٢٢ ] .
As for the rational proof of negating both, i.e., that He, exalted, is not a whole divisible into parts nor a universal comprising individuals and numbers, it is as follows: First - if it were true that He, exalted, is a whole composed of parts, then it would necessitate that He is helpless by Himself, in need of others, and that would necessitate that He resembles contingent things, and that is false regarding God as you have known. Second - if it were true that He, exalted, is a universal composed of individuals, then it would be possible for conflict to arise between them in will and creation; that is, one of them wills to bring something into existence and another wills to annihilate it. Then either both occur and the two opposites would combine, which is impossible, or one occurs and the incapacity of the other becomes apparent, which contradicts divinity, or they clash and neither occurs, so the incapacity of both becomes apparent; and as long as the occurrence of such conflict is possible, the attribute of perfection for them becomes non-necessary. And God has mentioned this proof in a simplified manner in His saying: 'If there were gods besides Allah, both would have been corrupted' [Al-Anbiyaa: 22].
Source pages p. 112

Commentary
This passage presents two rational proofs (adilla 'aqliyya) for God's oneness. The first proof argues that if God were a whole composed of parts (kull murakkab min ajza'), He would be dependent on those parts and would resemble contingent things (hawadith), which is impossible for God. The second proof argues that if God were a universal comprising multiple individuals (kulli mukawwan min afrad), conflict (tamani') could arise between these individuals in will and creation. Such conflict leads to one of three impossible outcomes: (1) both wills occurring simultaneously, combining opposites; (2) one will prevailing, showing the other's incapacity; or (3) neither occurring, showing both's incapacity. Since God must be perfect, multiple gods are impossible. The author cites the Qur'anic verse (21:22) as a simplified expression of this argument.

The Second Rational Proof: Multiple Gods Lead to Conflict
Buti presents the second rational proof against multiple gods using the impossibility of conflict.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the rational argument that if there were multiple gods, conflict in will would necessarily arise, leading to impossibility or imperfection.

Core Idea
Multiple gods cannot coexist because their wills would clash.

Buti's second proof tackles the possibility of multiple gods. If God were a universal comprising many individuals, conflict would be possible. Such conflict leads to contradiction or weakness, both incompatible with divinity. This lesson explores the three impossible outcomes and the Quranic confirmation.

Guided Path

Analyze the three outcomes

These are: both happen (contradiction), one prevails (weakness), neither happens (both weak).
3
See the Quranic support

The Necessity of Human Purpose After Proving God
Buti argues that reason cannot accept a purposeless existence after affirming a wise Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand why belief in a wise Creator logically requires human purpose and accountability, and why purposelessness is an absurd contradiction.

Core Idea
If God is Wise, existence cannot be meaningless.

This lesson follows Buti's proofs for God's existence and attributes. After establishing that the Creator is Wise and that absurdity is impossible for Him, Buti now poses a series of rhetorical questions. These questions force the reader to confront the logical outcome: human life must have a purpose; otherwise, the entire creation would be an absurdity inconsistent with God's wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage and its English translation to get a sense of the rhetorical flow and the questions Buti raises.
Notice the repetition of 'هل' (interrogative) which builds the argument.
2
Identify the key premises
Buti reminds us that we have concluded that God exists as a Wise Manager, and that absurdity cannot be attributed to Him. These premises are the foundation of the argument.
The attribute of wisdom (ḥikma) and negation of absurdity (ʿabath) are crucial.
3
Examine the rhetorical questions
List the scenarios Buti presents as unacceptable: living like animals, the intellect being a meaningless accident, the universe ending in nothing. For each scenario, ask why it would contradict God's wisdom.
The questions are not merely rhetorical; they are logical reductions to absurdity.
4
Understand the conclusion
Recognize that the ultimate absurdity is a perfectly coordinated universe subjugated to man ending in nothing. The impossibility of this absurdity logically entails that human existence must have a purpose and accountability.
The conclusion is implicit: if absurdity is impossible, then purpose is necessary.
5
Reflect on personal significance
Consider how this reasoning grounds moral responsibility and the need for religion in one's life. If life has purpose, then we must seek it.
This connects theology to daily life and ethics.

Commentary
This passage is a rhetorical argument that follows the proofs of God's existence. It aims to demonstrate that human existence must have a purpose and accountability, otherwise belief in a wise Creator would be contradicted. The author begins by recalling that we have concluded the investigation into God's existence, and the intellect readily affirms a Manager of the universe with attributes of wisdom and power. He then poses a series of rhetorical questions: can we ask about our function? Could we imagine having no function or responsibility? Would the intellect accept that we are merely like animals, eating, drinking, and procreating until death? He questions whether the intellect, which distinguishes humans and allows them to explore the universe's depths, is just a meaningless accident. The answer is that no rational person, after believing in a wise Creator, can even imagine such hypotheses, let alone believe them. The reason is that it is a clear attribute of God that He is Wise (ḥakīm) in all His creation and judgments, and absurdity (ʿabath) is inconceivable for Him. The ultimate absurdity would be to have this perfectly coordinated universe, subjugated to man with his wondrous intellect, end in nothingness and shatter without a goal. Thus, human purpose and a final accountability are necessary conclusions from the nature of God.

Creation Implies Accountability
Buti argues from creation's implication and cites Quran verses against purposelessness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the existence of creation rationally implies a Creator and moral accountability, as reinforced by Quranic verses against purposelessness.

Core Idea
Creation demands accountability — you are not a cosmic accident.

This lesson presents a powerful argument from creation itself: if a force brought you and the universe into existence, shouldn't you be concerned about your relationship with it? Buti then reinforces this rational inference with Quranic verses that explicitly reject the notion of purposeless creation, affirming that human life has meaning and that we will be held accountable.

Guided Path
1

Buti uses a direct, provocative style to engage the reader.
2
Note the cautionary footnote
In the source, Buti warns that calling Allah a 'force' is improper. Understand why such language is problematic.
This shows the author's precision in using proper theological language.
3
Examine the argument from universal responsibility
Observe the analogy: all creatures have assigned responsibilities; humans, being unique in reason, must also have responsibility.
4
Study the Quranic verses
Read the two verses from Al-Mu'minun and Al-Anbiya carefully. They directly state that creation is not in vain or for sport.
These verses are the scriptural foundation for the argument.
5
Connect to the core idea
Reflect on how the rational argument and the Quranic proofs together establish that life has purpose and accountability.
This is the takeaway: you are not here by accident.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If God exists as the cause of all causes, miracles are not only possible but expected.

Buti now turns to what he calls 'general science'—not experimental science but the broader rational science that includes theology. Having already proven God's existence through decisive proofs, this science is now asked about miracles. Buti shows that if general science were to deny miracles, it would be contradicting its own conclusions about God being the Creator and cause of all things.

The passage is a single paragraph with a dramatic tone.

These contradictions highlight the logical inconsistency of denying miracles after affirming God.

A consistent science that acknowledges God cannot deny miracles.

In this passage, Buti continues the dialogue with the skeptic who has previously been convinced of God's existence. When asked about miracles, science in its general meaning responds by pointing out the logical contradiction of denying miracles after affirming a Creator. Through rhetorical questioning and supporting quotes, Buti shows that the possibility of miracles follows necessarily from belief in God.

Understand the context

2
Identify the rhetorical strategy

5

This lesson solidifies the logical link between divine existence and miracles.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of two contradictory intellectual attitudes and an explanation of the Islamic stance on spirit summoning. Buti begins by identifying the 'astonishing foolishness' of someone who claims to be a Muslim yet argues against the existence of jinn, using the sole argument that he has not seen or sensed them. Buti labels this as mere ignorance. He then traces the root of this attitude to blind imitation of 'professional intellectual invaders' who dismiss belief in jinn, angels, and devils as animism and superstition, and attribute them to Muhammad (peace be upon him). The critics accept this without examining evidence. Buti then points out a contradiction: the same masters later speak about spirits and their summoning, and the same followers blindly believe in that too, again without critical thought. This shows their lack of intellectual independence. Buti then addresses the Islamic position on spirit summoning in a footnote: the world is full of spirits, but Islam requires empirical proof for any claim of summoning or communication, as it is a sensory matter. If proven by witnessed experience, it must be accepted, but the essence of the spirit remains unknown. Buti then returns to the main critique, calling the behavior 'foolishness': someone who denies scientific proofs when presented correctly but meekly accepts empty claims from Western thinkers. He quotes a proverb about being in water but head in the sky, indicating self-contradiction. Finally, Buti warns that reports of spirit communication are subject to truth and falsehood; there is no guarantee that the spirit is not a devil deceiving the person. He gives examples of deviants who were misled by whispers from devils into thinking they were prophets or beloved of God. Thus, while spirits exist, one should not trust claims of communication from them, especially if they claim to be prophets. The passage overall argues for intellectual consistency and reliance on empirical evidence for sensory claims, while affirming the unseen realities (jinn, angels) as taught by revelation.

The Argument from Harmony and Causality for a Single Creator
Buti argues that harmony and causality in the universe prove a single First Cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Buti uses the observed harmony in the universe and the principle of causality to prove the existence of a single Creator, and why this argument leads to the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
Harmony in the universe compels the mind to infer a single First Cause.

In this passage, Buti responds to an objection by presenting the most powerful evidence for the existence of God: the harmony and causality pervasive in the universe. He argues that the very fact that things are arranged in interdependent order forces the intellect to ascend from many causes to a single ultimate cause. This lesson walks through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of harmony
Buti begins by stating that the most prominent evidence for the Creator is the harmony and consistency (al-tanāsuq wa al-insijām) in the universe. This is not just complexity but a specific type of order.
He contrasts this with other possible evidences.
2
See causality as the core of harmony
Harmony is defined as the phenomenon of causality and causation (sababiyya wa 'illiyya) that runs through all parts of the universe. Without causality, there would be no harmony.
Causality means things are in need of each other.
3
Trace the causal chain backwards
As you examine the interconnected causes, you notice that the number of distinct causes decreases the deeper you go. Eventually, you reach a single great cause behind everything – the Necessary Being.
This is a logical deduction, not a leap of faith.
4
Apply the analogy of the watch
Buti uses an analogy: scattered machine parts cannot be attributed to one maker unless they fit together harmoniously. Similarly, the universe's harmony points to one Creator.
The more you see harmony, the more certain you become of the unity of the maker.

Commentary

Finally, the author notes that if God had not created humans in need, they would have no opportunity to discover this harmony, and thus would lose the strongest evidence for His existence. The argument follows a logical progression: from observed harmony to necessary causality, then from multiplicity of causes to a single first cause.

Both are manifestations of His divinity, justice, knowledge, mercy, and punishment. The passage insists that rejecting divine legislation is tantamount to rejecting the very purpose of creation.

The Quran as Bottom-Up Proof for God's Existence
Buti outlines a rational proof for God from Quran transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Quran's chain of transmission and the phenomenon of revelation serve as rational proof for the existence of God.

Core Idea
The Quran's definitive transmission demands a rational conclusion: God exists.

Buti introduces a second method for proving God's existence: working upward from observable reality. He starts with the Quran—not as a scripture to be believed, but as a historical document with a verifiable chain of transmission. From there, he moves to the phenomenon of revelation experienced by Muhammad, using rational arguments to establish that it truly came from God. This logical progression forces any rational person to accept God's existence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the starting point: the Quran
Read the passage and note that Buti begins with the Quran as a 'strange book' with a definitive chain of transmission (al-sanad al-qat'i). This is the empirical foundation.
Buti emphasizes that we are 'before' this book; it demands investigation.
2
Identify the phenomenon of revelation
Buti next identifies the key issue: the phenomenon of revelation (zahirat al-wahy) that Muhammad experienced. This is the 'scientific issue' to be examined.
Revelation is treated as an observable event, not a matter of faith.
3
Discover the verification arguments
Buti mentions two arguments: proof of correlation (burhan al-talazum) and argument of priority (qiyas al-awla). These are the rational tools to verify revelation.
Though not elaborated here, these arguments logically link revelation to God.
4
Reach the necessity of belief
Once revelation is verified, Buti concludes that we are 'before the necessity of believing in the existence of God.' This is the inevitable outcome of the reasoning.
The final sentence hints at a further proof for those still doubting.
