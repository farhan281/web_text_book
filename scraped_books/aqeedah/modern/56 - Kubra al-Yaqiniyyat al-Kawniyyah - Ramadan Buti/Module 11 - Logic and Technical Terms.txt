The Three Types of Necessary Implication
Buti classifies the three types of necessary implication and ranks their probative value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three types of necessary implication used in theological arguments and their relative strength in providing proof.

Core Idea
Not all implications are equally convincing; only the most specific type is immediate.

Before you can evaluate any argument that relies on a necessary connection between two things, you need a clear classification of what kind of implication is at work. Buti provides exactly that, distinguishing three degrees of evidential force. This passage lays out the types and ranks them, so you can assess which arguments carry weight and which need additional support.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage and note the three types
Read the Arabic passage once, then the translation. Pay attention to the three divisions of necessary implication that Buti presents: the non-evident, the evident in general sense, and the evident in most specific sense.
The first type is only mentioned at the end but is identified by contrast.
2
Understand the second type with examples
Focus on Buti's explanation of the second type (evident in general sense). He gives examples from contingency and necessity. Notice that you need reflection but no extra proof once you understand the meanings.
3
Grasp the third type's immediacy
The third type is the strongest. Buti's examples (ambulance to patient, groan to illness, voice in darkness) show that just conceiving the first thing immediately yields certainty of the second, without any reflection.
4
Note the ranking and the weakness of the first type
Buti ranks the third as strongest, then the second. The first type (non-evident) is not a proof by itself; it needs an external argument to establish the connection.
This ranking is crucial for evaluating arguments in kalam.

Commentary
This passage classifies the three types of necessary implication (al-luzum) used in arguments. The author begins by giving examples of the second type (al-luzum al-bayyin bi al-ma‘na al-‘amm, evident implication in the general sense): the indication of contingency in a thing that it is contingent, and the indication of the Necessary Existent that it is eternal. In these cases, you do not instantly grasp the implication; you need to reflect on the meanings of possibility and contingency, but once you do, you see the connection without needing an extra proof. Then he defines the third type (the most specific, al-akhass): here, merely conceiving the implicans (e.g., a groan) alone is enough to conceive the implication (that there is illness). Examples from natural and intellectual propositions show immediate recognition. He then ranks the three types: the third is strongest, then the second. The first type (non-evident) is not reliable by itself; it requires an external proof to confirm the link. This classification is crucial for evaluating the strength of theological arguments.

Definition and Types of Circular Reasoning
Author defines al-dawr and divides it into manifest and implicit types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of circular reasoning (al-dawr) and its two types—manifest and implicit—and why both are logically invalid.

Core Idea
Circular reasoning is always invalid, whether the circle is direct or hidden behind extra steps.

The author begins by defining circular reasoning (al-dawr) as a logical fallacy where a proposition depends on itself either directly or through a chain that returns to the start. This passage explains the two main forms: manifest circularity (direct mutual dependence or self-dependence) and implicit circularity (a longer loop). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for identifying flawed arguments in theology and logic.

The author uses letters A, B, C to show the dependencies.

Al-dawr (circular reasoning) is divided into two types: manifest (muṣarraḥ) and implicit (muḍmar). The manifest is as when we say: 'A depends on B, and B depends at the same time on A.' Or that a thing depends on itself in one respect, as when you say that the existence of A depends on the existence of A, like our example of the dependence of the world's existence on itself. The implicit is that you say: 'A depends on B, B depends on C, and C depends on A.' For a loop has increased in the scope of the circle, but it finally returns to depend on the first loop, so falsehood is also realized.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies circular reasoning (al-dawr). The author divides it into two types: manifest (muṣarraḥ) and implicit (muḍmar). Manifect circularity is direct: either A depends on B and B on A simultaneously, or a thing depends on itself in one respect (e.g., the world's existence depending on itself). Implicit circularity involves an intermediate: A depends on B, B on C, and C on A—a longer loop that still ends in dependence on the first, so it remains false. The passage follows a definition-with-division structure, providing clear examples for each type.

Defining Final Cause and Its Denial in God's Actions
Defines final cause and implies its denial in God's actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the concept of final cause as a human purpose and why it cannot be attributed to God's actions.

Core Idea
God's actions are not driven by any external purpose; only humans act for purposes.

Before we can understand why God's actions are free from final causes, we need a clear definition of what a final cause is. In this passage, the author defines 'final cause' (al-illah al-ghā'iyyah) as the purpose that arises in the human mind and drives one to take means toward that goal. This sets up a contrast with divine action, which is not motivated by any external purpose. By grasping this definition, you will see why theologians deny final causes in God's acts.
