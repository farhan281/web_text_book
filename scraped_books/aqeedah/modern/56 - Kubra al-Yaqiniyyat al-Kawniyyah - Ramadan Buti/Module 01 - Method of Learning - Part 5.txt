Commentary
This passage poses the methodological question of how to scientifically and rationally accept unseen matters (al-ghaybiyyat). It then provides three everyday examples to illustrate that accepting reports from trustworthy authorities is rational. The first example: a skilled doctor's warning about contaminated water, accepted without personal medical knowledge. The second: an official astronomical report of a lunar eclipse, accepted after verifying it is not a rumor. The third: an official announcement from an electricity company about a scheduled power cut, accepted as reliable. These examples show that in daily life, we rely on credible reports from experts; similarly, we should accept prophetic reports about the unseen based on the credibility of the messenger and the certainty of the transmission.

Core Idea
Certainty about the unseen is built on two proofs: trust in the source and reliability of the report.

He then asks a critical question: how can the scientific method, which is accepted by reason, be applied to such matters? Commentary
This passage is an argumentative explanation of how certainty about unseen matters is rationally achieved through a two-proof method. The author first defines 'the unseen' (al-ghaybiyyāt) as matters that the intellect cannot perceive directly because they have not yet occurred and there is no sensory model for them. He then poses the central question: how can the scientific method, which is accepted by reason, be applied to belief in these unseen matters? To answer, he provides three everyday analogies: (1) a doctor's warning about contaminated water, (2) astronomers' prediction of a lunar eclipse, and (3) an official announcement of a planned electricity cut. In each case, the reader would have certainty despite lacking direct sensory experience. The author then asks why this certainty occurs and answers: the intellect is compelled by two proofs. The first proof is certainty about the competence and trustworthiness of the source: the doctor is skillful and truthful, astronomy is a sound science, the electricity company is the responsible authority. The second proof is certainty that the report itself is conveyed reliably, through an official channel, not open to doubt or false interpretation. When both proofs are established, the second built upon the first, they necessarily produce certainty in the reported unseen matters. Finally, the author reinforces the analogy by pointing to the criminal law system: no one doubts its reality, and this belief is based on the same two proofs. Thus, belief in unseen matters is not irrational but follows a rigorous epistemic method.

Defining Islamic Society and Distinction from Sinlessness
Buti defines Islamic society by four criteria and distinguishes it from sinlessness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand Buti's definition of an Islamic society based on four operational criteria and how it differs from the concept of sinlessness.

Core Idea
An Islamic society is defined by general Sharia application, not by sinlessness of its members.

After refuting the historical excuse, Buti clarifies what is meant by an Islamic society. He distinguishes between the existence of an Islamic society—which is about the general application of Sharia—and the sinlessness of individuals, which is not required. This lesson presents the precise criteria that define an Islamic society and explains why sinlessness is not a condition.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of Islamic society
Buti gives four criteria: judiciary based on Islam, Islamic character pervading facilities/markets, absence of usury/intoxicants/open lewdness, and unrestricted religious rites.
These are operational, not utopian.
2
Understand the distinction from sinlessness
Buti emphasizes that sinlessness was never required by God for establishing Islamic rule; humans are sinful except prophets.
This corrects the misconception that Islamic society must be perfect.
3
Apply the definition to history
Buti asserts that these criteria were met through most of Islamic history, so the excuse that Islamic society never existed is false.
This ties back to the refutation in the previous lesson.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an excuse. The author, Buti, begins by analyzing the psychology of those who claim Islam is not practically applicable. He argues that their real problem is not the impracticality of Islam but their dislike of seeing Islam applied; they would protest if it were to be implemented. Then he asserts the historical continuity of Islamic society from the Prophet through the Ottoman period, emphasizing it was unbroken. He then makes a key distinction: the establishment of Islamic society (qiyam al-mujtama' al-Islami) is different from infallibility ('isma) from sins. He defines 'qiyam al-mujtama' al-Islami' as governance based on Islamic law, Islamic character in public life (no usury, no open alcohol, no public immorality), and freedom of religious rites. This was actually realized historically. Infallibility, on the other hand, was never present among any Muslim generation except prophets. God did not make infallibility a condition for Islamic rule. He gives examples: some Companions slipped and were punished, and later generations had sinners as well. The reason is human fallibility, not the absence of Islamic rule. The argument follows a clear order: expose the excuse, affirm historical reality, define terms, and refute the false link between sin and the non-existence of Islamic governance.

Commentary
This passage is a rhetorical exhortation and argument against those who offer excuses for disbelief after the truth of Islam has been made clear. The author (Ramadan al-Buti) begins by stating that reason's simplest judgment is that the fault lies with the one who disbelieves, neglects, and does evil after decisive proof has established the truth of the Islamic creed. The rational person must hate such evil, criticize it, and warn others. He then likens the reality of those who make excuses (المعتذرين) to a person with a squint eye: they look at the oppressor but attack the oppressed, staring at the evildoer but seizing the innocent. This behavior is described as 'squint' in both eye and mind, or a fabricated attempt to take revenge and conceal hatred and hypocrisy. The author then poses rhetorical questions about the benefit of such excuses, emphasizing the extreme seriousness of the matter (المصير - destiny). He declares it futility and heedlessness, but of a worse kind. The path is one, destiny one, end certain and near, and only the voice of reason can awaken one to this seriousness. He urges the reader to strive to listen to reason alone, distinguishing it from the noise of desires, environment, traditions, psychological complexes, pride, and bigotry. If one discerns reason, the problem ends, the covering is removed, and one discovers the supreme truth (الحقيقة الكبرى) and walks the right path. If one instead surrenders to the crowd of voices within, one will enjoy a deceptive mixture for a while, but then suddenly awaken to reason alone when it is too late. The author then directly addresses the reader as 'my brother human' (أخي الإنسان), urging him to contemplate his words with pure reason, distancing himself from complexes, environment, traditions, and psychological desires. If he does, he will be certain that all presented in the book is truth, and that he must prepare to end one life and begin another. The passage addresses a potential excuse: 'I cannot, my self overcomes me.' The solution is easy: turn to the Creator, present this weakness, and not mind coming stained with impurities, for God is Generous, Forgiving, Giving. The passage ends with a sample supplication (مناجاة) expressing guidance, repentance, weakness, and hope for forgiveness. The overall structure is argumentative and exhortative: first, the judgment of reason and the irrationality of excuses; then, the urgency and the need to listen to reason; then, the call to action and the simple solution of turning to God.

Europe's Two-Faced Advancement: Natural Sciences vs. Certain Perceptions
Presents a critique of Europe's imbalanced scientific progress and the hypothesis-first method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how Europe's methodological success in natural sciences contrasts with its failure in other areas of certainty, and the problematic practice of placing hypotheses before evidence.

Core Idea
Europe advanced in natural sciences but neglected higher certainties, leading to a flawed hypothesis-first approach.

This lesson highlights a crucial comment: while Europe made great strides in natural sciences through empirical methods, it lagged in other certain perceptions. The passage also critiques the method of placing the hypothesis first and then seeking evidence, which corrupts the search for truth in non-empirical fields.

This is particularly problematic for religious truths.

Lists Western thinkers and schools that place religion outside reason, with an applied example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the approaches of William James and Bentham in religious belief, and the problematic example of Abd al-Rahman Badawi's artificial Sufism, as illustrations of Western methodology's shortcomings.

Core Idea
Western thought often tries to save religion by divorcing it from reason, using subjective criteria like benefit or will.

This lesson explores specific examples of Western thinkers who attempted to rationalize religious belief through non-rational means. William James argued for the 'will to believe', Bentham proposed a utilitarian market of beliefs, and Abd al-Rahman Badawi's artificial Sufism demonstrates a lack of objectivity. These examples illustrate the broader failure of Western methodology in dealing with religious truth.

3

It shows how the Western approach can lead to distorted representations of religious phenomena.
