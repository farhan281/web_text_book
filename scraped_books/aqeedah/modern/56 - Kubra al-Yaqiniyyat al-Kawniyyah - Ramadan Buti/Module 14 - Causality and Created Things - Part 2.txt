Ruling on Causal Language and Seeking Blessings from Prophets
Buti answers a question on using causal language and extends to seeking blessings from prophets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand when it is permissible to use causal language about created things and why seeking blessings from prophets is even more permissible.

Core Idea
Causal language is permissible if belief in Allah as the true cause remains intact.

Buti addresses a practical concern: Muslims often use language that attributes effects to created causes. He clarifies that such speech is fine as long as one's belief is sound. He then extends this reasoning to seeking blessings from prophets, showing it is even more acceptable.

2
Understand the condition
The key distinction is whether one believes the created thing causes independently (kufr) or merely appears as a cause while Allah is the true cause (permissible).
3
Apply to prophets
Buti argues that if causal language is permissible for ordinary things, it is even more so for prophets, since they are honored by Allah.
This is an a fortiori argument.

There is no harm in using words that indicate the causality of things for each other if the belief is sound.

Perhaps you ask after this about the ruling of a Muslim using words that express the causality and effect of some things, due to long familiarity and the appearance of these things as effective intrinsic causes, like one's saying: 'This medicine has benefited me,' 'This doctor cured me,' and 'The crop has become abundant due to much rain.' But if it is accompanied by the belief that the true effecter is Allah, majestic is His glory, then there is no harm in it, because his expression is in accordance with the apparent way the universe has been established upon the law of created causation.

And there is no harm in seeking intercession via the messengers and prophets by even greater reason:

If that is the case concerning medicine, doctor, and rain—there is no harm nor offense in it—then it is even more so that there is no harm in that concerning the prophets, like seeking intercession and seeking blessing from them.

Commentary
This passage addresses a practical question: Is it permissible for a Muslim to use language that attributes effects to created things, such as saying 'this medicine helped me' or 'the rain made the crops grow'? The author notes that this might arise from long familiarity. He also addresses seeking blessings from prophets and their relics. The answer is conditional: if such speech is accompanied by the belief that the created thing independently causes the effect, then it is disbelief by consensus. But if the speech is merely an expression of apparent causality while believing that Allah is the true cause, then it is permissible, because it matches the observable law of created causation that Allah has established. Then the author extends this to prophets: if it is permissible for ordinary things like medicine, then it is even more permissible for prophets, since they are honored by Allah. This is an 'a fortiori' argument (من باب أولى).

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage presents the core principle of causality: God alone is the true Causer (musabbib al-asbab). All apparent causes are merely formal (sura) with no intrinsic independent effect. However, using everyday causal language—like when we say 'medicine healed him'—is permissible as long as the Muslim is certain that God is the sole Cause. The author then applies this principle to tawassul (seeking intercession) through the Prophet. Thus, tawassul is not merely a causal link but also a devotional act.
