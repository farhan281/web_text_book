Al-Buti identifies the three specialized sciences for hadith authentication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three specialized Islamic sciences developed for authenticating hadith and how they together form a precise scale to distinguish authentic reports.

Core Idea
Three Islamic sciences form a precise scale to verify hadith authenticity.

Al-Buti introduces the unique methodological tools that Islamic scholarship developed to verify transmitted reports. He explains that these specialized arts—mustalah al-hadith, jarh wa ta'dil, and tarajim al-rijal—are unparalleled in history. This lesson explores each science and how they combine to create a precise standard for distinguishing authentic hadith from others, including the crucial distinction between hadith yielding conjecture and certainty.

Definition of Sahih Hadith and Its Four Conditions
Al-Buti defines sahih hadith and its four conditions for authenticity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the definition of a sahih hadith and the four conditions that must be met, as well as what causes a hadith to be considered unsound.

Core Idea
Authentic hadith requires a continuous chain, upright narrators, and freedom from irregularity and hidden defects.

Al-Buti begins by laying out the foundational criteria for authentic hadith. Before we can accept a prophetic report, we need to verify its chain and content. This passage gives the precise standard that separates authentic reports from defective ones.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without analyzing. Then read the translation. Let the author's definition and conditions sink in.
Notice that the passage defines sahih and then contrasts it with what falls short.
2
Identify the four conditions
Look for the elements that make a hadith sahih: continuous chain, just narrators, no irregularity (shudhudh), and no hidden defect ('illa).
These conditions cover both the chain (sanad) and the text (matn).
3
Understand what violates the conditions
The passage explains that if any condition is missing—such as a broken chain, unjust narrator, textual irregularity, or hidden defect—the hadith is not sahih.
This helps you see why many hadiths are classified as weak.
4
Reflect on the nuance of 'first degree'
Note that sahih is the first degree of authenticity. It does not mean absolute certainty; it is a rigorous but probabilistic judgment.
This addresses a common misunderstanding.

The report rises to the first degree of authenticity (sihha) when it is established—upon investigation and research—that the chain of transmission (sanad) is continuous from the source of this report and its origin, transmitted by a just (adl) transmitter like him to its end from which it originated, without the report containing any irregularity (shudhudh) in its core or any hidden defect ('illa) in its narration. Then if the report falls short of this rank—by a link in the transmission chain being missing due to ignorance of him, or lack of confidence in his justice, or lack of certainty about his memory and precision, or because the text (matn) of the report is irregular relative to what is accepted from others—then it is not authentic.
Source pages p. 35

Commentary
This passage provides a formal definition of a sahih (authentic) hadith. The author states that a report attains the first degree of authenticity when, upon investigation, four conditions are met: (1) the chain of transmission (sanad) is continuous (muttasil) from its origin all the way to the final narrator; (2) all narrators are 'udul (just, upright); (3) the report has no shudhudh (irregularity) in its core meaning; and (4) it has no 'illa (hidden defect) in its narration. If any of these conditions are violated—such as a missing link in the chain due to an unknown narrator, or a narrator lacking justice, or uncertainty about retention and precision, or the text being abnormal compared to accepted transmissions—then the hadith is not sahih. This definition is the standard in hadith sciences.

Degrees of Certainty in Authentic Hadith
Al-Buti explains the spectrum from probable to certain in authentic hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand the three degrees of certainty in authentic hadith and why only mutawatir reports are used for building creed.

Core Idea
Authentic hadith ranges from probable to certain; only the certain (mutawatir) founds creed.

Al-Buti clarifies that within authentic hadith, there are levels of certainty. He describes three levels: ahad reports (probable), reports with two or three narrators (strong probability), and mutawatir (certain). The ruling for creed is that only certain reports are admissible. This lesson breaks down that spectrum and its theological implications.

Guided Path
1
Read the passage carefully
Read the Arabic passage once without trying to analyse it. Then read the translation. Let the author's own rhythm come through before you interpret.
Notice how Al-Buti uses a gradual progression from 'zann' to 'yaqin'.
2
Identify the three levels
The passage presents three levels of hadith based on the number of narrators in each chain link. Find each one and note the degree of certainty assigned.
The levels are: ahad (few narrators), two or three narrators, and multitudes (mutawatir).
3
Understand the decisive rule
The concluding sentence sets a doctrinal boundary: probable reports are not used in creed. Ask yourself why this rule is important for Islamic theology.
This connects to the learning objective.
4
Reflect on the implication
Consider how this classification affects the use of hadith in matters of faith. Not all sahih hadith are equal in certainty; only mutawatir gives yaqin.

But the authentic itself rises in varying degrees, starting from strong conjecture to certain perception. If the chain, which possesses the elements of authenticity, consists of individual narrators (ahad) through whom the report is transmitted, then it is nothing but a probable (zanni) report in the judgment of reason. If the links of the chain consist of two or three narrators, it remains a probable report, but it is a strong probability near to certainty. If every link of the chains becomes, due to multitude, groups (jumu') about which the mind is assured that they could not collude on lying, then the transmitted report acquires at that time the quality of certainty, and it is called the mutawatir report. As for the probable from the authentic report, Islamic ruling does not rely on it in the construction of creed ('aqida).
Source pages p. 35

Commentary
This passage explains the degrees of certainty within authentic hadith. The author states that even sahih hadith varies in strength, ranging from strong conjecture to certain knowledge. He outlines three levels: (1) If the chain is composed of a few individual narrators (ahad) at each level, the hadith remains probable (zanni) in the judgment of reason. (2) If there are two or three narrators at each level, it is still probable but a strong probability approaching certainty. (3) If at each level there are so many narrators that the mind is assured they could not have conspired to lie, then the report acquires the quality of certainty (yaqin), and this is called mutawatir. Finally, the author concludes that for the construction of creed ('aqida), Islamic ruling does not rely on probable hadith—only mutawatir or certain reports are used. This is a crucial theological point linking methodology to doctrine.

Human Origin: Known Only Through Revelation
Buti argues human creation from earth and descent from Adam is proven solely by transmitted revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that the origin of humans from earth and from Adam is a matter of transmitted revelation, not empirical science.

Core Idea
Our origin is known by reliable reports, not by observation.

Buti begins by stating two facts about human origin: we are created from earth as a species, and we descend from Adam. He then emphasizes that these facts cannot be proven by science or observation—they are known only through divinely transmitted reports (khabar mutawātir). This lesson explores why this matters and how the Quran provides the evidence.
