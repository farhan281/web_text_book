True Faith and the Demand for Scientific Rigor
Buti affirms that true Muslims do not surrender their faith to modern scientific claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand that true Muslims hold their faith above all else and that Islam is not opposed to genuine science, but demands rigorous definitions from its critics.

Core Idea
True faith is not negotiable, and science must be properly defined before critique.

Buti here addresses a common accusation that Islam hinders scientific progress. He begins by stating firmly that sincere Muslims will never abandon their faith for any scientific claim. Then he turns the tables on the accuser: instead of defending Islam, he demands that the critic provide a clear definition of science and a method to distinguish it from ignorance. This shifts the burden of proof and reveals the weakness of vague accusations.

Guided Path

3
Notice the shift

4
Reflect on the demand
Buti asks for a specific definition and a precise methodology. What does this reveal about the nature of the accusation he is responding to?

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument answering the question posed at the beginning: what power can control the destructive potential of human faculties and direct them solely toward righteousness? The author, Ramadan al-Buti, identifies true religion as the bridle that protects humanity from the danger of these innate qualities. He then defines the content of that true religion: a correct creed concerning man, the universe, life, and the afterlife. This creed, guided by reason and science, consists of faith in God's existence and oneness, His exclusive sovereignty and power, His creatorship of all else, His watchfulness over all, and His resurrection and just recompense. Belief in this creed, when it is firm and based on free rational inquiry, instills in a person the deep sense of being a servant of the one great God. As a result, the dangerous yet important human qualities—like possessiveness, power-seeking, and the drive for knowledge—are transformed from sources of conflict and oppression into constructive forces: possessiveness becomes a means for just prosperity, power becomes a tool for protecting rights and justice, and knowledge becomes a light that confirms God's existence and warns against exceeding servitude. The author summarizes that Islamic creed humbles the arrogant and elevates the oppressed, establishing just equality that eliminates exploitation. He cites historical evidence and the Quranic story of Pharaoh and the oppressed Israelites (Qasas 28:4-6) as a clear example. The passage concludes by reaffirming humanity's essential need to profess absolute servitude to God, emphasizing that God is not in need of this devotion.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained argument answering the question posed at the beginning: what power can control the destructive potential of human faculties and direct them solely toward righteousness? The author, Ramadan al-Buti, identifies true religion as the bridle that protects humanity from the danger of these innate qualities. The Social Impact of Islamic Creed and Quranic Evidence
Buti summarizes the social effect of Islamic creed and provides Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Islamic creed establishes social justice and equality, as exemplified by the story of Pharaoh.

Core Idea
Islamic creed humbles the arrogant and elevates the oppressed, creating just equality.

Buti concludes his argument by summarizing the social function of the Islamic creed: it brings down the tyrants and raises the oppressed. He cites the Quranic story of Moses and Pharaoh as a clear example.

Guided Path
1
Read the summary of social function
The passage begins with a concise statement: the Islamic creed brings down the 'متألهين والمتكبرين' (deifiers and arrogant) and raises the 'دهماء والمستضعفين' (common people and oppressed).
This is a powerful summary of social justice.
2
Observe the just limits

Equality does not mean sameness but just boundaries.
3
Examine the Quranic example

The verse is key evidence from revelation.

Servitude to God is the basis for freedom from human tyranny.

On the difference between Islam and Iman. Now that we have finished explaining the truths related to the section of prophecies, we have thus completed the analysis of the second part of the testimony of Islam, which is: the testimony that Muhammad is the Messenger of God. And thereby we have completed the explanation of the two parts of the testimony that must be affirmed for a Muslim's Islam to be complete. That, if you believe in the researches and truths related to the divine matters, and believe in what we have mentioned after of the truths related to prophecies, and submit to the obligations of prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, and zakat, being certain of their obligation and necessity to perform them, in execution of the command of God Almighty and realization of your servitude to Him — then the components of the Islam with which the prophets in general and our Prophet Muhammad in particular were sent have become available to you. Perhaps you ask: Is there a difference between the realities of Iman and Islam? And is there a distinction between their conditions? The answer is that there is a difference between what each of Islam and Iman applies to, except that there is a necessary connection between them in reality. For Islam, as you have known, is submission and compliance to both testimonies and the mentioned obligations, and it necessarily involves utterance with the tongue. In uttering the two testimonies, it is not sufficient unless one expresses the word 'I bear witness.' And one must submit in explicit wording to the obligation of the obligations that God Almighty has commanded. Thus, Islam is submission with the apparent being of a person; the application of the rulings of Islam in this world depends on it, such as inviolability of blood, permissibility of marriage, and legal inheritance. As for Iman, it is heartfelt belief in all of that, so that there remains no doubt in the soul concerning anything of what we have mentioned of the truths of Islam, and salvation on the Day of Judgment before God Almighty depends on it. And it becomes clear from this that a person does not have the rulings of Islam applied to him in both this world and the Hereafter together unless he is characterized by both Islam and Iman, by being inwardly convinced with his heart and outwardly acknowledging with his tongue. No matter how much a person utters the two testimonies and others, that does not avail him anything in reality unless he believes and is convinced of that in the depths of his heart. Rather, the rulings of this world are applied only to the outward, due to impossibility of knowing the inward, and taking the tongue as carrying truthfulness in speech. However, disagreement has occurred among the imams concerning [whether] if a man is a believer with his heart only, does that save him on the Day of Judgment, or is it not sufficient for him until he also acknowledges and declares with his tongue? Al-Nawawi transmitted from a group of scholars that mere heart certainty is not sufficient for salvation on the Day of Judgment if it is possible to acknowledge and utter with the tongue. Ibn Hajar, in his commentary on al-Arba‘in al-Nawawiyya, preferred what the majority of Ash‘aris and some verifying scholars of the Hanafis held, that verbal acknowledgment is only a condition for the application of worldly rulings, while on the Day of Judgment, heart certainty suffices him. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the issue of 'judging by other than what Allah has revealed' (al-hukm bi ghayr ma anzala Allah). The author begins by reminding the reader of a previously established principle concerning the limits of takfir, stating that once this principle is understood, one will not get lost in the many detailed examples. He then presents a specific case that is frequently discussed today: a person who judges by other than Allah's law in matters concerning himself, his family, or those under his authority.

The author then distinguishes two scenarios:
1. If the person's judgment is accompanied by decisive evidence (dalil qati') that he is denying Allah's law (juhud), or claiming that Islamic rulings are unsuitable for life, or showing contempt (izdira') for them, and the judgment he replaced was a matter known from the religion by necessity (ma'ruf min al-din bi al-darurah), then this act is one of the causes of apostasy (riddah). Even if the person continues to perform Islamic acts like prayer, he is considered an apostate unless he repents by declaring that Islamic Sharia is entirely suitable and that his previous judgment was false.
2. If there is no such decisive evidence, and the act could be due to heedlessness (istihrar), responding to whims (ra'unat al-nafs), or escape from restrictions, then takfir is not permissible. The person is not to be treated as an apostate, no matter how weak the evidence for this possibility is. The ruling is limited to fisq (sin) and 'isyan (disobedience), leaving the inner state to Allah.

The author then emphasizes the underlying principle: the basis of faith and disbelief is inward belief (i'tiqad). If a statement or action leads to a ruling of takfir, it is because that statement or action is a decisive indicator (dalalat qati'ah) of a disbelieving creed. If it is not a decisive indicator, then the ruling of apostasy cannot be applied.

To illustrate, the author quotes Imam Ahmad: if someone says 'wine is halal,' he is a kafir because his statement clearly indicates denial of a known prohibition. But if someone merely drinks wine, eats pork or carrion without declaring it halal, he is not automatically an apostate because it is possible he does so while still believing it is prohibited (mu'taqidan tahrimah).

The author concludes by stating that this is the consensus of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah (the majority of Muslims). Only the Khawarij and the Wa'idiyyah deviated from this consensus: the Khawarij declare takfir for major sins, and the Wa'idiyyah hold that sinful Muslims will abide eternally in Hell, contradicting the verse from Surah al-Nisa (4:48) which promises forgiveness for sins other than shirk. The author notes that the reconciliation between the apparently threatening verses and this verse has already been explained elsewhere.
