The Five Universals and Their Role in Definition
Author defines the five universals and their relation to definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five universals in a given definition and distinguish between essential and accidental attributes.

Core Idea
The five universals are the building blocks of every complete definition.

In this passage, the author introduces the five universals (al-kulliyyat al-khams) that constitute the components of definitions. Understanding these five is crucial for analyzing any definition in the Islamic sciences, whether in fiqh, usul, or kalam. The author explains that a real definition (hadd haqiqi) is composed of essential attributes, while other definitions may use accidental attributes. We will parse the five universals and see how they relate to the particular (juz'i).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of attributes
The author divides attributes into essential (dhatiyya) and accidental ('aradiyya). Find the sentence that states this distinction.
The first sentence: 'الحد إنما يتألف من الصفات الذاتية إن كان حقيقياً، وإلا فلا بد من الخمس التي العرضية.'
2
Distinguish shared vs distinctive
Within each category, attributes are either shared (mushtarak) between the defined and others, or distinctive (mumayyiz) for it. Match these with the five universals.
3
Map the five universals
For each of the five, note its name and whether it is essential/accidental and shared/distinctive. Create a mental table.
Essential shared: genus (jins); essential distinctive: differentia (fasl); combination of both: species (naw'); accidental shared: accidental general ('arad 'amm); accidental distinctive: property (khassa).
4
Understand the particular
The author defines the particular (juz'i) as that which prevents sharing. Compare it with the universal (kulli).
The universal can be predicated of many; the particular cannot be shared in conception.
5
Examine the caveats
The author notes that 'property' and 'species' can be used loosely. Identify these caveats and why they matter.
Property may be used for non-universal attributes, but then it does not distinguish. Species may be relative (higher is genus, lower is species).

He also mentions that 'species' can be used relatively. Finally, he introduces the particular (juz'i) as the opposite of the universal, which cannot be shared.

Parsing the Logicians' Complete Definition
Ibn Taymiyya states the logicians' view of complete definition using essential properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a complete definition (hadd tamm) according to Islamic logicians and distinguish essential properties (dhatiyat) from accidental properties (aradiyat) in a given example.

Core Idea
The complete definition relies on a distinction between essential and accidental that Ibn Taymiyya will question.

Before we enter Ibn Taymiyya's critique, we must first understand the position he is attacking. The logicians claim that a complete definition (hadd tamm) must be composed solely of essential properties (dhatiyat), not accidental ones (aradiyat). In this passage, he presents their view and their famous example: for 'human', the essential differentia is 'rational' and the accidental specific property is 'laughing'. We will parse this terminology to see exactly what is at stake.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of complete definition
The logicians define the complete definition as one composed of 'genus' (jins) and 'differentia' (fasl) from the 'essential properties' (dhatiyat), both shared and distinguishing. Accidental properties (aradiyat) like general accident and specific property are excluded.
This sets the standard for what counts as a proper definition in Aristotelian logic.
2
List the two types of essential properties
Essential properties are of two kinds: shared essentials (al-dhatiyat al-mushtaraka) which form the genus, and distinguishing essentials (al-dhatiyat al-mumayyiza) which form the differentia.
The text mentions 'al-dhatiyat al-mushtaraka wa al-mumayyiza'.
3
List the two types of accidental properties
Accidental properties are divided into general accident (al-arad al-amm) and specific property (al-khassa). They are not part of the essence and thus excluded from complete definition.
For example, for humans, 'laughing' (al-dahik) is a specific property.
4
Apply the example to the human case
Identify the differentia and the specific property for 'human' as given in the text: differentia = 'rational' (natiq), specific property = 'laughing' (dahik). Note that genus is not explicitly mentioned but is commonly 'animal'.
This example is standard in Islamic logic texts.
5
Recognize the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique
Ibn Taymiyya will argue that the very distinction between essential and accidental is invalid, thus undermining the foundation of the complete definition. This passage is setting up that critique.
Understanding the logicians' position is essential to follow the refutation.

This sets up Ibn Taymiyya's critique, which he will develop in the subsequent text, arguing that the distinction between essential and accidental is invalid and thus the definition based on it is baseless.

The Logicians' Three Distinctions
Exposition and internal critique of distinctions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three distinctions the logicians use to separate essential from accidental and explain why the logicians' own verifiers found them insufficient.

Core Idea
The logicians' three distinctions between essential and accidental are neither coherent nor sufficient — and their own verifiers admit it.

The logicians claimed that essential predicates are inside the reality of the subject, on which the reality depends, and prior to the quiddity, while accidental ones are outside, not depended upon, and subsequent. But even their own verifiers criticized each distinction. Let's examine them carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first distinction
Find where the passage says the essential is 'inside the reality' (dākhil fī al-ḥaqīqa) and the accidental is 'outside the reality' (khārij). This is the most immediate criterion.
This distinction is intuitive but fails for properties like 'wetness' for water, which seems inside but is actually accidental.
2
Identify the second distinction
Locate the claim that the essential is 'that upon which the reality depends' (tatawaqqaf al-ḥaqīqa ʿalayhi). The accidental does not have this dependency.
Dependency is ambiguous: does it mean existence or conceptual dependence?
3
Identify the third distinction
Find the statement that the essential is 'caused by the quiddity' (maʿlūl li al-māhiyya) and the accidental necessary is not, or is caused by a middle term.
This introduces causality, which itself is contested among logicians.
4
Examine the subdivision of the accidental
Note that the accidental is divided into necessary (lāzim) and contingent (ʿāriḍ). The necessary is further split into necessary for existence (e.g., shadow for a horse) and necessary for the quiddity (e.g., evenness for four).
The necessary for existence can be conceptually separated; the necessary for quiddity cannot.
5
Consider the internal criticism
The passage states that the logicians' own verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) criticized each of these three distinctions, showing they do not actually distinguish essential from accidental.
This indicates that the framework is fundamentally flawed from within the tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' (mainly Avicennan) distinctions between essential (dhātī) and accidental (ʿaraḍī) predicates. He begins by reporting their first distinction: the essential is inside the reality (dhāt) of the subject, while the accidental is outside. Then he reports a second distinction: the essential is that on which the reality depends; the accidental is that without which the reality still holds. They further subdivide the accidental into the necessary (lāzim) and the contingent (ʿāriḍ), and the necessary into necessary for the existence of the quiddity (like a horse's shadow) and necessary for the quiddity itself (like evenness for four). The difference is: if the quiddity can be conceived without the necessary, it is necessary for existence; if not, it is necessary for the quiddity. A third distinction: the essential is caused by the quiddity, while the necessary is not. Fourth: some necessary things are caused by the quiddity without a middle term, others through a middle. Fifth: the essential is prior to the quiddity in both mind and external world, while the necessary is subsequent. After listing these, Ibn Taymiyya notes that the logicians' own verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) have criticized each distinction and found them insufficient. He then adds further formulations: the essential is that whose absence precedes the quiddity in existence and conception, and that it does not lag behind in conceptualization. He also discusses their definition of 'cause' (ʿilla) as marked by the lām in 'because' (li-annahu), noting that later logicians like al-Rāzī misunderstood it as an attribute, increasing confusion. He concludes that the logicians have contradicted themselves, and his aim is to show the falsity of many of their logical claims by highlighting these fundamental problems.

Ibn Taymiyya's Broader Critique of the Distinctions
Critique and exposure of contradictions in logical distinctions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the logicians' distinctions are self-contradictory and fail due to confusion about key terms like 'illa.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya exposes that the logicians themselves contradict each other and their criteria are so confused they do not actually distinguish essential from accidental.

After listing the logicians' three distinctions, Ibn Taymiyya reports that the verifiers criticized each one. He then adds that later logicians like al-Rāzī misunderstood key terms like 'illa (cause), increasing confusion. The passage ends with his claim that many logical statements are falsified by these contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the purpose of the distinctions
The logicians aim to separate essential predicates (constitutive of the quiddity) from accidental ones. The three distinctions are their criteria.
2
Identify the internal criticism
The author states that the logicians' own verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) found each of the three distinctions insufficient to actually distinguish essential from non-essential.
This suggests the framework is flawed from within.
3
Analyze the confusion about 'illa
The logicians defined 'illa (cause) by the lām in 'li-annahu' (because). But al-Rāzī misunderstood it as an attribute subsisting in the described, which corrupted the meaning.
This confusion affects the third distinction (causality).
4
Recognize the contradictions
Ibn Taymiyya claims the logicians contradicted themselves, referring to his earlier expansions on their expressions and contradictions.
The passage does not list the contradictions, but signals that they exist.
5
Draw the final conclusion
The author's intent is to alert to comprehensive points that show the falsity of many logical statements, implying that the entire logical edifice based on these distinctions is unsound.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' (mainly Avicennan) distinctions between essential (dhātī) and accidental (ʿaraḍī) predicates. He begins by reporting their first distinction: the essential is inside the reality (dhāt) of the subject, while the accidental is outside. Then he reports a second distinction: the essential is that on which the reality depends; the accidental is that without which the reality still holds. They further subdivide the accidental into the necessary (lāzim) and the contingent (ʿāriḍ), and the necessary into necessary for the existence of the quiddity (like a horse's shadow) and necessary for the quiddity itself (like evenness for four). After listing these, Ibn Taymiyya notes that the logicians' own verifiers (muḥaqqiqūn) have criticized each distinction and found them insufficient. He then adds further formulations: the essential is that whose absence precedes the quiddity in existence and conception, and that it does not lag behind in conceptualization. He also discusses their definition of 'cause' (ʿilla) as marked by the lām in 'because' (li-annahu), noting that later logicians like al-Rāzī misunderstood it as an attribute, increasing confusion. He concludes that the logicians have contradicted themselves, and his aim is to show the falsity of many of their logical claims by highlighting these fundamental problems.

Ibn Taymiyya on Arbitrary Essential vs. Accidental Classification
Author shows logicians are inconsistent in classifying attributes as essential or accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the essential/accidental distinction and identify his key examples of inconsistency.

Core Idea
The logicians' classification of attributes is arbitrary, not grounded in reality.

Ibn Taymiyya targets a foundational distinction in Aristotelian logic: the difference between essential (dhati) attributes and accidental necessary concomitants (aradi lazim). He argues that this distinction is not objective but a matter of arbitrary preference. In this lesson, we will follow his reasoning and examine his key examples from numbers, animals, and colors. By the end, you will be able to explain why he claims the logicians contradict themselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
The logicians claim a real distinction between 'essential' (dhati) attributes, which are part of the definition, and 'accidental necessary' (aradi lazim) attributes, which necessarily accompany but are not part of the definition. Ibn Taymiyya denies this distinction has reality.
2
Follow Ibn Taymiyya's first analogy
He compares evenness/oddness for numbers to rationality/neighing for animals. In both cases, one cannot mentally separate the attribute from the subject when both are conceived together. This suggests the two types are similar in terms of mental inseparability.
The logicians typically treat rationality as essential but evenness as accidental.
3
Analyze the counter-argument and response
The logician might object that one can conceive of 'four' without thinking 'even'. Ibn Taymiyya retorts that one can conceive of 'man' without thinking 'rational' or 'animal'. If that is called an incomplete conception, the same applies to numbers. Thus, the claimed difference collapses.
This exposes the arbitrariness of which incompleteness is considered acceptable.
4
Examine the blackness/color example
One can think of 'blackness' without thinking 'color' or 'accident', but one cannot mentally suppose blackness without subsistence in another. Ibn Taymiyya argues this necessity is even stronger than for animality in man, yet logicians do not treat it as essential.
This further undermines the basis of the distinction.
5
Identify the inconsistency in logicians' examples
Logicians treat animality and rationality as essential, but numberity and parity as non-essential. For color-ness, they vacillate: sometimes essential like animality, sometimes non-essential like numberity. Ibn Taymiyya attributes this to their reliance on whether the attribute is known immediately or through proof.
The inconsistency reveals the classification is based on epistemological convenience, not objective reality.

Refuting the Logicians' Criterion: Essential vs. Necessary Accident
Author refutes the logicians' distinction between essential and necessary accident using the criterion of the middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the logicians' distinction between essential and necessary accidental attributes fails according to Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Core Idea
Some necessary accidental attributes are known directly without a middle term, disproving the logicians' criterion.

The logicians attempted to distinguish essential (dhātī) attributes from necessary accidental (ʿaraḍī lāzim) attributes by claiming that essential attributes are known without a middle term or cause, while necessary accidents require evidence. Ibn Taymiyya refutes this by pointing to attributes that are both necessary and known directly. Let's examine his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' distinction
The passage states that logicians distinguish essential from necessary accidental attributes using the presence or absence of a middle term (wasṭ) or cause (ʿilla). Essential attributes are claimed to be known without such evidence.
2
Find the author's counterexample
The author asserts that among necessary accidents (al-lawāzim) there are some that are established without any middle term or cause. Look for the phrase: 'من اللوازم ما يثبت بغير وسط'.
This is the key claim that undermines the distinction.
3
Analyze the meaning of 'middle term'
The author distinguishes between mental affirmation (external proof) and external existence, and differentiates between burhān limā (demonstration of the why) and burhān anna (demonstration of the that). The middle term can refer to either, and people differ in their need for evidence.
This shows the term is ambiguous and not a fixed criterion.
4
Conclude the invalidity
Since some necessary accidents are known without any middle term, the alleged distinction fails: the criterion 'essential = without middle term' does not exclude necessary accidents. The author notes that even Ibn Sīnā invalidated this distinction.
The critique is decisive on both interpretations of 'middle term'.

Ibn Taymiyya continues his critique of the logicians' categories of certainties. Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the nature of premises used in Aristotelian logic. Ibn Taymiyya begins by discussing empirical premises (mujarrabāt): they are essentially particular, because experience occurs on specific occasions. Commentary
This passage is a critique of the logicians' claim regarding moral judgments. Ibn Taymiyya argues that good (ḥusn) and evil (qubḥ) are not merely perceived pleasures or pains, as some claim, but are objective and known by universal agreement. He states that knowledge, justice, truthfulness, and benevolence are inherently agreeable to humans, and the pleasure in them is greater than in other things. This constitutes the meaning of an act being good; its opposite constitutes evil. Once the concepts of good and evil are understood, it becomes clear that these widely accepted propositions (mashhūrāt) are among the greatest certainties—even greater than abstract rational principles like the law of non-contradiction. For many people may never conceptualize universal rational principles, but every group of people approves of justice, knowledge, and benevolence and disapproves of their opposites. This universal agreement is based on sense, reason, and experience, making moral judgments more certain than purely rational axioms. He then turns to wisdom (ḥikma), which the logicians also divide into theoretical and practical. He insists that practical wisdom, which includes ethics, household management, and politics, is built entirely on these certain moral propositions. Quoting Ibn Qutayba, he affirms that wisdom is both knowledge and action. Moreover, every commanded action requires justice, which is universally commanded and injustice universally forbidden. The best laws and methodologies (i.e., divine revelations) perfect this. He supports this with Quranic verses commanding justice in all situations and prohibiting injustice, and concludes with the famous divine hadith (ḥadīth qudsī) where God declares He has forbidden injustice and emphasizes His independence and mercy. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya is arguing against the logicians' tendency to marginalize practical wisdom and ethical certainty in favor of abstract theoretical reasoning.
