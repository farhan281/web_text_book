Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Essence-Existence Distinction
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' claim that essence is distinct from existence in external reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the real distinction between essence and existence and identify his reduction of it to a mental distinction.

Core Idea
For Ibn Taymiyya, essence and existence are not two things in the world; the distinction is purely mental.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes on a foundational principle of Avicennan metaphysics: the separation of essence (māhiyya) from existence (wujūd) in external reality. He argues that this distinction is based on a false inference from mental knowledge to external subsistence. Let's trace his critique step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two corrupt principles
Ibn Taymiyya states that the logicians' entire discussion is built on two false principles: first, the distinction between essence and its existence; second, the distinction between essential and necessary. Focus on the first principle.
2
Unpack the logicians' claim
The logicians claim that the essence has a reality (thubūt) in the external world other than its existence. Ibn Taymiyya compares this to the Mu'tazilite view that the non-existent is a thing.
3
Trace the origin of their error
The logicians observed that before a thing exists, it can be known, intended, and distinguished from impossibilities. From this they inferred that it must have some subsistence in the external world.
This is a non sequitur: mental existence does not entail external subsistence.
4
Ibn Taymiyya's counter
Ibn Taymiyya argues that this subsistence is purely mental. What is known and intended is a mental entity, not something external. Thus, the distinction between existence and subsistence is only in the mind, not in external reality.
He emphasizes that mental concepts can be broader than external existence.

Commentary
This passage is a direct critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the ontological distinction that logicians (particularly Avicennan) make between essence (māhiyya) and existence (wujūd). He states that their entire discussion is built on two false principles: first, the separation of essence from existence, and second, the separation of essential from necessary. He focuses on the first. The logicians claim that the essence of a thing has a reality in the external world that is separate from its existence. This, Ibn Taymiyya says, resembles the Mu'tazilite view that the non-existent is a thing (shay'), which he considers extremely corrupt. The root of their mistake is that they observe that before a thing exists, it can be known, desired, and distinguished from impossibilities; from this they infer that it must have some sort of reality (thubūt) in the external world. They reason: if it had no reality, it could not be known. But Ibn Taymiyya counters that this reality is purely mental (dhihnī), not external. The object of knowledge is a mental entity, not something in the external world apart from existence. He emphasizes that what is in the mind may be broader than what actually exists, and it has no external existence or subsistence. Therefore, to distinguish between existence (wujūd) and subsistence (thubūt) and then claim both are in the external world is a grave error. The passage thus rejects any extra-mental reality for essences apart from their existence.

The Five Substances in Aristotelian Logic
Author enumerates the five substances and their definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and describe the five substances (form, matter, body, soul, intellect) as classified by the logicians.

Core Idea
The five substances are the building blocks of every physical and immaterial being.

The logicians divided substance into five categories based on inherence, substrate, and relation. Understanding this classification is crucial for engaging with later debates in kalam and falsafa, as well as for distinguishing between different conceptions of substance in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
Understand the two axes: inhering vs not inhering, substrate vs not substrate. The four combinations give the classification.
2
Map each category to its substance
First (inhering, not substrate) is form; second (substrate, not inhering) is matter; third (neither inhering nor substrate but related by governance) is soul; fourth (neither inhering nor substrate nor related) is intellect. Composite of form and matter is body.
3
Note the difference in definitions
The logicians' substance includes hylomorphic composites, while the mutakallimūn define substance as self-subsistent (jawhar fard).

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition of the logicians' division of substance. The author (Ibn Taymiyya) first enumerates their fivefold classification: form (the principle in a subject), matter (the substrate, called hyle), body (composite), soul (governing but not in a subject), and intellect (completely separate). He notes that most logicians consider form a substance in the category of substance, but later thinkers like Ibn Sina objected, arguing that substance must have existence not in a subject, which form lacks since it exists in matter. The author dismisses this as a verbal dispute; the predecessors defined substance more broadly as anything not in a subject (self-subsistent, occupying space, bearing attributes, etc.). He then attacks the underlying philosophical claim that existence is superadded to essence for possibles, and that form's existence is negative, calling both false. Finally, he remarks that the division of existence into necessary and possible is specific to Ibn Sina and his school, not universally accepted. For example, a mutakallim defines substance as a self-subsistent atom (jawhar fard), not as the philosophers' hylomorphic composite.

Author critiques Ibn Sina's denial that form is a substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's argument that form is not a substance and evaluate the author's critique that it is a verbal dispute.

The dispute over whether form is a substance hinges on definitions, not reality.

Ibn Sina and some later logicians denied that form (الصورة) is a substance, arguing that substance must have existence not in a subject. The author (Ibn Taymiyya) counters that this is a mere terminological disagreement and that the underlying metaphysical claims are false.

1
Identify Ibn Sina's definition of substance
Find the definition: 'Substance is that which, when it exists, its existence is not in a subject.' This is a key premise.
2
Apply to form
Form exists in matter, so according to this definition, form fails to be a substance.
3
Understand the author's rebuttal
The dispute is verbal because predecessors defined substance differently (e.g., self-subsistent, occupying space, bearing attributes). The author says there is no 'valid reasonable distinction' (فرق صحيح معقول).
4
Examine the deeper disagreement

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Avicenna on Substance
Author lists the five substances and criticizes Avicenna's new definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Avicenna's redefinition of substance and reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of it as a verbal dispute.

Core Idea
The dispute over whether form is a substance is merely terminological, not a real difference.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya lays out the philosophers' division of substance into five types, but then focuses on a controversy: Avicenna's refusal to call form a substance. Ibn Taymiyya argues that Avicenna's definition is a terminological shift, not a substantive distinction. Understanding this helps you see how logical definitions can be manipulated and why the Islamic tradition must ground its categories in reality, not convention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five substances
List the five divisions of substance as given by the philosophers: form, matter, body (composite), soul, and intellect.
Note that body is not a separate category but a composite of form and matter.
2
Locate Avicenna's redefinition
Find the definition of substance that excludes form: 'Substance is that whose existence is not in a subject.' Avicenna uses this to deny form the status of substance because form exists in matter.
The key phrase: 'الجوهر ما إذا وُجد كان وجوده لا في موضوع'.
3
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's objection
Ibn Taymiyya calls the dispute verbal (niza' lafzi) because earlier philosophers defined substance more broadly. He points out that Avicenna's definition is merely terminological and does not provide a sound rational distinction.
The critique is that the disagreement is about the label, not the reality.
4
Unpack the metaphysical basis
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that Avicenna's real (ma'nawi) distinction is based on two false claims: that existence is superadded to quiddity, and that form's existence is restricted by negations.
These are deeper ontological issues that Ibn Taymiyya says are false, but he does not elaborate here.

The Unproven Ten Categories
Author argues that the ten categories have no demonstrable proof, citing internal disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical grounds on which Ibn Taymiyya rejects the exhaustive enumeration of the ten categories.

Core Idea
The ten categories are an article of faith, not a rational proof.

Ibn Taymiyya here delivers a devastating blow to the Aristotelian framework of categories. He points out that the logicians themselves admit they cannot verify the completeness of the list, and they even disagree among themselves. This lesson walks you through his critique and shows how it applies to any claim of exhaustive classification.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the target claim
The target is the Aristotelian assertion that all divisions of existence are exhaustively enumerated in ten categories: substance and nine accidents. Locate where the author states 'لا دليل معهم على حصر أقسام الوجود في المقولات العشر'.
The claim is about enumeration (حصره), not about the categories' existence per se.
2
2. Find the internal concession
Notice that the author reports that the logicians 'agreed that there is no way to know the correctness of this enumeration' (اتفاقوا على أنه لا سبيل إلى معرفة صحة هذا الحصر). This is a crucial concession—they admit the list is unverifiable.
This is not Ibn Taymiyya's attack; he is highlighting their own admission.
3
3. Note the internal disagreement
The text then states that some logicians reduced the genera of accidents to three, and other opinions exist. This shows the categories are not even universally agreed upon among their proponents.
If the categories were self-evident or proven, there would be no such disagreement.
4
4. Evaluate the critique
Ibn Taymiyya's argument is: a claim that is unverifiable and disputed cannot be accepted as a definitive classification. He does not replace it with an alternative list; he simply rejects the pretension to exhaustive knowledge.
This is a common pattern in his critiques: he undermines the epistemological basis rather than offering a competing system.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of Simultaneous Existence for Correlatives
Ibn Taymiyya refutes simultaneous existence requirement for correlatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate Ibn Taymiyya's two arguments against the claim that every correlation requires both correlates to exist simultaneously.

Core Idea
Not all relations need both terms present; precedence and legal causes show otherwise.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a fundamental assumption of the logicians: that every relational attribute (iḍāfa) demands the coexistence of both relata. In this passage, he deconstructs this claim using two powerful arguments: a regress argument and concrete counterexamples from ordinary language and Islamic jurisprudence. By the end, you will see how a universal logical principle can be dismantled by a single counterexample from fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
Find the statement that the cause does not independently necessitate the attribute of causality; rather, both cause and effect together necessitate it. This implies that the relation of causality requires both terms to exist simultaneously.
The opponent is likely a philosopher or logician who treats all relations as requiring simultaneous existence.
2
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's regress argument
Ibn Taymiyya argues that if the opponent's view is accepted, then knowledge of the cause would require knowledge of the effect as well, leading to an infinite regress or a problematic interdependence. Understand how this reveals an inconsistency.
This is an internal critique: the opponent's premises lead to undesirable consequences.
3
Examine the counterexamples from precedence and fiqh
Ibn Taymiyya offers two types of counterexamples: (1) relations of priority ('this is before that') where the earlier exists before the later, and (2) legal causes (asbāb) like ownership of the nisāb, which is described as a cause of zakāh before zakāh is due. Verify that in these cases the relation exists with only one correlate present.
These are real-world examples that violate the alleged universal rule.
4
Assess the force of the refutation
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's arguments successfully refute the universal claim. The regress argument shows an internal problem; the counterexamples directly falsify the claim. Conclude that the opponent's doctrine is not universally valid.
Ibn Taymiyya's method is to use both logical consequences and empirical evidence.
