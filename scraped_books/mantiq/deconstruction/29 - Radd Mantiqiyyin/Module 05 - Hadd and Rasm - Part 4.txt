The Linguistic Meaning of 'Falak' as Circular Motion
Ibn Taymiyya explains the root meaning of 'فلك' as circular motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the core linguistic meaning of the Arabic word 'فلك' as circular motion, based on Quranic and lexical evidence.

Core Idea
The word 'falak' in Arabic fundamentally denotes circular motion, not just the celestial sphere.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin examines the literal meaning of 'فلك' as used in the Quran and classical Arabic. By tracing its root in the language, Ibn Taymiyya shows that the word inherently conveys rotation and circularity, a point often overlooked by those who restrict it to the celestial sphere alone. Understanding this lexical foundation is crucial for interpreting the Quranic verse 'All in an orbit they swim.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic verse and the key term
Read the verse (Q. 36:40) and locate the word 'فلك'. This is the term whose meaning is being investigated.
2
Extract interpretations from early authorities
Look at the statements of al-Dahhak and Ibn Abbas. Both explain 'فلك' as rotation or circular motion, and associate 'سباحة' (swimming) with speed.
3
Connect to classical lexical evidence
Consider al-Jawhari's examples: 'فلكة المغزل' (spindle-whorl) named for its roundness, and 'فلكة' meaning a round piece of land. This confirms that the root indicates circularity.
4
Synthesize the core meaning
Combine the Quranic context, early interpretations, and lexical evidence to conclude that 'فلك' fundamentally denotes circular motion.

How is the Week Known? Sense vs. Revelation
Author explains how day and month are known by sense, and week only by prophetic news.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between time periods known by sense and those known only by prophetic reports, and explain the epistemological implications.

Core Idea
The week has no sensible or rational basis; its only proof is prophetic news of creation in six days.

Ibn Taymiyya makes a striking claim: while day, month, and year are naturally observable, the seven-day week is purely a revealed concept. This passage from his refutation of the logicians shows how epistemology (how we know things) is shaped by revelation. You will learn to identify which time concepts are a priori (sensible) and which are a posteriori (prophetic).

Guided Path
1
Identify the time periods listed
List the four periods: day, month, year, week. Note that day, month, and year are grouped together as sensible, while the week is separate.
2
Note the epistemic source for each
Day is known by sense (hiss) and observation (mushahada). Month and year are known by counting in lunar calendars and by sighting in solar. The week is known only through prophetic reports (akhbar al-anbiya').
The Quranic example of the People of the Cave illustrates the difference between solar and lunar years.
3
Understand the argument for the week
The week has no definition known by sense or reason. It is known only because prophets reported that Allah created the world in six days. Therefore, the week is a revealed concept.
Allah legislated weekly gatherings to preserve this knowledge.
4
Apply the principle
The passage concludes that custom follows conceptualization. Those lacking prophetic revelation do not have names for the days of the week, as they lack the concept.
This is observed in the case of polytheistic Turks and other non-prophetic cultures.

Commentary
This passage classifies the types of time periods and how they are known. Ibn Taymiyya states that day, month, and year are known by sense perception (hiss) and observation (mushahada). The month and year are known by counting in lunar calendars and by sighting in solar calendars, as illustrated by the Quranic verse about the People of the Cave: 300 solar years equals 309 lunar years. However, the week (usbu') is different. It has no natural or rational definition; it is known only through prophetic reports that Allah created the world in six days. Consequently, Allah commanded communities to gather on one day of the week for worship, which preserves the memory of the week. This explains why languages of people without prophetic revelation (e.g., Turkish polytheists) have no names for the days of the week. The passage concludes with a general principle: custom follows conceptualization; one cannot know what one does not conceive.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Simple One
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the universal premise and argues the simple one exists only in the mind.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's two-pronged critique (epistemological and ontological) of the logicians' concept of the simple one as the principle of composites.

Core Idea
The simple one of the logicians is a mental construct, not a real existent.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a foundational claim of the logicians: that the one produces only one, and that analysis of composites ends in a simple one. He attacks both the empirical basis of the universal premise and the reality of the simple one itself. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to unpack his critique and see how it undermines key logical-metaphysical assumptions.

Guided Path
1
Locate the universal premise under attack
Find the claim 'al-wahid la yasduru anhu illa wahid' (the one does not produce except one). This is the target.
This is a universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya) about causality.
2
Identify the epistemological objection
Notice Ibn Taymiyya's objection: the logicians have never empirically observed a thing that is one from every aspect producing anything. He denies the premise is known by experience.
He questions the evidence for the universal claim.
3
Trace the ontological argument about the simple one
The logicians use the simple one as the endpoint of analysis (e.g., human composed of animality and rationality). Ibn Taymiyya says this simple one exists only in the mind, not externally.
He reduces a metaphysical principle to a mental abstraction.
4
Connect to the broader critique of logical principles
This passage shows Ibn Taymiyya's pattern: he challenges the epistemological basis of logical premises and argues their core concepts are mental constructs, not realities.
This is part of his larger attack on the foundations of Greek logic.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of a key logical and metaphysical principle held by the philosophers/logicians. Ibn Taymiyya begins by addressing their universal claim 'the one does not produce more than one' (al-wahid la yasduru anhu illa wahid). He states that this is a universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya) for which they have no empirical evidence; they have never observed a thing that is one from every aspect producing something, whether one or two. This undermines the metaphysical foundations of their logical system, as they rely on this mental abstraction to explain real existence. The critique follows the author's pattern: he questions the epistemological basis of their premises and reduces their concepts to mental constructs.

Mental Composition vs External Reality: Ibn Taymiyya's Critique
Ibn Taymiyya explains that the composition of human from animal and rational is a mental conception, not a real external division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mental composition (tarkib dhihni) and external predication (haml khariji) in Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' definition of 'human'.

Core Idea
Definitions are mental constructs, not descriptions of real external parts.

Ibn Taymiyya here takes on the logicians' standard definition of 'human' as 'rational animal'. He argues that this composition is valid only in the mind, not in external reality. This distinction has profound implications for how we understand definitions in Islamic sciences — especially in kalam and usul al-fiqh, where defining terms correctly is essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians claim that 'human' is composed of 'animal' and 'rational' as real parts. Find this claim in the Arabic: their saying 'insan murakkab min al-hayawan wa al-natiq'.
This is a standard hadd (definition) in Aristotelian logic.
2
Distinguish mental vs external composition
Ibn Taymiyya says the composition is only in the mind (tasawwur). Externally, the attributes 'animality' and 'rationality' are merely qualities (awsaf) subsisting in the human, not separate parts.
He uses the example of 'laughing human' to illustrate: 'laughing' becomes a part of the mental quiddity but externally is an attribute.
3
Understand the three modes of signification
The word 'human' indicates the whole meaning by mutabaqa (correspondence), its parts (like 'rational') by tadammun (inclusion), and necessary concomitants by iltizam (commitment). This is the standard semantic theory he references.
This helps clarify that the parts are only logically included, not physically separate.
4
Apply the critique to real examples
Consider any definition from Islamic sciences. For example, 'Riba is excess in a contractual exchange'. According to Ibn Taymiyya, 'excess' and 'contractual exchange' are mental parts of the definition, but externally they are attributes of a single act.
This prevents the error of assuming that definitions reveal metaphysical parts.
5
Assess the implications for theology and law
If definitions are merely mental constructs, then theological debates about divine attributes (e.g., whether God's knowledge is part of His essence) must be handled carefully: mental distinctions do not imply real separation.
Ibn Taymiyya's position supports a more nominalist approach to definitions.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the previous critique, providing a detailed analysis of how composition operates in the mind versus external reality. Ibn Taymiyya takes the logicians' example of 'human composed of animal and rational' and shows that this composition is only valid as a mental conception (tasawwur). When one conceives a human, one imagines a rational, sensitive, moving body; each aspect becomes a part of the mental quiddity (mahiyya). This is a direct critique of the logicians' view that external reality mirrors mental composition. The passage reinforces that their whole framework of hylomorphic composition is a mental construct, not a fact about the world. He thus defends a more nominalist or property-based ontology.

The Five Types of Composition in Ibn Taymiyya's Critique
Critique of logicians' five types of composition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list Ibn Taymiyya's five types of 'composition' (tarkib) and explain why he rejects the first two and denies the applicability of the rest to Allah.

Core Idea
Composition is not one thing; it is five, and none apply to Allah.

Ibn Taymiyya begins by referencing the logicians' rule that 'the one cannot be an agent and a recipient' because that would imply composition. He then unpacks the term 'composition' to reveal its ambiguity. By distinguishing five distinct types, he shows that the logicians' charge of composition against Allah is based on a vague term that fails to prove anything. This lesson guides you through those five types and Ibn Taymiyya's refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five types of tarkib
Read the Arabic list: existence+essence, general+special attribute (genus+differentia), essence+attributes, body from matter+form, body from atoms. Memorize them as a checklist.
These five cover all major debates in falsafah and kalam about divine simplicity.
2
Understand which types are rejected outright
Ibn Taymiyya says he has shown the first two (existence+essence, genus+differentia) are false. Note that these are metaphysical compositions, not physical. He does not specify his proof here, but accepts they are refuted.
3
Analyze the status of body composition
For types 4 and 5, he says most rational people deny that the body is composed in those ways. This is a broader critique that extends to physical composition theories (matter+form and atomism).
This shows Ibn Taymiyya engaging with natural philosophy as well.
4
Isolate what remains
After rejecting four types, only type 3 remains: an essence that has attributes. According to Ibn Taymiyya, this is the correct description of Allah. The logicians cannot use 'composition' as a refutation of divine attributes because that would be a different type that is not problematic.
5
Formulate the critique
Ibn Taymiyya's argument is that the word 'tarkib' is ambiguous. The logicians use it without specifying which type, so their objections to divine attributes are invalid. You should be able to explain why each type fails to apply to Allah.
Key skill: identifying ambiguous terms in theological arguments.

The Three Meanings of 'Composite' and Their Negation for Allah
Negation of three meanings of composite for Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the three senses of 'composite' that Ibn Taymiyya identifies and explain why each is negated for Allah by Muslim consensus.

Core Idea
A 'composite' can mean made by another, scattered then gathered, or separable parts—all impossible for Allah.

After listing the five types of composition, Ibn Taymiyya adds a further clarification: the word 'composite' (al-murakkab) can be used in three distinct senses. Each sense implies a form of dependence or change that cannot apply to the Creator. This lesson unpacks those three meanings and their negation, showing how Ibn Taymiyya uses linguistic precision to safeguard tawheed.

Guided Path
1
Locate the three meanings in the Arabic
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'al-murakkab yuqalu 'ala ma rakkabahu ghayruh, wa 'ala ma kanat ajza'uhu mutafarriqatan fajtama'at, wa 'ala ma yaqbal mufaraqata ba'dihi ba'dan.' Identify these three.
The three meanings are: (1) made by another, (2) parts scattered then assembled, (3) parts that can separate.
2
Understand the first meaning: made by another
If something is composite because another thing put it together, it depends on that maker. Allah is not made or caused by anything; He is the Creator. So this meaning is negated.
3
Understand the second meaning: scattered parts assembled
If something's parts were once separate and then came together, that implies temporality and change. Allah is eternal and unchanging, so this cannot apply.
This also relates to the philosophical argument that the world is composite in this sense, implying a creator.
4
Understand the third meaning: separable parts
If something's parts can be separated, that implies potential division and dependence. Allah is absolutely one, without any possibility of division.
This covers the notion of 'composition' that might be accused of implying multiplicity.
5
Apply the negation to Allah
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that these three meanings are negated of the Lord of the worlds by the consensus of Muslims. Understand that this consensus is a powerful argument: any claim that Allah is composite in these senses is false by agreement.
This shows Ibn Taymiyya's use of ijma' as a source of certainty.

Commentary
This passage is dialectical. Ibn Taymiyya begins by asserting that definitions are essentially detailed explanations of names; their purpose is to distinguish the defined from others through a description that is coextensive (comprehensive and exclusive). He then emphasizes that any non-self-evident claim requires a proof. The definer must support his definition by transmission from the Lawgiver (the Prophet ﷺ). He gives examples: khamr is defined by the hadith 'every intoxicant is khamr' and ghibah by the hadith 'mentioning your brother in a way he dislikes.' Then he presents an objection: the Prophet himself, when advising Fatimah bint Qais, mentioned disliked traits of Abu Jahm and Mu‘awiyah. If ghibah is forbidden, how could the Prophet do it? The answer is twofold: either we deny that this falls under the definition of ghibah, or if it does, it is permitted for a preponderant benefit, namely the obligation to advise the seeker of counsel. Thus, the definition remains valid; exceptions for necessity do not undermine it.

Definitions as Name-Elaborations Requiring Evidence
Ibn Taymiyya argues definitions are like names, requiring evidence for accuracy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's account of definitions as name-elaborations that require evidence, and apply it to evaluate a definition from Islamic jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Definitions do not reveal essences; they simply elaborate names and must be proven by evidence.

Ibn Taymiyya radically redefines the role of definitions. For logicians, definitions capture the essence (mahiyya) of a thing. Ibn Taymiyya reduces them to mere name-elaborations, like linguistic glosses. The purpose is not to uncover reality but to distinguish the defined from others. Most crucially, every definition requires external evidence – from revelation or consensus – because it is a claim about language and classification. In this lesson, you will learn to identify his critique and apply his method to real Islamic definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target
What claims about definitions is Ibn Taymiyya rejecting? He opposes the logicians' view that definitions capture essences or create new knowledge. Recognize that he reduces definitions to names.
2
Extract his alternative
Ibn Taymiyya states that definitions are like names: they detail what the name summarizes. The purpose is not to reveal the quiddity (mahiyya) but merely to distinguish the defined from other things.
This is a shift from essentialist to descriptive definitions.
3
Determine the conditions
A proper definition must be co-extensive and co-exclusive (tard wa 'aks), comprehensive and exclusive (jami' mani'). This ensures that the defining attribute applies to all instances of the defined and only to them.
These are standard conditions even in logical theory, but Ibn Taymiyya reinterprets them in a linguistic-evidence framework.
4
Examine the evidence requirement
Ibn Taymiyya insists that every definition must be supported by a dalil (evidence), typically from the Lawgiver (shari') or consensus. No claim about the meaning of a term is self-evident.
He gives examples: wine is intoxicant (backed by hadith), backbiting is mentioning your brother with what he dislikes (backed by hadith), pride is trampling truth and belittling people (backed by consensus?).
5
Test using the objection example
The objector argues that the Prophet himself backbited Fatima bint Qays' suitors. Ibn Taymiyya responds with two possibilities: either the speech is not included in the definition of backbiting, or it is included but permissible due to a greater benefit (advice). This shows that definitions are context-sensitive and require external evidence.
This illustrates how the evidence requirement allows for exceptions based on maqasid (objectives) of the Sharia.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logical Division of Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya critiques logicians' classification of attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' three-fold division of attributes and explain why he regards it as arbitrary.

Core Idea
The logicians' division of attributes collapses under the ambiguity of 'essence'.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya mounts a powerful critique against the Greek logicians' claim that definition can depict the essence without evidence, and against their classification of attributes into essential, necessary for the essence, and necessary for existence. He shows that the entire edifice rests on an ambiguous notion of 'essence' (mahiyya). By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate his argument and see how it undermines a key pillar of Aristotelian logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim about definition
The logicians claim that a definition conveys the depiction (taswir) of the essence in the listener's mind, and that this happens merely by the definer's statement, without any evidence. Find where Ibn Taymiyya states this and note his immediate verdict.
He calls it 'invalid' (bāṭil).
2
Extract the three-fold division of attributes
The logicians divide necessary attributes into: (1) essential (dhātiyya) included in the essence, (2) external necessary for the essence but not its existence, (3) external necessary for its existence. Locate this in the passage.
This is the classification he will deconstruct.
3
Understand the two interpretations of 'essence'
Ibn Taymiyya presents two possible meanings of 'essence': either external existents or mental conceptions. He then tests the logicians' division against each meaning.
This dual interpretation is the key to his critique.
4
See why the division fails under each interpretation
If 'essence' means external existents, the logicians' classification is arbitrary because essentiality is determined by reality, not their three categories. If 'essence' is a mental concept, then what is included or excluded varies by the conceiver. Ibn Taymiyya gives examples: one person may conceive a human as 'body, sensing, growing, moving, rational' – all parts become essence; another may conceive only 'rational animal' – the rest become non-essential. Thus the division is subjective.
This shows that the logicians' categories are not grounded in objective reality.

Refuting Greek Attribute Classification and the Islamic Alternative
Refutation of Greek attribute classification, Islamic method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Greek classification of attributes with Ibn Taymiyya's view and describe the Islamic method of definition by necessary attribute.

Core Idea
Attributes are not separate from essence in external reality; definitions need not use genus and differentia.

Ibn Taymiyya proceeds to critique the Greek logicians' division of attributes into three kinds: internal essential, external necessary for the essence, and external necessary for existence. He argues that this classification is invalid if applied to external existents, because the existence of a thing is identical to its reality. In mental conception, the classification is merely subjective and yields no real knowledge. He then presents the alternative method of Muslim theologians: defining by a single necessary attribute, without recourse to genus or general accident.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Greek classification
The logicians differentiate attributes as: (a) essential and internal to the essence, (b) external but necessary for the essence (without existence), (c) external and necessary for existence.
2
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's two critiques
a) If 'essence' means external reality, then existence and essence are identical, so no attribute can be external to the essence; all necessary attributes are part of the reality. b) If 'essence' means mental conception, then what is included or excluded is subjective and varies by conception, providing no objective knowledge.
3
Contrast with Islamic method
Muslim theologians define by a single necessary attribute (al-wasf al-mulazim). They do not use genus or differentia; they consider that sufficient.
4
Examine the implication
This method aligns with the Ash'ari view that attributes are not distinct from the essence in external reality; they are identical. Thus, a definition can be a simple description.
5
Apply to a theological example
Define 'God' as 'The Necessary Existent' – a single necessary attribute that suffices without listing genus or differentia.

He argues that sound reasoning is not about mastering universal syllogistic forms, but about recognizing when things are truly alike and when they are truly different—a capacity embedded in human nature by God. He contrasts this with the logicians' approach, which he claims leads to ambiguity and misguidance, just like using crooked scales.

Prophetic Teaching of Intellectual Examples
Answer to objection on role of prophets in teaching rational balance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how prophets teach correct rational syllogisms through intellectual examples, and why this refutes the dichotomy between reason and revelation.

Core Idea
Prophets do not only transmit reports; they perfect the fitra by teaching rational measures and intellectual examples.

Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: if the rational balance is known by innate nature, why did God send messengers with it? He answers that the prophets guide and correct the fitra, which may be heedless or corrupted. They teach not just reports, but intellectual examples (amthal 'aqliyya) that train the mind to recognize similarity and difference. This refutes the theologians who separate reason from revelation.

1
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
Look for the question: 'If this is known by reason, how did God make it something with which the messengers were sent?' This challenges the necessity of revelation for rational knowledge.

Extract the answer (jawab)
Find Ibn Taymiyya's response: he says the prophets struck intellectual examples (amthal 'aqliyya) and guided people to the correct rational syllogisms. They perfected the fitra that had become heedless or corrupted.
Note the key phrase: 'darabat lahum al-amthal al-'aqliyya'.
3
Analyze the concept of amthal 'aqliyya
What are these intellectual examples? They are analogies and comparisons that clarify rational principles. How do they differ from mere reports?
They engage the intellect, not just transmit information.
4
Understand the complementarity of reason and revelation
Ibn Taymiyya rejects the idea that reason and prophetic sciences are opposed. Instead, revelation perfects reason. How does this reshape the relationship?
Revelation removes corruption and directs attention.

Refutation of Essence as Cause of Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the causal reading of essence-attribute necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the claim that the essence is an efficient cause of its attributes and identify the ambiguity in 'by virtue of itself'.

Core Idea
Logical concomitance does not imply causal dependence.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya confronts a key claim of the logicians: that the essence (mahiyya) is a 'requirer' (muqtadiya) of its attributes. He dissects the meaning of 'requirer' to show that either it means entailment (which is trivial) or efficient causation (which is false). He then exposes the ambiguity in the phrase 'by virtue of itself' (li-mā hiya hiya), arguing that necessity can be either intrinsic or extrinsic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two interpretations of 'muqtadiya'
Ibn Taymiyya asks what 'muqtadiya' means. It could mean 'mustalzima' (entailing) or 'fa'ila' (efficient cause). Identify both possibilities in the Arabic.
2
See why the entailment interpretation is trivial
If 'muqtadiya' means entailment, then all entailing things are like that, so the logicians' claim does not single out some attributes. This makes their intention invalid.
3
See why the efficient causation interpretation is rejected
If 'muqtadiya' means efficient cause, then it is false because no essence causes any of its attributes. Ibn Taymiyya explicitly denies this.
4
Grasp the distinction between entailment and causation
The second path clarifies that most concomitants are not effects of their entailers. Entailment (luzum) does not imply efficient causation. Recognize that something can be a concomitant without being an effect.
5
Recognize the ambiguity in 'by virtue of itself'
The phrase 'li-mā hiya hiya' suggests that necessity is either by virtue of the essence itself or by virtue of something else. Ibn Taymiyya argues this division is not justified and the phrase is ambiguous.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' claim that the essence (mahiyya) is a cause ('illa) or efficient cause (fa'ila) of its attributes. Ibn Taymiyya opens with a distinction: if by 'muqtadiya' (requirer) you mean 'mustalzima' (entailing), then that is true of all entailers and does not single out some attributes—so the logicians' intention is invalid. If you mean 'fa'ila' (efficient cause), then no essence causes any of its attributes. He then presents a second path (al-tariq al-thani): even if we grant that the essence requires the concomitant in the sense of being a cause (as claimed) or in the sense of not being an effect (which is the truth), the core issue is that the relationship of entailment (luzum) does not imply causation. Most concomitants are not effects of their entailers. The phrase 'the essence by virtue of itself is a requirer' is ambiguous (mujmal) because 'by virtue of itself' suggests that necessity is either by virtue of the essence itself or by virtue of something else. The logicians' division of necessities into those that are self-evident and those requiring a middle term is thus built on an unclear foundation. Ibn Taymiyya uses examples from the created world: the attributes of a human, such as animality and rationality, are not caused by the essence but are created together. Similarly, the attributes of God are mutually necessary and not caused by the essence in the sense of efficient causation. The key lesson is that logical concomitance (luzum) is not equivalent to causal dependence.

Ibn Sina's Refutation of the Distinction between Essential and Necessary Attributes
Ibn Sina's refutation of the first distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's argument against the claim that non-essential necessary attributes can be mentally removed, and evaluate its force against a key distinction in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Some necessary attributes are as inseparable in the mind as essential ones—Ibn Sina destroys the logicians' first distinction.

Ibn Taymiyya presents Ibn Sina's incisive argument from the Ishārāt, which targets the logicians' claim that only essential attributes are impossible to remove in mental estimation. Ibn Sina shows that even non-essential necessary attributes, such as the triangle's angle sum, can be equally impossible to remove, depending on whether their necessity is known directly or through a middle term. Understanding this argument is crucial for debates on the ontology of attributes in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the distinction under attack
The logicians distinguish essential attributes (which cannot be mentally removed from the essence) from non-essential necessary attributes (which they claim can be removed in estimation). Ibn Sina targets this claim.
The key terms: essential (dhātī), necessary (lāzim), removal in estimation (raf' fī al-wahm).
2
Define the necessary non-constitutive attribute
Ibn Sina defines the necessary non-constitutive (al-lāzim ghayr al-muqawwim) as an attribute that accompanies the quiddity without being part of it, e.g., a triangle having angles equal to two right angles.
This attribute is not part of the definition of triangle but follows from it necessarily.
3
Analyze the two cases: with or without a middle term
If the necessity is known without a middle term (wasat), the attribute is necessarily necessary and impossible to remove in estimation. If it has a middle term, the middle term either is constitutive or necessary; in both cases, it leads to a necessary attribute without a middle term, also impossible to remove.
The middle term is what is expressed by 'because' (li-anna).
4
Apply the regress argument
If the middle term requires another middle term, infinite regress results, which is impossible. Therefore, there must be a necessary attribute without a middle term that cannot be removed. Thus, the original distinction fails.
This argument shows that some non-constitutive necessary attributes are as mentally inseparable as essential ones.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Sina's argument from his 'Isharat' refuting the logicians' claim that only essential attributes are impossible to remove in mental estimation, while non-essential (accidental necessary) attributes can be separated. He then argues that if such a necessity is known without a middle term (i.e., an intermediate cause or proof), it is necessarily necessary and cannot be mentally removed. If it has a middle term, then that middle term either is constitutive or necessary. If constitutive, then the attribute is still necessary (not constitutive) because the constitutive of the constitutive is constitutive. If the middle term needs another middle term, this leads to infinite regress; if it does not, then there is a necessary without a middle term. In all cases, there exists a necessary attribute that is impossible to remove in estimation. Ibn Taymiyya explains that Ibn Sina's target is the logicians' first distinction: that non-essential attributes can be mentally separated. Ibn Sina shows that necessary attributes, even if not constitutive, also cannot be removed. Thus the distinction collapses.

Refuting the Cause Requirement for Necessary Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' second distinction that essential attributes require no cause while necessary ones do.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical error in the claim that all necessary attributes require a cause, and construct a counterexample from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Necessary attributes can be immediate—no cause or proof required.

Ibn Taymiyya continues his systematic dismantling of the logicians' distinctions between essential and necessary attributes. Here he targets their claim that necessary attributes always require a cause (or middle term). He calls this distinction even more corrupt than the first, and provides a powerful counterexample: some necessary attributes are known without any proof. Let us examine his argument carefully.

Guided Path
1
Restate the logicians' second distinction
The logicians claimed that essential attributes (dhātī) require no cause for their necessity, whereas necessary attributes (lāzim) require a cause (ʿilla) or middle term (wasat) to establish their necessity. Identify this claim in the Arabic passage.
The 'cause' here is essentially the middle term of a syllogism that proves the attribute.
2
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's refutation strategy
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the universal claim that all necessary attributes need a cause. He points out that some necessary attributes exist without any cause or proof. Locate where he says 'there are among necessary attributes those that do not need a cause.'
This is a counterexample refutation: one instance of a necessary attribute without a cause disproves the general rule.
3
Apply the critique to a concrete Islamic example
Think of a necessary attribute in Islamic theology that is known immediately. For instance, the necessary existence of the Creator (wājib al-wujūd) is intrinsic and does not depend on a middle term; it is self-evident to the intellect. Alternatively, in geometry, the sum of angles in a triangle is necessary but requires a proof—yet the property itself is necessary. Ibn Taymiyya's point is that not all necessary attributes need a proof; some are immediate.
The distinction between essential and necessary attributes fails because it incorrectly assumes all necessary attributes require a cause.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Acquired Prophethood
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the philosophers' view that prophethood is acquired and souls are alike in essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reductio argument against the philosophers' doctrine of soul-identity and evaluate its theological implications.

Core Idea
If souls are identical in essence, then prophethood becomes a common human potential—reducing revelation to dreams.

In this passage from his refutation of the logicians, Ibn Taymiyya attacks a cornerstone of the philosophers' psychology: the claim that all rational souls are alike in essence. He shows that this leads to the absurd consequence that any person could equal or surpass the prophets, and that prophethood is merely acquired. We will dissect his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Start by locating the philosophers' assertion that prophethood is limited to a philosophical syllogism or Sufi imagination, and that it is a common capacity among people based on preparedness.
This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
2
Extract the underlying premise about souls
Notice Ibn Sina's view that rational souls are identical in essence and differ only due to bodily conditions like temperament and habit. This is the key premise that Ibn Taymiyya attacks.
The analogy of water in different vessels illustrates this.
3
Follow Ibn Taymiyya's reductio
If souls differ only accidentally, then the soul of the vilest person shares the same essence as the prophets, and many people could be close or superior. This leads to the conclusion that prophethood is acquired, not a divine gift.
Ibn Taymiyya uses rhetorical questioning to highlight the absurdity.
4
Evaluate the consequence
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya ties this to historical examples like al-Suhrawardi and Ibn Sab'in, who claimed prophetic status. The critique shows that this view reduces prophethood to the level of dreams.
This undermines the uniqueness and divine origin of prophethood.

Commentary

لا وسائط فإن الحنفاء ليس فيهم من يقول بإثبات البشر وسائط في الخلق والتدبير، عند الحنفاء كما يثبتها الفلاسفة. بل الرسل كلهم وأتباع الرسل متفقون على أنه لا يعبد إلا الله وحده. فهو الذي يسأل ويعبد، وله يصلى ويسجد، وهو الذي يجيب دعاء المضطرين، ويكشف الضر عن المضرورين، ويغيث عباده المستغيثين. {ما يفتح الله للناس من رحمة فلا ممسك لها وما يمسك فلا مرسل له من بعده} [سورة فاطر: 2]. {وما بكم من نعمة فمن الله ثم إذا مسكم الضر فإليه تحشرون} [سورة النحل: 53]. وليس عند الحنفاء أن أحداً غير الله يستقل بفعل شيء، بل غايته أن يكون سبباً، لا يستقل بإحداث شيء والأثر لا يحصل إلا به وبغيره من الأسباب وبصرف الموانع. والله تعالى هو الذي يخلق أحد غير الله بتأثير الأسباب وبدفع الموانع، مع خلقه سبحانه أيضاً لهذا السبب. لكن المقصود أنه ليس الوجود ما يستقل بإحداث شيء، ولا ثم شيء يوجب كل أثر إلا مشيئة الله وحده. فما شاء كان وما لم يشأ لم يكن. والرسل هم وسائط بين الله وبين خلقه في تبليغ رسالاته وأمره ونهيه، ووعده ووعيده، كما قال تعالى: {وما نرسل المرسلين إلا مبشرين ومنذرين} [سورة الأنعام: 48 والكهف: 56]. وقال: {إنا أرسلناك شاهدا ومبشرا ونذيرا وداعيا إلى الله بإذنه وسراجا منيرا} [سورة الأحزاب: 45-46]. فأخبر أنه أرسله شاهداً، كما قال: {ليكون الرسول شهيدا عليكم وتكونوا شهداء على الناس} [سورة الحج: 78] وقال: {فكيف إذا جئنا من كل أمة بشهيد وجئنا بك على هؤلاء شهيدا} [سورة النساء: 41]. وقال: {وكذلك جعلناكم أمة وسطا لتكونوا شهداء على الناس ويكون الرسول عليكم شهيدا} [سورة البقرة: 143]. ولما دفن النبي شهداء أحد قال: «أما أنا فشهيد على هؤلاء». وقوله مبشراً ونذيراً بالوعد والوعيد، وداعياً إلى الله بإذنه بالأمر والنهي. وقال تعالى: {وسل من أرسلنا من قبلك من رسلنا أجعلنا من دون الرحمن آلهة} [سورة الزخرف: 45].
There are no intermediaries! The monotheists do not include anyone who affirms humans as intermediaries in creation and governance, as the philosophers affirm. Rather, all messengers and their followers agree that none is worshipped except Allah alone. He is asked and worshipped, to Him is prayer and prostration, and He answers the prayer of the distressed, removes harm from the harmed, and succors His servants who seek help. {Whatever mercy Allah opens to people, none can withhold it; and whatever He withholds, none can send it after Him} [Surah Fatir: 2]. {And whatever blessing you have is from Allah; then when harm touches you, to Him you cry for help} [Surah An-Nahl: 53]. The monotheists do not hold that anyone other than Allah is independent in doing anything; rather, at most it can be a cause, not independent in producing an effect, and the effect only occurs through it and other causes and by the removal of obstacles. Allah, exalted, is the one who creates something other than Allah through the influence of causes and by removing obstacles, while He also creates this cause. But the point is that existence has nothing that independently produces an effect, nor is there anything that necessitates every effect except the will of Allah alone. So whatever He wills happens, and whatever He does not will does not happen. And the messengers are intermediaries between Allah and His creation in conveying His messages, His commands and prohibitions, His promise and threat, as Allah said: {We send not the messengers except as bearers of good news and warners} [Surah Al-An'am: 48 and Al-Kahf: 56]. And He said: {Indeed, We have sent you as a witness, a bearer of good news, and a warner, and a caller to Allah by His permission, and a lamp giving light} [Surah Al-Ahzab: 45-46]. He informed that He sent him as a witness, as He said: {That the messenger may be a witness over you, and you may be witnesses over the people} [Surah Al-Hajj: 78] and He said: {How will it be when We bring from every nation a witness and We bring you, [O Muhammad], against these as a witness} [Surah An-Nisa: 41]. And He said: {Thus we have made you a middle community that you may be witnesses over the people and the messenger may be a witness over you} [Surah Al-Baqarah: 143]. When the Prophet buried the martyrs of Uhud, he said: 'As for me, I am a witness over these.' And His saying 'bearer of good news and warner' concerning the promise and threat, and 'caller to Allah by His permission' concerning the command and prohibition. And He said: {And ask those of Our messengers whom We sent before you: Did We ever appoint gods to be worshipped besides the Most Gracious?} [Surah Az-Zukhruf: 45].

Commentary
This passage is a definitional clarification from Ibn Taymiyya regarding the proper role of messengers (rusul) in Islam. He begins by denying any intermediaries in creation or governance: 'لا وسائط' (There are no intermediaries). He affirms that the monotheists (ḥunafā') reject the philosophers' notion that humans or celestial beings can act as independent causal agents. Rather, all messengers agree that worship is due to Allah alone—He alone hears prayers, provides sustenance, and relieves distress. Ibn Taymiyya supports this with Quranic verses emphasizing that only God controls mercy and blessing. He then explains that according to monotheists, no created being is 'independent' (mustaqill) in causing any effect; they may be mere causes (asbāb) that do not produce effects except with other factors and by divine permission. Ultimately, everything depends on Allah's will alone. Following this, Ibn Taymiyya defines the true function of messengers: they are 'intermediaries' (wasā'iṭ) specifically in conveying revelation—God's commands, prohibitions, promises, and warnings. He quotes several verses showing that messengers are sent as bearers of good news, warners, and witnesses over their communities. The hadith about the Prophet being a witness over the martyrs of Uhud reinforces this role. In sum, the passage delineates the boundary between legitimate prophetic mediation in teaching and shirkful belief in causal intermediaries.
