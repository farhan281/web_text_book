Ibn Taymiyya's Three Refutations of Logic's Presumed Validity
Ibn Taymiyya presents three rebuttals to the claim that logic's longevity proves its validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain Ibn Taymiyya's three refutations of the argument that logic's longevity proves its validity.

Core Idea
Antiquity and widespread acceptance do not make an intellectual discipline true.

Ibn Taymiyya addresses a common defence of logic: that it has been refined by minds for over a thousand years and accepted by the virtuous. He offers three distinct rebuttals that challenge the evidential value of tradition and consensus. This lesson unpacks those rebuttals and shows how to critically evaluate claims based on age or popularity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Argument Being Refuted
Locate the logicians' claim that logic is a 'normative instrument' (āla qānūniyya) that safeguards the mind from error, and the later defence that its long history and acceptance prove its validity.
The defence is: 'These are sciences that have been refined by minds for more than a thousand years, and the virtuous have accepted them.'
2
Analyse the First Refutation: Historical Opposition
Ibn Taymiyya argues that intelligent people have always denied and corrected the logicians, from ancient times to the Muslim theologians (including the Rafidah), so there is no universal acceptance.
He cites that all groups of Muslim theologians have authored refutations of logic.
3
Analyse the Second Refutation: Internal Disagreement
Even within the philosophical tradition, there is constant criticism: Aristotle refuted earlier philosophers, Ibn Sina refuted Aristotle, and Abu al-Barakat refuted Aristotle. This shows that disagreement is the norm, not consensus.
Abu al-Barakat explicitly states that they seek truth, not partisanship.
4
Analyse the Third Refutation: Antiquity of False Religions
Idol worship is older and more widespread than philosophy, yet false. Similarly, the altered religion of the Jews is older than Aristotle's philosophy. Thus, age and acceptance do not indicate truth.
This is an argument by analogy: if antiquity were a criterion, false religions would be true.
5
Evaluate the Overall Critique
Assess whether Ibn Taymiyya's refutations are logically sound. He uses empirical evidence (history of opposition), internal logical inconsistency (philosophers disagreeing), and a compelling analogy (older religions). His arguments are valid and expose the weakness of the longevity argument.
Note that Ibn Taymiyya does not deny that logic has been studied; he denies that its prevalence indicates correctness.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the argument that the longevity and widespread acceptance of logical sciences prove their validity. Ibn Taymiyya first notes that the discussion of logic arose from the logicians' claim that it is a normative tool that prevents intellectual error. He then addresses a common defense: when their contradictions are shown, some respond that these sciences have been refined by minds for over a thousand years and accepted by the virtuous. Ibn Taymiyya gives three rebuttals. The first rebuttal denies the claim that all intelligent people accept logic; he points to continuous opposition from ancient philosophers to Muslim theologians, all groups (including the Rafidah) have refuted them. The second rebuttal argues that acceptance is not a proof, since even within the philosophical tradition, Aristotle refuted earlier philosophy, Ibn Sina refuted Aristotle, and later philosophers like Abu al-Barakat refuted Aristotle again, showing that disagreement is the norm, not consensus. The third rebuttal uses an analogy: idol worship is older and more widespread than philosophy, yet that does not make it true. Similarly, the corrupted Jewish religion is older than Aristotle's philosophy. Thus, antiquity and acceptance do not entail truth.

The Absurd Consequence of Denying Composition to Allah
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the logicians' denial of composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's conditional argument against the logicians' denial of composition and identify the absurd implication.

Core Idea
Denying composition to avoid deficiency makes every creature more perfect than Allah—the ultimate absurdity.

The logicians claimed they fled from composition because it implies deficiency. Ibn Taymiyya turns their own logic against them: if composition is a deficiency, then their alternative is far worse. In this lesson, you will trace his conditional argument and see how he exposes the logical corruption of their position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Locate where the logicians state they fled from composition to avoid deficiency. What is their alternative exactly?
They deny that Allah has any composition or real attributes.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's conditional premise
Find the sentence that begins 'If composition is a deficiency...' and state the condition and its consequence.
The consequence is that their alternative is worse than what they fled from.
3
Analyze the two consequences of the alternative
Ibn Taymiyya presents two implications of the logicians' alternative: either no external existence or every creature more perfect than Allah. Identify these in the Arabic.
The first is that the alternative exists only in minds; the second is that every possible existent is more perfect than Allah.
4
Assess the absurdity
Explain why each consequence is absurd, especially the second one which elevates a mosquito above the Lord of the worlds.
5
Understand the objection and response
Read the part where Ibn Taymiyya anticipates the objection that the logicians did not intend this. How does he respond?
He says the necessary implication of their statement is enough to show its corruption, regardless of intention.

Weakness in Syllogisms: Form vs. Matter
Ibn Taymiyya argues that analogical syllogism's weakness is in its matter, not form, and defends rotation and division as capable of certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish material weakness from formal weakness in a syllogism and explain why exhaustive division yields certainty.

Core Idea
A syllogism's weakness is in its matter, not its form; exhaustive division yields certainty.

Logicians often claim that the analogical syllogism (qiyas al-tamthil) used by jurists is only probable because of its form. Ibn Taymiyya rejects this, arguing that the probability stems from the content (māddah) of the premises, not the logical structure. He further defends rotation (dawrān) and division (taqsīm) as methods that can yield certainty when properly applied. This lesson will unpack his argument and show how it reframes the debate about certainty in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
What do the logicians say about analogical syllogism? They assert that its form is probable and that it relies on rotation and division, which they also deem only probable. Locate this claim in the passage.
2
Distinguish matter from form
Ibn Taymiyya argues that weakness is in the matter, not the form. Identify where he says: 'probability results from the matter, not from the form.' Why does this matter?
If the same content is cast into a comprehensive syllogism (qiyas al-shumul), it remains probable.
3
Examine the celestial sphere example
The logicians used the example of the celestial sphere being composite and thus originated. Ibn Taymiyya says this argument is weak because of its matter (the premise that the sphere is composite is not established). Note his critique of that specific proof.
This shows that the weakness is not due to analogical form but to the material content.
4
Understand the argument about division (taqsīm)
The logicians concede that division yields certainty if exhaustive. Ibn Taymiyya points out that this is sufficient: if the common element can be exhaustively divided and all alternatives eliminated except one, certainty follows. Read his exact words on this.
If taqsīm is not exhaustive, even qiyas al-shumul fails to give certainty.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider a ruling on a new intoxicant. The jurist uses taqsīm to list all possible 'illas and eliminates all except intoxication. This yields certainty that intoxication is the cause. Does this depend on the form of qiyas al-tamthil? No, it depends on the completeness of the division.
This application illustrates Ibn Taymiyya's point that the method is not inherently probabilistic.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' claim that analogical syllogism (qiyas al-tamthil) used by jurists is only probable and that rotation (dawran) and division (taqsim) are not methods yielding certainty. Ibn Taymiyya begins by explaining that the jurists often use analogical reasoning in probable matters, but the probability comes from the content (madda) of the premises, not the logical form (sura). If the same content were cast into a comprehensive syllogism (qiyas al-shumul), it would still be only probable. Thus, the logicians' attempt to distinguish 'their' certain syllogism from the jurists' probable one based on form is misguided. He gives the example of the proof for the creation of the celestial sphere (al-falak) used by the Jahmiyya and Qadariyya, which is weak due to its material content, not its form. Al-Amidi's example of arguing for the eternity of the world by its existence is similarly flawed because its matter is false. Then, addressing the claim that analogical syllogism relies on rotation or division—both of which are merely probable—Ibn Taymiyya declares it false. He points out that the logicians themselves admit that division yields certainty when exhaustive. If the common element can be exhaustively divided and all alternative causes eliminated except one, certainty results. If not, even the comprehensive syllogism fails. Thus, the weakness is not inherent to the method but to the possibility of exhaustive division.

Core Idea

Ibn Taymiyya responds to those who say that seeing God is impossible because vision requires conditions like color and direction, which God does not possess. Guided Path

The objector says: 'If God were seen, then color/direction would be necessary.' This is a conditional statement. Locate this in the Arabic: 'لو رؤي للزم كذا'.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of the Indeterminate Cause Objection
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' objection about indeterminate divisions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The possibility that the cause could be the whole or a part does not prevent certain knowledge of what the cause is not.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya confronts a classic objection to analogical reasoning (qiyas). The logicians argue that when the possible causes are limited, the cause might be the whole combination or a part that does not exist in the branch, making the analogy uncertain. Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges the possibility but shows that it does not undermine the ability to know negations. He provides two examples: one from fiqh (the story of Barīrah) and one from logic (animality vs. humanity). This lesson will guide you through his argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the logicians' claim in the Arabic: 'إذا انحصرت الأقسام فمن الجائز أن يكون معللاً بالمجموع، أو بالبعض الذي لا تحقق له في الفرع'. What are they asserting?
They argue that in analogical reasoning, the cause could be the whole combination or a part not present in the branch, making the analogy uncertain.
2

This shows that the cause could be one or the whole, but it does not prevent us from knowing that certain other things are not the cause.

Since animality is common, the cause cannot be what is specific to man (humanity). Thus, we know that humanity is not the cause.

Combine both examples: the objection does not invalidate analogical reasoning because even if the cause could be the whole or a part, we can still have knowledge of what the cause is not (negation). This preserves the validity of qiyas in many cases.
Ibn Taymiyya's point is epistemological: certainty about negations is possible even when positive identification is uncertain.

The Doctrine of Specific Cause ('Illa Qasira) and Its Rebuttal
Author rebuts the possibility of a cause being specific to a case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a cause that is specific (qasira) and one that is general (muta'addiya), and explain why a 'illa qasira invalidates analogical reasoning.

Core Idea
A cause that stops at the text is not a real cause—it is just the essence of the subject.

In debates about analogical reasoning, logicians sometimes argue that even if a certain description is the only possible cause for a ruling, it might be specific to that particular case. Ibn Taymiyya responds that this amounts to saying the ruling is based on the essence of the subject itself, which is not a genuine cause. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating whether an analogy is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Locate the logicians' claim: even if a cause is the only possible one, it might be specific to the original case ('illa qasira) and not generalizable.
The objection appears in the quoted statement.
2
Recognize the author's response
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya equates this with a previously refuted objection: the ruling is attached to the essence of the subject (dhat al-mahal), not a separate cause.
He says it is 'similar in meaning to the first question'.
3
Understand 'illa qasira
A 'illa qasira is a cause that is confined to the textual basis—it does not extend beyond that specific case. It is essentially the same as saying the ruling is due to the subject itself.
This makes analogy impossible because the supposed cause is not transferable.
4
Evaluate consequences for analogy
Determine: if the cause is qasira, analogical extension is invalid; if it is muta'addiya (transitive), analogy is valid. This distinction is critical in usul al-fiqh.
Contrast with the example of wine: if the cause is 'being wine' (qasira), nabidh cannot be analogized; if it is 'intoxication' (muta'addiya), it can.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of Ibn Sina on Moral Judgment
Ibn Taymiyya objects to Ibn Sina's claim that judgments of good and bad rely solely on custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's dialectical objection to Ibn Sina's claim that moral judgments rely solely on custom.

Core Idea
Moral ugliness is not mere custom; all rational minds intuitively judge injustice as ugly.

Ibn Sina argues that if isolated from society, a person would not deem stealing or lying as ugly. Ibn Taymiyya calls this a baseless claim and shows that when the situation is specified to unjust taking, universal intellect agrees on ugliness. This passage illustrates how a dialectical objection (naqd) works: it exposes ambiguity in the opponent's premise and provides a counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under attack
Find Ibn Sina's assertion that judgments of good and bad have no basis except common fame (shuhra). He says that if left alone with pure intellect, estimative faculty, and senses, a person would not judge stealing or lying as ugly.
Ibn Sina reduces moral judgment to social convention.
2
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's initial objection
He rejects the claim outright: 'We do not accept this, for this is a bare claim' (لا نسلم هذا فإن هذه دعوى مجردة). This is a standard dialectical move (man' or naqd).
3
Analyze the logical refinement
Ibn Taymiyya points out that the proposition 'taking a person's property is ugly' is ambiguous. It could be just or unjust. The discussion concerns unjust taking, which the agent knows is unjust.
He specifies the case to expose the flaw in Ibn Sina's generalization.
4
Observe the evidence provided
In the specific case of unjust taking (e.g., one partner overpowering another in a joint property), all rational people and their estimative faculties judge that act as ugly. This universal agreement suggests an innate, not conventional, judgment.
The evidence is the consensus of rational minds (ijma' al-'uqala').
5
Assess the refutation's strength
Ibn Taymiyya shows that when moral propositions are made precise, they are not purely conventional. His objection effectively rebuts the claim that such judgments lack basis in intellect or innate disposition.
The burden shifts back to Ibn Sina to explain why universal agreement occurs.

Ibn Taymiyya's Correction of Dialectical and Rhetorical Materials
Author refutes the narrow definitions of rhetorical and dialectical syllogisms, correcting their materials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' definitions and explain his broader understanding of the materials of dialectical and rhetorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Dialectical and rhetorical syllogisms are defined by audience and purpose, not by epistemic certainty.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya refutes the narrow definitions of dialectical and rhetorical syllogisms given by logicians. He argues that their materials are not inherently uncertain; rather, they are defined by context: dialectic uses any premises accepted by the disputant, and rhetoric uses well-known propositions for the public. This correction allows demonstrative truths to serve in dialectic and rhetoric, blurring rigid boundaries.

For dialectic: the material is musallamāt (accepted premises), which can be well-known or not, true or false. For rhetoric: the material is mashhūrāt (well-known propositions) used to address the public, which can be certain or conjectural.

Note that these materials can be certain (ʿilmiyya) or conjectural (ẓanniyya). Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that certainty does not exclude a proposition from serving rhetoric or dialectic, contrary to the logicians' view.

Contrast with the sophistical
The sophistical (sūfisṭāʾī) is falsehood disguised as truth, studied only to refute it, not for normal use. This clarifies that the other three types (demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric) are legitimate when true.
5
Connect to the broader framework

كون الأنبياء ثم قد يقولون : إن ما جاءت به الأنبياء فهو من جنس الخَطَابة التي قصد بها خطاب الجمهور، لم يقصد به تعريف الحقائق. هذا في الأمور العلمية، فإن مبادىء الأمور العلمية قد لا يجعلونها من البرهانيات، بل من المشهورات، كالعلم بحسن العدل وقبح الظلم. وأما العلميات فيقولون : إن الأنبياء لم يذكروا حقائق الأمور في معرفة الله والمعاد، وإنما أخبروا الجمهور بما يتخيلونه في ذلك لينتفعوا به في إقامة مصلحة دنياهم، لا ليعرفوا بذلك الحق. ويقولون : إنهم أرادوا بخطابهم للناس أن يعتقدوا الأمور على خلاف ما هي عليه وهي من جنس الكذب لمصلحة الناس وهم يعلمون هذه المرتبة . ثم النبي عندهم هل يعرف الأمور العلمية؟ فيه نزاع بينهم .

كون الأنبياء وهم يعظمون محمداً صلى الله عليه وآله وسلم ويقولون: لم يأت إلى العالم عندهم من ناموس أفضل من ناموسه ويفضّله كثير منهم على الفيلسوف، ومنهم من يفضل جنس واضعي النواميس على الفيلسوف وهم حائرون في أمور الأنبياء، ولهذا كلامهم في الأنبياء في غاية الاضطراب. ولم ينقلوا عن أرسطو وأتباعه فيهم شيئاً، بل ذكروا من كلام أفلاطن وغيره في النواميس ما جعلوا به واضعي النواميس من اليونان وغيرهم من جنس الأنبياء الذين ذكرهم الله في القرآن. ونحن نعلم أن الرسل جميعهم دَعَوا إلى عبادة الله وحده لا شريك له، كما قال تعالى: ﴿ وَسَتَلْ مَنْ أَرْسَلْنَا مِن قَبْلِكَ مِن رُّسُلِنَا أَجَعَلْنَا مِن دُونِ الرَّحْمَنِ وَالِهَةً يُعْبَدُونَ ﴾ [سورة الزخرف : ٤٥] وقال تعالى : ( وَلَقَدْ بَعَثْنَا فِي كُلِّ أُمَّةٍ رَسُولًا أَنِ اعْبُدُوا اللهَ وَاجْتَنِبُوا الطَّاغُوتَ ﴾ [سورة النحل : ٣٦]. والنواميس التي يذكرونها فيها شرك لا يأمر به أحد من أنبياء الله . فعلم أن كل من ذكروه من واضعي النواميس المخالفة لما اتفقت عليه الرسلُ فليس بنبي ولا متّبع لنبي . بل هو من جنس واضعي النواميس من ملوك الكفار، ووزرائهم وعقلائهم وعلمائهم، وعبادهم .

المقام الرابع - الوجه الحادي عشر : قولهم في الأنبياء كقول المتكلمين في الأئمة

وهم وإن عظموا الأنبياء ونواميسهم فلأجل أنهم أقاموا قانون العدل الذي لا تقوم مصلحة العالم إلا به . ويوجبون طاعة الأنبياء والعمل بنواميسهم وهي الشرائع التي جاؤا بها، ولكن هم عندهم لم يأتوا بالأمور العِلْمية، بل بالعمليات النافعة. والعلميات عندهم إما أن تكون التي عَلمها وما أمكنه إظهارها بل أظهر ما يخالف الحق عند المصلحة الجمهور، وإما أنه لم يعلمها. وإلا فهم يجوّزون للرجل أن يتمسك بأي ناموس كان ولا يوجبون اتباع نبي بعينه - لا محمد ولا غيره - إلا من جهة ارتباط مصلحة دنياهم بذلك، لا لأنه يعذَّب في الآخرة على مخالفة شريعة محمد أو غيره . ولهذا لما ظهرت التركُ الكفار وأراد مَنْ أراد منهم أن يدخل في الإسلام قبل ظهور إشارة بعضهم على هولاكو بأن الإسلام عليهم، أشار عليه بعضُ من كان معه من الفلاسفة بأن لا يفعل قال ذاك لسانه لا يدخل في عربي، ولا يحتاجون إلى شريعته ونحو هذا الكلام يبيّن أن الشريعة التي جاء بها محمد لا يحتاج إليها مثلكم وأمثالكم. وقد قيل إن الذي أراد الدخول في الإسلام وقال له منجمه هذا هو هولاكو . ولما قدم هولاكو الشام وتقلد القضاء من جهته بعضُ قضاة الشام الذين كانوا يعظمون صوفية الفلاسفة كابن عربي ونحوه، ودخل إلى البلد، أخذ يثني على ملك الكفار ويعظمه ويذكر ما يذكر من فضائله بزعمه. فقال له بعض الحاضرين : يا ليته كان مسلماً»! فقال القاضي وأي حاجة لهذا إلى الإسلام؟ سواء كان مسلماً أو لم يكن . وهذا بناء على هذا الأصل.

فالنبي عندهم يُشبه من بعض الوجوه أئمة المذاهب عند المتكلمين، كأبي حنيفة، ومالك والشافعي، وأحمد بن حنبل، وإسحاق بن راهويه وسفيان الثوري. قولهم في الأنبياء كقول المتكلمين في أئمة المذاهب
The speech of the people of philosophy concerning the prophets, peace be upon them.

Concerning the prophets, then they may say: What the prophets brought is of the genus of rhetoric, which aimed at addressing the general public, not intended to define the truths. This is in theoretical matters; for the principles of theoretical matters they may not make them demonstrative, but rather from the commonly accepted opinions, like the knowledge of the goodness of justice and the ugliness of injustice. As for practical matters, they say: The prophets did not mention the realities of things concerning knowledge of God and the afterlife; rather, they informed the public with what they imagined about that so that they might benefit from it in establishing their worldly interests, not so that they might know the truth thereby. And they say: They intended by their address to people to make them believe things contrary to what they are, and it is of the genus of falsehood for the benefit of people, while they know this status. Then the prophet according to them: does he know theoretical matters? There is dispute among them.

Concerning the prophets: They venerate Muhammad, peace be upon him, and say: There has not come to the world according to them a nomos better than his nomos, and many of them prefer him over the philosopher, and among them are those who prefer the genus of the lawgivers over the philosopher, and they are confused about the affairs of the prophets. Therefore, their speech about the prophets is in utmost confusion. And they did not report anything from Aristotle and his followers about them; rather, they mentioned from the speech of Plato and others concerning the nomoi what made the lawgivers from the Greeks and others to be of the genus of the prophets whom God mentioned in the Qur'an. And we know that all the messengers called to the worship of God alone with no partner, as He said: 'And ask those We sent before you of Our messengers: Did We make deities besides the Most Gracious to be worshipped?' [Surat al-Zukhruf: 45] And He said: 'And We certainly sent into every nation a messenger, [saying], Worship Allah and avoid taghut.' [Surat al-Nahl: 36]. And the nomoi that they mention contain polytheism which none of the prophets of God commanded. So it is known that everyone they mentioned among the lawgivers who contradict what the messengers agreed upon is not a prophet nor a follower of a prophet. Rather, he is of the genus of the lawgivers from the kings of the disbelievers, their viziers, their intellectuals, their scholars, and their worshippers.

The fourth station - the eleventh aspect: Their statement about the prophets is like the statement of the mutakallimun about the imams.

And even though they venerate the prophets and their nomoi, it is because they established the law of justice through which the world's interest cannot be established. And they obligate obedience to the prophets and action according to their nomoi, which are the laws they brought. But according to them, they did not come with theoretical matters, but with beneficial practical matters. And theoretical matters according to them: either the prophet knows them and it is not possible for him to manifest them; rather, he manifests what contradicts the truth for the benefit of the public, or he does not know them. Otherwise, they permit a man to adhere to any nomos, and they do not obligate following a specific prophet — not Muhammad nor any other — except from the aspect of the connection of their worldly interest to that, not because he will be punished in the hereafter for opposing the law of Muhammad or others. For this reason, when the disbelieving Turks appeared and some of them wanted to enter Islam before the emergence, and some of them suggested to Hulagu that Islam is against them, some of the philosophers who were with him advised him not to do so, saying: 'That tongue does not enter Arabic, and they have no need for his law.' And similar speech shows that the law brought by Muhammad is not needed by the likes of you and your ilk. It has been said that the one who wanted to enter Islam and his astrologer said this to him was Hulagu. And when Hulagu came to Syria and some of the judges of Syria who used to venerate the Sufi philosophers like Ibn Arabi and others were appointed as judges by him, and he entered the city, he began to praise the king of the disbelievers and venerate him and mention what he mentioned of his virtues according to his claim. So one of the attendees said to him: 'If only he were a Muslim!' Then the judge said: 'And what need does he have for Islam? Whether he is Muslim or not.' And this is based on this principle.

Thus the prophet according to them resembles in some aspects the imams of the schools according to the mutakallimun, like Abu Hanifa, Malik, al-Shafi'i, Ahmad ibn Hanbal, Ishaq ibn Rahawayh, and Sufyan al-Thawri. Their statement about the prophets is like the statement of the mutakallimun about the imams of the schools.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' view of prophets. He begins by quoting their claim that prophetic messages are of the genre of rhetoric (khataba), intended for the masses, not for conveying truth. In theoretical matters, they say the principles are taken from commonly accepted opinions (mashhurat), not demonstration. In practical matters, they assert that prophets did not reveal true knowledge of God and the afterlife but gave imaginative depictions for worldly benefit, even if those are false, for the public good.

Ibn Taymiyya then notes the philosophers' confusion: they praise Muhammad (peace be upon him) but treat him as a lawgiver (sahib namus) rather than a true prophet. They rely on Plato and others, equating Greek lawgivers with prophets. Ibn Taymiyya counters with Qur'anic evidence that all messengers called to tawhid, and the nomoi (laws) they mention contain shirk, so those lawgivers are not prophets.

In the 'eleventh aspect,' he compares the philosophers' view of prophets to the mutakallimun's view of imams (like Abu Hanifa etc.). The philosophers respect prophets for establishing a necessary legal order but deny them scientific knowledge. They allow adherence to any nomos and do not require following a specific prophet except for worldly expediency. To illustrate, Ibn Taymiyya recounts an anecdote about Hulagu and a philosopher advising him not to convert, and a Syrian judge who saw no need for Islam. He concludes that for philosophers, a prophet is like a school imam — a source of practical law, not divine truth.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: Prophets vs. Lawgivers

Core Idea
Philosophers reduce prophets to lawgivers for worldly order, akin to school imams.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' view of prophets. He begins by quoting their claim that prophetic messages are of the genre of rhetoric (khataba), intended for the masses, not for conveying truth. In theoretical matters, they say the principles are taken from commonly accepted opinions (mashhurat), not demonstration. In practical matters, they assert that prophets did not reveal true knowledge of God and the afterlife but gave imaginative depictions for worldly benefit, even if those are false, for the public good.

Ibn Taymiyya then notes the philosophers' confusion: they praise Muhammad (peace be upon him) but treat him as a lawgiver (sahib namus) rather than a true prophet. They rely on Plato and others, equating Greek lawgivers with prophets. Ibn Taymiyya counters with Qur'anic evidence that all messengers called to tawhid, and the nomoi (laws) they mention contain shirk, so those lawgivers are not prophets.

In the 'eleventh aspect,' he compares the philosophers' view of prophets to the mutakallimun's view of imams (like Abu Hanifa etc.). The philosophers respect prophets for establishing a necessary legal order but deny them scientific knowledge. They allow adherence to any nomos and do not require following a specific prophet except for worldly expediency. To illustrate, Ibn Taymiyya recounts an anecdote about Hulagu and a philosopher advising him not to convert, and a Syrian judge who saw no need for Islam. He concludes that for philosophers, a prophet is like a school imam — a source of practical law, not divine truth.

The Purpose of Dialectical Disputation in Islam
Author sets criteria for valid disputation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions Ibn Taymiyya sets for valid disputation and apply them to evaluate an argument.

Core Idea
Disputation without knowledge is condemned; true debate requires certain premises.

In this opening segment, Ibn Taymiyya outlines the proper role of jadal (disputation). He distinguishes between disputation based on knowledge, which is commendable, and disputation based on mere dialectical premises, which only serves to expose contradiction. This principle frames his entire critique of the logicians.

Guided Path
1
Locate the Quranic verse
Find the verse Ibn Taymiyya cites (3:66) and note its context: arguing without knowledge is condemned.
2
Extract the positive principle
From the verse and subsequent text, state the rule: Allah commands disputation only with knowledge.
3
Identify the exception
Ibn Taymiyya allows disputation using false premises only to show inconsistency, not to establish truth.
This is a limited exception for dialectical purposes.
4
Distinguish the Quran's purpose
The Quran aims to present truth, not to engage in dialectical contradictions.

The Fourth Station - The Twelfth Aspect: Their denial of the existence of jinn, angels, and revelation is speech without knowledge. Obstinacy and resistance, and the sermon is not resisted as the disputant resists. As long as a person is receptive to wisdom or good admonition or both, he has no need for disputation. If he resists, let him be disputed with in the best way. In clarifying error: And disputation is with knowledge, just as wisdom is with knowledge. Allah has condemned those who dispute without knowledge, saying, 'Here you are - you argued about that of which you have knowledge. Why do you argue about that of which you have no knowledge?' [Quran 3:66]. And Allah does not command the believers to dispute using a premise that the opponent concedes if it is not knowledge. If he said something false, Allah would not command to argue against them with falsehood. But this may be done to show the corruption of his statement and to show its contradiction, not to show the call to the true statement. The Quran's purpose is to show the truth and call the servants to it, not to mention what they contradict in their statements to show the error of one of them without specifying it. So the dialectical premises that are not knowledge have this benefit. And this is suitable for showing the error of people in general. Then sometimes they make prophethood to be from the category of rhetoric, and sometimes they make rhetoric one of the types of its speech, so they contradict themselves. The reason for that is that the Quran is a great and brilliant matter; they did not know its worth, nor did they realize through the Quran what it contains of knowledge and wisdom. They wanted to liken it to the speech of a people of disbelieving Jews and Christians, who are less astray than them in knowing Allah, His prophets, His books, His command and prohibition, and His promise and threat. If someone likened the Quran to the Torah and the Gospel, his error would be extremely apparent - and all are the speech of Allah. So how about the speech of these ignorant atheists? The Twelfth Aspect: Their denial of the existence of jinn, angels, and revelation is speech without knowledge. Their speech: The Twelfth Aspect is to say: They acknowledge the apparent sensibles and the inner sensibles. Their acknowledgment of the sensibles: that which a person senses in himself, like his sensing of his hunger and thirst, his pleasure and pain, his apparent and inner desire, his anger, his joy and sadness, and also what he imagines in himself of examples of sensibles that he has sensed, for he imagines that from himself. And they have distinguished between the faculty of 'imagination' and 'estimation'. Imagination is to imagine the sensibles, and estimation is to conceive in the sensibles a non-sensible meaning, as one conceives friendship and enmity. For the sheep conceives of the wolf a meaning which is enmity, and it is non-sensible, and it does not imagine. And it conceives in the goat a meaning of friendship. So they call the awareness of alliance and enmity 'estimation'. And they did not speak about that in the language of the Arabs, for 'estimation' in the language of the Arabs has another meaning, as has been explained in its place. And they have nothing that denies the existence of what can be seen only by some people inwardly, like the angels and jinn; nor do they have anything that denies the embodiment of these spirits as bodies so that they are seen by the apparent sense; nor do they have that the soul sees other than what is from its states, such as seeing at death existing things that it did not see while it was attached to the body. Likewise the sleeper, even if he may imagine, they have no knowledge that everything he sees is imagined, given the possibility that he sees in his sleep something of the same kind as what the dead person sees at the separation of his soul from his body. For the rational soul is the seer, and its attachment to the body prevents it from that. So when it becomes completely disengaged by death, it sees what it did not see, as Allah said: 'So We removed from you your cover, and your sight today is sharp.' [Quran 50:22]. And He said: 'Then why, when it [the soul] reaches the throat, and you at that time are looking on - and We are nearer to him than you, but you do not see -' [Quran 56:83-85]. The commentators said: He means the angels. And similarly, during sleep, a kind of disengagement may occur for it. For this reason, some people see something in their sleep, and it comes to pass as they saw it without any change at all; rather, what is seen in the dream is the same as what exists in wakefulness. As for the seeing of many people of the jinn during epilepsy and other than epilepsy, this is more widespread and famous than to be mentioned. And what some doctors claim that epilepsy is entirely from humors is clearly false. For the jinn's entering the human body and speaking upon his tongue with types of speech and other things is a matter that many people have known necessarily. And the imams of Islam have agreed upon it, just as they agreed upon the existence of jinn. And more than one person has seen them and addressed them. So this is a vast subject. What then of the noble angels, whom people see?

Commentary
This passage is the Twelfth Aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians. He argues that their denial of jinn, angels, and revelation is baseless speech without knowledge. He begins by discussing the purpose of disputation (jadal), emphasizing that it should be with knowledge and that Allah condemns arguing without knowledge. He notes that the Quran's goal is to present truth, not to engage in dialectical contradictions. Then he moves to the specific argument: the logicians acknowledge both external and internal senses, but they cannot deny the existence of beings that may only be perceived by some individuals (like angels and jinn) or through the disengaged soul at death or in dreams. He supports this with Quranic verses and empirical observations of jinn possession. The passage refutes the logicians' claim that such entities are unreal, asserting that they have no evidence for their denial.

The Logicians' Incoherent Classification of Prophethood and Rhetoric
Author exposes contradiction in logicians' classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical inconsistency in the logicians' classification of prophethood and explain Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Core Idea
Logicians contradict themselves by alternately categorizing prophethood as rhetoric and rhetoric as a type of its speech.

Ibn Taymiyya exposes a contradiction: the logicians cannot agree on whether prophethood belongs to the category of rhetoric or vice versa. This inconsistency reveals their ignorance of the Quran's true nature and demonstrates that their denial of revelation is baseless.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two claims
Find where Ibn Taymiyya says the logicians make prophethood from rhetoric, and also make rhetoric a type of its speech.
2
Spot the contradiction
If prophethood is from rhetoric, then rhetoric cannot be a type of its speech without circularity.
3
Connect to the Quran
Ibn Taymiyya argues this stems from not understanding the Quran's greatness; they equate it with human speech.

Core Idea

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya continues his systematic dismantling of the philosophers' claim that celestial souls have comprehensive knowledge of all events. Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Celestial Soul Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya argues that even if spheres had souls, they only intend their own motions, not the resulting particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the claim that celestial souls know all generated events and evaluate its internal consistency.

Core Idea
Intending an action does not entail knowing all its consequences.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya engages with the philosophers' claim that celestial souls have comprehensive knowledge of all events generated by their motions. He grants their assumption about celestial souls but shows that even then, knowledge of particulars does not follow. He then turns to their emanation theory to expose a deeper contradiction. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate both prongs of his critique and assess their logical force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers assert that celestial souls (al-nufus al-falakiyya) know everything that occurs due to the sphere's motion. Locate this claim in the passage.
Ibn Taymiyya opens with 'السادس' indicating a sixth argument, so this is one of several.
2
Analyze the concession and distinction
Ibn Taymiyya concedes the existence of celestial souls for the sake of argument. Yet he distinguishes between what the soul intends (its own motion) and the generated events that follow from separate causes. The soul may not be aware of those consequences.
This mirrors the human case: a person intends an action but may not know all its effects.
3
Trace the causal chain argument
The generated events (e.g., element mixture) are not directly caused by the sphere's motion alone; they involve separate causes and conditions. Therefore, knowledge of them is not necessary for the celestial soul.
Ibn Taymiyya breaks the supposed necessary connection.
4
Expose the emanation contradiction
Ibn Taymiyya points out an internal inconsistency: if the Active Intellect contains only universals, it cannot produce particular corporeal forms and knowledge. The philosophers' theory is self-contradictory.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
5
Evaluate the critique's overall validity
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's argument successfully refutes the philosophers' position. Does he rely on assumptions they would accept? Is the critique fair?
The critique targets the coherence of emanationism, not just empirical claims.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Theologians' Debates
Ibn Taymiyya critiques theologians' inadequate refutation of philosophers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the weakness in al-Shahrastani's debate with the Sabians and explain Ibn Taymiyya's critique of theological debates against philosophers.

Core Idea
Inadequate knowledge of Islam makes theological refutations weak and self-defeating.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya examines a recurring problem: theologians who argue against philosophers often do so with a shallow understanding of their own faith. He uses al-Shahrastani's famous debate as a case study. Let's dissect his critique and learn how to strengthen our own defenses of Islamic doctrine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Ibn Taymiyya is criticizing the debates of many theologians against the philosophers. He picks al-Shahrastani's debate from his book al-Milal wa al-Nihal as a concrete example.
Al-Shahrastani was a prominent theologian, so his mistake is significant.
2
Understand the two positions
The Hanifas (monotheists) accept mediation through humans (prophets), while the Sabian polytheists accept mediation through celestial beings. Al-Shahrastani tries to argue that human mediation is better.
Ibn Taymiyya notes that both sides are talking about mediation, but the concept differs fundamentally.
3
Locate the flaw in al-Shahrastani's argument
Ibn Taymiyya says that if mediation is understood as the Sabians (and philosophers) conceive it—as an ontological intermediary in creation and governance—then the Sabians' claim is more coherent. Al-Shahrastani's refutation fails because he accepts the wrong framework.
The weakness stems from 'weak knowledge of the reality of Islam.'
4
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's corrective
The true Islamic position is that there are no mediators in the sense of partners in Lordship. Only God creates, governs, and responds to supplication directly. Prophets and angels are only conveyors of revelation and intercessors by God's permission, not ontological intermediaries.
This distinction is crucial for proper tawhid.

He uses al-Shahrastani's debate as an example: al-Shahrastani compared the Hanifas (monotheists) and the Sabian polytheists over the issue of mediation (tawassut). The Hanifas accept mediation through humans (prophets), while the Sabians accept mediation through celestial beings. Al-Shahrastani tried to argue that human mediation is preferable. However, Ibn Taymiyya points out that if mediation is understood as the Sabians (and philosophers) conceive it—as an actual ontological link in creation and governance—then their claim would be more coherent. So al-Shahrastani's refutation was weak because he did not fully grasp the true Islamic position: that there are no mediators in the sense of ontological intermediaries in creation, provision, etc. Rather, in Islam, only God is worshipped and asked directly. The prophets and angels are merely means of conveying revelation and intercession by God's permission, not actual agents in the cosmos.

Commentary
This passage is a critical assessment by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' doctrine of intercession (tawassuṭ) and of al-Shahrastānī's attempt to rebut them. Ibn Taymiyya begins by declaring that the shirk and kufr of the philosophers—who believe that celestial beings (al-‘ulwiyyāt) are causal intermediaries—is worse than that of the Arab polytheists. He then explains why the theologians’ (ahl al-kalām) debates with the philosophers are deficient: they do not fully understand the true nature of the message brought by the prophets. In particular, he mentions al-Shahrastānī's debate between the ḥunafā' (monotheists) and the Ṣābi'a (pagan Sabians), where the ḥunafā' assert mediation through humans while the Ṣābi'a claim mediation through celestial beings. Al-Shahrastānī attempted to show that human mediation is preferable, but Ibn Taymiyya argues that given the philosophers' assumptions about the power of celestial beings, their position actually appears stronger. Consequently, al-Shahrastānī's refutation is weak because he lacked a proper grasp of the reality of Islam. The underlying issue is that the ḥunafā' do not accept any independent causal efficacy in creation, while the philosophers attribute real causal power to celestial spirits.
