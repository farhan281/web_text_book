The Requirement of Primary Differentiae in Genus Division
Author explains the requirement of primary differentiae in genus division and its difficulty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given division of a genus uses primary differentiae and construct the correct hierarchical division for any genus.

Core Idea
A valid definition requires stepwise division by primary differentiae, not a jump to far-removed species.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya highlights a subtle but crucial condition in logical definition: the differentia dividing a genus must be primary—it must directly divide the immediate genus without skipping intermediate levels. He uses concrete examples to show that violating this condition leads to definitions that are logically unsatisfactory, exposing the stringency of the logicians' own rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' requirement
Find the claim: the differentia must be primary (awwaliyya) relative to the genus. What does 'primary' mean here?
Primary differentia directly divides the immediate genus, not a higher genus.
2
Understand the problem with skipping levels
Consider the example: dividing 'body' directly into 'rational' and 'non-rational'. Why does Ibn Taymiyya object? Trace the skipped intermediate genera.
Body → growing/non-growing → animal/non-animal → rational/non-rational is the correct order.
3
Trace the correct hierarchical division
Follow Ibn Taymiyya's prescription: first divide 'body' by primary differentia into 'growing' and 'non-growing', then subdivide 'growing' into 'animal' and 'non-animal', then 'animal' into 'rational' and 'non-rational'.
Each step uses a differentia that is primary relative to the current genus.
4
Apply the same logic to the animal example
Explain why dividing 'animal' directly into 'biped' and 'quadruped' is not primary. What intermediate genus is missing?
'Animal' should first be divided into 'walking' and 'non-walking' because bipedality follows from walking, not from animality itself.
5
Assess the difficulty
Reflect on why this condition is very difficult to fulfill in practice. What knowledge of the entire hierarchy is required?
One must know all intermediate genera, which is often not available or requires deep ontological analysis.

The fourth: The differentia constitutes the species and divides the genus. If the condition in division is not observed, there are differentiae that are not primary to the genus, and that is not satisfactory in definitions. For verily, just as 'body' divides into 'growing' and 'non-growing' by a differentia that is below the primary—essential—so too it divides into 'sentient' and 'non-sentient' and into 'rational' and 'non-rational'. So whenever it is said: 'body' divides into 'rational' and 'non-rational', it has divided by what is not the primary dividing differentia. Rather, it should first divide into 'growing' and 'non-growing', then 'growing' divides into 'animal' and 'non-animal', then 'animal' into 'rational' and 'non-rational'. Likewise, 'animal' divides into 'biped' and 'quadruped', but this division is not by primary differentiae. Rather, 'animal' should be divided into 'walking' and 'non-walking', then the 'walking' divides into 'biped' and 'quadruped', because 'animal' is not apt for 'biped' and 'quadruped' by virtue of being animal, but by virtue of being walking, and it is apt for being walking by virtue of being animal.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth difficulty: the requirement that division of a genus must be by primary differentiae (fusul awwaliyya). Ibn Taymiyya explains that the differentia (fasl) both constitutes the species and divides the genus. If one does not observe the proper order of division, one may use a differentia that is not primary. For example, 'body' can be divided into 'growing' (nami) and 'non-growing', which is primary, but also into 'sentient' and 'non-sentient', or 'rational' and 'non-rational'. But the latter divisions are not primary because they skip intermediate genera. The correct order is to first divide 'body' into 'growing' and 'non-growing', then 'growing' into 'animal' and 'non-animal', then 'animal' into 'rational' and 'non-rational'. Similarly, 'animal' should not be directly divided into 'biped' and 'quadruped'; rather, first divide into 'walking' (mashi) and 'non-walking', then 'walking' into biped and quadruped. The reason is that the disposition for having two legs follows from being walking, not directly from being animal. This hierarchical division is necessary for a valid definition, but it is extremely complex and often ignored.

Key Terms
الفصل (al-faṣl): Differentia; the characteristic that distinguishes a species from its co-species within a genus.
الجنس (al-jins): Genus; a universal predicated of many differing in species.
النوع (al-naw'): Species; the specific kind constituted by genus and differentia.
فصل أولي (faṣl awwalī): Primary differentia; a differentia that directly divides the immediate genus without skipping intermediate logical levels.

Ibn Taymiyya’s Classification of Senses and the Superiority of Sight vs Hearing
Ibn Taymiyya classifies the senses and argues that sight is stronger but hearing is more comprehensive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare Ibn Taymiyya's classification of the senses and explain his reconciliation of the debate between the superiority of sight and hearing.

Core Idea
Sight is more perfect in perception; hearing is more comprehensive in knowledge.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya examines the five senses and their modes of perception, distinguishing those that require direct contact from those that do not. He then addresses a longstanding debate among scholars about whether sight or hearing is superior, citing a prophetic hadith and offering his own reconciliation. By understanding his analysis, you will grasp an important epistemological distinction that has implications for how knowledge is acquired in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the senses discussed
List the five senses Ibn Taymiyya mentions: sight (basar), taste (dhawq), smell (shamm), touch (lams), and hearing (sam'). Note that he groups taste, smell, and touch together as requiring direct contact.
2
Distinguish direct contact vs. non-contact
Sight perceives without direct contact with the object. Taste, smell, and touch require direct contact (mubashara). Hearing is different: it senses sounds but its primary purpose is to receive speech and reports.
3
Analyze the epistemic functions
Hearing's greatest purpose is knowledge of speech and reports from informants. This gives hearing a special role in transmitting knowledge, which is why some scholars, like Ibn Qutayba, prefer it.
4
Examine the disagreement and hadith
The majority prefer sight, citing the hadith 'The one who is informed is not like the one who witnesses.' Ibn Taymiyya does not reject this but reconciles by saying sight is stronger and more perfect (aqwa wa akmal), while hearing is more general and comprehensive (a'amm wa ashmal).
5
Apply the distinction
Consider how this applies to Islamic sciences: in jurisprudence, eyewitness testimony is considered stronger than hearsay; in learning, both hearing from a teacher and reading (sight) are valuable. Reflect on which sense you prioritize in your own learning and why.

Commentary
This passage, titled 'The third station: The distinction between the types of sensory things,' is a comparative epistemological analysis of the senses. Ibn Taymiyya first notes that the differences among sensory types affect various sciences more than the previous distinction (likely between tajriba by action vs. non-action). He then characterizes each sense: basar (sight) perceives without direct contact, while dhawq (taste), shamm (smell), and lams (touch) require direct contact. Hearing (sam') senses sounds, but its primary purpose is to receive speech and reports, which convey knowledge. This leads to a debate: some, like Ibn Qutayba, prefer hearing over sight due to its role in transmitting knowledge. The majority, however, prefer sight, citing the hadith 'The one who is informed is not like the one who witnesses.' Thus, sight is stronger (aqwa) and more perfect (akmal), while hearing is more general (a'amm) and more comprehensive (ashmal). He concludes that these two senses are the origin of the knowledge that distinguishes humans from animals.

Ibn Taymiyya on Experiential Knowledge (Tajriba) as Sensory Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes experiential knowledge (tajriba) as a category of sensory knowledge and explains its cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cause of experiential knowledge and explain why Ibn Taymiyya considers it a subset of sensory knowledge rather than a separate type.

Core Idea
Experiential knowledge is sensory knowledge based on repeated conjunction, not personal action.

Ibn Taymiyya expands the category of sensory knowledge (hissiyyat) to include experiential knowledge (tajriba). He argues that the cause of such knowledge is repeated observation of conjunction between events, whether one performs the action or not. This has direct implications for how we classify knowledge in Islamic epistemology and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate 'al-mujarrabāt' and 'al-ḥissiyyāt' in the Arabic passage. Note their definitions: mujarrabāt are experiential matters based on repeated conjunction; hissiyyāt are sensory perceptions of the five senses.
2
Extract the cause of experiential knowledge
The text states the cause is 'takarur iqtiran ahad al-amrayn bil-akhar' (repeated conjunction of one thing with another), either absolutely or with awareness of a suitable connection. This is the epistemological basis.
3
Analyze the scope of the cause
Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that this cause applies whether the repeated conjunction results from one's own action or the action of another. Personal agency is irrelevant to the knowledge produced.
This refutes the idea that tajriba requires personal experimentation.
4
Understand the classification implication
Because the cause is identical, there is no need to treat tajriba as a fundamentally different type of knowledge. It is a subset of sensory knowledge, unified by the same causal mechanism.

The Third Station – The Distinction Between the Types of 'Sensory Perceptions'. Likewise, what is known from the Sunnah of Allah Almighty – from the victory of His prophets and believing servants, and His punishments of His disbelieving enemies – is something that is known [by experience], and reflection upon it occurs, even if that is not something that the person himself has the power to do [by his own action]. And a person may know from the action of another what brings him experiential knowledge, even if he does not have the power to perform that action himself. Also, the cause necessitating knowledge of 'experiential matters' is the repeated conjunction of one of two things with the other, either absolutely or with awareness of 'the suitable [connection]'. This [cause] is shared by what is repeated by one's own action and what is repeated by the action of another. So its being by one's own action is a description that has no effect in necessitating knowledge; thus there is no need to make this a separate type from the other type, given their equality in the cause necessitating knowledge, except to clarify that 'experiential matters' encompass these two varieties, just as it is said regarding 'sensory perceptions' that they include what one sees, hears, smells, tastes, touches, and the like.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the concept of sensory knowledge (hissiyyat) and how experiential knowledge (tajriba) fits within it. Ibn Taymiyya begins by pointing out that what is known from Allah's sunnah—such as His aiding His prophets and punishing His enemies—is known through reflection and experience, even if one has not personally performed those acts. He then explains that the cause of knowledge in experiential matters is the repeated conjunction of two things, either absolutely or with awareness of an appropriate connection. This cause applies regardless of whether the person himself performed the repeated act or witnessed it in someone else. Hence, the fact that it is one's own action is irrelevant to the epistemological value. Therefore, there is no need to make experiential knowledge a separate type from other sensory knowledge, except to highlight that 'experiential matters' include both varieties. This is analogous to how sensory perceptions are said to include sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch. The key point is that the underlying cause of knowledge is the same, so the classification should reflect that unity.

Classification of the Senses and the Superiority of Sight Over Hearing
Ibn Taymiyya classifies the senses and argues that sight is superior to hearing in quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the five senses according to Ibn Taymiyya's criteria and explain why sight is considered more perfect while hearing is more comprehensive.

Core Idea
Sight is more perfect, hearing more comprehensive—both are essential for knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya delves into the nature of sensory perception, moving beyond mere biology to classify senses by their mode of contact and purpose. He engages with the classical debate on whether sight or hearing is superior, ultimately affirming the majority view while acknowledging the unique strength of hearing in acquiring transmitted knowledge. This lesson unpacks his classification and his nuanced verdict.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification criteria
Note how Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes senses based on direct contact with the object and the primary purpose of the sense. Sight perceives without direct contact; taste, smell, and touch require direct contact; hearing, though perceiving sounds indirectly, has its greatest purpose in apprehending speech and reports.
2
Extract the debate on superiority
Find the two positions: some (like Ibn Qutayba) prefer hearing because it conveys speech and knowledge; the majority prefer sight. Ibn Taymiyya cites the hadith 'The one who is informed is not like the one who witnesses' to support the majority view.
The hadith is key evidence for sight's superiority in quality of perception.
3
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's verdict
He says sight is stronger and more perfect (akmal), while hearing is more general and comprehensive (a'amm wa ashmal). He also notes that hearing provides more knowledge overall due to transmitted reports. So the final view is a balance: sight for perfection, hearing for extent.
4
Connect to Quranic evidence
Observe how he cites Quranic verses pairing 'hearing', 'sight', and 'heart' to show that these senses, when guided by the heart, lead to true knowledge. The verses about disbelievers and hypocrites illustrate that senses without heart understanding are useless.
This reinforces the epistemological and spiritual dimension of the senses.

Commentary
This passage classifies the senses and discusses the relative merits of sight and hearing. Ibn Taymiyya begins by noting that the differences among sensory types are greater than one might think. He distinguishes between sight, which does not require direct contact with the object, and taste, smell, and touch, which require direct contact. Hearing, although it perceives sounds, has its greatest purpose in understanding speech and reports of knowledge. This leads to the classical debate about which sense is superior: some, like Ibn Qutayba, prefer hearing because it conveys speech and knowledge; the majority prefer sight because it is more perfect in perception. Ibn Taymiyya sides with the majority, citing the prophetic hadith that the one who witnesses is not like the one who is informed, indicating the superiority of sight. However, he adds that hearing provides more knowledge overall because of its role in transmitting speech and reports. Thus, sight is stronger and more perfect, while hearing is more general and comprehensive. He then emphasizes that these two senses are the foundation of human knowledge that distinguishes humans from animals. To support this, he quotes numerous Quranic verses that pair hearing and sight with the heart, showing that their true function is to lead to understanding and faith. The verses about the disbelievers and hypocrites who have ears but do not hear, eyes but do not see, illustrate that the mere possession of senses does not guarantee knowledge; it is the heart's comprehension that matters. Finally, he returns to the topic to mention the other three senses—smell, taste, and touch—which are pure physical senses requiring direct contact, and which are not as instrumental in acquiring higher knowledge as sight and hearing.

Commentary
This passage is a critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' conception of 'divine science' (al-'ilm al-ilahi). Two Types of Division: Universal vs. Whole
Clarification of two types of division to refute an objection and a grammatical dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between division of a universal into its particulars (taqsim al-kulli) and division of a whole into its parts (taqsim al-kull), and apply this distinction to resolve disputes in grammar and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Not all divisions require the divided term to apply to the parts; only universal division does, not whole-part division.

Ibn Taymiyya clarifies a fundamental logical distinction that resolves an objection against the logicians and a grammatical dispute. He explains that division can be of two kinds: dividing a universal (like genus into species) where the universal name applies to each part, and dividing a whole (like a body into limbs) where the whole name does not apply to each part. This lesson will help you master this distinction and see its power in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Define taqsim al-kulli
Division of a universal into its particulars: the universal term applies to each particular. Example: 'Animal is divided into rational and non-rational' — each is an animal.
This is also called division of genus into species.
2
Define taqsim al-kull
Division of a whole into its parts: the name of the whole does not apply to the parts. Example: 'The house is divided into roof, walls, and ground' — no single part is a house.
This type is common in everyday language and Islamic law (e.g., division of inheritance).
3
Apply to the grammatical dispute
Early grammarians divided 'speech' (kalam) into noun, verb, particle as a whole into parts. Later grammarians like al-Juzuli objected that the divided term must apply to each part, confusing this with universal division. Ibn Taymiyya shows their error.
The early grammarians were correct because 'speech' is a composite whole, not a universal.
4
Test with Quranic examples
Ibn Taymiyya cites Quran 54:28 about water division (whole into parts) and 15:44 about gates (whole into parts). These illustrate taqsim al-kull.
These examples confirm the naturalness of whole-part division in Arabic.
5
Reflect on implications for the initial objection
The Shiite objection assumed the logicians' subject must be a universal divided into particulars. Ibn Taymiyya uses the two-type distinction to show that the subject could be a whole (e.g., 'every existent' as a whole) and still allow investigation of parts.
This does not fully vindicate the logicians but shows the objection is based on a misunderstanding of division types.

Commentary
This passage is a critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' conception of 'divine science' (al-'ilm al-ilahi). Proof is divided by direction: universal→particular, particular→universal, or particular→particular.

If all particulars are examined, the induction is complete and the conclusion is certain. But such cases are rare.

Complete vs. Incomplete Induction
Ibn Taymiyya reports the logicians' division of induction into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete from incomplete induction and identify when an inductive generalization is falsifiable.

Core Idea
Complete induction surveys all cases and gives certainty; incomplete induction only surveys most and can be refuted by a single counterexample.

The logicians divide induction into two types: complete (istiqra' tamm) and incomplete (istiqra' naqis). The former yields certain knowledge, while the latter only yields probability and can be false. The crocodile jaw example is a classic illustration of how an incomplete induction can be overturned by a single counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of complete induction
Complete induction examines every single particular under a universal. If all particulars share a property, the universal conclusion is certain.
Complete induction is rare in practice because it requires exhaustive enumeration.
2
Understand the definition of incomplete induction
Incomplete induction examines only most particulars. The conclusion is not certain and may be false if counterexamples exist.
The text warns that incomplete induction 'may be false'.
3
Analyze the crocodile example
Someone claims all animals move their lower jaw when eating, based on observing many animals. But the crocodile moves its upper jaw, thus falsifying the generalization. This shows the weakness of incomplete induction.
The counterexample disproves the universal statement.
4
Apply the distinction to Islamic sciences
If a jurist says 'all scholars hold that zakat is due on dates' based on surveying most scholars, that is incomplete induction. A single dissenter weakens the claim.
In usul al-fiqh, certainty is required for definitive rulings; incomplete induction gives only probability.

The Ambiguity of the Term Qiyas

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Logicians use qiyas for universal-to-particular syllogism; usul scholars use it for particular-to-particular analogy.

The passage highlights a crucial terminological confusion: logicians reserve the term 'qiyas' for the syllogism of inclusion (universal to particular), while many scholars of usul al-fiqh and kalam use it for analogy (particular to particular). The same Arabic word 'qiyas' is used for two different logical operations. This can cause misunderstandings when reading texts from different disciplines.
Ibn Taymiyya notes that the 'majority of rational people' use qiyas to include both meanings.
3
Determine the intended meaning from context
Look for clues: if the text discusses 'universal' and 'particular', it likely means syllogism. If it compares two specific cases to extend a ruling, it likely means analogy.
Discipline matters: logic texts usually mean syllogism; fiqh texts usually mean analogy.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Exhaustive division ensures no possibility is left out—essential for sound kalām arguments.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic provides a concrete example of exhaustive division (al-taqsīm al-ḥāṣir) in the classical debate about seeing God. Two opposing views are presented, each claiming a different corrector for vision. By examining this division, you will learn how exhaustive division works and why it is critical in Islamic theological reasoning.

Identify the principle of division
The division is based on the question: 'What qualifies something to be seen?' The corrector for vision (al-muṣaḥḥiḥ lil-ruʾya) is the attribute that makes an object visible.
In an exhaustive division, the principle must be clear and single.
2
List the two divisions
Group A says the corrector is pure existence (wujūd maḥḍ), so the more perfect the existence, the more worthy of being seen. Group B says it is deficient existence (wujūd nāqiṣ), which is closer to non-existence.
These are the only two possibilities stated; they are presented as mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.

Check exhaustiveness
Are there any other logical possibilities for a corrector? The division claims that existence is either perfect or deficient; there is no third state. This is a binary exhaustive division.
If a third possibility were overlooked, the division would not be ḥāṣir.
4
Apply the division to the theological claim
If perfect existence is the corrector, then God, having the most perfect existence, must be visible. If deficient existence is the corrector, then God, being purely perfect, cannot be seen. The division directly frames the theological conclusion.
The exhaustive division forces a choice and clarifies the stakes in the debate.

Core Idea
Not all circularity is invalid: co-circularity is possible, only pre-circularity is impossible.

In Islamic logic and jurisprudence, the concept of circular reasoning (دور) is often misunderstood as always fallacious. Ibn Taymiyya clarifies that circularity has three types, and the first type (cosmic) includes two subtypes with different logical statuses. This classification is essential for evaluating arguments in theology and fiqh where interdependence of concepts occurs.

This division is attributed to al-Amidi, while al-Razi considered cosmic circularity entirely impossible.

Determine the correct position according to Ibn Taymiyya
Ibn Taymiyya endorses al-Amidi's view: only pre-circularity is impossible; co-circularity is logically possible. This nuance is critical for arguments involving mutual dependence, like conditions and their objects.

Core Idea
Not all circularity is vicious: co-circularity is common and valid in cosmic relations.

Understanding this difference will help you avoid false accusations of circular reasoning when analyzing arguments in Kalam and other Islamic sciences.

Three Types of Logical Division
Author categorizes division into three types based on prevention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given division statement into one of the three types: preventing both conjunction and void, preventing conjunction only, or preventing void only.

Core Idea
Division is defined by what it prevents: coexistence, absence, or both.

In Islamic logic, division (taqseem) is used to categorize things mutually exclusively. Understanding the three types helps in analyzing juristic categories and theological opposites. Ibn Taymiyya presents these types with clear examples from everyday and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the division
Locate the two disjuncts in the statement. For example, in 'number is either even or odd', the parts are 'even' and 'odd'.
2
Check if the parts can coexist
Determine whether the two parts can occur simultaneously. If they cannot, the division prevents conjunction (mani'at al-jam').
3
Check if both parts can be absent together
Determine whether it is possible for neither part to hold. If not, the division prevents void (mani'at al-khuluw).
4
Combine the checks to determine the type
If both conjunction and void are prevented, it is the first type. If only conjunction is prevented, it is the second. If only void is prevented, it is the third.
Third type applies to condition-conditioned relationships.

Clarifying the Logical Condition (al-Shart)
Author clarifies the concept of condition and distinguishes it from grammatical condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the logical condition (shart) from the grammatical conditional and explain why condition and conditioned cannot both be absent.

Core Idea
The logical condition is a necessary condition; its absence entails absence of the result, but its presence does not guarantee the result.

The term 'shart' is used differently in grammar and logic. Ibn Taymiyya clarifies that the logical condition is that from whose absence the absence of the ruling follows, using examples from Islamic law, and warns against confusing it with grammatical 'if' clauses.

Guided Path
1
Note the definition of shart
Shart is defined as 'that from whose absence the absence of the ruling follows'. This means it is a necessary condition.
Examples: purification is a condition for prayer; life is a condition for knowledge.
2
Contrast with grammatical condition
Grammar's 'if' (e.g., 'if you study, you succeed') is a cause that brings about the result. The logical condition does not guarantee the result; it only makes it possible.
Grammatical condition is often sufficient, not just necessary.
3
Understand the impossibility of both being absent
Condition and conditioned cannot both be absent simultaneously. If the conditioned exists, the condition must exist; but the condition can exist without the conditioned.
Absurd examples: drowning without water, valid prayer without ablution.
4
Apply to Fiqh examples
Identify conditions for acts of worship: wudu for prayer, sanity for accountability. These are logical conditions, not grammatical ones.

The Four Categories of Entities Called Upon Besides Allah
Ibn Taymiyya enumerates and negates four categories of entities called upon besides Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the four categories of entities that people call upon besides Allah as derived from Surah Saba, and understand why each is negated, thereby reinforcing Tawhid.

Core Idea
Four categories — owner, partner, supporter, intercessor — cover every possible relationship, and the Quran negates them all.

Ibn Taymiyya, may Allah have mercy on him, extracts from the Quran a precise logical framework that categorizes any entity people call upon besides Allah. He shows that such an entity must be either an independent owner (malik), a partner (sharik), a helper (zahir), or an intercessor (shafi‘). The verse from Surah Saba (34:22-23) explicitly denies all these roles to anything other than Allah, leaving no room for shirk. By mastering this classification, you will be able to recognize and refute any form of improper invocation.

Guided Path
1
Read the Quranic evidence
Examine the two verses: al-Isra 17:57 (those who seek a means to their Lord) and Saba 34:22-23 (the core verse with the four negations). Understand the context.
The first verse establishes that even the entities called upon are themselves seeking nearness to Allah.
2
Extract the four categories
From Saba 34:22-23, identify the four negations: no ownership (lā yamlikūn), no partnership (mā lahum fīhimā min shirk), no supporter (mā lahu minhum min ẓahīr), and no intercession except by His permission (lā tanfa‘u al-shafā‘atu illā li-man adhina).
These correspond to the categories Ibn Taymiyya lists: mālik, sharīk, ẓahīr, shafī‘.
3
Understand each category's definition
Define each category precisely: mālik: an independent owner; sharīk: a partner sharing ownership; ẓahīr: a helper or supporter; shafī‘: an intercessor. Note that the intercessor is only effective if Allah permits.
The definition of each term is crucial to see why the negation is absolute.
4
Apply the logical implication
Since all four categories are negated for any entity besides Allah, there is no valid basis to call upon or rely on them. Any request to a created being must fall into one of these roles, all of which are negated.
This logical deduction reinforces the core principle of Tawhid: worship and supplication are due to Allah alone.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
All human requests fall into three exhaustive categories: separate property, partnership, or need-based assistance.

Ibn Taymiyya provides a logical classification (taqsim) of human interactions when one person asks another for something. This classification is not merely descriptive but serves as a precise analytical tool for understanding the nature of relationships. Core Idea
Polytheists' beliefs about God mirror the three categories of human relationship errors.

Ibn Taymiyya now applies the three-part classification to the beliefs of polytheists. He shows that each type of shirk corresponds to one of the categories: affirming an independent agent (fa'il mustaqill), partners, or helpers. This lesson will trace how the classification illuminates the nature of shirk, using the examples of the Magians and the Qadariyya.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three categories
From the previous lesson, list the three categories of mutual asking: separate property, partnership, helper/assistant.
This is the framework for analysis.
2
Identify the polytheists mentioned
The passage names the Magians (al-majus) and the Qadariyya (al-Qadariyya). Note that the Magians believed in an ancient evil independent agent. The Qadariyya believed that humans create their own actions independently of God.
Both groups are examples affirming an independent agent (fa'il mustaqill).
3
Map each group to a category
The Magians and Qadariyya both affirm an independent agent other than God who acts without dependence. This corresponds to the first category: each has distinct property/domain. The polytheists treat the false deity as having a separate realm of action, analogous to separate property.
Other polytheists may correspond to the other two categories, though the passage only develops the first.
4
Understand the Salaf's saying
The Salaf said: 'The Qadariyya are the Magians of this Ummah.' This equates the two groups because both attribute independent creative acts to other than God, making them fall into the same category of shirk.
The classification provides the logical basis for this equation.

فالمشركون بالله كل منهم في نوع من هذه الأنواع: منهم من أثبت فاعلاً مستقلاً غير الله، لكن لم يثبتوه مماثلاً له - لا في ذاته، ولا في صفاته، ولا في أفعاله. وهذا كالمجوس الذين أثبتوا قديماً شريراً يستقل بفعل الشر. وكذلك القائلون منهم أنه خلق الشر، والقدرية من جميع الأمم أثبتوا غير الله يحدث أشياء ينفرد بإحداثها دون الله، وإن كان الله خالقاً له. ولهذا قال السلف: «القدرية مجوس هذه الأمة».
Division of people: and for this reason, people, some with others, among kings and others, regarding what they ask of each other, do not go beyond these categories: either each of them has distinct property from the other, so he asks from this one what is in his property and from this one what is in his property. Or one of them is a partner of the other, so he asks from him what one asks from a partner. Or one of them is among the helpers and supporters and backers of the other, like the helpers of kings, and he is in need of them, so he asks from them what he needs. And if these conditions are absent, nothing remains except pure request and asking without any need of the asked to the asker or intercessor. The polytheists with God, each of them is in one of these types: among them is one who affirmed an independent agent other than God, but they did not affirm Him as similar to Him - neither in His essence, nor in His attributes, nor in His actions. This is like the Magians who affirmed an ancient evil one who independently does evil. And likewise those among them who said that He created evil, and the Qadariyya from all nations affirmed other than God who brings about things independently without God, even though God is their creator. Hence the Salaf said: 'The Qadariyya are the Magians of this Ummah.'

Commentary
This passage is a logical classification (taqsim) followed by its theological application. The author first divides all human interactions of mutual request into three exhaustive categories: (1) each party has separate property, so request is for what belongs to the other; (2) they are partners, so request is as from a partner; (3) one is a helper or supporter of the other, requesting aid. If none apply, it is pure begging. Then, Ibn Taymiyya applies this division to polytheists, showing they fall into these categories: some affirm an independent agent (fa'il mustaqill) distinct from God but not similar—like Magians believing in a separate evil creator—and also those who attribute creation of evil to another, like the Qadariyya. The Salaf's saying equating Qadariyya to Magians reinforces this. The logical structure highlights how theological errors mirror social relationships.

Key Takeaway
Ibn Taymiyya's application shows that shirk is not just idol worship but includes any belief that attributes independent agency to other than God, as seen in the Magians and Qadariyya.
