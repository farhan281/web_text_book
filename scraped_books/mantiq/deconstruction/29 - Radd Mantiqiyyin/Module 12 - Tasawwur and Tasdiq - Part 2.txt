Universal Propositions and Human Nature
Ibn Taymiyya argues that universal acceptance proves innate cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument from universal agreement to innate human nature.

Core Idea
Universal agreement on basic truths proves they stem from our shared humanity.

Ibn Taymiyya presents a powerful epistemological argument: if certain propositions are accepted by all nations, their cause must lie in what is common to all humans. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and shows how it grounds moral and practical knowledge in the fitra.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional claim
Ibn Taymiyya begins with 'If these propositions did not have a principle in the powers of man, they would not be well-known among all nations.' This is a conditional (if...then) that sets up the argument.
2
Recognize the universal fact
He asserts that these propositions are indeed well-known among all nations. This is the factual premise that triggers the conditional.
The universality is key: it cannot be due to cultural transmission because cultures differ.
3
Infer the cause in common nature
From the conditional and the fact, he concludes that there must be a cause in the shared innate nature (fitra) of all nations.
This is modus tollens: if not A then not B, but B is true, so A is true.
4
Determine the nature of the cause
He argues that the cause can only be from the necessary attributes of humanity (lawāzim al-insāniyya), because nations only share in what is essential to being human.
Accidental or cultural traits vary, so they cannot explain universal agreement.

This cause cannot be accidental or cultural, because nations differ in culture; it must be a necessary attribute (lazima) of humanity itself. This is an epistemological argument for the commonality of basic moral and practical knowledge across all human societies.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Universal Knowledge of Particulars
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the Avicennan doctrine of universal knowledge of particulars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the claim that universal knowledge suffices for knowledge of particulars, and evaluate its force in the context of Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Universal knowledge of particulars is a contradiction: the universal misses the particular.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes aim at the Avicennan doctrine that God knows all particulars in a universal manner. He argues that this claim is self-defeating because universal concepts, by their very nature, cannot capture the specificity of individual existents. He extends this critique to the subject of metaphysics itself, absolute existence, which he denies has any extra-mental reality. This lesson walks through his argument and its implications for theology and epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Find the exact position Ibn Taymiyya attacks: that God knows particulars universally. Underline the Arabic phrase 'يعلم الجزئيات على وجه كلي'.
This is the Avicennan position he deems contradictory.
2
Locate the contradiction
Ibn Taymiyya says this is a 'تناقض' (contradiction). Understand why: if knowledge is only universal, it cannot differentiate individuals. The universal is common, while each particular is unique.
He uses the example of changing particulars to emphasize the gap.
3
Trace the implication for God's knowledge
If God's knowledge were only universal, then He would not know any particular thing in its particularity. Ibn Taymiyya says this would mean 'قد عز عن علمه كل شيء في الوجود' – everything escapes His knowledge.
This contradicts the Qur'anic claim that nothing escapes His knowledge.
4
Analyze the critique of metaphysics
Ibn Taymiyya connects this to the subject of first philosophy (الوجود الكلي المطلق). He argues that this subject does not exist externally, so knowing it yields no knowledge of actual existents.
Thus, the universal concept of existence is mentally constructed, not real.
5
Evaluate the force of the critique
Does the argument successfully undermine the Avicennan position? Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya assumes that universal knowledge is purely abstract, while Avicenna might claim it encompasses particulars via their causes.
This evaluation is key to understanding the debate.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of Ibn Sina's doctrine that God knows particulars in a universal way. Ibn Taymiyya argues that this is a contradiction. He explains that if someone only knows universals and cannot know particular individuals, especially if they are changing, then they actually know nothing of existent particular things. Universal knowledge does not give knowledge of specific individuals. He then extends this to criticize the subject of metaphysics ('first philosophy') namely absolute existence, which is a universal concept that does not exist externally. Knowing this universal does not give knowledge of any specific existent. Therefore, the claim that God knows all particulars through a universal knowledge is self-defeating because universal knowledge by itself cannot capture particularity.

Key Terms
الكلي المشترك (al-kullī al-mushtarak): The common universal, a concept applicable to many individuals but lacking extra-mental existence; knowing it does not entail knowing instances. [Note: Ibn Taymiyya uses it polemically to challenge the reality of universals as objects of knowledge; earlier logicians treat it neutrally.]
الجزئيات (al-juz'iyyāt): Particular individuals or events; knowledge of them requires direct acquaintance, not just universal concepts. [Note: Stable term, but Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes the epistemic gap between universal and particular.]
الوجود الكلي المطلق (al-wujūd al-kullī al-muṭlaq): Absolute universal existence, the subject of metaphysics; it does not exist externally and knowing it does not give knowledge of specific existents. [Note: Ibn Taymiyya denies its reality as a subject of science, contrary to Avicennan metaphysics.]

External Causes of Beliefs and Desires
Author cites a hadith on devil's whisper and generalizes to principle of external influence on beliefs and desires.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's argument for the external causation of beliefs and desires and explain how a hadith is used as evidence for this principle.

Core Idea
External agents, not just reason, shape beliefs and desires — both corrupt and correct.

Among the many tools in the Islamic intellectual tradition for evaluating truth is the recognition that human beliefs are not formed in a vacuum. Ibn Taymiyya, in his critique of the claim that prophetic knowledge can be attained without prophets, establishes a fundamental epistemological principle: beliefs and desires are caused by external agents — devils, angels, and righteous humans. In this passage, he begins with a familiar hadith about the devil's whisper in prayer and generalizes from it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith cited
Read the hadith about the devil whispering during prayer. The Prophet informs of the whisper but does not command repeating the prayer. What does this imply about the effect of the whisper?
The whisper affects concentration but does not invalidate the prayer.
2
Derive the initial implication
The absence of a command to repeat prayer implies that the whisper, while real, does not nullify the act. This establishes that external influences can occur without rendering the action invalid.
This is a key move: acknowledging influence without annulment.
3
Extract the generalized principle
Ibn Taymiyya generalizes: corrupt beliefs and desires come from devils (human and jinn), and correct ones from angels and righteous humans. How does he justify this leap? By analogy with the devil's whisper.
The generalization is from a specific case to a universal causal mechanism.
4
Analyze the mechanism of influence
The passage describes a speaker (fāʿil) and a listener (qābil). Speech affects the heart only if the listener is receptive. This adds nuance: external causation operates through receptivity, not deterministically.
Receptivity explains why the same speech may affect different people differently.
5
Relate to the broader critique
Why does Ibn Taymiyya establish this principle? To argue that even if external influences shape beliefs, they do not replace the need for prophets; prophetic knowledge is a special divine influence.
This principle is foundational for his refutation of those who claim to attain prophetic knowledge without prophets.

This sets up a foundation for refuting the claim that one can attain prophetic knowledge without prophets, because even if external influences can affect the heart, they do not substitute for prophetic revelation.

Why the Philosophers' View of Intercession is Worse Shirk
Comparative critique of two forms of intercession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the philosophers' concept of intercession with that of the Arab polytheists and identify why the former constitutes greater kufr according to Ibn Taymiyya.

Core Idea
Denying divine will and action makes philosophers' 'intercession' worse than pagan requests.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya intensifies his critique by comparing the philosophers' view of intercession to that of the pre-Islamic Arabs. He argues that while the Arabs at least believed Allah hears and answers, the philosophers strip the intercessor of any active role and deny Allah’s knowledge of particulars. This makes their polytheism more severe. We will unpack this comparison and understand the logical grounds for Ibn Taymiyya’s judgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups being compared
Find the Quranic verse (3:79-80) and the description of Arab polytheists vs. the philosophers (al-falasifah) in the passage.
The verse condemns taking angels and prophets as lords.
2
Extract the Arab polytheists' belief about intercession
They believed that intercessors (like prophets and angels) would ask Allah and Allah would respond, as indicated by 'يسألُه ويَرْغَبُ إليه'.
They affirmed Allah's power and will.
3
Extract the philosophers' belief about intercession
The philosophers hold that the intercessor does not ask or act; rather, it is an 'emanation' (فيض) like sunlight reflecting off a mirror.
They deny Allah's knowledge of particulars and will.
4
Compare the theological implications
The Arab polytheists still affirmed Allah as Creator with power and will, while the philosophers deny these attributes, making their shirk more fundamental.
Denial of attributes is worse than association.
5
Assess why Ibn Taymiyya considers the philosophers' shirk worse
He uses a fortiori reasoning: if the Arabs are condemned for asking intercessors, then those who deny any divine role in intercession are even more guilty.
This is an argument from greater to lesser (qiyas al-awlawi).

The Emanation Model and Its Polytheistic Implications
Explanation and refutation of the emanation model of intercession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the philosophers' emanation model of intercession as presented by Ibn Taymiyya and identify how it leads to polytheistic grave visitation practices.

Core Idea
Philosophers reduce intercession to involuntary emanation, mirroring sun rays, leading to shirk.

Ibn Taymiyya goes beyond comparison to explain the specific mechanism of the philosophers' intercession: the concept of fayd (emanation). He describes how they imagine intercession as a passive flow of spiritual power, analogous to sunlight reflecting off a mirror. This emanation model, attributed to Ibn Sina, forms the basis for what Ibn Taymiyya calls innovated grave visitation. In this lesson, we will dissect this model and its polytheistic consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' model of intercession
Find where the author explains that when a person turns to a saint or spirit, 'there flows to them what flows from that magnified one... without any action or request'.
The model is passive and automatic.
2
Understand the analogy of the sun and mirror
The author compares the process to sunlight falling on a polished body and reflecting to another surface. The sun acts without intention, and the mirror merely reflects.
The intercessor is like the mirror, not an agent.
3
Trace the source of this idea
Ibn Taymiyya attributes this concept to Ibn Sina and his followers, like the author of 'al-Kutub al-Madnun biha'.
This identifies the target group.
4
Connect the model to grave visitation
The author states that those who hold this view visit graves with the intention of receiving emanation, which he contrasts with the legislated visitation for greeting and praying for the deceased.
This is a practical manifestation of the theological error.
5
Assess the polytheistic nature
Since the model denies any actual request to Allah and attributes effect to created beings (via emanation), it constitutes associating partners with Allah in a more subtle way.
It is a form of shirk because it seeks from other than Allah what only Allah can give.
