Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Philosophical Tawatur
Ibn Taymiyya exposes weakness of philosophers' tawatur and their ignorance of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that philosophical tawatur is epistemologically weak compared to prophetic tawatur, and evaluate its internal validity.

Core Idea
Prophetic tawatur surpasses philosophical tawatur because the latter relies on limited observers.

Ibn Taymiyya delivers a sharp critique of the philosophers' claim to certain knowledge through tawatur. He argues that their empirical observations and astronomical data depend on a small number of individuals, rendering their tawatur weak. In contrast, prophetic reports come through widespread transmission that yields certainty. This passage challenges the epistemological foundations of philosophy relative to revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers claim that their empirical observations (e.g., medicine, astronomy) are supported by tawatur, which they consider a source of certainty.
Ibn Taymiyya begins with the example of medicine to show the limited nature of such tawatur.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's objection
He argues that their tawatur is not widespread but based on a few individuals ('special tawatur'), thus it cannot yield genuine certainty.
He says: 'This at most is from the special tawatur that is like a single report.'
3
Analyze the comparative analogy
Ibn Taymiyya likens equating philosophy to prophecy to equating blacksmiths with angels or village chiefs with kings, emphasizing the vast gap in epistemic rank.
This hyperbolic analogy underscores the inferiority of philosophical knowledge.
4
Evaluate the overall argument
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's critique is logically sound: he applies the same standard of tawatur to both domains, but assumes that prophetic tawatur meets the highest criteria. Assess if his conclusion follows from his premises.
The argument is valid if one accepts the definition of tawatur as widespread transmission; it challenges the philosophers' consistency.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Awwaliyat-Mashhurat Distinction
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the logicians' distinction between primary and well-known premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemological flaw in the logicians' distinction between primary and well-known premises, and explain Ibn Taymiyya's alternative view of the middle term as a mental proof.

Core Idea
The distinction collapses when you realize the middle term is a mental proof, not a real intermediary.

Ibn Taymiyya's critique targets the heart of the logicians' epistemological hierarchy. They claimed that primary premises are those where the predicate inheres in the subject without any mediating cause, while well-known premises rely on external factors like public interest or habit. But Ibn Taymiyya shows this distinction rests on a faulty understanding of what a 'middle term' is. In this lesson, we will dissect his objection and see why he considers the distinction corrupt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim about awwaliyat and mashhurat
The logicians define awwaliyat as propositions where predication is immediate, without a middle term. Well-known premises (mashhurat) are those where the judgment is influenced by external causes like habits or public interest. This is the claim Ibn Taymiyya attacks.
2
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's test for distinguishing them
Ibn Taymiyya proposes a thought experiment: mentally strip away all habits, emotions, and societal influences, then present the subject and predicate to the intellect. If the intellect judges solely from the presence of the terms, it's primary; otherwise, it's well-known. He uses 'lying is ugly' as an example that fails the test.
This test shows that 'lying is ugly' is not primary because the intellect hesitates when isolated from social conventions.
3
Analyze the corrupt principle behind the distinction
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the logicians' distinction is based on a false theory about necessary attributes (sifat lazimah) — that some attributes are self-necessary with no real middle term, while others require a real middle term. He counters that the 'middle term' invoked is actually the middle term of a syllogism, which is a mental concept used in deduction, not an ontological intermediary.
Ibn Taymiyya mentions that Ibn Sina equated 'middle term' with the middle term of a syllogism (hadd al-awsat), which is a mental proof.
4
Formulate Ibn Taymiyya's alternative view
The proof (dalil) is a mental middle term; it does not correspond to a real intermediary in the actual existence of the attribute. One can deduce cause from effect or effect from cause, so the presence or absence of a deductive middle term does not define self-evidence. Thus, the distinction collapses.
Ibn Taymiyya provides examples of deduction from cause to effect and vice versa to illustrate that the middle term is just a mental tool, not a real factor in the subject-predicate relationship.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition in which Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' distinction between primary intelligibles (awwaliyat) and well-known premises (mashhurat). He begins by quoting the logicians: they claim that well-known premises resemble primary ones but differ in that a primary judgment is one in which the predicate inheres in the subject in both mental and external existence without any mediation. He then presents a test to distinguish them: one must mentally isolate oneself from all external influences—such as habits, social conventions, emotions, or considerations of public interest—and present the subject and predicate to the intellect. If the intellect judges solely based on the presence of the two extremes, the proposition is primary; otherwise it is well-known. He gives examples: 'Lying is ugly' is well-known because of public interest, and 'inflicting pain is ugly' is well-known because of tenderness. He argues that these are not primary because the intellect hesitates when presented with a self-evident truth like the law of non-contradiction but does not hesitate with 'lying is ugly'. Next, he notes that well-known premises can be true or false. True ones may be primary or require demonstration. Every primary is well-known but not vice versa. The reasons for well-knownness include being primary, connection to order, psychological emotions, or general induction. Then Ibn Taymiyya declares his own critique. He identifies the corrupt basis: the logicians define primary as 'the subject is established for the predicate by itself in both worlds, mental and external' and non-primary as 'relying on a middle term'. He says this distinction is based on a false principle about necessary attributes (sifat lazimah). The false principle holds that some attributes are self-necessary with no real middle term, while others require a real middle term. Ibn Taymiyya counters that the middle term (wasat) the logicians speak of is actually the middle term of the syllogism, which is a mental concept used by the reasoner (al-dalil). It is not a real intermediary in the actual existence of the attribute in the subject. He illustrates: one can deduce the effect from the cause, the cause from the effect, one effect from another, or the existence of one opposite from the non-existence of the other, and so on. Thus, the presence or absence of a 'middle term' does not distinguish primary from well-known premises. The critique is aimed at undermining the epistemological hierarchy that logicians build on this distinction.

Proof is More General Than Cause
Author argues that proof is more general than cause with examples and critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn Taymiyya holds that proof is more general than cause, and identify his critique of the essential/accidental distinction.

Core Idea
Proof is any indicator; cause is just one type. Necessity does not depend on human knowledge.

In Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh, the term 'dalil' (proof) is often used interchangeably with 'illa' (cause). Ibn Taymiyya challenges this conflation by asserting that proof is far broader—it includes any necessary concomitant, not just causal relationships. He illustrates this with examples from daily life and Islamic practice, and then takes aim at the logicians' claim that some attributes are known without a middle term while others require one. This lesson unpacks his argument and its implications for how we understand evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
Read the opening line: 'The proof is more general than the cause.' This is the claim Ibn Taymiyya defends throughout.
2
Examine the examples
Look at the examples: smell → intoxication → prohibition; shadows → zenith → zuhr time; stars → north → qibla. Notice that in each, the indicator (smell, shadow, star) is not the cause of the indicated (intoxication, zenith, north), but a necessary concomitant.
These examples show proof working without a causal link.
3

Essence Is Not the Cause of Its Necessary Concomitant
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that an essence is the cause of its necessary concomitant by giving counterexamples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the claim that an essence is the cause of its necessary concomitant by identifying counterexamples from Ibn Taymiyya's argument.

Core Idea
Necessary concomitance does not imply causation.

Ibn Taymiyya takes aim at a core assumption of the logicians: that a thing's essence is the cause of its necessary properties. If this were true, then every pair of necessarily connected things would be causally related. But as Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates, many necessary relationships—like fatherhood and sonship, or rationality and laughter—are mutual concomitants without one causing the other. This lesson walks through his refutation and its significance for distinguishing entailment from causation.

He gives the example of God's attributes: life, knowledge, power, and will are mutually necessary (mutalazima), each requiring the others, and all are necessary for the essence. None is a middle term for another. Thus, the logicians' division is invalid.

The Refutation of Immediate Consequence Claim
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' claim that conceiving a cause and effect necessitates knowing their relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reductio argument against immediate consequence and explain why it leads to an infinite regress.

Core Idea
Immediate consequence without a middle term leads to an infinite regress and absurdity.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya attacks a foundational claim of the logicians: that knowing a cause and its effect immediately yields knowledge of their relation. He uses a reductio ad absurdum to show that if this were true, then one would have to know all consequences of any conceived thing. This critique is crucial for understanding the necessity of middle terms in logical inference, especially in Islamic legal reasoning where the effective cause ('illa) must be explicitly stated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that when you conceive a cause and its effect, you necessarily know that one is the cause of the other, without needing a middle term. This is called 'immediate consequence' (al-luzum al-bayyin).
Ibn Taymiyya targets this precise claim.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's reductio structure
Ibn Taymiyya says: if the claim is true for one pair (cause1, effect1), then it must also be true for the effect1 and its own effect (effect2). Thus, conceiving effect1 and effect2 forces knowledge of their relation. This chain continues indefinitely.
The reductio shows that the principle cannot be restricted; it applies universally.
3
Trace the infinite regress and the absurd conclusion
If the principle holds for any pair, it holds for all pairs along the chain. Therefore, whoever conceives something must conceive all its necessary consequences, which is absurd because humans do not know all consequences of a thing.
This is the 'what you fled from' — the very consequence the logicians wanted to avoid.
4
Apply the critique to an Islamic legal example
Consider wine (khamr) and its intoxication. If knowing wine and intoxication forced knowledge that wine is haram, then knowing intoxication and its consequence (prohibition) would also force knowledge, and so on. In reality, a middle term like 'every intoxicant is haram' is needed.
This shows why legal reasoning relies on the 'illa (effective cause) as a middle term.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Near and Far Consequences
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' distinction between near and far necessary consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the logicians' distinction between near and far necessary consequences and identify its logical force.

Core Idea
The condition for immediacy either applies to all consequences or none; the distinction collapses.

Ibn Taymiyya targets a key distinction in the logical tradition: the claim that some consequences of a concept are known immediately (near) while others require a middle term (far). He shows that the logicians' own condition for immediacy—that both the subject and the consequence be conceived—applies equally to the consequence of a consequence, making the distinction arbitrary. This critique undermines a foundational assumption of syllogistic theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' distinction
State the claim: near consequences (lawāzim qarība) are known without a middle term when both the thing (malzūm) and the consequence (lāzim) are conceived; far consequences (lawāzim baʿīda) require a middle term to be known.
This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's refutation.
2
Extract the condition for near consequences
The condition for immediate knowledge is that both the malzūm and the lāzim are present in the mind. This is supposed to guarantee the known relation of luzūm without extra proof.
Ibn Taymiyya quotes the logicians' own formulation.
3
Apply the condition to the second consequence
Consider the first consequence (lāzim awwal) as the new malzūm. Its consequence (lāzim thānī) is then the far consequence. The same condition should apply: if you conceive the first consequence and its own consequence, then the relation should be immediate.
The logicians cannot escape this without inconsistency.
4
Construct the dilemma
Either the condition is required for every relation, making far consequences also immediate, or the condition is dropped for the second, in which case it should also be dropped for the first. Both options destroy the distinction.
Ibn Taymiyya concludes: 'Both estimates show the distinction collapses.'
5
Assess the validity of the critique
The critique is valid if the logicians' condition is indeed universal. Ibn Taymiyya forces them to apply their own standard consistently. The argument does not reject inference altogether, only an arbitrary demarcation.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logician's Causality
Ibn Taymiyya exposes a contradiction in the logicians' view of causality

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Causality cannot be both identical to and distinct from the cause's essence without contradiction.

Ibn Taymiyya dismantles the logician's claim about causality by exposing a hidden contradiction. The logician asserts both that causality is not distinct from the cause's essence and that the essence alone is insufficient for the causal relation. Ibn Taymiyya shows these are incompatible, and even if causality is considered a mental relation, it cannot require a cause. Let's trace his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logician's claim
The logician says: 'We do not concede that causality is distinct from the reality of the cause's essence. But we claim that conceiving the cause and its near effect necessitates knowledge that the cause is a cause.' In other words, causality is not an additional attribute, yet it is known through the relation.
Pay attention to the dual assertion: non-distinctness yet need for a relation.
2
Extract the first horn of the dilemma
If causality is not distinct (mugayara) from the cause's essence, then the essence alone should suffice for the causal relation. But the logician denies this, claiming the essence alone is not enough. Contradiction.
Ibn Taymiyya uses the logician's own premise against him.
3
Examine the second horn: distinct causality
If causality is distinct, then it would be an additional attribute requiring a cause itself, leading to infinite regress. The logician avoids this by saying causality is a mental relation, not real. But then it is pure non-existence in reality, and non-existence does not need a cause.
Ibn Taymiyya dismisses the mental concept escape as self-defeating.
4
Connect to usul al-fiqh
In Islamic jurisprudence, the 'illa (effective cause) of a ruling is a real attribute, e.g., intoxication is the 'illa for wine's prohibition. This 'illa is distinct from the substance of wine. The logician's ambiguity between essence and relation is alien to the robust causal theory of usul al-fiqh.
This shows the practical consequences of the logician's flawed framework.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Awwaliyat-Mashhurat Distinction
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' claim that primary axioms differ from well-known statements by lacking a middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to the logicians' criterion for distinguishing primary axioms from well-known statements and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
The logicians' sharp division between self-evident and well-known propositions collapses under scrutiny.

The logicians claim that primary axioms (awwaliyat) are known without any middle term, while well-known statements (mashhurat) require a middle term. Ibn Taymiyya disputes this distinction, arguing that it is baseless because the absence of a middle term is not a unique property of awwaliyat. This lesson walks you through his refutation and tests the logical consistency of the logicians' position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that awwaliyat (self-evident truths) are known without any proof (dalil), meaning no middle term. In contrast, mashhurat (well-known statements) require a middle term to be proven. This distinction underpins their classification of premises.
2
Find Ibn Taymiyya's refutation
Ibn Taymiyya argues that this distinction is invalid ('bāṭil'). He writes that the proof of awwaliyat being known without a proof is itself false, and that the necessary consequence with a middle is only known by knowledge of the middle. His wording: 'فبطل ما ذكروه من دليل الفرق بين الأوليات والمشهورات.'
The passage is dense; key is to see he targets the criterion itself, not the examples.
3
Understand the reasoning about the middle
Ibn Taymiyya points out that if 'middle' means a real intermediate (wasit) in the chain of knowledge, then both awwaliyat and mashhurat can be known without a middle in some cases. The logicians' definition of awwaliyat as 'without a middle' is arbitrary because many mashhurat are also accepted without explicit demonstration.
Think of 'justice is good' – it is mashhurat in many cultures but often treated as self-evident.
4
Assess the logical force
Ibn Taymiyya's objection is that the logicians have not provided a rigorous criterion. By making the absence of a middle the defining feature, they fail to exclude mashhurat that are also known without a middle. This undermines the epistemological hierarchy they build on this distinction.
The critique does not deny that some propositions are self-evident; it denies that the logicians' classification is sound.

Refuting the Ordered Chain of Consequences
Ibn Taymiyya argues against an ordered chain of consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reductio argument against the ordered chain of consequences and identify the key assumption that leads to the absurdity.

Core Idea
If knowing the first consequence needs no proof, then knowing the first thing entails knowing every subsequent consequence.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' notion that consequences form an ordered essential chain where the first is self-evident and each subsequent requires a demonstration. He exposes a fatal inconsistency: whatever condition applies to the first consequence must apply to the second relative to the first, leading to the absurd conclusion that knowing one thing means knowing all its consequences. This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians claim that consequences (lawāzim) are ordered essentially: the first implies the second, the second implies the third, and so on. The first is known without a middle term (bayyin), while subsequent ones require a proof (burhān).
2
Extract the condition for the first
The first consequence is known without a middle term because its implication is immediate. This is the key assumption: no intermediate step is needed.
The term 'bayyin' means self-evident or requiring no proof.
3
Apply the same condition to the second
Reason: The second consequence follows from the first just as the first follows from the initial thing. If the first's implication is immediate from the original, then the second's implication from the first should also be immediate—no new middle term is needed beyond the first itself.
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'Whatever you said regarding knowing the consequence of the first for the first subject, the same is said regarding the second consequence for the first.'
4
Realize the implication
If every subsequent consequence is immediate relative to the previous one, then from knowing the first thing, you immediately know all consequences in the chain. This is an infinite regress of immediate knowledge.
5
Conclude the absurdity
Thus, the logicians' claim implies that knowing one thing (e.g., that the world is created) would force knowledge of all its consequences (e.g., every theological truth). This is false; each separate truth requires its own proof. Therefore, the original claim is inconsistent and absurd.
Ibn Taymiyya declares: 'This is extremely corrupt.'

Al-Razi's Second Proof: Avoiding Infinite Regress
Al-Razi's second proof and its epistemic structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's proof for the existence of self-evident concomitants and explain its reliance on the impossibility of infinite regress.

Core Idea
Without self-evident concomitants, knowledge of unknown ones leads to infinite regress.

Al-Razi offers a second argument to support his distinction between primary and theoretical propositions. He argues that when we conceive a quiddity, some of its necessary concomitants are unknown, and to know them we must rely on other concomitants that are self-evident; otherwise, an infinite regress follows. This proof, while accepted in principle by Ibn Taymiyya, sets the stage for a deeper critique of how we define 'primary' and 'theoretical'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Al-Razi wants to prove that some attributes are entailed without a middle term (non-demonstrative) and some with a middle term (demonstrative).
2
Extract the proof structure
When we conceive a quiddity (mahiyya), some of its necessary concomitants (lawazim) are unknown. To come to know them, we rely on other known concomitants. If none of these were self-evident (without a middle term), we would face an infinite regress or remain ignorant. Both are false. Therefore, there must be self-evident concomitants.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Identify the conditional form
The argument can be formalized: If there are no self-evident concomitants, then either infinite regress or no knowledge. Infinite regress and no knowledge are false. Therefore, there are self-evident concomitants. This is a modus tollens inference.
The conditional premise is: No self-evident → (regress ∨ ignorance). The negation of the consequent is asserted.
4
Note Ibn Taymiyya's acceptance
Ibn Taymiyya does not reject the existence of a distinction; he accepts that some propositions need evidence and some do not. However, he will later challenge the absolute, non-relative characterization of this distinction.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: Primary Propositions Are Relative
Ibn Taymiyya's critique of al-Razi's distinction and his relativistic alternative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the distinction between primary and theoretical propositions is relative to the completeness of one's conception.

Core Idea
What is self-evident for one person may be theoretical for another.

Ibn Taymiyya accepts that some propositions need evidence and some do not, but he rejects al-Razi's absolute classification. He argues that the distinction is not based on an inherent order among concomitants, but on the need for evidence, which varies with the completeness of conception. This has profound implications: a proposition can be primary (badihi) for Zayd and theoretical for 'Amr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Al-Razi defined primary propositions as those in which the predicate is 'first' in both external existence and mental attribution, implying an absolute order.
2
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's objections
First, in external existence, there is no temporal or causal order among concomitants such that one is first and another second. Second, in the mind, the only intermediary is evidence, not an inherent order. Third, evidence is not necessarily a cause ('illa).
These objections dismantle al-Razi's definition.
3
Grasp the alternative distinction
Ibn Taymiyya says the real distinction is simply: primary propositions are those that do not require evidence (dalil), theoretical ones require evidence. This is agreed upon.
4
Analyze the relativity claim
He then argues that 'primariness' and 'theoretiness' are relative (idaafi). A proposition may be self-evident for Zayd because of his complete conception (tamaam al-tasawwur) and theoretical for 'Amr because of deficient conception.
For example, knowing that 'fire burns' might be immediate for one who has experienced it, but requires demonstration for someone who has not.
5
Connect to the certainty of common premises
Ibn Taymiyya notes that this relativity does not undermine the certainty of common premises (mashhurat); he promises to address that later.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: Mashhurat as Certain Propositions

Core Idea
Mashhurat are as certain as empirical data because they are more universally held.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the standard logical classification that limits certain propositions to primary, observational, empirical, and mass-transmitted types. He argues that widely accepted propositions (mashhurat) like 'justice is good' are not only obligatory to accept but also certain, even though they are not self-evident. This lesson walks through his key arguments and compares mashhurat to other certain knowledge types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim about mashhurat
The logicians claim that mashhurat are not certain because they depend on custom or social benefit, and thus are probabilistic (zanni) rather than yaqini.
2

He asserts that mashhurat are among the propositions that must be accepted and can serve as material for demonstration (burhan). Their certainty does not require being primary (awwali).
3

Ibn Taymiyya notes that mujarrabat (empirical) and mutawatirat (mass-transmitted) are also not primary yet are certain. Mashhurat are even more widespread and firmly held across all nations.
4

He argues that the fact that mashhurat are based on custom or social order does not negate their certainty, because empirical propositions themselves are customary (i.e., based on repeated experience). Thus the objection is inconsistent.

Commentary

Learning Objective

When the logicians claim that only five types of propositions (primary, sensory, empirical, intuitive, and mutawatir) constitute the matter of demonstration, they assert a complete classification. But Ibn Taymiyya points out a fatal flaw: they provide no proof for this enumeration, and even their supporters admit it is impossible to prove. This opens the door to questioning logic's claim to be an infallible tool.

Immediate Inferences: Contradiction, Conversion, Contrapositive
Ibn Taymiyya presents the logicians' treatment of immediate inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of contradiction, simple conversion, and conversion of the contradictory (contrapositive) to Islamic propositions.

Core Idea
Three key operations on propositions—contradiction, conversion, contrapositive—preserve truth or falsity in specific ways.

Ibn Taymiyya presents the logicians' preparatory topics, including immediate inferences that operate on single propositions. He gives the example 'Every human is an animal' to illustrate contradiction, simple conversion, and conversion of the contradictory. These operations are foundational for syllogistic reasoning. In this lesson, we will practice these operations using propositions from Islamic sciences, such as those from fiqh or kalam, to see how they work and how they preserve truth.

Guided Path
1
Define contradiction
From the passage: contradiction is two propositions differing in negation and affirmation such that the truth of one necessitates the falsity of the other. For 'Every human is an animal', its contradictory is 'No human is an animal' (false).
Contradictory statements must be exact opposites in quantity and quality.
2
Apply simple conversion
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth. For 'Every human is an animal', the conversion is 'Some animal is a human' (true). Note that universal affirmative converts to particular affirmative.
Not all conversions preserve truth; e.g., universal negative converts to itself, particular affirmative converts to itself.
3
Apply conversion of the contradictory (contrapositive)
This operation makes the subject the predicate and changes affirmation to negation (or vice versa). For 'Every human is an animal', the contrapositive is 'What is not an animal is not a human' (true).
This is equivalent to the modern contrapositive: if A then B implies if not B then not A.
4
Test with an Islamic proposition
Take a fiqh proposition: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (كل مسكر حرام). Find its contradictory, conversion, and contrapositive. Check if the operations preserve truth according to Islamic law.
This step reinforces the operations with a relevant example.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of Philosophical Belief in the Hereafter
Ibn Taymiyya argues that philosophers who deny bodily resurrection do not truly believe in the Day of Judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that philosophical doctrines on the afterlife fail to meet the Quranic standard for belief in the Last Day, and identify his grounds for this critique.

Core Idea
True belief in the Last Day requires affirming the bodily resurrection described in the Quran, not just spiritual survival.

Ibn Taymiyya takes on the claim that philosophers who affirm God and an afterlife of the soul are believers in the Last Day. He systematically shows that their views, despite superficial alignment, contradict the Quranic description. This lesson unpacks his critique.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' denial of bodily resurrection. He begins by drawing an analogy: like hypocrites (munafiqun) who outwardly practice Islam but inwardly reject it, many philosophers outwardly follow revealed religions while inwardly denying the truth of prophethood. He then addresses a potential objection: if someone says that these philosophers believe in God and the Last Day because they affirm a necessary being and the resurrection of souls, Ibn Taymiyya responds that even the Christians, who affirm bodily resurrection, are better than them. Yet the Christians, by denying specific details of the afterlife (like eating and drinking), are considered disbelievers in God and the Last Day. The philosophers, who do not even affirm bodily resurrection, are worse. He then enumerates three philosophical opinions on the resurrection of souls, all attributed to Al-Farabi: (1) absolute resurrection of all souls, (2) only knowledgeable souls persist while ignorant souls perish, and (3) denial of any soul resurrection, often coupled with denial of bodily resurrection. Many also believe in transmigration (tanasukh). Ibn Taymiyya asserts that none of these constitutes belief in the Last Day as defined by the Quran. He then quotes numerous Quranic verses describing the Day of Judgment as a literal gathering of all people, including bodies, with physical details like sweating, eating, and drinking. He concludes with a prophetic hadith about standing in sweat. The point is that true faith requires affirming the bodily resurrection and the Quranic account, not just abstract spiritual survival.

Using Quranic Evidence to Refute Denial of the Afterlife
Quranic evidence against denying the afterlife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how Ibn Taymiyya employs Quranic verses to argue against the philosophers' denial of bodily resurrection.

Core Idea
The Quran's vivid descriptions of the Last Day are Ibn Taymiyya's primary weapon against philosophical rationalism.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya assembles a barrage of Quranic verses detailing the Day of Judgment—its suddenness, terror, and physical reality. He then delivers a pointed conclusion: those who reject the Last Day, like the philosophers who deny bodily resurrection, are not among the Sabians praised in the Quran. This is not mere proof-texting; it is a methodological counter to epistemological claims that rational deduction alone suffices to assess the Hereafter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's target claim
Read the concluding sentence: 'These [people] do not believe in the Last Day. So they are not included among those praised from the Sabians.' The target is anyone who denies the afterlife—specifically the philosophers who reject bodily resurrection.
This is the core of the critique: exclusion from Quranic praise.
2
Extract the Quranic evidence
List the surahs cited: al-Qari'ah, al-Haqqah, al-Saffat, al-Ma'arij, al-Qamar, al-Muzammil, al-Hajj. Notice each highlights physical phenomena: people like scattered moths, mountains crushed, graves opening, children turning gray.
The verses emphasize sensory and tangible events, countering philosophical abstractions.
3
Analyze the rhetorical pattern
Observe the repetition and intensity. Ibn Taymiyya piles verse after verse without interruption, building overwhelming scriptural testimony. This mirrors the Quran's own method of 'repetition to confirm' (takrir li al-taqrir).
The cumulative effect is meant to demonstrate that the afterlife is a central, non-negotiable doctrine.
4
Connect to the conclusion
The final sentence ties the evidence to the exclusion: those who disbelieve in the Last Day are not among the praised group. This is a direct refutation of the philosophers who claim to follow 'true religion' yet reject this fundamental tenet.
Ibn Taymiyya is using the Quran as an epistemological authority that overrides rational skepticism.

This passage is a citation of multiple Quranic verses that describe the Day of Judgment and resurrection, followed by a concluding remark. The author is thus using scriptural evidence to condemn the philosophers' denial of bodily resurrection.

Refuting the Multiplicity Argument Against Divine Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the multiplicity argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the internal inconsistencies in the philosophers' argument that affirming real attributes entails multiplicity in God.

Core Idea
The philosophers' own terminology proves the multiplicity they try to deny.

Ibn Taymiyya tackles the philosophers' claim that affirming multiple divine attributes would introduce composition (tarkib) in God. He shows that they themselves describe God with multiple aspects (intellect, intelligible, lover, beloved, etc.), yet they then try to collapse these into one. This lesson walks through his exposure of their contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states that the philosophers argue that affirming attributes entails 'multiplicity' (al-takaththur). Notice that they fear this would imply composition in God.
The passage says: 'And their greatest pillars in denying [knowledge] is that it necessitates multiplicity and change.'
2
List the multiple descriptions the philosophers actually use
Ibn Taymiyya points out that Ibn Sina and others say God is 'intellect, intelligent, and intelligible; lover, beloved, and love; pleasure, one who experiences pleasure, and pleasure'—clearly multiple descriptions.
These are six distinct relational terms, showing that multiplicity is already present.
3
Examine their attempt to reduce multiplicity
The philosophers then obstinately claim that knowledge is love, love is power, etc., and that all attributes are identical with the essence. Ibn Taymiyya calls this 'mukabara' (obstinacy).
They cannot consistently hold both that attributes are many (in relation) and that they are one (in essence) without contradiction.
4
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion
He concludes that the philosophers have no real argument against attributes; their denial is based on a false inference from composition. Their own system already implies the multiplicity they reject.
This shows the weakness of their position: they flee from multiplicity but embrace it in their own terminology.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' arguments for denying divine attributes, particularly the arguments of multiplicity (takaththur) and change (taghayyur). The author begins by situating the discussion within a broader criticism of the philosophers' corrupt principles that lead them to deny God's knowledge and other attributes. He notes that even Abu al-Barakat, a philosopher, partially critiqued Aristotle, but the matter is more serious. Ibn Taymiyya then explains the philosophers' argument from multiplicity: affirming attributes such as knowledge, love, etc., would entail a multiplicity in the divine essence, which they avoid by claiming that these are identical to the essence. He exposes their inconsistency: they first affirm multiple relations (intellect, intelligent, intelligible) and then claim these are one, or equate distinct attributes (knowledge = love = power). Al-Tusi's attempt to reduce knowledge of things to the things themselves is also dismissed. The second argument is from change: knowledge of changing events would require that God's knowledge changes, making Him a locus of accidents. Ibn Taymiyya notes that the philosophers have no proof for this; their denial is merely a consequence of their prior denial of attributes. He contrasts them with the Kullabiyya, who denied attributes on a different basis (that the eternal cannot be indwelt by accidents) and whose arguments have been refuted by others. Finally, he presents two standard responses to the change argument: the first (by Mu'tazila and Ash'aris) denies the premise that knowledge of future and past are distinct, claiming only the relation changes; the second (implied) denies the other premise. The passage illustrates Ibn Taymiyya's method of exposing internal contradictions and weak foundations in philosophical theology.

The Change Argument: No Proof for Denying Divine Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the change argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the philosophical argument that divine knowledge of changing things requires change in God.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya shows the philosophers have no evidence for their denial of change in God's knowledge.

The philosophers argue that God's knowledge of temporal events would change as events occur, implying that God is a locus of accidents. Ibn Taymiyya retorts that this argument is baseless—the philosophers simply deny attributes and then apply the consequence. This lesson dissects his critique and contrasts it with the Kullabiyya's different stance.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' argument from change
The philosophers claimed that knowledge of changing things requires that God's knowledge changes (e.g., from 'will be' to 'was'), which would make Him a locus of accidents.
This is their main objection to affirming divine knowledge of temporal events.
2
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's key objection

Ibn Taymiyya: Motion of Spheres Is Not a Complete Cause
Ibn Taymiyya argues against complete causal knowledge via spheres

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A cause known is not enough; only a complete cause necessitates its effect.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the claim that celestial souls know all events by knowing sphere motions. He argues that even if those motions are causes, they are not complete causes—effects also depend on varying recipients. Thus, knowledge of motions alone cannot yield knowledge of events. This lesson dissects his reasoning and its implications for causality in Islamic thought.

The complete cause includes all conditions; here recipients (qawābil) are also necessary.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that effects differ due to differences in recipients. To know the effect, one must know both the agent and the recipient. Celestial souls know only their motions, not the earthly recipients.

Commentary

The invalidity of their making prophethood a part of philosophy. The theologians, when they enumerate the paths of knowledge, enumerate the paths of their likes and peers, mentioning the sensory and the necessary and theoretical rational matters, whether that enumeration is correct or not. They do not intend to mention the path by which the prophet knows what is revealed to him, rather this path is outside what they and their peers are capable of from the paths, and it is not of their kind in their view, contrary to the philosophers. For the prophet in their view is of the same kind as other intelligent ascetics, but he may be better, and prophethood in their view is a part of philosophy. This is the great misguidance. For all of philosophy – its possessor is not at the rank of the Jews and Christians after abrogation and alteration, let alone their rank before that, nor at the rank of the believers – the people of the Quran like the Companions and the Followers, let alone the rank of any of the prophets, let alone the messengers, let alone the resolute among them, rather the generality of intelligent people and the most ascetic of people are not comparable to the Followers with excellence, to the first predecessors. As for the rank of the first predecessors like Abu Bakr and Umar, no one reaches it. It has been established in the two Sahihs from the Prophet ﷺ that he said: 'There were muhaddathun (inspired ones) among the nations before you; if there is any in my nation, it is Umar.' And in another hadith: 'Indeed Allah placed truth on the tongue of Umar and his heart.' And Ali said: 'We used to say that tranquility speaks on the tongue of Umar.' And in at-Tirmidhi and others: 'If I were not sent among you, Umar would have been sent among you, and if there were to be a prophet after me, it would be Umar.' So as-Siddiq (Abu Bakr) is more complete than him. For as-Siddiq perfected his faith in the Prophet, so he only receives from the Prophet, and the Prophet is infallible. The muhaddath – like Umar – sometimes takes from his heart what is inspired to him and he is spoken to, but his heart is not infallible. So he must present what is cast into him to what the Messenger brought; if it agrees, he accepts it, and if it contradicts, he rejects it. For this reason, Umar retracted from some things, and the Companions would debate with him and argue against him; when the proof from the Book and the Sunnah was made clear to him, he would return to it and leave his own opinion. As-Siddiq only receives from the Messenger, not from his heart. So he is more complete than the muhaddath. There is no as-Siddiq after Abu Bakr better than him, and no muhaddath after Umar better than him.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the philosophers' claim that prophethood is a part of philosophy. Ibn Taymiyya begins by contrasting the theologians' admission that they do not know the path of prophetic revelation with the philosophers' claim that the prophet is merely an intelligent ascetic and prophethood is part of philosophy. He declares this a great misguidance, arguing that even the lowest believer is superior to the philosopher, who is at best equivalent to the Jews and Christians after abrogation. He then discusses the degrees: the first predecessors (Abu Bakr and Umar) are unattainable by later generations. He cites hadiths establishing Umar as a muhaddath (inspired person) and then explains the difference between as-Siddiq (Abu Bakr) and the muhaddath. Abu Bakr only receives from the Prophet who is infallible, while Umar receives from his heart which is not infallible and must be checked against revelation. This shows that Abu Bakr is superior because he relies solely on the Prophet, not on inner inspiration. The passage establishes a hierarchy: prophecy, then siddiqiyya (truthfulness), then muhaddathiyya (inspiration), with the first being the highest.

The Three Errors for Which al-Ghazali Declared Takfir
Author lists the three errors of philosophers that al-Ghazali declared takfir upon

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three specific philosophical doctrines for which al-Ghazali declared takfir in Tahafut al-Falasifa.

Core Idea
Three doctrines define the boundaries of Islam in philosophy: qidam al-‘ālam, inkār ‘ilm Allāh bi-l-juz’iyyāt, and inkār al-ma‘ād.

In his famous work Tahafut al-Falasifa, al-Ghazali singled out three doctrines held by the philosophers that he considered to be clear disbelief (kufr). Understanding these three is crucial for grasping the historical debate between philosophy and Islamic theology. This lesson will help you internalize these three specific points and their significance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first error: qidam al-‘ālam
Find the term 'قدم العالم' in the Arabic. This is the doctrine that the universe is eternal and uncreated. Al-Ghazali considered this contradictory to the Islamic belief that God created the world ex nihilo.
This is often the most well-known of the three.
2
Identify the second error: inkār ‘ilm Allāh bi-l-juz’iyyāt
Locate the phrase 'إنكار علم الله بالجزئيات'. This is the denial that God knows particular events or entities. Al-Ghazali argued that this undermines God's omniscience and perfection.
It relates to God's attribute of knowledge and its scope.
3
Identify the third error: inkār al-ma‘ād
Find 'إنكار المعاد'. This is the denial of bodily resurrection and the afterlife. Al-Ghazali held that this contradicts the clear texts of the Quran and consensus of Muslims.
This touches on eschatology.
4
Analyze why al-Ghazali singled out these three
Consider that these three points were seen as directly opposing core Islamic beliefs. Al-Ghazali did not declare all philosophical ideas as kufr, only these three. Think about their centrality to Islamic doctrine.
This shows a nuanced approach to takfir.

Messengers as Mere Intermediaries: Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Independent Prophetic Agency
Ibn Taymiyya argues that messengers are merely vehicles for delivering divine commands, not independent actors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the notion that prophets have independent causal power, and identify the Quranic evidence he uses to restrict their role to conveyance.

Core Idea
No one acts independently but God; prophets are only conduits for His command.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Radd al-Mantiqiyyīn is a critical response to any view that elevates prophets beyond their proper station. Here, Ibn Taymiyya argues that the monotheistic principle demands that no created being, including messengers, can bring about effects independently. He then redefines their role as mere intermediaries (wasā'iṭ) in conveying divine messages, backing this with multiple Quranic verses. Understanding this critique is essential for grasping the Islamic position on tawhid and the proper role of prophetic intercession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being critiqued
The passage explicitly rejects the idea that anyone other than God can act independently (يستقل بفعل شيء). This is the target: any assertion of independent agency for prophets or other beings.
2
Extract the positive monotheistic principle
Ibn Taymiyya states that everything is only a cause (سبب) that does not produce effects by itself. Effects require the cause, other causes, removal of obstacles, and ultimately God's creation. This is the foundational tawhid principle.
3
Locate the explicit role of messengers
He declares that messengers are 'intermediaries between God and His creation in conveying His messages.' Their function is tablīgh (conveyance), not independent action.
4
Analyse the Quranic evidence
The passage cites three verses (6:48, 33:45-46, 22:78, etc.) that describe prophets as bearers of good news, warners, witnesses, and callers to God by His permission. These restrict their agency to testimony and notification.
5
Summarise how this critique defends tawhid
By limiting prophetic role to conveyance, Ibn Taymiyya safeguards the doctrine that only God has independent will and power. Any suggestion otherwise is a breach of tawhid.

Commentary
This passage is a critical theological exposition by Ibn Taymiyya, aimed at refuting any notion that messengers possess independent power or efficacy. He begins by establishing the monotheistic (حنفاء) principle that no entity other than God acts independently; everything is merely a cause (سبب) that does not produce effects by itself but requires God's creation along with other causes and removal of obstacles. The effect (الأثر) only occurs through God's will. He then explicitly states that messengers are only intermediaries (وسائط) between God and creation in conveying (تبليغ) God's messages, commands, prohibitions, promises, and warnings. To support this, he cites Quranic verses that describe the messengers simply as bearers of good news and warners, witnesses (شهداء), and callers to God by His permission. The commentary clarifies that even the Prophet Muhammad's role is restricted to conveying, not creating or independently judging; he is a witness (شهيد) over the community, but this witnessing is a testimony, not an independent authority. The passage guards against the misunderstanding that prophets or their intercession are somehow autonomous actors alongside God.
