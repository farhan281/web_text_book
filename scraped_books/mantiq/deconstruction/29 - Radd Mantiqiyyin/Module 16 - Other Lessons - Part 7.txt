The Quranic Refutation of Shirk in Intercession
Author refutes the mushrikeen's intercession claim with Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quranic basis for rejecting polytheistic intercession and distinguish between legitimate and illegitimate intercession.

Core Idea
Intercession without permission is shirk; the Quran negates it absolutely.

This lesson examines Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the polytheists' claim that their idols intercede for them. He marshals multiple Quranic verses to show that intercession is only valid with Allah's permission. We will analyze his method of citing and explaining these verses to understand the core theological principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the polytheist claim
Find the specific claim made by the Arab polytheists regarding their idols: that they act as intercessors before Allah. The text states they said, 'These are our intercessors with Allah' (Quran 10:18).
2
Locate the Quranic negation
Ibn Taymiyya cites several Quranic verses that negate intercession unless by Allah's permission. Identify the recurring phrase: 'except by His permission' or 'only for whom He permits and approves' in verses like 2:255, 34:23, and 53:26.
3
Extract the core principle
From the verses, extract the condition for valid intercession: it must be by Allah's permission and for those with whom He is pleased. This is the key distinction between legitimate and shirk-based intercession.
This principle is absolute; no being can intercede independently.
4
Analyze the false analogy
The polytheists analogize Allah's court to a worldly king's court, where courtiers intercede. Why does Ibn Tayyimiyya reject this analogy? Consider that Allah has no needs, no courtiers, and no one can approach Him without His permission.
5
Apply the principle
Using the principle established, explain why seeking intercession from saints, angels, or prophets without explicit Quranic permission is shirk. How does this refute the claims of those who worship saints as intercessors?
This is directly relevant to tawḥīd and refuting grave-worship.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the mushrikeen's (polytheists) belief in intercession (shafa'a) and a clarification of the Quranic teaching on the subject. The author (Ibn Taymiyya) begins by stating his purpose: to point out that the disbelief of the logicians (the previously critiqued philosophers) is greater than that of the earlier polytheists. He then explains the polytheist practice: they took their idols as intercessors, believing that these idols would intercede for them with Allah, and that Allah would accept their intercession. The author cites Quran 10:18 to show that Allah rebukes them for worshipping what cannot harm or benefit. Then he presents several Quranic verses that negate any intercession unless by Allah's permission (e.g., 2:255, 6:51, 6:70, 32:4, 21:26-29, 34:22-23, 53:26). These verses establish that the only acceptable intercession is that which Allah permits. After citing these proofs, the author explains that the negated intercession includes that of the Arab polytheists and is similar to the shirk of Christians and extreme followers of saints (ghulat) who believe that angels, prophets, or saints intercede with Allah like courtiers intercede with worldly kings. They analogize Allah's court to a king's court, which is a false analogy. The author then cites Quran 39:3, where the polytheists say they worship only to be brought nearer to Allah, and shows that this occurs after the command to worship Allah with sincerity. The passage ends with the verse that Allah will judge between them. Throughout, the author uses Quranic evidence to argue that shirk in intercession is a major form of disbelief, and he draws parallels between different forms of shirk. The lesson demonstrates how Islamic theology uses logical refutation and scriptural evidence to defend tawhid.

Refuting Independent Agents: Tawhid al-Rububiyya in Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd
Ibn Taymiyya refutes various sects that affirm independent agents besides Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three groups that affirm independent agents besides Allah according to Ibn Taymiyya and explain why each is considered a form of shirk.

Core Idea
Recognizing independent agents besides Allah is the root of all shirk.

Ibn Taymiyya in this passage systematically identifies every group—dualists (Majus), those denying predestination (Qadariyya), and philosophers affirming an eternal world—as fundamentally violating tawhid al-rububiyya. The Salafic equation of Qadariyya with Majus is a powerful tool for diagnosing shirk in later theological movements. By studying this critique, you will sharpen your ability to detect subtle forms of associationism that persist in theological and philosophical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three groups
Find the three distinct categories Ibn Taymiyya lists: Magians (al-majus), Qadariyya (al-qadariyya), and proponents of the eternity of the world (al-qa'ilun bi-qidam al-'alam).
Each group posits an independent agent (fa'il mustaqill) besides Allah, but in different forms.
2
Understand the common error
Each group affirms an independent agent that acts without reliance on Allah. The Magians posit an evil principle; the Qadariyya claim humans independently originate actions; the eternalists attribute motion to celestial spheres, intellects, and souls.
Despite differences, all share the core heresy of attributing creative power to something other than Allah.
3
Analyze the Salafi identification
Explain why the Salaf equated Qadariyya with Magians: both attribute independent creative power to something other than Allah. The Qadariyya claim humans create their own actions, paralleling the Magian belief in an independent evil principle.
This equation is a polemical tool that highlights the seriousness of the Qadariyya error.
4
Apply the criterion of tawhid
Use Allah's sole creation as the measure. Ibn Taymiyya cites Quranic negations of ownership, partnership, and supporter for anyone besides Allah. Evaluate any theological claim against this standard: does it imply an independent agent?
The Sunni position affirms that Allah alone is Creator, while also affirming human capacity and causation within Allah's overarching power.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya identifying various forms of shirk that involve affirming an independent agent (fa'il mustaqill) besides Allah. He begins by stating that all polytheists fall into one of these types. The first type are those who affirm an independent agent other than Allah but do not consider it similar to Him in essence, attributes, or actions — such as the Magians who posit an evil principle that independently does evil. The second type are the Qadariyya (those who deny predestination) who claim that beings other than Allah can independently originate actions, even if Allah created them. The Salaf equated them with Magians. The third type are the proponents of the eternity of the world, notably Aristotle and his followers, who attribute movement and generation to the celestial spheres, and further to intellects and souls as independent agents. Ibn Taymiyya criticizes these philosophers for not truly affirming that Allah creates anything. He then affirms the Quranic negation of ownership, partnership, and support for anyone besides Allah, and declares that Allah alone is the Creator, Lord, and Owner of everything. He contrasts this with Ahl al-Sunnah who affirm divine predestination and also affirm human capacity and causation, while rejecting the positions of Jahm and his followers who negate all agency from creatures. The passage thus defends Sunni Tawhid and refutes various heretical views.

Revelation-Based Exegesis of Quran 34:23
Ibn Taymiyya interprets Quran 34:23, citing hadiths on angelic response to divine command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Ibn Taymiyya uses authentic hadiths and Arabic linguistic principles to interpret a Quranic verse, and contrast this method with speculative reasoning.

Core Idea
True knowledge comes from revelation, not speculative logic.

Ibn Taymiyya, in his critique of logicians, often turns to Quranic exegesis to demonstrate the sufficiency of revelation and sound linguistic reasoning. In this passage, he interprets Quran 34:23, which speaks of intercession and the angels' state when Allah speaks. He supports his interpretation with multiple authentic hadiths and a linguistic analysis of the phrase 'فزع عن قلوبهم'. This lesson guides you through his method, showing how he anchors every claim in scriptural and linguistic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verse and the key claim
Read the verse Quran 34:23 (provided in the Arabic). Notice that Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes between beneficial intercession (which Allah permits) and the polytheists' notion. This is his starting point: he clarifies the correct understanding of intercession.
2
Examine the first hadith (Abu Hurayrah)
Read the hadith about angels beating their wings and the eavesdropping jinn. Observe how the hadith explains the phrase 'فزع عن قلوبهم': the angels are in terror until it is removed, then they speak. This hadith provides a concrete narrative that interprets the verse.
3
Examine the second and third hadiths
Read the hadith from Ibn Abbas and the hadith from al-Nawwas. Note how they add details: the chain of glorification, the swooning of angels, and Jibril's role. These hadiths reinforce the same understanding: revelation involves a process of submission and fear, then removal of fear.
4
Analyse the linguistic point
Ibn Taymiyya explains that 'فزع عن قلوبهم' means 'He removed the terror from their hearts.' He gives analogies from Arabic: 'de-ticking a camel', 'de-sinning'. This is a linguistic principle: the verb form 'فعل' with 'عن' can indicate removal. He supports this with Salaf interpretations.
5
Synthesise the epistemological method
Now reflect: Ibn Taymiyya does not use Greek syllogisms or speculative theology. He anchors every point in Quran, authentic hadith, and Arabic language. His method shows that true knowledge comes from revelation and sound linguistic reasoning, not from abstract logical systems.

Whatever they bring as it is, it is the truth, but they add lies and increase.' Similarly, in the other well-known hadith from the narration of Nu'aym ibn Hammad, from al-Walid ibn Muslim, from 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Yazid from 'Abdullah ibn Abi Zakariyya, from Raja' ibn Haywah, from al-Nawwas ibn Sam'an who said: The Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, said: 'When Allah wants to reveal His command, He speaks with revelation. When He speaks, the heavens are seized with a violent tremor - or he said: a thunderclap - from fear of Allah. The inhabitants of the heavens swoon and fall prostrate to Allah. The first to raise his head is Jibril. Allah speaks to him from His revelation as He wills. Then Jibril passes on to the angels; whenever he passes a heaven, its angels ask him: What did our Lord say, O Jibril? He says: He said the truth, and He is the Most High, the Great. Then they all say the same as Jibril. Then Jibril ends with the revelation to wherever Allah commanded him from the heaven and earth.' And His statement: 'when terror is removed from their hearts' means: He removed the terror from them. And likewise more than one of the Salaf said: It was unveiled from their hearts. This is like saying: 'he de-ticked the camel' meaning he removed the tick from it, and it is said: 'he de-sinned' (taharraja), 'de-guilted' (tahawwaba), 'de-sinned' (ta'aththama), 'de-broke oath' (tahannatha) meaning he removed from himself sin, guilt, sin, and oath-breaking.

Commentary
This passage is an exegetical commentary by Ibn Taymiyya on Quran 34:23. He begins by explaining that Allah has established a beneficial intercession, unlike the polytheists' notion. He then quotes the verse, which describes the angels' state: they are in fear until terror is removed, then they confirm the truth. Ibn Taymiyya supports this interpretation with two authentic hadiths about how revelation occurs. The first hadith from Abu Hurayrah describes angels beating their wings in submission when Allah decrees, and then when terror is removed, they ask and answer. Eavesdropping jinn try to intercept. The second hadith from Ibn Abbas explains that shooting stars are not for omens but because of the glorification chain when Allah decrees. A third hadith from al-Nawwas describes the process of revelation with trembling and prostration. Finally, Ibn Taymiyya gives a linguistic explanation of "فزع عن قلوبهم" meaning removal of terror, using analogies from Arabic like "قرّد البعير" (de-tick the camel) and verbs like "تحرّج" (remove sin). This exegetical approach demonstrates that true knowledge comes from revelation and sound linguistic reasoning, not from speculative logic—a key theme in his critique of logicians.

Philosophers' Shirk: Worse than Arab Polytheism
Ibn Taymiyya argues that philosophers' shirk is greater than Arab polytheists'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the philosophers' concept of intercession constitutes greater shirk than that of the Arab polytheists.

Core Idea
Denying God's will and knowledge makes philosophical shirk more severe than polytheism's belief in intercessors.

Ibn Taymiyya does not merely critique polytheism; he ranks its forms. In this passage, he argues that the philosophers' position is worse because it strips God of His attributes of will and knowledge, turning intercession into a mechanical emanation. Understanding this ranking sharpens our ability to assess contemporary deviations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups being compared
Ibn Taymiyya compares philosophers who believe in the pre-eternity of the world (and emanation) with the Arab polytheists who acknowledged God as Creator by will and power.
The Arab polytheists at least affirmed God's will and power over creation.
2
Extract the philosophers' model of intercession
The philosophers claim that intercession occurs automatically through emanation (fayḍ), like sunlight reflecting off a mirror, without any conscious act or request by the intercessor.
This eliminates the role of divine will and knowledge.
3
Contrast with the Arab polytheists' view
The Arab polytheists believed intercessors ask God and God responds by His will. They affirmed God's knowledge and power over particulars.
Although polytheistic, their view preserved some divine attributes.
4
Analyze the implications for grave visitation
Ibn Taymiyya applies this to practice: legitimate visitation prays for the deceased; innovated visitation seeks needs from the dead. The philosophers' model justifies the latter without even invoking God's will.
This shows how a theological error leads to ritual innovation.
5
Evaluate Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion
He concludes that the philosophers' shirk is greater because it denies God's attributes more radically, making their intercession model devoid of divine involvement.
The severity of shirk is linked to the degree of negation of God's attributes.

The polytheists among the philosophers who assert the pre-eternity of the world are greater in polytheism, and what they claim that the intercessor does not ask God for intercession for their deities is greater disbelief than the polytheists of the Arabs. For they do not say that the intercessor asks God and God responds to his prayer, as the polytheists who say that God is the Creator by His power and will say. Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya comparing the shirk of the Arab polytheists with that of the philosophers (falasifa) who believe in the pre-eternity of the world. He begins by stating his thesis: the philosophers are worse in shirk and their concept of intercession is greater kufr. The Arab polytheists at least believed that God creates by His power and will, and that intercessors ask God and God responds. The philosophers, however, deny that God knows particulars or that He acts by will and power; rather, the world emanates from Him necessarily. They describe intercession as a mere emanation from the venerated being to the supplicant, like sunlight reflecting off a polished surface, without any conscious action or request. Ibn Taymiyya then contrasts the legitimate visitation of graves (sunnah) with the innovated polytheistic visitation (which seeks needs from the dead). Finally, he emphasizes that the philosophers' view is even worse because it eliminates God's knowledge and will entirely, making their shirk more severe.

Why Human Messengers Are Necessary
Author explains the wisdom of human messengers via Quran and tafsir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument for human messengers from this passage and identify the epistemological basis for their necessity.

Core Idea
Human messengers are necessary because humans cannot bear direct angelic contact without confusion.

Ibn Taymiyya addresses a common objection: why didn't God send an angel as a messenger? He argues that sending an angel would not resolve the epistemological gap—it would create ambiguity. The passage uses Quranic verses and historical examples to show that humans can only perceive angels in human form, leading to inevitable doubt. This lesson guides you through his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Ibn Taymiyya's central claim is that messengers to humans must be human. Locate the sentence where he states this directly, such as 'the mediation of humans in the message is like the mediation of the angel' and the digression on wisdom.
The claim is not that angels cannot be messengers, but that humans cannot receive from them directly.
2
Extract the key premise
Find the premise that humans cannot bear to receive from an angel directly. Look for the phrase 'لا يطيقون الأخذ عن الملك' and the supporting verse from Surah al-An'am.
This premise is supported by the explanation that if an angel came, it would have to appear as a man, causing confusion.
3
Analyze the supporting evidence
Ibn Taymiyya provides Quranic verses, a tafsir from Ibn Abbas, and examples from prophetic history (Gabriel as Dihyah, angels to Abraham and Lot, Gabriel to Mary). Identify each piece of evidence and how it supports the premise.
The examples show that angels always appear in human form when interacting with humans, and this leads to ambiguity about their true nature.
4
Understand the conclusion

Commentary
This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Radd Mantiqiyyin presents a theological argument for the wisdom of sending human messengers rather than angels. The author begins by noting that the mediation of humans in conveying the divine message is similar to the mediation of angels, citing Quranic verses that show God chooses messengers from both angels and humans (Surah al-Hajj: 75). He then focuses on the designation 'your companion' (صاحبكم) in the Quranic description of the Prophet Muhammad, interpreting it as a sign of God's favor: He sent a human who accompanies and is accompanied by people, being one of them. The core reason is that humans cannot bear direct reception from an angel; they are incapable of seeing angels in their true form. This is supported by quoting Surah al-An'am: 8-9, which states that if an angel were sent, it would have to appear in human form, leading to confusion. Ibn Taymiyya then provides a tafsir from Ibn Abbas explaining that sending an angel would result in immediate destruction or ambiguity. He reinforces this with examples from prophetic history: Gabriel appearing to the Prophet in human forms (as Dihyah or a Bedouin), angels appearing to Abraham and Lot as men, and Gabriel appearing to Mary as a man. The argument concludes that if humans cannot see angels except in human form, then sending an angel would not remove the doubt that the messenger is merely a human. Finally, he links the designation 'your companion' to the fact that the Prophet was an Arab sent with the language of his people, quoting Surah Ibrahim: 4 and Surah al-Tawbah: 128. The passage thus establishes the epistemological necessity of human messengers for human comprehension.

Ibn Taymiyya’s Refutation of the Angelic Messenger Claim
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that an angelic messenger is superior.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya’s argument against the superiority of an angelic messenger and evaluate its logical strength.

Core Idea
Sending an angel would not remove doubt; it would only add confusion.

Ibn Taymiyya, in his refutation of the logicians, addresses a theological objection: why did Allah send a human messenger rather than an angel? He argues that an angelic messenger would not resolve the people's doubt because angels can only appear in human form, causing confusion. This lesson unpacks his logical critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the claim that Ibn Taymiyya is attacking: that an angel would have been a better or more effective messenger.
The objection is implicit: some might argue that an angelic messenger would be more convincing.
2
Extract the main argument
Ibn Taymiyya argues that sending an angel would not remove the people's excuse (shubha) because angels cannot be seen directly by humans and must appear in human form, leading to confusion about their identity.
The key concept is 'iltibas' (confusion).
3
Examine the evidence
Ibn Taymiyya cites Qur'anic verses (e.g., 6:8-9, 17:95) and examples (Jibril appearing as Dihyah, angels coming to Ibrahim and Lut, Jibril to Maryam) to support his claim.
These examples show that angels always appear in human form when interacting with humans.
4
Assess the logical structure
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: if an angel were sent, it would have to appear as a human, so people would still say 'this is a human', and the doubt would remain. Thus the objection fails.
The critique is internally valid: it uses scriptural premises and logical reasoning.
5
Evaluate the alternative
Ibn Taymiyya's alternative is that a human messenger, being from among the people, is more appropriate and removes doubt because he is directly seen as a human, not an ambiguous figure.
This reinforces the wisdom (hikma) of Allah's choice.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya defending the wisdom of Allah sending human messengers, specifically the Prophet Muhammad, and refuting the claim that an angel would have been a better messenger. The passage is part of a larger work refuting the logicians and their objections. Ibn Taymiyya begins by citing the interpretation of Qur'anic verses, particularly the statement that if an angel were sent, the matter would be decided (i.e., immediate destruction) and the people would not be given respite. He explains, via Ibn 'Abbas and others, that an angel could only appear to humans in human form because humans cannot look at angels directly. This would cause confusion: people would not know whether the being is an angel or a human, thus the doubt would remain. He gives examples from the life of Jibril appearing in the form of Dihyah al-Kalbi or a Bedouin, and the angels who came to Ibrahim and Lut appearing as men, and Jibril's appearance to Maryam as a well-proportioned man. The conclusion: sending an angel would not remove the excuse (shubha) because the people would still say 'this is a human'. Ibn Taymiyya then references verses from Surah al-Isra' that mention that if there were angels on earth walking assuredly, then Allah would send an angel as messenger, but since humans inhabit the earth, a human messenger is appropriate. He then discusses the Arabic nature of the messenger: the Prophet was sent with the language of his people (Arabic). He cites verses from Surah Ibrahim and Surah al-Tawbah. There is a discussion about whether 'min anfusikum' (from among yourselves) refers specifically to Arabs or to all humans. Ibn Taymiyya says there is no doubt that the Prophet is from mankind, and from the best of mankind: Arabs, Quraysh, Bani Hashim. He then refutes the notion that the Prophet's statement 'I am but a human messenger' implies that an angel is better. He clarifies that this was not the intention. Finally, he adds a digression about the messenger being a conveyer of the Qur'an, not its producer. He contrasts two verses: one where 'messenger' refers to Jibril (angel) and one where it refers to Muhammad (human). Both convey the message from Allah. The word 'messenger' implies a sender, so it is clear that the messenger is merely a conveyer. This reinforces that the human messenger is sufficient and that no angel is needed.

Prophet as Conveyor, Not Author of the Quran
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Quran is divine speech and the Prophet is merely a conveyor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Prophet's role as conveyor (muballigh) from the false claim that he originated (ahdatha) the Quran, and refute the latter using textual evidence.

Core Idea
The Quran is Allah's speech; the Prophet only conveyed it—confusing conveyance with authorship is disbelief.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya dismantles a dangerous misconception: that attributing the Quran to the Prophet implies he authored it. By analyzing Quranic verses and Prophetic hadith, he shows that the Prophet is a messenger (rasul) who delivers Allah's speech, not its originator. This distinction is crucial for preserving the doctrine that the Quran is uncreated divine speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the false claim being refuted
Find the claim that some people imagine: that the Prophet originated (ahdatha) the Arabic Quran. Why does Ibn Taymiyya consider this a serious error?
The false claim leads to comparing the Prophet to the disbeliever who said the Quran is human speech.
2
Extract the distinction between conveyance and origination
Notice that the Quran is attributed to Allah as speech (kalam Allah) and to the Prophet as conveyance (tabligh). Ibn Taymiyya argues that if both attributions were of the same kind (origination), they would contradict.
Attribution by tabligh does not make the Prophet the source.
3
Analyze the Quranic evidence cited
Examine the verse from Surah al-Muddaththir (74:11-26) where the disbeliever says 'this is nothing but the speech of a human.' Ibn Taymiyya says this speech is the Quran, attributed to both human and angelic messengers by way of conveyance, not origination.
The one who denies its divine origin is threatened with Hell.
4
Recognize the voice/speech distinction
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes between the voice of the reciter (sawt al-qari') and the speech of Allah (kalam Allah), citing the hadith 'The voice is the voice of the reciter, and the speech is the speech of the Creator.' This prevents conflating human and divine attributes.
This distinction was critical in the controversy over the createdness of the Quran.
5
Summarize the role of messengers as intermediaries
Conclude that all prophets are intermediaries (wasa'it) who convey Allah's messages without addition or subtraction, as shown in verses from Surah al-Ma'idah, al-Jinn, and al-A'raf.
The final hadiths emphasize the duty of conveying even one verse.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the claim that the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) originated the Quran. The author begins by defining the fourteenth aspect of the fourth station: that the Messenger is a conveyor (muballigh) of the Quran from Allah, not an originator (muhdith). He argues that if the Quran were attributed to both Allah and the Prophet as originator, the two attributions would contradict each other. The Quran is attributed to Allah as speech and to the Messenger as conveyance. Ibn Taymiyya then cites the verse from Surat al-Muddaththir, where the disbeliever said, 'This is nothing but the speech of a human,' and Allah threatened him with Hell. The author explains that the speech referred to is the Quran, which is attributed both to a human messenger (the Prophet) and an angelic messenger (Jibril), but the intended meaning is that they conveyed it, not that they originated it. The one who claimed that the Prophet originated the Quran is akin to the disbeliever who said it is human speech. Ibn Taymiyya then quotes Surat at-Tawbah (9:6), where Allah calls the Quran 'the speech of Allah' and mentions that the polytheist hears it with the voice of the reciter. He distinguishes between the voice (sawt) of the reciter and the speech (kalam) of Allah, citing hadith where the Prophet said, 'The voice is the voice of the reciter, and the speech is the speech of Allah.' The author further supports this distinction with Quranic verses about lowering voices before the Prophet and Luqman's advice to his son, showing that the voice is attributed to the human. He then returns to the main theme, emphasizing that messengers are intermediaries (wasa'it) in conveying Allah's messages, citing verses from Surat al-Ma'idah, al-Jinn, and al-A'raf. Finally, he quotes hadith commanding believers to convey from the Prophet, even if it is one verse, and the promise of reward for those who hear and convey knowledge. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya upholds the Islamic doctrine that the Quran is the uncreated speech of Allah, not originated by any creature, and that the Prophet's role is that of a faithful conveyor.

The Heard Sound Is the Servant's Sound
Ibn Taymiyya defines heard sound as the servant's sound, citing Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how Ibn Taymiyya uses Quranic evidence to argue that the heard sound is the servant's sound, and distinguish his position from the claim that the recitation is uncreated speech.

Core Idea
The heard sound is the servant's, not Allah's — a cut of Quranic evidence.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya makes a crucial distinction about the nature of Quranic recitation. He argues that the audible sound we hear when someone recites the Quran is a created attribute of the human reciter, not the uncreated speech of Allah. This distinction is central to his refutation of certain theological positions that conflate the physical recitation with the divine attribute of speech. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate Ibn Taymiyya's argument and see how he uses Quranic verses to support it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Ibn Taymiyya opens by stating clearly: 'The heard sound is the sound of the servant.' Note that this is a direct assertion, not a proof yet.
The claim is the heart of the lesson: what is heard is created, not eternal.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
He then cites three verses: Surah al-Hujurat 2-3 and Surah Luqman 19. These verses attribute sounds (voices) to humans: raising voices, lowering voices, and the voice of donkeys. The attribution is to created beings.
Ibn Taymiyya uses these verses as evidence that sound is a property of the servant.
3
Analyze the implication of each verse
For each verse, consider: if the heard sound were Allah's speech, would these commands and descriptions make sense? For example, 'do not raise your voices above the voice of the Prophet' would imply raising voices above Allah's speech, which is impossible. Therefore, the sound must be the servant's.
This is an argument from impossibility (burhan al-imtina').
4
Understand the contrast
Ibn Taymiyya implicitly contrasts the heard sound with the divine attribute of speech (kalam nafsi). The latter is uncreated, not audible. The former is created and belongs to the reciter.
This distinction is crucial in the kalam debate on the nature of the Quran.
5
Conclude the argument
Thus, the heard sound is conclusively the servant's. Any claim that it is Allah's speech would lead to absurd consequences, which Ibn Taymiyya avoids by this definition.
The argument is a critical refinement of earlier positions.
