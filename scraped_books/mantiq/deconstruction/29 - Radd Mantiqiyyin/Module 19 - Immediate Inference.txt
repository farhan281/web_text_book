Certain Knowledge Does Not Require Universal Premises
Ibn Taymiyya gives examples from sensory and rational premises to show that particular knowledge is attained without universal premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's examples of immediate sensory and rational knowledge and explain why they do not require universal premises.

Core Idea
Particular knowledge can be certain without being deduced from universal premises.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a central claim of the logicians: that all certain knowledge must be derived from universal premises via syllogism. Here, he provides counterexamples from sensory perception and pure reason, arguing that we often know particular facts directly, without inference from a universal rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis under attack
The logicians claim that for any certain conclusion, one must have a universal premise in a demonstration (burhan). Ibn Taymiyya targets this necessity.
2
Extract the two categories of examples
He gives sensory examples (e.g., knowing the moon's light is from the sun by observing phases) and rational examples (e.g., 'one is half of two', 'the whole is greater than the part').
3
Analyze how the particular is known directly
For each universal proposition, he argues that the particular instance is known without inferring it from the universal. For instance, one knows that this whole is greater than its part immediately, not by applying the universal rule.
This is a key move: the psychological process of knowing does not require explicit syllogistic steps.
4
Assess the epistemological implication
The conclusion is that universal premises are not epistemologically prior or necessary for certain knowledge. Immediate apprehension, whether sensory or rational, suffices.
Ibn Taymiyya does not deny universals entirely; he denies their necessary role in every case of certainty.

The third station: Certain sciences do not necessarily require universal propositions. Sensory propositions, like the knowledge that the moon's light is borrowed from the sun when one sees the variation of its phases according to its different positions relative to the sun, just as it varies when it departs from it after conjunction, as on the night of the crescent, and when it is the night of the opposition at full moons. Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument showing that certain knowledge does not necessarily depend on universal premises. Ibn Taymiyya introduces the 'third station' and lists types of premises: sensory (hissiyya), experiential (mujarrabat), mutawatir, intuitive (hads), and rational (aqliyya). He gives an example of sensory knowledge: knowing that the moon's light is from the sun by observing its phases relative to the sun's position. He notes there is disagreement on whether intuition yields certainty. Then he gives rational examples: 'One is half of two', 'The whole is greater than the part', 'Things equal to the same thing are equal', and the laws of non-contradiction and excluded middle for opposites and contradictories. He argues that for each of these universal propositions, the particular instances can be known directly without inferring them via the universal premise. For example, if one knows the universal that every one is half of two, one does not need to apply that universal to know that this particular one is half of these two; rather, one knows the particular directly. Similarly, one can know that 'this whole is greater than its part' without the universal premise. And for contradictories, anyone who understands what contradictories are knows that a specific thing cannot be both existent and non-existent, and that it must be either existent or non-existent, without deducing it from the universal rule. Thus, universal premises are not necessary for such certain knowledge.

Immediate Inferences: Naqid, Aks, Aks al-Naqid
Author explains three immediate inference operations: naqid, aks, aks al-naqid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three immediate inference operations (contradictory, conversion, contrapositive) to derive conclusions from Islamic propositions.

Core Idea
A proposition's truth yields its contradictory's falsity and its conversion's and contrapositive's truth.

Logicians use immediate inferences to generate conclusions from a single proposition. In this lesson, we explore three operations: naqid (contradictory), aks (simple conversion), and aks al-naqid (contrapositive). These tools are essential for transforming propositions in syllogistic reasoning, especially for the less productive figures. Let's see how each operation works with concrete Islamic examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the contradictory (naqid) operation
The truth of a proposition implies the falsity of its contradictory. For example, if 'No pilgrim is an unbeliever' is true, then its contradictory 'Some pilgrim is an unbeliever' is false.
Contradictories are opposite in truth value.
2
Apply simple conversion (aks) to universal negatives
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth, but only for universal negatives (E) and particular affirmatives (I). From 'No pilgrim is an unbeliever' we infer 'No unbeliever is a pilgrim'.
For A (universal affirmative), conversion only yields a particular affirmative by limitation.
3
Apply contrapositive (aks al-naqid) to universal affirmatives
Take 'Every pilgrim is a Muslim.' The contrapositive is 'Whoever is not a Muslim is not a pilgrim.' This is valid by converting the contradictory.
Contrapositive works for A and O propositions.
4
Identify which operation is used in a given inference
Given an inference like 'Some Muslims are pilgrims' from 'Every pilgrim is a Muslim', recognize it as conversion by limitation (aks) of an A proposition.
Practice distinguishing the three operations.

Commentary
This passage explains the use of immediate inferences to derive conclusions from the less productive figures. The logicians use three operations: naqid (contradictory) — the truth of a proposition implies the falsity of its contradictory; aks (simple conversion) — swapping subject and predicate while preserving truth for universal negatives and particular affirmatives; and aks al-naqid (conversion of contradictory, i.e., contrapositive) — from 'Every A is B' we infer 'If not B then not A.' Examples illustrate: from 'No pilgrim is an unbeliever' we convert to 'No unbeliever is a pilgrim' (universal negative conversion). These operations allow logicians to derive needed conclusions from the second, third, and fourth figures.

Reasoning by Necessary Concomitance: A Distinct Type of Inference
Author explains reasoning from one universal to its necessary concomitant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish reasoning by necessary concomitance from analogical reasoning (qiyās al-tamthīl) and identify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Necessary concomitance lets you infer one thing from another without needing a shared cause.

Ibn Taymiyya expands the scope of valid inference beyond the syllogism and analogy. In this passage, he introduces a type of reasoning that relies on a direct relationship of necessary concomitance (talāzum) between two things. This is not the same as analogical reasoning, which requires a common cause ('illa). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two relata in each example
In each example given (stars, mountains, Ka'bah), identify which two things are linked by necessary concomitance. For instance, the Pleiades and nearby stars, or the existence of a mountain and the location of a place.
The relationship is not causal but of constant conjunction.
2
Determine whether the concomitance is perpetual or temporary
Note the duration of the concomitance: mountains and rivers have been concomitant since creation, while a tree as a sign of a house is temporary. This affects the strength of the inference.
Ibn Taymiyya uses this to show that reasoning by talāzum accommodates both permanent and temporary relations.
3
Compare to analogical reasoning (qiyās al-tamthīl)
Analogical reasoning extends a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared 'illa (effective cause). In talāzum, no shared cause is needed; the two things are directly concomitant. Check if the example relies on a common attribute or a direct relation.
The author explicitly says this is 'not from analogical reasoning'.
4
Practice with a new Islamic example
Consider: 'The sign of the start of Fajr is the appearance of the first true dawn (al-fajr al-sādiq).' Is this talāzum or qiyās? Identify the relata and the type of concomitance.
This is a standard example from fiqh of time determination.

The Third Topic - Reasoning from a universal to its necessary concomitant. Likewise, reasoning from the appearance of a star to the appearance of its counterpart in latitude, and reasoning from its rising to the setting of another, and the culmination of another, and similar evidences that people have agreed upon. Allah Almighty said: 'And by the stars they are guided.' And reasoning about times and places by places is a matter agreed upon by Arabs, non-Arabs, people of religions, and philosophers. And whoever knows the measure of the distances of the stars from one another and knows which of them accompany the dawn, he reasons from what he sees of them to the amount of night that has passed and what remains. And it is [reasoning] from one of two concomitants to the other. And whoever knows the mountains, rivers, and winds, reasons by them to the places that are concomitant with them. Then, if the concomitance is perpetual, with no known beginning, but rather since Allah created the earth – like the existence of mountains, great rivers: the Nile, the Euphrates, Sayhan, Jayhan, and the sea – the reasoning is consistent. Then one reasons by them to the directions, and one reasons by the directions to them. And what has a duration shorter than the duration of the Ka'bah, like buildings in cities and trees, reasoning by them is according to that. So it is said: The sign of such-and-such a house is that at its door there is a tree of such-and-such a description, and the two are concomitant for a period of time. Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of a type of inference known as reasoning by necessary concomitance (istidlāl bi al-talāzum). The author, Ibn Taymiyya, distinguishes this from analogical reasoning (qiyās al-tamthīl). He begins by stating the topic: reasoning from a universal to its necessary concomitant. He provides numerous examples from the natural world and everyday life, all drawn from Islamic heritage: using stars to determine directions or seasons, using landmarks like mountains and rivers, and using the Ka'bah's orientation to determine the cardinal directions. He emphasizes that this type of inference relies on a relationship of necessary concomitance (luzūm) between two things, and this relationship can vary in duration – from perpetual (e.g., mountains and rivers that have existed since creation) to temporary (e.g., a tree as a sign of a house). Importantly, he clarifies that when both relata are particular individuals, the reasoning is from particular to particular, but if the relation is expressed as a universal generalization, it becomes a reasoning from universal to universal. In all cases, it is distinct from analogical reasoning because it does not rely on a common attribute ('illa) but on direct concomitance.

Refuting the Two-Part Conclusion Claim
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' claim that the conclusion consists of only two parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in the logicians' claim that conclusions have only two parts and recognize that conclusions can have multiple components using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that a conclusion has only two parts by showing that real examples have three or more.

Logicians claimed that every conclusion in a syllogism consists of just two parts: a subject and a predicate. Ibn Taymiyya challenges this directly with examples from Islamic legal reasoning and everyday discourse. He shows that the predicate itself can contain multiple components, and even the subject can be complex. This critique forces us to reconsider the nature of propositions and how arguments are structured.

Guided Path
1
State the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that the required conclusion (al-maṭlūb) does not exceed two parts (juz'ayn), meaning a subject and a predicate.
2
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's counterexamples
Look at the nabidh example: 'Nabidh is forbidden by text' – this has three parts (nabidh, forbidden, by text). Similarly, 'Consensus is a definitive proof' has three parts. The definition of man as 'body, sentient, growing, moving by will, rational' has six parts.
3
Analyze the structure of the examples
In 'Nabidh is forbidden by text', the subject is 'nabidh', the predicate is 'forbidden by text', which itself contains two components. So the conclusion has three components, not two.
4
Examine the Quranic evidence
Ibn Taymiyya cites verses where the subject or predicate is lengthy, e.g., 'And the foremost, the first of the Emigrants and the Helpers...' (9:100). These show that a declarative sentence can be composed of many words when its content is qualified by many restrictions.
5
Assess the refutation's strength
Does the existence of multi-part conclusions disprove the logicians' claim? Ibn Taymiyya argues it does, because the claim was absolute. The examples show that conclusions can have more than two parts, so the logicians' restriction is empirically false.

Commentary
This passage is a direct refutation of the logicians' claim that the conclusion (المطلوب) in a syllogism consists of no more than two parts (subject and predicate). Ibn Taymiyya begins by rebutting the claim: if by 'two parts' they mean simply two terms (مفردان), that is not always true; the expression for the conclusion can involve multiple terms. He gives examples: if someone asks whether nabidh is forbidden by text, and the answer is 'nabidh is forbidden by text', the conclusion consists of three parts: 'nabidh', 'forbidden', and 'by text'. Similarly, the question about consensus being a definitive proof yields a three-part conclusion. The extreme case is the definition of man as 'body, sentient, growing, moving by will, rational', which gives six parts. Then Ibn Taymiyya explains that a sentence can be composed of many words if its content is qualified by many restrictions, citing several Quranic verses (Surah 9:100, 2:218, 8:75) where the subject or predicate is lengthy. Thus, the logicians' claim is empirically false: conclusions can have many components, and thus syllogisms can require many premises to establish them.

Ibn Taymiyya's First Argument: No Essence Without Existence
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the fixed external essence of possibles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's first argument against the fixed essence of possibles in the external world.

Core Idea
Essences are mental, not externally fixed; existence is not an accident.

Ibn Taymiyya begins his refutation by targeting the cornerstone of the philosophers' metaphysics: the claim that a possible being has a real essence in the external world distinct from its existence. He argues that this leads to absurdity, and insists that the only reality in the external world is the concrete existent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the notion that a possible being has a 'realized essence' (dhāt muhaqqiqa) in the external world that is distinct from its particular existence.
This is the Avicennan distinction between essence (māhiyya) and existence (wujūd).
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's reason for rejecting it
He argues that if such an essence existed, it would be established during non-existence, which is impossible. Existence cannot be an accident that befalls a pre-existing essence.
3
Compare with the philosophers' view
In Avicennan thought, quiddities are neutral; they can be considered in themselves without existence. Ibn Taymiyya rejects that such quiddities have any external reality apart from existence.
The philosophers claim quiddities are 'established in the external world' (thābita fī al-khārij).
4
Understand the correct distinction
The correct distinction is between what is in the mind (mental existence) and what is in the external world. Quiddities are valid as mental concepts, not as externally fixed entities.
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'The intelligible distinction is between what is known in the minds and what exists in concrete realities.'

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' claim that a 'possible being' (mumkin) can be considered as having an essence that is neither existent nor non-existent, while simultaneously being described as eternally necessary through another. He begins by stating that such a supposition combines contradictories: that which must exist eternally cannot accept non-existence at all. Then he gives three arguments (wujuh). First, the notion of a 'realized essence in the external world' distinct from the actual existing thing is false. In reality, there is no essence in the external world that is established during non-existence; existence is not an accident that befalls a pre-existing essence. Rather, the mental conception of a thing is one thing, and its external existence is another. He associates this view with the Mu'tazilite doctrine that 'the non-existent is a thing' and with the philosophers' claim that quiddities are fixed externally, both of which he deems false. The correct distinction is between what is known in the mind and what exists in concrete reality. Second, even if we posit a possible having an essence distinct from its existence, if that existence is necessary for it eternally, then the essence itself cannot ever be non-existent. The claim that 'in itself it has no existence' does not entail that it can be non-existent while affirming its necessary existence. Third, concerning efficient causation, the agent must precede its specific effect in time; simultaneity is impossible. The examples they give (like hand and ring) involve conditions, not efficient causes. The term 'cause' is ambiguous, covering agent, receptive capacity, and conditions. He then connects this to the issue of divine attributes: the logicians' argument that attributes would make God caused is based on an equivocation of 'necessary being.' The proof that contingent things need an agent does not prove that attributes need a substrate. Thus, the three arguments collectively dismantle the concept of a possible that is eternally necessary.

The Second Argument: An Eternal Necessary Cannot Be Non-Existent
Ibn Taymiyya argues eternal necessity excludes possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's second argument that the supposition of an eternally necessary possible is contradictory.

Core Idea
If something is eternally necessary, its essence cannot be non-existent at any time.

Ibn Taymiyya's second argument targets the heart of the logicians' claim: that a possible being can be considered 'in itself' as neither existent nor non-existent, while simultaneously being eternally necessary through another. He shows this is a direct contradiction.

Guided Path
1
State the logicians' position
They claim that a possible being, considered in its essence apart from its cause, is neither existent nor non-existent, but it becomes necessary through another eternally.
2
Follow Ibn Taymiyya's conditional reasoning
If we suppose such a possible has an essence distinct from its existence, and that existence is necessary for it eternally, then the essence can never be non-existent.
3
Examine the claim 'in itself it has no existence'
The logicians might argue that the essence itself is not existent. Ibn Taymiyya responds that this does not allow for it to be non-existent while its existence is necessary.
4
Identify the contradiction
The very notion of something that is eternally existent (by another) but whose essence is 'possible' in itself is incoherent because possibility implies potential non-existence, which is incompatible with eternal necessary existence.

Efficient Causation and Divine Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya refutes simultaneous causation and equivocation on 'Necessary Being'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument that efficient causes must precede their effects in time and how this relates to the critique of divine attributes.

Core Idea
Efficient causation requires temporal priority; the ambiguity of 'cause' leads to confusion about God's attributes.

Ibn Taymiyya's third argument focuses on the nature of efficient causation. He argues that a true efficient cause (al-ʿilla al-fāʿila) must precede its effect in time; simultaneity is impossible. He then links this to the logicians' denial of divine attributes, showing that their argument relies on an equivocation in the term 'Necessary Being.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about causation
The logicians claim that an efficient cause can be simultaneous with its effect, using examples like the hand moving a ring.
2
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's refutation
He argues that such examples involve conditions, not efficient causes. The movement of the hand is a condition for the ring's movement, not an efficient cause that brings it about.
The hand and ring example is from the philosophers (e.g., Ibn Sina).
3
Understand the ambiguity of 'cause'
The term 'cause' (ʿilla) is used for agent (fāʿil), receptive capacity (qābil), and condition (shart). Only the agent must precede temporally.
4
Apply to divine attributes
The logicians argue that if God had attributes, they would be caused by His essence, making Him dependent. Ibn Taymiyya responds that 'Necessary Being' has two meanings: (1) not needing an agent, and (2) not needing a substrate. Attributes are necessary in the first sense but not in the second. Thus, the argument against attributes fails.

Reasoning by Mutual Entailment from Specific to Specific
Ibn Taymiyya illustrates reasoning by mutual entailment with astronomical examples and applies it to prophetic reports.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a pair of mutually entailing specific entities and construct a valid reasoning from one to the other based on Ibn Taymiyya's model.

Mutual entailment between two specifics allows direct inference without a universal.

One type is mutual entailment, where two specific entities are necessarily connected such that each implies the other. In this lesson, you will learn to recognise such pairs and use them in reasoning.

Commentary
This passage defines a type of reasoning that Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes from formal syllogism: reasoning by specific indicators (al-istidlāl bi’l-mu‘ayyan). He begins by describing how one can reason from a specific thing to another specific thing that is equal in generality and particularity, i.e., they are mutually entailing (mutalāzim). These signs indicate His essence, not a universal concept that could be shared with others. The point is that reasoning from specific signs is valid and used by scripture, unlike the abstract universal syllogisms of the logicians.

Core Idea

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a foundational assumption of Aristotelian logic: that every valid inference requires a universal major premise. By analyzing his examples of prayer and purity, even and odd, and black and white, we will see how he reduces the logician's syllogism back to intuitive knowledge.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Defining 'evident necessity' fails to block regress; it entails knowledge of all necessary concomitants.

Ibn Taymiyya targets a common move in Islamic logic: distinguishing between evident and non-evident necessity to limit what one must know. In this passage, he shows that the opponent's definition of 'evident necessity' backfires, leading to the very infinite regress it was meant to avoid. By applying the same definition to the concomitant of a concomitant, he reveals that the distinction collapses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's strategy

2
State Ibn Taymiyya's application
Ibn Taymiyya applies the same definition of 'evident necessity' to the necessary concomitant of a necessary concomitant. That is, if the first concomitant's relation to the original term is evident, then the relation of the second concomitant to the first should also be evident.
If the definition holds for one level, it must hold for higher levels.
3
Trace the regress
Once you conceive the first necessary concomitant and its necessary concomitant, Ibn Taymiyya argues that you must recognize the causal relation between them. By the opponent's own logic, this forces knowledge of all necessary concomitants, since each level triggers the same rule.
The regress is not avoided; it is merely pushed to the next level.
4
Assess the critique's conclusion
Conclude that the opponent's attempted escape fails. The definition of 'evident necessity' does not limit knowledge; it expands it indefinitely, leading to the very infinite regress the opponent tried to avoid.
Ibn Taymiyya's argument is a logical reductio ad absurdum.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of Close vs Distant Concomitants
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the distinction between close and distant necessary concomitants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of the distinction between close and distant necessary concomitants and evaluate its logical consistency.

Core Idea
Same condition for knowing close and distant collapses the distinction.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes aim at a subtle logical distinction: that between 'close' necessary concomitants (known directly without a middle term) and 'distant' ones (requiring a middle term). He argues that the logician's own definition of closeness leads to an equivalence that destroys the distinction, forcing the opponent into inconsistency. By following his reasoning, you will learn how to detect hidden assumptions in logical classifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logician's claim
The opponent distinguishes between close and distant necessary concomitants: close ones have evident necessity and are known without a middle term, while distant ones require a middle term.
This is the target doctrine.
2
Extract the condition for closeness
According to the opponent, a close concomitant is known when both the entailed (malzum) and the concomitant (lazim) are conceived together. This is the condition for direct knowledge.
Ibn Taymiyya quotes: 'when the entailed and the necessary concomitant are both conceived, then the entailed must be known.'
3
Apply the condition to a chain of concomitants
Consider the first necessary concomitant (L1) and its own necessary concomitant (L2). When you conceive L1 and L2, you have conceived the original entailed (the malzum of L1) and its close concomitant (L1). By the same condition, L2 should also be known without a middle term.
This extends the condition recursively.
4
Observe the collapse
If the condition applies to L2, then knowing L1 gives knowledge of L2, and so on for all later concomitants. This is exactly what the opponent wanted to avoid. The distinction between close and distant vanishes.
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'So what you fled from follows.'
5
Assess the consistency demand
Ibn Taymiyya forces a choice: either keep the condition for both close and distant (then both are directly known), or remove it for both (then neither is directly known). In either case, the distinction fails.
He concludes: 'On both suppositions, the distinction ... is invalidated.'

The opponent claims that only close concomitants (with evident necessity) are known from conceiving the entailed without a middle term; distant ones require a middle term. Ibn Taymiyya points out that the opponent's own definition of close concomitant is that it is known when both the entailed and the concomitant are conceived. But if one conceives the first necessary concomitant and its second necessary concomitant, then the entailed and the close concomitant (the first) are conceived, so by the same reasoning, the necessity of the second should also be known without a middle term. This leads to the very conclusion the opponent wanted to avoid: that knowledge of one necessary concomitant leads to knowledge of all. Ibn Taymiyya then demands consistency: if the condition for knowing the first is the presence of both terms in conception, then that condition must apply to the second as well; if the condition is dropped for the second, it must be dropped for the first. In either case, the distinction between close and distant collapses, and the opponent's argument is invalidated.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of the Primary/Well-Known Distinction
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the distinction between primary and well-known propositions based on a middle term is false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in the logicians' distinction between primary and well-known propositions as presented by Ibn Taymiyya.

Core Idea
The distinction between primary and well-known collapses because it leads to circularity or infinite regress.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya targets a core epistemological claim of the logicians: that primary propositions (awwaliyyat) are known without a middle term (i.e., self-evidently), while well-known propositions (mashhoorat) require a middle term. By analyzing this distinction, you will see how Ibn Taymiyya exposes a hidden circularity or regress, undermining the entire epistemological framework. The lesson will guide you through his argument and show its implications for Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that primary propositions are known without a middle term (bi-ghayr wasaṭ), while well-known propositions are known through a middle term (bi-wasaṭ). This is the distinction Ibn Taymiyya attacks.
Note the terms: 'awwaliyyat' (primary) and 'mashhoorat' (well-known).
2
Understand the meaning of 'middle term'
The middle term (al-wasaṭ) is the mediating concept in a proof. Ibn Taymiyya insists that if it means a real ontological middle (fī nafs al-amr), then the distinction leads to problems.
Ibn Taymiyya's interpretation: the middle is not merely a mental link but a real intermediary in the order of existence.
3

Ibn Taymiyya argues that the logicians' claim is false because it either assumes what needs to be proven (circularity) or requires an infinite chain of middle terms (infinite regress). If the middle is real, then knowing a primary proposition without a middle would mean it has no cause, but then how do we know that? Alternatively, if every known proposition requires a known middle, we regress infinitely.
This is a classic critique: the distinction cannot be sustained without presupposing the conclusion.
4

Evaluate whether Ibn Taymiyya's critique holds. The logicians might respond that the middle for primary propositions is the intellect itself or direct intuition. Ibn Taymiyya would then ask: is that intellect known by another middle? This leads to the same problem.
Consider how this critique challenges the entire project of grounding certain knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya's Reductio on the Ordering of Concomitants
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that necessary concomitants are ordered in reality and that some are known without proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reductio argument against the hierarchical ordering of necessary concomitants and identify its logical consequence.

Core Idea
If the first concomitant needs no proof, neither does the second—ending in absurdity.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya targets a claim by the logicians that necessary concomitants (lawāzim) are ordered in reality, with each implying the next, and that some are known without a middle term. He exposes a fatal contradiction: if knowledge of the first concomitant is immediate, then the same should hold for all, leading to the absurd conclusion that knowing any object entails knowing all its necessary attributes. This lesson walks you through his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians claim that necessary concomitants are ordered in themselves, each necessitating the next, and that a concomitant known without a middle term is self-evident and needs no proof.
This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
2
Extract the reductio structure
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'Whatever you say about the first concomitant applies equally to the second.' If the first is known without a middle, then the second must also be known without a middle, by the same reasoning.
This challenges the asymmetry in the logicians' position.
3
Apply the reasoning to the second concomitant
Since both the first and second concomitants are equally necessitated by the essence, there is no principled reason to require a middle term for the second if none is needed for the first.
The logicians' own criterion collapses.
4
Derive the absurd consequence
If every concomitant is known without a middle, then anyone who knows a thing knows all its necessary concomitants. This is clearly false—many people know an essence without knowing all its attributes.
This absurdity shows the original claim is contradictory.
5
Conclude the refutation
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that the ordering and division of concomitants into self-evident and proof-dependent is merely epistemic, not ontological, and that the logicians' argument is internally inconsistent.
The critique is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

المقام الرابع - الوجه التاسع : التفاضل بين التجربيات والمشهورات فنقول : الناس إذا قالوا «العدل حسن والظلم قبيح فهم يعنون بهذا أن العدل محبوب للفطرة تحصل لها بوجوده لذة وفرح، نافع لصاحبه ولغير صاحبه، تحصل به اللذة والفرح وما تتنعم به النفوس . وإذا قالوا : الظلم قبيح فهم يعنون به أنه ضار لصاحبه ولغير صاحبه، وأنه بغيض يحصل به الألم والغم وما تتعذب به النفوس . ومعلوم أن هذه القضايا هي في علم الناس لها بالفطرة وبالتجربة أعظم من أكثر قضايا الطب، مثل كون السقمونيا تسهل الصفراء. فلِمَ كانت التجربيات يقينية، وهذه التي هي أشهر منها وقد جربها الناس أكثر من تلك لا تكون يقينية؟ مع أن المجربين لها أكثر وأعلم وأصدق، وجزئياتها في العالم أكثر من جزئيات تلك، والمخبرين بذلك عنها أيضاً أكثر وأعلم وأصدق التفاضل بين التجربيات والمشهورات فالإنسان من نفسه يجد من لذة العدل والصدق والعلم والإحسان، والسرور بذلك، ما لا يجده من الظلم والكذب والجهل والناس الذين وصل إليهم ذلك والذين لم يصل إليهم ذلك يجدون في أنفسهم من اللذة والفرح والسرور بعدل العادل وبصدق الصادق وعلم العالم وإحسان المحسن ما لا يجدونه في الظلم والكذب والجهل والإساءة. ولهذا يجدون في أنفسهم محبة لمن فعل ذلك وثناء عليه ودعاء له، وهم مفطورون على محبة ذلك واللذة به لا يمكنهم دفع ذلك عن أنفسهم، كما فطروا على وجود اللذة بالأكل والشرب والألم بالجوع والعطش. فلم كانت تلك القضايا من اليقينيات المعلومة بالحس والعقل كالتجربة، وغيرها، ولم تكن هذه من القضايا العقلية المعلومة أيضاً بالحس والعقل والأمر فيها أعظم؟ واللذة التي توجد بهذه لذة روحانية عقلية شريفة، والإنسان كلما كمل عقله كانت هذه اللذة أحب إليه من تلك اللذة . ثم الفلاسفة أثبتوا معاد الأرواح واللذة العقلية وهي مبنية على هذه القضايا التي سموها «المشهورات». فإن لم تكن معلومة كان ما أثبتوه من ذلك ليس فيه شيء من العلم بل يقولون ما يقوله غيرهم من أن اللذات الباطنة أقوى وأشرف من اللذات الظاهرة، ويدعون الضرورة في إثبات لذة وراء اللذات الحسية الظاهرة. وإذا كانت اللذة
The fourth station - the ninth aspect: the distinction between experiential propositions and popular propositions. We say: When people say 'justice is good and injustice is ugly,' they mean by this that justice is beloved by innate disposition, through its existence they obtain pleasure and joy, beneficial to its doer and others, and by it comes pleasure and joy and what souls delight in. And when they say 'injustice is ugly,' they mean that it is harmful to its doer and others, and that it is hateful, causing pain and grief and what souls are tormented by. It is known that these propositions are known to people by innate disposition and experience to a greater degree than most medical propositions, such as that scammony ejects bile. So why were experiential propositions deemed certain, while these, which are more famous and have been experienced by people more than those, are not deemed certain? Despite the fact that those who have experienced them are more numerous, more knowledgeable, and more truthful, and their particulars in the world are more than the particulars of those, and those who report about them are also more numerous, more knowledgeable, and more truthful. The distinction between experiential and popular propositions: A person finds within himself from the pleasure of justice, truth, knowledge, and benevolence, and joy thereby, what he does not find from injustice, falsehood, ignorance, and evil. Others who have received that and those who have not received that find within themselves pleasure, joy, and happiness from the justice of the just, the truthfulness of the truthful, the knowledge of the knowledgeable, and the benevolence of the benevolent, what they do not find in injustice, falsehood, ignorance, and evil. For this reason, they find within themselves love for the one who does that, praise for him, and supplication for him; they are innately disposed to love that and take pleasure in it, unable to repel that from themselves, just as they are innately disposed to experience pleasure in eating and drinking and pain from hunger and thirst. So why were those propositions among the certainties known by sense and reason, like experience and others, and these are not among the rational propositions also known by sense and reason, while the matter concerning them is greater? And the pleasure that exists through these is a spiritual, rational, noble pleasure; and the more complete a person's intellect is, the more beloved this pleasure is to him than that pleasure. Then the philosophers affirmed the resurrection of souls and intellectual pleasure, which are built upon these propositions that they called 'popular propositions.' For if they are not known, then what they affirmed from that contains nothing of knowledge; rather they say what others say: that inner pleasures are stronger and nobler than apparent pleasures, and they claim necessity in affirming a pleasure beyond sensory apparent pleasures. Commentary
This passage is a critical comparison between experiential propositions (tajribiyyat) and moral judgments, which logicians classify merely as popular propositions (mashhurat). Ibn Taymiyya argues that moral judgments are actually more certain than medical experiential propositions. He begins by explaining what people mean when they say 'justice is good' and 'injustice is ugly': they mean that justice is loved by innate disposition (fitra), brings pleasure and benefit, and injustice brings pain and harm. He then compares these to medical propositions like 'scammony ejects bile'. He questions why medical propositions are considered certain (yaqiniyya) while moral judgments are not, despite being more universally experienced, with more experiencers who are more knowledgeable and truthful, and more particulars in the world. He emphasizes the internal experience of pleasure from justice, truth, knowledge, and benevolence, which is found in all people, even those who haven't experienced it directly. This innate disposition cannot be repelled, similar to pleasure from eating and pain from hunger. He contrasts spiritual, rational pleasure with physical pleasure, noting that the more intellectual a person, the more they value spiritual pleasure. Finally, he points out a contradiction: the philosophers themselves affirm the resurrection of souls and intellectual pleasure, which are built upon these same moral propositions they call 'popular'. If these propositions are not certain, then the philosophers' own eschatological claims lack a knowledge base. Instead, they rely on the claim that inner pleasures are stronger—a claim that itself is not proven. The passage is a sustained refutation of the logicians' classification, using the philosophers' own arguments against them.
