The Manuscript Journey of Ibn Taymiyya's al-Mu'tabar
Editorial description of the manuscript's lineage and partial editing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the manuscript lineage of Ibn Taymiyya's al-Mu'tabar and identify the key scholars involved in its preservation and editing.

Core Idea
A Yemeni manuscript, carried by a king, rescued by a historian, and half-edited by a Qur'an scholar.

Before you can engage with Ibn Taymiyya's logical arguments, you need to know that the text itself has a dramatic history of survival. This lesson walks through the chain of custody of the original manuscript of al-Mu'tabar, showing how it reached India and why it remains unpublished. Understanding this history guards against relying on corrupted editions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original manuscript
The passage states the original copy (أم النسخ الهندية) came from Yemen, bearing handwriting of Zaydi imams. This is the basis of all Indian copies.
Note that it was dictated by Ibn Taymiyya himself to a student.
2
Trace its journey to India
The manuscript was among books brought from Yemen by King Siddiq Hasan Khan of Bhopal. After his death, it passed to the Asifiyya Library via a bookseller.
Siddiq Hasan Khan was a notable scholar-king, which adds credibility to the provenance.
3
Recognize the first modern scholar to value it
Imam Shibli al-Nu'mani was the first to notice its importance in India. He commissioned a copy and even published articles based on it.
Shibli's 1904 article on 'Error of Greek Logic' was derived from this same manuscript.
4
Understand the editorial efforts and their incompletion
Shaykh 'Abd al-Hamid al-Farahi copied half the manuscript and reorganized it, but left Hyderabad before finishing. Later, the editor delegated completion to Abu al-Jalal al-Nadwi, but the Ottoman Encyclopaedia never published it.
Farahi's initial work included reordering paragraphs, adding chapters and titles.
5
Assess the current state
The manuscript remains unpublished, with pages scattered, despite near completion by al-Nadwi.
This highlights the fragility of manuscript traditions even in modern times.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed biographical account of the manuscript history of Ibn Taymiyya's work on logic. The editor, Dr. al-Nadwi, explains that the original copy came from Yemen, brought by King Siddiq Hasan Khan of Bhopal, and ended up in the Asifiyya Library. He notes that Imam Shibli al-Nu'mani was the first to recognize its importance, and he published articles based on it. The editor then describes the manuscript's physical features: it is in Arabic script with marginal corrections, indicating it was dictated by Ibn Taymiyya himself. The manuscript is unorganized, lacking chapter divisions. Later, Shaykh 'Abd al-Hamid al-Farahi began editing it, arranging its paragraphs, but left it incomplete. The editor then assigned the task to Abu al-Jalal al-Nadwi, who completed the copy with help from the Nadwa copy. Finally, the Ottoman Encyclopaedia planned to print it but did not, leaving the work unfinished. This passage provides crucial context for understanding the textual transmission of Ibn Taymiyya's work.

Ibn Sina's Sources and the Batiniyya
Exposes Ibn Sina's connection to Batiniyya and lists Muslim refutations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Sina's connection to the Batiniyya as argued by Ibn Taymiyya and list the Muslim scholars who authored refutations against them.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya traces Ibn Sina's innovations to heretical sources to undermine the philosophers' authority.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya shifts from logical critique to historical genealogy. He claims that the philosophers, especially Ibn Sina, derived their novel theological ideas not from pure reason but from heretical sects like the Batiniyya (Isma'ilis). By exposing Ibn Sina's family ties to the Fatimid da'wa and listing orthodox refutations, Ibn Taymiyya prepares the ground for discrediting the entire philosophical tradition. Understanding this context is crucial for following his larger argument against Greek logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central accusation
Read the first part of the Arabic: Ibn Taymiyya claims Ibn Sina spoke about matters his predecessors never addressed. What is the source of these innovations?
The passage states Ibn Sina 'took from the heretics claiming affiliation with Islam, like the Isma'iliyya'.
2
Distinguish outward and inward beliefs
Ibn Taymiyya says the Batiniyya outwardly show 'Rafd' but inwardly hide 'pure disbelief'. This distinction is key to his polemic.
Rafd = rejection of the first caliphs; inner disbelief = outright atheism or esotericism.
3
Catalog the Muslim refutations
List the authors mentioned: al-Baqillani, Abd al-Jabbar, al-Ghazali, Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini. What do these scholars have in common?
They are all orthodox Sunni theologians/kalam scholars who wrote against the Batiniyya.
4
Connect to Ibn Taymiyya's larger project
Why would Ibn Taymiyya include this biographical attack in a work refuting logic? Consider how it undermines the credibility of the logicians who follow Ibn Sina.
It links the philosophical tradition to heresy, making it suspect for Muslims.

Commentary
This passage is a historical and biographical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya in his refutation of the logicians. He begins by stating the topic: the condition of Ibn Sina and the Batiniyya sect. He then explains that those who follow Ibn Sina (the philosophers) merely repeat his words. Ibn Sina's Batiniyya Roots: A Critique of Philosophical Authority
Author describes Ibn Sina's background and his family's involvement with the Batiniyya sect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze Ibn Taymiyya's biographical critique of Ibn Sina's philosophical origins and evaluate its rhetorical force.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya traces Ibn Sina's philosophy to Batiniyya heresy, undermining its claim to rational authority.

This lesson examines how Ibn Taymiyya uses biographical evidence to discredit Ibn Sina's logical and philosophical system. Rather than refuting specific arguments, he attacks the source of Ibn Sina's thought by linking it to a sect deemed heretical. Understanding this strategy helps you recognize how premodern Muslim scholars integrated ethical and religious critique into logical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Determine what exactly Ibn Taymiyya is trying to discredit. The passage targets Ibn Sina's philosophical project itself, not a particular argument.
2
Locate the biographical evidence
Find the specific information Ibn Taymiyya presents about Ibn Sina's family. He states that Ibn Sina's father and brother were among the atheists (malāḥida) and that Ibn Sina heard from them about 'the Intellect' and 'the Soul'.
3
Assess the rhetorical strategy
Notice how Ibn Taymiyya lists numerous Muslim scholars who wrote refutations against the Batiniyya. This establishes a tradition of combating heresy and makes Ibn Sina's association with them more damning by implying he belongs to a well-known deviant group.
The list includes well-known Sunni authorities, strengthening the condemnation.
4
Evaluate the effectiveness of the critique
Consider whether this biographical attack directly refutes any logical or philosophical claims. It does not; it is an ad hominem against the person of Ibn Sina. However, within the polemical context, it aims to discredit the entire philosophical tradition by showing its corrupt origins.
This strategy is common in Islamic theological debates where the moral character of the transmitter is scrutinized.

المقام الرابع - الوجه الرابع عشر : بناء ابن الزبير الكعبة الوجوه، ولا رماها بمَنْجَنِيق أصلاً. ولكن كان محاصراً لابن الزبير وكان ابن الزبير وأصحابه في المسجد، وكانوا يرمون بالمنجنيق له ولأصحابه، لا لقصد الكعبة. ولم يهدموا الكعبة، ولا وقع فيها شيء من حجارة الحَجّاج . بل كان ابن الزبير قد بناها على قواعد إبراهيم، وألصقها بالأرض، وجعل لها بابين كما أخبرته عائشة عن النبي أنه قال: لولا قومك حديثوا عهد بجاهلية لنقضت الكعبة وألصقتها بالأرض وجعلتُ لها بابين - باباً يدخل الناس منه وباباً يخرجون منه. ووصف لعائشة من الحجر ما هو من البيت قريباً من سبعة أذرع موضع انحناء الحِجْر ؛ وكانت قريش قد بَنتها فقَصَّرتْ بها النفقةُ فتركوا ما تركوه من الحِجْر ورفعوها. فلما ولي ابن الزبير شارو الناس في ذلك. فمنهم من رأى ذلك مصلحة، ومنهم من أشار عليه بأن لا يفعل وقال: هذه الكعبة هي التي كانت على عهد النبي . وعليها أسلم الناس. وهذا كان رأي ابن عباس وطائفة . والعراق فوليها عشرين سنة. وكان جباراً عنيداً مقداماً على سفك الدماء بأدنى شبهة. قيل قتل مئة ألف وعشرين ألفاً. وقيل إنه كان يكثر تلاوة القرآن ويعطي المال لأهل القرآن، ويتجنب المحارم. بنى مدينة «واسط» بين البصرة والكوفة، ومات بها سنة ٩٥هـ وله أربع وخمسون سنة . والفقهاء متنازعون في هذه المسئلة منهم من يرى إقرارها كقول ابن عباس وهو قول مالك وغيره. ويقال إن الرشيد شاوره أن يفعل كما فعل ابن الزبير، فأشار عليه ابن الزبير أن لا يفعل، ورأى أن هذا يُفضي إلى انتقاض حُرمة الكعبة باختلاف الملوك في ذلك - هذا يهدمها ليبنيها كما فعل ابن الزبير، وهذا يرى أن يعيدها كما كانت. ومنهم من يرى تصويب ما فعله ابن الزبير . ويقال إن الشافعي يميل إلى هذا. فلما قتل ابن الزبير كتب الحجاج إلى عبد الملك بن مروان يخبره بما فعل ابنُ الزبير، فيخبره بأنهم وجدوا قواعد إبراهيم، وأنه أرى ذلك لأهل مكة. فكتب إليه عبد الملك أن يعيدها كما كانت إلا ما زاده من الطول فلا يغيره ويذكر أن ما فعله ابن الزبير لا يعلم أصله . ثم إن عبد الملك حدثه بعض الناس بحديث عائشة، فقال: «وَدِدت أني وَلَّيْتُ ابنَ الزبير من ذلك ما تَوَلَّى».
The fourth station - the fourteenth aspect: The building of the Kaaba by Ibn al-Zubayr. The aspects, and he did not catapult it at all. But he was besieging Ibn al-Zubayr, and Ibn al-Zubayr and his companions were in the mosque, and they were catapulting at him and his companions, not aiming at the Kaaba. And they did not demolish the Kaaba, nor did any stone from al-Hajjaj's stones fall on it. Rather, Ibn al-Zubayr had built it on the foundations of Abraham, and attached it to the ground, and made for it two doors as 'Aisha informed him from the Prophet that he said: Were it not that your people are recent in pre-Islamic ignorance, I would have demolished the Kaaba and attached it to the ground and made for it two doors - a door for people to enter and a door for them to exit. And he described to 'Aisha from the walled enclosure (al-Hijr) what is of the house, nearly seven cubits, the place of the curve of al-Hijr; and Quraysh had built it, but the expenditure fell short, so they left what they left of al-Hijr and raised it. When Ibn al-Zubayr took charge, he consulted the people about that. Among them were those who saw that as a benefit, and among them were those who advised him not to do it and said: This Kaaba is the one that was in the time of the Prophet, and upon it the people embraced Islam. This was the opinion of Ibn 'Abbas and a group. And Iraq he ruled for twenty years. He was a tyrant, stubborn, audacious in shedding blood on the slightest suspicion. It was said he killed one hundred and twenty thousand. And it was said he was frequent in reciting the Qur'an and gave money to the people of the Qur'an, and avoided forbidden things. He built the city of Wasit between Basra and Kufa, and died there in the year 95 AH at the age of fifty-four. And the jurists differ on this issue: among them are those who see keeping it as it is, like the saying of Ibn 'Abbas, and that is the saying of Malik and others. And it is said that al-Rashid consulted him (Ibn al-Zubayr?) to do as Ibn al-Zubayr did, so Ibn al-Zubayr advised him not to do it, and he saw that this would lead to the violation of the sanctity of the Kaaba due to the disagreement of kings regarding that - this one demolishes it to rebuild it as Ibn al-Zubayr did, and that one sees to return it as it was. And among them are those who see correcting what Ibn al-Zubayr did. And it is said that al-Shafi'i leans toward this. When Ibn al-Zubayr was killed, al-Hajjaj wrote to 'Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan informing him of what Ibn al-Zubayr had done, and informed him that they had found the foundations of Abraham, and that he had shown that to the people of Mecca. So 'Abd al-Malik wrote to him to return it as it was except what he had increased in height, not to change it, and he mentioned that what Ibn al-Zubayr did is not known to have originated from him. Then 'Abd al-Malik was informed by some people of the hadith of 'Aisha, and he said: 'I wish that I had entrusted Ibn al-Zubayr with that which he undertook.'

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative from Ibn Taymiyya's 'Refutation of the Logicians.' It recounts the events surrounding the rebuilding of the Kaaba by 'Abd Allah ibn al-Zubayr and the subsequent juristic disagreement. The author first describes the siege of Ibn al-Zubayr by al-Hajjaj, clarifying that the Kaaba was not directly targeted. Then he explains Ibn al-Zubayr's rebuilding based on what he learned from 'Aisha about the Prophet's wish to restore the Kaaba to the foundations of Abraham with two doors. The building was raised and the Hijr enclosure partially excluded. When Ibn al-Zubayr consulted the people, some supported the rebuilding, but Ibn 'Abbas and others advised against changing it, arguing that the existing structure was the one from the Prophet's time. A digression describes al-Hajjaj's character and rule in Iraq. Then the author notes the ongoing juristic dispute: Malik and others favored keeping the Kaaba as it was, while al-Shafi'i reportedly approved of Ibn al-Zubayr's action. After Ibn al-Zubayr's death, al-Hajjaj, under orders from 'Abd al-Malik, reverted the Kaaba to its previous form, except for the height increase. Later, learning the hadith of 'Aisha, 'Abd al-Malik regretted not leaving the changes.
