Refutation of the Subjective Distinction: Essential vs Accidental Necessary
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' distinction of essential vs. accidental necessary attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn Taymiyya rejects the logicians' distinction between essential and accidental necessary attributes based on the subjectivity of knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowing an attribute directly or through proof says nothing about the attribute itself.

The logicians, following Ibn Sina, claimed that some attributes are known to belong to their subject without a middle term (e.g., animality for animal), while others require a proof (e.g., evenness for a large number). They used this to distinguish essential (dhati) from accidental necessary (aradi lazim). Ibn Taymiyya attacks this foundation in the passage. Let's follow his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Recall the logicians' claim
Identify the claim attributed to Ibn Sina: some attributes (like animality) are known without a middle term, others (like evenness) require one. This is the basis for calling the former essential and the latter accidental necessary.
The logicians say this distinction is in the reality of the attributes, not just in our knowledge.
2
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's first objection
Find where he says: 'This is a matter that goes back to a human's knowledge... not a difference that goes back to existents in themselves.' He distinguishes between our knowledge and the thing itself.
3
Understand the argument from variability of knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya points out that people differ: what one knows directly, another may need proof. For example, some may doubt whether a certain thing is an animal until they learn it. So the same attribute could be 'essential' for one person and 'accidental necessary' for another, which is absurd.
This shows the distinction is not objective.
4
Relate to the theory of linguistic indication
Ibn Taymiyya then ties the logicians' concepts of essence (mahiyya), internal part, and external necessary to the three types of indication: mutabaqa (correspondence), tadammun (inclusion), and iltizam (implication). He argues that these follow the speaker's intention, not external reality.
Thus the distinction is merely linguistic, not real.
5
Conclude the refutation
Since the distinction depends on subjective knowledge and linguistic convention, it has no reality in the things themselves. Therefore, the logicians' division of attributes into essential and accidental necessary is invalid.

Commentary
This is a critical passage from Ibn Taymiyya's *Radd Mantiqiyyin* where he refutes the logicians' distinctions between essential (ذاتي) and accidental necessary (عرضي لازم) attributes, and between essence (ماهية) and existence (وجود). The passage begins by presenting the logicians' claim, attributed to Ibn Sina, that some attributes (like animality for animal) are known directly without a middle term, while others (like evenness for number) may require a middle term. Ibn Taymiyya responds with two objections. First, he argues that this distinction pertains only to human knowledge, not to the things themselves; whether an attribute is known directly or through proof does not change the reality of the attribute. He then explains that the logicians' concepts of essence, internal part, and external necessary are merely based on linguistic denotation (مطابقة, تضمن, التزام) and follow the speaker's intention, not external realities. Second, he points out that people differ in their knowledge; what one person knows directly may require proof for another, so no objective distinction can be made. Next, Ibn Taymiyya addresses the distinction between 'necessary for the essence' and 'necessary for its existence'. He argues that if one imagines an essence stripped of a necessary attribute in the mind, that is merely a mental construct. If that mental essence is taken as the reality, it becomes identical with mental existence; if taken as external reality, it is like assuming an external existence stripped of attributes, which is false. Thus the distinction between essence and existence reduces to the distinction between mental and external existence. Finally, he states that the intended second principle is that the logicians' distinction between essential and accidental necessary has no reality, giving the example of evenness and oddness for number as being similar to essential attributes.

Essence and Existence: A Mental Distinction
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' distinction of essential vs. accidental necessary attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the distinction between essence and existence reduces to the distinction between mental and external existence.

Core Idea
Essence and existence are only distinct in the mind, not in external reality.

Ibn Taymiyya continues his critique by addressing the logicians' distinction between 'necessary for the essence' and 'accidental necessary for its existence'. He reduces this to the difference between mental conception and external reality. Let's trace his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target distinction
The logicians distinguish between attributes that are necessary for the essence (lazim li'l-mahiyya) and attributes that are necessary for its existence (lazim li-wujudiha). They claim one can imagine an essence without the former but not without the latter.
2
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's thought experiment
He gives examples: in the mind, one can conceive a horse with no shadow or a non-black raven. These are mental fictions. If we take this mental image as the reality, what does it mean?
The examples show that mental abstraction is possible, but it does not correspond to external reality.
3
Analyze the two interpretations
If the mental essence is taken as 'the reality of what is in the mind', then it is just mental existence, and there is no difference between essence and existence. If it is taken as 'the essence that is in the external', it would be like saying the external existent is stripped of its necessary attributes, which is false.
In both cases, the distinction collapses.
4
Generalize the conclusion
Ibn Taymiyya concludes: there is no difference between existence and essence except the difference between what is in the mind and what is in the external. Assuming an external essence without its necessary attributes is as absurd as assuming an external existence without them.
Thus the logicians' distinction is invalid.

Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' claim that definitions must exhaust all attributes.

He first clarifies that Muslim scholars define by a distinguishing description (hadd) that includes all individuals and excludes others, without needing to enumerate every attribute. The Prophetic Method: Qiyas al-Awla and Signs
Ibn Taymiyya explains the prophetic method of inference using signs and qiyas al-awla.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish qiyas al-awla from universal syllogism and analogical syllogism, and explain why Ibn Taymiyya considers it the correct method for theological inference.

Core Idea
The prophets infer from God's signs using qiyas al-awla, not universal or analogical syllogism.

In this passage from his Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin, Ibn Taymiyya presents the positive alternative to Greek logic that the prophets used: reliance on signs (ayat) and the a fortiori argument known as qiyas al-awla. Understanding this method is crucial to appreciating Ibn Taymiyya's critique and the Islamic epistemological framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify three types of inference
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya mentions three: inference by signs (ayat), qiyas al-awla, and the rejected universal and analogical syllogisms.
The prophets primarily use signs; if they reason by qiyas, it is only qiyas al-awla.
2
Understand the rejection of universal syllogism
Universal syllogism requires a genus under which all individuals are equal. God has no equal and no genus; thus this method fails for theological inference.

Grasp the structure of qiyas al-awla
If a perfection without defect is found in a creature, it is more worthy of the Creator. If a deficiency is negated from a creature, it is even more negated from God.
This is an a fortiori argument, not a comparison of equals.

Recognize Qur'anic patterns
Ibn Taymiyya points out that the rational demonstrative arguments in the Qur'an follow this method, e.g., proofs of God's Lordship, Oneness, etc.
These are the noblest sciences, perfecting the soul.
5
Connect to the ultimate goal
The perfection of knowledge requires intention and worship of God alone, which includes knowledge, love, and humility through the signs.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Threefold Classification

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The logicians' threefold classification excludes necessary concomitance.

The logicians claim that all reasoning is either syllogism, induction, or analogy. Ibn Taymiyya challenges this, arguing that reasoning by concomitance—where two things necessarily coexist—is a distinct and essential type. Analogy requires a shared attribute and a transfer from an original case; here the relation is direct coexistence.

Ibn Taymiyya calls this fourth type 'ayat' (signs) or reasoning by concomitance. It can be universal-to-universal or particular-to-particular.
The key is the idea of luzum: necessary coexistence.

Commentary

Reasoning from a specific sunrise to a specific day is reasoning from particular to particular, not analogical. He then extends this to navigation: using stars like al-Jady (the Pole Star) and Banat Naʿsh (Ursa Major) to determine the direction of the Kaʿba is reasoning from a particular star to a particular direction due to their fixed relation. This entire argument refutes the logicians' claim of exhaustiveness by presenting clear counterexamples that are not syllogistic, inductive, or analogical in the logicians' sense.

Reasoning by Concomitance: Examples and Distinctions
Ibn Taymiyya illustrates reasoning by concomitance with concrete examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between reasoning by concomitance (mulazama) and analogical reasoning (tamthil) using examples from astronomy and geography.

Core Idea
Concomitance is a necessary relation, unlike analogy which is based on shared attributes.

Ibn Taymiyya provides numerous examples of reasoning by concomitance: from sunrise to daylight, from stars to directions, from rivers to locations. In this lesson, you will examine these examples and understand how they differ from analogy.

Guided Path
1
List the examples given

2
Identify the type of concomitance in each
Determine which examples are universal-to-universal and which are particular-to-particular. Also classify as perpetual (since creation) or temporary.
For example, the Ka'ba's relation to directions is temporary in duration but still necessary.
3
Compare with analogical reasoning
Explain why each example is not analogical. Analogy requires a shared attribute between two separate cases; here the relation is direct coexistence without need for an original case.
In the star-to-direction example, the star itself is directly indicative of direction, not via a similar case.
4
Formulate a general definition
Synthesize the common feature: reasoning by concomitance infers from one thing to another that necessarily co-occurs, without needing a middle term or shared attribute.
The Arabic term 'mulazama' captures this necessary coexistence.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya against theologians (mutakallimun) who reject established truths from philosophy, specifically the spherical shape of the heavens. He begins by labeling this rejection an innovation (bid'a) and states that what is known through observation and sound calculation is sound knowledge, and that the heavens are round, not polyhedral. He condemns those who argue against the philosophers on this point, especially when the rational truth agrees with the Shari'a. Ibn Taymiyya then explains that the misguided methods of the theologians led people to doubt the authenticity of prophetic teachings. He asserts that sound reason never contradicts revelation; rather, they agree. The sphericity of the heavens is a case in point: it is a matter of consensus among the Salaf, including the Companions and Followers, with no known disagreement. He cites three scholars who reported this consensus: Ibn al-Munadi, Ibn Hazm, and Ibn al-Jawzi, and he mentions that numerous reports from the Salaf confirm it, consistent with the Qur'an and Sunnah. He then provides two Qur'anic verses (al-Anbiya' 33 and Ya Seen 40) that mention celestial bodies swimming in an orbit. Ibn Taymiyya then presents a chain of tafsir from Ibn Abbas and Mujahid, explaining the word 'falaq' (orbit) as resembling the whorl of a spindle, and 'yasbahun' (they swim) as rotating like a spindle in its whorl. Mujahid further explains 'husban' as the rotating mechanism of a handmill. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates that the Qur'anic interpretation aligns with the rational and empirical understanding of the heavens' spherical motion, reinforcing his critique against those who reject such knowledge.

He begins by affirming that the rational criterion (mīzān) is present in the Quran and is universal truth, not confined to the logic of the Greeks, though some of it appears there. Perfect Conception Does Not Yield Perfect Knowledge: Ibn Taymiyya on the Ninth Way
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the logicians' Ninth Way about perfect conception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to the logician's claim that perfect conception of cause and effect yields knowledge of their connection.

Core Idea
Perfect conception of the cause does not automatically grant knowledge of its effects.

In the 'Ninth Way,' the logicians claim that when you have a perfect conception (tasawwur tamm) of the essence of the cause and the effect, you necessarily affirm their connection. Ibn Taymiyya exposes the circularity and false implications of this argument. This lesson will walk you through his refutation.

The Lam al-Muwatti'a: Function in Quranic Oaths
Author defines the lam al-muwatti'a and its role in Quranic oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lam al-muwatti'a in a Quranic verse and explain its function in combining the answers of an oath and a condition.

Core Idea
The lam al-muwatti'a is the grammatical bridge that allows one answer to fulfill both an oath and a condition.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya defines a subtle grammatical tool used in the Quran: the lam al-muwatti'a. This lam, attached to conditional particles like 'ma' and 'in', signals that an implied oath precedes the condition, so the answer to the oath simultaneously serves as the answer to the condition. Understanding this concept is key to interpreting many Quranic verses where oaths and conditions intertwine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its literal meaning
The term is 'al-lām al-muwatti'a li-l-qasam' – the lam that prepares for the oath. Literally, 'muwatti'a' means 'preparing' or 'paving the way'. This lam is attached to a conditional particle to indicate an implied oath.
Recognize that this lam is not merely emphatic; it has a specific syntactic role.
2
Understand the relationship: condition, oath, and answer
When a sentence has a preceding condition and an oath, the answer to the oath suffices for both. The lam al-muwatti'a is introduced onto the conditional particle, making the answer serve double duty.
The answer is grammatically the answer to the oath, but semantically answers the condition as well.
3
Analyze the primary Quranic example
The verse from Aal Imran 3:81: 'wa-idh akhadha Allahu mithaqa al-nabiyyina...' – note the phrase 'la-tu'minunna bihi'. The lam in 'la-tu'minunna' is the answer to the oath, and 'ma' in 'ma ataytukum' is the conditional particle with the lam al-muwatti'a.
This verse is the central example; understand how the lam operates here.
4
Examine the analogies provided
Ibn Taymiyya gives several similar verses: al-Baqarah 2:145, al-Ankabut 29:10, al-Isra 17:86, Hud 11:8. Each contains 'la-in' (the lam al-muwatti'a with 'in') and a lam in the answer. Compare them to see the pattern.
These analogies reinforce that the pattern is consistent across the Quran.
5
Review the grammarians' explanation
Al-Mubarrad, al-Zajjaj, and al-Farra' confirm that this lam is a 'lam of verification' entering onto the conditional, just as it enters onto 'in'. They explain that the answer to the oath includes the answer to the condition.
This authoritative support strengthens the definition and prevents misinterpretation.

Commentary
This passage is a critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' distinction between two internal faculties: imagination (takhayyul) and estimation (wahm). He begins by stating how they define each: imagination deals with sensory forms, while estimation perceives non-sensory meanings such as friendship and enmity. He gives the example of a sheep perceiving enmity in a wolf and friendship in a goat. Ibn Taymiyya then points out that they are not using the term 'wahm' in the Arabic sense; rather, it has a different meaning in Arabic. He then shifts to the main critique: these logicians have no proof to deny the existence of angels and jinn, nor to deny that these spirits can take on bodies and be seen. He argues that the soul can see things beyond its normal states, as happens at death or in dreams. He supports this with Quranic verses from Surah Qaf and Surah al-Waqi'ah, which indicate that at death, the veil is removed and the soul sees what it did not see before. Similarly, sleep provides a partial abstraction, allowing the soul to see realities that correspond to wakefulness. He then brings empirical evidence: many people have seen jinn during epilepsy or otherwise, and the claim that epilepsy is only due to humors is false. The entry of jinn into human bodies and their speech is known by necessity and agreed upon by Islamic scholars. Thus, the logicians' denial is baseless. He concludes that if jinn are seen, then angels, who are superior, are even more likely to be seen by the prophets both internally and externally.

Core Idea

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Avicenna's Three Corrupt Principles
Ibn Taymiyya enumerates and attacks three foundational errors in Avicenna's metaphysical system.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three corrupt principles that Ibn Taymiyya attributes to Avicenna, and describe how he uses internal inconsistency to refute the third principle.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya refutes Avicenna not by rejecting reason, but by exposing internal contradictions.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya launches a direct attack on the foundations of Avicenna's theory of emanation. He does not simply dismiss the entire system; instead, he isolates three specific 'corrupt principles' that he considers false and shows that the third principle contradicts Avicenna's own doctrine of the Active Intellect. This is a masterful example of internal critique, a method widely used in Islamic theology (kalam) to test the coherence of philosophical systems.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opening claim
Ibn Taymiyya begins by stating that the discourse (Avicenna's theory) is built on corrupt principles. He also notes that this discourse is not from the ancient philosophers like Aristotle but is specific to Ibn Sina and his followers, and that other philosophers like Ibn Rushd have rejected it.
This sets the stage: Ibn Taymiyya is attacking the novelty, not the entire Peripatetic tradition.
2
Extract the three corrupt principles
Read the Arabic carefully and list the three principles: (1) events have no cause except celestial motion, (2) affirmation of separate intellects and souls, (3) the claim that emanation onto human souls comes from the celestial soul.
The first two are stated as false; the third is shown to be internally contradictory.
3
Analyze the internal contradiction
According to Avicenna's own system, what emanates from the Active Intellect is universal, not particular. Yet the third principle asserts emanation from the celestial soul, which would involve particular knowledge. Ibn Taymiyya asks: how can they say the emanation is from the celestial soul when the Active Intellect is supposed to be the source of all sublunar emanation?
This is a logical inconsistency: the third principle contradicts the established role of the Active Intellect.
4
Reflect on Ibn Taymiyya's method
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya does not bring external evidence; he uses the opponent's own premises to expose a contradiction. This is a powerful form of refutation (ilzam) in Islamic dialectic.
This method is common in kalam debates where the goal is to show that the opponent's position leads to absurdity.

Core Idea

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya continues his assault on the Avicennan cosmology by targeting the epistemic role of celestial souls. This lesson examines a hadith narration about the angels' experience when Allah speaks revelation. The author, Ibn Taymiyya, cites the hadith to ground the epistemological reality of wahy and then expands on the nature of divine decree (qada'), distinguishing between creative and legislative aspects.
