How to Evaluate a Manuscript's Authority using Physical and Collation Evidence
Publisher describes the physical manuscript: script, paper, size, and marginalia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to assess a manuscript's reliability by analyzing its physical description and collation statement.

Core Idea
A manuscript collated with the author's autograph is the gold standard for textual authority.

When studying Ibn Taymiyya's works, you must know which manuscripts are reliable. The publisher of this edition provides a meticulous description of the Asifiyya copy, including script, paper, dimensions, and most importantly, evidence of collation (muqabala) directly with the author's autograph. This lesson equips you with the criteria to judge manuscript authority, a skill essential for any serious student of Islamic intellectual history.

Guided Path

Identify the manuscript's provenance and scribe
Look for who wrote it and where it came from. The text says it was written by a student of Ibn Taymiyya, Mahmud ibn Ahmad al-Shafi'i, whose name appears at the end.
2
Examine the physical characteristics
Note the script type (naskh), ink (black with reddish tint), paper (strong brown), size (large format), pagination (modern Urdu numerals vs. original alphabetic). These details help confirm age and consistency.
3
Check the collation statement (muqabala)
The most important part: the end page states it was collated with the author's autograph in 728 AH, and that the reader read the beginning to the author, who added marginal notes. This establishes a direct link to the author.
4
Assess the marginalia and corrections
Many hands added headings, corrections, and variant readings. Frequent collation marks indicate the manuscript was used and corrected, adding layers of scrutiny.
5
Conclude the manuscript's authority
Based on the collation with the autograph and the scribe being a student, this manuscript is highly reliable. The publisher's own use of it for the edition confirms its value.

Refuting a Fabricated Hadith with Internal Criticism
Refutes a fabricated hadith via external and internal criticism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify internal contradictions in a fabricated hadith that refute its apparent meaning and the philosophical claims built upon it.

Core Idea
Even a fabricated hadith, when examined carefully, can be turned against those who misuse it.

How do you refute a forged hadith if you are not a hadith expert? Ibn Taymiyya shows us a powerful method: use the hadith's own wording against it. In this lesson, we examine his critique of a famous hadith that philosophers used to claim the intellect is the first creation. Ibn Taymiyya first notes that scholars agree it is fabricated, but then he goes further: even if one were to consider the text, its own statements prove that the intellect is not the first creation and is created, not eternal. This is a masterclass in internal criticism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its philosophical claim
Find the hadith quoted by Ibn Taymiyya: 'The first thing God created was the intellect...' This is used by philosophers to argue that the intellect (al-'aql) is the first emanation and thus eternal.
2
Check external criticism: who declared it fabricated?
Ibn Taymiyya lists scholars like ad-Daraqutni, Ibn Hibban, al-Uqayli, and Ibn al-Jawzi who declared the hadith mawdu' (fabricated). Note that ad-Daraqutni even named the fabricator. This shows the hadith is not authentic by the standards of hadith criticism.
External criticism alone is enough to reject it.
3
Analyze the wording for internal contradictions
Ibn Taymiyya examines two phrases. First: 'The first thing God created was the intellect' — he points out that the phrase 'first of its creation' means it was created at a time, not that it is the first of all created things. Second: 'I have not created a creation more honorable than you' — the past tense 'I have not created' implies that other creations existed before it. These internal clues contradict the claim that the intellect is the first creation.
Internal criticism works even if the hadith were authentic.
4
Connect to the philosophical claim and show the refutation
Philosophers use the hadith to claim the intellect is the first emanation (and thus eternal). Ibn Taymiyya shows that the hadith itself says the intellect is created (makhluq) and not first, so it cannot be the eternal first principle. Thus, the philosophical argument collapses using its own proof text.
This is a powerful refutation: turning the opponent's evidence against them.

أول ما خلق والحديث الذي يُروي «أَوَّلَ ما خلق الله العقل [۱۸۲] قال له : أقبل، فأقبل، ثم قال له : أدبر، فأدبر . فقال : وعزتي ما خلقتُ خلقاً أكرمَ علي منك، فبك/ آخُذُ، وبك أعطى، وبك الثواب، وبك العقاب»، هو حديث موضوع باتفاق أهل المعرفة بالحديث، كما ذكر ذلك الدار قطنى (۱) وبيَّن مَنْ وَضعه . وكذلك ذكر ضعفه أبو حاتم بن حِبَّان (۲) ، والعُقَيْلي (۳) ، وابن الجوزي (٤)، وغيرهم. ومع هذا فلفظه : «أوّلُ ما خلق الله العقل قال له فمدلوله أنه خاطبه في أول أوقات خلقه ليس مدلوله أنه أول المخلوقات. وفي تمامه أنه قال: «ما خلقت خلقاً أكرم علي منك ، فدل أنه خلق قبله غيره. وفيه أنه مخلوق.
And the hadith that is narrated: 'The first thing God created was the intellect. He said to it: Come forward, so it came forward. Then He said: Go back, so it went back. Then He said: By My might, I have not created a creation more honorable to Me than you; through you I take, through you I give, through you is reward, through you is punishment' — it is a fabricated hadith by agreement of the people of knowledge of hadith, as ad-Daraqutni mentioned and pointed out who fabricated it. Likewise its weakness was mentioned by Abu Hatim ibn Hibban, al-Uqayli, Ibn al-Jawzi, and others. And along with this, its wording: 'The first thing God created was the intellect, He said to it...' — its implication is that He addressed it at the first time of its creation, not that it is the first of created things. And in its completion, He said: 'I have not created a creation more honorable to Me than you', which indicates that He created other than it before it. And in it is that it is created.

Commentary
This passage is a thorough critique of a famous hadith often used by philosophers. Ibn Taymiyya first declares that the hadith is fabricated (mawdu') according to hadith scholars. He lists several authorities: ad-Daraqutni (who named the fabricator), Abu Hatim ibn Hibban, al-Uqayli, and Ibn al-Jawzi. Then he performs an internal criticism: even if one considered the text, its wording shows that the intellect was addressed 'in the first time of its creation' — meaning it was created at a time, not that it was the first creation. Moreover, the statement 'I have not created a creation more honorable to Me than you' implies that other creations existed before it (since it says 'I have not created' in the past tense, implying prior creations). The hadith itself admits that the intellect is created (makhluq), contradicting the philosophers who claim it is eternal.
