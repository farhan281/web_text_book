Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Greek Ethical Framework
Critique of philosophical ethics and its Muslim followers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the Greek virtue ethics and distinguish his conception of Islamic wisdom from the philosophical one.

Core Idea
Greek virtues stem from soul faculties; Islamic wisdom is knowledge combined with revealed action.

Ibn Taymiyya rejects the claim that Greek philosophical ethics resemble the revealed law. He exposes the Greek framework of temperance, courage, and justice derived from moderating desire and anger, and then criticizes Muslim authors like al-Ghazali for adopting it. This lesson walks through his critique and its implications for defining Islamic ethics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Greek framework
Read the passage and locate where Ibn Taymiyya describes the philosophers' basis: they divide the soul into desire (shahwa) and anger (ghadab), and prescribe moderation to yield temperance, courage, and justice. Note that these three aim to perfect the soul through theoretical knowledge.
The four virtues are temperance, courage, justice, and theoretical knowledge.
2
Pinpoint Ibn Taymiyya's objection
Identify his charge that claiming this framework resembles the Shari'a is 'a great calumny'. Why? Because the philosophers' virtues are based on human reason alone, not revelation.
He argues the foundation is entirely different.
3
Analyze the critique of Muslim followers
Find the names: Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (Mizan al-'Amal), Ikhwan al-Safa, Muhammad ibn Yusuf al-'Amiri. Ibn Taymiyya says they erred by building their ethics on this Greek principle while citing Islamic texts.
He does not name all, but implies their method is flawed.
4
Distinguish Islamic wisdom
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that the 'knowledge' praised in the Qur'an and Sunnah is not theoretical (nazari) but is 'wisdom' as knowledge and action (al-'ilm wa al-'amal). This is the key difference.
Islamic wisdom is inherently practical and tied to revelation.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the philosophers' practical ethics (al-hikma al-'amaliyya) and of Muslim scholars who adopted their framework. Ibn Taymiyya begins by rejecting the claim that the philosophers' ethics (household management, civic governance) resemble the revealed law; he calls it 'great calumny.' He then explains their basis: they divide the soul into desire (shahwa) and anger (ghadab), and prescribe moderation between excess and deficiency, producing temperance ('iffa), courage (shaja'a), and justice ('adl). These, combined with theoretical knowledge, constitute perfection. He then criticizes Muslim authors like al-Ghazali (in Mizan al-'Amal), Ikhwan al-Safa, and al-'Amiri for following this Greek scheme while claiming support from Islamic texts. Their error, he says, is that the 'knowledge' praised in the Qur'an and Sunnah is not theoretical speculation but knowledge combined with righteous action (al-'ilm wa al-'amal). This underscores Ibn Taymiyya's insistence that true Islamic wisdom is practical and revealed, not a product of Greek philosophical categories.

The Misclassification of Quranic Preaching as Rhetoric
Ibn Taymiyya refutes logicians' equation of Quranic preaching with rhetoric.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's argument against equating Quranic wa'z with the logicians' khataba.

Core Idea
Quranic preaching is command and prohibition, not merely well-known premises.

The logicians classified all discourse into five arts, placing preaching under rhetoric (khataba). Ibn Taymiyya shows from the Qur'an itself that wa'z is a divine command calling to action, not a genre of persuasion based on communal acceptance. This lesson dissects his refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' definition of khataba
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states the logicians' definition: khataba is that whose premises are well-known (mashhurat), whether it yields certainty or opinion.
2
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's Quranic evidence for wa'z
He cites verses showing wa'z involves command (amr) and prohibition (nahi), such as 4:66 and 24:17.
Note that wa'z is not limited to persuasion but includes imperative content.
3
Compare the two definitions
Contrast the logicians' formal definition (premises-based) with the Quranic usage (action-oriented). Ibn Taymiyya argues they are categorically different.
4
Explain why the mismatch refutes the classification
Since the logicians' definition does not capture the essence of wa'z, equating them is false. Ibn Taymiyya uses the Quranic text as an authority to override a foreign taxonomy.
This is a typical Islamic critique of Greek classifications.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' claim that Quranic preaching (wa'z) falls under the art of rhetoric (khataba) in their classification. Ibn Taymiyya begins by stating that the logicians define rhetoric as arguments whose premises are widely accepted (mashhurat), whether they yield mere opinion or certainty. He then shows from the Quran that 'wa'z' encompasses command, prohibition, encouragement, and deterrence, citing two verses: one from Surah an-Nisa where the response to being preached is obedience and reward, and one from Surah an-Nur where 'wa'z' is a prohibition. This demonstrates that wa'z is not limited to rhetorical premises. Next, he points to the key verse (Surah an-Nahl: 125) which mentions three methods: wisdom, good preaching, and argumentation. The logicians misrepresent this verse as if it said 'preaching and argumentation' as one category. Ibn Taymiyya explains the verse's true meaning by analyzing three states of human beings regarding truth. First, those who know and act upon the truth—these are invited with wisdom, which is knowledge and action combined. Second, those who know the truth but do not act due to their lower self—these are to be given good preaching. Third, those who do not know the truth and actively oppose it—these are not invited but rather argued with, but only when they first oppose. Note that the verse says 'and argue with them' as a separate command, not as a mode of invitation. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes the distinction: in the verse, 'argue' is a separate verb (jādilhum) after 'ad'u', showing it is not an invitation method but a defensive measure against an aggressor. The difference in wording—'good' for preaching and 'best' for argumentation—is because argumentation involves contention and requires extra care to be conducted in the best possible manner to correct the opponent's resistance. Preaching does not involve such mutual opposition. So as long as a person is receptive to wisdom or good preaching, there is no need for argumentation; argumentation is only used when someone resists the truth. This passage thus delineates the proper Islamic method of da'wa and critiques the Greek-influenced classification.

Three Human States and Their Da'wa Methods
Ibn Taymiyya explains the three human states and corresponding da'wa methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three human states Ibn Taymiyya identifies and match each to its method of da'wa from Surah an-Nahl:125.

Core Idea
People differ in knowledge and action; da'wa must match their state.

Building on verse 16:125, Ibn Taymiyya divides humanity into three categories regarding truth: those who know and act, those who know but don't act, and those who don't know and oppose. Each requires a different approach: wisdom, good preaching, or defensive argumentation. This lesson unpacks the typology.

Guided Path
1
List the three states from the passage
Find where Ibn Taymiyya says 'man has three states' and extract them: (1) knows truth and acts, (2) knows truth but does not act, (3) does not know truth and opposes.
2
Match each state to its method derived from the verse
State 1: invited with wisdom (hikma). State 2: preached with good preaching (maw'iza hasana). State 3: argued with (jidal) only when they oppose.
Note that argumentation is not an invitation method.
3
Explain why wisdom is defined as knowledge plus action
Ibn Taymiyya says 'hikma is knowledge of the truth and acting upon it', linking theory and practice.
This differs from purely speculative wisdom.
4
Identify the reason people need both wisdom and good preaching
Because even those who know truth may have desires that oppose action; hence both methods are needed for the general public.

Ibn Taymiyya's Absurdity Argument Against Universal Knowledge of Particulars
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that universals give knowledge of particulars using an absurdity argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reductio ad absurdum argument against the claim that universals yield knowledge of particulars.

Core Idea
Knowing a universal tells you nothing about any specific particular — an absolute epistemological gap.

Ibn Taymiyya here attacks a cornerstone of the Peripatetic epistemology: that the intellect knows particulars through universal concepts. He shows that this claim is not only false but leads to an absurdity. Let's walk through his logical demolition step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
Locate the statement that particulars are known to the intellects 'in a universal way' (على وجه كلي). This is the claim under attack.
2
Define the universal
Ibn Taymiyya defines the universal as 'the common meaning among particulars' (القدر المشترك بين الجزئيات). Recognize that a universal is an abstraction shared by many individuals.
3
Understand the denial
He asserts that knowledge of the universal 'does not yield knowledge of any particular at all' (لا يفيد العلم بشيء من الجزئيات ألبتة). Grasp the absolute nature of this denial.
He gives examples: knowing 'existence' gives no knowledge of any specific existent.
4
Follow the absurd consequence
He argues: if knowing a universal gave knowledge of all its particulars, then knowing the universal 'thing' (مسمى شىء) would make one know everything, since everything is a particular of 'thing'.
5
Identify the logical form
The argument is a modus tollens: If P (universals give particular knowledge), then Q (knowing 'thing' gives all knowledge). Not Q (absurd). Therefore, not P.
This shows the opponent's claim is false.

Ibn Taymiyya on the Unreliability of Spiritual Reports and Dreams
Ibn Taymiyya argues that error outweighs truth in reports from spirits and dreams, and only prophets can reliably distinguish them.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Untrained imagination and jinn lie more than they tell truth; only prophets are infallible sources.

In this passage from his critique of the logicians, Ibn Taymiyya tackles a fundamental epistemological problem: how can we distinguish true reports from false ones when they come from non-prophetic sources like spirits, dreams, or the imagination? Drawing on hadith and empirical observation, he argues that error is more common than truth in such reports. He then establishes that only prophets, who are infallible, can reliably make that distinction—and thus Muslims must believe what they bring.

Commentary
This passage directly refutes the claim that one can attain knowledge of the unseen without prophetic mediation. Ibn Taymiyya identifies the proponents: Batinis from the Shia (e.g., Brethren of Purity) and Sufis (e.g., Ibn Sab'in, Ibn Arabi), and also Abu Hamid al-Ghazali. Their claim is that through spiritual discipline and purification, one can directly know the realities taught by the prophets without needing the prophets' reports. Ibn Taymiyya declares this a corrupt principle. He then criticizes al-Ghazali specifically, stating that many Muslims condemned him for this view, because it effectively subordinates prophetic knowledge to personal spiritual experience: whatever agrees with one's unveiling is accepted, otherwise it is interpreted away. This undermines faith in the prophets and is ultimately derived from the principles of the philosophers (falasifa).

Key Terms
الباطنية (al-batiniyya): Esotericists who claim hidden inner meanings, including certain Shia and Sufi groups. In this work, it refers to those who reject the apparent meaning of revelation in favor of direct spiritual knowledge.
الرياضة (al-riyada): Spiritual discipline or ascetic practice aimed at purifying the soul, here criticized as a method to attain knowledge independently of prophets.
العقل الفعال (al-'aql al-fa''al): The Active Intellect, a philosophical concept (from Aristotle/Neoplatonism) thought to emanate knowledge to purified souls; Ibn Taymiyya lumps it with other sources of false knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya's Expansion of Philosophical Errors
Author enumerates numerous philosophical errors that no Muslim sect holds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the specific philosophical doctrines that Ibn Taymiyya argues are exclusive to the philosophers and rejected by all Muslim sects, and explain why they are incompatible with Islamic teachings.

Core Idea
The philosophers' errors extend far beyond what al-Ghazali listed, touching the core of Islamic creed.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the claim that the philosophers only made a few mistakes. He expands the list to include doctrines that no Muslim sect, whether orthodox or innovating, would accept. These include radical views on prophethood, angels, and the active intellect. By identifying these errors, you will sharpen your ability to distinguish Islamic orthodoxy from philosophical speculation.

Guided Path

2

Use your knowledge of Islamic creed (e.g., Ash'ari or Maturidi teachings).
4
Assess the rhetorical strategy
Ibn Taymiyya uses an 'expansion probe' – showing that the philosophers' errors are not few but manifold, making their system even more suspect. This strengthens his overall critique of Aristotelian-Avicennan logic and metaphysics.
Notice that he does not just list errors but asserts their rejection by all Muslim sects.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Philosophers on the Celestial Sphere
Author critiques conflicting views on the celestial sphere's nature and relation to God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's two-pronged critique of Aristotelian and Avicennan views on the celestial sphere and identify the contradictions he exposes.

Core Idea
Philosophers cannot agree on the celestial sphere; each view collapses into irrationality.

Ibn Taymiyya, in his critique of logic and philosophy, frequently exposes internal contradictions in the doctrines of the philosophers. Here he turns to their cosmology, specifically the nature of the celestial sphere (al-falak). He distinguishes between two camps—the early Aristotelians and the later Avicennans—and shows that both positions are rationally untenable. This lesson will guide you through his critical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two philosophical camps
Ibn Taymiyya refers to two groups: the early philosophers like Aristotle and his followers, and the later ones like Ibn Sina and his followers. Note how he separates their views.
The first camp includes Aristotle and his predecessors; the second includes Ibn Sina and later Peripatetics.
2
Extract the Aristotelian view
Aristotle holds that the celestial sphere is eternal by itself, having no originator. It moves for assimilation (tashabbuh) to the First Cause, which is not its creator but an object of aspiration. The First Cause is merely a condition, not an efficient cause.
Ibn Taymiyya criticizes this as making the First Cause not a creator but a passive object.
3
Extract the Avicennan view
Ibn Sina holds that the celestial sphere is an eternal possible being (mumkin al-wujud) that is caused by a self-necessitating agent (mujib bi al-dhat). The cause always coexists with its effect in time.
Ibn Taymiyya finds this contradictory: a possible being cannot be eternal; eternal existence is necessary.
4
Analyze the contradictions
Ibn Taymiyya points out that both views contradict reason and the consensus of rational people. The Aristotelian view fails to explain creation; the Avicennan view posits an eternal possible being, which is logically impossible. Moreover, the two camps contradict each other, showing the incoherence of philosophical cosmology.
This exposes the weakness of relying on Greek philosophy for metaphysical truths about the cosmos.

Refuting the Polytheists' False Analogy on Intercession
Author identifies and refutes polytheists' false analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a false analogical argument (tamthil fasid) and refute it by showing the disanalogy between the analogue and the target, using Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the polytheists' shafa'a analogy as a case study.

Core Idea
False analogies about God arise from anthropomorphism and ignoring divine transcendence.

Ibn Taymiyya identifies a specific false analogy used by polytheists to justify seeking intercessors: they compare God to an earthly king who can only be approached through intermediaries. This analogy is tempting but deeply flawed. In this lesson, we will dissect this false analogy and learn how to recognise and refute such logical errors in theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the analogy's source and target
The polytheists take the domain of earthly kings (where intermediaries are necessary) as the source, and apply it to God as the target.
This is a tamthil: the relationship between a subject and a great king is analogized to the relationship between a human and God.
2
Pinpoint the disanalogy
God is not like kings: He is all-powerful, all-knowing, and directly accessible. The need for intermediaries with kings arises from their limitations, which do not apply to God.
The key difference: God has no need for intermediaries; He hears all prayers directly.
3
Locate the Quranic refutation
The verse (Az-Zumar 3) exposes the polytheists' real intention: they worship intermediaries to get closer to God, which is shirk. The Quran negates this concept of intercession.
The verse does not merely reject the analogy; it declares the act itself as shirk.
4
Generalize the critique
Any argument that treats God as analogous to created beings in matters of worship is a false analogy, because it ignores divine transcendence and exclusive right to worship.
This principle can be used to refute similar arguments in contemporary theological discourse.
