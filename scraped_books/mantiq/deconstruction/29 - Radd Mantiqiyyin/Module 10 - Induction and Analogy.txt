Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Qiyas Shumul's Certainty
Author distinguishes between qiyas shumul, tamthil, and istiqra', and critiques the logicians' claim that qiyas shumul yields certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' distinction between qiyas shumul and tamthil, and evaluate the claim that only qiyas shumul yields certainty.

Core Idea
All syllogisms reduce to analogy, undermining the claim that universal syllogisms alone yield certainty.

Logicians classify reasoning into three types: universal-to-particular (qiyas shumul), particular-to-universal (istiqra'), and particular-to-particular (qiyas tamthil). They assert that only qiyas shumul yields certainty (yaqin). Ibn Taymiyya challenges this by arguing that every qiyas shumul ultimately depends on an analogical step, and vice versa. Understanding this critique is crucial for evaluating the epistemic weight of logical and legal reasoning.

3
Identify the error about certainty
Find where Ibn Taymiyya explicitly says the logicians' claim that qiyas shumul yields certainty while tamthil does not is 'خطأ' (an error). Understand that if they are interconvertible, one cannot be certain and the other not.
The error is stated right after the interconvertibility claim.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Certainty Distinction between Qiyas Shumul and Qiyas Tamthil
Ibn Taymiyya argues that all comprehensive syllogism returns to analogy and vice versa, and that their claim of certainty is wrong.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's objection to the logicians' distinction between comprehensive syllogism and analogy, and evaluate whether his critique is valid.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya collapses the logicians' hierarchy: no syllogism is inherently more certain than analogy.

Logicians claim that qiyas shumul (categorical syllogism) yields certainty (yaqin) while qiyas tamthil (analogy) yields only probability. Ibn Taymiyya rejects this, arguing that the two are mutually reducible, so they cannot differ in epistemic value. In this lesson, we will unpack his claim and assess its implications for Islamic methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of reasoning
Define qiyas shumul (comprehensive syllogism: a categorical syllogism that moves from universal to particular) and qiyas tamthil (analogical reasoning: moving from one particular to another via a shared cause or attribute).
In usul al-fiqh, qiyas tamthil is the standard method for extending rulings.
2
Understand the logicians' position
Logicians (e.g., in the Aristotelian tradition) claim that qiyas shumul yields certain knowledge because its premises are universal and necessary, whereas qiyas tamthil yields only probable knowledge because it relies on an attribute that may not be exclusive.
This distinction underpins their critique of juristic analogy as non-scientific.
3
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's claim of mutual reducibility
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that every comprehensive syllogism can be recast as an analogy, and every analogy can be recast as a comprehensive syllogism. For example, the categorical syllogism 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; wine is intoxicant; therefore wine is forbidden' can be turned into an analogy: 'Wine is forbidden because it is intoxicating; other intoxicants share this cause.'
This shows that the logical form is not fundamental; the underlying reasoning is the same.
4
Evaluate the implication for certainty
If the two forms are interchangeable, then any epistemic privilege for one over the other is arbitrary. Therefore, the logicians' claim that only qiyas shumul yields certainty is an error.
Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion directly challenges the epistemological foundations of the logicians' project.
5
Assess the strength of the critique
Is mutual reducibility always possible? Logicians might argue that categorical syllogisms require universal premises, which analogies may lack. However, Ibn Taymiyya's point is that every syllogism presupposes a universal principle (e.g., all intoxicants are forbidden), and that universal is derived from observation or revelation, which is the same process as analogy.
This critique is central to Ibn Taymiyya's defense of juristic analogy as a valid and certain method.

This critique is part of his larger project to deconstruct the logical framework and show that the logicians' 'scientific' method is no more certain than Islamic juristic analogy (qiyas fiqhi).

Sabr wa Taqsim: The Method of Establishing Causality and Refuting Occasionalism
Ibn Taymiyya on sabr wa taqsim and critique of occasionalism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of sabr wa taqsim in establishing causality and critique the occasionalist denial of causal powers using Ibn Taymiyya's arguments.

Core Idea
Causality is proven by eliminating all rival causes, not by mere conjunction.

Ibn Taymiyya explains that experiential knowledge (tajriba) yields certainty about causes only when we perform sabr wa taqsim – systematic testing and division to rule out competing factors. He then contrasts this method with the occasionalist position that denies inherent causal powers, aligning himself with the majority of rational people who affirm natures and powers in things. This lesson unpacks his method and his critique.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem of multiple potential causes
Ibn Taymiyya states that if an effect occurs with two factors (e.g., bread and water both present when satiety occurs), attributing the effect to one rather than the other is arbitrary unless we eliminate the rival. Without elimination, no causal attribution is valid.
This is the key epistemological challenge: how do we know which factor is the true cause?
2
Identify the solution: sabr wa taqsim
The method of sabr wa taqsim (testing and division) systematically negates the competing factor (muzahim). For example, in a controlled experiment, one varies only one factor at a time to see which is followed by the effect. This is the foundation of experience.
Ibn Taymiyya extends this from fiqh (where jurists use it to isolate the ‘illah) to natural sciences and everyday life.
3
Recognize the spectrum of certainty
The certainty of the causal claim depends on the certainty of eliminating rivals. If the elimination is certain (qat‘i), the causal belief is certain; if probable (zanni), the belief is probable. This mirrors the degrees of certainty in legal and empirical reasoning.
This links method to epistemic justification: causation is not all-or-nothing.
4
Contrast with occasionalist denial
Ibn Taymiyya then critiques those who deny causes (nafat al-ta‘lil), like Jahm and Abu al-Hasan al-Ash‘ari, who attribute all conjunctions to God's direct will without any causal power in things. He argues that the majority of rational people affirm that things have inherent powers (quwwa) and natures (tabi‘a) that produce effects, as evidenced by constant conjunctions.
This critique asserts that denying causes leads to arbitrary conjunctions and undermines knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of how experiential knowledge (tajriba) leads to causal judgments, followed by a critical contrast with the occasionalist denial of causality. Ibn Taymiyya first outlines the method: to establish a causal relationship, one must observe constant conjunction (dawaran) and suitability (munasaba), but crucially it requires 'sabr and taqsim' – systematic testing and division to eliminate competing factors (muzahim). Without this, if two potential causes are present, attributing the effect to one rather than the other is arbitrary. He notes that jurists (fuqaha’) use these methods in usul al-fiqh to establish ‘illah (ratio legis) for rulings, but it only works with negation of the rival. The level of certainty in causation depends on whether the elimination of competitors is certain or probable. He then extends this to ordinary knowledge in medicine, grammar, and everyday life, such as knowing that bread satiates or fire heats. After explaining the normative method, Ibn Taymiyya contrasts the view of those who deny causality, like Jahm and Abu al-Hasan al-Ash‘ari (occasionalists), who hold that observed conjunctions are merely due to God’s direct will, not genuine causal powers. Against this, he aligns with the majority of rational people (including Ahl al-Sunna and Mu‘tazila) who affirm that things have causal powers (quwwa) that produce effects, and they also affirm the reality of natures (taba’i‘) like instinct (ghariza).

Tajriba: Experimentation Beyond Voluntary Acts
Author illustrates experimental knowledge with common phenomena like day and night, seasons, and explains them through attraction of similars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the scope of tajriba as applied to involuntary natural phenomena and explain the principle of attraction of similars.

Core Idea
Tajriba encompasses both voluntary and involuntary repeated observations, grounding scientific and everyday knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya expands the concept of experimentation (tajriba) beyond human actions to include natural events observed repeatedly by intellect and senses. He then provides a causal mechanism—the attraction of like and repulsion of opposites—to explain phenomena like seasonal temperature changes. This lesson helps you see how empirical knowledge in Islamic tradition is built on common experience and natural principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tajriba
Find where the author defines tajriba: 'what a person experiences by his intellect and sense, even if it is not within his capabilities.' Note that it includes involuntary events.

These are 'customary propositions' (qadaya 'adiyya).

Extract the causal principle

Equivalence of Analogical and Categorical Syllogism

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Analogical and categorical syllogisms are identical in structure; both rely on a middle term (shared attribute) necessitating the conclusion.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya dismantles the logicians' claim that categorical syllogism (qiyas shumuli) is a distinct and superior form of reasoning to analogical syllogism (qiyas tamthili) used in fiqh. He argues that both are grounded in the same logical structure: a middle term (the shared attribute) that necessitates the judgment. This directly challenges the epistemological hierarchy assumed by the philosophers.

The Universal Applicability of Qiyas
Author refutes the view that syllogism only applies in juridical matters and not in rational sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that qiyas is valid in both rational and legal sciences, and evaluate his rebuttal to the objection that universal propositions suffice without exemplification.

Core Idea
Qiyas is universal: wherever a shared attribute dictates a judgment, reasoning works the same—in law, theology, or any science.

Some later theologians claimed that qiyas (syllogistic reasoning) only applies in legal matters, not in rational theology or philosophy. Universal Proposition Suffices in Reasoning without Example
Ibn Taymiyya argues that a universal proof suffices without an example in both shar'i and rational matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a universal proposition is sufficient for qiyas without needing an original case, and identify the role of the comprehensive description.

Core Idea
A universal proof makes the original case unnecessary in qiyas.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the common assumption that analogical reasoning in Islamic law always requires a specific original case (asl). He argues that when evidence establishes a universal link between a ruling and a comprehensive description, that universal evidence alone is sufficient. This principle is not limited to legal matters but extends to rational ones as well. Understanding this argument sharpens your ability to evaluate the structure of qiyas and the sufficiency of proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Ibn Taymiyya states that a universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya) is sufficient for the intended purpose without needing an example (tamthil). Find where he asserts this in the passage.
The claim is at the beginning: 'القضية الكلية كافية في المقصود من غير احتياج إلى التمثيل'.
2
Understand the condition for sufficiency
The universal proposition is sufficient when evidence (dalil) proves that the ruling (hukm) is attached to a comprehensive description (wasf jami'). This condition eliminates the need for a specific original case.
Ibn Taymiyya says: 'متى قام الدليل على أن الحكم معلّق بالوصف الجامع لم يحتج إلى الأصل'.
3
Recognize the role of instantiation
Although the universal is sufficient, it only exists concretely in particular instances. Specifying the original case helps realize the universal, but it is not a requirement for the proof itself.
The passage notes: 'الكلي لا يوجد إلا معيناً، كان تعيين الأصل مما يُعلم به تحقق هذا الكلي'.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument about the sufficiency of universal propositions in reasoning. Author refutes the logicians' claim that all inference is limited to three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' threefold division of proofs and evaluate its force.

Core Idea
The logicians' threefold division is itself an unproven claim, not a self-evident truth.

Ibn Taymiyya attacks a foundational pillar of Aristotelian logic: that all inference falls into one of three types. By showing that this division lacks evidence, he opens the door to other forms of reasoning, particularly those grounded in revelation and prophetic knowledge. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Certainty comes from material truth, not syllogistic form.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' hierarchy that places formal syllogism above analogy. He argues that when the material content is the same, both forms yield identical knowledge. This lesson unpacks his critique and shows why it matters for Islamic rationality.

Thus the criterion is the matter of knowledge, not the form. He elaborates: if the matter is certain, whether it is cast in the form of analogy or inclusive syllogism, it remains certain. Similarly, the form can be conjunctive (iqtirani) or exceptional (istithna'i) in their terminology, or any other clearer and more concise expression; the meaning is the same. He then gives an example: 'This is a human, and every human is created' – this can be expressed as an inclusive syllogism (all humans are created, this is a human, therefore this is created) or as an analogy (this is a human, and humans are created, so this is created). The point is to undermine the logicians' claim that only their formal syllogism gives certainty, arguing that the material (e.g., the truth of the premises) is what matters.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the logicians' definition of induction (istiqra'). Ibn Taymiyya begins by stating his thesis: induction is not an inference from a particular to a universal. He explains that if induction yields certainty (yaqīn), it must be complete induction (istiqra' tāmm), meaning all individuals have been examined. In such a case, one has observed a property in every individual and then inferred that property belongs to the universal common nature (al-qadr al-mushtarak). This, he argues, is actually an inference from one concomitant (multazim) to another, not from a particular to a universal. He illustrates the principle: if every individual of a universal category has a certain attribute, that attribute becomes a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of that universal. Therefore, the logicians' claim that this is an inference from a particular specific to a general universal is false. He then grounds this in the nature of evidence (dalīl) and indicated (madlūl): for an inference to be valid, the evidence must be necessarily connected (mulzam) to the indicated; otherwise, if the indicated could be absent while the evidence is present, we cannot be certain. Forcing a constant conjunction when it is not necessary leads to a contradiction (jam' bayn al-naqidayn). Thus, all valid inference is based on necessary connection (luzūm). The strength of the indication depends on the strength of the luzūm. He gives the example of created things indicating the Creator: everything created is necessarily connected to its Creator, so it indicates Him along with His attributes. The passage concludes mid-sentence, noting that the necessary concomitant either equals the necessary condition or is broader/narrower.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of analogical reasoning (tamthil) as defined by logicians. Ibn Taymiyya begins by rejecting the simplistic definition that analogy is 'inference from a particular to a particular.' If taken absolutely, this is erroneous. The correct understanding is that analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil) operates via a middle term (had aawsat) consisting of the shared cause ('illa) of the ruling, or the evidence (dalil) of the ruling along with the cause. Only then is it valid as 'qiyas 'illa' (reasoning by cause) or 'qiyas dalala' (reasoning by indication). He then turns to argument by resemblance (qiyas al-shabah), which, if valid, must reduce to one of these two types. The shared element between the original case (asl) and the branch (far') must either be the cause itself or something that necessitates the cause (a proxy or necessary concomitant). If the shared element is neither, then mere resemblance does not guarantee the same ruling, because the cause may be absent in the branch. In such a case, we cannot be certain of the validity of the analogy. However, validity can sometimes be known by the absence of any relevant difference (intifa' al-fariq) between the two cases, even if the exact cause is unknown. This is a more permissive notion in usul al-fiqh. He concludes with an example from the Quran regarding the brothers of Yusuf: they argued that since Yusuf's brother stole (the cup), Yusuf must have stolen before. This is a faulty qiyas because there is no shared cause or evidence; it is mere resemblance. This illustrates the misuse of analogical reasoning.

Commentary

This is a more permissive notion in usul al-fiqh. He concludes with an example from the Quran regarding the brothers of Yusuf: they argued that since Yusuf's brother stole (the cup), Yusuf must have stolen before. This is a faulty qiyas because there is no shared cause or evidence; it is mere resemblance. This illustrates the misuse of analogical reasoning.

Ibn Taymiyya's Reduction of Analogy to Categorical Syllogism
Ibn Taymiyya reduces analogical reasoning to categorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's reduction of analogical reasoning (tamthil) to a categorical syllogism and identify the middle term.

Core Idea
Analogical reasoning is valid only when reduced to a categorical syllogism with a universal middle term.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' treatment of tamthil as a distinct form. He argues that any valid analogy relies on a universal commonality that functions as the middle term in a categorical syllogism. In this passage, he explains how this reduction works and addresses how we know the commonality entails the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of valid analogy
The passage states that valid tamthil requires either eliminating differences (nafy al-fariq) or adducing a commonality (jami'). Focus on the latter as it leads to syllogistic form.
2
Recognize the commonality as a universal
The commonality (jami') is described as a universal that encompasses both the original case (asl) and the new case (far'). This universal serves as the middle term.
In usul al-fiqh, this universal is often called the 'illa (effective cause).
3
Extract the syllogistic structure
The entailment of the ruling for the universal forms the major premise: 'Every universal commonality entails the ruling.' The minor premise is that both cases are instances of that commonality. The conclusion is that both cases have the ruling.
This is a first-figure categorical syllogism (Barbara).
4
Address the objection about knowledge of entailment
Ibn Taymiyya anticipates the question: How do we know the commonality necessitates the ruling? He answers that this is known through the major premise, which is established independently (e.g., by induction or text).
Thus the syllogism is not circular.
5
Distinguish from direct particular-to-particular inference
He notes that arguing from one particular to another directly is only valid if there is a necessary entailment (luzum) between them, by essence or attribute. But this also ultimately relies on a universal relation.
This reinforces that true analogy is always syllogistic at its core.

Critique of Analogical Syllogism: Unsound Methods
Ibn Taymiyya reports Muslim scholars' critique that analogical syllogism does not yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two methods used to establish causation in analogical syllogism and explain why Muslim scholars considered them insufficient for certainty.

Core Idea
The two methods for proving causation in analogical reasoning are inherently conjectural and cannot yield certainty.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya presents the Muslim scholars' objections to analogical syllogism (qiyas al-tamthil). They argue that sharing a common attribute does not guarantee the same ruling unless that attribute is proven to be the cause. The two methods used to prove causation—'tard wa 'aks' and 'sabr wa taqsim'—are examined and found to be deficient. By understanding these critiques, you will see why analogical reasoning is considered non-demonstrative in strict logical terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
The passage criticizes analogical syllogism (qiyas al-tamthil) and the methods used to establish the cause ('illa). Note the opening claim: it does not yield certainty.
2
Understand the core objection
Two things sharing a common attribute does not entail sharing the same ruling unless the attribute is proven to be the cause. This is the fundamental epistemic gap.
The passage says: 'ليس من ضرورة اشتراك أمرين فيما يعمهما... اشتراكهما فيما حكم به على أحدهما'.
3
Analyze the first method: tard wa 'aks
This method claims that the ruling and cause are always together in existence and non-existence. The critique points out that it depends on induction, which is incomplete because the branch itself cannot be tested. Moreover, the cause in the root might be compound, so the common attribute alone is insufficient.
Note the phrase 'يجوز أن تكون علة الحكم في الأصل مركبة'.
4
Analyze the second method: sabr wa taqsim
This method enumerates all possible attributes of the root and eliminates all but one. The critique says it is uncertain because the ruling might be attached to the essence itself, not any attribute, leading to infinite regress if we seek an external cause.
See the concern: 'يجوز أن يكون الحكم ثابتاً في الأصل لذات الأصل لا لخارج'.
5
Synthesize the overall weakness
Both methods rely on assumptions that cannot be verified with certainty. Tard wa 'aks requires complete induction, which is impossible; sabr wa taqsim cannot exclude the possibility that the ruling is intrinsic to the root. Therefore, analogical syllogism does not yield certainty.

Objections brought by Muslim theorists against analogical reasoning. They said: It does not produce certainty. For it is not necessary that from the sharing of two things in something common they share in the ruling made about one of them, unless it is shown that what they share is the cause for that ruling. And everything that indicates it is probabilistic. For the support for that in intellectual matters, according to its proponents, does not go beyond 'correlation in existence and non-existence' (tard wa 'aks) and 'survey and division' (sabr wa taqsim). As for correlation, it means nothing other than the concomitant relationship between the ruling and the cause in existence and non-existence, and it inevitably requires induction. And there is no way to claim it in the branch, for that is not what is sought; thus induction is incomplete. Especially since it is possible that the cause of the ruling in the root is composed of attributes both common and other, and its existence in the attributes is actualized. So if the common attribute exists in the root, the ruling is established due to the completeness of its cause, and when it is absent it is absent due to the deficiency of the cause. And in that case, the existence of the common attribute in the branch does not necessitate the establishment of the ruling, because it is possible that the remaining attributes are absent, or some of them. As for survey and division, its summary goes back to the claim of enumerating the attributes of the root in a specific set and nullifying all except the one retained, and it is also not certain because it is possible that the ruling is established in the root due to the root itself, not due to an external cause; otherwise infinite regress would follow. And if it is established due to an external cause, then it is possible that it is due to something other than what was presented, even if it is not discovered upon investigation. And the matter is not like this in customary matters. For we do not doubt, with sound vision and absence of obstacles, the non-existence of a sea of mercury and a mountain of gold before us while we do not observe them, even if it is limited. So it is possible that it is caused by the whole, or by a part that does not exist in the branch. And the establishment of the ruling with the common attribute in a case, while other attributes accompanying it in the root are absent, does not necessitate its independence in causation, because it is possible that in that case it is caused by another cause, and there is no impossibility. And if there is no cause other than it, it is possible that it is a cause for its specificity, not for its generality.

Critique of Sabr wa Taqsim (Survey and Division)
Critique of the sabr wa taqsim method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why sabr wa taqsim does not yield certainty in identifying the 'illa according to the passage.

Core Idea
Sabr wa taqsim is uncertain because the ruling may be intrinsic to the root or caused by an unknown factor.

The passage now turns to the second method: sabr wa taqsim (survey and division). This method involves listing possible causes and eliminating them until one remains. The scholars argue that even this method is not certain, for several reasons, including the possibility that the ruling is due to the root itself or to an undiscovered cause.

Guided Path
1
Understand the method of sabr wa taqsim
Find how the passage describes it: enumerating attributes of the root in a specific set and nullifying all except the one retained.
Look for 'دعوى حضر أوصاف الأصل في جملة معيَّنة وإبطال كل ما عدا المستبقي'.
2
Identify the first objection
The passage says the ruling might be due to the root itself (li dhat al-asl) rather than an external cause. If so, no external 'illa can be identified.
This would lead to infinite regress if we keep asking for the cause of the cause.
3
Understand the second objection
Even if the ruling is due to an external cause, it might be something other than what we have identified, even if we didn't find it after investigation.
The passage uses an analogy: we are certain of the non-existence of a sea of mercury or a mountain of gold before us, but we cannot be certain we haven't missed a possible cause.
4
Consider the possibility of multiple causes
The passage notes that even if the common attribute is present and other attributes are absent, the ruling might still be due to another cause in that case, or the cause might be specific to that instance.

Physiognomy as a Weak Analogy: Qiyas al-Dalala

By understanding this critique, you will be able to recognize when analogical arguments in Islamic sciences overstep their epistemological bounds.

Indicative Analogy (Qiyas al-Dalala) in Physical Physiognomy
Opponents defend analogy and explain physical physiognomy as indicative analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two-step structure of indicative analogy and distinguish it from causal analogy.

Core Idea
Indicative analogy uses evidence of the cause, not the cause itself.

The opponents present physical physiognomy as a type of analogy where the commonality is evidence (dalil) of the cause. This is called qiyas al-dalala in usul al-fiqh. The inference goes from outer appearance to internal temperament (cause), then to inner character (effect). This lesson breaks down that structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inferences in indicative analogy
The text states: 'inference by the effect of the cause to its existence, then an inference by its existence to its other effect.' Recognize the first step: from effect1 (outer form) to cause (temperament). The second step: from cause to effect2 (inner character).
2

The original (asl) is the lion, where broad chest and courage are both effects of the same temperament. The branch (far') is a human with a broad chest. The shared attribute is the broad chest (effect1), which indicates the cause (temperament).
3
Explain why the commonality is evidence, not the cause
The text explicitly says the commonality between original and branch is 'the evidence of the cause, not the cause itself.' This is what differentiates indicative analogy (qiyas al-dalala) from causal analogy (qiyas al-'illa).
In causal analogy, the common attribute is the cause itself.
4
Compare with causal analogy
In causal analogy, you directly transfer the judgment because the shared attribute is the very cause of the ruling. In indicative analogy, you must first infer the cause from an effect, then apply. This is more indirect but still valid when the cause is established via dawran or taqsim.

Establishing the Cause: Dawran and Taqsim
Opponents defend analogy and explain physical physiognomy as indicative analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how co-presence and co-absence (dawran) and division (taqsim) are used to establish a causal relationship in analogical reasoning.

Core Idea
To know a cause, you need co-presence and co-absence or division.

The passage states that establishing the cause in the original case requires either the method of co-presence and co-absence (dawran) or division (taqsim). These are standard methods in usul al-fiqh to identify the 'illa. This lesson explains and applies these methods.

Guided Path
1
Define dawran (co-presence and co-absence)
Dawran means that the cause and effect are always together (co-presence) and never separate (co-absence). This indicates a causal connection. Example: whenever intoxication is present, prohibition is present; when absent, prohibition is absent.
This is a strong indicator of causation but not absolute proof, as other factors may coincide.
2
Define taqsim (division or elimination)
Taqsim involves listing all possible causes for a judgment and then eliminating each one except the one that meets the conditions of causality. For example, if the cause could be A, B, or C, and evidence shows A and B are not present when the judgment is present, then C is the cause.
3
Apply to the physiognomy example
In physical physiognomy, the temperament (mizaj) is established as the cause of both outer form and inner character. The text says this requires dawran or taqsim. You show that wherever a certain temperament exists, both the outer form and inner character are present; when the temperament is absent, so are those qualities.
Alternatively, you could eliminate other possible causes (e.g., diet, environment) to arrive at temperament.
4
Note the nuance about multiple judgments
If two judgments (e.g., outer form and inner character) share one cause in the original, it does not guarantee that both will transfer to the branch. The branch may have only one of the effects if the cause is not fully present. This highlights the complexity of analogical transfer.

Core Idea
Analogical reasoning is not inferior to universal syllogism—both rely on the same shared attribute.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya directly challenges the logicians' claim that universal syllogism (qiyas shumuli) is superior to analogical reasoning (qiyas tamthili). He uses the theological example of the principle 'the one does not originate except one' to demonstrate that whatever can be proven by universal syllogism can also be proven by analogy, and often more clearly. Understanding his argument helps us appreciate the logical foundations of analogical reasoning in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

The target is the logicians' hierarchy of argument forms.

Extract the analogical example from natural sciences

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Analogy is valid because the Creator is more deserving of every perfection found in creation.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn Taymiyya considers analogical reasoning (tamthil) necessary for establishing universal propositions.

Core Idea
The categorical syllogism depends on tamthil for its universal premises.

Ibn Taymiyya launches a devastating critique: the logicians' own universal premises cannot be justified without analogical reasoning. To claim that all members of a class share a property, one must recognize their similarity, which is precisely the operation of tamthil. Thus the very foundation of the demonstrative syllogism rests on the method the logicians disparage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal requirement
Note that categorical syllogisms require a universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya). Without a universal premise, the syllogism does not yield a necessary conclusion.
2
Find the condition for certainty of universality
Ibn Taymiyya states that certainty about the universality of a universal cannot be affirmed except with certainty about the similarity of its individuals in the common attribute (al-qadr al-mushtarak).
This is the key epistemological move.
3
Recognize the role of tamthil
This certainty about similarity is obtained through qiyas al-tamthil (analogical reasoning). Tamthil is the process of inferring that two or more individuals share an attribute based on observed likeness.
4
Conclude the dependence
Therefore, the categorical syllogism depends on tamthil for its most essential component—the universal premise. The logicians cannot claim that their syllogism is self-sufficient or more certain than analogy.
5
Reflect on the reversal
Ibn Taymiyya reverses the traditional hierarchy: tamthil is not a weak form of reasoning but the foundation for knowing universals. The logicians' disparagement of tamthil undermines their own system.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of the logical syllogism's claim to be necessary for certain knowledge. Ibn Taymiyya begins by acknowledging that a syllogism can produce knowledge if its premises are certain. However, he argues that the knowledge obtained does not require the formal 'logical syllogism' (i.e., the Aristotelian categorical syllogism); it can be obtained without it. He then attacks the certain materials (certain premises) of the logicians, claiming they do not yield knowledge of existing things or the purpose of purifying souls. The crux of his argument is that knowledge of universal propositions cannot be had without analogical reasoning (tamthil). To know a universal is to know that all individuals share a common attribute, which requires tamthil (analogy). Thus the logicians' own demonstrative syllogism depends on tamthil, which they disparage. He then qualifies: both types (categorical and analogical) are beneficial if their universal premises come from an infallible source (prophetic revelation) and are used in theology via the method of 'primary' (awla, meaning a fortiori). Without revelation, these syllogisms are not beneficial in theology or natural sciences as demonstrative; they yield only probable/customary knowledge. Finally, he addresses mathematics: it is true in the mind but has no external referent; it does not correspond to actual external objects. Thus logical demonstration cannot yield knowledge of the external world. The passage ends abruptly in the middle of discussing geometry and astronomy. The commentator should note that Ibn Taymiyya is making an epistemological critique: certain knowledge of existence requires revelation or direct experience, not just logical deduction.

The Dependence of Categorical Syllogism on Analogy
Ibn Taymiyya argues that categorical syllogism is founded on analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that categorical syllogism is epistemologically dependent on analogical reasoning, and evaluate its implications for the certainty of deductive logic.

Core Idea
Categorical syllogism gains certainty only from analogical reasoning with particulars.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the foundational claim of logicians that categorical syllogism (qiyas shumul) yields certain knowledge independently of analogy. He argues that in fact, any certainty we have about external objects comes from analogical reasoning (qiyas tamthil), which logicians dismiss as merely probable. In this lesson, we will unpack his dilemma and its implications for epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Ibn Taymiyya opens by stating that qiyas shumul is based on qiyas tamthil. Locate this claim in the Arabic and note its directness.
2
Understand the dilemma presented
He presents a dilemma: either logicians admit that qiyas tamthil is of the same genus as qiyas shumul, or they claim a difference. Trace both horns and their consequences in the text.
If same genus, then both can be certain or probable, undermining the logicians' distinction. If different, Ibn Taymiyya argues that certainty in external matters only comes through tamthil.
3
Follow the epistemological refutation
He asserts that knowledge of unknown external individuals is achieved by analogy to known ones, and that this is undeniable. This refutes the logicians' claim that categorical syllogism alone yields certainty.
4
Recognize the reversal
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that universals are formed by the intellect from particular examples via analogical reasoning, and that this is a distinctive function of intellect over sensation. The categorical syllogism thus depends on this analogical foundation.
The mind forms universals from particulars, and when particulars fade, errors occur, showing the priority of analogical reasoning.

Commentary

Reasoning by Specific Signs and Qiyas al-Awla
Contrast between reasoning by signs and qiyas al-awla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between reasoning by specific signs (ayat) and qiyas al-awla, and identify their use in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya expands valid reasoning beyond the Aristotelian syllogism to include specific signs and a fortiori analogy.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya critiques the exclusivity of the Aristotelian syllogism by presenting two additional forms of reasoning: reasoning by specific signs based on necessary concomitance, and the Quranic qiyas al-awla, which argues from the lesser to the greater. He shows that these forms are both valid and important in Islamic theology and exegesis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of reasoning
Read the passage and note that Ibn Taymiyya first discusses reasoning by specific signs (al-istidlal bi al-mu'ayyin) and then introduces qiyas al-awla (superior analogy). These are contrasted with the universal syllogism (qiyas) of the logicians.
2
Analyze reasoning by signs
Notice the examples: the Pole Star and the Plough, the sun and the east. These are pairs of specific things that are necessary concomitants (mulazam). The sign implies the signified, sometimes in both directions, sometimes one-way (as with God's signs). Understand that this reasoning does not require a universal middle term.
Ibn Taymiyya also includes the prophecies about the Prophet Muhammad in earlier scriptures as belonging to this category.
3
Understand qiyas al-awla
Extract the principle: if a created being possesses a perfection without defect, the Creator is more deserving of it; if a created being is free from imperfection, the Creator is even more free. This is a fortiori argument used in the Quran, as in Surat ar-Rum:28.
This form is not based on a universal premise but on a comparative intuition.
4
Contrast with Aristotelian qiyas
Ibn Taymiyya says that the object of 'qiyas' (the syllogism) is to establish universal concepts that prevent sharing between God and others. But the signs (ayat) point directly to God's essence without such universalization. Qiyas al-awla, on the other hand, uses tanzih (transcendence) to argue from creation to Creator.
This shows that the Quran uses multiple valid logical forms, not just one.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya on valid forms of reasoning outside the formal syllogism. He begins by describing reasoning from one specific entity to another that is equal in generality, such as between the Pole Star (al-Jady) and the Big Dipper (Banat Na'sh), or between the sun and the east. This is based on necessary concomitance (al-mulazama). He notes that the prophecies about the Prophet Muhammad in earlier scriptures are of this type: they match him precisely, not more general or more specific. Then he distinguishes signs (ayat) that imply the signified without the converse, like the signs of the Creator: their existence necessarily implies His existence, but His existence does not imply any particular sign. These signs point directly to God's essence, not to a universal concept that could be shared; that latter is the domain of 'logical reasoning' (al-qiyas, i.e., Aristotelian syllogism). Finally, he introduces 'qiyas al-awla' (the superior analogy) used in the Quran, which is a form of a fortiori reasoning: if a created being possesses a perfection, the Creator has it more so; if a created being is free from imperfection, the Creator is even more free. He cites the Quranic parable (Rum 28) where God argues that just as slaves cannot be partners with their masters, how can humans associate partners with God?

Key Terms
الآية (al-ayah): A specific sign that indicates something else, often by necessary concomitance (mulazama). [Note: In Ibn Taymiyya, ayah is contrasted with 'qiyas' (syllogism) as a more direct form of indication.]
قياس الأولى (qiyas al-awla): The analogy of the superior (a fortiori argument): what applies to a lesser applies even more to the greater. [Note: Not part of Aristotelian logic; a distinct Islamic argument form used in theology.]
الملازم (al-mulazim): A necessary concomitant; one of two things that necessarily coexist. [Note: Standard term in Islamic logic and theology.]

Ibn Taymiyya on the Equivalence of Syllogism and Analogy
Ibn Taymiyya argues that both syllogism and analogy can yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument for the equivalence of qiyas al-shumul and qiyas al-tamthil, and identify the grounds of his critique against logicians.

Core Idea
Syllogism and analogy are two sides of the same logical coin.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes aim at a central claim of Aristotelian logicians: that only syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) yields certainty, while analogy (qiyas al-tamthil) is merely probable. He argues that both forms can produce certainty or not, and that in reality each reduces to the other. This challenge has profound implications for Islamic legal theory, where analogy is the primary method of extending rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Ibn Taymiyya states that logicians claim only qiyas al-shumul produces certainty, not qiyas al-tamthil. Find this in the Arabic: 'هم يدَّعون أن المفيد لليقين هو قياس الشمول. فأما قياس التمثيل فيزعمون أنه لا يفيد اليقين.'
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's rebuttal
He immediately counters that both types can produce certainty or not: 'ونحن نعلم أن من التمثيل ما يفيد اليقين، ومنه ما لا يفيده كالشمول.' So certainty depends on the case, not the form.
Notice the symmetry he asserts.
3
Analyze the reduction argument
He claims that qiyas al-shumul reduces to qiyas al-tamthil and vice versa: 'قياس الشمول يؤول في الحقيقة إلى قياس التمثيل، كما أن الآخر في الحقيقة يؤول إلى الأول.' The middle term in shumul functions like the 'illa (cause) in tamthil.
This is the core logical equivalence.
4
Understand the historical disagreement
He notes three positions: usuliyyun restrict qiyas to tamthil, logicians to shumul, and jurists/mutakallimun accept both. This shows the definitional dispute over 'qiyas'.
Ibn Taymiyya sides with the third group, affirming both.

How a Known Instance Strengthens the Proof of a Genus

Core Idea

Qiyas al-Tard and Qiyas al-Aks: Analogical Reasoning by Similarity and Difference
Ibn Taymiyya defines two types of analogical reasoning: tard and aks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qiyas al-tard and qiyas al-aks by identifying whether similarity or difference drives the analogical inference.

Core Idea
The intellect's ability to see similarity and difference is the engine of analogical reasoning in Islamic logic.

Ibn Taymiyya grounds analogical reasoning in a basic cognitive operation: recognizing tamathul (similarity) and ikhtilaf (difference). This lesson unpacks his classification of qiyas into positive analogy (tard) and negative analogy (aks), showing how the common aspect (al-qadr al-mushtarak) or its absence determines the judgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cognitive basis
The passage begins by stating that knowledge of similarity and difference is among the greatest characteristics of the intellect. Notice that Ibn Taymiyya roots analogical reasoning in this natural cognitive faculty.
This is a key epistemological claim: analogy is not arbitrary but based on innate human perception.
2
Recognize the process for similar things
When the intellect sees two similar things (e.g., water and water, soil and soil), it knows they are alike and applies a universal judgment to the common aspect (al-qadr al-mushtarak). This is qiyas al-tard: positive analogy, where the ruling is extended due to similarity.
Mentioning the common aspect explicitly makes the reasoning clearer.
3
Recognize the process for different things
When it sees two different things (e.g., water and soil), it distinguishes between them by applying different judgments. This is qiyas al-aks: negative analogy, where difference leads to opposite rulings.
Qiyas al-aks is not simply the inverse of tard; it is a distinct inference based on dissimilarity.
4
Apply the distinction to analogical arguments
In any analogical argument, ask: Are we extending a ruling by similarity (tard) or differentiating by difference (aks)? Identify the common aspect (for tard) or the point of difference (for aks).
This distinction is foundational in usul al-fiqh when evaluating the validity of qiyas.

Qiyas al-'aks is reasoning from a cause to an opposite effect: those who follow the messengers are not punished, unlike the deniers, so the branch receives the opposite ruling. The purpose of 'ibar with the punished is to establish the opposite ruling in the branch. He then cites two Quranic verses: Surah Yusuf 111, which states that the stories of the prophets are a lesson for people of understanding, and Surah Al 'Imran 13, which describes the Battle of Badr as a sign for those of insight. These verses illustrate both types of analogical reasoning.

Refuting the Philosophers' Dream Argument for Prophecy
Ibn Taymiyya presents and critiques the philosophers' argument from dreams to prove prophecy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' analogical argument for prophecy from dreams and identify Ibn Taymiyya's critical response based on the disanalogy between sleep and wakefulness.

Core Idea
The philosophers' dream argument is an analogy that fails because the waking state differs fundamentally from sleep.

Ibn Taymiyya presents the argument of Ibn Sina and others that since knowledge of the unseen occurs in dreams, it is not impossible in wakefulness. This analogy is their main proof for the possibility of prophetic knowledge. However, Ibn Taymiyya will show that the analogy is weak because the soul's detachment in sleep is not the same as prophecy. In this lesson, you will analyze the structure of their argument and understand Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim of the philosophers
Find the statement that since knowledge of the unseen is possible in sleep, it is possible in wakefulness. In the Arabic: 'فوجب أن يكون أيضاً ذلك في اليقظة ممكنة'.
2
Extract the premises of their analogical argument
They use an analogy: if someone fails to know the unseen when awake but succeeds when asleep, it seems absurd, but experience (tajriba) and tawatur show it happens in dreams. So the analogy is: sleep is like wakefulness in this respect.
3
Note the philosophers' qualification
They admit they are not arguing for certainty, only to remove stubbornness: 'ولا نستدل بصحة إحدى الحالين على القطع بالحال الأخرى، ولكن على دفع استبعاد المنكر وحصول الرأي الأخلق الأولى'.
4
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's objection
Ibn Taymiyya points out the sleeper's soul is detached from the body, like death, implying that the state of prophecy is different. He says: 'النائم عن بدنه النائم تتجرَّدُ نفسه عن بدنه نوع تجرُّد، فإن النوم أخو الموت'.
5
Evaluate the critique
Ibn Taymiyya is not accepting the argument; he is showing its weakness by highlighting the dissimilarity between sleep and prophetic revelation. The analogy fails because the soul's condition in sleep (detachment) is not the same as in wakefulness, where the soul is engaged with the body.
