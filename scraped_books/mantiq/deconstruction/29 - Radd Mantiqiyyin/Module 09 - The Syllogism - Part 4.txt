Refuting the Necessity of Two-Premise Syllogism
Author argues that a single premise or complete conception suffices for knowledge, refuting logicians' claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that complete conception or a single premise can replace a two-premise syllogism for certain knowledge.

Core Idea
A complete conception can make the syllogism obsolete.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the foundational claim of logicians that all assent knowledge (tasdiq) requires a two-premise universal syllogism. In this passage, he argues that a single premise, or even a complete conception of a term, can directly yield knowledge of rulings. This is a critical refinement that elevates prophetic teaching above Greek formalism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states the logicians' position: that every sought-after knowledge requires a two-premise universal syllogism (qiyas shumuli).
The passage explicitly refutes 'their statement: The sought-after necessarily requires the syllogism... and it necessarily requires two premises.'
2
Extract the alternative method
Ibn Taymiyya offers two alternatives: a complete conception (tasawwur tamm) of the definiendum, or a single universal premise received from the Messenger.
The alternative does not need a middle term; knowledge is direct.
3
Trace the example of wine
Work through the example: knowing that 'wine' (khamr) means 'intoxicant' (tasawwur tamm) and knowing the single premise 'every intoxicant is forbidden' directly yields the ruling for any intoxicant.
This shows that no two-premise syllogism is needed.
4
Assess the refutation
Conclude that the logicians' necessity claim is false because knowledge can come from a single premise or complete conception. This refutes their broader claim that 'no assent knowledge except through logical syllogism.'
The passage ends by emphasizing that universal propositions from messengers yield knowledge in divine matters.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the logicians' claim that every tasdiq knowledge requires a two-premise universal syllogism (qiyas shumuli). The author, Ibn Taymiyya, begins by asserting that a single premise or even a complete conception can suffice for knowledge. He illustrates with the example of wine: if someone knows the complete meaning of wine as 'intoxicant,' then upon learning that wine is forbidden, he immediately knows that every intoxicant is forbidden. Alternatively, he might initially think 'wine' only applies to grape juice, but upon learning from revelation or prophetic usage that the term includes all intoxicants, the single premise 'every intoxicant is wine' suffices to know the ruling. Ibn Taymiyya then concludes that the logicians' requirement of a two-premise universal syllogism is false, and he refutes their claim that no assent knowledge exists except through logical syllogism. He emphasizes that universal propositions received from the prophets, such as 'every intoxicant is forbidden,' directly provide knowledge in divine matters without needing a formal syllogism.

Knowledge of God Does Not Require Syllogisms
Author argues that knowledge of the Creator and prophethood is not dependent on logical syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyze Ibn Taymiyya's arguments against the necessity of syllogisms for knowledge of God and prophethood.

Core Idea
Theological knowledge is based on signs and immediate knowledge, not on formal syllogisms.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes aim at the logicians' claim that certain knowledge, especially of the Creator and prophethood, must be obtained through categorical syllogisms. He argues that such knowledge is actually attained through clear signs (ayat) and necessary knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ḍarūrī), which require no theoretical syllogism. He further compares universal syllogism unfavorably to analogical reasoning, and asserts that people come to know the truthfulness of informants through many means other than syllogistic deduction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Find the logicians' position that Ibn Taymiyya is refuting: that knowledge of the Maker and prophethood must be established through a formal syllogism with two premises.
The passage begins: 'وإثبات العلم بالصانع والنبوات ليس موقوفاً على شيء من الأقيسة'.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's alternative epistemology
He proposes that knowledge is known by signs indicating a specific thing, and by necessary knowledge that does not require speculation.
Look for 'يعلم بالآيات الدالة على شيء معين' and 'يحصل بالعلم الضروري'.
3
Analyze the wine example
The example shows that if one fully conceives the meaning of 'wine' as every intoxicant, the universal 'every wine is forbidden' is already known without a syllogism.
The passage says 'فلو حصل له هذا التصور الجامع لم يحتج إلى قياس'.
4
Evaluate the strength of his critique
Consider whether his alternative (complete conception) actually replaces the syllogism or just pushes the need for reasoning back one step.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument that knowledge of God and prophethood does not require logical syllogisms. Ibn Taymiyya asserts that such knowledge is attained through clear signs (ayat) that point to a specific thing without any partner, and through necessary knowledge (al-'ilm al-daruri) that does not depend on theoretical reasoning. He compares universal syllogism to analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), claiming they are equally unproductive for gaining knowledge of what is specific to God and the Prophet without additional knowledge. He then discusses how people know the truthfulness of an informant through various means other than syllogism, and that even analogical reasoning can serve as well as universal syllogism. He argues that people know existing things and their states without a universal syllogism, so there is no need for two premises (minor and major). He illustrates with the example of wine: if one knows the universal 'every wine is forbidden,' he may not initially know that all intoxicants are wine; but when he later gains a complete conception of wine as every intoxicant, he already has the knowledge without a syllogism. The conclusion is that the syllogism beneficial for assent is dispensed with by the complete conception of the middle term, and that conception and assent are interchangeable: any conception can be turned into an assent and vice versa.

The Interchangeability of Conception and Assent
Author argues that conception and assent are interchangeable, challenging the logicians' distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's claim that every conception can be made an assent and vice versa, and evaluate its implications for the logicians' distinction.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya collapses the logicians' hard division between conception and assent.

In the latter part of the passage, Ibn Taymiyya makes a striking claim: everything that logicians call 'conception' (tasawwur) can be turned into an 'assent' (tasdiq), and vice versa. This challenges a foundational distinction in classical logic, where conception and assent are seen as two separate cognitive acts. He uses the example of wine to illustrate: a complete conception of wine as 'every intoxicant' already contains the assent that 'every wine is forbidden'.

3

How does the example show that a complete conception (tasawwur tamm) dispenses with the need for a syllogism (tasdiq)? If one fully conceives 'wine' as every intoxicant, the universal 'every wine is forbidden' follows directly.

Some Syllogisms Rest on Intuitive Premises
Introduction of the fifth aspect: syllogisms with intuitive premises and conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism's premises are considered intuitive (badihi) and explain why Ibn Taymiyya argues that some knowledge must be self-evident.

Core Idea
Even rigorous syllogisms depend on intuitive, unproven starting points.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that not all syllogisms are derived from prior reasoning; some are based on premises that are immediately known (badihi). He introduces the fifth aspect: the 'syllogism of inclusion' (qiyas al-shumul), which affirms a ruling for every individual. He then asserts that it is acknowledged that knowledge of some rulings for some individuals must be intuitive. Though the passage is incomplete, it highlights that logical reasoning cannot proceed without self-evident truths.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim
Recognize that Ibn Taymiyya states some syllogisms have premises and conclusion that are intuitive, meaning they require no further proof.
This is the fifth aspect of his critique.
2
Define qiyas al-shumul
Qiyas al-shumul is defined as knowledge that a ruling applies to every individual of a category. It is a universal categorical syllogism.
It affirms a ruling for all individuals.
3
Recognize logical necessity
Ibn Taymiyya points out that it is known and admitted that some knowledge of rulings for some individuals must be intuitive. This prevents an infinite regress of proofs.
Self-evident truths are foundational.
4
Connect to Islamic sciences
Apply this concept to Kalam or Fiqh: identify premises that are considered badihi, such as 'the world is changing' in Kalam or 'certainty is not overridden by doubt' in Fiqh.
These serve as intuitive starting points.

Self-Evident Syllogisms Are Superfluous
Ibn Taymiyya's critique of self-evident syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a categorical syllogism becomes redundant because its premises and conclusion are self-evident, and evaluate Ibn Taymiyya's critique of such syllogisms as wasteful.

Core Idea
If the premises are self-evident and the conclusion is even clearer, the syllogism is pointless.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges logicians by examining syllogisms whose premises and conclusion are self-evident. He argues that such syllogisms are redundant because the conclusion is already obvious. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to assess whether a formal inference adds value or merely wastes time. In this lesson, we will dissect his examples and reasoning to see when a syllogism is truly necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism structure
Extract the two premises and conclusion from Ibn Taymiyya's examples, e.g., 'Every intoxicant is khamr' and 'Every khamr is haram'. Note that these are universal categorical propositions.
This is a standard Barbara syllogism (first figure, AAA mood).
2
Assess self-evidence of premises and conclusion
Determine whether each premise and the conclusion are self-evident (badihiyya). Ibn Taymiyya concedes that the intoxicant example may not be self-evident for all, but assumes it for illustration. For his other examples (e.g., 'Every human is an animal'), he claims both premises and conclusion are self-evident.
Self-evidence depends on the knower's prior knowledge.
3
Apply the redundancy criterion
If the two premises are known in the same way (i.e., both self-evident), then the conclusion is even more obvious without the need for a syllogism. If they are known differently, only the unknown premise needs explanation; the syllogism is still partly redundant.
Ibn Taymiyya: 'إذا أفردنا ذلك في مقدمتين طريق العلم بهما واحد لم نحتج إلى القياس'.
4
Evaluate the practical consequence
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that using such syllogisms leads to 'wasting time, meaningless talk, and wearying the minds'. Consider whether this criticism holds for syllogisms in Islamic sciences like fiqh or kalam.
This is a pragmatic, not merely logical, critique.

Commentary
This passage argues that syllogisms with self-evident premises and conclusion are unnecessary. Ibn Taymiyya defines such a syllogism as 'qiyas al-shumul' (comprehensive syllogism) where the judgment applies to every individual. He then explains the epistemological principle: knowledge must ultimately rest on self-evident premises to avoid circularity and infinite regress. He illustrates with the example: 'Every intoxicant (musakkir) is khamr, and every khamr is haram.' Even if these premises are not self-evident for all, he uses them as an illustration. If both premises are self-evident, the conclusion is even more evident. He then considers cases where the premises have the same epistemological path: e.g., 'Every human is an animal' and 'Every animal is sentient, self-moving by will' are both self-evident, making the conclusion 'Every human is sentient, self-moving by will' clearer without need for a syllogism. Similarly, with substance/attribute examples. He concludes that if the two premises are known in the same way, the syllogism is redundant. If known differently, only the unknown premise needs explanation. Thus, he asserts that the logicians' logic leads to wasting time, meaningless talk, and mental exhaustion. He ends with another example: 'Every messenger is a prophet, every prophet is in Paradise' yields 'Every messenger is in Paradise' which is even more obvious. The overall point is a critique of the need for formal syllogisms when the premises are self-evident.

Self-Evident Premises and the Redundancy of Syllogism
Author argues that self-evident premises make syllogism redundant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism is redundant based on the self-evidence of its premises and conclusion, and explain Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological argument.

Core Idea
Self-evident premises make syllogism superfluous — knowledge is direct.

Ibn Taymiyya confronts the logicians on their own turf: if a syllogism's premises and conclusion are all self-evident, the syllogism adds nothing. He argues that foundational knowledge must end in self-evident truths to avoid circularity or infinite regress. This epistemological claim challenges the necessity of formal syllogism for all knowledge. In this lesson, you will unpack his argument and apply it to distinguish necessary from unnecessary reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundationalist claim
Ibn Taymiyya states that if a conclusion depends on two premises, those premises must ultimately be self-evident, otherwise circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul) follow. Find the sentence in the Arabic that expresses this.
2
Examine the khamr example
He offers the syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is khamr; every khamr is forbidden; therefore every intoxicant is forbidden.' He notes this is not actually self-evident but an illustration. Understand why he says if it were self-evident, the syllogism would be unnecessary.
The example is used to show the principle, even if it doesn't meet the condition in reality.
3
Distinguish unified vs. diverse ways of knowing
He contrasts cases where both premises are known by the same method (self-evidence) with cases where they differ. In the former, the conclusion is even more apparent. In the latter, syllogism is needed for the person who lacks one premise.
4
Evaluate the conclusion: waste of time
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that the logicians' method leads to waste of time, nonsense, and mental fatigue. Consider whether this critique applies to all syllogisms or only those with self-evident premises.
His critique is pragmatic: if the conclusion is already apparent, the syllogism is an unnecessary detour.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of the logicians' claim that syllogism is the only or primary way to knowledge. The author (Ibn Taymiyya) argues that when both premises and the conclusion are self-evident (badihiyya), the syllogism is redundant. He then gives an example: 'Every intoxicant is khamr' and 'Every khamr is forbidden.' Even though this example is not entirely self-evident (since the premises are known by text and consensus), it illustrates the principle. If each individual premise were known by self-evidence, then the conclusion would also be self-evident without need for syllogism. He contrasts this with cases where the way of knowing the premises differs, which does require syllogism. The second example: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Every animal is sensitive and moving by will' – here the conclusion 'Every human is sensitive and moving by will' is actually more apparent than the premises combined. Similarly, with more detailed definitions, the syllogistic form is unnecessary. He concludes that the logicians' method is wasteful. The key point is epistemological: self-evidence is foundational, and syllogism is only needed when premises are not all self-evident to the knower.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' claim that all knowledge requires syllogistic demonstration. 4

Commentary

Equivalence of Categorical and Analogical Syllogisms
Equivalence of categorical and analogical syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument that categorical and analogical syllogisms are equivalent and both can produce certainty.

Core Idea
Categorical and analogical syllogisms are two sides of the same coin: both rely on a shared ruling.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' claim that only the categorical syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) yields certainty. He argues that the analogical syllogism (qiyas al-tamthil) is equally capable of producing certainty, and in fact, the two types are fundamentally equivalent. This is a critical refinement of the classical logical hierarchy, with profound implications for how we assess arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that only qiyas al-shumul (categorical syllogism) produces certainty (yaqin), while qiyas al-tamthil (analogical syllogism) does not. Find this claim in the Arabic: 'هم يدَّعون أن المفيد لليقين هو قياس الشمول'.
2
Note Ibn Taymiyya's counterclaim
Ibn Taymiyya states that some analogies produce certainty, just as some categorical syllogisms do, and some do not. He says: 'ونحن نعلم أن من التمثيل ما يفيد اليقين، إفادة اليقين والظن ومنه ما لا يفيده كالشمول'.
3
Understand the reduction argument
Ibn Taymiyya argues that qiyas al-shumul ultimately reduces to qiyas al-tamthil, and vice versa. The basis is the shared ruling: categorical relies on individuals sharing a general ruling, analogy relies on two things sharing a ruling via a common cause ('illa). The cause in analogy functions like the generality in categorical.
The 'illa' is the effective cause linking the two cases in analogy.
4
Review the scholarly dispute
Ibn Taymiyya notes different positions: most usuliyyun say qiyas is only analogy; most logicians say only categorical; most jurists and theologians accept both. This shows the lack of consensus and further undermines the logicians' exclusivity claim.
5
Draw the conclusion
The outcome (ma'al) of both types is one: they both operate on a shared ruling. Therefore, the categorical syllogism has no epistemological superiority over analogy in terms of certainty.

Interchangeability of Analogy and Syllogism: The Blunt Instrument Case
Ibn Taymiyya illustrates the interchangeability of qiyas forms and discusses objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert a legal analogy (qiyas al-tamthil) into a categorical syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) and identify the premise targeted by the objection known as su'al al-mutalaba.

Core Idea
Analogy and syllogism are interchangeable; the real dispute often lies in the major premise.

Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates that the same argument can be expressed as an analogy or a categorical syllogism. Using the fiqhi example of killing with a blunt instrument, he shows how the common factor can be cast as the middle term. He then analyzes the objections that arise, highlighting that the most fundamental objection, su'al al-mutalaba, targets the major premise—the claim that the shared description always carries the ruling. Understanding this interchangeability equips you to see the logical structure behind legal reasoning and to pinpoint where disputes really lie.

Guided Path
1
Identify the analogy (tamthil) in the passage
Find the statement: 'قتل عمد عدوان محض لمن يكافىء القاتل، فأوجب القود كالقتل بالمحدّد'. This is an analogy: the killing is like sharp-instrument killing because both share the common factor of intentional aggression without mixture.
2
Convert it into a categorical syllogism (shumul)
Rewrite as: 'هذا قتل عمد عدوان محض للمكافىء، وما كان كذلك فهو موجب للقود'. This forms a categorical syllogism with a minor premise (this case has the description) and a major premise (everything with the description has the ruling).
3
Locate the minor premise and major premise
Minor premise: 'هذا قتل عمد عدوان محض للمكافىء' (this killing is purely intentional aggression against an equal). Major premise: 'وما كان كذلك فهو موجب للقود' (every such killing necessitates qisas).
4
Identify the objection (niza') mentioned
The disputant denies the minor premise by saying the killing is not purely intentional ('العمدية لم تتمخض'). This is a dispute in the minor premise, which in the analogy corresponds to denying the description in the branch.
5
Recognize su'al al-mutalaba
The passage explains that su'al al-mutalaba is an objection that targets the major premise: 'لا نسلم أن كل ما كان عمداً محضاً يوجب القصاص'. This denies the link between the description and the ruling, which is the pillar of qiyas.
Ibn Taymiyya says this is the greatest objection to qiyas and its answer is the pillar of qiyas.

The Interchangeability of Categorical and Analogical Reasoning
Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates the interchange of categorical and analogical reasoning with a fiqh example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the common logical structure underlying categorical and analogical reasoning in fiqh examples and explain how Ibn Taymiyya's critique reframes the relationship between the two.

Core Idea
Categorical and analogical reasoning are interchangeable: both rely on a universal common attribute.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the rigid separation between categorical syllogism (qiyas al-tashmil) and analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil). By examining a fiqh example of killing with a blunt instrument, he shows that both forms reduce to the same logical operation: establishing a common attribute as the basis for a ruling. This lesson walks through his argument and its implications for the science of qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Example and the Two Forms
Ibn Taymiyya presents a fiqh case: killing with a blunt instrument. The proponent argues it necessitates retaliation, like killing with a sharp instrument. This is expressed both as a categorical syllogism (qiyas al-tashmil) and as an analogy (qiyas al-tamthil).
Note that the example is purely illustrative; the dispute over the attribute's presence is not the focus.
2
Extract the Common Attribute (al-mushtarak)
The common attribute that makes the analogy work is 'deliberate murder, pure aggression against an equivalent'. This attribute is the basis for the ruling in both forms. In categorical form, it becomes the middle term.
This attribute is called by many names: 'illa, manat, jami', etc.
3
See How the Categorical Form Expresses the Major Premise
In categorical form, the major premise is: 'Whatever is a deliberate murder, pure aggression against an equivalent necessitates retaliation.' The minor premise is: 'This killing is such.' The conclusion follows.
The dispute then reduces to whether the attribute (pure deliberateness) is truly present in the branch.
4
See How the Analogical Form Uses the Same Attribute
In analogical form, the origin (killing with a sharp instrument) has the ruling (retaliation) because it possesses the attribute. The branch (killing with a blunt instrument) shares the attribute, so the ruling applies. The same attribute is the basis.
Thus, both forms require establishing that the common attribute is the cause of the ruling.
5
Recognize the Dispute as Identical: Su'al al-Mutalaba
Whether expressed as a categorical syllogism or an analogy, the core objection is the same: the opponent challenges whether the attribute is truly present or whether it is the cause. This is called 'the question of demand' (su'al al-mutalaba), and its answer is the foundation of qiyas.
The foundation is 'that the common attribute is the basis of the ruling' – the major premise in categorical form, the common attribute in analogy.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion by Ibn Taymiyya on the equivalence of categorical and analogical reasoning. He begins by stating that the syllogism of inclusion (qiyas al-tashmil) can be turned into a syllogism of analogy (qiyas al-tamthil) and vice versa. He provides a fiqh example: the case of killing with a blunt instrument. The proponent argues that such a killing is deliberate aggression against an equivalent person, so it necessitates retaliation, just like killing with a sharp instrument. The common attribute here is 'deliberate murder, pure aggression against an equivalent.' This attribute is called by many names: cause, basis, unifier, common element, etc. If one wishes to express this as a categorical syllogism, the major premise would be: 'Anything that is deliberate murder, pure aggression against an equivalent necessitates retaliation.' The dispute in both forms revolves around whether the attribute of 'pure deliberateness' is truly present. This dispute is about the minor premise (in categorical syllogism) or about the establishment of the attribute in the branch (in analogy). Ibn Taymiyya identifies the 'question of demand' (su'al al-mutalaba) as the most important challenge to qiyas. Its answer is the foundation of qiyas: that the common attribute is the basis of the ruling (the major premise). Denying the attribute in the origin or branch corresponds to denying the major premise. He then notes that if one provides a proof that the attribute causes the ruling in another instance, that is pure causal syllogism (qiyas al-ta'lil al-mahd). He gives the example of intoxicating nabidh being forbidden because it shares the attribute of causing heedlessness, which the text has indicated as the cause of prohibition. Finally, he ties both forms together: in both, establishing the major premise requires a proof that the ruling applies to all individuals by virtue of the common universal. Thus, the universal is the unifier, and the unifier is the universal.

The Terminological Nature of the Qiyas Dispute in Rational Matters
Ibn Taymiyya responds to the view that rational matters require only proof, not analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's diagnosis that the dispute over qiyas in rational matters is terminologically driven, and distinguish between the two methods of unification (ilgha' al-fariq and ibda' al-jami').

Core Idea
The dispute over qiyas in rational matters reduces to terminology, not logic.

Some later theologians claimed that rational matters (al-'aqliyyat) rely solely on direct proof (dalil) and not on analogy (qiyas). Ibn Taymiyya responds that this disagreement with the majority is merely terminological. He unpacks the opponents' reasoning and shows that both rational and legal reasoning involve a common unifier (jami') that links cases, making the distinction between origin and branch relative. The key is understanding how unification works, whether by eliminating differences or presenting the common attribute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opponents' Claim
Locate the passage where Ibn Taymiyya presents the view of later theologians (Abu al-Ma'ali, Abu Hamid, al-Razi, Abu Muhammad). They assert that in rational matters there is no qiyas, only dalil. Note their reasoning: they say there is no need to designate an origin and a branch because the proof covers both equally.
This is the starting point of the critique.
2
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's Initial Characterization
Ibn Taymiyya states that the dispute 'reduces to terminology' (yarja'u ila al-lafz). Understand that he is not denying that a real difference exists but arguing that the core logical operation is the same; the disagreement is only about how to label it.
Terminological disputes often mask underlying agreement.
3
Follow the Rebuttal: Relativity of Origin and Branch
Ibn Taymiyya argues that both rational and legal matters involve a 'general meaning' (ma'na 'amm) called the unifier (jami') and the common (mushtarak). The dispute occurs only over some instances, not all. The labeling of one case as origin (asl) and another as branch (far') is relative – if someone knows the branch's ruling by text but not the origin's, they would reverse the roles.
This shows that the origin-branch distinction is not inherent but depends on knowledge.
4
Examine the Two Methods of Unification
Ibn Taymiyya introduces two ways to establish the commonality between cases: ilgha' al-fariq (eliminating the difference) and ibda' al-jami' (presenting the unifier). He states that these methods occur in rational matters both with certainty (qat'iyyan) and conjecture (zanniyyan).
This parallels the methods used in legal qiyas, showing the same logical structure.
5
Compare with Legal Qiyas
Reflect on how the same methods apply in fiqh: a jurist might use ilgha' al-fariq to argue that no relevant difference exists between two cases, or ibda' al-jami' to explicitly state the common 'illa. Ibn Taymiyya's point is that rational arguments follow the same pattern, so denying qiyas in rational matters is inconsistent.
This strengthens the argument that the dispute is terminological.

Their reasoning is that in rational matters, one does not need to designate an origin and a branch; rather, the proof covers both cases equally. Ibn Taymiyya retorts that both rational and legal matters involve a general meaning (ma'na 'amm) called 'the unifier' and 'the common'. The dispute only occurs over some instances, not all. The labeling of one as origin and the other as branch is relative — if someone knows the ruling of the branch from a text but not the origin, they would reverse the roles. He then explains that unification (jam') can be done either by eliminating the difference or by presenting the unifier, and this applies in rational matters as well, with varying degrees of certainty. The passage concludes with a biographical note on Abu Muhammad (Ibn Qudama), praising his works, especially al-Mughni. This biography serves to identify one of the proponents of the view being critiqued.

Key Terms
العقليات (al-'aqliyyat): Rational matters; issues that are known through reason rather than revelation. [Note: In later Islamic thought, this includes metaphysical and logical issues.]
الدليل (al-dalil): Proof or evidence; in this context, a logical argument that is not necessarily analogical. [Note: Ibn Taymiyya uses it broadly to include both categorical and analogical forms.]
الجامع (al-jami'): The unifier; the common attribute that links origin and branch. [Note: Same as in lesson 1, used as the key concept in both rational and legal qiyas.]
إلغاء الفارق (ilgha' al-fariq): Eliminating the difference; a method of showing that no relevant difference exists between two cases. [Note: Common term in usul al-fiqh and jadal.]
إبداء الجامع (ibda' al-jami'): Presenting the unifier; explicitly stating the common attribute.

So the witnessed is mentioned so that the heart imagines a specific image, then the mind knows the generality of the judgment, namely that perfection and precision necessitate the knowledge of the agent, for a perfect, precise action does not come except from a knowledgeable one. And this is like other things known from general customary matters. If it is said: 'This medicine is a laxative for bile, and a concoctor for such-and-such humor,' etc., then experience only indicates specific things, not the commonality as a universal matter. But the mind knows that the basis is the common attribute by knowing the absence of other factors and its suitability, or without knowing its suitability. So mentioning the original case in rational analogy is to alert the mind to the universal commonality that necessitates the judgment, not because the mere existence of the judgment in one case necessitates its existence in another without there being a commonality that necessitates the judgment.

And if it is said: 'From where is it known that the commonality necessitates the judgment?' It is said: 'From where you learn the major premise in the syllogism of inclusion.' For if someone says: 'This is an agent who perfects his action, and every agent who perfects his action is knowledgeable,' then whatever is mentioned in his knowledge of this universal proposition exists in analogical reasoning, with the addition that there is an original case represented in which the judgment is found along with the commonality, whereas in the syllogism of inclusion, none of the individuals in which the judgment is established are mentioned. And it is known that mentioning the universal commonality with some of its individuals is more established in the mind than mentioning it abstracted from all individuals, by agreement of the rational people.

Therefore they say: The mind, by virtue of its perception of particulars, perceives among them a universal commonality. So universals occur in the soul after knowledge of specific particulars. Thus knowing universals through specific particulars is among the greatest causes for knowing universals. So how could mentioning them weaken the analogy, and not mentioning them strengthen it? And this is a peculiarity of the mind, for the peculiarity of the mind is its knowledge of universals by means of its knowledge of particulars. Whoever denies that denies the peculiarity of the human mind, and whoever considers mentioning them without any of their specific instances stronger than mentioning them with examples of their specific instances is obstinate. And rational people, by agreement, know that giving a universal example helps in knowing it, and that the situation when it is mentioned with an example is not like when it is mentioned abstracted from the example.

And whoever ponders all that people discuss of universals known by reason in medicine, arithmetic, natural sciences, crafts, trades, and other things, will find the matter to be so. For instance: It is said: 'The interior of a person becomes warm in winter and cold in summer, because in winter the air is cold, so the exterior of the body becomes cold, and heat flees to the interior of the body, because an opposite flees from its opposite, and a like is attracted to its like, so coldness appears on the exterior, because the like of a thing is attracted to it.' This is a universal absolute matter. Then if it is said: 'Therefore the interior of the earth becomes warm in winter, and the interior of all animals, and therefore the interiors become cold in summer due to the heat of the exteriors, so coldness flees to the interiors' — then whenever a person imagines the analogues, his knowledge of that universal strengthens, namely that an opposite flees from its opposite and a like is attracted to its like. And this is known in natural things, psychological things, and others. But when the analogues are mentioned, knowledge of that is strengthened. And this is sometimes expressed by saying: 'Likeness is the cause of coming together.'

Similarly, if it is said: 'The sun, when it falls on the sea or other things, heats it, so vapor rises from it, because heat dissolves moisture.' Then it is said: 'Just as vapor rises from a pot that contains hot water' — this example is something that confirms knowledge of the first.

Commentary
This passage is a critical defense of analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil) by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' claim that the categorical syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) is superior because it does not mention particular examples. Ibn Taymiyya argues that mentioning the original case (al-asl) – the particular example from the witnessed realm (al-shahid) – is essential to alert the mind to the universal commonality (al-mushtarak al-kulli) that grounds the judgment. He begins by giving examples from theology: the attributes of Allah are inferred from the witnessed realm using four comprehensive principles (al-jawami' al-arba'): definition, evidence, cause, and condition. The purpose of mentioning the witnessed example is to let the heart imagine a specific image, then reason grasps the universal judgment. He then compares this to empirical generalizations in medicine, such as the laxative effect of scammony, where experience gives specific instances but the mind identifies the common attribute. The objection is raised: how do we know that the commonality necessitates the judgment? Ibn Taymiyya replies that this is learned through the major premise in a categorical syllogism. He points out that whatever is present in the categorical syllogism (the universal proposition) is also present in analogical reasoning, with the addition of an example. And it is a universal agreement among rational people that mentioning the universal together with some of its individuals makes it more firmly established in the mind than mentioning it abstractly. He then explains the nature of the mind: universals are known only after particulars, and mentioning particulars aids in grasping universals. Therefore, it is absurd to claim that mentioning examples weakens the argument. He provides illustrations from natural phenomena: the internal warmth in winter and cold in summer, explained by the principles that opposites flee and likes attract. Providing multiple examples (earth, animals) strengthens the understanding of the universal principle. Similarly, the example of vapor rising from heated water confirms the general principle of heat dissolving moisture. Ibn Taymiyya concludes that analogical reasoning with examples is epistemically stronger, not weaker, than abstract categorical syllogism.

The Revealed Balance vs. Greek Logic
Ibn Taymiyya rejects the identification of the revealed mizan with Greek logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's historical argument that the revealed mizan cannot be identical to Greek logic, and evaluate its force.

Core Idea
The revealed mizan predates Aristotle, so it cannot be Greek logic.

Abu Hamid al-Ghazali, in his work 'al-Qistas al-Mustaqim', equated the five balances (mawazin) of revelation with the five syllogistic forms of Aristotelian logic. Ibn Taymiyya fiercely rejects this identification. He argues that the mizan was sent down with the prophets Noah, Abraham, and Moses—centuries before Aristotle. Therefore, Greek logic is at most a human attempt to codify reasoning, not the divine standard itself.

Reducing Conditional and Disjunctive Syllogisms to Categorical Form
Ibn Taymiyya responds to an objection by showing how conditional and disjunctive syllogisms reduce to categorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert a conditional (shartiyya muttasila) or disjunctive (munfasila) syllogism into a valid categorical (iqtirani) syllogism following Ibn Taymiyya's method.

Core Idea
All syllogisms, conditional and disjunctive, reduce to categorical form.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya responds to an objection that only categorical syllogisms can be reduced to analogical reasoning, not exceptional (istithna'i) syllogisms like conditional and disjunctive. He demonstrates that even these can be transformed into categorical syllogisms. The key is to identify the universal relationship of entailment (talazum) or exhaustive division (taqsim) and reformulate it as a universal categorical premise. With this tool, you can see the underlying unity of all syllogistic reasoning and apply it to Islamic arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional or disjunctive proposition
Find the proposition in the form 'If A then B' (muttasila) or 'Either A or B' (munfasila). In Ibn Taymiyya's example: 'If the prayer is valid, then the worshiper is purified' is a conditional. The disjunctive example is 'This is either even or odd'.
2
Extract the universal relationship
For a conditional, the universal relationship is 'Whenever A occurs, B occurs' — i.e., every individual that is A is B. For the prayer example: 'Every valid prayer has a purified worshiper' or more directly 'Every worshiper is purified' (as Ibn Taymiyya states). For a disjunctive, the universal is 'Everything is either A or B' — i.e., no individual is neither and none is both.
The universal may be known from divine text (shar') or intuition, but it must be true.
3
Formulate the universal as a categorical major premise
Express the universal relationship as a categorical proposition (A, E, I, O). For 'Every worshiper is purified' it is an A-proposition: 'All worshipers are purified' (كل مصل متطهر). For a disjunctive like 'Everything is either even or odd', you might use two categorical premises: 'Everything is either even or odd' is not a standard categorical, but you can treat it as a logical truth. Alternatively, use a hypothetical syllogism reduction.
Ibn Taymiyya shows that conditional 'If prayer valid then purified' becomes 'Every worshiper is purified' by focusing on the entailment relation.
4
Add the minor premise and derive the conclusion
Add a minor premise about the specific case. For the conditional: 'This person is a worshiper' (minor). Then conclude: 'This person is purified' (conclusion). This is a Barbara syllogism. For the disjunctive: 'This number is even' then conclude 'This number is not odd' via exclusion.
The conclusion follows necessarily from the premises, showing the reduction is valid.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange defending the reduction of all syllogisms to categorical form. Ibn Taymiyya presents an objection from an imagined opponent: the inclusive syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) reduces to analogical reasoning (tamthil) only in categorical, not in exceptional (istithna'i) syllogisms. The opponent argues that exceptional syllogisms are composed of conditional or disjunctive propositions, where the conclusion or its contradictory is explicitly mentioned, and the middle term cannot be a common factor as in tamthil. Ibn Taymiyya gives a multifaceted response. First, he notes that correlational and divisive universal propositions can be used analogically or inclusively, by comparing individuals and using the common meaning as the basis for judgment. Second, he asserts the main claim: every syllogism, including conditional and disjunctive, can be transformed into categorical form. He provides concrete examples: the conditional 'If the prayer is valid, then the worshiper is purified' becomes the categorical 'Every worshiper is purified' and the syllogism 'This is a worshiper, every worshiper is purified, so this is purified'. He explains that the middle term in this transformation is the relationship of entailment: affirming the antecedent yields the consequent, denying the consequent yields denial of the antecedent. He then notes that the universal premise (e.g., 'Every worshiper is purified') is known from divine law (shar'), not reason. If it were from reason, it would be analogical. He then treats the disjunctive example: 'This is either even or odd' is an intuitive universal, but its particulars are clearer. The point is that these syllogisms are reducible to categorical form, undermining the objection.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logicians' Epistemic Criteria
Ibn Taymiyya argues logicians have no proof for their claim that only specific proposition types yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the logicians' restriction of certain knowledge and evaluate his use of parity reasoning.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya exposes the logicians' double standard: they reject mutawatirat as proof yet accept sensibles, which are even more restricted.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' claim that only certain proposition types (sensibles, primaries, mutawatirat, mujarrabat, hadsiyyat) can serve as premises for certain proofs. He argues that their own criteria are inconsistent, particularly regarding sensory versus transmitted knowledge. This lesson unpacks his critique and the logical tools he uses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
Locate where Ibn Taymiyya states what the logicians assert about which propositions yield certainty. He quotes them as saying that mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hadsiyyat cannot constitute a proof for others because they are specific to those who have direct experience.
The logicians restrict certainty to syllogistic form and to these specific materials.
2
Find the central inconsistency Ibn Taymiyya highlights
He argues that sensibles (hissiyyat) are even more restricted to individuals than mutawatirat. If someone says 'I saw' that cannot be a proof for others, yet the logicians accept sensibles as a source of certain premises. Mutawatirat, by contrast, are transmitted to countless hearers and are more universally shared.
This is the core of his critical refinement.
3
Examine his use of analogy
Ibn Taymiyya draws an analogy: if mutawatirat are rejected because they are not universally shared, then sensibles should also be rejected for the same reason. He also considers a counter-argument that sensibles are shared in genus, and he replies that mutawatirat and mujarrabat are also shared in genus (e.g., everyone knows that eating causes satiation).
The analogy shows the logicians' criteria are inconsistent.
4
Assess the strength of the critique
Evaluate whether Ibn Taymiyya has successfully shown that the logicians' epistemic restrictions are arbitrary. Consider his reliance on empirical examples and the logical form of his argument (reductio ad absurdum).
This step requires critical thinking beyond the passage.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the logicians' epistemology. Ibn Taymiyya begins by stating that the logicians have no proof (burhan) for their philosophical sciences. He notes that they restricted certainty to syllogistic form and to specific types of propositions: sensibles (hissiyyat), primaries (awwaliyyat), mutawatirat, mujarrabat (experimented), and hadsiyyat (intuitive). He argues that there is no evidence to exclude other types of propositions, as previously indicated. Then he points out a contradiction: the logicians consider only what is commonly shared among humans in sensibles and intellectuals, but they contradict themselves regarding what is shared. He elaborates: humans share in some sensibles like seeing the sun, moon, stars, the genus of clouds and lightning, and hearing thunder. But they do not share in the exact instance of many sensibles—each group sees and hears things others do not. For smell, taste, and touch, there is no identical sharing at all; only similarity in genus. The same applies to knowledge by tawatur, experiment, and intuition: what is mutawatir for one group may not be for another, though they may agree in genus. Ibn Taymiyya then quotes the logicians' claim that mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hadsiyyat cannot constitute a proof (burhan) for others because they are specific to those who have direct experience. He refutes this by analogy: the same applies to sensory knowledge of smells, tastes, and touches. In fact, sharing in mutawatirat is greater because a mutawatir report can be transmitted to countless hearers, whereas sensory perception is limited to the one who senses it. If someone says 'I saw' or 'I heard', how can that be a proof for others? Even if multiple people share the same sensation, it does not extend to those who did not sense it. Thus, if the logicians claim that mutawatir report is specific to sensibles and cannot prove something to an opponent, then sensibles themselves are even more specific and also cannot serve as proof. He then considers a possible counter-argument: that sensibles are shared in genus (e.g., everyone knows colors and tastes in general). He responds that mutawatirat and mujarrabat are also shared in genus, as in the case of everyone knowing that eating and drinking leads to satiation and pleasure, which are universal experimented truths. Thus, the logicians' criteria are inconsistent and fail to exclude mutawatirat and mujarrabat while including sensibles.

Critique of Necessary Properties as Middle Terms
Author critiques the logicians' claim that necessary properties can serve as middle terms in demonstrations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaws in using interdependent necessary attributes as middle terms in demonstrative syllogisms.

Core Idea
Interdependent attributes cannot serve as middle terms without circularity or contradiction.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a cornerstone of Aristotelian demonstration: that necessary properties can be used as middle terms to prove each other. He shows that if all necessary attributes are interdependent, any attempt to use one as evidence for another collapses into circularity or contradiction. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for the validity of logical demonstrations in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Find the claim that some necessary attributes are conditions for others and can serve as middle terms. This is what Ibn Taymiyya is attacking.
The logicians assert a conditional relationship among inseparable attributes.
2
Analyze interdependence
Using the example of human attributes (animality, rationality, risibility), note that none exists without the other. They are mutually dependent (mutalazima).
The passage emphasizes that these attributes are inseparable.
3
Derive the contradiction
If each is a condition for another, then each could serve as a middle term, leading to infinite regress or circularity, and no attribute would be necessary by itself. Ibn Taymiyya says this is a contradiction.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Diagnose petitio principii
The logician's proof assumes a division of necessary attributes into those necessary by themselves and those through a middle. The opponent may not accept this division, so the proof begs the question.
Ibn Taymiyya calls this 'مصادرة' (circular reasoning).
5
Evaluate additional errors
Even if the division is granted, the argument becomes dialectical, not demonstrative, and its premises are not certain. Moreover, if the proof is still erroneous, the division itself is unsound.
These points reinforce the overall refutation.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of a key logical doctrine: that some necessary properties (lawazim) can be conditions for others and that this allows one to serve as a middle term in a demonstrative syllogism. He begins by describing the logicians' position: they claim that all necessary attributes of a thing are interdependent (mutalazima)—for example, the human attributes of animality, rationality, and risibility are such that none can exist without the others. However, they also maintain that among these, some are conditions for others, and that the condition can be used as the middle term to prove the conditioned. Ibn Taymiyya argues that this is impossible: if all are interdependent and each is a condition for another, then every attribute would become a middle term for every other, which would mean nothing is necessary by itself—a contradiction. He then diagnoses the fallacy as petitio principii: the logicians' demonstration assumes a division of necessary attributes into those that are necessary by themselves and those through a middle, but the opponent (presumably non-logicians) does not accept that division. If the logician responds that he is only arguing with those who accept the division, Ibn Taymiyya points out three errors: (1) The argument becomes dialectical, not demonstrative, contrary to its claim. (2) The foundational science of logic cannot rely on dialectical premises, or it ceases to be a science. (3) Even if the division were conceded, the proof is still erroneous, which shows the division itself is unsound. The entire argument is structured as a reductio ad absurdum, demonstrating that the logicians' position leads to self-contradiction.

He begins by asserting that prophets and saints have knowledge from revelation and inspiration that falls outside the logicians' syllogistic framework, which is based on sensory and intellectual data. However, Ibn Taymiyya notes that they provided no proof for this classification, and even their supporters admitted it is not exhaustive. Consequently, it does not follow that anything outside their classification is unknowable. Hence, logic cannot be a fail-safe instrument guaranteeing truth. He accuses most logicians of rejecting what is not derived from their syllogisms, which is sheer ignorance, especially when they reject prophetic reports. He quotes the Quran to illustrate their denial of what they do not comprehend. He draws analogies: an astrologer rejecting medicine, or a doctor rejecting astronomy, showing that ignorance breeds hostility. Finally, he concludes that if the noblest sciences cannot be attained through their method, then the logicians have no proof against what they reject, and their own knowledge is insignificant compared to what they are ignorant of, especially since their method does not lead to salvation or happiness.

Commentary

Ibn Sina distinguishes between sophist (using analogy with necessary) and contentious (using common acceptances). The passage thus refines the standard classification by emphasizing functional definitions over material ones.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of the Philosophical Analogy for Prophethood from Dreams
Ibn Taymiyya presents and refutes the philosophers' analogical proof of prophecy from dreams.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' analogical argument for prophecy from dreams and explain Ibn Taymiyya's refutation based on the divine origin of knowledge in sleep.

Core Idea
Prophecy is a divine gift, not an acquired human power—dreams prove nothing more than God's sovereignty over souls.

The philosophers, including Ibn Sina, argued that since the human soul can gain knowledge of the unseen in dreams, it can also do so in wakefulness, and thus prophecy is an acquired state available to anyone who perfects their soul. Ibn Taymiyya dismantles this argument by showing that knowledge in sleep is not from the soul's power but from divine illumination, and that prophecy is solely a gift from God, not a natural achievement.

This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' argument for prophecy based on dreams. He begins by noting that the philosophers consider prophethood an acquired state, and some even sought to receive scriptures like prophets. The root of this, he says, is that their conception of prophethood is akin to people's dreams. Hence their main proof for prophecy is dreams. He cites the verse on divine communication and a hadith about the believer's dream being speech from the Lord. The implication is that the knowledge gained in sleep is not due to the soul's natural power but is a gift from God, specifically for believers. Ibn Taymiyya's Proof for Angels as Voluntary Movers
Ibn Taymiyya argues from motion that angels must exist as willed movers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's categorical syllogism that proves angels as the agents of celestial motion.

Core Idea
All motion ultimately originates from voluntary motion, and only angels can move the heavens.

Ibn Taymiyya presents a rational argument for the existence of angels as the movers of celestial bodies. He categorizes motion into three types and eliminates natural and constrained motion as ultimate sources, leaving voluntary motion as the origin. Since humans do not move the heavens, he concludes that angels are the voluntary movers. This lesson breaks down his argument into a categorical syllogism and evaluates its logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The conclusion is that angels move the heavens and earth by voluntary motion. Look at the last sentence: the movers are living beings that move voluntarily, and these are the angels.
2
Extract the major premise
The major premise is implicit: 'All motion ultimately originates from voluntary motion.' Ibn Taymiyya establishes this by eliminating natural and constrained motion as primary causes.
3
Extract the minor premise
The minor premise is that there is motion in the heavens (celestial bodies move) and that humans do not cause it. This is stated: 'it is known that humans do not move the air, clouds, and other such bodies.'
4
Formalize the syllogism
Reconstruct the argument as a categorical syllogism: Major: All motion originates from voluntary motion. Minor: Celestial motion exists and is not caused by humans. Conclusion: Celestial motion is caused by voluntary movers, i.e., angels.

This is a first-figure syllogism (Barbara): All M are P (voluntary motion is the origin of all motion); All S are M (celestial motion is motion); All S are P (celestial motion has a voluntary origin). It is valid.
