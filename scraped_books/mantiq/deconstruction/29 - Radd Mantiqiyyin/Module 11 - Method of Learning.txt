The Four Stations of Ibn Taymiyya's Critique
Author outlines the four maqamat: two negative and two positive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given claim from Ibn Taymiyya's discussion into one of the four maqamat (positive/negative, tasawwur/tasdiq).

Core Idea
Four stations structure Ibn Taymiyya's critique: two refuting, two affirming.

Ibn Taymiyya has just described the logicians' fundamental claims. Now he unveils the structure of his own counter-argument. This four-part framework guides the entire remainder of the book. Without it, you will confuse his refutations with his affirmations and lose the thread of the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two negative stations
Read the passage and locate the two negative (salb) maqamat. The first negates that conceptualization (tasawwur) can only be attained by definition. The second negates that assent (tasdiq) can only be attained by syllogism.
Negative here means refuting a claim made by the logicians.
2
Identify the two positive stations
Find the two positive (ijab) maqamat. The third affirms that definition yields knowledge of conceptualizations. The fourth affirms that demonstration (burhan) yields knowledge of assents.
Positive here means establishing a claim, not merely rejecting.
3
Note the actual order in the book
The passage includes a footnote (1) indicating the book's sequence: first negative on definitions, then positive on definitions, then negative on syllogisms, then positive on syllogisms. This order interleaves negation and affirmation.
This order is not simply negative-then-positive; it alternates by topic.
4
Practice classifying statements
Take any claim about definitions or syllogisms that Ibn Taymiyya discusses. Decide: Is he refuting a logician's claim (negative) or establishing his own view (positive)? And does it concern conceptualization (tasawwur) or assent (tasdiq)? This will place the claim in one of the four maqamat.
For example: 'Definitions are unnecessary for all concepts' belongs to the first negative station.

Ijtihad and Ta'wil in Applying Legal Texts
Author explains the need for ijtihad in applying general legal terms to specific instances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why ijtihad is essential for applying general Shari'ah terms to specific cases, and to identify the error of relying solely on linguistic knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowing the law requires more than language — ijtihad connects general texts to specific realities.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya tackles a foundational issue: how do we know which specific individuals, actions, or objects fall under the general terms used in the Quran and Sunnah? He argues that while the terms themselves are known, determining their application to new or ambiguous cases requires ijtihad (effort) and ta'wil (interpretation). This is not a matter of mere linguistic competence, but of scholarly judgment that all Muslims — even those who reject qiyas — must accept.

Guided Path
1
Identify the epistemological problem
The author distinguishes between knowing the meaning of a Shari'ah term (e.g., 'khamr') and knowing whether a specific substance belongs under it. This is the gap between general text and particular instance.
2
Understand the role of ijtihad and ta'wil
When the applicability is hidden, ijtihad is required; this is what 'ta'wil' means here. Ta'wil is not arbitrary but a reasoned effort to determine the intended referent.
The author ties ta'wil to the process of tahqiq al-manat (verifying the legal cause).
3
Examine the examples
Review the list of fiqh cases: intoxicating drinks, gambling, tayammum, purification, limits of the face, etc. In each case, the answer is not self-evident from language alone.
This variety shows that ijtihad is needed across many areas of fiqh.
4
Recognize the error of pure linguistic reliance
The author criticizes the mistaken belief that rejecting qiyas allows one to rely solely on language. He calls this 'a very great error'.
5
Connect to Ibn Abbas's classification
Ibn Abbas's four types of tafsir show that there is a kind of knowledge that only scholars possess — not from language alone but from ijtihad.
This reinforces the necessity of scholarly effort.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological and methodological discussion from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians. It explains the nature of ijtihad and ta'wil in determining how general legal and linguistic terms apply to specific instances. The author begins by clarifying that the knowledge of whether existing individuals fall under the terms used in divine revelation can be either apparent or hidden. When hidden, it requires ijtihad, which is what is meant by 'ta'wil' in the speech of the Lawgiver. This is a domain where jurists and other scholars excel, differing in their ability to recognize that a specific case is the intended referent. Just as a skilled jurist quickly identifies a new incident and subsumes it under the relevant revealed or juristic statement, a physician does the same with diseases and medical texts. The reason is that revealed and scholarly texts are stated in absolute terms, describing things by their attributes and signs, so one must determine which particular exists matches that description.

When the applicability is hidden, some call this process 'tahqiq al-manat' (verification of the legal cause). The Lawgiver has attached rulings to specific descriptions, such as accepting testimony based on justice, treating a wife with kindness, providing maintenance with kindness, and facing the Sacred Mosque in prayer. After the general rule is known, one must examine specific cases to see if they meet that description. This requires a special consideration that cannot be known merely by the name.

Ibn Taymiyya then provides a long list of examples where ijtihad is needed to determine whether a particular type falls under a legal name: for instance, which intoxicating drinks are considered khamr, whether chess and backgammon constitute gambling (maysir), whether certain types of betting or competitions are permissible, whether sand can substitute for earth in tayammum, whether wet clothes or certain body parts are included in purification rulings, and many more. These are all real juristic debates where the answer is not obvious from language alone.

He emphasizes that this kind of ijtihad is unanimously agreed upon among Muslims, whether they accept qiyas (analogical reasoning) or reject it. Some ignorant people mistakenly think that rejecting qiyas means one can simply rely on language to know the law. This is a profound error. To reinforce this, he cites the well-known saying of Ibn Abbas that tafsir (interpretation) is of four types: that which the Arabs know from their speech; that which no one is excused for ignorance; that known only by scholars; and that known only by God. The type known by scholars includes the matters not known by mere language, such as the legal names (al-asma' al-shar'iyya) and the specific individuals and species whose inclusion in the term requires scholarly ijtihad.

The Method of Tajriba: Eliminating Alternatives to Establish Causality
Author explains the method of experimental reasoning through concomitance, suitability, and elimination of alternatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's method of tajriba and determine whether causal knowledge is certain or probable based on the strength of the elimination of alternatives.

Core Idea
Causal knowledge requires eliminating all alternative explanations, not just observing correlation.

Ibn Taymiyya here lays out the epistemological foundation of experimental reasoning (tajriba). He argues that mere observation of a regular correlation between two phenomena is insufficient to establish a causal relationship. Instead, one must systematically eliminate all competing factors using the method of sabr wa taqsim (survey and division). This lesson unpacks the three components of tajriba—dawaran, munasaba, and sabr wa taqsim—and shows how they yield certainty or probability depending on the completeness of elimination.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of tajriba
Find in the passage the three elements the author names: dawaran (concomitance), munasaba (suitability), and sabr wa taqsim (survey and division). Notice that he emphasizes that the foundation of all this is sabr wa taqsim.
Dawaran is the observed regular presence/absence; munasaba is the rational connection; sabr wa taqsim is the elimination of alternatives.
2
Explain the role of sabr wa taqsim
The author says: 'If the effect occurs coupled with two things, its attribution to one rather than the other is not more likely than the opposite.' This illustrates why mere correlation isn't enough. The method of sabr wa taqsim removes the competing factor (muzahim).
Without this elimination, any claim of causality is arbitrary.
3
Distinguish certainty vs. probability in causal knowledge
According to the passage, if the elimination of alternatives is probable, then the belief in causality is probable; if the elimination is certain, the belief is certain. Also requires certainty that there must be a cause and that only one descriptor suits.
The strength of the causal claim depends on the strength of the elimination process.
4
Apply the method to an example from fiqh
The author mentions that jurists use dawaran and munasaba to prove a descriptor is the 'illa of a ruling, but only with sabr wa taqsim. Try applying this to a known fiqh example: the prohibition of khamr (wine) is connected to its intoxicating effect. How would sabr wa taqsim confirm that intoxication is the cause?
You would list possible causes (e.g., color, taste, intoxication) and eliminate those that do not always accompany the prohibition.

Commentary
This passage describes the methodological foundation of experimental reasoning (tajriba) as understood by Ibn Taymiyya. The author explains that true empirical knowledge requires three elements: dawaran (concomitance), munasaba (suitability), and sabr wa taqsim (survey and division). The key point is that mere observation of a regular correlation between two things is insufficient to establish a causal relationship, because there may be multiple factors present. The process of sabr wa taqsim serves to eliminate alternative causes (muzahim). The author then applies this to fiqh: jurists use dawaran and munasaba to identify the effective cause ('illa) of a legal ruling, but this only yields certainty (qat') or probability (zann) depending on the strength of the elimination. He extends the same logic to customary sciences like medicine, grammar, and morphology, giving concrete examples such as eating bread satisfying hunger or wearing padded clothes providing warmth. The passage underscores that causality is not self-evident from simple observation; it requires a rigorous method of excluding competing explanations.

Qiyas al-Awla: The Prophetic Method of Inference
Author presents the prophetic method of reasoning by analogy of the superior (qiyas al-awla).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of qiyas al-awla and distinguish it from universal syllogism and analogical analogy.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-awla infers from creation to Creator by priority, not equality.

The prophets, peace be upon them, used a unique method of reasoning about God that respects His absolute uniqueness. They did not rely on the logicians' universal syllogism or pure analogy, because God has no equal. Instead, they used qiyas al-awla: whatever perfection exists in creation must be affirmed for God in a superior degree, and any imperfection denied of creation is even more necessarily denied of God. This lesson unpacks this prophetic method and its Quranic foundations.

Guided Path
1
Recognize God's absolute uniqueness
The prophets knew that God has no like (laysa ka-mithlihi shay'). Therefore, any reasoning that places God under a universal term alongside His creation is invalid. This is the starting point for qiyas al-awla.
Contrast with qiyas shumul, which assumes equality under a universal.
2
Identify the two directions of inference
Qiyas al-awla works in two directions: (1) For positive attributes of perfection, if a lesser being has a perfection without deficiency, then God has it to a superior degree. (2) For negative attributes of imperfection, if a lesser being is free from an imperfection, God is even more free from it.
3
Contrast with universal syllogism (qiyas shumul)
Universal syllogism assumes that the middle term applies equally to all individuals under a universal. This cannot hold for God, since He is unique. So the logicians' method fails for theological reasoning.
Ibn Taymiyya criticizes qiyas shumul for this reason.
4
Contrast with pure analogical analogy (qiyas tamthil)
Pure analogy (tamthil) posits equality between two cases based on a shared attribute. But God has no analogy in creation; the relationship is one of priority, not equality.
Qiyas al-awla is a distinct type, not reducible to tamthil.
5
Find Quranic examples of qiyas al-awla
The Quran uses this method extensively, e.g., in arguments for God's power and knowledge from the perfection of creation, and in arguments against associating partners with God using the impossibility of two masters.
These are rational demonstrative analogies ('aqliyya burhaniyya) as per the author.

Commentary
This passage is a critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' claim that all valid proofs (adilla) are limited to three types: syllogism (qiyas), induction (istiqra'), and analogy (tamthil). He begins by stating that what the logicians have said is either false or a needless elongation of the path to knowledge, likening it to a man who, when asked where his ear is, raises his hand high above his head and circles it to his left ear, instead of pointing directly. This is a critique of the complexity and inefficiency of the logical method. He then cites the Qur'anic verse (17:9) to emphasize that the straightest path is what Allah sent with His Messenger, implying that the logicians' path is crooked. Next, he directly refutes the tripartite classification: it is baseless and false. He also refutes their claim that knowledge requires exactly two premises, calling it baseless. He then dismantles their attempted proof by division (inference from universal to particular, particular to universal, or particular to particular). He argues that this division is not exhaustive: there is also inference from universal to universal (e.g., from the genus of daytime to the genus of sunrise) and from particular to particular that are directly concomitant (such that one necessitates the other). These, he says, are the 'ayat' (signs) - natural indicators that are not captured by their three types. He provides examples: inferring daytime from sunrise (particular to particular if considering specific instances, universal to universal if considering genera), and inferring the direction of the Kaaba from the stars (a particular inference using specific stars like al-Jadi and the polar star). The examples illustrate that valid inferences exist outside the logicians' framework, especially those based on empirical correlations that are not syllogistic, inductive, or analogical in the technical sense defined by the logicians.

Ibn Taymiyya's Four Refutations of Universal Acceptance
Ibn Taymiyya gives four arguments refuting the logicians' claim of universal acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate Ibn Taymiyya's four arguments against the logicians' claim that their science is universally accepted by rational people.

Core Idea
Universal acceptance is disproven by history, internal dissent, and reason's priority over authority.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes on the logicians' key rhetorical claim: that their science has been refined by rational minds and enjoyed universal acceptance. He does not engage with the technical details of logic here; instead, he dismantles the sociological and epistemological foundations of that claim using history, analogy, and the nature of rational inquiry. Understanding these four responses equips you to challenge any appeal to authority or consensus in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Logicians' Claim
First, locate in the Arabic the claim that logic is a science that has been 'refined by minds' and presumably accepted by all rational people. This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
The claim is implied in the phrase 'هذه علوم قد صقلتها الأذهان'.
2
Analyze the First Response: Historical Counterevidence
Ibn Taymiyya argues that rational people (العقلاء) have always criticized the logicians. He cites both pre-Islamic and Islamic periods, noting that Muslim theoreticians (نظار المسلمين) refuted logical, theological, natural, and even mathematical principles. Why does this historical disagreement undermine the claim of universal acceptance?
This is a factual refutation: the historical record contradicts the claim.
3
Analyze the Second Response: Internal Philosophical Dissent
Ibn Taymiyya points out that philosophers themselves disagree: Aristotle criticized earlier philosophers, Ibn Sina criticized the ancients, and Abu al-Barakat refuted Aristotle. This shows that the claim of universal acceptance is false even within the philosophical tradition.
The second refutation uses the philosophers' own history against them.
4
Analyze the Third Response: Analogy with False Religions
Ibn Taymiyya draws an analogy: idolatry is older and has more adherents than philosophy, yet that does not make it true. Similarly, the altered religions of Jews and Christians are older or contemporary with Aristotle but are false. What logical point does this analogy make about the relationship between popularity/antiquity and truth?
This is an analogical argument showing that age and numbers are not criteria for truth.
5
Analyze the Fourth Response: The Priority of Reason over Authority
Even if the claim of universal acceptance were true, Ibn Taymiyya argues that logic is a rational science, not based on tradition (نقل). Therefore, its validity must be judged by clear reason alone, not by the authority of those who profess it. Why does this final argument completely neutralize the logicians' appeal to consensus?
This is a meta-argument: the very nature of logic makes appeals to authority irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage is a critical and epistemological reflection by Ibn Taymiyya on the nature of reasoning and the diversity of human needs for proof. He begins by critiquing the claim of many theoreticians who insist that a particular method is the only way to reach a conclusion. He acknowledges that the method may be valid, but denies its exclusivity. He argues that God facilitates knowledge of proofs proportional to human need, so the proofs for God's existence and prophethood are numerous and varied. Most people do not need complex arguments; such arguments are only for those who lack simpler alternatives or turn away from them. Then he observes that some individuals, due to habit or a desire for distinction, prefer subtle, lengthy, and multi-premised arguments. For them, using complex logical or theological methods is appropriate, not because the truth depends on those methods, but because it suits their training. He further explains that engaging in subtle sciences trains the mind, analogous to practicing archery or horsemanship, which strengthens skills even outside combat. This training is a praiseworthy goal, and many Sunni scholars have engaged in mathematical and complex legal sciences (like algebra, inheritance calculations) to sharpen their intellects. He concludes by distinguishing three types of 'riyada' (training): physical, moral, and intellectual. The intellectual training is the subject of the 'mathematical' (riyadi) sciences. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya defends the validity of simpler arguments for most people while acknowledging the benefit of complex reasoning for certain individuals, but he firmly rejects the logicians' claim that their methods are universally necessary.

Critique of Unreliable Reports and False Analogies
Critique of a historical report and its false analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn Taymiyya's method of critiquing historical reports by assessing narrator reliability and exposing false analogies.

Core Idea
A weak narrator cannot be trusted even with a chain, and comparing the unknown to the known is fallacious.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya attacks a historical report claiming that a solar eclipse coincided with the death of the Prophet's son Ibrahim. He first dismisses the report due to the unreliability of its transmitter, al-Waqidi. He then dissects a false analogy that compares the certainty of a lunar crescent sighting to the claim of an eclipse. This lesson will train you to identify such critical moves in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the report being critiqued

2
Assess the transmitter's reliability
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that al-Waqidi is 'not a hujja (reliable authority) by consensus' even when he provides a chain (isnad). How much less trustworthy is a mursal (disconnected) report? This is a standard hadith criticism move.
The key term is 'hujja' – a proof or reliable narrator.
3
Extract the analogy being criticized
The opponent compares saying 'the eclipse occurred on the tenth' to saying 'the crescent appeared on the twentieth.' Identify the basis of the analogy: both are claims about astronomical events on specific days.
The analogy attempts to equate two types of knowledge.
4
Analyze why the analogy is false
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes between 'apparent custom' (ʿāda ẓāhira) known to all – like the crescent on a particular date – and specialized knowledge requiring investigation of causes and celestial motions. The eclipse claim belongs to the latter; thus the analogy collapses.
The fallacy is false analogy: treating unequal cases as equal.
5
Synthesize the critical method
Summarize: Ibn Taymiyya first undermines the source (narrator criticism), then deconstructs the logical structure (analogy critique). This two-pronged approach is common in Islamic critical tradition.
Use this method when evaluating any contested claim.

The Fourth Station - The Second Aspect: The revealed balance is justice.

And the balance: many commentators say it is justice. And some say it is that by which matters are weighed, and that by which justice is known. And similarly they said concerning His statement: {And the heaven He raised and He set the balance} [Surat al-Rahman: 7]: the parables and intellectual analogies that unite similars and separate dissimilarities. And when the word 'the Book' is mentioned absolutely, as in His statement: {And He revealed with them the Book with truth to judge between people concerning that in which they differed} [Surat al-Baqara: 213], the balance is included in it, because God has explained in His Book from parables and intellectual measures that by which truth and falsehood are known. And this is like the word 'wisdom', sometimes coupled with 'the Book' as in His statement: {And God revealed to you the Book and wisdom} [Surat al-Nisa: 113]; and sometimes the Book is mentioned alone, as in His statement: {Praise be to God who has revealed to His servant the Book} [Surat al-Kahf: 1], and when it is mentioned alone, wisdom enters into its meaning. And similarly for the word 'Quran' and 'faith'. He said: {And thus We revealed to you a spirit from Our command. You did not know what the Book is nor faith} [Surat al-Shura: 52]. And when the word 'Quran' is mentioned alone, it indicates faith, just as faith indicates the Quran; they are concomitant, and we will discuss this, God willing.

Commentary
This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Radd Mantiqiyyin defines the concept of al-mizan (the balance) as revealed by God, interpreting it primarily as justice ('adl). He begins by citing the commentators: many say al-mizan is justice itself, while some say it is the tool by which justice is known. He then links this to the verse about the heavens and the balance, explaining that the balance includes parables and intellectual analogies that distinguish between similar and different things. Crucially, Ibn Taymiyya argues that when the term 'al-kitab' (the Book) is mentioned alone, it encompasses al-mizan, because the Book contains all that is needed to distinguish truth from falsehood. He draws a parallel with the term 'al-hikma' (wisdom), which sometimes appears alongside 'al-kitab' and at other times is included within it when 'al-kitab' is mentioned alone. Finally, he extends this principle to 'al-quran' and 'al-iman': when one is mentioned alone, it includes the other. This shows that the revealed sources are comprehensive and self-sufficient, containing within them the means to judge truth and falsehood, without need for external man-made logical systems.

Sound Revelation and Sound Reason Cannot Conflict
Ibn Taymiyya states that when text and qiyas appear to conflict, one must be corrupt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible sources of corruption when naql and aql appear to conflict.

Core Idea
When revelation and reason seem to clash, check the sources before jumping to conclusions.

Ibn Taymiyya presents a foundational principle: there is no genuine conflict between a sound revealed text and a sound rational argument. If a conflict seems to exist, it must be because one of the proofs is defective. This lesson unpacks that principle and teaches you how to diagnose the corruption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of proof
Recognize the two types of evidence: naqli (transmitted) proofs, like Quran and hadith, and aqli (rational) proofs, like qiyas or logical reasoning. Both have conditions for soundness.
The passage mentions 'dalil naqli sahih' and 'dalil aqli sahih'.
2
Understand the core claim
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that a sound naqli proof can never contradict a sound aqli proof. This is an absolute principle; apparent contradictions are illusions.
3
Diagnose apparent conflicts
When a text and a qiyas seem to conflict, examine each. The text may be inauthentic (not reliably from the infallible) or misinterpreted (does not indicate the claimed meaning). The qiyas may have corrupted premises or use ambiguous/equivocal expressions (alfaz mujmala mushabbaha).
The passage lists two types of corruption for text: lack of authenticity or lack of indication. For qiyas: corruption of some or all premises.
4
Apply to a real example
Consider the apparent conflict between the Quranic prohibition of riba (usury) and the hadith allowing bay' al-salam (deferred payment). Check authenticity of the hadith, its interpretation, and the qiyas that might equate salam with riba.
This step uses a fiqh example to illustrate the diagnostic method.

Commentary
This passage establishes a foundational principle in Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians: there is no genuine conflict between a sound revealed text and a sound intellectual analogy. He states that it is impossible for a valid transmitted proof (dalil naqli sahih) to contradict a valid rational proof (dalil aqli sahih). Similarly, a legal qiyas that meets all conditions of validity cannot contradict any revealed text. If a contradiction appears, it is because one of the two proofs is defective. This argument positions Ibn Taymiyya against the claim that Greek logic is necessary for resolving such conflicts; rather, the problem lies in the quality of the proofs themselves.

The Fourth Station - the Seventh Aspect: The intellectual balance is the innate knowledge of similarity and difference. And the balance is a just balance that involves considering a thing by its like and its opposite, so it makes equal between similar things and distinguishes between different ones by what God has placed in the fitrah (innate nature) of His servants and their minds of the knowledge of similarity and difference.
If it is said: If this is known by reason, then how did God make it part of what the messengers were sent with? It is said: Because the messengers presented for the people intellectual parables by which they know similarity and difference. For the messengers guided the people and directed them to what they know justice by, and they know the correct intellectual analogies by which religious objectives are inferred. So the prophetic sciences are not confined to mere report, as those among the theologians who think that, and make what is known by reason a counterpart to the prophetic sciences. Rather, the messengers—peace be upon them—explained the intellectual sciences by which people's religion is perfected, both knowledge and action, and they presented parables. Thus the fitrah was perfected by what they alerted it to and guided it to, from what the fitrah was neglectful of, or the fitrah had become corrupted by what had come to it of corrupt opinions and desires. So they removed that corruption and clarified what the fitrah was neglectful of, until there came about for the fitrah knowledge of the balance which God sent down and His messengers explained.
The Quran and Hadith are full of this. God clarifies realities by intellectual analogies and presented parables, and clarifies the method of making equal between similar things and distinguishing between different ones, and criticizes those who deviate from that. Like His saying: 'Or do those who commit evil deeds think that We will make them like those who believe and do righteous deeds, equal in their life and their death? Evil is that which they judge.' And His saying: 'Will We treat the Muslims like the criminals? What is the matter with you? How do you judge?' That is, this is an unjust judgment, not just, because it involves making equal between different things. And He said: 'Or shall We make those who believe and do righteous deeds like the corrupters on earth? Or shall We make the pious like the wicked?' And from making equal between similar things is His saying: 'Are your disbelievers better than those [former] ones? Or have you an immunity in the scriptures?' And His saying: 'Or do you think that you will enter Paradise while there has not yet come to you the like of those who passed away before you? They were touched by poverty and hardship and were shaken.'
The Quran is full of that, but this is not its place. Rather, the purpose is to draw attention to the genus of the intellectual balance, and that it is true as God mentioned in His Book, and it is not specific to the logic of the Greeks, although there is a portion of it therein. Rather, it is the correct analogies that involve making equal between similar things and distinguishing between different ones, whether formulated in the form of a syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) or in the form of a simile (qiyas al-tamthil). The forms of simile are the origin and are more complete. And the balance is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), which is the unifier (al-jami'), and it is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat).
The sending down of the balance with the messengers is like the sending down of faith—which is the trust—with the messengers. Faith was not obtained except through them, as He said: 'And thus We have revealed to you a spirit of Our command. You did not know what the Book is nor [what] faith, but We have made it a light by which We guide whom We will of Our servants. And indeed, you guide to a straight path.' And in the two Sahihs from Hudhayfah ibn al-Yaman: he said, 'The Messenger of God narrated to us two hadiths. I have seen one and I am waiting for the other. He narrated to us that the trust (al-amanah) descended into the roots of men's hearts, and they learned from the Quran and learned from the Sunnah.' And he narrated to us about the removal of the trust: He said, 'A man sleeps one sleep and the trust is taken from his heart, and its trace remains like the trace of a dot (al-wakt). Then he sleeps a sleep and the trust is taken from his heart, and its trace remains like the trace of a blister (al-majl) like an ember you rolled on your foot so it blistered; you see it raised but without anything in it.' So in this hadith it is shown that the trust, which is faith, descended into the origin of hearts, for the root is the origin. And this only occurred through the messengers when they informed of what they informed, so that was heard, and then God inspired the hearts with faith and sent it down into the hearts. Likewise, God sent down the balance into the hearts when the messengers clarified justice and what is weighed with it.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya defining the 'intellectual balance' (al-mizan al-'aqli) as an innate human faculty and refuting the claim that rational reasoning is separate from prophetic revelation. He begins by defining the balance as the fitri (innate) knowledge of similarity and difference, which allows one to equate similar things and differentiate between dissimilar ones—a capacity placed by God in human nature (fitrah) and intellect. This is a direct challenge to the Greek logicians who claimed exclusive authority over valid reasoning. 

Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: if this balance is known by reason, why did prophets bring it? He answers by explaining that prophets did not invent a new rational faculty but rather directed and perfected the existing fitrah through intellectual parables (amthal 'aqliyya), guiding people to the correct analogies needed for religious objectives. Thus prophetic sciences are not merely reports but include the elucidation of rational sciences. The fitrah may have been neglected or corrupted by false opinions, and the prophets restored and completed it.

He then provides Qur'anic evidence where God criticizes unjust judgments that equate unlike things or differentiate like things, quoting verses that condemn equating believers with disbelievers or the righteous with the wicked. This shows that the balance is embedded in divine revelation. Ibn Taymiyya stresses that the balance is not exclusive to Greek logic; it encompasses both the syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) and analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), with the latter being more original and complete. The 'balance' is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), the unifier, and the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) in any valid inference.

Finally, he draws an analogy between the descent of the balance and the descent of faith (iman), identified with the trust (amanah) in the famous hadith of Hudhayfah. Just as faith was instilled in hearts through the prophets' messages, so too the balance is placed in hearts when prophets clarify justice and the criteria of weighing. This reinforces his central thesis: sound reasoning is a divine gift mediated by revelation, not a secular tool monopolized by any particular tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya defining the 'intellectual balance' (al-mizan al-'aqli) as an innate human faculty and refuting the claim that rational reasoning is separate from prophetic revelation. He begins by defining the balance as the fitri (innate) knowledge of similarity and difference, which allows one to equate similar things and differentiate between dissimilar ones—a capacity placed by God in human nature (fitrah) and intellect. This is a direct challenge to the Greek logicians who claimed exclusive authority over valid reasoning. 

Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: if this balance is known by reason, why did prophets bring it? He answers by explaining that prophets did not invent a new rational faculty but rather directed and perfected the existing fitrah through intellectual parables (amthal 'aqliyya), guiding people to the correct analogies needed for religious objectives. Thus prophetic sciences are not merely reports but include the elucidation of rational sciences. The fitrah may have been neglected or corrupted by false opinions, and the prophets restored and completed it.

He then provides Qur'anic evidence where God criticizes unjust judgments that equate unlike things or differentiate like things, quoting verses that condemn equating believers with disbelievers or the righteous with the wicked. This shows that the balance is embedded in divine revelation. Ibn Taymiyya stresses that the balance is not exclusive to Greek logic; it encompasses both the syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) and analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), with the latter being more original and complete. The 'balance' is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), the unifier, and the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) in any valid inference.

Finally, he draws an analogy between the descent of the balance and the descent of faith (iman), identified with the trust (amanah) in the famous hadith of Hudhayfah. Just as faith was instilled in hearts through the prophets' messages, so too the balance is placed in hearts when prophets clarify justice and the criteria of weighing. This reinforces his central thesis: sound reasoning is a divine gift mediated by revelation, not a secular tool monopolized by any particular tradition.

والقرآن والحديث مملوء من هذا يبين الله الحقائق بالمقاييس العقلية والأمثال المضروبة، ويبين طريق التسوية بين المتماثلين والفرق بين المختلفين، وينكر على من يخرج عن ذلك، كقوله: أَمْ حَسِبَ الَّذِينَ اجْتَرَحُوا السَّيِّئَاتِ أَن تَجْعَلَهُمْ كَالَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ سَوَاءً مَحْيَاهُمْ وَمَمَاتُهُمْ سَاءَ مَا يَحْكُمُونَ ، وقوله: أَفَتَجْعَلُ المُسلِمِينَ كَالمُجْرِمِينَ مَا لَكُمْ كَيْفَ تَحْكُمُونَ . أي، هذا حكم جائر، لا عادل فإن فيه تسوية بين المختلفين. وقال : أَمْ نَجْعَلُ الَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا وَعَمِلُوا الصَّالِحَاتِ كَالْمُفْسِدِينَ فِي الْأَرْضِ أَمْ نَجْعَلُ الْمُتَّقِينَ كَالْفُجَّارِ . ومن التسوية بين المتماثلين قوله : أَكُفَّارُكُمْ خَيْرٌ مِنْ أُولَئِكُمْ أَمْ لَكُم بَرَاءَةٌ فِي الزُّبُرِ ، وقوله: أَمْ حَسِبْتُمْ أَن تَدْخُلُوا الْجَنَّةَ وَلَمَّا يَأْتِكُم مَّثَلُ الَّذِينَ خَلَوْا مِن قَبْلِكُم مَّسَّهُمُ الْبَأْسَاءُ وَالضَّرَّاء وَزُلْزِلُوا .
والقرآن مملوء من ذلك، لكن ليس هذا موضعه. وإنما المقصود التنبيه على جنس الميزان العقلي، وأنها حق كما ذكر الله في كتابه وليست هي مختصة بمنطق اليونان وإن كان فيه قِسْط منها . بل هي الأقيسة الصحيحة المتضمنة التسوية بين المتماثلين والفرق بين المختلفين، سواء صيغ ذلك بصيغة قياس الشمول أو بصيغة قياس التمثيل. وصِيَغُ التمثيل هي الأصل وهي أكمل والميزان القدر المشترك، وهو الجامع، وهو الحد الأوسط.
إنزال الميزان مع الرسل كإنزاله الإيمان وهو الأمانة مع الرسل والإيمان لم يحصل إلا بهم، كما قال تعالى: وَكَذَلِكَ أَوْحَيْنَا إِلَيْكَ رُوحًا مَنْ أَمْرِنَا مَا كُنتَ تَدْرِى مَا الْكِتَابُ وَلَا الْإِيمَانُ وَلَكِن جَعَلْتَهُ نُورًا نَهْدِى بِهِ مَن نَشَاءُ مِنْ عِبَادِنَا وَإِنَّكَ لَتَهْدِي إِلَى صِرَاطٍ مُسْتَقِيمٍ. وفي الصحيحين عن حُذَيْفَةَ بن اليمان قال: حدثنا رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم حديثين قد رأيتُ أحدهما وأنا أنتظر الآخر . حدثنا أن الأمانة نزلت في جَذْرِ قلوب الرجال، فعلموا من القرآن وعلموا من السنة. وحدثنا عن رفع الأمانة ، قال : ينام الرجل النومة فتقبض الأمانة من قلبه ، فيَظَلُّ أثرها مثل أثر الوَكْتَ. ثم ينام الرجل النومة فتقبض الأمانة من قلبه فيظل أثرها مثل أثر المَجْلِ كجَمْرٍ دَحْرَجْتَه على رِجْلِك فَنَفِطَ، فتراه مُنتَبِراً وليس فيه شيء. فقد بين في هذا الحديث أن الأمانة التي هي الإيمان، أنزلها في أصل القلوب، فإن الجذر هو الأصل. وهذا إنما كان بواسطة الرسل لما أخبروا بما أخبروا به، فسُمِع ذلك، فألْهَمَ الله القلوبَ الإيمان وأنزله في القلوب. وكذلك أنزل الله سبحانه الميزانَ في القلوب لما بينت الرسل العَدْل وما يوزن به.
The Fourth Station - the Seventh Aspect: The intellectual balance is the innate knowledge of similarity and difference. And the balance is a just balance that involves considering a thing by its like and its opposite, so it makes equal between similar things and distinguishes between different ones by what God has placed in the fitrah (innate nature) of His servants and their minds of the knowledge of similarity and difference.
If it is said: If this is known by reason, then how did God make it part of what the messengers were sent with? It is said: Because the messengers presented for the people intellectual parables by which they know similarity and difference. For the messengers guided the people and directed them to what they know justice by, and they know the correct intellectual analogies by which religious objectives are inferred. So the prophetic sciences are not confined to mere report, as those among the theologians who think that, and make what is known by reason a counterpart to the prophetic sciences. Rather, the messengers—peace be upon them—explained the intellectual sciences by which people's religion is perfected, both knowledge and action, and they presented parables. Thus the fitrah was perfected by what they alerted it to and guided it to, from what the fitrah was neglectful of, or the fitrah had become corrupted by what had come to it of corrupt opinions and desires. So they removed that corruption and clarified what the fitrah was neglectful of, until there came about for the fitrah knowledge of the balance which God sent down and His messengers explained.
The Quran and Hadith are full of this. God clarifies realities by intellectual analogies and presented parables, and clarifies the method of making equal between similar things and distinguishing between different ones, and criticizes those who deviate from that. Like His saying: 'Or do those who commit evil deeds think that We will make them like those who believe and do righteous deeds, equal in their life and their death? Evil is that which they judge.' And His saying: 'Will We treat the Muslims like the criminals? What is the matter with you? How do you judge?' That is, this is an unjust judgment, not just, because it involves making equal between different things. And He said: 'Or shall We make those who believe and do righteous deeds like the corrupters on earth? Or shall We make the pious like the wicked?' And from making equal between similar things is His saying: 'Are your disbelievers better than those [former] ones? Or have you an immunity in the scriptures?' And His saying: 'Or do you think that you will enter Paradise while there has not yet come to you the like of those who passed away before you? They were touched by poverty and hardship and were shaken.'
The Quran is full of that, but this is not its place. Rather, the purpose is to draw attention to the genus of the intellectual balance, and that it is true as God mentioned in His Book, and it is not specific to the logic of the Greeks, although there is a portion of it therein. Rather, it is the correct analogies that involve making equal between similar things and distinguishing between different ones, whether formulated in the form of a syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) or in the form of a simile (qiyas al-tamthil). The forms of simile are the origin and are more complete. And the balance is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), which is the unifier (al-jami'), and it is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat).
The sending down of the balance with the messengers is like the sending down of faith—which is the trust—with the messengers. Faith was not obtained except through them, as He said: 'And thus We have revealed to you a spirit of Our command. You did not know what the Book is nor [what] faith, but We have made it a light by which We guide whom We will of Our servants. And indeed, you guide to a straight path.' And in the two Sahihs from Hudhayfah ibn al-Yaman: he said, 'The Messenger of God narrated to us two hadiths. I have seen one and I am waiting for the other. He narrated to us that the trust (al-amanah) descended into the roots of men's hearts, and they learned from the Quran and learned from the Sunnah.' And he narrated to us about the removal of the trust: He said, 'A man sleeps one sleep and the trust is taken from his heart, and its trace remains like the trace of a dot (al-wakt). Then he sleeps a sleep and the trust is taken from his heart, and its trace remains like the trace of a blister (al-majl) like an ember you rolled on your foot so it blistered; you see it raised but without anything in it.' So in this hadith it is shown that the trust, which is faith, descended into the origin of hearts, for the root is the origin. And this only occurred through the messengers when they informed of what they informed, so that was heard, and then God inspired the hearts with faith and sent it down into the hearts. Likewise, God sent down the balance into the hearts when the messengers clarified justice and what is weighed with it.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya defining the 'intellectual balance' (al-mizan al-'aqli) as an innate human faculty and refuting the claim that rational reasoning is separate from prophetic revelation. He begins by defining the balance as the fitri (innate) knowledge of similarity and difference, which allows one to equate similar things and differentiate between dissimilar ones—a capacity placed by God in human nature (fitrah) and intellect. This is a direct challenge to the Greek logicians who claimed exclusive authority over valid reasoning. 

Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: if this balance is known by reason, why did prophets bring it? He answers by explaining that prophets did not invent a new rational faculty but rather directed and perfected the existing fitrah through intellectual parables (amthal 'aqliyya), guiding people to the correct analogies needed for religious objectives. Thus prophetic sciences are not merely reports but include the elucidation of rational sciences. The fitrah may have been neglected or corrupted by false opinions, and the prophets restored and completed it.

He then provides Qur'anic evidence where God criticizes unjust judgments that equate unlike things or differentiate like things, quoting verses that condemn equating believers with disbelievers or the righteous with the wicked. This shows that the balance is embedded in divine revelation. Ibn Taymiyya stresses that the balance is not exclusive to Greek logic; it encompasses both the syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) and analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), with the latter being more original and complete. The 'balance' is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), the unifier, and the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) in any valid inference.

Finally, he draws an analogy between the descent of the balance and the descent of faith (iman), identified with the trust (amanah) in the famous hadith of Hudhayfah. Just as faith was instilled in hearts through the prophets' messages, so too the balance is placed in hearts when prophets clarify justice and the criteria of weighing. This reinforces his central thesis: sound reasoning is a divine gift mediated by revelation, not a secular tool monopolized by any particular tradition.

The Descent of the Balance Parallels the Descent of Faith
Ibn Taymiyya parallels the descent of the balance with the descent of faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's analogy between the descent of the intellectual balance and the descent of faith (amanah) via the prophets.

Core Idea
Just as faith was sent down into hearts through the prophets, so too the balance is instilled through prophetic guidance.

Ibn Taymiyya concludes by drawing a powerful analogy: the sending down of the balance with the messengers is like the sending down of faith (the trust). This reinforces his thesis that sound reasoning is a divine gift mediated by revelation, not a human invention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the analogy
Find the sentence that begins with 'inzal al-mizan ma'a al-rusul ka-inzali al-iman wa huwa al-amanah...'
The balance is likened to faith.
2
Understand the hadith
Read Ibn Taymiyya's quote from the hadith of Hudhayfah. What is the 'amanah' and how does it descend?
The amanah (trust) is faith (iman), and it descends into the roots of hearts.
3
Connect to the balance
How is the descent of the balance similar? Look at the last sentence: 'wa kadhalika anzala Allah subhanahu al-mizan fi al-qulub...'
God sends down the balance into hearts when the messengers clarify justice.
4
Extract the epistemological point
What does this analogy imply about the source of rational knowledge?
Rational knowledge (the balance) has a divine origin, just like faith.

Rather, it is the correct analogies that involve making equal between similar things and distinguishing between different ones, whether formulated in the form of a syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) or in the form of a simile (qiyas al-tamthil). Likewise, God sent down the balance into the hearts when the messengers clarified justice and what is weighed with it.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya defining the 'intellectual balance' (al-mizan al-'aqli) as an innate human faculty and refuting the claim that rational reasoning is separate from prophetic revelation. He begins by defining the balance as the fitri (innate) knowledge of similarity and difference, which allows one to equate similar things and differentiate between dissimilar ones—a capacity placed by God in human nature (fitrah) and intellect. This is a direct challenge to the Greek logicians who claimed exclusive authority over valid reasoning. 

Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: if this balance is known by reason, why did prophets bring it? He answers by explaining that prophets did not invent a new rational faculty but rather directed and perfected the existing fitrah through intellectual parables (amthal 'aqliyya), guiding people to the correct analogies needed for religious objectives. Thus prophetic sciences are not merely reports but include the elucidation of rational sciences. The fitrah may have been neglected or corrupted by false opinions, and the prophets restored and completed it.

He then provides Qur'anic evidence where God criticizes unjust judgments that equate unlike things or differentiate like things, quoting verses that condemn equating believers with disbelievers or the righteous with the wicked. This shows that the balance is embedded in divine revelation. Ibn Taymiyya stresses that the balance is not exclusive to Greek logic; it encompasses both the syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) and analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), with the latter being more original and complete. The 'balance' is the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak), the unifier, and the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) in any valid inference.

Finally, he draws an analogy between the descent of the balance and the descent of faith (iman), identified with the trust (amanah) in the famous hadith of Hudhayfah. Just as faith was instilled in hearts through the prophets' messages, so too the balance is placed in hearts when prophets clarify justice and the criteria of weighing. This reinforces his central thesis: sound reasoning is a divine gift mediated by revelation, not a secular tool monopolized by any particular tradition.
