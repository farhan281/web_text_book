Propositions: Types and Immediate Inferences
Author introduces propositions and immediate inferences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a proposition as categorical, conditional conjunctive, or conditional disjunctive, and identify its immediate inference (contradictory, converse, contrapositive).

Core Idea
Every syllogism is built from propositions; mastering proposition types is the key to valid reasoning.

Before we can construct or analyze a syllogism, we must understand its building blocks: propositions. This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin introduces the three types of propositions (categorical, conditional conjunctive, conditional disjunctive) and the immediate inferences (contradiction, conversion, contrapositive) used to test their validity. Let's break down this foundational material step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition types
The author states that every proposition (qadiyya) is either categorical (hamliyya), conditional conjunctive (shartiyya muttasila), or conditional disjunctive (shartiyya munfasila). Practise spotting these in an Islamic text: e.g., 'The world is temporally originated' is categorical; 'If the world is temporally originated, it has a creator' is conditional conjunctive; 'The world is either temporally originated or eternal' is conditional disjunctive.
2
Understand immediate inferences
The author lists three ways to derive a new proposition from a given one: contradictory (naqid), converse ('aks), and contrapositive ('aks al-naqid). For a categorical proposition, its contradictory negates the predicate; its converse swaps subject and predicate; its contrapositive negates and swaps. Note that truth is preserved only under certain conditions.
3
Apply the inference rules to a concrete example
Take the proposition 'Every act is judged by intention' (categorical universal affirmative). Its contradictory is 'Some act is not judged by intention'. Its simple converse is invalid (not all intended things are acts), but its contrapositive is 'Whatever is not judged by intention is not an act'. Check the validity using classical logic.

The Three Forms of Syllogisms
Author classifies qiyas into three forms by proposition type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three forms of syllogisms (categorical, conditional conjunctive, conditional disjunctive) based on their constituent propositions and identify the correct form of a given syllogism.

Core Idea
The form of a syllogism is determined by its proposition type: categorical, conditional conjunctive, or conditional disjunctive.

The passage not only classifies propositions but also directly links each proposition type to a corresponding syllogism form. This lesson focuses on the three forms of qiyas: categorical (tadakhul), conditional conjunctive (talazum), and conditional disjunctive (ta'anud). Understanding these forms is essential for constructing valid arguments in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three proposition types
Review the previous lesson: categorical (حمليّة) predicates something of a subject; conditional conjunctive (شرطيّة متصلة) is an if-then statement; conditional disjunctive (شرطية منفصلة) is an either-or statement. Each forms the basis of a distinct syllogism.
2
Match propositions to syllogism forms
The author states: syllogisms are divided into three forms based on their form (صورة). قياس تداخل (categorical syllogism) corresponds to the categorical proposition; قياس تلازم (conditional conjunctive syllogism) corresponds to the conditional conjunctive; قياس تعاند (conditional disjunctive syllogism) corresponds to the conditional disjunctive. Note the Arabic names: tadakhul (inclusion), talazum (implication), ta'anud (opposition).
3
Distinguish form from material
The passage mentions that in terms of material (مادة), the syllogisms revert to the five types (burhani, jadali, etc.). This reminds us that a syllogism's validity depends both on its form and on the truth of its premises (material).
4
Identify the scope: qiyas shumul
The author concludes that all of this concerns قياس الشمول (syllogism of inclusion). This is contrasted elsewhere with tamthil (analogy) and istiqra' (induction). So the three forms here are all deductive syllogisms, not analogies or inductions.

Ibn Taymiyya's Classification of Certain Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya classifies certain knowledge into mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hudsiyyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hudsiyyat based on Ibn Taymiyya's criteria, and explain why mujarrabat require both sense and reason.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge from experience is never purely sensory—reason is always required.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the simplistic view that experiential knowledge (mujarrabat) comes purely from the senses. In this passage, he classifies certain knowledge into three types: widely transmitted reports (mutawatirat), experiential knowledge (mujarrabat), and intuitive knowledge (hudsiyyat). He insists that universal propositions like 'drinking water quenches thirst' are known through a combination of sense and reason, not sense alone. This refinement has profound implications for how we assess certainty in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Find in the Arabic where Ibn Taymiyya explicitly names mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hudsiyyat. Note how he groups them as types of shared certain knowledge.
The categories are not mutually exclusive in his usage, but distinct in source.
2
Examine the role of sense vs. reason
For mujarrabat, notice his argument: sense only perceives particulars (e.g., a specific death), while the universal judgment (e.g., cutting the neck causes death) requires reason. Identify the phrase 'بما يتركب من الحس والعقل' (by what is composed of sense and reason).
This is the core of his critique of purely empirical accounts.
3
Distinguish mujarrabat and hudsiyyat
Ibn Taymiyya explains the difference based on human control: if the action is volitional (like eating or taking medicine), it is mujarrab; if outside human power (like lunar phases), it is hudsi. Find the Arabic conditional structure 'فإن كان... وإن كان'.
The alternative classification he mentions is not his own; he prefers the unified 'mujarrab' usage.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the knowledge that wine is unlawful. Is it mutawatir (from revelation), mujarrab (experienced effect), or hudsi (inferred from underlying cause)? Classify each component.
This tool helps evaluate which epistemic foundation supports a given claim.

Commentary
This passage is a critical classification of certain knowledge by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by discussing mutawatirat (widely transmitted reports), noting that certain sensory experiences are shared universally, such as seeing celestial bodies, while others are more specific, like local landmarks. He then extends this to matters known through tawatur and tajriba (experience), giving examples like knowledge of Mecca, the existence of prophets, and their claims. Following this, he defines mujarrabat (experiential knowledge) as that obtained by both sense and reason. He illustrates with universal propositions: drinking water quenches thirst, cutting the neck causes death, severe beating causes pain. He emphasizes that particular observations are made by sense, but the universal judgment requires a combination of sense and reason. He notes a terminological division: some call all such knowledge 'experiential', while others split it into 'experiential' (when the action is volitional, like eating) and 'intuitive' (when the effect is beyond human power, like lunar phases). He argues that the first usage is more linguistically correct, citing the Arabic term 'mujarrab' for a person seasoned by trials, whether voluntary or not.

Logical Meaning Across Expressions
Shows that the same logical implication can be expressed in multiple linguistic forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify different linguistic formulations that express the same logical implication.

Core Idea
The same logical inference can be dressed in many verbal robes.

This passage shows that the relationship of implication (between antecedent and consequent) is not tied to a specific wording. Whether you say 'If prayer is valid, then the worshipper is pure' or 'Every valid prayer requires purity,' the logical content is identical. The author insists that intellectual meanings, not words, are the true subject of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core implication
Find the two propositions that are linked by entailment: 'prayer validity' (ملزوم/antecedent) and 'worshipper purity' (لازم/consequent). The author uses this pair in all his examples.
The logical relation is that the antecedent necessitates the consequent.
2
List the different linguistic forms
Go through the Arabic text and list each distinct form: conditional (if...then), categorical (every...is...), negated categorical (whoever is not...), statement of entailment (A entails B), statement of condition (purity is a condition), and statement of implication (it follows from...).
The author enumerates at least six different phrasings.
3
Verify sameness of logical meaning
For each form, check whether the same implication holds: if the prayer is valid, purity must be present; if purity is absent, prayer is invalid. All forms convey this bidirectional entailment.
The unity of meaning is confirmed by the fact that the truth conditions are identical.
4
Generalize the principle
Understand that the author's point is universal: any logical relationship (e.g., condition, cause, definition) can be expressed in multiple ways. The logician should focus on the intellectual meaning, not get trapped in one verbal formula.
This principle is important for interpreting arguments in Islamic texts.

Commentary
This passage is the sixth aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' claim that all knowledge requires a syllogism. He begins by describing the structure of a categorical syllogism: the major premise is more general than the minor or equal, the conclusion is more specific than the major and more general than the minor. He uses the three terms (major, minor, middle) to illustrate. Then he argues that universal propositions originate from sensory experience of particulars; for example, seeing many humans leads to the universal 'every human is rational'. He then poses the key question: is knowledge of a universal proposition mediated by a syllogism or not? If it always requires a syllogism, and each syllogism requires a universal premise, then we face circularity or infinite regress. Thus, some universals must be known immediately (badihiyya), a point conceded by the logicians. Next, he introduces the relativity of the intuitive (badihi) and theoretical (nazari) status of propositions: it is not inherent but depends on the knower. He provides examples: the falsity of Musaylimah can be known through prophetic report, direct experience, recurrent report, or inference. Similarly, the crescent moon is known by sight, recurrent report, or calculation. Thus, the same proposition can be known by different means by different people. He concludes that universal propositions can be known without syllogism by some, so it is false to claim that what Zaid knows by syllogism cannot be known without it by another. This passage refutes the logicians' insistence on syllogistic necessity.

The Composite Nature of Empirical and Intuitive Propositions
Ibn Taymiyya dissects empirical and intuitive propositions, showing their composite nature of sense and intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' view that empirical propositions are purely sensory, and identify the role of intellect in forming universal judgments from repeated observations.

Core Idea
Empirical propositions are not pure sense; they require intellect to grasp the universal common factor.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' claim that empirical and intuitive propositions can serve as secure foundations for demonstration. He argues that these propositions are not purely sensory but are composites of sense and intellect. In this lesson, we will dissect his argument that the universal judgment (al-qada' al-kulli) arises only when the intellect grasps a common factor from repeated observations. This has profound implications for the certainty of knowledge derived from experience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Ibn Taymiyya is critiquing the logicians' view that sensory propositions (al-hissiyyat) can provide certain demonstration (burhan) against an opponent. He denies this by showing their composite nature.
2
Understand the composite structure
He analyses that empirical propositions involve both sense (particular experiences) and intellect (the universal judgment). The sensory part provides particular instances, but the universal judgment is added by the intellect.
3
Examine the role of repetition
Through repeated observations, the intellect perceives a common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak) and forms a universal judgment. This is the core of the critique: the universal is not given by sense alone.
4
Apply the analysis to intuitive propositions
Ibn Taymiyya shows that hadsiyyat (intuitive) are similar, with repetition not by human action, e.g., celestial observations like the moon's light variation, leading to the intuition of a common cause.
5
Connect to habitual knowledge
Even knowledge of language and customs follows this pattern. For example, hearing a speaker repeatedly use a word with a certain meaning allows the intellect to infer a habitual intention, showing the widespread applicability of the composite structure.

Commentary
This passage is from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' claim that sensory propositions can provide demonstration against an opponent. He argues that both empirical (tajribiyyat) and intuitive (hadsiyyat) propositions are composed of sense and intellect, and thus are not purely sensory. The author begins by stating the topic: sensory propositions cannot provide a demonstration. He then explains that probable propositions originate from empirical ones. For example, when a medicine is used and the disease is removed or pain occurs, this observation is empirical. The particular sensory experiences are obtained by the external senses, and the resulting pleasure or pain in the soul is known by the inner sense, which is similar to outer senses. The universal belief that a certain genus causes pleasure or pain is empirical, because the sensory data contain nothing universal. The universal judgment in the heart is a composite of sense and intellect. For instance, the belief that certain foods cause satiety, or that poisons cause death, are empirical judgments. Through repeated observation of particular instances, the intellect grasps the common factor (al-qadr al-mushtarak) and forms a universal judgment. Ibn Taymiyya then shows that intuitive propositions (al-hadsiyyat) are similar. They differ only in that the repetition is not by human action; examples include astronomical observations: seeing the moon's light vary with its position relative to the sun leads to the intuition that its light is derived from the sun. Similarly, the fixed stars' uniform motion leads to the intuition of a single sphere. Some logicians call these 'empirical' as well. The author concludes that most habitual knowledge—whether due to psychic will or natural power—is of this composite type, including knowledge of language meanings. For instance, hearing a speaker's utterance repeatedly with the same meaning allows one to infer his habitual intention. This knowledge is called 'intuitive'. Moreover, one can infer the habit of a type from an individual's action, like seeing a man entering the mosque before Friday prayer and deducing he intends to pray. However, such habitual knowledge can fail, and theoretical reasoning from necessary to necessary is different. Ultimately, most philosophical sciences, including astronomy, are based on this kind of habitual knowledge, not on pure demonstration.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Excluding Common Opinions from Certainty
Ibn Taymiyya rejects exclusion of wahmiyyat and mahmudat from certainty.

Core Idea
Common opinions are grounded in fiṭra and deserve certainty, not mere dialectical acceptance.

Commentary

This classification shows that mashhurat are not a homogeneous category; they depend on external factors like culture, religion, or human nature.

Al-Razi's Test to Distinguish Awwali from Mashhur Propositions
Al-Razi explains the test to differentiate awwaliyat from mashhurat using an imagined state.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A proposition is primary only if pure reason affirms it without any external influence.

How can you tell if a judgment comes from pure reason or from social convention? Al-Razi, commenting on Ibn Sina, provides a powerful mental experiment: strip yourself of all habits and emotions, and see if the intellect still affirms the proposition. This test separates primary axioms from well-known opinions that rely on external factors like custom, emotion, or public benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition's subject and predicate
Take the proposition you want to test and clearly identify its subject (موضوع) and predicate (محمول). For example, in 'lying is ugly,' the subject is 'lying' and the predicate is 'ugly'.
2
Imagine yourself in a state of pure reason
Al-Razi instructs you to imagine yourself as if you were created in that very moment, without any prior training, habits, emotions, or awareness of social utility. Strip away all influences from tenderness, pride, habit, or public welfare.
This mental step is crucial to isolate the intellect from external causes.
3
Present the terms to your intellect
With your mind in that purified state, present the subject and predicate together. Ask yourself: does the intellect immediately and necessarily affirm that the predicate belongs to the subject?
Do not rely on memory or prior beliefs.
4
Observe the intellect's response
If the intellect judges the relation without hesitation and without needing any further reason, the proposition is primary (awwali). If it hesitates or requires additional justification, the proposition is well-known (mashhur).
Al-Razi gives the example: the law of non-contradiction is affirmed immediately, while 'lying is ugly' causes hesitation.
5

Based on the test, classify the proposition as either awwali (primary) or mashhur (well-known). Remember that a primary proposition is always well-known, but a well-known proposition may be false or require proof.
Well-known propositions are not necessarily false; they just need verification.

The Logicians' Argument: Knowledge of Essence Does Not Necessitate Knowledge of Its Concomitants
Ibn Taymiyya presents the logicians' argument and their response to a doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logicians' conditional argument against the transitivity of essential knowledge, and explain the role of the additional condition (conceptualization of the effect) in their response.

Core Idea
Knowing an essence does not automatically reveal its remote implications; only with additional conceptualization does the relationship become known.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya presents the logicians' own argument that knowledge of an essence does not entail knowledge of all its necessary concomitants. They anticipate an objection: if it entailed the near concomitant, and that near concomitant entailed the next, an infinite chain would be required—which is impossible. The logicians respond by distinguishing between conceptualizing the cause alone and conceptualizing it together with the effect. This lesson will help you analyze the logical structure of their argument and response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The logicians assert that knowledge of the essence (mahiyya) does not necessitate knowledge of its necessary concomitant (lazim). This is the thesis they are defending.
Note the careful phrasing: 'lā yaqtadī' means 'does not necessitate', not 'does not imply' in the usual sense.
2
Find the supporting argument (reductio)
The logicians argue: if knowledge of essence did necessitate the near necessary concomitant, then by transitivity it would require knowledge of an infinite chain of concomitants, which is impossible. Therefore the initial assumption is false, so knowledge does not necessitate.
This is a valid conditional argument: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
3
Locate the doubt (shakk) and the response
A doubter might object that the reductio proves too much: if knowledge does not necessitate, how can any concomitant be known? The logicians respond by introducing a condition: knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of the effect only when the conceptualization (tasawwur) of the effect is also present.
Causality ('illiyya) is a relational attribute, not inherent in the essence alone.
4
Assess the overall logical structure
The passage contains a conditional argument (modus tollens) followed by a clarification that knowledge of essence does necessitate knowledge of the near concomitant under a specific condition. This shows the nuance in the logicians' position.
This illustrates how Islamic logicians handle potential counterexamples through the introduction of conditions.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Certainty Hierarchy
Ibn Taymiyya argues that commonly accepted moral truths are more certain than experiential propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that commonly accepted moral propositions (mashhurat) are more certain than experiential propositions (tajribiyyat) and identify the empirical and innate basis he uses.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya flips the hierarchy: moral truths, not empirical ones, are the most certain.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya challenges the standard logical classification that ranks empirical propositions as certain and commonly accepted moral opinions as less certain. He uses the example of 'justice is good' to show that such propositions are known by innate nature (fitra) and universal experience, making them epistemically superior to even the most reliable medical generalizations. Let's walk through his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' classification
Find the two types of propositions: experiential (tajribiyyat) and commonly accepted (mashhurat). Note which one logicians consider certain.
The logicians treat tajribiyyat as yaqini, but mashhurat as less certain.
2
Examine Ibn Taymiyya's interpretation of moral statements
When people say 'justice is good,' they mean it brings pleasure and benefit; 'injustice is evil' brings pain and harm. This is a claim about human experience.
3
Compare to experiential propositions
Ibn Taymiyya contrasts 'justice is good' with 'scammony purges yellow bile.' He asks: if the latter is certain, why not the former, since more people have experienced justice and injustice?
The comparison highlights the wider experiential basis of moral truths.
4
Assess the strength of experience
He notes that those who have experienced moral truths are more numerous, more knowledgeable, and more truthful, and their reports are more widespread.
5
Draw the conclusion
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that these commonly accepted propositions are among the greatest certainties, known by sense, reason, and experience.
This directly challenges the logicians' hierarchy.

The Fourth Station - The Ninth Aspect: The distinction between experiential propositions and commonly accepted ones. We say: When people say 'Justice is good and injustice is evil,' they mean by this that justice is loved by innate nature, that through its presence there is pleasure and joy, beneficial to its possessor and others, and that through it pleasure, joy, and that which makes souls happy are obtained. And when they say 'Injustice is evil,' they mean that it is harmful to its possessor and others, and that it is hateful, and through it pain, grief, and that which tortures souls are obtained. It is known that these propositions are, in people's knowledge of them by innate nature and experience, greater than most medical propositions, such as the fact that scammony purges yellow bile. So why are experiential propositions certain, while these, which are more famous than them and which people have experienced more than those, are not certain? Even though those who have experienced them are more numerous, more knowledgeable, and more truthful, and their particulars in the world are more than the particulars of those, and those who report about them are also more numerous, more knowledgeable, and more truthful. Man himself finds from the pleasure of justice, truthfulness, knowledge, and goodness, and the joy therein, what he does not find from injustice, lying, and ignorance. People who have received these and those who have not received them find in themselves of pleasure, joy, and happiness from the justice of the just, the truthfulness of the truthful, the knowledge of the knowledgeable, and the goodness of the good what they do not find in injustice, lying, ignorance, and evil. Therefore they find in themselves love for the one who does that, praise for him, and supplication for him, and they are innately disposed to love that and take pleasure in it; they cannot repel that from themselves, just as they are innately disposed to experience pleasure in eating and drinking and pain in hunger and thirst. So why were those propositions among the certainties known by sense and reason like experience, and why were these not among the intellectual propositions also known by sense and reason, even though the matter is greater? The pleasure found in these is a noble spiritual intellectual pleasure, and the more a person's intellect is perfected, the more beloved this pleasure is to him than that pleasure. Then the philosophers affirmed the resurrection of spirits and intellectual pleasure, and it is built upon these propositions which they called 'commonly accepted ones.' If they were not known, then what they affirmed in that would contain nothing of knowledge; rather they would say what others say, that inner pleasures are stronger and nobler than outer pleasures, and they claim necessity in affirming a pleasure beyond the sensory outward pleasures. And if pleasure is either the perception of the agreeable, as they may claim, or it is a consequent and necessary concomitant of the perception of the agreeable, as others say and which is correct, then it is known that knowledge, justice, truthfulness, and goodness are agreeable to human beings, so they take pleasure in that; indeed their pleasure in that is greater than in other things. This is the meaning of an act being good, and the meaning of it being evil is the opposite. If the meaning of good and evil is conceptualized, it is known that these commonly accepted propositions are among the greatest certainties, for they are agreed upon by nations because they know them by sense, reason, and experience. Indeed the agreement of people on these is greater than their agreement on intellectual matters, and even greater than their agreement on most things they mention. There may be groups of people who live for a time and do not conceive of the universal intellectual propositions which they made as principles of knowledge, such as 'Negation and affirmation cannot be combined,' although it is known that this specific thing, if it exists, is not non-existent; but the universal proposition may not occur to them. Indeed they may not conceive of the supposition of the conjunction of its existence and non-existence, for this supposition is impossible and does not occur to most people. But there is no group except that it deems justice, truthfulness, knowledge, and goodness as good, and their opposite as evil. Also, 'wisdom' according to them and all nations is of two types: knowledge and action. Practical wisdom is built upon these commonly accepted propositions. Malik (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'Wisdom is knowledge of the religion and acting upon it.' And Ibn Qutayba said: 'Wisdom according to the Arabs is knowledge and action.' Practical wisdom according to them and others includes the science of ethics, household management, and civic management. The foundation of all that is these commonly accepted propositions. Indeed every commanded action must involve justice. Justice is commanded in all actions, and injustice is absolutely prohibited. Therefore the best laws and methods came to realize and perfect all of this. So Allah obligated justice for everyone over everyone in every situation, as He said: 'O you who believe...'

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' classification of propositions into experiential (tajribiyyat) and commonly accepted (mashhurat), where the former are deemed certain and the latter not. He refutes this by showing that the commonly accepted moral propositions, such as 'justice is good' and 'injustice is evil,' are more certain than medical experiential propositions because they are rooted in innate human nature (fitra) and universal experience. Ibn Taymiyya proceeds in the author's order: First, he interprets what people mean when they utter these moral judgments: they refer to the inherent pleasure or pain these actions produce in the soul. He points out that these judgments are known by fitra and experience to a greater degree than typical experimental propositions like 'scammony purges yellow bile.' If experiential propositions are deemed certain, why are these, which are more famous and more widely experienced, not also certain? The experiencers are more numerous and reliable. Next, he elaborates on the universal human experience of pleasure from justice, truth, knowledge, and goodness, and pain from their opposites. This pleasure is spiritual and intellectual, and it increases with the perfection of the intellect. He then points out a contradiction: the philosophers themselves base their doctrine of spiritual resurrection and intellectual pleasure on these very 'commonly accepted' propositions. If those were uncertain, their own doctrines would lack a foundation. Moreover, they claim that inner pleasures are stronger, which itself relies on these moral propositions. Ibn Taymiyya defines pleasure as perception of the agreeable or a concomitant of it; knowledge, justice, etc., are agreeable to humans, so they yield pleasure—greater than bodily pleasures. This is the meaning of good and evil. He argues that these propositions are among the greatest certainties because all nations agree on them, based on sense, reason, and experience. Universal agreement on these is greater than on abstract intellectual axioms like the law of non-contradiction, which many people never consciously formulate. Yet every society approves of justice and truthfulness. Finally, he connects this to wisdom (hikma), which consists of knowledge and action. Practical wisdom—ethics, household management, politics—is built entirely on these commonly accepted propositions. Even Islamic law (Sharia) emphasizes justice for all, as per the Quranic verse. Thus, Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates that the philosophers' own framework undermines their classification, and that these moral truths are foundational and certain.

The Role of Mashhurat in Practical Wisdom and Sharia

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' classification of propositions into experiential (tajribiyyat) and commonly accepted (mashhurat), where the former are deemed certain and the latter not. He refutes this by showing that the commonly accepted moral propositions, such as 'justice is good' and 'injustice is evil,' are more certain than medical experiential propositions because they are rooted in innate human nature (fitra) and universal experience. Ibn Taymiyya proceeds in the author's order: First, he interprets what people mean when they utter these moral judgments: they refer to the inherent pleasure or pain these actions produce in the soul. He points out that these judgments are known by fitra and experience to a greater degree than typical experimental propositions like 'scammony purges yellow bile.' If experiential propositions are deemed certain, why are these, which are more famous and more widely experienced, not also certain? The experiencers are more numerous and reliable. Next, he elaborates on the universal human experience of pleasure from justice, truth, knowledge, and goodness, and pain from their opposites. This pleasure is spiritual and intellectual, and it increases with the perfection of the intellect. He then points out a contradiction: the philosophers themselves base their doctrine of spiritual resurrection and intellectual pleasure on these very 'commonly accepted' propositions. If those were uncertain, their own doctrines would lack a foundation. Moreover, they claim that inner pleasures are stronger, which itself relies on these moral propositions. Ibn Taymiyya defines pleasure as perception of the agreeable or a concomitant of it; knowledge, justice, etc., are agreeable to humans, so they yield pleasure—greater than bodily pleasures. This is the meaning of good and evil. He argues that these propositions are among the greatest certainties because all nations agree on them, based on sense, reason, and experience. Universal agreement on these is greater than on abstract intellectual axioms like the law of non-contradiction, which many people never consciously formulate. Yet every society approves of justice and truthfulness. Finally, he connects this to wisdom (hikma), which consists of knowledge and action. Practical wisdom—ethics, household management, politics—is built entirely on these commonly accepted propositions. Even Islamic law (Sharia) emphasizes justice for all, as per the Quranic verse. Thus, Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates that the philosophers' own framework undermines their classification, and that these moral truths are foundational and certain.

عن النبي لله و أنه قال : «لم يبق بعدي من النبوّة إلا المبشرات، وهي الرؤيا الصالحة يراها الرجلُ الصالح أو تُرَى له». وقال : الرؤيا الصالحة جزء من ستة وأربعين جزءاً من النبوة. وفي الصحيحين عن عائشة قالت : أول ما بدىء به رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم من الوحي الرؤيا الصادقة في النوم، فكان لا يرى رؤيا إلا جاءت مثل فَلَقِ الصُّبح . ثم حُبّب إليه الخلاء، فكان يخلو بغار حِرَاءٍ فيتحنّتُ فيه، وهو التعبدُ، الليالي ذواتِ العَدَد. وقد قال تعالى : ( اللهُ أَعْلَمُ حَيْثُ يَجْعَلُ رِسَالَتَهُ ). فلا ريب أن ما يجعله الله في النفوس وغيرها يجعله بعد إعدادها لذلك وتسويتها لما يُلقى فيها. فهذا ونحوه حق يقول به السلف وجمهور المسلمين. وإنما يُنكر ذلك مَنْ ينكر الحِكم والأسباب من أهل الكلام. والنبي صلى الله عليه وسلم بدىء أولاً بالرؤيا الصادقة. فإن رؤيا الأنبياء وحي معصوم، كما قال ابن عباس وعُبيد بن عُمير وغيرهما: رؤيا الأنبياء وحي وقرأ قول إبراهيم عليه السلام : إِنِّي أَرَى فِي الْمَنَامِ أَنِّي أَذْبَحُكَ. ثم إن النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم نقل من درجة إلى درجة، ثم بعد هذا جاءه الملك فخاطبه بالكلام. فأحياناً يأتيه في الباطن فيكلمه، وأحياناً يتمثل له في صورة رجل فيكلمه. ثم عُرج به إلى ربه ليلة الإسراء. فما ادعوه من أن الرؤيا قد يحصل بها معرفةُ المغيبات حق. وهذا يُحتج به على من ينكر هذا الجنس مطلقاً، ولكن لا تجعل النبوة كلها من هذا الجنس . فمن الباطل ما ادعوه في النبوة وفي كيفيتها، حيث زعموا أنه ليس هناك مَلَك حي يأتي بالوحي من الله ، ولا الله كلام يتكلم به يَسْمعه المَلَكُ فينزل به، ولا يعرف الله جزئيات الأمور حتى يكتبها عنده، أو حتى يُخبر بها الملك والملك يخبر بها النبي ، أو يخبر بها النبي ابتداء . وزعموا أنه ليس الله ما يدبر به أمر السموات والأرض إلا مجرد حركة الفلك . وأثبتوا النبوة كما أثبتها الفلاسفة نبوة حال كثير من أحوال أوساط المسلمين خير منها . فإن كثيراً من أوساط المسلمين له من العلم والعمل أعظم مما أثبته هؤلاء للأنبياء. فإنهم جعلوا خواص النبوة نوعين : القوة العلمية التي ينال بها العلم، إما بواسطة القياس المنطق، وإما بواسطة التجرد الذي هو كتجرد النائم حتى تتصل بالنفس الفلكية ; والثاني القوة العَمَلية، وهو أن تكون نفسه قوية على التصرف في هَيُولى العالم بحيث تحدث عنها عجائب. والنوع الأول يتضمن أمرين: أحدهما معرفة العلوم الكلية بالقياس المنطقي، والثاني معرفة الجزئيات بهذا الاتصال. ثم الخيال يصور المعقولات في الصور المناسبة لها ويَنْقُشُها في الحس المشترك، فيرى الإنسانُ في باطنه صوراً ويسمع أصواتاً. وتلك الصور عندهم ملائكة الله ، وتلك الأصوات كلام الله . ولهذا كان الملاحدة من المتصوفة على طريقهم كابن عربي وابن سبعين وغيرهما قد سلكوا مسلك ملاحدة الشيعة كأصحاب رسائل إخوان الصفا، واتبعوا ما وجدوه
From the Prophet, may God's peace and blessings be upon him, he said: 'Nothing remains after me of prophethood except the good tidings, and they are the righteous dream that a righteous man sees or is shown to him.' And he said: 'The righteous dream is a part of forty-six parts of prophethood.' In the two Sahihs from Aisha, she said: The first thing the Messenger of God, may God's peace and blessings be upon him, began with of revelation was the true dream in sleep; he would not see a dream except that it came like the break of dawn. Then solitude was made beloved to him, so he would seclude himself in the cave of Hira and worship there, which is devotion, for many nights. And God Almighty said: 'God knows best where to place His message.' There is no doubt that what God places in souls and other things, He places after preparing them for that and making them suitable for what is cast into them. This and the like is the truth held by the Salaf and the majority of Muslims. Only those who deny wisdom and causes among the people of kalam deny that. The Prophet, may God's peace and blessings be upon him, began first with the true dream. Indeed, the dreams of prophets are infallible revelation, as Ibn Abbas, Ubayd ibn Umayr, and others said: The dreams of prophets are revelation, and he recited the saying of Abraham, peace be upon him: 'I see in a dream that I am sacrificing you.' Then the Prophet, may God's peace and blessings be upon him, was transferred from one degree to another, then after this the angel came to him and addressed him with speech. Sometimes he would come to him inwardly and speak to him, and sometimes he would appear to him in the form of a man and speak to him. Then he was taken on the Night Journey to his Lord. So what they claim, that through dreams one can obtain knowledge of the unseen, is true. This is used as evidence against those who deny this kind absolutely, but one should not make all of prophethood of this kind. It is false what they claim about prophethood and its nature, where they claim that there is no living angel who brings revelation from God, nor God's speech that He speaks that the angel hears and then descends with it, nor does God know particular matters so that He writes them down with Him, or that the angel informs the prophet, or the prophet informs about them directly. And they claimed that there is nothing with which God administers the affairs of the heavens and the earth except mere motion of the celestial sphere. And they affirmed prophethood as the philosophers affirmed it, a prophethood such that the condition of many average Muslims is better than it. For many average Muslims have knowledge and action greater than what these people attribute to the prophets. They made the specific characteristics of prophethood two types: the scientific power by which one attains knowledge, either through logical syllogism or through aloneness (tajarrud) like the aloneness of the sleeper until it connects with the celestial soul; and the second is the practical power, namely that his soul is strong enough to act upon the matter (hayula) of the world such that wonders arise from it. The first type includes two things: one is knowledge of universal sciences by logical syllogism, and the second is knowledge of particulars through this connection. Then the imagination depicts the intelligibles in forms suitable for them and engraves them in the common sense, so a person sees images within himself and hears sounds. Those images, according to them, are the angels of God, and those sounds are the speech of God. Consequently, the heretics among the Sufis followed their path, like Ibn Arabi, Ibn Sab'in, and others, and they followed the path of the heretical Shi'a, like the authors of the Epistles of the Ikhwan al-Safa, and they followed what they found.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' conception of prophethood and revelation. He begins by establishing the orthodox view that the beginning of revelation to the Prophet Muhammad was through true dreams, which for prophets are infallible revelation. He cites hadith and the example of Abraham's dream. He then explains that the Prophet progressed through stages: from dreams to angelic communication, both inward and in human form, culminating in the Night Journey. While acknowledging that dreams can provide knowledge of the unseen, he warns that prophethood should not be reduced to that alone. Ibn Taymiyya then attacks the philosophers (referring to the falasifa like Al-Farabi and Ibn Sina) who deny the existence of a physical angel bringing revelation and divine speech. They instead claim that God administers the universe only through celestial motion. They reduce prophethood to two powers: a scientific power attained either through logical syllogism (qiyas mantiqi) or through a state of purification (tajarrud) that connects the soul to the celestial soul (nafs falakiyya); and a practical power of acting upon the world's matter (hayula). This scientific power produces universal knowledge via syllogism and particular knowledge via the connection. The imagination then dresses intelligibles in sensory images perceived as angels and sounds perceived as divine speech. Ibn Taymiyya vehemently criticizes this as false, noting that even average Muslims surpass what philosophers attribute to prophets in knowledge and action. He warns that this flawed conception leads to the heretical Sufi doctrines of Ibn Arabi and Ibn Sab'in, as well as the Shi'i philosophical claims in the Epistles of Ikhwan al-Safa. The passage thus defends the traditional Islamic understanding of prophethood as a divine choice and direct revelation, not a natural acquisition through logical reasoning or celestial connection.
