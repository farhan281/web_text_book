The Eight Parts of Aristotelian Logic
Ibn Taymiyya enumerates the eight parts of Aristotelian logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the eight parts of Aristotelian logic as enumerated by Ibn Taymiyya and identify the Greek name for the first part.

Core Idea
Aristotle's logic is divided into eight parts: simple expressions, propositions, syllogism, and five arts.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya presents the traditional structure of Aristotelian logic as transmitted in Islamic scholarship. He lists eight parts, beginning with simple expressions (al-mufradat) and ending with sophistical fallacies (al-safsata). He also notes that some logicians reduce the count to seven by merging syllogism and demonstration. This classification is foundational for understanding the scope of logic in the Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three core components
List the first three parts: simple expressions (categories), first composition (propositions), and second composition (syllogism). These build on each other.
These correspond to Porphyry's Isagoge, Perihermeneias, and Prior Analytics.
2
Recognize the five arts
The remaining five parts are the arts: demonstrative, dialectical, rhetorical, poetic, and sophistical. These are treatises on how to use syllogisms for different purposes.
These correspond to Aristotle's Topics, Rhetoric, Poetics, and Sophistical Refutations.
3
Note the Greek name for part one
Ibn Taymiyya mentions that the first part is called 'Isagoge' (إيساغوجي) and that Porphyry introduced it after Aristotle.
The Isagoge is an introduction to the Categories.
4
Observe the variation in number
Some logicians combine syllogism and demonstration into one part, making the total seven. Ibn Taymiyya records this as a disagreement.
This shows that the structure of logic was not monolithic in the tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a classificatory definition. Ibn Taymiyya first notes that the art of definition (sina'at al-hadd) was invented as a convention by Aristotle, and that logicians glorify him for it. He then enumerates the eight traditional parts of Aristotelian logic. The first part is 'simple expressions' (al-mufradat), which are the categories (al-maqulat al-ma'qula al-mufrada). The second is 'first composition' (al-tarkib al-awwal), i.e., the composition of propositions (tarkib al-qadaya). The third is 'second composition' (al-tarkib al-thani), i.e., the composition of syllogism from propositions (tarkib al-qiyas min al-qadaya). Then four treatises: the Demonstrative (al-burhani), Dialectical (al-jadali), Rhetorical (al-khatabi), Poetic (al-shi'ri), and Sophistical (al-safsata). Ibn Taymiyya adds that the first part is called 'Isagoge' (Eisagogi) in Greek, and some say that Porphyry (Furfuriyus) introduced it after Aristotle. He also notes that some logicians combine syllogism and demonstration into one, making the parts seven, claiming that this was Aristotle's view. This passage gives a clear overview of the structure of logic as traditionally taught.

The Eight Parts of Logic: Structure and Classification
Author enumerates the eight parts of Aristotelian logic and notes a disagreement on combining syllogism and demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the eight parts of Aristotelian logic and explain the disagreement about whether syllogism and demonstration are separate or combined.

Core Idea
The eight parts of logic are simple terms, propositions, syllogism, and the five arts.

Ibn Taymiyya reports the standard classification of Aristotelian logic as held by its proponents. This classification divides logic into eight parts, covering everything from simple concepts to complex arguments. Understanding this structure is essential before critiquing it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first three parts
The first part is simple intelligible categories (المقولات), the second is the composition of propositions (القضايا), and the third is the composition of syllogisms (القياس). Note that some logicians combine the third (syllogism) with the fourth (demonstration).
2
List the five arts
The remaining five parts are the arts: demonstration (برهان), dialectic (جدل), rhetoric (خطاب), poetics (شعر), and sophistry (سفسطة). These are the methods of argumentation derived from the syllogism.
The five arts correspond to Aristotle's Organon.
3
Note the disagreement on enumeration
Some logicians consider syllogism and demonstration as one part, making seven parts total. This reflects a difference in how they categorize logical works.
Ibn Taymiyya uses this disagreement to show the conventional nature of the classification.

The Greek Names of Logic's Parts: Terminology and Arabization
Author lists the Greek names for each part of logic and comments on their arabization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to match each part of logic to its Greek name and explain how Arab scholars adapted these terms.

Core Idea
The parts of logic have Greek names like Isagoge, Hermeneias, Analytica, Topics, Rhetoric, Poetics, and Sophistics.

Ibn Taymiyya not only enumerates the eight parts but also provides their original Greek names. He then remarks that Arab scholars arabized these terms, leading to variations. This lesson will help you recognize the foreign origin of these logical terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Greek name for each part
Start with the first part: Isagoge for simple terms (Porphyry's introduction). Then Hermeneias for propositions, Analytica Priora for syllogism, Analytica Posteriora for demonstration, Topics for dialectic, Rhetoric for rhetoric, Poetics for poetry, Sophistics for sophistry.
2
Understand the meaning of the Greek names
Hermeneias means 'expressions' (عبارات). Analytica means 'analysis'. Topics means 'places' (topoi). These names reflect the content of Aristotle's works.
3
Observe the process of arabization
Ibn Taymiyya notes that Arabs adapted these foreign words, approximating them to their own language, which is why spellings vary. For example, 'Isaghuji' is the arabized form of 'Isagoge'.
This linguistic adaptation shows the cross-cultural transmission of knowledge.

The Historical Innovation of Denying Rational Good and Evil
Ibn Taymiyya argues that denying rational good/evil is an innovation from al-Ash'ari's time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical origin of the denial of rational good and evil according to Ibn Taymiyya and evaluate its status as an innovation (bid'ah).

Core Idea
Denying rational good/evil is a later innovation from al-Ash'ari's era, not from the Salaf.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that the negation of rational good and evil is not an original Islamic doctrine but emerged as a bid'ah in the time of Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari. He names several scholars who affirmed this view and emphasizes that the Salaf never held such a negation. This lesson explores the historical context and the authoritative basis for Ibn Taymiyya's claim.

Guided Path
1
Locate the claim of bid'ah
Find the sentence where Ibn Taymiyya says negation occurred in al-Ash'ari's time.
The phrase 'كون نفيه من البدع التي حدثت زمن الأشعري' is key.
2
Identify the historical trigger
Understand that al-Ash'ari needed this negation to defend his position on predestination against the Mu'tazila.
The text mentions 'لما ناظر المعتزلة في القدر بطريق الجهم بن صفوان'.
3
Recognize the Salaf's position
Note that Ibn Taymiyya says the Salaf's discourses on rulings imply rational good/evil.
Look for 'ما يؤخذ من كلام الأئمة والسلف في تعليل الأحكام'.
4
Assess the strength of the bid'ah claim
Consider whether naming specific scholars as witnesses strengthens the argument.
The naming of Abu Nasr al-Sijzi and others adds historical weight.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion by Ibn Taymiyya regarding the historical origin and logical basis of the negation of rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-'aqliyyayn). He begins by asserting that this negation is an innovation (bid'ah) that emerged in the time of al-Ash'ari, specifically from his debate with the Mu'tazila on predestination (qadar). Ibn Taymiyya names several scholars from the Shafi'i, Maliki, and hadith schools who held that this negation is an innovation. He then states that the Salaf (early Muslims) and their imams never denied rational good and evil absolutely; rather, their discourses on legal rulings and divine wisdom imply the opposite. Ibn Taymiyya criticizes the negators (al-nufah) for having no valid proof. He specifically refutes al-Amidi's argument that good and evil are accidents ('arad) which cannot subsist in accidents. Ibn Taymiyya replies that this is unnecessary because accidents can be described with attributes (like 'intense blackness') without requiring a separate accident substance. He further clarifies that good and evil are not fixed, intrinsic attributes; they vary with conditions, just as benefit and harm vary. For human actions, rational good and evil are based on benefit and harm, which are known by reason. He then mentions that al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din) ultimately affirmed rational good and evil for human actions. For divine actions, the issue depends on whether God's love, anger, etc., are distinct from His will—a position held by the Salaf—or identical to it, as held by some Mu'tazila and Jahmiyya. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Salaf's position is the sound one.

The Logical Refutation of the Negators' Proof
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the negators' proof based on the accident argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the argument that good and evil cannot be rationally known because they are accidents, using Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Core Idea
Good and evil are not fixed intrinsic accidents; they are relational attributes known by reason through benefit and harm.

Ibn Taymiyya dismantles the main proof of those who deny rational good/evil: that good and evil are accidents ('arad) and an accident cannot subsist in another accident. He shows this argument is flawed and offers a positive account of good and evil as relational qualities. This lesson examines the logical structure of his refutation.
