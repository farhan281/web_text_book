Ibn Taymiyya explains his initial view of Greek logic and the occasion for writing this refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya was not an outright rejecter; he initially accepted many truths of Greek logic before discovering errors.

This preface opens Ibn Taymiyya's landmark refutation of Greek logic. It reveals his personal intellectual journey: he began with a low opinion of logic's utility but believed its propositions were true, until his research uncovered errors. The immediate catalyst was a meeting in Alexandria with a blind admirer of the philosophers. Understanding this context is crucial to appreciating his nuanced critique.

Commentary
This passage is the preface of Ibn Taymiyya's book "Refutation of the Logicians". It begins with the basmalah and shahadah, then moves to the author's personal account. Ibn Taymiyya states that he always held a low opinion of Greek logic: it is unnecessary for the intelligent (who can reason without formal training) and useless for the dull (who cannot grasp it). However, he initially believed its propositions to be generally true based on many true instances he observed. Later, he discovered errors in some of its propositions and wrote a brief critique. The immediate catalyst was a meeting in Alexandria with someone who exaggerated the worth of the philosophers through sensationalism and blind imitation. In response, Ibn Taymiyya dictated a refutation in a single sitting between Zuhr and Asr, then continued revising it in subsequent sessions until completion. He clarifies that this work was not his primary intellectual endeavor; his main effort was his writings against the philosophers on theological matters (ilahiyyat), such as his book 'Muwafaqat sahih al-manqul li-sarih al-ma'qul'. This preface establishes the author's critical stance toward Greek logic and situates the present work within his broader project of defending Islamic theology.

The Four Stations of Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation
Author lists the four sections of the book: two negative and two positive stations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the four stations of Ibn Taymiyya's refutation in al-Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin and their logical rationale.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya structures his refutation in four stations: negative and positive for definitions, then syllogisms.

Ibn Taymiyya does not merely attack Greek logic; he systematically dismantles it from two angles and then constructs an Islamic alternative. In this short outline, he reveals the architecture of his entire book, showing that he has both a destructive and a constructive phase for the two pillars of logic: definitions (tasawwurat) and syllogisms (tasdiqat). Understanding this structure helps you follow his argumentative strategy across the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main topics
The outline mentions 'definitions and concepts' (al-hudud wa al-tasawwurat) and 'syllogisms and assents' (al-aqisa wa al-tasdiqat). These are the two pillars of classical logic.
Ibn Taymiyya treats each topic with both a negative (critique) and positive (construction) station.
2
Order the four stations
The first station (negative on definitions) is not listed here but is implied from context. The passage lists: 2nd (positive on definitions), 3rd (negative on syllogisms), 4th (positive on syllogisms). The full order is: 1) negative on definitions, 2) positive on definitions, 3) negative on syllogisms, 4) positive on syllogisms.
This order shows a pattern: for each topic, Ibn Taymiyya first clears the ground by refuting false claims, then establishes the correct Islamic view.
3
Analyze the strategic purpose of the structure
By separating negative and positive stations, Ibn Taymiyya ensures that his refutation is not merely destructive. He offers an alternative. This mirrors the method of mutakallimun in 'adab al-bahth': first refute the opponent's proof, then provide your own proof.
This structure makes the book both a critique of Greek logic and a positive contribution to Islamic epistemology.

This is a critical passage from Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of the logicians. He begins by recalling that he had previously commented on the logicians in a session. Later, he came across the book 'Kitab al-Ara' wa al-Diyanat' by al-Nawbakhti, an earlier Muslim theologian, and noticed that al-Nawbakhti had already discussed the same point: that the philosophers are not a single coherent school but are deeply divided among themselves. Ibn Taymiyya then explains that Aristotle, the 'first teacher' of the Peripatetics, is the originator of Greek logic, which the logicians follow, along with his physics and metaphysics. However, the philosophers are not a unified nation with one doctrine; rather, they are diverse sects with immense differences—greater than the differences between Jews and Christians. This diversity, Ibn Taymiyya argues, stems from their distance from following the messengers and revealed books. He supports this with a hadith from al-Tirmidhi: 'No people went astray after guidance except they were given to disputation (jadal).' He then quotes the Quranic verse 'They did not strike it for you except as disputation; rather they are a contentious people' (al-Zukhruf 58). From this, Ibn Taymiyya concludes that the only judge between people is a revealed book and a sent prophet, citing al-Baqarah 213 and al-Nisa 59. He then elaborates on the concept of 'the Balance' (al-mizan) mentioned in the Quran. Many commentators say it means 'justice'; others say it is the means by which matters are weighed, i.e., the rational analogies (aqisa 'aqliyya) that distinguish truth from falsehood. Ibn Taymiyya argues that when the Book is mentioned absolutely, the Balance is included in it because the Quran itself contains these rational analogies. Similarly, when the Book is mentioned alone, wisdom (al-hikma) is included. Thus, Ibn Taymiyya's central point is that the Revelation is sufficient for judging truth and falsehood, and the Greek logic of the philosophers is unnecessary.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' (falasifa) doctrine regarding the celestial sphere (falk). He opens by citing a Quranic verse (Fussilat 22-23) rebuking those who thought Allah does not know all their deeds, then notes that the polytheist Arabs—despite their shirk—affirmed that Allah created the heavens and earth by His will and power. Even common Muslims and Arab polytheists recognized this. Ibn Taymiyya contrasts this with the philosophers: the reality of their claim is that Allah did not create anything. He distinguishes two groups: (1) the ancients like Aristotle who say the celestial sphere is eternal and moves to imitate the First Cause (al-'illa al-ula), denying that God is either a necessitating cause (mujib bi-dhat) or an agent by will (fa'il bi'l-mashi'a); (2) later philosophers like Ibn Sina who claim that God is a necessitating cause, producing an eternal effect that is possible but coeternal, which contradicts rational necessity. Both groups contradict the masses of rational people, even their own predecessors. Aristotle's view makes the Necessary in Itself dependent on another, which Ibn Sina himself denied. Ibn Taymiyya then critiques those who posit a necessitating cause (like Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd, al-Suhrawardi): they say the celestial sphere is a condition for the existence of contingent things, not an originator. They do not affirm any originator for events; the celestial sphere moves by its own choice, having a celestial soul, and nothing above it produces events. Even if they call it 'caused', their view is worse than the Qadariyya (who at least affirm Allah created the animal's power). The philosophers deny actual creation. If they claim the motion emanates from the First Cause, that is impossible because a simple unchanging thing cannot produce varying events. Concluding, Ibn Taymiyya asserts that the philosophers' disbelief (kufr) is greater than that of Jews, Christians, and Arab polytheists, because even the polytheists affirmed Allah's sovereignty and sought intercession through their idols, acknowledging Allah's power and will. The passage ends with a verse (Yunus 18) showing that the polytheists made their idols intercessors. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya's argument follows the structure: cite the philosophers' claim, contrast with the recognition of God's creative will by common polytheists, expose the logical absurdities and inconsistencies, and rank the kufr.

Key Terms
الفلك (al-falak): The celestial sphere or heavens; the subject of the philosophers' claims about its eternity, motion, and emanation from the First Cause. [Note: In classical astronomy, falak is the celestial orb; here Ibn Taymiyya uses it to critique the philosophers' metaphysical cosmology.]
العلة الأولى (al-'illa al-ula): The First Cause; the ultimate source of existence according to the philosophers, which the celestial sphere imitates in its motion. [Note: In falsafa, al-'illa al-ula is the Neo-Platonic One; Ibn Taymiyya rejects it as a substitute for the personal God of Islam.]
موجب بالذات (mujib bi-dhat): Necessitating by essence; a description of God as a cause that necessarily and eternally produces an effect without will. [Note: Ibn Sina's terminology; Ibn Taymiyya critiques it because it negates God's free will and power.]
فاعل بالمشيئة (fa'il bi'l-mashi'a): Agent by will; one who acts volitionally, creating things by choice and power, which Ibn Taymiyya affirms and the philosophers deny in the case of God. [Note: Standard Ash'ari and Sunni theology; Ibn Taymiyya uses it to oppose the philosophers' determinism.]
القدرية (al-Qadariyya): An early Islamic sect that upheld human free will and denied predestination; here compared unfavorably to the philosophers. [Note: Ibn Taymiyya often critiques the Qadariyya for overemphasizing human power, but here he uses them as a slightly better example than the philosophers.]

The Impossibility of Emanation from a Simple Unchanging Cause
Ibn Taymiyya refutes philosophers' celestial sphere doctrine and compares kufr degrees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that a simple unchanging cause cannot produce varying effects, and apply it to evaluate the philosophers' emanation theory.

Core Idea
A simple unchanging cause cannot be the source of multiple changing effects.

In a dense but crucial passage, Ibn Taymiyya states that 'a simple thing that is in a single state eternally and forever does not produce anything, let alone varying events.' This is a powerful logical refutation of the Neoplatonic emanation model used by philosophers to explain the celestial sphere's motion. This lesson will help you see how one logical principle can dismantle an entire metaphysical system.

Guided Path
1
Locate the claim
Find where Ibn Taymiyya says the simple unchanging cause cannot produce anything.
It appears just before the conclusion of the passage.
2
Identify the philosophical model under attack
The emanation model where the First Cause remains simple and unchanging yet gives rise to the celestial sphere's motion and multiplicity.
This is the Neoplatonic-Aristotelian framework adopted by Ibn Sina.
3
Understand the logical principle
A cause that is perfectly simple and unchanging cannot produce a plurality of effects that are temporally varying.
The principle is: from one simple thing, only one simple effect can arise (ex uno simplici non nisi unum).
4
Apply to the celestial sphere
The philosophers claim the sphere moves to imitate the First Cause, but if the First Cause is simple and unchanging, it cannot be the source of the sphere's motion which is a change.
Motion itself is a change; the sphere's motion is a series of varying events.
5
Generalize the principle
This principle can be used to refute any theory that posits a simple first cause for a changing world.
It is a standard argument in kalam against emanation.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the philosophers' (falasifa) doctrine regarding the celestial sphere (falk). He opens by citing a Quranic verse (Fussilat 22-23) rebuking those who thought Allah does not know all their deeds, then notes that the polytheist Arabs—despite their shirk—affirmed that Allah created the heavens and earth by His will and power. Even common Muslims and Arab polytheists recognized this. Ibn Taymiyya contrasts this with the philosophers: the reality of their claim is that Allah did not create anything. He distinguishes two groups: (1) the ancients like Aristotle who say the celestial sphere is eternal and moves to imitate the First Cause (al-'illa al-ula), denying that God is either a necessitating cause (mujib bi-dhat) or an agent by will (fa'il bi'l-mashi'a); (2) later philosophers like Ibn Sina who claim that God is a necessitating cause, producing an eternal effect that is possible but coeternal, which contradicts rational necessity. Both groups contradict the masses of rational people, even their own predecessors. Aristotle's view makes the Necessary in Itself dependent on another, which Ibn Sina himself denied. Ibn Taymiyya then critiques those who posit a necessitating cause (like Ibn Sina, Ibn Rushd, al-Suhrawardi): they say the celestial sphere is a condition for the existence of contingent things, not an originator. They do not affirm any originator for events; the celestial sphere moves by its own choice, having a celestial soul, and nothing above it produces events. Even if they call it 'caused', their view is worse than the Qadariyya (who at least affirm Allah created the animal's power). The philosophers deny actual creation. If they claim the motion emanates from the First Cause, that is impossible because a simple unchanging thing cannot produce varying events. Concluding, Ibn Taymiyya asserts that the philosophers' disbelief (kufr) is greater than that of Jews, Christians, and Arab polytheists, because even the polytheists affirmed Allah's sovereignty and sought intercession through their idols, acknowledging Allah's power and will. The passage ends with a verse (Yunus 18) showing that the polytheists made their idols intercessors. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya's argument follows the structure: cite the philosophers' claim, contrast with the recognition of God's creative will by common polytheists, expose the logical absurdities and inconsistencies, and rank the kufr.
