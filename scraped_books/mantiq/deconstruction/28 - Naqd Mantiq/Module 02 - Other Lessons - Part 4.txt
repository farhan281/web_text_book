The Domain of Qiyas: Why Theologians Err More Than Jurists
Ibn Taymiyya argues that theologians and philosophers err more because they misuse qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that theologians and philosophers are more prone to error because they apply qiyas to matters beyond its proper scope.

Core Idea
Qiyas is valid only when applied to its proper domain; misuse leads to error and opposition to fitra.

In this part of the passage, Ibn Taymiyya contrasts the error rates of two groups: jurists and physicians on one hand, and theologians and philosophers on the other. He claims error is more common among the latter because they seek to use qiyas (analogical/syllogistic reasoning) to attain knowledge that cannot be reached that way. This leads them to oppose human nature (fitra) and prophethood. Ibn Taymiyya's distinction is crucial: he does not reject all qiyas—he affirms that pure fiqh and medicine are beneficial—but he condemns the speculative use of qiyas in matters that require direct knowledge from revelation or innate intuition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the comparative claim
Find where Ibn Taymiyya compares error rates. He says 'error and mistake among theologians and philosophers is more than among jurists and physicians.' Note this explicit comparative judgement.
2
Find the reason for greater error
He provides a reason: 'they sought through qiyas what cannot be known by qiyas.' This indicates the misuse of the method. They attempt to use analogical reasoning to know things that are beyond its epistemic reach.
What are those things? Likely divine attributes, metaphysical realities that can only be known through revelation or sound fitra.
3
Analyze the consequence: opposition to fitra and prophethood
Ibn Taymiyya says this misuse caused them to oppose fitra and prophethood, turning them into 'devils of humans and jinn.' This is a strong rhetorical condemnation highlighting the dangers of misapplied reasoning.
4
Distinguish between beneficial and harmful qiyas
Ibn Taymiyya contrasts the harmful qiyas of the theologians with pure medicine and pure fiqh, which he calls 'beneficial knowledge.' This shows that he does not reject qiyas altogether; he condemns its speculative extension beyond proper domains.
Fiqh uses qiyas shar'i (analogical reasoning in legal rulings) which is valid, while philosophers use qiyas in metaphysics where it is not applicable.

Commentary
This passage is a critical comparison by Ibn Taymiyya between practical sciences (medicine and fiqh) and speculative disciplines (theology and philosophy). He begins by noting that Hippocrates' medical statements were accepted and empirically confirmed, even though he predated the art of logic. This shows that sound knowledge does not depend on Aristotelian logic. He then acknowledges that natural science (physics) is considered higher than medicine, but it is connected to it: understanding specific physical bodies leads to knowledge of general principles and inference from part to whole. However, he points out that debates between jurists and theologians often occur, but error is more common among theologians and philosophers. Their speech is less beneficial because they attempt to use analogical reasoning (qiyas) to reach knowledge that cannot be attained that way, and they contradict human nature (fitra) and prophetic guidance. This opposition turns them into sources of deception. In contrast, pure medicine and pure fiqh are genuinely beneficial sciences.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Divine Science and Logical Syllogism
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' divine science and logic's usefulness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's arguments against the logicians' divine science and their claim that logical syllogism is essential for knowledge.

Core Idea
Greek logic is useless: it cannot prove metaphysical claims, and no expert in any Islamic science ever used it.

Ibn Taymiyya launches a two-pronged attack on the logicians. First, he argues that their 'divine science' is mostly mental constructs with no external reality, and that its topics—like existence, necessity, the ten categories—cannot be demonstrated by logical syllogism. Second, he provides empirical evidence: no master of any science, from medicine to fiqh, ever relied on Greek logic. This passage challenges the very foundation of the logicians' claim that logic is the indispensable tool for all knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
The logicians claim that divine science (al-ilm al-ilahi) is the goal of philosophy and that its topics can be proven by logical syllogism. Ibn Taymiyya targets this claim.
2
Deconstruct the first argument: divine science is mental, not real
Ibn Taymiyya states that most of divine science is 'knowledge of mental judgments, not external realities' and lists topics like existence, necessity, cause, etc., for which no logical syllogism exists.
He mentions the ten categories as an example of mere enumeration.
3
Understand his critique of the ten categories
He cites a poem listing the categories (substance and nine accidents) through the example of Zayd, then dismisses them as 'mere enumeration' (istiqra), not syllogistic proof.
4
Follow his second argument: logic is useless
He argues that the logicians' craft is either applied to areas that do not need it or to areas where it cannot be used, making it entirely useless and even harmful.
5
Examine his empirical evidence
Ibn Taymiyya claims that no expert in any field—medicine, mathematics, grammar, fiqh, kalam—ever mastered their science using Greek logic. All the great imams predated its translation.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' claims about their 'divine science' (al-ilm al-ilahi) and the usefulness of Greek logic. He begins by dismissing the logicians' boast that their first teacher (Aristotle) declared divine science the goal of philosophy. In reality, Ibn Taymiyya retorts, the truth in that field is scanty, and most of its content is mere mental constructs with no external reality. Furthermore, the topics of metaphysics — existence, necessity, possibility, cause, effect, the division of quiddity into matter and form, and the ten categories — cannot be proven by logical syllogism. To illustrate the ten categories, he cites a poem listing them through the example of a man named Zayd: 'Zayd the tall, the black, son of Malik; in his house yesterday, he was reclining; in his hand a sword, he brandished it, then he ceased.' This enumeration of the categories is at best a mere inductive survey, not a syllogistic demonstration. Ibn Taymiyya then extends his critique: since the logicians' craft (sina'at al-mantiq) is either applied to areas that do not require logical syllogism or to areas where it cannot be applied, it is entirely useless. Worse, it harms people by occupying their hearts from beneficial knowledge and deeds, blocking the path to truth and casting them into error. He then provides an empirical argument: no expert in any field — medicine, mathematics, secretarial arts, grammar, language, prosody, jurisprudence, usul al-fiqh, kalam — ever achieved mastery through logic. All the great imams of these Islamic sciences predated the translation of Greek logic. Even the prophetic sciences are independent of it. The passage ends abruptly with a reference to fiqh and usul al-fiqh being connected, implying the continuity of Islamic knowledge without Greek logic. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya's method is to deconstruct the logicians' own claims and counter with concrete historical evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the superiority of Islamic knowledge over Greek philosophy. The author begins by asserting the manifest benefit of logic in Islamic sciences, noting that Muslims, through the blessing of prophetic revelation, achieved greater perfection in the sciences inherited from the Greeks. He then turns to the origins of logic in Aristotle and the Sabian innovators, describing the division of philosophy into theoretical (mathematics) and practical (ethics, economics, politics). While acknowledging that Greek philosophy has some merit compared to completely ignorant peoples, he argues that those with even a remnant of natural disposition (fitra) are better off than the misguided philosophers. The most misguided among the people of revealed religions, like ignorant Christians and Samaritans, are still superior to the philosophers in knowledge and guidance. This sets the stage for his critique of logic's limited utility.

الفطرة (al-fitrah): Natural disposition; the innate human orientation towards truth and goodness, contrasted with Greek philosophy. [Note: Stable term in Islamic theology; Ibn Taymiyya uses it here to assert superiority of revealed knowledge.]

Commentary
This passage is a theological argument establishing the necessary connection (تلازم) between three core Islamic beliefs: Tawhid (the oneness of Allah), belief in the messengers, and belief in the Last Day. The author, Ibn Taymiyya, uses Quranic evidence to demonstrate that these three are inseparable. He begins by citing verses from Al-A'raf and Ibrahim which show that messengers called to Tawhid and warned against shirk. He then states that the messengers universally commanded Tawhid and forbade worship of created beings. The author draws the key inference: those who disbelieve in the messengers are polytheists (مشركون). Thus, Tawhid and belief in messengers are inseparable (متلازمان). Similarly, belief in the Last Day and belief in messengers are inseparable, making all three inseparable. He supports this with verses like Al-An'am 150 which combine denial of signs and disbelief in the Hereafter with shirk. Then he cites Az-Zumar 45 to show that those who dislike mention of Allah alone are those who do not believe in the Hereafter. He further cites Al-Mulk 8-9 and Az-Zumar 71 to show that messengers warned of the Last Day. Al-An'am 128-130 shows that the warning reached all jinn and humans. Al-Kahf 103-105 equates disbelief in signs (messengers) with disbelief in the meeting with Allah (Last Day). Finally, he cites Fatir 24, An-Nisa 163-165, and Al-An'am 48-49 to assert that the message was universal and that belief in messengers brings security. The argument is cumulative: the Quran repeatedly ties these three together, so they cannot be separated. The author's purpose is to refute any claim that one can accept Tawhid while rejecting prophethood or the afterlife, or vice versa.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Philosophy as Shirk
Ibn Taymiyya links tawhid, iman, and righteous deeds, then attacks philosophers for promoting shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the philosophers and evaluate its internal validity.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya argues that philosophy, not theology, is the root of shirk.

Ibn Taymiyya links tawhid, iman, and righteous deeds as inseparable. He then turns to the Quran to show that the enemies of prophets are devils who embellish false speech, and that those who reject the Hereafter are swayed by them. His target is the philosophers (mutafalsifa), whom he accuses of originating shirk through their teachings on talismans and idols.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The philosophers' tawheed is a verbal claim that negates Allah's attributes, making it a cause of shirk, not its cure.

Ibn Taymiyya exposes a fundamental error in the philosophers' concept of tawheed. They claim to affirm God's oneness, but their tawheed is merely verbal—a matter of speech, not action. In this lesson, we will analyze his critique, focusing on the contrast between verbal affirmation and the worship-based tawheed brought by the messengers. Guided Path

Identify the philosophers' claim

Contrast with prophetic tawheed
Ibn Taymiyya states that the tawheed brought by the messengers necessarily includes 'ikhlas al-din' (sincere devotion) and worshipping Allah alone. This is an integral, non-negotiable part. The philosophers, he asserts, do not know this aspect.

4
Assess the consequence
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that this ta'til contains such disbelief and misguidance that it is among the greatest causes of associating partners with Allah. The very tool they claim upholds tawheed actually facilitates shirk.
This is the core logical move: a position that claims tawheed but leads to its opposite is self-defeating.

The meaning of 'sultan' as revelation and its role in argumentation
Ibn Taymiyya defines 'sultan' as revelation and uses it to critique disputation without divine authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's redefinition of 'sultan' as divine proof and its implications for valid argumentation.

Core Idea
True authority is revelation, not human reason or power.

Ibn Taymiyya reinterprets the Quranic term 'sultan' (often meaning authority) as divine revelation. He supports this with Ibn Abbas's statement that every 'sultan' in the Quran is the proof (hujja). This redefinition is central to his critique of philosophers who argue without divine authority.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of 'sultan'
In the passage, Ibn Taymiyya explicitly says: 'And 'authority' (sultan) is the revelation sent down from Allah.' This is after quoting verses about disputation without authority.
2
Identify the supporting evidence
He cites Quranic verses: 'Or have We sent down to them an authority...' (Ar-Rum 35) and 'He has not sent down any authority for it.' (Al-A'raf 71). Also, he mentions Ibn Abbas's statement recorded by al-Bukhari that every 'sultan' in the Quran is 'the proof' (al-hujja).
3
Understand the implication for argumentation
If 'sultan' means revelation, then any argument not based on revelation lacks true authority. This is why disputation without revelation is condemned.
The term 'jadal' (disputation) in the passage is linked to those who argue without revelation.
4
Connect to the critique of philosophers
Ibn Taymiyya applies this to the philosophers (falāsifa) who use logical arguments not grounded in revelation. They are like those described in the Quran who dispute without 'sultan'.
5
Apply the concept
Consider any argument in Islamic theology: if it does not derive its authority from revelation, it is baseless. This is a key principle in Ibn Taymiyya's methodology.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Intellect Without Revelation
Ibn Taymiyya uses Quranic verses to argue against the sufficiency of intellect without revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the sufficiency of intellect without revelation and explain his use of Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
Worldly strength without revelation is worthless.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that rational strength and worldly power do not guarantee truth or salvation. In this passage, he uses Quranic verses to show that even nations superior in intellect and power were destroyed because they rejected the messengers. This lesson examines how Ibn Taymiyya employs scriptural evidence to refute the claim that human intellect alone can attain guidance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Find the claim that Ibn Taymiyya is attacking. It is the belief that intellectual strength and worldly power are sufficient for success without following the messengers.
The target is implied: the Hanafi verdict on Ibn Sina and the Quranic examples.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
Ibn Taymiyya cites multiple verses from Ghafir, Ar-Rum, Al-An'am, and others. Each verse shows a nation that was stronger and more knowledgeable but still perished when they rejected the messengers.
Pay attention to the phrase 'قوّة' which encompasses both theoretical and practical power.
3
Analyse the grounds of critique
Determine the type of objection: It is revelation-based. Ibn Taymiyya uses the Quran as authoritative evidence to demonstrate the failure of worldly strength without prophecy.
He does not use logical argument but scriptural narrative.
4
Assess the internal validity
Check if the argument is self-consistent. Ibn Taymiyya presupposes the truth of the Quran. Within his own framework, the argument is valid because scripture is the ultimate source of knowledge.
This step is about understanding the epistemological foundation of the critique.

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of External Composition
Author refutes the claim that external reality is composed of genus and differentia

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against external composition by genus and differentia and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
Genus and differentia are mental abstractions, not external parts.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the very foundation of Aristotelian definitions: the claim that genus and differentia compose real entities. He forces a choice: either this composition is in the external world or in the mind. By dismantling the external possibility, he shows that what logicians take as real components are only conceptual tools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim under attack
The logicians assert that reality (haqiqa) is composed of genus (jins) and differentia (fasl) – genus as common part, differentia as distinguishing part. Locate this claim in the Arabic: 'الحقيقة مركبة من الجنس والفصل'.
2
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's dilemma
He poses a conditional disjunction: the composition is either in the external world (al-kharij) or in the mind (al-dhihn). Recognize this as a disjunctive syllogism – if one is false, the other must be true.
3
Trace the refutation of external composition
Ibn Taymiyya argues: in the external world only individual sensible beings exist. In each human, there is one substance with two attributes (e.g., sensation/motion and speech). If 'animality' and 'rationality' were separate substances, there would be two substances in one human, which is false. Thus external composition fails.
He also notes the impossibility of a substance composed of two accidents.
4
Note the analogy to Christian hypostases
Ibn Taymiyya compares the logicians' alternating between calling genus/differentia substance and attribute to the Christian doctrine of hypostases (aqanim), which he calls highly contradictory. This exposes internal inconsistency in the logicians' position.
5
Assess the force of the critique
The argument successfully eliminates the external option, forcing the logician to admit that composition is only in the mind. Though the text cuts off, the implication is clear: the mental composition is merely conceptual, not real.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: Differentia vs. Common Meaning
Author critiques logicians who consider only common meanings without distinguishing differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error of logicians who rely solely on common meanings without considering the distinguishing differentia, and apply Ibn Taymiyya's critique to a case in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Ignoring the differentia while focusing on common meaning is the root of flawed analogy.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya attacks a fundamental error in the logicians' method: they consider only the common meanings shared by species and individuals, neglecting the unique distinguishing features (al-fāriq al-mumayyiz). Using the example of human nutq (speech/reason) versus animal capacities, he shows that even when there is a 'common measure', the realities differ in essence. This lesson will walk you through his argument and equip you to recognize this fallacy in analogical reasoning within Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common meaning (al-ma‘nā al-mushtarak) in the passage

Common meaning is the basis of the logicians' analogy.

Examine the instances: human vs. animal and human vs. plant
Contrast the specific realities: human nutq involves soul and body with discernment and will; horse movement lacks such depth. Human growth involves pleasure and intention; plant growth is mechanical.
Ibn Taymiyya uses these to show that common meaning does not guarantee identical reality.

Extract Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion about common meanings
He states that the sharing of names across categories does not entail equality of meaning in external reality; the mind perceives similarity (mushābaha), not equivalence (mumāthala).
This is the epistemological core of his critique.

This shows the practical danger of neglecting differentiae.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' method of analogical reasoning (qiyās) that focuses on common meanings while neglecting the unique differentiating features (al-fāriq al-mumayyiz) of individuals. Ibn Taymiyya begins by analyzing human nutq (speech/reason) as a capacity belonging to both the soul (discernment, knowledge, inner speech) and the body (the heart's discernment and spoken expression). Although humans and animals share a common measure (qadr mushtarak) in attributes like sensation and movement, the specific reality of these attributes in humans is fundamentally different: a human's sensation and voluntary acts are animated by will, purpose, and pleasure in a way that an animal's are not. Ibn Taymiyya then cites the hadith about names to illustrate the uniquely human characteristics of striving (al-ḥarth) and intending (al-hamm). He extends the argument to growth and nourishment: while plants also grow, human growth is tied to sensory perception, movement, and intentional pursuit. Within the human species, there is even variation: the Arab's speech and discernment may be more complete than others, and some humans are intellectually inferior to beasts. The core critique emerges in the final part: the fact that diverse individuals (afrād), varieties (aṣnāf), and species (anwā') fall under broader categories (ajnās) does not imply that the common meaning (al-ma'nā al-mushtarak) is identical or equal across them. In external reality, there is no 'common thing' shared; the mind merely perceives similarity (mushābaha), not equivalence (mumāthala). The error of the 'logicians' (al-qiyāsiyyūn) is to consider only the commonality and ignore the distinguishing differential, leading to flawed analogical inferences. The Arabs and Muslims, by contrast, excel at discerning differences while acknowledging commonalities, and this is reflected in their cognitive faculties, language, sciences, and legal rulings. This is why Muslim theologians debated the Sabian Persian theologians effectively: they did not fall into the trap of false analogies.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents listing the chapters and main topics covered in Ibn Taymiyya's 'Naqd al-Mantiq' (Critique of Logic). It provides an overview of the book's structure, starting with discussions on the virtue of the first three generations of Islam, criticisms of certain Ash'ari and philosophical positions, and detailed refutations of Ibn al-Jawzi's views on attributes. The latter part of the index focuses on logic itself, including sections on the corruption of logic, the claim that learning it is a collective obligation, the condemnation of logic by Islamic scholars, and a critique of the five syllogisms and analogical reasoning. The passage serves as a roadmap for the reader, indicating the book's critical stance against Greek logic and its defense of traditional Islamic reasoning methods.
