Ibn Taymiyya's Three Meanings of Ta'wil
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes three meanings of 'ta'wil' and rebukes those who misuse the term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of ta'wil as defined by Ibn Taymiyya and identify the misuse of the term by various groups.

Core Idea
Ta'wil has three distinct meanings; reducing it to one is a linguistic and theological error.

Ibn Taymiyya launches a critique against those who restrict ta'wil to a meaning contrary to the apparent sense of a text. He argues that the Quranic usage and the tafsir tradition show two other meanings. This lesson will guide you through his tripartite distinction and the logical consequences of ignoring it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the groups being critiqued
Who are the two groups Ibn Taymiyya rebukes? What do they claim about ta'wil? One group says texts are taken literally but ta'wil is known only to Allah; the other equates apparent meaning with wording.
2
Extract the first meaning of ta'wil
What is the Quranic and prophetic usage? How does it differ from the common misconception? It means the ultimate reality, not necessarily contrary to apparent meaning.
See Quran 7:53 and 'A'isha's hadith.
3
Extract the second meaning
How do many mufassirun use ta'wil? What evidence does Ibn Taymiyya provide? He cites Mujahid's statement that the firmly grounded know ta'wil of mutashabih, meaning tafsir.
4
Extract the third meaning
What is the technical mutakallimun meaning? How does it relate to the apparent meaning? It always contradicts the apparent meaning and requires external evidence.
5
Analyze the critique
Why does Ibn Taymiyya say both groups are mistaken? How does his tripartite distinction resolve the confusion? The contradiction arises from assuming ta'wil only means the third sense.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the term 'ta'wil' by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by rebuking two groups who misuse the term based on a misunderstanding of the Quranic verse 'And none knows its interpretation except Allah.' One group claims that texts are taken literally but their true ta'wil (which they define as contrary to the apparent meaning) is known only to Allah. Another group equates the apparent meaning with the wording alone. Ibn Taymiyya points out the contradiction in the first group and the error in both. Then he clarifies that the word 'ta'wil' has three distinct meanings due to different terminologies. The first meaning is the ultimate reality or outcome of the speech, even if it is consistent with the apparent meaning. This is the Quranic and prophetic usage, as seen in the verse about the Day of Judgment and 'A'isha's hadith where she describes the Prophet 'interpreting' the Quran in his supplication. The second meaning is tafsir (exegesis), which is the common usage among Quran commentators. He cites Mujahid's statement that the firmly grounded in knowledge know the ta'wil of ambiguous verses, meaning they know its interpretation. The third meaning is the technical one used in logic and kalam: turning the word away from its apparent meaning to a contrary meaning based on a separate evidence. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that this third meaning always contradicts the apparent meaning. The passage serves to expose the error of those who restrict ta'wil to the third meaning and ignore the first two, thereby misunderstanding the Quranic verse.

Three Meanings of Ta'wil and Ibn Taymiyya's Critique
Author defines three meanings of ta'wil and critiques the later technical usage as distortion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of ta'wil as defined by Ibn Taymiyya and reconstruct his critique of the later technical usage as a distortion of the Quranic and salaf understanding.

Core Idea
Ta'wil shifted from reality and tafsir to an arbitrary departure from apparent meaning.

The term ta'wil has a rich history, but not all meanings are equal. Ibn Taymiyya clarifies three distinct usages, warning against a later technical definition that opens the door to distorting the Quran. This lesson will walk you through each meaning and the critique that follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first meaning: the actual reality (al-waqi')
Find the Quranic verse cited (7:53) and the hadith of Aisha. Ibn Taymiyya uses these to show that ta'wil originally referred to the actual event or reality that a verse speaks about, especially eschatological events. This meaning is known only to Allah until it occurs.
The Prophet's supplication 'interpreting' the Quran is an example of applying the verse's meaning in action.
2
Identify the second meaning: tafsir (exegesis)
Ibn Taymiyya states that many commentators use ta'wil synonymously with tafsir, i.e., explanation of meanings. He supports this with the quote from Mujahid, who said that the firmly grounded in knowledge know the ta'wil of ambiguous verses, meaning their tafsir.
This meaning is legitimate and does not require departing from apparent meaning.
3
Identify the third meaning: the later technical term
Read the description: 'diverting the word from its apparent meaning to something contrary due to an external evidence.' This is the usage introduced by later scholars of fiqh, usul, and kalam. Note that Ibn Taymiyya says this was not in the usage of the salaf.
This meaning is the target of his critique.
4
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the third meaning
He argues that this technical ta'wil often distorts words from their rightful places, akin to the esoteric interpretations of the Qaramita and Batiniyya. He cites Imam Ahmad's book refuting those who interpreted the ambiguous verses incorrectly. Both groups (those who say only Allah knows this ta'wil and those who say the firmly grounded know it) are mistaken because the premise is false: this ta'wil is not a legitimate category.
The critique is based on historical usage and the danger of arbitrariness.
5
Recognize the two erroneous positions that follow from the third meaning
The passage explains that later scholars, assuming the Quranic verse refers to their technical ta'wil, split into two camps: one saying only Allah knows it, another saying the firmly grounded know it. Ibn Taymiyya rejects both because the underlying concept is flawed. The correct view is that the third meaning is an innovation and should not be called ta'wil.
The salaf never used ta'wil in this way.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya (likely from his Naqd al-Mantiq) on the term 'ta'wil.' He distinguishes three usages. The first is the Quranic usage in Surah al-A'raf (7:53) referring to the actual future event that the verse speaks of—a reality that will come to pass on the Day of Judgment. He supports this with the hadith of Aisha where the Prophet's supplication 'interpreted' the Quran. The second meaning is 'tafsir' or exegesis, which is the common usage among Quranic commentators; he cites Mujahid, who states that the firmly grounded in knowledge know the ta'wil of the ambiguous verses, meaning they understand their interpretation. The third meaning, which is the focus of his critique, is the later technical usage among scholars of usul al-fiqh and kalam: interpreting a word in a way contrary to its apparent meaning based on an external evidence. Ibn Taymiyya argues that this third meaning was unknown to the early Muslims (salaf); it was innovated by later scholars. These later scholars mistakenly thought that the Quranic verse about ta'wil refers to this technical meaning, leading to two erroneous positions: one group says only Allah knows this ta'wil, another says the firmly grounded know it. Ibn Taymiyya condemns both, asserting that this kind of ta'wil is essentially distorting words from their proper places, akin to the esoteric interpretations of the Qaramita and Batiniyya. He cites Imam Ahmad's book 'al-Radd 'ala al-Zanadiqa wa al-Jahmiyya' which refuted those who interpreted the ambiguous verses incorrectly. This passage guards against the error of conflating legitimate tafsir with arbitrary allegorical interpretation that deviates from the apparent meaning without proper evidence. The author insists that the Prophet and the early Muslims knew the meanings of the Quran, and no imam claimed otherwise.

The Two Types of Speech: Informative and Performative
Author classifies speech into informative and performative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any utterance as either khabar (informative) or insha' (performative) based on Ibn Taymiyya's definitions.

Core Idea
Speech is either truth-apt informative or non-truth-apt performative.

Logic begins with classifying speech. Ibn Taymiyya divides all speech into two fundamental types: informative (khabar) and performative (insha'). This classification is not merely theoretical; it affects how we evaluate the truth of statements and the validity of commands. In this lesson, you will learn the precise definitions and test them against examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the first sentence: 'والأقوال: إخبارات، وإنشاءات كالأمر والنهي'. The author explicitly names two types of speech.
The example given for insha' is command and prohibition.
2
Extract the defining features
For khabar, the passage implies it can be true or false (أصدق الخبر). For insha', it is not truth-apt; rather, it is evaluated by its validity (e.g., a command can be just or unjust). Identify these features from the text.
3
Apply the classification to the Qur'an
The author states that the Qur'an contains both: its news is most truthful (khabar) and its command is most just (insha'). This shows how the classification applies to a real text.
Notice the verse quote: 'فَبِأَيِّ حَدِيثٍ بَعْدَ اللَّهِ وَآيَاتِهِ يُؤْمِنُونَ'.
4
Test with new examples
Consider a statement like 'Zakat is obligatory.' Is it khabar or insha'? According to this classification, it is a khabar (an informative statement about a ruling), even though it has prescriptive force. The classification focuses on the form of the utterance, not its effect.
In usul al-fiqh, this distinction is crucial for understanding how legal texts communicate.

Why the Qur'an Excels Both Types of Speech
Author asserts Qur'an's supremacy in both speech types and condemns rivals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the Qur'an is superior to any other discourse and identify the logical basis for his condemnation of rival claims.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is the benchmark for truthfulness in both informative and performative speech.

After classifying speech into khabar and insha', Ibn Taymiyya makes a bold evaluative claim: the Qur'an is the best and most truthful in both categories. He then draws a sharp line: anyone who follows any other speech that contradicts the Qur'an is among the greatest oppressors. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind that conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard of evaluation
The author states 'أحسنُ الحديث وأصدقه كتاب الله' – the best and most truthful speech is the Book of Allah. He uses superlatives to establish a standard.
2
Break down the two criteria
He separates the informative aspect ('خبره أصدق الخبر') from the performative aspect ('وأمره أحكَمُ الأمر'). This means the Qur'an is supreme in both truth-content and normative guidance.
Notice the parallel structure: each type of speech is evaluated by its own excellence.
3
Analyze the rhetorical question
The verse 'فَبِأَيِّ حَدِيثٍ بَعْدَ اللَّهِ وَآيَاتِهِ يُؤْمِنُونَ' is a challenge: if the Qur'an already provides perfect speech, what other narration could be needed? This implies that no other speech can rival it.
The rhetorical question functions as a logical argument: if the Qur'an is perfect, then anything else is superfluous or inferior.
4

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of an opponent who accuses followers of the Salafi madhhab of 'tastar' (concealing themselves behind the label) and who restricts the Salafi madhhab to tawhid and tanzih, excluding tajsim and tashbih. Ibn Taymiyya begins by turning the accusation back on the accuser: if the opponent blames those whom he acknowledges as the best of the Ummah (the Salaf), he is like the Khawarij who accused the Prophet of injustice—a self-defeating position. He then examines the term 'tastar' in three scenarios: if the accuser has power, then the concealment is due to his oppression; if he is weak, then blame is pointless; if he is neither, there is no basis for the accusation. He clarifies that openly professing the Salafi madhhab is praiseworthy and must be accepted externally, leaving internal states to Allah. Finally, he addresses the opponent's claim about tawhid and tanzih. Ibn Taymiyya points out that these terms are ambiguous (mushtarak) because different theological schools define them differently. The Jahmiyya and Mu'tazila equate tawhid with negating all attributes, labeling any affirmation as tajsim/tashbih. The Sifatiyya (those who affirm attributes) use the terms to distinguish between affirming and denying only the transmitted attributes. Thus, the opponent's assertion that the Salafi madhhab is only tawhid and tanzih without tajsim and tashbih is meaningless without specifying which definition is intended. The passage demonstrates Ibn Taymiyya's method of critiquing through logical analysis of language and exposing the contextual relativity of polemical labels.

The Inseparability of Tawheed, Risala, and Akhira
Ibn Taymiyya uses Quranic evidence to prove the inseparability of tawheed, belief in messengers, and the Last Day.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three pillars that Ibn Taymiyya argues are mutually entailing and trace his inductive Quranic method for proving their inseparability.

Core Idea
Tawheed, belief in messengers, and belief in the Last Day are one inseparable whole.

Ibn Taymiyya, in his critique of Greek logic, does not only tear down; he builds. Here, he uses a cascade of Quranic verses to show that the core of Islam is an indivisible triad. Understanding his method is key to seeing how he grounds theology in scripture rather than abstract reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument within Ibn Taymiyya's broader critique of Greek logic and philosophy. Here, he uses extensive Quranic evidence to demonstrate that the core principles of Islam—tawheed (monotheism), belief in the messengers (risala), and belief in the Last Day (akhira)—are inseparable. He begins by referencing a previous point about those who engage in logic ("this industry") and states that their efforts are insufficient for salvation, let alone achieving the bliss of the Hereafter. Then he launches into a series of Quranic verses. The verse from Al-A'raf (7:38) shows that those who followed leaders astray will receive double punishment. The verse from Ghafir (40:82-85) indicates that when the disbelievers saw Allah's punishment, they affirmed tawheed but it did not avail them because they had rejected the messengers earlier. He then gives the example of Pharaoh from Yunus (10:90-91): when drowning, Pharaoh declared faith in the God of Israel, but Allah rejected it because he had previously disbelieved and was a corrupter. This shows that faith at the time of punishment is not accepted if one previously rejected the messengers. Next, he cites the covenant of the Children of Adam from Al-A'raf (7:172-173), where Allah took a pledge from them to affirm His lordship, and the verse from Ibrahim (14:9-10), where the messengers were met with the accusation of being mere humans trying to turn people away from ancestral worship. Ibn Taymiyya then generalizes: the Quran repeatedly shows that all messengers commanded tawheed and forbade shirk, and that believers in tawheed are the people of happiness while polytheists are miserable. He concludes that tawheed and belief in the messengers are mutually entailing (multaziman), and so are belief in the Last Day and belief in the messengers. Therefore, all three are interdependent. He supports this with Al-An'am (6:150), which links denying signs and disbelief in the Last Day with shirk (equating others with Allah), and Az-Zumar (39:45), which states that those who do not believe in the Hereafter are averse to the mention of Allah alone. Finally, Al-Mulk (67:8-9) shows that the messengers warned the inhabitants of Hell, but they denied the message. The overall structure is inductive: from specific Quranic examples to a general principle of interdependence. The argument is not a formal syllogism but a cumulative scriptural proof, common in Ibn Taymiyya's methodology.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Necessity of Definitions
Ibn Taymiyya argues against the necessity of definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's twin arguments (circularity and infinite regress) against the logicians' claim that definitions are necessary for conception, and evaluate their strength.

Core Idea
Definitions cannot be the sole path to knowledge—circularity or infinite regress would result.

Ibn Taymiyya targets a foundational claim of Aristotelian logic: that all non-self-evident conceptions require definitions. In this passage, he deploys two devastating objections. The first is a logical regress argument showing that the logicians' claim leads to either circularity or infinite regress. The second is an empirical observation: logicians have never agreed on a single definition, so if definitions were necessary, no one would know anything. Together, these arguments undermine the epistemological necessity of definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Find the phrase: 'Non-self-evident conception can only be attained through a definition.' This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
2
Reconstruct the regress argument
Ibn Taymiyya asks: How did the definer acquire the definition? If they already knew the defined without a definition, the claim is false. If they used another definition, that leads to circularity (dawr) if the definitions depend on each other, or infinite regress (tasalsul) if each definition requires a prior one.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Understand the empirical objection
Ibn Taymiyya observes that logicians have not produced a single agreed-upon definition. If things are only known through definitions, then no one has any knowledge—an absurd consequence.
This challenges the practical success of the claim.
4
Evaluate the critique's internal validity
Check if Ibn Taymiyya's own reasoning is sound. The regress argument is logically valid: if P leads to an impossibility, then not P. The empirical objection is inductive but raises a plausible doubt.
5
Summarize the significance
Ibn Taymiyya does not reject definitions entirely; he rejects the claim that they are the exclusive or necessary means to acquire non-self-evident conceptions.

This aligns with the Islamic emphasis on empirical and rational knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument from Ibn Taymiyya against the logical doctrine of definition (ḥadd). He opens by denying that definitions provide any unique cognitive benefit beyond what names already convey. He uses the example of 'man' (insān): saying 'Man is a rational animal' (ḥayawān nāṭiq) does not give any more discrimination than the simple name 'man' or 'human being' (bashar), except in terms of explicit comprehension and restriction. The name, when understood, already distinguishes the referent from others. Therefore, the supposed benefit of a definition—precise demarcation—is already achieved by names themselves.

He then addresses a potential counter: that definitions reveal the general and specific meanings or attributes. Ibn Taymiyya replies that this is not exclusive to definitions; it can be known through other means. Whatever a definition provides is already available in names or in descriptive sentences (al-jumal al-murakkaba). These two types cover all knowledge; neither requires the artificial procedure of definition. Hence, the definition has no special utility—its benefit is already present in ordinary language without effort.

Then he turns to the ninth aspect: the distinction between essential (dhātī) and accidental (ʿaraḍī) attributes. He grants that there are shared and specific attributes, but the claim that some are essential (constitutive of the essence) and others are accidental (necessary but not constitutive) is false. All attributes that accompany the defined (maḥdūd) both positively (ṭardan) and negatively (ʿaksan) are of one genus. Thus, no real difference exists between differentia (faṣl) and property (khāṣṣa), or between genus (jins) and common accident (ʿaraḍ ʿāmm). To illustrate, he considers the attributes of speech (nuṭq) and laughter (ḍaḥk) in man. Both are necessary and specific. If the external essence is meant, both are equally true. If the mental essence, both are equally comprehended. The logicians' claim that one (speech) is essential because the essence is inconceivable without it is merely a psychological or relative matter—the mind's order of understanding, not an objective feature of the thing itself. Thus, the whole division is baseless.

Refutation of Mental Order Criterion for Essential vs Accidental

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Essential vs accidental cannot be determined by mental priority; objective reality alone decides.

Ibn Taymiyya attacks a key pillar of Aristotelian logic: the method logicians use to tell apart essential attributes (those that form part of a thing's quiddity) from accidental ones. They claim that whatever concept the mind must grasp first is essential, and what comes later is accidental. Understanding his critique will sharpen your ability to evaluate definitions and avoid arbitrary reasoning.

Guided Path
1

4

He asserts that the real distinction must be based on a description fixed in the thing itself, independent of perception. What is essential is part of the quiddity; what is accidental is not – regardless of mental priority.
5

Ibn Taymiyya's argument is internally consistent: he uses empirical observation (varying mental orders) and logical reasoning (if the order is stipulated, it is arbitrary). The critique does not rely on the very methods it attacks; it appeals to common sense and reality.

Even if grasping a universal with many individuals is easier absolutely, it requires knowing each individual. The parts of a definition (like 'animal') have many individuals, so they are harder to grasp as universal. Thus, defining a species by its genus and differentia is defining the easier (the species) by the harder (the parts), which is contrary to proper method.

Definitions Must Distinguish: The Principle of Ease
Author argues definitions require distinction, and fewer individuals ease distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition violates the principle of distinction by defining the easier through the harder.

Core Idea
Defining the easy by the hard is the cardinal sin of logic.

The author presents a crisp epistemological critique: if definitions are meant to clarify, they must not introduce more difficulty than the original term. Here he argues that using broader universals (which are harder to grasp) defeats the purpose. Let's break down his reasoning and see why this matters for Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Find the sentence that states the necessary condition for definitions. It is: 'الحدود لا بد فيها من التمييز' – definitions inevitably require distinction.
Distinction (tamyiz) means separating the definiendum from everything else.
2
Follow the comparative ease argument
The author links ease to number of individuals: fewer individuals make distinction easier, more make it harder. This is an intuitive psychological claim.
He even concedes that perceiving a universal absolutely might be easier, but that is because the absolute is obtained from each individual.
3
Locate the consequence for definitions
The parts of a definition (genus and differentia) are often broader universals. 'فإذا كان ضبطها كلية أصعب وأتعب من ضبط أفراد المحدود' – if grasping them universally is harder than grasping the individuals of the definiendum, then the definition is backwards.
This is the heart of the critique: definitions become harder than the thing defined.

The author concludes: 'فكان ذلك تعريفا للأسهل معرفة بالأصعب معرفة' – that is defining the easier to know by the harder to know, which is contrary to what is obligatory.
The obligation is that definitions should elucidate, not obscure.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' doctrine regarding shared essential attributes (sifat dhatiyya mushtaraka). Ibn Taymiyya presents his sixteenth objection. He begins by considering the attribute of animality (hayawaniyya) and rationality (natiqiyya) as examples. He identifies two possible interpretations of 'shared' (ishtirak). First, if the logicians claim that the very attribute existing externally is numerically identical across individuals, he deems this false because determinate individuals (mu'ayyanat) cannot have a numerically shared attribute—each individual is unique and conceptualization prevents sharing. Second, if they mean that a similar attribute (mithl tilka al-sifa) is present in the other species, he then argues that there is indeed a shared aspect (qadr mushtarak) between the animality of man and horse, but also a distinguishing aspect (qadr mumayyiz). He draws an analogy: man and horse both have sounds and both have discrimination (tamyiz), but man's sound is speech (natiq) and horse's is neighing (sahil); each sound is designated by a specific name. Just as the realities of these sounds differ, so too should the animalities differ in definition (hadd) and reality (haqiqa). He then proposes that instead of claiming exact similarity, one should say there is both a shared and a distinguishing aspect in animality, just as in sounds. He concludes by noting that sensation and voluntary movement can be attributed to either the body or the soul, but there remains a difference between the two descriptions (body vs. soul) as between two realities. The passage guards against the simplistic notion that essential attributes are univocally shared across species, emphasizing that even where there is commonality, there is also specific difference.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logicians' Definitions
Ibn Taymiyya critiques logicians' definitions and distinguishes two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Ibn Taymiyya's two categories of definitions (unproductive formal definitions vs. legitimate scholarly definitions of terms) and explain his epistemological grounds for rejecting the former.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya does not reject all definitions—only those that obscure rather than clarify.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya launches a sharp critique against the logicians' approach to definitions (hudud). He argues that these formal definitions are not only wasteful but actually harmful: they make things less clear and lead to endless, inconclusive disputes. Yet he does not dismiss all definitional inquiry; he distinguishes between definitions that merely 'distinguish' the defined (which he rejects) and definitions that clarify the scope of a term (hadd al-ism), which he considers a legitimate subject of scholarly discussion. Understanding this nuance is crucial for engaging with his broader project of reforming Islamic intellectual methods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique
Look for the phrase 'عامة الحدود' (most definitions) and note that he is criticizing the logicians' formal definitions, not all definitions per se. He describes them as 'حشو لكلام كثير' (filled with much talk).
2
Extract his main objection
Ibn Taymiyya lists several problems: definitions waste time, exhaust the thinker, cause blindness and misguidance, and open the door to disputation. Crucially, he says things are clearer before explanation than after. Identify each consequence in the text.
3
Find the distinction between two types of definitions
Look for the transition at 'هذا في الحدود التي تشترك في تمييز المحدود' (This concerns definitions that share in distinguishing the defined). Then note the contrast with 'وأما متى أدخل أحدهما في الحد ما أخرجه الآخر' (But when one includes in the definition what the other excludes). The first type is merely distinguishing (tamyeez); the second type involves inclusion/exclusion and is the proper subject of scholarly discussion.
The first type is what logicians do; the second is what scholars do.
4
Apply the distinction to an example
Consider the example of khamr (wine). The debate over whether khamr is specific to grape juice or includes all intoxicants is an example of the second type (hadd al-ism). Ibn Taymiyya accepts this kind of discussion. In contrast, a logician's definition of khamr in terms of genus and differentia would fall under the first type and be rejected.
The Prophet's definition of gheebah is given as a model of clear, concise definition that does not cause confusion.

Commentary
This is a critical passage where Ibn Taymiyya attacks the logicians' approach to definitions (hudud). He begins by stating that most definitions are verbose, filling with unnecessary talk, and actually make things less clear than they were before. He argues that they waste time and exhaust the thinker, leading only to blindness and misguidance. Moreover, they open the door to disputation (mira' and jidal) because each logician brings objections against the other's definition, claiming his own is safe, but upon investigation they are all equal or close, with no clear preference. Thus, Ibn Taymiyya says, either all definitions are accepted, all rejected, or accepted from some angle. He then distinguishes two types of definitions: those that merely distinguish the defined from other things (which he rejects as useless), and those that involve inclusion and exclusion, such as defining whether wine (khamr) is specifically fermented grape juice or every intoxicant. This latter type, he says, is a proper subject of scholarly discussion, as exemplified by the Prophet's definition of backbiting (gheebah) as 'mentioning your brother with what he dislikes.' The critique is that the logicians' definitions are not the exclusive path to knowledge; rather, the prophetic method of simple, clear description is sufficient and superior.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logicians' Definitions
Author critiques logicians' definitions as merely verbal explanations, not conveying essences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Ibn Taymiyya's concept of definition (as verbal explanation) from the logicians' concept of definition (as essence-revealing), and identify which type a given Islamic definition belongs to.

Core Idea
Verbal definitions explain names, not essences.

When the Prophet defined 'khamr' as 'whatever clouds the intellect', he did not provide a genus and differentia in the Aristotelian sense—he gave a verbal explanation. Ibn Taymiyya seizes on this to argue that all definitions are essentially clarifications of names, not revelations of realities. In this lesson, we will unpack his critique and learn to distinguish between defining a name and defining a thing.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's main claim
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that all definitions by speech are definitions of the name (hadd al-ism), akin to translation or clarification. They do not convey the essence of the thing. Locate this claim in the Arabic: 'فكل ما كان من حد بالقول فإنما هو حد للاسم'.
2
Analyze his supporting examples
He cites the Prophet's definition of khamr as 'what clouds the intellect' and the definition of pride as 'rejecting the truth and disdaining people.' Notice that these do not list genus and differentia; they describe properties. Ask yourself: Do they succeed in telling you what the thing is, or just what it does?
3
Distinguish hadd al-ism from hadd al-haqiqa
For Ibn Taymiyya, a real definition (hadd al-haqiqa) would reveal the essence, but he claims logicians' definitions only mention some attributes. The phrase 'ليس فيه من التمييز إلا ذكر بعض الصفات' indicates that the alleged real definition merely lists a few properties without real distinction.
In standard logic, hadd haqiqi gives genus and differentia. Ibn Taymiyya reduces it to a weak description.
4
Evaluate the epistemological implication
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that verbal definitions cannot produce new knowledge of essences for someone who has not already conceived the thing. Consider: If you have never tasted wine, does the definition 'intoxicating beverage from grapes' give you its reality?
This challenges the foundational claim of the logicians that definitions are instruments for acquiring new concepts.
