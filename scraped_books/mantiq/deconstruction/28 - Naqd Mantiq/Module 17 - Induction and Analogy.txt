Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Logic: The Early Muslims as Proof
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the early Muslims attained knowledge without logic, and logic causes confusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of Aristotelian logic and identify the epistemological basis of his argument.

Core Idea
The early Muslims achieved perfect knowledge without logic, proving it is unnecessary and often harmful.

Ibn Taymiyya, in his Naqd al-Mantiq, attacks the claim that formal logic is essential for sound knowledge. He points to the early Muslim generations, who were the best of this ummah, yet they never studied Greek logic. Their knowledge was deeper and more certain. But Ibn Taymiyya is not just making a historical observation; he is building an epistemological argument. In this lesson, we will unpack his reasoning and see how he uses historical evidence, induction, and a comparison with grammar to undermine the pretensions of the logicians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of the critique
Find where Ibn Taymiyya defines logic as an 'artificial craft' (صناعة اصطلاحية) and note that he distinguishes it from sound innate reasoning. He is not rejecting all reasoning, only the formal Greek system.
He says logic makes 'what is near to knowledge far, and what is easy difficult'.
2
Locate the historical evidence
Ibn Taymiyya states that the best three generations never paid attention to logic, yet they were the deepest in knowledge. This is an empirical observation that logic is not necessary.
He uses the phrase 'بالاستقراء' (by induction) to describe this observation.
3
Analyze the induction and its conclusion
He claims that those who engage in logic are the most confused and doubtful. Any truth they achieve comes from sound premises and intellect, not from logic.
This is a sweeping empirical claim; discuss its strength and scope.
4
Examine the concession and the comparison with grammar
He concedes that logic can help someone leave a worse state, but that is not a recommendation. He then compares logic to grammar, arguing that grammar is more precise and deals with forms of meanings, whereas logic is an artificial craft about innate matters.
This comparison underlines his view that logic is superfluous for those with sound fitra.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of logic by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by stating that the early Muslim generations (the three best centuries) were the pinnacle of knowledge and hearts, yet they had no need for logic. Their knowledge was deeper and more certain than anything produced by logicians. He then claims, based on induction (istiqra'), that those who engage in logic are the most doubtful and confused, and any true knowledge they attain comes from sound premises and their own intellect, not from logic. Introducing logic into sound sciences only makes them more difficult and verbose. He acknowledges that logic might help someone leave a worse state, like a polytheist becoming a Magian, but that is not a recommendation for people of truth. Finally, he compares logic to grammar, arguing that grammar is more precise and beneficial because it deals with the forms of meanings, while logic is an artificial craft about innate matters that do not need special conventions. The comparison uses the example of the early Muslims' natural faculty (fitra) being sufficient for knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Universal Syllogism for Particular Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the primacy of universal syllogism for particular knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that analogical reasoning and sensory perception provide more certain knowledge of particulars than universal syllogism, and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
Particular knowledge flows better from sense and analogy than from universal syllogisms.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the logicians' claim that their universal syllogism (qiyas shumul) is the ultimate measure of all knowledge. In this passage, he presents his 'second aspect': for particular, concrete things, sense perception and analogical reasoning are not only sufficient but superior. He uses everyday examples to show that universal syllogisms are often superfluous, and that intellectual intuition directly grasps the equality of likes without syllogistic mediation. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim under attack
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states what the logicians claim about universal syllogism. The passage opens with 'fabaṭala qawluhum: innahu mīzān al-'ulūm al-kulliyyah al-burhāniyyah'. That is the target: the claim that universal syllogism is the measure of all demonstrative sciences.
This sets the stage for his refutation.
2
List the alternative methods he proposes
Ibn Taymiyya enumerates three ways to know particular things: inner/outer sense (al-ḥiss al-bāṭin wa al-ẓāhir), analogical reasoning (al-qiyās al-tamthīlī), and a syllogism with only particular terms (ḥudūd juz'iyyah). Underline these in the translation.
He claims these methods give 'aṣaḥḥ wa awḍaḥ wa akmal' knowledge.
3
Examine his examples showing redundancy of universal syllogisms
He gives two examples: seeing Zayd and 'Amr in different places makes the universal principle 'one body cannot be in two places' unnecessary; weighing identical dirhams makes the axiom 'things equal to one thing are equal' redundant. For each, explain why the universal premise is not needed when direct sense or analogy suffices.
These examples illustrate his claim that the universal syllogism adds no new knowledge.
4
Understand his argument for analogical reasoning giving certainty
He moves to analogical reasoning (qiyas tamthil): if one knows two things are similar (e.g., two dinars), and knows a ruling (ḥukm) of one (natural, customary, or legal), one directly knows the same ruling for the other without requiring a universal syllogism. He calls this self-evident by intellectual intuition (badihah aqliyyah). Find where he compares this to knowing that one is half of two.
He refutes the idea that an implicit universal syllogism is in the mind.
5
Assess the internal validity of his critique
Check whether Ibn Taymiyya himself uses logical reasoning. He gives an example of a particular syllogism: Zayd is brother of 'Amr, 'Amr is brother of Bakr, so Zayd is brother of Bakr. This is a valid categorical syllogism but with particular terms. Does this actually refute the need for universals? Consider whether his argument works or if he conflates different uses of universals.
This step helps evaluate the critique critically.

So their statement that it is the measure of all demonstrative sciences is false. But some particular, individual, specific things are known by it, and those are known better by other means. This is the second aspect: we say: as for actual existing things, they are known by inner and outer sense, and they are known by analogical reasoning, and they are known by a syllogism that has no universal, inclusive, or general premise, but rather its three terms—the minor, middle, and major—are particular individuals, and the two premises and conclusion are particular propositions. And knowledge of these specific things by these methods is more correct, clearer, and more complete. For he who sees Zayd in one place and 'Amr in another place with his own eye is independent of inferring that by the fact that one body cannot be in two places. Similarly, he who weighs dirhams each of which is a thousand dirhams is independent of inferring that each of them is a thousand dirhams by the fact that they are equal to the weight, which is one thing, and things equal to one thing are equal. And by this method, particular propositions are known through analogical syllogism. And whoever says that this is through an inclusive syllogism that takes place in the mind—that if this were accidental it would not be habitual—has spoken falsely. Thus similarity and difference in attribute or measure may be known by inner and outer sensation, and the knowledge that the two likes are equal and that the greater and larger are greater and more preferable is known by intellectual intuition. And likewise, the syllogism composed of particular propositions, such as the knowledge that Zayd is the brother of 'Amr, and 'Amr is the brother of Bakr, so Zayd is the brother of Bakr.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' claim that their universal syllogism is the ultimate measure of knowledge. He begins by stating that their claim is false, then presents what he calls the 'second aspect': particular things are better known by other means. He enumerates these means: inner and outer sense, analogical reasoning (qiyas tamthili), and a syllogism composed of particular premises where all terms are individuals. He argues that knowledge obtained through these methods is more correct, clearer, and more complete. To illustrate, he gives everyday examples: seeing Zayd and 'Amr in different places makes the inference that one body cannot be in two places unnecessary; weighing identical dirhams makes the inference about equality redundant. He criticizes the logicians (ahl al-kalam) for adding superfluous speech that does not produce new knowledge. Next, he extends the discussion to analogical reasoning in practical matters: if one knows two things are similar in certain aspects (natural, customary, or legal), one directly knows the ruling of the other without needing a universal syllogism. He asserts that analogical syllogisms yield greater certainty than inclusive syllogisms and that requiring a universal syllogism is superfluous. He then refutes the claim that an implicit universal syllogism occurs in the mind, calling it false. His reasoning is that people who have experience do not think of such syllogisms; rather, mere knowledge of similarity leads them to equate the rulings. This is due to intellectual intuition (badihah aqliyyah), just as one intuitively knows that one is half of two. He concludes that similarity and difference are known by sensation, and the equality of likes is known by intellectual intuition. Finally, he gives an example of a syllogism with particular propositions: Zayd is the brother of 'Amr, and 'Amr is the brother of Bakr, therefore Zayd is the brother of Bakr. This shows that particular knowledge does not require universal quantification.
