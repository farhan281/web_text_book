Critique of Logicians' Certainty vs Ahl al-Hadith
Author contrasts logicians' doubt with Ahl al-Hadith's certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author's grounds for rejecting logicians' claim to certainty and contrast it with the epistemology of Ahl al-Hadith.

Core Idea
Logicians' doubt is no match for Ahl al-Hadith's certainty.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya launches a sharp critique against the logicians and philosophers, accusing them of wallowing in doubt and perplexity. He contrasts their state with the firm certainty (yaqeen) of Ahl al-Hadith, who rely on revelation and transmitted knowledge. Understanding this critique helps you see how traditional Islamic scholars defended the epistemological superiority of prophetic knowledge over speculative reason.

Commentary
This passage is a critical evaluation of the state of certainty among logicians and philosophers, contrasting them with Ahl al-Hadith. The author begins by describing Fakhr al-Din al-Razi as the foremost in 'perplexity and doubt' but criticizes him for being excessive in casting doubt rather than seeking verification. The author then contrasts al-Razi with other thinkers who may establish some truth, though occasionally on falsehood—yet the author concedes that doubt in falsehood is better than stubborn persistence. However, he notes that few persist in pure falsehood. Next, the author relates an anecdote about Ibn Wāsil al-Hamawi, a distinguished later scholar, who confessed that after a night of considering various arguments, he reached no conclusion. This illustrates the pervasive uncertainty among philosophers. To emphasize this, the author quotes al-Khattabi's couplet comparing their arguments to glass that shatters, seemingly true but each breaking the other. The author then asks rhetorically if such arguments deserve to be taken seriously, and questions how such people can criticize Ahl al-Hadith, who possess genuine knowledge, certainty, and tranquility. The author insists that Ahl al-Hadith know with certainty and do not doubt. Furthermore, even their common folk possess certainty and beneficial knowledge that the leading philosophers lack. The passage concludes by addressing the accusation that Ahl al-Hadith merely follow taqlid (blind imitation) without evidence, but the author points out that the certainty of knowledge differs from the certainty of... (the text breaks off). The overall argument is that the epistemically uncertain state of the logicians and philosophers is inferior to the firm certainty of Ahl al-Hadith, and that the critics are themselves in no position to judge.

Istihsan: Abandoning Qiyas for Textual Evidence
Author critiques blind qiyas and defends istihsan with historical examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rationale behind istihsan as a method for overriding weak qiyas with textual evidence, using the Hanafi school as a case study.

Core Idea
Istihsan is not arbitrary preference—it is abandoning a weak analogy for a stronger textual evidence.

Ibn Taymiyya critiques rigid adherence to analogical reasoning (qiyas) when it contradicts direct textual evidence (nass). He defends the Hanafi principle of istihsan (juristic preference) by contrasting two students of Abu Hanifa: Zufar, who applied qiyas excessively despite limited hadith knowledge, and Abu Yusuf, who prioritized texts and sound analogies after traveling to learn the Sunnah. This lesson unpacks the methodological lesson that agreement on a weak qiyas leads to error, while istihsan preserves the primacy of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The passage contrasts two approaches: blindly following qiyas versus abandoning it when textual evidence contradicts it. Notice that the author (Ibn Taymiyya) considers the latter superior.
2
Define Istihsan in Context
Istihsan is defined by the author as 'abandoning qiyas for a text'. It is not arbitrary caprice but a preference for a stronger evidence (nass) over a weaker analogy.
This refutes the misconception that istihsan is subjective.
3
Analyze Zufar's Weakness
Zufar is criticized for excessive application of qiyas despite weak knowledge of texts. His analogies led to forbidding the lawful and permitting the unlawful. The key phrase: 'kathir al-tard lima yazunnuhu min al-qiyas' (excessive in applying what he supposed to be qiyas).
Zufar's fault is not using qiyas per se, but using it without textual grounding.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Strength
Abu Yusuf is praised for his hadith knowledge and his willingness to recant when presented with stronger textual evidence. His disagreements with Abu Hanifa were based on texts and sound analogies learned during his travels.
Abu Yusuf's example shows that correct ijtihad requires comprehensive knowledge of revelation.
5
Extract the General Principle
The author concludes that agreeing upon a weak qiyas obliges one to follow it consistently, which leads to error when better evidence exists. The lesson is that jurists must prioritize textual evidence over fixed analogies.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the practice of qiyas (analogical reasoning) in Islamic jurisprudence, specifically addressing the concept of istihsan (juristic preference). The author, Ibn Taymiyya, argues that blindly following qiyas when there is a contradictory textual evidence (nass) is inferior to abandoning the qiyas for the text. He supports this with the example of the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa warned against the analogies of his student Zufar, who was known for excessive application of qiyas despite limited hadith knowledge. In contrast, Abu Yusuf, another student, was more grounded in hadith and often based his rulings on texts and sound analogies. Ibn Taymiyya notes that Abu Yusuf's disagreements with Abu Hanifa were usually based on stronger textual evidence gained during his travels to Hijaz. The author concludes that many jurists fall into error when they agree on a weak qiyas because agreement obliges them to apply it consistently, even when better evidence exists. The passage does not contain a formal logical argument but uses historical examples to illustrate a methodological point: that adherence to qiyas must be tempered by direct textual evidence.

Distinguishing Quranic Nazar from Innovated Speculation
Ibn Taymiyya refutes the claim that Ahl al-Sunna reject rational proof and speculative reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical fallacy of equivocation (ishterak al-lafz) in the accusation that Ahl al-Sunna reject rational proof and distinguish the Quranic concept of nazar from the mutakallimun's innovation.

Core Idea
Rejecting false innovations does not mean rejecting the entire genus of rational proof.

Ibn Taymiyya here addresses a common accusation levelled against Ahl al-Sunna: that they reject all forms of rational reasoning and speculative thought. He shows that this accusation rests on a terminological confusion (ishterak al-lafz). The Quran itself commands nazar (speculation), tafakkur (reflection), and tadabbur (contemplation). What Ahl al-Sunna reject is the particular speculative theology (kalam) innovated by the mutakallimun, not the generic nazar commanded by revelation. This lesson will teach you to recognize and avoid such equivocations when discussing Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the accusation
Locate the claim that Ahl al-Sunna reject nazar and istidlal. Note that Ibn Taymiyya presents this as the mutakallimun's misrepresentation.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's affirmative defense
Find where he states that Ahl al-Sunna do not reject what the Quran commands regarding nazar, tafakkur, tadabbur, etc. This shows they accept legitimate rational reflection.
3
Identify the source of confusion: ishterak al-lafz
Ibn Taymiyya explicitly says 'there occurred an ambiguity (ishtirak) in the term nazar and istidlal'. Understand that the same word is used for two different concepts: Quranic nazar and the mutakallimun's speculative method.
This is the classic fallacy of equivocation.
4
Analyze the analogy with 'usul al-din'
Ibn Taymiyya compares the situation to how the mutakallimun call their doctrines 'usul al-din'. When Ahl al-Sunna reject them, they are accused of rejecting the foundations of religion, but in reality they only reject false foundations.
This reinforces the pattern of misrepresentation through terminological usurpation.
5
Apply the lesson to a contemporary example
Consider a modern claim: 'Salafis reject kalam, so they reject rationality.' Using this lesson, identify the equivocation: Salafis reject a particular theological method, not all rational reflection.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the charge that Ahl al-Sunna are not people of rational proof and speculative reasoning. Ibn Taymiyya begins by affirming that Ahl al-Sunna do not reject what Allah has commanded in the Quran—indeed, the Quran repeatedly exhorts nazar (speculation), tafakkur (reflection), tadabbur (contemplation), and i'tibar (consideration). He then identifies the source of the confusion: the term 'nazar' is shared between the Quranic concept and the innovations of the mutakallimun. When Ahl al-Sunna reject the false nazar invented by the mutakallimun (such as their particular dialectical methods), the mutakallimun misinterpret this as a rejection of all nazar. Ibn Taymiyya draws an analogy: the mutakallimun call their own doctrines 'usul al-din' (foundations of religion), and when Ahl al-Sunna reject those doctrines, they are accused of rejecting the foundations of religion itself. In reality, they only reject the false 'usul al-din' that have no authority from Allah. The lesson is that terminology must be carefully distinguished—the rejection of a particular innovation does not imply rejection of the entire genus.

Belief as a Word – The Foundation of Creed
Author defines belief as a word and contrasts tawhid and shirk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author defines belief as a 'word' (kalima) and distinguish between the best and worst words in terms of Islamic creed.

Core Idea
Your creed is a word in your heart – the best is tawhid, the worst is shirk.

This lesson unpacks the author's claim that belief itself is a 'word' – not a mere utterance but the inner conviction that forms the foundation of one's religious identity. The author then contrasts the best word (tawhid) with the worst (shirk), establishing a clear criterion for evaluating any belief system.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'word' in the passage
Note that the author equates belief (الاعتقاد) with the word (الكلمة) that a person believes. This is not a spoken phrase but an inner conviction.
In earlier Mantiq texts, kalima often means a proposition (qadiyya); here it is used in a theological sense as the core creed.
2
Extract the positive and negative examples
The best word is the statement of tawhid ('there is no god but Allah'), and the worst word is shirk (associating partners with Allah). Notice the use of 'أطيب' and 'أخبث' to rank them.
3
Understand the consequence
The author states that researching or acting upon evil beliefs (shirk) only increases misguidance, distance from truth, and knowledge of their falsehood.
4
Reflect on the shift in usage
In logic, 'kalima' is a proposition; here it becomes the foundational creed. This shift is intentional to root belief in the heart rather than mere mental operations.

The mirage and the darkness show that false beliefs yield nothing and lead to confusion.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic presents a fundamental epistemological distinction: not all who carry knowledge truly understand it. He divides scholars into two ranks—those who possess deep insight (basira) and active strength (quwwa) to derive and propagate the truth, and those whose role is to preserve and transmit texts faithfully. The Philosophers' Redefinition of 'Aql and Nafs
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the translation of false philosophical doctrines, using the ten intellects example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast the philosophical definitions of ‘aql and nafs with their Islamic usages and identify the shift in meaning.

Core Idea
When philosophers call something 'intellect' or 'soul', they mean something entirely different from what Islam intends.

The philosophers claim that the first emanation is an 'intellect' and each sphere has a 'soul'. Ibn Taymiyya demands that these terms be translated clearly. The philosophers define 'aql as the spirit abstracted from matter, and nafs as the spirit governing the body. This lesson analyzes these definitions and exposes how they differ from the Islamic understanding.

Guided Path
1
Locate the philosophers' definitions
Find where in the passage the philosophers define 'aql and nafs. Write them down.
They say 'aql is the spirit abstracted from matter; nafs is the spirit governing the body.
2
Identify key elements of each definition
For 'aql, note the aspect of abstraction and separation from matter. For nafs, note its role in moving the body and becoming pure intellect upon separation.
These are not ordinary definitions but tailored to their cosmic system.
3
Compare with Islamic usage
In standard Islamic usage, 'aql is the faculty of reason inherent in humans, not a separate substance. Nafs can mean the self or soul, but not a sphere's soul.
The shift is from intrinsic faculty to independent entity.
4
Evaluate the shift
Why does Ibn Taymiyya highlight this? Because the philosophers are using familiar words with radically new meanings, causing confusion.
This is a common tactic: redefining terms to make false theories seem acceptable.

Critique of the Emanation of Intellects and Souls
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the translation of false philosophical doctrines, using the ten intellects example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' argument for the ten intellects and nine souls and identify the logical flaws Ibn Taymiyya points out.

Core Idea
The emanation theory is built on arbitrary claims that contradict Islamic monotheism.

The philosophers assert that the First Being emanates one intellect, which then emanates another, and so on, each sphere having an intellect and a soul. They claim movement requires a soul, and intellect is needed for perfection. Ibn Taymiyya presents this as an example of their confusion and notes disagreements among them. This lesson examines the structure of their argument and the weaknesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the emanation chain
Find the description: the first intellect emanates from the Necessary Being; then the second from the first, etc. Also, each sphere has an intellect and a soul.
This is the core of the emanation theory.
2
Find the reasons for souls and intellects
The philosophers argue: each sphere needs a soul because its movement is voluntary; each soul needs an intellect because the intellect is perfect and the moving thing seeks perfection.
These are the justifications they give.
3
Note the assumption about unity
They say the First can only emanate one thing because multiplicity requires a body. This is a key Neoplatonic principle.
Ibn Taymiyya does not accept this premise.
4
Locate the critique of disagreement
At the end, Ibn Taymiyya says 'they have much disagreement about emanation'. This undermines the certainty of their claims.
If they disagree, their theory is not established.

Ibn Taymiyya refutes the Sabian principle that the One only produces one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's two-pronged critique of the Sabian emanation principle and identify its flaws using both revelation and observable reality.

Core Idea
The 'One only produces one' principle is false: every effect requires an agent and a receiver.

The Sabian philosophers argued that from a single simple cause, only one effect can proceed. Applying this to Allah, they claimed only one thing emanates from Him. Ibn Taymiyya refutes this by combining Qur'anic evidence with a simple observation: nothing in the created world comes from a single factor alone. Every generation or production involves two principles—like father and mother, agent and receiver. In this lesson, you will learn how Ibn Taymiyya dismantles this famous principle and what it means for Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target principle
Find the Sabian claim that 'the One only produces one' in the Arabic. Understand that it is a metaphysical rule borrowed from Neoplatonism.
The phrase appears as 'الواحد لا يصدر عنه ويتولد عنه إلا واحد'.
2
Examine the Qur'anic response
Locate the verses Ibn Taymiyya uses (especially Q25:33 and Q6:101) to show that Allah creates directly, not through emanation, and creates in pairs.
He quotes Mujahid's tafsir of 'شفع ووتر' linking even/odd to creation and Allah.
3
Analyze the observable world
Note Ibn Taymiyya's argument that every effect requires two factors: an agent (fāʿil) and a receiver (qābil), like sun and earth, fire and wood. There is no example of a single cause producing a single effect.
This is an empirical observation from the natural world.
4
Deconstruct the analogies
Look at how he tackles the examples of radiation and sound. He shows that even these require duality, so the analogies backfire.
Radiation from the sun either is an attribute (not a separate creation) or requires the earth as receiver.
5
Synthesize Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion
The principle is false both by revelation and empirical observation. Allah creates directly, and nothing emanates from Him; rather, He creates from pairs.
The refutation is complete: no 'one from one' exists in reality.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the famous emanationist principle often attributed to the Sabians or philosophers: 'The One only produces one' (الواحد لا يصدر عنه إلا واحد). The author begins by citing Qur'anic verses that promise Allah will bring the truth and best explanation against any example (مَثَل) brought by opponents. He then states that the Sabians brought such an example: the claim that because Allah is one, only one thing can proceed from Him. Ibn Taymiyya declares that Allah countered this by showing that the One does not produce or beget anything at all—rather, He creates each thing directly, and He creates from every pair two kinds (i.e., pairs). To support this, he quotes Mujahid's interpretation of 'even and odd' (الشفع والوتر) from Sahih al-Bukhari: the even is creation (because everything created has a counterpart), and the odd is Allah, who has no likeness. Then Ibn Taymiyya makes a philosophical argument: any effect or generated thing in the world requires two principles—a fatherly agent (فاعل) and a motherly receiver (قابل), like the sun and earth, or fire and wood. The idea of a single thing emanating from a single source simply does not occur in reality. He then addresses two analogies used by the proponents of emanation: radiation from the sun, and sound from a striking movement. He argues that if radiation is an attribute of the sun, then it is not a separate creation; if it is the reflected light on earth, then it requires both sun and earth. Similarly, sound requires a striker and a struck object. Thus, these analogies actually refute their own claim because they involve duality. The lesson guards against the misunderstanding that the principle 'the One only produces one' is a necessary metaphysical truth. Ibn Taymiyya shows that it contradicts Islamic teachings of direct creation and the observable structure of the universe.

Ibn Taymiyya's Linguistic Critique of 'Illah (Cause)
Ibn Taymiyya argues that Quranic terms are more comprehensive and appropriate than Greek logical terms like 'cause'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's linguistic critique of the logical term 'illa and evaluate its implications for theological discourse.

Core Idea
Quranic terms, rooted in revelation, are more precise than Greek logical terms like 'cause'.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Quran's terminology is superior to the borrowed Greek logical terms used by the philosophers and logicians. In this passage, he focuses on the term 'illa (cause), showing that its original Arabic meaning—'change' or 'illness'—makes it inappropriate for describing Allah. This lesson will walk you through his critique and help you apply his method to other terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target term
Locate the word 'illa in the Arabic passage. Notice how Ibn Taymiyya defines its original meaning: 'change' (taghayyur), like an illness that alters the body.
The term 'illa in Arabic logic usually means 'cause' in syllogisms, but here Ibn Taymiyya redirects to its literal meaning.
2
Understand the linguistic argument
Ibn Taymiyya claims that the Quran's way of negating attributes from Allah (e.g., no child, no begetting) is more comprehensive and upright than negating with the word 'illa. Why? Because 'illa implies change, which is incompatible with Allah's immutability.
He also mentions that 'ma'lul' (effect) is originally only used for drinking water a second time, not a general term for effect.
3
Compare with Quranic methodology
The Quran names things with attributes appropriate to the ruling. For Allah's transcendence, the Quran uses explicit negations of specific imperfections, not a single term like 'illa that carries unwanted connotations.
This shows the Quran's 'jawami' al-kalim' (comprehensive speech) is superior.
4
Evaluate the critique
Assess whether Ibn Taymiyya's argument is valid. Does the original meaning of 'illa truly render it unsuitable for all theological uses? Or is he overextending the critique? Consider the role of conventional usage (istilaah) in technical terms.
This step requires critical thinking—do you agree that a term's etymology disqualifies it from technical use?

Ibn Taymiyya's Distinction Between Natural Reasoning and Formal Logic
Author discusses holy souls receiving knowledge without formal logic, then refutes absolute exclusivity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's distinction between natural syllogistic reasoning and the formal craft of logic, and evaluate his argument against the claim that formal logic is the only path to knowledge.

Core Idea
Formal logic's claimed necessity is refuted by the existence of prophets and saints who know without it.

Ibn Taymiyya begins by acknowledging that even the logicians admit that some souls, like prophets, receive knowledge without formal syllogistic reasoning. However, they argue that such individuals are naturally wise and the syllogism occurs innately. In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya deconstructs this position, distinguishing between the syllogism as a natural mental process and the artificial craft of logic, and he exposes the falsehood of claiming that formal logic is the only path to knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim about holy souls
Note that logicians say holy souls receive knowledge without the formal syllogistic path, but they qualify it as natural (bi al-tab').
This concession is the starting point.
2
Distinguish between the two meanings of qiyas
Ibn Taymiyya separates the natural syllogism (jins al-qiyas) from the formal craft (sina'a qanuniyya). Logicians confuse the two.
The craft is the scales, the natural syllogism is the mental act.
3
Analyze the analogy of the Arab and the poet
Just as an Arab speaks correctly without grammar, or a poet composes without prosody, so some reason correctly without formal logic. This shows the craft is not necessary for everyone.
The analogy's strength: it shows natural ability can suffice.
4
Assess Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of exclusivity
Even if the craft benefits some, claiming no other path is a statement without knowledge and is confirmed falsehood. Why? Because it contradicts observable reality (prophetic knowledge).
This is an epistemological argument against absolutism.

Commentary
This passage continues Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' overreach. Universal mental notions cannot capture the unique reality of each existent.

Logicians claim that logic yields certain knowledge, but Ibn Taymiyya argues that it only produces abstract universals in the mind. How can a universal concept grasp the unique reality of a particular existent? In this lesson, you will dissect his argument and see why he concludes that logical syllogisms do not give us verified knowledge of the external world.

The author argues: (1) each external existent has a unique reality by which it is distinguished; (2) that reality is not universal; (3) knowledge of a universal (the common matter) does not give knowledge of that particular reality. Note the additional critique of analogical reasoning
The author points out that logicians themselves criticize qiyas al-tamthil (analogical reasoning) as only providing probability (zann), which further weakens the claim that logic is a reliable tool for certain knowledge.
This is a secondary point that reinforces the main critique.

Commentary
This is a critical passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd Mantiq, attacking the claim that Greek logic can provide certainty in divine matters. Ibn Taymiyya then narrows his focus: the originator of logic (Aristotle) designed it to weigh their own sciences, which are of little benefit, mostly worldly, and even dispensable in worldly matters. He then emphasizes that intelligence and moral virtues alone do not bring salvation; they are worthless without the foundational principles of faith: belief in God, His oneness, sincere worship, faith in His messengers, the Last Day, and righteous deeds. He draws an analogy: intellectual strength is like bodily strength—useless without proper foundation. Similarly, political power and knowledge are useless without worship and faith. The passage concludes with the mutual entailment of these principles: true worship of God necessarily includes faith in His messengers and the Last Day, and those who fail in this deserve punishment unless the prophetic proof has not reached them. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya stresses that logic and philosophy are insufficient for the ultimate good and that only revelation and faith provide certainty and salvation.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for Naqd Mantiq, Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of Aristotelian logic. It outlines the book's structure and key arguments. The topics begin with critiques of specific scholars (al-Karaji, Ibn al-Jawzi) and then move to the core attack on logic. The author declares that learning logic is not a communal obligation (fard kifaya) and that its proponents are extremists. He cites the condemnation of logic by Islamic scholars. The discussion covers the five types of syllogisms (al-aqisa al-khamsa), compares rhetorical and dialectical syllogisms, and challenges the logicians' elevation of categorical syllogism over analogical reasoning (tamthil). A major section criticizes definitions (hudud) in seven points: (1) non-evident conceptions are not solely attained through definitions; (2) logicians have not produced a sound definition for anything; (3) only a few scholars even use definitions, so how can others master sciences without them?; (4) definitions cannot convey the true realities of things; (5) definitions are universal statements that do not yield specific conception; (6) understanding a definition's words requires prior conception of the meaning; (7) definitions only distinguish the defined from other things. This table of contents shows Ibn Taymiyya's comprehensive critique, from epistemological claims to practical utility, all grounded in Islamic sources.

Key Terms
المنطق (al-mantiq): Ibn Taymiyya uses this term to refer to Aristotelian logic, which he considers a foreign and harmful innovation. [Note: In earlier texts (e.g., al-Ghazali), mantiq is a neutral tool; Ibn Taymiyya redefines it polemically as a corrupted discipline.]
الحد (al-hadd): A definition that logicians claim yields true conception of essences; Ibn Taymiyya argues it is insufficient and often misleading. [Note: In classical logic, hadd is a key tool; Ibn Taymiyya denies its epistemic necessity and efficacy.]
القياس التمثيل (al-qiyas al-tamthil): Analogical reasoning used in Islamic jurisprudence; Ibn Taymiyya defends it against the logicians' preference for categorical syllogism. [Note: In post-Avicennan logic, tamthil is often subordinated; Ibn Taymiyya restores it as superior for practical sciences.]
