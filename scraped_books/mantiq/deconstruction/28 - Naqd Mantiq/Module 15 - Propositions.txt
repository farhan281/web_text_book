Ibn Taymiyya on the Universality of Affirmation of Divine Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya argues that debates over divine attributes are universal across religious groups, not unique to Hanbalis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace Ibn Taymiyya's dialectical argument that affirmation of divine attributes is common across Islamic schools and other religions, and evaluate its rhetorical force.

Core Idea
Affirmation of divine attributes transcends Hanbalism; it is the consensus of the Salaf and beyond.

In this passage from the Naqd al-Mantiq, Ibn Taymiyya refutes the notion that only the Hanbalis affirm the literal divine attributes like face, hand, and eyes. He marshals evidence from major Islamic schools, earlier theologians, and even non-Muslim groups to show the universality of this position. Understanding his dialectical method will sharpen your ability to analyze claims of exclusivity in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
Find the passage where Ibn Taymiyya says 'this question is not specific to these people'. This is his response to an implied objection that affirming attributes is a Hanbali peculiarity.
The phrase 'الوجه الرابع: أن هذا السؤال لا يختص بهؤلاء' signals the main assertion.
2
List the groups he cites as affirmers
Read from 'بل إثبات جنس هذه الصِّفات قد أتفق عليه سلف الأمة...' and enumerate: Salaf, fuqaha, muhaddithun, Sufis, Kullabiyya, Karramiyya, Ash'ariyya. Then note he extends to Shi'a, Jews, Christians, Sabians, and philosophers.
This accumulation strengthens his case for universality.
3
Examine his use of authority
He quotes al-Ash'ari's Maqalat to show that even the chief Ash'ari theologian affirms this doctrine. Check the citation and its role in the argument.
Using an opponent's authority (al-Ash'ari) to support his own position is a classic dialectical move.
4
Analyze the rhetorical structure
Notice how he moves from the strongest evidence (Quran, Salaf) to wider circles (other schools, non-Muslims). This creates an impression of overwhelming consensus.
The crescendo builds a case that only a fringe group would deny these attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic, specifically addressing the issue of divine attributes. He begins by stating that he will not prolong the discussion because the main point is not about diving into details here. He then makes a methodological point: the names of Allah and His attributes are known only through divine revelation (sam'iyyah), not through human opinion. Therefore, those who refrain from applying certain terms (like 'accidents') to Allah are more excusable than those who refrain from using the revealed names (like 'face' and 'hand') for His attributes. He explains that among human attributes, some are accidents (like knowledge and power) and some are self-subsistent bodies (like face and hand). Calling these 'limbs' is more specific than calling them 'bodies' because it implies acquisition, benefit, disposal, and divisibility. 

Next, he presents the fourth aspect: the question of affirming these attributes is not exclusive to the Hanbalis; rather, affirming the genus of these attributes is agreed upon by the Salaf and the imams from various schools: jurists, hadith scholars, Sufis, and theologians like the Kullabiyya, Karramiyya, and Ash'ariyya. All affirm the attributes of face, hand, etc. He cites al-Ash'ari's book 'Maqalat al-Islamiyyin' to confirm that this is the doctrine of Ahl al-Hadith, and quotes the creed: affirmation of the throne, two hands without modality, two eyes without modality, and a face, all based on Quranic verses. 

Ibn Taymiyya then expands his argument: all the imams of the sects are among the 'people of affirmation' (ahl al-ithbat). Whatever Abu al-Faraj (Ibn al-Jawzi) or others mention as peculiar to the Hanbalis is actually found among many other groups: hadith folk, Sufis, Malikis, Shafi'is, Hanafis, etc. Moreover, even groups that do not follow the Sunnah, like the Shi'a, have even more extreme views on both affirmation and negation. Finally, he extends this observation to the People of the Book (Jews and Christians) and the Sabians (including philosophers), who also have opposing views on affirmation and negation of divine attributes. He notes that among these groups, some go further in affirmation than many Muslim theologians who affirm attributes. However, the general tendency toward affirmation is stronger among the followers of the messengers than those who oppose them.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Logicians' Classification of Mashhurat
Ibn Taymiyya refutes logicians' classification of mashhurat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to the logicians' classification of mashhurat as merely probable premises and evaluate his claim that they are rational intuitions.

Core Idea
Mashhurat are not just conventional; they are intuitive and more certain than sciences.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya attacks the standard logical classification that divides premises into demonstrative (certain), rhetorical (probable), and dialectical (conceded). He argues that the logicians have misunderstood the epistemic status of mashhurat, which are actually self-evident rational intuitions. This critique challenges the very foundation of the logicians' epistemology and forces us to re-examine the nature of certainty in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
Locate the logicians' assertion that rhetorical syllogisms yield only probability while demonstrative yield certainty, and that mashhurat are accepted due to fame, not because they are known. Ibn Taymiyya targets this distinction.
The logicians' claim is summarized in the opening line of the passage.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's alternative criterion for classification
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya shifts the basis of classification from the epistemic output (certainty/probability) to the mode of acceptance: if a premise is accepted because it is known, it is demonstrative; if because of fame, it is rhetorical. He emphasizes that rhetorical premises can yield certainty and demonstrative premises can be well-known.
The key phrase is: 'the division of the materials of the syllogism occurs according to the aspects from which they are accepted.'
3
Examine his examples of mashhurat and his rebuttal
Look at the logicians' examples: 'Knowledge is good, ignorance is evil' and similar moral judgments. Ibn Taymiyya counters that these are not mere conventions but rational intuitive propositions, more certain than mathematical and natural sciences. He dismisses the logicians' attempt to attribute them to illusion.
Ibn Taymiyya states: 'they are among the rational intuitive sciences, the certainty of minds in them is greater than their certainty in many of the mathematical and natural sciences.'
4
Understand his teleological account of goodness as rational intuition
Ibn Taymiyya provides a positive account: goodness of an action is its being conducive to what is sought for its own sake (e.g., knowledge, justice). This shows that moral judgments are rooted in rational intuition about ends, not mere fame. Thus, mashhurat are actually badihiyyat.
The passage says: 'the goodness of an action is its being conducive to what the living being seeks by its essence... So the goodness of an action and its evil is due to its being conducive to the purpose intended by essence.'

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' classification of premises into demonstrative, rhetorical, and dialectical based on their epistemic status. He begins by attacking the claim that the rhetorical syllogism yields only probability while the demonstrative yields certainty. He argues that both rhetorical and dialectical can yield probability, and demonstrative premises can be well-known and conceded. The true basis for classification, he insists, is the aspect from which the premise is accepted: if accepted because it is known, it is demonstrative; if accepted because of fame, it is rhetorical, even if it yields certainty. He then examines their examples of 'well-known' premises that they claim are not certain—such as 'knowledge is good, ignorance is evil'—and asserts that these are actually rational intuitive propositions, more certain than mathematical sciences. He dismisses the logicians' attempt to attribute these to illusion or convention as confusion. Finally, he provides a teleological account of goodness: an action's goodness lies in its being conducive to what is sought for its own sake, such as knowledge, truth, and justice. Thus these moral judgments are rooted in rational intuition about ends, not mere convention.
