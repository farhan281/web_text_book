Critique of Untrustworthy Transmissions and the Mishnah
Author argues against accepting translations except from reliable sources, citing example of fabricated Sabbath report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's criteria for accepting scriptural claims from the People of the Book and evaluate a reported tradition against these criteria.

Core Idea
Claims from other scriptures must be verified from authentic sources; the Mishnah is not a reliable authority.

Ibn Taymiyya provides a critical principle for dealing with claims attributed to earlier scriptures. He warns against accepting transmissions from the People of the Book without verification, because they are known to distort their texts. This lesson will guide you through his argument and its application to a specific example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qur'anic basis
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya begins by quoting Qur'an 3:93, which commands asking the People of the Book to bring the Torah if they claim something contradictory. This establishes a scriptural mandate for verification.
The verse is used to justify demanding evidence for any claim that contradicts the Qur'an.
2
Extract the reason for distrust
Ibn Taymiyya explains that the People of the Book twist their tongues and write false books, attributing them to Allah. This is why acceptance of reports (tarjama) is limited to trustworthy sources.
Tarjama here means transmission of religious reports, not just linguistic translation.
3
Analyze the counterexample
He gives a fabricated report attributed to Musa: 'Hold fast to the Sabbath as long as the heavens and earth last.' The argument is to demand which book contains this statement, knowing it is not in their authentic scriptures.
This exemplifies the principle of demanding verifiable evidence.
4
Understand the reference to al-Muthanna
Ibn Taymiyya mentions the Mishnah (al-Muthanna) as a secondary text that is not from Allah, citing a hadith of Abdullah ibn Amr that its recitation is a sign of the Hour.
This shows that even widely used texts among the People of the Book are not authoritative for Muslims.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument from Ibn Taymiyya about verifying the authenticity of scriptural claims. It begins by referencing a Qur'anic verse (Al 'Imran 3:93) that commands asking the People of the Book to bring their scripture if they claim something contradictory. Ibn Taymiyya explains why translations and reports from them are not accepted except from trustworthy sources: they are known to twist their tongues and write false books, attributing them to Allah. He then gives a concrete example: a fabricated report attributed to Prophet Musa (Moses) saying 'Hold fast to the Sabbath as long as the heavens and earth last.' Ibn Taymiyya argues that we can demand to see which book contains this statement, knowing it is not in their authentic scriptures but is a forgery. He further notes that the Jews have additional collections: prophecies (220 of them) and the book of al-Mishna (al-Muthanna), which Abdullah ibn Amr reported as a sign of the Hour—the recitation of books other than the Qur'an. The passage thus establishes a critical principle: claims must be verifiable from authentic scriptures, and secondary texts like the Mishnah are not reliable sources of divine revelation.

The Epistemological Role of Scriptural Evidence in Islamic Logic
Author cites many Quranic verses and a hadith about angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how Ibn Taymiyya uses scriptural evidence (Quran and Hadith) to ground theological claims about angels, and distinguish this approach from purely rationalist epistemology.

Core Idea
Scripture, not reason alone, is the primary source of certain knowledge about the unseen.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya assembles a host of Quranic verses and a hadith to describe the reality of angels. This is not a random list; it is a deliberate epistemological move. For the Islamic intellectual tradition, scriptural evidence is the foundation for claims about ghayb (the unseen). This lesson examines how such textual evidence functions in logical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the theological claim being supported
Determine the overall assertion about angels that Ibn Taymiyya is defending. He affirms their literal existence, their roles as honored servants, and refutes misconceptions (e.g., that they are female or divine).
The verses collectively build a positive and negative case.
2
Locate the types of scriptural evidence
Categorize the verses: those describing actions (glorification, prostration), those describing roles (taking souls, recording deeds), those refuting false beliefs (denying daughters of Allah).
Each category strengthens a different aspect of the claim.
3
Analyze how the evidence is structured
Note the sequence: from general statements about angels' worship, to specific roles, to refutations. This structure builds a cumulative case.
The arrangement is not chronological but thematic.
4
Assess the epistemological weight
According to Ibn Taymiyya, why is this collection sufficient? Because the Quran is the most authoritative source of knowledge about the unseen, and multiple verses reinforce each other.
Contrast with rationalist philosophers who might demand demonstrative proof.

The footnotes indicate textual corrections and a reference to Muslim's Sahih. This collection serves as a foundation for an argument about the reality and attributes of angels, likely in the context of refuting philosophical or theological positions that deny or distort the Islamic understanding of angels.

Systematic Compilation of Quranic Descriptions of Angels
Author cites many Quranic verses and a hadith about angels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize the different roles and attributes of angels as mentioned in the Quranic verses cited by Ibn Taymiyya, and explain how each category contributes to the overall Islamic conception of angels.

Core Idea
The Quran presents a multi-faceted description of angels, each aspect reinforcing their identity as honored servants of Allah.

In this lengthy passage, Ibn Taymiyya compiles verses that together form a coherent picture of angels. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how a collection of authentic hadiths from al-Sahihayn functions as cumulative evidence in an epistemological argument.

Core Idea
A collection of authentic hadiths provides cumulative epistemic weight beyond any single report.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya gathers multiple hadiths from al-Sahihayn about angels. These are not random; they serve as foundational evidence for an argument about the reliability of transmitted knowledge. Understanding how such compilations function is crucial for assessing the authority of scriptural evidence in Islamic logic and epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source authority
Notice that each hadith is explicitly attributed to al-Sahihayn, the most authentic hadith collections. This establishes the baseline reliability of each report.
The sources are Bukhari and Muslim, as indicated by 'al-Sahihayn'.
2
Recognize the variety of topics
The hadiths cover different angelic activities: praying rows, worship in Bayt al-Ma'mur, responding with Amin, descending with decrees, attending remembrance gatherings. They are diverse yet all involve angels.
This variety shows that angelic actions are attested across multiple contexts.
3
Identify the common thread
All hadiths concern the existence and actions of angels. By citing multiple independent reports, the author reinforces the truth of these events from multiple angles.
Common thread: angels are real beings with specific actions known through reliable transmission.
4
Assess cumulative epistemic value
The author does not comment because the mere multiplicity of authentic reports strengthens the case. In Islamic epistemology, multiple corroborating chains (tawatur) yield certainty.
Each hadith is sound; together they provide a robust evidence base.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the use of the terms 'cause' ('illa) and 'generation' (tawallud) in theological discourse. He explains why this is problematic: a son is of the same kind and a likeness of the father, even if subordinate. Therefore, those who attribute a son or generation to God are guilty of anthropomorphism (tashbih) and making rivals (andad) for God. The philosophers who speak of emanation of intellects and souls from God in the manner of generation in fact take these entities as gods and lords, sometimes worshipping them alone.

Finally, he cites Quranic verses that deny God having a son or partner, and points out that the polytheists who claimed kinship between God and the jinn or angels committed the same error. The passage thus emphasizes that misuse of causal and generative language leads to theological corruption.

The Insufficiency of Intellect Without Revelation
Ibn Taymiyya critiques philosophers using Quranic verses and a historical anecdote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the claim that intellectual acumen alone suffices for salvation, using Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
Clever intellect without revelation leads to ruin.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that philosophical reasoning, divorced from prophetic guidance, is futile and even dangerous. He supports this with Quranic verses and a striking remark from Hanafi jurists about Ibn Sina. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for the relationship between reason and revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
What claim or attitude is Ibn Taymiyya attacking? He targets those who rely solely on human intellect and argue against Allah's signs (ayat Allah) without divine authority.
The target is not reason per se, but reason without revelation.
2
Extract the Quranic evidence
List the Quranic verses cited by Ibn Taymiyya (Ghafir 56, 69-75, 83; al-Ahqaf 26; al-Rum 6-11; al-An'am 5-6). Notice the common theme: intellectual and material strength does not save from punishment if one rejects the messengers.
These verses collectively demonstrate that even powerful and knowledgeable nations were destroyed.
3
Analyze the historical remark
What did the Hanafi jurists of Bukhara say about Ibn Sina? They called him a 'clever disbeliever' (kafir dhakiyyan). How does this support the argument?
It shows that intellectual brilliance is not a guarantee of true knowledge or faith.
4
Reconstruct the logical structure
Ibn Taymiyya's argument: (1) Allah gave previous nations greater power, perception, and knowledge, yet they were destroyed when they rejected the messengers. (2) Therefore, mere intellect without revelation is insufficient for salvation. (3) The philosophers, like those before them, are at risk.
This is a historical-inductive argument, not a formal syllogism.
5
Evaluate the critique's validity
Does the critique hold? Consider whether the Quranic examples are relevant to the philosophers' claims. Ibn Taymiyya presupposes that philosophers' arguments are akin to the arrogance of past nations.
The critique is not about logical form but about epistemic foundation.
