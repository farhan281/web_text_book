The Salaf's Method on Ambiguous Verses (Mutashabihat)
Author describes the Salaf's avoidance of interpreting ambiguous Quranic verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Salaf's method for handling mutashabihat and evaluate the historical evidence used to support it.

Core Idea
The Salaf accepted mutashabihat without interpretation, opposing the use of logic on divine attributes.

In this passage from Naqd al-Mantiq, Ibn Taymiyya presents the Salaf's method regarding ambiguous Quranic verses. He argues that the early Muslims did not engage in ta'wil (allegorical interpretation) of divine attributes, and he provides a powerful historical example to demonstrate their stance. Understanding this method is crucial for evaluating later theological debates about the role of logic in interpreting revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Salaf's claimed method
Read the first part of the passage where the author states what the Salaf did and did not do regarding mutashabihat. Note their positive actions (transmission with belief) and their negative actions (no interpretation, no allegory, no comparison).
2
Extract the argumentative structure
The author provides a rational proof: if the Salaf had interpreted the attributes, it would have been transmitted. Explain the logic: concealment is impossible for widely needed matters. This is an argument from transmission (naql).
This is a form of logical reasoning even though the author rejects Greek logic.
3
Analyze the Sabigh incident
Read the story of Sabigh. Identify: What was Sabigh doing? How did Umar respond? What was the consequence? How does this serve as evidence for the Salaf's extreme silence?
4
Evaluate the relevance to the logic debate
Consider how this passage critiques the mutakallimun who used logical ta'wil. The Salaf's method is presented as normative; those who use logic to interpret attributes are deviating from the righteous path.

He remained there like a mangy camel; whenever he came to a gathering they would say: 'The command of the Commander of the Faithful,' and they would disperse from him, until he repented and swore by Allah that he no longer found anything of what was in his heart. Then Umar permitted sitting with him. When the Khawarij emerged, he was approached and told: 'This is your time.' He said: 'No, the admonition of the righteous servant benefited me.'

Commentary
This passage is part of an argument by the author (Ibn Taymiyya) that the early Muslims (the Salaf) did not engage in interpretation (ta'wil) or allegorical explanation of the Quranic verses dealing with God's attributes. The author states that the Salaf transmitted the Quran and hadith with firm belief and no doubt, and they did not explain or interpret the attributes, nor did they liken them to creation. If they had done so, it would have been reported, because it is impossible for such matters to be hidden. To prove their extreme silence on this, the author recounts the famous incident with Sabigh. When Umar learned that Sabigh was asking about ambiguous verses, he prepared palm branches to discipline him. During a sermon, Sabigh asked about the verses from Surah al-Dhariyat. Umar questioned him, and seeing that Sabigh had hair (i.e., was not shaven as the Khawarij were known to be), he did not execute him but had him beaten and exiled to Basra with an order that no one sit with him. The people isolated Sabigh until he repented. Later, when the Khawarij appeared, Sabigh was invited to join them but refused, saying Umar's admonition had benefited him. This story illustrates the Salaf's strong opposition to unnecessary inquiry into mutashabihat, which is used as evidence against those who use logic to interpret such texts.

The Empirical Proof of Sunna's Preeminence
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the greatest honor in Islam belongs to those who follow hadith and sunna.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's empirical argument from hiss and tawatur for the supremacy of the people of hadith and evaluate its strength.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya proves the honor of sunna by the historical fact that the greatest funerals belong to its followers.

Ibn Taymiyya does not rely on abstract proofs to establish the superiority of the people of sunna. Instead, he appeals to an undeniable historical fact: the massive funerals of scholars like Imam Ahmad. In this lesson, we will dissect how he uses empirical observation (hiss) and widespread transmission (tawatur) as evidence for his claim, and then test whether this method is convincing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
What is the main thesis Ibn Taymiyya is trying to prove? Locate the sentence where he states that the greatest honor belongs to followers of sunna.
Look for the first few lines: 'معلوم بالحس والتواتر لكل من سمع كلام المسلمين، لا تجد في الأمة عُظْمَ أحدٌ تعظيما أعظم مما عُظُموا به'.
2
Find the evidence type
Ibn Taymiyya says this is known 'by sense and widespread transmission' (bil-hiss wa al-tawatur). What does he mean by that? How does he use examples like Ahmad's funeral as evidence?
The funeral is a sensory event (hiss) reported by many (tawatur).
3
Trace the causal link
Ibn Taymiyya claims that Ahmad's greatness is due to following hadith and sunna. Does he prove causation or just correlation? How does he argue that eminence comes from sunna?
He states explicitly: 'وهو إنما نَبُل عند الأمة باتباع الحديث والسُّنة'.
4
Test the argument's validity
Is this argument logically sound? Could there be other reasons for the huge funeral? How would a Mu'tazili respond? Evaluate the strength of the empirical evidence.
Consider alternative explanations: political influence, personal charisma, etc.

Commentary
This passage is an extended argument by Ibn Taymiyya establishing that the true measure of honor in the Muslim community is adherence to the transmitted sources of hadith and sunna, not speculative theology (kalam) or philosophy. He begins by stating that this is evident to anyone who has heard the words of Muslims. The greatest figures in the community have been magnified beyond all others, and those who oppose them are only respected to the degree they agree with them. He cites Imam Ahmad's famous statement that the test between them and the innovators is the funeral prayer: the innovators' funerals are small while the people of sunna's funerals are massive. Then he provides the example of Imam Ahmad's own funeral, attended by an enormous crowd, and claims even twenty thousand Jews and Christians converted that day, all because of Ahmad's adherence to hadith and sunna. He then extends this to other great imams: al-Shafi'i, Ishaq, al-Bukhari, Malik, al-Awza'i, al-Thawri, and Abu Hanifa—all rose in status only because they followed the sunna. Any criticism of these scholars is only on points where they could not follow the sunna due to ignorance, weakness, or preference. Finally, he asserts that even in theological matters of belief (masa'il i'tiqadiyya khabariyya), no leader of any sect gains eminence except through affirmation and the sunna. The passage cuts off while beginning to discuss the Mu'tazila, but it is clear he intends to show that even they are praised only when they agree with the sunna.

Key Terms
الحس والتواتر (al-hiss wa al-tawatur): Knowledge gained through direct sensory experience and widespread transmission, indicating undeniable certainty. [Note: Standard in Islamic epistemology; no significant shift.]
الحديث والسنة (al-hadith wa al-sunna): The prophetic traditions and established practices, considered the primary sources of religious authority. [Note: Stable across tradition.]
المسائل الاعتقادية الخبرية (al-masa'il al-i'tiqadiyya al-khabariyya): Theological issues that are known through transmitted reports, such as matters of the unseen and attributes. [Note: Used here to contrast with rational theology.]
الإثبات (al-ithbat): Affirmation of reports and attributes, as opposed to negation or ta'wil. [Note: In Ibn Taymiyya's usage, often opposed to the Mu'tazili negation of attributes.]

The Two Reasons for Praising al-Ash'ari
Author enumerates two types of praiseworthy acts of al-Ash'ari: agreement with Sunni and refutation of opponents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two categories of praiseworthy acts that Ibn Taymiyya attributes to al-Ash'ari in this passage.

Core Idea
Al-Ash'ari's merit lies only in his Sunni alignment, not in innovation.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya defends Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari by narrowing the basis of any praise for him to just two categories: agreement with Ahl al-Sunnah and refutation of opponents. This is a strategic move to disarm critics who accuse al-Ash'ari of theological innovation. By showing that al-Ash'ari's followers only celebrate him for Sunni orthodoxy, Ibn Taymiyya argues that al-Ash'ari's true value is his closeness to the traditionalist position, not any novel doctrines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of praise
Read the sentence: 'So their merits are of two types: either agreement with Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Hadith, or refutation of those who oppose the Sunnah and Hadith by exposing the contradiction of their arguments.' Notice that these are the only two grounds mentioned.
2
Note who is praised by these categories
The passage states that al-Ash'ari and his followers are praised only for these reasons. Recognize that this includes al-Ash'ari himself, as well as later Ash'arites like al-Bayhaqi and Ibn 'Asakir.
3
Contrast with figures not praised
Ibn Taymiyya contrasts al-Ash'ari with his teacher Abu Ali al-Jubba'i and his companion Abu Hashim, who were not like that. They are not praised because they did not align with the Sunnah or refute opponents in the same way.
4
Understand the argument strategy
Ibn Taymiyya is defending al-Ash'ari by redefining the basis of his reputation. Instead of focusing on his theological innovations (which critics attack), he highlights his agreement with orthodoxy and his refutations. This turns a potential weakness into a strength.

Commentary
This passage explains the two reasons for which al-Ash'ari and his followers are praised. The author, Ibn Taymiyya, is defending al-Ash'ari by showing that his merit lies in his agreement with the Sunni tradition and his refutation of heretical groups like the Jahmiyyah, Mu'tazilah, etc. The two types of merits are: (1) agreeing with Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Hadith, and (2) refuting those who oppose them. If he were not so close to the Sunnah, he would have been grouped with his fellow theologians like Abu Ali al-Jubba'i and Abu Hashim, who are not praised in this way. Thus, the passage argues that al-Ash'ari's true value is his alignment with orthodoxy, not any innovations in theology.

The Fatwa Against Cursing the Ash'aris: Legal Reasoning in Ibn Taymiyya
Author narrates caliph al-Qadir's actions and fatwa forbidding cursing of Ash'aris.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal reasoning used by al-Damaghani and Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi to prohibit cursing the Ash'aris and explain why bloodshed can only be justified by Qur'an and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Cursing fellow Muslims requires textual justification, not sectarian bias.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya recounts a historical fatwa where leading jurists ruled that cursing the Ash'aris is impermissible. Their reasoning illustrates a key principle of Islamic jurisprudence: that no legal ruling, especially one involving bloodshed, can be based on anything other than the Qur'an and Sunnah. The Sharia as the Scale for Miracles
Author explains the criterion for distinguishing karamah from fitna.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn Taymiyya's criterion of the Sharia to evaluate extraordinary events and distinguish karamah from fitna.

Core Idea
The Sharia is the scale that distinguishes a saintly miracle from a satanic trial.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya establishes a fundamental principle: any extraordinary event must be weighed by the Sharia. If the person is upright in following the Sharia, the event is a karamah; if not, it is a fitna. This principle protects believers from being deceived by spectacular acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the extraordinary event
Look at the kharq (miraculous occurrence) and the person through whom it appears. What is the nature of the event?
2
Assess the person's uprightness (istiqama)
Determine whether the person adheres to the Sharia. Is he known for following the Sunna? Or does he deviate?
3
Apply the scale of the Sharia
If the person is upright, the event is a karamah (honor). If not, it is a fitna (trial), similar to the Dajjal's signs.
4
Recall the Dajjal analogy
The Dajjal performs amazing feats like reviving the dead, but because he is not upright, these are a misguidance. This shows that spectacular acts alone are not proof of sanctity.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya on the correct criterion for evaluating extraordinary events (kharq) and the historical relationship between the Ash'ari and Hanbali schools. It opens by establishing that any miraculous occurrence must be weighed by the scale of the Sharia—if the person is upright, it is a karamah; if not, it is a fitna (trial), similar to the signs performed by the Dajjal. The one who clings to the Sharia will recognize such events as trials, regardless of their spectacular nature. The author then applies this principle to the theological schools: the faqih Abu Muhammad forbade cursing and ordered disciplining the curser because he supported the foundations of religion—namely, agreement with the Quran, Sunnah, and Hadith, and refutation of those who oppose them. He then quotes Shaykh Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi, who stated that the Ash'aris only gained acceptance among people through their affiliation with the Hanbalis, a fact evident in their works before the Qushayri fitna in Baghdad. Ibn Asakir is quoted affirming that the Hanbalis and Ash'aris were historically united until the fitna of Ibn al-Qushayri. After recounting this, Ibn Taymiyya notes that both before and after the fitna, praise for Ash'ari is only given when he agrees with the Sunnah and Hadith, and blame is only due to opposition to them. This consensus among all groups demonstrates that the truth lies in glorifying the Sunnah and Hadith. Consequently, the degree of a person's status among them is proportional to their agreement with the Sunnah. Ash'ari himself, because he was closest to the position of Imam Ahmad and earlier Sunnī imams, is esteemed above his followers. Similarly, al-Baqillani, being the closest to that position, is greater than others. In contrast, figures like al-Juwayni and al-Ghazali, who opposed Ash'ari's principles in some matters, are only honored for their areas of agreement with the Sunnah and Hadith—most of which they adopted from the Shafi'i school of fiqh, which itself accords with the Sunnah.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya on the correct criterion for evaluating extraordinary events (kharq) and the historical relationship between the Ash'ari and Hanbali schools. It opens by establishing that any miraculous occurrence must be weighed by the scale of the Sharia—if the person is upright, it is a karamah; if not, it is a fitna (trial), similar to the signs performed by the Dajjal. The one who clings to the Sharia will recognize such events as trials, regardless of their spectacular nature. The author then applies this principle to the theological schools: the faqih Abu Muhammad forbade cursing and ordered disciplining the curser because he supported the foundations of religion—namely, agreement with the Quran, Sunnah, and Hadith, and refutation of those who oppose them. He then quotes Shaykh Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi, who stated that the Ash'aris only gained acceptance among people through their affiliation with the Hanbalis, a fact evident in their works before the Qushayri fitna in Baghdad. Ibn Asakir is quoted affirming that the Hanbalis and Ash'aris were historically united until the fitna of Ibn al-Qushayri. After recounting this, Ibn Taymiyya notes that both before and after the fitna, praise for Ash'ari is only given when he agrees with the Sunnah and Hadith, and blame is only due to opposition to them. This consensus among all groups demonstrates that the truth lies in glorifying the Sunnah and Hadith. Consequently, the degree of a person's status among them is proportional to their agreement with the Sunnah. Ash'ari himself, because he was closest to the position of Imam Ahmad and earlier Sunnī imams, is esteemed above his followers. Similarly, al-Baqillani, being the closest to that position, is greater than others. In contrast, figures like al-Juwayni and al-Ghazali, who opposed Ash'ari's principles in some matters, are only honored for their areas of agreement with the Sunnah and Hadith—most of which they adopted from the Shafi'i school of fiqh, which itself accords with the Sunnah.

The Criterion for Praising Scholars: Adherence to Sunna
Ibn Taymiyya argues that Ash'ari scholars are valued only by their conformity to Sunna.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Scholars are praised or blamed only by their conformity to the Sunna, not their school.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that the true measure of a scholar's worth is not his affiliation with a theological school like the Ash'aris, but his adherence to the Sunna and Hadith. He uses this criterion to rank major figures such as al-Ash'ari, al-Baqillani, al-Juwayni, and al-Ghazali. Understanding this principle helps you see how Ibn Taymiyya reorients the evaluation of Islamic scholarship away from sectarian labels and toward scriptural fidelity.

Commentary
This passage is a critical historical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya, arguing that the true criterion for honoring scholars is their adherence to the Sunna and Hadith, not their affiliation with a theological school. He begins by stating that the Ash'aris were originally united until the fitna of Ibn al-Qushayri. Finally, he notes that later Malikis like al-Baji and Ibn al-Arabi are similarly judged, while earlier greats like Ibn Habib and Ibn Sahnun are on another level. The commentary shows that Ibn Taymiyya uses history to support his thesis that the Sunna is the ultimate measure.

Ibn Taymiyya's Criterion: Sunna and Hadith
Ibn Taymiyya evaluates scholars by their adherence to sunna and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn Taymiyya's criterion of sunna and hadith to evaluate the orthodoxy of an Islamic scholar or sect based on a passage.

Core Idea
Agreement with sunna, not school affiliation, determines scholarly standing.

In this critical passage, Ibn Taymiyya demonstrates his method for assessing the orthodoxy of scholars and sects. He argues that the true measure is not affiliation with a particular school (madhhab) but conformity to the sunna and hadith of the Prophet. By examining historical examples and the case of Ibn Hazm, we can learn to apply this criterion ourselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion
Find the explicit statement that defines the measure of truth. Ibn Taymiyya states that acceptance or rejection of doctrines is based on agreement or disagreement with sunna and hadith.
Look for phrases like 'بموافقة السنة والحديث' (by agreement with sunna and hadith).
2
Apply it to the Seljuks and their opponents
Notice how Ibn Taymiyya evaluates the Rafida and Qaramita: they were overcome by the Seljuks, who reestablished sunna. The vizier Nizam al-Mulk and scholar Abu al-Ma'ali gained stature by 'upholding the sunna and rejecting innovation'.
This shows that political power is secondary; the key is their role in promoting sunna.
3
Analyze the Maliki scholars
Later Malikis like al-Baji and Ibn al-Arabi are venerated only for their agreement with sunna, while earlier figures like Ibn Habib and Ibn Sahnun are 'another matter'—possibly implying they mixed other elements.
Consider why Ibn Taymiyya differentiates between them.
4
Examine the nuanced case of Ibn Hazm
Ibn Hazm is praised for his agreement with sunna on qadar and irja', and for defending hadith, but criticized for holding unique views on the Companions and for mixing philosophical and Mu'tazili ideas in his theology.
Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges his hadith expertise but faults him for deviating from the meaning of Ahl al-Hadith while using their wording.
5
Synthesize the criterion
Conclude that for Ibn Taymiyya, true orthodoxy is determined by substantive agreement with the sunna as understood by the salaf, not by nominal affiliation or selective use of hadith.
This prepares you to evaluate other scholars using the same standard.

Commentary
This passage is a critical evaluation by Ibn Taymiyya of various Islamic sects and scholars, assessing them based on their conformity to the sunna and hadith. Ibn Taymiyya begins by stating that the only valid criterion for accepting or rejecting doctrines is their agreement with the Qur'an, sunna, and hadith. He then gives historical examples: the Rafida and Qaramita gained power early in the Seljuk era but were eventually defeated by the Seljuks, who reinstated the sunna. The Seljuk vizier Nizam al-Mulk and scholar Abu al-Ma'ali (al-Juwayni) gained status by upholding the sunna and rejecting innovation. Similarly, later Maliki scholars like al-Baji and Ibn al-Arabi are only respected for their adherence to sunna and hadith, while earlier Malikis like Ibn Habib and Ibn Sahnun represent a different category. Turning to Ibn Hazm, Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges that Ibn Hazm is praised for his agreement with sunna on issues like qadar and irja', and for his defense of hadith, but criticizes him for holding unique positions on the superiority of the Companions and for mixing philosophical and Mu'tazili ideas in his discussion of God's attributes. Ibn Taymiyya concludes that although Ibn Hazm is more knowledgeable in hadith than al-Ash'ari, al-Ash'ari shows greater agreement with Ahmad ibn Hanbal on the Qur'an and attributes. Ibn Hazm, despite his merits, compromised by using the wording of Ahl al-Hadith but the meanings of the philosophers and Mu'tazila.

Ibn Hazm’s Denial of Inner Meanings and Miracles of Saints
Ibn Taymiyya criticizes Ibn Hazm's denial of ma'ani and kharq al-'adat as deviations from Sunni orthodoxy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Hazm’s positions on denying inner meanings (ma'ani) and the breaking of habits (kharq al-'adat), and identify how these deviate from mainstream Sunni theology.

Core Idea
Ibn Hazm’s extreme literalism led him to deny deeper meanings and saintly miracles.

Ibn Taymiyya criticizes Ibn Hazm for denying the ma'ani (inner meanings) in commands and prohibitions, and for denying kharq al-'adat (miracles of saints). This lesson will examine these two specific deviations and their implications for understanding Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Define ma'ani in Islamic legal theory
Ma'ani refers to the underlying meanings or purposes behind scriptural commands and prohibitions. In usul al-fiqh, they are essential for deriving rulings via analogy (qiyas).
Consider how jurists use 'illa (effective cause) to extend rulings.
2
Understand Ibn Hazm's denial
Ibn Hazm denied that commands and prohibitions have any inner meaning beyond the literal wording; he rejected the concept of ma'ani altogether.
Ibn Taymiyya says 'نفى المعاني في الأمر والنهي والاشتقاق'.
3
Define kharq al-'adat and karamat
Kharq al-'adat is a break in natural habit, often used to describe miracles (karamat) of saints. Mainstream Sunni theology affirms such miracles as possible.
Ash'aris hold that God can break habits through saints as a sign of their piety.
4
Compare Ibn Hazm's view with mainstream
Ibn Hazm denied kharq al-'adat for non-prophets, thus rejecting karamat al-awliya'. This placed him outside the Sunni consensus.
Ibn Taymiyya lists this as a fault: 'نفى خرق العادات ونحوه من كرامات الأولياء'.

Commentary

He also references heresiographers like al-Warraq, al-Nawbakhti, and al-Ash'ari who wrote about such deviations. The passage emphasizes that clear religious truths were abandoned by some leaders, highlighting the danger of intellectual and spiritual deviation.

Commentary
This passage is a historical and theological critique by Ibn Taymiyya of the origins of Greek logic and philosophy, contrasting it with the prophetic tradition. He begins by stating that the early philosophers (Pythagoras, Socrates, Plato) used to travel to the land of the prophets (Syria) and learn from Luqman the Wise and later from followers of David and Solomon. However, Aristotle did not do so; he only had a small amount of 'correct Sabianism' (a remnant of prophetic teaching) and thus invented syllogistic logic (al-ta'alim al-qiyasiyya) on his own. This invention became a law followed by his followers. Ibn Taymiyya then compares Aristotle to the innovators (mubtadi'a) among Muslim theologians, such as Abu al-Hudhayl and Hisham ibn al-Hakam, who founded their own doctrines. In contrast, the true Muslim imams (like Malik, Hammad, al-Thawri) only spoke according to the revealed message. The ignorance of the prophetic way leads people to substitute with these innovations, causing the spread of innovations (bida') and concealment of prophetic ways, leading to destruction. He then cites the saying that clinging to the Sunnah is salvation, and compares the Sunnah to Noah's ark. Just as Noah's ark saved those who boarded it, the Sunnah saves those who follow it. The Quran and Sunnah, when pondered, reveal the truth and falsehood in the sayings of philosophers and others. Finally, he praises the Companions as the most knowledgeable and upright, quoting Ibn Mas'ud urging to follow the dead (the Companions) rather than the living, for they were the best in heart, knowledge, and free from affectation, chosen by Allah for His Prophet's companionship.

Key Terms
البدع (al-bida'): Innovations in religion that depart from the established prophetic practice (Sunnah); Ibn Taymiyya equates Greek logic with such innovations. [Note: In earlier theological discussions, 'bid'a' often meant any innovation in creed; Ibn Taymiyya applies it here to secular philosophy.]
السُّنة (al-sunnah): The prophetic way, including the Quran, hadith, and practices of the early Muslim community, contrasted with human inventions. [Note: No significant shift; the term is stable across the tradition, though Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes its comprehensive role.]
التعاليم القياسية (al-ta'alim al-qiyasiyya): The syllogistic teachings invented by Aristotle; the author uses it derogatorily to mean Greek logic as a human construct. [Note: In standard Mantiq, 'qiyas' is a neutral term. Ibn Taymiyya reframes it as an innovation.]

Commentary
This passage is a critical historical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya regarding the nature of disagreement within the Hanbali school and its relationship with the Ash'ari school. Critique of Transmitted Creeds: Ibn Taymiyya on Understanding vs. Text
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the transmission of creed via personal understanding.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the distinction between a transmitted creed and a transmitter's understanding, and evaluate the reliability of a transmission using Ibn Taymiyya's criteria.

Core Idea
Transmission of a scholar's creed is always filtered through the transmitter's understanding.

Ibn Taymiyya warns that when later scholars transmit the creed of an earlier imam, they often present their own understanding rather than the original words. In this passage, he uses the example of al-Bayhaqi transmitting Imam Ahmad's creed to illustrate this problem. By understanding this critique, you will be able to critically assess historical claims about what a scholar believed.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's general claim
Ibn Taymiyya states that even among groups claiming to follow the Sunnah, conflict exists, but a truth-holding group remains. Notice that he establishes the context of diversity within the Sunni tradition.
2
Locate the Ash'ari-Hanbali relationship
Find where Ibn Taymiyya describes Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari as a follower of Imam Ahmad and mentions friendships between their followers. This sets the stage for the transmission example.
3
Examine the transmission example
Focus on al-Bayhaqi's transmission of Ahmad's creed. Ibn Taymiyya says Bayhaqi transmitted it 'in his own wording' and said 'Abu Abdullah used to be...' rather than quoting directly. This is the key evidence.
The phrase 'in his own wording' (بلفظ نفسه) is crucial.
4
Understand the analogy to fiqh transmission
Ibn Taymiyya compares this to a jurist writing on an imam's school according to his own understanding. He generalizes: people transmit sayings of imams like they transmit Shari'a—based on their own reception and comprehension.
This analogy shows the epistemological principle behind the critique.
5
Formulate the critical principle
The principle is that transmitted creed is not identical to the original because transmission involves personal understanding. Therefore, asserting 'Imam Ahmad said X' requires verification of the chain and wording.
This principle applies to any claim about a scholar's position.

Scholarly Condemnation of Logic: Ibn al-Salah and al-Amidi
Author recounts scholarly condemnation of logic and the anecdote of Ibn al-Salah removing a school from al-Amidi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the obligation of logic and analyze the historical example of Ibn al-Salah's action against al-Amidi.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya uses historical precedent and scriptural proof to condemn Aristotelian logic as a harmful innovation.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya moves from theoretical critique to concrete historical evidence. He argues that the claim of logic's obligation is baseless, supported only by extremists. To drive his point home, he recounts a famous incident where the renowned hadith scholar Ibn al-Salah ordered the removal of a madrasa from the logician al-Amidi, considering it more virtuous than retaking Acre from the Crusaders. This lesson helps you trace how Ibn Taymiyya combines internal criticism, external authority, and scripture to delegitimize logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim under attack
Ibn Taymiyya targets the assertion that studying Aristotelian logic is obligatory (wajib) in Islam. Locate where he states that this claim comes from 'extremists and the ignorant among their followers.'
The obligation claim is central because it would give logic religious legitimacy.

Note his observation that even expert logicians do not consistently apply its rules due to length, lack of benefit, or corruption. He compares logic to 'lean camel meat on a rugged mountain'—inaccessible and useless.
This internal critique undermines the practical value of logic.

Ibn Taymiyya cites a fatwa signed by leading Shafi'i and Hanafi scholars prohibiting logic. Then he narrates how Ibn al-Salah removed a school from al-Amidi, saying it is 'better than taking Acre.'
The anecdote shows that even a brilliant scholar like al-Amidi was not exempt from condemnation.
4
Interpret the scriptural proof
Ibn Taymiyya applies Quran 83:29 ('the criminals laughed at the believers') to frame logicians as mockers of true faith, despite their intellectual acumen.
He uses the verse to invert the hierarchy: the less intelligent believer is superior in faith.
5
Evaluate the argumentative strategy
Consider how Ibn Taymiyya blends ridicule (lean camel analogy), historical precedent (fatwa and story), and scripture to delegitimize logic without engaging its technical content.
This approach is typical of his critique: he targets the social and religious authority of logic, not its internal rules.

Commentary
This passage is a critical section from Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd Mantiq, where he argues against the necessity of Aristotelian logic and recounts the historical opposition of Muslim scholars. He begins by asserting that those who resort to logic have abandoned the truth commanded by God, so attributing any obligation to logic within Islam is invalid. Notably, he points out that even the most skilled logicians do not consistently apply its rules due to its length, lack of benefit, corruption, or ambiguity—comparing it to the meat of a lean camel on a rugged mountain, neither accessible nor useful. This sets the stage for the main point: Muslim scholars and imams have continuously condemned logic and its practitioners. Ibn Taymiyya mentions a fatwa signed by leading Shafi'i and Hanafi scholars prohibiting logic and punishing its people. He then narrates the famous story of Shaykh Abu Amr ibn al-Salah (d. 643 AH) ordering the removal of a madrasa from the logician and theologian al-Amidi (d. 631 AH), considering this act more virtuous than retaking Acre from the Crusaders—highlighting the severity of the opposition. Interestingly, he acknowledges al-Amidi's deep knowledge of theology and philosophy and his good Islam, yet still justifies the action. Finally, Ibn Taymiyya reflects on the nature of subtle intellectual matters: they require intelligence, and logicians may look down on those less sharp, even if the latter have stronger faith. He cites Quran 83:29 to illustrate how the 'criminals' (logicians) mock the believers. The passage thus combines theological critique, historical anecdote, and scriptural proof to condemn logic as a harmful innovation.

Key Terms
المنطق (al-mantiq): Here, 'logic' refers to Aristotelian logic as studied in Islamic philosophy, which Ibn Taymiyya critiques as an un-Islamic innovation. [Note: Ibn Taymiyya redefines mantiq polemically: for him it is not a neutral tool but a foreign, corrupting discipline.]
وجوب (wujub): Obligation: the claim that studying or using logic is obligatory in Islam, which Ibn Taymiyya refutes. [Note: No significant shift; term stable across figh.]
أبو عمرو بن الصلاح (Abu Amr ibn al-Salah): A prominent Shafi'i hadith scholar and jurist (d. 643 AH) known for his opposition to logic and philosophy. [Note: Historical figure; no semantic shift.]

Using the Index of Persons in Naqd al-Mantiq
Alphabetical list of persons with page references.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate all page references for a given scholar in the index of Naqd al-Mantiq and use cross-references to find alternate names.

Core Idea
The index of persons is your roadmap to the scholars and thinkers debated in Ibn Taymiyya’s work.

The index of persons at the end of a classical text like Naqd al-Mantiq is more than a mere list—it is a gateway to the author's intellectual context. By learning how to read it, you can rapidly trace how Ibn Taymiyya engages with specific scholars across his work. This lesson will teach you to navigate the index efficiently, using Arabic alphabetization and cross-references.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scholar’s name
First, determine the name you are searching for, e.g., 'al-Baqillani'. In the index, names are listed by the first part of the name (e.g., 'الباقلاني' under the letter 'ب'). Be aware that many scholars are listed by their kunya or nisba.
2
Locate the name alphabetically
The index is arranged alphabetically by the Arabic alphabet. Find the section for the first letter of the name, then scan within that letter. For example, 'الباقلاني' appears after 'ب' and before 'ت'.
Some entries use '=' to link alternate names, e.g., 'أبو حامد = الغزالي'.
3
Read the page numbers
After the name, you will see a series of page numbers (e.g., '٢٤، ٢٨، ٧٤'). These pages contain discussions about that person. Note that numbers may be mixed Arabic and Western digits.
Multiple page numbers indicate multiple occurrences in the text.
4
Use cross-references
If you see an entry like 'أبو حامد = الغزالي', it means 'Abu Hamid' is the same as 'al-Ghazali'. Use these cross-references to find all pages under any of the person’s names.
This helps when the same scholar is referred to by different titles.
