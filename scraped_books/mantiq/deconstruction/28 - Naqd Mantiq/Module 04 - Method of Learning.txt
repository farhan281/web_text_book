Malik's Principle on Divine Attributes: Meaning Known, Modality Unknown
Malik's response on the nature of divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Malik's four-part response to the question of istiwa and explain the epistemological boundary between meaning and modality.

Core Idea
Affirm the meaning, accept modality unknown, believe, never ask how.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya presents Imam Malik's response to a question about Allah's istiwa. This response became a foundational method for handling divine attributes. We will break down the four elements of Malik's response and understand their logical and epistemological implications. This lesson is crucial for anyone engaging with the Salafi methodology in 'aqidah.

Guided Path
1
Locate Malik's direct speech
Find in the Arabic where Malik begins: 'الاستواء غير مجهول، والكيف غير معقول، والإيمان به واجب، والسؤال عنه بدعة'. Notice the four distinct clauses.
2
Identify the object of each clause
First clause: the attribute (istiwa) itself is not unknown in meaning. Second: the modality (kayf) is not comprehensible. Third: belief in it is obligatory. Fourth: asking about the how is an innovation.
The subject of the first clause is the attribute's meaning; the second is the modality.
3
Contrast meaning and modality
The first two clauses create an epistemological boundary: meaning is affirmed (ghayr majhul) while modality is denied (ghayr ma'qul). This is the key Salafi principle of tafwid al-kayf.
Meaning is known from language; modality is beyond human reason.
4
Understand the consequences
Because the attribute is real, belief is obligatory. But because modality is unknown, asking 'how' is an innovation (bid'a). This shows the severity of violating this principle.
Malik expelled the questioner, demonstrating the seriousness.
5
Apply the principle to another attribute
Take a different attribute (e.g., Allah's hand). Formulate the same four-part response: al-yad ma'luma, al-kayf majhul, etc.
Practice elsewhere to internalize the pattern.

Commentary
This passage presents the stance of Imam Malik on the divine attribute of al-istiwa (establishment over the Throne). When asked how (kayf), Malik's response became a foundational principle for the Salaf: the attribute's meaning is known (established in language), its modality is unknown (unfathomable), belief in it is obligatory, and asking about how is an innovation. The author then criticizes those who interpret istiwa as istila (domination), saying they deviate from Malik's path. The principle is then generalized to all divine attributes mentioned in the Quran and Sunnah—such as nuzul (descending), maji' (coming), yad (hand), wajh (face)—each is to be affirmed in meaning without modality. Finally, the author cites Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, a leading Hanafi jurist, to show that the early jurists were united on accepting these texts without interpretation (ta'wil). This passage underscores the epistemological boundary: reason cannot penetrate the how of divine attributes; acceptance without modality is the path of the righteous predecessors.

Generalizing Malik's Principle to All Divine Attributes
Generalization of Malik's principle to all attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extend Malik's response to istiwa to other attributes like nuzul, maji', yad, wajh, and identify the supporting consensus from Hanafi jurists.

Core Idea
The method for one attribute is the method for all.

After quoting Malik, the author asserts that this answer is sufficient for all attributes mentioned in the Quran and Sunnah. He further supports this with a statement from Muhammad ibn al-Hasan that the early jurists agreed on affirming these texts without interpretation. This lesson will explore the generalization and its basis in scholarly consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the list of attributes
In the Arabic, find the list: النزول، المجيء، اليد، الوجه، and ‘غيرها’. These are examples of attributes treated similarly to istiwa. Note that they are all mentioned in the Quran and hadith.
The passage states: 'في جميع الصفات، مثل: النزول، والمجيء، واليد، والوجه، وغيرها'.
2
Observe the formula repeated
The author explicitly applies Malik's formula to an example: 'فيقال في مثل النزول: النزول معلوم، والكيف مجهول، والإيمان به واجب، والسؤال عنه بدعة'. This is an exact parallel to the istiwa response.
The same four-part structure is used: known meaning, unknown modality, obligatory belief, forbidden inquiry.
3
Recognize the basis for generalization
The author states: 'إذ هي بمثابة الاستواء' – all these attributes are of the same category. They are all revealed in the Book and Sunnah and pertain to Allah's essence or actions.
The basis is that they are all revealed attributes, not that they are identical in meaning.
4
Consider the supporting consensus
The author cites Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, a leading Hanafi jurist, who reported that all jurists from east to west agreed on believing in the Quran and hadiths about Allah's attributes 'without interpretation' (min ghayr tafsir). This provides an ijma' (consensus) for the Salafi methodology.
The phrase 'من غير تفسير' here means without ta'wil (allegorical interpretation), not without explaining the meaning.
5
Apply the generalization to a new attribute
Take an attribute like 'al-wajh' (face). Using the principle, state: al-wajh ma'lum, al-kayf majhul, etc. Recognize that this applies to every attribute whose literal meaning is known but modality is unknown.
Practice ensures understanding of the scope.

Salaf Methodology on Divine Attributes
Author cites al-Qushayri and al-Sabuni on Salaf methodology for attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three errors the Salaf avoid (tashbih, takyif, tahrif) and explain the Salaf position on affirming attributes without modality.

Core Idea
The Salaf affirmed Allah's attributes as revealed, refraining from both negation and anthropomorphism.

In this passage, the author cites al-Qushayri and al-Sabuni to establish the consensus of the early Muslims (Salaf) on how to approach the divine attributes. Rather than interpreting them away or describing their modality, the Salaf believed in them as they came and remained silent about the 'how.' This lesson unpacks that methodology and its key terms, showing why the Salaf considered any departure from it an innovation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rejected deviations
From al-Sabuni's statement, extract the three errors: tashbih (comparing Allah's attributes to creation), takyif (describing the modality of attributes), and tahrif (distorting the meanings of revealed texts). These are the poles the Salaf avoided.
These are not only theological errors but methodological ones.
2
Recognize the positive method of the Salaf
The Salaf 'affirmed and were silent.' They believed in the attributes exactly as revealed without asking 'how' (bilā kayf). This middle path avoids both ta'til (negation) and tashbih.
This is known as the 'salafi' methodology, upheld by Ahl al-Sunnah.
3
Understand the role of ijma' in this doctrine
The author highlights that al-Qushayri reported consensus (ijma') of the Salaf on this methodology. Departing from this consensus is considered harmful and places one outside the community.
Ijma' is a source of authority in Islamic theology.
4
Connect the Quranic proof texts
The passage ends with two verses: 'Nothing is like Him' (42:11) and 'None is comparable to Him' (112:4). These verses are used by the Salaf to negate any likeness while affirming attributes.
These verses are the scriptural foundation for the method.

Commentary
This passage presents the foundational approach of the Salaf regarding Allah's attributes. The author begins by quoting an earlier authority (likely al-Qushayri) who states that the Salaf neither described (with modality) nor interpreted (with metaphorical reinterpretation) the attributes mentioned in revelation. Instead, they affirmed them as they came and remained silent about their modality. The quote then declares that anyone who interprets these attributes has abandoned the Prophet's way and the community, specifically mentioning the innovation of Jahm ibn Safwan (founder of the Jahmiyya). The author then comments, noting that the Imam (al-Qushayri) related consensus (ijma') on this matter, and that departing from this consensus is harmful. He argues that if refraining from interpretation inevitably led to corporealism (tajsim), the Salaf would have fled from that and would have interpreted; but they did not, because they were the most knowledgeable of the Ummah about what is permissible and impossible for Allah. Next, the author cites al-Sabuni, who describes the creed of the people of hadith: they affirm Allah through His attributes as revealed, without tashbih (likening to creation), without takyif (qualifying modality), and without tahrif (distorting words). They avoid both ta'til (negation of attributes) and tashbih, affirming transcendence while negating deficiencies. The author then praises Allah for guiding the Sunnis to this middle path, and concludes by citing two Quranic verses that encapsulate this principle: 'There is nothing like unto Him' (42:11) and 'And there is none comparable to Him' (112:4).

The Badriyyun Principle: Knowing the Boundaries of Religious Knowledge
Author quotes Sa'id ibn Jubayr and al-Hasan al-Basri on limiting religious knowledge to the Salaf and revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of adherence to the knowledge of the Salaf to distinguish between legitimate and speculative theological inquiry.

Core Idea
If the Companions didn't know it, it's not from the religion.

Sa'id ibn Jubayr and al-Hasan al-Basri give us two powerful statements that define the Salafi methodology: stick to what the earliest Muslims knew, and consider ignorance of Allah's unrevealed attributes as part of faith. These are not anti-intellectual but epistemological boundaries that protect revelation's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle from Sa'id ibn Jubayr
Find the exact phrasing: 'Whatever the participants of Badr did not know is not from the religion.' This sets a temporal and authority-based limit: what was unknown to the most virtuous companions cannot be a required part of religion.
2
Analyze al-Hasan al-Basri's reported saying
Mutarraf's statement: 'Praise be to Allah, from whose faith is ignorance of other than what He has described Himself with.' This is paradoxical: faith includes ignorance because true faith means acknowledging that human reason cannot go beyond revelation.
3
Connect both statements to the broader Salafi epistemology
Both quotes emphasize that knowledge of Allah is limited to revelation. The Badriyyun are the highest authority; their knowledge delimits orthodoxy. Ignorance of what Allah did not reveal is not a deficiency of faith but a requirement of it.

The author begins by enumerating twelve methods of acquiring knowledge: speech (kalam), reflection (nazar), inference (istidlal), disputation (muhajja), argumentation (mujadala), unveiling (mukashafa), addressing (mukhataba), ecstasy (wajd), tasting (dhawq), and others like them. He then declares that the People of Hadith possess the essence and purity of all these methods, attributing to them the highest degrees in intellect, balanced analogy, correct opinion, sound speech, authentic reflection, guided inference, upright dialectic, perfect intuition, truthful inspiration, penetrating insight and unveiling, correct hearing and addressing, and greatest ecstasy and tasting. He extends this praise to Muslims relative to other nations, and specifically to Ahl al-Sunnah wal-Hadith relative to other sects. The reasoning for this superiority is empirical: anyone who surveys the world will find Muslims are sharper and sounder in intellect and achieve more in a short time than others in centuries. Moreover, this is due to the belief in the established truth (i'tiqad al-haqq al-thabit) which strengthens and corrects perception, supported by Quranic verses (Muhammad 47:17 and An-Nisa 4:66-68) that show guidance and obedience increase guidance and strength. Ibn Taymiyya then provides four lines of evidence (tara') to demonstrate this superiority: (1) in points of dispute, truth always sides with them; (2) opponents acknowledge them and return to them; (3) believers testify for them; and (4) all sects seek their support against others and accuse others of misguidance more than they accuse them. The fourth evidence is only introduced but not elaborated in this passage.

Harmful and Beneficial Reasoning According to Ibn Taymiyya

Reasoning is only beneficial when based on revelation; otherwise it misleads.

Core Idea

The Method of Istinbat: Deriving Knowledge from Texts
Author contrasts istinbat with philosophical and analogical methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the method of istinbat (derivation from texts) and contrast it with philosophical speculation and analogical opinion as presented in the passage.

Core Idea
True religious knowledge is derived from the texts, not from philosophy or analogy.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya describes how the inheritors of the prophets rely on istinbat—deriving knowledge directly from the Qur'an and hadith. He sharply distinguishes this method from philosophical fantasy and analogical opinion. This lesson will help you recognize the core of the author's epistemological argument and its implications for Islamic methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term 'istinbat'
Find the word 'istinbat' and note how the author describes it as 'splitting rivers' and 'extracting treasures' from texts. This is the central positive method.
Istinbat is contrasted with qiyas (analogy) here, so it is a direct extraction, not analogical extension.
2
List the negative methods rejected
The author rejects 'philosophical fantasy' (khayal falsafi), 'analogical opinion' (ra'y qiyasi), and 'innovative views' (ara' mubtadi'at). Note these are the alternatives to istinbat.
3
Extract the basis of authority
The author grounds religious knowledge in following the Prophet (ittiba'), citing Quran 3:31. Istinbat is the means to follow properly.
4
Recognize the contrast with philosophers
The passage later states that those who venerate philosophy are furthest from knowledge of hadith and its following. This contrast completes the picture.

Who Are the True People of Hadith? A Comprehensive Definition
Author defines Ahl al-Hadith broadly, including inner and outer aspects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Ahl al-Hadith as Ibn Taymiyya does, including both outward and inward aspects of preservation, understanding, and practice.

Core Idea
Ahl al-Hadith are not just narrators, but those who preserve, understand, and follow the hadith inwardly and outwardly.

Ibn Taymiyya provides a striking definition of Ahl al-Hadith that goes beyond mere transmission. This lesson will help you appreciate the breadth of his definition and its implications for religious authority.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three levels of translation/interpretation as defined by Ibn Taymiyya and apply them to an Islamic text.

Core Idea
Knowing a word is not knowing a meaning; knowing a meaning is not knowing its truth.

Ibn Taymiyya presents a hierarchical classification of translation and interpretation, moving from mere word substitution to conceptual understanding and finally to evidential verification. This framework is essential for critically engaging with texts, especially when evaluating truth claims from diverse sources. The passage challenges the assumption that translation is solely a linguistic operation.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the levels of translation and interpretation (tarjama wa tafsir) according to Ibn Taymiyya. It occurs in the context of his critique of Greek logic, where he argues that the truth found in philosophical works must be evaluated against the Quran, and that requires proper translation and interpretation. He distinguishes three levels, proceeding from the most superficial to the most profound. The first level is mere word-for-word substitution (tarjamat mujarrad al-lafẓ), where one replaces a term with a synonymous term. This is useful because many people are tied to particular words and cannot abstract meaning. The second level is translation of meaning and its clarification (tarjamat al-ma‘nā wa bayānih), where one conveys the intended concept to the audience, even if the words are familiar. This involves depicting the meaning through examples, likenesses, or attributes composed from known terms. The third level is establishing the truth of the meaning (bayān ṣiḥḥat dhālik al-ma‘nā wa taḥqīqih) by providing evidence and reasoning that confirms its correctness, either by a simple proof or by showing its cause. This level aims at assent (taṣdīq), not just conceptualization. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that engaging with foreign philosophical texts requires all three levels to properly distinguish truth from falsehood.

Commentary
This passage is a critical and epistemological argument by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by stating a historical observation: innovations appear in every nation, and the ways of the messengers become hidden, leading to destruction. This sets up the importance of clinging to the Sunnah, which he compares to Noah's Ark. The analogy is that just as only those who believed and followed Noah boarded the ark and were saved, those who follow the Sunnah are saved, and those who abandon it are destroyed. The Sunnah is the embodiment of the message from Allah. He then extends this to the study of philosophy and other misguided creeds: the believing scholar, when he reflects on their statements, finds that the Qur'an and Sunnah reveal their true state and distinguish truth from falsehood. 

Next, Ibn Taymiyya praises the Companions of the Prophet as the most knowledgeable and upright in jihad. He quotes Ibn Mas'ud's famous statement warning against following living people who may be subject to fitnah, and urging adherence to the departed Companions who had the purest hearts, deepest knowledge, and least affectation (takalluf). Affectation here means pretentiousness, speaking or acting without true knowledge, or seeking what cannot be attained. Ibn Taymiyya explains that this combination—perfect righteousness of heart and depth of knowledge—is rare among later generations. He contrasts two groups: those with pure hearts but lack of knowledge, and those with deep knowledge but prone to misguidance. The Companions uniquely combined both. 

Then he criticizes later deeply learned people (especially theologians and ascetics) for being afflicted with blameworthy affectation: they speak and act without knowledge and seek the unattainable. In contrast, the Companions were the least affected; their words were few but full of wisdom that guided entire nations. Others fill pages with affectations and absurdities, which are the greatest superfluities. The passage thus serves to ground knowledge in the Sunnah and the example of the Companions, rejecting the pretensions of later philosophers and theologians.

The Prophetic Method as the Complete Path to Knowledge
Author explains the purpose of the method—knowledge of theoretical and practical matters—and cites the hadith of istikhara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn Taymiyya considers the prophetic method superior to any other method for attaining both theoretical and practical knowledge.

Core Idea
The prophetic method combines perfect knowledge, will, and power.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya sets the stage for his critique of Greek logic by defining the method that truly leads to knowledge: the method of the Prophet. He argues that even if others can know and explain, the Prophet is superior in knowledge, desire to guide, and ability to explain. This is not a rejection of reason but a claim about the most complete source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of realities

Theoretical matters concern beliefs; practical matters concern actions.
2

Notice the conditional: 'فمتى كان غير الرسول قادرًا على علم بذلك أو بيان له أو محبة لإفادة ذلك فالرسول أعلم بذلك وأحرص على الهدى وأقدر على بيانه منه' — whenever another can know, explain, or love to convey, the Prophet is more knowledgeable, more eager, and more capable.
This is not absolute denial of others' ability, but affirmation of superiority.
3
Understand the implication for method
Conclude that the prophetic method is the most comprehensive and reliable for attaining truth in both belief and action. This is the foundation for Ibn Taymiyya's critique of purely rational methods.

The Three Perfect Attributes from the Hadith of Istikhara
Author explains the purpose of the method—knowledge of theoretical and practical matters—and cites the hadith of istikhara.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Perfect knowledge, will to do good, and power are the attributes of perfection.

The hadith is evidence that these attributes are complete only in Allah, and by extension the Prophet manifests them perfectly.

Understand the tripartite framework
These three attributes together make the prophetic method perfect: perfect knowledge ensures correct belief, perfect will ensures sincere intent, perfect power ensures effective action.
This framework is crucial for Ibn Taymiyya's epistemology.

Invalidity of Censuring with Unscriptural Terms like Hashw and Tajsim
Author argues that censuring with invented names lacks basis in revelation or consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author's argument that censure and praise are religious rulings requiring scriptural evidence, and apply this principle to determine the validity of using invented names in theological disputes.

Core Idea
Praise and blame are religious rulings; they must be based on names revealed by Allah.

In this passage, the author critiques the common practice of using terms like 'hashwi' or 'mujassim' to condemn opposing theological groups. He argues that such censure lacks any basis in revelation, consensus, or the statements of the Salaf. More fundamentally, he establishes that praise and blame are acts of religious legislation, and therefore require clear textual authorization. This lesson walks you through his multi-layered argument and teaches you to critically assess any theological epithet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target practice
What exactly is the author criticizing? He is attacking the use of invented names like 'hashw' and 'tajsim' as epithets for censuring theological opponents.
2
Find the author's first proof: false generalization about innovators
The author refutes a mistaken claim that all innovators claim to follow the Salaf. He shows that well-known innovating sects like the Khawarij and Rawafid openly reject the Salaf, so the claim is false.
This undermines the opponent's credibility.
3
Locate the fourth 'wajh' (aspect): absence from authoritative sources
The author states that the term is not found in the Book of Allah, the Sunnah, the speech of the Sahaba, Tabi'un, or any recognized imam. This is the core textual evidence for its invalidity.
4
Understand the distinction between mujtahid and muqallid regarding censure
The author argues that a mujtahid must have direct textual evidence (nass, ijma', or derived proof) to censure, while a muqallid cannot use an epithet that his own qualified authority does not employ.
This prevents blind imitation in theological judgments.
5
Grasp the overarching principle: praise/blame are ahkam shar'iyya
Praise and censure are religious rulings that require divine authorization. Using an invented name amounts to legislating a religion not permitted by Allah.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Taqlid in Religious Criticism
Ibn Taymiyya attacks the use of taqlid to criticize others in religious rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical invalidity of using taqlid as a basis for religious criticism according to Ibn Taymiyya's reasoning.

Core Idea
Praise and blame are religious rulings; they require revealed evidence, not blind imitation.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya targets a widespread but flawed practice: using blind imitation (taqlid) to criticize others in religious matters. He exposes the inconsistency and corruption of this approach by distinguishing between the mujtahid and the muqallid, and by grounding praise and blame in revealed authority. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for religious discourse.

Guided Path
1

2

3

4
Extract the broader principle
Ibn Taymiyya asserts that praise and blame are religious rulings (ahkam shar‘iyya) and must be based on names authorized by Allah. Using innovated names amounts to legislating without permission. This grounds the critique in a fundamental epistemological principle.

Ibn Taymiyya's Defense of Salaf's Epistemological Supremacy
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Salaf's knowledge came exclusively from the Prophet, refuting the Jahmiyya-Kullabiyya claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the Salaf's knowledge, derived solely from the Prophet, is superior to any later speculative theology.

Core Idea
The Salaf's knowledge came from the Prophet, so no later generation can improve it.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya mounts a powerful defense of the Salaf's authority. He argues that the early Muslims acquired their knowledge exclusively from the Prophet (peace be upon him), making it superior in completeness and certainty. This directly challenges theologians who claim to follow the Salaf's method while innovating speculative doctrines. Let's break down his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Find where Ibn Taymiyya names the group he is refuting: 'the Jahmiyya-Kullabiyya, such as the author of this speech, Abu Muhammad, and his likes.' Their claim is to follow the method of the Salaf (tariqa al-salaf).
Ibn Taymiyya coins the compound 'Jahmiyya-Kullabiyya' to link two deviant trends.
2
Extract the evidence for Salaf's superiority
List the proofs Ibn Taymiyya provides: the hadith about the best generations, the statement of Ibn Mas'ud, the saying about following the Salaf's traces, and al-Shafi'i's testimony. These establish the Salaf's superiority in knowledge, intellect, and faith.
3
Understand the logical inference
Ibn Taymiyya uses the hadith 'no time will come but that which is after it is worse' to argue that later times cannot produce better knowledge, especially of Allah. The Salaf's knowledge is the best; therefore, any claim of improvement is false.
This is an implicit appeal to the principle of degradation over time, supported by prophetic tradition.
4
Analyze the core rhetorical question
He asks: 'How do you claim the method of the Salaf, when the utmost of what the Salaf had was that they were followers of the Messenger of Allah?' This exposes the contradiction: the Salaf's knowledge came entirely from the Prophet, not from speculation.
The Quranic verses (57:9, 57:28-29, 3:164, 42:52) reinforce that the Prophet brought light and knowledge, so the Salaf needed no additional method.
5
Summarize the refutation
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that anyone claiming to follow the Salaf must adhere exclusively to revealed knowledge. The Jahmiyya-Kullabiyya's use of speculative theology (kalam) contradicts the very method they claim to follow.
The passage does not present a formal syllogism but uses dialectical reasoning.

The Epistemological Difference Between Prophetic and Scholarly Transmission
Author distinguishes transmission of creed from prophetic transmission, emphasizing infallibility difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the epistemological status of transmitting prophetic reports and transmitting scholarly creeds, and apply this distinction to evaluate the reliability of transmitted theological positions.

Core Idea
Prophetic transmission is protected by infallibility; scholarly transmission is not.

When reading the creed of Imam Ahmad as transmitted by later scholars, we must remember that the transmitters were fallible humans. In contrast, the Prophet's statements are infallible, so any apparent contradiction must be resolved by abrogation. Ibn Taymiyya draws an analogy between the two types of transmission but emphasizes the critical difference: Allah preserves the revelation, but not the words of scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of transmission
Find in the passage where Ibn Taymiyya compares transmitting an Imam's creed (like Ahmad's) to transmitting the Shari'a from the Prophet. Note that both involve a transmitter paraphrasing or conveying based on understanding.
The analogy is between a fiqh author presenting an Imam's school and a hadith transmitter conveying the Prophet's teaching.
2
Understand the role of 'isma (infallibility)
The Prophet is infallible, so any contradictory reports from him must be reconciled by abrogation (naskh). Non-prophets are not infallible and may contradict themselves unintentionally.
This is the key point: the epistemological guarantee differs.
3
Recognize the analogical structure
Ibn Taymiyya uses an analogy: transmission of an Imam's creed is like transmission of Shari'a, but with a crucial difference (the 'illa of infallibility does not fully apply to the far').
The analogy highlights both similarity and dissimilarity.
4
Apply the distinction
When evaluating a transmitted creed (e.g., from Imam Ahmad), recognize that the transmitter may have misunderstood or paraphrased. The creed is not guaranteed to be free from error, unlike the Qur'an and authenticated sunna.
This justifies critical examination of historical theological texts.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument comparing the transmission of a scholar's creed (like Imam Ahmad) to the transmission of prophetic statements. The author starts by describing how a writer on Imam Ahmad's creed paraphrases his positions in his own words, analogous to a fiqh author presenting an Imam's legal school. He then notes that people transmitting religious rulings often say 'the ruling of Allah is such' based on their understanding, even if others understand better. This leads to differences in narration from Imams, just as there are differences in hadith. However, the Prophet is infallible (ma'sum), so any apparent contradiction in his statements must be resolved by abrogation (naskh). Non-prophets may contradict themselves unintentionally. But even with prophetic reports, there is need for discernment and some transmissions are more reliable. Given that transmitters of the Prophet's Shari'a (through deduction, istidlal) themselves differ greatly, it should be no surprise that similar differences occur in transmitting from other scholars. The reason Allah preserves the Qur'an and sunna but not the words of others is because the revelation is the proof (hujja) and guidance, and if error could creep in without clarification, the proof would be lost. Moreover, there is no prophet after Muhammad to correct misunderstandings. So the transmission of prophetic knowledge is protected, while transmission from other scholars is not. This justifies caution in accepting transmitted creeds.

This is due to the blessing of the Prophet's comprehensive speech and the unique knowledge given to the Muslim nation. Finally, he critiques the origin of logic: it was invented by Aristotle, the first teacher of the Sabian innovators, to weigh their wisdom and philosophy, which is divided into theoretical and practical. The theoretical part, considered the entrance to truth, consists of mathematics, while the practical part concerns ethics and politics. Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges that these sciences and works distinguish their practitioners from ignorant nations without revelation, and that they contain benefits for worldly reform and even elements of truth and justice that overlap with what the messengers brought. However, he implies that logic itself is not a reliable source of truth; it is merely a tool for a pagan philosophical system, and its benefits are subsumed under the superior, revelation-based sciences.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the claim that the art of logic (mantiq) produces true knowledge and guidance. Ibn Taymiyya begins by describing the effect of logic on people who were previously following inherited false beliefs (like ignorant Christians, Jews, and Rafida). He claims that logic causes them to abandon those false beliefs, but instead of embracing the truth, they become either deceptive hypocrites who mislead the masses or heretical hypocrites who reject both truth and falsehood. Thus, logic does not actually lead them to truth; it only makes them worse off in a different way. He then generalizes: any supposed benefit from logic—such as sharpening the mind or clarifying expressions—is merely relative to a previous worse state, not due to any intrinsic perfection of logic. He compares this to a polytheist becoming a Magian or a Magian becoming a Jew: it is an improvement relative to their previous state, but it is not a foundation for the people of manifest truth. Ibn Taymiyya then argues that this phenomenon is not unique to logic; any precise field, like grammar (nahw), displays the same relative improvement. In fact, grammarians surpass logicians in verification, precision, division, and definition. He explains that meanings (ma'ani) are innate and intellectual, not requiring a conventional system, whereas their verbal molds (alfaz) vary. Learning the best forms and molds for meanings with a sound innate disposition is more beneficial than an artificial craft (like logic) that deals with innate matters requiring no special convention. He then praises the benefit of grammar for the sciences in general, and especially for the Islamic sciences, noting that Muslims who inherited ancient sciences and expressed them in Arabic produced works of greater perfection, verification, comprehensiveness, and conciseness than the originals. This is due to the blessing of the Prophet's comprehensive speech and the unique knowledge given to the Muslim nation. Finally, he critiques the origin of logic: it was invented by Aristotle, the first teacher of the Sabian innovators, to weigh their wisdom and philosophy, which is divided into theoretical and practical. The theoretical part, considered the entrance to truth, consists of mathematics, while the practical part concerns ethics and politics. Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges that these sciences and works distinguish their practitioners from ignorant nations without revelation, and that they contain benefits for worldly reform and even elements of truth and justice that overlap with what the messengers brought. However, he implies that logic itself is not a reliable source of truth; it is merely a tool for a pagan philosophical system, and its benefits are subsumed under the superior, revelation-based sciences.

Ibn Taymiyya's Classification of Logicians' Discourse
Author states that logicians' discourse is confined to definitions and syllogisms, and criticizes it as artificial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize logicians' discourse into definitions (hudud) and syllogisms (aqyisa) as Ibn Taymiyya does, and identify his critique of artificiality (takalluf).

Core Idea
Logic is nothing but definitions and syllogisms – and most of it is forced.

Ibn Taymiyya sets out to describe the essence of the logicians' enterprise. He reduces it to two pillars: definitions (hudud) that produce concepts, and syllogisms (aqyisa) that produce assents. He then diagnoses a pervasive problem: takalluf, or artificiality. In this lesson, we will follow his reduction and his critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Find where Ibn Taymiyya says 'all their speech is confined to the definitions (hudud) that yield concepts and to the syllogisms (aqyisa) that yield assents.' Notice that he uses 'all' (kull) to claim completeness. Ask yourself: do logicians actually say this?
This is a strong claim of reduction.
2
Subdivide definitions into three types
Look at the three types: haqiqiyya (real), rasmiyya (by description), and lafziyya (verbal). Real definitions give the essence, descriptive give distinguishing marks, and verbal explain the meaning of a word. Mark the Arabic terms.
Ibn Taymiyya groups them all; classical logicians often distinguish them.
3
Subdivide syllogisms into three types
Find the three types: umum wa shumul (generality and inclusion, i.e., categorical syllogism), shabah wa tamthil (analogy), and istiqra' (induction). Note that Ibn Taymiyya includes analogical reasoning and induction as types of qiyas, broadening the usual Aristotelian sense.
This expansion is part of his critique of the logicians' narrow definition.
4
Understand the critique of takalluf
Ibn Taymiyya says most of their speech is not free from takalluf. He gives two possibilities: either they force themselves to know what they don't (claiming certainty where there is none) or they force complexity when the matter is already simple and known. Reflect: does this apply to any logical arguments you have seen?
This is a general accusation, not a detailed refutation.

And this station I do not mention in it the points of dispute so that it is said: it is arguing from the different to the different. Rather, I describe the genus of their speech, and I say:
There is no doubt that all their speech is confined to the definitions (hudud) that yield concepts (tasawwurat), whether the definitions are real (haqiqiyya) or by description (rasmiyya) or verbal (lafziyya), and to the syllogisms (aqyisa) that yield assents (tasdiqat), whether they are syllogisms of generality and inclusion (umum wa shumul) or analogy and likeness (shabah wa tamthil) or induction and tracking (istiqra' wa ta tabbu').
And most of their speech is not free from artificiality (takalluf), either in knowledge or in speech: either they force themselves to know what they do not know, so they speak without knowledge, or the thing is known to them.
Source pages p. 304

Commentary
This passage is a critical overview of the logicians' methodology. Ibn Taymiyya begins by clarifying that he is not entering into specific points of dispute, but rather describing the general nature of their discourse. He states that all logical discourse reduces to two categories: definitions (hudud) which produce concepts (tasawwurat), and syllogisms (aqyisa) which produce assents (tasdiqat). He subdivides definitions into three types: real (haqiqiyya) which give the essence, by description (rasmiyya) which give distinguishing marks, and verbal (lafziyya) which explain the meaning of a word. Similarly, he subdivides syllogisms into three types: categorical syllogisms of generality and inclusion (like the standard Aristotelian syllogism), analogical reasoning (tamthil), and induction (istiqra'). He then critiques that most of their speech is forced (takalluf), either because they claim knowledge they do not have, or because they engage in unnecessary complexity when the matter is already known. This sets up his broader critique: the logicians' apparatus is not as useful or certain as they claim.
