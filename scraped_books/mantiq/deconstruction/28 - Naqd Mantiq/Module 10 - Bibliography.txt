Defining Beneficial Consideration as Revelation-Based
Author defines useful nazar as consideration of Quran and Sunnah, and criticizes speculative reasoning leading to corrupt belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between misleading and guiding evidence based on Ibn Taymiyya’s criteria, and identify the epistemic consequences of each for Islamic belief.

Core Idea
Only reasoning anchored in revelation yields knowledge; speculative logic corrupts the heart.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya’s critique of logic tackles a fundamental question: What kind of reasoning actually produces certainty? He warns against arguments that look correct but rest on false premises or invalid forms, which he calls ‘misleading evidence’. Such reasoning, even when sincerely believed, only produces corrupt belief (i‘tiqād fāsid). In contrast, the only ‘beneficial consideration’ is that which is based on the guiding evidence of the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding this distinction is crucial for any student of Islamic thought who wants to separate sound theological method from sophistry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of evidence
The passage contrasts ‘misleading evidence’ (dalīl muḍill) with ‘guiding evidence’ (dalīl hādī). The first can have false premises or an invalid form; the second is defined by its source: the Book of Allah and the Sunnah.
2
Trace the consequence of misleading evidence
According to Ibn Taymiyya, using a misleading evidence while believing it correct leads to a corrupt belief in the heart. He attributes the sophistries of philosophers and theologians to this cause.
3
Understand why mere conceptual study fails
Considering the very concept one wants to judge, without external evidence, does not yield knowledge. Instead, it can generate deceptive doubts that the heart mistakes for proofs due to its eagerness.
4
Locate the definition of beneficial consideration
The author defines beneficial consideration as that which is based on a guiding evidence, and stipulates that the guiding evidence in absolute terms is the Quran and Sunnah. He asserts that the Shari‘ah contains the two types of consideration that truly benefit.
5
Apply the distinction to a real case
Consider a theological argument that is logically valid but uses a premise not derived from revelation. According to Ibn Taymiyya, such an argument is misleading and can produce corrupt belief.

And if the consideration is in a misleading evidence, and the considerer believes it to be correct, either because its two premises or one of them contains falsehood, or because the two premises are correct but the composition is not valid — then thereby a corrupt belief arises in the heart. Commentary
This passage is a critical examination of the nature of beneficial consideration (nazar). The author explains what happens when a person uses false premises or invalid syllogisms while believing them correct: it leads to corrupt belief (i'tiqad fāsid), which he identifies as the source of most sophistries (shubuhāt) of those who oppose revelation, such as philosophers and speculative theologians. He then argues that considering a known concept itself (without external evidence) does not produce knowledge; in fact, it can generate doubts mistaken for proofs due to the heart's eagerness. The beneficial consideration is defined as one based on a guiding evidence (dalīl hādī), which in general is the Quran and Sunnah. The Shari'ah encompasses two types of consideration (presumably theoretical and practical) and alone yields guidance. Thus, the author establishes revelation as the only reliable source of knowledge, dismissing both blind speculation and the rationalist method of the philosophers.

Parsing the Bibliography of Naqd Mantiq
List of book titles and page numbers from the index of Naqd Mantiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize the sources in Naqd Mantiq's bibliography by Islamic science and identify works central to Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Core Idea
A bibliography maps the intellectual battlefields an author engages.

This lesson helps you read a classical bibliography not as a dry list, but as a window into the author's sources, allies, and opponents. The bibliography of Naqd Mantiq reveals which works Ibn Taymiyya considered important enough to cite or critique. By parsing it, you gain insight into the intellectual landscape of his critique of logic.

Guided Path
1
Scan for major authors
Quickly identify well-known scholars like Ibn Taymiyya, al-Ghazali, Ibn Arabi, al-Ash'ari, and al-Razi. Their presence indicates which traditions are being engaged.
Some works appear only by title without author; you may need cross-reference.
2
Note equivalences
Some entries show an equals sign (=) linking alternative titles (e.g., 'رد المازري على الغزالي = الكشف والإنباء'). This helps identify the same work under different names.
These equivalences are crucial for accurate citation in research.
3
Categorize by Islamic science
Group the works into categories: Hadith (Sahih al-Bukhari, Musnad Ahmad), Kalam (Maqalat al-Islamiyyin), Sufism (Fusus al-Hikam), Fiqh (Risala al-Shafi'i), Logic (fatwa on prohibition of logic), etc.
Some works span multiple disciplines; choose the primary one.
4
Identify polemical works
Look for titles that explicitly refute or attack: 'الرد على الزنادقة', 'فتيا في تحريم المنطق', 'فضائح المعتزلة'. These indicate direct targets of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
The fatwa on logic is especially relevant to the theme of Naqd Mantiq.
5
Assess scope and diversity
Note that the bibliography includes non-Muslim sources (Jewish books like 'المشنا' and 'النبوءات'). This shows Ibn Taymiyya's engagement with other traditions.
Understanding the breadth of sources helps contextualize his arguments.
