Distinguishing Certainty with Knowledge from Certainty without Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes certain judgment with knowledge from without, and discusses error due to physical causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between jāzim bi-ghayr 'ilm and jāzim bi-'ilm based on Ibn Taymiyya's internal awareness criterion.

Core Idea
Certainty without knowledge is self-aware emptiness; certainty with knowledge is self-aware fullness.

Ibn Taymiyya opens a crucial epistemological distinction: not all certainty is equal. Some people are certain but know they do not truly know, while others are certain and feel the inner presence of knowledge. He argues that this inner awareness is as direct as our awareness of our own emotions. In this lesson, we will dissect this distinction and understand how error fits within the norm of sound human cognition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of certainty
Read the first two clauses of the Arabic passage. Identify who are the 'jāzim bi-ghayr 'ilm' and 'jāzim bi-'ilm'. What does each find within himself?
The key phrase is 'yajidu min nafsihi' (he finds from himself).
2
Analyze the self-awareness analogy
Ibn Taymiyya compares knowing that you know to knowing that you are hearing, seeing, loving, etc. Why is this comparison powerful? What does it reveal about the nature of knowledge?
Knowledge here is an immediate state, not a discursive proof.
3
Examine the error exception
He acknowledges error occurs (e.g., taste distorted by bile). How does he argue that occasional error does not undermine the general reliability of perception?
Error has specific causes like disease.
4
Apply the fitrah principle
Ibn Taymiyya grounds sound cognition in the original human disposition (fitrah) created by God. How does this theological foundation explain why error is the exception, not the norm?
Fitrah is the norm (asl); error is accidental.
5
Test the distinction
Given a scenario where a person is certain about a matter (e.g., the prohibition of wine), how would you determine whether his certainty is with or without knowledge according to Ibn Taymiyya?
Focus on the presence or absence of inner awareness of knowing.

Desire: The one who judges with certainty without knowledge finds from himself that he is not knowledgeable about what he judged, and the one who judges with knowledge finds from himself that he is knowledgeable; for being knowledgeable and not knowledgeable is like being hearing and seeing and not hearing and seeing. So he knows from himself that just as he knows from himself that he is loving, hating, willing, disliking, joyful, sorrowful, blessed, tormented, and so on. Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exposition by Ibn Taymiyya on the nature of certainty (jazzm) and its relationship to knowledge ('ilm). He opens by contrasting two types of people: one who judges with certainty but without knowledge, and one who judges with certainty based on knowledge. The first, he says, finds within himself that he is not truly knowledgeable about what he judges, while the second finds that he is knowledgeable. This internal awareness is analogous to our awareness of our own sensations, emotions, and volitions—such as hearing, seeing, love, hate, joy, and sorrow. Ibn Taymiyya then addresses a possible objection: what about a person who doubts his own knowledge while actually knowing? He argues that such a person is like one who claims to know something he does not know; it is a contradiction. Similarly, one who mistakenly thinks he saw or heard something he did not is in error. Error can occur in both affirmation and negation, but the occurrence of error in specific cases does not negate the general reliability of our perceptions. For example, a person may taste something sweet as bitter due to an excess of yellow bile, but this does not mean he is always mistaken about tastes. The root cause of such errors is analogous to a disease affecting the body; the underlying norm (asl) is soundness (sihha) in perception and intellect. God created humans upon the fitrah, and the exceptions to sound perception are known and identifiable by their specific causes, such as bile affecting taste or squinting affecting vision. This passage sets the epistemological foundation: certainty is self-evident, and occasional error does not undermine the fundamental reliability of human cognition.

Desire as an Epistemic Obstacle
Ibn Taymiyya attributes false certainty to desires and cites Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how desire (hawa) causes false certainty according to Ibn Taymiyya, using his Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
Desire turns falsehood into certainty in the heart.

Ibn Taymiyya begins by urging self-reflection: most people who are certain about what they should not be certain of are driven by desire. He cites two Quranic verses that link misguidance to following desire without knowledge. He then applies this to Jews and Christians, showing how stubbornness and pride produce false certainty. This analysis reveals that epistemological error often has a moral and psychological root.

Guided Path
1
Locate the Quranic verses cited
Find the two verses Ibn Taymiyya uses: Al-An'am 6:119 and Al-Qasas 28:50. Notice they both connect following desire (hawa) to misguidance without knowledge.
These verses ground his argument in revelation.
2
Identify the psychological mechanism
Ibn Taymiyya says that desires such as pride, envy, and hardness of heart (as exemplified by the Jews) cause one to insist on falsehood with certainty. Identify these desires in the passage.
Desire here is not just sensual but includes intellectual arrogance.
3
Contrast the cases of Jews and Christians
Notice that Jews are certain of their falsehood, while Christians are more astray but less certain. This shows that certainty does not equal truth; it can be produced by desire.
Ibn Taymiyya is interested in the epistemic state, not just belief content.
4
Apply the tool: diagnose a false certainty
Think of a contemporary theological or juristic disagreement where one party is absolutely certain but the other is not. Using Ibn Taymiyya's framework, what desire could be driving that certainty?
This step bridges to Islamic sciences like kalam or usul.

Commentary
This passage is a critical epistemological argument by Ibn Taymiyya, connecting false certainty to the role of desire (hawa) and then refining the definition of tawatur (mass transmission). He begins by urging self-reflection: most people who are certain about something that does not warrant certainty are driven by desires. The Criterion of Tawatur: Certainty Over Numbers
Ibn Taymiyya defines tawatur by occurrence of certainty, not a fixed number.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Ibn Taymiyya's criterion for tawatur and distinguish it from definitions based on a fixed number of narrators.

Core Idea
Tawatur is defined by the certainty it produces, not by a number.

After discussing desire, Ibn Taymiyya turns to tawatur (mass transmission). He argues that the criterion for tawatur is the actual occurrence of certain knowledge in the listener, not a specific number of narrators or conditions. He aligns himself with 'the verifiers' (al-muḥaqqiqūn) who held this view. This critique targets logicians and usulis who try to quantify tawatur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard definition of tawatur that Ibn Taymiyya opposes
Many scholars define tawatur as a report transmitted by a specific minimum number (e.g., 4, 5, 20) in every generation. Ibn Taymiyya rejects this. Find the phrase 'they did not restrict it to a number or a description'.
The opposing view is implicit: logicians who set numerical thresholds.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's criterion
He says: 'the verifiers made the existence of knowledge of the reported content the criterion for obtaining tawatur... whenever knowledge occurs, that is what is considered.' So the criterion is the subjective experience of certainty.
This is a radical shift from objective to subjective standard.
3
Understand the analogy with sensory knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya compares tawatur to sense perception: we find our souls certain of what we have sensed, and similarly certain of mass-transmitted reports. This shows that certainty is the common element.
The analogy strengthens his argument by grounding it in everyday experience.
4
Apply the tool: evaluate a report's tawatur
Take a well-known historical account, e.g., the revelation of the Quran. Would Ibn Taymiyya say it is mutawatir? Yes, because it produces certainty. Now consider a report known only by a handful of chains. It may not produce certainty, so it is not mutawatir regardless of how many narrators are specified.
This distinguishes his view from those who would label a report with 5 narrators as mutawatir even if the listener is not certain.

The Immediacy of Self-Knowledge: Ibn Taymiyya's Argument Against Infinite Regress
Author argues that knowledge of one's own knowledge is immediate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that knowledge of one's own knowledge is immediate and cannot be proven without circularity or infinite regress.

Core Idea
Self-knowledge is foundational; any proof would require an infinite regress.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya addresses a fundamental epistemological issue: can a person prove that they know something? He argues that such proof is impossible because it would require an infinite chain of proofs or circular reasoning. Instead, he asserts that self-knowledge is immediate and directly perceived. This argument is crucial for understanding the foundations of knowledge in Islamic thought, particularly in theology and legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Read the passage and locate where Ibn Taymiyya asserts that a person finds himself knowing (يجد نفسه عالمة). This is the claim that self-knowledge is immediate and does not require proof.
2
Understand the reductio argument
Ibn Taymiyya argues that if proving one's own knowledge required a proof, that proof would require knowing its premises, leading to an infinite chain (تسلسل) or circularity (دور). Trace this reasoning in the text.
The argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum: assume the opposite leads to absurdity.
3
Apply to mutawatir reports
The passage connects this to how we know mutawatir reports: we find ourselves knowing them directly, similar to sensory knowledge. Identify how the criterion for mutawatir is subjective certainty, not a fixed number.
4
Recognize the epistemological principle
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that knowledge is perceived when its cause exists, whether the cause is self-evident or theoretical. This establishes that some knowledge is foundational and not dependent on further proof.

This parallels the argument used against those who deny the validity of recurrent (mutawatir) reports: we simply find ourselves knowing with certainty the content of such reports, just as we know what we perceive directly. The verifiers (al-muhaqqiqun) made this subjective certainty the actual criterion for a report being mutawatir, not a fixed number of transmitters. Then Ibn Taymiyya asserts that a person finds himself knowing, and this is a fact. He explains that one cannot prove one's own knowledge by a proof, because any proof would rely on premises that one must already know, which would require a prior awareness of knowing those premises, leading to an infinite regress or circular reasoning. Instead, knowledge is directly perceived when its cause is present, whether the cause is a self-evident truth or a theoretical argument whose premises are known. This argument was used to refute those who deny that theoretical reasoning (al-nazar) yields knowledge at all. Ibn Taymiyya notes that there is further detail to this issue, but it is not the place to elaborate.

The Epistemological Foundation: Faith and Remembrance
Author argues that true knowledge originates from faith and divine guidance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemological foundation of knowledge according to Ibn Taymiyya's passage and explain why reasoning depends on divinely given foundational knowledge.

Core Idea
True knowledge is rooted in faith and remembrance, not unaided reason.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd al-Mantiq challenges the primacy of pure reason by grounding all knowledge in divine guidance. It argues that remembrance of Allah produces faith, which is the root of all knowledge, and that even rational investigation requires prior foundational knowledge that is not acquired through investigation. Faith (iman) is explicitly called the root (asl) of all knowledge. The author cites a companion's statement: 'We learned faith, then we learned the Qur'an, and so our faith increased.' This shows that faith precedes detailed knowledge.

The author makes a logical point: any investigation or reasoning (nazar) requires prior known truths that are not themselves acquired by reasoning. These serve as the root and means for further inference.
This is a key critique of purely rationalist epistemologies.

Connect to the critique of pure reason
This shows that unaided reason is insufficient; all reasoning depends on divinely bestowed foundational knowledge. The seeker must ask Allah for guidance and rely on remembrance.

He argues that each has limitations: sensations give only particulars, experience and intuition rely on analogy, and even self-evident truths are mental constructs. True knowledge of external existents requires the combination of sense and intellect.

Badihiyyat are universal but pertain only to mental constructs (e.g., arithmetic truths). They do not inform about external existents without sense.

Ibn Taymiyya argues that knowledge of external reality requires both sense (to perceive particulars) and intellect (to formulate universal judgments). Neither alone suffices.

Commentary
This passage is a critical epistemological analysis by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by listing types of knowledge: inward and outward sensations, intellectual matters, self-evident truths (badihiyyat), mutawatir reports, empirical matters (tajribiyyat), and some add intuitive matters (hadsiyyat). He then critiques each: Sensations only grasp particular determinate things; they cannot produce universal propositions because sense perception is always of individuals. Empirical matters (tajribiyyat) are also based on particular sensed things, and when the intellect generalizes from them, it does so by analogy (qiyas al-tamthil), not certain inference. Intuitive matters (hadsiyyat), according to those who accept them, are similar to empirical ones but involve no action (e.g., knowing the moon's phases change due to its position relative to the sun). They also yield particulars, only becoming general through analogical reasoning. Self-evident truths (badihiyyat) are primary innate knowledge, like arithmetic truths. These are universal but concern mental constructs, not external realities. For example, knowing that one is half of two is about abstract numbers, not any specific external thing. To judge about external existents, sense is required alongside the intellect. Ibn Taymiyya then argues that the intellect alone cannot obtain knowledge of external general things without sense; rather, it deals with estimated universals (like one, two, straight, curved, necessary, possible) that may or may not have external instances. To know whether these correspond to external reality, sense is necessary. When sense and intellect combine (like sight and reason), one can perceive specific external realities and derive their universal judgments, including comparisons and distinctions. This combination is the true function of the intellect and its syllogism. If sense is alone, it only perceives particular existents; if intellect is alone, it only knows abstract universals without knowing their external existence. He illustrates with the example of 'one hundred is a tenth of a thousand': this is a mental truth independent of any external objects; but to know that a particular hundred exists, sense is needed. The passage thus refutes the claim that any single source (sense, experience, intuition, or innate reason) alone gives universal knowledge of external realities.

The Third Argument: Universal Premises Must Be Known Without Syllogism
Third argument for the dispensability of syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's third argument against the necessity of syllogism and explain why universals must be known axiomatically.

Core Idea
Syllogism itself depends on universal premises known without syllogism.

Ibn Taymiyya uses the logicians' own assumption against them: if all universal knowledge requires a syllogism, then an infinite regress or circularity follows. Therefore, some universal premises must be axiomatic (badihi). He then argues that if one universal can be known without syllogism, others can too, depending on intellectual capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting point
Ibn Taymiyya begins by noting that every syllogism requires universal premises (qadiyya kulliyya). He then asks: how are these premises known?
2
Recognize the impossibility of regress
If every universal premise required another syllogism to be known, we would have an infinite chain (tasalsul) or a circle (dawr), both unacceptable.
This is a standard reductio ad absurdum.
3
Conclude axiomatic knowledge
Thus, there must be universal premises that are known without syllogism, i.e., axiomatic (badihi) like the axioms the logicians themselves accept.
4
Extend the conclusion
If one universal can be known without qiyas, then any universal can be, because there is no constant distinction; it depends on intellectual strength and experience.
Ibn Taymiyya uses this to break the logicians' exclusivity claim.

The Fourth Argument: The Logicians' Claim is Unsubstantiated
Fourth argument: the logicians' claim is unsubstantiated and refuted by experience

Learning Objective

The claim that all non-axiomatic knowledge requires syllogism is an unsubstantiated generalization.

This is clarified by the fourth aspect, which is that we say: grant that the form and matter of logical syllogism yield universal sciences, but where does one know that universal knowledge is not attained, so that these pretenders who say what they have no knowledge of, and those who follow them among the people of religions and their scholars, say: 'What is not axiomatic among concepts and assents is known only through definition and syllogism'? Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's third and fourth arguments against the necessity of syllogism for universal knowledge. In the third argument, he establishes that every syllogism requires universal premises, but these premises cannot be known by sense perception or by another syllogism (to avoid circularity/regress). Therefore, there must be some universal premises that are known without syllogism, i.e., axiomatic (badihi). Since such axiomatic universal knowledge exists, it follows that universal sciences can dispense with syllogism. Moreover, if one universal science can be known without syllogism, others can too, because the line between what can be known directly and what cannot is not fixed; it depends on intellect, mental clarity, and experience with particulars. Hence, no universal knowledge necessarily requires logical syllogism. In the fourth argument, he concedes for argument's sake that syllogism can yield universal sciences, but questions the claim that non-axiomatic concepts and assents are only known through definition and syllogism. He argues that this claim is unsubstantiated; the claimant has not examined his own experience, which would show him attaining universal knowledge without syllogism. Even if he personally lacks such experience, he cannot generalize that all humans are like him, given their diverse capacities and divine gifts. The argument uses Islamic epistemological principles and refutes the logicians' exclusivism.
