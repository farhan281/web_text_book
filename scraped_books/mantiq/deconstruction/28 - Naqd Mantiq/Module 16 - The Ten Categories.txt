Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Metaphysics
Ibn Taymiyya attacks metaphysics as mostly mental constructs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to metaphysics (al-ilahiyyat) and evaluate his claim that most of it is mental constructs.

Core Idea
Metaphysics is mostly empty mental speculation, not knowledge of external reality.

Ibn Taymiyya begins this passage by acknowledging the high status philosophers give to metaphysics, calling it 'first philosophy' and 'divine science'. However, he immediately counters that its actual truth content is meager. This lesson walks through his critique: why he considers metaphysics a collection of mental judgments rather than real knowledge, and what this means for the authority of philosophical reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Locate where Ibn Taymiyya mentions 'what is after nature' (mā ba‘d al-ṭabī‘a) and 'divine science' (al-‘ilm al-ilāhī). Note how he describes the philosophers' veneration of this field.
Philosophers call it 'first philosophy' and the 'universal science'.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's core objection
Find the sentence where he says 'the truth in it among the issues is small and meager' and 'most of it is knowledge of mental judgments, not external realities.' Understand that he is attacking the epistemological basis of metaphysics.
3
Analyze his reasoning for the objection
Notice his claim that 'most of it is not based on a logical syllogism.' This challenges the philosophers' method. Ask: if metaphysics is not grounded in syllogistic reasoning, what is it based on according to Ibn Taymiyya?
He implies it is speculation about abstract concepts like 'absolute existence' and 'necessity'.
4
Identify the epistemological type of objection
Classify Ibn Taymiyya's objection: is it empirical, linguistic, or based on revelation? Here it is epistemological: he distinguishes mental judgments from external realities. This is a key theme in his overall critique of Greek logic.
He does not deny the existence of metaphysical concepts, but their reality outside the mind.

As for the science of metaphysics (what is after nature), even though they venerate it and say it is the first philosophy and the universal science that looks at existence and its accidents, and their later ones call it 'the divine science' (al-ilm al-ilahi), and their first teacher claimed that it is the goal of their philosophy and the end of their wisdom, the truth in it among the issues is small and meager. Most of it is knowledge of mental judgments, not external realities. Most of it is not based on a logical syllogism. For the absolute existence, necessity, possibility, the absolute cause, the effect, and the division of that into the two parts of quiddity, namely matter and form, and into the two causes of its existence, namely the efficient and the final cause, and the discussion of the division of existence into substance and the nine accidents, which are quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, possession, action, and passion, as some of them recited concerning it: 'Zayd, the tall, the black, son of Malik, in his house yesterday, was leaning, in his hand a sword, he drew it, then it ceased to exist. So these are ten categories together.'

Commentary
This passage is a sharp critique of metaphysics (al-ilahiyyat) by Ibn Taymiyya. He begins by describing how philosophers exalt metaphysics as the first philosophy and the universal science of existence, and their later scholars call it the divine science. Their 'first teacher' (Aristotle) claimed it is the ultimate goal of philosophy. However, Ibn Taymiyya asserts that very few true propositions exist in it; most of it consists of mental judgments that have no external reality. Moreover, most of its content is not based on logical syllogism. He then lists the typical topics of metaphysics: existence in itself, necessity, possibility, cause, effect, the division of quiddity into matter and form, and the division of existence into substance and nine accidents (quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, possession, action, passion). He mocks this by citing a couplet that uses the example of a person named Zayd and his various attributes to illustrate the ten categories, implying that such classification is arbitrary and pedantic. The couplet ends with 'these are ten categories together,' showing the weakness of the philosophical enterprise.

The Ten Categories as a Target of Critique
Ibn Taymiyya mocks the ten categories with a poetic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ten Aristotelian categories and explain why Ibn Taymiyya ridicules their classification using the poetic example.

Core Idea
The ten categories are an arbitrary list, easily mocked by attaching them to a single individual.

After critiquing metaphysics in general, Ibn Taymiyya turns to a specific example: the ten categories (substance and nine accidents). He cites a couplet that applies all ten categories to a man named Zayd, showing how the classification can be reduced to a trivial description. This lesson unpacks that critique and its implications for the pretensions of Aristotelian logic and metaphysics.

Guided Path
1
List the ten categories as mentioned in the Arabic
Find the list: substance (jawhar) and the nine accidents: quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, possession, action, passion. Write them down.
The Arabic terms are: الكم، الكيف، الإضافة، الأين، متى، الوضع، الملك، أن يفعل، أن ينفعل.
2
Connect the categories to the poetic example
Read the couplet: 'Zayd, the tall, the black, son of Malik, in his house yesterday, was leaning, in his hand a sword, he drew it, then it ceased to exist.' Identify which part of the couplet corresponds to which category: tall=quality, black=quality, son of Malik=relation, in his house=place, yesterday=time, leaning=position, in his hand a sword=possession, he drew it=action, it ceased=passion. Zayd himself is the substance.
The couplet is a clever satire: it shows that all ten categories can be described in a single sentence about a trivial event.
3
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's rhetorical purpose
Why does Ibn Taymiyya include this couplet? He is mocking the philosophers' claim that the categories are necessary and universal divisions of reality. By applying them to an everyday situation, he shows they are mundane and not profound metaphysical truths.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: if the categories were truly fundamental, they would not be so easily parodied.
4
Evaluate the force of the critique
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's use of the couplet is a fair criticism of Aristotelian categories. Does it actually refute the usefulness of the categories, or is it merely a rhetorical trick? Reflect on what the categories are supposed to accomplish in logic and metaphysics.
Some defenders of categories might say that the list is not meant to be exhaustive of everything, but rather a tool for analysis.
