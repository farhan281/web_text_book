On the Necessity of Certainty in Knowledge
Author argues that one's own certainty is self-evident and cannot be denied without proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between necessary knowledge (al-'ilm al-daruri) and inferential knowledge, and explain why the denial of another's certainty requires proof.

Core Idea
Certainty of one's own knowledge is self-evident; to deny it requires proof, not mere lack of experience.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya challenges the notion that only logical demonstration can produce certainty. He argues that a person's direct awareness of their own certainty is a form of necessary knowledge that cannot be dismissed without evidence. The anecdote of Najm al-Din al-Kubra illustrates how those who possess such certainty are confident in reporting it, and skeptics must bring a proof—not just their own lack of experience—to refute it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
The logicians and theologians being criticized hold that certainty (yaqin) is only attained through demonstrative proof (burhan). The author rejects this by asserting that self-awareness of certainty is itself certain knowledge.
The target claim is that only inferential reasoning yields certainty.
2
Extract the author's argument
The author argues: one's knowledge of being knowing is based on existence and inner feeling, which is necessary. Therefore, denying another's certainty without proof is invalid. Their own lack of experience does not prove others lack it.
The argument uses the principle that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
3
Analyze the anecdote
Najm al-Din al-Kubra, a respected scholar, asserts 'I know the knowledge of certainty' when questioned by two theologians. The author uses this to show that those who have direct certainty can be trusted, and their claim cannot be dismissed without proof.
The anecdote serves as a concrete example of the general principle.
4
Evaluate the critique
The author's critique is that the logicians' demand for demonstrative proof for everything leads to the absurd conclusion that even self-evident certainty must be proven. The critique is valid if one accepts that self-awareness provides direct knowledge.
This aligns with the broader Islamic epistemological tradition that includes necessary knowledge (daruri) alongside acquired knowledge (kasbi).

Commentary
This passage presents a critical argument against those who deny that scholars possess certainty. The author begins by stating that a person's awareness of his own knowledge is based on his existence and self-awareness, which is a necessary truth. Therefore, it is invalid for others (referring to the logicians and theologians) to claim that what is in people's hearts is not knowledge, unless they bring proof. Their own failure to experience such certainty does not mean others lack it, especially when those reporting certainty are reliable and knowledgeable. The author then gives an example: Najm al-Din al-Kubra, a respected Sufi scholar, was visited by two theologians—Fakhr al-Din al-Razi and a Mu'tazili—who asked about his claim to have 'knowledge of certainty'. He affirmed it, and they questioned how such certainty is possible. This anecdote illustrates that true certainty is a direct experience that cannot be dismissed by argument. The author implies that the logicians' insistence on demonstrative proof as the only source of certainty is misguided.

Ibn Taymiyya on Prophetic Esoteric Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya argues that if philosophers can convey knowledge to their elite, prophets can too, and hadith scholars are the true heirs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of the philosophers' claim that prophets cannot convey esoteric knowledge, and explain why hadith scholars are the true heirs to prophetic inner knowledge.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya turns the philosophers' own method against them to defend prophetic transmission of esoteric knowledge.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya directly confronts the philosophers' objection that prophets are unable to convey hidden knowledge to their followers. He exposes a self-contradiction: if the philosophers can transmit their specialized knowledge to their elite through speech and writing, then prophets can certainly do the same. He then goes further to identify the real carriers of prophetic esoteric knowledge—the Companions and, through them, the scholars of hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
The philosophers claim that prophets cannot convey esoteric (bāṭin) knowledge, especially to the common people. They argue that such knowledge can only be transmitted to an elite (khāṣṣah).
2
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's refutation via parity
Ibn Taymiyya points out that if philosophers themselves can acquire and transmit such knowledge to their own elite through speech and writing, then it is equally possible for prophets. If it were impossible, then the philosophers would have no way to know it themselves.
3
Examine the rebuttal regarding means of transmission
The philosophers themselves use speech and writing to convey knowledge; therefore, they cannot deny that prophets can do the same. The impossibility they claim is self-refuting.
4
Establish the true elite: the Companions and hadith scholars
Ibn Taymiyya lists the Companions who were most knowledgeable of the Prophet's inner affairs (e.g., the caliphs, the ten promised Paradise, and others). The hadith scholars, by studying these Companions, become the true heirs to this esoteric knowledge, just as each school's elite knows the inner teaching of their imam.
He gives the example of Imam Malik and his student Ibn al-Qasim, who knew Malik's 'secret issues' (masa'il al-sirr).
5
Compare with other groups
Ibn Taymiyya notes that the elite of philosophers, theologians, and even heretical groups (Qaramitah, Batiniyyah) all claim knowledge of their imam's inner teachings. By analogy, the hadith scholars have the most legitimate claim to prophetic esoteric knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians/philosophers who claim that prophets cannot convey esoteric or hidden knowledge to the common people. Ibn Taymiyya begins by pointing out a logical inconsistency: if such knowledge is possible at all, then it is possible for the philosophers themselves to acquire it, as they claim. If it is not possible, then they have no way to know it themselves. He then addresses the means of transmission: expression through speech and writing. If the philosophers can write and speak their knowledge, then prophets can do the same. The philosophers' objection that such knowledge can only be conveyed to the elite, not the masses, is turned against them: the prophets do have an elite (the Companions) who receive this knowledge, just as the philosophers have their own elite. Ibn Taymiyya then establishes that the Companions listed—the caliphs, the ten promised Paradise, and other prominent figures—were the most special and knowledgeable of the Prophet's inner affairs. The hadith scholars, by studying these Companions, inherit this knowledge, just as each sect's elite knows the inner teaching of their imam. He compares this to the elite of philosophers, theologians, and even heretical sects who claim esoteric knowledge. Finally, he illustrates with the example of Imam Malik: his closest student, Ibn al-Qasim, was most knowledgeable of Malik's inner states, and his narrations are relied upon, including the 'secret issues' (masa'il al-sirr) that some have criticized. This argument demonstrates that esoteric knowledge is transmitted through the closest associates, and the hadith scholars are the true heirs of the Prophet's inner knowledge.

Refuting Rational Denial of Naskh via Quranic Authority
Author refutes rational argument against naskh using Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of the rational argument against abrogation and identify how he uses Quranic evidence to ground the permissibility of naskh in divine will and benefit.

Core Idea
Abrogation is not a change in God's nature but an exercise of will and wisdom.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya addresses a rational argument that denies abrogation (naskh) by asserting that God does not change His commands. Rather than engaging in abstract philosophical debate, Ibn Taymiyya turns directly to the Quran—specifically the verse about the change of qibla—to show that abrogation is both attested and rational. By highlighting divine will and benefit, he demonstrates that naskh is not contradictory to God's perfection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's rational claim
The opponents say that God does not abrogate what He has forbidden nor forbid what He has commanded, implying naskh is impossible.
This claim is based on a misconception that abrogation implies imperfection.
2
Locate the Quranic response
Ibn Taymiyya cites the verse from Al-Baqarah (2:142) about the change of qibla, which was a clear instance of naskh.
The verse records the Jews' objection and God's reply.
3
Extract the two key points
The verse shows that abrogation depends on divine will (man yasha' – 'whom He wills') and that the new command may be more beneficial (ahsan wa anfa').
These two points answer the rational objection: God acts according to His will and wisdom.
4
Connect to theological debate
The verse is foundational for mutakallimun who debate whether obligation follows pure will or benefit, but both views allow naskh.
Thus the Quranic evidence makes the rational argument irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of a rational argument against abrogation (naskh). The author states that any rational argument brought by the opponents can be understood and refuted from the Quran itself. He specifically addresses the claim that God does not abrogate what He forbids nor forbid what He commands—an objection to naskh based on reason. The author responds by citing the Quranic verse where the change of qibla is mentioned, which involves abrogation. The verse records the foolish saying of the people (identified as Jews) questioning the change. Then the verse responds by affirming that east and west belong to God and He guides whom He wills. The author explains that this highlights two aspects: (1) abrogation is subject to the divine will, and (2) the new command may be more beneficial (صلح and نفع). Thus the verse grounds naskh in both divine sovereignty and wisdom. The author notes that this verse is foundational for the theologians (mutakallimun) who debate whether obligation follows pure will or benefit, but in either case, abrogation is permissible (جائز). The passage thus defends the legitimacy of naskh against rational objections by appealing to Quranic authority. The argument does not rely on a formal syllogism but on direct scriptural evidence and commentary.

Key Terms
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later divine command; the author defends its permissibility against rational denial. [Note: No significant shift; naskh is a standard usul al-fiqh term.]
الحجة العقلية (al-hujja al-'aqliyya): A rational argument used by opponents (likely logicians or Jews) to deny abrogation, claiming God does not change His commands. [Note: In Ibn Taymiyya's critique, 'rational argument' often refers to Greek-influenced reasoning that he deems insufficient against revelation.]
المشيئة الإلهية (al-mashi'a al-ilahiyya): Divine will; the author grounds abrogation in God's absolute will and wisdom. [Note: Stable term in Islamic theology; not specific to this work.]
المتكلمون (al-mutakallimun): Theologians who debate the basis of obligation (taklif) and use this verse to support their positions. [Note: In Mantiq texts, 'mutakallimun' often refers to dialectical theologians as opposed to logicians.]

Permissibility of Worldly Benefit from Non-Muslims
Author defends permissibility of worldly benefit from non-Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the evidence (Quran, Sunnah, and analogy) Ibn Taymiyya uses to support the permissibility of benefiting from non-Muslim knowledge in worldly matters.

Core Idea
Prophetic precedent permits worldly benefit from disbelievers; total prohibition is unfounded.

In this passage from his critique of the logicians, Ibn Taymiyya addresses a common objection: that all knowledge from non-Muslims is tainted. He argues forcefully that in purely worldly matters like medicine and arithmetic, benefiting from their works is not only allowed but supported by clear prophetic examples. We will examine his reasoning and the evidence he marshals from the Quran, the Prophet's biography, and the practice of the early community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states his conclusion about benefiting from non-Muslim knowledge in worldly matters. He says 'this is permissible' (فَهٰذَا جَائِز).
The claim is that using works of disbelievers in medicine, arithmetic, etc. is allowed.
2
List the prophetic examples
He provides several examples: the Prophet's sharecropping with Khaybar Jews, hiring a polytheist guide, accepting advice from Khuza'ah (which included disbelievers), and Abu Talib's support. List these and note how each involves trusting or benefiting from a non-Muslim.
These examples are from the Two Sahihs, giving them high authority.
3
Extract the Quranic evidence
He cites Quran 3:75 about trustworthy People of the Book. Note how this verse establishes that some disbelievers can be entrusted with wealth, implying trustworthiness in other matters.
This verse is used to support the principle of accepting testimony/reports from trustworthy non-Muslims in worldly affairs.
4
Analyze the conditional limit
He adds a condition: this is permissible 'if there is no overriding harm such as his authority' (إِذَا لَمْ يَكُنْ فِيْهِ مَفْسَدَةٌ رَاجِحَةٌ مِثْلُ وِلَايَتِهِ). Understand that the permissibility is conditional on absence of greater harm.
This shows Ibn Taymiyya's balanced approach, not blanket permission.

Analogical Reasoning in Ibn Taymiyya's Argument
Author uses analogies from prophetic practice to support permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the analogical arguments (qiyas) Ibn Taymiyya uses to justify worldly benefit from non-Muslims, identifying the original case (asl), ruling (hukm), effective cause ('illa), and new case (far').

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya uses qiyas from prophetic practices to extend the ruling of permissibility.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya does not simply assert a ruling; he provides multiple analogies (tamthilat) to support his position. By comparing the controversial practice of using non-Muslim knowledge to accepted practices like sharecropping or hiring guides, he makes a powerful case. We will break down these analogies using the standard framework of qiyas (analogical reasoning) in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the new case (far')
The far' is the practice under dispute: benefiting from the works of non-Muslims in worldly matters like medicine and arithmetic. This is what Ibn Taymiyya wants to prove permissible.

2
Find the original cases (asl)
He lists several asl: dwelling in their houses, wearing their clothes, sharecropping (Khaybar), hiring a polytheist guide, accepting advice from Khuza'ah. Each is an established permissible practice involving interaction with non-Muslims.
These asl are all from the Prophet's sunnah, making them strong precedents.
3
Extract the effective cause ('illa)
The 'illa is the common feature that justifies extending the ruling from asl to far'. In all cases, the 'illa seems to be: 'a transaction or benefit in worldly matters from a non-Muslim that does not involve religious compromise and is based on trustworthiness.'
Ibn Taymiyya does not explicitly state the 'illa, but it is implied by the condition of trustworthiness and absence of harm.
4
Reconstruct the analogical argument
Formulate the qiyas: Asl (e.g., sharecropping with Khaybar Jews) has hukm of permissibility. Far' (benefiting from medical knowledge) shares the same 'illa (worldly benefit with trustworthiness). Therefore, far' is also permissible.
Each example functions as a separate analogical argument supporting the same conclusion.
5
Assess the strength of the analogy
Evaluate whether the 'illa is consistent across all cases. For instance, sharecropping involves physical labor, while learning medicine involves knowledge transfer. However, Ibn Taymiyya likely sees the common element as 'benefiting from their expertise in worldly affairs without religious risk.'
The strength depends on the acceptance of the 'illa; later scholars accepted this reasoning.

The intended point is to alert that whoever claims, by his state or his words, that a group other than the people of Hadith have attained of the hidden unseen realities concerning the matter of creation, resurrection, the beginning, the return, the matter of faith in Allah and the Last Day, and knowing the Necessary Existent, the rational soul, and the sciences and ethics by which souls are purified and reformed...

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument establishing the epistemological superiority of the prophetic tradition over philosophical speculation. The author begins by stating that following the Messenger of Allah is the source of special divine favors. He then cites Quran 57:28-29, which promises a double portion of mercy and light for those who believe in both Allah and His Messenger. The verse is interpreted to show that this promise is specifically for those who follow Muhammad, and that the People of the Book (Jews and Christians) cannot claim this bounty. Next, the author brings a hadith from the two Sahihs (Bukhari and Muslim) that illustrates the concept of divine favor: the Muslims are hired last for the shortest period relative to the other communities but receive double the wages. When the Jews and Christians complain, the employer (representing Allah) explains that it is His bounty to give as He wills. The author then explicitly states that the Quran and Hadith together prove that Allah gives to the followers of this Messenger what He did not give to previous peoples. He extrapolates that if this is true for all Muslims, then those who are 'lower' than the People of the Book—namely the Sabians and especially the philosophers (mutafalsifa)—are even further from this favor. The author then narrows the focus to 'Ahl al-Hadith wal-Sunna,' calling them the most specific followers of the Prophet. They are singled out with knowledge, forbearance, and doubled reward. A quote from a Salaf (early Muslim) is given: 'The people of Sunnah in Islam are like the people of Islam among the religions,' meaning that just as Islam is the true religion among false ones, so are the people of Sunnah the true Muslims among sects. The author then redirects to the main critique: some ignorant people claim that the Companions were deficient in knowledge or military strength. He refuses to expand on that, but instead clarifies the real point: anyone who claims that a group other than Ahl al-Hadith has attained true hidden knowledge about creation, resurrection, the afterlife, faith, the knowledge of God (Wajib al-Wujud), the rational soul (al-nafs al-natiqa), and the sciences and ethics that purify souls—such a claim is false. This is a direct attack on the philosophers (mutafalsifa) who claimed to have knowledge of these matters through reason alone, apart from revelation. The author argues that only the followers of prophetic tradition possess true knowledge of metaphysical and ethical truths.

Ibn Taymiyya's Conditional Rational Syllogism
Ibn Taymiyya presents a rational syllogism proving the superiority of Ahl al-Hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional structure of Ibn Taymiyya's proof for the superiority of Ahl al-Hadith and verify its logical validity.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism links the perfection of the Messenger to the virtue of his followers.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya uses a conditional rational syllogism (qiyas 'aqli) to prove that the most knowledgeable and following of the Messenger are the most virtuous. This is not merely a rhetorical claim but a logical inference based on the premise that the Messenger is the most perfect. We will analyze the logical form step by step, extracting the antecedent, consequent, and the implied affirmation of the antecedent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional statement
Find the 'if...then' structure in the Arabic. The word 'متى' (whenever) introduces the antecedent. Locate the segment: 'متى كان الرَّسول أكمل الخلق وأعلمهم بالحقائق وأقومهم قولا وحالا' — this is the condition (antecedent).
2
Extract the antecedent and consequent
The antecedent (muqaddam) is: 'the Messenger is the most perfect of creation, the most knowledgeable of realities, and the most upright in speech and state.' The consequent (tali) is: 'the most knowledgeable of people of him are the most knowledgeable of creation, and the greatest in agreement and following are the most virtuous.'
The consequent is split into two parts, both following from the same antecedent.
3
Recognize the implied affirmation of the antecedent
The syllogism does not explicitly state that the Messenger is indeed the most perfect, but this is taken as given from Islamic belief. Thus the inference is modus ponens: If P then Q; P; therefore Q. The conclusion is that the most knowledgeable follower (Ahl al-Hadith) is the most virtuous.
This is a valid conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i muttasil).
4
Assess validity and compare with fitrah
Ibn Taymiyya claims the syllogism is sound and also evident to sound disposition (fitrah). Modus ponens is logically valid. The conclusion necessarily follows if the premises are true.
The argument is not merely a logical exercise but rooted in fitrah.

The Just Comparison and Refutation of Objection
Ibn Taymiyya refutes an objection about deficiencies among Ahl al-Hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's refutation of the objection against Ahl al-Hadith and apply the principle of just comparison (muqabalah 'adilah) in evaluating groups.

Core Idea
Comparing whole to whole, not part to part, is the just standard in group evaluation.

After presenting the rational syllogism, Ibn Taymiyya anticipates an objection: the innate disposition (fitrah) that recognizes the virtue of Ahl al-Hadith might be altered by the negligence and aggression found among some of its members. He refutes this by arguing that such flaws are more abundant in other groups and that the proper method is just comparison: comparing the whole of Ahl al-Hadith with the whole of others, not focusing on individual shortcomings.

Identify the objection

Identify the refutation

Understand the principle of just comparison
The 'just comparison' (المقابلة العادلة) means evaluating groups as totalities, not picking flaws from one group and virtues from another. This prevents unfair bias.
4
Assess the strength of the refutation
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the fitrah is not changed by the existence of some deficient individuals, but rather by lack of knowledge of Hadith itself. The objection is rebutted by showing that the flaws are more common in other groups and that a fair comparison upholds the superiority of Ahl al-Hadith as a whole.
The refutation does not deny that there are flaws, but contextualizes them.

The Prophet's Perfection Precludes Superior Knowledge by Others
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Prophet's perfect attributes preclude anyone from having superior knowledge or guidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument from the Prophet's perfect attributes to refute the claim that any group possesses superior knowledge of realities.

Core Idea
The Prophet's perfect knowledge, will, and power make it impossible for anyone else to surpass him in imparting truth.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes on the implicit challenge from those who claim that philosophers or mystics have access to deeper truths than what the Prophet taught. He constructs a powerful argument using the very attributes of the Prophet that are established by revelation and reason. By carefully tracing this argument, you will learn how to use the principle of perfection to refute claims of superiority over prophetic knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Find the implicit claim that someone other than the Prophet can impart greater knowledge of realities to their elite. Ibn Taymiyya states it is impossible that any sect has knowledge the hadith scholars lack.
The claim is not explicitly named, but it is the philosophical/mystical assertion of superior knowledge.
2
Extract the three perfect attributes
Identify the attributes Ibn Taymiyya uses: knowledge (العلم), will to do good (إرادة الإحسان), and power (القدرة). These are mentioned after the Istikhara dua.
These are the criteria for perfection in teaching: knowing, wanting, and being able.
3
See how these attributes are established
The Prophet's own dua shows his humility and dependence on Allah, yet he is the most knowledgeable and capable of creation. The hadith about seeking food and guidance reinforces that all good comes from Allah, but the Prophet is the best channel.
The argument does not rely on raw revelation alone; it uses logical comparison.
4
Apply the principle of superiority
If the Prophet is the most knowledgeable, most loving of teaching, and most capable of expression, then anyone inferior cannot surpass him in imparting knowledge to their elite. Therefore, the Prophet's elite (Companions) received the highest possible knowledge directly from him.
This is a comparative argument: the superior in a quality cannot be surpassed by an inferior in the effect of that quality.
5
Draw the conclusion
Conclude that no group (philosophers, mystics, etc.) can claim knowledge of realities that the hadith scholars (who follow the Prophet) do not have. The hadith scholars are the true inheritors.
This refutes claims of esoteric or philosophical superiority over prophetic tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' claim that certain rational judgments—such as the goodness or badness of actions—are not derived from pure reason but from the faculty of estimation (wahm). He then argues that those propositions which the logicians call 'widely known but not known' are actually intuitive rational principles (badihiyya 'aqliyya), and the mind's certainty about them surpasses its certainty about mathematical and natural sciences. He cites the consensus of mutakallimun from Islam and beyond that these are intuitive, though they may not articulate it well. He explains goodness and badness in terms of actions being productive of purposes sought for themselves. He distinguishes two meanings of 'haqq' and 'batil': one in statements (affirmation/negation), and another in actions (useful/beneficial vs. pointless). Next, he refutes the idea that intuitive premises can be overturned by syllogism. The argument is that syllogisms themselves depend on intuitive premises; if those could be false, then we would have a conflict, and rejecting the intuitive for the syllogistic is not more justified than the reverse. Moreover, conclusions from syllogisms (with more steps) are more prone to error than direct intuition. He gives the example of denying Allah's attribute of being above the Throne as a false conclusion from such flawed reasoning. Finally, he remarks that earlier philosophers did not rely on prophetic premises, but later ones (like Ibn Sina) mixed genuine religion with Sabianism, while some mutakallimun naively accepted the logicians' framework to support prophethood.

He begins by stating the position he is attacking: those who affirm ethical judgments (al-tahsin wa al-taqbih) in matters of action claim that when we consult pure reason alone, we find no such judgment; they give examples like the relation between existents (distinct or immanent) or directionality, and they assert these come from wahm not fitra. Ibn Taymiyya dismisses this as manifest confusion. He then argues that those propositions which the logicians call 'widely known but not known' are actually intuitive rational principles (badihiyya 'aqliyya), and the mind's certainty about them surpasses its certainty about mathematical and natural sciences. He cites the consensus of mutakallimun from Islam and beyond that these are intuitive, though they may not articulate it well. He explains goodness and badness in terms of actions being productive of purposes sought for themselves. He distinguishes two meanings of 'haqq' and 'batil': one in statements (affirmation/negation), and another in actions (useful/beneficial vs. pointless). Next, he refutes the idea that intuitive premises can be overturned by syllogism. The argument is that syllogisms themselves depend on intuitive premises; if those could be false, then we would have a conflict, and rejecting the intuitive for the syllogistic is not more justified than the reverse. Moreover, conclusions from syllogisms (with more steps) are more prone to error than direct intuition. He gives the example of denying Allah's attribute of being above the Throne as a false conclusion from such flawed reasoning. Finally, he remarks that earlier philosophers did not rely on prophetic premises, but later ones (like Ibn Sina) mixed genuine religion with Sabianism, while some mutakallimun naively accepted the logicians' framework to support prophethood.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of Aristotelian logic's claim to be a universal measure of knowledge. Ibn Taymiyya begins by giving an example from prophetic experience: the Prophet (peace be upon him) saw and heard things invisible to others, which is a form of external or internal perception. This demonstrates that there is knowledge that lies outside ordinary experience. He then moves to universal axiomatic sciences, noting that they have no definition, and challenges logicians: how can they deny that some souls have axiomatic knowledge that is not considered axiomatic by them? He asserts that such knowledge is possible and affirmed by most people, including non-prophets. Denying such truths amounts to disbelief, and they have no syllogistic proof for their denial. In each case, logic fails. He uses a powerful analogy: just as money scales are not used to weigh wood and lead compared to gold and silver, the matters of prophethood are far greater than gold in the realm of knowledge. Since logic has no scale for them, it is like a defective, unjust, ignorant scale—either rejecting truth or failing to weigh it. He concludes by asking how logicians can claim that this faulty scale is the standard of true sciences and certain wisdom, when it cannot handle the most important truths. The only honest admission is to acknowledge its incapacity.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained critique by Ibn Taymiyya against the claim that Greek logic (mantiq) is useful or necessary for acquiring knowledge, especially in the Islamic sciences. He begins by arguing that the sciences in question—likely the categories and divisions of logic—do not require logical syllogism; most rely on mere induction, which is fallible. Hence logic is useless. Moreover, it occupies the heart and leads people away from beneficial knowledge. The great Islamic scholars of grammar, language, jurisprudence, etc., never paid attention to logic; they preceded its translation. The prophetic sciences, including fiqh and usul al-fiqh, are too exalted for such a tool; the best three generations (the Salaf) never used it, yet they achieved the highest degree of knowledge, depth, sincerity, and clarity. In fact, those who delve into logic are the most doubtful and confused, and any genuine knowledge they have comes from sound premises and intellect, not logic. Introducing logic into sound sciences only makes things verbose and complicated. Finally, Ibn Taymiyya concedes that logic might benefit someone who is already in disbelief and error—a backhanded admission that in the right (i.e., wrong) hands it could be useful, but for the Muslim community it is detrimental.

Refutation of the Logical Syllogism's Claim to Universal Knowledge
Author refutes the logicians' claim that their syllogism is the measure of certain universal sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the Aristotelian syllogism and explain why he argues that it does not produce universal certain knowledge.

Core Idea
The logical syllogism only yields particular judgments already known by sense or analogy, not new universal knowledge.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic attacks the foundational claim of the logicians that their syllogism (al-qiyas al-'aqli al-mantiqi) is the measure of all certain universal sciences. Ibn Taymiyya argues that the only universal premises are self-evident propositions (badihiyyat), and that the syllogism's conclusions are merely particular and already known by other means. Let's examine his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under attack
What do the logicians assert about their syllogism? They claim it is the measure (mizan) of universal certain sciences (al-'ulum al-kulliyya al-burhaniyya). This is the target of the critique.
The claim is that their syllogism is the only way to achieve certain knowledge about universal matters.
2
Find the author's central objection
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the syllogism's premises are either self-evident (universal) or particular. Since only self-evident premises are universal, and the syllogism deals with particular things, it cannot yield universal conclusions. Most premises are particular, so the conclusion is particular.
Self-evident premises are few; most premises in real arguments are sensory or particular.
3
Examine the objection and response
An objector suggests that sense and reason together complete demonstration. The author responds that this only gives a specific particular proposition, which does not need a syllogism. The example: knowing that one person's wealth is more than another's by combining sense (amounts) and reason (two > one).
The point is that such knowledge is already available directly, not via a formal syllogism.
4
Consider the alternative means of knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya proposes that actual existing things are known by sense (internal/external), analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), or a syllogism with particular terms. These methods are sounder and clearer than the logicians' universal syllogism.
For example, seeing two people in different locations makes one independent of the syllogism that one body cannot be in two places.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' claim that their rational syllogism (al-qiyas al-'aqli al-mantiqi) is the measure (mizan) of universal certain sciences (al-'ulum al-kulliyya al-burhaniyya). The author proceeds in a dialectical manner: first stating the problem, anticipating an objection, answering it, and then elaborating with a second aspect. 

The author begins by stating that if the principles of demonstration (usul burhanihim) do not contain knowledge of a universal proposition about existing things, then there is no knowledge in that. He points out that the only indisputable elements are the self-evident propositions (al-qadaya al-badihiyya), which have universality. However, the rest of the principles lack universal knowledge. Therefore, only purely intellectual self-evident propositions should be used as premises of demonstration, since they are universal; other propositions are particular. Hence, the question arises: how can particular propositions serve as premises of demonstration? 

An objection is then raised: it might be said that particular things are known by sensory propositions and universal things by reason, and the combination of both completes the demonstration. For example, sense knows that this person has a thousand dirhams and that person has two thousand, and reason knows that two is more than one, so the conclusion that this person's wealth is more is reached. The author responds by admitting this is correct but argues that this only yields a specific particular proposition (qadiyya juz'iyya mu'ayyana). Such specific particular things do not require a syllogism; they can be known without one, or by analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), or by a syllogism from two particulars. He gives examples: knowing that one thing is like another by sense, then inferring the ruling applies analogically; or knowing relative ages like Zayd > 'Amr and 'Amr > Khalid. These are known without the comprehensive syllogism (qiyas al-shumul) that the logicians defined. Thus, the logical syllogism alone does not yield any universal science established in external reality. Therefore, the claim that it is the measure of certain universal sciences is invalid. 

The author then introduces a second aspect: actual existing things are known by internal and external sense, by analogical reasoning, and by a syllogism whose terms are particular individuals and whose premises and conclusion are particular propositions. This knowledge is sounder and clearer. He illustrates with examples: seeing Zayd and 'Amr in different locations makes one independent of the syllogism that one body cannot be in two places; weighing coins directly makes one independent of the syllogism about equality to a standard weight. Hence, such syllogisms do not produce new knowledge but merely add unnecessary talk. However, they may be useful in certain contexts or when someone denies sense perception. 

The commentary should note that the author is critiquing the foundationalist claim that logical demonstration is the only path to certain knowledge. He argues that in practice, particular knowledge is often known directly through sense or analogy, and the universal syllogism is unnecessary. This is a standard critique of Aristotelian logic by Islamic rationalists like Ibn Taymiyya.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians' claim that knowledge and certainty can only be obtained through their specific logical syllogism. Ibn Taymiyya begins by sarcastically stating that the logicians claim no one is given knowledge except through a logical syllogism, going so far as to attribute this to the prophets and even to Allah's knowledge of creation. He then points out that they have no evidence for this universal negative claim—that certainty cannot be obtained without their method—except their own ignorance. To clarify, he presents the 'fifth aspect': the principles they list (sensory data, self-evidents, empirics, intuitives) do indeed produce certainty, but they have no proof that certainty cannot come from other sources. He then notes that this claim is not even the position of their own leaders. Those with intellect and faith must oppose them. He goes on to describe the negative effects: those who delve into logic often become hypocrites and heretics, and logic is held in suspicion among reasonable people. He illustrates with an anecdote of a Persian student who, after studying logic, asked his teacher where in logic the chapter on abandoning prayer is, revealing the absurdity of logic's irrelevance to religious practice. Ibn Taymiyya argues that unless a person already has a strong foundation of religion and intellect, logic corrupts them. He notes that many early Muslim scholars considered logic like arithmetic—neutral and not indicative of the truth of Islam. Finally, he distinguishes between those who only see the apparent order of logic (definition, propositions, syllogisms) and the reality that it contains many corrupt and false elements. The passage concludes with praise to Allah.

Using Quranic Evidence in Critiquing Logic
Lists Quranic verses as evidence for critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Ibn Taymiyya uses Quranic citations to ground his critique of Greek logic in Islamic revelation.

Core Idea
Quranic evidence replaces syllogistic proof in Ibn Taymiyya's epistemology.

In his critique of Aristotelian logic, Ibn Taymiyya does not merely argue theoretically; he grounds his critique in the Quran itself. This lesson examines a collection of verses he cites to show that true knowledge comes from revelation, not from human speculative reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of istishhad
Istishhad is citing a Quranic verse as evidence. In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya is not using the verses as premises in a logical syllogism but as authoritative proof-texts that establish the primacy of revelation over reason.
The term istishhad is stable across Islamic sciences.
2
Categorize the themes of the verses
The verses cover a wide range: the test of faith (Al-Ankabut 1-3), proper debate (Al-Ankabut 46), the limits of worldly knowledge (Ar-Rum 7), divine creation and unity (multiple surahs), the Day of Judgment (Saba, Az-Zumar), and the role of prophets (Al-Ahzab). Group them to see the comprehensive nature of the evidence.
3
Explain how these verses collectively support the critique
By amassing verses that affirm revelation as the source of guidance and knowledge, Ibn Taymiyya constructs an epistemological argument: if the Quran already provides true knowledge, then Greek logic is not a necessary or superior method. The verses also criticize those who rely on speculation and false arguments.
This is not a formal logical proof but a demonstration of revelation's sufficiency.
4
Contrast Quranic evidence with syllogistic premises
In Aristotelian logic, premises are either self-evident or derived from prior definitions. Here, the premises are divinely revealed and carry certainty (yaqin). Ibn Taymiyya uses this to argue that Islamic epistemology is founded on revelation, not human speculation.
This contrast is central to his critique of the claim that logic is universal.

Commentary
This passage presents a collection of Quranic verses from various surahs. In the context of Naqd Mantiq, Ibn Taymiyya cites these verses as evidence to support his critique of Aristotelian logic. The verses cover numerous themes: the test of faith (Al-Ankabut 1-3), proper argumentation with People of the Book (Al-Ankabut 46), the nature of this world and the Hereafter (Ar-Rum 6-11, 7), the authority of revelation versus conjecture (Ar-Rum 35), the reality of death and divine guidance (As-Sajdah 11, 24), remembrance of Allah's favors and the mission of the Prophet (Al-Ahzab 9, 45-46, 70-71), the Day of Judgment and the questioning of false deities (Saba 22-23, 40-41), the role of angels and the efficacy of good deeds (Fatir 1, 10, 24), the covenant with Adam against Satan (Ya-Sin 6), the oneness of Allah and the false attribution of offspring (As-Saffat 1-3, 149-166, 151-157, 158), the warning against following desire and the description of the Qur'an and past prophets (Sad 26, 29, 45, 75, 86), and the universality of parables in the Qur'an, divine unity, and the final gathering (Az-Zumar 27, 45, 67, 71).
