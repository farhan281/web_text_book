The Logical Proof of the Superiority of the Prophet's Followers
Author demonstrates via correct logical syllogism that the Prophet's followers are the most knowledgeable and best.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and validate the categorical syllogism used by Ibn Taymiyya to prove the superiority of the Prophet's followers, and to articulate the objection and response using the principle of fair comparison.

Core Idea
The Prophet's perfection implies his followers' superiority—a syllogism grounded in fitra and defended against objections.

This passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd al-Mantiq applies a rigorous logical syllogism to establish a theological truth: that the followers of the Prophet (peace be upon him) are the best of creation. While the conclusion is 'apparent by innate disposition,' Ibn Taymiyya reinforces it with a formal reasoning structure. He then anticipates and refutes a potential objection, showing how fair comparison must be applied. In this lesson, you will break down the logical structure and evaluate its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Find the final proposition Ibn Taymiyya is trying to prove. Look for the 'لزم' clause: the most knowledgeable of the Prophet and the greatest in following him are the best of creation.
2
Extract the premises
The first premise is about the Prophet's perfection: he is the most perfect, most knowledgeable, and most upright. The second premise (implicit) is that closeness to perfection yields perfection.
3
Reconstruct as a categorical syllogism
Express the argument as: All who are most perfect of creation are the best. The Prophet is most perfect. Therefore, his most devoted followers are best. Identify the middle term as 'the Prophet's perfection'.
4
Analyze the objection and response
The objection claims that faults among Sunni followers alter the fitra, disproving the claim. The response: faults are more among opponents, and fair comparison requires weighting good and bad proportionally.
This is the principle of muqabilat al-jumlati bi al-jumlah (comparing part with part).
5
Evaluate the argument
Check if the syllogism is logically valid: if the Prophet is perfect, then those who most emulate him are indeed excellent. The rebuttal shows the objection misapplies fitra by ignoring overall comparison.

And that has been clarified by the correct intellectual syllogism about which there is no doubt, even if it is apparent by innate disposition to everyone of sound disposition. For whenever the Messenger is the most perfect of creation and the most knowledgeable of them of realities and the most upright in speech and state, it necessarily follows that the most knowledgeable of people about him is the most knowledgeable of creation about that, and that the greatest of them in agreement with him and following him is the best of creation. Commentary
This passage presents a logical proof (qiyas 'aqli) that the followers of the Prophet (peace be upon him) are the best of creation. The author begins by stating that this conclusion is established by a correct intellectual syllogism, though it is also evident by innate disposition (fitra). The syllogism is: Whenever the Messenger is the most perfect of creation, the most knowledgeable of realities, and the most upright in speech and action, then those who know him best are the most knowledgeable of creation, and those who most agree with and follow him are the best of creation. This is a conditional categorical argument. The author then addresses an objection: people might claim that the fitra is altered by the shortcomings seen among some who claim to follow the Sunnah and Hadith. The response is that such shortcomings are more prevalent among opponents, and fair comparison requires contrasting the good of one group with the good of the other, and the bad with the bad. The real cause of distorted fitra is lack of knowledge of Hadith and Sunnah, combined with the fact that opponents often have some genuine knowledge and good deeds, which creates a false impression. The author concludes by noting that he will not name individuals, but refers to Ibn Qutaybah's work for abundant examples.

Key Terms
القياس العقلي (al-qiyas al-'aqli): Intellectual syllogism, a logical argument based on reason, yielding certain conclusion. [Note: In Ibn Taymiyya's usage, qiyas 'aqli often refers to sound reasoning in contrast to flawed Greek syllogistic; he does not limit it to Aristotelian figures.]
الفطرة (al-fitrah): Innate human disposition that recognizes truth; here it naturally grasps the superiority of the Prophet's followers. [Note: The term is stable in Islamic theology, but Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes its role in epistemology, sometimes contrasting it with acquired reasoning.]
مقابلة الجملة بالجملة (muqabilat al-jumlati bi al-jumlah): Comparing part with part, i.e., fair evaluation by contrasting like with like in praise and blame. [Note: This is a methodological principle in Ibn Taymiyya's works, not a standard term in logic.]

Ibn Taymiyya's Refutation of Logic's Exclusive Claim
Ibn Taymiyya refutes logicians' claim that syllogism is the only path to knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the logicians' claim that their syllogism is the only path to certain knowledge.

Core Idea
The logicians' tool is not necessary; natural reasoning and divine illumination suffice for many.

Ibn Taymiyya acknowledges that logicians admit some souls receive knowledge without formal logic, but they still insist the art is necessary for others. He dismantles this by distinguishing between the genus of reasoning and the legal craft, showing that even if the tool could help, claiming it as the only path is a lie.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Find where Ibn Taymiyya states what logicians say: they claim the logical art prevents errors but admit that some (prophets, saints) can dispense with it. The key is whether anyone can dispense entirely with the syllogism itself.
Look for 'النفوس القدسية' and the analogy with Arabic grammar.
2
Distinguish the two meanings of qiyas
Notice the difference between the genus of reasoning (natural syllogism) and the formal art ('الصناعة القانونية'). Ibn Taymiyya says dispensing from one does not mean dispensing from the other.
This is a crucial distinction for his argument.
3
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's counter-argument
He states that even if the formal art benefits some, it is false to say it is the only path to knowledge. He calls this 'كذب محقق' (a confirmed lie).
His conclusion is epistemological, not merely methodological.
4
Recognize the epistemological stance
Knowledge can come without the formal logical method, via natural reasoning or divine inspiration ('النفوس القدسية'). This undercuts the logicians' exclusivity.
This aligns with his broader critique in Naqd al-Mantiq.

Commentary
This passage is a critical rebuttal by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' exaggerated claims about the necessity of their syllogistic method. He opens by acknowledging that logicians admit that some souls (like prophets and saints) receive knowledge without syllogistic reasoning, but they argue that such individuals are naturally wise and form syllogisms instinctively, just as a native Arabic speaker speaks correctly without studying grammar. However, Ibn Taymiyya points out two distinct issues: first, dispensation from the genus of syllogism itself (i.e., reasoning from premises), and second, dispensation from the formal logical art (the 'legal tool' that supposedly prevents error). The logicians claim that their art is a safeguard for the mind, but Ibn Taymiyya declares that this claim is corrupt and has been refuted elsewhere. He then asserts that even if the logical method could benefit some people, it is absolutely false to claim that it is the only path to certain knowledge—this is a lie. He grounds his critique in the tradition of Muslim theologians, who have consistently refuted the logicians' pretensions. He cites al-Baqillani's classification of syllogisms into five types: demonstrative (burhani), rhetorical (khatabi), dialectical (jadali), poetic (shi'ri), and sophistic (mughlati). He dismisses the poetic and sophistic as irrelevant to the pursuit of truth—poetic yields only imagination, sophistic is falsehood disguised as truth. His focus is on the remaining three: demonstrative (certain), rhetorical (persuasive), and dialectical (probable). Thus, Ibn Taymiyya not only attacks the exclusive claim of the logicians but also subsumes their system within a broader classification that includes non-apodictic modes of reasoning.

The Five Types of Syllogisms and Ibn Taymiyya's Dismissal of Two
Ibn Taymiyya refutes logicians' claim that syllogism is the only path to knowledge, then classifies syllogisms into five types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five types of syllogisms according to the logicians and explain why Ibn Taymiyya dismisses the poetic and sophistic as irrelevant to truth.

Core Idea
Only three of the five syllogisms deal with truth: demonstrative, rhetorical, dialectical.

After critiquing the logicians' exclusivity claim, Ibn Taymiyya presents their classification of syllogisms into five types: demonstrative, rhetorical, dialectical, poetic, and sophistic. He immediately sets aside the poetic (imagination) and sophistic (falsehood disguised as truth), focusing on the three that deal with truth in various degrees.

Guided Path
1
List the five types
Know the names in Arabic: البرهاني (demonstrative), الخطابي (rhetorical), الجدلي (dialectical), الشعري (poetic), المغلطي (sophistic). Understand each type's purpose.
This classification is standard in Aristotelian logic as transmitted in the Islamic tradition.
2
Understand the purpose of each
Demonstrative yields certainty (yaqin); rhetorical persuades (iqna'); dialectical uses accepted premises (musallamat); poetic stirs imagination (takhayyul); sophistic resembles truth but is false.
Ibn Taymiyya focuses on the truth-related types.
3
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's attitude
He says 'لا غرض لنا فيهما هنا' (there is no purpose for us in them here) regarding poetic and sophistic. His concern is with syllogisms that produce knowledge or belief in truth.
This shows his pragmatic and truth-oriented approach.
4
Connect to his broader critique
The logicians' claim that only the demonstrative (their formal art) yields knowledge is undermined by the existence of other valid syllogisms used in everyday reasoning and religious discourse.
Ibn Taymiyya's acceptance of rhetorical and dialectical syllogisms broadens the epistemic landscape.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the logicians' classification of syllogisms (qiyas) into five types: demonstrative (burhani), rhetorical (khatabi), dialectical (jadali), poetic (shi'ri), and sophistical (mughlati). He criticizes them for not affirming or denying prophethood, and states that disbelief in messengers is unbelief regardless of whether one denies them explicitly. He then addresses later philosophers who attempted to define 'well-known' premises as those accepted due to the speaker's authority, but he dismisses these as additions compelled by argument. Finally, he comments on the mixing of Hanifiyya (true monotheism) with Sabianism (heresy) among later philosophers and ancient Sabians, clarifying that some ancient Sabians were believers. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya uses this critique to undermine the logicians' epistemological framework and its detachment from revelation.

The Prophet's Statement: Explanation, Not Syllogism
Author argues that the Prophet's statement is clarification, not deduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between syllogistic deduction (istidlal) and explanatory clarification (bayan) in Islamic legal discourse, using Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the logicians' treatment of the Prophet's hadith.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya argues that the Prophet's 'every intoxicant is khamr, every khamr is haram' is bayan, not qiyas.

Ibn Taymiyya engages the logicians' claim that the Prophet used syllogistic reasoning. He concedes that valid syllogisms yield knowledge, but examines a key hadith to show that the Prophet's intention was not deduction but clarification of the scope of prohibition. This lesson breaks down his argument and its implications for the use of logic in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' interpretation
The logicians see the hadith as a categorical syllogism: major premise (every khamr is haram), minor premise (every intoxicant is khamr), conclusion (every intoxicant is haram). They present it as a paradigm of deductive reasoning.
This is a valid Barbara mood of the first figure.
2
Note Ibn Taymiyya's concession
He agrees in principle that if the two premises are known and properly formed, the conclusion follows necessarily. But he questions whether this hadith was actually used for deduction.
His agreement is limited to the abstract logical form, not its application.
3
Examine the hadith context
The Prophet spoke to people who already knew that khamr is haram. They asked about new drinks like al-mizr (corn drink). The Prophet explained that all intoxicants are included in the meaning of khamr. This is an explanation (bayan) of the term, not a deduction from premises.
The companions did not dispute the major premise; they needed clarification of the minor.
4
Distinguish istidlal from bayan
Istidlal (deduction) uses known premises to reach a new conclusion that was not previously known. Bayan (explanation) clarifies the meaning of a term or the scope of a ruling that is already established. The Prophet's statement is bayan.
This distinction is crucial for understanding revelation's method.
5
Assess the implication for logic
Ibn Taymiyya's critique undermines the logicians' claim that religious knowledge relies on formal syllogisms. Instead, prophetic teaching operates through explanation and naming, not chain of deduction.
This is part of his broader attack on the universal applicability of Greek logic.

Types of Qiyas: Conjunctive Categorical and Conditional Disjunctive
Divides qiyas into two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive categorical and conditional disjunctive syllogisms and identify their structure.

Core Idea
Syllogisms are either categorical with two premises or conditional with a conditional premise.

This passage divides qiyas into two types: the conjunctive categorical (hamli iqtirani) and the conditional disjunctive (istithna'i talazumi). In this lesson, we will break down each type and see how they are used in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Define conjunctive categorical
A syllogism with two categorical premises and a categorical conclusion. Example: 'Every intoxicant is haram. Wine is an intoxicant. Therefore wine is haram.' Three terms: middle, subject, predicate.
2
Define conditional disjunctive
A syllogism with a conditional premise (if...then...) and a categorical premise that affirms or denies one part. Example: 'If this is wine, then it is haram. This is wine. Therefore it is haram.' This is modus ponens.
The categorical premise is the 'istithna' (exception).
3
Compare the two types
Both yield necessary conclusions, but their structures differ: one uses categorical statements, the other uses a conditional.
4
Apply to a Kalam argument
The proof for God's existence: 'The world is originated. Everything originated has a cause. Therefore the world has a cause.' This is categorical. Alternatively: 'If the world is originated, it has a cause. The world is originated. Therefore it has a cause.' This is conditional.

This is the key move: the logicians' critique of tamthil is undermined because their own foundations rely on it.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya on the logicians' claim that certain premises provide universal knowledge for demonstrative syllogisms. The author begins by dismissing most of the logicians' discourse as verbose and misguided, stating that the beneficial truth is innate and does not require their technical apparatus. He then narrows the focus to an aspect of their syllogistic theory: the syllogism only produces knowledge via a universal affirmative premise. This is agreed upon, hence no syllogism from two negatives or two particulars. Universal sciences must therefore be the foundations of syllogisms.

Ibn Taymiyya then reports the logicians' list of principles for demonstrative syllogism: certain sciences including inner and outer sensory perceptions, rational principles, self-evident truths (badihiyyat), mutawatir (recurrent reports), empirical (mujarrabat), and some add intuitive (hadsiyyat). He systematically refutes each as yielding true universal propositions.

First, sensory perceptions (inner and outer) do not yield universal propositions; they grasp only particular, determinate things. Second, empirical knowledge (experiences) also concerns particular sensibles; the intellect's generalization to similars is via analogy (tamthil), which is the analogical syllogism they seek to avoid. Third, intuitive knowledge (hadsiyyat) is akin to empirical but without active experimentation; it too yields only particulars, becoming universal only through analogy.

Finally, self-evident truths (badihiyyat) like mathematical axioms (one is half of two) deal with absolutes, not external particular existents. When we judge on an external existent, it must be via sense or intellect, which themselves rely on inner or outer sensation. Thus, none of these sources provide the universal premises needed for demonstrative syllogism without recourse to analogical reasoning, which the logicians typically reject as non-demonstrative. The commentary highlights Ibn Taymiyya's method: he accepts the necessity of universal premises but denies that the logicians' sources can supply them without the very tamthil they disparage.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the alleged capacity of demonstrative syllogism (qiyas burhani) to produce certain universal knowledge about external realities. He begins by asserting that the purely rational, self-evident sciences (al-'ulum al-awwaliyya al-badihiyya al-'aqliyya al-mahda) are concerned only with mental estimates (al-muqaddarat al-dhihniyya) like number and magnitude, not with external existing things. Then he notes that the materials of demonstrative syllogism as classified by logicians are generally perceived only as particular matters, not universal: they include inner and outer sense, recurrent reports (tawatur), experience (tajriba), and intuition (hads). Even the faculty that perceives primary intuitive universals only apprehends mental estimative things. Consequently, among the principles and premises of demonstration there is nothing by which a universal proposition about external entities can be known. Since the syllogism yields knowledge only through a universal proposition, it is impossible to obtain certain knowledge from the form and matter they prescribe. He emphasizes this is evident to the contemplative mind and promises to clarify the source of confusion later. He then highlights the contrast between the natural, rational, scriptural, legal, faith-based method and the syllogistic, logical, theological method. He restates that by consensus and reason, the logical syllogism is only useful via a proposition, and the propositions they consider materials of demonstration contain no universal proposition about external things – only pure reason conceiving mental estimates. Without a universal proposition about external things in the principles, no knowledge results. He anticipates an objection: perhaps the intuitive premises are universal and thus provide knowledge of external things. He responds that the other principles (sense, tawatur, etc.) are particular, so only the purely rational intuitive premises should be used as premises of demonstration, but they are about mental estimates, not external particulars. Another objection: by combining particular sensory premises with universal rational premises, a particular conclusion can be reached, like knowing by sense that this person has 1000 dirhams and that person has 2000, and by reason that two is greater than one, thus concluding that the latter's wealth is greater. He concedes this works but points out it yields only a particular determinate proposition, not a universal one. Such particular matters do not require the elaborate categorical syllogism (qiyas shumul) as defined by logicians – they can be known without any syllogism, or through analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), or from two particulars (e.g., knowing Zayd is older than 'Amr and 'Amr older than Khalid). Thus, he concludes, the rational logical syllogism they formulated does not by itself yield any of the universal sciences established in external reality.

Knowing Particulars Without the Universal Syllogism

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the alleged capacity of demonstrative syllogism (qiyas burhani) to produce certain universal knowledge about external realities. He begins by asserting that the purely rational, self-evident sciences (al-'ulum al-awwaliyya al-badihiyya al-'aqliyya al-mahda) are concerned only with mental estimates (al-muqaddarat al-dhihniyya) like number and magnitude, not with external existing things. Then he notes that the materials of demonstrative syllogism as classified by logicians are generally perceived only as particular matters, not universal: they include inner and outer sense, recurrent reports (tawatur), experience (tajriba), and intuition (hads). Even the faculty that perceives primary intuitive universals only apprehends mental estimative things. Consequently, among the principles and premises of demonstration there is nothing by which a universal proposition about external entities can be known. Since the syllogism yields knowledge only through a universal proposition, it is impossible to obtain certain knowledge from the form and matter they prescribe. He emphasizes this is evident to the contemplative mind and promises to clarify the source of confusion later. He then highlights the contrast between the natural, rational, scriptural, legal, faith-based method and the syllogistic, logical, theological method. He restates that by consensus and reason, the logical syllogism is only useful via a proposition, and the propositions they consider materials of demonstration contain no universal proposition about external things – only pure reason conceiving mental estimates. Without a universal proposition about external things in the principles, no knowledge results. He anticipates an objection: perhaps the intuitive premises are universal and thus provide knowledge of external things. He responds that the other principles (sense, tawatur, etc.) are particular, so only the purely rational intuitive premises should be used as premises of demonstration, but they are about mental estimates, not external particulars. Another objection: by combining particular sensory premises with universal rational premises, a particular conclusion can be reached, like knowing by sense that this person has 1000 dirhams and that person has 2000, and by reason that two is greater than one, thus concluding that the latter's wealth is greater. He concedes this works but points out it yields only a particular determinate proposition, not a universal one. Such particular matters do not require the elaborate categorical syllogism (qiyas shumul) as defined by logicians – they can be known without any syllogism, or through analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil), or from two particulars (e.g., knowing Zayd is older than 'Amr and 'Amr older than Khalid). Thus, he concludes, the rational logical syllogism they formulated does not by itself yield any of the universal sciences established in external reality.

Ibn Taymiyya’s Critique of the Categorical Syllogism
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that categorical syllogism is useless and begins a third argument about universal premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya’s two main conclusions and his third argument against categorical syllogism, and identify the circularity/infinite regress objection.

Core Idea
The categorical syllogism is useless: it fails for universals and is unnecessary for particulars.

Ibn Taymiyya delivers a decisive blow to the categorical syllogism by showing it is neither needed nor beneficial. He then launches a third, deeper argument: every syllogism depends on universal premises, but those premises themselves cannot be proven by another syllogism without running into circularity or infinite regress. This forces the logician to admit self-evident truths, undermining the claim that syllogism is the primary route to knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conclusions Ibn Taymiyya draws
He first states that universal syllogism does not benefit universal matters, as previously argued, and is not needed for particular matters since sense, analogy, and determinate syllogisms suffice. Thus he concludes the syllogism has no benefit at all.
This is a summary of earlier critiques.

Find the analogy with definitions

He previously critiqued definitions in earlier sections.

Extract the third argument’s starting point
He begins: every syllogism requires a universal premise (qadiyya kulliyya). Sense perception cannot grasp universals; they are known by intellect. The argument is cut off, but the implication is that self-evident truths (badihiyyat) are necessary.

If categorical syllogism depends on self-evident premises that are not themselves derived syllogistically, then the syllogism is not the foundational tool of knowledge. Instead, foundational knowledge comes from immediate intuition or revelation.
Ibn Taymiyya does not reject all reasoning, only the primacy of the Greek-style syllogism.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Necessity of Syllogism for Universal Knowledge
Ibn Taymiyya critiques logicians' claim that all knowledge requires syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that the logicians' universal negative claim about knowledge is unproven and evaluate the strength of his objection.

Core Idea
The logicians' universal negative claim about knowledge is itself unproven.

Ibn Taymiyya grants that the logical syllogism can produce knowledge, but he attacks the logicians' assertion that all non-intuitive knowledge requires it. In this passage, he exposes that the logicians have no proof for their universal negative proposition, and he argues that self-examination reveals many instances of knowledge attained without syllogism. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for the necessity of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Universal Negative Claim
Locate the logicians' claim that 'what is not evident from conceptions and assents is known only by definition and syllogism.' This is a universal negative proposition that Ibn Taymiyya targets.
The claim is explicit: 'إن ما ليس ببديهي من التصورات والتصديقات لا يُعْلَمُ إلا بالحد والقياس'.
2
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's Concession
Notice his initial concession: he supposes that the form and matter of syllogism can produce universal sciences. This shows he is not rejecting syllogism outright.
This concession strengthens his critique by showing he is not merely biased against logic.
3
Examine His First Objection: Lack of Proof
Ibn Taymiyya asks how the logicians can know that universal knowledge is only attained through syllogism. He points out that 'lack of knowledge is not knowledge of non-existence' – their claim is baseless.
This is an epistemological objection about the burden of proof.
4
Examine His Second Objection: Self-Examination
He argues that if the logicians examined themselves, they would find many universal sciences and conceptions without syllogism or definition. He challenges them to introspect.
This appeals to empirical evidence from personal experience.
5
Consider the Implications: Extending the Critique
Ibn Taymiyya extends the critique to all humanity, prophets, and even God, showing the absurdity of claiming that God's knowledge depends on syllogism.
This reinforces the point that the logicians' claim is too broad and unsubstantiated.

Commentary
This is a critical passage where Ibn Taymiyya presents the fourth aspect of his critique against the logicians. He begins by granting, for the sake of argument, that the form and matter of the logical syllogism can produce universal sciences. However, he questions how the logicians can know that universal knowledge cannot be attained without syllogism. He accuses them of making a universal negative claim without proof, merely claiming that non-intuitive conceptions and assents require definition or syllogism. He then argues that such a claim stems from a lack of self-examination; if they examined themselves, they would find many universal sciences and conceptions obtained without syllogism or definition. Furthermore, he challenges the logicians' assumption that all humanity is like them, and that God only grants knowledge through logical syllogism. Ibn Taymiyya even points out the absurdity of extending this claim to the prophets and to God Himself. He concludes that the logicians' universal negative proposition is baseless, covering what only God can count.

The categorical syllogism in the hadith of intoxicants
Hadith forming a categorical syllogism on intoxicants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the terms and figure of a categorical syllogism in a hadith and verify its validity.

Core Idea
This hadith is a perfect Barbara syllogism: intoxicants are khamr, khamr is haram, so intoxicants are haram.

The Prophet ﷺ said: 'Every intoxicant is khamr and every khamr is haram.' This concise statement actually contains a complete logical argument. In this lesson, we will parse it as a categorical syllogism and see how it serves as a foundation for Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
In the hadith, find the three distinct terms: the minor term (subject of conclusion), major term (predicate of conclusion), and middle term (appears in both premises).
The minor term is 'intoxicant' (muskir), the major term is 'haram', the middle term is 'khamr'.
2
Locate the premises and conclusion
Separate the statements: 'Every intoxicant is khamr' is the minor premise, 'every khamr is haram' is the major premise, 'every intoxicant is haram' is the conclusion.
The conclusion is explicitly stated.
3
Determine the figure
Check the position of the middle term. In the first figure, the middle term is subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise.
Here 'khamr' is predicate in minor premise and subject in major premise, so it is first figure.
4
Identify the mood and validity
All three propositions are universal affirmatives (A propositions). The mood is AAA. The first figure AAA is valid (Barbara).
All conditions for validity are satisfied.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of hadith statements that together constitute a categorical syllogism. The first statement 'Every intoxicant is khamr and every khamr is haram' is a compound proposition linking two universal affirmatives. The second 'Every intoxicant is khamr' restates the minor premise. The third 'Every intoxicant is haram' is the conclusion. In logical terms, this is a first-figure syllogism (Barbara): All A are B (intoxicants are khamr), all B are C (khamr is haram), therefore all A are C (intoxicants are haram). This hadith is frequently employed by scholars to demonstrate that nabidh (date wine) and other intoxicants are forbidden analogically to khamr. The text here simply quotes the hadith without explicit commentary, but the logical structure is self-evident.
