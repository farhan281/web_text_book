Critique of Philosophers and Theologians
Author critiques arguments of philosophers and theologians, then relates a personal anecdote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between rejecting a doctrine and rejecting its evidence, using Ibn Taymiyya's critique of theologians and philosophers.

Core Idea
Criticizing a proof is not criticizing the truth it aims to prove.

Ibn Taymiyya levels a sweeping critique against philosophers and theologians, claiming their arguments are riddled with falsehood. But he does not reject every doctrine they discuss; instead, he separates the truth of a belief from the strength of its evidence. His personal anecdote about tawhid illustrates this crucial distinction, which is vital for anyone engaging in Islamic scholarly debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups under critique
Locate where Ibn Taymiyya names 'al-Mutakallimun' (theologians) and 'al-Falasifah' (philosophers). Note that he contrasts them with Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Hadith.
2
Extract his general claim about their arguments
Find the sentence: 'Everything these people say contains falsehood, either in the evidence or in the issues.' Understand that he divides their errors into two categories.
3
Analyze the anecdote: doctrine vs. evidence
In the story, Ibn Taymiyya affirms tawhid is true but challenges the specific evidence presented. He says: 'I have no doubt about tawhid, but I doubt about this particular evidence.' This shows he separates the doctrine from the argument.
4
Recognize the broader implication
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that objection (i'tirad) and false dialectic are not knowledge. The lesson is that criticizing an argument does not necessarily mean rejecting the underlying belief.

Commentary
This passage begins with praise for the followers of the Sunnah and Hadith, who are described as the saved sect. The author then contrasts them with the people of kalam (theologians) and philosophers, who oppose the Sunnah. He asserts that their syllogisms (aqyisa) are weak and their statements are full of falsehood. He then recounts a personal anecdote from his youth when he debated a supporter of these groups about the evidence for tawhid (monotheism). His interlocutor initially marveled at the claim that everything they say contains falsehood, but when challenged to produce evidence, the author demonstrated the flaw in it. The anecdote illustrates that one can affirm a true doctrine (tawhid) while rejecting the specific arguments for it. Finally, the author begins to list proofs that these people are weak in knowledge, starting with their own doubt and confusion, but the enumeration is cut off.

The Fragile Certainty of Philosophers vs. the Firm Certainty of Hadith Scholars
Ibn Taymiyya contrasts the fragile arguments of philosophers with the firm certainty of hadith scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's critique of philosophical argumentation and his defense of the epistemological status of hadith scholars.

Core Idea
True certainty is not the product of formal logic but of direct knowledge that feels itself to be knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya opens with a striking metaphor: philosophical arguments are like glass—they appear true but shatter when examined. In contrast, the people of hadith possess an unshakeable certainty that is self-aware. In this lesson, we will unpack his critique and see how he turns the tables on the philosophizing theologians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the metaphor
What does Ibn Taymiyya compare the philosophers' arguments to? Find the phrase 'كالزجاج' (like glass) and understand its implications: fragile, appearing true but breaking.
2
Extract the accusation
What do the philosophers say about hadith scholars? Locate the phrase 'جَزموا بغير دليل' (they decided without evidence) and note the charge of taqlid.
3
Find Ibn Taymiyya's response
How does he dismiss the accusation? He concedes it may apply to some commoners but then distinguishes between two types of certainty.
4
Note the epistemological shift
The focus moves from external proof to internal self-awareness of knowledge. This is the crux of his argument.

Arguments that crumble like glass, you think them true, and every breaker is broken. So if the state of their arguments is like this, then what vain falsehood and padding is greater than this? And how is it fitting for the likes of these to criticize the people of hadith and the Sunnah, who are the greatest of people in knowledge, certainty, tranquility, and serenity? They know and know that they know, and they are certain of the truth, not doubting nor wavering. As for what the scholars of hadith and their elite have been given of certainty, knowledge, and guidance, it is a matter too great for description, but among their common people there is certainty, knowledge, and beneficial knowledge, the like of which has not been attained by the imams of the philosophizing theologians. And this is apparent and witnessed by everyone. The utmost that one of them can say is: They have decided without evidence, and are determined without proof, and they only have blind following. And this may be the case among many common people, but the certainty of knowledge is not the certainty of desire. For the one who is certain without knowledge finds in himself that he is not knowledgeable of what he is certain of, while the one who is certain with knowledge finds in himself that he is knowledgeable. Since being knowledgeable and not knowledgeable is like being hearing and seeing and not hearing and not seeing. So he knows that from himself just as he knows from himself being loving and hating, desiring and disliking, happy and sad, favored and tormented, and other such things. And whoever doubts that he knows while he knows is like one who decides that he knows while he does not know. And that is similar to one who doubts that he heard and saw, or decides that he heard and saw what he did not hear and see. Error or falsehood occurs to a person in each of the two sides of negation and affirmation, but this occasional error or falsehood does not prevent the person from being certain about what he does not doubt of that, just as he is certain of what he finds of tastes and smells, even though he may experience such perversion that he finds the sweet bitter. So the accidental causes of error in the inner or outer sense and the intellect are like the accidental disease of movement of body and soul, and the origin is soundness in perception and movement, for God created His servants upon the fitrah. And these matters are known to be erroneous by their specific causes, like the yellow bile that affects taste, and like squint in the eye, and the like. Otherwise, whoever examines himself regarding what he is certain of will find that most people who are certain about what should not be certain are only certain due to some desire, as Allah said: 'And indeed many lead astray by their desires...'

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians and philosophers, defending the epistemological certainty of the scholars of hadith. Ibn Taymiyya begins by likening the arguments of the philosophers to fragile glass that shatters itself, highlighting their worthlessness. He questions how such flawed arguers can criticize the people of hadith, who are described as possessing supreme knowledge, certainty, and tranquility. The author then addresses the common objection that the hadith scholars are merely following tradition without proof (taqlid). He concedes that this may apply to some commoners, but then makes a crucial distinction: the certainty born of knowledge is fundamentally different from the certainty born of desire (hawa). A person who is certain without knowledge internally feels his lack of knowledge, whereas one certain with knowledge feels his knowledge. This self-awareness is similar to knowing one's own states like love, hate, desire, etc. He then argues that error does occur, but it does not negate the possibility of true certainty. Errors have specific causes, like sour bile affecting taste or squint affecting sight. The original state (fitrah) is soundness; just as a healthy body is the norm, sound perception is the norm. Finally, he asserts that most people who are certain about falsehoods are driven by desire, quoting the Quranic verse 'And indeed many lead astray by their desires' (though incomplete). The passage thus serves to vindicate the knowledge of hadith scholars and expose the weakness of philosophical certainty.

Certainty of Knowledge vs. Certainty of Desire
Ibn Taymiyya distinguishes between true certainty and false certainty driven by desire.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish certainty based on knowledge from certainty based on desire, and explain the role of fitrah in sound judgment according to Ibn Taymiyya.

Core Idea
Certainty is not monolithic; the certainty of knowledge feels like knowledge, while the certainty of desire feels like ignorance.

Ibn Taymiyya makes a crucial epistemological distinction: the certainty of knowledge is self-aware, while the certainty of desire is blind. He argues that sound perception is the norm (fitrah) and errors have specific causes. In this lesson, we will analyse this distinction and its implications for the reliability of hadith scholars' knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Extract the two types of certainty
Identify the phrases 'جزم العلم' and 'جزم الهوى'. Note that Ibn Taymiyya explicitly distinguishes them.
2
Find the internal criterion
How does a person know which type they have? He says the one with knowledge feels knowledgeable, the one without feels ignorant.
Look for 'يجد من نفسه أنه عالم' vs 'يجد من نفسه أنه غير عالم'.
3
Analyse the analogy to sense and self
He compares knowledge certainty to knowing one's own hearing, seeing, love, etc. This shows that self-awareness is key.
4
Understand the fitrah principle
He states that God created humans on fitrah (sound nature). Error is exceptional, caused by specific factors like bile or desire.
The origin is soundness; error has known causes.
5
Identify the main cause of false certainty
He concludes that most false certainty is due to desire (hawa), quoting the Quran.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the logicians and philosophers, defending the epistemological certainty of the scholars of hadith. Ibn Taymiyya begins by likening the arguments of the philosophers to fragile glass that shatters itself, highlighting their worthlessness. He questions how such flawed arguers can criticize the people of hadith, who are described as possessing supreme knowledge, certainty, and tranquility. The author then addresses the common objection that the hadith scholars are merely following tradition without proof (taqlid). He concedes that this may apply to some commoners, but then makes a crucial distinction: the certainty born of knowledge is fundamentally different from the certainty born of desire (hawa). A person who is certain without knowledge internally feels his lack of knowledge, whereas one certain with knowledge feels his knowledge. This self-awareness is similar to knowing one's own states like love, hate, desire, etc. He then argues that error does occur, but it does not negate the possibility of true certainty. Errors have specific causes, like sour bile affecting taste or squint affecting sight. The original state (fitrah) is soundness; just as a healthy body is the norm, sound perception is the norm. Finally, he asserts that most people who are certain about falsehoods are driven by desire, quoting the Quranic verse 'And indeed many lead astray by their desires' (though incomplete). The passage thus serves to vindicate the knowledge of hadith scholars and expose the weakness of philosophical certainty.

When Qiyas Contradicts Revelation: Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Blind Analogy
Ibn Taymiyya critiques those who rigidly apply qiyas contradicting revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a qiyas argument conflicts with a revealed text and evaluate why Ibn Taymiyya considers the one who breaks such a qiyas better than the one who applies it consistently.

Core Idea
Consistency in false analogy is worse than inconsistency in seeking truth.

Ibn Taymiyya often warns against the blind application of qiyas (analogical reasoning) when it contradicts the clear texts of revelation. In this passage, he argues that a person who rigidly adheres to a flawed qiyas is actually more corrupt than one who recognizes the conflict and abandons the qiyas. You will learn to identify such conflicts and apply his criterion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qiyas being applied
Find the analogical reasoning in the text: what is the 'illa (cause) and what is the original case (asl) and new case (far')? In the passage, Ibn Taymiyya is criticizing those who use qiyas to derive rulings that contradict texts.
The qiyas may be implicit; look for the claim of equivalence.
2
Identify the conflicting nass
Determine which revealed text (Quran or hadith) is being contradicted by the qiyas. In the passage, the example of Allah's istiwa' is known by revelation, not by qiyas.
The nass may be a clear ruling or a theological truth.
3
Apply Ibn Taymiyya's criterion
According to Ibn Taymiyya, the person who applies the qiyas consistently is more corrupt than the one who breaks it, even if the latter appears inconsistent. The reason is that the qiyas itself is false due to its opposition to nass.
Consistency in error is not a virtue; truth is paramount.

Istihsan: Preferring Revelation over Blind Qiyas
Ibn Taymiyya introduces istihsan as the proper response when qiyas contradicts texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istihsan and explain how it serves as a corrective to corrupt qiyas.

Core Idea
Istihsan is the juristic preference for a text over a conflicting analogy.

In the previous lesson, we saw Ibn Taymiyya's critique of blind qiyas. Here he introduces the concept of istihsan (juristic preference) as the correct approach: when qiyas conflicts with a text, the text should take precedence. This lesson defines istihsan and shows its importance.

Guided Path
1
Define istihsan
Istihsan literally means 'seeking the good'. In usul al-fiqh, it refers to abandoning a qiyas in favor of a stronger evidence, usually a text or consensus.
Different schools define it differently; here Ibn Taymiyya uses it as preference for nass.
2
Identify the examples in the passage
Ibn Taymiyya cites the practice of Abu Hanifa and others as proponents of istihsan. He narrates Abu Hanifa's warning against Zufar's excessive qiyas.
Note that Ibn Taymiyya endorses istihsan in this context as superior to rigid qiyas.
3
Apply the concept
When encountering a qiyas that seems valid but contradicts a text, the correct approach is to prefer the text (istihsan). This is better than forcing the qiyas.
Istihsan is not arbitrary; it is based on a recognized source of law.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Fabricated Predictions
Ibn Taymiyya denounces false prophecies and their sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two main methods of lying about future events that Ibn Taymiyya exposes and explain why he considers them epistemologically invalid.

Core Idea
False predictions exploit worldly desires and lack the divine criterion of truth.

Ibn Taymiyya turns his critical eye from logical fallacies to the practical harm of fabricated prophecies. He argues that lies about cosmic events (like future wars or political changes) are even more rampant than lies about religion, because worldly aspirants are desperate for validation. Identifying and Refuting Fabricated Reports (Makdhub) in Theological Arguments
Ibn Taymiyya refutes false reports used as evidence by opponents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a fabricated report (مكذوب) used as evidence in a theological argument and refute it by pointing out internal contradictions with established Islamic facts (Quran, consensus, geography).

Core Idea
A fabricated report collapses when its internal elements contradict established facts.

In theological debates, opponents sometimes rely on fabricated reports (مكذوب) to support their positions. Ibn Taymiyya, in this passage, shows us a powerful method to dismantle such arguments: not by attacking the report's chain of transmission alone, but by exposing an internal contradiction with fundamental Islamic facts. The geographical impossibility of the Ashab al-Suffa being in Mecca during the Mi'raj is enough to reject the report as a lie. In this lesson, you will learn to apply this same critical technique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fabricated report (المكذوب)

The term المكذوب refers to a fabricated report, but here it's used as a noun for the false story itself.
2
Extract the core factual claim
What does the report assert? For the first report: that the Ashab al-Suffa, who lived in Medina, heard the Prophet's conversation with Allah during the Mi'raj, which occurred in Mecca.
3
Find the internal contradiction
Ibn Taymiyya points out that the Mi'raj was in Mecca by Quranic text and consensus, but the Suffa was only in Medina. Therefore, the report is geographically impossible and hence false.
This is a purely logical contradiction: two facts cannot coexist.
4
Expose the underlying dangerous implication
Why does Ibn Taymiyya also mention the claim about the Ashab al-Suffa fighting with polytheists? He shows that such fabricated reports are used to justify following whatever happens in reality (predestined events) as if it were divine will, even if it's sinful. Why Accusing Opponents of Defying Reason Is Not a Proof
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the practice of dismissing opposing views as defiance of reason without proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rhetorical fallacy of 'mukabara li-l-uqul' and explain why Ibn Taymiyya rejects it as a valid argument.

Core Idea
Calling someone a denier of reason is not a proof; it is a rhetorical shortcut that replaces genuine argument.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya takes on an opponent who dismisses others as 'defying reason' without offering any evidence. He shows that this move is empty: it neither proves the opponent wrong nor establishes one's own position. Instead, it functions as a rhetorical device to avoid the hard work of rational proof (hujja aqliyya). Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to spot fallacious argumentation in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target
The objector (Abu al-Faraj) claims that the affirmers' views are 'mukabara li-l-uqul'—defiance of reason. Notice that Ibn Taymiyya does not accept this as a given; he questions its basis.
The term 'mukabara' is used here as a label, not an analysis.
2
Note the absence of proof
Ibn Taymiyya points out that the objector brought no proof at all—neither transmitted nor rational. He merely relied on taqlid of a group, which is not a valid argument.
Taqlid here means blind following, not the technical usul sense.
3
Examine the breakdown of 'mukabara'
Ibn Taymiyya splits the accusation into two possibilities: (1) the defiance is in what they affirm, (2) in a contradiction in their position. He shows that (1) is false because even extreme anthropomorphists were not dismissed outright; they were refuted with rational proofs. (2) is not applicable because the affirmers do not necessarily hold contradictory positions.
This logical disjunction (either/or) is a key analytical move.
4
Observe the reductio
Ibn Taymiyya notes that even the most extreme deniers of the Creator do not claim that their opponents' views are 'mukabara' in the sense of being self-evidently false. They acknowledge that rational inquiry (nazar) is needed. Thus the objector's method is inconsistent with the very tradition he appeals to.
This shows that the accusation is not a standard epistemological claim even among the nufāt.

Commentary
This is a critical passage from Ibn Taymiyya's Naqd Mantiq where he attacks the interlocutor's method of dismissing opponents as defying reason ('mukabara li-l-uqul') without providing actual proofs. The passage is part of a larger refutation of an objector (likely Abu al-Faraj). He then addresses the objector's claim that certain people should not be engaged (i.e., reasoned with). Ibn Taymiyya responds by reminding that Allah sent messengers to all people, so none are beyond the scope of address. Next, he tackles the claim that the opponents 'defy reason'. He breaks this down into two possibilities: either the defiance is in what they affirm, or in their contradictory combination of affirming some things while denying limbs (i.e., anthropomorphism). He dismisses the first possibility by noting that even extreme anthropomorphists like Hisham ibn al-Hakam were never accused of defying reason outright; their opponents used rational proofs that required rigorous reflection. They use rhetorical tactics to stir aversion, but that is not a valid proof. The lesson is that accusing someone of defying reason is not a substitute for actual rational argumentation.

Why Syllogism Cannot Correct Intuition
Author refutes the claim that syllogism can correct intuitive premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument against the claim that syllogism corrects intuition and evaluate its logical strength.

Core Idea
Syllogism depends on intuition; it cannot correct what it presupposes.

The logicians claim that syllogistic reasoning can correct errors in intuitive judgments. Ibn Taymiyya here turns this claim on its head, showing that syllogisms themselves rely on intuitive premises. If those premises are suspect, the syllogism cannot rescue them — the more complex the proof, the greater the chance of error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Locate the logicians' assertion that syllogism can correct intuitive (badiha/fitrah) judgments. The author phrases it: 'their claim that intuition and innate disposition may judge something that syllogism shows to be false.'
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's core observation
Notice his immediate objection: 'syllogism necessarily has intuitive, innate premises.' Every syllogism rests on premises that are themselves known intuitively. This is the foundation of his critique.
3
Follow the reductio ad absurdum
The author argues: if intuitive premises could be false and only corrected by another syllogism, then we have a self-contradiction because the syllogism's own premises would be in question. This leads to an infinite regress or circularity.
He says: 'the intuitive premises would be in conflict with themselves and with the requirement of the syllogism whose premises are intuitive.'
4
Note the probabilistic argument
The author adds that error is more likely in reasoning with many premises than in a single intuitive judgment. 'What is known by syllogism and imaginative premises is closer to error than what is known by mere innate disposition.'
5
State the conclusion
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that syllogism cannot override intuition; rather, intuitive knowledge is epistemically more reliable than syllogistic reasoning.

Critique of the Philosophers' Theory of Prophecy as Intuition
Author exposes and refutes the Sabian-influenced theory of prophecy based on hads (intuition).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the philosophers' claim that prophecy is based on intuition and refute it using the author's critique that intuition reduces to analogy and visions are common to all.

Core Idea
Prophecy is not intuitive genius but divine revelation; the philosophers reduce it to a natural human capacity.

The philosophers, influenced by Sabianism, claimed that prophethood is a natural perfection of the soul achieved through purification and intuitive power. In this passage, the author exposes the flaws in this theory by showing that intuition is merely analogical reasoning, and visions are akin to dreams. He then highlights the dangerous implication that prophethood becomes acquirable rather than a divine gift.

Guided Path

Identify the philosophers' claim about prophecy
Find where the author summarizes the philosophers' view: they made the sciences of the prophets from intuitive sciences (al-hads) due to the strength and purity of their souls. The prophet knows by intuition what others do not know, and sees and hears representations of angels within himself.
This is the core claim: prophecy is a natural psychological phenomenon of extreme intuition.

The author states: 'Maa'lum anna al-hads raji'un ila qiyas al-tamthil' (it is known that intuition reduces to analogical syllogism). This is crucial because analogical reasoning is probabilistic (zanni), not certain (yaqini). Thus prophetic knowledge would be uncertain.
This connects back to earlier discussions on qiyas al-tamthil.

Extract the second objection: visions are like dreams
The author argues that what the prophet sees and hears in himself is of the kind of dreams (min jins al-ru'ya). This degree is attained by many common people and disbelievers, so it cannot be the essence of prophethood.
The comparison to dreams shows it is not exclusive or miraculous.
4
Identify the theological consequence: prophethood becomes acquirable
The philosophers say prophethood is 'muktasaba' (acquirable), denying the descent of angels and direct divine speech. The author highlights this as a serious error, as it contradicts revelation.
This is a key doctrinal point: prophecy is a divine gift, not a human achievement.
5
Analyze the anecdote about Moses
The author cites a philosopher who told Moses: 'I believe you in everything except that the Cause of causes spoke to you.' This illustrates that the philosophers' framework cannot accept direct divine communication, exposing the inadequacy of their theory.
The anecdote serves as a concrete example of the logical conclusion of their position.

Ibn Taymiyya's Dilemma: Definitions Lead to Circularity or Regress
Author refutes the logicians' claim about definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's dilemma argument against the necessity of definitions and identify the two logical consequences (circularity and infinite regress).

Core Idea
Definitions cannot be necessary for all knowledge — they lead to either circularity or infinite regress.

The logicians claim that every non-self-evident concept must be defined through a composite of genus and differentia. Ibn Taymiyya attacks this claim by examining the definer himself. If the definer already knows the defined thing without a definition, the claim is false. If he relies on another definition, that definition must itself be known, leading to an endless chain or a circular loop. This dilemma reveals the impossibility of the logicians' position. In this lesson, we will unpack his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Restate the logicians' claim being attacked
Identify the precise claim: 'The concept that is not self-evident is not attained except through definition.' This is the target of the refutation.
The claim is about concepts, not propositions.
2
Identify the definer's two possible states of knowledge
For any given definition, the definer either knows the defined thing without a definition (case A) or through another definition (case B).
3
Trace the consequence of each case
In case A, the definer's knowledge disproves the claim that definition is necessary. In case B, the other definition opens two sub-possibilities: either it is the same as the first (circularity) or it leads to yet another definition (infinite regress). Both are logically unacceptable.
4
Conclude the refutation
Since both horns of the dilemma are impossible, the original claim that all non-self-evident concepts require definitions is false. Some concepts must be known without definition.
This does not reject definitions entirely, only their alleged necessity.

Ibn Taymiyya's Second Objection: No Universal Definitions
Author argues that logicians have not agreed on any single definition for anything.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's objection that the logicians' claim that definitions are necessary for knowledge leads to absurd skepticism, and identify the sophistry involved.

Core Idea
If definitions are the only path to knowledge, and none is agreed upon, then no one knows anything—this is sophistry.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya presents his second objection against the logicians' theory of definitions. He argues that if knowledge depends entirely on definitions, and yet there is no definition that all logicians accept, then it follows that no human being has ever attained knowledge. He labels this conclusion as extreme sophistry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Logicians claim that things are only conceived through definitions (al-hadd). This is the premise Ibn Taymiyya targets.
2
Observe the lack of agreement
Ibn Taymiyya notes that logicians have not agreed on any single definition for anything. What one claims, another disputes.
3
Trace the logical implication
If no definition is universally accepted, and definitions are necessary for knowledge, then no one has knowledge. This is a conditional argument.
4
Recognize the consequence
The conclusion is that no human being has any knowledge (ma'rifa). Ibn Taymiyya calls this extreme sophistry (safsata), as it leads to radical skepticism.
5
Understand the objection's force
Ibn Taymiyya is not rejecting definitions outright, but showing that the logicians' exclusive reliance on them leads to an absurd outcome, thus undermining their position.

Ibn Taymiyya's Historical Refutation of Necessary Definitions
Author argues that most knowledgeable people, including prophets and early Muslims, knew without definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's historical argument that knowledge is independent of composite definitions, and evaluate its strength.

Core Idea
Prophetic knowledge and early Muslim scholarship prove definitions are not necessary for true knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya presents his third objection against the logicians' claim that definitions are indispensable for acquiring knowledge. He marshals historical evidence: prophets, their followers, and the first three generations of Muslims possessed profound knowledge without ever using the composite definitions of genus and differentia. This empirical observation undermines the logical doctrine's universality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
What exactly do logicians assert about definitions? From the passage, they claim that knowledge is dependent on definitions (al-ma'rifa mutawaqqifa 'alayha).
This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
2
List the counterexample groups
Ibn Taymiyya cites prophets, the first three generations of Muslims (the salaf), and experts in medicine and calculation as groups who had knowledge without burdening themselves with composite definitions.
These groups are considered the most knowledgeable in their fields.
3
Analyze the argument structure
The objection is an inductive generalization: since many knowledgeable groups did not use definitions, the universal claim that all knowledge requires definitions is false. The argument also traces the origin of definitions to later theologians and philosophers, linking them to increased disagreement.
This is a historical and empirical refutation.
4
Evaluate the evidence
Consider whether the examples fully disprove the necessity of definitions. Could the early Muslims have used definitions implicitly? The strength of the argument depends on accepting that their knowledge was indeed certain and definition-free.
Critics might argue that definitions are implicitly present even if not formally stated.
5
Assess the implication
If definitions are not necessary, the logical tradition's claim to epistemic priority collapses. This shifts the burden of proof to logicians to show why definitions are necessary for others.
Implication: knowledge can be acquired through revelation, innate reason, or experience.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Univocal Essential Attributes
Ibn Taymiyya's critique of univocal essential attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that shared essential attributes have a common and a specific aspect, and identify why the logicians' claim of numerical identity fails.

Core Idea
Sharing an attribute across species does not mean numerical identity of the attribute.

Ibn Taymiyya attacks a cornerstone of the logicians' theory of definition—the claim that essential attributes like 'animality' are univocally identical in every species. This lesson unpacks his objection and shows how it reframes our understanding of generic terms in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
The logicians assert that essential attributes (e.g., animality) are shared in reality, meaning the same attribute exists in both man and horse. Ibn Taymiyya targets this univocity assumption.
This claim underlies many definitions in classical logic.
2
Distinguish two meanings of 'shared'
Ibn Taymiyya offers two interpretations: (1) numerically the same attribute in external reality (impossible for distinct individuals), or (2) a similar attribute exists. He rejects the first and reframes the second.
The Arabic 'ishtirak' can mean both.
3
Analyze his key examples
He compares animality of man and horse, and sounds (speech vs. neighing). Both have a common aspect (qadr mushtarak) but also a distinguishing aspect (mumayyiz). The specific reality differs.
Note how he uses sensory and spiritual levels (body and soul).
4
Connect to the hadith about names
The Prophet's hadith naming al-Harith and al-Hammam shows that human attributes (activity, concern) are unique to humans, even within the common framework of animality.
This reinforces the idea that common terms do not imply identical realities.
5
Generalize the error
The logicians' mistake is treating generic terms as univocal in external reality. The correct approach is to acknowledge both commonality and specificity in definition.
This critique has implications for how definitions are constructed.

السادس عشر : أن في الصفات الذاتية المشتركة والمختصة، كالحيوانية والناطقية، إن أرادوا بالاشتراك أن نفسَ الصَّفة الموجودة في الخارج مشتركة فهذا باطل؛ إذ لا أشتراك في المعيَّنات التي يمنع تصورها من وقوع الشركة فيها. وإن أرادوا بالاشتراك أن مثل تلك الصفة حاصلة للنوع الآخر، قيل لهم لا ريب أن بين حيوانية الإنسان وحيوانية الفَرَس قدرًا مشتركا، وكذلك بين صوتيهما وتمييزهما قدرًا مشتركا، فإن الإنسان له تمييز وللفَرَس تمييز، ولهذا صوت هو النطق، ولذاك صوت هو الصهيل، فقد خُصَّ كلَّ صوتٍ باسم يخصه، فإذا كان حقيقة أحد هذين يخالف الآخر ويختص بنوعه، فمن أين جعلتم حيوانية أحدهما مماثلة لحيوانية الآخر في الحد والحقيقة؟! وهلا قيل: إن بين حيوانيَّتهما قدرًا مشتركًا ومميزا، كما إن بين صوتيهما كذلك؟ وذلك أن الحسّ والحركة الإرادية إما أن توجد للجسم أو للنفس، فإن الجسم يحس ويتحرّك بالإرادة ، والنفس تحس وتتحرك بالإرادة، وإن كان بين الوصفين من الفرق ما بين الحقيقتين. وكذلك النطقُ هو للنفس بالتمييز والمعرفة والكلام النفساني، وهو للجسم أيضًا بتمييز القلب ومعرفته والكلام اللساني. فكلّ من جسمه ونفسه يوصف بهذين الوصفين، وليست حركة نفسه وإرادتها ومعرفتها ونطقها مثل ما للفَرَس، وإن كان بينهما قدر مشترك، وكذلك ما يقوم بجسمه من الحس والحركة الإرادية ليس مثل ما للفرس، وإن كان بينهما قدر مشترك، فإن الذي يلائم جسمه من مطعم ومشرب و ملبس ومنكَح ومشموم ومرئي ومسموع بحيث يُحِسُّهُ ويتحرك إليه حركة إرادية ليس هو مثل ما للفرس. فالحس والحركة الإرادية هي بالمعنى العام لجميع الحيوان، وبالمعنى الخاص ليس إلا للإنسان، وكذلك التمييز سواء؛ ولهذا قال النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم: أحبُّ الأسماء إلى الله عبد الله وعبد الرحمن، وأصدق الأسماء الحارث وهمام ، وأقبحها حربٌ ومُرَّة) رواه مسلم. فالحارث هو العامل الكاسِبُ المتحرّك ، والهمام هو الدائم الهـم الـذي هو مقدم الإرادة، فكل إنسان حارث فاعل بإرادته، وكذلك مسبوق بإحساسه.
The sixteenth [point]: Regarding shared and specific essential attributes, such as animality and rationality, if they mean by 'shared' that the very attribute existing in the external world is shared, this is false; for there is no sharing in determinate individuals whose conception prevents the occurrence of sharing in them. And if they mean by 'shared' that the like of that attribute exists in the other species, it is said to them: There is no doubt that between the animality of man and the animality of the horse there is a common aspect, and likewise between their sounds and their discrimination there is a common aspect. For man has discrimination and the horse has discrimination; and for man there is a sound which is speech [nutq], and for the horse there is a sound which is neighing [sahil]; each sound has been specified with a name particular to it. So if the reality of one of these two differs from the other and is specific to its species, how have you made the animality of one of them equivalent to the animality of the other in definition and reality?! Why not say: between their animalities there is a common aspect and a distinguishing aspect, just as between their sounds there is likewise? That is because sensation and voluntary movement either belong to the body or to the soul; indeed, the body senses and moves voluntarily, and the soul senses and moves voluntarily, though between the two attributes there is the difference between the two realities. Likewise, speech [nutq] belongs to the soul by way of discrimination, knowledge, and internal speech, and it also belongs to the body by way of the heart's discrimination, knowledge, and tongue-speech. So each of his body and his soul is described by these two attributes. His soul's movement, will, knowledge, and speech are not like those of the horse, though between them there is a common aspect. Similarly, what arises in his body of sensation and voluntary movement is not like that of the horse, though between them there is a common aspect. For what suits his body of food, drink, clothing, marriage, smell, sight, and hearing such that he senses it and moves towards it voluntarily is not like that of the horse. So sensation and voluntary movement are, in the general meaning, for all animals, and in the specific meaning, they are only for man; likewise discrimination is the same. For this reason, the Prophet ﷺ said: 'The most beloved names to Allah are Abdullah and Abdurrahman, the truest names are al-Harith and Hammam, and the ugliest names are Harb and Murrah' – reported by Muslim. Al-Harith is the worker, the earner, the mover; and al-Hammam is the one constantly concerned, which is the precursor of will. So every human is al-Harith, acting by his will, and likewise preceded by his sensation.

Commentary
This passage is a critical objection by Ibn Taymiyya against the logicians' claim that essential attributes shared across species, such as animality, are identical in their external reality. He begins by distinguishing two meanings of 'shared' (ishtirak). If they mean that numerically the same attribute exists in different species, that is impossible because determinate individuals cannot be shared. If they mean that a similar attribute exists, he argues that there is indeed a common aspect (qadr mushtarak) but also a distinguishing aspect (mumayyiz). Using the example of human and horse: both have animality, but human animality is not identical to horse animality, just as human speech (nutq) differs from horse neighing (sahil). He extends this to sensation and voluntary movement, which exist in both body and soul, but the specific instantiation differs. He supports his argument with the hadith about names: al-Harith (the active worker) and al-Hammam (the constantly concerned) describe human nature in a way that does not apply to animals. The core point is that common terms like 'animality' refer to a concept with a common and a specific aspect; the logicians' error is treating them as univocal in reality.

Key Takeaway
Generic terms like 'animality' refer to a common aspect across species, but each species has a specific reality; treating them as identical in external reality is a logical error.
