Ibrahim's Argument in Multiple Syllogistic Figures
Author analyzes Ibrahim's Quranic argument against worship of celestial bodies using multiple figures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibrahim's argument in the first, second, fourth, and conditional syllogistic forms using the 'setting' (afool) as the middle term.

Core Idea
Ibrahim's Quranic argument fits multiple syllogistic figures, but the first figure is the most natural.

This passage shows how the classical logicians analyzed a famous Quranic argument — Prophet Ibrahim's reasoning against worshiping celestial bodies — and demonstrated its validity across different syllogistic figures. By studying these reconstructions, you will understand how a single argument can be expressed in multiple logical forms, and why the first figure is preferred for its clarity and ease.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conclusion
What is Ibrahim trying to prove? The conclusion is that the celestial bodies (star, moon, sun) are not his Lord. In logical terms: 'This (star/moon/sun) is not my Lord.'
This is a negative categorical proposition.
2
Extract the Minor Premise
From the Quranic narrative: Ibrahim observes the star setting. The minor premise is: 'This (the star) is a setting thing.' This is the subject (the star) and the predicate (setting).
The minor premise comes from the observation (al-mushahada).
3
Extract the Major Premise
Ibrahim declares: 'I do not love those that set.' The author interprets this as: 'Nothing that sets is my Lord.' This is a universal negative proposition: 'No setting thing is my Lord.'
This premise is derived from Ibrahim's statement about love of setting things.
4
Reconstruct in the Second Figure
The author first presents the argument in the second figure: Major premise: 'Nothing that sets is my Lord' (universal negative). Minor premise: 'This is a setting thing' (particular affirmative). Conclusion: 'This is not my Lord' (particular negative). This is valid in the second figure (Camestres mood).
Second figure: middle term (setting) is predicate in both premises.
5
Convert to First and Fourth Figures
The author then shows the argument in the first figure by swapping premises: 'Nothing that sets is my Lord' becomes major, 'This is a setting thing' becomes minor, yielding the same conclusion. In the fourth figure, he makes the major premise 'What sets is not my Lord' and minor 'This is a setting thing', concluding 'My Lord is not this', which converts to 'This is not my Lord.'
First figure is natural; fourth is awkward and avoided.

Refuting Universal Negatives with Third-Figure Syllogisms
Author explains how Allah refuted Jewish denial of revelation using a third-figure syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid third-figure categorical syllogism and use its particular conclusion to refute a universal negative claim.

Core Idea
The third figure yields only particular conclusions, yet a single true particular suffices to refute a universal falsehood.

This lesson unpacks a powerful Quranic refutation: the Jews claimed that Allah never sent revelation to any human, thereby denying the prophethood of Muhammad ﷺ. Allah countered by citing Moses, a human who received revelation. The logical structure is a third-figure syllogism. Understanding how its particular conclusion invalidates a universal claim equips you to deploy similar arguments in theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Jewish syllogism and its flaw
The Jews implicitly argued: 'Muhammad is a human; no human has received revelation; therefore Muhammad has not received revelation.' The minor premise is true, but the major (universal negative) is false—yet they assumed it true. The conclusion is false because the major premise is unsound.
This is not a valid refutation; it's a logical error based on a false premise.
2
Extract the Quranic refutation syllogism
Allah's response: 'Say: Who sent down the Book that Moses brought?' This implies two premises: 'Moses is a human' (true) and 'Moses had the Book sent down to him' (true). Both premises are accepted by the Jews.
These premises are affirmative and both have 'Moses' as subject.
3
Determine the figure and mood
The middle term (Moses) is the subject of both premises, making this the third figure. Both premises are universal affirmative (A), and the conclusion is particular affirmative (I). The mood is Darapti (AAI-3), which is valid in classical logic.
Remember: third figure requires a particular conclusion; universals are not allowed.
4
Verify the conclusion's logical force
The conclusion is 'Some humans have had revelation sent down to them' (particular affirmative). This directly contradicts the Jews' universal negative claim 'No human has had revelation sent down.' A single true particular disproves a universal negative; the Jews cannot deny the premise about Moses.
The third figure's restriction to particular does not weaken the refutation—it is exactly what is needed.

The Middle Term's Dual Role in First and Fourth Figures
Al-Banani quotes al-Sa'd on the middle term not being repeated in first and fourth figures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the middle term is not considered repeated in first and fourth figures by distinguishing between its reference as concept (mafhum) and individual (dhat).

Core Idea
The middle term is not repeated—it shifts from concept to individual.

In this brief but crucial passage, al-Banani relays al-Taftazani's insight resolving a potential objection about the middle term in certain syllogistic figures. Understanding this distinction prevents a common misunderstanding about term repetition and deepens your appreciation of how logicians handle the subtleties of term reference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figures mentioned
The passage refers to the first and fourth figures. In these figures, the middle term appears once as subject and once as predicate. Note that this could be seen as a repetition of the same term.
2
Understand the potential objection
If the middle term is repeated without change, it might violate the principle that a term should not be used ambiguously in a syllogism. Al-Taftazani addresses this concern.
3
Grasp al-Taftazani's solution
He distinguishes two modes: when the middle term is predicate, it refers to its concept (mafhum) or universal meaning; when it is subject, it refers to the individual (dhat) or external instantiations. Hence it is not a repetition of the same sense.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the term 'knowledge' in a kalam syllogism. As predicate (e.g., 'All knowledge is created'), it means the universal concept; as subject (e.g., 'God's knowledge is eternal'), it refers to the specific attribute. The term is not repeated.
5
Check why this matters for validity
Without this distinction, some valid syllogisms in Islamic theology might be mistakenly rejected as having an ambiguous middle term. This principle preserves their validity.

Two Meanings of Predicate Being True of Subject
Commentator explains the meaning of predicate being true of subject and its implications for middle term repetition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between identity and predication interpretations of 'the predicate being the concept of the subject' and explain why the second interpretation leads to middle term repetition in the first and fourth figures.

Core Idea
Predication does not mean identity; it means the predicate concept applies to the subject.

In this passage, the commentator dissects a key claim by al-Bannani about what it means for a predicate to be 'true of' a subject. Two interpretations are considered: one equates to essential identity (clearly false), the other to genuine predication (the ordinary sense). Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how the middle term functions in categorical syllogisms, especially why it must be repeated in certain figures. The commentator then links this to his own logical introduction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
Find in the Arabic where al-Bannani contrasts two possible meanings of 'the predicate is the concept of the subject'. The first interpretation is that the subject's essence is identical to the concept of the predicate. The second is that the concept of the predicate is true of (sadaqa 'ala) the subject.
Look for the phrases 'عين مفهوم المحمول' and 'يصدق عليه مفهوم المحمول'.
2
Evaluate the first interpretation
The commentator states the first interpretation is clearly corrupt (فساده ظاهر). Why? If subject and predicate are identical, every proposition becomes a tautology (e.g., 'man is man'), which is not informative. In logical reasoning, we need predication to convey new information.
This interpretation would make all propositions trivial and destroy the utility of syllogistic arguments.
3
Understand the second interpretation
The second interpretation is that the concept of the predicate is true of the subject (يصدق عليه مفهوم المحمول). This is the ordinary sense of predication: the predicate applies to the subject without identity. For example, 'man is rational' – 'rational' is a property truly attributed to 'man' without meaning the essence of man is identical to rationality.
This is the standard logical relation in a categorical proposition.

The first figure requires the minor premise to be affirmative. 'You are not able' appears negative, so an objection arises. Note the condition: minor premise must be affirmative for a valid conclusion.

The major premise 'everyone who is not able... is not my Lord' is a simple negative (muhassala saliba), which is allowed as the major premise in the first figure.

Commentary
This passage demonstrates how a logical argument can be extracted from a Quranic dialogue. Al-Bannani analyzes the verse of Ibrahim (peace be upon him) when he debates the idolater Namrud. The implicit argument is: 'You cannot bring the sun from the west. Everyone who cannot bring the sun from the west is not my Lord. Therefore, you are not my Lord.' This is a first-figure syllogism. However, the first figure requires the minor premise (the one containing the subject of the conclusion) to be affirmative. But the minor 'You cannot bring the sun from the west' appears negative. The objection questions this. The solution: the negation in this premise is not a simple negation (salb) but a modal negation ('adool). In modal negation, the negation is attached to the predicate, not the copula. Hence, the proposition is actually affirmative in quality (since it affirms the negation of the predicate) and thus qualifies as an affirmative modal (mu'addala mawjiba). This satisfies the condition. The major premise 'Everyone who cannot do that is not my Lord' is a simple negative (muhassala saliba) and needs no adjustment. The author notes that it need not be quantified in the usual negative form (e.g., 'no one who...') because the meaning is clear. This lesson illustrates the interplay between logical form and natural language.

Quranic Conditional Syllogism on Tawhid
Author analyzes Quranic verse as a conditional syllogism proving oneness of God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the modus tollens argument from the Quranic verse 'Law kāna fīhimā ālihatun illā llāhu lafasadatā' and identify each component.

Core Idea
The Quran proves Tawhid using modus tollens: if many gods, corruption; no corruption; therefore no many gods.

In this lesson, we examine how classical Islamic logicians analysed a key Quranic verse as a perfect example of a conditional syllogism. The verse itself contains a conditional premise, and the negation of the consequent is so obvious that it is omitted. Understanding this argument will sharpen your ability to see logical structure in revealed texts and to use formal reasoning in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Locate the 'if...then' statement in the verse: 'If there had been gods in them besides Allah, they would have been corrupted.' This is the major premise (shartiyya muttasila). Note that it expresses a necessary connection (luzumiyya) because multiple gods logically imply corruption.
The antecedent (muqaddam) is 'there were gods in them besides Allah'; the consequent (tali) is 'they would have been corrupted.'
2
Find the negation of the consequent
The author states that the negation of the consequent is omitted because it is obvious: 'but they have not been corrupted.' This is the excepted premise (istithna'). In the real world, the heavens and earth are not corrupted, so the consequent is false.
The negation of the consequent (naqid al-tali) is the implicit second premise.
3
Derive the conclusion
Applying modus tollens: if the antecedent implies the consequent, and the consequent is false, then the antecedent must be false. So the conclusion is 'There were no gods in them besides Allah' — the negation of the antecedent, which directly refutes polytheism.
The conclusion (natija) is 'lam yakun fīhimā ālihatun illā llāh'.
4
Examine the necessary connection
The author explains why the antecedent necessitates the consequent: multiple gods would each act independently, leading to conflict and chaos, which would corrupt the universe. This is supported by additional verses (Al-Mu'minun 91, Al-Isra' 42) that elaborate the consequences.
The luzum (necessary connection) is not merely logical but also based on rational reflection on divine attributes.
5
Confirm the refutation
The conclusion (no gods besides Allah) contradicts the polytheists' claim (multiple gods). Since the proof is decisive, the false claim is invalidated, as indicated by the subsequent glorification (tasbih) in the verse.
The Qur'an says 'Subhan Allah' after the argument, confirming the refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran uses logical inference; recognizing it reveals deeper coherence.

This lesson examines a specific Quranic verse from Surah An-Nisa that the author treats as a hidden categorical syllogism. By following the author's analysis, you will learn to see how divine speech can convey arguments, not just statements. This skill is essential for anyone who wants to understand the Quran's reasoning style and to apply logical analysis to Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Locate the minor premise (sughra)
Find the statement in the verse that identifies the subject group. The author identifies 'then those are with the believers' as the minor premise.
This premise states that those who repent are included among the believers.
2
Recognize the entailment
Being 'with the believers' implies sharing in what they receive. The author explicitly says this entails partnership in reward.
This step is not a separate premise but a conceptual link.
3
Locate the major premise (kubra)
Find the universal statement about the reward. The verse says 'Allah will give the believers a great reward'.
This premise applies to all believers.
4
Derive the conclusion
Combine the two premises: if the repentant are with the believers, and the believers receive reward, then the repentant also receive reward.
The conclusion is not explicitly stated but logically follows.
5
Verify the syllogistic form
Check that this is a first-figure categorical syllogism (Barbara): All A are B (repentant are believers), All B are C (believers receive reward), therefore All A are C.
The author implicitly uses this valid form.

Juristic Analogy as First-Figure Syllogism
Author shows how qiyas al-tamthil reduces to first figure categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recast any juristic analogy (qiyas) into a first-figure categorical syllogism and evaluate whether its premises yield certainty.

Core Idea
Juristic analogy and categorical syllogism are structurally identical; only premise certainty differs.

Many students of logic and usul al-fiqh assume that juristic analogy (qiyas al-tamthil) is a weaker form of reasoning compared to the categorical syllogism. In this passage, the author argues that the two are actually the same logical form. By mapping the branch, root, and cause onto the minor, major, and middle terms, any valid analogy becomes a first-figure syllogism. Understanding this equivalence sharpens your ability to assess the strength of legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of the analogy
In any juristic analogy, there is a root case (asl) with an established ruling, a branch case (far') to which the ruling is extended, and a cause ('illa) shared by both. For example, in the text, root = wheat (burr), branch = corn (dhurra), cause = measurement (kayl) for Hanafis.
The ruling is the same: prohibition of riba.
2
Map the components to syllogistic terms
Make the branch the minor term (subject of the conclusion), the root or the ruling's predicate (e.g., 'prohibited in riba') the major term, and the cause the middle term. In the text: minor = corn, major = 'prohibited in riba', middle = 'measured thing'.
The major term is often the ruling attributed to the root.
3
Construct the first-figure syllogism
Form two premises: the minor premise asserts that the branch has the cause (e.g., 'Corn is a measured thing'), and the major premise asserts that everything with the cause has the ruling (e.g., 'Every measured thing is riba-prohibited'). The conclusion follows: 'Corn is riba-prohibited.' This is first figure because the middle term is subject of the major and predicate of the minor.
Check that the middle term appears exactly once in each premise.
4
Evaluate premise certainty to determine conclusion certainty
The author argues that the conclusion's certainty depends only on the premises. If both premises are certain (e.g., from Quran or consensus), the conclusion is certain. If they are probable, the conclusion is probable. The logical form is neutral.
This refutes the claim that analogy inherently yields only probability.

Reducing Qiyas Usuli to a Categorical Syllogism
Author shows reduction of qiyas usuli to first figure syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reformulate a qiyas usuli (analogical argument from usul al-fiqh) as a first-figure categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Every qiyas usuli is a categorical syllogism of the first figure.

The author uses the classic riba example of sesame seed and wheat to show that what jurists call qiyas usuli is logically identical to a first-figure categorical syllogism. By mapping the root (asl), branch (far'), and effective cause ('illa) to the major, minor, and middle terms, you can see the underlying syllogistic structure. This lesson will walk you through that transformation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of qiyas usuli
Find the root (asl), branch (far'), and effective cause ('illa) in the usuli argument. In this example: asl = wheat (burr), far' = sesame seed (dhurra), 'illa = measurability (kayl).
2
Map components to syllogistic terms
The branch (far') becomes the minor term (hadd asghar), the root (asl) becomes part of the major term (hadd akbar) after generalization, and the 'illa becomes the middle term (hadd awsat). Here: minor = sesame seed, major = 'riba-prohibited', middle = 'measurable'.
The major term is actually 'riba-prohibited', which is derived from the ruling on the root.
3
Construct the premises in order
Form the minor premise: 'Sesame seed is measurable.' Form the major premise: 'Everything measurable is riba-prohibited.' Note that the major premise is a universal affirmative (A) and the minor is also affirmative (A). This yields a Barbara syllogism.
Check that the middle term appears once in each premise.
4
Write the conclusion and verify figure
The conclusion is 'Sesame seed is riba-prohibited.' The middle term is subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor, which is the first figure. The mood is AAA (Barbara), which is valid.
This pattern holds for any qiyas usuli: the 'illa always serves as middle term.

Critique of the Tamthil/Shumul Distinction
Author critiques logicians' distinction between tamthil and shumul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author rejects the logicians' distinction between tamthil (analogy) and shumul (inclusive syllogism) and identify the true basis of certainty in an argument.

Core Idea
Certainty depends on premises, not on the form of reasoning.

Logicians commonly distinguish between tamthil (analogical reasoning) which they claim does not yield certainty, and shumul (inclusive syllogism) which they claim does. The author argues this distinction is a mistake because both forms reduce to the same categorical syllogism. What matters for certainty is the quality of the premises, not the label. This lesson will unpack that critique and its implications for Islamic sciences.

The author argues that both tamthil and shumul reduce to the same logical structure: 'مرجعهما في الحقيقة إلى واحد' (their reference is in reality one). This is supported by the previous example showing that a qiyas usuli (a type of tamthil) is a first-figure categorical syllogism (shumul).

The author asserts that whether a conclusion is certain depends solely on the premises: 'كون النتيجة قطعية أو غير قطعية راجع في كل منهما إلى المقدمات التي ركب منها الدليل' (whether the conclusion is certain or not depends in each of them on the premises from which the proof is composed).

Later scholars have debated whether the distinction is entirely without value, but the logical point stands.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Usuli qiyas is not independent logic; it reduces to the first-figure categorical syllogism.

A first-figure categorical syllogism has the form: A is B, B is C, therefore A is C. In logical terms: major premise (B is C), minor premise (A is B), conclusion (A is C).

Mu'arada fi al-Asl and the Multiplicity of Deduced Causes
Basis of mu'arada fi al-asl on multiplicity of deduced causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how mu'arada fi al-asl depends on the ruling on ta'addud al-'ilal (multiplicity of deduced causes) and identify the two positions on this issue.

Core Idea
Mu'arada fi al-asl only refutes the opponent if multiple deduced causes are prohibited.

The author deepens the discussion by revealing that mu'arada fi al-asl is not always effective—it depends on a deeper principle: whether multiple deduced causes (al-'ilal al-mustanbata) are permitted. This principle is itself disputed among usul scholars, so the validity of this type of mu'arada varies by school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key principle
The author states that mu'arada fi al-asl is 'based on the view that prohibits multiplicity of deduced causes.' Find this sentence and note that it connects the form of argument to a substantive usul rule.
2
Understand the two positions on ta'addud al-'ilal
If multiple deduced causes are permitted, then both the arguer's and objector's causes could be valid, so the objection does not disprove the original. If prohibited, the objector's alternative cause can refute the arguer's claim that his cause is the only valid one.
3
Apply to the example
In the riba example, if multiple causes are allowed, Shafi'i's ta'm and Hanbali's kayl could both be causes, so the objection does not defeat Shafi'i's proof. If multiple causes are prohibited, then only one cause is correct, and the objection challenges Shafi'i's claim.
4
Recognize the scholarly disagreement
The author explicitly says scholars differ on accepting this type of mu'arada because they differ on ta'addud. This shows how logical tools are embedded in substantive legal theory.

Commentary
This passage defines mu'arada (counter-argument) when it is not via qalb (reversal). The author divides it into two types: mu'arada fi al-asl (counter-argument in the original) and mu'arada fi al-far' (counter-argument in the branch). Some usul scholars also call the latter 'al-farq bayna al-asl wa al-far' (distinction between original and branch). The general definition of mu'arada is: presenting a proof contrary to what the opponent has proven. This applies to all types of proofs, not just qiyas. However, the specific division into two types applies only to qiyas. Mu'arada fi al-asl occurs when the objector proposes another attribute that could serve as the effective cause (jilla). The author gives the example: al-Shafi'i says the cause of riba in wheat is ta'm (edibility), so the Hanafi and Hanbali counter by proposing kayl (measured quantity) as an alternative cause. The author then notes that this type of mu'arada depends on the position that prohibits multiple deduced causes (al-'ilal al-mustanbata). If multiple causes are permitted, then both the arguer's and the objector's causes could be valid, and the objection would not necessarily refute the original argument. Because scholars differ on the permissibility of multiple causes, they also differ on whether this type of objection is accepted.

Three Types of Mu'arada with a Riba Example
Author distinguishes mu'arada bil qalb, bi al-mithl, and bi al-ghayr with a riba syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of mu'arada (qalb, mithl, ghayr) in a jadal exchange and analyze the underlying categorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Counter-objection is not just reversal—similarity and difference also overturn arguments.

In jadal (dialectical debate), a mu'arada is a counter-objection that uses the opponent's own reasoning to refute them. The author here distinguishes three types: reversal (qalb), similarity (mithl), and difference (ghayr). He then provides a concrete example from the prohibition of riba, where two categorical syllogisms clash over the effective cause ('illa). This lesson will walk you through the distinction and the syllogistic structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of mu'arada
The text defines three types: mu'arada bil qalb (objection by reversal), mu'arada fi al-asl (objection in the original), and mu'arada fi al-far' (objection in the branch). The latter two correspond to mu'arada bil mithl (by similarity) and mu'arada bil ghayr (by a different attribute). Note that only the first type was previously explained; here the author extends the framework.
Mu'arada bil qalb uses the opponent's premises to derive the opposite conclusion. Mu'arada bil mithl presents a parallel case with a shared attribute. Mu'arada bil ghayr uses a different attribute.
2
Extract the first syllogism (proponent)
The proponent argues: 'This is measured by volume (kail). Everything measured by volume is subject to riba, because the cause of riba is kail. Therefore, this is subject to riba.' Identify the middle term (kail) and the conclusion (riba ruling). Note the implied principle that only one cause is assumed (preventing multiplicity of causes).
This is a categorical syllogism with major premise 'Every kail is riba', minor 'This is kail', conclusion 'This is riba'.
3
Extract the second syllogism (Shafi'i objector)
The Shafi'i objects by similarity (mu'arada bil mithl): 'Apples are food (mattu'm). All food is subject to riba (even if not measured by volume). Therefore, apples are subject to riba.' Here the middle term changes to 'mattu'm' and the cause shifts from kail to food. Note that this syllogism also assumes a single cause (mattu'm) and produces a conclusion that contradicts the first.
The objector uses a different attribute to produce a conflicting ruling, demonstrating how similarity works.
4
Analyze the contradiction
The first proof implies: riba applies to kail but not to food that is not measured by volume. The second proof implies: riba applies to all food but not to non-food items measured by volume. The two conclusions are incompatible. The author shows that each proof is a valid categorical syllogism, but they cannot both be true given the shared assumption of a single cause.
This contradiction is the essence of mu'arada: the objector forces the proponent to either reject one premise or abandon the assumption of a single cause.
5
Classify the type of mu'arada in the example
Since the Shafi'i uses a parallel attribute (mattu'm) that is similar in function (measurable food items), this is mu'arada bil mithl. If he had used a completely different attribute (e.g., weight), it would be mu'arada bil ghayr. The text ends before completing the third type, but the framework is clear.
The example illustrates how the same logical structure (categorical syllogism) can generate opposing rulings when different 'illas are invoked.

Refuting the Theologians' Conditional Syllogism on Allah's Istiwa'
Author presents and refutes the mutakallimun's conditional syllogism denying Allah's istiwa'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional syllogism (modus tollens) used by the mutakallimun to deny Allah's istiwa', and critique its unsoundness by identifying its false major premise.

Core Idea
Theologians' valid syllogism fails because its major premise falsely equates divine and created attributes.

The mutakallimun (theologians) sought to defend Allah's transcendence (tanzih) by denying the attribute of istiwa' (rising over the Throne), using a conditional syllogism. Their argument is logically valid in form, but the author shows it rests on a false premise that contradicts the Qur'an and sound reasoning. In this lesson, we will dissect their syllogism and understand the author's refutation.

Guided Path
1
Extract the conditional syllogism
Find the conditional statement: 'If He were rising over the Throne, He would resemble created beings.' Then the negation: 'He does not resemble created beings.' And the conclusion: 'Therefore, He is not rising over the Throne.'
The text gives this explicitly in the Arabic.
2
Identify the logical form
This is modus tollens: If A then B; not B; therefore not A. Is this form valid? Yes, it is a valid inference regardless of content.
Validity concerns form only, not truth of premises.
3
Locate the major premise and its assumption
The major premise is 'If He were rising over the Throne, He would resemble created beings.' This assumes that istiwa' on a created object necessarily implies resemblance to creatures. Identify why this assumption is questionable.
The author will dispute this equation.
4
Understand the author's refutation
The author declares the conclusion a slander because it contradicts seven Qur'anic verses. He promises to elaborate on the falsity of the major premise from multiple aspects. For now, note that the syllogism is unsound despite being valid.
The refutation focuses on the false premise, not on flawed logic.
5
Apply the lesson to other attributes
The passage states that all divine attributes are of the same category; refuting this argument for istiwa' refutes it for all attributes. Consider how the same logical fallacy could be applied to other attributes like Allah's seeing or hearing.
This shows the broader relevance of the critique.

This passage is a critical refutation of the theologians' (mutakallimun) logical argument against Allah's attribute of istiwa' (rising over the Throne). Refuting the Conditional Syllogism on Allah's Istiwa'
Author refutes the mutakallimun's conditional syllogism denying Allah's istiwa'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a conditional syllogism and refute it by challenging the connection (rabṭ) between antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism is only as strong as the connection between its parts.

The mutakallimun attempted to deny Allah's attribute of istiwa' using a conditional syllogism. In this lesson, we will dissect their argument and see how the author refutes it by pointing out the invalid connection (rabṭ) between the antecedent and consequent. Understanding this refutation will equip you with a powerful tool for evaluating conditional arguments in theology and beyond.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conditional Major Premise
Find the conditional statement that forms the major premise: 'If He were rising over the throne, He would be similar to created beings.'
This is a shartiyya muttasila.
2
Identify the Minor Premise and Conclusion Form
The minor premise is 'But He is not similar to created beings' (negation of consequent). This yields modus tollens: if P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
The form is valid, but soundness depends on the major premise.
3

Learning Objective

Refuting a Syllogism by Denying the Major Premise

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Denying the major premise is a legitimate jadal move when the premise is false in its universality.

In this passage, an opponent presents a categorical syllogism: Allah is established on the Throne; everyone established on a created thing resembles the created; therefore Allah resembles the created. The author does not deny the minor premise but instead denies the major premise's universality. This lesson teaches you how to identify and execute this dialectical response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's categorical syllogism
Find the two premises and the conclusion. The opponent's argument: Major premise: 'Everyone who is established on a created thing is similar to the created.' Minor premise: 'Allah is established on the Throne (a created thing).' Conclusion: 'Thus Allah is similar to the created.'
The syllogism is in the first figure (Barbara), but the author attacks the major premise, not the form.
2
Locate the author's response (man')
The author explicitly says 'al-jawab 'an hadha bi al-man'' (the answer to this is by denial). He denies the truth of the major premise, not the minor.
Man' is a standard jadal move; it does not concede the premise but challenges its truth.
3
Understand why the major premise is false
The author argues that istiwa' over creation is of two types: one that entails similarity (created istiwa') and one that does not (Creator's istiwa'). Therefore, the universal claim 'every istiwa' entails similarity' is false.
The reasoning relies on a real distinction (qismayn) within the subject term.
4
Recognize the dialectical force
By denying the major premise, the author preserves the truth of the minor premise (which is from the Qur'an) and shows the conclusion does not follow, thus refuting the opponent without compromising divine attributes.
This preserves the integrity of the revealed text while engaging rationally.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of an opponent's argument against the divine attribute of istiwa' (Allah's establishing Himself over the Throne). The opponent uses a categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani) to claim that if Allah is established on the Throne, He would resemble creation, which is impossible. Core Idea
A universal claim is false if the predicate is more specific than the subject; only a particular judgment holds.

The author further analyzes the major premise 'Everyone who is established on a created thing is similar to the created' and shows it is only true as a particular ('some'). This is because the predicate 'similar to the created' is more specific (akhas) than the subject 'established on a created thing,' so a universal judgment is invalid. Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition and its quantifier
The opponent's major premise is a universal affirmative (qadiyya kulliyya mujiba): 'Every A is B' where A = 'established on a created thing' and B = 'similar to the created'.
The quantifier 'every' (kull) makes it universal.
2
Analyze the relationship between subject and predicate
The author states that the predicate (being similar to the created) is more specific (akhas) than the subject (istiwa' over a created thing). This means some instances of A are not B.
The specificity relationship is key: the predicate applies to a subset of the subject.
3

The author compares the false universal to a false modalized proposition.

Refuting a syllogism about Allah's istiwa by denying the major premise
Author refutes a categorical syllogism about divine istiwa by denying the major premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute a categorical syllogism about divine attributes by denying a false universal premise and verifying the truth of a premise using revealed evidence.

Core Idea
A false conclusion does not always mean the minor premise is false; the major premise may be the source.

In this passage, the author responds to a syllogism used to argue that Allah's istiwa (establishment over the Throne) implies resemblance to creation. The opponent claims the conclusion is disbelief, so the minor premise ('He is established over the Throne') must be false. The author refutes this by denying the major premise instead, showing it is not universally true. You will learn how to analyse such arguments and apply the logical tool of premise denial (al-man') in theological debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism's components
Locate the minor premise (al-ṣughrā): 'He is established over the Throne.' The major premise (al-kubrā): 'Everything established over a created thing resembles the created thing.' The conclusion: 'He resembles the created thing.'
The opponent asserts the conclusion is false (kufr), so at least one premise must be false.
2
Examine which premise the opponent attacks
The opponent claims the minor premise is false because the conclusion is false. They consider the major premise true. Note their reasoning: if the conclusion is false, the minor must be false (assuming major true).
This is a common error: assuming the wrong premise is the source.
3
See how the author denies (al-manʿ) the major premise
The author argues the major premise is false because it is a universal affirmative ('all'). He shows it is only true as a particular ('some'), since Allah's istiwa does not entail resemblance. He provides evidence: the predicate ('resembles') is more specific than the subject ('established'), so universal quantification fails.
The author uses logical analysis of quantifiers (sūr) to expose the error.
4
Understand the proof for the minor premise's truth
The author cites Quranic verses (seven instances) where Allah affirms His istiwa. Since Allah is most truthful, the minor premise must be true. Therefore, the falsehood lies in the major premise.
Revealed evidence (naql) is used to confirm a premise, showing the integration of logic and scripture.
5
Generalise the refutation method
The author concludes that any conjunctive syllogism leading to disbelief must have a false premise. By identifying the false premise (here the major), the argument is refuted. Practice this by evaluating similar syllogisms on other attributes.
This method is part of jadal (dialectical debate) and is used in kalam to defend orthodox positions.

Refuting a Categorical Syllogism Through Counter-Syllogism
Author exposes false premise in syllogism and demonstrates counter-syllogism as refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a counter-syllogism (mu'arada bi al-mithl) from the first figure to invalidate a false categorical syllogism about divine attributes.

Core Idea
A false syllogism about Allah is best refuted by a true syllogism from the same figure.

In this passage, the author addresses a common tactic used by opponents of divine attributes: they construct a categorical syllogism to deny an established attribute like al-istiwa (establishment over the throne). The author first demonstrates that the minor premise of their syllogism is actually true, as it is affirmed in the Qur'an, and the false premise is the major. He then introduces the method of mu'arada bi al-mithl – countering by the like – which involves building a parallel first-figure syllogism with premises drawn from revelation to produce a conclusion that contradicts the opponent's. This is a powerful tool in Islamic dialectics for defending orthodox theology against sophistical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's syllogism
Find the two premises and conclusion in the opponent's argument about istiwa. They claim: 'Establishment over the throne necessitates resemblance to creation; everything that necessitates resemblance is rejected; therefore, establishment over the throne is rejected.' Recognize the minor premise (istiwa necessitates resemblance) and the major premise (everything necessitating resemblance is rejected).
The opponent's conclusion is meant to deny the attribute of istiwa.
2
Check the truth of each premise against revelation
Verify each premise with the Qur'an. The minor premise that Allah established (istiwa) is affirmed in seven verses, so it is true. The major premise that 'everything necessitating resemblance is rejected' is false because Allah's istiwa is described in a way befitting His majesty, and resemblance to created beings is not necessitated.
The author cites Qur'an 4:87 and 4:122 to assert that Allah's statements are the most truthful.
3
Construct a counter-syllogism from the first figure
Build a new syllogism with two true premises derived from revelation: 'Allah's establishment over His throne He affirmed in His Book' (minor) and 'Everything Allah affirmed in His Book is true without doubt' (major). The conclusion: 'Allah's establishment over His throne is true without doubt.' Ensure the syllogism uses the first figure (subject-predicate, middle term as subject of major and predicate of minor).
The counter-syllogism must be formally valid and have evident premises.
4
Verify the form and content
Check that the counter-syllogism is in the first figure (e.g., Barbara mood: all M are P, all S are M, therefore all S are P). Confirm that both premises are indisputably true from revelation and that the conclusion directly contradicts the opponent's conclusion.
The author's example: 'Allah's establishment is affirmed in the Book; everything affirmed in the Book is true; therefore Allah's establishment is true.'
5
Apply the method uniformly
Recognize that this technique of countering by the like is uniform for all such arguments: any syllogism that denies a revealed attribute can be refuted by constructing a parallel first-figure syllogism with premises from the Qur'an.
The author states this method is 'mutarid fi jami' alladhi nafaw biha ba'd sifat Allah' (uniform in all those by which they denied attributes).

Reduction to the First Figure and Compound Syllogism
List of topics on reduction and compound syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reduce any valid categorical syllogism to the first figure using conversion and distinguish between simple and compound syllogisms.

Core Idea
All valid syllogisms reduce to the first figure, which is the most natural and self-evident form.

The outline mentions reduction of the second, third, and fourth figures to the first through conversion, and then discusses simple vs. compound syllogisms. This lesson focuses on those reduction techniques and the distinction between simple and compound syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Understand reduction via simple conversion
The second figure reduces to the first by converting its major premise (simple conversion). For example, 'All A are B; no C is B; so no C is A' becomes 'All A are B; no B is C; so no A is C' after conversion.
Simple conversion (العكس المستوي) swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth.
2
Apply reduction to the third and fourth figures
The third figure reduces to the first by converting its minor premise. The fourth figure reduces by converting one premise or reordering. The outline notes that the third figure returns mostly to the first via conversion of the minor.
3
Distinguish simple vs. compound syllogism
Simple syllogism (قياس بسيط) has exactly two premises and a conclusion; compound syllogism (قياس مركب) has more than two premises or a chain of syllogisms. The outline mentions 'متصل النتائج' (connected conclusions) and 'مفصول النتائج' (separated conclusions).
Compound syllogism are often used in extended arguments.
4
Connect to theological arguments
Theological proofs (e.g., the argument for the existence of God in al-Sanusiyya) often use compound syllogisms where intermediate conclusions serve as premises for subsequent steps.

Conditional Syllogism (al-Qiyās al-Istithnā'ī)
List of topics on conditional syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a conjunctive conditional syllogism and determine which moods are productive.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms combine a conditional premise with an exceptive premise; only modus ponens and modus tollens yield necessary conclusions.

The outline introduces conditional syllogism, focusing on the conjunctive type (muttaṣil). It lists productive and sterile moods and mentions al-Farabi's view on the role of the premises. This lesson unpacks those concepts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish conditional from categorical syllogism
In conditional syllogism, one premise is a conditional (e.g., 'If A then B') and the other is an exceptive categorical (e.g., 'A is the case' or 'B is not the case'). The conclusion follows from the truth of the conditional and the exceptive.
Contrast with categorical syllogism where all premises are categorical.
2
Identify the two productive moods of the conjunctive conditional
The outline lists 'الضربان المنتجان من الشرطي المتصل' (the two productive moods of the conjunctive conditional): modus ponens (affirming the antecedent) and modus tollens (denying the consequent). For example: 'If it rains, the ground is wet; it rains; so the ground is wet.'
The two sterile moods are affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent.
3
Understand the role of the particle of exception
The particle 'إلا' or 'لكن' often introduces the exceptive premise. Al-Farabi states that the major premise is the conditional and the minor is the exceptive categorical.
This clarifies the structure: major premise = if...then..., minor premise = the exception.
4
Apply to Islamic legal reasoning
Conditional syllogisms appear in fiqh: 'If the water is impure, then it cannot be used for ablution; this water is impure; therefore it cannot be used.' This is a valid modus ponens.

Conjunctive and Disjunctive Conditional Syllogisms
Headings outlining the rules and subdivisions of conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive and disjunctive conditional syllogisms and identify their subtypes.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms rest on an exceptive premise, either conjunctive (if-then) or disjunctive (either-or).

This lesson introduces the types of conditional syllogisms (al-qiyas al-istithna'i). After covering categorical syllogisms, the text turns to arguments that use conditional premises. You will learn about conjunctive (muttasil) and disjunctive (munfasil) conditionals, and within disjunctives, three varieties based on the type of exclusive disjunction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional major premise
A conditional syllogism has a major premise that is either conditional (if...then) or disjunctive (either...or). The minor premise is an exceptive (istithna') that affirms or denies part of the major.
2
Classify as conjunctive or disjunctive
If the major is 'if A then B', it is conjunctive. If it is 'either A or B', it is disjunctive. Note that disjunctives have three subtypes: exclusive of conjunction, exclusive of emptiness, exclusive of both.
3
Recognize the productive moods
For conjunctive conditionals, there are two productive moods: affirming the antecedent (modus ponens) and denying the consequent (modus tollens). For disjunctive, the productive moods depend on the subtype.
