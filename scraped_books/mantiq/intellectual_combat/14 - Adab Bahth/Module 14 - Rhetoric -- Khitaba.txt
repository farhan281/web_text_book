Arabic Rhetorical Conditionals vs Logical Conditionals
Author notes that Arabic has expressions resembling conditionals that are actually rhetorical figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a genuine logical conditional from a rhetorical conditional in Arabic.

Core Idea
Not every 'if...then' in Arabic is a logical implication; some are rhetorical figures with false connection.

In Arabic, we often encounter sentences that look like logical conditionals but are actually rhetorical flourishes. The author warns that these should not be treated as logical propositions. This lesson teaches you to identify when a conditional in Arabic is a genuine logical implication and when it is a rhetorical figure (badī' ma'nawī).

Guided Path
1
Identify the surface form
Look for conditional particles like 'in' or 'law' that introduce a conditional structure.
Not all such forms are logical; treat them as candidates.
2
Check the truth of the connection
Determine if the consequent actually depends on the antecedent. In rhetorical conditionals, the connection is false (rabt kādhib).
A false connection means the antecedent could be true while the consequent is false, or the implication is not meant to be taken literally.
3
Consider the context and purpose
Rhetorical conditionals often serve a literary, persuasive, or aesthetic purpose, not a logical one.
For example, a poet may use a conditional to express a vivid image.
4
Apply the logical test
A genuine logical conditional must have a real implication; if you can imagine a scenario where the antecedent is true and the consequent false, it is not a logical conditional.
This test helps separate rhetoric from logic.

Commentary
This passage issues a caution: one may encounter Arabic utterances that have the grammatical form of a connected conditional (i.e., with 'law' or 'in') but whose logical connection (الربط) between antecedent and consequent is actually false (kadhib). Such expressions are considered part of Arabic badī' (rhetorical figures), specifically 'conceptual rhetorical figures' (al-badī' al-ma'nawī). The author warns that these should not be treated as logical conditionals; they are valid in Arabic due to some other linguistic subtlety (sirr min asrār al-'Arabiyya) that does not apply in the science of logic. Thus, the logician must be careful not to mistake a poetic or rhetorical conditional for a genuine logical implication.
