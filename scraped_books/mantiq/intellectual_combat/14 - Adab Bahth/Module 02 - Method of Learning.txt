The Permissibility of Purified Logic
Author defends the study of logic, citing scholars who purified it from philosophy and quoting a poem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between prohibited philosophical logic and permissible purified logic, and explain the basis for the difference of opinion among scholars.

Core Idea
The prohibition of logic applies only to philosophical logic, not to the logic purified by Islamic scholars.

This passage addresses a long-standing debate: Is studying logic permissible for Muslims? The author, writing a textbook on Adab al-Bahth, defends his inclusion of logic by arguing that the logic he presents has been purified from philosophical errors. He cites a poem that clarifies that earlier prohibitions targeted the works of philosophers, not the logical method itself when cleansed. Let’s break down his argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
The author states that the logical principles in his memorandum are 'free from the impurities of philosophical doubts' and are from what 'the scholars of Islam purified.' This sets up the central distinction.
2
Extract the opposing view
The poem mentions that al-Nawawi, Ibn al-Salah, and al-Suyuti forbade logic. Note that these are major scholars, so their prohibition must be addressed.
The author does not reject these scholars but reinterprets their ruling.
3
Analyze the poem's resolution
The poem says: 'The place of those opposing statements is what the philosophers authored. As for what those who submitted purified, it must be known.' This resolves the conflict by narrowing the prohibition to philosophical works.
The term 'aslama' (submitted) refers to becoming Muslim, i.e., Islamized.
4
Assess the author's position
The author aligns with the view that purified logic is not only permissible but necessary ('must be known'). He implies that the study of logic for Adab al-Bahth is legitimate.
This is a critical refinement of the earlier absolute prohibition.

The Subject of Adab al-Bahth: Universal and Particular Investigations
Author defines the subject of adab al-bahth and its universal-particular classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between universal and particular investigations in adab al-bahth and list the three universal categories.

Core Idea
The subject of debate is not single proofs but the universal operations that govern them.

In this passage, the author defines the subject matter of adab al-bahth. He divides it into universal investigations — the broad categories of logical operations in debate — and particular investigations, which are specific applications. Understanding this distinction is key to mastering the art of debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of the subject
Find where the author defines 'mawdu'' of this art. It is 'al-abhath al-kulliyyah allati tandariju tahtaha abhath juz'iyyah' – the universal investigations under which particular investigations fall.
The subject is not the particular arguments themselves, but the general types.
2
Extract the three universal investigations
The author lists: al-munaqadah (contradiction), al-naqd (refutation), and al-mu'aradah (opposition). These are the universal categories.
Each is a general logical operation in debate.
3
Understand particular investigations
Particular investigations are specific instances of these universals, e.g., opposing a specific proof or refuting a specific argument.
They are subsumed under the universals.
4
Recognize the perspective of acceptability
The subject considers these investigations 'min haythu hiya muwajjahah maqbūlah aw ghayr maqbūlah' – as to whether they are directed and acceptable or not.
The art judges the validity of objections.

Commentary
This passage defines the subject (mawdu') of the art of adab al-bahth. The subject consists of universal investigations (al-abhath al-kulliyyah) that encompass particular investigations (abhath juz'iyyah). The universal investigations are three: contradiction (al-munaqadah), refutation (al-naqd), and opposition (al-mu'aradah) at the universal level. The particular investigations are applications of these, such as opposing a specific proof or refuting a specific argument. The art of debate determines whether these are acceptable or not. The author gives two examples: any refutation that shows that a proof fails to produce its conclusion (naqd bi al-takhalluf) or that the proof entails falsity (istilzam al-fasad) is an accepted function (wazifah maqbūlah). On the other hand, invalidating a premise before it has been proven (ifsad al-muqaddimah qabla ithbatiha), even while providing evidence for its invalidation, is considered usurpation (ghasb) and is unacceptable. The author then reminds the reader that the subject of any science is defined as that about which essential accidents are investigated, citing examples: medicine studies the human body, arithmetic studies number. Thus, the subject of adab al-bahth is the universal investigations and their particular applications, considered from the perspective of acceptability.

Acceptable and Unacceptable Objections in Debate
Author specifies acceptable and unacceptable objection types in debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between acceptable refutations (naqd bi al-takhalluf and istilzam al-fasad) and unacceptable usurpation (ghasb) in debate.

Core Idea
Not all refutations are legitimate; only those that demonstrate logical failure are accepted.

The author also gives criteria for acceptable objections. He distinguishes two valid refutation methods — showing the conclusion does not follow (naqd bi al-takhalluf) and showing the argument entails absurdity (istilzam al-fasad) — from the unacceptable practice of attacking an unproven premise without establishing it first. This distinction is crucial for fair debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two accepted refutation methods
The passage mentions 'kull naqd bi al-takhalluf aw istilzam al-fasad' – every refutation by way of non-fulfillment or entailment of corruption is an accepted function.
These are the only two methods explicitly endorsed.
2
Understand what each method aims to show
Naqd bi al-takhalluf: showing that the conclusion does not logically follow from the premises. Istilzam al-fasad: showing that the argument leads to a false or absurd consequence.
Both attack the logical structure, not the premise's truth.
3
Recognize the unacceptable method
The passage states 'kull ifsad li al-muqaddimah qabla ithbatiha ma'a iqamat dalil al-ifsad fahuwa ghasb ghayr maqbul' – invalidating a premise before it is established, even with evidence, is usurpation and unacceptable.
The issue is timing: the premise must be established before it can be attacked.
4
Note the qualification
The phrase 'ma'a iqamat dalil al-ifsad' indicates that even if you provide proof for the invalidation, it is still unacceptable because the premise was not yet established.
This prevents premature objections that disrupt the flow of proof.

Classification of Speech and Its Relevance to Debate
Author divides kalam into mufrad and murakkab and explains conditions for munazara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any expression as singular or compound and determine whether munazara (dialectical debate) applies to it according to the rules of Adab al-Bahth.

Core Idea
Debate requires complete propositions; singular terms allow only inquiry, and performative compounds are debatable only when reported.

In this lesson, we examine how the Adab al-Bahth tradition classifies expressions (kalam) into singular and compound, and how this classification determines the applicability of dialectical debate (munazara). Understanding this taxonomy is essential for knowing when and how a debate can properly commence, especially when dealing with performative statements like commands in hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
In the Arabic text, locate the statement 'الكلام ينقسم إلى مفرد ومركب'. Distinguish between singular (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). Singular terms indicate a single meaning without composition.
The author references an earlier logical introduction for the full explanation.
2
Determine the scope of munazara for singulars
Note that the author explicitly says 'والمفرد لا تجري فيه المناظرة'. Singular terms do not admit debate. However, if the term is unfamiliar (غريب), one may seek clarification (الاستفسار).
Debate is about truth-claims; singular terms are not propositions.
3
Examine the incomplete compound
The incomplete compound (المركب الناقص) like a qualifying phrase (التقييدي) admits debate only if it is a qualifier (قيد) of a proposition. Example: 'الحيوان الناطق' qualifies 'animal'.
The debate is about the qualification itself when attached to a proposition.
4
Analyze the complete compound: performative vs. declarative
The author distinguishes the performative compound (المركب الإنشائي) from the declarative. Performative includes command, prohibition, etc. If uttered by the speaker himself, no debate. If reported from another, the narrator must verify the transmission.
This distinction is crucial for hadith sciences.
5
Evaluate the author's rebuttal of the opposing view
Some claimed performative compounds are never subjects of research. The author counters by pointing to prophetic hadiths of performative nature that require verification from the narrator. This shows performative compounds can be debated after transmission verification.
The author's view is called 'الأظهر' (the more apparent).

Commentary

Why Definitions Are Debatable: Implicit Propositions in Ta'rif
Author explains that definitions imply propositions, enabling debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the complete declarative propositions implied by a definition in an Islamic text and determine which of the five standard claims are being contested in a debate.

Core Idea
A definition's surface is a term; its depth is a set of debatable propositions.

You might think that a definition like 'man is a rational animal' is just a compound term, not something you can debate. But the scholars of munazara show that every definition silently asserts several claims: that it is a proper definition (hadd), that it uses essential attributes, that it excludes non-members and includes all members, and that it avoids logical absurdities. This lesson unpacks those hidden propositions and shows how they make definitions a proper subject of debate.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the surface form of a definition
Look at a definition like 'al-insan hayawan natiq'. It is a qualifying compound (murakkab taqyidi) that functions like a singular term. The author notes that it appears to be a singular expression, not a full proposition.
The surface form is deceptive: it seems atomic but is actually a bundle of claims.
2
Identify the first implicit claim about definition type
The definition implies it is a 'hadd' (essential definition) not a 'rasm' (accidental description). This is the first debatable proposition: is it a real definition?
The author lists this as the first claim: 'that this definition is a hadd not rasm'.
3
Identify the claim about essential vs accidental properties
The definition claims its components are essential (dhatiyyat), not accidental (aradiyyat). This is the second implicit proposition.
One could challenge whether 'rational' is indeed essential to humanity.
4
Identify the claims of jami' and mani'
The definition implies it is comprehensive (jami') — covering all humans — and exclusive (mani') — preventing non-humans from entering. These are the third and fourth claims.
These are standard conditions for a valid definition in Islamic logic.
5
Identify the claim of freedom from absurdity
The definition implies it does not involve circular definition (dawr sabqi) or infinite regress (tasalsul). This is the fifth implicit proposition.
A definition that uses the defined term implicitly would be circular.

Responding to Claims: Badihi, Nazari, and the Three Objections
Procedural guide for responding to claims in dialectic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of responding to a tasdiq claim in a munazara by classifying the claim's epistemic type (badihi jali, badihi khafi, nazari) and determining the appropriate response (accept, deny+request proof, or raise one of the three objections).

Core Idea
The respondent's first move is to classify the claim's epistemic type.

In a munazara, when an opponent presents a tasdiq claim, the respondent must follow a systematic procedure. This passage outlines that procedure: first, determine whether the claim is badihi (self-evident) or nazari (theoretical). If badihi, is it clear (jali) or hidden (khafi)? Based on this classification and whether the opponent has provided proof or reminder, the respondent either accepts, denies, or raises one of three objections. This lesson walks you through this decision tree, which is essential for structured debate.

Guided Path
1
Classify the claim as badihi or nazari
Read the claim and decide: Is it self-evident (badihi) or does it require proof (nazari)? If the claim is obviously true to any rational person, it is badihi. Otherwise, it is nazari.
Badihi claims are further divided into jali (clear) and khafi (hidden). A badihi jali is immediately obvious, while a badihi khafi may need a reminder (tanbih) to become clear.
2
If badihi, check if jali or khafi
If the claim is badihi, determine its clarity. If it is jali (e.g., 'The whole is greater than the part'), it must be accepted immediately. If it is khafi (e.g., 'The world is temporally originated' might be considered badihi by some but requires a reminder), proceed to check for a reminder.
A badihi khafi claim is self-evident but not immediately apparent; the opponent must provide a reminder (tanbih) to make it clear.
3
Check for proof (dalil) or reminder (tanbih)
If the claim is nazari, check if the opponent has provided a proof. If it is badihi khafi, check if a reminder has been given. If no proof or reminder is provided, the respondent's only action is to deny the claim (man' al-da'wa) and request the missing evidence.
The opponent must then supply the proof (for nazari) or reminder (for badihi khafi).
4
Choose the respondent's action
Based on the classification and evidence status, select the response: (a) if badihi jali, accept without objection; (b) if nazari without proof or badihi khafi without reminder, deny and demand evidence; (c) if nazari with proof or badihi khafi with reminder, the respondent may raise one of three objections: man' (prevention, which includes munaqada and naqd tafsili), naqd (critique, i.e., naqd ijmaili), or mu'arada (counterargument).
The three objections were detailed earlier in the text; this lesson focuses on when to use them.
5
If raising an objection, identify which type
Understand the distinction: man' attacks the claim or its proof by pointing out a contradiction or a detailed flaw; naqd (ijmail) is a general critique undermining the proof; mu'arada presents an alternative proof conflicting with the opponent's claim. The opponent must then respond to the objection.
This step assumes familiarity with the definitions of man', naqd, and mu'arada from previous lessons.

كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ ووصف بعض المخلوقين بالقدرة فقال : ﴿ إِلَّا الَّذِينَ تَابُوا مِن قَبْلِ أَن تَقْدِرُوا عَلَيْهِمْ ﴾ [المائدة ] ، ووصف نفسه بالسمع والبصر فقال إن اللهَ سَمِيعٌ بَصِيرٌ ﴾ [الحج / ٧٥] ووصف بعض المخلوقين بالسمع والبصر في قوله : (إِنَّا خَلَقْنَا الإِنسَنَ مِن نُّطْفَةٍ أَمَشَاحِ نَبْتَلِيهِ فَجَعَلْتَهُ سَمِيعًا بَصِيرًا ﴾ [الإنسان ٢] ووصف نفسه بالحياة فقال : اللهُ لَا إِلَهَ إلَّا هُوَ الْحَيُّ الْقَيُّومُ ﴾ الآية [البقرة/ ٢٥٥] وَتَوَكَّلْ عَلَى الْحَيِّ الَّذِي لَا يَمُوتُ [الفرقان ٥٨]، ووصف بعض خلقه بالحياة فقال : يُخْرِجُ الْحَيَّ مِنَ الْمَيِّتِ وَيُخْرِجُ الْمَيِّتَ مِنَ الْحَيّ ﴾ [الروم/ ١٩] وَجَعَلْنَا مِنَ الْمَاءِ كُلَّ شَيْءٍ حَيَّ وَسَلَامٌ عَلَيْهِ يَوْمَ وُلِدَ وَيَوْمَ يَمُوتُ وَيَوْمَ يُبْعَثُ حَيًّا ﴾ [مريم/ ١٥] وأمثال هذا كثيرة جدا في القرآن كما أوضحناه في غير هذا الموضع، ووصف نفسه بالاستواء على العرش في سبع آيات من كتابه، ووصف بعض خلقه بالاستواء أيضًا كقوله : لِتَسْتَوُوا عَلَى ظُهُورِهِ، ثُمَّ تَذْكُرُوا نِعْمَةَ رَبِّكُمْ إِذَا اسْتَوَيْتُمْ عَلَيْهِ ﴾ [الزخرف/ ۱۳] وكقوله: ﴿ فَإِذَا اسْتَوَيْتَ أَنتَ وَمَن مَّعَكَ عَلَى الْفُلْكِ الآية [المؤمنون/ ۲۸] وقوله: ﴿ وَاسْتَوَتْ عَلَى الْجُودِي ﴾ [هود/ ٤٤]، فاستواؤه وقدرته وسمعه وبصره وحياته وسائر صفاته منزهة عن مشابهة صفات المخلوقين، واستواء المخلوقين وسمعهم وبصرهم وقدرتهم وإرادتهم كل ذلك مناسب لحالهم وبين صفاته ـ تعالى ـ وصفاتهم في الجميع كالفرق الذي بين ذاته وذواتهم، فافهم ما ذكرنا وتمسك بنور الوحي الذي أوضحنا ؛ فالسلامة محققة في اتباع الوحي، وليست محققةً في شيء غيره، ولم يضمن الله لإنسان أن يكون غير ضال في الدنيا ولا شقي في الآخرة إلا متبع هداه الذي هو القرآن، كما في قوله - تعالى -: ﴿ فَمَنِ اتَّبَعَ هُدَايَ فَلَا يَضِلُّ وَلَا يَشْقَى [طه/ ۱۲۳] .
قد علمت مما ذكرنا أن مذهب السلف مبني على ثلاثة أسس : الأول منها تنزيه الله - جل وعلا ـ عن مشابهة خلقه في ذواتهم أو صفاتهم أو أفعالهم . والثاني : الإيمان بما ثبت في الوحي الصحيح من صفات الله على أساس ذلك التنزيه . الثالث: العجز عن الإحاطة لقوله : ( وَلَا يُحِيطُونَ بِهِ عِلْمًا ) . فالسلفي إذا سمع قوله - تعالى -: ﴿ ثُمَّ اسْتَوَى عَلَى الْعَرْشِ ﴾ [الأعراف/ ٥٤] امتلأ قلبه من الإجلال والإعظام والتقديس والتنزيه لتلك الصفة التي أثنى الله بها على نفسه، وهي أنه استوى على عرشه، فيجزم قلبه جزمًا باتا بأن ذلك الاستواء الذي مدح الله به نفسه الكريمة المقدسة بالغ من غايات الكمال والجلال والتنزيه ما يقطع علائق أوهام المشابهة بينه وبين صفات المخلوقين سبحانه وتعالى عن ذ ذلك علوا كبيرًا، فيكون معتقدًا لمضمون قوله : لَيْسَ كَمِثْلِهِ شَيْ وقوله : وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ إلى غير ذلك من الآيات .
[He is] over all things Powerful. And He described some of the created beings with power, saying: 'Except those who repented before you could overpower them' [al-Ma'idah], and He described Himself with hearing and sight, saying: 'Indeed Allah is Hearing, Seeing' [al-Hajj/75], and He described some of the created beings with hearing and sight in His saying: 'Indeed We created man from a drop of mixed fluid, testing him, and made him hearing, seeing' [al-Insan/2], and He described Himself with life, saying: 'Allah — there is no deity except Him, the Ever-Living, the Sustainer of existence' [al-Baqarah/255] and 'And rely upon the Ever-Living who does not die' [al-Furqan/58], and He described some of His creation with life, saying: 'He brings the living out of the dead and brings the dead out of the living' [al-Rum/19] and 'And We made from water every living thing' and 'Peace be upon him the day he was born and the day he dies and the day he is raised alive' [Maryam/15]. And examples of this are very many in the Quran, as we have clarified elsewhere. And He described Himself with istiwa' over the Throne in seven verses of His Book, and He described some of His creation with istiwa' as well, such as His saying: 'That you may settle yourselves upon their backs' [al-Zukhruf/13] and His saying: 'Then when you and those with you have boarded the ship' [al-Mu'minun/28] and His saying: 'And it settled upon [Mount] Judi' [Hud/44]. So His istiwa', His power, hearing, sight, life, and all His attributes are transcendent above resemblance to the attributes of the created beings. And the istiwa' of the created beings, their hearing, sight, power, and will — all of that is appropriate to their state. And between His attributes — exalted is He — and their attributes in all respects is like the difference between His essence and their essences. So understand what we have mentioned and hold fast to the light of revelation which we have clarified. For safety is realized in following revelation, and it is not realized in anything else. And Allah has not guaranteed for any person that he will not be astray in this world nor wretched in the Hereafter except for the one who follows His guidance, which is the Quran, as in His saying: 'Whoever follows My guidance will neither go astray nor be wretched' [Taha/123]. You have learned from what we have mentioned that the way of the Salaf is built upon three foundations: the first of them is the transcendence (tanzih) of Allah — mighty and sublime — above resemblance to His creation in their essences, attributes, or actions. The second: belief (iman) in whatever is established in sound revelation of Allah's attributes on the basis of that transcendence. The third: the inability (ʿajz) to encompass Him, due to His saying: 'But they do not encompass Him in knowledge' [Ta-Ha/110]. So the Salafi, when he hears His saying: 'Then He established Himself (istawa) over the Throne' [al-A'raf/54], his heart is filled with veneration, grandeur, sanctification, and transcendence for that attribute with which Allah has praised Himself, namely that He established Himself over His Throne. So his heart asserts with absolute certainty that that istiwa' with which Allah has praised His noble holy self is of such extremes of perfection, majesty, and transcendence as to sever any connections of notions of resemblance between it and the attributes of the created beings — glorified and exalted is He above that by a great exaltation. Thus he believes the import of His saying: 'There is nothing like unto Him' and 'Nor is there to Him any equivalent' and other such verses.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological and methodological conclusion (khatima) comparing the way of the Salaf (early Muslims) with what is called the way of the Khalaf (later theologians). The author first provides extensive Quranic evidence that Allah describes Himself and His creation with the same terms (power, hearing, sight, life, istiwa') but with a fundamental difference: His attributes are transcendent and free from any resemblance to creation, while the attributes of creatures are appropriate to their limited state. The difference is as great as that between His essence and their essences. The author then states the three foundations of the Salaf methodology: (1) tanzih (transcendence) — affirming that Allah is absolutely unlike His creation in essence, attributes, and actions; (2) iman (belief) — affirming whatever attributes are established in sound revelation, based on that principle of transcendence, without asking 'how' or making comparisons; (3) 'ajz (inability) — recognizing that human intellect cannot fully encompass the reality of Allah, as He says 'they do not encompass Him in knowledge.' The author illustrates the Salafi approach with the example of the verse about istiwa' (Allah's establishing over the Throne): the Salafi does not deny the attribute (like the Mu'tazila) nor does he liken it to creation (like the anthropomorphists), but rather affirms it with veneration and certainty that it is utterly unique, consistent with 'there is nothing like unto Him.' This methodology safeguards against both tashbih (likening God to creation) and ta'til (denying attributes). The passage emphasizes that safety is only in following revelation (the Quran) as the source of guidance, echoing verse 20:123.
