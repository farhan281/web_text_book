The Unity of Time is Essential for Contradiction
Author emphasizes temporal unity for contradiction and critiques Uṣūlī abrogation view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of temporal unity for contradiction and apply it to critique the Uṣūlī position on abrogation.

Core Idea
Without unity of time, there is no contradiction—later reports do not negate earlier truths.

In logic, two propositions are truly contradictory only if they meet several conditions, including unity of time. This lesson explores a crucial warning from the author who shows how many Uṣūl scholars overlooked this condition when discussing abrogation of mutawātir reports by later āḥād reports. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to detect false contradictions in Islamic legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Mutawatir reports produce self-evident certainty without any inferential step.

In this passage, the author introduces mutawatir (successive report) as the second type of hidden self-evident knowledge. Understanding mutawatir is crucial because it grounds many certainties in Islamic sciences, such as the existence of historical cities or the transmission of the Qur'an. The author gives examples: 'Baghdad exists' and 'Damascus exists'. These are known through tawatur because countless people have reported them, and it is impossible that all colluded on a lie.

Distinguishing Lami in Sanad from Lami in Burhan
Author distinguishes lami in sanad from lami in burhan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan lami (causal demonstration) and burhan inni (existential demonstration) based on whether the middle term is the cause of the conclusion.

Core Idea
In burhan, the middle term either causes the conclusion (lami) or merely indicates it (inni).

In the study of adab al-bahth, the term 'lami' appears in two distinct contexts: sanad (support in debate) and burhan (demonstration). This lesson focuses on the latter, where the key distinction is whether the middle term of a syllogism is the actual cause of the conclusion. Understanding this difference is essential for evaluating the epistemic strength of demonstrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of demonstration
The passage divides burhan into lami (causal) and inni (existential). Locate the definitions: lami when the middle term is the cause, inni when it is not.
2
Check the role of the middle term
For lami, the middle term is 'illa (cause) of the conclusion; for inni, it is not. The author tests this by asking 'why' (lima) about the conclusion: if the middle term answers it, it is lami.
3
Apply the test to an example
The medical syllogism: 'This person has putrefied humors; every such person is feverish; therefore he is feverish.' Verify that the middle term (putrefied humors) answers 'why is he feverish?' as it is the cause.
4
Contrast with sanad
The author warns that 'lami' in sanad is a different concept, with different counterparts. Avoid conflating them: sanad lami is a type of objection, not a demonstration.

For burhan, the division is based on whether the middle term (hadd awsat) is the cause of the conclusion. If it is, the demonstration is called lami (causal); if not, it is called inni (existential). The author explains the naming: in lami burhan, when asked 'why?' (lam) about the conclusion, the answer is the middle term, which is the cause. In inni burhan, the answer is not the cause; instead, it uses 'inna' (if/indeed) to establish a correlation. The example given for lami burhan is the medical syllogism: putrefied humors cause fever. The syllogism (first figure: 'This has putrefied humors; every such person is feverish; therefore he is feverish') yields a conclusion whose cause is the middle term (putrefied humors). Thus, answering 'why is he feverish?' with 'because of putrefied humors' is valid because it is the cause. The inni example is cut off but would illustrate a case where the middle term is not the cause, e.g., correlating fever with a symptom that is not causative. This distinction is vital for understanding the epistemological status of demonstrations: lami yields certain causal knowledge, while inni yields correlational knowledge.

Burhan Lami and Burhan Inni: Cause vs. Coexistence
Author distinguishes causal from non-causal demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given categorical syllogism in an Islamic text is a burhan lami or burhan inni, based on whether the middle term is the cause of the conclusion.

Core Idea
Burhan lami answers 'why'; burhan inni only answers 'that'.

In Islamic logic, demonstration (burhan) is the highest form of proof. But not all demonstrations are equal: some reveal the cause of the conclusion, while others only establish that it is true. This distinction—between burhan lami (causal) and burhan inni (existential)—is crucial for evaluating the depth of a proof. In this lesson, you will learn to distinguish them using a clear criterion: is the middle term the cause of the conclusion or not?

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion and middle term
In the syllogism, locate the conclusion (al-natija) and the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) that connects the two premises. The middle term appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
2
Check if the middle term is the cause
Ask: Does the attribute expressed by the middle term produce or originate the attribute in the conclusion? If yes, the middle term is a cause; if no, it is merely a sign.
3
Classify as lami if causal
If the middle term is the cause, the demonstration is burhan lami. The answer to 'why?' is the middle term itself.
4
Classify as inni if non-causal
If the middle term is not the cause, it is burhan inni. The answer to 'why?' would be incorrect if given as the middle term.
5
Test with the medical example
In the example: putrefaction of humors causes fever, so 'this patient had putrefied humors' → 'this patient has fever' is lami. Reversing the order gives inni: fever as a sign of putrefaction, not cause.

Qur'anic Syllogism: The Verse of Repentance as a Logical Proof
Author demonstrates a syllogism from Quran 4:146 to show logical deduction in revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the major and minor premises from a Qur'anic verse and construct the implicit categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
The Quran contains logical syllogisms; reason and revelation are not opposed.

This passage shows how a Qur'anic verse (4:146) implicitly contains a categorical syllogism. The author, a classical Islamic logician, identifies the minor premise ('they are with the believers') and the major premise ('Allah will give the believers a great reward') and derives the conclusion. This demonstrates that logical reasoning is not foreign to Islamic texts but is embedded in the Quran itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion hinted in the verse
Read the verse carefully. The conclusion is not explicitly stated but logically follows. The author argues that the verse implies that those who repent will receive a great reward.
2
Find the minor premise
Locate the part of the verse that connects the subject (repentant) to the middle term. The author identifies 'they are with the believers' as the minor premise.
In Arabic: فَأُولَئِكَ مَعَ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ
3
Find the major premise
Locate the part of the verse that connects the middle term (believers) to the predicate (great reward). The author identifies 'and Allah will give the believers a great reward' as the major premise.
In Arabic: وَسَوْفَ يُؤْتِ اللَّهُ الْمُؤْمِنِينَ أَجْرًا عَظِيمًا
4
Form the syllogism and verify the conclusion
Combine the premises: All who repent are with the believers (minor). All believers receive a great reward (major). Therefore, all who repent receive a great reward. This is a valid first-figure syllogism (Barbara).
5
Reflect on the epistemological certainty
The author emphasizes that conclusions of logical syllogisms are true without doubt when premises are correct. This applies to Qur'anic arguments as well.
