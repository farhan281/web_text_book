Core Idea

The term 'dalalah' is central to Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh. The author presents two technical definitions: one focused on the cognitive act of understanding, and the other on the inherent potential of a sign to convey meaning. All indications arise from combining two indicator types with three modes: convention, reason, and nature.

Every indicator is either verbal or non-verbal, and each can indicate in three ways: by convention (wad'), by reason ('aql), or by nature (tab'). The Three Types of Denotation in Islamic Logic
Author preliminarily defines the three denotations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam, and explain how they relate to each other.

Core Idea
Every word carries three layers: full correspondence, inclusion, and necessary concomitance.

In Islamic logic, a word does not merely name an object; it also implies its parts and external necessities. Understanding these three types of denotation is essential for reading any legal or theological text. This lesson unpacks each type with clear examples from fiqh and usul.

Guided Path
1
Define dalalat al-mutabaqa
It is the word's primary, conventional meaning. For example, 'house' denotes the entire building.
2
Define dalalat al-tadammun
It is the word's reference to a part of its primary meaning. For 'house', tadammun refers to 'wall' or 'roof'.
3
Define dalalat al-iltizam
It is the word's reference to something external but necessarily connected. For 'roof', iltizam implies 'house'.
4
Compare their conventionality
Mutabaqa is always wad'i; the other two are debated among scholars.

Commentary
This passage presents three madhhabs (schools) concerning the nature of two types of denotation: dalalat al-tadammun (denotation by inclusion) and dalalat al-iltizam (denotation by concomitance). The first madhhab is partially visible at the beginning: the view that both types are conventional (wad'iyya), because the primary cause in understanding them is convention, and the cause of the cause is itself a cause. The second madhhab, attributed to most rhetoricians (bayaniyyun), holds that both are rational ('aqliyyatan). Their argument: the utterance was only placed for the literal (mutabaqa) meaning, but it is the intellect that infers from that literal meaning the part (in tadammun) or the external necessary (in iltizam). The third madhhab, followed by most usuliyyun (legal theorists), distinguishes: tadammun is conventional, iltizam is rational. Their proof: the signified in tadammun is part of the literal signified, and since literal denotation is conventional, its part is also conventional. But iltizam is not part of the literal meaning; rather, the intellect moves from the literal meaning to its necessary concomitant. This division is fundamental in Islamic logic and jurisprudence for understanding how words convey additional meanings beyond their primary referents.

Defining Knowledge Through Classification of Belief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of the definition of knowledge ('ilm) as assertive, conforming, fixed belief, and distinguish it from other types of belief such as conjecture, doubt, and illusion.

Core Idea
Knowledge is firm truth beyond doubt.

In this passage, the author systematically classifies belief to isolate the precise definition of knowledge ('ilm). This is a foundational exercise in Islamic epistemology, used by mutakallimun to define the object of certain knowledge. By parsing the divisions, you will see how each term excludes a type of invalid belief, leading to a rigorous definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major division of belief
The author splits belief (i'tiqād) into assertive (jāzim) and non-assertive (ghayr jāzim). Find these terms in the Arabic and note that non-assertive includes conjecture (ẓann), doubt (shakk), and illusion (wahm).
Non-assertive beliefs lack decisiveness.
2
Within assertive, split into conforming and non-conforming
Assertive belief is either conforming (muṭābiq) to reality (true) or non-conforming (false). The phrase 'ghayr muṭābiq' refers to false beliefs, e.g., the philosophers' belief in the eternity of the world.
Conforming means corresponding to how things actually are.
3
Within conforming, split into fixed and non-fixed
Conforming belief is either fixed (thābit) i.e., not admitting doubt in any case, or not fixed. The fixed one is knowledge. An example of non-fixed is the ijtihad of a correct mujtahid, because it is subject to change.
Fixed means stable and immune to doubt.
4

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For quantified propositions, contradictories must differ in both quality and quantity.

Guided Path

Recall the types of propositions

The author introduces nine conditions for contradiction. In this lesson, we focus on the first four: unity of predicate, subject, time, and place. These ensure that the opposition is genuine.

Guided Path

The conditions prevent false contradictions. If any condition is not met, it is possible for both propositions to be true or both false.
2

3

Time can be a specific moment or period.
5
Fourth condition: unity of place
Both propositions must refer to the same location. Example: 'Zayd prayed in the mosque' and 'Zayd did not pray at home' can both be true because places differ.
Place is a crucial factor in many rulings.

Commentary

The Condition of Self-Necessitation and Invalid Syllogisms
Author defines the logical syllogism (qiyas) and explains the condition of self-necessitation (liddhatihi) with examples of invalid syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a formally valid syllogism and arguments that only appear valid but rely on external premises or material content.

Core Idea
A syllogism is valid only if the conclusion follows necessarily from the form alone; equality and half-ness arguments fail because they need extra axioms.

The author introduces the crucial condition 'lidhatihi' (by itself) to ensure formal validity. He then provides three illustrative examples of syllogisms that violate this condition. By analyzing these examples, you will learn to identify when a conclusion follows from the premises themselves versus when it depends on external factors.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition 'lidhatihi'
The phrase means the conclusion must follow necessarily from the premises alone, without any additional premises or reliance on the specific terms. The author distinguishes three violations: no entailment, entailment via external premise, and entailment via material particularity.
This condition separates formal logic from material truth.
2
Analyze the equality syllogism
The argument 'Zaid equals Amr, Amr equals Bakr, therefore Zaid equals Bakr' seems valid, but it requires an extraneous premise: 'What is equal to the equal of something is equal to that thing.' Without that, nothing follows. So it fails 'lidhatihi'.
3
Analyze the half-ness syllogism
The argument 'Two is half of four, four is half of eight, therefore two is half of eight' is invalid because 'half of half' is not generally true (half of half is not half, but a quarter). The relation is not transitive.
4
Analyze the stone-whinnying syllogism
The argument 'No man is stone, no stone is whinnying, therefore no man is whinnying' produces a true conclusion, but the form is invalid. Exchanging 'whinnying' with 'rational' yields a false conclusion, proving the truth was due to the specific terms, not the form.
This illustrates entailment 'likhusus al-madda' (due to particularity of matter).

And in the terminology of the logicians: a statement composed of two or more propositions in such a manner that it necessarily entails by itself another proposition. And by their saying 'or more' enters the compound syllogism as will be clarified later, God willing. And by their saying 'necessarily entails by itself' excludes what does not entail a proposition at all, as if you said: 'Man is an animal, and stone is inanimate' – for they do not entail a proposition. And by their saying 'by itself' excludes what entails another proposition not by itself, but due to an extraneous proposition or due to particularity of matter. An example of what entails a proposition not by itself but by an extraneous premise: the syllogism of equality, as your saying: 'Zaid is equal to Amr, and Amr is equal to Bakr.' It produces: 'Zaid is equal to Bakr'; that is by means of an extraneous premise: 'What is equal to the equal of something is equal to that thing.' So if there were no true extraneous premise, it would entail nothing. Similarly, if you said: 'Man is distinct from horse, and horse is distinct from articulate' – it does not entail that man is distinct from articulate; because if you said: 'What is distinct from something distinct from a thing is distinct from that thing' it is not true, because it may not be distinct from it, as in the mentioned example. And likewise the syllogism of half-ness: it entails nothing, as your saying: 'Two is half of four, and four is half of eight' – it does not entail that two is half of eight, due to the falsity of: 'Half of half is half'. And an example of entailment due to particularity of matter: your saying: 'No man is a stone, and no stone is a whinnying [thing].' It produces: 'No man is a whinnying [thing].' It is true due to the particularity of matter, not due to the essence of the premises. Evidence: if you put 'rational' instead of 'whinnying' in the major premise, it would not be true, as if you said: 'No man is a stone, and no stone is rational.' It produces: 'No man is rational' – which is false. The structure of the composition of the two premises is the same, and its truth in the case of 'whinnying' and its falsity in the case of 'rational' indicates that that truth is only entailed by the premises due to particularity.

Commentary
This is a definitional passage. The author first places logical syllogism (qiyas) as the chief objective of the art of logic, concerned with assents (tasdiqat) which are nobler than concepts. He then gives the linguistic meaning of qiyas as measurement, and the technical definition: 'a statement composed of two or more propositions that entails by itself another proposition.' He unpacks each part of the definition. 'Composed of two propositions' excludes single propositions, even those compound in meaning. 'Or more' includes compound syllogisms. 'Entails by itself another proposition' excludes two conditions: first, collections that imply no conclusion at all (like 'man is an animal, stone is inanimate'); second, collections that do imply a conclusion but not by themselves—either because they need an external premise (like the equality syllogism, which requires the axiom of transitivity) or because the entailment depends on the particular material content (like the example with 'whinnying' where the conclusion is true but not due to form). The passage examines each case with examples: the equality syllogism works only if we add the external premise; the half-ness syllogism fails because 'half of half' is not true; the 'stone-whinnying' syllogism gives a true conclusion only because of the specific terms, not because the form is valid. Substituting 'rational' for 'whinnying' produces a false conclusion with the same form. Thus, a genuine logical syllogism must be such that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises alone, without external aids or material accidents. This distinction between formal and material validity is fundamental in Islamic logic.

Author defines verbal and reminding definitions and explains their relational difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between verbal and reminding definitions based on the listener's prior knowledge.

Core Idea
Verbal and reminding definitions are identical in referent; only the listener's memory differs.

The author begins by classifying definitions into four types, starting with the verbal and reminding. While they may seem distinct, he argues they are identical in content. The key lies in the state of the listener: whether he never knew the meaning (verbal) or has forgotten it (reminding). Know that the divisions of definition according to the people of this art are four:
First: the verbal definition, its criterion is what we have presented: that it is the definition of a word by another synonymous word that is clearer to the listener, like defining 'ghadanfar' (lion) by 'asad' (lion). We have clarified it with examples in the logical introduction. It is known that this type of definition is relative, because the fame of one of the two synonyms differs according to persons. So you see some do not know wheat except by the name 'burr', and some do not know it except by the name 'hinta'. And measure accordingly.
The second division of definition is known as the reminding definition, its criterion is that it is the bringing of a meaning into the mind of the listener that was known to him previously, but his knowledge of it had vanished at the time of definition, until he was reminded of it by the definition. It is not hidden that the difference between the verbal and the reminding is relational, because the consideration is in the state of the listener. If he had no prior knowledge of the meaning of the reality being defined, then the definition is verbal. If he had prior knowledge of it but it had vanished from his mind, and the definer wants to bring its absent meaning into his mind by definition, then the definition is reminding. By this you know that the two mentioned definitions are identical in reality and referent, because the difference between them is due to something external to their reality, which is the state of the listener regarding prior knowledge of the matter or its absence.
Note: Know that the word indicating the subject is called the 'title' (عنوان), its exact meaning is called the 'concept' (مفهوم), and its individuals existing externally are called the 'referents' (ماصدق). They are so called because they are that to which the concept applies.
The third division of definition is the real definition, its criterion is that it is the definition of the essence whose individuals have existence in the external world, by the definition (حد) and the description (رسم), in the manner we have already clarified, like defining human as 'rational animal' and the like.
The fourth division of definition is the nominal definition, which is the definition of an imagined essence whose existence in the external world is not known.

Real and Nominal Definitions: Essence and Existence
Author defines real and nominal definitions and clarifies the terms title, concept, and referent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify definitions as real or nominal based on whether the defined essence has known external existence.

Core Idea
Real definitions describe essences that exist; nominal definitions describe imagined essences without known existence.

After covering verbal and reminding definitions, the author turns to the two more fundamental types: real and nominal. The real definition (al-haqiqi) defines an essence that actually has individuals in the external world, using complete description (hadd) or partial description (rasm). The nominal definition (al-ismi) defines an imagined essence without known external existence. This lesson also introduces the terminology of 'title', 'concept', and 'referent', which are essential for understanding definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify real and nominal definitions
Find the criteria for each in the passage. Real: defines an essence with external existence, using hadd or rasm. Nominal: defines an imagined essence without known external existence.
Real example: 'human is rational animal'. Nominal example: a phoenix.
2
Understand the title-concept-referent triad
The word indicating the subject is the title (عنوان). Its exact meaning is the concept (مفهوم). The external individuals are the referents (ماصدق).
These terms are used in logic and later in usul al-fiqh.
3
Apply the existence test
To decide between real and nominal, ask: Are there known individuals of this essence in the external world? If yes, real; if no, nominal.
If existence is uncertain, it may be nominal until proven otherwise.
4
Classify examples
Given a definition, classify it as real or nominal and justify.
Example: 'angel' is real in Islamic belief; 'phoenix' is nominal.

Distinguishing Haqiqi and Khariji Propositions
Author distinguishes haqiqi and khariji propositions using a color example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqi and khariji propositions and determine under which consideration a proposition is true or false based on the existence or essence of its subject.

Core Idea
A proposition can be true even if its subject doesn't exist externally, if considered by its essence.

This lesson introduces a fundamental distinction in Islamic logic: the difference between haqiqi (real) and khariji (external) propositions. Using a simple example about colors, the author shows how a statement can be false under one consideration but true under another. Understanding this distinction is essential for properly evaluating logical statements and definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Look for the terms 'haqiqi' and 'khariji' in the passage. These refer to two ways of considering a proposition: one based on actual existence, the other on essence.
2
Understand the example setup
The author imagines a scenario where only black exists externally, and whiteness does not exist at all. The statement 'Whiteness is a color' is evaluated under both considerations.
3
Apply khariji consideration
Under the khariji (external) view, truth requires the subject to actually exist. Since whiteness does not exist externally, the statement is false.
4
Apply haqiqi consideration
Under the haqiqi (real) view, truth is based on the essence: if whiteness were to exist, it would be a color. Even though it doesn't exist, the proposition is true because the predicate 'color' is inherent in the meaning of whiteness.
5
Generalize the principle
A haqiqi proposition is true for any subject that, by its nature, would have the predicate if it existed. A khariji proposition requires the actual existence of the subject for its truth.

The author notes that he already stated earlier that the correct position is to demand correction, contrary to those who say transmission is not a valid subject for debate. 4
Think of an example from a definition
In a definition like 'human is rational animal', 'animal' is a part (genus) and 'rational' is another part (differentia). The definition itself is a whole composed of these parts. Understanding what counts as a part helps evaluate definitions.
This example ties the concept to the theory of definitions (hadd).
5
Memorize the definitions as technical terms
Use the mnemonic: 'al-juz' is the part, al-juz'iyya is the particularity. These are anchor terms for logical analysis.
Commit them to memory; they will reappear in discussions of division and classification.
