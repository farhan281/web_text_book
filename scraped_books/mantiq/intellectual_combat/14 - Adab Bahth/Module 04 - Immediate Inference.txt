Negation of a Broad Term Implies Its Narrower Contradictory
Author explains a logical relationship where each of two terms is more general than the negation of the other

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that negating a more general term necessitates the affirmation of the more specific contradictory, and identify when the converse does not hold.

Core Idea
Negating the broader term forces the narrower contradictory to be true, because contradictories cannot both be false.

In Islamic logic, understanding relationships between terms is crucial for building valid arguments. This lesson unpacks a subtle rule: if term A is more general than not-B, then negating A gives us B, but not the other way around. This principle, rooted in the law of excluded middle, is used in kalam and fiqh to draw conclusions from negations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms and their negations
Locate the two terms (e.g., A and B) in the passage and explicitly write down their negations (not-A, not-B). For example, let A = 'living creature', B = 'non-human entity'. Then not-A = 'non-living', not-B = 'human'.
2
Check the relations of generality
Verify that each term is more general than the negation of the other. This means that the set of things that are not-B is entirely contained within A, and the set of things that are not-A is entirely contained within B. In our example, humans (not-B) are living (A), and non-living (not-A) are non-human (B).
3
Apply the implication: negate the broader to affirm the narrower
According to the principle, if you negate the more general term, you must affirm the other term. For instance, if something is not a living creature (negation of A), then it must be a non-human entity (B), because not-A is entirely within B. Similarly, if something is not a non-human entity (negation of B), then it must be a living creature (A).
This relies on the law of excluded middle: if not-A is false, then A is true, but here we are not directly using that.
4
Verify the converse does not hold
Check that affirming one term does not imply the negation of the other. For example, if something is a living creature (A), it could be human (which is not B) or animal (which is B). Therefore, A does not imply not-B. The relationship is asymmetric: implication only works from negation to affirmation, not in reverse.
The passage explicitly says 'without converse' (بلا عكس).

Distinguishing the Whole (Kull) from Totality (Kulliyya)
Author defines 'whole' as composed of parts and 'totality' as judgment on every individual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a judgment on the aggregate (kull) and a judgment on every individual (kulliyya) using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
The whole is the aggregate; totality is the universal judgment on every individual.

In logic, terms like 'kull' can refer either to a collection considered as a whole or to a judgment that applies to each member separately. This lesson, based on the passage, clarifies the critical difference between 'the whole' (al-kull) and 'totality' (al-kulliyya) using Quranic and everyday examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'kull'
Find where the author defines 'kull' as composed of two or more parts. Note the phrase: 'the judgment falls upon its aggregate, not upon each of its individuals.'
This is the core distinction.
2
Contrast with 'kulliyya'
Read the definition of 'kulliyya' as a judgment that applies to every individual of the subject. Example: 'Every human is an animal' means each human individually possesses animality.
Note the difference: aggregate vs. distributive.

The author cites '...eight of them will bear the Throne' (Al-Haaqqa 17). Explain why this is a judgment on the aggregate: the eight angels bear it together, not each alone.
If it were each alone, the verse would mean each angel bears the entire Throne, which is not intended.

Test your understanding

Commentary
This passage is a definitional exposition distinguishing between two concepts: 'the whole' (al-kull) and 'totality' (al-kulliyya). The author begins by noting that after understanding universal and particular, we now explain the meaning of whole, part, totality, and partiality. 

First, 'the whole' (al-kull) in terminology is defined as something composed of two or more parts. Its key distinguishing feature is that a predicate is attributed to the aggregate of its parts, not to each individual part. The author illustrates this with a Quranic example: '... eight of them will bear the Throne of your Lord.' The judgment of bearing the Throne applies to the eight angels as a collective unit, not to each individually. If it applied to each, then each angel would bear the Throne alone, which is not the intended meaning. Thus, the judgment is on the aggregate.

Second, 'totality' (al-kulliyya) is defined as a judgment that applies to every single individual of the subject individually. For example, 'Every human is an animal' means that each individual human is independently an animal; the judgment follows each one.

Then the author introduces a rule from the scholars of meanings ('ulama' al-ma'ani) concerning the word 'kull' when combined with a negation particle. If the negation comes before 'kull', it indicates the collective whole (al-kull al-majmu'i). That is, the judgment (negated) applies to the totality as a group, not to each individually. Examples from poetry and common sayings illustrate this: 'Not everything a person wishes does he attain' means that not all wishes are attained, but some are, so the negation does not apply to each wish individually. Similarly, 'Not every white thing is fat' means some white things are fat, some are not; the negation is not universal across individuals. This rule is crucial for correctly interpreting statements involving 'kull' with negation.

The Rule of Negation with 'Kull'
Author presents a rule from scholars of meanings about negation before 'kull'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret statements where 'kull' is preceded by a negation particle, distinguishing the collective scope from the universal negative.

Core Idea
When 'kull' follows negation, it indicates the collective whole, not a negation of each individual.

The scholars of meanings (ma'ani) established a crucial rule: when the word 'kull' (every/whole) is combined with a negation particle placed before it, the negation applies to the aggregate, not to each individual. This lesson unpacks that rule with examples from Arabic poetry and common sayings.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Find the exact statement: 'If the negation particle precedes the word 'kull', then it is from the collective whole, so the judgment does not fall upon the subject except collectively, and it does not follow each individual.'
This is the key principle.
2
Examine the first example
Read the line of poetry: 'Not everything that a person wishes does he attain.' Explain that the negation applies to the whole set of wishes, not each individually, because some wishes are attained.
The poet's meaning is that not all wishes are attained, not that no wish is attained.
3
Examine the second examples
Consider the proverbs: 'Not every white thing is fat' and 'Not every round thing is a cake.' Interpret them: the negation indicates that the predicate does not hold for the entire class, but it may hold for some individuals.
This shows the collective scope: some white things are fat, some are not; similarly for round things.
4
Apply the rule to a new example
Given: 'Not all believers are righteous.' Determine if this means no believer is righteous, or that some believers are not righteous. Use the rule: since negation precedes 'all', it is collective: some believers are not righteous.
Contrast with 'All believers are righteous' (if negated after 'all', it would be 'All believers are not righteous' meaning none, which is different.)

The Four Relations Between Concepts
Author defines and proves the four mutual relations of concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the four relations between any two concepts based on whether they always coincide, never coincide, or sometimes coincide and separate.

Core Idea
The four relations exhaust all possibilities; they are the foundation for logical evaluation of propositions.

In this lesson, we explore the author's proof that exactly four relations can exist between any two concepts. By understanding the criteria of coincidence and separation, you will learn to recognize equality, disparity, absolute generality and specificity, and generality from one aspect. These relations are not mere abstractions but are directly applied to determine truth in categorical propositions.

Guided Path
1
The exhaustive division
The author proves four relations by considering whether two concepts always coincide, never coincide, or sometimes coincide. There is no fifth possibility.
2
First two relations: equality and disparity
If they never separate, they are equal (e.g., human and rational). If they never coincide, they are disparate (e.g., human and stone).
3
The subcase of partial coincidence
When they sometimes coincide and separate, it branches into two: either only one concept can exist without the other (absolute generality/specificity), or both can exist separately (generality from one aspect).
4
Examples to solidify
Memorize the examples: animal (general) and human (specific) for absolute generality; human and white for generality from one aspect.

Applying the Four Relations as a Scale for Propositions
Author applies the four relations as a scale to evaluate categorical propositions, focusing on disparity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for disparity to determine that both universal and particular negatives are true and both affirmatives are false when the terms are disparate.

Core Idea
The relation between terms determines the truth value of propositions immediately, without need for further inference.

In this lesson, we see how the four relations serve as a scale (mizan) to judge the truth and falsity of categorical propositions. The author demonstrates this with the relation of disparity: if two terms are disparate, every negative proposition is true and every affirmative is false. This principle is foundational for immediate inference and for testing the validity of arguments.

Guided Path
1
Recall the relation of disparity
Disparate concepts never share any instance, like human and stone.
2
Apply to universal and particular affirmatives
Affirmatives claim some or all instances of one term are instances of the other. Since they share none, every affirmative is false.
3
Apply to universal and particular negatives
Negatives claim that some or all instances of one term are not instances of the other. Since they share none, every negative is true.
4
Generalize: this rule holds for any disparate pair
The truth values are determined solely by the relation, independent of the specific terms.

Distinguishing Antecedent and Consequent in Conditional Propositions
Defining muqaddam and tali for conditional types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify muqaddam and tali in both connected and disjunctive conditional propositions and explain why order matters in muttasila but not in munfasila.

Core Idea
In connected conditionals, order reveals logical dependence; in disjunctives, order is the speaker's choice.

Conditional propositions consist of two parts: the muqaddam (antecedent) and the tali (consequent). However, the nature of this ordering differs between connected (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila) conditionals. This lesson clarifies that distinction and its implications for logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts
In any conditional, find the two component propositions. By default, the first is called muqaddam and the second tali, but this only reliably applies to muttasila.
2
Check for logical dependence in muttasila
In a connected conditional, the muqaddam must entail the tali; reversing may break truth. Example: 'If it is a human, it is an animal' is true; reversed, it is false. Test with an Islamic example: 'If it is a human, it is rational' is true and its converse is also true because of equality (tasawi), but generally reversal fails.
The footnote in the source clarifies special cases of equality.
3
Recognize arbitrariness in munfasila
In a disjunctive conditional, there is no entailment between parts; both are alternatives. The order can be switched without changing meaning. Example: 'The number is either even or odd' is equivalent to 'odd or even'.
4
Apply the distinction
Given a conditional, first determine whether it is muttasila or munfasila. If muttasila, the order is fixed by logical dependence; if munfasila, the order is interchangeable. This helps in analyzing the logical structure of arguments in Islamic sciences.

The Definition and Essential Conditions of Contradiction
Definition of contradiction and its two conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define contradiction (tanāquḍ) and identify its two essential conditions: difference in quality and mutual exclusivity of truth and falsity.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires opposite truth values, not just opposite wording.

Contradiction is the most fundamental relation in logic, used to expose inconsistency in arguments, especially in jadal (debate). This lesson unpacks its technical definition and shows why mere difference in wording is insufficient—the propositions must oppose each other in truth value.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the linguistic root
The Arabic term tanāquḍ comes from 'naqḍ', meaning to break or annul. Two things each 'break' the other. What does this suggest about the relation? It implies mutual exclusivity: they cannot coexist.
2
Identify the technical conditions
The author lists two conditions: (1) difference in quality (affirmative vs. negative), and (2) necessary result that one is true and the other false. Note the third hidden condition: they must be about the same subject, predicate, time, place, etc., which the author assumes from previous chapters.
These conditions prevent false contradictions like 'Zaid is standing' vs. 'Amr is not standing' (different subject) or 'Zaid is standing' vs. 'Zaid is not sitting' (different predicate).
3
Check the negation of the definition
If both are false or both true, it is not contradiction. Why? Because contradictory propositions must exhaust all possibilities: if both could be false, there would be a third option (e.g., 'Some humans are not animals' and 'Some humans are animals' can both be false if all humans are animals – wait, that's not quite right). Actually, the author says 'if both are false or both are true, then no contradiction'. This means that in a genuine contradiction, it is impossible for both to be false or both true.
Example: 'Every human is an animal' (true) and 'No human is an animal' (false) – can both be false? No, because if the first were false, some human would not be animal, making the second false? Actually, if the universal affirmative is false, its contradictory (particular negative) is true, not the universal negative. So the universal negative being false does not mean both are false. The key is the logical square: the universal negative is a contrary, not a contradictory.

Applying Contradiction Rules to Different Proposition Types
Rules for forming contradictions for each proposition type.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory for any singular, universal, particular, or indefinite proposition by applying the correct change of quality and (if quantified) quantity.

Core Idea
Singular propositions need only a quality swap; quantified propositions need both quantity and quality swaps.

Now that you understand what contradiction is, you must learn to produce it accurately. Each type of proposition has a specific rule. This lesson walks through the rules with Islamic examples, training you to avoid the common mistake of swapping only quality for all propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
First, determine the quantity: is it singular (about one individual), universal (every/no), particular (some/some not), or indefinite (no quantifier)? The passage treats indefinite as particular. For example, 'Zaid is standing' is singular; 'Every human is an animal' is universal; 'Some human is not an animal' is particular.
2
Apply the rule for singular propositions
If singular, only change the quality: affirmative becomes negative, negative becomes affirmative. Check the example: 'Zaid is standing' (singular affirmative) contradicts 'Zaid is not standing' (singular negative). No change in quantity because it's inherently singular.
Singular propositions have no quantity variation; they are like 'this particular individual'. So they function as 'universal' in a sense but the rule is simpler.
3
Apply the rule for quantified propositions (universal/particular)
For quantified propositions, you must change both quality and quantity: universal affirmative ↔ particular negative; universal negative ↔ particular affirmative. For instance, from 'Every human is an animal' (universal affirmative) to 'Some human is not an animal' (particular negative).
The quantity swap ensures that both contradictories cannot be false at the same time (if one is false, the other must be true).
4
Handle indefinite propositions
Indefinite propositions have no quantifier but are treated as particular. So the rule: affirmative indefinite → universal negative; negative indefinite → universal affirmative. Example: 'Human is an animal' (indefinite affirmative) contradictory is 'No human is an animal' (universal negative).
This equivalence is conventional; some logicians treat indefinite as ambiguous, but here it's explicitly equated with particular.

Author explains necessity of converting universal and particular quantifiers for contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory of any universal or particular categorical proposition by applying the quantifier exchange rule.

Core Idea
For genuine contradiction, flip both quantity and quality.

In Islamic logic, contradiction (tanāquḍ) is the foundation of many arguments, especially in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh. To obtain a true contradictory pair, you cannot simply negate a proposition; you must also change its quantifier. This lesson unpacks that rule using the author's own examples.

1
Identify the proposition's quantity and quality

Apply the quantifier exchange
If the proposition is universal, change it to particular; if particular, change to universal. Keep the quality the same for now.
This avoids both being true or both false.

Also change the quality from affirmative to negative or vice versa. This ensures one is true and one false.
The classic square of opposition: contradictory pairs differ in both quantity and quality.

Verify the pair is contradictory
Check that the two cannot be both true or both false. Use the author's examples to confirm: two universal propositions can both be false; two particular can both be true.

The essential condition for contradiction: unity of the relational aspect
Author refines the condition for contradiction to unity of the relational aspect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two propositions are contradictory by checking unity of the relational aspect (al-nisba al-ḥukmiyya).

Core Idea
Contradiction boils down to one condition: the relation affirmed must be the very relation denied.

In previous discussions, you encountered nine conditions for contradiction (unity of subject, predicate, time, etc.). Here, the author makes a critical refinement: all these conditions are manifestations of a single core condition – unity of the relational aspect (al-nisba al-ḥukmiyya). Understanding this unification is key to mastering immediate inference.

Guided Path
1
Locate the author's claim
Find the sentence where the author states that contradiction is realized by unity in one thing. That one thing is al-nisba al-ḥukmiyya (the relational aspect).
2
Define al-nisba al-ḥukmiyya
It is the specific relation between subject and predicate that is either affirmed or denied in a proposition. For example, in 'Zayd is standing', the relation is 'standing'.
This is not the same as the predicate itself; it is the link or attribution.
3
Apply the condition
For two propositions to be contradictory, the same relation must be affirmed in one and denied in the other. If the relation differs, there is no contradiction.
Example: 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not standing' share the same relation, so they are contradictory.
4
Distinguish from other differences
Other differences (e.g., subject, time, place) matter only insofar as they affect the relational aspect. The essence is unity of the relation itself.
The nine earlier conditions are necessary but not individually sufficient without this unity.

Contradiction vs. Specification in Uṣūl
Author explains why a particular negative does not contradict a universal affirmative in uṣūl al-fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between logical contradiction and Uṣūlī specification, and determine which principle applies to a given pair of scriptural texts.

Core Idea
A particular negative does not always contradict a universal affirmative; in Uṣūl, it may specify it.

In logic, a universal affirmative is directly refuted by a particular negative. But in Uṣūl al-Fiqh, the same pattern often arises between two scriptural texts—yet they are not contradictory. The author explains that the apparent conflict is resolved by the principle of specification (takhṣīṣ), which restricts the general ruling to a subset of cases. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting Islamic legal evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logical rule
Recall that in logic, a universal affirmative (e.g., 'Every A is B') is contradicted by a particular negative (e.g., 'Some A is not B'). This is an immediate inference called 'contradiction' (tanāquḍ).
This rule is undisputed in logic.
2
Recognize a universal affirmative in a scriptural text
Look for a verse or hadith that expresses a general ruling covering all individuals. For example, Q. 2:228 ('Divorced women wait three months') has the force of 'Every divorced woman waits three months.'
The linguistic form (e.g., definite plural, 'al-' + noun) often indicates generality.
3
Recognize a particular negative in another text
Find another verse or hadith that states an exception. For example, Q. 65:4 ('Pregnant women their term is delivery') is a particular negative: 'Some divorced women do not wait three months.'
The particular negative may not use explicit quantifiers; its force is understood from context.
4
Apply the principle of specification (takhṣīṣ)
In Uṣūl, the particular negative is not a contradiction but a specification: it confines the universal to its non-excepted individuals. The earlier general text remains valid for the majority, and the specific text adds a condition or exception.
Specification requires evidence (dalīl) and does not nullify the general ruling entirely.
5

The author also mentions Q. 33:49 (divorce before consummation) as another specification.

Core Idea
Genuine contradiction requires the particular to negate the universal, not merely carve out an exception.

In this lesson, we examine a key distinction in Aristotelian logic: when does a particular negative proposition truly contradict a universal affirmative? The author uses Quranic verses to show that many apparent counterexamples are actually specifications that leave the universal intact. This distinction is crucial for accurate reasoning in Islamic legal theory and theology.

Contradiction in Conditional Propositions: Rules and Dialectical Benefit
Author defines contradiction for conditional propositions and explains how proving the contradictory of an opponent's statement refutes his proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory of a conditional proposition and apply the rule that proving the contradictory of an opponent's statement refutes his proof.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a conditional flips both quality and quantity while keeping antecedent and consequent identical.

You already know how to contradict a categorical proposition. The same logic extends to conditionals, but with an extra requirement: the antecedent and consequent must stay the same. This lesson shows you exactly how to find the contradictory of any conditional statement and why this skill is a powerful secret weapon in debate—especially when you want to destroy your opponent's proof without directly attacking it.

Guided Path
1
Extract antecedent and consequent
For any conditional proposition 'If A then B' or 'Whenever A, B', isolate the antecedent (A) and consequent (B). These must remain unchanged in the contradictory.
2
Determine quality and quantity
Identify if the conditional is affirmative or negative (quality) and universal or particular (quantity). Universal conditionals often use 'whenever' (كلما) or 'never' (ليس البتة). Particular conditionals use 'it may happen' (قد).
Quantified conditionals behave like categoricals in terms of quantity.
3
Flip quality and quantity
Use the square of opposition: the contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative; of universal negative is particular affirmative. Apply the same to conditionals: if the original is universal affirmative, its contradictory is particular negative (e.g., 'Whenever A, B' → 'It may happen that A and not B').
The text gives examples: 'كلما كان هذا إنسانًا كان حيوانا' (universal affirmative) contradicts with 'قد لا يكون إذا كان هذا إنسانًا كان حيوانا' (particular negative).
4
Understand the dialectical benefit
If you prove that your opponent's statement's contradictory is true, you have effectively proven that your opponent's statement is false, and therefore his proof (based on a false premise) is invalid. Similarly, if you disprove the contradictory of your own claim, you prove your claim true.
This tool is crucial in jadal/munazara to shift the burden of proof.

We will also learn a proof method called hypothesis (الافتراض) that demonstrates the validity of conversion using a syllogism in the third figure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional type
Determine whether the proposition is a connected conditional (e.g., 'Whenever A, then B') or a disjunctive conditional (e.g., 'Either A or B'). Connected conditionals have natural order; disjunctives do not.
2
Apply conversion rule for connected conditionals
For a universal affirmative connected conditional, the converse is a particular affirmative connected conditional. For example, 'كلما كان إنسانًا كان حيوانًا' (universal) converts to 'قد يكون كلما كان حيوانًا كان إنسانًا' (particular).
The converse is weaker; it does not claim universality.
3
Understand why disjunctives lack conversion
Disjunctives have no conversion because the order of disjuncts is arbitrary, chosen by the speaker. Reversing them yields no necessary judgment.
4
Construct the hypothesis proof
Posit a term synonymous with the subject of the original proposition. Then make two premises: predicate the original predicate on that term (P1), and predicate the original subject on that term (P2). These premises form a third-figure categorical syllogism that yields the converse as conclusion.
This method establishes that conversion is a valid immediate inference.

Definition and Domain of Conversion
Author defines conversion and specifies its domain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which propositions admit conversion and which do not, based on natural vs. speaker-chosen order.

Core Idea
Conversion only works when the order of terms is inherent, not arbitrary.

Conversion ('aks) is an immediate inference that swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality. But not every proposition can be converted—only those with a natural order. This lesson clarifies the domain of conversion by distinguishing between categoricals/connected conditionals and disjunctive conditionals.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of 'aks
Conversion is defined as swapping the subject and predicate while keeping the truth value and quality (affirmative/negative) unchanged. This is an immediate inference because it does not require a middle term.
2
Identify 'natural order' propositions
Propositions with natural order (al-tartīb al-ṭabīʿī) have a fixed, inherent relation between subject and predicate. Examples are categoricals and connected conditionals. The order is not arbitrary.
3
Recognize the restriction for disjunctives
Disjunctive conditionals lack natural order because the arrangement of parts depends on the speaker's choice. Consequently, conversion yields no necessary judgment, so it is invalid.
4
Apply the conversion rule to connected conditionals
For universal affirmative connected conditionals, the converse is particular affirmative. Example: 'Whenever something is human, it is animal' converts to 'Whenever something is animal, it is human' (possible, not necessary).
This parallels categorical conversion: A → I.

Proving Conversion by Assumption (Iftirāḍ)
Author presents and illustrates the proof of conversion by iftirāḍ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a proof of conversion using the iftirāḍ method with a categorical syllogism in the third figure.

Core Idea
Conversion is proven by assuming a synonym and constructing a third-figure syllogism.

In this lesson, we examine the proof of conversion ('aks) by iftirāḍ (assumption). The author presents a clear, step-by-step method that uses a synonym of the subject to derive the converse through a categorical syllogism. This is one of three recognized proofs, and it demonstrates the soundness of conversion without relying on modal considerations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its type
Locate the original proposition (al-mun'akis). Its type determines the converse. In the example, 'Every human is an animal' is a universal affirmative (A), which converts to a particular affirmative (I).
2
Assume a synonym for the subject
Choose a word that has the same meaning as the subject of the original. For 'human', the synonym is 'nāṭiq' (rational). This synonym will serve as the middle term.
3
Construct two premises and derive the conclusion
Predicate the original predicate on the synonym (Every nāṭiq is animal) and the original subject on the synonym (Every nāṭiq is human). This yields a third-figure syllogism (middle term is predicate in both premises). The conclusion is 'Some animal is human', which is exactly the converse.
The syllogism mood is Darapti (AAI) in the third figure.

Author defines conversion by contraposition and gives examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contraposition swaps terms with their negations and preserves quality, but changes quantity for universal negatives.

In this lesson, we delve into a powerful immediate inference: conversion by contraposition ('aks al-naqīḍ al-muwāfiq). Unlike simple conversion, contraposition lets you derive a new proposition by replacing the subject and predicate with their opposites while keeping the quality unchanged. We will learn the precise rule and see how it applies to universal affirmatives and negatives, with concrete examples from the text.

Guided Path

Contraposition by the Contradictory (Aks al-Naqid al-Mukhalif)
Defines the second type of contraposition with procedure and example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply 'aks al-naqīḍ al-mukhālif to any categorical proposition and verify the truth equivalence of the result.

Core Idea
Swap subject with contradictory of predicate, flip quality—truth stays preserved.

In this lesson, we examine a specific type of immediate inference known as 'aks al-naqīḍ al-mukhālif (contraposition by the contradictory). Unlike simple conversion, this operation changes both the terms and the quality of the proposition while preserving truth. Mastering this tool is essential for transforming propositions in logical proofs, especially in Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition's form and terms
Determine the quantity (universal/particular) and quality (affirmative/negative). Write the proposition in natural order: subject first, predicate second. Note the first term (subject) and last term (predicate).
For example, 'Every human is an animal' has subject 'human' and predicate 'animal'.
2
Replace the first term with the contradictory of the last
Take the contradictory (negative) of the predicate term. This becomes the new subject. For 'animal', the contradictory is 'non-animal'.
3
Replace the last term with the original first term
The original subject becomes the new predicate. For 'human', it becomes the predicate of the result.
4
Flip the quality
If the original was affirmative, make the new proposition negative; if negative, make it affirmative. This reflects the 'mukhalif' (contradictory) in quality.
This step is crucial—it distinguishes this operation from simple conversion.
5
Verify truth equivalence
Check that the derived proposition is true whenever the original is true. For 'Every human is an animal', the result 'No non-animal is a human' is equivalent: if every human is an animal, then anything that is non-animal cannot be human.
Truth preservation is guaranteed by the rules of the operation.

Using Absoluteness to Prove Possibility in Dialectic
Author explains that the absolute proposition is more specific than the possible, and applies it to the debate on seeing Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the entailment from absoluteness to possibility to counter a dialectical denial of possibility.

Core Idea
Proving actuality entails possibility, but not vice versa.

In dialectic, an opponent may deny that a claim is possible. Instead of directly proving possibility, you can prove the claim is actually true. Because absoluteness (itlāq) is more specific than possibility (imkān), establishing the absolute automatically establishes the possible. This technique is illustrated in the classical debate on the beatific vision: the Mu'tazila deny its possibility, but Ahl al-Sunna can respond by proving its actuality from scriptural sources, thereby entailing possibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modality under dispute
The opponent denies that a certain proposition is possible (e.g., 'Seeing Allah on the Day of Judgment is impossible'). Recognize that the modality at issue is possibility (imkān).
2
Construct a proof for actuality
Instead of directly arguing for possibility, provide evidence that the proposition is actually true (i.e., it is realized in reality). For example, cite Qur'anic verses or hadith that show the actual occurrence of the event.
The proof must be stronger than what the opponent accepts.
3
Apply the entailment rule
Since absoluteness (itlāq) entails possibility (imkān), once you have established the proposition as absolute (actual), you can conclude that it is possible. The opponent's denial of possibility is refuted.
Remember: the converse does not hold; possibility does not entail absoluteness.
4
Anticipate the opponent's objection
The opponent might argue that you have not proven the actuality itself. Ensure that your proof for absoluteness is sound and accepted by the opponent or by the agreed-upon source of knowledge in the dialectical context.

Refuting a Universal Negative with a Particular Affirmative
Author explains how to refute the Jewish universal negative claim by producing a particular affirmative counterexample from the Torah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute a universal negative claim by constructing a particular affirmative counterexample using contradictory opposition.

Core Idea
A single counterexample refutes a universal negative.

In dialectical debate, the most efficient way to refute a universal negative claim is to produce a single counterexample. This is because a universal negative and a particular affirmative are logical contradictories: if one is true, the other must be false. The classic example from the Quran refutes the Jewish claim that Allah never sent down a book to any human by pointing to Musa and the Torah. In this lesson, you will learn how to apply this immediate inference to any similar debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal negative claim
Find the claim made by the opponent expressed as a universal negative (e.g., 'No human received a book from Allah' — a kulliyya saliba).
Universal negatives deny the predicate for every subject.
2
Determine its contradictory
The contradictory of a universal negative is a particular affirmative: 'Some human received a book from Allah.'
In Aristotelian logic, E and I are contradictories.
3
Find a verifying instance
Locate a concrete example that satisfies the particular affirmative. Here, Musa is a human, and the Torah was revealed to him.
The Quranic verse (6:91) provides this instance.
4
Conclude the refutation
Since the particular affirmative is true, the universal negative must be false. The claim is successfully refuted.
No need for a universal affirmative; a single counterexample suffices.

Classification of Tabāyun: Difference and Contrast
Author divides tabāyun into mukhālafa and muqābala, then lists types of muqābala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of terms as either tabāyun mukhālafa or tabāyun muqābala, and within muqābala, distinguish between contradictories (naqīḍān) and contraries (ḍiddān).

Core Idea
Opposition in logic is not just difference—it includes both difference and contrast.

This passage from the Adab al-Bahth tradition lays out the foundational classification of opposition (tabāyun). Understanding this classification is crucial for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences, because the type of opposition between terms determines how they can be used in logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two branches of tabāyun
The author divides tabāyun into 'mukhālafa' (difference) and 'muqābala' (contrast). Difference means the two terms are simply distinct like 'man' and 'horse'; contrast means they are opposed like 'black' and 'white'.
2
Focus on tabāyun muqābala
Under muqābala, there are two types: contradictories (naqīḍān) and contraries (ḍiddān). Memorize their definitions: contradictories cannot be both true nor both false; contraries cannot be both true but can be both false.
The author also mentions two causes for the removal of contraries, but only the first is given: the existence of a third contrary.
3
Apply the classification to a real theological example
Consider the terms 'Muslim' and 'kafir'. They are mutually exclusive and exhaustive for a person's religious status: one cannot be both, and if not one, then the other. Hence they are contradictories (naqīḍān). Contrast this with 'red' and 'green', which are contraries (both cannot be true of the same object but can both be false if the object is blue).
