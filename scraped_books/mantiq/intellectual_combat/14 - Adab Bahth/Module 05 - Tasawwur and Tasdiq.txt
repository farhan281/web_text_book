Classification of Originated Knowledge and Definition of Tasawwur
Author classifies originated knowledge into four types and defines tasawwur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any piece of originated knowledge into one of the four categories and correctly define conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) according to Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Originated knowledge is a 2x2 grid: conceptual vs. assertive, each either necessary or theoretical.

This opening passage lays the foundational taxonomy for all discussions of knowledge in Islamic logic. The author quickly establishes the four-fold classification of originated knowledge and gives the precise definition of conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) as pure apprehension without judgment. Understanding this grid is essential before moving to the rules of definition and syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of classification
The author specifies that the discussion concerns 'originated knowledge' (al-ilm al-hadith), which is the knowledge of creatures, excluding divine knowledge.
This sets the scope: only human knowledge.
2
Recognize the two primary divisions
Originated knowledge is first divided into conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) and assertive knowledge (tasdiq). Conceptual knowledge is apprehension of a meaning without judgment; assertive knowledge involves judgment (true/false).
These are the two fundamental categories.
3
Apply the secondary division to each
Each primary division is further divided into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari). Necessary knowledge requires no reasoning; theoretical knowledge is acquired through inference.
This yields a 2x2 matrix.
4
Compute the four types
Multiplying the two divisions: necessary conceptual, theoretical conceptual, necessary assertive, theoretical assertive. Verify this matches the author's enumeration.
The author lists them in order.
5
Extract the definition of tasawwur
The author defines conceptual knowledge as 'apprehension of the meaning of a single term without intending to affirm anything for it or negate from it'. Identify the key feature: no affirmation or negation.
The example given is the concept of 'pleasure' (al-ladhdha).

Commentary
This passage provides a foundational classification of originated knowledge (al-ʿilm al-ḥādith), which is knowledge pertaining to creatures as opposed to the divine knowledge of Allah. The author begins by stating that the detailed discussion on the definition of knowledge has been omitted for brevity, as the student is expected to grasp its intended meaning. The core classification is then presented: originated knowledge is divided into two primary types: conceptual (taṣawwur) and assertive (taṣdīq). Each of these is further subdivided into necessary (ḍarūrī) and theoretical (naẓarī), yielding four possible categories: (1) necessary conceptual, (2) theoretical conceptual, (3) necessary assertive, and (4) theoretical assertive. The logic mirrors a 2x2 matrix, where the two distinctions intersect. After enumerating the four, the author defines conceptual knowledge (taṣawwur) as the apprehension (idrāk) of the meaning of a single term (maʿnā al-mufrad) without any engagement with affirming or negating a predicate. The example provided is the apprehension of the meaning of 'pleasure' (al-ladhdha). This definition emphasizes that taṣawwur is a pure conceptual grasp without judgment, setting it apart from taṣdīq which involves assent or denial. The passage is concise, as it omits the definition of taṣdīq, likely to be covered later.

Know that acquired knowledge is divided into four divisions: because it is either conceptual knowledge or assent knowledge, and each of them is either necessary or acquired, so the total is four, by multiplying two by two. First: necessary conceptual knowledge. Second: acquired conceptual knowledge. Third: necessary assent knowledge. Fourth: acquired assent knowledge. And know that conceptual knowledge in terminology is the apprehension of the meaning of a single term without attempting to affirm anything for it or negate anything from it, like apprehending the meaning of pleasure, and pain, and the meaning of bitterness, and the meaning of sweetness, and apprehending the meaning of human, and the meaning of writer. So the apprehension of each single term from what we mentioned and its like – i.e., understanding the intended meaning of that single term – without attempting to affirm anything for it or negate anything from it is what is called in terminology conceptual knowledge, and it is a derivation from 'form', as if the form of the single term is impressed in the mind for the conceiver to apprehend its meaning. And apprehension in terminology is: the soul's arrival at the meaning in its entirety; if it arrives at it not in its entirety, then it is what is called in terminology 'awareness'. As for assent knowledge, it is the affirmation of one thing for another actually, or its negation from it actually, and its approximation to the mind: that it is what is called in the terminology of rhetoricians predicative attribution, and in the terminology of grammarians the nominal sentence which is the subject and predicate, or the verbal sentence which is the verb and subject, or the verb and substitute of the subject. So if you say for example: 'The writer is human', then your apprehension of the meaning of (the writer) only is conceptual knowledge, and your apprehension of the meaning of (human) only is conceptual knowledge, and your apprehension that the human is actually a writer, or is not actually a writer, is what is called assent. And it is subject to truth and falsehood, so they called it 'tasdiq' (assent) by the more honorable of the two possibilities. And that assent – which is the apprehension of the occurrence of a relation actually or its non-occurrence actually – is a type of knowledge is the view of the majority, and it is the truth. And we have omitted mentioning what opposes it and refuting it for the sake of brevity.

Commentary
This passage presents the foundational classification of acquired knowledge (al-ilm al-hadith) into four types. The author begins by explaining that acquired knowledge is divided into conceptual knowledge ('ilm tasawwur) and assent knowledge ('ilm tasdiq), and each of these is further divided into necessary (daruri) and acquired (nazari). Multiplying two by two yields four categories: necessary conceptual, acquired conceptual, necessary assent, and acquired assent. The author then defines tasawwur: it is the apprehension of the meaning of a single term without any judgment of affirmation or negation. Examples include apprehending the meanings of pleasure, pain, bitterness, sweetness, human, and writer. The term 'tasawwur' derives from 'sura' (form), as if the form of the concept is impressed in the mind. Apprehension (idrak) is defined as the soul's attainment of the meaning in its entirety; partial attainment is called awareness (shu'ur). Next, tasdiq is defined as the actual affirmation or negation of one thing for another, equivalent to predicative attribution in rhetoric and to the nominal or verbal sentence in grammar. For example, saying 'the writer is human' involves separate conceptual apprehensions of 'writer' and 'human', but the judgment that the human is actually a writer constitutes tasdiq. Tasdiq is subject to truth and falsehood, and it is called 'tasdiq' (assent) by the more honorable of the two possibilities (since it can be true or false, they named it after truth). The author asserts that the majority view—that tasdiq is a type of knowledge—is correct, and omits counterarguments for brevity.

Author defines tasawwur as apprehension without judgment and distinguishes idrak from shu'ur.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tasawwur is the apprehension of a simple meaning without any judgment.

Locate the definition of tasawwur

Understand idrak vs shu'ur

Study the examples

He then reports a debate: is the act of assent itself a simple fourth conception, or is it composite, made up of all four? Assent and Concept: The Logical Prelude
Author states the relationship between concept and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why every assent (tasdiq) necessarily presupposes a concept (tasawwur), and why not every concept amounts to assent.

Core Idea
Every assent requires a concept, but a concept alone is not yet assent.

The author opens this foundational passage with a crucial epistemological distinction: all acts of judgment (tasdiq) are built upon prior simple conceptions (tasawwur). Before you can affirm or deny anything, you must first have a clear concept of the terms involved. This hierarchy is essential for rigorous reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two kinds of cognition
Identify that Islamic logicians divide knowledge into tasawwur (simple conception) and tasdiq (assent or judgment). The passage mentions both, so note that they are the two key categories.
2
Locate the key claim
The first sentence states: 'كل تصديق تصور' (every assent is a concept). This means assent necessarily includes a concept; you cannot have assent without first having conceived the subject and predicate.
3
Understand the dependency
Since assent involves affirming or denying a predicate of a subject, you must already have the concepts of the subject and predicate. Thus, assent is built upon prior concepts.
4
Note the asymmetry
The passage also says 'وليس كل تصور تصديقاً' (not every concept is an assent). A concept can be held without any judgment, such as simply understanding the meaning of 'Zaid' without affirming anything about him.
This asymmetry establishes that concepts are more fundamental than assents.

Subject and Predicate in Logic and Grammar
Author defines subject and predicate and relates them to grammar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical subject and predicate in a proposition and distinguish them from their grammatical counterparts.

Core Idea
The subject is the foundation; the predicate is carried by it — like a building's foundation and roof.

Having established the relationship between concept and assent, the author now defines the two core components of any proposition: subject (mawdu') and predicate (mahmul). He explains their logical roles, relates them to grammatical and semantic categories, and uses the vivid analogy of a foundation and roof to clarify why these terms are so named.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logical subject and predicate
The author defines mawdu' as what is known in semantics as al-musnad ilayh and in grammar as mubtada' (topic) or fa'il (agent). Mahmul is al-musnad in semantics and khabar (predicate) or fi'l (verb) in grammar.
The logical subject is the entity about which something is said; the logical predicate is what is said about it.
2
Understand the foundation analogy
The subject is called mawdu' because it serves as the foundation (asas) for the predicate, just as a building's foundation supports the roof. The predicate is called mahmul because it is 'carried' by the subject.
3
Apply to examples
In 'Zaid is knowledgeable' and 'Zaid is hitting', Zaid is the subject (foundation), and 'knowledgeable' and 'hitting' are predicates (attribute/action carried by Zaid).
The predicate cannot exist without the subject — an attribute needs a described object, an action needs an agent.
4
Connect to grammar
The logician's subject is broader than the grammatical subject: it includes both the mubtada' in nominal sentences and the fa'il in verbal sentences. This broader scope is crucial for analyzing propositions in Islamic scholarly texts.

Necessary vs Theoretical Knowledge
Classification of knowledge into necessary/theoretical and definitions of nazar/fikr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given piece of knowledge as necessary or theoretical, and distinguish between nazar and fikr.

Core Idea
Knowledge that requires reflection is theoretical; self-evident knowledge is necessary.

In the previous lesson, we learned about concepts (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). Now we see that each of these may be either necessary (daruri) or theoretical (nazari). This distinction is foundational for understanding how we acquire knowledge in the Islamic sciences. Identify the two categories
Note that both concepts and assents are divided into necessary and theoretical. Necessary knowledge is self-evident; theoretical knowledge requires investigation.

Nazar is thought that leads to knowledge or predominant opinion. Fikr is the movement of the soul among intelligibles. Contrast with takhayyul which deals with sensibles.

The Means to Theoretical Concepts: The Definers
Introduction to the types of definitions for acquiring theoretical concepts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five types of definers (al-mu'arrafat) and distinguish between them.

Core Idea
Theoretical concepts are acquired through definitions that clarify meaning.

Having established that theoretical knowledge requires reflection, the author now introduces the tools by which we acquire theoretical concepts. These are the definers (al-mu'arrafat), which include five types: definition (hadd), description (rasm), verbal definition (lafzi), division (qisma), and example (mithal). Collectively, they are called explanatory discourse (al-qawl al-sharih).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the role of definers
Understand that theoretical concepts are not self-evident; they must be acquired through some defining method. The author lists five methods, which will be explained later.
2
Identify each type of definer
The five types are: hadd (definition by genus and differentia), rasm (description by properties), lafzi (verbal definition, e.g., synonym), qisma (division), and mithal (example).
These are collectively called al-qawl al-sharih (explanatory discourse).
3
Relate definers to Islamic sciences
Consider how each type is used in fiqh, usul, or kalam. For instance, hadd is used to define terms like 'salah' or 'riba'; rasm describes something by its characteristics; lafzi clarifies by synonym; qisma breaks a concept into parts; mithal gives an instance.

Conceptual Knowledge (Tasawwur) vs Assent (Tasdiq)
Author divides knowledge into conceptual and assertive with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) from assertive knowledge (tasdiq) using examples from the passage.

Core Idea
Tasawwur grasps a meaning; tasdiq judges it true or false.

After dividing knowledge into necessary and theoretical, the author further splits each into conceptual and assertive. This distinction is crucial because definitions produce concepts, while proofs produce judgments. Let's understand the difference.

Guided Path
1
Define tasawwur and tasdiq
Tasawwur is the simple apprehension of a meaning without affirming or denying anything. Tasdiq is the assent that affirms or denies something about a concept.
Tasawwur is like having the concept of 'tree'; tasdiq is saying 'the tree is tall'.
2
Identify tasawwur examples
In the passage, tasawwur examples: concept of 'one', 'two', angels, Paradise, the doctor's diagnosis of a disease. These are concepts without judgment.
Note that even complex concepts like 'angels' are still tasawwur if no judgment is made.
3
Identify tasdiq examples
Tasdiq examples: 'one is half of two', 'the whole is greater than the part', mathematical statements. These are judgments that can be true or false.
The examples show that tasdiq involves a relation between concepts.
4
Understand the relationship
Tasawwur is simpler and more basic; tasdiq builds on concepts. All tasdiq contains tasawwur of its terms, but not vice versa.
This is why definitions (mu'arrafat) lead to tasawwur, and proofs (hujja) lead to tasdiq.

Tools for Theoretical Knowledge: Definitions and Proofs
Author states the paths to theoretical knowledge: definitions and proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two main tools for acquiring theoretical knowledge: definitions (mu'arrafat) for concepts and proofs (hujja) for judgments.

Core Idea
Definitions clarify concepts; proofs establish truths.

The passage concludes by telling us the pathways to theoretical knowledge. For conceptual knowledge, we use definitions (mu'arrafat) of various types. For assent, we use proofs (hujja), especially the logical syllogism. This sets the agenda for the entire study of logic.

1
Identify the tool for theoretical tasawwur

2
Identify the tool for theoretical tasdiq

The most certain syllogism is 'burhan' (demonstration), composed of certain premises.
3
Understand the purpose of each tool
Definitions give you a clear concept; proofs give you certain or probable judgment. Both are necessary for complete knowledge.
The rest of the logic course will explain each of these tools in detail.
4
Apply the distinction
When you encounter a new theoretical matter, ask: Do I need to clarify a concept (use definitions) or do I need to prove or disprove a claim (use syllogism)?
This helps you choose the correct logical method.

Proof and Demonstration in Islamic Logic
Definition of proof and demonstration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hujja (proof) and burhan (demonstration) and identify burhan as a syllogism composed of certain premises.

Core Idea
Proof is the logical syllogism; demonstration is proof from certain premises.

This passage establishes the foundational epistemological tool of Islamic logic: the path to theoretical assent is proof (al-hujja), which the author identifies with the logical syllogism in its various forms. Among proofs, a special subset called demonstration (al-burhan) is singled out for yielding certainty by being composed of certain premises. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the goal: theoretical assent
The passage begins by stating that the aim is knowledge of theoretical assent (al-tasdiq al-nazari). This is a judgment about reality, as opposed to mere conception (tasawwur).
Recall the earlier lesson on the two types of knowledge: tasawwur and tasdiq.
2
Locate the path: al-hujja
The text says the path to theoretical assent is al-hujja (proof). It immediately equates this with al-qiyas al-mantiqi (the logical syllogism) in its various types. So proof here is not any argument, but specifically the syllogism.
Other forms of argument (e.g., induction, analogy) are not considered proof in this technical sense.
3
Distinguish the special type: al-burhan
The text adds that any syllogism composed of certain premises (muqaddimat yaqiniyya) is called burhan (demonstration). This is a subset of hujja that guarantees certainty.
Burhan corresponds to what Aristotle called a demonstration — a syllogism that produces scientific knowledge.
4
Apply the distinction to an Islamic science example
Consider a theological argument: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' If the premises are certain (e.g., from revelation or necessary reason), this is a burhan. If the premises are only probable, it remains a hujja but not a burhan.
In Kalam, proving the existence of God requires a burhan, i.e., certain premises.

This lesson focuses on the first type of linguistic indication: conformity (muṭābaqah). In logic, understanding how a word directly conveys its full meaning is foundational before exploring parts or implications. The source defines conformity clearly with examples like 'man' and 'woman'.

The author provides 'رجل' (man) indicating 'إنسان ذكر' (human male), and 'امرأة' (woman) indicating 'إنسان أنثى' (human female). These illustrate that the word conveys the complete meaning without remainder.

Author gives three criteria to differentiate essential from accidental attributes

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Essential attributes define an essence; accidental ones are dispensable.

In logic, distinguishing essential from accidental attributes is crucial for sound definitions. The author presents three tests: whether the essence can be conceived without the attribute, whether the attribute has a cause, and whether removing the attribute destroys the essence. These criteria help clarify why 'rational' is essential to humans but 'laughing' is not.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
The passage asks: Why is 'rational' considered part of the human essence while 'laughing' is not? Recognize that this distinction is fundamental to defining things correctly.
2
First criterion: Perception without the attribute
Ask: Can the reality of the essence be perceived without this attribute? For 'rational', no; for 'laughing', yes. This is the 'defining attribute' (al-sifa al-nafsiyya) test from theology.
This matches the concept of essential property in kalam.
3
Second criterion: Causality
Check whether the attribute has an external cause. Essential attributes are uncaused (e.g., rationality is intrinsic); accidental attributes have a cause (e.g., laughter is caused by humor).
If an attribute is caused, it is likely accidental.
4
Third criterion: Hypothetical removal
Imagine removing the attribute mentally. If the essence ceases to exist, it is essential (e.g., remove rationality, no human); if the essence remains, it is accidental (e.g., remove laughter, human remains).
This is a common test in logic.
5
Apply all three to a new example
Take the attribute 'walking' for humans. Test: Can human be conceived without walking? (yes, as a newborn). Is walking caused? (yes, by muscles). If walking removed, does human cease? (no). Thus, walking is accidental. Practice with other attributes.

Chapter on Assent and Explanation of Disputation Concerning It. (1) We have already presented the definition of assent with clarification, and mentioned its division into necessary and theoretical, and gave examples of that in logical terminology. And according to the practitioners of the art, it is divided into immediate assent and theoretical assent. Immediate assent according to them is that which does not need consideration and inference, whether it needs prompting or does not need it. Immediate assent according to them is divided by the first division into two parts: the first is the clear immediate, and the second is the hidden immediate. The clear immediate is divided according to them into four sections: The first: the primary immediate, whose criterion is that it is every proposition in which the mind judges the affirmation of its predicate to its subject merely by conceiving them—I mean the subject and predicate—without needing any mediating term at all, like your saying: 'One is half of two,' 'The whole is greater than the part,' 'Contradictories neither coexist nor are both absent,' and so on; for merely your conceiving of one and half and two, and your conceiving of whole, greater, and part, and your conceiving of contradictories and of absence and coexistence, makes you judge the affirmation of the predicate to the subject in the three mentioned examples as is clear without needing anything extra. The second section of the divisions of the clear immediate: what they call the innate clear immediate, whose criterion is that it is every proposition in which the mind judges the affirmation of the predicate to the subject after conceiving them by means of a natural categorical syllogism, that it is in the sense of being ingrained in human nature that cannot be absent from one's mind; due to the necessity of its perception following the perception of the two terms of the proposition, like the judgment that four is even; because when you conceive the meaning of four and evenness, the mind necessarily perceives that it is a number divisible into two equal parts, and perceives that every number so divisible is even. The form of the mentioned natural syllogism is that you say: Four is a number divisible into two equal parts, and every number that is like that is even, yielding from the first figure: Four is even. This syllogism is not absent from your mind, and this result is what is called the innate clear immediate. And we have already presented what increases this clarification in the discussion of types of signification in the topic of the necessary concomitant in the mind and outside together. The third type of the types of clear immediate: the experimental, whose criterion is that it is every proposition in which the mind judges the affirmation of the predicate to the subject by means of experimentation and repeated observations beneficial for knowledge that this repeated occurrence in one pattern without exception must have a cause, even if one does not know the reality of this cause. This section is clear, and among its examples are your saying: Water quenches, bread saturates, fire burns, scammony purges bile, oxymel soothes it, and so on, because they are propositions known by experimentation, and the repetition of observation of the sequence of the effect in them upon the cause, so certainty about them is obtained by means of that.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of types of immediate knowledge (tasdiq badihi). The author begins by reminding that tasdiq (assent) is divided into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari), but here he focuses on the immediate (badihi) assent, which is further divided into clear (jali) and hidden (khafi). This classification shows that 'immediate' knowledge is not monolithic; it ranges from self-evident axioms to knowledge derived from innate reasoning to empirical generalizations. The Three Types of Tasdiq in Debate
Author classifies tasdiq into three types and explains their debate status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any proposition as clear self-evident (badihi jali), hidden self-evident (badihi khafi), or theoretical (nazari) based on the criteria in the passage.

Core Idea
Tasdiq is threefold: clear self-evident, hidden self-evident, and theoretical—only the latter two are open to debate.

This passage establishes the foundational epistemology of Islamic debate by classifying all propositions into three categories. You must understand these categories to know when you can challenge an assertion and when you must accept it as axiomatic. The author provides clear definitions and examples that will serve as your guide.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of tasdiq
Find in the Arabic the three divisions: badihi jali, badihi khafi, and tasdiq nazari. Note that the author calls them 'three divisions' (ثلاثة أقسام).
2
Extract the defining characteristics
For each type, note the key feature: badihi jali requires no reflection and is immediately known; badihi khafi is also self-evident but requires a tanbih (reminder) to become clear; nazari requires full inference (istidlal) and proof (dalil).
3
Apply criteria to given examples
The author gives examples: 'the whole is greater than the part' (badihi jali), the mutawatir report of Baghdad (badihi khafi), and 'I am created, so I need a creator' (nazari). Classify each using the criteria.
4
Recognize the debate status of each type
The passage states that badihi jali is not debatable—disputing it is called mukabara (obstinacy). Badihi khafi and nazari are the only types subject to debate. Understand why: badihi jali is too obvious to legitimately contest.

The clear, and two of the hidden type. Know that the other type of the two types of assent is theoretical assent, and we have previously stated that the theoretical is that which in its perception requires reflection. The definition of theoretical assent according to the people of this art is: every proposition whose predicate's affirmation for its subject the intellect does not judge except after examination of it and inference upon it, like your saying: The world is originated and its Creator is eternal, meaning that His existence has no beginning. You have already known from what has passed that assent is three divisions: the first is self-evident clear assent, the second is self-evident hidden assent, and the third is theoretical assent. As for clear self-evident assent, it is not a subject for debate, and it is not permissible for anyone to object to it at all, and disputing it is called obstinacy (mukabara), which is unacceptable, and its practitioner does not desire truth but rather desires falsehood, such as raising his own status and lowering that of his opponent. So if someone says to you: the whole is greater than the part, one is half of two, four is even, the sun is bright, water quenches thirst, and the like, you have no right to dispute any of that; rather you must accept it because it is self-evident clear. As for hidden self-evident [assent], it does not need a proof because it is self-evident; it only needs a reminder (tanbih). And 'tanbih' in their terminology is a compound [argument] intended to remove concealment, not inference. An example of it in intuition is the conjunctive syllogism we mentioned concerning that the rise of river water is the cause of the rise of well water, and we explained that al-Bannani said: contemplating the middle term in it is sufficient for perceiving the desired conclusion. An example of tanbih in mutawatir is that you say: The existence of Baghdad has been reported by a number whose collusion upon lying is customarily impossible, and everything reported by such a number is true; this yields from the first figure: The existence of Baghdad is true. This syllogism is for tanbih, not for inference, because it is known that what is reported by that number is true, and because mutawatirat are among self-evident matters. An example of inference upon theoretical assent is your saying: I am created after I was non-existent, and everything created after being non-existent must have a creator; this yields from the first figure: I must have a creator. And you have known from what has passed that debate concerning assent occurs only in two types, no third: the first is theoretical assent, which needs proof, and the second is hidden self-evident assent, which needs tanbih. These are the details of the methods of debate in them: Know first that when a proponent of assent presents to you a theoretical assent or a hidden self-evident assent, each of them has two states: As for the two states of theoretical assent, the first of them is that he presents it to you stripped of proof, in order to falsely suggest that it is among the self-evident propositions that need no proof.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of assent (tasdiq) and explains their roles in debate. The author begins by recalling that assent is divided into three: clear self-evident, hidden self-evident, and theoretical. The clear self-evident (badihi jali) is not open to debate—disputing it is considered obstinacy (mukabara), as it is immediately known, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part' or 'the sun is bright'. The hidden self-evident (badihi khafi) does not require a full proof (dalil) but only a reminder (tanbih) to bring it to awareness; the author gives examples like the mutawatir report of Baghdad's existence, which is known through widespread transmission but may need a syllogism to make explicit the logical necessity. The theoretical assent (tasdiq nazari) requires genuine inference (istidlal) via a proof, exemplified by the syllogism inferring that one must have a Creator because one was created after non-existence. The author then states that debate is only valid on two types: theoretical and hidden self-evident, because the clear self-evident is beyond dispute. Finally, he introduces that each of these two types has two states when presented in debate, beginning with the first state of theoretical assent: when it is presented without proof to falsely appear self-evident.

Debatable Assent: Tanbih and Istidlal
Author distinguishes between tanbih and istidlal in debate on hidden self-evident and theoretical assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a hidden self-evident proposition (requiring tanbih) and a theoretical proposition (requiring istidlal) in a debate context, using the passage's examples.

Core Idea
Only theoretical and hidden self-evident assents are debatable—the former needs proof, the latter only a reminder.

The passage specifies that clear self-evident propositions are not debatable. This lesson focuses on the two debatable types and the distinct argumentative tools they require: tanbih (reminder) for hidden self-evident, and istidlal (inference) for theoretical. Understanding the difference prevents applying the wrong argumentative method.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two debatable types
The passage states that debate occurs only on two types: theoretical assent (nazari) and hidden self-evident assent (badihi khafi). Note that clear self-evident is excluded.
2
Define tanbih and istidlal as per the passage
Tanbih is a compound argument that removes hiddenness without being a full proof; it is used for badihi khafi. Istidlal is full inference requiring a dalil, used for nazari. The author says tanbih 'does not need a proof' but only a reminder.
3
Analyze the example of tanbih: mutawatir report
The author gives the syllogism: 'The existence of Baghdad is reported by a number whose collusion on lying is impossible; everything reported by such a number is true; therefore, the existence of Baghdad is true.' He says this is a tanbih, not istidlal, because mutawatirat are self-evident (badihi) and the syllogism only clarifies.
4
Analyze the example of istidlal: creation argument
The author gives: 'I am created after non-existence; everything created after non-existence must have a creator; therefore, I must have a creator.' This is istidlal because it requires inference to reach a conclusion not known immediately.
5
Distinguish the two by the role of proof
In tanbih, the conclusion is already implicitly known; the syllogism merely makes it explicit. In istidlal, the conclusion is not known before the proof. The passage says for badihi khafi 'it does not need a proof because it is self-evident; it only needs a reminder.'
