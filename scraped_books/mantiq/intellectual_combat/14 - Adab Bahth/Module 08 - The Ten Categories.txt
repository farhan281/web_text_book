Classifying Nouns as Universal or Particular
Author states consensus on particular and universal extremes, and discusses disputed intermediate words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of nouns are universally agreed to be particular or universal, and explain the dispute over demonstratives, pronouns, and relative pronouns.

Core Idea
Language has clear-cut universal and particular nouns, but some words blur the line.

In this passage, the author presents a consensus on the extremes: proper nouns for persons and genera are particular; common nouns for genera are universal. But between them lies a disputed category: words like 'this' and 'that'. You will learn to locate where scholars agree and where they disagree.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extremes
From the passage, extract which nouns are particular by consensus (e.g., 'alam al-shakhs and 'alam al-jins) and which are universal by consensus (ism al-jins like 'asad', 'insan', 'rajul').
These extremes are explicitly stated with the phrase 'bil-ijma'' (by consensus).
2
Understand the basis of consensus
Why are proper nouns particular? Because they refer to a specific individual and cannot be predicated of many. Why are common nouns universal? Because they can be applied to any member of a class.
This aligns with the definitions of juz'i and kulli given earlier.
3
Locate the intermediate category
The author mentions three types of words that are disputed: asma' al-ishara (demonstratives), dama'ir (pronouns), and mawusulat (relative pronouns).
These are called 'al-wasita' (the intermediate) because they fall between the clear extremes.
4
Analyze the arguments for each view
One group argues they are universal because they can be used for any referent (e.g., 'hadha' can point to anything). Another argues they are particular because in actual usage they always refer to a specific thing. A third says they are universal in wad' (positing) but particular in usage.
The passage presents these positions without extensive justification.
5
Note the resolution
The author states that most specialists in 'ilm al-wad' choose the view that these words are particular. The passage ends with 'Allahu a'lam'.
This is not a decisive resolution but indicates a preferred scholarly opinion.

Univocal and Ambiguous Universals
Author divides the universal into univocal and ambiguous with definitions and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between univocal and ambiguous universals and identify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A universal may apply equally or by degrees—univocal vs. ambiguous.

In logic, universals are not all the same. Some apply equally to all their individuals, while others apply with gradation. This lesson from the adab al-bahth tradition clarifies this distinction, which is crucial for understanding how terms are used in definitions and analogical reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Locate the two Arabic terms متواطىء (univocal) and مشكك (ambiguous) in the passage. They are the two types of universals being introduced.
2
Examine the defining criterion
The passage states the division is based on whether the meaning is equal (استوت) among individuals or varies (تفاوت). Understand that this is the core distinction.
3
Analyze the examples
For univocal, the text gives 'human', 'man', 'woman'—all share the essence equally. For ambiguous, it gives 'light' and 'whiteness'—these differ in intensity among individuals.
Consider why the author chose these examples.
4
Understand the explanatory note
The author notes that for univocal, differences are due to additional attributes beyond the essence; for ambiguous, differences are in the core meaning itself (strength/weakness).
This clarifies that the division is about the universal itself, not accidental attributes.

Sixfold Division of Universals by External Existence
Classifies universals into six types based on external existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify universals into the six categories based on the existence or non-existence of their individuals in the external world, and identify the first two types with their rational status.

Core Idea
Universals are classified by the existence of their individuals, yielding six categories from three binary splits.

This passage introduces a detailed classification of universals that goes beyond simple definitions. By understanding whether a universal has no individuals, one individual, or many individuals—and whether those individuals are possible or impossible to exist—you gain a precise tool for analyzing concepts in Islamic logic and theology. This classification is fundamental for evaluating definitions and arguments about existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main branches
The author begins by noting that a universal can have no individuals, one individual, or many individuals in the external world. This gives three primary categories.
These are determined solely by the count of existing individuals, not by possibility.
2
Split each branch into two based on possibility
Each of the three categories is further subdivided: the existence of an individual may be rationally impossible (mustahil 'aqlan) or rationally possible (ja'iz 'aqlan). This creates six total divisions.
For the 'many individuals' case, the subdivision would be based on the actual status of those individuals (e.g., possible or impossible).
3
Apply to the first two types
Type 1: No individual exists, and existence is impossible (e.g., conjunction of opposites). Type 2: No individual exists, but existence is possible (e.g., a sea of mercury). These are complete examples from the text.
The third type (one individual exists) is cut off but follows the same logic.

Understanding Tabayun and Its Two Types
Author defines tabayun and its division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify tabayun as a relation of mutual exclusion between concepts and distinguish between tabayun al-mukhalafa and tabayun al-muqabala.

Core Idea
Opposition does not always mean complete incompatibility; some opposites can coexist.

In Islamic logic, the relations between terms are fundamental for constructing sound definitions and arguments. The author introduces tabayun (opposition) as one of four such relations and immediately divides it into two subtypes. Understanding this division helps you avoid the common mistake of assuming that all opposites are mutually exclusive in existence.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four term relations
The four relations are tasawi (equivalence), umum/khusus mutlaq (absolute general-specific), umum/khusus min wajh (general-specific from one aspect), and tabayun (opposition). Tabayun means the two terms have no common instances.
2
Identify the two divisions of tabayun
The author divides tabayun into tabayun al-mukhalafa (opposition of difference) and tabayun al-muqabala (opposition of contrast). The passage only elaborates on the first.
3
Understand tabayun al-mukhalafa
In tabayun al-mukhalafa, the two essences are mutually exclusive (neither is the other) but they do not have extreme incompatibility (ghayat al-munafah), meaning they can coexist in a single subject.
Extreme incompatibility would prevent coexistence.
4
Study the examples given
The author provides three examples: coldness and whiteness (snow is both), sweetness and blackness (black date is both), speech and sitting (a person can be seated and speaking). Each pair shows distinct essences that can coexist.

And the rule of tabayun al-mukhalafa is that the two realities are mutually opposed in their essences, but there is no extreme incompatibility between them, meaning that they can coexist in one essence. So the reality of cold, for example, opposes the reality of whiteness, because every meaning established for it as cold is negated for it as whiteness, and vice versa, but there is no obstacle to cold and whiteness coexisting in one essence like snow, for it is white and cold. And like sweetness and blackness, and like speech and sitting; for the reality of sweetness opposes the reality of blackness, but there is no extreme incompatibility between them, meaning that it is permissible for them to coexist in one locus like the black date, for it combines sweetness and blackness. Commentary
This passage defines tabayun (opposition), one of the four term relations, and divides it into two types: tabayun al-mukhalafa (opposition of difference) and tabayun al-muqabala (opposition of contrast). The author only elaborates on the first type. The definition given for tabayun al-mukhalafa is that two realities are mutually exclusive in their essences (i.e., neither is the same as the other), but they do not have complete incompatibility (غاية المنافاة), meaning they can coexist in a single subject. The author provides several examples: (1) coldness and whiteness – cold and white are different realities; a thing can be both cold and white, like snow. (2) Sweetness and blackness – a black date is both sweet and black; these properties are distinct but can coexist. (3) Speech and sitting – a person can be seated and speaking simultaneously. In each case, the two realities are mutually opposed (one is not the other), but they do not contradict each other such that one forces the absence of the other. The author does not elaborate on the second division, tabayun al-muqabala (opposition of contrast), which presumably involves complete incompatibility (like black and white as colors? or existence and non-existence), but it is not discussed here. This passage is crucial for understanding how terms can be related without being entirely separate; it shows that opposition does not necessarily imply impossibility of coexistence.

Verifying Tabayun al-Mukhalafa with Examples
Author illustrates tabayun al-mukhalafa with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a given pair of concepts meets the conditions for tabayun al-mukhalafa: mutually exclusive essences but no extreme incompatibility.

Core Idea
Two things can be different in essence yet perfectly compatible in existence.

Now that you know the definition of tabayun al-mukhalafa, this lesson sharpens your ability to apply it. The author's examples—cold/white, sweet/black, speech/sitting—are your practice ground. By the end, you will be able to decide for any new pair whether it falls under tabayun al-mukhalafa or its opposite, tabayun al-muqabala.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two essences being compared
Extract the two realities (haqiqatan) from the pair. For example, coldness and whiteness are distinct essences: coldness is a temperature quality, whiteness is a color quality.
2
Check mutual exclusivity of essences
Verify that neither essence is the same as the other. If something is cold, it is not whiteness; if something is white, it is not coldness. They are mutually exclusive in definition.
This is the 'tabayun' aspect.
3
Test for extreme incompatibility
Ask: Is there a real-world subject where both can be true together? If yes, there is no extreme incompatibility (ghayat al-munafah). For cold and white, snow is a valid subject. For sweetness and blackness, a black date works.
Extreme incompatibility would mean impossibility of coexistence in any subject.
4
Conclude the type
If essences are mutually exclusive and there is at least one possible subject where both coexist, it is tabayun al-mukhalafa. If no such subject exists (e.g., black and white on the same surface), it would be tabayun al-muqabala.
The passage does not specify the second type, but you can infer it.

The author begins with a notification (تنبيه) to the student that the term 'influencer' (المؤثر) as used by both the people of truth (Ahl al-Haqq, i.e., Muslims) and the people of falsehood (Ahl al-Batil, i.e., philosophers and naturalists) is restricted to three types. He enumerates them. The first is the influencer by choice (المؤثر بالاختيار), which is the Creator, Allah, whose power and will are behind every occurrence. This is the only true influencer according to Ahl al-Haqq. The second is the influencer by nature (المؤثر بالطبيعة), posited by naturalists (الطبائعيون), such as fire's burning. The third is the influencer by cause (المؤثر بالعلة), which is a necessary causal relation like the movement of a ring following the movement of the finger, as held by philosophers. The author then provides a proof (برهان) that these three are exhaustive. The proof proceeds by a logical division: the influencer, considered in itself, either can or cannot leave its effect. If it can, it is the influencer by choice (since it selects effect over none). If it cannot, then its effect either depends on conditions (شروط) and absence of impediments (موانع) or not. If it depends, it is the influencer by nature (like fire — it must burn, but only if the fuel is present and no water impedes). If it does not depend, it is the influencer by cause (a necessary, immediate causal connection). This division is exhaustive and mutually exclusive, proving that any influencer must fall into one of these three categories. Note that the author uses the term 'influencer' in a broad sense that includes both true (divine) and false (natural/causal) influences. The proof relies on a dichotomous classification based on the possibility of leaving the effect, which is a standard logical technique.

The Four Logical Relations Between Universals
Author enumerates the four logical relations and provides a proof of their enumeration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four logical relations holds between any two given universal terms and justify your choice with an example from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The four relations are the only possible ways two universals can be related.

In this lesson, you will encounter the four relations that govern how two universal terms (kulliyyāt) relate to each other. This classification is fundamental for understanding definitions, syllogisms, and debates in the Islamic logical tradition. The passage enumerates them and claims a proof, though the proof is not given here. Your task is to internalize these four relations and be able to apply them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four relations by name and definition
Memorize the Arabic terms: التباين (complete opposition), المساواة (equality), العموم والخصوص المطلق (absolute generality/specificity), and العموم والخصوص من وجه (generality from one aspect). Understand that these are the only possible relations between any two universal terms.
2
Associate each relation with an example from Islamic sciences
For each relation, recall a pair of terms: e.g., human and stone (opposition), human and rational animal (equality), animal and human (absolute generality), white and animal (generality from one aspect). Islamic contexts: e.g., 'wājib' and 'ḥarām' (opposition), 'insān' and 'nāṭiq' (equality), 'ḥayawān' and 'insān' (absolute generality), 'abyaḍ' and 'ḥayawān' (generality from one aspect).
3
Determine the relation by checking coextensiveness and inclusion
Given any two universals, ask: Are their extensions completely disjoint? (opposition) Do they have exactly the same extension? (equality) Does one fully contain the other? (absolute generality) Do they partially overlap? (generality from one aspect). This logical test yields the correct relation.
4
Verify the claim that only four relations exist
Note that any two universals must be either identical in extension (equality), non-overlapping (opposition), one-way inclusion (absolute generality), or partial overlap (generality from one aspect). No other logical possibility exists; this is a complete enumeration.

The Four Logical Relations and Their Truth Conditions
Outline of the four logical relations and their truth conditions in propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four logical relations between two terms and determine the truth values of universal and particular propositions based on those relations.

Core Idea
The four logical relations dictate which propositions about two terms are true and which are false.

This passage presents a foundational classification in Islamic logic: the four possible relations between any two terms (disparity, equality, absolute generality, and partial overlap). Understanding these relations is essential for evaluating categorical propositions, as each relation imposes a specific pattern of truth and falsehood for universal and particular affirmatives and negatives. By mastering this, you gain a precise tool for assessing logical relationships in any Islamic science discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
In a proposition, locate the subject and predicate. For example, in 'Every human is an animal', the terms are 'human' and 'animal'.
2
Determine the logical relation
Check whether the two terms: (1) have no overlap (disparity), (2) are fully coextensive (equality), (3) one fully contains the other (absolute generality/specificity), or (4) partially overlap (generality from one aspect).
Use examples like 'human' and 'horse' (disparity), 'human' and 'rational animal' (equality), 'animal' and 'human' (absolute generality), 'human' and 'white' (partial overlap).
3
Recall the truth condition
From the passage, for each relation the truth pattern is fixed: disparity -> both negatives true, both affirmatives false; equality -> both affirmatives true, both negatives false; absolute generality (subject more specific) -> both affirmatives true; (subject more general) -> both particulars true, universals false; partial overlap -> both particulars true, universals false.
Memorize: sidq al-salibayn wa kidhb al-ījābayn for disparity; sidq al-ījābayn wa kidhb al-salibayn for equality; etc.
4
Apply to the four proposition forms
Using the truth condition, determine the truth of the universal affirmative (A), universal negative (E), particular affirmative (I), and particular negative (O). For example, in disparity: A false, E true, I false, O true.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of the four relations that can hold between two terms in logic, and it explains how these relations determine the truth values of universal and particular propositions in which those terms appear as subject and predicate. The four relations are: (1) Disparity (tabāyun) – where the two terms have no overlap; (2) Equality (musāwāh) – where they are coextensive; (3) Absolute generality and specificity (al-ʻumūm wa al-khuṣūṣ al-muṭlaq) – where one term is entirely contained within the other but not vice versa; and (4) Generality and specificity from one aspect (al-ʻumūm wa al-khuṣūṣ min wajh) – where they partially overlap. The text then gives the truth conditions: In disparity, the two negatives are true and the two affirmatives false (e.g., 'No human is a horse' and 'No horse is a human' are true, while 'Every human is a horse' is false). In equality, both affirmatives are true and both negatives false. In generality and specificity from one aspect, the particulars are true and the universals false. When the subject is absolutely more specific, the affirmatives are true (every member of the specific is a member of the general) and the negatives false. When the subject is absolutely more general, the particulars are true (some general are specific) and the universals false. These rules are foundational for evaluating categorical propositions in logic.
