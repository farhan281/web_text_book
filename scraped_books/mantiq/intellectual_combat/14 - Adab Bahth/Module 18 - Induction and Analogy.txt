Using Division and Examination (Sabr wa Taqsīm) as a Proof
Author explains division and sabr as a proof method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of exhaustive division and elimination (sabr) to identify the correct proposition or cause in a given theological or usul al-fiqh problem.

Core Idea
Divide exhaustively, eliminate the false, and the remainder is the truth.

This passage introduces a powerful logical tool: combining exhaustive division (taqsīm) with critical examination (sabr) to arrive at certainty. You'll see how this method works in two domains: proving the existence of a Creator and determining the effective cause (ʿilla) in juristic reasoning. By the end, you'll be able to replicate this reasoning in your own analyses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exhaustive division
Find where the author lists all possible alternatives. In the theological example, the division is threefold: creation without a creator, self-creation, or creation by a creator. In the fiqh example, the possible causes of riba in wheat are measurement, taste, or staple/stored.
Ensure the division is mutually exclusive and covers all possibilities.
2
Apply sabr: examine and eliminate false options
For each option, check its validity using known facts or rational arguments. In the theological case, the first two are deemed absurd. In the fiqh case, measurement is eliminated because a handful of wheat (not measured) still involves riba; being staple/stored is also eliminated because a handful is not a staple. Only taste remains.
The elimination must be decisive; otherwise the proof is probabilistic.
3
Determine the conclusion from the remainder
After eliminating all false options, the remaining one is necessarily true. Thus, the creator hypothesis is affirmed, and taste is identified as the cause of riba in wheat.
This is a form of disjunctive syllogism (modus tollendo ponens) applied to an exhaustive list.
4
Assess certainty level
The author notes that if the enumeration and division are categorical (ḥaṣr qaṭʿī) and the refutations are certain, the proof yields categorical certainty. In usul al-fiqh, this method is used to establish the ʿilla with confidence.
Check whether the division is truly exhaustive and each elimination is sound.

How to Find the Cause: Taqsīm and Sabr in the Bedouin Example
Author provides the Bedouin hadith example to illustrate sabr and taqsīm in determining the cause of expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of taqsīm (division) and sabr (examination) to identify the valid 'illa in a legal ruling from Islamic jurisprudence.

Core Idea
The cause is found by systematically eliminating irrelevant possibilities, not by guessing.

The story of the Bedouin who came to the Prophet (ﷺ) is a classic illustration of how Islamic jurists determine the effective cause (ʻilla) of a ruling. The Prophet ruled that the man must free a slave for having intercourse during Ramadan. But what exactly made that ruling apply? Was it because he was a Bedouin? Because his wife was a free woman? Or because of the act of intercourse itself? Using the method of division and examination (taqsīm wa sabr), the jurist tests each possibility and eliminates those that fail. In this lesson, you will walk through that process and learn how to apply it to other cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Possible Causes
List all attributes of the case that could plausibly be the cause (ʻilla) of the ruling. In this example, the Bedouin had three salient attributes: he was a Bedouin, his wife was a free woman, and he had intercourse during Ramadan. The method begins by enumerating these as candidate causes.
The enumeration must be exhaustive of plausible alternatives. In practice, the jurist considers all attributes that might be relevant.
2
Test Each Candidate with Sabr (Examination)
For each candidate, check if the ruling would still apply if that attribute were removed or changed. If the ruling remains the same, the attribute cannot be the distinguishing cause. This is the core of sabr: you 'test' each division by considering a case lacking that attribute.
Sabr means 'probing' — you examine whether the attribute is necessary for the ruling.
3
Eliminate Unsuitable Attributes
Apply the test: For 'being a Bedouin', consider a city dweller who commits the same act. The ruling (freeing a slave) is identical, so 'being Bedouin' is eliminated. For 'wife being a free woman', consider intercourse with a slave concubine — again the same ruling, so eliminated. Both fail the test because the ruling appears even when the attribute is absent.
Elimination is based on finding a counterexample where the ruling holds but the supposed cause does not.
4
Determine the Valid Cause
After elimination, only one candidate remains: the act of intercourse during Ramadan. Since it survives the test, it is determined to be the 'illa. The author says it 'becomes determined' (yataʻayyan) as the cause.
The conclusion is certain because the enumeration was exhaustive and the eliminations are sound.
5
Recognize the Inductive Structure
This reasoning is a form of inductive — specifically eliminative — induction. It moves from a set of particular cases (the three candidates) to a general causal claim. In Islamic logic, this is considered a valid method for discovering the 'illa when the enumeration is complete and the sabr is correctly performed.
This method is a cornerstone of qiyas (analogical deduction) in usul al-fiqh.

Mapping Juristic Analogy to Categorical Syllogism
Author explains equivalence of juristic analogy to first-figure categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to map the components of a juristic analogy (far', asl, 'illa) to the terms of a categorical syllogism (minor, major, middle) and identify it as a first-figure syllogism.

Core Idea
Juristic analogy is structurally identical to a first-figure categorical syllogism.

This passage clarifies a crucial bridge between usul al-fiqh and mantiq: the analogical reasoning (qiyas al-tamthil) used by jurists is not a loose comparison but a rigorous logical deduction. The author shows how the far' (branch), asl (root), and 'illa (cause) map directly to the minor, major, and middle terms of a categorical syllogism, placing it in the first figure. Understanding this equivalence lets you see fiqh rulings as logically valid conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of juristic analogy
Locate the far' (branch/new case), asl (root/original case), and 'illa (effective cause) in the analogy. In the example: far' = rice (dhurra), asl = wheat (barr), 'illa = measurement (kayl) for Hanafis/Hanbalis.
The 'illa can vary by madhab; the structure remains.
2
Map each component to a logical term
The far' becomes the minor term (subject of conclusion), the asl becomes the major term (predicate of conclusion), and the 'illa becomes the middle term (linking the premises).
Middle term appears in both premises but not in conclusion.
3
Construct the categorical syllogism
Write two premises: 1) Major premise: 'Everything that has the 'illa (measurement) has the ruling (riba prohibition).' 2) Minor premise: 'Rice (far') has the 'illa (measurement).' Conclusion: 'Therefore, rice has the ruling (riba prohibition).'
This matches the first figure: middle term is subject of major premise and predicate of minor premise.
4
Verify it is first figure
In the first figure, the middle term is subject of major premise and predicate of minor premise. Check that the syllogism satisfies this and that the conclusion follows necessarily.
The author asserts that the analogy 'becomes a categorical syllogism of the first figure' when mapped this way.

Why Analogy and Induction Can Yield Certainty
Author refutes al-Akhdari's statement that analogy and induction do not produce certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the claim that analogy and induction are inherently uncertain by grounding their validity in the certainty of their premises.

Core Idea
Analogy and induction yield certainty when their premises are certain.

Al-Akhdari's famous didactic poem al-Sullam al-Munawraq states that analogy (tamthil) and induction (istiqra') do not produce certainty. The author of our text declares this a mistake shared by most logicians. This lesson dissects why that categorical denial is unfounded and how the certainty of these arguments depends entirely on their premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Akhdari's target claim
Locate the exact statement in the Arabic: 'ولَا يُفِيدُ القَطْعَ بِالدَّلِيلِ قِيَاسُ الِاسْتِقْرَاءِ وَالتَّمْثِيلِ'. Understand that al-Akhdari denies that either analogy or induction can produce certain knowledge.
2
Recall the reduction to categorical syllogism
From the previous lesson, both tamthil and shumul (induction as universalisation) reduce to the same categorical syllogistic form. If the premises of a syllogism are certain, its conclusion is certain regardless of the method's name.
This reduction shows that the form itself is not defective.
3
Recognise the error as a categorical mistake
Al-Akhdari's error is to attribute uncertainty to the method itself, whereas certainty flows from the premises. The same syllogistic structure can yield certainty or probability depending on the quality of the premises.
Most logicians made the same mistake by focusing on the method rather than the epistemic status of the premises.
4
Apply the critique to a concrete Islamic example
Consider the induction that all intoxicants are forbidden based on multiple narrations and analogies. If the premise 'every intoxicant is forbidden' is certain from revelation, then the induced generalisation is certain. Al-Akhdari would have to deny this certainty, which contradicts Islamic legal methodology.
