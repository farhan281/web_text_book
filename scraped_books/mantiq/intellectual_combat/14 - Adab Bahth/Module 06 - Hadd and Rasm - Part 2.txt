Verbal and Attentional Definitions: Same Content, Different Circumstances
Author defines verbal and attentional definitions and distinguishes them by the learner's state.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a verbal definition and an attentional definition based on the listener's state of prior knowledge.

Core Idea
Verbal and attentional definitions are identical in content; only the learner's prior knowledge differs.

In Islamic logic, definitions are classified according to the learner's familiarity. The verbal definition introduces a new term to an unacquainted listener, while the attentional definition reminds one who once knew but forgot. This distinction is crucial in teaching, as the same words can serve both purposes depending on the student's background.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of the definition
Ask whether the definition is meant to introduce a new meaning or to recall a forgotten one. This determines whether it is verbal or attentional.
The definitional content may be identical.
2
Consider the listener's background
Determine if the listener has prior knowledge of the meaning. If they never knew, it is verbal; if they knew but forgot, it is attentional.
The listener's state is the only distinguishing factor.
3
Apply the author's rule
Use the rule: verbal definition substitutes an unfamiliar word with a clearer synonym; attentional definition brings to mind a previously known but forgotten meaning.
The author gives examples like 'ghadanfar' and 'asad' for verbal, and implies a reminder scenario for attentional.
4
Recognize the identity of content
Understand that both definitions are identical in reality (haqiqa) and referent (musadaq). The difference is only in the learner's circumstance.
This is a nuanced but important point to avoid confusion.

Real vs. Nominal Definitions in Islamic Logic
Author distinguishes real from nominal definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a real definition (ta'rīf ḥaqīqī) and a nominal definition (ta'rīf ismī) and classify any definition from classical Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Real definitions describe what exists; nominal definitions describe what we imagine.

In the study of definition (al-ta'rīf), logicians separate definitions that capture the essence of things that actually exist from those that merely clarify the meaning of a term for something whose existence is unknown. This lesson unpacks that distinction using the classification from our matn, helping you apply it to real Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quiddity (mahiyya) being defined
Every definition defines a quiddity. First, locate what is being defined in the passage: is it a real entity or a mental concept?
For example, 'man' (insan) is a quiddity with external existence; 'phoenix' ('anqa') is imagined.
2
Check for external existence
Determine whether the quiddity is known to have individuals (afrad) existing outside the mind (fi al-kharij). The author states that real definitions require external existence; nominal definitions do not.
You may need evidence from revelation or empirical science to confirm existence.
3
Classify as haqiqi or ismi
If the quiddity exists externally, the definition is real (haqiqi); if it is only imagined (mutakhayyala) and existence is unknown, it is nominal (ismi).
Nominal definitions still have meaning, but they do not assert that the defined thing exists.
4
Apply the classification to Islamic examples
Practice by taking definitions from Islamic sciences—'angel' in kalam, 'jinn' in fiqh, 'wudu' in usul—and determine which type they are.
For 'angel', external existence is affirmed by revelation, so its definition is real. For 'ma'yus minhu' (a hypothetical case), it may be nominal.

Real definitions describe essence as it exists; nominal definitions explain what a name means.

This lesson clarifies the foundational distinction in Islamic logic between definitions that capture the thing itself and definitions that explain the meaning of a term. The Conditions of Jam' and Man' in Verbal Definitions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A proper definition must include all instances and exclude all non-instances.

In this passage, the author explains that logicians require verbal definitions to be both inclusive (jam') and exclusive (man'), while linguists often accept looser definitions that fail these conditions. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences, where precision is often required.

The Four Conditions for a Valid Definition
Author lists four ways to object to definitions based on conditions of validity and clarity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four conditions for a valid definition and determine which objection applies when a condition is violated.

Core Idea
A valid definition must exclude non-instances, include all instances, avoid circularity, and be clearer than the definiendum.

Definitions are only useful if they meet certain logical conditions. This lesson unpacks the four conditions that logicians require for any real definition (ḥadd or rasm). Violating any one of them gives grounds for a valid objection.

Guided Path
1
Check coextensiveness (muṭṭarid)
Does the definition exclude everything that is not the definiendum? If not, it is not exclusive (māni‘) — the first objection applies.
Example: Defining 'human' as 'living being' fails because it includes animals.
2
Check converseness (mun‘akis)
Does the definition include all instances of the definiendum? If not, it is not comprehensive (jāmi‘) — the second objection applies.
Example: Defining 'human' as 'rational animal' includes all humans, but defining 'human' as 'animal that laughs' might miss non-laughing humans.
3
Check for impossibility (muḥāl)
Does the definition lead to a logical contradiction or circularity, such as circular priority (dawr sababī)? If so, the third objection applies.
Example: Defining 'cause' as 'that which produces an effect' and 'effect' as 'that which is produced by a cause' is circular.
4
Check clarity (wudūḥ)
Is the definition more apparent and clearer than the definiendum? If not, it fails its purpose — the fourth objection applies.
Example: Defining 'fire' as 'the element that rises' is not clearer than 'fire' itself.

The Four Conditions for a Valid Definition
Author lists four conditions for a valid definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a definition is valid by checking the four conditions: muttarid, mun'akis, no impossibility, and clarity.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be coextensive, comprehensive, non-circular, and clearer than the term defined.

In this passage, the author explains the four essential conditions that a definition must satisfy to be considered valid (not invalid). These conditions apply to both real definitions (hadd) and descriptions (rasm). By understanding these conditions, you will be able to identify flawed definitions that fail to properly capture the essence or extension of a term.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definition
Find the term being defined (al-mu'arraf) and the definition provided. In the text, the author does not give a specific example, but we can apply the conditions to any definition.
For practice, consider a definition like 'al-insan: hayawan natiq'.
2
Check coextension (muttarid)
Does the definition exclude everything outside the definiendum? It should act as a 'preventer' (mani'). If it leaves out some non-members or includes some members incorrectly, it fails.
For 'hayawan natiq': it excludes non-rational animals, so it is muttarid.
3
Check conversion (mun'akis)
Does the definition include all members of the definiendum? It should be 'comprehensive' (jami'). If it fails to cover some instances, it is not mun'akis.
For 'hayawan natiq': it includes all rational animals, so it is mun'akis.
4
Check for impossibility (muhal)
Does the definition lead to a contradiction or circularity (dawr sabqi)? For example, if the definition uses the definiendum itself or depends on it in a loop.
Avoid definitions like 'al-shams: what rises in the morning' if 'morning' is defined by sunrise.
5
Check clarity (azhar wa awdah)
Is the definition more apparent and clearer than the definiendum? If the definiendum is already well-known, the definition should not be more obscure.
For instance, defining 'al-mawt' (death) as 'cessation of life' is clearer than 'death' itself.

Fourth Type of Tahrir al-Murad: Clarifying Definition Type
Author explains the fourth type where the definer clarifies the kind of definition he intended.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In debate, a definitional objection fails if it attacks a different type of definition than what was intended.

When a debater objects to a definition, he may assume it is a real definition (haqiqi) or a complete definition (hadd tamm), when in fact the definer intended a nominal or incomplete definition. The fourth type of tahrir al-murad teaches the definer to clarify the intended type, thereby neutralizing the objection. This lesson will equip you to recognize and apply this dialectical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definer's definition
Read the definition given by the definer. Note the wording: does it seem to describe an essence or just identify the term? Example: 'human is the rational mortal animal' (hadd tamm) vs. 'human is the laughing animal' (rasm).
2
Determine the intended type
Consider the definer's likely intent: real (haqiqi), nominal (lafzi/ismi), complete (tamm), or incomplete (naqis). Real definitions capture the essence; nominal definitions explain the meaning of the term without essence; complete definitions have genus and differentia; incomplete miss one.

Identify the objector's assumption
What type does the objector assume? The passage lists three common misassumptions: taking a verbal definition as real, taking an incomplete definition as complete, or taking a nominal definition as real. The objection is based on this mistaken assumption.
The objector's objection is valid only if the definition actually claims to be the assumed type.
4
Apply the definer's clarification
The definer states the intended type, showing that the objection misses the mark. For example, 'I did not claim this is a real definition; it is a nominal definition.' The objection then becomes irrelevant.
This move does not prove the definition is correct, only that the objection is based on a misunderstanding.

Identify the objection

2
Locate the ambiguous term

Mapping Tahrir al-Murad Types to Premise Rebuttal
Author summarizes that first two types rebut the minor premise, last two rebut the major premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify each type of tahrir al-murad as rebutting either the minor or major premise of the objector's syllogism.

Core Idea
The four types of tahrir al-murad each target a specific premise in the objector's proof.

Having learned the four types of tahrir al-murad, we now see which part of the objector's argument each type attacks. The objector's proof is a syllogism: the minor premise states the definition fails some condition, the major premise declares any such definition invalid, and the conclusion rejects the definition. Understanding which premise a type rebuts helps you deploy the correct response in a debate. The first two types—clarifying the definiens and parts of the definition—show the definition does not actually fail the condition, thus rebutting the minor. The last two—clarifying the scientific school and type of definition—show the condition is irrelevant, thus rebutting the major.

Guided Path
1
Reconstruct the objector's syllogism
The objector's argument is a categorical syllogism: minor premise: the definition fails condition X; major premise: every definition that fails X is invalid; conclusion: the definition is invalid. Identify the minor and major premises as the targets of rebuttal.
The author assumes this structure; it is not explicitly stated but is necessary for the analysis.

Map the first two types to the minor premise
Clarifying the definiens (تحرير المراد من المعرف) and clarifying parts of the definition (تحرير المراد من بعض أجزاء التعريف) both show that the definition does not actually fail the condition. This rebuts the minor premise because the condition is shown to be satisfied.
These types provide a direct counterexample to the objector's claim about the definition.
3
Map the last two types to the major premise
Clarifying the scientific school (تحرير المراد من المذهب العلمي) and the type of definition (تحرير المراد من نوع التعريف) both show that the condition is not required for this school or definition type. Clarifying Intent to Defuse Definitional Objections
Author explains how clarifying intent shifts premises of objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use tahrir al-murad to refute an objection against a definition by identifying whether to prevent the minor or major premise of the objector's syllogism.

Core Idea
Clarifying intent turns an objection against a definition into a misunderstanding of its type.

When someone objects that a definition is not exclusive (mani')—for example, that it fails to exclude birds—the definer can respond by clarifying the true nature of his definition. The text shows how the definer can say his definition is merely verbal (lafzi), which does not require exclusivity. More generally, the technique of 'tahrir al-murad' (clarifying the intended meaning) allows the definer to pinpoint which premise of the objector's argument is mistaken: the minor premise or the major premise.

Guided Path
1
Reconstruct the objector's syllogism
Identify the major premise ('Every definition that is not exclusive is invalid'), the minor premise ('This definition is not exclusive'), and the conclusion ('Therefore this definition is invalid'). This formalization reveals where the objection can be challenged.
2
Find the definer's response
The definer says: 'I prevent this definition from being real; it is only verbal.' This denies the minor premise by showing the definition is not of the type that requires exclusivity.
Verbal definitions only need to convey meaning, not exclude all others.
3
Distinguish premise prevention types
The text states: clarifying the definiendum or parts of the definition prevents the minor premise; clarifying the scientific position or type of definition prevents the major premise. This classification guides the definer's strategy.
The objector's major premise might be false for certain types of definitions.
4
Apply to a new example
Consider a fiqh example: A student defines 'salah' as 'specific actions and words'. An opponent objects: 'That includes all actions and words.' Respond by clarifying that the definition is verbal (lafzi) and means 'the actions and words known in the shari'a.' This prevents the minor premise by showing the definition is not real.

Defending Definitions Against the Objection of Obscurity
Response to objection of obscurity using relativity of clarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to respond to the objection that a definition is not clearer than the definiendum by invoking the relativity of clarity.

Core Idea
Clarity is relative: what is obscure to one may be clear to another.

When someone objects that your definition is not clearer than the term defined, they assume an absolute standard of clarity. But the author demonstrates that clarity is relative to the audience. This lesson teaches you to deny such objections by citing the relativity of clarity and obscurity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims the definiens is less clear than the definiendum. In the example, the definiendum is 'بر' (wheat) and the definiens is 'حنطة' (also wheat).
2
Recognize the assumption
The objector assumes clarity is an absolute property. The defender must show it's relative.
3
Apply the relativity principle
State that clarity and obscurity are relative; what is clear to one may be obscure to another.
Use the Arabic phrase: الظهور والخفاء نسبيان.
4
Construct the response
Deny the objection by asserting that for the intended audience, the definiens is clearer. Example: 'I deny that حنطة is less clear than بر; because some people find حنطة clearer, and I addressed them.'

Resolving Circular Definitions through Differentiation of Aspects
Response to circularity objection using differentiation of aspects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between vicious and non-vicious circularity and apply the concept of differentiation of aspects (انفكاك الجهة) to defend a definition against a circularity objection.

Core Idea
Circularity is not always vicious; if dependencies involve different aspects, it is permissible.

Circularity is a powerful objection, but not all circularity invalidates a definition. The author introduces two crucial distinctions: prior vs. simultaneous circularity, and the differentiation of aspects. This lesson focuses on the latter, using the example of defining conventional verbal signification to show how mutual dependence can be harmless when the aspects differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the circularity claim
The objector asserts that the definition leads to circularity: A depends on B and B depends on A.
Example: Defining 'conventional verbal signification' as 'the word such that when uttered, its meaning is understood after knowledge of the convention'.
2
Determine the type of circularity
Is it prior (دور سبقي) or simultaneous (دور معي)? Prior circularity involves temporal or logical priority and is vicious. Simultaneous may be non-vicious.
The text mentions both, but focuses on using differentiation of aspects to avoid even prior circularity.
3
Apply differentiation of aspects (انفكاك الجهة)
Show that the dependency of A on B is from one aspect, while the dependency of B on A is from a different aspect. Thus no vicious circle.
In the example: understanding meaning depends on knowledge of the specific assignment (تعيين) of the word. Knowledge of convention depends on the absolute meaning (مطلق المعنى), not the specific assignment.
4
Conclude the defense
Since the aspects differ, the definition does not entail vicious circularity.

Responding to Circularity via Difference of Aspect
Author explains how rejecting circularity in definitions by distinguishing aspects of dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition is accused of circularity and apply the difference-of-aspect response to defend it.

Core Idea
Circularity is not always vicious if the aspects of dependence differ.

In dialectical logic, one common objection against definitions is that they imply a vicious circle (dawr). However, as this passage shows, the defender can refute this by distinguishing the respects in which the dependencies occur. This is known as infikak al-jiha (difference of aspect). Let us analyze how this works with a concrete example from the definition of conventional verbal signification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under attack
The definition of conventional verbal signification (dalala wad'iyya lafziyya) is given: 'the word being such that when uttered, its meaning is understood after knowledge of the placement (wad') of the word.'
2
Recognize the objector's argument
The objector claims this definition implies a vicious circle: understanding meaning depends on knowing wad', and knowing wad' depends on understanding meaning.
This is the standard objection of circularity.
3
Isolate the defender's response
The defender denies the implication by distinguishing aspects: understanding meaning depends on knowledge of the specific assignment (ta'yin) of the word, while knowing wad' depends on the absolute meaning (mutlaq al-ma'na), not the specific assignment.
The two dependencies refer to different respects.
4
Apply the concept of infikak al-jiha
Since the aspects of dependence differ, there is no single circular dependence. Thus the definition escapes the charge of circularity.
This is the key logical move taught in this passage.

Implicit Claims and the Uniqueness of Complete Definitions
Author expands on objections rooted in implicit claims of definitions and argues against multiple definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the implicit claims in a complete definition and prove that a single quiddity cannot have two distinct complete definitions.

Core Idea
A complete definition uniquely determines the essence; two different differentiae would produce two essences.

Every definition carries hidden assumptions. When a logician claims his definition is complete, he implicitly asserts that it uses the proximate genus and differentia and that all parts are essential. This lesson unpacks those implicit claims and examines a classic proof: why one reality cannot have two different complete definitions. Understanding this prevents confusion in defining key terms in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize implicit claims in a definition
When someone says 'This is a complete definition (hadd tam),' they implicitly claim two things: (1) it uses the proximate genus and differentia, and (2) all parts are essential (dhati), not accidental.
These claims are not stated explicitly but are logically entailed.
2
Identify possible objections to these claims
An objector can challenge that the parts are accidental, or that the alleged genus and differentia are not proximate, or that another complete definition exists for the same reality.
Each objection targets an implicit claim.
3
Understand the proof against multiple definitions
The complete definition is composed of proximate genus and differentia. The differentia is the essential distinguisher. If there were two different differentiae, each would combine with the genus to yield a distinct essence, contradicting the oneness of the quiddity.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: multiple definitions lead to multiple essences, which is impossible.
4
Apply the proof to an Islamic science example
Consider the definition of 'ibada (worship): obedience with humility before Allah. The genus is 'obedience'; the differentia is 'with humility'. If someone proposed another differentia, it would change the essence, showing that only one complete definition is possible.

Al-Naqd: The Cause Without Its Ruling
Author defines al-naqd in usul al-fiqh and distinguishes it from jadal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of al-naqd in usul al-fiqh and distinguish it from the broader jadal usage.

Core Idea
In usul, naqd is the cause existing without its ruling, not just any logical failure.

In usul al-fiqh, the validity of a cause ('illa) is tested by objections. One key objection is al-naqd. But naqd has a specific technical meaning here, different from its use in general disputation. This lesson unpacks that definition as given by the author.

Read the definition of naqd in usul

2
Contrast with the broader jadal naqd
The passage says in jadal it applies to any fall behind or entailment of impossibility. In usul, it is narrowed to the cause-ruling relationship.
3
Understand the objection's logic
If the cause exists without the ruling, it shows the cause is not valid for that ruling. This is a challenge to the causal link.

The Four Loci of al-Man' in Usul al-Fiqh
Author enumerates the four loci of prevention in usul with an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four loci of al-man' in usul al-fiqh and apply them to a qiyas example.

Core Idea
Al-man' in usul has exactly four technical locations for attacking qiyas.

We have seen that al-man' in jadal can target any premise. But in usul al-fiqh, it has a narrower, systematic meaning: four specific places where an objector can prevent a qiyas. This passage by the author enumerates them with an example, showing how the objection is applied to the components of analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the context
The author is mapping the jadal objection of al-munaqada (real prevention) to the usul term al-man'. Understand that this is a technical transfer from general disputation to the specific methodology of usul al-fiqh.
2
Memorise the four loci
Learn the four: (1) prevention of the ruling of the original (man' hukm al-asl), (2) prevention of the existence of the claimed cause in the original (man' wujud ma yadda'ihi 'illa fi al-asl), (3) prevention of it being the cause of the ruling (man' kawnihi 'illat al-hukm), (4) prevention of its existence in the branch (man' wujudihi fi al-far').
3
Understand the example
The author gives an example for the first locus: A Hanbali argues that the skin of a dead animal is impure and not purified by tanning, analogously to dog skin. The opponent can prevent the ruling that the skin is impure (man' hukm al-asl). This shows how the objection targets the basis of the analogy.
The example is incomplete but illustrates the concept.
4
Check the sequence
Note that these four loci correspond to the structure of qiyas: the original ruling (asl), the presence of the 'illa in the asl, the causal link, and the presence of the 'illa in the far'. This systematic coverage is what makes al-man' a powerful objection tool.

Mu‘āraḍa fī al-Aṣl – Opposition in the Original
Defines mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl and sets conditions based on the debate over multiple derived causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl objection and explain why it depends on the position that multiple derived causes are prohibited.

Core Idea
Opposition in the original challenges the cause by offering an alternative, assuming only one derived cause can be correct.

This lesson focuses on the first type of mu‘āraḍa (opposition) in analogical reasoning: mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl. The author defines it as the objector presenting another description suitable as a cause, which challenges the opponent's ʿilla. A key example is the debate over ribā in barley between al-Shafi‘i (ṭu‘m) and the Ḥanafī/Ḥanbalī (kayl). Understanding this type requires knowing the uṣūlī disagreement on whether multiple derived causes (al-ʿilal al-mustanbaṭa) can be accepted simultaneously.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find the sentence where the author defines mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl. Note that it involves the objector presenting an alternative description (waṣf) suitable as a cause.
The definition begins: 'أما المعارضة في الأصل فضابطها عندهم أن يُبدي المعترض وصفا آخر صالحًا للتعليل'
2
Analyze the ribā example
Al-Shafi‘i says the cause of ribā in barley is ṭu‘m (edibility). The Ḥanafī says it is kayl (standard measure). Identify which one is the original cause and which is the alternative.
This is a classic uṣūlī dispute; note that both descriptions are derived causes.
3
Understand the underlying assumption
The author explains that this type of opposition only works if one holds that multiple derived causes cannot coexist. If multiple causes were allowed, both could be valid and the objection fails.
This is the 'khulf' (disagreement) mentioned in the verse from Maraqi al-Sa'ud.
4
Distinguish textual from derived causes
Textual causes (al-ʿilal al-manṣūṣa) are unanimously allowed to be multiple; therefore mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl does not apply to them. The author gives examples from wuḍū’ and ghusl.
This is why the opposition is only relevant for derived causes.

As we explained clearly, contrary to the author of al-Muraqi and al-Baji, and therefore the author of Maraqi al-Sa'ud said that objection by way of reversal (qalb) is not accepted, in his verse: '(1) And reversal is to prove that which the ruling opposed, by the description, and objection by it is not accepted.' As for opposition other than reversal, it is of two types: opposition in the original (mu‘āraḍa fī al-aṣl) and opposition in the branch (mu‘āraḍa fī al-far‘). Some scholars of uṣūl call it: the distinction between the original and the branch. You have learned from what preceded that the definition of opposition (mu‘āraḍa) is that it is the presentation of evidence contrary to what the opponent has presented as his evidence, and it applies to all proofs, whether analogical or otherwise. The uṣūliyyūn who say it is of two types: opposition in the original and opposition in the branch, intend opposition specifically in analogical reasoning. As for opposition in the original, its definition according to them is that the objector presents another description suitable for being a cause (ʿilla). For example, al-Shafi‘i says: the cause of ribā in barley is the nutritive quality (ṭu‘m). So the Ḥanafī and Ḥanbalī oppose him by presenting another description suitable for being a cause, which is the standard measure (kayl). It is not hidden that this type of opposition is based on the position of prohibiting multiple derived causes; because on the position of permitting their multiplicity, there is no obstacle to both the proponent's cause and the objector's cause being correct. The derived causes differ regarding the permissibility of their multiplicity, and therefore disagreement exists on accepting objection by this type of opposition. As for the textual causes (al-‘ilal al-manṣūṣa), there is no disagreement on the permissibility of their multiplicity, such as urine, feces, touching (kissing), and sleep for nullification of wuḍū’, and such as intercourse, ejaculation, and the end of menstruation for the obligation of ghusl. This type of opposition does not apply to them. The author of Maraqi al-Sa'ud indicated what we mentioned in his verse: 'And textual causes are multiple; regarding derived causes, there is known disagreement.' The definition of the second type of opposition, which is opposition in the branch, is that the objector presents a description that prevents the ruling in the branch and is absent from the original. For example, analogizing a gift (hiba) to a sale in prohibiting gharar: the objector says: 'A sale is a contract of exchange, and exchange is a bargain that is spoiled by gharar, whereas a gift is pure benevolence and is not spoiled by gharar; if nothing is received, the donee suffers no loss.' So the fact that a gift is pure benevolence is an opposition in the branch, not present in the original, preventing analogy to it. Fasad al-i'tibar is when an analogy contradicts a clear text or consensus.

Understanding Fasād al-Waḍʿ in Usul al-Fiqh
Defines fasād al-waḍʿ and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of fasād al-waḍʿ in usul al-fiqh arguments and classify them into one of the four types of misderivation.

Core Idea
Fasād al-waḍʿ exposes when a legal premise logically supports the opposite of the intended ruling.

In usul al-fiqh debates, not every objection targets the truth of premises; some challenge the structure of reasoning. Fasād al-waḍʿ (corruption of position) is one such objection, pointing out that the evidence is used in a form that is actually more suitable for deriving the opposite ruling. This lesson unpacks its definition, four classical examples, and the first method of responding.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the criterion of fasād al-waḍʿ
Read the definition: the evidence is placed on a form (hay'a) that is not suitable for the ruling, but rather suitable for its opposite or contradictory. Note the phrase 'على غير الهيئة الصالحة لأخذ الحكم منه'.
This is about structural suitability, not factual truth.
2
Identify the four types of misderivation
The passage lists four: taking expansion (tawsīʿ) from restriction (taḍyīq), alleviation (takhfīf) from severity (taghlīẓ), affirmation (ithbāt) from negation (nafī), and negation from affirmation. Memorize these pairs.
Each pair shows a mismatch between the premise's natural implication and the derived ruling.
3
Analyze each example to see the mismatch
For each example, identify the premise and why it naturally supports the opposite. For instance, in the zakat example: the premise 'zakat is relief for the poor' naturally supports immediacy (to relieve need), but the derived ruling is delay (expansion).
Focus on the logical support relationship.
4
Understand the first method of answer
The first answer is to deny that the evidence necessitates the opposite (manʿ anna al-dalīl yaqtaḍī naqīḍ al-ḥukm). The opponent claims a necessary implication; the respondent can argue that the evidence does not logically require that opposite.
This is a denial of the opponent's presupposition.
5
Apply the tool to new arguments
Practice by reading an unfamiliar usul argument and checking whether the premise is used in a form that suits the intended ruling or its opposite.
Use the four types as a checklist.

Classification of universals by existence of individuals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal term into one of six categories based on the existence of its individuals in the external world.

Core Idea
Universals are not all equal; they differ by whether, how many, and how their instances exist.

This lesson unpacks the six-fold division of universals with respect to their individuals. This classification is used in Islamic logic to analyze terms like 'Allah', 'prophet', 'angel', etc.

Guided Path

Distinguishing al-Kull from al-Kulli and Related Terms
Terminological distinctions between kull, kulli, and kulliyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between 'al-kull' (the whole), 'al-kulli' (the universal), 'al-kulliyya' (totality), and explain the tests involving the copula of equivalence and the partition tool 'imma'.

Core Idea
'Al-kull' and 'al-kulli' are easily confused but logically distinct; the copula of equivalence and partition test reveal the difference.

The passage devotes several lines to these distinctions. Mastering these terms prevents critical errors in logical analysis and theological discussion, as the difference affects how we reason about wholes vs. universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
The passage lists: 'الكل' (the whole), 'الكلي' (the universal), 'الكلية' (totality). Notice their distinct Arabic forms. 'الكل' refers to a collection as a whole; 'الكلي' refers to a concept applicable to many individuals.
2
Understand the copula of equivalence test
The passage states that 'الكلي' can be predicated on its individuals with haml al-muwata'a (e.g., 'Zayd is an insan'), but 'الكل' cannot be predicated on its parts (you cannot say 'the wall is the house' meaning identity).
3
Understand the partition test
'الكلي' can be divided with the partition tool 'إما' (e.g., 'al-insan is either good or bad'), but 'الكل' cannot (you cannot divide 'the house' into either the roof or the wall as exclusive parts).
4
Analyze negation interaction
The passage mentions negation with 'كل'. For example, 'لا كل إنسان' (not every human) vs 'كل لا إنسان' (every non-human). This shows how scope of negation affects meaning.

Classification of Naqd in Usul al-Fiqh
Author enumerates the first defect (naqd) and its types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of naqd and match each type with its examples from fiqh.

Core Idea
Naqd reveals when a claimed cause fails to produce its ruling — a key defect in Usuli reasoning.

In Usul al-Fiqh, a defect (qadih) in the cause ('illa) voids the legal reasoning. The first such defect is naqd, where the ruling does not follow from the cause. This lesson breaks down the three subtypes of naqd: due to a mukhassis, due to a mani' or loss of shart, and without cause. Memorizing these types and their examples equips you to evaluate causal arguments in jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of naqd
Read the passage: naqd is defined as 'the ruling not following from its cause' (takhalluf al-hukm 'an al-illa). This is the core defect.
2
Distinguish the three subtypes
Notice the three categories: (1) due to mukhassis (specifier), (2) due to mani' (preventive) or missing shart (condition), (3) without any reason. Each is a distinct way the cause fails.
3
Match examples to each subtype
For subtype 1: 'araya sale, blood-money on 'aqila, and musarra milk. For subtype 2: paternity in qisas (mani'), absence of nisab/hira for theft (fawat shart). For subtype 3: no examples given, but it is the residual category.
These are standard examples from Hanafi/Shafi'i fiqh.
4
Test your recall using the mnemonic
Use the three examples of subtype 1 as a group to remember: 'Araya, 'Aqila, Musarra' (all start with 'A or M). For subtype 2, associate prevention (mana') with paternity, and condition (shart) with nisab/ihsan.

He then enumerates three subtypes: (1) the ruling diverges because a mukhassis (specifying factor) exists, giving three examples from fiqh; (2) the ruling diverges due to a mani' (preventive) or loss of a shart (condition), with examples from criminal law; (3) the ruling diverges without any apparent reason. The passage serves as a mnemonic anchor for the student to memorize the types of naqd and their examples.
