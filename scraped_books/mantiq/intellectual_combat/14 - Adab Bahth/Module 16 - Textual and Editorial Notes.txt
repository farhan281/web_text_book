The Logical Condition for Contradiction and Its Application to Abrogation
Author critiques usul scholars' claim that mutawatir cannot be abrogated by khabar ahad due to time difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a later khabar ahad does not logically contradict an earlier mutawatir report due to the temporal condition of tanāquḍ.

Core Idea
Abrogation does not require contradiction because later rulings can supersede earlier ones without conflict, thanks to different temporal contexts.

In this passage, the author takes on a common conviction among usul scholars: that a mutawatir report cannot be abrogated (naskh) by a later khabar ahad, because that would imply a contradiction. He exposes the logical flaw in this reasoning by invoking the precise definition of tanāquḍ (contradiction) from logic, which requires unity of time. Since the earlier and later reports apply to different times, there is no logical conflict; both are true in their own temporal scope. This lesson will help you see how a fine-grained logical condition can resolve a seemingly intractable debate in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition of tanāquḍ
In classical logic, two propositions are truly contradictory (tanāquḍ) only if they agree in subject, predicate, and time. The author emphasizes that temporal unity is essential.
Contradiction is not merely opposition; it requires simultaneity.
2
Identify the usul scholars' claim
The majority of usul scholars assert that a later khabar ahad cannot abrogate a mutawatir report, implying that doing so would contradict the earlier certain report.
They assume that the later report would falsify the earlier one.
3
Apply the condition to the example
The author provides the Qur'anic verse (Al-An'am 6:145) and the later hadith prohibiting domesticated donkeys. The verse was true in its time; the hadith is true in its later time. No contradiction exists because the times differ.
The verse listed only four forbidden items; the donkey prohibition came later, so at the verse's time it was not yet forbidden.
4
Conclude the validity of abrogation
Since there is no logical contradiction, the later khabar ahad can abrogate the earlier mutawatir ruling. The author declares that the correct view is permissibility of such naskh.
This shows how logical precision can correct a widespread error in Islamic legal theory.

This passage is a critical note from the author challenging a widely held position in usul al-fiqh: that mutawatir reports cannot be abrogated (naskh) by khabar ahad. Logical Contradiction vs Usuli Specification
Author distinguishes logical contradiction from usuli specification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a particular negative is a takhsis (specification) of a universal affirmative in usul al-fiqh rather than a logical contradiction.

Core Idea
Not every logical contradictory is a usuli contradiction; specification harmonises texts.

In logic, a universal affirmative is directly contradicted by a particular negative. However, in usul al-fiqh, such a pair often appears without contradiction because one text specifies the other. This lesson clarifies that difference using a Qur'anic example and explains the principle of takhsis.

Guided Path
1
Recall the logical rule
Remember that in logic, a universal affirmative (e.g., 'Every A is B') is contradicted by a particular negative ('Some A is not B'). This is a strict contradiction.
This rule is foundational in logic and applies to propositions regardless of content.
2
Identify the usuli scenario
In usul al-fiqh, a text may be general (aam) like a universal affirmative, but another text may restrict its application. The latter is called a mukhasis (specifier) and does not refute the first but limits its scope.
The key is that in usul, texts are reconciled through specification, not treated as logical contradictories.
3
Compare logical and usuli treatments
In logic, the particular negative would negate the universal. In usul, the particular negative is seen as a specification (takhsis), meaning the general applies only to cases not excluded by the specific evidence.
The difference arises because usul deals with revealed texts that are assumed to be compatible.
4
Apply to the Qur'anic example
Consider the verse 'Divorced women shall wait for three menstrual periods' (Al-Baqarah 2:228). The phrase 'the divorced women' functions as a universal affirmative: every divorced woman must wait. Another verse or hadith specifying that pregnant divorced women do not wait (e.g., 'and for those with pregnancy, their term is until they deliver') is not a contradiction but a specification.
Thus, the universal remains valid for non-pregnant divorced women.

Commentary
This passage is a critical note clarifying a difference between logic and usul al-fiqh regarding contradiction. In logic, a universal affirmative proposition (e.g., 'All A are B') is negated by a particular negative ('Some A are not B'); this is a genuine contradiction. However, in usul al-fiqh, when a universal affirmative text from Qur'an or Sunnah encounters a particular negative from another text, the latter is not considered a contradiction but a specification (takhsis) of the universality. Thus, what constitutes logical contradiction does not directly apply in usul because the discipline deals with textual evidences that can be harmonized through specification.

Classifying Propositions: Quantity, Quality, Quantifier, and Contradiction
Lists section headings on conditional propositions, quantification, and contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements in proposition classification: quantity, quality, quantifier, and the conditions for contradiction.

Core Idea
Every proposition is classified by quantity (universal/particular) and quality (affirmative/negative), with quantifiers indicating the subject's scope.

The latter part of the table of contents outlines how propositions are categorized. You will learn the definitions of quantity (kamm) and quality (kayf), the role of quantifiers (sur), and the essential conditions for contradiction. This foundational knowledge is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish quantity and quality
The passage defines 'quantity' and 'quality' in terminology. Quantity refers to whether a proposition is universal (all) or particular (some). Quality refers to affirmative or negative.
Memorize the Arabic terms: al-kamm and al-kayf.
2
Understand the quantifier and its expressions
The quantifier (al-sur) is the linguistic marker for quantity. The passage lists its divisions and expressions. Recognize that quantifiers are separate from the subject in Arabic grammar.
The passage notes that the indefinite (al-muhmala) is equivalent to the particular.
3
Apply classification to categorical propositions
The headings describe cases for categorical propositions (hamliyya) when the subject is universal or particular. Use the summary to see how quantity and quality combine (e.g., universal affirmative, particular negative).
The 'natural proposition' is a special type discussed in the notices.
4
Master the conditions for contradiction
The passage states that contradiction (tanaqud) requires changing the quantifier and unity in nine matters (e.g., subject, predicate). This is essential for logical opposition.
For example, 'All actions are created by Allah' contradicts 'Some actions are not created by Allah'.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents or a list of section headings from a logic work on conditional propositions and the classification of propositions. It begins with the division of the conjunctive conditional (al-shartiyya al-muttasila) into implicative (luzumiyya) and coincidental (ittifaqiyya). It then discusses the distinction between these two types, the rule for identifying the coincidental conditional, and a warning about an error made by al-Zamakhshari and Abu Hayyan regarding the true condition in coincidental conditionals. Several 'tanbihat' (notices) follow, covering the truth-value of conditionals, terminology for the parts of a conditional (muqaddam and tali), and the acceptance of some conditionals by rhetoricians. There is also a discussion of mubalagha (hyperbole) in rhetoric, listing its three types: tabligh, ighraq, and ghuluww. Next, the passage turns to the disjunctive conditional (al-shartiyya al-munfasila), stating its rule and its three divisions: mutually exclusive and exhaustive (mani'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw ma'an), mutually exclusive but not exhaustive (mani'at al-jam' al-mujawwiza li al-khuluw), and exhaustive but not mutually exclusive (mani'at al-khuluw al-mujawwiza li al-jam'). A chapter (fasl) follows on the division of propositions by quantity (kamm) and quality (kayf). It defines 'quantity' and 'quality' in terminology, then explains the quantifier (sur) and its divisions and expressions. The passage then covers the states of the categorical proposition (hamliyya) when its subject is universal or particular, summarizing the divisions of the categorical by quality and quantity. Further notices discuss: indefinite propositions being equivalent to particular, the subject referring to extension (masadiq) and the predicate to intension (mafhum), the 'natural proposition' (qadiyya tabi'iyya), the quantifier being separate from the subject, the application of quantifiers to conditional propositions, and 'particularity' (al-khusus) in conditionals. Finally, there is a chapter on contradiction (tanaqud), defining it, noting the necessity of changing the quantifier to achieve contradiction, and listing nine matters in which unity is required for contradiction (e.g., predicate, subject).
