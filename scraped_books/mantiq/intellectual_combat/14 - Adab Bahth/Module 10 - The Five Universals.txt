True vs. Relative Particulars
Author introduces the division of particular into haqiqi and idafi, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a haqiqi particular and an idafi particular, and identify examples of each from the Arabic classification.

Core Idea
Particularity is not always absolute — some particulars are particular only in relation to others.

We have learned that terms are either universal or particular. But is all particularity the same? The author now divides the particular into two types: true (haqiqi) and relative (idafi). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing definitions, because a 'particular' in one context may function as a universal in another. In this lesson, we will examine the author's classification and examples.

The author uses 'al-'alam' which is a proper noun like Zayd or 'the sun'. Some also include indexicals.
3
Note the incomplete idafi definition
The passage cuts off before defining idafi, but you should infer that idafi (relative) particular is particular relative to something else, e.g., a species is particular relative to its genus but universal relative to individuals.
Later pages complete this, but for now we work with what we have.
4
Connect to logical analysis
Understand why this division matters: in a definition or syllogism, you must know whether a term is absolutely particular (haqiqi) or only relatively particular (idafi) to avoid errors in predication.

Homonym and Univocal Universals in Islamic Logic
Author defines and exemplifies homonym (mushtarak) and univocal (munfarid) universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mushtarak (homonym) and munfarid (univocal) universals by analyzing the multiplicity of their referents and apply this distinction to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A universal term is homonym if it has multiple distinct lexical meanings; univocal if it has one mental reality.

In Islamic logic, understanding the difference between homonymy and univocity is crucial for accurate definition and argumentation. This passage from our text clarifies this division using key examples from Arabic, such as 'ayn and qur'. By mastering this distinction, you will be better equipped to analyze terms in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh) and theology (kalam).

Guided Path
1
Identify the division criterion
The passage divides the universal based on the multiplicity of its referents (musamma). Notice that the criterion is whether the word has two or more distinct meanings assigned by separate linguistic conventions.
2
Extract the definition of mushtarak
A word with multiple referents, each designated by a separate convention (wad' khas), is called mushtarak. Understand that this is not mere contextual ambiguity but lexical homonymy.
3
Analyze the examples of mushtarak
Consider 'ayn (eye/spring) and qur' (purity/menstruation). Each has two entirely different referents, each requiring its own definition. Confirm that both are mushtarak.
4
Define munfarid and contrast it
A word with a single referent is munfarid, like 'insan' (human) or 'hayawan' (animal). The mental reality (al-haqiqa al-dhihniyya) is one, even though it applies to many individuals externally.
This distinction is key: multiplicity in external individuals does not make a term mushtarak.

Commentary
This passage defines two divisions of the universal (kulli) based on the number of distinct referents (musamma) it has. The author first states that the universal is divided into mushtarak (homonym) and munfarid (univocal). If the word has two or more distinct referents, each assigned by a separate linguistic convention, it is called mushtarak. The author gives two examples: 'ayn, which can mean the eye (al-basira) or a flowing spring (al-jariya), and qur', which can mean purity (tuhr) or menstruation (hayd). These are classic examples in Arabic linguistics. If the word has only one referent, it is munfarid, like 'insan' (human) or 'hayawan' (animal). The author explains that the mental reality (al-haqiqa al-dhihniyya) which is the meaning of the word is single; the multiplicity is only in the external individuals, as previously discussed in the context of universals. This distinction is crucial in logic for understanding ambiguity and univocity in terms.

Key Terms
كلي (kulli): A universal term that can be predicated of many individuals; here divided by referent multiplicity. [Note: In later texts, kulli is often restricted to the five universals; here it is a broader classification.]
مشترك (mushtarak): A word with multiple distinct referents, each assigned by a separate linguistic convention. [Note: Stable term in Arabic logic and linguistics, but in logic it specifically concerns ambiguity in terms of predication.]
منفرد (munfarid): A word whose referent is a single mental reality, though it may apply to multiple external individuals. [Note: No significant shift; this term is stable.]

Classifying Universals by External Existence
Author introduces the six-fold division and explains the first three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal into one of the first three types of the six-fold division based on the existence and possibility of its individuals in the external world.

Core Idea
A universal need not have many individuals; even one individual suffices, and possibility matters.

The author divides universals into six types based on whether individuals exist in the external world and whether such existence is intellectually possible. The first three types cover universals with no individuals (impossible or possible) and those with just one individual (allowing others). Understanding this classification sharpens your ability to distinguish between conceptual terms and their real-world referents, a key skill in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division criterion
The author first splits universals by whether any individual exists externally: none, one, or many. Each of those three splits further into two based on intellectual possibility of existence. Your task is to internalize this two-level criterion.
Memorize the six-fold structure: (none impossible, none possible, one no other, one other possible, many all impossible, many some possible?) – but source covers only first three.
2
Analyze Type 1: None, Impossible
Example: 'combination of two opposites' (جمع الضدين). No individual can exist because it is logically self-contradictory. Ask yourself: why is existence impossible? Because the concept itself entails a contradiction.
This type is absent from external world necessarily.
3
Analyze Type 2: None, Possible
Example: 'a sea of mercury' – mercury is normally liquid but can freeze at very low temperatures, so such a sea is not impossible, though it does not actually exist. The concept does not entail a contradiction.
Intellectual possibility (jawaz) is key; actual existence is not required.
4
Analyze Type 3: One, Others Possible
Example: 'Ilah' (God). The word is a universal because its meaning can apply to multiple objects (the Arabs understood it as such, as per Quran 38:5). However, by reason and revelation, only one individual truly exists. The test: can you conceive of other individuals? If yes, it's this type.
The author uses 'Ilah' to show that a universal can have a single actual individual while still being a universal in meaning.
5
Apply the classification to new examples
Given a term like 'sun' (shams) or 'prophet', determine which type it belongs to using the same criteria: existence of individuals and possibility of others. The sun is one individual, but other suns are not impossible (though they do not exist). So it resembles Type 4 (not covered here).
Practice with Islamic terms: e.g., 'Qur'an' (many copies exist, so many individuals).

Commentary
This passage presents the classification of universals into six types based on whether their individuals exist in the external world and the possibility of such existence. The author first sets up the division: a universal either has no external individuals, one external individual, or many external individuals. Each of these three categories is further subdivided into two, yielding six types. The first two types are given immediately: Type 1: a universal with no individuals, where existence of an individual is intellectually impossible (e.g., combining two opposites, like 'partner of God' in a logical sense). Type 2: a universal with no individuals, but existence is intellectually possible (e.g., 'a sea of mercury' – mercury is normally liquid but can freeze at very low temperatures, so such a sea is possible though not actual). The author notes that mercury is a flowing metal that does not freeze except below zero at forty degrees. Then the text continues with Type 3: a universal from which one individual exists, while other individuals are possible (like 'Ilah' – God). The author argues that 'Ilah' is a universal term because the Arabs could conceive of multiple gods, as mentioned in the Quran. A key benefit of it being a universal is that the negative 'la' of genus (used to negate an entire genus) enters upon it in the shahada, and this particle only enters universals. However, reason, revelation, and consensus confirm that only one individual truly deserves worship. The author then addresses an objection: there are many false objects of worship. The answer is that those are nonexistent by sacred law (shar'an) just as if they were nonexistent physically; they are not true objects of worship, but fabrications. Finally, the text begins Type 4: a universal with one individual, but other individuals are possible (e.g., 'Shams' – the sun), but the explanation is cut off at the page end.

A Universal with a Single Existing Individual: The Case of 'Ilah'
Definition of a universal with one existing individual and refutation of the objection about multiple false gods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a universal that applies to only one existing individual and explain the logical and linguistic evidence for its universality.

Core Idea
A universal is defined by conceptual applicability, not by the number of real individuals.

In Islamic logic, a universal (kulli) is a concept that can apply to more than one individual in the mind, even if only one such individual actually exists. This lesson explores the powerful example of 'Ilah' (God), which is a universal genus noun, yet sound intellect and revelation confirm only one real individual: Allah. Understanding this nuance is crucial for grasping the logical structure of the Shahada and defending tawhid against objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'kulli'
The author states that 'Ilah' is a kulli (universal). Notice that the defining feature of a universal is that the mind can apply it to multiple individuals, not that multiple individuals actually exist.
2
Examine the linguistic evidence
The author points out that the negative 'la' of genus enters upon 'Ilah' in the Shahada. This construction is only used with universal nouns of genera. This grammatical rule proves 'Ilah' is a universal in Arabic.
The 'la' of genus negates the entire genus, which only makes sense if the noun is universal.
3
Distinguish logical universality from actual existence
Although 'Ilah' is a universal, sound intellect, the Book, Sunnah, and consensus confirm only one existing individual: Allah. The logical universality does not require multiple real instances.
Do not confuse the logical property of a term with the number of its referents in reality.
4
Understand the refutation of the objection
The objection states that false gods exist in abundance. The author replies that legally non-existent (ma'dum shar'an) is like sensibly non-existent; these false gods are not true individuals of the universal 'Ilah'. They are merely invented claims.
The author uses a legal criterion to exclude false gods from the extension of 'Ilah'.

Core Idea

Identify the question

Classifying the Five Universals: Genus, Differentia, Species, Property, Common Accident
Author explains the division of universals into five based on essence and accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given universal into one of the five categories based on its relation to the quiddity.

Core Idea
The five universals are an exhaustive classification based on a binary tree of essence and accident.

In this passage, the author systematically divides universals into five categories by examining whether the universal is part of the quiddity (essence) or not, and whether it is broader or coextensive. This classification is fundamental for constructing precise definitions in Islamic logic and sciences. By learning this tree, you will be able to quickly identify the logical role of any universal term.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quiddity (mahiyya)
Begin by clarifying what the species is; the quiddity is its essence. For example, the quiddity of 'human' is 'rational animal'.
2
Is the universal the complete quiddity?
If the universal exactly matches the quiddity of the species, it is the species itself (naw‘). If not, proceed.
E.g., 'human' is the complete quiddity of humans.
3
Is it inside or outside the quiddity?
Inside means the universal is a constituent part of the essence. Outside means it is accidental, not part of the essence.
4
If inside, is it broader or equal?
A broader inside universal is the genus (jins); an equal inside universal is the differentia (fasl).
E.g., 'animal' is broader (genus), 'rational' is equal (differentia) for human.
5
If outside, is it broader or equal?
A broader outside universal is the common accident (‘araḍ ‘āmm); an equal outside universal is the property (khāṣṣah).
E.g., 'walking' is broader (common accident), 'laughing' is equal (property) for human.

Three Criteria for Essential vs. Accidental in Definitions
Author lists three criteria for distinguishing essential from accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three criteria (indispensability, uncausedness, inseparability) to determine whether an attribute is essential (dhati) or accidental (aradi) in a given definition.

Core Idea
Essential attributes are indispensable, uncaused, and inseparable from the essence; accidents are not.

In defining things, logicians distinguish attributes that belong to the essence (dhati) from those that are external (aradi). But what is the real difference? This passage gives three clear criteria—like a checklist—to test any attribute. Let's examine each one and see how they work together.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attribute in question
Look at the attribute being tested. Is it claimed to be part of the essence or just an accident? For example, in the passage, 'rational' and 'laughing' for man.
2
Apply the first criterion: Conceivability
Can the essence (e.g., human reality) be conceived without this attribute? If yes, it is accidental; if no, it is essential. The passage says the essence cannot be conceived without rationality but can be conceived without laughter.
3
Apply the second criterion: Causality
Is the attribute caused by something else? Essential attributes are not caused; accidents are caused. For example, laughter is caused by rationality, so it is accidental.
4
Apply the third criterion: Removal
If we suppose the attribute is removed, does the essence remain? If the essence disappears, the attribute is essential; if the essence remains, it is accidental. For man, removing rationality makes the essence inconceivable, but removing laughter does not.
5
Cross-check all three
All three criteria should agree. If an attribute meets all three tests for essentiality, it is dhati. If it fails any, it is aradi. This ensures consistency.

Three Criteria for Essential vs Accidental
Author explains three criteria distinguishing essential from accidental attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the three criteria to determine whether a given attribute is essential or accidental in an Islamic definition.

Core Idea
Essential attributes define what something is; accidental attributes have causes and can be removed without destroying the essence.

In this lesson, the author presents three clear differences (fawārīq) that separate essential (dhātī) from accidental (ʿaraḍī) attributes. These criteria are crucial for constructing proper definitions in Islamic sciences. You will learn how to test attributes using these criteria, especially using the example of 'rationality' for humans.

Guided Path
1
Recall the first difference
Although not directly in this passage, the first difference (from a previous lesson) is that the essential defines the essence, while the accidental can be separated without affecting the definition. Keep this in mind as a foundation.
This lesson builds on the first difference.
2
Apply the second difference: causality
Ask: Does it make sense to ask 'why' about this attribute? For essential attributes like rationality, asking 'why is man rational?' is meaningless because it is intrinsic. For accidental attributes like laughter, asking 'why did you laugh?' is natural and has a cause.
Essential traits are not caused (lā yuʿallal); accidental traits are caused (yuʿallal).
3
Apply the third difference: removability
Imagine removing the attribute entirely from the essence. If the essence can no longer exist, the attribute is essential. For example, if you remove rationality from a human, that being is no longer human. If you remove laughter, the human remains.
This criterion tests whether the attribute is constitutive of the essence.
4
Handle exceptions with jibillah
The text clarifies that occasional absence (e.g., insanity) does not refute essentiality. The definition refers to the natural disposition (jibillah) inherent in the kind, not every individual. So a rational animal remains the definition even if some individuals temporarily lack rationality.
This nuance prevents false counterexamples from undermining definitions.

It is possible that a single thing exists externally in which the realities of different conceptual divisions are realized according to different considerations. An example of this is what is termed in technical language the relative species, which we have explained previously. For instance, animal: it is a genus relative to the individuals under it such as man and horse, and it is a species relative to what is above it such as the growing thing (al-nami), because animal is a species of the genus al-nami, and its other species is plant, because it grows (i.e., increases gradually). Likewise al-nami: it is a genus relative to the individuals under it such as animal and plant, and a species relative to what is above it such as body, because from body there are growing things and non-growing things like stone. So if you were to divide animal saying: animal is either a genus or a species, meaning by its being a genus its relation to what is under it, and by its being a species its relation to what is above it, then this division is called a consideration division (taqsim i'tibari), because you have divided animal into genus and species according to two different considerations. Genus and species in this example differ in concept (mafhum), because the concept of species is different from the concept of genus as previously explained in the five universals. So the two parts of this division differ in concept, but in this mentioned example they are identical in extension (misdaq), because the very thing meant by the genus is the very thing meant by the species. So what is meant by both is only animal, but it becomes a genus in one consideration and a species in another consideration. Thus the concept differs by the two considerations, while the extension is one. And similarly if you say: al-nami is either a genus or a species, meaning by its being a genus its relation to what is under it like animal and plant, and by its being a species its relation to what is above it like body, and so on.

This passage explains the concept of relative predication within the five universals. This illustrates that the five universals are not fixed attributes but depend on the relationship being considered. This is a key point in logic: one thing can be described by different universals from different angles.
