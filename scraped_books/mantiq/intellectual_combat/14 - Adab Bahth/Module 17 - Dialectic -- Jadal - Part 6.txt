Al-Qawl bi al-Mujib: Accepting Evidence but Denying Its Application
Author defines al-qawl bi al-mujib, contrasts with naqd and mu'arada, and discusses its scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish al-qawl bi al-mujib from naqd and mu'arada in a dialectical exchange.

Core Idea
Al-qawl bi al-mujib accepts the evidence but denies its relevance – a subtle but crucial dialectical weapon.

In Islamic dialectics, not every objection rejects the opponent's evidence. One of the cleverest moves is to accept the evidence entirely yet claim it does not apply to the case at hand. This is known as al-qawl bi al-mujib. In this lesson, we will explore how this concept is defined, how it compares to other objections like naqd and mu'arada, and why it is considered a distinct and powerful tool in usul al-fiqh.

2

The author contrasts this with naqd, where the evidence itself is rejected.

The author says both involve the signification not following, but in naqd the evidence is nullified, while in qawl bi al-mujib the evidence is accepted. Detail the difference.

Commentary
This passage is an analytical exposition of the dialectical term 'al-qawl bi al-mujib' (argument by affirmative), as used in usul al-fiqh and dialectical debate. The author begins by linking the term to the concept of 'shuhra' (fame/well-knownness): a known premise may be omitted because it is taken for granted, and the objection of qawl bi al-mujib arises when the opponent claims that the argument's conclusion does not actually apply to the case under dispute, even though he accepts the premises. The author then notes that some usul scholars treat both qawl bi al-mujib and qalb (inversion) as forms of mu'arada (counterargument). He mentions that Fakhr al-Razi categorizes them as 'qawadih' (defects) in the cause, but the author finds this too narrow, since qawl bi al-mujib can apply to all types of evidence, not just causal reasoning. The author then contrasts qawl bi al-mujib with naqd (refutation): in naqd, the opponent claims the conclusion does not follow and thereby nullifies the evidence; in qawl bi al-mujib, the opponent accepts the evidence as valid but argues that it does not pertain to the disputed point. Thus, while both involve the conclusion not following, they differ in that qawl bi al-mujib does not reject the evidence itself. The author concludes that it is best classified as a type of mu'arada, or alternatively as a specific kind of man' (prevention) that occurs after the evidence is accepted. The passage ends by distinguishing this usul usage from the rhetorical one, where qawl bi al-mujib is a form of semantic embellishment. Throughout, the author engages with earlier authorities, refining the definition and clarifying the boundaries of the term. This discussion is crucial for understanding the precise mechanics of objection in Islamic dialectics (jadal).

Al-Qawl bi al-Mujib: Distinguishing a Dialectical Objection
Author defines al-qawl bi al-mujib and distinguishes it from naqd and mu'arada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify al-qawl bi al-mujib in a dialectical exchange and explain how it differs from al-naqd and al-mu'arada using criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Al-qawl bi al-mujib accepts the proof but denies its application.

In Islamic dialectics (adab al-bahth), objections take different forms. One subtle but important objection is al-qawl bi al-mujib, where the objector concedes the validity of the proof but argues that the conclusion does not apply to the case at hand. This passage from the author clarifies its nature and distinguishes it from other objection types, especially al-naqd and al-mu'arada.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Identify where the author defines al-qawl bi al-mujib. Note the two key elements: (1) the objector accepts the proof itself, and (2) the import (madlul) is absent from the proof in the objector's claim.
2

Find the sentence that compares al-qawl bi al-mujib to al-naqd. What do they share (absence of import from proof) and how do they differ? In naqd, the proof itself is rejected; in al-qawl bi al-mujib, the proof is accepted.
Use the example: In naqd, the objector says 'your proof is invalid'; in al-qawl bi al-mujib, he says 'I accept your proof, but it doesn't apply here.'
3
Identify the relation to mu'arada
The author says al-qawl bi al-mujib is closest to being a type of mu'arada. Underline the reason: the objector brings another proof to show that the original proof does not apply to the point of contention.
Remember: mu'arada is a counter-argument with a new proof.
4
Consider the alternative view
The author mentions an alternative: that it is a special kind of man' (preclusion) that occurs after accepting the proof. What is the basis of this view? It precludes the proof's applicability to the specific case.
5
Note the terminological boundary

Commentary
This passage defines and clarifies the term 'al-qawl bi al-mujib' (affirming the requirement) used in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh) and dialectics. The author begins by stating that it invalidates the proof, linking back to an earlier clarification. He then explains that in this objection, the import or implication (al-madlul) that the opponent claims follows from the proof is not actually present, even though the objector concedes the proof itself. This distinguishes it from 'al-naqd' (refutation), where the proof is directly challenged. However, they share the feature that the import is absent from the proof. The author leans toward considering 'al-qawl bi al-mujib' as a type of counter-argument (mu'arada) because the objector brings another proof to show that the opponent's proof does not apply to the case in dispute. An alternative suggestion is that it is a special kind of preclusion (man'), occurring after the acceptance of the proof, but this preclusion is for the reason that the proof does not pertain to the point of contention. The author finds this view plausible. He then notes that this is the technical meaning in usul al-fiqh, as opposed to its use in rhetoric (balagha) where it is a figure of speech. This clarification helps the student avoid confusing the two disciplines.

Key Terms
القول بالموجب (al-qawl bi al-mujib): An objection where the opponent accepts the proof but denies that its conclusion applies to the disputed case. [Note: In rhetorical terminology, it is a figure of speech; the author distinguishes his usul usage from that.]
النقض (al-naqd): A direct refutation of the proof itself, as opposed to accepting it.
المعارضة (al-mu'arada): A counter-argument that presents another proof to show that the original proof does not hold in the context.

The Objection that a Ruling Fails to Achieve Its Benefit
Author defines the objection and provides an example.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A ruling's benefit can be attacked by showing it backfires.

In dialectical argumentation within usul al-fiqh, one powerful way to challenge a ruling is to argue that it fails to achieve the very benefit (maslaha) for which it was legislated. This lesson unpacks the structure of this objection using a classic example: whether permanently prohibiting marriage with certain in-laws actually prevents immorality or, as the objector claims, increases it.

Author constructs a counterargument by the like against the false syllogism.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A counterargument by the like refutes falsehood by constructing a parallel valid syllogism that yields the truth.

In the third aspect of refuting the false syllogism denying istiwa', the author employs a powerful dialectical tool: counterargument by the like (mu'arada bi al-mithl). This involves constructing a similar conditional syllogism that leads to the opposite, true conclusion, thereby exposing the falsity of the original. This lesson will teach you how to apply this method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original false syllogism
The deniers' argument: 'If Allah is established on the Throne, He resembles creation. Resemblance is impossible, so He is not established on the Throne.' This is a conditional syllogism with a false major premise.

2
Construct the opposite conditional
The author proposes: 'If Allah were not established on the Throne, He would not have praised Himself for being on the Throne in seven verses.' This conditional is opposite in content but similar in form to the original.
The new conditional must have a true consequent to be effective.
3
Verify the truth of the conditional's components
The antecedent (not istiwa') is hypothetical, but the consequent (no praise) is false because the Qur'an does praise istiwa'. So the conditional is not directly used; instead, we affirm the contradictory of the consequent (He did praise).
The method relies on the truth of the consequent's negation.
4
Apply modus tollens to the counter conditional
From the conditional 'If not P then not Q', and the fact that Q is true (He praised Himself), we can infer not (not P) = P. So we conclude 'He is established on the Throne'.
This is a valid modus tollens argument if the conditional is true.
5

The conclusion matches the Qur'anic affirmation of istiwa'. This counterargument refutes the original syllogism by producing the opposite true conclusion using the same logical form.
The author notes this is a valid counterargument (mu'arada bi al-mithl) that yields the truth.

Author demonstrates mu'arada bi al-mithl to refute denial of Allah's istiwa'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a mu'arada bi al-mithl to refute an opponent's conditional argument using a qiyas istithna'i.

Core Idea
Mu'arada bi al-mithl defeats false conditionals by producing a true conditional that yields the opposite conclusion.

This passage demonstrates the dialectical method of mu'arada bi al-mithl (counter-argument by the like) in a theological context. The opponent claims that Allah's istiwa' (establishment over the Throne) implies anthropomorphism. The author counters by constructing a similar conditional syllogism that leads to the true conclusion that Allah is established over His Throne, without resemblance. This tool is essential for defending Islamic theological positions in debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim as a conditional
The opponent states: 'If He were established over the Throne, He would resemble creation.' This is a conditional proposition (shartiyya) with antecedent 'He is established' and consequent 'He resembles creation.'
Recognizing the logical form is the first step to countering it.
2
Construct a counter-conditional that contradicts the opponent's consequent
The author proposes: 'If He were not established over the Throne, He would not have praised Himself for it.' The consequent 'He would not have praised Himself' is the contradictory of the opponent's implied belief (that Allah did praise Himself).
The counter-conditional must be plausible and grounded in revelation (e.g., seven verses).
3
Test the necessary connection (luzum)
Verify that the connection between antecedent and consequent in the counter-conditional is necessary (luzumiyya). Here, it is logically and theologically sound: if Allah were not established, He would not have praised Himself for it.
The strength of the mu'arada depends on the obviousness of the connection.
4
Identify the syllogistic form (qiyas istithna'i)
The counter-argument is an exceptional syllogism: the major premise is the conditional, and the minor premise excepts the contradictory of the consequent (affirming the praise). This yields the contradictory of the antecedent (affirming establishment). The form is valid: (¬E → ¬P), P ∴ E.
This is modus tollens in effect: affirming the denial of the consequent leads to the denial of the antecedent.
5

The same logic applies to attributes like hearing, sight, power, and will. The opponent accepts these without tashbih, so singling out istiwa' is inconsistent. This exposes the fallacy in the opponent's argument.
Consistency is a key dialectical principle.

This passage is a dialectical refutation of the claim that affirming certain divine attributes (like hearing, sight) necessarily entails anthropomorphism (tashbih), while others (like istiwa') do not. The author presents two arguments. First, language inherently requires that attributes match their subject; therefore, the Creator's greatness and the creature's lowliness are fundamentally different, a fact understood by early Muslims. Second, the author turns the opponent's reasoning against them: if affirming hearing and sight for God is problematic because in Arabic these terms refer to physical senses, then the same applies to istiwa'. The conclusion is that there is a categorical difference between the Creator's and creatures' attributes, just as there is between their essences. The passage ends by urging adherence to revelation as the sole guarantee of safety from error.

Three Responses to a Refutation
Author lists three ways to respond to a refutation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the three standard responses to a refutation in a disputation example.

Core Idea
Three defensive moves: deny the proof, deny the consequence, or deny the impossibility.

When an opponent refutes your argument, you cannot simply remain silent. The tradition of adab al-bahth provides three standard responses that must be justified with evidence. This lesson anchors these three moves so you can recognise them in real debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's refutation
First, understand what the opponent is claiming: they assert that your proof is incomplete, that your conclusion does not follow, or that your argument leads to an impossibility.
The refutation itself determines which response is appropriate.
2
Examine the first response: deny the complete proof
If the opponent says your proof is missing a premise or is incomplete, you can respond by denying that their objection holds, or by providing the missing premise. The key is to show that the proof (tamam dalil al-da'wa) is indeed complete.
This response attacks the refutation's claim about the proof's completeness.
3
Examine the second response: deny the missing consequence
If the opponent claims your conclusion does not follow from your premises (takhalluf al-madlul), you can insist that the consequence does follow. You may need to clarify the logical connection or the terms used.
This response defends the validity of the inference.
4
Examine the third response: deny the implication of impossibility
If the opponent argues that your proof leads to an absurd or impossible conclusion (istilzam al-muhal), you can either deny that the conclusion is impossible, or deny that your proof requires it. You might show that the supposed impossibility is not actually impossible, or that it follows from a misinterpretation.
This response defends against reduction to absurdity.

Core Idea
Counterargument is not denial; it is a positive proof of the opposite.

In dialectical debate, when an opponent presents an argument, you may not always attack its form or premises. Sometimes, the most effective method is to provide a counterargument that establishes something directly opposed to the opponent's claim. This passage from Al-Adab al-Bahth introduces the three types of counterargument based on what is established: the contradictory, its equivalent, or something more specific. Additionally, counterarguments are divided into those attacking the cause and those attacking the proof. Understanding these distinctions equips you to respond strategically in scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of counterargument

These are all ways of countering the original claim.
2
Distinguish contradictory, equivalent, and more specific
The contradictory is the exact opposite in truth value. The equivalent is a proposition that is not contradictory but equally opposite in strength (e.g., permissible vs forbidden). More specific is a subcase that opposes the general claim (e.g., disliked vs forbidden).
In debate, choosing the right level of opposition affects the burden of proof.
3
Differentiate counterargument in the cause vs. in the proof
The passage divides counterargument into two types: 'counterargument in the cause (العلة)' and 'counterargument in the proof (الدليل)'. The former attacks the underlying 'illa of the opponent's ruling; the latter attacks the evidence or argument itself.

Understanding al-Qawl bi al-Mujib: Saying with the Affirmative in Dialectic
Author enumerates the aspects of the 'saying with the affirmative' objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four aspects of al-qawl bi al-mujib and distinguish it from al-naqd and al-mu'arada.

Core Idea
Al-qawl bi al-mujib is not mere agreement; it's a strategic concession that turns the opponent's words against them.

In jadal (dialectic), not all objections are outright denials. One subtle and powerful tool is 'al-qawl bi al-mujib' – accepting the proponent's statement but interpreting it to your advantage. This lesson breaks down its four aspects and clarifies its relation to other objection types like al-naqd and al-mu'arada.

Guided Path
1
First Aspect: Defect in the Negative
Identify the first cause: the objection occurs when there is a defect in the negative side of the proponent's statement. For example, if the proponent says 'This is not prohibited,' the respondent accepts the positive implication but points out a flaw in the negative reasoning.
This targets the negation part of the argument.
2
Second Aspect: Upon Affirmation
The objection occurs upon a positive affirmation. The respondent accepts the proponent's affirmative claim but uses it to draw an unwanted consequence. For instance, accepting 'Prayer is obligatory' but then arguing it proves the act is temporal.
Focus on the affirmative side.
3
Third Aspect: Inclusiveness of Wording
The objection arises because the proponent's wording encompasses an agreed-upon form. The respondent accepts the literal wording but applies it to a case the proponent did not intend, thereby turning the argument.
e.g., if the proponent says 'Every action that brings reward is good,' the respondent applies it to an action the proponent considers bad.
4
Fourth Aspect: Silence on an Unknown Premise
The respondent notes that the proponent remained silent about a premise that is not well-known, using that silence to reinterpret the statement advantageously.
This exploits gaps in the proponent's presentation.
5
Compare with Naqd and Mu'arada
The author corrects Fakhr al-Razi who limited al-qawl bi al-mujib to objections on the cause. He likens it to al-naqd (contradiction) but ultimately classifies it as a type of mu'arada (counterargument). Understand the difference: naqd highlights inconsistency, mu'arada provides a parallel argument, while al-qawl bi al-mujib accepts and repurposes.
This step solidifies the concept's place in jadal.

Core Idea
Accusations of tashbih, denial of zahir, and distortion of 'istiwa' are answered by questioning the opponent's methodological principle.

This passage presents three standard criticisms leveled against the theological method of the later scholars (mutakallimun). After listing these accusations, the author turns to critique the very principle that the later scholars claim for themselves: that their school is more judicious and knowledgeable. Understand the targeted principle
