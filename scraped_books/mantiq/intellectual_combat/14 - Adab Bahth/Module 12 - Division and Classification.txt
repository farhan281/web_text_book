Distinguishing Between Kulli (Universal) and Kull (Whole)
Author distinguishes between kulli and kull regarding division and conjunction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a universal (kulli) and a whole (kull) by identifying whether a statement uses division (or) or conjunction (and).

Core Idea
Universal divides into individuals; whole conjoins parts.

This passage clarifies a subtle but crucial distinction. You might think 'universal' and 'whole' are similar, but they behave differently under logical operations. Understanding this will help you correctly analyze classifications and predications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key contrast
Find where the author says 'بخلاف (الكل)' – that is the pivot between kulli and kull.
2
Examine the examples for kulli
The author says 'Animal is either human or horse' – note the use of 'إما...أو' (either...or) to divide the universal into its particulars.
3
Examine the examples for kull
Contrast with 'The chair is either wood or nails' which is rejected. Instead, the correct predication is 'The chair is nails and wood' with 'and' (الواو).
4
Understand the rule
Kulli can be divided by the tool of division (أداة التقسيم) into its instances; kull cannot be divided that way but can be predicated on its parts via conjunction (حمل مواطأة العطف).

The second aspect: That the universal (al-kulli) may be divided by means of the tool of division into its particulars, such as one saying: 'Animal is either human or horse, or mule or donkey, etc.' Contrary to the whole (al-kull), it is not permissible to divide it into its parts by means of the tool of division, so it is incorrect to say: 'The chair is either wood or nails,' nor to say: 'The tree is either trunks or branches.' Rather, it is permissible to predicate the whole upon its parts by way of predicative conjunction (haml muwata'at al-'atf) specifically, meaning the conjunction of some of its parts with some others, as in your saying: 'The chair is nails and wood,' and 'The tree is trunks and branches.' As for the part (al-juz'), it is that from which, together with something else, a whole is composed, like nails in relation to the chair, and wood in relation to it, and the trunk in relation to the tree, and branches in relation to it. And the particular proposition (al-juz'iyya) in terminology is the proposition whose predicate is judged to apply to some individuals of its subject, not all of them, negatively or affirmatively. An example of the affirmative: 'Some animal is human,' and an example of the negative: 'Some animal is not human.' Author defines juz' (part) and juz'iyya (particular proposition).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A part composes a whole; a particular proposition judges some, not all.

After distinguishing universals from wholes, the author defines two more crucial terms: 'part' and 'particular proposition'. These definitions are foundational for understanding composition in logic and quantification in propositions.

3

A particular proposition is a proposition whose predicate is judged to apply to some individuals of the subject, not all, either affirmatively or negatively.
4
Study the examples
Affirmative: 'Some animal is human.' Negative: 'Some animal is not human.' Notice the quantifier 'some' (بعض).

Division of Speech: What Can Be Debated
Author divides speech into simple and compound and states debate rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any expression as simple or compound and determine whether it can be debated in munazara.

Core Idea
Only incomplete compounds that qualify a proposition can be debated.

In this passage, the author of Adab al-Bahth lays down a foundational rule for debate: not every expression is a proper subject of munazara. He divides speech (kalam) into simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab), and explains which type may be debated and which only allows clarification. This classification is essential for understanding the boundaries of scholarly disputation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
The author divides speech (kalam) into simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). Simple is a single word; compound is composed of more than one word.
2
Rule for simple expressions
Simple expressions never admit debate (munazara), but if the term is obscure, one may request clarification (istifsar). This is not a debate move.
Istifsar is distinct from munazara.
3
Understand incomplete compound
Compound is further divided into complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis). The incomplete compound is a phrase that does not form a complete sentence, such as a restrictive phrase (taqyidi) like 'rational animal'.
The restrictive compound is a subtype of incomplete compound.
4
Condition for debate
An incomplete compound can be debated only if it serves as a condition (qayd) of a proposition. That is, its meaning restricts or qualifies the subject or predicate of a claim.
If it is not a condition, it is not debatable.

Commentary
This passage introduces the classification of speech (kalam) into two types: simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). The author notes that this division was already explained in the logical introduction. He then gives the rule for each type in the context of munazara (debate). Simple expressions, such as single words, do not admit debate; however, if the term is obscure, one may request clarification (istifsar). This is not debate but a preliminary clarification. The incomplete compound (murakkab naqis) is a phrase that does not form a complete sentence, such as a restrictive phrase (taqyidi) like 'rational animal' (al-hayawan al-natiq). Such phrases can be debated only if they serve as a condition (qayd) of a proposition (qadiyya). For example, if someone says 'the rational animal is risible', the phrase 'rational animal' is a subject that can be scrutinized. Thus, the debate is about the condition's validity or definition, not about the phrase in isolation.

Key Terms

مفرد (mufrad): Simple: a single word or term with no internal composition, e.g., 'Adam' or 'standing'. [Note: Standard definition in logic and grammar.]
مركب (murakkab): Compound: composed of more than one word; further divided into complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis). [Note: Same as in grammar; logic emphasizes the completeness for truth-evaluation.]
المركب الناقص (al-murakkab al-naqis): Incomplete compound: a phrase that is not a complete sentence, e.g., 'rational animal'. [Note: In grammar, it is not predicated; in logic, it can be a term in a proposition.]
المركب التقييدي (al-murakkab al-taqyidi): Restrictive compound: a descriptive phrase that restricts the meaning, like an adjective-noun combination. [Note: Specific term in adab al-bahth; not common in other logical works.]
المناظرة (al-munazara): Debate or disputation: a structured exchange of arguments following procedural rules. [Note: Central term in adab al-bahth; in broader logic, it is one of the five arts (jadal).]
الاستفسار (al-istifsar): Request for clarification: an inquiry about the meaning of an obscure term, not a debate. [Note: Distinct from munazara; used when a term is unfamiliar.]
قيد (qayd): Condition or restriction: a qualification that limits the scope of a proposition. [Note: Common in usul al-fiqh and logic; here it refers to the function of a restrictive phrase.]

Four Implicit Claims in Logical Divisions
Author explains how dividing a universal into its particulars implies four claims that can be contested.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four implicit claims in any logical division and explain how each can be contested in a disputation.

Core Idea
Every division hides four contestable claims: exhaustiveness, specificity, exclusivity, and purity.

In classical Islamic disputation (munazara), even a simple division like 'body is either moving or at rest' carries logical commitments. The author unpacks four implicit propositions that turn any division into a potential site of debate. Understanding these claims allows you to either defend a division or challenge an opponent's.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division and the divided whole
Find the universal being divided (muqsim) and the parts listed. In the example, 'body' is divided into 'moving' and 'at rest'.
2
Extract the claim of exhaustiveness (hasir)
The division implicitly claims to cover all genuine types of the divided whole. Check if any part is missing. For body, the claim is that no body is without motion or rest or both.
A challenger might argue that a body can be neither (e.g., a body in a vacuum with no forces).
3
Extract the claims of specificity and exclusivity
Each part must be more specific than the whole (akhas), and the parts must be mutually exclusive (mutabayin). For moving and rest, are they truly exclusive? Could a body be both moving and at rest in different respects?
Mutual exclusivity means no overlap in reality, not in concept.
4
Extract the claim of purity
Only genuine types of the divided whole are included; no extraneous categories. The division should not be 'impure' by including something that is not a type.
If a third category like 'changing' were added, that might be a type of body but would break the claimed two-part division.

The Four Implicit Claims in a Division
Author explains the four implicit claims in a division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four implicit claims in any given division and construct objections against each.

Core Idea
Every division carries four hidden claims that can be challenged.

In Islamic debate (munazara), when you divide a universal into its particulars, you are not merely listing parts; you are making four logical commitments. This lesson unpacks those claims using the example from kalam: 'Body is either moving or stationary'. Understanding these claims is essential for critiquing a division in usul al-fiqh or kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divided item and its parts
Locate the term being divided (muqassam) and the listed parts (aqsam). In the example, 'body' is divided into 'moving' and 'stationary'.
The divided item must be a universal or genus.
2
Check exhaustiveness (hasr)
Verify that the division covers all possible types of the divided item. In the example, is every body either moving or stationary? Could there be a body that is neither? If not, the division is not exhaustive.
An exhaustive division leaves no instance outside the listed parts.
3
Check absolute specificity (akhas mutlaqan)
Each part must be a proper subset of the divided item. For instance, 'moving' is a subset of 'body' because every moving thing is a body, but not every body is moving. If a part were equal to the divided item, the division would be invalid.
The part must be absolutely more specific, not equal or overlapping.
4
Check mutual exclusivity (mubayana)
No two parts should share any individual. In the example, 'moving' and 'stationary' are mutually exclusive: a body cannot be both moving and stationary in the same respect.
Overlap would violate the purpose of division.
5
Check for foreign elements
Ensure that every part is a genuine type of the divided item. For example, 'green' would not be a type of 'body' in this context.
Foreign elements are those not belonging to the divided item's species.

Inductive Division – Definition and Limitations
Author defines inductive division as empirical enumeration of all members of a genus, lacking rational certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish inductive division from mental division and identify examples of inductive division in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Inductive division is only as strong as your empirical survey; the intellect remains open to other possibilities.

In the study of definition and classification, logicians distinguish between divisions that are rationally necessary and those that are merely empirical. This lesson unpacks the concept of inductive division (taqsim istiqra'i), a type of division that relies on observation and tracking rather than logical necessity. Using examples from Arabic grammar and natural philosophy, the author shows how such divisions lack absolute certainty and remain open to revision.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the contrast with mental division
Understand that inductive division is defined against the backdrop of mental (rational) division. In mental division, the intellect judges the confinement of divisions with necessity (e.g., 'existent' must be either eternal or contingent). In inductive division, the intellect does not judge necessity; the confinement is based on empirical enumeration.
This contrast is the key to understanding the limitations of inductive division.
2
Identify the method: tracking (tatabb')
The author emphasizes that the person performing inductive division does so by tracking (tatabb') and induction (istiqra'). They examine individual cases until they believe no division remains unmentioned. This is a method of empirical investigation.
The term 'tatabb'' implies a thorough, case-by-case survey.
3
Analyze the examples given
The passage gives three examples: elements (water, earth, air, fire), parts of speech (noun, verb, particle), and types of subject (explicit, implicit). For each, ask: is this based on rational necessity or empirical observation? The elements are empirical: we observe four elements, but reason can conceive of others. In grammar, the division of word into three types is based on comprehensive linguistic induction, but it is not logically necessary – theoretically a fourth type could exist.
The examples come from natural philosophy and Arabic grammar (Nahw).
4
Assess the certainty level
Determine that inductive division yields probable knowledge (zanni) rather than certain knowledge (yaqini). The author states 'the intellect deems it possible that other divisions exist.' Therefore, such divisions are open to revision if new empirical evidence emerges.
This is in contrast to mental division, which yields certainty.
5
Apply the distinction to a new case
Imagine a division like 'mammals are either land-dwelling or aquatic.' Is this inductive or mental? It is inductive because it is based on observed habitats; reason allows for amphibians (which exist). So it is not exhaustive.
This step encourages active application of the concept.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish rational division from inductive division and determine the certainty level of each type using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Rational division yields certainty through logical opposites; inductive division relies on empirical investigation and can be certain or probable.

In logic and Islamic sciences, the way we divide a concept into its parts determines the strength of our conclusions. Some divisions are logically necessary, while others depend on observation. In this passage, the author explains two key types of division: rational and inductive, showing how each achieves exhaustive enumeration (hasr) and what level of certainty it provides.

Guided Path

Identify the two types of division

The Arabic terms are key: تقسيم عقلي for rational, تقسيم استقرائي for inductive.
2
Analyze the method of rational division
Rational division uses the principle of contradiction: divide into the thing and its opposite (negation/affirmation). Example: the creator is either the creatures or not. This yields exhaustive division with certainty.
The author states rational division is 'absolutely certain' (قطعي مطلقا).
3
Analyze the method of inductive division
Inductive division traces external individuals. Examples: elements, parts of speech, subject types, predicate types. The intellect does not guarantee completeness; it depends on investigation.
Inductive division may be certain if enumeration is complete, like the predicate's threefold division.
4
Assess the certainty of each type
Rational is always certain. Inductive may be certain (if enumeration is complete, like predicate division) or probable (like elements where other possibilities exist).
The author emphasizes that inductive division can be certain when no further division is possible.
5
Recognize the stylistic adaptation
Inductive division can be presented in rational form using negation/affirmation for control, but this does not change its essential inductive nature.
This is done to restrict divisions and prevent dispersal.

Two Conditions for a Valid Division
Author states two conditions for valid division of a whole into its constituent parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to verify whether a division of a whole into its parts satisfies the conditions of exhaustiveness and distinctness in predication.

Core Idea
A valid division must exhaust all parts and keep each part distinct from the whole in predication.

In the Islamic scholarly tradition, analyzing a concept often involves breaking it down into its constituent parts. However, not every division is logically sound. This passage from the Adab al-Bahth tradition gives two precise conditions that ensure a division is valid and meaningful. Mastering these conditions will sharpen your ability to evaluate definitions and classifications in disciplines like Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
Read the passage and locate the two conditions: first, the division must be exhaustive (hāṣir), meaning it covers all parts and excludes extraneous ones. Second, each part must be distinct (mubāyin) from the other parts and from the whole in terms of predication (haml), not in terms of actual existence (tahaqquq).
The terms hāṣir and mubāyin are key in the Ilm al-Wad' tradition.
2
Understand the first condition (hāṣir)
A valid division must include every part that composes the whole and prevent any non-part from being listed as a part. If a division omits a part or includes something external, it fails the first condition.
Think of a complete set: no missing members, no intruders.
3
Understand the second condition (mubāyin)
Each part must be predicatively distinct from the whole and from other parts. This means you cannot truthfully say that any part is the whole itself (e.g., 'the nail is the chair'), nor can two parts be co-extensive. However, this distinctness is only in predication; in reality, the parts exist when the whole exists.
The example clarifies: wood and nails are not chairs, but when the chair exists, wood and nails necessarily exist.
4
Apply to the chair example
Test the division 'chair = wood + nails' against the two conditions. The parts are exhaustive (no missing part) and mutually exclusive. Neither wood nor nails can be called 'chair' (distinct in predication). Yet when the chair exists, both wood and nails exist (no real separation). The division passes.
This example illustrates both conditions perfectly.
5
Test a counterexample
Imagine someone divides 'book' into 'cover and pages'. Is this exhaustive? Missing the spine? Can you say 'the cover is a book'? That would violate the second condition. Practise identifying failures.
This step helps internalize the logic.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given division satisfies the conditions of exhaustiveness (hasr), exclusivity (man'), distinctness (mubayana), and specificity (akhass mutlaq) for both whole-into-parts and universal-into-particulars divisions.

A valid division must cover all parts, exclude extras, keep parts distinct, and ensure specifics are absolutely more specific.

In this passage, the author lays out the logical rules for dividing a whole into its parts or a universal into its particulars. These conditions prevent confusion and ensure that classification serves clear reasoning. We will walk through each condition with the concrete examples provided, from the chair (whole-into-parts) to the possible (universal-into-particulars).

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of division
First, determine whether the division is of a whole into its parts (like a chair into wood and nails) or of a universal into its particulars (like possible into existent and non-existent). The conditions differ slightly.
2
Check exhaustiveness (hasr)
Does the division cover all parts of the whole or all particulars of the universal? For whole-into-parts, the parts must compose the whole. For universal-into-particulars, the division must be comprehensive: if mental, cover all rational possibilities; if inductive, cover all observed instances.

For universal division: Check specificity (akhass mutlaq)
Each division must be absolutely more specific than the divided universal. It cannot be equal, more general, or overlapping in a way that violates specificity. The relation must be 'akhass mutlaq' (absolutely more specific).
The text explicitly prohibits equality, general-specific from one aspect, or absolute generality of the division over the universal.

Check mutual distinctness (mubayana)
Each division must be distinct (mubayin) from the others and, for whole-into-parts, also distinct from the whole in terms of predication. For whole-into-parts, distinctness is in predication (you cannot predicate the whole of a part). For universal-into-particulars, distinctness is required; however, some divisions may be equal or have overlapping relations, as long as they are distinct in meaning.
The text notes that for mental division, distinctness must be in both intellect and existence; for conventional division, only in intellect.

Second: That each part be distinct from the other parts, and also distinct from the divided whole with respect to predication, not with respect to realization. An example that fulfills the two conditions is your saying: The chair is wood and nails; because the parts of the chair are confined to wood and nails, and no other part from outside the parts of the chair enters into them, and the nails are distinct from wood, and both of them (nails and wood) are distinct from the chair with respect to predication, since it is not permissible for you to say: The nails are a chair, nor the wood is a chair, even though when the chair is realized externally, its parts are necessarily realized, namely wood and nails. That is the meaning of our saying: not with respect to realization. As for the division of a universal into its particulars, three conditions are required for its validity: First: That it be encompassing all the divisions of the divided whole, meaning that it be comprehensive of all the mental divisions if the division is mental, and comprehensive of all the divisions existing externally if it is inductive, and that it prevent the entry of a division from outside the divisions of the divided whole. This passage defines the conditions for a valid division (taqsim), which is a fundamental logical operation used to clarify concepts. Core Idea
A valid division requires parts that are mutually exclusive and each more specific than the whole.

In this passage, the author introduces the third condition of division: each part must be distinct (mubaayin) from every other part. He also clarifies that each part must be more specific (akhass) than the whole, not equal or more general. He distinguishes mental division (taqseem 'aqli) from conventional division (taqseem i'tibari) and provides two examples: a mental division of the possible into existent/non-existent, and an inductive division of element into four parts. Understanding these conditions ensures that divisions in Islamic sciences are logically rigorous.

Conditions for a Correct Division (Taqsim) and Their Violations
Author enumerates conditions for a correct division and exemplifies each violation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify violations of the four conditions of a correct division: exhaustiveness, prevention of non-members, proper specificity relative to the divided, and mutual exclusivity among divisions.

Core Idea
A division is correct only if it exhausts the genus, excludes non-species, stays within the genus, and has non-overlapping parts.

In Islamic logic, dividing a genus into its species is a common operation in fiqh and kalam. But not every list of attributes is a valid division. This lesson teaches you the four conditions that a division must meet and shows you how to spot violations by examining concrete examples from the tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divided whole (المقسم)
Look at what is being divided. In the examples, it is always 'animal' (الحيوان). Determine the genus or universal that is the subject of division.
The divided must be a genus or universal that can be split into species.
2
Check exhaustiveness (شرط الجمع)
Does the division cover all species of the genus? If any species is missing, the division is incomplete (غير حاصر). Example: dividing animal into rational and neighing omits braying animals.
This condition is also called 'encompassing'.
3
Check prevention of non-members (شرط المنع)
Does the division include only species of the genus? If a division includes something that is not a species of the divided genus, the condition is violated. Example: including 'white' in a division of animal admits snow and ivory.
The division must prevent foreign elements from entering.
4
Check that each division is absolutely more specific than the divided (أخص مطلقا من المقسم)
Each division must be a proper subset of the genus. Four sub-cases: (a) equal to the divided (e.g., sentient = animal), (b) more general (e.g., growing > animal), (c) overlapping partly (e.g., white and animal), (d) completely separate (e.g., stone and animal). All violate the condition.
If a division is not a proper subset, it fails.
5
Check mutual exclusivity among divisions (مباينة الأقسام بعضها بعضا)
The divisions must have no overlap. Three sub-cases: (a) two divisions are equal in extension (e.g., rational and human), (b) one is absolutely more general than another (e.g., sentient vs rational), (c) they have partial overlap (e.g., black vs rational). All violate mutual exclusivity.
Overlap of any kind between divisions is invalid.

Since 'stone' is not a type of animal, the division is invalid. The second example violates the condition that parts must be mutually exclusive of each other: 'animal is either rational, human, neighing, or braying' – here 'rational' and 'human' are synonymous in extension (متساويان في الماصدق), overlapping. The third example violates the condition that no part should be absolutely more general (أعم مطلقا) than another: 'animal is either sentient, rational, or neighing' – 'sentient' applies to all animals, making it more general than the others, which are subsets. The fourth example illustrates generality and specificity from one perspective (عموم وخصوص من وجه): 'animal is either black, rational, or neighing' – 'black' overlaps partially with 'rational' and 'neighing' (e.g., a black human is both black and rational; a white horse is neighing but not black). The author notes that such overlaps do not invalidate the example itself, as a single example can illustrate multiple aspects. This lesson trains the student to recognize invalid divisions by checking each condition.

Three Types of Effective Cause (Mu'aththir)
Author classifies effective causes into three categories: by choice, by nature, and by necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any effective cause into one of the three types (choice, nature, necessity) using the criteria of ability to refrain and dependence on conditions.

Core Idea
The threefold division of effective causes is proven by a complete logical disjunction.

In Islamic theology and philosophy, understanding how causes produce effects is crucial for distinguishing God's voluntary action from natural and necessary causation. This passage presents a rigorous classification of effective causes (mu'aththir) into three types, supported by a clear logical proof. By mastering this classification, you will be able to analyze causal claims in theological debates, especially those involving divine attributes and creation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term 'effective cause'
The passage defines 'mu'aththir' broadly as that which brings about an effect. Note that this includes divine, natural, and necessary causes.
The term is used by both 'people of truth' (Ahl al-Haqq) and 'people of falsehood' (philosophers/naturalists).
2
Know the first dichotomy: can it refrain?
The author begins the proof by dividing every effective cause into two: those that can refrain from producing the effect (choice) and those that cannot. This is an exhaustive disjunction.
If the cause can refrain, it is the 'effective cause by choice' – this applies only to God.
3
For the 'cannot refrain' group, apply the second dichotomy: does it depend on conditions?
Among causes that cannot refrain, some depend on the existence of a condition and absence of an obstacle (nature), while others do not depend on any condition (necessity).
Examples: fire (nature) requires ignition and no water; finger-ring movement (necessity) requires no external condition.
4
Identify each type with its example
Now match the types to the examples given: (1) by choice: God's creation; (2) by nature: fire burning; (3) by necessity: finger moving a ring.
Note that the philosophers claim God's causation is by necessity, which is rejected by the author.
5
Recognize the logical structure of the proof
The proof is a complete disjunctive syllogism at two levels. First: either can refrain (choice) or cannot. Second: if cannot, either depends on conditions (nature) or does not (necessity). This yields exactly three possibilities.
This ensures no fourth type exists, hence the 'confinement to three'.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to outline the main divisions of words in Islamic logic and distinguish between compound and simple, and within simple words, between universal and particular.

Word classification is the bedrock of all logical analysis in the Islamic tradition.

In this lesson, we survey the topics that form the foundation of logical discourse on words (alfaz). The table of contents from a classical Mantiq text lists the key divisions: compound vs. simple, then the simple word divided into universal and particular, each with further subdivisions.
