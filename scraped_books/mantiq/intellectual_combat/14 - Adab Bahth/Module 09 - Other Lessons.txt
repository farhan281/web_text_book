Distinguishing 'Alam al-Jins from Ism al-Jins
Author distinguishes between proper noun for genus and common noun for genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'alam al-jins and ism al-jins by analyzing whether external individuals are disregarded in the meaning.

Core Idea
The key difference: 'alam al-jins ignores individuals; ism al-jins does not.

This passage clarifies a subtle but crucial distinction in Arabic logic between two types of genus nouns: proper noun for genus ('alam al-jins) and common noun for genus (ism al-jins). Through the example of 'Usama' (a proper noun for lion genus) and 'asad' (common noun for lion genus), the author explains that the difference lies in whether external individuals are considered in the meaning. Understanding this distinction sharpens your precision in analyzing definitions and logical terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Locate 'alam al-jins and ism al-jins in the passage. Note the example: 'Usama' ('alam al-jins) and 'asad' (ism al-jins).
These are both genus nouns, but they differ in how they treat individuals.
2
Understand the common aspect (al-qadr al-mushtarak)
Recognize that both terms share a common mental meaning, e.g., the concept of lionness (animality + predation). This single meaning is the 'common aspect' that unifies all individuals of the genus.
The common aspect is a single mental concept; multiplicity only exists in external individuals.
3
Check consideration of external individuals
For 'alam al-jins, the common aspect is considered with absolute disregard for external individuals. For ism al-jins, the common aspect is considered without entirely disregarding external individuals; some individuals are implicitly part of the meaning.
The phrase 'bi-qat' al-nazar' (with disregard) is key for 'alam al-jins.
4
Apply the test to examples
Given a word, ask: Does it denote the mental meaning while completely setting aside external individuals? If yes, it is 'alam al-jins. If not (i.e., it still implies some individuals), it is ism al-jins.
Test with 'Usama' (proper noun for lion genus) vs 'asad' (common noun for lion).

Commentary
This passage defines the distinction between 'alam al-jins (proper noun for genus) and ism al-jins (common noun for genus) by focusing on the consideration of individual instances. The author begins by giving the example of 'Usama' as a proper noun for the genus of lion, and 'asad' as the common noun for the genus. The key difference is that 'alam al-jins considers the common aspect (al-qadr al-mushtarak) while completely disregarding the external individuals (bi-qat' al-nazar 'an al-afrad). In contrast, ism al-jins considers the common aspect but does not entirely disregard the existence of some external individuals; rather, it implicitly includes them. The author clarifies that the mental meaning of 'asad' is a single concept (e.g., animality plus predation) that is shared among individuals. Multiplicity exists only in the external individuals. 'Alam al-jins, like 'Usama', is a proper noun that points to that single mental meaning, specifying it in the mind (like a proper noun specifies a person in external reality). So 'alam al-jins specifies its referent in the mind, while 'alam al-shakhs specifies its referent externally. Then the author explains ism al-jins: it also denotes the mental meaning but without disregarding the external individuals; the existence of some individuals is considered in the meaning, leading to multiplicity. For those who consider ism al-jins and nakira (indefinite noun) the same, the same principle applies: ism al-jins includes consideration of some external individuals. The conclusion: if the meaning is specified in the mind with complete disregard for external individuals, it is 'alam al-jins; otherwise, it is ism al-jins.

Extracting the Legal Cause of Riba from Hadith
Author cites hadith to identify legal cause of riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal cause ('illa) of riba from the hadith and explain how the author derives it.

Core Idea
The legal cause of riba is measurement by weight or volume, proven by clear prophetic text.

In this lesson, we examine how the author uses a prophetic hadith to definitively identify the 'illa (legal cause) of riba. This is a foundational step in usul al-fiqh: extracting the effective attribute that triggers a ruling. Understanding this process allows you to apply the prohibition of riba to new commodities through qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Locate the textual evidence
Find the hadith cited: 'And similarly everything that is measured or weighed.' This is the primary proof the author uses.
The author also references a second narration mentioning 'the balance' to reinforce the point.
2
Identify the claimed 'illa
The author asserts that the hadith indicates the 'illa is 'the knowledge of quantity by measurement (kayl) and weight (wazn).'
This is not the items themselves but the attribute of quantification.
3
Assess the author's justification
The author attests to the hadith's authenticity and argues it is 'like clear text' (ka al-nass al-wadih) on the matter.
He references his own work Adwā' al-Bayān for a full discussion of its authenticity.
4
Apply the 'illa to analogous cases
Once the 'illa is established, any good traded by weight or volume falls under the same ruling of riba prohibition.
This is the practical purpose of identifying the 'illa: extension through qiyas.

Commentary
This passage is a definitive statement establishing the legal cause ('illa) of riba. The author begins by referencing six items that are explicitly prohibited in riba. He then cites the prophetic hadith: 'And similarly everything that is measured or weighed.' He attests to the authenticity of this hadith, referencing his own work Adwā' al-Bayān. The author argues that this hadith is clear textual evidence that the cause (al-'illa) of riba is the determination of quantity through measurement (kayl) and weight (wazn). He adds another narration from the two Sahihs where the Prophet said 'and similarly the balance,' further reinforcing that measurement is the operative cause. This is a classic example of extracting a legal cause from a textual indication. In Islamic legal theory, establishing the 'illa is crucial for extending rulings to new cases.

The First Calamity of the Khalaf Method
Author criticizes the khalaf approach, listing three inseparable calamities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first calamity of the Khalaf methodology and explain why the author considers it an attack on the Qur'an.

Core Idea
The Khalaf's ta'wīl stems from falsely equating ẓāhir with anthropomorphism.

In this passage, the author launches a sharp critique of the Khalaf (later theologians) who resort to metaphorical interpretation (ta'wīl) to avoid anthropomorphism. He argues that their method necessarily leads to three inseparable calamities, each worse than the last. Here he presents the first: that when the Khalaf hear the verse about Allah's istiwā' (establishing over the Throne), they immediately claim its apparent meaning is resemblance to creatures. This, the author says, is a great calamity because it wrongfully accuses the Qur'an of implying something unworthy of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of criticism
The author is critiquing the methodology of the Khalaf (later theologians). Recognize that 'madhhab al-khalaf' refers to those who use ta'wīl to reinterpret divine attributes.
Khalaf here is a polemical label, not merely 'later scholars'.
2
Extract the claimed entailment
The author states that the Khalaf method 'necessarily entails three calamities in an inseparable entailment'. Note that the entailment is presented as necessary (mustaliq) and inseparable (lā yanfakku).
The author is making a causal claim about the implications of the method.
3
Analyze the first calamity
The first calamity is that when the Khalaf encounter the verse 'Thumma istawā 'alā al-'arsh' (Then He established Himself over the Throne), they say: 'Indeed, the apparent meaning (zāhir) of this istiwā' is resemblance to the istiwā' of creatures.' The author considers this a calamity because it implies the Qur'an's apparent meaning is defective, requiring reinterpretation.
The author labels this as 'attacking the texts of the Mighty Qur'an and fabricating lies'.
4
Evaluate the author's critique
The author's objection is epistemological: the Khalaf falsely assume that the zāhir of divine attributes inevitably implies tashbīh (anthropomorphism). The Salaf, by contrast, affirm the zāhir as it befits Allah without ta'wīl or tashbīh.
Consider the contrasting Salaf position: 'bilā kayf' (without asking how).

Commentary
Here the author critiques the methodology of the Khalaf (later theologians), who are known for using metaphorical interpretation (ta'wīl) to avoid anthropomorphism. The author argues that this approach necessarily leads to three calamities, though only the first is detailed in this passage. The Three Calamities of the Khalaf Method
Critique of the Khalaf method of interpreting divine attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three calamities of the Khalaf method and explain why each is considered a logical and theological error.

Core Idea
The Khalaf method errs by mislabeling revelation, denying it, then replacing it with human conjecture.

The author presents a sharp critique of the method used by later theologians (madhhab al-khalaf) in interpreting Quranic verses about Allah's attributes. He argues that their approach leads to three inevitable calamities, each building on the previous one. By examining this passage, you will learn to recognize a chain of reasoning that moves from mischaracterizing the text, to rejecting it, to inventing an alternative meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of the critique
Note that the author is attacking a specific interpretive method (madhhab al-khalaf), not all theological reasoning. The method involves claiming that the immediate apparent meaning (zahir) of verses on divine attributes like istiwa' necessarily implies tashbih (anthropomorphism).
This sets up the first calamity.
2
Extract the first calamity
The first calamity is the accusation that Allah and the Quran have an apparent meaning that is 'foul and impure'—i.e., that the text itself suggests tashbih. The author argues this is a fabrication against Allah because the Prophet did not explain the verses in that way, yet he was obligated to clarify any potentially misleading text.
The author uses the Prophet's silence as evidence that the immediate meaning is not problematic.
3
Extract the second calamity
Based on the first calamity, the Khalaf method proceeds to negate the attribute entirely (here, istiwa'). The author calls this a grave transgression because it denies what Allah has affirmed in seven verses, without any scriptural warrant.
This shows how one error leads to a more serious one: from mislabeling to outright denial.
4
Extract the third calamity
After negating the original attribute, the Khalaf method substitutes an interpretation of their own invention—in this case, interpreting istiwa' as istila' (conquest). The author points out that this substitution is not derived from revelation but from human reasoning.
The third calamity completes the chain: fabrication, denial, then replacement with human conjecture.
5
Assess the overall argument structure
The author's critique follows a logical progression: he first shows that the Khalaf method's starting point is false, then demonstrates the consequences of that false premise. The argument is built on textual evidence (the Quranic verses and the Prophet's practice) and logical consistency.
Consider how the author uses the Prophet's failure to reinterpret as a key premise.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of the theological method of the later theologians (madhhab al-khalaf) in dealing with the divine attributes, specifically the attribute of istiwa' (establishment over the Throne). The author identifies three sequential calamities that necessarily follow from this method. The first calamity is the claim that the immediate apparent meaning (zahir) of verses describing Allah's attributes implies tashbih (anthropomorphism), which the author considers a foul and impure accusation against Allah and His Book. The author argues that no speech could be more foul than one whose apparent meaning likens the Creator to creation, and that this alleged meaning would be blame and diminution, not praise. Since the Prophet did not clarify such verses or reinterpret them despite the necessity of timely clarification, the claim that they need reinterpretation is a fabrication. The second calamity is the outright negation of the attribute—here, istiwa'—based on the prior claim that its apparent meaning is unfit for Allah. The author calls this a grave transgression, as it negates what Allah has praised Himself with in the Qur'an. The third calamity is the substitution of the affirmed attribute with a metaphorical interpretation—namely, interpreting istiwa' as istila' (conquest or domination), which they invent without scriptural warrant. The author thus exposes a chain of errors: first misrepresenting the revelation, then denying its clear statements, and finally replacing them with human conjecture.
