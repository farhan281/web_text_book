Identifying 'Adool (Modal) Propositions
Author defines 'adool propositions with examples and relationship to muhassala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a categorical proposition is 'adool (modal) or muhassala (simple) based on the position of negation relative to the copula.

Core Idea
The position of negation after the copula makes a proposition 'adool; before makes it muhassala.

In Islamic logic, propositions are classified by how negation interacts with the copula, affecting syllogistic validity. This passage defines 'adool (modal) propositions with clear examples, showing how the placement of negative particles like 'laysa' relative to the copula determines the type. Mastering this distinction is essential for analyzing arguments in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Locate the copula (raabita)
In Arabic, the copula is often the pronoun of separation (damir al-fasl), e.g., 'huwa' in 'Zayd huwa 'aalimun'. Identify this word as the anchoring point.
2
Find the negation word
Look for words like 'laysa', 'ma', or 'la' that express negation. Their position relative to the copula is critical.
3
Check the position of negation
If negation appears after the copula and before the predicate, the proposition is 'adool. If negation appears before the copula (or in its place), it is muhassala (simple).
4
Determine the quality and type
An 'adool proposition with one negation is affirmative (mu'addala mawjiba). With two negations (e.g., 'laysa ... laysa'), it is negative modal (mu'addala saliba), which resolves to an affirmative simple.

Commentary
This passage provides a formal definition of a 'adool (modal) proposition in logic. The definition hinges on the position of the negation relative to the copula (raabita). In technical terms, a proposition is 'adool when the negation is attached to the predicate after the copula. In languages like Arabic that lack an explicit copula, grammarians substitute the 'pronoun of separation' (damir al-fasl), e.g., 'huwa' in 'Zayd huwa 'aalimun' (Zayd is knowledgeable). Importantly, the simple negative and the affirmative modal are semantically indistinguishable in meaning, though their logical forms differ. This distinction is crucial for analyzing arguments where the form affects syllogistic validity.

Predicate Types: Existential vs. Non-Existential Subjects
Author classifies predicates into those that require their subject to exist and those that do not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given predicate as either requiring its subject to exist or not, based on the nature of the attribute.

Core Idea
Not all affirmative statements imply the real existence of their subject.

When we say 'Zayd is standing', we assume Zayd exists. But we can also say 'Zayd is possible' without Zayd existing. This lesson unpacks the logical distinction between predicates that require subject existence and those that do not, a key precision in Islamic logic often applied to divine attributes and theological propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of predicates
The author divides predicates into two types: (1) those that describe an existential quality (وصف وجودي) like 'standing' or 'white', and (2) those that do not, like 'possible' or 'known'. The first type requires the subject to exist; the second does not.
The distinction hinges on whether the predicate denotes a real accident that inheres in a substance.
2
Understand why existential predicates require subject existence
An existential predicate like 'white' indicates an accident (عرض) that cannot exist without a substance (جسم). Therefore, affirming such a predicate of a subject forces the subject to exist as a bearer of that accident.
This is rooted in the kalām principle that accidents cannot subsist by themselves.
3
Examine the counterexamples: predicates not requiring existence
Predicates like 'possible' or 'known' do not denote a real accident inhering in the subject. They are mental considerations (اعتبارات عقلية). Thus, we can truly say 'Zayd is possible' even if Zayd does not exist in the external world.
This applies to theological discussions about the attributes of God and the status of non-existent possibles.
4
Test the classification with examples from Islamic sciences
Consider the statement 'Allah is knowing' – the predicate 'knowing' is an existential attribute that requires Allah's existence. Conversely, 'The world is possible' does not require the world to exist at the time of predication; it is a judgment about its nature.
This distinction is critical in kalām debates about the status of divine attributes and the ontological status of possibles before creation.

Contradiction vs. Specification: Usul al-Fiqh and Logic
Author distinguishes contradictory propositions in usul vs. mantiq with Quranic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the treatment of contradictory propositions in usul al-fiqh (specification) and in logic (contradiction), and correctly apply the distinction to Quranic examples.

Core Idea
A universal affirmative and its particular negative are logical contradictories, but in usul al-fiqh the latter specifies the former.

In logic, a universal affirmative and a particular negative are direct contradictories — they cannot both be true. But when the same pair of statements appears in the Quran and Sunnah, usul al-fiqh treats them differently: the particular negative is not seen as a contradiction but as a specification (takhṣīṣ) of the general rule. This lesson unpacks that critical distinction using concrete Quranic evidence and explains why the same propositions are treated differently across disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions in the Quranic example
Find the universal affirmative: 'Every divorced woman waits three periods' (from Al-Baqarah 2:228). Then find the particular negative: 'Some divorced women do not wait three periods' (from Al-Ahzab 33:49, referring to those divorced before consummation).
2
Determine their logical relation per mantiq
According to the square of opposition, a universal affirmative (كلية موجبة) and a particular negative (جزئية سالبة) are contradictories: they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. In logic, if one is true, the other must be false.
Al-Akhdari's Sullam states: 'If a universal affirmative occurs, its contradictory is a particular negative.'
3
Apply the usuli notion of takhṣīṣ
In usul al-fiqh, the later verse (Al-Ahzab) is not taken as a counterexample that invalidates the earlier general rule. Instead, it specifies the general rule by restricting its application to women not divorced before consummation. Both verses remain valid.
Takhṣīṣ is defined as restricting a general term to some of its individuals by a proof.
4
Compare consequences across disciplines
In logic and in the discipline of research and disputation (jadl), the particular negative would invalidate the universal affirmative because they are contradictories. In usul, the specification prevents contradiction, so no invalidation occurs.
This shows that methodological frameworks differ; one must not conflate the disciplines.

Contradiction vs. Specification: Usul al-Fiqh vs. Logic
Author contrasts treatment of universal affirmative and particular negative in usul vs. logic/debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish how a particular negative proposition functions as a specification in usul al-fiqh versus a contradictory that invalidates the universal affirmative in logic and debate.

Core Idea
In logic, a particular negative contradicts a universal affirmative; in usul, it specifies without nullifying.

The relationship between universal affirmative and particular negative propositions is not uniform across Islamic disciplines. In logic and debate, the particular negative directly contradicts the universal affirmative, making it false. In usul al-fiqh, however, the same proposition does not invalidate the universal but instead restricts its scope through specification (takhṣīṣ). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly interpreting Quranic rulings and avoiding errors in juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Quranic verses and their logical forms
Locate the universal affirmative (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:228) and the particular negative (Surah Al-Ahzab 33:49). Note that the first asserts a rule for all divorced women; the second carves out an exception for those divorced before consummation.
The first is 'every divorced woman waits three quru''; the second is 'some divorced women do not wait three quru''.
2
Recognize the logical relationship
According to Aristotelian logic (and al-Akhdari's Sullam), the particular negative is the exact contradictory of the universal affirmative. This means they cannot both be true; establishing the particular negative invalidates the universal.
This is the standard logical rule: if universal affirmative is true, its contradictory particular negative must be false, and vice versa.
3
Understand the usul principle of takhṣīṣ
In usul al-fiqh, specification (takhṣīṣ) allows a general statement to be restricted to some of its individuals by evidence, without canceling the general ruling for the remaining individuals. Here, the particular negative specifies that the general 'iddah rule does not apply to women divorced before consummation, but it remains valid for all other divorced women.
Takhṣīṣ is a key principle for harmonizing seemingly conflicting textual evidence.
4
Compare outcomes across disciplines
In logic and debate, the particular negative would be taken as a refutation of the universal affirmative, rendering the universal false. In usul, it is not a refutation but a qualification. The universal remains true for the non-excepted cases.
This shows that the same proposition can have different epistemic functions depending on the discipline.
5
Apply the distinction to a new example
Consider a general rule: 'Every sale is permissible' and an exception: 'Sales of alcohol are not permissible.' In logic, the exception contradicts the general, banning all sales. In usul, the exception specifies that 'every sale' means 'every sale except alcohol,' so the general rule still holds for other sales.
Practice by finding similar pairs in fiqh manuals.

Commentary
This passage clarifies a key epistemological difference between usul al-fiqh and the disciplines of logic and debate (mantiq/bahth wa munazara). The author begins by stating that in usul, specifying one of two propositions by the other prevents the contradiction that would otherwise render one invalid in logic. He then provides a well-known Quranic example: the verse from Surah al-Baqarah (2:228) asserting that all divorced women must wait three quru' is a universal affirmative (every divorced woman waits). The verse from Surah al-Ahzab (33:49) states that women divorced before consummation have no 'iddah, which is a particular negative (some divorced women do not wait). In logic, the particular negative is the direct contradictory of the universal affirmative, so proving it would invalidate the universal. However, in usul, this particular negative does not nullify the universal affirmative; rather, it acts as a specification (takhsis) of the general rule. Specification means restricting the general application to some individuals based on evidence. Thus, the universal remains valid for cases not specified. The author cites al-Akhdari's Sullam to affirm the logical relationship, then emphasizes that in logic and debate, the particular negative does invalidate the universal. The key lesson is that the same propositional relationship may function differently across disciplines: in debate, it destroys the opposing proof; in usul, it merely qualifies it. The student must understand that the rules of contradiction in logic are not directly transferable to usul al-fiqh without considering the principle of takhsis.

Truth Values of Propositions by the Four Relations
Author specifies truth values per the four relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine which categorical propositions (A, E, I, O) are true or false given the logical relation between subject and predicate.

Core Idea
The four relations uniquely determine truth values of universal and particular affirmatives and negatives.

This lesson equips you with the rules to evaluate the truth of categorical propositions based on the logical relation between their terms. These rules are foundational for syllogistic reasoning and are used extensively in Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam to assess arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relation
Determine which of the four relations (tabāyun, musāwāt, umūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh, or absolute generality/specificity) holds between the subject and predicate of the proposition.
Use definitions: tabāyun = no common individuals; musāwāt = identical extensions; umūm min wajh = partially overlapping; absolute = one term entirely includes the other.
2
Apply rule for tabāyun
When the terms are in complete opposition, both universal negatives (e.g., 'No A is B' and 'No B is A') are true, and both affirmatives are false.
Example: 'No human is stone' and 'No stone is human' are true; 'Every human is stone' is false.
3
Apply rule for musāwāt
When the terms are equal in extension, both universal affirmatives (e.g., 'Every A is B' and 'Every B is A') are true, and both negatives are false.
Example: 'Every rational animal is human' and 'Every human is rational animal' are true.
4
Apply rule for umūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh
When terms overlap partially, both particular affirmatives (e.g., 'Some A is B' and 'Some B is A') are true, but both universal affirmatives are false.
Example: 'Some animal is white' and 'Some white thing is animal' are true; 'Every animal is white' is false.
5
Apply rule for absolute generality/specificity
If one term is absolutely more specific (subject is the species, predicate is the genus), then both universal affirmatives are true and negatives false. If the subject is absolutely more general (subject is genus, predicate is species), then both particular affirmatives are true but universal affirmatives are false.
Example with specific subject: 'Every human is animal' (true); with general subject: 'Some animal is human' (true) but 'Every animal is human' (false).

Commentary
This passage explains how to determine truth values in categorical propositions when the subject and predicate are related by one of the four relations. It gives rules: For tabāyun (opposition), both universal negatives (e.g., 'No A is B' and 'No B is A') are true, and both affirmatives false. For musāwāt (equality), both universal affirmatives are true, both negatives false. For 'umūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh (overlap), the particular affirmatives are true but the universal affirmatives false. For absolute generality/specificity, if the subject is the more specific term (e.g., 'man' as subject, 'animal' as predicate), the universal affirmative and its converse are true; if the subject is more general, the particular affirmatives are true but universals false. These rules are foundational for evaluating syllogisms.
