Epistemological Conditions for Universal Claims
Author challenges the epistemological basis of the opponent's universal claim about direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two valid epistemic warrants for a universal proposition and evaluate whether a theological claim meets them.

Core Idea
A universal proposition without complete induction or demonstrative proof is epistemologically empty.

In Islamic logic, not every claim that sounds universal is actually justified. This passage from Husn al-Muhajja shows how the author demands rigorous epistemological backing for a claim about directionality. He challenges the opponent's universal statement by asking: How did you know it? He then explains the only two valid ways to know a universal proposition, and demonstrates that neither applies. You will learn to recognize when a universal claim lacks proper grounding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal claim
Find the opponent's universal proposition in the Arabic: that every two existents must be directionally related. This is the claim under attack.
The claim is quoted at the beginning: 'كل موجودين فلا بد ان يكون واحد منهما في جهة من الآخر'.
2
Recognize the two epistemic routes
Note the author's statement that universal propositions are known either by complete induction (istiqra') or by a rational demonstrative syllogism (qiyas 'aqli burhani) admitting no exceptions.
This sets up the dilemma for the opponent.
3
Test the induction route
Consider whether the opponent could have performed complete induction. The author argues they have not perceived all corporeal existents, nor even all on earth, nor even their own souls.
The impossibility of complete observation is highlighted.
4
Test the demonstrative syllogism route
Ask whether the opponent has provided a certain rational proof. The author implies they have not, and challenges them to produce it.
No such proof is offered in the passage.
5
Conclude the critique
Since neither epistemic condition is met, the universal claim is baseless. Moreover, applying it to God is unfounded.
The author turns the critique to theology.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the opponent's claim that every two existents necessarily have a directional relationship. The author begins by identifying the opponent's claim as a universal proposition (al-qadiyya al-kulliyya). He then points out the epistemological basis required for such a claim: universal propositions can only be known through either complete induction (istiqra') or a rational demonstrative syllogism (qiyas 'aqli burhani) that admits no exceptions. The author then challenges the opponent's ability to fulfill either condition: if they claim induction, they are lying because they have not perceived all corporeal existents, not even those on earth, let alone the heavens or even their own souls. Therefore their claim is baseless. The author then turns the critique to theology: how can they apply this universal statement to God when they have not perceived His reality? This shows the logical flaw: an unsubstantiated universal claim cannot be extended to matters of the unseen. The passage demonstrates proper method—that epistemological justification precedes metaphysical assertion.
