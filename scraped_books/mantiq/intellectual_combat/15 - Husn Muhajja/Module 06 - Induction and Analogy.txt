How Are Universal Propositions Known?
Author states the criteria for knowing universal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two sources of certainty for a universal proposition according to the author.

Core Idea
A universal proposition requires complete induction or a demonstrative syllogism.

The author challenges the anthropomorphists' universal claim by first asking how they know it. He states that any universal proposition is known either by induction (istiqra') or by a demonstrative rational syllogism (qiyas 'aqli burhani) that admits no exception. This sets the logical criteria for evaluating their claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal claim
Find the statement the opponents make that applies to all existents. It is: 'every two existents must be in a direction from the other.'
2
Recognize the two epistemological sources
The author says a universal proposition is known either by complete induction (istiqra') or a demonstrative syllogism that admits no exceptions.
Complete induction means observing every instance. Demonstrative syllogism means a valid deductive argument.
3
Understand the implication
If the opponents cannot provide either, their claim is not rationally justified. This sets up the refutation that follows.

No!! You are anthropomorphists, describing Allah Almighty with attributes of deficiency, from which even the creation is exalted. We say: Allah Almighty has no relation to creation in direction, place, time, or any attribute. Then we say: You claim that you have known by intellects that every two existents must be that one of them is in a direction from the other. So we ask you: this general statement, how did you know it? And what is your proof for it? It is known that the universal proposition is known either by induction or by a demonstrative rational syllogism that admits no exception. If you claim that you know it by induction, then you are liars, because you have definitely not perceived all the corporeal creatures that Allah created; rather, you have not perceived the existents in the lowest heaven, rather you have not perceived the existents on the surface of the earth; rather, you do not even perceive the reality of yourselves?! And since this is the case, and you cannot escape from it, why the claim? Then how after this do you claim that this statement applies even to Allah Almighty? It is amazing from you that you claim that you are transcendent and bound by the Book and Sunnah, then you release this incoherent judgment like this!! And we ask you: Have you perceived the reality of Allah Almighty, so that you know that He is in the direction of above, and you know that He cannot exist except in a direction and place, so you issued this statement?! So it becomes clear by this statement that induction does not help you. So what remains for you from the indication of intellects? Do you say that you have perceived this by rational syllogism, so you say: all the existents that we see are in a direction and place, and Allah exists, therefore He must be in a direction and place? So we say to you: This statement applies only to what you have witnessed of bodies. You have seen around you many bodies, each in a direction from the other, so you imagined Allah as a body, and said: He is also in a direction and place. Otherwise, we perceive the existence of some existents, not one of which is in a direction from the other; so your statement 'every existent must be in a direction' becomes false. And here we give you some examples that you cannot escape from, so we say: 1 - The feeling of love and hatred: it exists without doubt. When a person loves, love exists in him; when he hates, hatred exists in him; and a person can love a person or thing and hate another thing, so love and hatred exist together in him in this case. And a person believes in the existence of love and hatred in himself and affirms it, without needing to conceive a direction in which this hatred or love resides. So where is your love, O man, from your hatred? Are they in a direction from each other? So these matters, without needing to conceive a direction in which they reside, we affirm their existence.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the anthropomorphists' claim that all existents must be in a direction relative to each other, and that God must therefore be in a direction. The author begins by accusing the opponents of anthropomorphism (tashbih) by describing God with attributes of deficiency. The author then states the opponent's universal proposition: every two existents must be in a direction relative to each other. They ask how this universal proposition is known—either by induction (istiqra') or by a demonstrative rational syllogism (qiyas 'aqli burhani) that admits no exception. The author first refutes the possibility of induction, arguing that the opponents have not perceived all corporeal creatures—not even all existents on earth, let alone in the heavens or even the reality of themselves. Therefore, induction is insufficient. Then the author addresses the possibility of a rational syllogism: the opponents argue that all observed existents are in a direction and place, and since God exists, He must also be in a direction and place. The author counters that this syllogism only applies to bodies they have witnessed; they have imagined God as a body. There exist existents that are not in a direction relative to each other, such as feelings of love and hatred. These are real existents that a person affirms without any spatial location. Thus, the universal claim is false. The counterexample of love and hatred suffices to refute the opponents' syllogism. This passage illustrates the logical principle that a single counterexample disproves a universal claim, and that induction must be complete to yield certainty.

Why Induction Cannot Support the Claim
Author refutes the possibility of induction as a source for the universal claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the opponents' claim cannot be known through incomplete induction.

Core Idea
Incomplete induction covers too few instances to yield a certain universal proposition.

The author directly attacks the possibility that the opponents arrived at their universal claim through induction. He argues that they have not perceived all corporeal creatures—not even those on earth, let alone in the heavens or themselves. Therefore, their induction is incomplete and cannot produce certainty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponents' claim to induction
The opponents might claim to have known their universal statement by induction, i.e., by observing many existents in a direction.
2
Identify the author's critique
The author lists what they have not perceived: all corporeal creatures, existents in the lowest heaven, existents on earth, and even their own realities. This shows their observation is extremely limited.
The author uses a rhetorical escalation: 'you have not perceived... not even...'.
3
Recognize the logical consequence
Because their induction is incomplete, it cannot produce a certain universal proposition. The author concludes that induction does not help them.
This is a standard critique: incomplete induction yields probability, not certainty.

A Counterexample Refutes the Universal Claim
Author provides a counterexample to refute the universal claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use a counterexample to refute a universal claim and identify the fallacy in the opponent's syllogism.

Core Idea
A single counterexample disproves a universal claim.

After dismissing induction, the author turns to the opponents' syllogistic argument. He points out that their syllogism only applies to bodies they have seen; they have illicitly generalized. Then he provides a clear counterexample: feelings of love and hatred, which are existents with no spatial direction. This single counterexample refutes their universal claim.

Guided Path
1
Reconstruct the opponent's syllogism
The opponents argue: 'All existents that we see are in a direction and place; Allah is an existent; therefore Allah is in a direction and place.' Identify the major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
The major premise is based on observation of bodies only.
2
Identify the flaw
The major premise is not universal—it only applies to what they have seen (bodies). The syllogism therefore commits a hasty generalization.
Also, the minor premise assumes Allah is like observed existents.
3
Provide a counterexample
The author gives the example of love and hatred. These are real existents that have no spatial direction. This directly contradicts the claim 'every existent is in a direction.'
One counterexample is sufficient to refute a universal claim.
4
Recognize the logical force
The universal claim is now proven false. The opponent cannot claim that all existents must be in a direction because love and hatred are existents that are not in a direction.
This is a classic logical move: a single counterexample (naqid) destroys a universal proposition.
