Refutation of Anthropomorphism: God's Transcendence of Place and Direction
Author refutes anthropomorphists who claim God is outside the world in a direction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical structure of the author's refutation of the anthropomorphist claim that God is outside the world in a direction.

Core Idea
The refutation hinges on God's state before creation: place and direction are created, so God cannot acquire them without implying change and limitation.

In this passage, the author engages in a dialectical refutation of the anthropomorphist position. He first summarizes the opponents' argument, then systematically deconstructs it by appealing to the consensus that place and direction are created and by examining God's state before creation. This lesson will guide you through the steps of this jadal exchange, helping you see how the author exposes the logical inconsistency in the opponents' claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
Find the passage where the author presents what the anthropomorphists say about God's location. What is their main argument?
The opponent argues: 'If God is not inside the world, He must be outside; if outside, then in a direction; the best direction is above.'
2
Extract the opponent's syllogistic reasoning
The opponent uses a form of disjunctive syllogism: either inside or outside; not inside; therefore outside. Then from outside to direction and ultimately to above. Write this reasoning in logical form.
This reasoning assumes that 'outside' necessarily implies directionality.
3
Locate the author's first refutation move
The author asks a question about God's state before creation. What is that question and how does it challenge the opponent?
The author asks: 'Before God created the world, was He in a direction or place?' The consensus is that place and direction are created, so the answer must be no.
4
Analyze the consequences drawn by the author
The author then shows that if the opponent insists God is now in a direction, it implies a change in God from placeless to placed. Why is that problematic?
This would mean the creation affected the Creator, which is absurd and contradicts divine transcendence.
5
Identify the dialectical pattern (jadal)
The author first lets the opponent state their case, then identifies a premise both sides accept (place and direction are created), and uses it to force a contradiction in the opponent's position. This is classic jadal.
The opponent is cornered: either they deny the createdness of place (which is disbelief) or they accept that God changed (which is also disbelief).

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation (jadal) of the anthropomorphist position that God is outside the world in a direction. The author first summarizes the opponents' argument: they claim that if God is not inside the world, He must be outside it; and if outside, then in a direction; and since directions are multiple, He must be in the most perfect direction, above. The author then refutes this by appealing to the state before creation — when only God existed. The author poses a series of rhetorical questions: Was God in a direction or place before the world? Since place and direction are created, the answer is no. So the opponents' claim that God is now outside the world in a direction implies that God changed from being placeless to being located, which is impossible. The author concludes that attributing such change to God implies creation affects the Creator, which is absurd. The refutation follows a systematic pattern: first, establishing the consensus that place and direction are created; second, showing the contradiction in the opponents' view by asking about God's state before creation; third, demonstrating the logical consequence that God would have become limited due to the world's existence. The commentary should note the dialectical structure: the author presents an opponent's argument, then refutes it by exposing contradictions and impossible consequences.

The Pre-Creation Argument: God's Location Before the World
Author forces opponent to admit that before creation God was neither inside nor outside.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that God was neither inside nor outside the world before creation and use it to refute the claim that such a statement is a contradiction.

Core Idea
Before creation, the world didn't exist—so God couldn't be inside or outside it.

In this passage, the author responds to opponents who claim that saying 'God is neither inside nor outside the world' is a contradiction (رفع النقيضين). Using a clever temporal probe, the author forces them to admit that before creation, God necessarily had no spatial relation to the world. This exposes the incoherence of their objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponents assert that the statement 'God is neither inside nor outside the world' is a raising of contradictories (رفع النقيضين), i.e., impossible. Find this in the first line.
The Arabic phrase is 'رفع للنقيضين'.
2
Locate the temporal probe
The author asks about God's state before creation ('قبل أن يخلق الله العالم'). This is a powerful move because the world did not yet exist, so spatial concepts like inside/outside are undefined.
3
Analyze the forced dilemma
The opponent is given a choice: inside or outside? Both are absurd because there is no world to be inside or outside of. So the opponent is forced to admit that before creation God was neither inside nor outside.
The author says 'فكيف يكون خارجه أو داخله' (so how can He be outside it or inside it?).
4
Evaluate the consistency of the opponent
If the opponent concedes that before creation God was neither inside nor outside, they have agreed with the very statement they criticized. The author calls this 'تهافت' (incoherence) because their position is self-contradictory.
The author says their statement is 'تهافت' and 'ليس متناقضاً' (not contradictory).

Refuting Direction for God: A Kalam Reductio Against Anthropomorphism
Author refutes direction for God after creation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the opponent's disjunctive syllogism about God's direction after creation and explain why the author's critique shows it leads to anthropomorphism and change.

Core Idea
Spatial direction for God implies anthropomorphism and change, both absurd.

In this passage, the author confronts an opponent who argues that after creation, God must be in a direction. The opponent's claim seems logically structured, but the author demonstrates that it leads to unacceptable consequences. You will analyse the logical form of the opponent's argument and then follow the author's multi-pronged refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent claims that after creation, God must be either outside or inside the world. This is the disjunctive premise.
2
Extract the full argument
The opponent implicitly denies that God is inside (since they judge He is outside), so the argument is: either inside or outside; not inside; therefore outside. This is a valid disjunctive syllogism.
The author later critiques the assumption that these disjuncts are applicable to God.
3
First critique: Conception implies anthropomorphism
The author points out that to judge that God is not inside, the intellect must first conceive of Him as possibly inside. Conceiving God inside the world is anthropomorphism (tajsim).
4
Second critique: Change leads to blasphemy
If God went from not being outside to being outside, He changed. This change is either a deficiency or a perfection. If deficiency, God was imperfect; if perfection, He acquired it. Both are blasphemous.
5
Final critique: Contradiction and epistemological challenge
If the opponent denies change, then the claim that God became outside after creation is contradictory. Also, the opponent cannot know that inside/outside are opposites for God without knowing His essence.

Commentary
This passage handles the opponent's objection: they concede that before creation God was neither inside nor outside, but claim that after creation He must be either outside or inside. The author rebuts by showing that this implies that the intellect conceives of God as possibly inside the world, which is anthropomorphism (تجسيم). Moreover, it implies that God changed—from not being outside to being outside. If that change is a deficiency, it is blasphemy; if a perfection, it implies God was imperfect before, also blasphemy. If they deny change, then the claim that He became outside after creation is contradictory. Finally, the author questions how the opponent can know that inside/outside are opposites for God without knowing His essence, thus undermining the entire objection.
