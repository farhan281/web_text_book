Author lists his own works from 1 to 42.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between manuscript (makhṭūṭ) and printed (ṭubi‘a) works in a classical scholar's bibliography and infer the scope of his scholarly output.

Core Idea
A scholar's bibliography reveals his intellectual priorities and the genres he engaged with.

In the Islamic tradition, scholars often appended lists of their own works to their biographical entries or collected works. These lists are not mere inventories; they demonstrate the range of a scholar's contributions. In this passage, the author lists 42 works, some published in print and others still in manuscript. Your task is to learn how to read such a list: identify key terms, note the pattern of topics, and understand what distinguishes a printed work from a manuscript in this context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the status markers
Look for the words طبع (printed) and مخطوط (manuscript) after each title. They tell you whether the work was available in print or existed only in handwritten form at the time of listing.
2
Catalog the subjects
Scan the titles for key topics like 'creed', 'fiqh', 'hadith', 'Sufism', 'refutation'. The author engages in multiple Islamic sciences. Notice the diversity.
3
Note the repetition and formatting
Some works are marked 'مرتين' (twice), meaning printed multiple times. Also note that some items are unnumbered or have missing numbers, which is common in hand-copied lists.
4
Infer the author's approach
Many titles are refutations (رد) or comments (تعليقات). This suggests the author is a defender of traditional Sunni positions, particularly Ash'ari and Sufi, against Wahhabi or anthropomorphic views.
5
Contextualize within the tradition
Recognize that such a list is part of the biographical (tarājim) literature. Knowing the tools of bibliography helps you verify a scholar's influence and locate his works.

Identify the list format

4
Reflect on the purpose of such a list
This list serves as a guide for students and researchers: it tells them which works are available in print and which are still in manuscript form. It also showcases the author’s scholarly output and his contributions to contemporary debates.
In Islamic tradition, such lists are often included at the end of a book to allow readers to locate the author’s other works.
