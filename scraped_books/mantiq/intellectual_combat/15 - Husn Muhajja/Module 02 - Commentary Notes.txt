Refuting al-Albani: The Logical Proof from Tanzih
Author refutes al-Albani's charge of innovation and gives proof from Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and validate the refutation of a theological claim using a conditional argument from the Quranic principle of tanzih.

Core Idea
The verse 'Nothing is like Him' negates both spatial inside and outside.

In this passage, the author defends the Ash'ari position that Allah is neither inside nor outside the world against al-Albani's charge of innovation. He uses a clear logical structure: if Allah were spatial, He would be like His creation, contradicting Quranic tawhid. We will dissect this reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
What does al-Albani say about those who affirm 'la dakhil wa la kharij'? He calls them misguided and implies they deny Allah's existence. The author restates this to set up the refutation.
2
Locate the author's Quranic proof
The author cites the verse 'Laysa kamithlihi shay' (Nothing is like Him) as the foundation. He declares it a good and correct inference against both inside and outside.
3
Unpack the conditional argument for the 'inside' scenario
The author argues: if Allah were inside the world, He would be part of it and thus similar in genus. He gives examples: paper and iron are both bodies but different genera; a bird and stones differ yet both are bodies. This similarity (mumathala) is negated by the verse.
The author uses a modus tollens: if inside, then similarity; but no similarity, so not inside.
4
Understand the concept of mumathala (genus-similarity)
Mumathala here means sharing a genus, like both being bodies or matter. The verse negates any kind of similarity, even at the level of being a body or substance. So Allah cannot be inside because that would entail composition and corporeality.
5
Extend the argument to the 'outside' scenario
The passage cuts off, but the author begins to discuss the 'outside' case: if Allah were outside, then either connection or disconnection would follow, implying similarity or spatial relation. The same logic applies: both are impossible due to the verse.
The complete argument, though truncated, shows a balanced treatment of both spatial possibilities.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of al-Albani's claim that the statement 'Allah is neither inside nor outside the world' is innovation and implies denial of existence. The author begins with the basmala and praise, then states his purpose: to clarify the truth in this matter of creed, defending Ahl al-Sunna against accusations from anthropomorphists and corporealists. He then presents the proof from the Quranic verse 'Laysa kamithlihi shay' (nothing is like Him). The argument proceeds: if Allah were inside the world, He would be part of it and thus similar in genus; but this is impossible because He is unlike anything. Similarly, if He were outside the world, that would also imply similarity or connection, which is impossible. The author expands on the first scenario: being inside would make Him part of the world's matter and thus like bodies, which cannot be. He gives examples: paper and iron are both bodies but different in genus; a bird and stones are different genera but both bodies. The implication is that Allah cannot be inside because that would necessitate composition and similarity. The second scenario is cut off, but presumably leads to a similar impossibility. Thus the verse proves that Allah is neither inside nor outside.
