The Quranic Proof Against Spatial Location
Author presents Quranic proof against spatial location for God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the two logical arguments from the verse {ليس كمثله شيء} that rule out God being inside or outside the world, and explain why each would entail similitude.

Core Idea
Negating spatial location follows directly from negating all similitude.

The author begins by invoking the Quranic verse 'There is nothing like unto Him' as a direct refutation of any claim that Allah is inside or outside the world. He then unpacks the proof: if Allah were inside, He would be of the world's genus; if outside, He would require a specifier. Both entail a kind of likeness, which the Quran denies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verse and its role
Find the verse cited in the Arabic and note that the author calls it a 'good and correct inference' (استدلال جيد وصحيح). This establishes the Quran as the primary evidence.
2
Extract the two branches of the argument
The author says if God were inside the world, He would be similar in genus; if outside, He would need a specifier. Write down these two branches.
3
Understand the first branch (inside)
If God were inside the world, He would be part of it, and thus of its genus (matter/body). The author gives examples: paper is not part of iron because of genus difference, but both share the genus of 'body'. So God would be similar to bodies.
4
Understand the second branch (outside)
If God were outside, He would either be connected or separated by a finite/infinite distance. Such spatial relations are properties of contingent beings that need a cause (mukhassis). Thus He would resemble contingent things.
The author does not elaborate why distance requires a specifier; it is a kalam principle that any spatial relation implies need for a cause.
5
Link to the verse
The verse negates all forms of similitude (mumathala). Since both inside and outside entail some similitude, they are impossible for God. Therefore the conclusion that God is neither inside nor outside is valid.

The world. That is: Allah Almighty is neither inside the world nor outside it. One may argue from the Book (Quran) regarding this and say: His statement 'There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the All-Hearing, the All-Seeing' is proof against His being outside the world and against His being inside. This is a good and correct inference. An explanation of the inference is as follows, as mentioned by Ibn Marzuq from the scholar Abdullah ibn Jalal: If He were either in the world or outside it, He would be similar. Explanation of similarity: As for the first (inside): because if He were in the world, He would become of its genus, so what is incumbent upon it would be incumbent upon Him. I say: That is, He would become of the category of bodies and the substance of the world, because if He were inside the world He would be a part of it, and whatever is a part of something is similar to it in genus. For example, paper is not of the genus of iron except by considering that both are bodies and substances; hence paper cannot be a part of iron. Similarly, a bird is not a part of stones due to difference in genus, except by considering that both are bodies. As for the second (outside): because if He were outside, it would necessitate either connection or separation, either by a finite or infinite distance, and all of that leads to His need for a specifier. End of the scholar's words. I say: The people who say that Allah is outside the world and understand the reality of this statement are anthropomorphists, whether they admit this or not. Because they say that Allah is outside the world in a direction from the world, which is the direction of above, that is the place in which they say Allah is. They argue by saying: Whoever is described as neither outside the world nor inside is non-existent, because it is inconceivable to have something neither inside nor outside the world. So in terms of rational possibility – according to them – either He is inside the world or He is outside it. And the possibility of Him being inside is invalid, so He must be outside. And since it is necessary that He is outside the world, it is necessary that He is in a direction, and directions are multiple, and it is impossible that He be under the world or to its right, etc., so He must be above, because this direction is a direction of perfection and is befitting of Allah. Thus their object of worship after this explanation is outside the world and in the direction of above. This is the gist of their speech. And they may add to this by saying: It is impossible to conceive of two existents unless one of them is in a direction from the other. And other such speech. So we say, and by Allah is success: Their speech is contradictory and indicates the feebleness of their intellects, and none is deceived by it except an ignorant person who does not understand the meanings of words nor comprehend them.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the anthropomorphist position that God must be located either inside or outside the world, with the conclusion that He is outside and above. The author begins by presenting a proof from the Quranic verse {There is nothing like unto Him} to demonstrate that God is neither inside nor outside, because either would imply similitude (mumathala). The proof is elaborated: If God were inside the world, He would be of its genus and a part of it, requiring similarity in genus. If He were outside, He would either be connected or separated by a distance, which would entail needing a specifier (mukhassis) – an attribute of contingent beings. The author then quotes and refutes the opposing argument: the anthropomorphists claim that any existent must be either inside or outside; since inside is impossible (as God transcends material composition), He must be outside, and then must be above. The author dismisses this as contradictory (mutahafit) and foolish, arguing that it stems from ignorance of the meanings of terms. This is a classic theological (kalam) refutation using logical reasoning: the opponents employ a conditional syllogism (if inside impossible then outside, and if outside then above), but the author rejects the premise that existence implies spatial location, pointing to the Quranic negation of similitude.

Refuting the Anthropomorphist Conditional Syllogism
Author refutes the anthropomorphist disjunctive syllogism for God's location

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the false dichotomy in the anthropomorphist's argument (God must be either inside or outside) and refute it using the Quranic principle of negation of similitude.

Core Idea
The anthropomorphist's false dilemma collapses when you realize existence does not entail spatial location.

After presenting the Quranic proof, the author turns to refute the opposing argument. The anthropomorphists claim that any existent must be either inside or outside the world; since inside is impossible for God (as He is not material), He must be outside, and then must be above. The author dismantles this reasoning, labeling its proponents as mujassima and showing their argument as contradictory and based on ignorance.

Guided Path
1
State the anthropomorphist's conclusion
Find in the Arabic where they say: 'وجب أن يكون خارج العالم, ثم وجب أن يكون فوقه' (He must be outside the world, then above). They conclude God is outside and in a direction (above).
2
Identify their main premise
The premise is: 'إن من يتصف بأنه لا خارج العالم ولا داخل فهو معدوم' (Whoever is neither inside nor outside is non-existent). So they assume existence requires spatial location.
3
Analyze their conditional syllogism
Formally, it is a disjunctive syllogism: Either inside or outside; inside is impossible; therefore outside. Then from outside, they infer a direction (above). This is valid only if the disjunction is exhaustive and exclusive.
The author does not lay out the syllogism formally, but it is implicit.
4
Find the author's refutation
The author calls their speech 'متاهفت' (contradictory) and says it shows 'سخف عقولهم' (feebleness of intellects). The earlier Quranic proof shows the premise is false: existence does not require spatial location. So the disjunction is false.
5
Identify the fallacy
The fallacy is a false dichotomy (false dilemma): assuming only two possibilities (inside/outside) and ignoring the possibility that God exists without spatial location. This is common in anthropomorphist arguments.

Dialectical Refutation of Anthropomorphism: The Qiyam bi al-Nafs Dilemma
Author challenges the claim that God being qa'im bi nafsihi entails location.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional dilemma used to refute the claim that God's self-subsistence entails location, and apply it to similar theological arguments.

Core Idea
If qiyam bi al-nafs is univocal, you're an anthropomorphist; if equivocal, your analogy fails.

In this passage from a Kalam debate, the author skillfully uses a conditional dilemma to counter an opponent who argues that because God is qa'im bi nafsihi (self-subsistent), He must be in a direction. The author questions the meaning of qiyam bi al-nafs: is it the same for God and bodies? Depending on the answer, the opponent either falls into anthropomorphism (tashbih) or loses the analogy. This lesson will dissect that dialectical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent argues: God is qa'im bi nafsihi; everything qa'im bi nafsihi is in a place/direction; therefore God is in a place/direction. Extract these two premises from the passage.
The first premise is stated explicitly; the second is implied in the question.
2
Locate the author's conditional question
The author asks: 'Is the meaning of qiyam bi al-nafs shared (mushtarak) between God and bodies?' This is the pivot of the argument.
The term 'mushtarak' indicates a univocal vs. equivocal distinction.
3
Trace the dilemma
If the meaning is shared, then the analogy holds but leads to anthropomorphism (tashbih), which the opponent must reject. If the meaning is not shared, then the analogy is invalid because the term is equivocal.
The opponent is forced into a dilemma: either accept tashbih or abandon the argument.
4
Observe the additional constraint
The author reminds the opponent that they have already admitted that place and direction are created. This contradicts the opponent's claim that God is in a created place/direction.
This further weakens the opponent's position by highlighting inconsistency.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange (jadal) where the author refutes an opponent's claim that God being qā'im bi nafsihi necessarily places Him in a direction. The author poses a conditional question: If the term 'self-subsistence' (qiyām bi al-nafs) is used univocally for God and bodies, then the analogy would lead to anthropomorphism—a position the opponent presumably rejects. If, however, the term is equivocal, then the analogy is invalid, and the opponent cannot argue from the attribute of bodies to God. Moreover, the author reminds the opponent that they have already admitted that place and direction are created, which contradicts their current claim that God is in a place or direction. The argument thus forces the opponent into a dilemma: either they fall into anthropomorphism, or they must abandon their claim that God occupies a direction. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the rhetorical force is clear: the opponent cannot consistently maintain both positions.

Refuting the Greater Analogy: Direction Not a Divine Perfection
Author refutes the claim that direction is a perfection for Allah by showing the flaw in qiyas al-awla.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for valid qiyas al-awla and evaluate the claim that direction is a perfection for Allah.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-awla fails when the attribute is conditional upon corporeality.

Opponents use qiyas al-awla to argue that since direction is a perfection for bodies, Allah must possess direction even more perfectly. However, as the author demonstrates, this analogy is flawed because direction is not an absolute perfection—it depends on being a dense physical body. In this lesson, we will dissect both the opponents' argument and the author's refutation, learning how to critically assess analogical reasoning in theological contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponents' analogical argument
The opponents reason: 'Direction is a perfection for bodies. Allah is the most perfect being. Therefore, Allah must have direction to a greater degree.' This is a qiyas al-awla, where the perfection in the creature is used to argue for a greater perfection in the Creator.

Core Idea
Using a verse to answer a spatial question implies you understand it spatially; denying that contradicts your own evidence.

In Kalam debates, opponents often cite Quranic verses as proof without explaining their meaning. The author here demonstrates a powerful technique: first, let the opponent commit to using a verse as evidence for a spatial claim. The key is to link the use of the verse to the spatial context.

Generalize: whenever an opponent uses a scriptural text to support a temporal or spatial claim, but then refuses to interpret it, point out that their use of the text already implies a specific interpretation. The contradiction lies in claiming ignorance after implying knowledge.
This technique is useful in debates about divine attributes like 'yadullah' (hand of Allah) or 'nuzul' (descent).

Refuting a Claim of Ignorance: The Dialectical Tool of Exposing Self-Contradiction
Author exposes self-contradiction in those who claim not to understand the verse yet use it as evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and construct a dialectical refutation by exposing the self-contradiction in an opponent's claim.

Core Idea
Claiming ignorance while judging contradictions is self-defeating.

Have you ever heard someone say they don't understand a verse, yet they confidently declare that a certain opinion 'contradicts the verse'? In this short but powerful passage, the author exposes the logical inconsistency in such a stance. By analyzing this dialectical move, you will learn how to dismantle claims that appear humble but are actually self-contradictory. This is a classic jadal technique: showing that the opponent's actions contradict their words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent claims ignorance of the verse's meaning. Note the exact wording: they say they do not understand the meaning of the āyah.
This claim is often made to avoid committing to an interpretation.
2
Examine their action
Despite claiming ignorance, they judge whether other statements contradict the verse. For example, they say 'this contradicts the verse' or 'this does not contradict it'.
This action presupposes at least some comprehension of the verse.
3
Draw out the implication
Judging contradiction requires understanding the verse's meaning. Without understanding, one cannot identify agreement or opposition. So the act of judging contradiction implies knowledge of the meaning.
This is a necessary condition: understanding is a prerequisite for such judgments.
4
Expose the contradiction
The opponent both claims ignorance (no understanding) and acts as if they have understanding (by judging contradiction). These two positions are inconsistent. They cannot logically hold both.
The contradiction is between what they say and what they do.
5
Conclude the refutation
Therefore, the opponent's claim of ignorance is falsified by their own practice. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: their position collapses under its own weight.
This is a clean dialectical refutation that leaves the opponent without a coherent stance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical implication of a relational claim about Allah's attributes and use it to refute an absurd consequence.

Core Idea
If Allah is above the Throne, then the Throne is below Him — an unavoidable logical consequence.

In theological debates, claims about Allah's attributes are tested by their logical implications. The claim that Allah is above the Throne (fawq al-'arsh) seems to establish a spatial relationship. But what does it entail? The author shows that it forces the Throne to be below Allah, and furthermore implies that Allah is limited from below — an idea the Salaf never endorsed.

Identify the claim
Find the claim that 'Allah is above the Throne.' This is a relational proposition where 'above' indicates a spatial direction.
The claim is explicit in the first sentence: 'بما أنّ الله فوق العرش'.
2
Extract the logical consequence
If A is above B, then B is below A. Therefore, from 'Allah is above the Throne' it necessarily follows that 'the Throne is below Allah.'
This is an immediate inference based on the meaning of 'above'.

Test against Salaf's statements
The author notes that none of the Salaf (early pious predecessors) said 'we are under Allah and Allah is above us.' This shows the consequence contradicts the authoritative tradition.
The absence of such a statement among the Salaf indicates the claim is an innovation.
4

If Allah is above something, then He has a lower boundary, making Him limited (maḥdūd) from below. This is an unacceptable attribute for God.
Limitation implies finitude, which is improper for the Creator.

Dialectical Refutation of Directional Attributes
Author uses dialectical questioning to refute the affirmation of a direction for Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the dialectical method of asking the opponent to affirm or deny each direction to expose his inconsistency, and identify the burden of proof for the remaining direction.

Core Idea
By systematically negating each direction, the opponent is forced to agree with you on all but one, shifting the proof to him.

The author presents a clever dialectical exchange. He asks the questioner whether Allah is below the world, behind it, etc. The questioner denies every direction except 'above'. This reveals that the questioner already agrees on most points; the burden is now on him to provide evidence for that one remaining direction. This lesson teaches you how to use such sequential questioning in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's initial objection
The opponent asks: 'How can you deny all directions?' implying that such blanket negation is unreasonable.
2
Use the first question
Ask: 'Do you say Allah is below the world?' If yes, he is a disbeliever; if no, he agrees with you.
3
Continue through all directions
Ask about behind, left, right, etc. The opponent will deny each because he doesn't believe Allah is in those directions.
4
Note the exception
The opponent only affirms 'above'. So he agrees on all other directions.
5
Shift the burden of proof
Ask him for the evidence for 'above'. The debate then returns to established knowledge, and the opponent must provide proof.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

When is a Negation a Contradiction? The Condition of the Subject
Ibn Jalal explains the condition for contradiction using the wall example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the necessary condition for two negations to constitute a logical contradiction and apply it to theological examples.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires a subject capable of receiving one of the opposites.

The objector claims that saying 'Allah is neither inside nor outside the world' is impossible because it raises both contradictories. The Generality-Specificity Principle and the Pre-Creation Argument
Abu Hafs al-Fasi explains the logic of generality/specificity and the author adds the pre-creation argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the logical principle that negating the more general does not entail affirming the more specific, and use the pre-creation argument to compel agreement in a theological debate.

Core Idea
Negating 'inside' does not affirm 'outside'; the specific is not entailed by the negation of the general.

Abu Hafs al-Fasi provides the most rigorous logical refutation: 'outside' is not the contradictory of 'inside'; the true contradictory is 'not inside'. Since 'outside' is more specific than 'not inside', negating 'inside' does not force a commitment to 'outside'. The author then presents a devastating historical argument: before creation, Allah could not have been inside or outside the world, so the opponent must admit the very negation they objected to, destroying their position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logical terms
The terms are 'inside' (dakhil), 'outside' (kharij), and the negation 'not inside' (la dakhil).
2
Determine the relationship between 'outside' and 'not inside'
'Outside' is more specific (akhass) than 'not inside' (a'amm). Everything outside is not inside, but not everything not inside is outside.
3
Apply the logical rule
Negating the more specific ('outside') does not follow from negating the more general ('inside'). The negation of an akhass is a'amm min nafiy al-a'amm.
4
Transfer to the divine case
When we say 'Allah is not inside', we negate the general. This does not imply the specific 'outside', which requires spatial contiguity not applicable to Allah.
5
Use the pre-creation argument
Ask about Allah's state before the world existed. Since no world existed, He could not be inside or outside it. The opponent is forced to admit the very negation they rejected, proving their objection self-refuting.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of the charge that the Islamic creedal statement 'Allah is neither inside nor outside the world' amounts to a logical impossibility (raf' al-naqidayn). The author first outlines a dialectical strategy to force the opponent to admit that Allah is not in any direction except perhaps 'above,' then presses for the evidence of that direction. He then directly addresses the objection. The author cites Ibn Jalal, who explains that contradiction only arises when the subject is capable of receiving one of the two opposites. If the subject cannot be described by either, there is no contradiction; the example of a wall (neither blind nor seeing) illustrates this. This is the answer of Ahl al-Sunna and all Muslims except anthropomorphists. Then Abu Hafs al-Fasi gives the detailed logical analysis: the contradictory of 'inside' is 'not inside', not 'outside'. 'Outside' is more specific than 'not inside'. Therefore, negating 'inside' does not affirm 'outside'; it only asserts the negation of the specific. The negation of the specific (no outside) is more general than the negation of the general (no inside), but the general does not entail the specific. The misconception arises from the estimation faculty (wahm) failing to grasp entities that do not admit spatial relations. Finally, the author presents a devastating tu quoque: ask the opponent about Allah's state before creation. If they say inside or outside, the world did not exist. Thus they are forced to admit that Allah was neither inside nor outside, proving their own objection false. The entire argument is an exercise in logical precision about the conditions for contradiction and the hierarchy of generality and specificity.

Refutation of the Claim that Allah Must Be Inside or Outside
Author refutes opponent's argument about Allah's location.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical refutation of the claim that Allah must be inside or outside the world, and identify the logical implications (luzum) used by the author.

Core Idea
The opponent's view leads to anthropomorphism and change, which are impossible for Allah.

This passage presents a powerful dialectical argument. The opponent initially concedes that Allah is neither inside nor outside, but then modifies the claim for after creation. The author shows that even the modified claim forces the opponent into absurdities. By understanding this refutation, you will learn how necessary implications (luzum) are used to dismantle weak theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's initial concession
The opponent agrees that Allah is neither inside nor outside. The author accepts this as true.
2
Spot the opponent's modification
The opponent says this is true only before creation; after creation Allah must be either inside or outside (they choose outside).
3
Trace the first implication (tajsim)
The author argues that to judge Allah is outside, one must have conceived both inside and outside; the conception of inside is anthropomorphism (tajsim).
4
Trace the second implication (taghayyur)
Before creation Allah had no 'outside'; after creation He does. This implies change (taghayyur) in Allah, whether called deficiency or acquired perfection, both impossible.
5
Identify the contradiction
The opponent claims no change but affirms a new attribute (relation to world). This is a contradiction (tanaqud).

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of an opponent's argument regarding God's relation to the world. The author begins by presenting the opponent's initial claim: that Allah is neither inside nor outside the world. The author notes that if the opponent accepts this, they have actually agreed with the author's position. However, the opponent then objects that this statement was true only before creation, but after creation Allah must be either inside or outside. The author's response unfolds in two stages. First, he argues that the opponent's own reasoning forces them to conceive of Allah as possibly inside the world (because they must conceive of both possibilities to judge one as false), and this conception amounts to anthropomorphism (tajsim). Second, the author demonstrates that their view implies a change in Allah: before creation, Allah had no 'outside', but after creation He is said to have an outside. This change is either a defect or a perfection. If a defect, it implies imperfection in Allah; if a perfection, it implies He was previously deficient. Both are impossible and blasphemous. If the opponent denies change, then they contradict themselves by affirming a new attribute. The author concludes with a rhetorical question about how they can speak of opposites in relation to Allah without knowing His essence. The passage ends with a brief statement that these points suffice for the intelligent student, and a prayer. All examples and reasoning are rooted in Islamic theological discourse, using necessary implications (luzum) to refute the opponent's position.
