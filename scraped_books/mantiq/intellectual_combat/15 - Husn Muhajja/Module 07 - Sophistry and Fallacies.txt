Refuting Direction with Accidental Attributes: Fatherhood
Author refutes the direction claim using fatherhood as a counterexample.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a counterexample using accidental attributes to refute the claim that every existent must have a spatial direction.

Core Idea
Accidental attributes exist without being located anywhere.

The author uses a simple example from everyday life: the attribute of fatherhood. This attribute exists after a man has children, yet it cannot be pointed to in any direction. This demonstrates that existence does not entail spatial location.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
The universal claim is that every existent must be in a direction (jiha). Find where the author states this claim.
The claim is implied but not quoted directly; the author is refuting it.
2
Extract the counterexample
Examine the example of fatherhood (ubuwwa). How does the author describe its existence?
3
Understand why fatherhood is existent but not localizable
Fatherhood is an attribute that comes into being after marriage and childbirth. It exists, but we cannot point to it or assign it a spatial location.
4
Verify that the counterexample refutes the universal claim
If fatherhood exists without direction, then the claim that all existents have direction is false. Check whether the opponent could object.

Therefore, it is possible for an existent to exist without being in a direction. 2. A man before he marries and procreates is not a father. When he marries, he becomes a father. Therefore, he acquired an existential attribute, which is fatherhood. Therefore, fatherhood exists. So where is the direction of 'fatherhood'—is it in the man himself or in someone else? So where is the direction of 'one', for example? And can you, O man, point to 'one' with your finger, or at least specify its direction within yourself? Therefore, it has been verified for us that there are two existents, neither of which is in a direction relative to the other. Yet you claimed that whenever there are two existents, one must be in a direction relative to the other. So the falsity and incoherence of your statement have become apparent to you. So why then do you stubbornly insist and claim that this statement of yours is known by rational necessity? It has become clear to every rational person that its falsity is known by rational necessity. And after all this, O reader, take note that their judgment regarding direction, space, and so on falls within the category of concepts (taṣawwurāt) among the divisions of knowledge—especially since they point to it with their fingers. Consequently, this entails that they claim to conceive the reality of at least one of His attributes; and the falsity of this is known to every Muslim.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the claim that every existent must have a spatial direction. The author provides three counterexamples to demonstrate the falsity of this claim. First, the attribute of fatherhood: a man acquires the property 'father' after having children, yet this attribute exists without being located in any direction—it is an existential descriptor that cannot be pointed to. Second, numbers: we have concepts like 'one' and 'two' in our minds; they exist as mental entities, yet no one can point to 'one' with a finger or assign it a location in the mind. Third, two items of knowledge (e.g., knowledge that the world exists and knowledge that rain falls from the sky): these are two distinct existents but are not spatially related—one is not above, below, or beside the other. Therefore, the claim that every two existents must be in a direction relative to each other is false. The author concludes that their statement is contradictory and known to be false by rational necessity. Furthermore, the author warns that their judgment about direction and space falls under the category of conceptions (taṣawwurāt), but they mistakenly treat it as if they can point to it, which implies they claim to conceive the reality of one of God's attributes—an impossibility, as every Muslim knows.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use mental concepts as counterexamples to refute the necessity of spatial direction for all existents.

Numbers exist in the mind but have no location.

The author asks: where is the direction of 'one'? Numbers like 'one' and 'two' undeniably exist in our minds, yet no one can point to them or assign them a spatial location. This further undermines the claim that all existents must be in a direction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked

Recognize numbers as mental existents

3
Confirm that numbers have no spatial direction
You cannot point to 'one' with a finger or specify its direction in the mind. This shows numbers exist but are not located.
4
Conclude that the counterexample refutes the universal claim
If numbers (which exist) have no direction, then the claim that all existents have direction is false.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the accusation of sophistry when a claim contradicts obvious experience.

Core Idea
Knowledge exists without spatial relations between pieces of knowledge.

The author presents a third counterexample: we have knowledge of the world's existence and knowledge that rain falls from the sky. These two items of knowledge exist, but they are not spatially related—one is not above, below, or beside the other. Anyone who claims otherwise is a sophist.

Critique of Qiyas al-Awla for Divine Direction
Author refutes the claim that direction must be attributed to God via qiyas al-awla.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for applying qiyas al-awla and critique its misuse in theological arguments.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-awla applies only to absolute perfections; direction is not absolute.

The opponents use qiyas al-awla to claim that since direction is a perfection in bodies, Allah must have it in a superior way. The author dismantles this argument by showing that qiyas al-awla is valid only for perfections that are unrestricted, and direction is inherently conditioned on being a body. This lesson walks through the logical error and the correct limits of this form of analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opponent's Argument
The opponents claim: bodies have direction, direction is a perfection, so Allah must have direction in a more perfect way. Recognize this as an application of qiyas al-awla.
Qiyas al-awla is a prominent argument in kalam for affirming attributes, but its misuse leads to tashbih.
2
Analyze the Author's Refutation via Limb Analogy
The author points out that limbs are perfections for humans but cannot be attributed to Allah. This shows that not all perfections of creatures are transferable to the Creator.
The limb analogy exposes the flaw: conditioned perfections do not qualify for qiyas al-awla.
3
Understand the Restriction of Qiyas al-Awla
Qiyas al-awla is restricted to 'absolute pure perfections' (kamaalat muḥaḍḍa muṭlaqa). Direction is not absolute because it requires a dense body.
The author distinguishes between unconditional perfections (like knowledge) and conditional ones (like direction).
4
Grasp the Conclusion: Direction as Deficiency
Considered in itself, direction is a restriction and thus a deficiency, not a perfection. Therefore, qiyas al-awla cannot be used to attribute it to Allah.
The argument concludes that the opponents' claim is invalid and leads to anthropomorphism.

In the matter of direction by qiyas al-awla, as some ignoramuses among your shaykhs imagine, they say to you: We know that every body is in a direction from another, and direction is a perfection attribute, and as long as it is a perfection attribute, we must affirm it for Allah. This is your statement, and it is fallen and contradictory.
How do you know that the perfection attribute that belongs to bodies must be attributed to Allah the Exalted? Do you not know that limbs and organs are perfection attributes for humans and animals, yet it is not permissible to attribute organs to Allah the Exalted? Attributing organs to Allah is pure anthropomorphism. And besides, qiyas al-awla is only used in absolute pure perfections, and direction is not of this type; rather it is conditioned on the thing described being a dense body like other bodies we observe.
As for direction considered in itself, it is an imperfection attribute, because it is a restriction in the essence of existence.
We do not want to delve deep into rational considerations for you, lest your following us in this speech be cut off, because we realize that you are not deep divers in this field – and according to the investigators, not in any other – but our discourse with you is in accordance with what Allah has required from believers: to argue in the best manner, and as advice for you in the religion you claim to follow. And by this the invalidity of your speech becomes clear to you.

Commentary
This passage is a critique of a common argument used by the opponents to prove that Allah must have a direction (jiha). The argument uses qiyas al-awla (superior analogy): since bodies have direction and direction is a perfection, Allah, who is more perfect, must also have direction. The author refutes this by pointing out that qiyas al-awla is only valid for absolute perfections, i.e., attributes that are perfect in themselves without any condition. Direction, however, is not absolute; it is a perfection only when attached to a dense body. In itself, direction is a restriction and thus a deficiency. The author uses the analogy of limbs: limbs are perfections for humans but cannot be attributed to Allah because they imply bodily form. Similarly, direction implies a body and is not an absolute perfection. The author then notes that they are not delving into deep rational arguments because the opponents are not capable of following, but rather are arguing in the manner prescribed by the Quran (al-mujadala bi-llati hiya ahsan). The conclusion is that the opponents' argument is invalid.

Key Takeaway
Qiyas al-awla is a valid argument only for absolute perfections; applying it to conditioned attributes like direction leads to anthropomorphism and is logically invalid.
