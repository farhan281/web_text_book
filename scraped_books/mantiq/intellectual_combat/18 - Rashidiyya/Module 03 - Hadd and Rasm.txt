Definition of al-Bahth: Linguistic and Terminological Meanings
Author defines 'al-bahth' linguistically and terminologically, listing three technical meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three technical meanings of 'al-bahth' and identify which is intended in the context of munazara.

Core Idea
Al-bahth has three meanings; only the third (debate) is the intended sense in the art of munazara.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the foundational term 'al-bahth' (investigation) by first giving its linguistic sense and then its three technical meanings. Understanding this distinction is crucial because the entire discipline of munazara (debate) depends on a precise definition of its subject. The commentary also explains why the other two meanings are less suitable in this context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition
The passage states that linguistically, 'al-bahth' means 'examination and searching' (al-fahs wa al-taftish). This is the broadest sense.
2
Extract the three terminological meanings
The commentator lists three distinct technical uses: (1) carrying something upon something (predication), (2) establishing a reportive relation by evidence (inference), and (3) debate (al-munazara).
These are not equally applicable to all contexts.
3
Determine the intended meaning
The passage explicitly says 'wa al-murad huna thalith al-ma'ani' – the intended meaning here is the third (debate). This is because the work deals with the rules of debate.
4
Analyse why other meanings are rejected
The second meaning (inference) is possible but does not apply to the act of objection (al-man'). The first meaning (predication) is too broad, covering every judgment in mind or speech.
This shows the precision required in technical definitions.
5
Examine the commentator's elaboration via footnotes
Footnote (1) explains the metaphor 'image of the seer' – mental matters are made clear like sensory objects. Footnote (2) reinforces that the third meaning is preferred due to the upcoming definition of debate.
The commentary (sharh) is not just translating but expanding and justifying the base text.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'al-bahth' (investigation) from a sharh (commentary) perspective. The author begins by presenting the matn (text) which states that these are the rules of investigation containing what must be present in the art of debate, arranged like a visible image into universal matters that yield correct particular investigations. Then the commentary explains the term 'al-bahth' linguistically as 'examination and searching' (al-fahs wa al-taftish) and terminologically as having three distinct meanings: (1) 'carrying something upon something' (haml shay' 'ala shay') which refers to predication, (2) 'establishing a reportive relation by evidence' (ithbat al-nisba al-khabariyya bi-l-dalil) which is inference, and (3) 'debate' (al-munazara). The commentator clarifies that the intended meaning in this context is the third (debate). He then addresses potential objections: the second meaning is not inappropriate, but it does not apply to the act of objection (al-man' itself, as objection is a form of debate. The first meaning is too broad, applying to any mental or spoken judgment, so it is not specific enough. The footnotes elaborate: footnote (1) explains the metaphor of 'the image of the seer' – that conceptual matters are made clear like sensory objects. Footnote (2) reiterates the three meanings and notes that the third is preferred due to the upcoming definition of debate. This analysis is firmly rooted in the Islamic intellectual tradition; for example, the distinction between carrying (haml) and establishing (ithbat) parallels discussions in logic about tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent).

The Role of Muqaddima in Scholarly Works
Author defines muqaddima and its function in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'al-muqaddima' as used in Islamic scholarly works and explain its function as a prerequisite for insight.

Core Idea
The introduction is the vanguard of insight, not insight itself.

When opening any classical Islamic text, the first section is usually the 'muqaddima'. But is it just a fancy preface, or does it have a precise logical function? The author explains that the muqaddima is that upon which the successful pursuit of the main goals depends, provided one proceeds with insight (basira). Qutb al-Din al-Razi adds a clarification that the muqaddima enables insight; it does not itself require insight to be understood. This distinction is crucial for how you read and benefit from introductory material.

Guided Path
1
Locate the three-part structure
The text divides the book into an introduction (muqaddima), investigations (abhath), and a conclusion (khatima). The muqaddima comes first and is about definitions.
This structure is common in Islamic logical works.
2
Understand the definition of muqaddima
The muqaddima is defined as 'that upon which the commencement of the goals depends, with insight (basira)'. It is like the vanguard of an army that prepares the way for the main force.
The phrase 'with insight' indicates that the introduction should equip the reader with understanding, not just be a formality.
3
Analyze Qutb al-Din al-Razi's refinement
He corrects a potential misinterpretation: the muqaddima itself does not depend on insight; rather, it is a condition for the student to have insight when starting the main content.
This prevents the circular notion that you need insight to begin the introduction.
4
Apply the army analogy
The vanguard (muqaddimat al-jaysh) precedes the army and secures the path. Similarly, the muqaddima of a book prepares the ground for the main discussions.
The analogy reinforces that the muqaddima is a necessary tool, not an ornament.

The Five Linguistic Roots of al-Munazara
Author defines munazara through its linguistic roots.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five linguistic roots of 'al-munazara' and explain how each root contributes to the technical meaning of formal debate.

Core Idea
Al-munazara derives its name from sight, waiting, confrontation, and equality—each reflecting a dimension of debate.

Technical terms in Islamic sciences are often rooted in Arabic words that carry layers of meaning. The author traces 'al-munazara' (debate) to five possible roots: nazir (counterpart), nazar (sight), the soul's turning to intelligibles, intizar (waiting), and muqabala (confrontation). Each root captures a different aspect of the dialectical process. Understanding these derivations enriches your appreciation of what munazara truly entails—mutual engagement, insight, patience, and direct exchange.

Guided Path
1
List the five derivations
The text states that munazara is taken from: (1) al-nazir (counterpart), (2) al-nazar (sight), (3) turning of the soul to intelligibles and contemplating them, (4) al-intizar (waiting), (5) al-muqabala (confrontation).
These are not competing etymologies but complementary aspects.
2
Connect each root to debate
For each root, the author implies an aspect of debate: from nazir, the idea that both parties aim at a common point; from nazar, the act of seeing and examining; from the third, mental reflection; from intizar, patience and allowing the other to speak; from muqabala, face-to-face opposition.
The phrase 'wajh al-munasaba' (aspect of appropriateness) links each root to the concept.
3
Recognize the first root's implication
The text notes that from nazir there is an indication that the two debaters share a single source or goal (ma'khadhuhuma shay' wahid). This highlights the cooperative aspect of debate: both seek truth.
This differentiates munazara from mere quarrelsome argument.
4
Summarize the multifaceted definition
By considering all five roots, you see that munazara involves equality of parties (nazir), perceptual and intellectual sight (nazar), deep contemplation (ta'ammul), patience (intizar), and direct confrontation (muqabala).
A comprehensive definition integrates these dimensions.

Understanding Munazara's Definition and Its Four Causes
Author defines munazara, addresses objections, and explains its four causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four causes of munazara within its definition and explain how the author refined earlier definitions to address objections.

Core Idea
Munazara is defined by its four causes: formal, material, efficient, and final.

This lesson unpacks the classical definition of munazara (debate) as given by the author. We'll see how he improved upon earlier definitions by replacing 'consideration from two sides' with 'turning of the two opponents' and adding 'showing the truth' to exclude mere disputation. Then we'll extract the four causes embedded in his definition.

1
Identify the finalized definition
Locate the author's definition: 'توجه المتخاصمين في النسبة إظهارا للصواب' (the turning of the two opponents toward the relation for showing the truth). This is the core we will analyze.
2
Trace the objections to earlier definitions
The text mentions two objections: (1) 'consideration' excludes mere denial by the questioner; (2) 'two sides' is broader than 'two opponents'. See how the author's rewording solves these.

Defining the Claimant (Mudda'i)
Author defines claimant and addresses first objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the essential characteristics of a claimant (mudda'i) in dialectical debate and distinguish him from a refuter or opponent.

Core Idea
The claimant is defined by his intent to affirm, not by mere speech.

In dialectical debate, not every speaker is a claimant. The author refines the definition to exclude those who merely refute or oppose. This lesson unpacks the precise definition and the first objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
The author defines the claimant as 'the one who speaks from the perspective that it is affirmation.' Note that the definition centers on the intention to affirm.
2
Recognize the objection
An objector claims that this definition would include the one who performs cursory refutation (naqd ijmaili) and the opponent (mu'arid). These are not claimants in the terminology of dialecticians.
Cursory refutation means invalidating the proof without detailed analysis.
3
Understand the author's response
The refuter and opponent do not undertake to affirm the ruling. They aim to negate the claimant's affirmation or oppose his proof. Therefore, they are excluded from the definition of mudda'i.
Intent is key: mudda'i seeks to establish, others seek to undermine.
4
Apply the distinction
Given a statement like 'This argument is invalid,' determine whether the speaker is a mudda'i. Since the statement negates, the speaker is not a mudda'i but a naqid or mu'arid.
Practice: Is a mufti issuing a fatwa a mudda'i? Yes, if he intends to affirm the ruling.

Types of Proof and the Questioner (Sa'il)
Author defines types of proof and questioner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a posteriori (dalil inni) and a priori (dalil limmi) proofs, and define the questioner (sa'il) in both a strict and general sense.

Core Idea
The questioner is defined by his intent to negate, and proofs can be causal or effectual.

The passage clarifies that a claimant may use one of two proof types: a posteriori (from cause to effect) or a priori (from effect to cause), often used interchangeably. Then it defines the questioner (sa'il) strictly as the refuter, but also in a general sense including the preventer and opponent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proof types
Note the two terms: dalil inni (a posteriori) reasons from cause to effect, e.g., fire to heat. Dalil limmi (a priori) reasons from effect to cause, e.g., smoke to fire.
The text says they are often used interchangeably.
2
Understand the claimant's titles
A claimant using dalil inni is called mustadill (proof-adducer); using dalil limmi is called mu'allal (reason-giver). The title depends on the type of proof employed.
3
Define the questioner strictly
The questioner (sa'il) is one who sets himself to negate the claimant's ruling without providing a proof. This applies specifically to the refuter (naqid).
The naqid invalidates the claimant's proof without offering a counter-proof.
4
Extend to the general meaning
In a broader sense, sa'il includes the preventer (mani', who demands proof) and the opponent (mu'arid, who brings counter-evidence).
This general use is indicated by the phrase 'may also apply to a more general meaning'.
5
Apply the classification
Given a debate scenario, classify each participant: the claimant, the refuter, the preventer, the opponent. For example, in a debate on whether the world is created, identify who is the sa'il and what type of proof the claimant uses.
Practice: In a debate about the permissibility of qiyas, identify the roles.

Real vs Verbal Definition: Haqiqi and Lafzi
Author explains the two types of definition: real and verbal, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a real definition (al-ta'rif al-haqiqi) and a verbal definition (al-ta'rif al-lafzi) by analysing the intention of the definer and whether the definition imparts new knowledge of the essence.

Core Idea
Real definitions reveal essences; verbal definitions only clarify word meanings.

In the study of logic, definition (ta'rif) is the tool for acquiring concepts. But not every definition cuts to the heart of a thing. Some definitions, like 'man is a rational animal', give you new knowledge about the thing itself. Others, like 'al-ghadanfar is the lion', only tell you what a word means. This passage from the Rashidiyya explains this crucial division, laying the foundation for all subsequent discussion of definition in the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definition
Look for the terms 'haqiqi' and 'lafzi' in the Arabic. The author states that definition is either real or verbal.
2
Understand the purpose of each type
For haqiqi: it aims to acquire an image not yet obtained. For lafzi: it aims to explain the meaning of a word.
The purpose determines the type.
3
Examine the subcategories of haqiqi
Note that haqiqi is further divided: if the thing's existence is known, it is 'according to reality' (bi-hasabi al-haqiqa); if unknown, it is 'according to the name' (bi-hasabi al-ism).
This subdivision applies only to haqiqi definitions.
4
Apply the distinction to examples
The example 'man is a rational animal' is haqiqi because it produces a new image of a known thing. 'Al-ghadanfar is the lion' is lafzi because it only clarifies that the word 'ghadanfar' means 'lion'.
Also, scholars consider definitions of existence as lafzi.

Definition of al-Muqaddima in Jadal
Defines the premise as that on which proof validity depends.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-muqaddima (premise) as used in jadal and distinguish it from its use in syllogistic logic.

Core Idea
A premise is the foundation on which the proof's validity rests, whether or not it is part of the proof.

In dialectical disputation (jadal), terms often take on specialized meanings. The word 'muqaddima' usually means 'proposition' in Aristotelian logic, but here the author defines it specifically as 'that on which the validity of the proof depends'. This lesson unpacks that definition and explains why it matters for the rules of objection.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find the sentence: 'المقدمة : ما يتوقف عليه صحة الدليل'. This is the core definition.
The author follows it with a concrete fiqh example.
2
Analyze the key components
Identify 'ما يتوقف عليه' (that on which it depends) and 'صحة الدليل' (validity of the proof). The premise is not just any statement but one that the proof relies on for its soundness.
The author adds 'أعم من أن يكون جزءا من الدليل أو لا' – it doesn't matter if the premise is itself part of the proof or not.
3
Compare with syllogistic use
In syllogistic logic, a muqaddima is a premise in a formal argument. Here, it is broader: any proposition whose acceptance is necessary for the proof to be sound, even if it is external to the inferred argument.
This shift is typical in jadal texts where the focus is on the opponent's commitments.
4
Understand the role in defining man'a
The author states that the definition of premise completes the definition of objection (man'a). This is because man'a is defined as demanding proof for a specific premise. So understanding 'premise' is essential for understanding 'objection'.

Al-Man'a: The Single Objection vs. Munna'
Explains that a valid objection targets a single premise; multiple challenges become munna'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a single objection (man'a) and a multiple objection (munna') in a disputation, and apply the rule that a valid man'a targets only one premise.

Core Idea
A single objection challenges one premise; challenging multiple premises makes it a multiple objection (munna'), not a single valid objection.

In the heat of debate, an opponent might reject several premises at once. But the rules of jadal treat this not as one objection but as multiple separate ones. This lesson uses the fiqh example from the passage to clarify the distinction between man'a and munna', and explains why this matters for the discipline of disputation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection sequence in the example
The objector says: 'لا نسلم أن محل النزاع متناول النص' then 'لا نسلم أن كل ما هو متناول النص فهو جائز الإرادة' then 'لا نسلم أن كل ما هو جائز الإرادة فهو مراد'.
There are three separate challenges, each directed at a different premise.
2
Understand the author's verdict
The author states: 'ذلك منوع لا منع واحد' – this is a multiple objection (munna'), not a single objection (man').
A single man'a would only target one premise at a time.
3
Grasp the definition of man'a
The author defines man'a as 'طلب الدليل على مقدمة معينة من حيث هي مقدمة' – demanding proof for a specific premise insofar as it is a premise.
The phrase 'معينة' (specific) indicates a single premise is the target.
4
Apply the distinction to a new scenario
If an opponent says 'I reject all your premises', that is munna'. If they say 'I reject the second premise', that is a single man'a.
This helps disputants respond appropriately: a munna' requires multiple replies, while a man'a can be answered with one argument.

Understanding al-Sanad and al-Naqd in Disputation
Author defines support and refutation in disputation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sanad and naqd and identify whether a sanad is sound or unsound based on the criteria of generality.

Core Idea
Sanad strengthens an objection; Naqd invalidates a complete proof—both are tools of dialectical precision.

In Islamic disputation, not every move is a direct proof. Sometimes you need to support an objection (sanad) or refute a completed argument (naqd). This lesson defines these two key terms and explains how to evaluate the strength of a sanad. The passage also warns about a common difficulty: proving that a proof depends on a specific condition.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two definitions
Find where the author defines 'sanad' and 'naqd'. Note that sanad is mentioned first, then naqd. Read the Arabic carefully.
Sanad is 'what is mentioned to strengthen the objection'.
2
Distinguish linguistic vs. terminological meaning
The author gives a linguistic meaning for sanad (something to lean on) and a terminological meaning specific to disputation. Understand that the terminological meaning adds the context of objection.
Also note that 'mustanad' is a synonym.
3
Analyze the classification of sanad
The passage says sanad can be sound or unsound. A sound sanad is more specific or equal to the opposite of the objected premise; an unsound sanad is more general absolutely or in some respect. Compare these criteria.
This classification helps determine if the support actually strengthens the objection.
4
Understand the difficulty of proving dependency
The author discusses a subtle point: for an objection to be heard, the objector may need to prove that the proof depends on what he objects to. But often what seems necessary is actually a consequence of the real condition, not the condition itself. Grasp why proving this dependency is 'extremely difficult'.
This section warns against overly strict requirements for objections.
5
Apply the definitions to naqd
Naqd is defined as invalidating the proof after its completion by presenting a witness (shahid) that shows the proof does not deserve acceptance. Contrast this with sanad: one supports an objection, the other attacks a finished proof.
Naqd is a stronger move; it requires a counterexample or direct contradiction.

Defining Naqd: Two Definitions Compared
Author defines shāhid and compares two definitions of naqd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the well-known definition of naqd (takhalluf) and the author's definition (ibṭāl al-dalīl), and explain why the author's definition is preferred.

Core Idea
Naqd is not just failure of the ruling to follow—it includes any invalidation of the proof.

We have learned that al-shāhid is what indicates the corruption of a proof. But what exactly is naqd (objection)? Two definitions are competing: the well-known one as 'non-occurrence of the ruling from the proof' and the author's definition as 'invalidation of the proof.' This lesson examines the objections to the well-known definition and why the author's definition is superior, despite possible defences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is naqd (نقض) as a technical term in munāẓara. It is the opposite of man‘ (prevention).
Know that naqd is directed against the proof of the mu‘allil.
2
Extract the well-known definition
The well-known definition is 'non-occurrence of the ruling from the proof' (تخلُّف الحكم عن الدليل). Notice it focuses on the ruling not following.
3
Extract the author's definition
The author defines naqd as 'invalidation of the proof' (إبطال الدليل). This is broader—it includes takhalluf and other forms like implying an impossibility.
The author deviated from the well-known definition.
4
Analyze the objections to the well-known definition
First objection: naqd is not limited to takhalluf (non-occurrence) but includes cases where the proof implies impossibility. Second: naqd is a quality of the objector, not a quality of the ruling's absence.
These objections show the well-known definition is too narrow and misattributes the subject.
5

The Three Objections to Real Definitions: Man', Naqd, and Mu'arada
Author explains how real definitions are subject to man', naqd, and mu'arada due to implicit claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of objections (man', naqd, mu'arada) that can be directed at a real definition, and explain why definitions are subject to these objections due to their implicit claims.

Core Idea
A real definition contains implicit claims, making it vulnerable to the same logical objections as any claim.

Definitions may seem like neutral descriptions, but this passage reveals that they carry hidden assertions: that the given terms truly capture the essence, that the first part is the genus, and the second the differentia. These implicit claims open the definition to logical challenges. In this lesson, you will learn the three standard objections—man' (prevention), naqd (refutation), and mu'arada (opposition)—and how they apply to real definitions, not just to overt propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three objections in the text
Find the terms al-man', al-naqd, and al-mu'arada in the Arabic. Understand that man' is denying a premise (e.g., 'we do not accept that this is the definition'), naqd is showing failure of tard (comprehensiveness) or 'aks (exclusiveness), and mu'arada is presenting a different definition.
These are standard dialectical tools from jadal.
2
Recognize the implicit claims in a real definition
The text states that a real definition includes da'awa dimniyya (implicit claims): that the whole is a definition, the first part is the genus, and the second is the differentia. Locate this assertion in the Arabic.
These claims are not stated explicitly but are logically necessary for a definition to function.
3
Understand the painter analogy
The commentator compares the definer to a painter: just as a painter is not objected to while drawing, a definer is not making an overt claim. However, the definition implies a judgment (hukm) that it is a correct definition. The objections target this implicit judgment.
This analogy clarifies why objections are valid even though definition is a 'depiction'.
4
Distinguish real vs. verbal definitions
The passage contrasts real definitions (ta'rif haqiqi) with verbal definitions (ta'rif lafzi). Verbal definitions are purely linguistic and do not carry implicit claims about essence, so they are not subject to these objections.
This boundary prevents misapplication of objections to mere word explanations.
5

Then think how one could object using man' (deny it is the 'illa), naqd (show a counterexample where the 'illa is present but the ruling is absent), or mu'arada (propose a different 'illa).
Practice makes the dialectical logic concrete.

The passage clarifies that real definitions are subject to these objections, unlike verbal definitions (ta'rif lafzi) which are purely linguistic. The answer to objections is by proving the implicit premise or by showing the correctness of the definition. Implicit Claims in Definitions: The Real Target of Objections
Author explains that definitions contain implicit claims which allow objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the implicit claims (da'awa dimniyya) in a definition and explain how they enable objections like man', naqd, and mu'arada.

Core Idea
Every definition entails implicit claims that are the real target of objections.

In this passage, the author reveals that definitions are not merely neutral depictions; they contain hidden assertions that the definition is correct, that the first part is the genus, and the second the differentia. These implicit claims are what adversaries actually object to when they say 'we do not accept this definition.' By understanding these implicit claims, you will see why objections are possible and how to defend a definition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature of definition (tahdid)
The author says definition is like painting: the definer depicts the defined in the mind, but the definer is not making an explicit judgment. At most, the definition implicitly conveys a judgment that this is the definition and that is the defined. So start by seeing definition as a depiction, not an assertion.
2
Recognize the implicit claims
The definer implicitly claims three things: (1) that what he has provided is the definition of the defined, (2) that the first element is the genus, and (3) that the second element is the differentia. These are not stated but are understood from the act of defining.
3
Connect implicit claims to types of objections
Objections (man' = denial, naqd = refutation by counterexample, mu'arada = opposition by alternative) target these implicit claims. For example, man' denies the claim that the definition is correct; naqd shows the definition fails to include all instances; mu'arada offers a different definition.
4
See how implicit claims make real definitions vulnerable but also defensible
Real definitions are harder to defend because proving the implicit claims requires knowledge of essences. But recognizing these claims is the first step to building a defense.

Defending Definitions: Real vs. Conventional and Verbal
Author distinguishes real from conventional/verbal definitions in difficulty of answering objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish real, conventional, and verbal definitions by their susceptibility to objections and the methods used to defend them.

Core Idea
Real definitions require knowledge of essences; conventional and verbal definitions can be defended by mere transmission.

This passage contrasts three types of definitions. Real definitions aim to capture the essence of a thing, making them extremely difficult to defend because proving them requires deep knowledge of essential properties. In contrast, conventional definitions (technical terms in a science) and verbal definitions (linguistic meanings) can be defended more easily by simply citing usage from specialists or linguists, explaining the relationship between terms, or clarifying intent. Understanding this difference is crucial for knowing how to handle objections in different fields.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of definitions
Real (haqiqi) definitions seek essential properties; conventional (i'tibari) definitions are technical terms in a science; verbal (lafzi) definitions explain linguistic meanings. Each has different implications for objections.
2
Understand why real definitions are hard to defend
Real definitions claim to capture the essence. Answering objections requires proving that the genus and differentia are truly essential. This is difficult, as Ibn Sina noted.
3
See how conventional and verbal definitions are similar
Both entail a judgment about usage: conventional about technical usage, verbal about linguistic meaning. They do not claim essential truth, so they are easier to defend.
4
Learn the three methods of defense for conventional/verbal definitions
The passage gives three methods: a) Naql (transmission) from an authority; b) Explanation of the usage relationship (wajh isti'mal); c) Clarifying that a different meaning is intended (bayan irada). These are quick and do not require deep knowledge.
5
Apply the distinction in practice
When you encounter a definition, first determine its type. Then choose the appropriate defense strategy. If it's real, you need to prove essences; if conventional or verbal, you can rely on transmission or usage.

Tard and 'Aks: The Two Conditions for a Correct Definition

Core Idea
A correct definition must be coextensive with the defined (tard) and converse in negation ('aks).

In logic, a definition (ḥadd or rasm) is only correct if it perfectly matches the defined thing. This lesson introduces the two criteria for that match: tard (coextensive applicability) and 'aks (converse applicability). Understand tard (coextensive applicability)
Tard means that whenever the definition (ḥadd) applies to something, the defined (maḥdūd) also applies. This ensures the definition does not accidentally include things outside the defined. Test: if a definition includes a non-instance, it fails tard.
