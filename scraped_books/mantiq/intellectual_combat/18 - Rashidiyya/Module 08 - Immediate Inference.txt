Definition of Mulazama (Entailment) Between Judgments
Author defines mulazama as a judgment necessitating another and restricts it to judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given relation between two judgments qualifies as mulazama based on the condition of necessitation.

Core Idea
Mulazama is the necessitation of one judgment by another, not mere coexistence.

In this passage, the author defines the term mulazama (entailment) as a logical relation between judgments. Understanding this concept is crucial for evaluating inferences in Islamic logic, especially for immediate inferences (al-'aks). The author carefully distinguishes genuine entailment from accidental co-occurrence and clarifies why mulazama is restricted to judgments rather than individuals.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find the sentence that defines mulazama: 'كون الحكم مقتضيا لآخر' (a judgment's necessitation of another). This is the core meaning.
2
Understand the necessitation condition
The author explains that necessitation means when the first judgment exists, the second exists simultaneously, as in 'the sun is rising' and 'daytime exists'. This is necessary simultaneity, not mere temporal concurrence.
3
Distinguish from coincidence
The passage explicitly contrasts entailment with accidental co-occurrence, like 'man is rational' and 'donkey is braying' – both are true but one does not necessitate the other. This excludes coincidental pairs from being mulazama.
4
Apply the restriction to judgments
The author gives two reasons why mulazama is limited to judgments: either by terminological convention, or because entailment between individuals ultimately reduces to entailment between judgments (e.g., 'Zaid is a human' entails 'Zaid is an animal').

المُلازَمَةُ : كَوْنُ الحُكْم مُقْتَضِيَّا لَآخَرَ . يحتاج إليه في وجوده ما يكون مؤثرا فيه - كما ذكرنا - يصح تعريف العلة المطلقة ، ولا يصدق على الشرط كالوضوء للصلاة، لكن لا يصدق تعريف العلة التامة على مجموع العلة والشروط إلا أن يدعي كون الشروط خارجة عن التامة . الملازمة هي والتلازم والاستلزام في اصطلاحهم بمعنى واحد ، وهو كون الحكم مقتضيا لآخر أي لحكم آخر بأن يكون إذا وجد المقتضى وجد المقتضي وقت وجوده ، ككون الشمس طالعة ، وكون النهار موجودا ، فإن الحكم بالأول مقتض للحكم للآخر، ولا يصدق معنى الاقتضاء على المتفقين في الوجود ، ككون الإنسان ناطقا والحمار ناهقا، فلا حاجة إلى تقييد الاقتضاء بالضروري ، ثم إنه خص الملازمة بالحكم - وإن كانت قد تتحقق بين المفردات أيضا - إما لأنها مختصة في الاصطلاح بالقضايا، وإما لأن التلازم بين المفردات في الحقيقة تلازم بين الأحكام، كما يظهر بأدنى تأمل .
Al-Mulazama: it is that a judgment necessitates another. It needs in its existence something that has effect in it – as we mentioned – the definition of the absolute cause applies, and it does not hold for a condition like wudu for prayer, but the definition of the complete cause does not apply to the combination of cause and conditions unless one claims that the conditions are excluded from the complete cause. Al-Mulazama, al-talazum, and al-istilzam in their terminology are one meaning, which is that a judgment necessitates another, i.e., another judgment, such that when the necessitator exists, the necessitated exists at the time of its existence, like the sun rising and daytime existing, for the judgment of the first necessitates the judgment of the second. The meaning of necessitation does not apply to two things that coincidentally exist together, like man being rational and the donkey braying. So there is no need to restrict necessitation to necessary (entailment). Then he singled out mulazama for judgments – even though it can also occur between individuals – either because in terminology it is specific to propositions, or because the entailment between individuals is in reality an entailment between judgments, as appears upon minimal reflection.

Commentary
This passage defines the logical term mulazama (entailment/concomitance). The author first states that mulazama is a judgment's necessitation of another judgment. He then briefly discusses the concept of cause, noting that the definition of an absolute cause applies here but not to mere conditions like wudu for prayer. He then clarifies that mulazama, talazum, and istilzam are synonymous in their terminology, all meaning that one judgment necessitates another such that when the first exists, the second exists simultaneously. The example of the sun rising and daytime existing illustrates necessary entailment. The author stresses that mere coincidence (like man being rational and a donkey braying) does not constitute entailment, so no need to restrict it to necessary connections. Finally, he explains why mulazama is restricted to judgments, although entailment can also hold between individuals: either because the term is conventionally limited to propositions, or because entailment between individuals ultimately reduces to entailment between judgments.
