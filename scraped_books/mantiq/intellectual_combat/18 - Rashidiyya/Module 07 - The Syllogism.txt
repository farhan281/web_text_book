The Definition of Evidence (Dalil) and Its Defense
Author defines evidence as a compound of two propositions and defends it against two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the author's definition of evidence from the famous definition and explain how the two objections are answered.

Core Idea
Evidence is defined by purpose, not success: a compound of two propositions intended to lead to the unknown.

In Islamic logic, evidence (dalīl) is the basic building block of argumentation. The author offers a precise definition that avoids pitfalls of the famous definition, and then addresses two key objections. Understanding this definition will help you evaluate whether an argument qualifies as evidence in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Locate the author's definition: 'a compound of two propositions to lead to a theoretical unknown.' Then find the famous definition: 'that from knowledge of which knowledge of something else necessarily follows.' Compare them.
2
Understand the problem with the famous definition
The famous definition is criticized for including necessary concomitants (like fire and heat) where knowledge of one gives knowledge of the other without acquisition. The author's definition avoids this by focusing on the purpose of leading to an unknown.
3
See the reinterpretation of entailment
If the famous definition is taken generally, 'entailment' (istilzām) must be reinterpreted as suitability for transition (munāsaba) rather than logical impossibility of separation. This saves the definition but requires adjustment.
4
Learn the two objections
First objection: The definition does not include false evidence (dalīl fāsid) because false evidence does not actually lead to the conclusion. Second objection: Evidence can have more than two premises, so the definition is incomplete.
5
Analyze the answers
Answer to first: The lām in 'li-l-ta'diya' indicates purpose, so the definition covers any compound intended to lead, whether it succeeds or not. Answer to second: A chain of more than two premises is in reality multiple evidences; each pair of premises forms its own evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of evidence (dalīl). It begins by stating the author's preferred definition: 'a compound of two propositions to lead to a theoretical unknown.' This is contrasted with the famous definition: 'that from knowledge of which knowledge of something else necessarily follows.' The famous definition is criticized for including necessary concomitants (e.g., fire and heat: if you know heat, you know fire) when the intended meaning is only acquired knowledge. The commentator then discusses that if the famous definition is restricted to certain evidence, entailment is fine; if general, entailment must be interpreted as suitability for transition, not logical necessity. The author's definition avoids these issues. However, two objections remain: (1) it does not include false evidence (dalīl fāsid), e.g., a fallacious syllogism that does not lead to the conclusion; (2) it does not cover evidence with more than two premises. The answers: (1) the lām in 'li-l-ta'diya' indicates purpose, so the definition applies to any compound intended to lead, whether it succeeds or not; (2) a chain of more than two premises is actually multiple evidences—each pair of premises forms its own evidence. The commentary then provides examples in the footnotes: fire and heat as an example of entailment, and a clarification of why the famous definition needs adjustment. The text cuts off during the answer to the second objection.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary defining the logical term 'al-dalil' (the proof) and defending its definition against potential objections. The author, following the matn, states that the proof is 'the composite of two propositions for leading to an unknown theoretical'. He then notes that this definition is superior to the famous definition ('that from knowledge of which knowledge of something else follows') because the famous definition, taken literally, would apply to antecedents with evident consequents (like fire and heat), requiring reinterpretation. The author explains that if the famous definition is interpreted as requiring assent (tasdiq) and acquisition, it works for certain proofs, but if taken generally, 'implication' must be understood as suitability for transition, not inseparable connection. This nuanced interpretation, drawn from the author's gloss, ensures that no counterexample applies without forced answers.

Two objections are addressed: (1) the definition excludes defective proofs that do not actually lead to the required conclusion, and (2) it excludes proofs composed of more than two propositions. For the first, the author points out that the lam in 'lil-ta'ddi' indicates purpose, not actual attainment, so a proof is defined by its intended function, regardless of success. For the second, he argues that any proof with more than two premises is actually multiple proofs, as a single proof truthfully consists of only two propositions. He also prefers 'two propositions' over 'two premises' because 'premise' (muqaddima) is often defined as a part of the proof, which could imply circularity.

The passage then distinguishes between the philosophical definition and the usuli definition. According to the philosophers (hukama'), the proof is the composite of two premises (e.g., 'The world is changing; everything changing is originated'), while according to the usuliyyun (legal theorists), the proof is the subject itself (e.g., 'the world') from which, through sound reflection, one reaches a conclusion. The philosophical proof has matter (the two premises) and form (the syllogistic structure, typically first figure).

Finally, the author notes the distinction between 'dalil' (proof) and 'amara' (indication): 'dalil' is restricted to that from which certain knowledge follows, while 'amara' is for probable conclusions. The passage cautions that 'implication' in definitions must be understood as suitability for transition, not strict logical consequence, to avoid excluding non-evident syllogisms like the fourth figure.

The author, following the matn, states that the proof is 'the composite of two propositions for leading to an unknown theoretical'. Two objections are addressed: (1) the definition excludes defective proofs that do not actually lead to the required conclusion, and (2) it excludes proofs composed of more than two propositions. Two Definitions: Philosophers vs Legal Theorists
Author distinguishes definitions of proof according to philosophers and legal theorists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare and contrast the philosophical and usuli definitions of proof (al-dalil) and identify the different components of each.

Core Idea
For philosophers, proof is a composite syllogism; for usuliyyun, it is the simple subject that, when reflected upon, yields knowledge.

The author notes that his definition above aligns with the philosophers (al-hukama'), but the legal theorists (al-usuliyyun) have a different conception. For the usuliyyun, the proof is not the composite syllogism but rather the subject itself (like 'the world') from which, through sound reflection, a conclusion is reached. Understanding this distinction is crucial when reading texts from different disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophical definition
The philosophers define al-dalil as the composite of two premises. For example, the proof for the world's temporal origin is the composite 'The world is changing; everything changing is originated'.
This composite has matter (the premises) and form (syllogistic structure).
2
Identify the usuli definition
The usuliyyun define al-dalil as 'what can be reached by sound reflection on its states to an informative required'. They consider the subject itself (e.g., 'the world') as the proof, because reflecting on it leads to the conclusion.
For them, the proof is simple, not composite.
3
Compare the two examples
For the same conclusion (the world is originated), the usuli says 'the world' is the proof, while the philosopher says the composite 'the world is changing, etc.' is the proof.
The difference hinges on whether the proof includes the premises or is the starting point.
4
Understand the matter and form of the philosophical proof
The philosophical proof has matter (the two premises: subject and predicate) and form (affirmation of minor, universality of major, typically first figure).
This is why the philosophers view the proof as composite.

Dalil vs Amara: Certainty and Probability
Author distinguishes dalil (certain proof) from amara (probable indication) and clarifies implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalil (proof) and amara (indication) based on whether the conclusion is certain or probable.

Core Idea
Dalil leads to certainty; amara leads only to probable opinion.

The author concludes by noting that technically, 'proof' (dalil) can be restricted to that from which certain knowledge follows, while that which leads only to probable opinion is called 'amara' (indication). This distinction is important for evaluating the strength of evidence in Islamic sciences. He also reiterates that 'implication' (istilzam) should be understood as suitability for transition, not strict logical entailment, to avoid excluding non-evident syllogisms like the fourth figure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of dalil in the restricted sense
Find the statement: 'that from assent to which certain assent to something else follows'. This is the specific definition of dalil that yields certainty.
2
Identify the definition of amara
The author says: 'the antecedent of opinion is called amara'. That is, that from which probable opinion follows is an indication.
Amara does not produce certainty.
3
Understand the distinction
The previous definition of dalil (composite for leading) included both certain and probable proofs. But in this restricted sense, only certain proofs are called dalil; probable ones are amara.
This is a terminological precision.
4
Note the caution about istilzam
The author emphasizes that 'implication' in these definitions must be interpreted as suitability for transition (munasaba musahhiha lil-intiqal), not as necessary logical connection, so that non-evident syllogisms (like fourth figure) are still included.
This prevents the definition from being too narrow.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary defining the logical term 'al-dalil' (the proof) and defending its definition against potential objections. The author, following the matn, states that the proof is 'the composite of two propositions for leading to an unknown theoretical'. He then notes that this definition is superior to the famous definition ('that from knowledge of which knowledge of something else follows') because the famous definition, taken literally, would apply to antecedents with evident consequents (like fire and heat), requiring reinterpretation. The author explains that if the famous definition is interpreted as requiring assent (tasdiq) and acquisition, it works for certain proofs, but if taken generally, 'implication' must be understood as suitability for transition, not inseparable connection. This nuanced interpretation, drawn from the author's gloss, ensures that no counterexample applies without forced answers.

Two objections are addressed: (1) the definition excludes defective proofs that do not actually lead to the required conclusion, and (2) it excludes proofs composed of more than two propositions. For the first, the author points out that the lam in 'lil-ta'ddi' indicates purpose, not actual attainment, so a proof is defined by its intended function, regardless of success. For the second, he argues that any proof with more than two premises is actually multiple proofs, as a single proof truthfully consists of only two propositions. He also prefers 'two propositions' over 'two premises' because 'premise' (muqaddima) is often defined as a part of the proof, which could imply circularity.

The passage then distinguishes between the philosophical definition and the usuli definition. According to the philosophers (hukama'), the proof is the composite of two premises (e.g., 'The world is changing; everything changing is originated'), while according to the usuliyyun (legal theorists), the proof is the subject itself (e.g., 'the world') from which, through sound reflection, one reaches a conclusion. The philosophical proof has matter (the two premises) and form (the syllogistic structure, typically first figure).

Finally, the author notes the distinction between 'dalil' (proof) and 'amara' (indication): 'dalil' is restricted to that from which certain knowledge follows, while 'amara' is for probable conclusions. The passage cautions that 'implication' in definitions must be understood as suitability for transition, not strict logical consequence, to avoid excluding non-evident syllogisms like the fourth figure.

Ensuring Proof Necessitates Conclusion
Commentary on condition for valid proof: terms must not be independent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposed proof is independent from its conclusion and explain why such a proof is invalid.

Core Idea
A proof must necessitate its conclusion; independence breaks that necessity.

In logic, not every relationship can serve as a proof. This passage from the Rāshidiyya explains that if the proof (the middle term) can exist without the conclusion, the connection is severed. We will explore this essential condition and test it with examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand entailment requirement
A valid proof (dalil) must guarantee its conclusion: if the proof exists, the conclusion must also exist. If the conclusion can be absent while the proof is present, the proof fails.
This is a necessary condition for any deductive argument.
2
Analyze the independence example
The text uses 'man' and 'white' as an example of two things that can exist without each other. A man can be not white, and something white can be not a man. Therefore, neither can serve as a proof for the other.
Independence means no entailment in either direction.
3
Distinguish generality types
The passage states the proof cannot be 'more general absolutely' (a'amm mutlaqan) nor 'more general in some respect' (min wajh). If it is more general in any way, it can exist without the specific conclusion.
Even partial generality breaks the necessity.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Suppose you want to prove that Zaid is rational using 'Zaid is an animal'. 'Animal' is more general than 'rational' (includes non-rational animals). Zaid being an animal does not guarantee rationality, so the proof fails.
The proof must be at least as specific as the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a condition for a valid proof (دليل). It states that two things can be independent of each other, like 'man' and 'white': a man can exist without being white, and something white can exist without being a man. In logic, when constructing a proof (syllogism), the middle term (the proof) must have a necessary connection to the conclusion. If the middle term is more general (أعم) than the predicate in every respect (مطلقا) or even in some respect (من وجه), the proof fails. This is because the purpose of a proof is that when the proof exists, the proven (مدلول) must also exist. If the proof can exist without the proven, the implication is not necessary. Thus, to ensure validity, the middle term cannot be broader than the predicate. For example, to prove something about all humans, using 'animal' as a middle term would be invalid because animal is more general (includes non-humans).
