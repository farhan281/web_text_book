The Core Definition of al-Da'wa
Author defines al-da'wa as a proposition containing a judgment intended to be proven.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the definition of al-da'wa and explain why it is a proposition (qadiyya).

Core Idea
Al-da'wa is a proposition containing a judgment intended to be proven – the heart of every argument.

In this passage, the author gives a precise definition of al-da'wa, the claim that forms the basis of any debate or proof. Understanding this definition is essential because every argument in kalam, fiqh, and usul revolves around a da'wa. We will break down the definition into its logical components.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is al-da'wa (الدعوى). Look for the word and the definition that follows.
2
Extract the genus
The author says it is 'ما يشتمل على الحكم' (that which includes the judgment). What broader category does this belong to? It is a proposition (qadiyya) – a statement that can be true or false.
3
Identify the differentia
The specific part is 'المقصود إثباته' (intended to be proven). This distinguishes it from other propositions that are not being claimed for proof.
4
Check completeness
Does this definition include all claims and exclude non-claims? Consider if a self-evident proposition still counts as a da'wa (the author later notes it can be, but only an obstinate person would deny it).

And 'al-da'wa': that which includes the judgment intended to be proven, and that is called 'mas'ala', 'mabhas', 'natija', 'qa'ida', and 'qanun'. And 'al-matlub' is more general, either conceptual or assertoric, and is also called 'matlab'. And it may be said 'al-matlab' for that by which conceptualizations are sought, and 'al-da'wa' is that which (i.e., a proposition) includes the judgment, inclusion of the whole over the part, intended to be proven by evidence or made apparent by indication. And it may be that the claimed judgment is intuitive and primary, and it can be said: if the judgment is such, the discussion (munazara) does not occur, because only a disputer or obstinate one denies it. And that is called, from the perspective of being objected to or its evidence, 'mas'ala' and 'mabhas'; and from the perspective of being derived from evidence, 'natija'; and from the perspective of being universal, 'qa'ida' and 'qanun'. And 'al-matlub' is more general than 'al-da'wa': conceptual, like the quiddity of man, or assertoric, like 'the world is originated'. And it is called, from the perspective of being a place of seeking, 'matlab' also. And it may be said 'al-matlab', without 'al-matlub', for that by which conceptualizations are sought, like his saying 'what is man?', and for assertions, as is said 'is the world originated?'. Because al-da'wa applies only to assertoric information, while al-matlub applies to both conceptual and assertoric information.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'al-da'wa' (the claim) and enumerates its various names according to different contexts. The author begins by stating that al-da'wa is a proposition (qadiyya) that contains a judgment intended to be proven. He then explains that such a proposition can be called a 'mas'ala' or 'mabhas' when it is subjected to questioning or investigation; a 'natija' when it is derived as a conclusion from evidence; and a 'qa'ida' or 'qanun' when it becomes a universal principle from which particular rulings are derived. The passage further distinguishes 'al-da'wa' from 'al-matlub' (the sought): al-da'wa is restricted to assertoric (tasdiqi) matters, whereas al-matlub is more general, encompassing both conceptual (tasawwuri) and assertoric inquiries. This clarifies that not every sought matter is a claim; some are requests for definitions, like 'what is man?', while others are requests for proofs, like 'is the world originated?'. The commentary also addresses a nuance: if the claimed judgment is intuitively self-evident, it cannot be genuinely debated in a munazara, as only a obstinate disputant would reject it. Overall, the passage provides a precise terminological framework for understanding the roles a single proposition can play in logical discourse.

The Contextual Names of the Claim
Author lists contextual names of al-da'wa based on its role in argumentation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contextual name given to a claim based on its role: under investigation, derived as conclusion, or used as a universal principle.

Core Idea
One claim, many names: the same proposition is called differently depending on its logical function.

The author explains that the same proposition that is a da'wa can be called by different names depending on the perspective. Whether it is being questioned, derived as a conclusion, or used as a general rule, each name highlights a different aspect. Let's explore each name.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base proposition
Start with the same da'wa, e.g., 'the world is originated'.
2
When it is challenged
If someone questions the claim or its evidence, it is called mas'ala (مسألة) or mabhas (مبحث).
3
When it is concluded
If the claim is derived from a proof, it is called natija (نتيجة).
4
When it becomes a universal rule
If the claim is general and used to derive particular rulings, it is called qa'ida (قاعدة) or qanun (قانون).
5
Summarize the mapping
Understand that the same proposition changes name based on context.

Distinguishing al-Da'wa from al-Matlub
Author distinguishes al-da'wa from the broader term al-matlub.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-da'wa (claim) and al-matlub (the sought) and classify inquiries as conceptual (tasawwuri) or assertoric (tasdiqi).

Core Idea
Not every sought matter is a claim; conceptual questions ask 'what is it?', while claims assert a judgment.

The passage clarifies a crucial distinction: al-da'wa only applies to assertoric propositions (like 'the world is created'), while al-matlub is broader, including also conceptual questions (like 'what is man?'). This distinction prevents confusion in munazara.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of da'wa
It is a proposition containing a judgment intended to be proven.
2
Understand matlub
Matlub is anything sought, either conceptual (what is it?) or assertoric (is it true?).
3
Compare scope
Da'wa is restricted to the assertoric part, while matlub includes both.
4
Apply to examples
'What is the definition of prayer?' is a matlub tasawwuri. 'Is prayer obligatory?' is a da'wa.
