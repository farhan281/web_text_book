The Hadith Index: A Scriptural Map for Jadal
List of hadith references cited in the book's dialectical discussions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to navigate the hadith index and explain how each entry serves as a proof-text for a dialectical argument in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
The hadith index is the key linking revealed evidence to dialectical reasoning.

In the study of jadal among usuliyyin, scriptural evidence is paramount. The index at the end of Jadal Usuliyyin collects the prophetic traditions cited in the book. This lesson teaches you to read this index as a scholar would: not as a list to memorize, but as a toolkit for argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Observe the structure
Each entry begins with a hadith phrase, often the first few words, followed by a page number (not included here). This is the standard way to cross-reference hadith in classical works.
The index is not organized alphabetically; it is a sequential list as they appear in the book or by topic.
2
Identify the key phrase
The key phrase is enough to identify the hadith. For example, 'إنما الأعمال بالنيات' is the famous hadith from Bukhari and Muslim.
Some hadith are abridged; you should locate the full text in the original hadith corpus.
3
Link to jadal
Consider how each hadith could support a position. For instance, 'الأرض كلها مسجد إلا الحمام والمقبرة' (the whole earth is a mosque except bathroom and graveyard) could be used in a dispute about praying in graveyards.
The context of the jadal is usually given in the main text of the book.
4
Practice with a sample
Pick one hadith, e.g., 'أيما إهاب دبغ فقد طهر', and imagine two scholars arguing about the purity of tanned leather. How might each side use this hadith?

The Hadith 'Disputation in the Quran is Kufr' and Its Role in Jadal
List of hadith references cited in the book's dialectical discussions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the hadith 'جدال في القرآن كفر' and explain its function as a boundary for permissible dialectical argument in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
This hadith itself warns against blameworthy disputation, shaping the adab al-jadal.

Not all jadal is permitted. The hadith 'جدال في القرآن كفر' (Disputation in the Quran is disbelief) places a limit on theological argument. This lesson explores this hadith's meaning and its implications for the practice of jadal among usuliyyin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith
Locate the hadith in the index: 'جدال في القرآن كفر'. It is a standalone entry, indicating its importance.
This hadith is often cited in works on adab al-jadal.
2
Analyze the wording
The term 'jadal' is used negatively here. Compare with the technical term 'jadal' which can be praiseworthy if within bounds.
The word 'kufr' (disbelief) is severe, showing that some debate can lead to disbelief.
3
Contextualize the hadith
This hadith likely refers to disputing about the Quran without knowledge or denying clear verses. Scholars differentiate between such blameworthy jadal and the disciplined jadal of usul al-fiqh.
The index includes this hadith among many others, showing that jadal arguments must be aware of their limits.
4
Apply to jadal practice
Explain why usuliyyin still engage in jadal: they distinguish between munkar jadal (blameworthy) and the jadal that seeks truth using proper methodology.
The book Jadal Usuliyyin itself is a demonstration of permissible jadal.

How to Use a Fihris al-Athar (Hadith Index) as a Tool in Jadal
Lists hadith proofs cited in the book, arranged alphabetically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate and interpret hadith evidence from a fihris al-athar and understand its role in supporting arguments in Islamic disputation.

Core Idea
A fihris al-athar is your rapid-reference weapon for scriptural evidence in any jadal exchange.

In classical Islamic disputation (jadal), citing the right hadith at the right moment can make or break an argument. Scholars compiled indices like the Fihris al-Athar to allow rapid access to relevant narrations. This lesson teaches you how to read such an index, understand its abbreviations, and use it to find scriptural backing for positions in usul al-fiqh and fiqh debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the alphabetic organization
Notice that entries are grouped under Arabic letters (e.g., أ, ج, ر, ع, ق, ك). Each letter represents the first letter of the hadith's main subject or narrator.
The index is not a full database but a quick-reference tool.
2
Read the hadith excerpt
Each entry is an abbreviated version of a longer hadith. Focus on the key ruling, event, or narrator mentioned. For example, under 'Alif' you find rulings about a woman's 'iddah.
Abbreviations may omit chains of transmission; only the matn (text) is given.
3
Recognize the narrator or authority
Companion names in parentheses indicate the source of the narration. For instance, '(علي)' under 'Alif' indicates that 'Ali transmitted the ruling.
Multiple narrators for the same topic show differing opinions.
4
Understand the legal context
Connect the hadith to a specific fiqh issue. The entry about 'any woman who married during her 'iddah' relates to marriage and waiting periods.
Knowing the context helps you deploy the hadith effectively in debate.
5
Apply to jadal
Imagine you are in a disputation and need evidence. You can quickly flip to the relevant letter and cite the hadith to support your position or challenge your opponent.
The index presumes you already know the topic; it speeds up recall.

It lists hadith proofs cited throughout the book, organized by the first letter of the hadith's main subject or narrator. The index begins with letter Alif (أ), recording a hadith about water purity and another about a woman's 'iddah when marrying during her waiting period, with differing rulings attributed to 'Ali, Ibn Mas'ud, 'Umar, and 'Ali. This index serves as a rapid-reference tool for scholars engaged in jadal, allowing them to locate the exact scriptural evidence for positions debated in the work.

Reading Scholarly Bibliographies in Jadal
Lists reference works cited in the book Jadal Usuliyyin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the range of disciplines and chronological scope of sources cited in an Islamic scholarly work on jadal.

Core Idea
A bibliography is a map of the scholarly tradition that supports an argument.

This passage is a list of works cited in a book about jadal (dialectical reasoning) in Islamic legal theory. Bibliographies are not mere formalities; they reveal the author's intellectual debts and the breadth of evidence. By examining the titles, you can see how jadal draws from tafsir, fiqh, usul al-fiqh, sira, language studies, and even contemporary debates. This lesson will show you how to read a bibliography as a tool for understanding the foundations of a scholarly argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject areas
Scan the list and note which Islamic sciences appear: tafsir (e.g., Ruh al-Ma'ani), fiqh (e.g., Rawdat al-Nazir), usul al-fiqh (e.g., Sharh Tanqih al-Fusul), sira (e.g., al-Sira al-Nabawiyya), language (e.g., al-Sihah), and contemporary works (e.g., al-Salafiyya by al-Buti). This shows that jadal is interdisciplinary.
Works like Dala'il al-Tawhid indicate a theological underpinning.
2
Note the chronological range
Observe the dates: from al-Shafi'i (d. 204 AH) to al-Buti (b. 1929). The author uses classical authorities and modern scholars alike. This demonstrates that jadal arguments are not confined to one era.
Look for the earliest and latest dates to gauge the tradition's depth.
3
Recognize the chain of transmission (isnad)
Many entries include editors, publishers, and edition numbers. In Islamic scholarship, knowing which edition is used is part of the isnad (chain) of knowledge. A critical edition ensures reliability, which matters when citing an argument.
For example, the Risala is edited by Ahmad Shakir, a respected hadith scholar.
4
Connect sources to jadal themes
Think about how each work might be used in a dialectical debate. For instance, al-Qarafi's Sharh Tanqih al-Fusul is a usul work that discusses analogy and argumentation, directly relevant to jadal. Al-Buti's book addresses the concept of Salafiyya, which could be a point of contention.
This step turns a list into a research tool.

Navigating a Bibliography in Islamic Scholarship
Lists the sources referenced in the book, including classical texts on usul and logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a bibliography entry in a classical Islamic text and locate specific works cited.

Core Idea
A bibliography is a map of a scholar's intellectual resources — reading it critically reveals the tradition behind the argument.

This lesson teaches you how to read and interpret a bibliography from a work on jadal (dialectics) in Islamic jurisprudence. The passage is not an argument but a list of sources, yet it is essential for understanding the scholarly context of the book. You will learn to parse entries, identify authors and titles, and relate the works to the book's theme.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the structure of entries
Each entry is numbered, typically with a short title, author's full name (often with death date in hijri/CE), editor or commentator, publisher, edition, and publication date. Some entries also include references to modern reprints.
The format may vary; for example, entry 158 gives the translator and reviewer.
2
Identify the author and his lifespan
Look for the author's name and death date. For example, entry 164: Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (d. 505 AH). This helps place the work historically.
Some entries like 165 have no death date; assume the author is contemporary.
3
Determine the work's subject matter from its title
Titles often include keywords like 'Usul', 'Jadal', 'Mantiq', 'Minhaj'. For example, entry 163: 'Muntaha al-Wusul wa al-Amal fi Ilmay al-Usul wa al-Jadal' directly relates to usul and jadal.
Even works on hadith (e.g., 174) or history (e.g., 172) are included because they are referenced in the book.
4
Note publication details and editorial quality
Reliable modern editions are identified by editor name (e.g., 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Sulayman al-'Uthaymin for entry 157) and reputable publisher (e.g., Dar al-Kutub al-'Ilmiyyah). An edition with missing data (entry 161) or no date (entry 168) should be treated with caution.
Stray symbols like '·༩' or 'rose' are likely OCR artifacts or original publisher logos; ignore them.
5
Relate the work to the book's theme of jadal
Note which works are directly about jadal (e.g., 166: al-Minhaj fi Tartib al-Hujjaj by al-Baji) and which are foundational usul texts that supply principles for dialectical reasoning.
This helps you understand the intellectual resources the author draws upon.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography of sources used in the book 'Jadal Usuliyyin' (Dialectics of Jurists). It lists approximately 30 works, ranging from classical texts on logic, jurisprudence, and dialectics to modern editions. The list includes foundational works such as Ibn Khaldun's Muqaddimah, al-Ghazali's al-Mankhul, and Ibn al-Hajib's Muntaha al-Wusul, as well as specialized texts on jadal like al-Baji's al-Minhaj fi Tartib al-Hujjaj. The bibliography shows the breadth of Islamic scholarship relied upon in the study of jadal, encompassing usul al-fiqh, hadith, history, and criticism. It also reflects the editorial work of contemporary scholars who have made these texts accessible. While not a logical argument, this bibliography provides essential context for understanding the intellectual resources behind the book's content. Note that some entries have formatting irregularities or unclear publication details (e.g., 'rose' and 'Maison neuve et la' likely refer to publisher names in Latin script).
