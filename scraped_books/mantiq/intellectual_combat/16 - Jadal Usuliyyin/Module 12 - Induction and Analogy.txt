Establishing Cause by Coexistence and Absence
The author uses a hadith about a tax collector's gift to illustrate establishing a cause via the method of coexistence and absence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the method of co-existence and absence (dawrān) and apply it to determine the effective cause ('illa) in a given case.

Core Idea
The gift's presence with the job and absence without it proves the job is the cause.

In this lesson, we examine a famous hadith about a tax collector's gift to understand how Islamic jurists establish the causal link between a ruling and its effective cause. The Prophet's rebuke and Ibn al-Qayyim's commentary illustrate the logical method of dawrān—observing co-existence and absence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phenomenon to be explained
The gift given to the tax collector is the phenomenon we need to explain. Why was it considered a bribe?
2
Observe co-existence
Notice that the gift existed only when the man held the office of tax collector. The gift co-exists with the job.
3
Observe absence
Imagine the man sitting at home without the job. Under that condition, the gift would not come. The gift is absent when the job is absent.
4
Draw the causal inference
Since the gift co-exists with the job in presence and absence, the job is the cause ('illa) of the gift. This is the method of dawrān.
5
Apply to usul al-fiqh
This method is used to identify the 'illa of a ruling for analogy (qiyas). For example, the prohibition of intoxicants is tied to the cause of intoxication.

Qiyas and Ilgha' al-Awsaaf from the Hadith of Kissing While Fasting
Uses the hadith of kissing while fasting to illustrate qiyas and ilgha' al-awsaaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify analogical reasoning in a hadith and apply ilgha' al-awsaaf to determine which attributes are legally irrelevant.

Core Idea
The Prophet’s comparison of kissing to rinsing teaches that analogy depends on ignoring irrelevant descriptions and focusing on the effective cause.

In this lesson, we explore a famous hadith where the Prophet compares kissing while fasting to rinsing the mouth. The author uses this to extract two fundamental principles of Islamic legal reasoning: analogical reasoning (qiyas) and the elimination of ineffective descriptions (ilgha' al-awsaaf). By understanding how the Prophet draws this analogy, you will learn how jurists extend rulings from a principal case to a subsidiary case based on a shared effective cause (ʿillah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases compared
In the hadith, the Prophet compares kissing while fasting to rinsing the mouth while fasting. Both are acts that precede a prohibited action: kissing precedes intercourse, and rinsing precedes drinking. Identify these two cases and note the ruling for each: rinsing is permitted, so by analogy kissing is permitted.
2
Determine the common effective cause (ʿillah)
The shared effective cause is that both acts are mere introductions to the prohibited act (intercourse or drinking), not the prohibited act itself. The prohibition applies to the act itself, not to its precursors. This is the basis for the analogy.
The ʿillah is the attribute that triggers the ruling.
3
Apply ilgha' al-awsaaf (eliminate irrelevant descriptions)
The description 'kissing' might seem different from 'rinsing', but since the effective cause is the same (both are introductions), the specific description of the act is irrelevant. Ilgha' al-awsaaf means discarding attributes that do not affect the ruling, retaining only the relevant attribute (being an introduction).
This step is crucial: not all differences matter; only the effective cause matters.
4
Construct the analogical argument
Now formalize the analogy: The principal case (aṣl) is rinsing the mouth, which is permitted because it is an introduction to drinking. The subsidiary case (farʿ) is kissing, which is also an introduction to intercourse. Since they share the same ʿillah (being an introduction), the ruling of permission applies to kissing. This is valid qiyas.
The argument type is analogical, as the author notes: tashbih al-shay' bi-nazirihi.

Understanding Qiyas al-Tamthil: Definition and Examples
Author defines qiyas al-tamthil and provides two examples from usul al-fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a tamthil argument and construct one using an usul al-fiqh example.

Core Idea
Tamthil transfers a ruling from one case to another via a shared attribute.

In usul al-fiqh, analogical reasoning (qiyas) is central, but a distinct form called tamthil operates between particular cases. This passage defines tamthil and gives two classic examples from al-Shirazi and al-Sam'ani. By examining them, you'll see how the common attribute (ma'na mushtarak) justifies the transfer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case and new case
In the first example, the original case (asl) is the permissibility of specifying the Sunna by the Book (takhsis). The new case (far') is the permissibility of abrogating the Sunna by the Book (naskh). The shared attribute is that both acts clarify the intended scope of a general statement.
The common attribute is that both naskh and takhsis clarify what was not intended by general wording.
2
Extract the shared attribute
Al-Shirazi explicitly states that abrogation is clarification regarding times, while specification is clarification regarding individuals. Thus, both operations clarify the original general ruling; this common meaning justifies the analogy.
3
State the ruling being transferred
The ruling from the original case is that specification of Sunna by Book is permissible. This ruling is transferred to the new case: abrogation of Sunna by Book is permissible. The transfer depends on the shared attribute of being a clarification.
4
Analyze the second example's logical structure
Al-Sam'ani argues that a prohibition of one of two things implies permission to choose either, just as a command of one of two things implies permission to choose either. The shared attribute is that both command and prohibition are types of obligation (to act or to abstain) that allow choice when directed at one of two things.
The ruling transferred is 'permissibility of doing either' from the command case to the prohibition case.

Commentary
This passage defines qiyas al-tamthil (analogical reasoning) and gives two examples from usul al-fiqh. The definition by al-Jurjani states that tamthil is establishing a ruling for one particular case because it is established for another particular case due to a common attribute (ma'na mushtarak). Al-Ghazali's definition emphasizes that what holds for a thing holds for its counterpart by necessity. The first example from al-Shirazi concerns abrogation of the Sunna by the Qur'an. He argues that just as it is permissible to specify the Sunna by the Qur'an (takhsis), similarly it is permissible to abrogate the Sunna by the Qur'an (naskh), because abrogation is like specification with respect to time. The second example from al-Sam'ani deals with the Lawgiver's prohibition of one of two things. He likens it to a command of one of two things: a command to do one of two things does not require doing both, so a prohibition of one of two things does not require abstaining from both. This is tamthil because the same logical structure applies: permission to choose one applies in both cases.

Qiyas al-'Aks: Proving by Disproving the Opposite
Author defines qiyas al-'aks and illustrates with the argument that mafhum al-laqab is not a proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of qiyas al-'aks and reconstruct its logical form using a conditional (modus tollens) schema.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-'aks proves a claim by showing its opposite leads to absurdity.

The Arabic logical tradition developed a method called qiyas al-'aks, which is distinct from the simple conversion of propositions. It is a form of argument used especially in usul al-fiqh and jadal. In this passage, the author defines it as invalidating the opposite of one's claim to prove the claim itself, and then illustrates it with a famous argument about whether the implication of a title (mafhum al-laqab) is a proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired claim (mudda'a)
What is the claim that the arguer wants to prove? In the example, it is that mafhum al-laqab is not a proof.
2
Identify the opposite claim (naqid)
The opposite of the desired claim: that mafhum al-laqab is a proof.
3
Trace the conditional reasoning
Assume the opposite is true. Then deduce an unacceptable consequence: if mafhum al-laqab were a proof, then reporting a specific action would require knowing that others do not perform it, which is impractical.
This is the conditional: If P then Q.
4
Observe the invalidation of the consequence
We observe that rational people do accept such reports without that knowledge, so the consequence is false (not-Q).
5
Conclude the desired claim
Since the opposite leads to a false consequence, the opposite itself must be false, so the original claim is true. This is modus tollens.
Therefore, mafhum al-laqab is not a proof.

Commentary
This passage defines qiyas al-'aks (inverse analogy) as a method where one seeks to disprove the opposite of one's claim, and by doing so, one's own claim is proven. Al-Futuhi's definition is that it is an inference that starts from the negation of the desired conclusion, shows that negation leads to an absurdity, and thus confirms the original conclusion. The example given is from scholars who argue that mafhum al-laqab (the implication of a title, e.g., 'If the text says 'Zayd eats,' does it imply others do not?') is not a proof. Their argument is: if such implication were valid, then reporting a specific action (e.g., 'Zayd is eating') would require knowing that no one else is eating, which is impractical. Since it is considered acceptable by reason to report such specific actions without that knowledge, the implication cannot be a proof. This is a clear instance of qiyas al-'aks: assuming the opposite (that mafhum al-laqab is a proof) leads to an unacceptable consequence, so the opposite is false, and thus the original claim (it is not a proof) stands.

Objections to Using Qiyas for Establishing Usul and the Condition of Certain Cause
Author presents and refutes objections to using qiyas for establishing usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the objection against using qiyas for usul and evaluate the Maliki response based on the certainty of the cause.

Core Idea
Qiyas can establish principles if its cause is certain, not merely probable.

In usul al-fiqh, a key debate is whether analogical reasoning (qiyas) can be used to prove foundational principles (usul) or only specific rulings (furu'). This lesson examines an objection that qiyas yields only probability and therefore cannot be used for matters requiring certainty. We will see how the Maliki school defends its use by distinguishing between certain and probable causes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection claims that qiyas is only suitable for branches (furu'), not principles (usul), and yields only probability (dhann), while usul require certainty (qaṭ').
2
Locate the Maliki response
Al-Baji and other Malikis argue that qiyas can be used for usul if the underlying cause (illa) is certain (maqtu' biha), not merely probable.
This shifts the debate from the tool (qiyas) to the quality of the cause.
3
Analyze the example of the Followers' consensus
The Malikis used qiyas to claim that consensus of the Followers is authoritative, but it was objected that this is improper use. The Maliki response is that the cause (consensus in one era) is certain, making the qiyas valid.
The cause is the general principle that consensus is authoritative.
4
Distinguish certain vs. probable cause
According to the passage, qiyas only leads to probability when its cause is probable. But if the cause is known to be certain, qiyas can yield certainty.
This distinction is crucial for the validity of the argument.
5
Apply to the Shafi'i Basmala example
The Shafi'i argues that the Basmala is part of every sura based on the fact that it is written in mushafs and recited in prayers without objection. The opponent says this is qiyas with a probable proof. The Shafi'i replies that the cause (consensus on writing and recitation) is certain, so it is not qiyas but a certain proof.
The Shafi'i's defense shows that the same reasoning can be framed as a certain cause.

Core Idea
Al-Shirazi permits qiyas in customary matters only when an indicator (amara) is present.

Most usuliyyun reject qiyas in customary matters because they vary and lack fixed criteria. However, al-Shirazi offers a nuanced view: qiyas is permissible where there is an indicator that guides the analogy. This lesson unpacks both positions and shows how indicators work in concrete examples from fiqh.

Qiyas in Customary Matters: The Example of Menstruation in Pregnancy
Author presents al-Baji's example of qiyas for a pregnant woman menstruating, including the objection and response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Maliki's analogical argument for a pregnant woman menstruating and evaluate the Hanafi objection and response using the concept of indicator (amara).

Core Idea
Customary matters can be proven by qiyas if supported by an empirical indicator (amara).

Al-Baji presents a concrete example of qiyas in a customary matter—menstruation during pregnancy—to illustrate how jadal refines the application of logical principles. The Maliki uses empirical correlation as an indicator to justify the analogy, while the Hanafi objects on principle. Their exchange reveals the nuanced rule: qiyas is permissible in custom when there is an underlying indicator.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant's position (mudda'i)
The Maliki claims that a pregnant woman can menstruate. Find this assertion in the passage.
The claim is implicit in the Maliki's argument that the blood is hayd.
2
Extract the analogical argument
The Maliki uses two analogies: (a) pregnancy does not prevent istihada, so by analogy it should not prevent hayd; (b) the blood appears at the right time and description, like non-pregnant women.

The Hanafi/Hanbali objects that customary matters cannot be established by qiyas because they rely on experience, not reason.
The objection is a general principle: qiyas is not permissible in customary matters.

The Maliki concedes the principle but introduces a qualifier: qiyas is impermissible only when there is no indicator (amara). Here, there is an indicator: the empirical pattern that hayd and istihada co-occur in a woman's lifecycle.
The response transforms the debate from absolute prohibition to conditional permissibility.

The argument is empirically grounded: the co-occurrence of hayd and istihada in childhood, adulthood, and menopause provides strong evidence that they are linked. Since pregnancy does not block istihada, it should not block hayd.
This is a form of analogical reasoning supported by an empirical generalization.

Commentary
This passage is a concrete dialectical illustration of the debate over using qiyas in customary matters. Al-Baji presents a case: the Maliki argues that a pregnant woman can menstruate, using two analogies. First, pregnancy is a temporary condition ('arid) that does not prevent istihada (non-menstrual bleeding), so by analogy it should not prevent hayd (menstrual blood) – similar to breastfeeding. The Hanafi or Hanbali objects on the grounds that this is a customary matter, and qiyas is not permissible in custom. Here, there is an indicator: the close relationship between hayd and istihada. Both types of blood appear and disappear together in a woman's lifecycle – a young girl sees neither, a menstruating woman sees both, and a menopausal woman sees neither. This empirical pattern shows they are tied together. Since pregnancy does not prevent istihada, it cannot prevent hayd. The passage ends by noting that al-Tilimsani al-Maliki (the compiler's source) actually supported the Hanafi position, indicating complexity within the Maliki school. This example shows how jadal (dialectic) refines the application of logical principles to specific cases.

Textual differentiation between two cases blocks analogical reasoning.

In Islamic legal dialectics (jadal), the validity of an analogy (i'tibar) can be challenged by a direct textual distinction (nass). This lesson explores two classic debates between Hanafis and Shafi'is/Malikis where the Prophet's statements separate the rulings, and examines how the proponent of the analogy may respond.

Identify the analogies

In the second, the Hanafi analogizes the number of divorces among women (talaq) to the 'idda period, considering both as numbers that bring separation.
2
Find the textual objections
The Shafi'i and Maliki object by citing hadith: the Prophet encouraged tanning but forbade takhleel, establishing a textual distinction. Similarly, the Prophet said 'Divorce is for men, 'idda is for women,' separating the two rulings.

The detailed example is the Hanafi analogy that a Muslim should be killed in retaliation for killing a dhimmi (protected non-Muslim), analogizing the Muslim to another Muslim. The Maliki objects that this analogizes a disbeliever to a Muslim, which is invalid because the usul (principles) distinguish between them: a Muslim has greater inviolability (hurma). As evidence, the Maliki notes that slander against a Muslim incurs hadd punishment, but slander against a disbeliever does not. This highlights the need to respect existing distinctions between categories when performing analogy.

Key Takeaway
An analogy is invalid when the branch and root differ in their analogous rulings, because this signals that the categories are fundamentally distinct.
