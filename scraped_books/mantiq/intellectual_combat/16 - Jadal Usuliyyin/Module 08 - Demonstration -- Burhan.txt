Reductio ad Absurdum: Qiyās al-Khalf and Dalīl al-Tamānu'
Defines reductio and gives Quranic tamānu' example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio ad absurdum argument from the Quranic verse 'If there were gods besides Allah, they would both be corrupted' and verify its logical validity.

Core Idea
Reductio proves a truth by showing its opposite leads to absurdity.

This lesson introduces qiyās al-khalf, a powerful indirect proof method used extensively in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh. You will see how the Quran itself employs this logic to demonstrate tawḥīd. The argument from mutual exclusivity (dalīl al-tamānu') is a classic example: assume multiple gods, deduce corruption, observe order, therefore one God.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim to be proved
The desired conclusion is that there is only one God (tawḥīd). The author aims to prove this by refuting its opposite: the existence of multiple gods.
The opposite (naqīḍ) is the claim that there is more than one god.
2
Formulate the hypothetical assumption
The reductio starts by assuming the opposite for the sake of argument: 'Suppose there were multiple gods in the heavens and earth.' This is the premise the opponent holds.
This is the 'abandoned and fabricated premise' mentioned in the text.
3
Derive a contradiction from the assumption
From multiple gods, we deduce conflicting wills ('irādāt mutanāzi'a) leading to corruption (fasād) of the heavens and earth. The author explicitly states that this corruption would result from divergence of wills.
The contradiction is that the heavens and earth are observed to be in order, not corrupted.
4
Observe the factual reality that contradicts the derived consequence
The Quran points to the evident fact that the heavens and earth are sound (ṣāliḥatān) and not corrupted. This observation is the 'established premise' that contradicts the derived corruption.
This step corresponds to affirming the consequent of the conditional, but actually it's the negation of the consequent.
5
Apply modus tollens to reject the assumption and affirm the original claim
Since the assumption leads to a false consequence (corruption), the assumption itself must be false. Therefore, the opposite of the assumption—oneness of God—is true.
Logically: If P (multiple gods) then Q (corruption). Not Q (no corruption). Therefore not P (no multiple gods).

D - Reductio ad absurdum: It is proving a thing by the falsity of its opposite, like proving truth by the falsity of falsehood. It is called reductio ad absurdum for one of two reasons: either because it requires returning from the result to the opposite to take the required from the abandoned and fabricated premise, which is the opponent's false premise, by demonstrating its falsity to prove the truth of its opposite; or because it is attributed to the opposite (al-khalf), which is falsehood contradicting truth. This form of inference among the theologians is called the argument from mutual exclusivity (dalīl al-tamānu'). Among the examples of this type of inference in the Noble Qur'an is His statement: 'If there were in them [the heavens and the earth] gods besides Allah, they would both be corrupted.' The exposition of this inferential form is: If there were in the heavens and the earth gods, there would be conflicting wills between negation and affirmation, and from this conflict their corruption would result due to the divergence of wills. But since it is established that the heavens and the earth are sound and not corrupted, then what would be a cause of corruption—namely, the multiplicity of gods—has been nullified, and oneness has been established because corruption does not occur.

Commentary
This passage defines 'qiyās al-khalf' (reductio ad absurdum), a method of proof that establishes a claim by showing that its opposite leads to an impossibility. The author gives two possible reasons for the name: (1) it involves going back from the result to the opposite premise (the opponent's false premise) to extract the desired conclusion by proving that premise false; (2) it is attributed to 'al-khalf' meaning falsehood that contradicts truth. In Islamic theology (kalām), this is known as 'dalīl al-tamānu' (argument from mutual exclusivity). The Quranic example is Surah Al-Anbiya (21:22): 'If there were in them gods besides Allah, they would both be corrupted.' The author unpacks the implicit reasoning: the existence of multiple gods would lead to conflicting wills, resulting in corruption of the heavens and earth; but the heavens and earth are observed to be in order, not corrupted; therefore, there cannot be multiple gods, establishing the oneness of God (tawḥīd). This is a classic reductio: assume the opposite (multiple gods), derive a contradiction (corruption), reject the assumption, affirm the original (oneness).

The Definition of Rational Proof (al-Dalil al-'Aqli)
Author defines rational proof using al-Juwayni's definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristics of a rational proof according to Imam al-Juwayni and distinguish it from a transmitted proof.

Core Idea
Rational proofs indicate through inherent descriptions, not convention.

The passage opens by defining rational proofs and then cites al-Juwayni's precise definition. This definition is crucial for understanding how reason functions in Islamic argumentation. Let's break down its components.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The term is 'al-dalil al-'aqli' (rational proof). Notice that the author first gives a general description then quotes al-Juwayni.
2
Extract the core of al-Juwayni's definition
'ma dalla 'ala al-matlubi bihi bi wasfin huwa fi nafsihi 'alayhi ghayru muftaqirin ila wadi'in wa istilah.' It indicates the sought by a description inherent in itself, not needing a positor or convention.
3
Analyze each element
(a) 'dalla 'ala al-matlub' (indicates the sought), (b) 'bi wasfin huwa fi nafsihi 'alayhi' (by a description in itself), (c) 'ghayr muftaqir ila wadi' wa istilah' (not needing a conventional positor). This distinguishes it from transmitted proofs which rely on convention.
4
Contrast with transmitted proofs
Transmitted proofs (adilla naqliyya) depend on revelation and convention; rational proofs are known by reason alone.
This definition is foundational in usul al-fiqh for distinguishing types of evidence.

The Fourth Topic: Rational Proofs. By them we mean what reason guides to, of logical proofs and arguments used by a person to convince others, rationally, of opinions, ideas, concepts, and information he holds. We may be guided here by the definition of Imam al-Juwayni in defining the rational proof: that it indicates the sought by a description that is in itself indicative, not depending on a positor or convention. The Proper Function of Rational Proofs in Islamic Argumentation
Author explains the function of rational proofs in relation to transmitted proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two main functions of rational proofs (support/substitute) and why they should not oppose transmitted proofs, using the author's argument.

Core Idea
Rational proofs serve to support or substitute revelation, never to contradict it.

After defining rational proofs, the author turns to their function. This is critical for understanding how rationality and revelation relate in Islamic thought. He explicitly states what rational proofs do and do not do.

Al-Razi's Mulazama Argument for the Linguistic Reality of Shar'i Terms
Razi uses mulazama to prove terms become haqiqa for shar'i meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's conditional argument (mulazama) and identify its components: malzum, lazim, entailment, and the falsity of the lazim.

Core Idea
Al-Razi uses the falsity of an unavoidable consequence to prove that shar'i terms are genuine Arabic.

Islamic scholars debated whether terms like salat and zakat remain merely metaphorical when used for new legal meanings or become genuine Arabic words. Al-Razi provides a clever proof using the logical concept of mulazama (entailment). His argument shows that if these terms were not truly Arabic, then the entire Qur'an would not be Arabic—since they appear in it—yet the Qur'an is explicitly Arabic. Thus, the terms must be real Arabic. This lesson dissects that argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim (Malzum)
The claim that is being challenged is that the indication (ifada) of these terms for the shar'i meanings is not linguistic (i.e., they are metaphorical). This is the antecedent of the conditional.
In the passage, Razi states this as 'the conveying of these meanings by these terms, if it were not linguistic'.
2
Identify the Necessary Consequence (Lazim)
If the claim were true, then a necessary consequence would follow: the Qur'an would not be entirely Arabic, because these terms appear in the Qur'an with those meanings. This is the consequent.
Razi states: 'if their conveying of these meanings were not Arabic, it would necessarily follow that the entire Qur'an is not Arabic.'
3
Show the Falsity of the Consequence
The consequence is clearly false because the Qur'an itself declares that it is Arabic (e.g., Surah Yusuf:2 and Surah Ibrahim:4). Therefore, the lazim is corrupt (fasid).
In the argument, fasad al-lazim is established by two Quranic verses.
4
Apply Modus Tollens (or Reductio)
Since the lazim is false, the malzum must also be false. Thus, the original claim—that the terms are not linguistic—is false. Therefore, the terms' indication is indeed linguistic (haqiqa).
Razi concludes: 'the corruption of the entailer indicates the corruption of the entailed.'

The Quranic Proof for Ijma' and Three Objections
Quranic proof for ijma' with three jadal objections and answers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Quranic proof for ijma' and analyze the three objections and responses in a dialectical jadal exchange.

Core Idea
The Quranic threat against following non-believers proves the authority of consensus.

This passage from a usul al-fiqh work presents a classic jadal exchange: a proof for ijma' from Quran 4:115, followed by three objections from opponents and the author's responses. You will learn how the proof is constructed, what the objections challenge, and how the answers defend the proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant's position
The author (mudda'i) asserts that ijma' is a valid legal proof (hujja). He uses Quran 4:115 as evidence. Find the precise claim: that following the way of the believers is obligatory and therefore ijma' is authoritative.
The passage begins by citing al-Amidi to establish that the majority accept ijma'; then the specific proof is given.
2
Reconstruct the proof's logical structure
The verse threatens those who oppose the Prophet and follow other than the way of the believers. The author argues: (1) It is improper to combine a prohibited act and a permissible act in a single threat. (2) Opposing the Prophet is prohibited. (3) Therefore, following other than the way of the believers must also be prohibited. (4) If that is prohibited, following the believers' way is obligatory, which establishes ijma' as a proof.
The key move is that the conjunction in the threat implies each conjunct is prohibited individually.
3
Analyze the first objection and response
Objection: The threat is conditional on both actions together, so following non-believers is only prohibited when combined with opposing the Prophet. Response: The grammatical separation of the two clauses shows that the threat applies to each individually; otherwise the separation would be meaningless.
The response relies on a linguistic argument about the structure of the verse.
4
Analyze the second objection and response
Objection: The condition 'after guidance has become clear' applies to both conjuncts, so following non-believers is only prohibited when all guidance is clear. Response: The condition refers only to guidance in tawhid and prophethood, not subsidiary rulings, which is conceded.
This narrows the scope of the condition, salvaging the proof for subsidiary matters.
5
Analyze the third objection and response
Objection: The words 'other' (ghayr) and 'way' (sabil) are singular, so they do not imply generality; they only cover clear cases like disbelief. Response: In construct form (idafa), the singular implies generality, and the possibility of making an exception (istithna') proves that.
This is a classic usul al-fiqh principle: idafa with a singular noun can indicate generality.

وَالْجَوَابُ: أَنَّهُ وَإِنْ كَانَ مُفْرَدًا إِلَّا أَنَّهُ يَقْتَضِي الْعُمُومَ لِأَنَّهُ مُضَافٌ، وَيَدُلُّ عَلَيْهِ الِاسْتِثْنَاءُ مِنْهُ فَيُقَالُ إِلَّا سَبِيلَ كَذَا، وَالِاسْتِثْنَاءُ مِعْيَارُ الْعُمُومِ.
The first topic: Objections concerning authority. That is, a claimant uses consensus (ijma') as evidence for a ruling on an issue, then the objector faces him by saying that consensus is not used as evidence in legal rulings and is not suitable as a proof from which rulings are derived. In fact, the authoritativeness of consensus is a point of agreement among the majority of legal theorists, and only a few deviants whose opinion is not considered and has no support disagreed. Imam Sayf al-Din al-Amidi, may Allah have mercy on him, said: 'The majority of Muslims agree that consensus is a legal proof that every Muslim must act upon, contrary to the Shi'a, the Khawarij, and al-Nazzam from the Mu'tazila.' The scholars of usul have argued for the authority of consensus with many proofs. The opponents have raised a number of objections to these proofs, which did not reach the level of refuting those proofs or preventing their use as evidence. These proofs and objections are detailed in books of usul al-fiqh, and we suffice here to present one proof accompanied by the objections against it and the responses to those objections.
The majority of legal theorists argued for the authority of consensus from the Qur'an by His saying, may He be exalted: 'And whoever opposes the Messenger after guidance has become clear to him and follows other than the way of the believers - We will give him what he has taken and cause him to enter Hell, and evil is the destination.' [Quran 4:115] The aspect of argument from the verse is that Allah, may He be exalted, combined opposing the Messenger, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, and following other than the way of the believers in the threat, where He said: 'We will give him what he has taken and cause him to enter Hell...' Therefore, it necessarily follows that following other than the way of the believers is prohibited, because if it were not prohibited, it would not have been combined with the prohibited act, which is opposition, in the threat. For it is not proper to combine a prohibited and a permissible act in a threat. And if following other than the way of the believers is prohibited, then following their way is obligatory, because there is no escape from the two. And from the obligation of following the way of the believers necessarily follows that consensus is a proof, because what is meant by 'way' is what they choose of statement, belief, or action.
The opponents have objected to using this verse as evidence with several aspects of objections. We select a number of them accompanied by the answers to them:
The first: That Allah, may He be exalted and glorified, made the threat conditional on following other than the way of the believers and opposing the Messenger together. So following other than the way of the believers is prohibited only when combined with opposing the Messenger.
The answer to this objection is that the threat is actually made conditional on each one of them; and if it were not so, there would be no separation between them. So if the separation is without meaning, that would be meaningless, and meaninglessness is impossible for Allah, may He be exalted.
The second: Even if we concede that the threat is conditional on each one of them, we do not concede that following other than the way of the believers is absolutely prohibited, but only with the condition that guidance has become clear, because the clarification of guidance is a condition in the first conjunct due to His saying: '...after guidance has become clear to him...', so it is also a condition in the second conjunct because it is under its ruling. So the prohibition of following other than the way of the believers depends on the clarification of all types of guidance.
The answer to this objection is that the statement that the condition in the first conjunct is a condition in the second conjunct does no harm. For there is no dispute that the guidance that is a condition for prohibiting opposition is only the evidence for tawhid and prophethood, not the evidence for subsidiary rulings. So this guidance is a condition for following other than the way of the believers, and that is conceded.
The third: Even if we concede that Allah's saying: '...and follows other than the way of the believers...' necessitates the prohibition of opposition, the two words 'other' (ghayr) and 'way' (sabil) are singular, and the singular does not necessitate the prohibition of everything that differs from their way. Rather, it applies to an agreed-upon case, which is the case of disbelief and the like, about which there is no dispute.
The answer: That even if it is singular, it implies generality because it is in construct form. And the exception from it indicates that, for it is said 'except such-and-such way', and the exception is the criterion of generality.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange about the authority of ijma' (consensus) as a proof in Islamic law. The author first establishes that the majority of scholars accept ijma' as a valid proof, citing al-Amidi. Then he selects one specific proof from the Qur'an (verse 4:115) and presents three objections along with their answers.

The proof: The verse threatens those who oppose the Prophet and follow other than the way of the believers. Since it would be improper to combine a prohibited act (opposing the Prophet) with a permissible act in a single threat, following other than the way of the believers must also be prohibited. And if that is prohibited, following the believers' way is obligatory, which establishes ijma' as a proof.

The first objection: The threat is conditional on both actions together, so following non-believers is only prohibited when combined with opposing the Prophet. The answer: The grammatical structure (separation of the two clauses) shows that the threat applies to each individually; otherwise the separation would be meaningless.

The second objection: Even if the threat applies to each, the condition 'after guidance has become clear' applies to both actions. So following non-believers is only prohibited when all types of guidance are clear, which would be impossible for subsidiary rulings. The answer: The condition refers only to the guidance of tawhid and prophethood, not to every detail, and this is agreed upon.

The third objection: The words 'other' and 'way' are singular, so they do not imply generality; they only cover clear cases like disbelief. The answer: When a singular noun is in construct (idafa) it implies generality, as shown by the possibility of making an exception from it.

All examples here are from the Islamic sciences: the verse itself, the discussion of tawhid and prophethood, and the concepts of consensus and legal rulings.
