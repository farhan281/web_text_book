Three Categories of Sources in Islamic Research
Author categorizes his sources into three types: specialized jadal works, general usul works, and dictionaries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify primary sources for an Islamic studies research project into specialized works, general disciplinary works, and lexical aids.

Core Idea
Every Islamic research rests on three pillars: specialized treatises, general foundational works, and precise lexicons.

When embarking on research in an Islamic science like jadal (dialectical debate), knowing where to look is half the battle. In this passage, the author lays out his source categories: specialized jadal works, general usul al-fiqh works, and Arabic dictionaries. Understanding this taxonomy will help you build your own research bibliography with intellectual depth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first category: specialized jadal works
Look for books explicitly focused on jadal. The author names works like al-Mu'wana and al-Minhaj. These are the primary sources for the subject.
2
Identify the second category: general usul works
These are broader works on usul al-fiqh that contain discussions on jadal. Examples include al-Burhan and al-Mahsul. They provide context and theoretical foundations.
3
Identify the third category: lexical works
Dictionaries and lexical tools like al-Qamus al-Muhit help define key terms precisely. They are essential for conceptual clarity.
4
Evaluate the author's use of sources
The author says benefit varied: some helped theoretical aspects, others practical. This shows the need to use different sources for different purposes.

Commentary
This passage lists the sources used by the author. He divides them into three categories. The first category is the specialized jadal works written by usul scholars, such as those by al-Shirazi, al-Baji, al-Juwayni, Ibn 'Aqil, and al-Tufi. He notes that these are few and he benefited from them to varying degrees. The second category is the general usul al-fiqh works, a long list including classics like al-Burhan, al-Mahsul, al-Mustasfa, etc. The third category is Arabic dictionaries and lexical works used to define terms, such as al-Qamus al-Muhit and Lisan al-'Arab. He also mentions biographical works like Siyar A'lam al-Nubala'. This classification shows the breadth of sources used in the research.
