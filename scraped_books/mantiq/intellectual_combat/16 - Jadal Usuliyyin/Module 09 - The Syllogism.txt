Enthymemes in the Quran: The Omitted Premise
Definition of enthymemes with a Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the omitted premise in a Quranic enthymeme and reconstruct the full categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Most Quranic arguments are enthymemes; the missing premise is clear from context.

The author introduces al-aqisa al-idmariyya, or enthymemes, as syllogisms where one premise is not stated explicitly but is implied by the context. He observes that the majority of Quranic proofs are enthymematic. The example from Surah Al Imran refutes the Christian claim about Jesus by drawing an analogy to Adam. In this lesson, we will learn how to spot the missing premise and complete the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify explicit statements in the Quranic verse
Find what is directly stated: Jesus is like Adam; Adam was created from dust; God said 'Be'. Also note the implied conclusion: 'do not be among the doubters'.
The verse is clear about the similarity and creation, but the logical punchline is implicit.
2
Determine the claim being refuted
The Christians claim Jesus is the son of God because he had no father. The verse refutes this by analogy.
The refutation hinges on the parallel with Adam.
3
Reconstruct the full syllogism
The implicit middle term is 'created without a father'. The full syllogism: Major premise: Whoever is created without a father is not a son of God (implied). Minor premise: Jesus was created without a father. Conclusion: Jesus is not the son of God.
This is a categorical syllogism in the first figure (Celarent).
4
Check which premise is omitted in the text
The text states the similarity to Adam and the creation, but the major premise that Adam is not a son of God is omitted; it is obvious from the context of the analogy.
The omitted premise is indicated by the phrase 'like Adam'.

Enthymematic Syllogisms: Quranic Arguments with Implied Premises
Author defines enthymematic syllogisms and gives a Quranic example with explanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify enthymematic syllogisms in Quranic verses and reconstruct the omitted premise to complete the argument.

Core Idea
Quranic arguments often omit a premise, yet the logic is complete and compelling.

This lesson introduces a distinctive feature of Quranic reasoning: the enthymeme, where one premise is left unstated but understood from context. We will examine how the Qur'an refutes the Christian claim about Jesus by omitting a key comparative premise, challenging us to supply it. Mastering this tool sharpens our ability to appreciate the rhetorical and logical power of divine speech.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the pattern of omission
Identify that a premise is missing in the Quranic argument, as indicated by the author: 'most Quranic proofs have one of the premises omitted.'
2
Locate the explicit parts
Find the stated premises and conclusion in the verse: Jesus is like Adam, created from dust; the truth is from your Lord. The implication is that the comparison proves something.
3
Infer the omitted premise
Using the context of the refutation of Christian claims, reconstruct the omitted premise: 'If creation without a father makes Jesus divine, then Adam (created without parents) would be more deserving of divinity.'
This is the unstated conditional that makes the argument valid.
4
Formalize the syllogism
Recast the argument as a conditional syllogism: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P. Here P: 'Jesus is divine because he had no father'; Q: 'Adam is divine'. Since Christians deny Q, P is false.
This clarifies the logical structure and confirms validity.

The first omitted premise is the comparison: 'Jesus was created without a father; Adam was created without a father and mother.' The second omitted premise is the conditional: 'If Jesus were divine due to lacking a father, then Adam would be more deserving of divinity.' The argument then concludes that Jesus is not divine. The author notes that in a complete logical form, the argument would explicitly state these premises.

Qiyas al-Awla: The Analogy of Superior Worth
Explains qiyas al-awla using the hadith of the mother's hajj and the analogy of debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a qiyas al-awla argument from a hadith and identify the effective cause (illa) that makes the subsidiary case more deserving.

Core Idea
If a lesser case merits a ruling, the greater case deserves it even more.

The hadith of the mother's hajj demonstrates a powerful logical tool: qiyas al-awla. The Prophet ﷺ uses an analogy that is not merely suggestive but one of moral and logical priority. Understanding this tool sharpens your ability to extract rulings from revealed texts using the underlying effective cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (asl) and its ruling
Find the case where the ruling is already established. In the hadith, the original case is 'paying a human debt'. The ruling is 'it must be paid'.
The original case is stated in the Prophet's question: 'Do you see that if your mother had a debt, would you pay it?' She said yes.
2
Identify the subsidiary case (far') and the proposed ruling
The subsidiary case is 'fulfilling the mother's vow to perform Hajj' (a debt to Allah). The proposed ruling is that it must also be fulfilled. Note how the Prophet applies the same ruling to this new case.
3
Identify the effective cause (illa) that connects them
Both cases involve an obligation that must be fulfilled. But the hadith highlights that Allah's claim is even greater because He is more deserving of fulfillment. The illa is 'the duty to fulfill obligations', with Allah's obligations being of higher priority.
4
Recognize the principle of priority
Qiyas al-awla works when the subsidiary case has a stronger connection to the illa. Here, the debt to Allah is more obligatory than a human debt, so if human debt is to be paid, divine debt is even more so.
This is a common pattern in Islamic legal reasoning.

Commentary

The author explains that the analogy works on the basis of priority: if a human debt is to be paid despite human stinginess, then a debt to Allah is even more deserving of payment. This illustrates that when a ruling (e.g., obligation to fulfill a vow) is established for a less deserving case (human debt), it is even more strongly established for a more deserving case (divine debt). The author emphasizes that the Lawgiver's purpose is to indicate the underlying effective causes (иллал) that justify the ruling, not merely to state the ruling arbitrarily. The argument is that mentioning the comparison serves to clarify the reason, not to create a new ruling; otherwise, the comparison would be superfluous.

Analogical Reasoning: Tanqih al-Manat and Ilgha' al-Fariq
Demonstrates tanqih al-manat and ilgha' al-fariq via the hadith of the black child.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply tanqih al-manat and ilgha' al-fariq to a hadith to identify the true effective cause (illa) and eliminate irrelevant differences.

Core Idea
Tanqih al-manat refines the cause; ilgha' al-fariq eliminates irrelevant differences.

In this lesson, we examine a famous hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) uses analogical reasoning to address a man's concern about his child's skin color. This example from usul al-fiqh illustrates two critical methods for valid qiyas: tanqih al-manat (refining the effective cause) and ilgha' al-fariq (eliminating irrelevant distinctions). By analyzing this case, you will learn how to distinguish between superficial attributes and the true underlying cause in legal analogies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases in the analogy
The hadith presents an original case (asl): red camels producing a gray calf. The new case (far') is the man's black child born to non-black parents. Recognize these as the two situations being compared.
The asl is the camel color phenomenon; the far' is the human child.
2
Extract the attributes mentioned
List the attributes: the camel's red color, the calf's gray color, the parents' skin color (assumed non-black), the child's black color. Note which attributes are being considered for the cause.
3
Apply tanqih al-manat: strip away irrelevant attributes
The shared attribute 'difference in color' is not the effective cause (illa). The Prophet refines the cause by pointing to 'hereditary strain (al-'irq)' as the true reason, stripping away the specific colors as irrelevant.
Tanqih al-manat removes superficial similarities to identify the real commonality.
4
Apply ilgha' al-fariq: eliminate irrelevant differences
The obvious difference (human vs. camel, specific colors) is eliminated as irrelevant because the underlying cause (heredity) applies equally to both cases. Ilgha' al-fariq confirms that no meaningful distinction prevents the analogy.
5
Identify the true 'illa
The effective cause is 'hereditary influence' (al-'irq or 'urq). The Prophet concludes that just as camels inherit color from ancestors, so too can humans. This 'illa then supports the ruling that the child's color is natural and not a reason to doubt paternity.
The 'illa must be valid across both cases.

The qiyas here is based on the shared 'illa of hereditary influence, not on the superficial similarity of color.

Inference by Division (Taqsīm) and Burhān al-Khalf
Author explains two types of argument by division and Ghazali's burhan al-khalf with a Mu'tazila example on wajib mukhayyar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid inference by division (taqsīm) and identify its two types using Islamic legal examples.

Core Idea
Inference by division eliminates false options to reveal the truth – it is the engine of burhān al-khalf.

This lesson unpacks a key argumentative tool used by uṣūlī scholars: inference by division (istidlāl bil-taqsīm). You will learn its two forms, how it relates to al-Ghazālī's proof of the opposite (burhān al-khalf), and see it applied in a classic Mu'tazila debate on the nature of a wājib mukhayyar (obligation of choice). By mastering this method, you strengthen your ability to construct rigorous arguments in Islamic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of taqsīm
The author first defines two types: (1) listing the opponent's possible positions and showing all are false, so the truth is something else; (2) listing possible grounds for a ruling and eliminating all but one, so the ruling is attached to the remaining ground. Understand the difference: one is about refuting opponents, the other about discovering the correct ruling.
2
Connect taqsīm to burhān al-khalf
Al-Ghazālī's proof of the opposite (burhān al-khalf) is a special case: instead of proving the desired conclusion directly, you invalidate its opposite. This reduces to division and elimination – a form of taqsīm. Follow his logic: if one opposite is false, the other must be true.
Later logicians sometimes call this 'qiyās al-khalf'.
3
Analyze the Mu'tazila argument step by step
The Mu'tazila argue about the wājib mukhayyar (e.g., expiation options). They divide the characteristics: either equal in benefit or not equal. If equal, all are obligatory because choosing one without a preferrer is impossible. If not equal, the preponderant one becomes specific. Trace how each branch leads to a ruling that excludes the free-choice view.
The division is exhaustive – no third possibility exists.
4
Evaluate the validity of the conclusion
Check if the division is exhaustive and exclusive. The Mu'tazila aim to show that the wājib mukhayyar cannot be a free choice among undetermined options. Their reasoning holds if the two cases cover all possibilities. Confirm that the elimination of the free-choice view follows necessarily from the premises.
A non-exhaustive division would invalidate the conclusion.

B. Inference by investigation (sabr) and division: Among the proofs that the usul scholars rely on to establish the correctness of their usul doctrines is inference by division. It has two types: One: that he mentions the divisions that the opponent might rely on and shows the corruption of all of them, thus establishing that the truth is contrary to them. The second: that he mentions the divisions on which the ruling could be attached and shows the corruption of all except one, thus establishing that the truth is that one (1). Imam Abu Hamid al-Ghazali calls this type of rational inference 'the proof of the opposite (burhan al-khalf)', and says about it: that the arguer does not address the intended conclusion, rather he invalidates its opposite. When one of the two opposites is invalidated, the other is determined... The gist of it reduces to division, investigation, and invalidation of some parts to determine what remains of the parts. There is another type: enumerating a whole into its parts and invalidating all the parts to invalidate the whole (2). That is because the Mu'tazila said about the obligation of choice (al-wajib al-mukhayyar) that the obligatory therein is all the characteristics, not one unspecified. They based their argument on investigation and division. They said, as reported by al-Tufi: The characteristics of the obligation of choice are either equal in achieving the benefit of the obligated person or not equal. If they are equal, for example, his benefit in expiation by freeing a slave is like his benefit in expiation by fasting or feeding, then all of them are obligatory, because choosing expiation by some of them when they are equal in benefit would be a preferment without a preferrer, which is impossible. And if they are not all equal in benefit but some characteristic has the benefit exclusively or is preponderant, for example, if freeing a slave has the benefit of expiation or is more preponderant in obtaining it than fasting or feeding, then that specific characteristics must be determined, and it is the specific obligation, not on choice (3).

Commentary
This passage explains the method of istidlal bil-taqsim (inference by division) as used by usul scholars. It begins by identifying two types: the first type involves listing all possible positions an opponent could hold and showing each to be false, thereby proving that the truth is something else. The second type involves listing all possible grounds for a ruling, eliminating all but one, thus proving that ruling is attached to that remaining ground. Then al-Ghazali's concept of 'burhan al-khalf' (proof of the opposite) is introduced: instead of proving the desired conclusion directly, the arguer shows that its opposite is false. If one opposite is invalidated, the other is necessarily true. The method reduces to division and elimination. A second variant of burhan al-khalf involves enumerating a whole into its parts and invalidating all parts, thereby invalidating the whole. As an example, the Mu'tazila argument on the 'obligation of choice' (wajib mukhayyar) is presented. They divided the characteristics of the act: either they are equal in benefit or not. If equal, then all are obligatory because choosing one without a preferrer is impossible. If not equal, then the one with the specific or preponderant benefit becomes the specific obligation. Thus, through this division, they concluded that the obligation is either on all or on a specific one, never on a non-specific choice. This illustrates the second type of taqsim.

Inference from General Ruling to Particular Instance in Jadal
Author defines inference from general to particular and provides an example from al-Tufi on prayer in a usurped house.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the method of al-istidlāl bi-ḥukm al-kull ʿalā al-juzʾ in a jadal argument from usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
The general ruling applies to particulars because they are manifestations of the universal.

In dialectical argumentation (jadal), scholars often need to apply a general ruling to a specific disputed case. How do they justify this move? This lesson unpacks the method of 'inference by the ruling of the universal upon the particular' as explained by al-Tufi. We will see that the key is recognizing the particular as a specific form of the universal, with specification being merely accidental.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal matter (kullī)
Locate the general category or universal action-form that the disputed case falls under. In al-Tufi's example, it is 'prayer' in its universal form, as prescribed by the Law.
The universal has a ruling that applies to all its instances in abstract.
2
Identify the particular instance (juzʾī)
Find the specific case in question. Here, it is 'prayer performed in a usurped house' – a particular event involving Zayd or any mukallaf.
The particular is an instantiation of the universal action-form.
3
Recognize the accidental nature of specification
Note that the particular's specific circumstances (like the place being usurped) are accidental (ʿāriḍ) to the universal ruling. The Law's ruling is directed to the universal form, not to the accidental features.
This establishes that the general ruling remains valid unless a specific text modifies it.
4
Apply the general ruling to the particular
Conclude that the general ruling (e.g., prayer is valid) applies to the particular case (prayer in a usurped house), because the particular is a manifestation of the universal. However, the final ruling may depend on ijtihad if there is no clear conflicting text.
This method is used in jadal to defend a position against objections based on accidental properties.

Qiyas al-'Aks: Proving by Invalidating the Opposite
Author defines qiyas al-'aks and illustrates with an usuli example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct a qiyas al-'aks argument from a usul al-fiqh debate.

Core Idea
Prove your claim by showing its opposite leads to absurdity.

This lesson introduces qiyas al-'aks, a dialectical tool where you prove your position by invalidating its opposite. It's a powerful way to refute opponents in usul al-fiqh debates without needing transmitted evidence. We'll see how this works through the classic example of mafhūm al-laqab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being proven (al-mudda'a)
Find the positive assertion the arguer wants to establish. In the example, it's 'Mafhūm al-laqab is not a valid proof.'
2
Identify the opposite (naqīḍ) assumed for refutation
The arguer assumes the contradictory of the claim: 'Mafhūm al-laqab is a proof.'
3
Trace the conditional reasoning from the opposite to an absurdity
Assume the opposite; then deduce an unacceptable consequence. Here: if it were a proof, then saying 'Zayd is eating' would require knowing no one else eats, which is not required in ordinary speech.
4
Conclude that the opposite is false, hence the original claim is true
Since the consequence is absurd or rejected by rational people, the opposite (that mafhūm al-laqab is a proof) is invalid. Therefore the original claim (that it is not a proof) is established.
This mirrors the logical form: ∼P → Q, ∼Q, therefore P (if not-P implies absurdity, and absurdity is false, then P must be true).
