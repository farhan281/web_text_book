Qiyas al-'Aks: Definition and Prophetic Example
Tufi defines qiyas al-'aks and illustrates it with a hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qiyas al-'aks and determine its logical structure using al-Tufi's definition and the prophetic hadith.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-'aks derives opposite rulings from inverse relationships in obedience and disobedience.

This lesson introduces qiyas al-'aks, a key logical tool in usul al-fiqh. Al-Tufi defines it through a prophetic hadith where the Prophet infers reward for lawful intercourse from the punishment for unlawful intercourse. You will learn the structure of this inference and how it connects divine commands to opposite rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim in the hadith
The Prophet claims that lawful sexual intercourse is charity (rewarded). The companions question this, and the Prophet argues by pointing to the opposite: if it were unlawful, it would be a sin.
2
Extract the logical structure
The argument is: if intercourse were haram, it would be a sin; therefore, since it is halal, it is rewarded. This is qiyas al-'aks: the ruling of the opposite act is inverse.
This is not a simple conversion but an inference from opposite circumstances.
3
Define qiyas al-'aks according to al-Tufi
Al-Tufi defines it as: 'what one is punished for doing, one is rewarded for leaving, and vice versa.' This is based on the principle that obedience is doing commanded and leaving prohibited, and disobedience is the reverse.
The definition captures both directions: punishment for doing implies reward for leaving, and reward for doing implies punishment for leaving.
4
Apply the definition to the hadith
The act of intercourse is punished when unlawful (zina), so it is rewarded when lawful (with spouse). This matches al-Tufi's definition exactly.
5
Distinguish from other types of qiyas
Qiyas al-'aks contrasts with qiyas al-tard (co-extensive analogy) where the ruling follows the same direction. Here, the ruling is inverted based on the opposite circumstance.
This distinction is important in usul al-fiqh to avoid confusing different inferential methods.

From that is the saying of the Prophet صلى الله عليه وسلم in specifying the types of aspects of charity: 'In such-and-such is charity, in such-and-such is charity, and in the sexual intercourse of one of you is charity.' They said: O Messenger of Allah, does one of us fulfill his desire? He said: 'Yes. Do you see that if he placed it in what is unlawful, would he sin?' They said: Yes. He said: 'Likewise, he is rewarded.' Al-Tufi said: This is from the category of qiyas al-'aks (analogy by converse), and its meaning is that what one is punished for doing, one is rewarded for leaving, because obedience is doing what is commanded and leaving what is prohibited, just as disobedience is the opposite of that.

Commentary
This passage defines a specific type of logical inference used in Islamic jurisprudence: qiyas al-'aks (analogy by converse). The author presents a prophetic hadith as evidence. In the hadith, the Prophet tells his companions that even fulfilling one's sexual desire with one's spouse is an act of charity. When they express surprise, he argues that if it were done unlawfully it would be a sin, so when done lawfully it is rewarded. Al-Tufi explains that this is a case of qiyas al-'aks: the ruling on an act and its opposite are inversely related. If an action is punished, its lawful counterpart is rewarded. The principle is that obedience involves performing commanded acts and avoiding prohibited ones, and disobedience is the reverse. This form of argument is common in usul al-fiqh.

Al-Tufi's Critique of the Definition of Taklif Using Ibahah
Al-Tufi critiques the definition of taklif using ibahah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify violations of tard and 'aks in a definition using an usul al-fiqh example.

Core Idea
A definition must include all and only its definiendum; al-Tufi shows how ibahah breaks both conditions.

Al-Tufi, a prominent Hanbali usuli, demonstrates a critical test for definitions: they must satisfy tard (covering all instances) and 'aks (excluding non-instances). In this passage, he examines whether the definition of taklif as 'address with a command or prohibition' holds if ibahah (permissibility) is considered a type of taklif. By applying Quranic examples, he reveals a double violation, showing the power of these logical conditions in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under scrutiny
Locate the definition of taklif being tested: 'address with a command or prohibition'. This is the definiens, while taklif is the definiendum.
The definition is stated at the beginning of the passage.
2
Understand tard and 'aks
Tard means the definition applies to every instance of the definiendum (no missing cases). 'Aks means the definition applies only to instances of the definiendum (no extra cases). These are the two conditions for a valid definition.
Tard = covering all; 'aks = excluding others.
3
Apply the test assuming ibahah is taklif
Al-Tufi takes the scenario where ibahah is considered a taklif. He then checks tard: does the definition (command/prohibition) exist whenever taklif exists? He gives Quranic commands like 'Eat and drink' which indicate ibahah, not obligation. Thus tard fails because the definition includes ibahah but ibahah is not taklif (under the assumption).
The examples are from the Quran: 'Eat and drink', 'When you have eaten, disperse', 'When you are released, then hunt'.
4
Check 'aks under the same assumption
Al-Tufi then checks 'aks: does taklif exist only when the definition exists? He argues that ibahah, as a taklif, lacks command/prohibition, so taklif can exist without the definition. Thus 'aks also fails.
This shows the definition is too narrow and too broad simultaneously.
5
Conclude the definition's deficiency
Since both conditions are violated, the definition of taklif as 'address with command or prohibition' is invalid if ibahah is considered taklif. This demonstrates the critical role of tard and 'aks in evaluating definitions.
The passage ends by stating the definition is refuted.

من ذلك فإن التعريف يعتبر ناقصًا إذا اختل فيه هذان الشرطان . ذلك ما علق به الطوفي على تعريف مصطلح «الإباحة»، قال: اختلف الأصوليون في الإباحة، هل هي تكليف أم لا؟. فإن قلنا ليست تكليفا صح تعريف التكليف بما ذكر، فيكون كل تكليف خطابا بأمر أو نهي وكل خطاب بأمر أو نهي تكليفا. وإن قلنا : الإباحة تكليف، انتقض التعريف المذكور بها من جهة الطرد، وهو وجود الحد بدون المحدود، لأنه ليس كلما وجد الخطاب بأمر أو الخطاب بأمر أو نهي وجد التكليف، لأن الإباحة مثل قوله : ... وَكُلُوا وَاشْرَبُوا ) ، فَإِذَا . طَعِمْتُمْ فَانتَشِرُوا (2) ، و. .. وَإِذَا حَلَلْتُمْ فَأَصْطَادُوا ...)(3) ، ونحوه من المباحات، خطاب بأمر وليس تكليفا . وكذلك من جهة العكس، وهو انتفاء المحدود عند انتفاء الحد، لأنه ليس كلما انتفى الخطاب بأمر أو نهي قد ينتفي، ويكون التكليف موجودًا في المباحات، إذ حقيقة الإباحة : التخيير بين الفعل وتركه نحو إن شئت فافعل، وإن شئت لا تفعل ؛ ؛ وحقيقة التخيير غير حقيقة الأمر والنهي . فإذا قلنا : الإباحة تكليف، فقد صح وجود التكليف انتفاء الأمر والنهي
Therefore, the definition is considered deficient if these two conditions are violated. That is what al-Tufi commented on regarding the definition of the term 'ibahah'. He said: The usuliyyun differed concerning ibahah: is it a taklif or not? If we say it is not a taklif, the definition of taklif as mentioned is valid, so every taklif is an address with a command or prohibition, and every address with a command or prohibition is a taklif. If we say ibahah is a taklif, the definition is refuted by it from the aspect of tard, which is the existence of the definition without the definiendum, because it is not the case that whenever an address with a command or prohibition exists, taklif exists; for ibahah, such as His saying 'Eat and drink'... and 'When you have eaten, disperse'... and 'When you are released, then hunt'... and similar permissible acts, is an address with a command but not a taklif. Likewise from the aspect of 'aks, which is the non-existence of the definiendum when the definition is absent; because it is not that whenever the address with a command or prohibition is absent, taklif is absent; rather taklif can exist in permissible acts, since the reality of ibahah is giving the choice between action and omission, such as 'If you wish do it, and if you wish do not do it', and the reality of choice is other than the reality of command and prohibition. So if we say ibahah is taklif, then taklif is established even in the absence of command and prohibition.

Commentary
This passage presents a critical argument by al-Tufi against a potential definition of taklif. It begins by stating that a definition is deficient if it fails two conditions: tard (the definition must cover all instances of the definiendum) and 'aks (the definition must exclude non-instances). Al-Tufi applies this to the term 'ibahah' (permissibility). The usuliyyun differ on whether ibahah is a taklif (obligation). If ibahah is not a taklif, then the definition of taklif as 'address with a command or prohibition' works perfectly. However, if ibahah is considered a taklif, then the definition fails both conditions. For tard, the definition (address with a command or prohibition) would include ibahah, but ibahah is not a taklif, so the definition extends to something outside the definiendum. Al-Tufi gives Quranic examples of commands that indicate permissibility, not obligation. For 'aks, even if there is no explicit command or prohibition, taklif could still exist because ibahah itself is a form of choice, not a command or prohibition. Thus, the definition of taklif as 'address with command or prohibition' is invalid if ibahah is included as taklif. This example illustrates the critical role of tard and 'aks in evaluating definitions.
