Definition of Istidlal (Inference) by Usuli Scholars
Defines istidlal linguistically and terminologically, citing multiple usuli scholars.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istidlal means actively seeking evidence, not just passively being guided.

Knowing the precise definition of istidlal is essential for understanding the dynamic of debate (jadal) in Islamic jurisprudence. This passage presents two competing technical definitions from major usuli scholars. By analyzing their differences, you will grasp the core of the evidentiary process in usul al-fiqh and munazara.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root

2
Contrast the two technical definitions
Compare al-Baji's definition (al-ihtida' bi al-dalil – being guided by the evidence) with the majority's definition (talab al-dalil – seeking the evidence). Note that 'ihtida' implies passive guidance, while 'talab' implies active search.
This contrast is key to understanding different perspectives on the role of the seeker.
3
Analyze Ibn 'Aqil's analogical argument
Examine how Ibn 'Aqil justifies 'talab al-dalil' by comparing istidlal to other istif'al forms: isti'lam (seeking knowledge), istikhbar (seeking news), etc. This linguistic analogy reinforces that istidlal means actively seeking the dalil.
Ibn 'Aqil's reasoning is a strong argument for the dominant definition.
4
Apply the definition to the jadal context
Understand that istidlal applies to both the questioner (sā'il) demanding evidence and the respondent (mas'ūl) seeking evidence from sources. This dual application is essential for the procedure of munazara.

Definition of transmitted evidence with Baqillani's quote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia of transmitted evidence as defined by al-Baqillani.

Core Idea
Transmitted evidence is language-based signs set up by divine or human convention.

This passage introduces the category of transmitted evidence and quotes al-Baqillani's definition. We will parse this definition to understand its key components: the idea of installation (nasb) and the role of language and convention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find the term that al-Baqillani is defining in the quote. The author introduces it as 'al-sam'iyyat' (the heard things), which is synonymous with transmitted evidence.
2
Extract the genus
What broader category does Baqillani place transmitted evidence in? He says 'they indicate' (tadullu), so they are a type of indicator (dalil). The genus is 'adilla' (evidences).
3
Extract the differentia
What distinguishes transmitted evidence from other evidence? The phrase 'bi-nasb nasib iyyaha adillah' (by the installation of an installer as evidences) and that they are represented by languages and expressions. The differentia includes both the act of installation and the linguistic medium.

Identify the two sources of signification
Baqillani mentions two: 'tawqif min Allah' (directive from Allah) and 'istilah sadara 'an al-ikhtiyar' (convention arising from choice). This shows transmitted evidence can be divine or conventional.
5
Relate to rational evidence
The author contrasts transmitted with rational evidence. Note that transmitted evidence depends on language and installation, whereas rational evidence depends on intellect alone.
This contrast is foundational in usul al-fiqh.

The Two Types of Transmitted Evidence: Religious and Linguistic
Division of transmitted evidence into two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between religious transmitted evidence and linguistic transmitted evidence based on their sources and authority.

Core Idea
Transmitted evidence divides into divine scriptures and Arab customary usage.

After defining transmitted evidence, the author divides it into two subcategories: religious (shar'iyya) and linguistic (lughawiyya). Religious transmitted evidence comes directly from the Lawgiver, while linguistic transmitted evidence is based on the customary usage of the Arabs. Understanding this division is crucial for correctly interpreting Islamic legal sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage states 'fa al-adilla al-naqliyya imma naqliyya shar'iyya... aw naqliyya lughawiyya'. So transmitted evidence is either religious or linguistic.
2
List the sources of religious transmitted evidence
The author lists: the Book (Quran), the Sunnah, Ijma (consensus), and the statement of a Companion (qawl al-sahabi). These are all directly guided to by the Lawgiver.
3
Understand linguistic transmitted evidence
It is based on 'tawadu' al-'arab' (convention of the Arabs) on a familiar custom and specific habit in discourse. This means that Arabic speech is properly understood according to the way Arabs normally use it.
4
Recognize the consequence of ignoring custom
Any understanding contrary to that custom is considered erroneous. This principle is vital for tafsir and fiqh to avoid arbitrary interpretations.
5
Apply the division to a real example
Take the Quranic verse 'wa aqim al-salah' – its linguistic meaning is established by Arab custom, but its legal ruling (obligation of prayer) comes from religious transmitted evidence (the Quran itself).

Inference by Concomitance: Defining al-Mulazama
Author defines the first type of rational proof: inference by necessary concomitance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mulazama technically in usul al-fiqh and identify a real example of necessary entailment between judgments.

Core Idea
Mulazama is the necessary link making one judgment entail another.

In usul al-fiqh, rational arguments come in many forms, but one foundational type is inference by concomitance (al-mulazama). The author first clarifies the linguistic root meaning 'inseparable connection' then gives a precise technical definition: a judgment that, when it occurs, necessitates another judgment with necessary necessity. The classic example of smoke and fire illustrates mutual entailment. Grasp this concept to understand how jurists establish rulings through necessary relationships.

Guided Path
1
Examine the linguistic definition
Linguistically, mulazama means 'impossibility of separation' (imtina' al-infikak). Synonyms luzum and talazum reinforce the idea of inseparability.
This sets the stage that mulazama is not mere correlation but a binding relationship.
2
Parse the technical definition
Technically, mulazama is 'a judgment that necessitates another judgment with necessary necessity (iqtida' daruri).' When the first judgment occurs, the second must occur inevitably.
The Arabic phrase 'لو وقع يقتضي وقوع حكم آخر' expresses the conditional nature of the entailment.
3
Apply the smoke-fire example
By day, seeing smoke necessarily indicates fire; by night, seeing fire necessarily indicates smoke. This demonstrates mutual necessary entailment.
The example shows that mulazama can be bidirectional, but it need not be – one judgment may entail another without the reverse.
4
Connect to usul al-fiqh
In usul, mulazama is used to infer a hidden ruling from a known ruling via a necessary concomitant. For instance, the prohibition of khamr necessarily entails prohibition of nabidh because both share the 'illa (effective cause) of intoxication.
This link is the basis of qiyas (analogical reasoning) – identifying the necessary concomitant (the 'illa) extends the ruling.

The Definition of al-Mulazamah (Mental Concomitance)
Author defines mulazamah linguistically and technically, focusing on mental concomitance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mulazamah linguistically and technically, and distinguish between physical and mental concomitance.

Core Idea
Mulazamah is the inseparable connection between two things, both in reality and in the mind.

In this lesson, we unpack the foundational concept of al-mulazamah (concomitance) as used by usulis. The author begins with a linguistic and technical definition, then narrows to mental concomitance, where conceiving one thing necessarily brings another to mind. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how inference by concomitance works in Islamic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Extract the linguistic definition
Find the phrase 'امتناع انفكاك الشيء عن الشيء' which defines mulazamah as the impossibility of separation. Note that lizum and talazum share the same meaning.
2
Extract the technical definition
Identify the phrase 'كون الحكم مقتضيا للآخر، على معنى أن الحكم بحيث لو وقع يقتضي وقوع حكم آخر اقتضاء ضروريًا'. This establishes a necessary causal link between two rulings, like smoke and fire.
The example of smoke and fire illustrates physical concomitance.
3
Identify the shift to mental concomitance
The author states 'ونريد هنا بالملازمة؛ الملازمة الذهنية' – mental concomitance. This is about conceptual necessity: the conception of one (malzum) entails the conception of the other (lazim), as with blindness and sight.
Mental concomitance differs from physical; it is about ideas, not external objects.
4
Examine al-Tufi's reinforcement
Al-Tufi reiterates that mulazamah is the inseparability of two things, and that inference proceeds from malzum to lazim because a malzum without its lazim is impossible.
This clarifies the inferential use: existence of malzum proves existence of lazim.
5
Understand al-Ghazali's synonym 'al-istidlal bi al-khassiyyah'
Al-Ghazali calls this inference 'by property' (khassiyyah), meaning an inseparable attribute. The existence of a thing indicates its inseparable property.
This terminological variation enriches the concept but does not change its logical core.

Application of al-Mulazamah: Al-Razi's Argument on Quranic Language
Author illustrates mulazamah with al-Razi's argument on Quranic language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's argument as a conditional syllogism (modus tollens) and identify the malzum and lazim.

Core Idea
Al-Razi uses modus tollens: falsify the necessary consequent to refute the antecedent.

This lesson takes the theoretical concept of mulazamah and shows it in action through a concrete argument by Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. He uses inference by concomitance to prove that commonly used metaphorical religious terms have become linguistic realities. By analyzing his argument, you will see how usulis apply conditional reasoning in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Razi's claim (mudda'a)
Al-Razi's chosen position is that using some linguistic words for new religious meanings metaphorically, when those meanings become prevalent, makes the words linguistic realities (haqiqat lughawiyya).
This is the conclusion he aims to prove.
2
Extract the conditional premise (mulazamah)
Al-Razi states: 'If the indication of these words for these meanings were not linguistic (i.e., not Arabic), then the entire Quran would not be Arabic.' This is the conditional: malzum (non-Arabic indication) leads to lazim (Quran not entirely Arabic).
The malzum is the antecedent; the lazim is the consequent.
3
Find the exception (istithna')
Al-Razi proves the lazim is false: he quotes Quranic verses stating the Quran is Arabic ('إنا جعلناه قرآنا عربيا'). Therefore, the consequent (Quran not entirely Arabic) is false.
This is the second part of modus tollens: denial of the consequent.
4
Draw the conclusion (natijah)
Since the lazim is false, the malzum must also be false. Therefore, the words' new meanings are indeed linguistic (Arabic). The conclusion is that the usage is a linguistic reality.
The inference follows the rule: fasad al-lazim yadullu 'ala fasad al-malzum.
5
Verify the logical form
The argument is a valid modus tollens: If P then Q; Not Q; Therefore Not P. P = words' new meanings are not Arabic; Q = Quran is not Arabic. Not Q is shown (Quran is Arabic), so Not P follows (words' new meanings are Arabic).
This formalization confirms the argument's validity.

Mulazama is a mental necessary connection used to infer one thing from another.

Definition of al-Inqita' (Incapacity) in Jadal
Author defines 'al-inqita'' as the end of debate by one party's incapacity, with scholarly definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the different scholarly definitions of al-inqita' and explain why it marks the end of a debate.

Core Idea
Al-inqita' is not just stopping; it is the intellectual incapacity to continue defending one's doctrine.

In the discipline of jadal (dialectical debate), knowing when a debate ends is as important as knowing how it begins. Scholars of usul al-fiqh and kalam defined 'al-inqita'' carefully to distinguish genuine incapacity from arbitrary termination. This lesson examines the definitions of three major scholars and the linguistic roots of the term.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context
Jadal has a beginning and an end. The end is marked by one disputant's inability to continue, called al-inqita'.
2
Analyze Abu Ya'la al-Farra's definition
He defines it as incapacity to reach the intended purpose of the debate, comparing it to a traveler who cannot reach his destination.
This definition emphasizes the goal-oriented nature of debate.
3
Analyze al-Baji's definition
Al-Baji defines it simply as the inability of one debater to correct his own statement.
This focuses on self-correction as a sign of capacity.
4
Analyze Ibn 'Aqil's linguistic analysis
Ibn 'Aqil traces the root meaning of 'inqita'' as separation, distinguishing two types: physical distance (e.g., rope cut) and cessation (e.g., speech interruption).
This shows the term's flexibility in application.

Identifying Fasad al-I'tibar: Two Dialectical Methods
Author explains two methods to recognize fasad al-i'tibar: from explicit texts and from usul principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an analogy (i'tibar) is invalid by recognizing a textual differentiation or a conflict of principles, and respond appropriately in a jadal debate.

Core Idea
Fasad al-i'tibar is exposed by nass or usul — text or principle.

In Islamic dialectical reasoning, not every comparison between rulings is valid. The author presents two clear ways to detect an invalid analogy (fasad al-i'tibar): when the Lawgiver’s explicit text differentiates between the two rulings, or when the rulings rest on opposing principles like expansion and restriction. This lesson equips you to spot such invalid comparisons and to mount or rebut objections in a munazara setting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods
Recognize that fasad al-i'tibar is known either from explicit text (nass) that distinguishes two rulings, or from principles (usul) such as one ruling being based on expansion (tawsi'a) and the other on restriction (tadyiq).
These are complementary, not mutually exclusive.
2
Apply the text-based method

The arguer must either refute the textual evidence or reconcile the rulings.
3
Apply the principles-based method
Check the foundational legal principles (usul) of the two rulings. If one is built on expansion (e.g., allowing qada' in Ramadan) and the other on restriction (e.g., limiting kaffara), analogy between them is invalid due to conflicting underlying principles.

Genus-Establishing Cause: Conditions of Tard and Aks
Author classifies causes and details conditions for genus cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions of tard (consistency) and aks (converse) to evaluate the validity of a causa legis (illa) in a legal argument.

Core Idea
A valid genus-cause must satisfy both tard and aks or it collapses.

In usul al-fiqh and jadal, not every claimed cause (illa) for a ruling is valid. The author distinguishes two types of causes, focusing on the genus-establishing cause which must function like a definition. This lesson equips you with the twin tests—tard (consistency) and aks (converse)—to assess whether a cause truly grounds a ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimed cause and ruling
Find where the author or debater states that X is the cause for ruling Y. For example, 'partnership is the cause for preemption'.
2
Check tard: Does the ruling always follow the cause?
Tard requires that whenever the cause exists, the ruling exists. If you find a case where the cause is present but the ruling is absent, tard is broken.
Example from the passage: If partnership exists but no preemption, the cause fails.
3
Check aks: Does the ruling never occur without the cause?
Aks requires that whenever the ruling exists, the cause also exists. If you find a case where the ruling exists without the cause, aks is broken.
Example: Apostasy claimed as cause for permitting blood; but blood is also permitted through retaliation, so aks fails.
4
Assess qualification needs if not exclusive
If the ruling can occur in other classes, the cause must be restricted (e.g., specifying the four riba items). Otherwise, it is invalid.
The author uses the example of riba: saying 'cause is sustenance' without limiting to four items would be refuted by gold and silver.

Commentary

14 - أما الأساس الثالث من بين أسس المنهج الجدلي عند الأصوليين، وهو الاستدلال، فالمراد به إقامة الدليل على صحة الدعوى، وقد سماه بعض الأصوليين «التقريب»، وعرفوه بأنه سوق الدليل على وجه يستلزم المطلوب . .» والأدلة التي استخدمها الأصوليون في الاستدلال لآرائهم هي إما أدلة نقلية من الكتاب أو السنة أو الإجماع أو عمل الصحابة أو مألوف العرب في الخطاب، أو أدلة عقلية وقد ذكرت منها في هذا البحث مقرونة بأمثلتها التطبيقية من واقع البحث الأصولي : الملازمة، والسبر والتقسيم وقياس الأولى، وقياس المختلف فيه على المتفق عليه وقياس التمثيل والاستدلال بحكم الكل على الجزء، وقياس العكس.
As for the third foundation among the foundations of the dialectical method according to the usulis, which is istidlal (reasoning), what is meant by it is the establishment of evidence for the correctness of the claim. Some usulis have called it 'taqrib' (approximation) and defined it as 'the driving of evidence in a manner that entails the required result'... And the evidences that the usulis used in reasoning for their opinions are either transmitted evidences from the Book, the Sunnah, consensus, the practice of the Companions, or the customary usage of the Arabs in discourse, or rational evidences which I have mentioned in this research accompanied by their practical examples from the reality of usul research: entailment (mulāzama), survey and division (sabr wa taqsīm), analogy of the superior (qiyās al-awla), analogy of the disputed upon the agreed (qiyās al-mukhtalaf fīhi ʿalā al-muttafaq ʿalayhi), analogical reasoning (qiyās al-tamthīl), reasoning from the ruling of the whole to the part (al-istidlāl bi ḥukm al-kull ʿalā al-juzʾ), and inverse analogy (qiyās al-ʿaks).

Commentary
This passage identifies istidlal as the third foundation of the dialectical method. It defines istidlal as presenting evidence that proves the claim, and notes it is also called taqrib. Then it categorizes evidences into transmitted (naqliyya: Quran, Sunnah, ijma', practice of Companions, Arab customary usage) and rational (ʿaqliyya). For rational evidences, it lists seven types that the author has included in his research: entailment, survey and division, analogy of the superior, analogy of the disputed upon the agreed, analogical reasoning, reasoning from whole to part, and inverse analogy. This is a classification of argument types used by usulis in jadal.
