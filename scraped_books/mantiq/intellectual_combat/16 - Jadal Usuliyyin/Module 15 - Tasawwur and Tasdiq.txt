Classification of Knowledge: Certain and Uncertain Types
Classifies knowledge into certain and uncertain types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the types of belief (mutawatirat, tajribiyyat, hadsiyyat, taqlid, jahl, shakk, zann, wahm) and classify any given epistemological claim into its correct category.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either certain (mutawatirat, tajribiyyat, hadsiyyat) or uncertain (taqlid, jahl, shakk, zann, wahm).

In Islamic epistemology, scholars distinguish between certain knowledge (yaqin) and various degrees of uncertainty. This passage from an usul al-fiqh text categorises types of belief based on their source and certainty. You will learn to parse each category and apply it to real juristic arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two broad divisions
The passage first separates knowledge that arises 'for a cause' (li-mujib) from that which does not. Locate the phrase 'ما يكون لموجب' and 'ما لا يكون لموجب' in the Arabic.
This is the primary division between justified and non-justified beliefs.
2
Categorise certain knowledge under 'li-mujib'
Under 'for a cause', note three subcategories: mutawatirat (from reason alone), tajribiyyat (from senses and reason), and hadsiyyat (intuition). These produce certainty.
These are derived from 'senses and reason' or 'reason' as sources.
3
Examine uncertain belief types under 'la li-mujib'
First, identify non-justified belief (i'tiqad al-muqallid). Then separate certain but false belief (jahl) from non-certain belief.
Jahl is certain but does not conform to reality; it is still a type of belief.
4
Distinguish among shakk, zann, and wahm
For non-certain belief, the passage describes hesitation between two sides. If equal, it is shakk (doubt). If one side preponderates, the preponderant is zann (conjecture) and the preponderated is wahm (illusion).
These are degrees of probability, not certainty.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider the ruling on nabidh (date wine). Is its prohibition based on mutawatirat (certain) or on zann (conjectural)? Determining this affects the strength of the legal ruling.
This shows how the classification directly impacts juristic reasoning.

Commentary
This passage classifies types of knowledge and belief. It begins by naming certain knowledge that comes from reason alone (mutawatirat) or from senses and reason (tajribiyyat and hadsiyyat). Then it contrasts these with beliefs not based on proof (muqallid's belief), which are not certain. Next, it distinguishes certain but false belief (jahl) from uncertain belief. Uncertain belief is divided into equal hesitation (shakk), preponderant (zann), and preponderated (wahm). The author notes that examples of these categories are abundant in usul al-fiqh works.

The Quran as the Foundation of All Transmitted Evidence
Author explains that all transmitted religious evidence traces back to the Quran, citing Imam al-Haramayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the epistemological chain from any transmitted Islamic evidence back to the Quran, identifying the roles of miracles, mutawatir reports, and Ijma.

Core Idea
All transmitted religious evidence ultimately traces back to the Quran as its source of authority.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational principle in Islamic epistemology: that every transmitted evidence (sam'iyyat) is ultimately rooted in the Quran. The author, citing Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni, explains how the Quran itself establishes the authority of the Sunnah, Ijma, solitary reports, and qiyas. Understanding this chain is crucial for any student of usul al-fiqh or kalam, as it grounds all secondary proofs in the primary revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
The author asserts that all naqli (transmitted) evidences trace back to the Quran. This is the thesis being argued.
The claim is directly stated in the first sentence.
2
Examine Juwayni's chain of reasoning
Juwayni starts with the speech of Allah, then shows that the Prophet's truthfulness is confirmed by miracles, which rely on customary knowledge. This establishes the foundational chain: Quran → Prophet → miracle → custom → certainty.
Note that miracles are linked to knowledge via 'ittirād al-ʿurf' (continuity of custom).
3
Trace the hierarchy of evidences
Juwayni orders the evidences: Book is the origin; Sunnah is received from the Book; Ijma is based on a mutawatir report; solitary reports and qiyas depend on mutawatir or Ijma. Each step relies on the previous.
Ijma is not an independent proof but is shored up by a mutawatir report.
4
Recognize the role of Arabic linguistic custom
The author adds that understanding the Quran and Sunnah requires familiarity with 'ma'luf al-ʿarab fī al-khiṭāb' (customary Arabic discourse). This is essential for all linguistic transmitted evidence.
This highlights the integration of language and revelation.

Commentary
This passage delves into the epistemological foundation of religious transmitted evidence, asserting that all such evidence ultimately traces back to the Quran. The author cites Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni to elaborate this point. Juwayni explains that the speech of Allah (kalam Allah) is the ultimate source; the Prophet's statement is based on it, and the Prophet's truthfulness is confirmed by miracles. The miracle serves as a sign that verifies the prophet's claim, relying on the customary linkage between signs and knowledge. Juwayni then discusses the hierarchy: the Book is the origin, the Sunnah receives from it, and Ijma is linked to the text of the Book, though it is not itself at the level of explicit text. Rather, Ijma derives its authority from a mutawatir report, as the impossibility of collusion among all scholars points to a certain underlying report. Even solitary reports (khabar al-wahid) depend on mutawatir or Ijma. Thus, all sam'iyyat are anchored in the Quran. Finally, the author notes the importance of Arabic linguistic custom, emphasizing that revelation came in accordance with that custom, so understanding it requires mastery of Arabic grammar and usage. This reinforces the linguistic transmitted evidence discussed earlier.
