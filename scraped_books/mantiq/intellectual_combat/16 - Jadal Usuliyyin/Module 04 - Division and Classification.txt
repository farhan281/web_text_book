The Five Topics of Jadal in Istidlal with Other Evidences
Author enumerates five jadal investigations and the conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and distinguish the five investigations of jadal in istidlal with legal evidences beyond the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Five sources of legal reasoning beyond Qur'an and Sunnah each have their own jadal rules.

This passage maps out the structure of the author's research on jadal (dialectical reasoning) applied to the remaining legal evidences that supplement the Qur'an and Sunnah. The author divides the topic into five distinct investigations, each focused on a specific source: consensus, Companion statements, istishab, the implication of discourse (fahwa al-khitab/mafhum al-muwafaqa), and the indication of discourse (dalil al-khitab/mafhum al-mukhalafa). Understanding this classification is the first step to mastering how jadal operates across different types of evidence in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching category
The passage begins by stating 'Reasoning with the remaining legal evidences' – that is, all proofs besides the Qur'an and mutawatir Sunnah. Note that the author does not cover jadal in Qur'an and Sunnah here, only these subsidiary evidences.
Think of this as a niche area within usul al-fiqh: how dialectical reasoning applies to secondary proofs.
2
List the five investigations in order
The author enumerates five investigations (mabahith). Extract each one: (1) consensus, (2) Companion's statement, (3) istishab (presumption of continuity), (4) implication of discourse (fahwa al-khitab / mafhum al-muwafaqa), (5) indication of discourse (dalil al-khitab / mafhum al-mukhalafa). Write them down in sequence.
The Arabic terms are provided; note the synonym pairs in parentheses.
3
Differentiate between the two discourse-based investigations
The fourth investigation uses 'fahwa al-khitab' (implication of discourse), which is the concept of harmony: what is implied by the text as logically included. The fifth uses 'dalil al-khitab' (indication of discourse), which is the concept of divergence: what the text implies by its restriction. Understand that these are two different modes of inferring from a text beyond its literal words.
In some usul works, 'fahwa al-khitab' equals mafhum al-muwafaqa, and 'dalil al-khitab' equals mafhum al-mukhalafa.
4
Note the conclusion as a separate element
After the five investigations, the author mentions a conclusion (khatima) that summarises the results and opens future perspectives. This indicates the book has a final synthesising section beyond the five topics.
The conclusion is not a sixth investigation; it is a reflective wrap-up.

Differentiating Rulings for Lost Camels and Sheep: The Method of Tafrīq
The author provides a hadith where the Prophet gives different rulings for lost camels and sheep, distinguishing them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the underlying attribute that causes differentiation (tafrīq) between two seemingly similar cases from a Prophetic hadith.

Core Idea
Tafrīq reveals that apparent similarity hides real difference when attributes are examined.

In this hadith, the Prophet distinguishes between a lost camel and a lost sheep, giving different rulings for each. This is not arbitrary—it is based on the inherent characteristics of each animal. As a tool in jadal, tafrīq allows a jurist to defend a distinction between two cases that opponents might claim are identical. Studying this example will teach you how to locate the differentiating attribute that justifies a ruling difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Recognize that the cases are a lost camel and a lost sheep. Both are lost animals, but the Prophet gives different rulings.
2
Extract the ruling for each case
For the sheep: 'it is for you, or for your brother, or for the wolf' meaning the finder can take it. For the camel: 'what have you to do with it?' meaning leave it alone.
3
Identify the attribute that differs
The Prophet mentions the camel's self-sufficiency: it has water vessel, shoes, finds water and eats trees. The sheep is vulnerable to wolves and cannot survive alone. So the differentiating attribute is the degree of self-sufficiency.
4
Construct the tafrīq argument
The opponent claims that both cases are similar and should have the same ruling. The jurist counters by pointing out the differing attribute (self-sufficiency) that justifies different rulings.

Naqli Evidences: Types and Definition
Author classifies naqli evidences into shar'i and linguistic, with al-Baqillani's definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between naqli and aqli evidences, explain al-Baqillani's definition of sam'iyyat, and differentiate between shar'i and lughawi subtypes.

Core Idea
Textual evidences are either legal or linguistic, both rooted in divine appointment or Arab custom.

Islamic legal reasoning begins with a clear classification of evidences. The author first divides all evidences into textual (naqli) and rational (aqli). Then, drawing on al-Baqillani, he defines naqli as auditory proofs set up by an establisher through divine appointment or convention. This lesson unpacks that definition and the subsequent subdivision into legal (shar'i) and linguistic (lughawi) naqli.

Guided Path
1
Identify the master division
Read the first sentence: evidences are of two types, naqliyya and aqliyya. Note that naqliyya are also called sam'iyyat (auditory proofs).
This overarching division is foundational in usul al-fiqh.
2
Extract al-Baqillani's definition
Locate the sentence starting with 'يعرفها الباقلاني'. Break it down: sam'iyyat are 'evidence by being set up as indicators by an establisher' and are represented by languages/expressions indicating meanings through tawqif (divine appointment) or istilah (convention).
The definition includes both divine and human elements.
3
Analyze the components of the definition
Identify the 'nassib' (establisher), the 'lugat' and 'ibarat' (languages/expressions), and the two sources: tawqif and istilah. Understand that tawqif originates from God, istilah from human choice.
Al-Baqillani's definition covers both revealed and conventional language.
4
Recognize the subdivision
After the definition, the author splits naqli into shar'i (legal) and lughawi (linguistic). Shar'i includes the Book, Sunnah, Ijma', and qawl al-sahabi. Lughawi relies on Arab custom.
Shar'i evidences are directly from the Lawgiver; lughawi are based on linguistic convention.
5
Classify examples
Given an evidence, decide whether it is shar'i (e.g., a Quranic verse) or lughawi (e.g., the meaning of a word based on Arab usage). Test with a few examples.
This classification affects how jurists prioritize evidence.

The evidences used in the sciences of Islamic law are of two types: textual (naqliyya) and rational (aqliyya). As for textual evidences, they are what are known as sam'iyyat (auditory proofs). Al-Baqillani defines them, saying: 'The sam'iyyat are evidence by being set up as indicators by an establisher; they are represented by languages and expressions indicating meanings through an appointment (tawqif) from God or through convention (istilah) arising from choice.' But the speech of God is not established for us except through one whose truthfulness is established by a miracle, when he reports the speech of God. So the final recourse of the sam'iyyat is to the speech of God, received from the Messenger of God, and the basis for trust in receiving from him is the establishment of his truthfulness, and the indicator of his truthfulness is the miracle. The miracle indicates in the manner of its occurrence as a confirmation of the statement, and that is based on the continuous custom in the occurrence of indications for knowledge, and the establishment of knowledge of the principle of speech belonging to God—it indicates.'

Tracing Shar'i Evidences to the Quran
Author explains that all shar'i naqli evidences trace back to the Quran, with al-Juwayni's epistemological chain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Juwayni's epistemological chain from naqli evidences back to the Quran and explain the role of miracles in establishing prophetic authority.

Core Idea
The Quran is the ultimate source; the authority of all other shar'i evidences depends on it.

After classifying naqli evidences, the author makes a crucial claim: all legal textual evidences ultimately trace back to the Quran. He supports this by quoting Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwayni, who lays out the epistemological chain. This lesson unpacks that chain, showing how the Quran's authority is transmitted through the Prophet's miracles and the continuous custom of cause-and-effect relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Read the sentence starting with 'وترجع الأدلة النقلية الشرعية في أصولها جميعا إلى القرآن الكريم'. This is the central thesis: the Quran is the origin of all shar'i naqli.
This claim asserts the Quran's primacy over other sources.
2
Locate al-Juwayni's argument
Al-Juwayni's quote begins with 'الأصل في السمعيات كلام الله تعالى'. Break down his chain: the origin is God's speech (Quran). It is transmitted via the Prophet, whose truthfulness is established by miracles. The miracle indicates divine confirmation through continuous custom.
The chain has four links: Quran → Prophet → miracle → continuous custom.
3
Understand the role of the miracle
The miracle (mu'jiza) is not just any event; it serves as a sign from God that the Prophet is truthful. Al-Juwayni says the miracle 'indicates in the manner of its occurrence as a confirmation of the statement'.
Miracles are epistemological proofs in kalam.
4
Recognize the foundation in custom
The final link is 'continuous custom in the occurrence of indications for knowledge'. This refers to the idea that we infer causation from regular patterns. So the knowledge of the Quran's origin is based on a customary connection between miracles and truthfulness.
This is an epistemological principle used by mutakallimun.

The Four Sections of Jadal Etiquette According to the Usuliyyun
Author lists the four topics of the chapter on jadal etiquette.

Core Idea
Jadal etiquette covers the debater's character, relationship, subject, and style.

Jadal (dialectic) is a disciplined practice in Islamic sciences, used in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and tafsir. The usuliyyun (scholars of usul al-fiqh) developed a comprehensive etiquette (adab) for debate, dividing it into four essential sections. This lesson unpacks those sections and shows how they apply to real scholarly debates.

1

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Shirazi's classification to determine whether qiyas is permissible in a given customary matter based on the presence of an indicator (amara).

Core Idea
Qiyas in customary matters depends on an indicator, not on the matter being customary per se.

This passage presents al-Shirazi's departure from the majority usuli position. While most scholars reject qiyas in customary matters due to variability, al-Shirazi distinguishes matters with an indicator (amara) from those without. Understanding this sharpens your ability to evaluate analogical arguments in fiqh, especially in contentious bioethical issues like menstruation during pregnancy.

المطلب الثالث : الاعتراض على الاستدلال بالقياس فيما طريقه العادة والتجربة

الاتجاه الغالب عند الأصوليين أن القياس لا يجري في الأمور العادية، ولا يكون حجة فيها، لأن هذه الأمور تختلف باختلاف الأشخاص والأحوال والأزمان، فليس لها ضوابط يمكنه تحققه في غيرها والقياس مبني على وجود الجامع بين المقيس والمقيس عليه، فامتنع القياس في العادات لأجل ذلك(2) وقد ذهب الإمام أبو إسحاق الشيرازي إلى التفصيل في المسألة، فقال: «أما ما طريقه العادة والخلقة والجبلة فعلى ضربين : ضرب عليه أمارة وضرب لا أمارة عليه .

فأما الضرب الذي عليه أمارة، فيجوز إثباته بالقياس، وذلك مثل الشعر هل تحل فيه الروح أم لا ؟ ومثل الحامل هل تحيض أم لا؟ فإن على هاتين المسألتين أمارة، ألا ترى أنا في مسألة الشعر والعظم نستدل بالنماء والاتصال ونقيس على سائر الأعضاء وهم يقيسون على أغصان الشجر من حيث أنه لا يحس ولا يألم؟ فكل واحد منا يتعلق في ذلك بأمارة دالة على الحكم، وهكذا نحن نستدل مسألة في الحامل أنها تحيض ؛ أن الحمل لو كان يمنع دم الحيض لمنع دم الاستحاضة، ألا ترى أن الصغر لما منع أحدهما منع الآخر، فكذلك الكبر والرضاع لما لم يمنع أحدهما لم يمنع الآخر؟ وهم يقولون : لو كان هذا دم حيض لانقطعت به العدة وحرم الطلاق. وأما الضرب الذي لا أمارة عليه، وذلك كإثبات أقل الحيض وأكثره فما أشبه ذلك مما طريقه العادة والخلقة ولا أمارة عليه فلا مدخل للقياس فيه» (1) .
The Third Request: Objection to using qiyas in matters whose way is custom and experience. The predominant orientation among the usuliyyin is that qiyas does not apply to customary matters, nor is it a proof in them, because these matters differ according to individuals, conditions, and times, so they have no controls that can be realized in other cases, and qiyas is based on the existence of a common factor between the analogized and the analogized-upon, so qiyas is prevented in customs for that reason. And Imam Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi went into detail on the issue, saying: 'As for matters whose way is custom, natural constitution, and innate nature, they are of two types: a type that has an indicator and a type that has no indicator. As for the type that has an indicator, it is permissible to prove it by qiyas, such as: does hair have a soul or not? And such as: can a pregnant woman menstruate or not? For these two issues have an indicator. Do you not see that in the issue of hair and bone we infer by growth and connection and analogize to the rest of the limbs, and they analogize to tree branches because it does not feel or pain? So each of us relies on an indicator indicating the ruling. And likewise we infer in the issue of the pregnant woman that she menstruates: that if pregnancy prevented menstrual blood, it would prevent istihada blood. Do you not see that when youth prevented one of them, it prevented the other, and likewise when old age and breastfeeding did not prevent one, they did not prevent the other? And they say: if this were menstrual blood, the waiting period would be interrupted by it and divorce would be prohibited. As for the type that has no indicator, such as proving the minimum and maximum of menstruation and what resembles that from matters whose way is custom and natural constitution and for which there is no indicator, then qiyas has no entry into it.'

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical discussion of the scope of qiyas (analogical reasoning) in customary matters. It begins by stating the majority view of the usuliyyin: qiyas does not apply to matters of custom and experience because these lack fixed controls and vary across persons, times, and conditions. Qiyas requires a common factor (jami') between the original and new case, which is absent in such variable matters. However, Imam Abu Ishaq al-Shirazi offers a nuanced distinction. He divides customary matters into two types: those with an indicator (amara) and those without. The presence of an indicator makes qiyas permissible. He provides two examples: (1) Whether hair has a soul (ruh) – both parties use indicators like growth and sensation. (2) Whether a pregnant woman can menstruate – the indicator is that pregnancy does not prevent istihada (non-menstrual bleeding), so by analogy it should not prevent hayd (menstrual blood) either. He further argues that if youth (sighar) prevented both types of blood, and old age and breastfeeding did not prevent either, then consistency requires that the same principle apply. The opposing view (that it would interrupt the waiting period and prohibit divorce) is noted. The second type, without an indicator (e.g., establishing the minimum or maximum duration of menstruation), cannot be settled by qiyas. This classification became influential in later usul works.
