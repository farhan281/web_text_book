Two Pillars of Jadal Research: History and Induction
Author explains his two methodological pillars: historical tracing and inductive analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the historical method (al-ta'rikh) from the inductive method (al-istiqra') as used in Islamic jadal research, and explain why both are necessary.

Core Idea
Jadal research requires history to find sources and induction to extract theory.

When studying a complex topic like jadal (dialectical reasoning) in Islamic thought, a researcher must reconstruct the tradition from scattered sources. The author of this passage proposes two complementary methods: historical tracing to locate the sources, and inductive analysis to derive theoretical principles. This lesson unpacks those methods so you can see how a rigorous Islamic research methodology works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods named by the author
The author explicitly lists two foundations: al-ta'rikh (historical method) and al-istiqra' (inductive method). Note the Arabic terms and their order.
2
Understand the role of the historical method
The author uses history to trace the roots of jadal: he searches history books, biographies, and encyclopedias to find works on jadal, their authors, and historical periods. This establishes the source base.
Think of this as the 'data collection' phase.
3
Understand the role of the inductive method
Induction is used more for the theoretical side. The author surveys the usul al-fiqh literature to extract rules and patterns of jadal. This builds the theoretical framework.
Induction here means generalizing from multiple instances found in texts.
4
Compare the two methods
History gives the 'who, when, where'; induction gives the 'what and why'. Both are needed: history without induction is mere listing; induction without history lacks context.

Methodological Tools: Induction, Analysis, and Balance
Author explains his methodological approach of theoretical extraction, inductive collection, analytical synthesis, and balancing theory with practice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three core methodological components (induction, analysis, balance) used in studying jadal among usuliyyin.

Core Idea
The rigorous study of jadal requires a balance of theory and practice through induction and analysis.

This passage introduces the author's own methodology for researching the dialectical method (jadal) among usuliyyin. He reveals that he extracted theoretical foundations from actual usul al-fiqh works, applied them practically, and used induction, analysis, and balance as guiding principles. Understanding this approach is essential for anyone seeking to critically engage with classical Islamic dialectical texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Author's Sources
Note that the author extracts theoretical foundations from the actual works of usuliyyin (scholars of usul al-fiqh). He also applies them practically, but to a lesser extent because the usuliyyin themselves provided many applications.
This shows the interplay between theory and practice from primary sources.
2
Understand the Role of Induction (Istiqra')
Induction here is not mere counting; it is a systematic compilation and re-presentation of the material gathered from usul works. The author uses induction as a method to collect and organize the applied instances of jadal.
Istiqra' is a common term in Islamic methodology, meaning extensive survey.
3
Recognize Analysis (Tahlil) as the Natural Outcome
After induction, analysis follows naturally. The author analyzes the collected material to identify common elements and thereby derives theoretical categories. This is how he arrived at the theoretical headings for his research.
Analysis transforms raw compiled data into conceptual frameworks.
4
Appreciate the Balance (Tawazun) Between Theory and Practice
The author maintains that balance is a key methodological principle governing the entire research. He strives to ensure that neither the theoretical nor the practical side dominates, while acknowledging that scarcity of material sometimes challenges this balance.
Balance is a conscious effort, not a given; it requires constant adjustment.

Essential Elements of Islamic Research Methodology
Author outlines his method for writing the thesis: proper Quranic citation, hadith verification, biographical translation, and scholarly documentation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the four essential elements of Islamic research methodology as outlined in the author's approach: takhrij, tarjamah, and tawthiq.

Core Idea
Proper citation and biographical verification are the backbone of credible Islamic scholarship.

In this passage, the author lays out his methodological commitments for writing his thesis. These are not trivial; they are the tools that ensure the work is trustworthy and meets the standards of the Islamic scholarly tradition. You will learn to recognize each element, understand its purpose, and see how it is applied in real research.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between Quranic and hadith citation requirements
Read the first two elements (a and b). Note that for Quranic verses, the author requires correct writing with diacritics and referencing to surah and verse. For hadith, he also requires correct writing and referencing to the books of Sunnah using the international edition numbering. Identify the common principle (takhrij) and the specific tools (CD-ROM for hadith).
The CD-ROM mentioned is a historical tool; today digital databases serve the same purpose.
2
Understand the meaning of takhrij
Takhrij is the process of locating a Quranic verse or hadith in its original source. For Quran, that means surah and verse; for hadith, it means the specific hadith collection and hadith number. This is not the same as tafsir or commentary.
The term takhrij is stable across Islamic sciences; it always means source attribution.
3
Recognize the role of biographical documentation (tarjamah)
Read element (c). The author provides biographies for most scholars mentioned, using classical sources when possible. He omits biographies only for very famous figures like Companions. This practice ensures that readers can verify the credibility of cited authorities.
Tarjamah here means biography, not translation. Be alert to this shift in meaning.
4
Appreciate the overarching principle of scholarly documentation (tawthiq)
Read element (d). The author stresses rigorous documentation for all information. This is the meta-principle that ties the other elements together: every claim must be traceable to a reliable source.
Tawthiq is the foundation of academic integrity in Islamic studies.

The First Section: Jadal in the Arabic Language
Just as is the case with most Arabic words, the term 'jadal' has a linguistic root to which it returns, and this root has its original meaning that it implies, and also has words derived from it, each taking its own meaning. These derived words from a single root may differ in meanings, but there is – and this is predominant – a common factor that usually collects all the meanings, which is the original meaning implied by the linguistic root. Such is the case with the root (j-d-l) that we are investigating its linguistic meaning.

The First Subsection: The Linguistic Root of the Root (j-d-l) and Its Uses in the Arabic Language
The root (j, d, l) in the Arabic language implies the meaning of firmness, strength, and intensity in something, with harmony and integration between its parts and components, such that each performs its function automatically without defect or deficiency. Ibn Faris said: 'The jim, dal, and lam are one root, which is of the category of the firmness of a thing while it has a continuity.' This original meaning of the root is recorded in every meaning of the words derived from this root, which are used in Arabic discourse. From these words, we can mention the following:

1. al-Jadl: Its meaning is intensity of twisting. You say, 'jadaltu al-habla ajduluhu jadlan' if you tightened its twisting and twisted it firmly. From this, the reins of the she-camel are called 'al-jadil' – it is a rope twisted from leather or hair that is around the neck of the camel or she-camel, and its plural is 'judul'. Also, the male of livestock is called 'jadil'.

2. al-Jadwal: It is a small river, and it is extended, and its water is stronger in the cohesion of its parts than the spread-out flowing water. Its plural is 'jadawil'.

3. al-Majdul: It is the man who is slender in build without emaciation, in other words: delicate in limbs, firmly twisted. The original meaning of this root is twisting and joining one thing to another and making it firm.

4. al-Jadil: It is the boy when he becomes strong, and the jadil from the offspring of the she-camel is the one that has grown strong and walks with its mother.

5. al-Judul: They are the limbs, their singular is 'jadl'. The 'judul' of a person are the bones of the arms and legs. It is said: a shank majdulah (firmly twisted) and a forearm ajdal (similarly).

Commentary
This passage presents the three essential conditions for the validity of rational inquiry (nazar) according to the Islamic legal theorists (usuliyyun). The author begins by stating that without these conditions, rational inquiry fails to produce reliable conclusions and may even lead to intellectual deviation. The first condition is that the reasoner (al-nazir) must be 'complete in faculty' (kamil al-alah). This means he must be mature ('aqil baligh) and possess the necessary intellectual capacities, including knowledge of the Arabic language, the deeper meanings of Sharia, and the sources of legal rulings. The author supports this with quotations: al-Baqillani emphasizes 'completeness of intellect', and al-Shirazi provides a powerful analogy of a blind man with healthy eyes but no light—the faculty of sight is missing. Similarly, without the proper intellectual faculty, inquiry is fruitless. The second condition is that inquiry must be based on evidence (dalil), not on specious argument (shubha). Evidence can be textual (nass), rational ('aql), or empirical (tajriba insaniyya). Specious arguments often arise from fleeting thoughts, imitation, or mere habit. Al-Shirazi warns that if inquiry mistakenly follows a shubha instead of a dalil, the seeker will miss the goal entirely. The third condition is that the reasoner must understand the modes of indication (wujuh dalalat al-adilla) of the evidence. Because a single piece of evidence can yield multiple meanings with varying strengths, the reasoner must know which indication is stronger and give it priority. Ignorance of this leads to error. Furthermore, evidences themselves have different degrees of authority (hujiyya); some must be given precedence over others. The reasoner must arrange them correctly, apply methods of tarjih (preponderance) when they conflict, and then draw the proper inferences. Al-Shirazi's final quote summarizes that evidence must be 'fulfilled with its conditions.' For example, in usul al-fiqh, a clear textual evidence (nass qat'i) is given precedence over a speculative one (zanni), and a rational proof cannot override a definitive text. Thus, these three conditions ensure that reasoning is sound and leads to true knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents the three essential conditions for the validity of rational inquiry (nazar) according to the Islamic legal theorists (usuliyyun). The author begins by stating that without these conditions, rational inquiry fails to produce reliable conclusions and may even lead to intellectual deviation. Condition 3: Understanding the Modes of Indication of Evidences
Third condition: reasoner must perceive modes of indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the mode of indication (wajh dalālah) of a given evidence and prioritize stronger indications over weaker ones.

Core Idea
Evidence speaks in multiple voices; knowing which is strongest is the key to sound inference.

The third condition for sound reasoning is that the reasoner must understand the modes of indication (wujūh dalālat al-adillah) of the evidences. Even if the inquiry is based on proper evidence, the reasoner can still err if he misinterprets its strength or gives precedence to a weaker indication over a stronger one. Al-Bāqillānī and al-Shīrāzī emphasize that the reasoner must know not only that the evidence is present, but also how it indicates its meaning and the hierarchy of its authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third condition
Locate in the Arabic: 'ج - أن يدرك وجوه دلالة الأدلة'. Understand that 'wujūh dalālah' refers to the various ways an evidence can indicate its meaning.
2
Understand the need for this condition
The passage states that evidence often bears more than one meaning, and some indications are stronger than others. Without knowing this, the reasoner may favor a weak indication and err.
3
Analyze al-Bāqillānī's statement
Read: 'أن يكون عالما بحصول الدليل، وبالوجه الذي لحصوله عليه صار دليلا ومتعلقا بمدلوله'. What two things must the reasoner know?
He must know the occurrence of the evidence and the aspect by which it becomes evidence connected to its meaning.
4
Understand the ranking of evidences
Evidence has different levels of authority (ḥujiyya). The reasoner must give precedence to stronger evidence over weaker, and delay what must be delayed. Al-Shīrāzī says: 'يقدم ما يجب تقديمه ويؤخر ما يجب تأخيره'.
5
Apply to a conflict scenario
Suppose a clear Qur'anic verse (qat'ī al-dalālah) conflicts with a solitary hadith (zannī al-thubūt). How should the reasoner prioritize based on modes of indication?
The verse has stronger indication and should be given precedence over the hadith.

Ordering Evidences and Avoiding Errors in Reasoning
Author enumerates conditions for valid logical investigation from usul al-fiqh scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify causes of error in nazar, including shubha, improper ordering, and ignoring evidence attributes, and apply al-Sam'ani's fourth condition that reasoning be used only for acquired knowledge.

Core Idea
Error arises from fallacy, wrong order, ignorance of evidence, or wasting effort on self-evident truths.

Al-Sam'ani and others detail that even with correct evidence, errors occur if evidence is misplaced, its attributes are unknown, or a fallacy is mistaken for evidence. This lesson covers these pitfalls and the fourth condition that nazar should be limited to acquired knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish evidence from fallacy
Check if the argument is based on genuine evidence (dalil) or a fallacy (shubha) by verifying that it truly leads to knowledge and is not a specious resemblance.
The passage warns that looking at a shubha is a primary cause of error.
2
Rank multiple evidences
When multiple evidences are present, arrange them by strength according to usul al-fiqh hierarchy (e.g., Qur'an > Sunnah > Ijma' > Qiyas).
Giving priority to weaker evidence over stronger is a major error.
3
Verify evidence attributes
Ensure that the evidence possesses all necessary attributes (e.g., authenticity, clarity) to serve as a valid proof for the ruling.
Ignorance of attributes or adding a corrupting attribute leads to error.
4
Assess if the question requires reasoning
Confirm that the issue is not self-evident (daruri) but requires acquired knowledge (iktisabi); otherwise, reasoning is wasted.
Al-Sam'ani's fourth condition: nazar should be used only for non-necessary knowledge.

Al-Juwaynī's Method of Sibr wa-Taqsīm
Al-Juwaynī explains his method of sibr wa-taqsīm for defining ʿilm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Juwaynī's method of sibr wa-taqsīm to identify the essence of a concept by systematically eliminating alternatives.

Core Idea
Elimination (sibr wa-taqsīm) reveals essence by excluding what it is not.

Al-Juwaynī's approach to defining knowledge was not to supply a definition upfront, but to survey all related mental states and systematically eliminate those that were not knowledge. This method, called sibr wa-taqsīm, is a logical tool for conceptual clarification. In this lesson, you will walk through his process and learn to apply it to any contested concept in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
List all candidates
Identify all possible options that could be the concept under investigation. Al-Juwaynī starts with mental states: ignorance (jahl), doubt (shakk), opinion (ẓann), blind acceptance (taqlīd), and knowledge (ʿilm).
In other contexts, list the relevant alternatives exhaustively.
2
Eliminate the obviously different
Remove those that are clearly not the concept. Ignorance (jahl) is a belief contrary to reality, so it cannot be knowledge. Doubt (shakk) wavers equally between two options, and opinion (ẓann) falls short of certainty; knowledge is firm and certain, so they are eliminated.
Ensure the basis for elimination is shared with the concept's essential features.
3
Compare with the nearest cousin
The remaining candidate (taqlīd) shares many features with knowledge: both are firm beliefs that correspond to reality. Al-Juwaynī notes that the muqallid's belief is firm and correct, yet it is not knowledge. The difference must be isolated.
The nearest cousin is the hardest to distinguish, so careful analysis is required.
4
Isolate the essential element
Determine what distinguishes the true concept from its nearest cousin. Here, knowledge is based on evidence or justification (ʿilm requires daleel), while taqlīd lacks that. This distinction, not the belief itself, is the differentia.
This step often requires deeper reflection on the nature of the concept.
5
Capture the essence
If possible, formulate a definition using the essence; otherwise, accept the intuitive grasp. Al-Juwaynī says a verbal definition is helpful but not necessary. The goal is to internally grasp the essence.
Definition (hadd) is a tool, not the end goal.

Commentary
This passage details al-Juwaynī's method for arriving at the essence of ʿilm without a strict definition. He calls it 'sibr wa-taqsīm' (search and division). First, he lists all possible mental states: ignorance (jahl), knowledge (ʿilm), doubt (shakk), opinion (ẓann), and taqlīd (blind acceptance). The difference between ʿilm and taqlīd is not in the belief itself but in the justification. By eliminating all false or non-certain states, what remains is ʿilm: a firm, certain belief that matches reality and is based on evidence. If one can formulate a verbal definition using this essence, fine; if not, the intuitive grasp suffices. This shows a pragmatic approach: definition is a tool, not an end.

Introduction: Chapter Two - Applications of Jadal among the Usuliyyin. The attention of the Usuliyyin was not limited to highlighting the theoretical aspects of jadal alone, rather it extended to studying its practical applications as well. The applications of jadal are numerous and uncountable, and the Usuliyyin have mentioned many examples of them in their books specialized in jadal, and in general usul works there are also many examples of applications of jadal. The purpose of this study is not to enumerate all these applications, because that is something no researcher can achieve, but rather to present applied examples of how to conduct jadal. It is known that jadal revolves in most cases around the proofs with which each party argues for its opinion regarding the claim it holds, since disagreement in issues is not about the issues themselves but rather about the proofs on which they rely. If a disputant can prove the incoherence of his opponent's proofs, he can - easily - expose the incoherence of his claim and the invalidity of the opinion he held and built on those proofs. For this reason we found that the scholars of usul in their dialectical writings were keen to present applied forms of jadal concerning legal proofs. In this chapter of the treatise we will try to cover as much as possible of the applications of jadal among scholars of usul al-fiqh as they mentioned them in their books that they authored on jadal. It may appear to the careless observer at first glance that these applications are applications related to practical issues and not applications of theoretical issues. I hasten to say that the disagreement revolves around a practical issue, yes, but the jadal concerning it is not about the issue itself but rather shifts to its backgrounds and theoretical rules, so jadal revolves around them, and the result of this jadal is what decides the ruling on the issue afterwards. This is in addition to the fact that jadal on a theoretical issue only appears after the disagreement arises on the practical issue; thus jadal is only in the field of theoretical usul action related to those issues, not in their apparent practical aspect. Four Types of Objections to Sunnah in Jadal
Author outlines four categories of objections to using the Sunnah as evidence in dialectical argumentation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and categorize the four types of objections to using the Sunnah as evidence in dialectical argumentation.

Core Idea
Objections to Sunnah in jadal are not only about authenticity.

When scholars debate using a hadith as evidence, they don't just argue about whether it's authentic. This lesson introduces the four main categories of objections that arise in classical usul al-fiqh dialectic: chain, text, cause, and actions. Recognizing these categories helps you analyze and respond to objections systematically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of Sunnah in Islamic evidence
The Sunnah is the second source of legislation after the Qur'an. Scholars rely on it when the Qur'an does not provide explicit evidence. But using a hadith always invites potential objections.
2
Learn the four categories of objections
The author classifies objections into four types: 1) attacking the chain (isnad), 2) objecting to the text itself (similar to objections against Qur'anic verses), 3) objecting based on the specific cause (sabab) of a hadith, and 4) objecting to using the Prophet's actions as evidence.
These categories cover the main loci of criticism in jadal al-usuliyyin.
3
Distinguish between objections to chain and text
Chain objections focus on authenticity: is the hadith reliably transmitted? Text objections focus on meaning: is the wording clear, general, or subject to interpretation? Both are common but require different responses.
The author notes that text objections are similar to those against the Qur'an.
4
Understand objections based on cause and actions
Some hadiths are tied to a specific occasion (sabab); an objection may argue the ruling is limited to that context. Other objections target the evidentiary value of the Prophet's actions: are they obligatory, recommended, or merely permissible?
These categories show that Sunnah evidence can be challenged from multiple angles.

The Logical Conditions for Valid Qiyas According to the Imams
Disputes on the validity and conditions of qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a valid qiyas argument and evaluate whether a given qiyas meets the standards of the imams.

Core Idea
Qiyas is only valid if it is based on a manifest and controlled cause ('illa) and is used in matters that accept conjecture.

This passage from the jadal manual lists several conditions and disputes about qiyas. Al-Shafi'i rejects qiyas that is not based on a manifest 'illa, while Ahmad considers it a proof of necessity. Understanding these conditions is crucial for constructing sound analogical arguments in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Issue
Find the statements about qiyas in the passage. Note that al-Shafi'i rejects qiyas without a manifest and controlled cause. Also note that qiyas is considered a conjectural proof.
The passage says: 'إنكار الشافعي الاحتجاج بقياس لا يقوم على علة ظاهرة منضبطة' and 'القياس دليل ظني'.
2
Extract the Conditions
From the passage, list the conditions for qiyas: it requires a manifest 'illa; it is only used where definitive proof is not required; it can be used for necessity according to Ahmad.
Also note that qiyas cannot be used for hudud or kaffarat according to some imams (though not explicitly in this segment).
3
Apply to an Example
Consider a qiyas: 'Wine is prohibited because it is an intoxicant; therefore, every intoxicant is prohibited.' Check if the cause ('illa) is manifest and controlled.
This is a classic qiyas that meets al-Shafi'i's condition.
4
Assess Validity According to Imams
Would al-Shafi'i accept this qiyas? Yes, because the 'illa is manifest. But would he accept a qiyas based on a hidden 'illa? No.
Imam Ahmad might accept it even if the 'illa is not fully manifest, as a proof of necessity.
5
Formulate a Jadal Exchange
Now construct a debate: a claimant uses qiyas without a manifest 'illa; the objector challenges based on al-Shafi'i's stance.
The respondent may argue that the 'illa is indeed manifest or that necessity justifies its use.

Parsing Jurisprudential Questions for Dialectical Analysis
Author lists jurisprudential questions under sales for dialectical analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse a list of fiqh questions and identify the key logical variables at play in each.

Core Idea
Fiqh questions are the raw material for dialectical construction.

The author presents a list of sales issues, each a potential point of debate. Your task is to extract the core question and the logical structure that a dialectician would use to argue for or against a ruling. This parsing exercise trains you to see the logical skeleton beneath the surface of legal disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the headings
Notice the shift from 'al-Jadal' (dialectics) to 'al-Buyu'' (sales). This frames the list as topics for dialectical reasoning, not rulings.
The heading 'al-Jadal' is a methodology, not a fiqh chapter.
2
Extract each mas'alah as a distinct question
Separate each question by looking for the interrogation marker 'هل' or the phrasing. For example, 'هل يجوز بيعها؟' marks a clear question.
There are multiple questions: some explicit, some implied.
3
Determine the logical variable in each question
For each mas'alah, identify what is being disputed: permissibility, validity, or conditions. For instance, 'بيع الرطب بالتمر' centers on the prohibition of riba.
The variable often hinges on a 'illah (effective cause) that distinguishes cases.
4
Classify the type of dispute
Is the dispute about the existence of a ruling, the applicability of a principle, or the exception? For example, 'fuduli' sale: is it void or suspended?
This classification helps in constructing opposing proofs.
