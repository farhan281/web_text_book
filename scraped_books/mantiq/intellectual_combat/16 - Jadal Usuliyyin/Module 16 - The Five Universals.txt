Counter-Evidence (Mu'arada): How to Handle Conflicting Proofs
Author explains objection by counter-evidence, dividing it into apparent (zahir) and causal, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of mu'arada (by zahir and by 'illa) and apply the strategies of jam' (reconciliation) and tarjih (preference) to resolve conflicting evidence in a jadal debate.

Core Idea
Mu'arada is not the end of debate — it is the beginning of reconciliation or preference.

In jadal, when an opponent brings a counter-evidence that appears to contradict your proof, the debate does not collapse. Instead, you have two powerful tools: jam' (reconciliation) and tarjih (weighing). This lesson walks you through two real examples from Hanafi-Shafi'i debates on prayer and wudu, showing how these tools work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original argument (istidlal)
The claimant presents a proof from a text or action. In the first example, the Maliki/Shafi'i uses the hadith of the Prophet raising his hands to his shoulders to support the ruling of raising hands in prayer.
2
Recognize the mu'arada (counter-evidence)
The opponent brings a different proof that appears to contradict. The Hanafi cites the hadith of the Prophet raising his hands to his ears.
Mu'arada can be by action or statement. Here it is by action.
3
Choose your response strategy: jam' or tarjih
You can reconcile by interpreting both proofs as referring to different aspects (jam'), or you can argue that your proof is stronger based on narrators or other criteria (tarjih).
The author mentions both as valid responses.
4
Apply the strategy to the example
For jam': interpret 'shoulders' as referring to the palms reaching the shoulders and 'ears' as the fingertips reaching the ears — thus both reports describe the same action at different angles. For tarjih: argue that Abu Humayd's hadith is narrated by ten companions, making it stronger.
The Hanafi in the text uses tarjih by saying 'our hadith is more deserving'.

The eighth section: Objection to the deduction by action through counter-evidence.
And counter-evidence may be by an apparent [text] or by a cause.
A - Counter-evidence by an apparent [text]:
And the apparent [text] may be an action or a statement.
A - 1 - As for the action: an example is that the Maliki or the Shafi'i deduces the raising of the hands to the shoulders in the takbir of ihram by prayer by what is narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) raised his hands level with his shoulders (1).
Then the Hanafi counters with what Wail ibn Hajar narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) raised his hands opposite his ears (2). And the answer to this objection is either by reconciling the two proofs, so that what Abu Humayd narrated is interpreted as [raising] the palms, and what Wail narrated as the tips of the fingers reaching the ears, so that there is a reconciliation of the two reports and an use of both proofs; or the answer is by weighing, by saying: Our hadith is more deserving [of acceptance] because with Abu Humayd ten of the Prophet's companions narrated it and agreed with him, so it is more deserving of correctness.
2 - And as for counter-evidence by statement: an example is that the Maliki deduces that covering the entire head is obligatory in ablution by what is narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) wiped his head with his hands, coming forward and back, he began with the front of his head until he went with them to his nape, then he returned them to the place where he started (4). Then the opponent counters with the saying of the Exalted: "...and wipe your heads" (5), and the letter ba' implies partiality in the speech of the Arabs. And the way to answer this is that the Maliki, who is the one deducing, should prevent what he mentioned (i.e., the opponent's interpretation).

Commentary
This passage covers the eighth section on objection by counter-evidence (mu'arada), which is a key part of jadal. The author states that mu'arada can be either by an apparent text (zahir) or by a cause ('illa). He then focuses on mu'arada by apparent text, which itself can be an action or a statement. The first example is a mu'arada by action: the Maliki/Shafi'i uses the hadith of the Prophet raising his hands to his shoulders in prayer. The Hanafi counters with another hadith that raises hands to the ears. The response is either to reconcile the two hadith by interpreting each differently (palms vs. fingertips) or to weigh which hadith is stronger (the one with more narrators). The second example is a mu'arada by statement: the Maliki uses the hadith of the Prophet wiping his head coming back and forth to argue for wiping the entire head. The opponent (presumably a Hanafi or others) counters with the Qur'anic verse 'wip your heads', where the ba' indicates partiality, meaning only part of the head needs to be wiped. The response is that the Maliki should reject (man') the opponent's claim about the ba' implying partiality. This passage illustrates the dialectical technique of mu'arada and the responses of jam' (reconciliation) and tarjih (weighing).

Key Terms
المعارضة (al-mu'arada): Counter-evidence: bringing a different proof (from action or statement) that apparently contradicts the opponent's evidence. [Note: In later logic, mu'arada may also refer to a form of syllogistic refutation. Here it is specifically dialectical.]
الجمع بين الدليلين (al-jam' bayn al-dalilayn): Reconciliation of two seemingly conflicting proofs by interpreting each so that both can be applied, avoiding contradiction.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): Weighing or preferring one proof over another based on criteria such as number of narrators or strength of chain.
ظاهر (zahir): An apparent text (either a reported action or statement) that serves as evidence. [Note: In usul al-fiqh, zahir often means the literal meaning of a text. Here it is broader.]

Key Takeaway
Mu'arada is countered either by reconciling the two proofs (jam') or by preferring one over the other (tarjih), and both strategies keep the debate constructive.
