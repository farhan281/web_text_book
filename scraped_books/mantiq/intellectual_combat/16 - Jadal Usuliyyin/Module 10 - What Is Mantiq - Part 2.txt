Classifying Sciences by Their Subject: Correlatives and Non-Relatives
Classifies sciences by subject (correlatives vs. non-relative) with examples from usul and mantiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given Islamic science as having a relative (correlative) or non-relative subject, and identify whether the subject includes both correlatives or only one.

Core Idea
The subject of a science determines its unity; correlative subjects must include both pairs.

This passage from a jadal manual explains how the subject matter of a science is classified based on whether it involves correlatives (mutadha'ifan) or is non-relative. The author shows that dialectic (jadal) enters the investigated matter from one of the correlatives. Understanding this classification helps us see how different Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and logic are structured around their subjects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of subject
The author divides sciences into those with relative (idafi) subjects and those with non-relative subjects. Note that only relative subjects allow for a science that includes both correlatives.
A relative subject exists only in relation to another concept, like father to son.
2
Locate the criterion for scientific unity
The author states that difference in subject necessitates difference in science. So if the subject is not relative, it cannot be split.
3
See how relative subjects are treated
The author gives two subcases: (a) where both the relative and the related-to are the subject (like usul al-fiqh), and (b) where only one is the subject (like logic, where the clarifying statement and evidence are treated as one unit).
In logic, the subject is the clarifying statement (qawl shar'h) and the evidence (dalil) insofar as they lead to concept and assent.
4
Apply to a new example
Consider another Islamic science, such as kalam, and determine whether its subject is relative or not, and if relative, which of the two subcases it falls under.
For example, the subject of kalam is the divine essence and attributes, which are not correlatives in the same sense.

The Objection of al-Qawl bi-l-Mujib
Author defines the term 'القول بالموجب' as a dialectical objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyze the dialectical objection known as al-qawl bi-l-mujib, and apply it to Qur'anic evidence.

Core Idea
Al-qawl bi-l-mujib concedes meaning but denies relevance to the dispute.

In dialectical debates (jadal), objectors use specific strategies to challenge arguments without outright rejecting the evidence. One such strategy is al-qawl bi-l-mujib, defined in this passage. The objector concedes the opponent's intended meaning (madlul) of a Qur'anic verse but argues that this meaning is not the actual point of dispute, thereby rendering the evidence irrelevant. This lesson breaks down the definition and its application to Qur'anic inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection type
Recognize that al-qawl bi-l-mujib is a dialectical objection where the objector does not deny the evidence itself but its relevance.
It is distinct from objections that reject the validity of the evidence.
2
Understand the concession
The objector concedes the madlul (indication) of the opponent, meaning they accept that the verse implies what the opponent claims.
Concession is the first step; the dispute is not about the indication.
3
Recognize the dispute shift
The objector then claims that this conceded indication is not the point of dispute (mahall al-niza'), so the evidence does not address the real disagreement.
The objection reframes the debate by shifting the focus.
4
Note the universal application
The passage states that this objection applies to all types of Qur'anic evidence (nass, zahir, 'amm, mujmal) with respect to the method of inference.
It is a general tool in jadal.

Objecting to Nass: The Ijmal Objection
Author explains the ijmal objection with the Maliki-Hanafi example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct an ijmal objection against a claim of nass, and evaluate the response using the Maliki-Hanafi example.

Core Idea
An ijmal objection claims the text is too ambiguous to be nass, but the response shows the ambiguity is resolved by known elements.

In jadal, one way to object to an opponent's use of a Qur'anic text is to claim that the text is not nass—it is ambiguous (mujmal). This lesson examines how such an objection is made and answered, using the debate between a Maliki and a Hanafi over the verse on man and fida'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of nass
Find the part of the passage where the Maliki asserts that the verse is nass for the permissibility of man and fida'.
2
Locate the objector's claim of ambiguity
Identify the Hanafi's objection: the verse is mujmal because the limit 'until war lays down its burdens' is unknown.
3
Analyze the specific ambiguous element
What exactly is claimed to be unknown? The phrase 'hatta tada'a al-harbu awzaraha'—the limit is the laying down of burdens. The Hanafi says this limit is unknown.
4
Examine the responder's clarification
The Maliki responds by explaining that 'awzar' is known to mean weapons, and its laying down is also known. Thus the limit is determined, not ambiguous.
He argues that the specification is by something explained (mufassar), not by something ambiguous.
5
Evaluate the objection's success
Based on the responder's clarification, does the ijmal objection hold? The author suggests it does not, because the ambiguity is resolved.
This shows the need to carefully define what constitutes ambiguity.

The first demand: Objection to inference from the explicit text (nass). What is meant by nass: the word whose clarification is raised to its furthest extent. And it is said: 'It is every word that does not admit except one meaning.' And disputation in inference from nass is that the objection occurs by preventing it from being nass in the place of inference, and that is either by a claim of ambiguity (ijmal) or by a claim of probability (ihtimal). A - Preventing inference from nass by claim of ambiguity: Example: the Maliki argues for the permissibility of man and fida' by His saying, exalted: '...either a favour or a ransom until war lays down its burdens.' So this is nass for the permissibility of man and fida'. Here the objection from the Hanafi comes, saying: This verse is not nass in the place of inference, but rather it is ambiguous in its indication of its meaning, because the verse permitted man and fida' up to an unknown limit, since His saying, exalted: '...until war lays down its burdens' appears in it, so this limit is unknown and it is possible that it has occurred, just as it is possible that it has not occurred. So it is ambiguous by this, and therefore claiming nass in it is not permissible. And the Maliki's response to this objection is to show that there is no ambiguity in the verse, for His saying, exalted: '...until war lays down its burdens' does not necessitate ambiguity because its genus is known. For 'awzar' is known, and that is weapons, and their laying down is also known. And the objection could have been conceded if the specification were by something ambiguous, but the specification here is by something explained, namely the laying down of weapons. So as long as war does not lay down its burdens, the judgment of the verse must be continued. So the limit is not unknown; rather it is known and determined by the laying down of weapons. Preventing inference from nass by claim of probability: From that: Abu Hanifa's inference regarding the permissibility of freeing an unbelieving slave in zihar, by His saying, exalted: '...the freeing of a neck...', considering that the verse is nass for the permissibility of everything that is called a neck, whether believing or unbelieving, so whoever restricts the description of faith in the neck has added something to the nass. And it is objected to him that the verse is not nass in the place of inference, because the wording is absolute, occurring upon descriptions not restricted by some of them. So His saying, exalted: '...the freeing of a neck...' admits a believing neck and admits an unbelieving neck. And if it admits both to the same degree, it is permissible to restrict it to the believing neck. And this objection can be replied that nass is what is explicit in a ruling, even if the wording is probable in something else, and it is not a condition that it admit only one meaning, because that is rare.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of how objections are made against inferences from explicit Qur'anic texts (nass) in dialectical argumentation (jadal). The author first defines nass as a word that has been clarified to the utmost degree, or that admits only one meaning. The objection to inference from nass is made by denying that the text qualifies as nass in the specific context of the argument. This denial can take two forms: (1) claiming that the text is ambiguous (mujmal) and (2) claiming that it merely carries probability (muhtamil) rather than certainty. For each form, the author provides a concrete example from Islamic legal debates. In the first example, a Maliki uses the verse about man and fida' to argue for permissibility, but a Hanafi objects that the verse is ambiguous because the limit (until war lays down its burdens) is unknown. The Maliki responds by clarifying that 'awzar' (burdens) is known to mean weapons, so the limit is actually known. In the second example, Abu Hanifa uses the absolute phrase 'freeing a neck' to argue for freeing any slave, including an unbeliever. The objector argues that the wording is not nass because it is absolute and could mean either a believing or unbelieving neck. The response is that nass does not require exclusivity; it is enough that the wording is explicit for a particular ruling, even if it might have other probabilities.

Objecting to Nass: The Ihtimal Objection
Author explains the ihtimal objection with the Abu Hanifa example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish an ihtimal objection from an ijmal objection, and analyze the response that nass does not require exclusivity of meaning.

Core Idea
An ihtimal objection argues that a text has multiple probabilities, but a nass can still be explicit for one ruling even if it admits other possibilities.

The second way to object to a claim of nass is to argue that the text carries probability (ihtimal) rather than certainty. This lesson explores that objection through the example of Abu Hanifa's inference on freeing a neck. The response introduces a crucial nuance: nass is about explicitness for a ruling, not about having only one possible meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of nass
Find where Abu Hanifa asserts that the verse 'the freeing of a neck' is nass for freeing any slave, including an unbeliever.
2
Locate the objector's claim of probability
The objector says the verse is not nass because the word 'raqabah' is absolute and could mean a believing neck or an unbelieving neck. This probability prevents it from being nass.
3
Determine the degree of probability
The objector claims both meanings are equally probable ('bi darajatin wahidah'). This equal probability makes it impermissible to claim nass for one meaning over the other.
The objection assumes that nass requires certainty that excludes all other possibilities.
4
Examine the responder's counter-argument
The responder (likely the author or another scholar) replies that nass is what is explicit in a ruling, even if the wording admits other probabilities in other contexts. It is not a condition that nass admits only one meaning, because that is rare.
This distinguishes nass from a word that is qat'i (definitive) in every respect.
5
Evaluate the validity of the ihtimal objection
Based on the response, the ihtimal objection is weak because it sets an overly strict condition for nass. The existence of other probabilities does not negate the explicitness of the ruling in question.
The author suggests that nass can be explicit for a ruling while still being probable in other matters.

If it is intended for the revocably divorced, then the Maliki argues by analogy that the cause—being in 'idda—applies to both, and the command to use analogy justifies extending it. Thus, the apparent meaning of the command serves as evidence for the validity of the cause. The second example comes from al-Shafi'i regarding the prohibition of selling food for food except with equality. The hadith mentions the attribute 'food' in the context of the ruling, which indicates that 'being a foodstuff' (tu'm) is the cause for the prohibition. This is an instance of using the apparent meaning: the mention of the attribute in the ruling signals causation.

General meaning of a text can establish the cause ('illa) for analogical extension across cases.

The Conditions of Validity for a Legal Cause (Illa)
Defines the cause for the genus and explains tarad wa 'aks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a claimed cause (illa) satisfies the conditions of tarad (consistency) and 'aks (exclusivity) using Islamic legal examples.

Core Idea
A valid cause must be both present with the ruling and absent without it.

In Islamic legal reasoning, establishing a cause (illa) for a ruling is not enough; the cause must be coextensive with the ruling. This lesson introduces the twin conditions of tarad and 'aks, which ensure that the cause is both necessary and sufficient. Using real examples from fiqh, you will learn to identify when a cause is invalidated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimed cause and ruling
Read the arguer's statement to extract what is being presented as the cause (e.g., partnership) and what ruling it justifies (e.g., pre-emption).
The cause is often introduced by terms like 'is the cause for' or 'necessitates'.
2
Check consistency (tarad)
Ask: Does the ruling always occur when the cause is present? If the cause is present but the ruling is absent, the condition of tarad is violated.
Example: If partnership exists but pre-emption is denied, the cause 'partnership' fails tarad.
3
Check exclusivity ('aks)
Ask: Does the ruling occur only when the cause is present? If the ruling appears without the cause, the condition of 'aks is violated.
Example: If pre-emption is granted without partnership, the cause fails 'aks.
4
Evaluate validity
If either condition is violated, the cause is invalid. Only if both hold is the cause valid for analogical reasoning (qiyas).
5
Handle restricted causes
If the ruling can occur in other instances, the cause must be restricted (taqyid) to a specific set, as in the usury example with the four items.
Without restriction, a general cause like 'staple food' would be invalidated by gold and silver.
