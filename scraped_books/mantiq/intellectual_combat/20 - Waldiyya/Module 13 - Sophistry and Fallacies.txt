The Dialectical Defense of Including Kina'a in Majaz
Dialectical exchange on including kina'a in majaz and the role of qarina for tajwiz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange on kina'a and majaz and explain the role of qarina in achieving certainty.

Core Idea
Kina'a belongs to majaz when the wording prevents the literal meaning.

In usul al-fiqh, majaz is broader than in balaghah. This lesson walks through a dialectical exchange where an objector claims kina'a cannot be majaz because it allows the literal meaning. The author responds by distinguishing cases and clarifying that the preventing qarina is required for certainty, not mere allowance. Understanding this exchange sharpens your ability to navigate definitions and objections in Islamic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mudda'i and mu'tarid
The author (mudda'i) holds that kina'a falls under majaz as defined by usuliyyun. The objector (mu'tarid) argues that kina'a's allowance of the literal meaning excludes it from majaz, which requires a qarina preventing the literal meaning.
The objector's position is introduced with 'فان قلت'.
2
Extract the objector's major premise
The objector implicitly relies on a conditional: if allowance suffices for majaz, then qarina is not a condition. Since qarina is a condition, allowance cannot suffice. This is the logical backbone of the objection.
The formula: 'كل من يكفيه الجواز يجب عليه القرينة' is the major premise later challenged.
3
Analyze the author's response on kina'a
The author agrees that kina'a generally permits the literal meaning, but points out that in some cases (like 'ليس كمثله شيء'), the wording itself prevents the literal meaning. Thus, the preventing qarina exists for those instances, not for all kina'a.
The example Qur'anic verse demonstrates a qarina within the wording itself.
4
Understand the distinction between certainty and allowance
The author refines the objector's premise: the preventing qarina is required only for certainty (qat') about the figurative meaning, not for mere allowance (tajwiz). This denies the universal conditional assumed by the objector.
The response uses man' al-mulazama (denial of the connection).
5
Formalize the logical move
The objector's conditional: 'If allowance suffices, then qarina is required.' The author denies this by showing the consequent is only true when certainty is needed, not allowance. Thus, the conditional is not universally valid.
This is a classic jadal maneuver of restricting the scope of a conditional.

The Two Types of Qarina and Their Logical Implications
Classification of qarina into certain and probable with logical implications for majaz and haqiqa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify qarina as certain or probable and state the compatibility rules between majaz and haqiqa.

Core Idea
Certain qarina yields certain majaz, incompatible with certain haqiqa; probable qarina allows coexisting probable meanings.

After the dialectical exchange on kina'a, the author concludes with a systematic classification of qarina into two types: certain (qat'iyya) and probable (muhtamila). This classification has direct implications for how we determine whether a figurative or literal interpretation is valid. The logical rules provided here are foundational for usul al-fiqh discussions on textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of qarina
The passage explicitly divides qarina into qat'iyya (certain) and muhtamila (probable). These correspond to the certainty level they impart to the figurative meaning.
This classification is common in usul al-fiqh contexts.
2
Understand the corresponding types of haqiqa
Similarly, haqiqa (literal meaning) can be certain (qat'iyya) or probable (muhtamila), depending on contextual evidence.
The author pairs each type of qarina with a type of haqiqa.
3
Apply the compatibility rules
Certain majaz (backed by qarina qat'iyya) is incompatible with certain haqiqa. Probable majaz (backed by qarina muhtamila) does not contradict probable haqiqa, though it does contradict certain haqiqa.
The rule: 'المجاز القطعى مناف للحقيقة القطعية والمجاز المحتمل لا ينافي الحقيقة المحتملة وإن كان منافيا للحقيقة القطعية'.
4
Recognize the logical refinement
This classification refines the earlier debate: the preventing qarina required for certainty is the certain qarina. For probable majaz, only a probable qarina is needed, and it does not negate a probable literal reading.
This explains why kina'a can coexist with probable literal meaning in some cases.

Why Not to Use Universal Sophisms as Examples
Objects to the example using a universal fallacy and cautions against it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a dialectical example improperly restricts the scope of discussion and explain the objection using the author's reasoning.

Core Idea
A universal fallacy example can narrow a debate by its own generality.

In scholarly debates, examples are not neutral; they shape the argument. This passage from the author's commentary warns that using a 'universally occurring sophism' (such as the fallacy of composition) as an example may inappropriately limit the discussion. The author invites us to ponder the precision needed in selecting examples for philosophical exchanges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the previous speaker's proposal
The previous speaker used a universally occurring sophism as an example for his argument. Recognize that the author is responding to this choice.
The author says 'if he had exemplified with a universally occurring sophism' – this indicates the original speaker did use such an example.
2
Recognize the author's objection
The author objects: 'it is not permissible to specify the discourse with a universally occurring sophism.' The key phrase is 'takhsis al-kalam' (specifying/restricting the discourse).
The objection is that the example restricts the discussion in an inappropriate way.
3
Understand 'universally occurring sophism'
A 'mughalata 'amma al-wurud' is a fallacy that applies across all subjects, like ambiguity, composition, or division. It is not specific to any one science or topic.
Examples: 'the whole is greater than the part' misapplied, or equivocation on a term.
4
Analyze why this is problematic
Using such a general fallacy as an example can shift the debate away from the specific logical principle at hand. The discussion becomes about the universal fallacy itself rather than the intended point.
The author warns that this is a mistake in dialectical method.
5
Apply the caution
In your own arguments, ensure examples are concrete and relevant to the subject, not so broad that they obscure the issue. This lesson applies to all fields of Islamic science where debate occurs.

The Two Sides of Fallacy: Form and Matter
Author defines fallacy as faulty syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material fallacies in a syllogism and identify examples from Islamic scholarly arguments.

Core Idea
A fallacy is either a broken form or a deceptive premise.

The author introduces the definition of fallacy (mughalata) as a faulty syllogism. But what makes a syllogism faulty? It can fail in two distinct ways: its logical structure (form) may be invalid, or its premises (matter) may be false or circular. Understanding this distinction is the first step to diagnosing errors in any proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two fault types
Read the definition: the fallacy is faulty 'either in form... or in matter'. Notice that the author gives two separate conditions for a syllogism to be productive (correct). A failure in either counts as a fallacy.
2
Analyse the formal fault
The formal fault means the syllogism is not in a productive figure, lacking a condition of quality, quantity, or modality. For example, a categorical syllogism in the second figure with two affirmative premises is formally invalid.
3
Analyse the material fault
The material fault is either the conclusion being identical to a premise (repetition) or a premise being false but resembling truth in wording or meaning. This makes the argument persuasive but unsound.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Take a real argument: 'The Quran is miraculous, every miraculous speech is from Allah, so the Quran is from Allah.' If someone argued 'The Quran is miraculous, every miraculous book is from Allah, but the Quran is a book', that is formally valid. But if the premise 'every miraculous book is from Allah' is false (matter fault), the argument fails materially.

General Fallacies and the Art of Reversal (Qalb)
Author defines general fallacies and reversal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a general fallacy and explain how the dialectical technique of qalb (reversal) exposes it.

Core Idea
General fallacies can prove anything; qalb turns them back on the claimant.

The author introduces a special category: 'general fallacies' (al-mughalitat al-'amma al-wurud). These faulty proofs are so powerful that they can be used to argue for any theoretical proposition, even contradictory ones. The dialectical response is qalb (reversal): the opponent uses the same proof structure to derive the opposite conclusion, showing its invalidity. This technique is central to Islamic disputation (jadal).

Guided Path
1
Define general fallacies
A general fallacy is a faulty proof that can be employed for any theoretical claim, even to prove both p and not-p simultaneously. This makes it a versatile tool for deception.
2
Understand the role of qalb
Qalb is a counter-argument where the opponent takes the same proof (same premises or form) and reverses it to prove the opposite conclusion. The opponent does not change the terms or structure, only applies it to the negation.
3
Note the conditions of qalb
The opponent's reversal must 'establish and explain without substitution or alteration'. That is, the same logical machinery is used, but the conclusion is reversed. This shows the original proof is invalid because it proves both sides.
4
Apply to a kalam example
Consider a skeptic arguing: 'If the world were temporally originated, it would require a creator; but the world is temporally originated; so it has a creator.' A general fallacy might use the same form to prove the opposite: 'If the world were eternal, it would not require a creator; but the world is eternal (from some premise); so it has no creator.' The qalb would show that the original proof's premises also lead to the opposite conclusion.

Recognizing General Sophisms
Author defines general sophisms and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define general sophisms and identify their key characteristic of being applicable to any claim, even contradictions.

Core Idea
General sophisms are false proofs that can be twisted to prove anything, even logical impossibilities.

General sophisms (al-mughalatat al-'ammat al-wurud) are a dangerous class of fallacious arguments because they can be used to 'prove' any theoretical claim, no matter how absurd. Abu al-Fath defines them and warns that they can even be used to argue for the conjunction of two contradictories. Understanding their structure is the first step to refuting them.

Analyzing the Conditional Syllogism in General Sophisms
Author defines general sophisms and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) used in the general sophism example and identify its components: implication and exception.

Core Idea
The general sophism example is a conditional syllogism where the exception is 'whichever it is', making it seem inevitable.

Abu al-Fath illustrates the general sophism using a conditional syllogism. He explains its structure: a conditional premise (if the thing exists or not, the demand follows) and an exception (one of them is the case). Understanding this structure helps us see why the argument is formally flawed.

Countering the General Sophism with Mu'arada and Refutation
Author defines general sophisms and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the technique of mu'arada (counterargument) and naqd (refutation) to counter a general sophism, as demonstrated in the passage.

Core Idea
The best defense against a general sophism is to expose the false major premise by using a counterargument of the same form or by denying the entailment.

After presenting the general sophism, Abu al-Fath shows how an opponent can counter it using mu'arada bi'l-qalb (opposition by inversion). The opponent uses the same argument form with different content to reach the opposite conclusion. The prover then responds by choosing that the subject is non-existent and denying that the entailment holds. This lesson unpacks the dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Read the mu'arada example
The opponent says: 'if the tree whose existence and non-existence entail non-animal is fixed, then non-animal is fixed; but one is fixed, so non-animal is fixed.' Note the parallel structure.
2
Understand the response
The prover answers: 'We choose that it is non-existent, and we do not grant the establishment of the demand.' This denies the entailment from that particular subject.
3
Identify the refutation methods
The author mentions naqd (refutation) and man' (prevention) of the major premise. Analyse why the opponent's major premise is false.
4
Generalize the principle
The passage concludes that in every mu'arada there is a deficiency, and the prover can always deny the entailment or point to the possibility of the opponent's proof being false.

General Sophism for All Theoretical Claims
First example: sophism for all theoretical matters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a general sophism applicable to any theoretical claim and explain why it is fallacious.

Core Idea
A sophism that proves too much proves nothing.

The first example from Abu al-Fath shows a sophism that can be used to 'prove' any claim, including the conjunction of contradictories. This lesson breaks down its conditional structure and reveals why it is a false proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the sophism
The sophism is used to argue for any demand (al-matlub). The claim is that the existence and non-existence of a thing both entail the demand. Recognize that this is a conditional argument: if existence entails demand, and if non-existence also entails demand, then regardless of the thing's status, the demand follows.
The sophism is presented as a proof for 'all theoretical matters'.
2
Extract the logical form
Write the argument formally: Let P = 'thing exists', Q = 'demand holds'. The premise is (P → Q) ∧ (¬P → Q). From this, the conclusion Q is drawn. This is a valid inference (constructive dilemma), but the trick is in the material truth of the premises.
The premises are allegedly true for any demand, which is impossible.
3
Identify the fallacy
The fallacy is that the premises are assumed to be true for any demand, but they cannot both be true for contradictory demands. For example, if the demand is 'the world is eternal', then both existence and non-existence cannot entail it. The argument proves too much, making it unreliable.
This is a fallacy of proving too much (mughalata bi-ithbat al-kathir).
4
Apply to the example of contradictories
The text mentions using the sophism for the conjunction of two contradictories. If we take the demand as 'both contradictories are true', then the argument would claim that both existence and non-existence of any thing entail that contradictories are true, which is absurd.
This highlights the self-defeating nature of the sophism.

Sophisms for Specific Types: Third Figure and Specificity
Examples of sophisms for specific theoretical claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sophisms that misuse the third figure of the syllogism or the relationship between more specific and more general terms to reach false conclusions.

Core Idea
A valid-looking syllogism can be a sophism if its premises are mutually inconsistent.

Abu al-Fath gives examples of sophisms used for a single type of theoretical claim: one using the third figure of the categorical syllogism about contradictories, and others involving al-akhass and al-a'amm. This lesson analyzes these examples and reveals their hidden fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third figure sophism
The text says: 'Whenever two contradictories are combined, one of them is realized; whenever two contradictories are combined, the other is realized; from the third figure, a particular affirmative necessary conclusion is produced.' Recognize that the premises are about the same subject (two contradictories combined), and the conclusion would be that both are realized, which is contradictory.
The third figure requires a particular conclusion, but here the premises are universal?
2
Analyze the akhass/a'amm sophism
The sophism states: 'The speaker of the more specific is a speaker of the more general (i.e., whoever says 'human' says 'animal'), and the speaker of the more general is truthful, so the speaker of the more specific is truthful.' The fallacy is that truthfulness does not transfer from general to specific: being truthful about animals does not guarantee truth about humans.
This is a fallacy of composition or illicit transference.
3
Analyze the contrapositive sophism
The text says: 'The more specific occurs on condition of the occurrence of the more general, otherwise the occurrence of its opposite on this condition would necessitate the occurrence of the opposite of the more general on the condition of the occurrence of the more specific by contrapositive, which is impossible.' This is a convoluted way of saying that denying the specific leads to a contradiction, which supposedly proves the specific. The fallacy is the misuse of contrapositive and assuming the more general must be false if the specific is false.
The argument is valid if the conditional is reciprocal, but it is not.
4
Common structure: proving too much
All these sophisms attempt to prove a claim that is too strong, leading to contradictions or absurdities. The third figure example would prove both contradictories true; the akhass/a'amm example would prove any specific claim true if a general claim is true; the contrapositive example would imply that every specific instance must exist.
The pattern is that the premises are either false or lead to an impossible conclusion.

Identifying Fallacious Arguments in Dialectical Disputations
Author gives examples of fallacious arguments used in theoretical sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in a third-figure syllogism that claims a necessary conclusion from contradictory premises.

Core Idea
Fallacies mimic valid reasoning; their defect lies in violating the conditions of the middle term.

This passage presents three examples of fallacious arguments used in theoretical sciences. The first mimics a third-figure syllogism but fails because the middle term is not taken universally. Learning to spot such flaws is essential for sound theological and philosophical disputation.

1
Identify premises and conclusion
Read the first example: 'Whenever two contradictories are combined, one of them is realized; and whenever two contradictories are combined, the other is realized. From the third figure, a necessary particular affirmative is produced.' The premises are two universal statements about contradictories, and the claimed conclusion is a particular affirmative.
2
Check the figure and mood
This is presented as a third-figure syllogism. In the third figure, the middle term is the subject of both premises. Here, the middle term is 'two contradictories combined' (a phrase). For a valid third-figure syllogism, the middle term must be distributed at least once.
3
Identify the logical flaw
The middle term 'two contradictories combined' is not taken universally; it is a general condition but not quantified. Thus, the premises do not guarantee the conclusion. This is an example of the fallacy of illicit middle (al-ḥad al-awsaṭ ghayr kullī).
The text says 'produced (yuntaj)' but it is not truly valid.
4
Generalize the pattern
This fallacy often occurs when one attempts to deduce a necessary conclusion from premises that lack proper distribution of the middle term. Recognizing this pattern helps avoid erroneous reasoning in dialectical contexts.

And an example of the fallacy that is used to argue for one type of theoretical claim is that one says, for example: Whenever two contradictories are combined, one of them is realized; and whenever two contradictories are combined, the other is realized. Some virtuous scholars said: And another example is to say: Whoever asserts the specific asserts the general, and whoever asserts the general is truthful, so whoever asserts the specific is truthful. Commentary
This passage provides examples of fallacious arguments (mu'ghalata) used in theoretical sciences. The first example claims a third-figure syllogism produces a necessary particular affirmative from two premises about contradictories, but this is not valid because the middle term is not taken universally. The second example: 'Whoever asserts the specific asserts the general' is not always true, as one may not know the general. The third example involves a conditional chain leading to an absurdity by contraposition. These are meant to illustrate common fallacies in dialectical contexts.
