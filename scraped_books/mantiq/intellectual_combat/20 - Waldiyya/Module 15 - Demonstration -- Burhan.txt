Causal Demonstration (Ta'lil) vs Inference (Istidlal)
Author defines causal demonstration and inference with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between limmi and inni proofs and reconstruct their categorical syllogisms from arguments in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Ta'lil moves from cause to effect; istidlal moves from effect to cause.

In this lesson, we examine two types of demonstration in Islamic logic: ta'lil (causal demonstration) and istidlal (inference). The author illustrates them with a medical example: fever and putrid humors. Understanding the direction of reasoning is crucial for evaluating arguments in kalam, fiqh, and other disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of reasoning
Look for the causal connective like 'because'. In 'this is feverous because his humors are putrid', the cause (putrid humors) is given as the reason for the effect (fever). This is ta'lil (limmi). In the reverse, it is istidlal (inni).
2
Extract the causal relationship
Note the cause and effect. Cause: putrid humors. Effect: fever. In ta'lil, the cause is the middle term. In istidlal, the effect is the middle term.
3
Reconstruct the categorical syllogism
For ta'lil: Major premise: Every person with putrid humors is feverous. Minor: This person has putrid humors. Conclusion: This person is feverous. For istidlal: Major: Every feverous person has putrid humors. Minor: This person is feverous. Conclusion: This person has putrid humors.
4
Label the proof type
If the argument goes from cause to effect, it is limmi (ta'lil). If from effect to cause, it is inni (istidlal). Both are valid but differ in epistemological strength.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the fiqh argument: 'Nabidh is forbidden because it is intoxicating, and every intoxicating substance is forbidden.' This is a limmi proof: cause (intoxication) to effect (forbiddeness).

Commentary
This passage defines two types of demonstration in Islamic logic: ta'lil (limmi) and istidlal (inni). Ta'lil is reasoning from the cause to the effect, while istidlal is reasoning from the effect to the cause. The example uses a medical syllogism: fever (effect) and putrid humors (cause). This distinction is crucial for understanding different ways of establishing knowledge. The limmi proof proceeds from the cause (illa) to the effect (ma'lul), giving certainty about why something is true. The inni proof proceeds from the effect to the cause, establishing that something is true but not necessarily why. In fiqh, for example, proving a ruling from its 'illa (ratio legis) is limmi, while inferring the 'illa from the ruling is inni.

The Conditions for Valid Taqrib
Defines taqrib and its completeness criteria.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given proof's taqrib is complete based on the logical relation between its premises and conclusion.

Core Idea
A proof must produce the exact claim, an equivalent, or a specific instance—not a general or contradictory one.

In Islamic dialectical argumentation, the questioner may object not to the truth of the premises but to the way the proof is presented—specifically, whether the proof genuinely entails the claim. This objection, known as preventing taqrib, is a key move in munazara. This lesson explains the precise conditions under which a proof's taqrib is considered complete or incomplete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the proof
Start by clearly stating the claim (mudda'a) and the proof (dalil) as presented in the argument. What exactly is being argued, and what premises are given?
2
Determine the logical product of the proof
Examine what conclusion the proof actually supports. This may differ from the intended claim. The product is the natural conclusion that follows from the premises.
3
Compare the product to the claim
Classify the relation between the product and the claim. Is it exactly the same ('ayn), logically equivalent (musawi), more specific (akhass), more general (a'am), or contradictory (mubayin)?
Use the definitions: a'am means the product is broader and includes cases not in the claim; akhass means the product is narrower and implies the claim.
4
Apply the taqrib completeness rule
Taqrib is complete only if the product is 'ayn, musawi, or akhass. If it is a'am (absolutely or from one aspect) or mubayin, the taqrib is incomplete and can be objected to.
An example from the text: claiming 'some animals are actually writers' and proving 'some animals are actually laughing' yields a'am min wajh, so taqrib incomplete.
5
Practice with Islamic examples
Take arguments from fiqh or kalam and evaluate their taqrib. For instance, using the example from the text or a new one like 'Wine is forbidden because it is an intoxicant'.
Always ask: does the proof's conclusion match the claim exactly, or is it broader/narrower/contradictory?

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'taqrib' (approximation) in the context of dialectical argumentation. The author explains that a questioner may object to the taqrib of a proof presented by the respondent. Taqrib means presenting the proof in such a way that it entails the claimed conclusion. The objection can be stated directly as 'we do not accept the entailment' or more concisely as 'the taqrib is not accepted' or 'incomplete'. The key point is that taqrib is only considered complete if the proof yields exactly the same claim, a logically equivalent claim, or a more specific claim (where the specific implies the general). If the proof yields a more general claim (e.g., the premise supports a broader conclusion than claimed) or a contradictory claim, then the taqrib fails. Examples clarify: claiming 'some animals are actually writers' and using the argument that they are amazed leads to 'some animals are actually laughing' — this is more general since not all laughing animals are writers, so taqrib is incomplete. The contradictory example shows a proof that leads to the opposite conclusion. The final conclusion reiterates the conditions. The benefit note adds that since preventing taqrib is valid, any objection that treats it as an estimated constraint or summary refutation is considered a usurpation (ghasb) and thus invalid, because the questioner's interference is unnecessary when the taqrib itself is correctly prevented.

Classification of Proofs into Four Types
Author classifies proofs into four types and explains how strength is determined.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given proof as either certain proof (dalil qat'i), intellectual indication (amara 'aqliyya), induction (istiqra'), or analogy (tamthil) based on the criteria of certainty/uncertainty and composition of premises.

Core Idea
Proofs are not all equal; they are classified by certainty and composition.

In this passage, the author lays out a fundamental classification of proofs (adilla) that is essential for evaluating arguments. He distinguishes between certain and probable proofs, and further subdivides probable proofs into induction and analogy. This classification is not merely theoretical—it directly impacts how we weigh evidence in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories of proof.
The author first divides proofs into two: those that give certainty (qat'i) and those that give probability (zanni).
2
Subdivide the probabilistic proofs.
The probabilistic proofs are further divided into induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). Induction moves from many particular cases to a general rule; analogy transfers a ruling from one case to another based on a common attribute.
3
Subdivide the certain proofs.
Certain proofs are divided into those composed of certain premises (which yield certain proof) and those not composed of certain premises (which yield an intellectual indication, amara).
4
Combine to get the four types.
Thus we have: (1) certain proof (dalil qat'i), (2) intellectual indication (amara 'aqliyya), (3) induction, (4) analogy.
Note that jurists often call all four 'qiyas' broadly.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological classification of proofs (adilla) according to their strength. The author begins by stating that evidence (dalil) entails its conclusion either certainly (qat'an) or probabilistically (zannan). He then subdivides: the probabilistic is either induction (istiqra') or analogy (tamthil). The practical import: in debate, if an objector makes a mu'arada, the opponent can repel it by proving his own evidence is stronger; this ultimately entails corruption (fasad) of the objector's argument. He also addresses the case where the arguer's goal is to create doubt (like al-Razi's argument denying necessity); then mu'arada is not appropriate, and one should repel by naqd or munaqada instead, unless the arguer's apparent aim is proof. This passage is a typical epistemological expansion of matn material, common in hashiya/comments on jadal texts.

How Proofs Vary in Strength
Author classifies proofs into four types and explains how strength is determined.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why certain proof is strongest, why probabilistic proofs can differ in degree, and what factors increase their strength.

Core Idea
Certainty is absolute; probability admits degrees of strength.

The previous lesson classified proofs; now we examine their relative strength. The author tells us that certain proof is strongest, while the other three are equal in kind but can vary in strength depending on factors like multiplicity and the strength of their premises. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for weighing arguments.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the strength ordering.
The author states that certain proof is stronger than the other three. The remaining three are equal in type (mutasawiya bi-hasab al-naw') but one may be stronger than another by preponderance of probability (ghalabat al-zann).
2
Understand how certain proof cannot become stronger.
Certain proof does not become stronger by multiplicity (e.g., having multiple proofs for the same conclusion) nor by itself (i.e., it is already maximal).
3
Understand how intellectual indication becomes stronger.
Amara becomes stronger by multiplicity (having more indicators) and also in itself: if its premises are more probable, it is stronger.
4
Understand how induction becomes stronger.
Induction becomes stronger when more instances are examined (the more extensive the induction, the stronger).
5
Understand how analogy becomes stronger.
Analogy becomes stronger in itself (by the strength of the 'illah) and, according to Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, also by multiplicity (having multiple analogies). The author says this is the more evident view.

How to Repel Counter-Arguments (Mu'arada, Naqd, Munaqada)
Author classifies proofs into four types and explains how strength is determined.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to select the appropriate method (mu'arada, naqd, or munaqada) to repel a counter-argument based on the strength of the original proof and the intent of the arguer.

Core Idea
The method of refutation depends on the purpose and strength of the argument.

The author ends the passage with a practical note on handling objections. If someone makes a counter-argument (mu'arada), you can repel it by showing your evidence is stronger. But if the arguer's aim is to create doubt (like al-Razi), then mu'arada is not sufficient; you should use naqd (rebuttal) or munaqada (contradictory argument). This distinction is crucial for effective dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard response to a mu'arada.
If an objector brings a counter-argument (mu'arada), the opponent can repel it by proving his own evidence is stronger. This ultimately entails corruption (fasad) of the objector's evidence, i.e., showing it is false or invalid.
2
Recognize the condition for mu'arada.
The counter-argument must be in the position of being countered; if it is too weak, it might not count. The author says verifiers (muhaqqiqun) do not usually deny the counter-argument's ability to be countered based on its weakness.
3
Handle the case where the arguer aims to cause doubt.
If the arguer's goal is to create doubt (shakk), not to prove, then mu'arada is not satisfactory. Instead, use naqd (rebuttal that shows the argument fails) or munaqada (argument that contradicts it). As an example, al-Razi argued to deny necessity (nafi al-luzum).
4
Apply the appearance rule.
Since superficiality (zahir al-hal) often suggests the arguer is trying to prove, you may still use mu'arada in that case. But if the intent is clearly doubt, then avoid mu'arada.
