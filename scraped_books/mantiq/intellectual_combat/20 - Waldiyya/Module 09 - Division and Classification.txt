Understanding Naql (Transmission) and Its Role in Reporting
Definition and examples of a transmitter (naqil).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a 'naqil' in a given text and distinguish a transmitted proposition from a non-transmitted one.

Core Idea
A naqil reports without commitment; knowing this separates source citation from critical endorsement.

The commentator defines what it means to be a transmitter (naqil) and gives examples from theological and legal works. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating how scholars cite authorities: whether they simply report or also adopt the statement as true.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'naqil' in the passage
Locate where the commentator defines 'naqil' in the Arabic. Note that it is derived from the verb 'naqala' (to transmit).
2
Extract the definition
The commentator says: a naqil is one who reports something from another without commitment (bi-la iltizam). This means the transmitter does not necessarily endorse the truth of the transmitted content.
3
Apply the definition to the given examples
The examples: 'He said in al-Mawaqif: God speaks with an eternal speech' and 'The Imam said: intention in ablution is sunna.' In both cases, the author is a naqil because he is reporting statements without indicating his own view.
The context may later clarify whether the author commits to the transmitted statement.
4
Distinguish naqil from non-naqil
A non-naqil is not transmitting from another; they are making their own statement or judgment. The passage contrasts 'naqil' with 'laysa bi-naqil' (not a transmitter).
This distinction is crucial when analyzing source reliability in Islamic texts.

Distinguishing Disjunction (Tardid) from Division (Taqsim)
Distinction between disjunction and division, and three levels of opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between a disjunctive proposition (tardid) and a logical division (taqsim), and identify the three levels of opposition (munafaat).

Core Idea
Disjunction judges truth of propositions; division classifies concepts—confusing them leads to logical errors.

The commentator presents a valuable insight from al-Sayyid al-Sharif: the statement 'the number is either even or odd' can be interpreted as a disjunctive proposition or as a division. The difference lies in intention: disjunction predicates one of the alternatives of the subject as a truth-bearer, while division splits the concept into its parts. Understanding this prevents confusion between logical judgment and conceptual classification.

Guided Path
1
Locate the example 'al-‘adadu immā zawjun aw fardun'
Find this phrase in the Arabic. The commentator uses it to illustrate the ambiguity between tardid and taqsim.
2
Analyze the intention behind the statement
If the speaker intends predication (haml), the statement is a categorical proposition with an omitted quantifier, meaning 'The number (as a concept) is either even or odd' – this is a disjunctive-like judgment. If the speaker intends division (taqsim), the subject is the concept of number, and the two attributes are attached to create subclasses; it is not a truth-bearing proposition but a classification.
The commentator notes that if the quantifier were added, it would still be a categorical proposition resembling a disjunction.
3
Identify the three levels of opposition (munafaat)
The commentator lists three scopes: (1) in propositions (disjunctives), (2) in simple terms regarding truth on a single essence (categoricals resembling disjunctives), and (3) in simple terms regarding existence in one place (categoricals like 'even and odd are incompatible'). Each level has a different logical function.
4
Apply the distinction to a new example
Take a statement like 'The created world is either eternal or originated.' Determine whether it is a disjunction (judging that each part of the world is either eternal or originated) or a division (splitting the concept of 'created world' into eternal and originated parts).
Use the criteria: if the speaker intends to assert a truth about the subject, it is disjunction; if he intends to enumerate sub-categories, it is division.

Understanding Qism in Logical Division
Defines the term qism in logical division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define and identify the qism (division/part) in any logical division and distinguish it from the maqsām (divided whole).

Core Idea
A qism is a part subsumed under the divided and more specific than it.

In logical division, we break a concept into its parts. The qism is each resulting part. It is always more specific than the whole it belongs to. Understanding qism is fundamental to constructing and analyzing classifications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divided whole (maqsām)
Find the concept that is being divided. For example, 'animal' in 'animal is divided into rational and non-rational'.
The maqsām is the broader concept.
2
Locate the resulting parts (aqsām)
The qism is each item that results from the division. In the example, 'rational animal' and 'non-rational animal' are both aqsām.
Each qism is subsumed under the maqsām.
3
Check specificity
Each qism must be more specific (akhaṣṣ) than the maqsām. Rational animal is a type of animal, so it is more specific.
This distinguishes qism from a synonym.

Three Conditions of Valid Division: Jam', Man', and Tabāyun
Author enumerates and explains the three necessary conditions for a valid logical division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the three necessary conditions of a valid logical division: jam' (inclusiveness), man' (exclusion), and tabāyun al-aqsām (mutual exclusivity) to both real and conventional divisions.

Core Idea
A valid division must cover all parts, exclude extras, and ensure parts are mutually exclusive.

Logical division, whether of a universal into particulars or a whole into parts, must satisfy three conditions to be valid. The author explains jam' (comprehensive enumeration), man' (exclusion of non-parts), and tabāyun al-aqsām (mutual exclusivity) with an important distinction between real and conventional division. Understanding these conditions prevents incomplete, overlapping, or irrelevant classifications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition: al-jam' (inclusiveness)
The division must list all parts that fall under the divided; nothing that belongs to the divided should be omitted. This is also called al-ḥaṣr (exhaustive enumeration).
Example: dividing 'animal' into 'human, horse, bird' would fail jam' if 'fish' is omitted.
2
Identify the second condition: al-man' (exclusion)
The division must exclude any part that does not belong to the divided; no extra or foreign parts should be included.
Example: dividing 'substance' into 'body and soul' includes 'soul' which may not be a substance, violating man'.
3
Identify the third condition: tabāyun al-aqsām (mutual exclusivity)
The parts must be mutually exclusive. In real division, parts cannot share any instance. In conventional division, parts must be conceptually distinct even if they apply to the same instance.
Author emphasizes that tabāyun is a separate condition from jam' and man'; it ensures each part is more specific than the divided.
4
Distinguish real vs. conventional division using the five universals
The division of 'universal' into genus, species, differentia, property, and accident is a conventional division because these concepts can all apply to one thing (e.g., 'colored'). They are mutually exclusive in concept but not in reality.
In real division, such overlap is not allowed.
5
Understand the relationship between real and conceptual exclusivity
Real exclusivity implies conceptual exclusivity, but not vice versa. Conventional divisions have conceptual exclusivity without real exclusivity. This relationship is described as 'general and specific in some respect'.
Reflect on why the five universals are a conventional division and how that affects fiqh classifications.

The Two Types of Logical Division: Mental and Inductive
Author defines two types of logical division and discusses their criteria.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mental (aqli) and inductive (istiqra'i) divisions and identify which type a given classification belongs to.

Core Idea
A division is mental if its parts are known by the concept alone; otherwise it is inductive.

The author discusses how logical divisions are restricted to two fundamental types: mental and inductive. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether a classification is certain a priori or depends on empirical investigation. The passage also notes a further subdivision of the inductive type into certain-by-evidence and inductive proper.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
The author states that the restriction (hasr) of division is itself restricted to two types. This is the claim you need to examine.
The Arabic 'حصر الحصر' means 'the restriction of the restriction'.
2
Understand the criterion for mental division
A division is mental ('aqli) if the mind is certain of its parts merely by considering the concept (mafhum) of the divided thing, without looking at external matters.
This is an a priori certainty.
3
Contrast with inductive division
If the division does not meet the mental criterion, it is inductive (istiqra'i), meaning it requires empirical investigation or external evidence.
Inductive divisions are not certain by themselves.
4
Note the alternative subdivision
Some scholars further split the inductive type into that which the mind is certain of by evidence or indication (qat'i) and what is otherwise (zanni or purely inductive).
This shows the flexibility of classification.
5
Recognize the meta-point
The very restriction of division to two or three types is itself a mental act ('aqli), not derived from induction.
This prevents an infinite regress.

Division types reveal the epistemic foundation of classification: reason, investigation, or proof.

In Islamic logic, division (taqsim) is not merely a taxonomic exercise; it has deep epistemological implications. Knowing whether a division is rational, inductive, or certain determines the strength of the conclusion. The example is dividing terrestrial elements into earth, water, air, and fire. The mind can conceive other elements (e.g., light, clouds), but exhaustive investigation found only these four. So the division is based on empirical investigation, not rational necessity.

The example is dividing existent into necessary by essence (wajib bi-dhat) and necessary by other (wajib bi-ghayr). The mind might think of a third category, but a proof (the impossibility of infinite regress) shows that only these two are possible.
This division is used in Kalam to prove God's existence.

When an inductive division is presented in the form 'either A or not-A', the final part 'not-A' becomes unrestricted because its concept covers more than what was actually found by investigation. This is a subtle point: the unrestrictedness is in the concept, not in the actual known instances.

Dialectical Defense of Divisions: Tahrir and Premise Denial
General method of responding to attacks on exhaustiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid dialectical response to an objection against the exhaustiveness of a division using tahrir and premise denial.

Core Idea
The responder can deny the minor or major premise by specifying (tahrir) the category or divided concept to exclude the alleged intermediate.

When an objector claims a division is not exhaustive, the divider has a structured response: deny the minor premise (that the proposed category is indeed possible/actual) or deny the major premise (that this division must be exhaustive in the sense claimed). This is done through 'tahrir' (specification) of the category or the divided concept, showing that the alleged intermediate does not belong. This passage provides the general method and introduces the concept of 'wasta' (intermediate) that must be excluded from the divided concept.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector typically says the division is not exhaustive because another category is possible or actual. Determine whether the objection targets the minor premise (the existence/possibility of that category) or the major premise (the division's exhaustiveness claim).
For rational divisions, the objection is based on mental possibility; for inductive, on actuality.
2
Choose which premise to deny
If the objector says 'another category is possible,' the responder can deny the minor premise by specifying that the alleged category is not truly possible or actual (using tahrir). If the objector misidentifies the division type, the responder can deny the major premise by clarifying the division type (e.g., 'this is rational, so only mental possibility matters, not actuality').
Tahrir involves refining the definition of the category or divided concept to exclude the intermediate.
3
Apply tahrir to the divided concept
To prevent an intermediate, the divider can define the divided concept in a way that excludes the alleged middle category. For example, define 'mujtahid' in a way that includes only those who meet specific criteria, leaving no room for an intermediate 'semi-mujtahid.'
The term 'wasta' (intermediate) is explicitly mentioned as something the divided concept should not include.
4
Craft the response
Combine the denial with tahrir. Example: 'I deny that such a category is possible because the divided concept specifically means X, which does not include that intermediate. Therefore, the division is exhaustive.'
The passage emphasizes that this method works for all division types.

Core Idea

In this passage, the author outlines different types of divisions and specifies how objections and answers vary accordingly. The Objection of Part Being Qasim in Reality
Author explains the objection and two standard responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the objection of part being qasim in a division, and respond using tahrir or the conventional division argument.

Core Idea
Overlapping parts invalidate a division—unless the terms are revised or the division is merely conventional.

In logical division, the parts must be mutually exclusive. This lesson presents a classic objection: when one part is actually a subset of another, the division fails. We will explore how the logician responds by either redefining the broader part or claiming the division is conventional.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the claim that the division is invalid because one part is a qasim (counterpart) of the other in reality. This happens when one part is absolutely more general.
The third condition of division is mutual exclusivity (tabayun).
2
Study the example
The body is divided into animal and growing body. Notice that animal is a species of growing body, so growing body is more general. This violates exclusivity.
The example shows how a seemingly valid division can be flawed.
3
Learn the first response: tahrir
By 'tahrir' (revision), we restrict the broader term to mean 'growing body that is not animal'. This makes the parts mutually exclusive.
This is a common technique when the general term is compared with the specific.
4
Learn the second response: conventional division
Deny the major premise that every division requires real exclusivity. In conventional divisions (i'tibari), it is enough that parts are distinct in the mind.
This response prioritizes the division's usefulness over strict realism.

Author defines the third condition of division and presents objection with answers.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A division is invalid if one part is absolutely more general than another; the logician can fix it by specifying the general term.

We have learned that a valid division requires its parts to be mutually exclusive (tabayun). Now the author considers an objection that arises when one part is absolutely more general than another. Guided Path
1
Recall the third condition of division
The parts of a division must be mutually exclusive: no part should be a species of another part. This is called tabayun.

Alternatively, the respondent can deny the universality of the major premise by arguing that division is a mental (i'tibari) operation. This answer relies on the idea that the division is not about real existence but about conceptual classification.

Other answers include: specify the more specific part, change the division entirely or partially, or use the two objections (naqdayn) technique. Choose the strongest response based on the context.

Core Idea

When Does a Division Fail? Overlap of Divisions
Author explains the objection to division due to absence of mutual exclusivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a division is invalid due to overlap (tasadaq) between its divisions and distinguish it from a valid mutually exclusive division.

Core Idea
A valid division requires that its parts are mutually exclusive; overlap invalidates it.

A division, whether of a universal into its species or a whole into parts, must satisfy several conditions. One crucial condition is that the divisions be mutually exclusive (tabayun). Otherwise, the division is logically flawed and cannot serve its purpose of distinguishing things. This lesson examines the objection to a division based on overlap, using examples from Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions of valid division
Recall that a valid logical division (taqsim) typically requires: (1) the dividing principle is clear, (2) the sum of parts equals the whole, and (3) the parts are mutually exclusive. Focus on the third condition: no two divisions should apply to the same individual.
This lesson addresses objections when the third condition is violated.
2
Understand what 'overlap' means
Overlap (tasadaq) occurs when two or more divisions are both true of a single instance. For example, if you divide 'animal' into 'human' and 'white animal', a white human is both, causing overlap.
The text clarifies that overlap implies generality from one aspect ('umum min wajh) between divisions.
3
Analyze the examples given in the passage
First example: 'Animal is either human or white animal.' These two categories overlap in 'white human', so the division is invalid. Second example: 'Human is either Roman, Abyssinian, or white.' Here, a white Roman human falls under both 'Roman' and 'white', again invalid.
The passage cites these as classic instances where the division fails because divisions are not mutually exclusive.
4
Generalize the rule
Formulate the objection as a conditional argument: 'If a division has overlapping parts, then it is invalid. This division has overlapping parts. Therefore, it is invalid.' The logical form is a modus ponens.
The purpose of division (distinction between parts) is defeated when overlap occurs.

Handling Objections for Equivalence and Synonymy in Division
Author explains the negation of the third condition of division and lists four types of relationships that violate it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why logicians do not object to overlap in equivalence/synonymy divisions by citing the refined understanding of division as relative, and apply the objection-response pattern.

Core Idea
Equivalence and synonymy are not opposed as overlap but as self-partition, requiring a different response than partial generality.

The author notes that when divisions are synonymous or equivalent, the objection is not simply that they overlap, but that the division is invalid because a thing becomes a part of itself. He then explains the response: deny a premise based on a relative interpretation of the division. This lesson unpacks that dialectical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about equivalence and synonymy
The author states that for equivalence (tasāwī) and synonymy (tarāduf), logicians do not object by saying there is overlap; instead they object that the division is invalid because it entails that the thing is a part of itself.
Example: dividing 'animal' into 'laughing' and 'human' (equivalent) makes 'laughing' and 'human' the same, so 'animal' is divided into itself.
2
Understand the objection: self-partition
The objection is that if the divisions are equivalent, then the whole is divided into parts that are actually identical to the whole or to each other, which is absurd. The same applies for synonymy: 'human' and 'man' are synonymous, so division is pointless.
The text says: 'يلزم فيه كون الشيء قسما لنفسه' (it entails that the thing is a part of itself).
3
Study the response: denying a premise based on relative division
The logician responds by denying the minor or major premise (or both) based on a refined understanding of the division: the division is relative (i'tibārī) and not real. That is, the parts are considered equivalent only in a certain respect, and that does not harm the division.
The text says: 'بتحرير التقسيم بأنه اعتبارى لا يضره تساوى الأقسام' (by refining the division as relative; equivalence does not harm it).
4
Contrast with the handling of partial generality
For partial generality, the objection is about actual overlap, and the response is different (not covered in this passage). Understanding the difference is key to applying the right objection.
The passage focuses on the first three types (including absolute generality which was dealt with earlier).

Division of the Whole into Its Parts: Definition and Key Characteristics
Author defines division of whole into parts and its characteristics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the division of the whole into its parts from the division of a universal into particulars, and identify the conditions that apply to each.

Core Idea
In division of a whole into parts, the parts are not predicated of the whole.

We often think of division as breaking a universal into its instances. But there is another kind: analysis of a whole into its constituent parts. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows why the rules differ, using the author's explanation and a subtle example about water.

2

The author explains that this division is for acquiring the quiddity of the whole, not of the parts. Therefore, no new restrictions are added to compose the part, because the part's reality is distinct from the whole's.

3

Because the parts are not predicated of the whole, you cannot use a disjunctive particle (like 'either') between the parts in this type of division.
In division of universal into particulars, each particular can be predicated of the universal, so disjunctive particles are appropriate.

The critique shows that external quiddity (water) behaves differently from intelligible quiddity (human).

Commentary
This passage introduces a new section: the division of the whole into its parts (taqsīm al-kull ilā ajzā'ih). The author defines it as 'the acquisition of the quiddity of the divided thing by mentioning its parts'. He then cites a commentator who clarifies that this division is not for acquiring the quiddity of the divisions (the parts themselves), but for acquiring the quiddity of the whole that is being divided. Thus, this type of division does not involve adding new restrictions or composition to the parts. The reason is that the part, insofar as it is a part, has a reality distinct from the whole; therefore, it is not permissible to use a disjunctive particle (like 'either') between the parts in this division, because each part cannot be predicated of the whole (unlike the division of a universal into its particulars, where each particular can be predicated of the universal). The author then addresses a nuance: sometimes the part and the whole are united in quiddity, as in the example of water and a portion of water: a drop of water is called water. However, he argues that this union in quiddity is not sufficient for predication in all cases; otherwise, we could predicate 'Zayd' of 'Amr' because they share the same human quiddity. But he notes that quiddity is intelligible in the case of Zayd and Amr, whereas in water it is external, so the analogy is not exact. The passage then restates the conditions for any division: combination (jam'), prevention (man'), and distinctness (tabāyun) of divisions, and notes that these conditions are more appropriate for the division of the universal into particulars. The author concludes by addressing the beginners and apologizing for not providing more detail due to lack of time or energy.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the division of a whole into its parts by detailing its three conditions, types, and the method of raising objections. The three conditions are: (1) al-hasr (inclusion) – all parts of the divided must be listed; otherwise the quiddity is incomplete. (2) tabayun al-aqsam (mutual exclusivity) – the parts must be mutually exclusive; if parts overlap, essential repetition occurs. The passage notes that in reality, the whole and part have a relationship of absolute generality (whole implies part but not vice versa), but if part is considered qua part, they are equivalent. (3) dukhul kull qism fi al-qism (each part must actually be a part of the divided) – no extraneous element should be listed, otherwise the composite would not be the same as the divided. This condition corresponds to 'man' (prevention) mentioned earlier. The passage then states that this division can only be essential (haqiqi) or inductive (istiqra'i); it cannot be arbitrary or rational, though some scholars disagree. Some say it has four types: istiqrai (inductive), 'aqli (rational), qat'i (definitive), and ja'li (conventional). Examples are given: dividing a mixture (ma'jun) into honey and black seed, dividing man into animal and rational, and dividing a book into its chapters. The text then explains how to object to this division by denying one of the three conditions. For example, if inclusion is denied, the objection is that a part is missing; if exclusivity is denied, there is overlap; if membership is denied, a non-part is included. The divider can defend by showing the condition is met. The passage concludes with a reminder to derive objections and defenses, referencing two works: al-Taqrir and al-Tahrir.

Objecting to a Division: Refuting and Defending
Method of objection and defense for division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to formulate objections against a division by denying each condition and construct a valid defense.

Core Idea
Every division can be challenged by attacking one of its three conditions; the divider must defend by proving the condition holds.

This passage teaches a standard dialectical method: to object to a division, deny one of its three conditions (inclusion, exclusivity, membership). The objector must show that the division fails that condition, while the divider must demonstrate that it is satisfied. This method is essential in Islamic scholarly debate (munazara).

Guided Path
1
Identify the division under attack
Locate the division in the text. For example, the division of a mixture into honey and black seed. Understand what is being divided.
2
Choose which condition to deny
The objector can deny hasr (say a part is missing), tabayun (say parts overlap), or dukhul (say a non-part is included). For each, formulate a specific objection.
3
Construct the objection
State clearly: 'This division is invalid because condition X is not met.' Provide evidence from the division to support the claim.
4
Defend the division
The defender must show that the condition is indeed met. Use the definitions from the lesson to argue that all parts are included, are exclusive, and are genuine parts.
5
Practice with a new example
Take a division from an Islamic science, e.g., division of intellectual obligations into 'obligation of the first principles' and 'obligation of acquired knowledge'. Object and defend.

It has many benefits: it disperses flatulence, kills worms, benefits catarrh, headache, and eye ailments, etc. And derive the objection against it, i.e., this division, by the absence of the first, second, and third conditions. As for the refutation by absence of the first, it is said: This division is invalid because it is not comprehensive of its parts, there being another part included in the divided outside the parts. And by the absence of the third, that it does not prevent others, because a certain part is included in the parts but is not part of the divided. And derive its refutation. And its conditions are also three: first, inclusion, i.e., collection of the parts of the divided, by mentioning all that was a part of the divided; second, mutual exclusivity of the parts in reality if the parts are not predicated, and in the mind if the parts are predicated, otherwise repetition in essentials would occur and the quiddity would not be obtained; third, prevention, i.e., the entry of each part into the divided, meaning that nothing that is not a part of the divided be mentioned among the parts. Like the division of a mixture into honey and black seed (with damming of shin or its opening), and its division into honey and not honey, and the division of man into animal and rational, and the division of the author's book into its parts. It occurs to the mind that the division of the whole into its predicated parts is an arbitrary division, so reflect. Then each of these four is refuted by the absence of the first, second, or third condition, and the divider defends based on al-Tahrir. Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the division of a whole into its parts by detailing its three conditions, types, and the method of raising objections. The three conditions are: (1) al-hasr (inclusion) – all parts of the divided must be listed; otherwise the quiddity is incomplete. (2) tabayun al-aqsam (mutual exclusivity) – the parts must be mutually exclusive; if parts overlap, essential repetition occurs. The passage notes that in reality, the whole and part have a relationship of absolute generality (whole implies part but not vice versa), but if part is considered qua part, they are equivalent. (3) dukhul kull qism fi al-qism (each part must actually be a part of the divided) – no extraneous element should be listed, otherwise the composite would not be the same as the divided. This condition corresponds to 'man' (prevention) mentioned earlier. The passage then states that this division can only be essential (haqiqi) or inductive (istiqra'i); it cannot be arbitrary or rational, though some scholars disagree. Some say it has four types: istiqrai (inductive), 'aqli (rational), qat'i (definitive), and ja'li (conventional). Examples are given: dividing a mixture (ma'jun) into honey and black seed, dividing man into animal and rational, and dividing a book into its chapters. The text then explains how to object to this division by denying one of the three conditions. For example, if inclusion is denied, the objection is that a part is missing; if exclusivity is denied, there is overlap; if membership is denied, a non-part is included. The divider can defend by showing the condition is met. The passage concludes with a reminder to derive objections and defenses, referencing two works: al-Taqrir and al-Tahrir.

The Three Conditions of a Valid Division and Its Four Types
Author explains the three conditions of a valid division and lists its four types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions of a valid division (taqsim) and distinguish its four types using the examples from the text.

Core Idea
A valid division must be complete, exclusive, and each part must belong to the whole — no room for confusion.

Division (taqsim) is a fundamental logical operation used to analyze wholes into parts. The author lays down three conditions that any proper division must meet: completeness, mutual exclusivity, and proper inclusion. He also lists four types of division. Understanding these conditions will sharpen your ability to evaluate classifications in fiqh, usul al-fiqh, and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
The text explicitly states three conditions: hasr (completeness) — all parts must be enumerated; tabayun (mutual exclusivity) — parts must not overlap; and dukhul kull qism fi al-qism (inclusion) — each listed part must genuinely be part of the whole.
These conditions ensure the division captures the essence of the divided thing.
2
Understand each condition through examples
The electuary example (ma'jun divided into honey and black seed) illustrates the third condition: the whole is the sum of parts, not each part separately. Violating hasr would mean missing some parts; violating tabayun would mean overlapping categories.
Reflect on what would happen if one condition is missing.
3
Distinguish the four types of division
The text lists four types: inductive (istiqra'i), rational ('aqli), categorical (qat'i), and conventional (jalii). The author restricts valid division to real (haqiqi) or inductive only, rejecting conceptual or rational divisions according to some.
Note the debate about types; the author mentions the Qawanin reference.
4
Apply the conditions to a real classification
Take a classification from Islamic law, e.g., dividing acts into wajib, mandub, mubah, makruh, haram. Check if the division satisfies hasr (all types listed), tabayun (no overlap), and inclusion (each is a legal ruling).
This tests your ability to use the conditions critically.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions and types of division (taqsim). The author first explains the three conditions: completeness (hasr), meaning all parts of the divided thing must be listed; mutual exclusivity (tabayun), meaning the parts must not overlap in reality or intellect; and inclusion of each part in the divided thing (dukhul kull qism fi al-qism), meaning no extraneous element is included. The example of dividing an electuary (ma'jun) into honey and black seed illustrates the third condition: the whole is predicated as a sum, not each part separately. The author then states that division is either real (haqiqi) or inductive (istiqra'i), not conceptual or rational, though some disagree. Then four types are given: inductive, rational, categorical, and conventional. This passage is foundational for understanding how logicians analyze classification.

Key Terms
الحصر (al-hasr): The condition that all parts of the divided thing are enumerated in the division. [Note: Stable term across mantiq texts.]
تباين الأقسام (tabayun al-aqsam): The parts must be mutually exclusive in reality (if non-predicable) or in intellect (if predicable). [Note: Related to the concept of tana'u' in takhalluf.]
دخول كل قسم في القسم (dukhul kull qism fi al-qism): Each part must be a part of the whole; the division should not include what is not a part.
تقسيم (taqsim): Division of a whole into its parts, which can be real (haqiqi) or inductive (istiqra'i), etc. [Note: In later texts, taqsim is often synonymous with tasnif.]
