The Fallacy of Division and Its Resolution
Author presents a fallacy and its resolution in division logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of division in universal-partition arguments and explain its resolution using the correct observation of the muqassam.

Core Idea
The fallacy of division arises when you confuse the divided thing with its parts; the cure is to consider it in itself.

This lesson examines a classic fallacy that commonly occurs when dividing universals into their particulars. The author, following al-Sayyid al-Sanad, identifies the logical error and provides a precise answer. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to evaluate definitions and classifications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divided thing and the parts
In any division, there is a muqassam (the thing being divided) and aqsam (the parts). For example, dividing 'hukm' into 'wajib, mandub, etc.' The muqassam is 'hukm', the aqsam are the individual rulings.
2
Recognize the two problematic perspectives
The fallacy considers the muqassam either as realized in some parts (so it does not include the other parts) or as realized in all parts (so each part becomes a co-part of the muqassam).
These are presented as the only two possibilities, leading to absurdities.
3
See why each leads to absurdity
If realized in some parts, you get division into itself and other (impossible). If realized in all parts, you get division into its own co-parts (circular).
The absurdity shows the fallacy's premise is flawed.
4
Understand the correct answer
The answer is to consider the muqassam in itself, regardless of its realization in any part. This avoids the dilemma.
The muqassam is a universal concept; it is not confined to its instances.
5
Analyze the formalization and conditions
The fallacy is formalized as a conditional syllogism: either the muqassam is realized in some parts or in all parts. Both are rejected. The author then checks the minor premise (the disjunction): if it is mani'at al-jam' (prevents conjunction), the argument is complex; if not, the premise is false. For a valid division syllogism, the disjunction must be mani'at al-khuluw (prevents emptiness).
This shows the importance of disjunction type in division arguments.

It is reported from al-Sayyid al-Sanad a fallacy that is common in all divisions of universals into their particulars: that the divided thing has no realization except within the parts. So if it is taken from the aspect of its realization within some parts, it does not include the part, so the division of the thing into itself and other than itself follows. And if it is taken from the aspect of its realization within all parts, each of the parts becomes a co-part of the divided thing, so the division of the thing into its co-parts follows. And the answer to it is: we observe the divided thing in itself, disregarding its realization within any of the parts. And the formulation of this fallacy is that this division is either concomitant such that its divided thing is realized within some parts, or concomitant such that its divided thing is realized within all parts. The first is invalid because it entails division into itself and other, and the second is invalid because it entails division of the thing into its co-parts. It follows that this division is a syllogism with a single result of composition, but it is invalid either materially or formally, because the minor premise is either a disjunctive that prevents conjunction or not. On the second, the minor is denied because the divided thing is here observed in itself disregarding those two aspects. On the first, the production is composite. For among the conditions for a division syllogism to produce is that the disjunctive in it be real or preventing emptiness as is established in its place.

Commentary
This passage presents a known fallacy (mughalata) in the logic of division, attributed to al-Sayyid al-Sanad, and provides its resolution. The fallacy targets all divisions of universals into their particulars. The argument is that the divided thing (muqassam) has no existence except within the parts (aqsam). Therefore, if we consider the divided thing as realized in some parts, it does not encompass the other part, leading to the impossibility of dividing the thing into itself and something else. Conversely, if we consider it as realized in all parts, then each part becomes a co-part (qasim) of the divided thing, resulting in the division of the thing into its own co-parts, which is circular. The answer is simple: we consider the divided thing in its essence, irrespective of its realization in any part. The fallacy is then formalized as a conditional syllogism: either the division is such that the divided thing is realized in some parts, or in all parts. The first leads to self-division (invalid), the second leads to co-part division (also invalid). The conclusion would be that this division is a compound syllogism with a single product, but it is flawed either materially (the minor premise is false) or formally (the disjunction is not exclusive). The correct solution is that the minor premise (the disjunction) must be a true exclusive disjunction (mani'at al-khuluw) for the syllogism to be valid. Otherwise, we deny the minor premise because the divided thing is considered in itself. This is a critical refinement typical of a hashiya, correcting a misunderstanding about the conditions of division.

Objection and the Condition of Exhaustive Division
Discusses the nature of objection and the requirement of exhaustive division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to formulate an objection (i'tirad) against a rational division by demonstrating its lack of exhaustiveness (hasr) using a categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
An objection must show the division fails to cover all possible parts.

This lesson unpacks the nature of objection in dialectic and the critical condition that a rational division must be exhaustive. You will learn how to identify a non-exhaustive division and construct a valid objection, which is a key skill in theological and juristic debates where rational classifications are used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of division
Determine whether the division is rational ('aqli) or empirical. The passage specifies that if it is rational, the opponent can object by proposing another conceptually possible part.
2
Understand the exhaustiveness condition
A valid division must be exhaustive (hasr), covering all possible parts. If the opponent can conceive of another part within the divided whole, the division is non-exhaustive.
3
Formulate the objection as a categorical syllogism
The objection takes the form: 'Every non-exhaustive division is invalid. This division is non-exhaustive (because another part is permissible). Therefore, this division is invalid.' Recognize this as a first-figure Barbara syllogism.
This logical structure ensures the objection is sound.
4
Apply the objection to a new Islamic case
Take a rational division, e.g., division of acts into obligatory and prohibited. Can the opponent posit a third category (e.g., permissible) to refute the division? Practice constructing the objection syllogistically.
This tests your grasp of the condition.

Commentary
This section begins a new chapter on objections to exhaustive division (hasr) of a universal into its particulars. The author notes that division is conceptually a demand of tasawwur (conceptualization) in reality, though it may appear in the form of tasdiq (assertion), a point of disagreement between al-Sharif and al-Taftazani. The Kalima Fallacy and Its Resolution
Presents the famous Kalima Fallacy and its resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of dividing a universal into itself and its opposite, and resolve it by distinguishing the universal from its species.

Core Idea
Dividing a universal into its species is not dividing it into itself; the universal is more general.

This lesson examines a famous fallacy that repeatedly appears in logical debates: the claim that dividing a universal into its species amounts to dividing a thing into itself and another. Using the example of 'kalima' (word) divided into noun, verb, and particle, you will learn to recognize the error and apply the correct resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy in the example
Read the example: dividing 'kalima' into noun, verb, particle. The opponent objects that this divides the thing into itself because every kalima is one of those parts.
This is the Kalima Fallacy.
2
Analyze the opponent's reasoning
The opponent treats the divided whole (kalima) as identical to one of its species, ignoring that 'kalima' is a universal (genus) that applies to all species.
He confuses the universal with a particular instance.
3
Apply the author's resolution
The correct answer: the kalima under division is more general (a'amm) than any particular part. It is the genus, not one of the species. Therefore, dividing it into its species is valid and does not involve self-division.
The universality of the divided whole is key.
4
Generalize to other Islamic examples
This fallacy can occur in any division of a genus into its species, e.g., dividing 'hukm' (ruling) into wajib, mandub, etc. Practice spotting the fallacy and responding.

Conditions of Complete Taqrib
Author defines taqrib and explains its completion depends on the relation between the proof's conclusion and the claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a given evidence-conclusion relation constitutes a complete taqrib by comparing it to the claim in terms of generality or specificity.

Core Idea
Taqrib is complete only when the evidence's conclusion is identical, equivalent, or more specific than the claim – never more general or opposite.

In dialectical debate, evidence (dalil) is brought to support a claim (mad'aa). But not every valid inference constitutes a proper taqrib (approximation). This passage from the matn lays down strict conditions: the conclusion of the evidence must be exactly the claim, or equivalent to it, or more specific. If it is more general or opposite, the taqrib fails. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the evidence's conclusion
From the passage, note that the claimant asserts something, and the evidence produces a conclusion. For example, the claim 'some animal is a writer' and the evidence concludes 'some animal is laughing'.
The claim and conclusion may differ in quantity, quality, modality, or scope.
2
Determine the relation between the conclusion and the claim
Classify whether the conclusion is exactly the same (ayn al-mad'aa), equivalent (musawi), more specific (akhass), more general (a'amm), or opposite (mubayin). The passage emphasizes that more general may be absolute or from one aspect.
Use the examples given: laughing is more general than writer; inanimate is opposite to animal.
3
Apply the rule for complete taqrib
If the relation is identical, equivalent, or more specific, the taqrib is complete (tamm). If it is more general (absolutely or from one aspect) or opposite, there is no taqrib at all.
The author rejects the view that more general yields incomplete taqrib; it yields none.
4
Test with the passage's examples
Walk through the examples: 'some animal is a writer' vs 'some animal is laughing' – more general from one aspect, so no taqrib. 'This is not an animal' vs implied 'this is an animal' – opposite, so no taqrib.
5
Extend the rule to modality and proposition type
The passage notes that the rule applies regardless of modality (necessary vs perpetual) and whether the proposition is real, external, or mental. Thus a necessary claim cannot be supported by a perpetual conclusion unless the perpetual is equivalent or more specific.
This prevents loose reasoning in theological arguments about divine attributes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A counterargument can mirror the form or change it; understanding both is essential for jadal.

This passage applies the general sophism to the eternity debate and then classifies how an opponent can respond. Proof that debate ends in one of two incapacities

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Debate cannot go on forever because the human soul is created and finite.

In this passage, the author proves a key principle of munazara: every debate must eventually end in one of two incapacities. He offers the proof in two logical forms: first as a categorical syllogism, then as a non-standard conditional syllogism. By understanding this argument, you will see how Islamic dialectic grounds its rules in rational necessity.

In a debate, it is not uncommon for each party to bring evidence that directly contradicts the other's conclusion. The author of our passage explains a powerful dialectical outcome: when each evidence implies the opposite of its own intended claim, a second-figure categorical syllogism can demonstrate that both evidences are invalid. This concept, known as 'al-musāqaṭa' (fall), is essential for understanding how arguments are evaluated in Islamic dialectics (jadal). We will unpack how the second-figure syllogism is used to reach this mutual nullification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting claims
In the debate, the proponent (mu'allil) and the objector (mu'āriḍ) each have a claim. The proponent's evidence is supposed to support his claim, but it is alleged to imply the opposite. Similarly for the objector. Find the claims in the text.
2
Understand the condition for a valid evidence
The author states: a correct evidence (dalīl ṣaḥīḥ) with all its premises does not indicate contrary to its own implication (madlūl). Why? Because that would entail the combination of two contradictories (ijtimā' al-naqīḍayn). Grasp why this is a logical impossibility.
3
Recognize the second-figure syllogism
The author says that if the proponent's evidence implies the opposite of its conclusion, then a proof from the second figure (shakl thānī) is produced that the evidence is invalid. Review the second figure: the middle term is predicate in both premises, and it yields a negative conclusion. Here, the conclusion is 'this evidence is not correct.'
4
Apply the same reasoning symmetrically
The same logic applies to the objector's evidence. So both evidences are shown invalid by separate second-figure syllogisms. This is the symmetry.
5
Conclude the dialectical outcome (al-musāqaṭa)
With both evidences invalid, both claims remain without proof. The outcome is that both arguments fall (tasqit). This is not a draw; it is a nullification of both positions, which is a recognized result in jadal.

The invalidity of the consequent: it is as if the proponent also says that my evidence invalidates your claim, thus invalidates your evidence that you used to object... Know that what the objector's evidence produces is the objector's claim, and what the proponent's evidence produces is the proponent's claim. (Since a correct evidence with all its premises does not indicate contrary to its implication; otherwise, the two contradictories would combine.) The proponent's evidence indicates contrary to its implication, so a proof from the second figure is produced that the proponent's evidence is not a correct evidence. Likewise, the objector's evidence indicates contrary to its implication, so a proof from the second figure is also produced that the objector's evidence is not a correct evidence. A branch of the first is his statement: 'So the proponent's claim remains without evidence', and it branches from the second: 'and the objector's claim remains without evidence', but he omitted it for the sake of parallelism. When this is the case, its outcome is fall (tasqit), and that is the desired result.

Commentary
This passage deals with the dialectical situation where both parties in a debate produce contradictory evidences. The author argues that if the proponent's evidence implies the opposite of its own intended conclusion, then a second-figure syllogism shows that evidence to be invalid. The same applies to the opponent's evidence. The result is that both claims are left without evidence, leading to 'tasqit' (fall) — both arguments collapse. This is a key concept in jadal (dialectics). The author uses the second-figure syllogism, which is known for producing negative conclusions. An Islamic example: if one claims that nabidh is permitted because it is not wine, and the objector claims it is forbidden because it intoxicates, and each uses a flawed analogy, then both evidences cancel out, leaving no valid proof.
