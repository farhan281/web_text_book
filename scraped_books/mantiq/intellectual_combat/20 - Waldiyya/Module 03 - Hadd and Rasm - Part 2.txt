Ta'lil and Istidlal: Why-Proof vs That-Proof
Defines and contrasts ta'lil and istidlal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a limmi proof (ta'lil) from an inni proof (istidlal) and construct each using an Islamic science example.

Core Idea
Ta'lil proves from cause to effect; istidlal proves from effect to cause.

Demonstration comes in two directions: a limmi proof explains the cause to prove the effect, while an inni proof uses the effect to infer the cause. This lesson shows how to recognize and apply both, with examples from classical medical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of explanation
In ta'lil, the cause is stated first and then the effect is concluded. In istidlal, the effect is stated first and the cause is concluded.
2
Locate the examples in the passage
Find the two examples: one starts with feverish because of putrified humors (ta'lil), the other starts with putrified humors because of feverish (istidlal).
3
Contrast the two syllogisms
In ta'lil, the major premise is 'Every putrified-humors person is feverish' (cause to effect). In istidlal, the major premise is 'Every feverish person has putrified humors' (effect to cause).
Both are valid categorical syllogisms but differ in direction of proof.
4
Relate to Islamic sciences
In Kalam, when proving the existence of the Creator from the temporal origination of the world (huduth), that is istidlal (inni). When proving that the world must have a cause because of its nature, that is ta'lil (limmi).

Classification of Truth and Metaphor: Linguistic and Intellectual
Classifies truth and metaphor into linguistic and intellectual types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between linguistic and intellectual truth and metaphor, and classify given examples accordingly.

Core Idea
Truth and metaphor apply both to word usage and to grammatical attribution of actions.

This passage from the Waldiyya establishes a foundational division: truth (ḥaqīqa) and metaphor (majāz) each have two types—linguistic and intellectual. The linguistic type concerns the conventional meaning of words, while the intellectual type concerns the attribution of actions to their agents. This classification is essential in Islamic rhetoric and legal theory, where the correct interpretation of expressions depends on recognizing whether a metaphor operates at the word level or the attribution level.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories: truth and metaphor.
The passage states that each of truth (ḥaqīqa) and metaphor (majāz) is divided into two types: linguistic and intellectual.
2
For linguistic truth and metaphor, focus on the word itself.
Linguistic truth uses a word in its original conventional meaning; linguistic metaphor uses a word for something else with a relation (ʿalāqa) and a contextual indicator (qarīna) preventing the literal meaning.
3
For intellectual truth and metaphor, focus on the attribution (isnād) of an action.
Intellectual truth attributes an action to its true agent in appearance; intellectual metaphor attributes it to something related, diverting from the true agent with a contextual indicator.
4
Apply the definitions to the examples given.
In 'God caused the herbage to grow,' the attribution is intellectual truth because God is the true agent. In 'Spring caused the herbage to grow,' it is intellectual metaphor because spring is not the true agent but a related cause.

Author defines ghasb as premature falsification of an unproven premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of ghasb (usurpation) and distinguish it from mu'arada (opposition) and sund qat'i (decisive support) in dialectical exchanges.

Core Idea
Ghasb is attacking an unproven claim—a procedural violation in Islamic dialectic.

In adab al-bahth, the rules of engagement are precise. One key violation is ghasb, where a disputant claims falsity of a premise before the proponent has proven it. Understanding ghasb protects the integrity of debate by ensuring each side has a fair chance to present their case. This lesson unpacks the definition and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputant's action
Check whether the disputant is explicitly claiming the falsity of a claim or premise. This is the core of ghasb.
Mere questioning without claiming falsity does not constitute ghasb.
2
Check timing relative to proof
Determine if the claim of falsity is made before the proponent has provided evidence for the premise. Ghasb only occurs before proof.
If after proof, it becomes mu'arada (opposition).
3
Check the status of the premise
Verify that the premise is not self-evident (badihi) nor already conceded (musallam) by the opponent. Ghasb applies to unestablished premises.
If the premise is self-evident, it cannot be denied; if conceded, it is not in dispute.
4
Check if the disputant provides evidence
Ghasb requires that the disputant supports the falsification with a proof (dalil). If no evidence is given, it may be a different type of objection or simple denial.
The passage says whether or not the disputant preceded it with a request for proof, but the falsification must be with evidence.
5
Distinguish from mu'arada and sund qat'i
If the objection occurs after the proponent's proof, it is mu'arada. If the falsity follows logically from a decisive sund without an explicit claim of falsity, it is not ghasb.
Sund qat'i entails the contradictory without an independent claim of falsity.

Commentary
This passage is a chapter (fasl) from a commentary on the rules of objection (man') in dialectical debate. It defines a specific type of invalid objection called 'ghasb' (usurpation). The author explains that ghasb occurs when the disputant attempts to falsify an unproven claim (mudda'a ghayr mu'allal) or a premise of the proponent's proof, before the proponent has provided evidence for it. The key condition is that the claim or premise is not self-evident (badihi) nor conceded (musallam) by the opponent. The disputant's falsification must be accompanied by a proof (dalil). In such a case, the act is called ghasb—a logical faux pas because the disputant is attacking a position not yet established. The author then distinguishes ghasb from two other situations: (1) When the disputant's objection implicitly leads to the contradictory of the disputed premise through a decisive supporting proof (sund qat'i), this is not ghasb because the falsity follows logically, not from an independent claim. (2) If the disputant objects after the proponent has already argued for the premise, it is called mu'arada (opposition), not ghasb. Examples illustrate mu'arada: the proponent claims 'This phantom is human' and the disputant directly refutes by saying it is a stone. The phrase 'ما قال' at the end suggests the disputant simply denies without giving a reason, which is also a valid opposition after proof. The passage is technical, showing the precise rules that govern respectful dialectical engagement according to the adab al-bahth tradition.

Distinguishing Ghasb from Mu'arada and Naqd
Author defines ghasb and distinguishes it from mu'arada and naqd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish ghasb from mu'arada and naqd in a dialectical exchange, and identify which type of objection is being made.

Core Idea
Ghasb is an invalid objection to an unsupported claim; mu'arada and naqd are valid responses to evidence.

In Islamic dialectics, the rules of objection are precise. Not every challenge to an opponent's claim is permissible. The term 'ghasb' refers to an illegitimate demand for evidence—specifically, calling for proof on a point that has not yet been supported. This lesson dissects the definition of ghasb and clarifies how it differs from two legitimate forms of counter-argument: mu'arada (counter-argument with evidence) and naqd (refutation of the evidence itself). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for any student of jadal.

Guided Path
1

This is the basis for distinguishing ghasb from legitimate objections.
3
Distinguish ghasb from mu'arada and naqd.
Mu'arada is a counter-argument directed at a claim that already has evidence; naqd is a refutation of the evidence itself. Both are permissible because they target supported items.
Genuine mu'arada is not ghasb because it responds to evidence.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'ghasb' (often translated as 'usurpation' or 'objection by analogy' in dialectical contexts) and clarifies its scope in relation to other dialectical moves. The author begins by stating that ghasb is, in the terminology of the dialecticians, the arguer's (sā'il) reasoning against the invalidity of something that it is legitimate to challenge (mā ṣaḥḥa man'uh). That is, the arguer demands evidence for a claim or premise that has not yet been supported by evidence. The passage then specifies that invalidating an unsupported claim or premise constitutes ghasb, because both are validly challengable, and challenging them is part of the arguer's role. However, a distinction is made: if the challenge uses the word 'man'' (challenge) or its derivatives, it is metaphorical for the absolute request for evidence; but if it uses another phrase like 'we do not accept' (lā nusalim), it is not metaphorical. Next, the author distinguishes ghasb from genuine mu'arada (counter-argument) and total refutation (naqḍ ijmalī). Genuine mu'arada is not ghasb because it invalidates a claim with evidence after the opponent has already provided evidence for it; since challenging a claim after evidence is not valid, invalidating such a claim is not ghasb. Similarly, total refutation (naqḍ ijmalī) is not ghasb because it invalidates an argument with another argument, and such invalidation is not ghasb. Moreover, one cannot challenge the evidence itself because challenge applies only to what can be reasoned about, and evidence (a complete syllogism) cannot be subject to further reasoning since it is composed of two premises and thus not a single proposition. This passage clarifies the boundaries of permissible dialectical moves, ensuring that objections are correctly categorized.

Author defines taqrib and explains when it is valid.

Core Idea
Taqrib is valid only when the proof's conclusion matches, is equivalent to, or is more specific than the claim.

In jadal (dialectical debate), the questioner may object to the arguer's presentation of proof. The author defines 'taqrib' as presenting a proof that implies the claim. However, not every valid syllogism counts; the taqrib must produce a conclusion that stands in a specific logical relation to the original claim. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and explanatory section introducing the concept of 'man' al-taqrib' (prevention of the syllogistic presentation) in jadal. The author begins by noting that because this type of prevention occurs frequently, it is given a separate section. He defines taqrib as the method of presenting a proof in such a way that it implies the claim, before actually applying the proof to the claim. The author then explains how the questioner might prevent taqrib by denying the implication or demanding clarification. The core technical point: taqrib is valid only if the proof produces one of three relations to the claim: exact identity (ayn), equivalence (musawi), or more specific (akhass). Producing a more general (a'am) result is invalid. He gives clear examples from the Islamic logical tradition: if the claim is 'this is a human', a proof that 'it is rational, and every rational is human' gives exact identity. A proof that 'it is laughing, and every laughing is human' gives equivalence (since laughing and human are co-extensive). A proof that 'it is rational and black, and every rational black is Zanjee' gives a more specific result (Zanjee is more specific than human). A proof that 'it is breathing, and every breathing is animal' gives a more general result (animal is more general than human), which is invalid. He also discusses the third figure example where the conclusion is particular (some animal is human) and only yields one premise, not a valid compound syllogism. This guards against the misunderstanding that a general result suffices; taqrib requires a valid entailment.

Real and Metaphorical Prevention in Dialectical Objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of metaphorical prevention (man') in adab al-bahth and explain why prevention is never literal when objecting to a premise or claim.

The Hanafi takes 'lam yatahammal al-khabath' to mean the water weakens and becomes overwhelmed (so it becomes impure), while the Shafi'i takes it to mean the water rejects impurity (so it remains pure). This is an example of mu'arada bil-qalb because the opponent (Shafi'i) uses the same evidence (the hadith) as the arguer (Hanafi) in both matter and form, but with a different interpretation. The effect of this type of opposition is to invalidate the original argument because a single sound argument cannot support contradictory conclusions. For the other types, the invalidity of the arguer's argument is not determined; rather, it is known generally that one of the two arguments is false, but not which one.

Refutation (Naqd): Definition, Types, and Methods
Definition and explanation of refutation with its types and methods

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A proof is refuted either by showing it fails in another case or by showing it leads to absurdity.

Refutation (naqd) is a key tool in Islamic dialectics. It allows a critic to challenge a proof without necessarily disproving every premise. This passage from a classical logic commentary explains two types of refutation—general and detailed—and the two methods by which a proof can be shown invalid. Understanding these distinctions is essential for engaging in rigorous theological and juristic debate.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of naqd
In the passage, naqd is either unqualified or qualified by 'ijmali'. When qualified as ijmali, the invalidity is traced to an unspecified premise. When qualified as tafsili, it is equivalent to direct contradiction (munaqada). Identify which type the author describes as leaving the invalid premise unidentified.
The shift alert notes that earlier works used naqd and munaqada interchangeably; here the author clarifies the distinction.

The author states that the evidence for invalidity is restricted to two matters: takhalluf (failure) and istilzam al-fasad (entailment of corruption). Find the Arabic terms for these methods and note their definitions.
Takhalluf: the proof applies to another case but the claim does not follow. Istilzam al-fasad: the proof leads to an absurd or false conclusion.

Understanding Ifham and Ilzam in Dialectic
Definition of ifham and ilzam with grammatical analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the passive meanings of ifham and ilzam and correctly interpret the idafa construction in 'إفحام المعلل' and 'إلزام السائل'.

Core Idea
In dialectic, ifham is the respondent's being silenced, not the act of silencing.

This passage defines two pivotal terms in the jadal tradition: ifham (إفحام) and ilzam (إلزام). The author clarifies their grammatical form to resolve a common confusion. Let's unpack the precise definitions and the grammatical analysis that pins down the meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Locate in the Arabic where the author defines ifham and ilzam. Ifham is the 'incapacity of the respondent (mu'allal)' and ilzam is the 'incapacity of the questioner (sa'il)'. Note that ifham also applies to the definer and divider.
The terms are defined by their subject: mu'allal for ifham, sa'il for ilzam.
2
Understand the passive nuance
The author states that ifham and ilzam are 'masdar mabni lil maf'ul' (verbal nouns built for the passive). This means they denote an action received by the subject, not performed. So 'ifham' is the state of being silenced, not the act of silencing.
This is crucial: the grammatical form inverts the expected active meaning.
3
Analyze the idafa construction
The phrase 'إفحام المعلل' is an idafa (construct state) where the masdar (ifham) is added to its object (al-mu'allal). In a passive masdar, the object is the one who undergoes the action. Therefore, 'إفحام المعلل' means 'the respondent's being silenced', not 'the respondent's silencing of someone else'.
This grammatical analysis resolves the potential objection raised in the passage.
4
Verify the resolution of the objection
The author mentions an objection: if ifham is the act of silencing, how can it also mean the incapacity? The answer: because the masdar is passive and added to its object, it refers to the state of the object (the respondent) after the action, i.e., his incapacity.
The same logic applies to ilzam: إلزام السائل means 'the questioner's being forced to concede'.
5
Apply to ilzam analogously
Repeat the same analysis for ilzam. Identify that 'إلزام السائل' is also a passive masdar added to its object (al-sa'il), so it means the questioner's being obliged, not his act of obliging.
This symmetry is intentional and underscores the precision of dialectical terminology.
