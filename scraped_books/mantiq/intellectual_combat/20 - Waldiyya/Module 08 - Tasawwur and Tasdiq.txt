The Threefold Division of Statements
Author divides what one says into three: definition, division, and proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between definition, division, and proposition in any Islamic logical or juridical text.

Core Idea
Every meaningful statement in Islamic logic is either a definition, a division, or a proposition.

This passage establishes the foundational taxonomy of statements used in disputation. Before constructing arguments, you must classify what you are saying: are you defining a term, dividing a genus into species, or making a claim that can be true or false? Understanding this tripartite division is essential for navigating texts in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and logic itself. The author also explains why he avoids the term 'kalām' and uses 'shay'' instead, and why he begins with 'iʿlam'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage states that whatever you say is either a definition (taʿrīf), a division (taqsīm), or a proposition (taṣdīq). Write down these three terms in Arabic and English and note their function: definition clarifies a concept, division partitions a genus, proposition is a truth-bearing statement.
2
Understand the choice of 'shay'' over 'kalām'
The author deliberately avoids the common expression 'when you say a speech (kalām)' because it might imply that the division includes the speech itself (a self-referential problem). Instead he says 'when you say something (shay')', which is more general and avoids embarrassment. This shows the precision required in logical terminology.
3
Recognize the rhetorical device 'iʿlam'
The passage notes the use of 'iʿlam' (know) as an attention-getter, often followed by 'fa-' for emphasis. This signals that the following content is important and must be memorized. It is addressed generally to all seekers of benefit.
4
Clarify the meaning of 'taṣdīq'
The passage explains that 'taṣdīq' here means the proposition itself (the thing assented to), not the mental act of assent. It is synonymous with 'qadiyya' (proposition) by convention. This resolves a common confusion between the linguistic expression and the epistemological act.

Tahrir al-Murad: Clarifying Intended Meaning
Defining tahrir al-murad and its role in dialectic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tahrir al-murad and explain its role in resolving dialectical objections.

Core Idea
Tahrir al-murad prevents disputes by revealing the intended meaning behind words.

In dialectical debates, the literal meaning of a word may not match the speaker's intention. Tahrir al-murad is the process of clarifying that intention, often used to counter objections based on apparent meaning. This lesson introduces its definition and purpose.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic meaning
Linguistically, tahrir al-murad means 'clarification of intent' either by speech or writing. It is a broad term for making clear what is meant.
2
Learn the terminological definition
Technically, it is when the speaker intends a meaning not apparent from the word itself, so that the opponent's objection is based on the apparent meaning. This shows its defensive use.
3
Identify when tahrir al-murad is needed
It is used when the intended meaning is hidden or when the opponent misunderstands due to context or other reasons. The author lists cases: meaning apparent from word or context, or objection due to a mistaken assumption.
4
See the example from the passage
The author discusses using a general term for a specific instance, and how tahrir al-murad can clarify whether the usage is literal or metaphorical. This connects to the next lesson.

Chapter: On explaining tahrir al-murad. Know that tahrir al-murad linguistically is the clarification of the intended meaning of something absolutely, or clarification in writing just as taqrir is clarification by expression. And terminologically, it is the intention of the clarifier of a meaning that is not apparent from that word, so the objection of the questioner is based on the apparent meaning of the word. Sometimes the intended meaning is apparent from the word or from a context clue (qarina), and the questioner's objection is based on imagining contrary to the apparent due to some reason. Like intending the specific meaning from the general word: before this intention it is literal (haqiqa), and it is said it is an imperfect literal, and it is said it is metaphorical (majaz), and it is said if the general is applied to the specific considering its generality, it is literal, and if applied considering its specificity, it is metaphorical. And this is the meaning of their saying that 'man' for example, if applied to an individual of its individuals insofar as he is an individual of it, it is literal, and if applied to an individual by the relation of generality and specificity, it is metaphorical in it. Al-Muhaqqiq al-Taftazani said: If the word of the general is applied to the specific not considering its specificity but considering its generality, it is not metaphorical at all, as when you see Zayd and say 'I saw a man' or 'I saw a person' - the word 'man' or 'person' is not used except for what it was coined for, but it happens to apply externally to Zayd. And it is understood from this that if it is applied to the specific considering its specificity, it is metaphorical by the context of opposition, for example if we divide 'breathing' into human and animal, and someone objects that it would necessitate that a part of a thing be a part of it. The answer is that by 'animal' is meant what is other than human, by the context of mentioning it in opposition to human. But it is not valid to intend the metaphor absolutely, i.e., the metaphorical meaning. And it is said that the description of the meaning as metaphorical is a metaphor, naming the denoted by the name of the denoter. And 'majaz' originally is a verbal noun from 'jaza al-makan' (to pass through), transferred to the word that passes beyond its original place, or the word with which one passes, meaning that they pass with it beyond its original place. It is possible that 'majaz' is a noun of place from their saying 'ja'altu kadha majazan ila hajati' (I made such a thing a way to my need), so the metaphor is a way to conceive its meaning. And 'haqiqa' originally is a verbal adjective meaning established, from 'haqqa al-shay' (it is true) or 'haqqaqtu al-shay' (I established it), transferred to the word that is established in its original place, and the ta' is for transferring from adjective to noun, which is apparent. (Without the relation) - 'fi' with fatha is used for intellectual things, and with kasra for sensory things, and it is said the opposite. And it is the appropriate connection (munasaba) that justifies the transfer from the literal meaning to the metaphorical meaning. (Considered) i.e., observed between the two meanings; if the relation exists but is not observed, it is not metaphorical.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of tahrir al-murad (clarification of intended meaning), a critical tool in dialectical exchanges. The author defines it linguistically and terminologically, then discusses the semantic status of using a general term for a specific instance. The key debate is whether such usage is haqiqa (literal) or majaz (metaphorical). The author presents several opinions, citing al-Taftazani, who distinguishes between applying a general term to a specific instance in its general sense (which is literal) versus in its specific sense (which is metaphorical). The example of dividing 'breathing' into human and animal illustrates how contextual opposition (qarina al-muqabala) can indicate a metaphorical intent. The passage then delves into the linguistic origins of the terms 'haqiqa' and 'majaz', and stresses that for a metaphorical usage to be valid, there must be an observed relation (munasaba) between the literal and metaphorical meanings. This chapter is foundational for understanding how logicians and dialecticians handle ambiguity and defend interpretations.

Haqiqa and Majaz in General-to-Specific Usage
Distinguishing haqiqa and majaz in general-to-specific applications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqa and majaz when a general term is applied to a specific instance, using the criterion of considering generality vs specificity.

Core Idea
Applying a general term to a specific is haqiqa if used in the general sense, and majaz if used in the specific sense.

When you say 'I saw a man' referring to Zayd, are you using 'man' literally or metaphorically? The author and al-Taftazani clarify that it depends on whether you are considering the generality or specificity of the term. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the issue
When a general term is used for a specific individual (e.g., 'man' for Zayd), scholars debate whether it is literal (haqiqa) or metaphorical (majaz). The passage presents multiple opinions.
2
Extract al-Taftazani's key distinction
Al-Taftazani distinguishes: if the general is applied considering its generality (i.e., the meaning includes all individuals), it is haqiqa. If applied considering its specificity (i.e., focusing on that particular individual), it is majaz.
The example: seeing Zayd and saying 'I saw a man' is haqiqa because 'man' is used in its general sense.
3
Apply with the division example
When dividing 'breathing' into human and animal, using 'animal' to mean 'non-human' is majaz due to the context of opposition (qarina al-muqabala). The intended meaning is not the literal 'animal' but 'other than human'.
4
Check the condition for valid majaz
The passage concludes that for a usage to be majaz, there must be an observed relation (munasaba) between the literal and metaphorical meanings. If the relation exists but is not observed, it is not considered majaz.

The Seven Certain Premises: Intellect and Illusion
Author enumerates and defines the seven types of axiomatic premises (badihiyyat).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven types of certain premises (badihiyyat) and classify them according to whether the judge of truth is the intellect or the illusion.

Core Idea
All certain knowledge rests on self-evident premises that require no external proof.

This passage from al-Waldiyya enumerates the seven types of axiomatic premises that form the bedrock of demonstrative reasoning. Understanding these types is crucial for constructing valid arguments in any Islamic science, because every demonstration ultimately traces back to these self-evident starting points.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two judges of truth
The passage states that the judge of a proposition's truth is either the intellect or the illusion. Recognize this primary division as the basis for classifying the seven types.
The intellect covers six types; illusion covers only one.
2
List the subcategories under intellect
Under intellect, identify awaliyyat (primary axioms), qadaya qiyasatuhu ma'aha (innate syllogistic), mushahadat (observed, subdivided into external and internal), mutawatirat (recurrent), mujarrabat (experiential), and hadsiyyat (intuitive). Note the definitions and examples for each.
Mushahadat split into sensory (external) and wijdaniyyat (internal).
3
Understand the single category under illusion
The illusion (wahm) provides wahmiyyat, which are certain only about physical things. Recognize the example: 'every body is in a direction' is true for physicals, but 'every existent is in a direction' is false for abstracts.
This distinction is crucial in kalam for discussions about Allah's attributes.
4
Analyze Al-Sharif's epistemological ranking
Al-Sharif ranks the types by clarity: awaliyyat are clearest, then innate syllogistic, then mushahadat, then wahmiyyat. He notes that mujarrabat, hadsiyyat, and mutawatirat are evidence for oneself but not proof for others unless shared conditions hold.
This ranking affects how premises are used in debate (jadal).

Illusion-Based Certainty: Wahmiyyat in Physical vs. Abstract Domains
Author enumerates and defines the seven types of axiomatic premises (badihiyyat).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the judgments of illusion (wahm) on physical and abstract matters, and explain why wahmiyyat provide certainty only in the physical domain.

Core Idea
The illusion faculty gives certainty about physical things but fails in abstract matters.

This lesson zooms in on the seventh type of certain premise—wahmiyyat—which arise from the illusion faculty. The passage contrasts the reliable judgment of illusion regarding physical objects with its false extension to abstracts. This distinction is critical in Islamic theology to avoid attributing physical attributes to Allah.

Guided Path
1
Locate the discussion of wahm
In the passage, after the intellect-based types, the author turns to when the judge is the illusion (wahm). He states that wahm gives certain premises about physical things (mahsusat).
This is the only type under illusion.
2
Analyze the example provided
The example: 'every body is in a direction' is a wahmiyya that is true for physical objects. But extending to abstracts like 'every existent is in a direction' is false. Note that intellect affirms the first but denies the second.
The example shows the domain specificity of wahm.
3
Understand why wahmiyyat are certain in their proper domain
The passage says the intellect confirms wahm's judgments on physical things. This means that for physical objects, wahm is a reliable source of certainty. The reason is that wahm is designed to perceive material aspects like location.
This is epistemologically important: certainty can come from senses and illusion, not only intellect.
4
Apply to Islamic theology
In kalam, the argument that 'Allah is not in a direction' uses this distinction: since Allah is not a body, the wahm's judgment about direction does not apply. Theologians reject anthropomorphism by limiting wahmiyyat to physical beings.
This is a key tool in refuting the Mushabbiha (anthropomorphists).
