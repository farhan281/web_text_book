The Definition of Munazara: Subject and Etymology
Commentator defines munazara by its subject matter and derives its definition from its etymology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the subject of munazara determines its definition and identify the two possible etymological roots of the term.

Core Idea
Munazara's definition stems from its subject: comprehensive investigations; its name from discourse between counterparts.

This lesson explores how the science of disputation (munazara) is defined. The commentator explains that sciences are distinguished by their subjects, so the definition of munazara must begin with its subject. However, since the subject itself (investigation) is identical to the term munazara, the author provides an etymological definition instead. You will learn how the subject of a science governs its definition and how etymology can serve as an alternative when subject-based definition is circular.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of subject in defining a science
The commentator states that sciences are differentiated by their subjects. The subject is the essential unifying aspect that governs the entire science. For example, in usul al-fiqh, the subject is the evidence (adilla) from which rulings are derived.
2
Identify the subject of munazara
The subject is 'al-abhath al-kulliyya' (comprehensive investigations) that include particular investigations. This is derived from the essence of munazara.
Note that investigation (bahth) is central.
3
Recognize the circularity problem
Since the conception of 'investigation' is identical to the conception of 'disputation', defining munazara by its subject would be circular. So the author turns to etymology.
4
Analyze the etymological definition
The term munazara is derived either from 'nazir' (counterpart), indicating two opposing parties, or from 'nazar' (sight), indicating intellectual insight. Both roots capture essential aspects of disputation.

Commentary
This passage provides the definition of munazara (disputation). The commentator explains the methodology: since sciences are distinguished by their subjects, and the subject of science is the essential unifying aspect, it is appropriate to begin by stating the subject. However, in the case of munazara, its subject (comprehensive investigations) is derived from the definition of 'investigation' itself, which is synonymous with 'disputation'. Therefore, the author of the matn defined munazara etymologically instead: it is derived from either 'nazir' (counterpart, indicating that it involves two opposing parties) or from 'nazar' (seeing, indicating the intellectual insight used in argumentation). This definition serves to give a broader understanding rather than a strict subject-predicate definition.

The Four Causes in the Definition of Munazara
Author reveals four causes in definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the formal, material, efficient, and final causes as they are implicitly contained in the definition of munazara.

Core Idea
A comprehensive definition implicitly includes all four causes.

The author points out a subtlety: the definition of munazara—'mutual refutation between mu'allil and sa'il for truth'—beautifully contains the four causes. By analyzing the terms, you can extract each cause. This lesson will walk you through that extraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition being analyzed
Locate the definition of munazara that the author praises: it involves 'mudafa'a' (refutation) between mu'allil and sa'il aiming at 'izhar al-haqq' (manifestation of truth).
The definition is the one the author favors over the famous one.
2
Extract the formal cause
The term 'mudafa'a' indicates the formal cause—the form of the argument or refutation itself.
Formal cause is the structure or essence.
3
Extract the material cause
The relation (nisba) understood from 'mudafa'a' indicates the material cause—the subject matter or content of the dispute.
Material cause is the underlying substance or topic.
4
Extract the efficient cause
The mu'allil (respondent) and sa'il (objector) are the efficient causes—the agents who perform the refutation.
Efficient cause is the agent or source of change.
5
Extract the final cause
The phrase 'izhar al-haqq' (manifestation of truth) indicates the final cause—the purpose or goal of the munazara.
Final cause is the end for which the action is done.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the definition of munazara. The author first points out a subtlety: the new definition (as mutual refutation between sa'il and mu'allil for truth) does not apply to disputes about definitions and divisions, but it can be extended. Then he highlights the elegance of the definition: it implicitly contains the four causes (formal, material, efficient, final) as follows: the term 'mudafa'a' indicates the form, the relation indicates the matter, the two parties indicate the agent, and the purpose (izhar al-haqq) indicates the end. This is a sign of a comprehensive definition. The author then explains why they abandoned the famous definition: 'nazar bil-basira min al-janibayn fi al-nisba bayn al-shay'ayn izhar al-sawab'. He lists three major objections. First, 'nazar' (looking/thinking) is ambiguous: if it means 'arrangement of known matters to an unknown', it excludes mere prevention (man') which is a part of munazara; if it means 'imaginative movements', it is ambiguous. Second, the word 'basira' (insight) is redundant because 'nazar bi' already implies insight. Third, 'both sides' is too general; it does not specifically indicate the mu'allil and sa'il, and general terms do not imply specific ones without a custom. The author then gives a pedagogical note: a student must know the definition (hadd or rasm) of a science before studying it, to avoid wasting time. He also notes that the mu'allil (respondent) is one who makes a statement that is analyzed, so the definer or divider is not included. The sa'il is the objector, and his refutation naturally comes first. The passage concludes with the three reasons for abandoning the famous definition, emphasizing that 'nazar' is ambiguous, 'basira' is redundant, and 'both sides' is too general. This critical refinement shows the superiority of the new definition.

Critiquing the Famous Definition of Munazara
Author critiques famous definition with three objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three logical objections against the famous definition 'nazar bil-basira min al-janibayn' and explain why each objection undermines the definition.

Core Idea
The famous definition is flawed due to ambiguity, redundancy, and overgenerality.

The author departs from the well-known definition of munazara—'insight from both sides concerning the relation between two things for the manifestation of correctness'—and gives three precise logical reasons why it fails. This lesson walks through each objection and its implications, showing the rigour required in defining Islamic scholarly disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the famous definition under critique
Locate the phrase: 'al-nazar bil-basira min al-janibayn fi al-nisba bayn al-shay'ayn izhar al-sawab'. This is the target of the critique.
This definition was widely used before the author's preferred one.
2
Analyze the first objection: ambiguity of 'nazar'
The word 'nazar' when used with the preposition 'bi' can mean either 'imaginative movements' or 'rational thought/arrangement of known matters to arrive at an unknown'. If the former, the definition contains an ambiguous term without context; if the latter, it excludes the mode of prevention (man'), which is a legitimate part of munazara.
This is a classic fallacy of ambiguity.
3
Analyze the second objection: redundancy of 'basira'
The preposition 'bi' in 'nazar bi' already implies the use of insight (basira), just as 'nazar ila' implies sight, 'nazar bi' implies anger, etc. So mentioning 'basira' explicitly is superfluous.
Redundancy in a definition violates the principle of conciseness.
4
Analyze the third objection: overgenerality of 'janibayn'
The expression 'both sides' (al-janibayn) is more general than the specific disputants mu'allil and sa'il. A general term cannot indicate a specific subset unless there is a customary usage (urf). Since the definition relies on the linguistic meaning, it fails to pinpoint the agents.
The definition is not specific enough.
5
Assess the overall critique
The three objections show that the famous definition is either ambiguous, redundant, or too broad, making it unacceptable for a rigorous definition of munazara. The author's preferred definition avoids these pitfalls.
This demonstrates the importance of precision in defining terms in Islamic logic.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the definition of munazara. The author first points out a subtlety: the new definition (as mutual refutation between sa'il and mu'allil for truth) does not apply to disputes about definitions and divisions, but it can be extended. He lists three major objections. First, 'nazar' (looking/thinking) is ambiguous: if it means 'arrangement of known matters to an unknown', it excludes mere prevention (man') which is a part of munazara; if it means 'imaginative movements', it is ambiguous. He also notes that the mu'allil (respondent) is one who makes a statement that is analyzed, so the definer or divider is not included. The sa'il is the objector, and his refutation naturally comes first. The passage concludes with the three reasons for abandoning the famous definition, emphasizing that 'nazar' is ambiguous, 'basira' is redundant, and 'both sides' is too general. This critical refinement shows the superiority of the new definition.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive explanation of the real definition (al-ta'rif al-haqiqi). It begins by stating that the second division (of definitions) is the real definition, which aims to detail (tafsil) the defined thing by mentioning the general part first and the specific part second. The general part can be the genus (jins) or the broad accident (al-'arad al-'amm), and the specific part is the differentia (fasl) or the inseparable property (al-khassa al-lazima). This definition covers all types of defined things, whether existent or non-existent in terms of quiddity. The passage then discusses the well-known requirement that the general part must precede the specific, though there are differing opinions: some say it is only obligatory for perfect definition, others say it is never obligatory. The definition is based on the impossibility of a quiddity being composed of two equal parts or of a definition being a single term. Examples are given: perfect hadd ('human is a rational animal'), imperfect hadd ('human is a body rational'), perfect rasm ('human is a laughing animal'), and imperfect rasm ('human is a bipedal naturally laughing'). The condition of equality (musawa) is central: according to later scholars and some earlier ones, the definition must be coextensive with the defined in truth; otherwise it is invalid due to lack of comprehensiveness (adam al-jam') or lack of exclusivity (adam al-man'). However, the verifying earlier scholars allowed imperfect definitions that are broader or narrower, except for perfect definitions where equality is agreed upon. The passage then clarifies the distinction between real (haqiqi) and nominal (ismi) definitions: haqiqi reveals the essence and requires the defined to exist, while ismi reveals the concept and can apply to non-existent things. It further divides definitions into verifying (tahqiqi) and alerting (tanbihi), producing eight types in total. Verbal definitions (ta'rif lafzi) belong to assent (tasdiq) because they inform about word usage, not concepts. The passage concludes by emphasizing that the eight types are all conceptual (tasawwurat), but a definition may become an assent if it functions as a claim or premise.

Types of Definitions: Real, Nominal, Verifying, and Alerting
Author classifies definitions into real/nominal and verifying/alerting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify definitions as real (haqiqi) vs nominal (ismi) and as verifying (tahqiqi) vs alerting (tanbihi), and distinguish them from verbal definitions (lafzi).

Core Idea
Definitions are classified by whether they aim at essence (real) or concept (nominal), and whether they teach new knowledge (verifying) or remind (alerting).

This lesson explores the rich taxonomy of definitions presented in the passage. The author distinguishes between real definitions (revealing essence) and nominal definitions (revealing concept), and further between verifying definitions (providing new knowledge) and alerting definitions (reminding of stored knowledge). Understanding these categories helps us appreciate the different roles definitions play in Islamic scholarship.

A real definition requires the defined to exist, whereas a nominal definition can apply to non-existents.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive explanation of the real definition (al-ta'rif al-haqiqi). It begins by stating that the second division (of definitions) is the real definition, which aims to detail (tafsil) the defined thing by mentioning the general part first and the specific part second. The general part can be the genus (jins) or the broad accident (al-'arad al-'amm), and the specific part is the differentia (fasl) or the inseparable property (al-khassa al-lazima). This definition covers all types of defined things, whether existent or non-existent in terms of quiddity. The passage then discusses the well-known requirement that the general part must precede the specific, though there are differing opinions: some say it is only obligatory for perfect definition, others say it is never obligatory. The definition is based on the impossibility of a quiddity being composed of two equal parts or of a definition being a single term. Examples are given: perfect hadd ('human is a rational animal'), imperfect hadd ('human is a body rational'), perfect rasm ('human is a laughing animal'), and imperfect rasm ('human is a bipedal naturally laughing'). The condition of equality (musawa) is central: according to later scholars and some earlier ones, the definition must be coextensive with the defined in truth; otherwise it is invalid due to lack of comprehensiveness (adam al-jam') or lack of exclusivity (adam al-man'). However, the verifying earlier scholars allowed imperfect definitions that are broader or narrower, except for perfect definitions where equality is agreed upon. The passage then clarifies the distinction between real (haqiqi) and nominal (ismi) definitions: haqiqi reveals the essence and requires the defined to exist, while ismi reveals the concept and can apply to non-existent things. It further divides definitions into verifying (tahqiqi) and alerting (tanbihi), producing eight types in total. Verbal definitions (ta'rif lafzi) belong to assent (tasdiq) because they inform about word usage, not concepts. The passage concludes by emphasizing that the eight types are all conceptual (tasawwurat), but a definition may become an assent if it functions as a claim or premise.

Division of a Whole into Its Parts
Defines division of whole into parts and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the division of a whole into its parts from the division of a universal into particulars, and verify the three conditions for a valid division.

Core Idea
Division of a whole into parts is analysis, not predication.

This passage defines a fundamental logical operation: breaking a composite whole into its constituent parts. Unlike dividing a universal into its particular instances, this division does not allow each part to be predicated of the whole. You will learn the three conditions that make such a division valid and why common mistakes arise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'taksim al-kull ila ajza'ihi' (division of the whole into its parts). Find the Arabic definition: 'huwa tahsil mahiyyat al-muqassam bi dhikr ajza'ih'.
The definition focuses on attaining the quiddity of the whole by listing all its parts.
2
Understand what the division is NOT
The author emphasizes that this division does not involve adding restrictions to the parts (laisa fihi damm quyud) and that the disjunctive particle 'or' cannot be used because parts are not predicable of the whole.
Contrast with division of universal into particulars where each part (e.g., 'man') is predicable of the whole ('animal').
3
Examine the predication issue
Parts may share quiddity with the whole (e.g., a drop of water and water), but this does not guarantee predication. Why? Because 'Zayd' and 'Amr' share quiddity but cannot be predicated of each other.
Mental parts are predicated from the aspect of unity, not particularity.
4
Learn the three conditions
The passage mentions three conditions for absolute division: collection (al-jam') – all parts are included; prevention (al-man') – no non-part is included; mutual exclusivity (al-tabayun) – parts do not overlap.
The author notes that these are conditions for absolute division, and the passage's earlier statement was general.
5
Apply to an example
Take the division of a prayer (salat) into its integral parts: takbir, ruku', sujud, etc. Check the three conditions: Are all essential parts collected? Is there any non-essential included? Are they mutually exclusive?
This example is from Fiqh – the conditions ensure a sound division.

General and Specific: Real vs Metaphorical Usage
Explanation of real vs metaphorical use of general terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real and metaphorical usage when a general term is used for a specific meaning, applying al-Taftazani's criterion of whether the generality or particularity is considered.

Core Idea
Using a general term for a specific instance is real if you consider its generality; metaphorical if you consider its particularity.

A common scenario in disputation is using a general term to refer to a specific individual. Scholars disagreed on whether this is real or metaphorical. Al-Taftazani provides a clear distinction based on the consideration of generality vs particularity. This lesson unpacks that distinction with examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem: intending specific from general
Read the part where the author mentions that intending the specific meaning from a general term is debated: some say it is real, some say it is a deficient reality, some say it is metaphorical, and some make a conditional distinction.
The example of 'man' used for Zayd is central.
2
Extract al-Taftazani's distinction
Al-Taftazani states: if the general term is used for the specific 'with consideration of its generality' (i.e., you mean the general class and it happens to apply to this individual), it is not metaphor. If used 'with consideration of its particularity', it is metaphor.
The Arabic: 'if it is used with consideration of its generality, it is real; if with consideration of its particularity, it is metaphorical'.
3
Apply to the example of 'I saw a man' for Zayd
When you see Zayd and say 'I saw a man', you are not using 'man' metaphorically because you intend the general class, and Zayd happens to fall under it. The word is used in its true meaning.
Al-Taftazani's example: 'If you see Zayd and say I saw a man, the word man is not used metaphorically.'
4
Analyze the example of dividing 'breathing' into human and animal
The passage gives a disputational example: dividing 'breathing' into 'human' and 'animal'. An objector says this makes 'animal' part of 'breathing' (since 'animal' is a part of the division). The response uses Tahreer al-Murad: 'animal' here means 'other than human' by the contextual indicator of contrast.
This shows that the general term 'animal' is used for a specific (non-human animals) with consideration of particularity, hence metaphorical, and the contextual indicator (contrast) validates it.

Overall, the passage serves as a thorough guide on how and when a disputant can clarify that a non-apparent meaning was intended, emphasizing the conditions of a proper relationship and contextual indicator.

Conditions for Valid Metaphor: Relationship and Contextual Indicator
Conditions for valid metaphorical intention in Tahreer al-Murad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two necessary conditions for a valid metaphorical meaning in Tahreer al-Murad: a considered relationship and a contextual indicator, and explain why both are required.

Core Idea
For a metaphor to be valid in Tahreer al-Murad, there must be a considered (noticed) relationship between the meanings and a contextual indicator that prevents the real meaning.

A metaphorical usage is not arbitrary. The passage lays out two essential conditions: a relationship (علاقة) that connects the literal and metaphorical meanings, and a contextual indicator (قرينة) that reveals which meaning is intended. This lesson breaks down these conditions and their application.

Guided Path
1
Extract the condition from the text
Find the statement: 'It is not correct to intend the metaphorical meaning without being certain of the valid considered relationship mentioned in the science of eloquence.' Note the key words: 'valid relationship' and 'considered' (i.e., noticed).
المجاز requires 'العلاقة المصححة المعتبرة' (valid considered relationship).
2
Understand the relationship (علاقة)
The relationship is the connection between the literal and metaphorical meanings that justifies the transfer. Examples: similarity (شبه), effect (سببية), etc. Without a relationship, no metaphor exists.
The passage lists many types, but focus here on the requirement.
3
Understand the contextual indicator (قرينة)
The contextual indicator is what reveals the intended meaning and prevents the literal meaning. It can be situational or verbal. Without it, the utterance would be ambiguous or misinterpreted.
The passage states: 'the contextual indicator prevents the intention of the real meaning'.
4
Apply to an example
Take the metaphor 'Zayd is a lion' (for a brave man). The relationship is similarity. The contextual indicator is the fact that Zayd is a man, not a lion, which prevents the literal meaning.
The condition: both relationship and indicator must be present and noticed.

The Contextual Indicator and Its Scenarios
Requirements for contextual indicator based on the role of the clarifier

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the requirement for a contextual indicator in Tahreer al-Murad when the respondent is the original speaker versus a third party, and when the respondent is providing a proof.

Core Idea
If the respondent is the original speaker, his own statement 'I meant this' is a strong contextual indicator; otherwise, an external indicator is required.

The requirement for a contextual indicator depends on who is performing Tahreer al-Murad. This lesson examines the two main scenarios: when the clarifier is the original speaker and when he is a third party. The passage also discusses the special case of providing a proof (istidlal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the part starting from 'As for the contextual indicator... it is not obligatory for him... if the clarifier is preventing... whether he is the person himself or someone else.' Note the distinction.
Two cases: 1) The respondent is the original speaker (المعلل), 2) The respondent is someone else (شخص غيره).
2
Understand the case when the respondent is the original speaker
When the original speaker says 'I meant this', that statement itself is among the strongest preventing contextual indicators. No external indicator is needed.
The text: 'If the respondent is the person himself, his statement 'I meant this' is among the strongest preventing contextual indicators.'
3
Understand the case when the respondent is a third party
If a third party responds on behalf of the original speaker, he must rely on a contextual indicator from the original utterance to show that the intended meaning was not the apparent one.
This is the typical case in disputation where a defender clarifies a scholar's statement.
4
Special case: when the respondent is providing a proof (مستدل)
If the respondent is using Tahreer al-Murad as a premise in an argument, then the metaphorical usage must have a contextual indicator that prevents the real meaning, regardless of who is speaking.
This adds a stricter requirement: the indicator must be present, not just the speaker's intention.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and explanatory section from the Waldiyya, focusing on the concept of 'Tahreer al-Murad' (تحرير المراد) — the clarification of intended meaning in disputation. The author begins by stating the importance of this concept for answering objections, then provides a linguistic and terminological definition. In language, Tahreer al-Murad means expressing the intended meaning absolutely or in writing. In terminology, it is the act of the respondent (whether he is the objector, the person himself, or someone else) intending a real or metaphorical meaning that is not apparent from the utterance. This causes the questioner's objection to be based on the apparent meaning, while the intended meaning might be apparent from the utterance or from a contextual indicator. The author discusses the case of intending a specific meaning from a general term, explaining different opinions on whether it is real or metaphorical. He cites al-Taftazani, who clarifies that if the general term is used for the specific with consideration of generality, it is not metaphor; e.g., saying 'I saw a man' when seeing Zayd is real usage. However, if the general term is used with consideration of particularity (e.g., by a contextual indicator of contrast), it becomes metaphor. The author gives an example: dividing 'breathing' into human and animal leads to an objection that part of a thing (animal) is a part of the same thing; the response is that 'animal' here means 'other than human' due to the contextual indicator of contrast. The passage then delves into the definition of metaphor (مجاز) and reality (حقيقة), their etymology, and the condition for metaphor: a valid relationship (علاقة) and a contextual indicator (قرينة). The relationship must be noticed; without it, there is no metaphor. The author then enumerates twenty-eight types of relationships (according to one count), including similarity, effect, manifestation, adjacency, partial/whole, causality, condition, etc., and notes that al-Taftazani raised the count to twenty-five, while the author of al-Tawdih summarized them into nine. After listing examples, the author restricts that one cannot intend an arbitrary meaning (e.g., 'horse' from 'book') without a proper relationship. Next, the discussion turns to the contextual indicator (القرينة) that prevents intending the real meaning. The author distinguishes between cases where the clarifier is the person himself versus someone else; if the clarifier is the person, his statement 'I meant this' is a strong contextual indicator. The passage ends with a nuanced discussion on the scope of metaphor, comparing the terminology of early scholars (who include metonymy) and later scholars (who distinguish them). The author argues that metonymy (كناية) allows the intention of the real meaning, so clarification with it is problematic, but some metonymies prevent the real meaning due to the particular word (e.g., 'There is nothing like Him'). Overall, the passage serves as a thorough guide on how and when a disputant can clarify that a non-apparent meaning was intended, emphasizing the conditions of a proper relationship and contextual indicator.
