The Three Categories of Statements: Definition, Division, and Assent
Commentary explaining the threefold classification of statements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given statement as a definition, a division, or a proposition (assent) based on the criteria established in this passage.

Core Idea
Every utterance is either a definition, a division, or a proposition—the three fundamental categories of logical discourse.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the matn's claim that when you say something, it falls into one of three categories: definition, division, or assent. These categories are foundational because they determine how we analyze statements in logic and other Islamic sciences. Let's explore each category and how to distinguish them, noting the commentator's clarifications on real vs. verbal definition and the term 'tasdiq'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of utterance
Ask: Is this statement giving a definition of a term? If yes, it is a definition (ta'rif). Is it dividing a concept into parts or species? If yes, it is a division (taqsim). Is it asserting a claim that can be true or false? If yes, it is a proposition (tasdiq).
The commentator notes that 'definition' unqualified means real definition (hadd haqiqi); verbal definitions fall under assent.
2
Distinguish real vs. verbal definition
Real definition (ta'rif haqiqi) reveals the essence of a thing. Verbal definition (ta'rif lafzi) simply explains the meaning of a word. The passage states that verbal definitions are considered under assent, not under definition.
This distinction prevents confusion when analyzing statements that seem to define but are only explications of terms.
3
Understand 'tasdiq' as proposition
The term 'tasdiq' (assent) is here applied to the proposition (qadiyya) itself. The commentator explains this usage either as naming the known by the name of knowledge (Imam al-Razi) or as part for whole (philosophers).
Be alert to this shift: later texts may use 'tasdiq' for the act of assent, but here it means the proposition.
4
Check for subtypes of speech
The passage also mentions incomplete compounds (murakkab naqis) and complete constructional compounds (murakkab tamm insha'). These are not the three categories but further subdivisions of speech. The three categories focus on the content, not the grammatical form.
The conditional itself is a complete compound, but the classification into definition/division/assent depends on its meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the matn's statement: 'If you say something, it is either a definition, a division, or an assent.' The commentator begins by explaining the word 'i'lam' (know) as a general call to attention for any seeker of benefit. He then analyzes the phrase 'idha qulta shay'an' (when you say something), noting that 'shay'an' means a word (lafz), and if it is unspecified (muhmal) it is according to the Mizaniyyun, or speech (kalam) meaning a word that indicates by convention, which is linguistic speech. The tanwin in 'shay'an' indicates diversification. If the 'if' (idha) is quantified with universality, as the grammarians hold, then the resulting conditional proposition is not itself a subject-matter of the sciences but a preamble, so one cannot object that scientific propositions must be universal affirmative categoricals. The commentator then explains the division: 'fadha' (that thing said) is either a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi), since the unqualified term 'definition' immediately brings to mind real definition as opposed to verbal; verbal definition falls under assent. Or it is a division (taqsim). Or it is an assent (tasdiq), i.e., a proposition (qadiyya). The commentator clarifies the term 'tasdiq' as applied to a proposition: according to Imam al-Razi, it is like using the name of knowledge for the known object; according to the philosophers, it is naming the whole by its part. Alternatively, if 'tasdiq' originally means 'assent to it', then the application is that assent is true of it. Next, the commentator expands on the types of speech: an incomplete compound (murakkab naqis) is where part indicates part of the meaning and one cannot stop; a single word (mufrad) is the opposite; a complete compound of the type of construction (murakkab tamm insha') allows stopping and does not admit truth/falsity. The word 'idha' (if) is for ignoring (ihmal), so it is not objected that the division excludes letters and insignificant words, as both are included in 'thing'. Then a potential objection is raised: if this conditional statement were a scientific issue, it would have to be a universal affirmative categorical (since all scientific issues are according to authorities), but it is a conditional with universal negative quantifier (muhmala), so it cannot be an issue. The commentator refutes: we deny that it is truly conditional; it may be interpreted as categorical. Also, scientific issues need not be universal; they can be particular or individual. The claim that universal negatives of sciences are universal is itself based on majority and is a universal negative statement. Finally, an answer is given: this conditional is not an issue but a preface. Throughout, the commentator uses examples from the Islamic sciences: references to 'the Shaykh' (likely Ibn Sina or al-Razi) and the formulation of propositions typical in kalam. The passage thus guards against misunderstanding the status of introductory statements in the sciences.

Recognizing a Preamble vs. a Scientific Issue
Commentary defending the preambular status of the opening conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a preamble (tawti'a) from a scientific issue (mas'ala) in a logical text based on the criteria discussed in this passage.

Core Idea
Not every logical statement is a scientific issue; some are preambles that set the stage for the real topics.

In this passage, the commentator vigorously defends the opening conditional statement against the objection that it cannot be a scientific issue because it is not a universal affirmative categorical. He argues that it is actually a preamble (tawti'a), not a mas'ala. This distinction is crucial for understanding the structure of logical works and for correctly interpreting introductory statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether the statement is presented as a scientific issue or a preface
Look for explicit markers: the commentator states 'this proposition is not among the issues of the sciences but rather a preface to them'. If the author says similar words, treat it as a preamble.
The passage also says 'this conditional is not an issue but a preface to the issues' at the end.
2
Check the form: conditional vs. categorical
The objection claims that scientific issues must be universal affirmative categorical (hamliyya). The opening statement is a conditional (shartiyya) with a universal quantifier (muhmala). The commentator counters that it can be interpreted as categorical.
Understanding the distinction between conditional and categorical propositions is essential.
3
Examine the function: does it establish a new principle or does it frame the discussion?
A preamble sets the stage for the issues that follow; it is not itself a subject-matter. The commentator emphasizes that this conditional serves as a preamble, not as a substantive claim.
In many logical texts, introductory remarks are not considered part of the subject-matter.
4
Assess the necessity of universality for scientific issues
The objection presupposes that all scientific issues must be universal. The commentator denies this, allowing particular or even individual issues. So even if a statement is not universal, it could still be a mas'ala—but the opening statement is still a preamble.
This shows that the classification depends on authorial intention, not just form.

Two Meanings of the Particular: Real and Relative
Author explains the two senses of 'particular' and relates them to the universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the real particular (al-juz'ī al-ḥaqīqī) and the relative particular (al-juz'ī al-iḍāfī), and identify their respective relationships with the universal.

Core Idea
The particular has two faces: one never universal, the other universal in disguise.

In logic, the term 'particular' is not one-dimensional. The author distinguishes two senses: the real particular, which can never be predicated of many, and the relative particular, which is particular only in relation to a higher concept. This distinction is crucial for correctly applying the five universals and for understanding how definitions work in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Locate in the Arabic where the author gives the two meanings of 'al-juz'ī': 'ما يمتنع فرض صدقه على كثيرين' (what is impossible to suppose its truth about many) and 'ما يندرج تحت شيء آخر بالفعل أو بالإمكان في نفس الأمر' (what falls under another thing actually or potentially in reality).
2
Understand the real particular
The first meaning is the real particular, like the individual Zayd. It cannot be predicated of more than one. Its relationship with the universal is total opposition (tabāyun kullī) — no shared instances.
Example: Zayd is a real particular; the concept 'human' is universal.
3
Understand the relative particular
The second meaning is the relative particular, like 'human' under 'animal'. It falls under something higher and may itself be universal relative to lower individuals.
Example: 'human' is particular relative to 'animal', but universal relative to Zayd.
4
Compare relationships with the universal
The real particular has total opposition with the universal. The relative particular has a relationship of 'generality and specificity in some respect' (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh) — they overlap partially.
For example, 'human' and 'animal' overlap (all humans are animals, but not all animals are humans).
5
Apply the distinction to new terms
Given a term, determine if it is a real particular, relative particular, or universal. For instance, 'the Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ)' is a real particular; 'mammal' is a universal; 'horse' is a relative particular under 'animal'.
Practice with terms from fiqh: 'the specific prayer of Fajr' (real particular) vs. 'prayer' in general (relative particular under 'acts of worship').

Commentary

The first is the real particular (al-juz'ī al-ḥaqīqī): that which cannot be predicated of many, like the individual Zayd. Its relation to the universal is total opposition (tabāyun kullī), since they share no instance. This is more general (aʿamm) than the first meaning. The relation between a relative particular and the universal is 'generality and specificity in some respect' (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh), meaning they intersect: some relative particulars are universal in another context. The passage concludes 'faʿrif dhālika al-maʿnā' (so know that meaning), emphasizing the need to internalize this distinction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conceptual division only requires distinction in meaning, not in external reality.

In this lesson, we encounter a common objection to the division of universals: if one thing can be all five, isn't the division invalid? The author answers by distinguishing real division from conceptual division, introducing the key notion of i'tibar (consideration). Through a detailed example, you will see how a single entity like 'colored' can be classified under all five universals without contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's syllogism
The objector argues: (1) Any division with overlapping parts is invalid. (2) The five-universals division has overlapping parts (e.g., 'colored' is genus, species, etc.). (3) Therefore, the division is invalid. Locate this reasoning in the text.
The objector mistakes conceptual division for real division.
2
Find the author's denial of the major premise
The author does not deny overlap but denies the major premise by stating that this is a conceptual division (taqsim i'tibari), not a real one. For conceptual division, only conceptual distinction is required.
