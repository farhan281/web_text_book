Definition of Tasdiq and Its Interpretations
Defines tasdiq and its various interpretations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the definition of tasdiq as a complete composite admitting truth and falsity and identify the four interpretations for calling the proposition 'tasdiq'.

Core Idea
Tasdiq is the proposition itself, not just the act of assent.

In this lesson, we explore how the term 'tasdiq' is defined in the context of munazara. The passage from al-Waldiyya clarifies that tasdiq refers to the proposition (qadiyya) itself—a complete composite that can be true or false. It then offers four interpretations for this usage, ranging from customary reality to metaphorical extension. Understanding this definition is crucial for grasping the subsequent discussions on claims and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined: 'tasdiq'. The passage equates it with 'qadiyya' (proposition).
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The definition states: 'a complete composite that admits truth and falsity.' Here, the genus is 'complete composite' and the differentia is 'admits truth and falsity'.
In the Aristotelian tradition, this is a hadd (definition) by genus and differentia.
3
Analyze the four interpretations
The passage lists four ways to interpret calling the proposition 'tasdiq': customary reality, part-for-whole metonymy, applying the name of knowledge to the known, and interpreting tasdiq as 'that which is assented to'. Identify each in the text.
These interpretations show the fluidity of terminology.
4
Assess which interpretation the author favors
The author ends with 'fa-fham' (understand), suggesting all are acceptable but not specifying a preference.
This indicates a pragmatic approach to terminology.

The third chapter of the three chapters on explaining the discussion that takes place concerning tasdiq, i.e., the proposition (qadiyya), which is a complete composite that admits truth and falsity. Applying the term tasdiq to the proposition is either a customary reality (haqiqa 'urfiyya) or of the type of applying the name of the science to a part of it to the whole according to the philosophers (hukama'), or of the type of applying the name of knowledge to the known according to the Imam, or considering tasdiq in the sense of 'that which is assented to' and so it is applied to it—understand. Al-Taftazani said in al-Talwih: Know that the composite that admits truth and falsity is called, from the aspect of its containing a judgment, a proposition (qadiyya); and from the aspect of its admitting truth and falsity, an informative statement (khabar); and from the aspect of its conveying a judgment, an assertion (ikhbar); and from the aspect of its being a part of a proof, a premise (muqaddima); and from the aspect of being sought by a proof, a sought conclusion (matlub); and from the aspect of being obtained from a proof, a result (natija); and from the aspect of occurring in a science and being asked about, a problem (mas'ala). The essence is one, and the difference of expressions is due to difference of aspects. Commentary
This passage is a definitional exposition of the term tasdiq (assent/affirmation) and its various designations within the context of munazara (dialectical debate). The author begins by identifying the third chapter as dealing with the discussion that takes place concerning tasdiq, which is equated with the proposition (qadiyya)—a complete composite that can be true or false. He then notes that calling the proposition 'tasdiq' can be understood in several ways: either as a customary usage, or as a part-for-whole metonymy (as the philosophers do), or as applying the name of knowledge to the known (following the Imam), or by interpreting tasdiq as 'that which is assented to'. The author cites al-Taftazani's al-Talwih to show that the same reality (the composite admitting truth/falsity) receives different names depending on the aspect considered: qadiyya (from containing a judgment), khabar (from admitting truth/falsity), ikhbar (from conveying judgment), muqaddima (as part of a proof), matlub (as sought by proof), natija (as obtained from proof), and mas'ala (as occurring in a science). All refer to the same entity. The passage then explains that tasdiq, when uttered by someone, is called 'da'wa' (claim) or 'al-mudda'a' (the claimed), whether explicit or implicit, theoretical or intuitive. It emphasizes that insha' (performatives) are not included because they are not subject to truth/falsity and thus not called claims. The speaker of tasdiq is called 'al-mu'allil' (the reasoner) because it is his responsibility to provide a reason (ta'lil) for the claim. Ta'lil is defined as explaining the cause of something, similar to inference (istidlal), whether a priori (limmi) or a posteriori (inni). The passage closes by restating that the speaker has the right to provide an explanation that shows the intermediary through which assent to the sought conclusion is obtained, and that this is synonymous with inference.

The Seven Designations of a Proposition in Munazara
Al-Taftazani's enumeration of seven designations for a proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven terms used for a single proposition based on different aspects and explain the principle of aspectual designation.

Core Idea
One reality, many names based on aspect.

In this lesson, we examine a key passage from al-Taftazani's al-Talwih, quoted in the text, which demonstrates that a single proposition (the composite admitting truth/falsity) receives seven different names depending on which aspect is considered. This illustrates the precision of Islamic logical terminology and the importance of context in classifying statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the seven terms
Search the passage for the seven names: qadiyya, khabar, ikhbar, muqaddima, matlub, natija, mas'ala. List them in order of appearance.
Each term highlights a different aspect: judgment, truth-value, conveyance, role in proof, etc.
2
Match each term to its defining aspect
For each term, find the phrase introduced by 'min haythu' (from the aspect of). For example, qadiyya is from the aspect of containing a judgment (ishhimaluhu 'ala al-hukm).
3
Understand the principle of wahdat al-dhat
Al-Taftazani concludes 'the essence is one, and the difference of expressions is due to difference of aspects.' Explain this in your own words.
This is a key semantic principle: the referent is the same, but the term varies with the viewpoint.
4
Apply the principle to a new example
Think of an example from fiqh or kalam where the same object is called different names based on aspect.
E.g., the same action can be called 'wajib' (obligatory) from the aspect of duty, and 'fard' (required) from the aspect of divine command.

Da'wa, Mudda'a, Mu'allil, and Ta'lil in Munazara
Defines da'wa, mudda'a, mu'allil, and ta'lil in munazara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasdiq, da'wa, and mudda'a, and explain the obligation of ta'lil for the mu'allil.

Core Idea
Every claim in debate demands a reason from its claimant.

This lesson moves from the definition of tasdiq to its role in the dialectical context. When a tasdiq is uttered by a speaker, it becomes a 'da'wa' (claim) and is also called 'al-mudda'a' (the claimed). The speaker is then termed 'al-mu'allil' (the reasoner) because it is his right and duty to provide a 'ta'lil' (explanation of the cause) for his claim. We will unpack these interrelated terms and their significance in the structure of munazara.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transformation from tasdiq to da'wa
Find in the passage where it says 'if someone says it [tasdiq] explicitly or implicitly, it is called da'wa'. Note that the same reality is now named based on the act of utterance.
The shift is from the logical entity (tasdiq) to the communicative act (da'wa).
2
Understand the exclusion of insha'
The passage explicitly excludes performative utterances (insha') from being called da'wa. Why? Because insha' does not admit truth/falsity and thus is not a tasdiq.
This highlights that da'wa is inherently truth-apt.
3
Define mu'allil and his obligation
The speaker of the da'wa is called mu'allil, and it is his 'haqq' (right/duty) to provide ta'lil. The passage says 'whether he actually provides a reason or not' – meaning the obligation is inherent.
Ta'lil is defined as explaining the cause ('illa) of the claim.
4
Clarify the relationship between ta'lil and istidlal
The passage equates ta'lil with istidlal (inference) and mentions two types: limmi (a priori) and inni (a posteriori). This connects the dialectical obligation to logical reasoning.
Thus, providing a reason is not just any explanation but a logical inference.

Core Idea
Certainty in Islamic knowledge arises from seven distinct sources, each with its own epistemological basis.

In Islamic logic, not all premises are equal: some are certain and form the bedrock of knowledge. This passage from the Al-Waldiyya lists the seven types of certain premises, from self-evident truths to empirical generalizations and recurrent reports. Mastering this classification will help you evaluate the grounds of any proposition in theology, jurisprudence, and the sciences.

Commentary
This passage lists the seven types of certain premises (muqaddimat qat'iyya) that form the foundation of knowledge in Islamic logic. The list is standard from the later tradition, often attributed to al-Mawaqif. Each type is defined: (1) awwaliyat are basic self-evident truths like 'the whole is greater than the part'; (2) qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha are immediately known through an inherent syllogism; (3) mushahadat are sense perceptions; (4) mujarrabat are empirical generalizations from repeated experience; (5) hadsiyat are intuitive judgments, e.g., inferring the existence of a wise Creator from the order in creation; (6) mutawatirat are historically certain reports; (7) wahmiyat are judgments of the estimation (wahm) applied to physical objects, which are generally true for sensible things but can be false for abstract ones. Incomplete Composite as a Propositional Condition
Author defines incomplete composite and explains its role as a condition in categorical and conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an incomplete composite (murakkab nāqiṣ) and explain its role as a condition in categorical and conditional propositions, using examples from Islamic dialectic.

Core Idea
An incomplete composite is not a sentence; it gains meaning only as a qualifier in a proposition.

In Islamic dialectic (munāẓara), not every expression can stand alone as a complete judgment. The incomplete composite (murakkab nāqiṣ) is an expression upon which it is not permissible to pause—it does not form a full proposition. Yet when used as a condition (qayd) for the subject, predicate, or relation in a categorical or conditional proposition, it becomes a conceptual assent (taṣdīq maʿnā). This lesson defines the incomplete composite and clarifies its function through examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of murakkab nāqiṣ
The author defines it as an expression upon which it is not permissible to pause (lā yaṣiḥḥ al-sukūt ʿalayh). This means it cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. Identify what makes an expression incomplete in Arabic.
Think of phrases like 'in the house' or 'the tall man' – they are incomplete without a verb or copula.
2
Identify the three places it can qualify a proposition
The author states that the incomplete composite can be a condition for the subject (maḥkūm ʿalayh), the predicate (maḥkūm bihī), or the relation (nisba). Recognize these three roles in a proposition.
For example, in 'Zayd the just came', 'the just' is an incomplete composite qualifying the subject.
3
Analyze the example: 'This is a Roman human'
The author says that 'This is a Roman human' (hādhā insān rūmī) is equivalent to 'This is human and Roman' (hādhā insān wa rūmī). The word 'Roman' is an incomplete composite that adds a predicate without forming a separate proposition.
This demonstrates how an incomplete composite functions as a conceptual assent (taṣdīq maʿnā) rather than a full judgment.
4
Distinguish the two senses of composite
The author explains that 'composite' (murakkab) can refer to the whole (e.g., 'came in Zayd' as a phrase) or to any element entering into a composite (e.g., 'Zayd' alone). Understand that the incomplete composite in the dialectical sense is the part that enters into a larger compound.
The first sense is the entire composite; the second sense is any component.
