The Logical Adverb 'Albatta': Derivation and Meaning
Author explains the grammatical derivation and meaning of 'albatta'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the linguistic derivation of 'albatta' and explain its logical function as an adverb denoting absolute division without any disjunction.

Core Idea
'Albatta' means 'absolutely' – it cuts off any alternative or disjunction.

In the previous lesson, we encountered the word 'albatta' in logical divisions. This word is not arbitrary; it carries a deep linguistic meaning that aligns perfectly with its logical use. Understanding its derivation from 'cutting off' will help you appreciate why logicians chose this term to indicate a complete, unrestrictive division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the root meaning
The word 'albatta' comes from the root ب-ت-ت (b-t-t), meaning 'to cut off'. Recognize that the basic sense is a decisive severance.
This root is also used in words like 'batt' (cutting) and 'batatan' (completely).
2
Trace the grammatical formation
According to al-Jawhari, 'albatta' was originally a verbal noun (masdar) in the accusative, meaning 'cutting absolutely'. The definite article 'al-' was added, dropping the nunation. The hamza may be elided.
The form is irregular; it is treated as a fixed adverbial phrase.
3
Understand the logical interpretation
Al-Radi explains that 'albatta' means 'the decided statement' (al-qawl al-maqṭūʿ bihi), implying certainty and no disjunction. The lam (ل) may indicate a specific known instance, mental generality, or the genus.
In logic, it marks a division that leaves no middle ground.

Al-Jawhari said: [the word 'albatta'] is from the first and second [patterns], its pattern like 'rahmah', a verbal noun in the accusative as a source, meaning 'cutting', i.e., cutting off the unrestricted absolutely. Then the definite article was applied to it, so the nunation was dropped, and its hamza is cut off, which is contrary to analogy. It is reported that [someone?] cut its hamza. The lam is necessary in it. Al-Shaykh al-Radi said: 'albatta' means the decided statement, and it is as if the lam is originally for the covenant, meaning the known piece that has no disjunction in it. And it is possible that the lam is for mental generality or for the genus.

Commentary
This passage is a brief grammatical and etymological note on the word 'albatta' (البته), which appears in the previous discussion as an adverb meaning 'absolutely' or 'unrestrictedly'. The author cites the lexicographer al-Jawhari, explaining that the word is derived from 'batta' meaning 'to cut off', and it is used as a verbal noun (masdar) with the meaning of 'cutting off the unrestricted'. When the definite article 'al' is prefixed, the nunation is dropped, and the hamza is often elided (considered irregular). The author also quotes al-Radi, who interprets 'albatta' as meaning 'the decided statement' (al-qawl al-maqtu' bihi), implying certainty and lack of disjunction. The lam (ل) may indicate the covenant (al-'ahd), mental generality, or the genus. This note serves to clarify the precise meaning of the term used in logic.
