Linguistic Analysis of the Basmala
Linguistic analysis of the basmala phrase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the basmala as either a nominal or verbal sentence and explain the function of the ba' (preposition) in it.

Core Idea
The basmala is not fixed; its grammatical analysis reveals deep theological subtleties.

The commentator begins by explaining why the author used 'bismillah' without the common addition 'al-rahman al-rahim'. He then analyzes the phrase as either a nominal sentence ('my beginning is with the name of Allah') or a verbal sentence ('I begin with the name of Allah'). The choice affects the status of the adverb and the function of the ba'. Understanding this analysis is crucial for appreciating the precision of Islamic scholarly discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
Notice the commentator presents the basmala as either a nominal sentence (Basran view) or a verbal sentence (Kufan view). Look for the Arabic phrases 'جملة اسمية' and 'جملة فعلية'.
The nominal sentence implies a state, the verbal an act.
2
Determine the adverbial status
On the nominal interpretation, the adverb 'bismillah' is stable (mustaqirr); on the verbal, it is not. Reflect on how this changes the meaning.
Stable adverb indicates a circumstantial meaning.
3
Analyze the ba'
The commentator lists three possible functions: mulabasa, musahaba, isti'ana. Find these terms and consider how each would affect the meaning.
Focus on the rebuttal of the objection that isti'ana would make Allah's name a tool.
4
Evaluate the rebuttal
The commentator refutes the objection by arguing that Allah's name is like a tool only in specific respects, not literally. Identify the two points of analogy.
This shows how logical analysis defends theological positions.
5
Consider the delayed saying
Finally, the commentator suggests the ba' might be attached to a delayed verb 'aqulu'. Think about how this changes the sentence structure.
This demonstrates the flexibility of Arabic grammar.

(ومحمده) عطف على بسم الله عطف المفرد على المفرد والباء قال الأستاذ رحمه الله اتباعا بالكتاب المبين واقتداء بسنة النبي الأمين واقتفاء بالسلف الصالحين. (بسم الله) أي متبركا باسم الله أبتدئ. اعلم أن لفظة «الله» اسم خاص بذاته تعالى لا يطلق على غيره أصلا مشعر بجميع صفات الكمال الإشهاد الذات بها. فقيل هو علم جامد لا اشتقاق له وهو أحد قولي الخليل وسيبويه والمروي عن أبي حنيفة والشافعي والغزالي. وقيل مشتق. وأصله الإله حذفت الهمزة لتقلها وأدغم اللام في التلفظ لكون المتجانسين في كلتين. والإله مأخوذ من أله يفتح اللام إذ عبد. أي تعبد: أي اتخذ غيره عبدا. وقيل مأخوذ من وله وهو الحيرة. إذ العبد يتحير في تصور كنه ذاته تعالى وصفاته بطريق النظر ولا يحيطون به علما، ولهذا يقال لا يعرف الله تعالى أحد حق معرفته. ولا يصفه الواصفون حتى وصفه. ولم يأت بلفظه الرحمن الرحيم أو لم يكتبه تغييرا للأسلوب وجلبا للقلوب وإيذانا بأنه ليس بمقتضى الحديث إذ المشهور من الخبر المأثور بين المحصلين في البسملة كل أمر ذي بال لم يبدأ فيه ببسم الله فهو أبتر ولعدم عد كتابة أمرا ذا بال هضما لنفسه على ما أخرجه أبو عوانة وأبو حيان كل أمر ذي بال لا يبدأ فيه بسم الله الرحمن فهو أجذم، واعلم أن لفظ «اسم» إذا دخل عليه الباء إما أن يضاف إلى لفظة الله تعالى أولا الرحيم فعلى الثاني يمتنع حذف الهمزة من الخط وتطويل مركز الباء عنها. وعلى الأول إما أن يذكر بعده الرحمن الرحيم أولا فعلى الثاني يجوز الحذف مع التطويل وعلى الأول يجب (وبحمده) الواو
And now I begin the intended subject with the help of the worshipped King: (Bismillah). He deviated from the well-known method for the sake of the art (or for strangeness) or as a notification that the performance of the duty can be achieved by any way, or for other subtleties. It is a nominal sentence, meaning 'my beginning is with the name of Allah', which is the choice of the Basrans; or a verbal sentence, meaning 'I begin with the name of Allah', which is the choice of the Kufans. The adverb on the first interpretation is stable (mustaqirr), and on the second it is not stable, although it is considered stable by some investigators also. The ba' here is either for accompaniment (mulabasa), companionship (musahaba), or assistance (isti'ana). And what is said that the ba' of assistance only enters upon the tool—if the ba' were for assistance, then the name of Allah would have to be a tool. This is refuted by likening His name to a tool either in lacking purposefulness with respect to the action or in that the action does not occur without it. And what occurs without His name? (Then it is as if it did not occur). Moreover, in the glorious speech (Quran) the ba' of assistance has entered upon Him, and interpreting it is forced. It is also possible that the preposition is attached to a delayed saying, i.e., the poor miserable one says 'accompanying or seeking assistance with the name of Allah', and its precedence is to indicate restriction. And Allah is the name of the self that is necessarily existent, encompassing all attributes. And it was said that it is the choice of the majority of jurists and narrated from the Greatest Imam, and it is the most known of known things according to Sibawayh such that he said when seen in a dream: 'I have been forgiven for that.' The chosen view is that it is Arabic, not Hebrew or Syriac. Some virtuous ones said: just as minds are perplexed about the essence of His being, likewise understandings are perplexed about the word that indicates Him, whether it is Arabic or foreign, derived or non-derived, a proper name or otherwise, a name specific to Him or predominant. The addition of 'ism' to Him (Allah) is of the type of adding the general to the specific, and it is lamiyya (for possession) like 'shajar al-arak' (arak tree). It is not necessary to show the lam correctly; rather its meaning (specialization) suffices. Al-Fadil al-'Isam said: According to meaning, this idafa is bayaniyya (explicative), so showing 'min' in it is free from difficulty, but the imams of Arabic made it lamiyya, and it is not clear what motivated them. (And with His praise) is an apposition to 'bismillah', apposition of a singular to a singular, and the ba'—the teacher (rahimahu Allah) said following the clear book, emulating the Sunnah of the trustworthy Prophet, and following the righteous predecessors. (Bismillah) meaning 'with blessing through the name of Allah I begin.' Know that the word 'Allah' is a specific name for His being, not applied to any other at all, indicating all attributes of perfection (which the self bears witness to). It is said that it is a non-derived proper noun—one of the two opinions of al-Khalil and Sibawayh, and narrated from Abu Hanifa, al-Shafi'i, and al-Ghazali. And it is said it is derived. Its origin is 'al-ilah', the hamza was removed for its lightness (or heaviness?) and the lam was assimilated in pronunciation because of the two similar letters being in two words. 'Ilah' is taken from 'aliha' (with fatha on lam) meaning 'he worshipped' i.e., 'he took as a servant.' And it is said it is taken from 'waliha' meaning confusion, because the servant is confused in conceiving the essence of His being and attributes by way of reflection and they cannot encompass Him in knowledge. Therefore it is said no one knows Allah truly, and the describers do not describe Him until He describes Himself. And he did not come with the wording 'al-Rahman al-Rahim' or did not write it—changing the style and attracting hearts, and indicating that it is not according to the hadith, since the famous transmitted report among the investigators about the basmala: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with bismillah is truncated'; and because writing is not considered a matter of importance, humbling himself, as narrated by Abu 'Awana and Abu Hayyan: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with bismillah al-Rahman is cut off.' Know that the word 'ism' when the ba' enters it, either it is added to the word 'Allah' and then 'al-Rahim', or to 'al-Rahim' directly. In the second case, it is forbidden to omit the hamza in writing and to elongate the center of the ba' from it. In the first case, either 'al-Rahman al-Rahim' is mentioned afterwards or not. In the second (not mentioned), it is permissible to omit with elongation; in the first (mentioned), it is obligatory. (And with His praise) (waw).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the basmala. The commentator explains why the author used 'bismillah' without 'al-rahman al-rahim', analyzing it as either a nominal or verbal sentence. He discusses the function of the ba', whether for assistance, accompaniment, etc. He rebuts the objection that the ba' of assistance would make Allah's name a tool by analogy. Then he discusses the word 'Allah'—whether it is derived or proper noun, Arabic or foreign, and its definition as the necessary existent. He mentions the opinions of scholars like Sibawayh, Abu Hanifa, etc. He also discusses the idafa (construct phrase) and whether it is of the lam-type or bayan-type. Finally, he discusses the ruling of the basmala in hadith and the omission of 'al-rahman al-rahim' in the original, and the rules for writing the alif of 'ism' after the ba'.

Theological and Lexical Analysis of the Name 'Allah'
Theological and lexical analysis of the name 'Allah'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the derived and non-derived opinions about the word 'Allah' and state the theological implications.

Core Idea
Allah is not just a name; its linguistic origin points to worship or divine bewilderment.

The commentator delves into the word 'Allah' itself, discussing whether it is a derived term or a non-derived proper noun. He presents opinions from leading scholars like al-Khalil, Sibawayh, Abu Hanifa, al-Shafi'i, and al-Ghazali. The derivation from 'aliha' (to worship) or 'waliha' (to be perplexed) leads to insights about the essence of worship and the limits of human knowledge of God.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main opinions
Look for the phrases 'علم جامد' (non-derived proper noun) and 'مشتق' (derived). Note the authorities cited for each.
Al-Khalil and Sibawayh hold it is non-derived; others say derived.
2
Trace the derivation if derived
The commentator says the origin is 'al-ilah', with hamza removed and lam assimilated. Find the Arabic phrase 'أصله الإله حذفت الهمزة لتقلها'.
The reason for hamza removal is phonetic ease (thiqal or khiffa?).
3
Understand the two proposed roots
Two roots are given: 'aliha' (to worship) and 'waliha' (to be confused). Note how each connects to Allah's nature.
Worship implies servitude; confusion implies incomprehensibility.
4
Consider the theological implications
Reflect on the statement: 'لا يعرف الله تعالى أحد حق معرفته' and how the derivation from 'waliha' supports this.
This is a key theological point about tawhid and human limitation.
5
Examine the idafa (construct phrase)
The commentator discusses whether 'ism Allah' is lamiyya (possessive) or bayaniyya (explicative). Find the terms 'لامية' and 'بيانية'.
The type of idafa affects the meaning of the phrase.

Then he discusses the word 'Allah'—whether it is derived or proper noun, Arabic or foreign, and its definition as the necessary existent. He mentions the opinions of scholars like Sibawayh, Abu Hanifa, etc. He also discusses the idafa (construct phrase) and whether it is of the lam-type or bayan-type. Finally, he discusses the ruling of the basmala in hadith and the omission of 'al-rahman al-rahim' in the original, and the rules for writing the alif of 'ism' after the ba'.

Key Takeaway
The linguistic analysis of the name 'Allah' reveals that the very word points to either worship or bewilderment, both central to Islamic monotheism.
