Real vs. Conceptual Exclusivity in Division
Author distinguishes real tabaayun from conceptual tabaayun in division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish real exclusivity (tabāyun fī al-wāqiʿ) from conceptual exclusivity (tabāyun fī al-ʿaql) in a given division and correctly classify divisions as real (ḥaqīqī) or conceptual (iʿtibārī).

Core Idea
Real exclusivity prevents co-predication; conceptual exclusivity only requires distinct concepts.

In the previous lesson, we saw that division requires mutual exclusivity of its parts. But what does 'mutual exclusivity' actually mean? The author now refines this: there are two kinds of exclusivity — one based on external reality, the other based on conceptual distinctness. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying division in Islamic logic, especially when the same entity can be described by multiple universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of exclusivity mentioned
The passage defines two types. Read the first sentence: 'التباين قسمان'. Identify the two categories and note their names: التباين في الواقع and التباين في العقل.
2
Check if divisions can apply to one thing
For real exclusivity, check if any individual can be described by all divisions simultaneously. If not, it is real. For conceptual exclusivity, check if the concepts are distinct even if they apply to the same thing.
3
Determine the division type from the example
The author uses the five universals applied to a colored thing. Since the same object is simultaneously genus, species, etc., but these concepts are distinct, the division is conceptual (iʿtibārī). Note why it is not real: because the divisions are co-extensive.
4
Understand the relationship between the two types
Real exclusivity always implies conceptual exclusivity, but not vice versa. Conceptual exclusivity can exist without real exclusivity. The author notes that between them there is 'عموم وخصوص من وجه' (generality and particularity in some respect). Think of examples from Islamic sciences to solidify this.

Exclusivity (tabaayun) is two types: one is exclusivity in reality, which is that the divisions do not apply to a single thing, and this is in real division. The other is exclusivity in the intellect, which is the distinctness of the concept of the divisions such that one is not a part of another nor its specification, and this is in conceptual division. It is not harmful in it that the divisions apply to a single thing, like the applicability of the concepts of the five universals to the colored thing. For if we say: the universal is either genus or species or differentia or property or general accident, then this is a conceptual division in which the concepts of the divisions are mutually exclusive, and they are mentioned in the books of logic. It was only a conceptual division, not real, because all of them apply to the colored thing. It should be known that between exclusivity in reality and exclusivity in the intellect there is absolute generality and particularity according to verification, because whenever exclusivity in reality is realized, exclusivity in the intellect is realized, but not vice versa, for exclusivity in the intellect is realized in conceptual division while there is no exclusivity in reality in it, for the divisions in it are co-extensive. Rather, between them is generality and particularity in some respect. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage defines the two types of tabaayun (exclusivity) relevant to division in logic. The author first states that exclusivity is of two kinds: real and conceptual. Real exclusivity means that the divisions (the resulting species or parts) cannot all be truly predicated of a single individual. For example, in dividing animals into rational and non-rational, no single animal can be both rational and non-rational. Conceptual exclusivity means that the concepts of the divisions are distinct—one is not part of the definition of the other or a specification of it—even if they apply to the same individual. The author gives the example of the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, accident) applied to a colored thing: the same object can be described by all five universals, yet their concepts are distinct. This shows why the division of the universal into these five is conceptual, not real. The passage then clarifies the relationship between the two types: real exclusivity always implies conceptual exclusivity, but the converse is not true. That is, if divisions are mutually exclusive in reality, their concepts are necessarily distinct; but conceptual exclusivity can exist without real exclusivity, as in the example. The author concludes that the two types have a relationship of 'general and particular in some respect' (عموم وخصوص من وجه), meaning they overlap partially. This nuanced understanding is crucial for correctly applying division in logical analysis.

Three Modes of Mentioning the Divided Whole
Author explains the three ways the divided whole can appear in the divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three modes in which the divided whole appears in a division and classify examples from Islamic sciences accordingly.

Core Idea
The divided whole may be explicit, implicit in the concept, or omitted yet understood.

In logic, when we make a division (taqsīm), we break a whole into parts by adding restrictions. But the divided whole itself can appear in different ways: sometimes it is repeated in each part, sometimes it is built into their meanings, and sometimes it is left out but must be assumed. Understanding these modes helps you read and evaluate divisions correctly.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the concept of division
A division consists of a divided whole (al-muqassam) and restrictions (quyūd) that produce the parts. The divided whole is the subject being divided.
2
Mode 1 – Explicit mention
The divided whole is stated in each division explicitly. Example: 'Man is either a white man or a black man.' Here 'man' appears in both options. The divisions are called 'detailed concepts' (mafhūmāt tafṣīliyya).
3
Mode 2 – Implicit in the concept
The divided whole is not stated but enters into the definition of each division. Example: 'The word is either a noun, a verb, or a particle.' Each part's definition includes 'word' (the divided whole) implicitly. These divisions are 'total concepts' (mafhūmāt ijmāliyya).
4
Mode 3 – Omitted but understood
The divided whole is left out entirely; only the restrictions appear. Example: 'Man is either white or black' meaning 'a white man or a black man'. The divided whole must be estimated, or the division would be too broad.
5
Synthesize and practice
These modes can combine. Analyze any division by asking: is the divided whole mentioned, part of the concept, or omitted? Then determine if the divisions are detailed or total concepts.

Commentary
This passage is an extended explanation of the three modes in which the divided whole (al-muqassam) can appear in the divisions (al-aqsām). The author begins by clarifying that the term 'division' here refers to the resulting divisions themselves, not the act of dividing. Because division is essentially combining restrictions (quyūd) with the divided whole, the question arises: how is the divided whole represented in each division? The author enumerates three possibilities. First, the divided whole may be explicitly mentioned in each division, as in 'man is either a white man or a black man.' Here 'man' appears explicitly in both alternatives. Second, the divided whole may be included in the concept of each division without being explicitly stated; the example is 'the word is either a noun, a verb, or a particle.' In this case, the concept of each part (noun, verb, particle) inherently includes the idea of 'word' (the divided whole). Third, the divided whole may be completely omitted, but it is understood or 'estimated' (muqaddar) along with the restrictions. For instance, 'man is either white or black' means 'man is either a white man or a black man.' If the divided whole were not estimated, the divisions would become too general (e.g., 'white' is more general than 'white man'), leading to the logical error of dividing a thing into itself and something else. The author also notes that these three modes can coexist in a single division. This detailed classification helps the logician correctly analyze the structure of a division and avoid fallacies. The passage also touches on the epistemological status of the divisions: when the divided whole is explicitly mentioned, the divisions are 'detailed concepts' (mufaṣṣala); when it enters into the concept, they can be 'total concepts' (ijmāliyya) or 'detailed' depending on the level of specification.
