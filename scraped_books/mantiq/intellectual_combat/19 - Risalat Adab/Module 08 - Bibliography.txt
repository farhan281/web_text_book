Navigating the Bibliography of Adab al-Bahth
List of manuscript and printed sources for Adab al-Bahth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key manuscript and printed sources foundational to the study of Adab al-Bahth and explain their specific roles in the tradition.

Core Idea
The bibliography is a map of the scholarly tradition—knowing each source's type and purpose is essential for serious study.

This lesson introduces the bibliography of the modern edition of Risalat al-Adab. While it may seem like a simple list, each entry represents a layer of the tradition: the original text, its commentaries, modern textbooks, and reference works. Learning to navigate this list trains you to identify the intellectual lineage behind any work of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two sections
Notice the bibliography is divided into 'Manuscripts' and 'Printed Works'. Manuscripts are the oldest, unprinted copies; printed works are modern editions or recent compositions. This division reflects the textual history.
Manuscripts are listed first because they are the primary sources.
2
Identify the central text and its commentaries
The first manuscript entry is Tashkubri Zadah's 'Risalat al-Adab', with five copies. Then two commentaries: al-Kafawi's glosses and al-Nasih's super-commentary. This shows the text's importance and the commentarial tradition.
Commentaries (shuruh) and glosses (hawashi) are secondary works that explain or critique the base text.
3
Categorize the printed works by genre
Scan the printed list. You'll see modern textbooks (e.g., al-Shanqiti's work), biographical dictionaries (e.g., al-A'lam), lexical works (e.g., al-Ta'rifat), and historical works (e.g., al-Shaqa'iq). Each serves a different purpose in research.
For Adab al-Bahth, lexical works help with definitions; biographies give context on authors.
4
Connect each source to its use
Think about how you would use each type: a manuscript is for textual criticism; a commentary for understanding difficult points; a lexical work for defining key terms; a biography for learning about the author's background.
This skill transfers to any Islamic science—knowing what each source type offers saves time.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography from the modern edition of Risalat al-Adab, listing key manuscript and printed sources for the science of Adab al-Bahth (the discipline of disputation). The editor organizes the references into two sections: manuscripts (makhṭūṭāt) and printed works (maṭbū‘āt). Among the manuscripts, the primary text is Tashkubri Zadah's 'Risalat al-Adab', of which five copies exist. Two important commentaries are also listed: al-Kafawi's glosses on the commentary, and al-Nasih's super-commentary. The printed section opens with al-Shanqiti's 'Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara', a foundational modern work. Other printed works include reference biographies (al-A'lam by al-Zirikli), historical works (al-Insaf by Tashkubri Zadah himself), and lexical or definitional works (al-Ta'rifat by al-Jurjani and al-Tawqif by al-Manawi). The variety of sources shows the interdisciplinary nature of Adab al-Bahth, drawing from logic, jurisprudence, biography, and lexicography. A student of Islamic logic should recognise that these works form the textual tradition upon which the study of dialectical debate rests.
