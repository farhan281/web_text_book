How Scribes Confuse Text and Commentary in Manuscripts
Author explains how scribes confused matn and sharh in manuscripts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cause of textual disagreement between manuscripts of a scholarly work and explain the role of scribal practices.

Core Idea
The main cause of textual variation in Islamic manuscripts is scribal conflation of matn and sharh.

This passage from the front matter of Risalat Adab alerts us to a common problem in manuscript transmission: scribes who extracted the core text (matn) from the commentary (sharh) often mixed them up, leading to variant readings. Understanding this issue is essential for anyone working with classical Islamic texts, as it helps distinguish between authorial revisions and scribal errors.

Guided Path
1
Understand the roles of matn and sharh
The matn is the original text, while the sharh is a commentary explaining it. Scribes often combined them in a single manuscript, using visual cues to differentiate them.
In this context, al-Adab refers to a specific treatise; but the principle applies broadly.
2
Identify scribal conventions
Scribes wrote the sharh in black ink and the matn in red ink, or placed a line above words of the matn. These conventions helped readers distinguish the two layers.
Such conventions were not always consistently applied, leading to confusion.
3
Analyze the consequence
When extracting the matn from the sharh, words from either layer could be misattributed, causing variants among manuscript copies.
This confusion, not authorial revision, is the main source of textual disagreement.
4
Apply to manuscript study
When comparing copies of a classical text, consider that variants may arise from scribal confusion rather than from different authorial versions. This insight guides textual criticism (tahqiq).

Commentary
This passage is a front-matter note explaining the textual history of the Risalat Adab. The author observes that many scribes did not copy the text (matn) independently but extracted it from the commentary (sharh). Because the commentary was widespread, scribes adopted conventions to distinguish the two: they wrote the commentary in black ink and the text in red ink, or placed a supralinear stroke over the words of the text. However, these conventions were not always followed consistently, leading to confusion: words that belong to the text end up in the commentary and vice versa. This, the author concludes, is the primary cause of variation among manuscript copies. The passage highlights the importance of careful textual criticism when using manuscripts of such works.

Parsing the Colophon of an Islamic Scholarly Chapter
Author closes the chapter with a prayer for divine aid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the closing formula of an Islamic scholarly chapter and identify its key components: declaration of scope, attribution of success to Allah, and prayer for inspiration of correctness.

Core Idea
The colophon is not just a closing; it is a profession of divine dependence.

This passage is the closing statement (colophon) of a chapter in a classical Islamic logic textbook. The author declares that he has presented the full scope of what was intended, then immediately attributes all success and correct understanding to Allah. Colophons like this are common in Islamic scholarly works, serving both as a structural marker and a reminder of the author's humility before Allah. Let's break down this short but rich sentence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the declarative opening phrase
The passage begins with 'هذا غاية ما يراد في هذا الباب' – 'This is the utmost of what is intended in this chapter.' The demonstrative 'هذا' refers to the content just presented. 'غاية' implies the farthest limit or complete scope. 'ما يراد' indicates what was intended to be covered. Recognize that this phrasing signals completion.
The phrase 'غاية ما يراد' is a formulaic expression of scope.
2
Parse the prepositional phrase of attribution
The second part starts with 'ومن الله التوفيق' – 'and from Allah is success.' Here 'من الله' is a prepositional phrase indicating the source. 'التوفيق' means divine aid or the ability to achieve correctness. This clearly attributes all success to Allah alone.
The conjunction 'و' connects this prayer to the opening statement; it is not a separate clause but a continuation.
3
Analyze the final phrase 'إلهام الصواب'
The passage ends with 'وإلهام الصواب' – 'and inspiration of correctness.' 'إلهام' refers to divine inspiration placed in the heart. 'الصواب' means correctness in truth itself. Combined, the author prays that Allah not only grants success but also inspires correct understanding.
This dual request (success + inspiration) is a common scholarly supplication.
4
Reflect on the theological significance
This colophon underscores the Islamic belief that all knowledge and the ability to convey it correctly come from Allah. The author humbly acknowledges his dependence on divine aid, even after completing a rigorous logical exposition. This serves as a model of scholarly humility.
In Islamic intellectual tradition, proper adab requires attributing success to Allah.

Commentary

He then concludes with a prayer: 'and from Allah is success (al-tawfiq) and inspiration (ilham) of correctness (al-sawab).' This formulaic ending acknowledges that all success comes from Allah, and that the correct understanding is divinely inspired. It is a common feature in Islamic scholarly works to end chapters with humility and supplication.
