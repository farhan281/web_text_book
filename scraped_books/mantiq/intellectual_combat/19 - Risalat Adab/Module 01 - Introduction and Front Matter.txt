Core Idea
Risalat al-Adab is the classic manual of Islamic debate etiquette, not Greek logic.

This lesson introduces the foundational text for the discipline of adab al-bahth. We begin by parsing its title page to understand exactly what this treatise is about, who wrote it, and why it matters for Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path

Extract the title

2
Identify the subject
The subtitle 'في علم آداب البحث والمناظرة' clarifies that this is a work on the science of inquiry and debate (adab al-bahth wa al-munazara). This is a distinct Islamic logical methodology focusing on the etiquette and procedures of dialectical exchange.
3
Identify the author
The author is 'شكري زادة' (Shukri Zada), who died in 968 AH. He is known primarily for this concise treatise, which became a standard textbook in Islamic seminaries.
4
Recognize the genre
This work belongs to the genre of adab al-bahth, which differs from Greek logic in its emphasis on dialectical exchange, proper questioning, and response etiquette rather than formal syllogistic deduction.
Adab al-bahth is sometimes called 'Islamic dialectics' and is essential for theological and legal debates.

Introduction to the Critical Edition
Praise be to God, Lord of the Worlds, and prayers and peace be upon the best of prophets and the most honorable of messengers, and upon his family and companions and those who follow them in goodness until the Day of Judgment.
To proceed: This is a treatise on the science of Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara by the scholar of the Romans, Tashkopri Zadeh, may God grant him goodness and more. I have adopted a new methodology in presenting it, such that I have presented the matn six times as follows:
1. The Bare Matn: I presented the matn stripped of any addition or alteration, except for vocalization.
2. The Detailed Matn: I presented the phrases of the matn in detail, scattered each part in its own line.
3. The Segmented Matn: I segmented the matn into paragraphs followed by dotted lines, to facilitate commentary on them, and mentioned some quotations and restrictions related to those paragraphs.
4. The Collated Matn: I collated the wording of the matn against five manuscript copies.
5. The Commented Matn: I explained the phrases and words of the matn, drawing upon some commentaries and glosses, and some books authored in this art.
6. The Verified Matn: I blended the Collated Matn and the Commented Matn, dropping many of the collations and explanatory phrases, retaining only the most important of those comments, content with the presence of what I left in their places in the Collated or Commented Matn.
So whoever wants to memorize the matn, let him refer to the Bare Matn; whoever wants to conceptualize its issues, let him refer to the Detailed Matn; whoever wants to comment on it, let him refer to the Segmented Matn; whoever wants to fix the wording and phrases of the matn and know what belongs to it and what is excluded from it, let him refer to the Collated Matn; whoever wants to know the meanings of its words and phrases, let him refer to the Commented Matn; and whoever wants all that, let him refer to the Verified Matn.
I intend to add in later editions two matns:
The Tabulated Matn: where the issues of the matn are placed in tables.
The Tree-diagram Matn: where the matn is drawn using explanatory diagrams and tree diagrams.
I have some inclination to add a ninth matn, the Versified Matn; because versification is not by Tashkopri Zadeh, and my work in this book is specific to the matn, not to what pertains to it. The same is said about the tenth matn, which is the matn translated into Turkish.
I have prefaced this work with four introductions:
First: On defining the principles of the science of Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara.
Second: On introducing the author of the matn, Tashkopri Zadeh.
Third: On introducing the Verified Matn.
Fourth: On explaining the manuscript copies relied upon in the edition.
I ask God to benefit by this work the community of students, and to forgive its author, its editors, its reader, its student, and its memorizer, and to reward them with the best reward, and to grant us beneficial knowledge, righteous action, and a sound heart. Amen.

Core Idea
A matn is not one text—its editions serve distinct cognitive aims.

In this lesson, we explore the author's classification of matn editions. Reflect on which edition you would use for different stages of mastering a text: e.g., use mujarrad for initial memorization, then mufassal for deep understanding, and finally muhaqqaq for critical study.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The opening is a compact creed: God answers, the Prophet guides, and success is from God alone.

Every Islamic scholar begins with praise and prayer. This is not mere tradition—it orients the reader to the epistemological stance of the work. Here, the author emphasizes that God answers every asker, that the Prophet was sent with the strongest proofs, and that all success comes from God. 2
Note the key qualifiers
The phrase 'bi-l-basira' specifies that this is intellectual vision, not sense perception. 'From both sides' emphasizes mutuality and cooperation. 'Manifest correctness' clearly states the goal: uncovering objective truth, not defeating an opponent.
3
Distinguish from mere argument
Contrast this definition with common adversarial debate, where winning is the goal. Here, the aim is truth, and both sides participate in a principled investigation. This sets the normative framework for the entire treatise.
In later texts, munazara is distinguished from jadal (dialectic) by this truth-seeking focus.

How to Read an Islamic Bibliography
Bibliography listing 23 sources on Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the author, title, editor, publisher, edition, and date from any standard entry in an Islamic bibliography.

Core Idea
Bibliographies are the roadmap to the Islamic intellectual tradition.

This lesson introduces you to the structure of a specialized bibliography for the science of Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara. Knowing how to parse such entries is essential for locating primary and secondary sources in any Islamic discipline. We will work through a sample entry to identify its components.

Guided Path
1
Locate the entry number
Every entry begins with a number in parentheses, e.g., (8). This helps you navigate the list and cross-reference with citations in the text.
2
Identify the title of the work
The title follows the number, usually in bold or larger font. It may include a subtitle. Example: 'جامع الشروح والحواشي' (Comprehensive Collection of Commentaries and Glosses).
Titles are often descriptive in Arabic.
3
Find the author's name
The author's name comes after the title, often introduced by the word 'لـ' (by). Example: 'لعبد الله محمد الحبشي' (by Abdullah Muhammad al-Habashi). Note that names may include honorifics like 'القاضي' (the judge) or 'العلامة' (the scholar).
4
Check for an editor or commentator
If the work is a critical edition or commentary, the editor's name appears after the author, introduced by 'تحقيق' (edited by). Example: 'تحقيق الدكتور مازن المبارك' (edited by Dr. Mazin al-Mubarak).
If no editor is mentioned, the entry refers to a single-author work.
5
Extract the publication details
The final part contains the publisher, place, edition number, and date (both Hijri and Gregorian if available). Example: 'المجمع الثقافي، أبوظبي، ط2، 1427هـ 2006م' means: Cultural Complex, Abu Dhabi, 2nd edition, 1427 AH / 2006 CE.
Edition is marked with 'ط' (taba'a), and date often has both calendars.

Understanding the Different Textual Versions in a Critical Edition
Passage lists chapter and section headings of Risalat Adab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the purpose of each distinct version of the matn listed in a critical edition (bare, detailed, segmented, collated, annotated, verified).

Core Idea
A critical edition presents multiple textual versions to show the evolution and verification of the matn.

The final part of the TOC lists six versions of the matn (المتن المجرد, etc.). This lesson explains what each one represents and why editors include them.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish 'bare text' from 'detailed text'
المجرد is stripped of commentary; المفصل includes more explanation or variant readings.
The bare text is closest to the author's original wording.
2
Understand 'segmented' and 'collated'
المجزأ might be divided into sections; المقابل likely shows side-by-side comparisons of manuscripts.
Such versions help scholars see differences between copies.
3
Recognize 'annotated' and 'verified'
المشروح has commentary; المحقق is the critically established text.
The verified text is the one editors consider most authoritative.
4
Consider the editorial purpose
These versions allow scholars to trace how a text was transmitted and refined.
They are essential for understanding the textual history.

Survey the overall structure

4
Understand the editorial progression
These versions represent increasing levels of scholarly intervention: from the bare text (no additions) to the verified text (critical apparatus). The commented text (mashruh) includes explanations, while the corresponding text (muqabil) might indicate parallel readings.
