Conversion Rules for Temporal, Existential, and Absolute Propositions
States conversion rule for temporal, existential, and general absolute propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conversion rule for temporal, existential, and general absolute propositions and explain why a more specific proposition cannot be derived from the conversion.

Core Idea
Temporal propositions convert to absolute, not temporal.

In Islamic logic, propositions qualified by time or existence have special conversion properties. This lesson clarifies that temporal, existential, and general absolute propositions all convert to a general absolute proposition. Understanding this rule prevents mistaken inferences when moving from modal premises to conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three proposition types
The author mentions 'temporal' (al-waqtiyatan), 'existential' (al-wujudiyatan), and 'general absolute' (al-mutlaqa al-'amma). Temporal propositions assert truth at a specific time; existential assert existence at some time; general absolute assert truth at all times without exception.
2
State the conversion rule
Each of these three types converts to a general absolute proposition. That means after swapping subject and predicate, the resulting proposition holds at all times, not just at the original time.
The 'mentioned ways' refer to the standard rules of simple conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi) adapted for modal propositions.
3
Recognize what does NOT follow
The author warns that a more specific proposition, such as the 'absolute conditionally' (al-hiniyya al-mutlaqa), does not necessarily follow from the conversion because the two descriptions (subject in original and subject in conversion) may be different.
The 'absolute conditionally' is a proposition that is true at some time but not necessarily all times; it is more specific than the general absolute.
4
Analyze the example
The example: 'Every eclipsed thing is illuminated temporarily, not always' (temporal universal). Its conversion is not 'Every illuminated thing is eclipsed temporarily' but rather a general absolute proposition like 'Some illuminated thing is an eclipsed thing' (without temporal restriction). This shows that the temporal qualification is lost in the conversion.
The original subject (eclipsed) and the converted subject (illuminated) do not necessarily share the same temporal property; hence the more specific temporary claim cannot be inferred.

The Non-Conversion of Possible Propositions and the Three Objections Answered
Claims possible propositions do not convert, then presents three objections and their answers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the dialectical structure of objections and answers regarding the conversion of possible propositions, and determine why the author denies conversion.

Core Idea
Possible propositions do not convert because the subject may not be possible for what the predicate actually holds.

This passage addresses a subtle point in modal logic: the conversion of possible propositions. The author states they do not convert and provides an example. Then three objections from opponents are mentioned, each with a brief answer. This lesson will walk through the logical dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim and the example
The author claims that possible propositions (like 'Every A is possibly B') do not convert. The example given: 'Every illiterate person is a writer potentially' is true, but 'Some writer is illiterate potentially' is false because writers are actually literate.
The example shows that possibility may not be symmetric.
2
Examine the reason for non-conversion
The reason: when converting, the subject of the converse must be possible for what the predicate actually holds. But it could be that the subject of the original (e.g., illiterate) is not possible for the predicate (writer) in reality.
The author focuses on actual vs. potential attribution.
3
Identify the three objections
The author refers to 'three aspects' (al-wujuh al-thalatha) by which opponents argued that possible propositions do convert. These are not fully spelled out but can be inferred from the answers: (1) if A is possibly B then B is possibly A; (2) a syllogism with a possible minor premise proves conversion; (3) a necessary negative converts to a necessary negative.
The objections are standard arguments from modal syllogistic.
4
Analyze the three answers
The answers: (1) the objection would require the subject to be actually true of what the predicate potentially holds, which is not always the case; (2) the minor premise being possible does not produce a valid conclusion in the first figure; (3) a necessary negative does not convert as necessary—the modality changes.
Each answer defends the original claim that possible propositions do not convert.

Conversion of Universal Negative Propositions
States conversion rules for various types of universal negatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of universal negatives convert to which forms, and construct a counterexample to show why necessary negatives do not convert as themselves.

Core Idea
Not all universal negatives convert equally; necessary and conditional ones resist direct conversion.

In the Islamic logical tradition, conversion ('aks) is a fundamental operation that preserves truth under certain conditions. This passage presents the subtle rules for converting different types of universal negative propositions (sawalib kulliyya). Understanding these rules is crucial for correctly inferring conclusions in theological and legal reasoning, as a mistaken conversion can lead to false proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal negative categories
The passage classifies universal negatives into: the two permanent (da'imatan), the two general (ammatan), the necessary (daruriyya), the conditional (mashruta), and the two special (khassatan). Note which categories are mentioned.
These categories are based on modal and temporal conditions.
2
Learn the conversion outcomes
The two permanent convert to the absolute permanent; the two general convert to the general conditional by the last two ways. The necessary and conditional do NOT convert as themselves. The two special convert to the general conditional with the condition 'non-permanence in some'.
3
Analyze the counterexample for necessary negatives
The passage provides: 'Nothing of the writer is illiterate by essential necessity' is true, but its conversion 'Nothing of the illiterate is a writer by necessity' is false. This shows necessary negatives do not convert as themselves.
The predicate 'illiterate' here is taken as the essence (dhat) to test necessity.
4
Understand why conversion fails
Conversion as itself would require the same modality (necessity) in the converse, but the example shows the converse is not necessarily true. The necessity is attached to the subject of the original, not to the subject of the converse.
5
Apply the special rule
The two special propositions convert to the general conditional with an added condition: the converse is not permanent for all individuals, but only for some. This restricts the universality of the converse.

Modal universal negatives do not always convert as themselves; the scope of 'non-permanence' determines validity.

Refuting al-Razi's Proofs for Modal Negative Conversion
Author presents and refutes al-Razi's two proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical fallacy in al-Razi's reductio proof for conversion of necessary negatives and reconstruct the author's refutation.

Core Idea
Modal conversion fails when a reductio argument changes the domain of individuals.

Imam al-Razi proposed two proofs for the conversion of necessary and conditional negative propositions 'as themselves.' This passage presents his arguments alongside the author's refutation. The first proof uses a reductio that appears compelling until you notice a subtle shift in the domain of individuals, which the author exposes as the flaw. The second proof, based on mutual incompatibility, is dismissed as even weaker because it mismatches the terms involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the targeted conversion claim
Razi claims that a necessary negative proposition 'No B is C necessarily' converts to 'No C is B necessarily', and similarly for conditional negatives. This is an immediate inference about modal propositions.
Also known as conversion per accidens with necessity.
2
Reconstruct the first proof (reductio)
Assume the original 'No B is C necessarily' is false. Then 'Some B is C possibly' is true. If that possible becomes actual, then 'Some B is C actually' is true, which by affirmative conversion gives 'Some C is B actually'. But this contradicts the necessary negative 'No C is B necessarily'. Hence the original must be true.
This is a classic reduction to absurdity (khalf).
3
Find the fallacy in the first proof
The author argues that assuming the possible becomes actual adds a new individual to the class of C that was outside the original negation. The original 'No B is C necessarily' applied only to the original individuals of C. The new individual is not affected by that negation, so no contradiction arises. The fallacy is a subtle domain shift.
The author says: 'it adds an individual to the individuals of C that was outside it in the state of negation.'
4
Identify the weakness in the second proof
Razi's second proof claims that incompatibility (munāfāh) must be mutual: if 'No B is C' then necessarily 'No C is B'. The author objects that the incompatibility in the original is between the essence of the subject B and the description of the predicate C, while in the converse it would be between the essence of C and the description of B. These are different pairs, so the mutual requirement is not met.
The author says: 'So where is one from the other?'
5
Apply the lesson: check domain constancy
When evaluating any conversion argument, especially with modals, always check whether the domain of individuals remains the same. If a reductio introduces new individuals, the argument is invalid. This principle applies to all immediate inferences in Islamic logic.
This protects against many fallacious proofs in mantiq.

The Conditions for Valid Conversion: Khunji's Mistake
Critique of Khunji's conversion of universal negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposed conversion of a negative proposition is valid by checking if it follows from the original alone without extraneous premises.

Core Idea
A true conversion must follow from the original proposition alone — no extra premises allowed.

In this lesson, we examine al-Khunji's argument that universal negatives convert when taken as haqiqiyya propositions. The author refutes this by showing that Khunji's proof relies on an extraneous premise, violating the definition of conversion. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to distinguish genuine logical inference from spurious derivations in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify Khunji's claim
Khunji asserts that if a proposition is taken as haqiqiyya (including impossibilities in the subject), then universal negatives convert as permanent particulars. Locate this claim in the Arabic passage.
The passage says: 'إذا صدق لا شيء من ج ب بالفعل صدق لا شيء من ب دائما ج دائما' (If 'No C is B actually' is true, then 'No B is always C always' is true).
2
Find the extraneous premise
Khunji's proof uses an additional premise: 'كل ب دائما ب بالفعل' (Every B is always B actually). This premise is not part of the original proposition; it is an extraneous premise.
3
Understand why this invalidates the conversion
The author argues that a true conversion must follow from the original alone. Adding an external premise makes it a syllogism, not a conversion. Therefore, Khunji's conclusion is not a valid conversion.
The text says: 'العكس ما لزم من الأصل فقط، بل منه مع الغير و هو المقدمة الأجنبية فلا يكون عكسا' (Conversion is what follows from the original only, not from it along with another, the extraneous premise, so it is not a conversion).
4
Appreciate the general principle
This critique teaches a key principle: any logical operation that requires additional assumptions beyond the original statement is not an immediate inference. This applies to all conversion, obversion, and contraposition.
This principle guards against conflating syllogistic reasoning with immediate inference.

Correct Conversion of Universal Negatives and Particular Negatives
Alternative proof for universal negative conversion and classification of particular negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the correct proof for converting universal negatives without extraneous premises, and identify which particular negatives (if any) convert.

Core Idea
Universal negatives convert by relying on the self-evident truth that 'what is B is B actually in the mind'; most particular negatives do not convert.

After refuting Khunji's extraneous-premise method, the author provides a sound alternative proof for universal negative conversion. This lesson unpacks that proof step by step and then examines the special case of particular negatives, where only the two privative propositions convert. Mastering this distinction is essential for applying conversion correctly in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Start with the original universal negative
Assume the original proposition 'No C is B actually' (لا شيء من ج ب بالفعل) is true. This is a universal negative about actuality.
The author uses 'بالفعل' (actually) to indicate the present actual world.
2
Derive the statement 'Nothing of B is always C always'
From the truth of the original, it follows that 'Nothing of B is always C always' (لا شيء من ب دائما ج دائما). This is a stronger statement with temporal universality.
This step uses the fact that if no C is B actually, then no B can ever be C (under the assumption of stability or by modal reasoning). The author claims this follows from the original.
3
Apply the principle of self-identity in the mind
The author then states: 'Not some B actually is C always' is true because 'what is B is B actually according to the mind'. This is the conversion: 'Not some B is C actually' (ليس بعض ب بالفعل ج دائما).
This avoids an extraneous premise by using the evident fact that in thought, each B is B. The description of the subject can be mental or external.
4
Handle particular negatives: only privative ones convert
The passage says that particular negatives generally do not convert because the subject may be more general (e.g., 'Some animal is not human' converts to 'Some human is not animal'? That fails). However, two privative propositions (الخاصتين) convert as themselves due to the conditions of permanent negation.
Privative propositions involve a permanent negation and mutual exclusion, ensuring the converse holds.

Fallacy in Conversion: Misidentifying the Predicate as a Simple Term
Explains a fallacy in conversion due to relational predicates, with a nuance and an ancient example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a proposition's predicate is a relation (nisba) and correctly convert such propositions according to the rules of aks.

Core Idea
A relational predicate cannot be converted like a simple term; the relation must become the new subject.

In immediate inference, conversion (al-aks) is straightforward when terms are simple. But when the predicate is actually a relation, a common fallacy occurs: one mistakenly treats the relational term as the subject or predicate in the converse. This lesson will show you how to spot such relational predicates and perform the correct conversion. We'll also see a special case where the relation is to a non-determinate thing, where simple conversion may hold, and an important example from al-Kashshi involving infinite extension.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition and its terms
Look at the proposition's subject and predicate. Is the predicate a simple categorical term (like a genus or species) or a relation (like 'on the throne', 'in the wall', 'was young')? Words like 'on', 'in', 'was' often indicate a relation.
A relation typically involves two entities: the subject and the object of the relation.
2
Check if the predicate is a relation to a determinate thing (amr muhassal)
If the object of the relation is a concrete, determinate entity (e.g., throne, wall, young), then the predicate is a nisba ilā amr muḥaṣṣal. This is the case where the fallacy most often occurs.
If the object is non-determinate (e.g., non-fors, non-human), the conversion behaves differently.
3
Perform the correct conversion
For affirmative propositions: convert 'Every A is (relation) to B' to 'Some thing that is (relation) to B is A' (particular affirmative). For negative propositions: convert 'No A is (relation) to B' to 'Every thing that is (relation) to B is A' (universal affirmative).
This differs from simple conversion of categoricals: E converts to E, but here E converts to A.
4
Verify the converse against the original
Check that the original proposition entails the converse. For example, if every king is on the throne, then there exists at least one thing on the throne that is a king. If no body is extended to infinity, then every extended thing is a body (by contrapositive of the relation).
The entialment is immediate and valid only with the correct relational conversion.

Two examples are given to illustrate: first, 'Some existent is possibly non-existent' is true, but its contrapositive 'Some of what is not possibly non-existent is not existent' is false. Second, 'Some human is not a writer' has a false contrapositive 'Some writer is not a human'. Then the author presents an argument by 'the Sheikh' (likely Ibn Sina) who claims that contraposition of particular affirmatives is always valid. The sheikh argues that there must exist something outside the subject (J) and predicate (B) that makes the contrapositive true. The author refutes this by pointing out that the first example itself disproves it: 'Some existent is possibly non-existent' is true even though there is nothing outside the terms 'existent' and 'possibly non-existent'. Furthermore, even if the sheikh's condition were granted, it would not prove that the contrapositive follows logically from the original proposition. This critique shows the importance of counterexamples in logical analysis.
