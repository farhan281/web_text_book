Dependency of the Intellectual Universal on Mental Existence
Author explains the dependency of the universal in the intellect on mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mental existence (al-wujud al-dhihni) and intellectual existence (al-wujud al-'aqli) and explain the dependency of the universal in the intellect (al-kulli al-'aqli) on mental existence.

Core Idea
The universal in the intellect does not float free – it depends on mental existence.

In this passage, the author clarifies the relationship between two key modes of existence: mental existence and intellectual existence. The universal in the intellect, which is the object of logical analysis, is not self-standing. Its actualization is conditional upon mental existence. This distinction has profound implications for how we understand concepts in Islamic logic and epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of existence mentioned
Look for al-wujud al-dhihni (mental existence) and al-wujud al-'aqli (intellectual existence) in the Arabic. Note that mental existence is the broader category.
2
Trace the dependency chain
The passage states: the mental concept depends on mental existence; the universal in the intellect depends on intellectual existence. Draw the hierarchy: mental existence > intellectual existence > universal in intellect.
3
Examine the consequence of denial
The author says denying intellectual existence negates the universal. Understand why this is a necessary logical consequence: if the universal depends on intellectual existence, removing the latter removes the former.
4
Analyze the objection and response
A hypothetical objection claims the intellectual is a branch of relation (fara' al-idafa). The response reroutes the difference (ikhtilaf) to mental existence. Parse this subtle move: the objection is deflected by arguing that any difference arises from mental existence, not from the universal itself.

Commentary
This passage defines the relationship between mental existence (al-wujud al-dhihni) and the universal in the intellect (al-kulli al-'aqli). It states that the existence of a mental concept depends on the actualization of mental existence. Then it repeats with emphasis: the universal in the intellect depends on intellectual existence (al-wujud al-'aqli). This makes the universal in the intellect a subset of mental existence. The author then says that whoever denies intellectual existence denies the universal; the truth is to affirm it. The investigation of intellectual existence is left to the philosopher (al-hakim). Then a hypothetical objection is raised: 'the intellectual is also a branch of relation...' (fara' al-idafa). The response is that the difference (al-ikhtilaf) should be attributed to the difference in mental existence. The passage ends with a reference to Lawami' al-Asrar.

Author critiques Ibn Sina's separation of essential and connective concomitance, siding with Fakhr al-Din al-Razi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the critique of Ibn Sina's separation of essential and connective concomitance and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
Essential concomitance is just a special case of connective concomitance; separating them leads to logical errors.

Ibn Sina distinguished between essential concomitance (conceptual implication) and connective concomitance (propositional implication), a division that became foundational in later logic. But not all logicians agreed. The commentator points out that the definition fails because it does not exclude separable concomitants unless 'accompany' means perpetual. But even then, accidental perpetual pairs (e.g., 'whenever man is rational, the donkey brays') would be included, so necessity must be added.
This shows the definition is under-inclusive and over-inclusive.

The core claim: essential concomitance (luzum al-haml) is not separable from connective concomitance (luzum al-ittisal). The commentator illustrates: 'Rational necessitates laughter' means exactly 'If something is rational, then it is capable of laughter'. Thus conceptual implication reduces to a conditional proposition.

The commentator identifies the source of error as Ibn Sina's division of logic into topics of concepts (tasawwurat) and topics of assents (tasdiqat). This split, though popular, is blamed for many mistakes, including the false separation of the two concomitances.
