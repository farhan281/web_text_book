Reductio on Impossibilities and the Distinction of Quiddities
Author presents a reductio and its refinement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a reductio ad absurdum argument in a logical problem and evaluate the distinction between existential and privative quiddities that resolves it.

Core Idea
Not every established thing must be existent; privative quiddities can be established without existing.

In classical Islamic logic, a debate arose over whether non-existents and impossibilities have any reality. This passage presents a reductio that forces their existence, then offers a crucial refinement: a distinction between two types of quiddity. Understanding this distinction is key to avoiding fallacies about non-existence.

1
Identify the assumption under attack
Find the supposition that the reductio begins with: that impossibilities (mumtani'āt) and non-existents (ma'dūmāt) do not exist externally. This is the assumption to be disproved.
2
Trace the first branch of the reductio
Assume their non-existence is not established externally. Then its opposite (existence) must be established. This implies impossibilities and non-existents exist while being non-existent, a contradiction (conjunction of opposites).
3
Trace the second branch of the reductio
Assume their non-existence is established externally. The author argues that an established thing cannot subsist in a negated subject, so their existence follows again, leading to a second contradiction.
Note the key premise: an established (thābit) cannot subsist in a negated (manfī).
4
Identify the refinement
The solution distinguishes between an established thing whose quiddity is existential (wujūdiyya) and one whose quiddity is privative ('adamiyya). The second branch fails for privative quiddities, as they can subsist in a negated subject.
5
Evaluate the resolution
Check whether the distinction successfully avoids the reductio. Non-existence itself (a privative) can be established without requiring existence, so the reductio is blocked.

Core Idea
Not all necessary consequents are existential; privative thabit resolves apparent contradictions.

This lesson examines a classic dialectical argument: opponents claim that even denying impossibles leads to contradiction. You will see how a careful distinction between two types of thabit (constant) defuses the dilemma.

The opponent argues: (1) If the consequent is not constant, then its opposite (existence) is constant → contradiction. (2) If it is constant, then existence follows because a constant cannot subsist in a negated subject → contradiction. So on either assumption, contradiction follows.

This shows the importance of ontological distinctions in logical reasoning.

The Fallacy of Replacing Part with Whole: The Least Motion Example
Identifies the fallacy of 'replacing part with whole' regarding least motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an argument assumes the existence of a disputed entity (like 'least motion') and thereby commits the fallacy of replacing part with whole.

Core Idea
A flawed assumption makes even a valid disjunctive syllogism worthless.

This passage exposes a subtle logical error: assuming the existence of a 'least motion' to prove an indivisible part. The argument appears valid in form, but its premise is false. Understanding this fallacy helps you spot similar errors in theological and legal reasoning where unproven entities are assumed.

Guided Path
1
Locate the disjunctive premise
The argument states: the least motion is either divisible or not divisible. Identify the two alternatives as the starting point of the reasoning.
The disjunction is exclusive and exhaustive.
2
Examine the first alternative (divisible)
If the least motion is divisible, then it would not be the least – its part would be lesser. This leads to a contradiction, so the argument rejects this branch.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Examine the second alternative (indivisible)
If the least motion is indivisible, then the distance covered by that motion must also be indivisible; otherwise the motion would split. Thus an indivisible part is supposedly established.
This conclusion depends on the assumption that the least motion exists.
4
Spot the hidden assumption
The entire disjunctive argument assumes that a 'least motion' (aqall al-haraka) actually exists. The refutation directly denies this: 'We do not grant that the least motion is actual, for every motion is divisible ad infinitum.'
Without this premise, the argument is unsound.
5
Generalize the fallacy
The fallacy is 'replacing the part with the whole': the argument treats the least motion as a real entity (the 'part') when in fact motion is infinitely divisible (the 'whole'). It surreptitiously substitutes the part for the whole, drawing an invalid conclusion.
This fallacy often appears when a disputed concept is treated as given.

The Liar Paradox and Its Resolution in Islamic Logic

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The liar paradox dissolves when we recognize that the speaker's intent defines what the statement refers to.

The liar paradox has baffled thinkers for centuries. In Islamic logic, the resolution lies not in formal tricks but in a fundamental principle: the referent of a statement is determined by the speaker's intention (iradat al-mukhbir). This lesson unpacks the paradox and shows how this principle avoids contradictions, demonstrating the integration of intention into logical analysis.

Guided Path

State the paradoxical claim
Identify the initial statement: 'All my speech at this hour is false' when it is the only speech uttered.

Assume it is true: then all speech at that hour is false, including itself, so it must be false. Assume it is false: then some speech at that hour is true, but only this statement exists, so it must be true. Both lead to contradiction (ijtima' al-naqidayn).
3
Identify the source of paradox
The paradox arises because the statement includes itself in the set of all speech it refers to, creating a self-referential loop.
4
Apply the resolution: speaker's intent
The author states 'al-mukhbar 'anhu innama yata'ayyan bi-iradat al-mukhbir' – the referent is only determined by the speaker's intent. The speaker intends to refer to all other speech except this very statement, breaking the loop.
This is the key contribution: the resolution is not formal but based on intention.
5
Test the resolution
If the statement is taken as referring to all speech except itself, then it can be false (if other speech is true) or true (if other speech is false), but no contradiction arises because it does not self-refer.
The paradox disappears once self-reference is excluded by intent.

Core Idea
Absence of a specific instance does not prove absence of the general claim.

In logical argumentation, one may mistakenly think that if a specific case does not exist, then the general claim must also be false. This passage from the logic tradition diagnoses that error, showing through conditional reasoning and contraposition why the inference is invalid. Guided Path

Identify the specific and the claim

The fallacy attempts to infer 'the claim is false' from 'the specific does not exist'. Recognize that this is an invalid move: the non-existence of a part does not necessitate the non-existence of the whole.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a fallacy. The author addresses a logical error in inferring the non-existence of a claim from the non-existence of a more specific thing. The argument proceeds by examining the relationship between a specific case ('akhass) and a general claim (al-mudda'a). The author states that if the specific exists, the claim is established. If the specific does not exist, the claim must still be true in general; otherwise, the specific would be equivalent to the general, which is impossible for a valid specific-general relationship. The author further considers cases where non-existence encompasses both the specific and the claim, leading to contradictions. Through conditional reasoning and contraposition (aks al-naqid), the author shows that assuming the claim's falsehood leads to absurdities, such as the non-existence of all things. The solution emphasizes that the failure of the specific to exist does not necessitate the non-existence of the claim under the same respect, and that the fallacy arises from misunderstanding the scope of non-existence. This analysis is typical in logic texts for diagnosing and resolving paralogisms.

Key Takeaway
The non-existence of a specific instance does not logically entail the non-existence of the general claim; assuming so is a fallacy of denying the antecedent.
