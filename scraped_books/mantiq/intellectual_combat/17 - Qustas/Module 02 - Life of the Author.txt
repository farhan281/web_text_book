Biographical Analysis of al-Samarqandi
Presents the life of al-Samarqandi and the debate over his death date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the reliability of different manuscript-based claims for a classical Islamic scholar's death date.

Core Idea
Biographical certainty in Islamic logic often depends on later manuscript evidence, not contemporary records.

This lesson examines the life of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi, a great logician whose biography is clouded in uncertainty. We'll explore how scholars use manuscript evidence to resolve disputes about his death date — a skill essential for evaluating sources in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the controversy
The passage lists many proposed death years for al-Samarqandi (599 to 722 AH). Note the range and the sources that proposed them (e.g., Western scholars, translators).
Controversies often arise from lack of contemporary documentation.
2
Examine the manuscript evidence
Ramazan Şeşen cites a manuscript of a theological work by al-Samarqandi in the Laleli Library with an explicit death date: 22 Shawwal 722 AH. A marginal note says 'correct twenty'.
Manuscripts from near the author's time are more reliable.
3
Check corroboration
Gholamreza Dadkhah independently examined the same manuscript and supported the 722 AH date after a six-page analysis of all proposed dates.
Cross-validation by multiple scholars strengthens the case.
4
Evaluate the evidence
Consider why other dates were proposed (conjecture, lack of access to manuscripts). Determine which date has the strongest documentary support.
Manuscript evidence with a precise day and month is more convincing than vague years.

About Samarqandi: Shams al-Din Muhammad ibn Ashraf Husseini Samarqandi is one of the renowned but neglected logicians of the golden age of Islamic logic, contemporary with great logicians such as Khwaja Nasir al-Din Tusi, Najm al-Din Katibi, Siraj al-Din Urmawi, Qutb al-Din Shirazi, and Allameh Hilli. Samarqandi mentions some of these greats by name and brings the views of others without naming them, examining and criticizing them. He is one of the influences on the logical thoughts of Qutb al-Din Razi in his book Sharh al-Matali'. Although Qutb al-Din Razi never mentions him by name, he occasionally presents, examines, and critiques his ideas. Currently, a copy of Samarqandi's Sharh al-Qustas in Qutb al-Din Razi's handwriting exists, indicating Samarqandi's importance from Razi's perspective. This copy was written in the year 711 AH (in the youth of Qutb al-Din Razi) and is now preserved in the Tire Necib Paşa Library in Suleymaniye, Turkey. Life: Samarqandi, as his name indicates, was born in Samarqand or a city in Transoxiana. Very little information is available about Samarqandi's life, and there is much disagreement even about his birth and death dates; for example, for his birth, the years 600 and 638 AH have been written, and for his death, the years 599, 600, 649, 675, 690, 704, 709, 721, and other years. It seems there is no ancient document about Samarqandi's birth date, and some contemporaries have proposed the mentioned years by conjecture; for instance, Nicholas Rescher and Muhammad Mehran, the translator of Rescher's book into Arabic, in a footnote respectively proposed the years 1240 CE (638 AH) and 1204 CE (600 AH). As for the year of Samarqandi's death, as we said, the differences are far greater. For example, Edward Van Dyck recorded Samarqandi's death year in both CE and AH as 1203 CE and 599 AH. Nicholas Rescher, in contrast, considered the death year as 1304 CE (704 AH), and Muhammad Mehran in a footnote suggested 1291 CE (690 AH). An undated writing by Ramazan Şeşen on the web reports that in a manuscript of one of Samarqandi's theological books in the Laleli Library in Suleymaniye, number 2432, page 1136, it is written: 'The death of the author on the twenty-second of Shawwal in the year seven hundred and twenty-two,' and next to it is written 'correct twenty.' Hence, Şeşen definitively considered Samarqandi's death as 22 Shawwal 722 AH and thus rejected other dates. Gholamreza Dadkhah, in his introduction to Samarqandi's book 'Ilm al-Afaq wa al-Anfus,' in support of Şeşen's statement, examined all these dates in over six pages and, citing the Laleli manuscript, considered Samarqandi's death as Shawwal 722 AH and brought multiple reasons for the inaccuracy of other dates.

Using Manuscript Evidence to Establish a Scholar's Death Date
Editor presents a manuscript inscription establishing the death date of al-Samarqandi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract a death date from a manuscript inscription and evaluate its reliability using internal textual cues.

Core Idea
Manuscript marginalia are primary sources for Islamic biographical data.

Biographical information about early scholars often relies on manuscript evidence. In this passage, the editor cites a manuscript inscription that records al-Samarqandi's death date. By learning to read such inscriptions carefully, you gain a key skill for historical research in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of manuscript note
Determine whether the inscription is a colophon, ownership note, or marginal addition. This passage appears to be an ownership or annotation note on a manuscript.
The context of the note affects its reliability.
2
Locate the date phrase
Find the explicit mention of month, day, and year. Here it is 'twenty-second of Shawwal 722 AH'.
Look for the formula 'fi yawm... min shahr... sana...'.
3
Check for confirmatory marginal notes
Note any additional annotations like 'عشرين صح' (twenty correct), which confirms the numeral.
Such marginalia often indicate that the copyist verified the date.
4
Assess the reliability of the source
Consider the manuscript's provenance: it is from the Laleli library and is a copy of the author's own theological work, which increases credibility.
Other proposed dates may be less reliable if based on secondary sources.

Understanding al-Samarqandi's Scholarly Context
Closing remark revealing al-Samarqandi's concurrent studies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to infer an author's scholarly preoccupations from a closing remark in a classical work.

Core Idea
Al-Samarqandi wrote Qustas while immersed in other sciences.

This lesson examines a brief but revealing remark from Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi at the end of his Qustas al-Afkar. Though not a logical argument, this statement offers a window into the author's intellectual life and the circumstances under which he composed his work. Understanding such remarks helps situate a text within its author's broader scholarly project.

Guided Path
1
Locate the remark in its context
Recognize that this is the closing line of Qustas al-Afkar. The editor quotes it to give insight into the author's state of mind.
The passage appears at the end of the work, suggesting it is a concluding reflection.
2
Identify key phrases about the author's condition
Note the Arabic phrases 'اشتغال القلب' (occupation of the heart) and 'استيعاب الفكر' (engagement of the mind). These indicate that al-Samarqandi was not writing with a free and focused mind.
'Heart' here refers to the seat of intellectual and emotional focus.
3
Interpret the phrase 'تحصيل علوم أخرى'
This phrase means 'acquiring other sciences.' It tells us that while writing Qustas, al-Samarqandi was also studying other disciplines, possibly mathematics or astronomy, which he was known for.
Al-Samarqandi was a prolific scholar in logic, philosophy, and mathematics.
4
Connect to the broader scholarly context
Reflect on why an author would include such a remark. It may serve to acknowledge limitations or to highlight the depth of his engagement. It also humanizes the author and shows the multi-tasking nature of scholarly life.
Such remarks are common in prefaces and conclusions of classical works.

Samarqandi's Life and Intellectual Context
Editor discusses Samarqandi's lifespan, teachers, and students.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key biographical details of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi and evaluate the scholarly controversies surrounding his teachers and students.

Core Idea
Samarqandi’s biography shows how intellectual networks and textual analysis are essential in Islamic logical history.

Understanding a scholar's life helps us contextualize his works and assess later claims about his influences. In this lesson, we will examine the editor's findings on Samarqandi's lifespan, journeys, teachers, and students, and see how careful textual evidence can correct historical errors.

Guided Path
1
Locate Samarqandi's lifespan and travels
From the passage, find the estimated birth and death years (638–722 AH) and note his presence in Tabriz (688 AH) and Khujand (712 AH). These details help place his works in time and space.
The editor relies on internal references in Samarqandi's writings.
2
Identify his only known teacher
The passage states only Burhan al-Din al-Nasafi is recorded as his teacher. Note that al-Nasafi wrote commentaries on logic and was in Baghdad after 675 AH.
Other suggested teachers, like Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, are rejected by the editor.

Evaluate the studentship controversy

This shows the importance of chronological verification.
4
Connect biography to logical innovations
Samarqandi's unique contribution to mixed syllogisms (haqiqiyya, kharijiyya, dhihniyya) is noted. His travels and access to different scholarly circles likely influenced his work.
The editor suggests his mathematical studies in Tabriz/Maragheh may have shaped his logical approach.

Samarqandi's Place in Islamic Logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key logical innovations of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi and explain why his approach to haqiqi and khariji propositions distinguished him from his contemporaries.

Core Idea
Samarqandi's mixed syllogisms with haqiqi and khariji propositions mark a turning point in post-Avicennan logic.

The biography of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi is not just a list of facts; it reveals a pivotal moment in Islamic logic. This lesson explores why his work on haqiqi and khariji propositions was controversial and how his relationship with scholars like Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi shaped his legacy. Understanding this context helps you appreciate the debates that drove logical innovation.

This passage is a modern editor's introduction to a critical edition of al-Samarqandi's Qustas al-Afkar, written in Persian. Editor enumerates Samarqandi's logical books and their manuscript status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major logical works of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi and describe their manuscript status as reported in scholarly sources.

Core Idea
The logical works of Samarqandi are foundational for Islamic logic, yet many remain only in manuscript.

Before diving into al-Qustas itself, we must understand its place among Samarqandi's writings. The editor provides a valuable inventory of Samarqandi's logical books, their relationships, and their current state of publication. This lesson will help you navigate the scholarly landscape around this important text.

Guided Path
1
List the six logical works
Identify the six logical works of Samarqandi as enumerated by the editor: three sections within comprehensive works (al-Mu'taqadat, al-Anwar al-Ilahiyya, Sharh al-Anwar al-Ilahiyya), plus three independent works (Bisharat al-Isharat, Nistas al-Afkar, and the subject of the edition, al-Qustas).
Note that the first three are not standalone logical books but parts of larger theological or philosophical works.
2
Distinguish comprehensive works from commentaries
Observe that al-Mu'taqadat and al-Anwar al-Ilahiyya are comprehensive works covering many sciences; their logic sections are only one part. In contrast, Bisharat al-Isharat is a commentary on Ibn Sina's al-Isharat, and Nistas al-Afkar and al-Qustas are independent logic treatises.
This distinction affects how we study Samarqandi's logical doctrines — do we need to consult the larger work for context or not?
3
Identify manuscript and publication status
For each work, note what the editor says about existing manuscripts, critical editions, and whether they have been published. For example, al-Mu'taqadat's logic section has an announced but unpublished edition; Nistas al-Afkar has conflicting titles and multiple modern editions.
This information is crucial for anyone planning to research Samarqandi's logic.
4
Recognize title confusion
Pay attention to the editor's warning that some titles are mistakenly used for Nistas al-Afkar. The same book is called al-Qustas, Qustas al-Mizan, etc. Also note that the title 'Bisharat al-Isharat' is shared by commentaries on different base texts.
Title ambiguity can lead to misidentification of works in catalogues and references.

Commentary
This passage is the editor's introduction to the book 'Qustas' by Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi. It begins by noting that Gholamreza Dadkhah has identified thirty books and treatises by Samarqandi, whose manuscripts are extant and scattered in libraries worldwide. The editor then lists the logical works of Samarqandi. The first three are sections on logic within larger comprehensive works: (1) the section on logic in 'al-Mu'taqadat', (2) the section on logic in 'al-Anwar al-Ilahiyya', and (3) the section on logic in 'Sharh al-Anwar al-Ilahiyya'. The editor mentions that Najm al-Din Pahlavan has announced a critical edition of the first, but it remains unpublished. Dadkhah himself has edited the next two, with hopes of publication soon. Additional works include 'Bisharat al-Isharat' (also known as 'al-Bisharat fi Sharh al-Isharat') authored in 680 AH; Pahlavan has also edited this but not published, while Ali Oujabi edited it but only published the section on conceptions from the logic in a commemorative volume. The editor clarifies that many books titled 'al-Bisharat fi Sharh al-Isharat' exist: some are commentaries on Ibn Sina's 'al-Isharat' in logic and philosophy, others on 'Isharat al-Usul' (a work on usul and hadith by Ibrahim Karbasi al-Isfahani). The latter was commented on in three volumes by the author's son, Mirza Abu al-Ma'ali, who also used the same title. The editor also quotes Aqa Buzurg al-Tehrani's entry on a commentary by Awḥad al-Din Anwari. Then on page 23, the editor discusses the work 'Nistas al-Afkar fi Tahqiq al-Asrar' (authored 683 AH), noting that it has been mistakenly called by other names. He mentions that Edward van Dyck and Nicholas Rescher refer to a print by Aloys Sprenger in Calcutta, 1854, which the editor could not find. Later, Najm al-Din Pahlavan produced a Turkish translation and edition (with errors) as a doctoral thesis in 2010, published in 2014. Subsequently, Ali Ahmad Ismail Muhammad prepared a master's thesis on the same book at Cairo University in 2015. The editor expresses uncertainty whether this thesis was a critical edition or merely a study.

Samarqandi's Contributions to Logic and Dialectic
Editor introduces Samarqandi's works on logic and dialectic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three major works of al-Samarqandi and describe how his Risalah fi Adab al-Bahth combines logic with usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Samarqandi transformed logic from pure theory into a practical tool for debate.

This passage from the editor's introduction gives us a glimpse into the scholarly output of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi, a key figure in the development of Islamic dialectic. By examining his titles, we can trace how he moved from traditional Aristotelian logic to a new discipline that integrated logical argumentation with the juristic rules of debate. Understanding this shift is essential for appreciating how later scholars like al-Taftazani structured their theological discussions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three works
Locate the three titles mentioned: 'Sharh al-Qustas fi al-Mantiq', 'Risalah fi Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazarah', and 'al-Ma'ab fi Sharh al-Adab'. Note the dates and what each text is about.
The first is a commentary on his own logic text; the second is a treatise on debate etiquette; the third is another commentary on the same subject.
2
Understand the progression from logic to dialectic
Notice that the first work is strictly on logic (mantiq), while the second introduces 'Adab al-Bahth' (dialectic). This shows Samarqandi's move from abstract logical theory to applied argumentation, blending logic with the principles of usul al-fiqh.
The term 'Adab al-Bahth' refers to a rigorous method for conducting scholarly dispute, not mere politeness.
3
Recognize the innovation
Samarqandi's Risalah is considered a foundational text in the genre of Adab al-Bahth. It provided a structured framework for formulating objections and responses in kalam and fiqh debates. Later scholars built upon this method.
This genre became standard in the Islamic curriculum, especially in theological seminaries.
