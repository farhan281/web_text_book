Binary and Ternary Propositions
Author defines binary and ternary propositions and classifies them based on the copula.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify propositions as binary, complete ternary, or incomplete ternary based on the copula.

Core Idea
The copula determines whether a proposition is binary or ternary.

In this lesson, we examine the author's distinction between binary and ternary propositions. The key factor is the presence or absence of an explicit copula (rabita). Understanding this classification is essential for parsing logical propositions correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Look at the Arabic sentence. Is there an explicit word like 'huwa' (non-temporal) or 'kana' (temporal) connecting subject and predicate? If yes, it is ternary; if no explicit copula, it is binary.
Remember that verbs and derived nouns contain an implicit copula.
2
Check the predicate type
For binary propositions, verify that the predicate is a verb or derived noun (ism mushtaq). If it is a solid noun (ism jamid), the proposition cannot be binary; it must have an explicit copula.
Solid nouns like 'rajul' (man) do not indicate a relation to a subject.
3
Distinguish between complete and incomplete ternary
If the explicit copula is non-temporal (e.g., 'huwa'), it is complete ternary. If temporal (e.g., 'kana'), it is incomplete ternary.
Non-temporal copula binds the predicate to a specific subject; temporal copula indicates time but not a specific subject.
4
Classify the proposition
Based on the previous steps, assign the proposition to one of the three categories: binary, complete ternary, or incomplete ternary.
This classification is foundational for logical analysis in Islamic sciences.

Copula Omission and the Refutation of al-Imam's Ternary Claim
Author discusses copula omission across languages and refutes the claim that verb predicates make propositions essentially ternary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an explicit copula is necessary even when the predicate is a verb or derived noun, and refute the claim that such propositions are essentially ternary.

Core Idea
A verb's implicit pronoun does not specify the subject; the copula is needed to fix reference.

This lesson tackles a subtle point in propositional logic: whether verbs or derived nouns already contain a copula implicitly. Al-Imam al-Razi argued that such propositions are ternary in nature even if binary in wording, making an explicit copula redundant. Through cross-linguistic examples and logical analysis, the author provides two refutations that preserve the necessity of the copula for subject specification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Imam's claim
Al-Imam (Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) claims that a proposition whose predicate is a verb or derived noun is binary in wording but ternary by nature. The relation (nisba) is indicated implicitly by the predicate itself, so adding an explicit copula would be repetition.
The Imam's position assumes the implicit relation is sufficient.
2
Understand the author's first refutation
The implicit relation in the verb does not specify the subject. For example, 'kataba' means 'he wrote' but does not tell us which Zaid. The copula is needed to tie the predicate to a specific subject, removing ambiguity.
This shows the need for an explicit copula for subject specification.
3
Follow the second refutation
The pronoun embedded in the predicate (the subject pronoun) is a noun by agreement of language scholars. It indicates a relation to some subject, but not a particular one. Thus, the explicit copula is still required to avoid ambiguity.
The pronoun does not pick out a specific individual.
4
Compare with Persian copula usage
In Persian, the copula is mandatory and cannot be omitted without changing meaning. The author lists various forms (temporal, non-temporal, vowel endings) showing that omitting the copula is grammatically prohibited. This reinforces the idea that the explicit copula is essential.
Cross-linguistic evidence supports the necessity of the copula.

Core Idea
Subjecthood and predicatehood are not symmetrical; a term may be necessary as subject but not as predicate.

In this passage, the author establishes a fundamental distinction: the relation of a term to another as subject is different from its relation as predicate. This asymmetry is crucial for understanding propositional structure and the logic of conversion. Three Types of Propositions by Quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any proposition as particular (makhṣūṣa), indefinite (muhmala), or quantified (maḥṣūra) based on the quantity of its subject term.

Core Idea
A proposition's quantity depends on whether its subject is a particular concept, has no quantifier, or has a universal/particular quantifier.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational classification of propositions by quantity. The author distinguishes three types: particular (makhṣūṣa), indefinite (muhmala), and quantified (maḥṣūra). Understanding this classification is essential for evaluating syllogistic arguments, as the quantity of premises affects validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the proposition
Look at the proposition and find the subject term—the thing being talked about. For example, in 'Zayd is standing,' the subject is 'Zayd'.
2
Check if the subject is a particular concept
If the subject refers to a specific individual (like a proper name or a definite description that picks out one thing), the proposition is particular (makhṣūṣa). Example: 'Zayd is standing' is particular.
Particular propositions have a subject that cannot be predicated of many.
3
Check for a quantifier word
If the proposition includes a quantifier such as 'all', 'every', 'some', or 'not all', it is quantified (maḥṣūra). Example: 'All men are mortal' is universal quantified; 'Some men are just' is particular quantified.
Quantifiers determine the scope: universal (kullī) or particular (juz'ī).
4
If neither, it is indefinite
If the subject is not a particular concept and there is no quantifier, the proposition is indefinite (muhmala). Example: 'Man is animal'—no quantifier, subject is general but unspecified in quantity.
Indefinite propositions are ambiguous; they could be interpreted as universal or particular depending on context.

The Four Quantified Proposition Types and the Universal Negative Quantifier
Author enumerates quantified types and defends the negative status of 'كل ليس' constructions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of quantified propositions by their quantifiers, and explain why 'كل ج هو ليس ب' is considered a universal negative proposition.

Core Idea
The quantifier 'كل ليس' makes a universal negative, even when word order places the copula before negation.

The author lists the four quantified propositions: universal affirmative, particular affirmative, universal negative, and particular negative. He then focuses on the universal negative quantifier 'كل ليس' and addresses a debate: Is 'كل ج هو ليس ب' affirmative or negative? Based on Avicenna, he argues it is negative in force, as 'ليس' negates regardless of position.

Guided Path
1
List the four quantified proposition types
The author enumerates: universal affirmative (quantifier كل), particular affirmative (بعض or واحد), universal negative (لا شيء, لا واحد, كل ليس), and particular negative (discussed later).
2
Understand the quantifier 'كل ليس' for universal negative
The phrase 'كل ج ليس هو ب' uses 'كل' as a restricting quantifier, not an affirmative. If affirmation follows 'كل', the proposition is universal affirmative; if negation follows, it is universal negative.
3
Analyze the controversy about word order
When the copula precedes the negation, e.g., 'كل ج هو ليس ب', scholars debate: Avicenna (from al-Shifa') considers it negative; others consider it affirmative. The author resolves that it is 'affirmative with a negated predicate' (موجبة سالبة المحمول), which is equivalent to a universal negative.
The key is that 'ليس' is inherently negative, so its position doesn't change the logical force.

Classifying 'No J is B': Affirmative with Negated Predicate
Author analyzes whether 'No J is B' is a simple negative or an affirmative with negated predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposition like 'لا شيء من ج ب' is a simple negative or an affirmative with negated predicate, and distinguish the particles 'ليس', 'لا', and 'غير' in this context.

Core Idea
The form 'No J is B' is technically an affirmative with a negated predicate, not a simple negative.

In classical logic, the structure of propositions matters for analysis. The common phrase 'لا شيء من ج ب' appears negative, but closer inspection reveals it is an affirmative proposition with a negated predicate, equivalent to a negative. Understanding this distinction is crucial for accurate logical evaluation. In this lesson, we will dissect the author's argument and learn to classify such propositions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the phrasing
Identify the proposition 'لا شيء من ج ب' and note that it contains 'لا شيء' (nothing) and a predicate after the copula.
The literal structure is 'No thing of J is B'.
2
Distinguish absolute negation
The author says 'لا شيء من ج ب' does not convey absolute negation (السلب المطلق); rather, it affirms that J is described in such a way that B is negated from it.
Absolute negation would be a direct denial like 'ليس ج ب'.
3
Examine alternative phrasings
Compare the original to two rephrasings: 'لا شيء من ج إلا و ينتفي عنه ب' and 'كل ج هو ليس ب'. Notice how they express negation more directly.
The second phrasing uses 'ليس' as a negation particle after the copula.
4
Understand the classification
Some consider it affirmative because the part after the copula is the predicate. The author's verdict: it is 'موجبة سالبة المحمول' (affirmative with negated predicate), which is logically equivalent to a negative.
Equivalence means the two forms entail each other.
5
Apply the particle distinction
When the negation word is 'ليس', the proposition is equivalent to a negative. When it is 'لا' or 'غير', it is simply affirmative. Also note 'ليس واحد' for particular negation.
This distinction is based on the function of each particle.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional analysis of the logical form 'لا شيء من ج ب' (No J is B). The author begins by noting that the literal phrasing does not express absolute negation; rather, it expresses that B is negated while the description of J remains permanent. To achieve a pure negative, one could rephrase as 'لا شيء من ج إلا و ينتفي عنه ب' or 'كل ج هو ليس ب'. Some logicians considered it an affirmative because the part after the copula (ب) is taken as the predicate, making 'ليس ب' a negated predicate. The author declares the correct view: it is an 'affirmative with negated predicate' (موجبة سالبة المحمول) which is logically equivalent to a negative, meaning they entail each other. The justification is that the particle 'ليس' is specifically designed to negate the predicate from the subject; therefore, whether it comes after or before the copula, the negation effect remains. Hence, the Shaykh (Ibn Sina) classified this proposition as negative. The author then distinguishes cases where the negation word is 'لا' or 'غير' instead of 'ليس'; then it is unequivocally affirmative because these particles are not tools of negation like 'ليس'. Finally, 'ليس واحد' (not one) is similar to 'ليس' and can be used for particular negation.

Key Terms

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The truth of a deviated proposition hinges on whether the negation is in the meaning of the two sides, not just the wording.

Deviated propositions (al-qadaya al-munharifa) are a special class where the subject or predicate involves negation in a non-standard way. Classical logicians developed a precise rule to evaluate their truth. This rule, based on whether the two sides differ in the inclusion of negation in meaning, allows you to quickly determine truth or falsehood across all materials. In this lesson, you will learn this rule and practise it on examples from Islamic logic texts.

Identify the deviated proposition

First Rule for Truth of Deviated Propositions
Author states the first rule for truth of deviated propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the first rule for truth of deviated propositions to determine whether a given deviated proposition is true or false, citing the condition of difference in meaning of negation.

Core Idea
Truth of deviated propositions depends on whether negation enters the two sides in meaning, not wording.

Deviated propositions invert the standard quantifier placement, and their truth conditions are not intuitive. This lesson unpacks the first rule from the commentator, showing you exactly when such propositions are true and when they are false. You will learn to distinguish differences in meaning from mere verbal differences, and handle special cases like singular predicates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and predicate
Locate the subject (whether singular or universal) and the quantified predicate (has the quantifier attached). Determine the type of the predicate: singular, universal affirmative, or particular negative.
2
Check the entry of negation in the two sides
Compare how negation enters the subject and predicate. If they differ in the meaning (معنى) of negation — e.g., negation is in one side but not the other, or negates a part in one and the whole in the other — then the proposition is true in all materials. If they differ only in wording (لفظ) without meaning difference, the proposition is false.
Example: 'ليس ليس الإنسان كل الحيوان' differs in wording only because double negation equates to affirmation.

4
Handle special case: singular predicate
If the predicate is a singular term (e.g., Zayd), the materials combine necessity and impossibility. For example, 'كل إنسان زيد' is a composite of necessity (for one person) and impossibility (for others). Recognize that this can also introduce ambiguity, as with 'this writer'.

Core Idea
Truth of deviated propositions hinges on predicate type and modal material.

The rule provides conditions under which such propositions are true or false, depending on whether the two sides differ in negation and on the modal material (necessity, impossibility, possibility). Learning Objective

The second rule fails because it assumes uniform modal material across propositions.

However, actual logical analysis shows that only one of them is true, indicating a flaw in the rule's foundation. This critique highlights the importance of testing the comprehensiveness of logical rules.

Disambiguating 'All' in Quantified Propositions
Author distinguishes three meanings of 'all' and clarifies the logical quantifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three meanings of 'كل' (kull) is intended in a given logical proposition and explain why the other two are excluded.

Core Idea
In logic, 'كل' means each individual, not the universal concept or the whole as a totality.

When you hear 'every human is mortal', what exactly does 'every' refer to? Logicians have debated this. This passage from the Qustas clarifies that the quantifier 'all' in a categorical proposition refers distributively to each member of the subject class, not to the abstract universal or the collective whole. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between logical and metaphysical universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three meanings of 'كل'
From the passage, list the three distinct meanings: 1) 'الجيم الكلي' (the universal concept of J), 2) 'الكل من حيث هو كل' (the whole as a whole), and 3) 'كل واحد' (each individual). Recognize that the first two are explicitly excluded from the logical quantifier.
The passage also mentions a non-logical use of 'كل' in phrases like 'اليد بعض البدن' (the hand is part of the body), which is not a deviant proposition.
2
Understand the logical meaning
The passage states that when we say 'كل جب', we mean 'كل واحد' (each individual). This is the distributive sense: the predicate applies to every single member of the subject class separately.
Think of it as: for each x that is J, x is B.
3
Differentiate the universal J from the whole as a whole
The passage raises a doubt: is 'universality of J' (كلية ج) the same as 'all as a whole'? It clarifies that the first is the logical universal (a concept in the mind) and the second is the natural universal (the essence considered as a totality). They are not identical, but confusion arises because of the ambiguous phrase 'من حيث هو'.
The logical universal (الجيم الكلي) is the abstract meaning, e.g., 'humanity' as a mental concept. The natural universal (الكل الطبيعي) is the essence that exists in individuals.
4
Apply the distinction to a real proposition
Take a common Islamic example: 'كل إنسان حيوان' (every human is an animal). According to the passage, this means each individual human is an animal. It does not mean that the universal concept 'humanity' is the universal 'animality', nor that the whole of humanity as a single entity is an animal.
This distinction protects against interpreting universal propositions as statements about universals (which would be metaphysics) rather than about individuals (which is logic).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The quantifier 'كل' does not refer to the universal concept or the whole, but to each individual.

The word 'كل' (every/all) appears frequently in logical propositions, but its meaning is not always the same. In this passage, the author warns us of two false interpretations that would render our arguments invalid. Understanding the correct meaning is essential for constructing sound syllogisms in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three possible meanings of 'كل'

The author says that 'each individual' is a part of both the universal concept and the whole. This shows the universal concept and the whole are broader, while 'each individual' is a constituent.

When taken naturally, the universal is a part of each individual (e.g., 'humanity' is part of Zayd). When taken rationally, it is a mental abstraction with no external existence.

If we used the universal concept, we could not apply the predicate to individuals.

Later scholars rejected this type, but it is part of the classical scheme.

Truth Conditions of the Real Negative Proposition
Author defines the real negative proposition and its two truth conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two truth conditions of the real negative proposition and distinguish it from the mental negative.

Core Idea
A real negative is true either because its parts don't exist or because they can't exist.

In Islamic logic, propositions about non-existent or impossible things are handled with precision. The real negative proposition tells us exactly when such a statement is true. This short passage from the Qustas gives the two conditions that make a real negative true, which also apply to the mental negative. Understanding these conditions is essential for evaluating statements in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh about what does not or cannot exist.

Guided Path
1
Understand the 'real negative'
A real negative (السالبة الحقيقية) denies that a predicate applies to a subject in external reality. For example, 'There is no god besides Allah' denies the existence of another god in the real world.
Contrast with the mental negative (السالبة الذهنية), which denies in the mind only, often used for necessary truths like 'The species is universal'.
2
Learn the first truth condition: absence of conditions
The real negative is true if the 'mentioned conditions' are absent. This means either the subject does not exist in external reality, or the judgment itself is negated (i.e., the relation does not hold). For example, 'The king of France is bald' is false because the subject does not exist, so its denial is true.
The 'mentioned conditions' refer to the requirements for an affirmative proposition to be true: existence of subject, predicate, and their relation.
3
Learn the second truth condition: impossibility of the extreme
The real negative is also true if the subject or predicate is impossible in reality (imtina' al-taraf). For example, 'The conjunction of two contradictories is possible' is false because the predicate 'possible' cannot apply to an impossible subject. Therefore, its negation is true.
This covers cases where the terms themselves are inherently contradictory or impossible, like 'a square circle'.
