Ibn Sina's Condition for Disjunctive Premises and Ambiguity in Compound Syllogisms
Author discusses Ibn Sina's requirement and first method of compound syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Sina's requirement for the disjunctive to be positive when it shares parts with categorical premises, and identify the ambiguity of figures in the first method of compound conjunctive-disjunctive syllogisms.

Core Idea
When a disjunctive premise shares parts with categoricals, its positivity is required; and in compound syllogisms, the figure becomes ambiguous, so division is by premise type.

The author discusses Ibn Sina's view from al-Shifa that when a disjunctive premise is in the minor and shares a part with the categorical premises, it must be positive. However, the author notes a consideration: if the disjunctive is major, it can be positive or negative as long as its parts are positive. Then the passage introduces the first method for compound syllogisms combining conjunctive and disjunctive premises, where the middle term must be a complete part of both premises, causing the figure to be indistinguishable.

Guided Path
1
Locate Ibn Sina's requirement
The text: 'If the disjunctive is in the minor, sharing parts in one of the limits, and the categoricals are in the place of the major and they do not share a limit, he required the positivity of the disjunctive.' Identify what 'positivity' means: the disjunctive must be affirmative (not negative).
2
Examine the author's consideration
The author remarks that there is consideration ('fihi nazar') and argues that whether the disjunctive is positive or negative, as long as its parts are positive, it produces a conclusion. So Ibn Sina's requirement might be too strict.
The author seems to allow a negative disjunctive as long as its individual parts are affirmative.
3
Understand the first method for conjunctive-disjunctive compound
The middle term must be a complete part of both premises. Because the antecedent and consequent of the conjunctive are not distinguished from the disjunctive's parts, the figure of the syllogism becomes ambiguous.
4
Analyse the figure ambiguity
If the conjunctive is minor, figures 1 and 2 are not distinguished; if major, figures 1 and 3 are not distinguished. Therefore, division is not by figures but by whether the conjunctive is minor or major.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of syllogisms involving disjunctive and conjunctive premises, typical of advanced logic treatises. It begins with an example: 'Every number is either odd or even' and 'Everything that is odd or even is composed of units' which yields 'Every number is composed of units.' Finally, the passage introduces the fifth section on compound syllogisms from conjunctive and disjunctive premises, outlining the first method. Here the middle term must be a complete part of both premises. Because the antecedent and consequent of the conjunctive are not distinguished from the disjunctive's parts, the figure of the syllogism becomes ambiguous: if the conjunctive is minor, figures 1 and 2 are not distinguished; if major, figures 1 and 3 are not distinguished. Therefore, for this method, the division is not by figures but by whether the conjunctive is minor or major.

Mixed Conditional-Disjunctive Syllogisms: Classification and Conditions
Classification of mixed conditional-disjunctive syllogisms and their production conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four divisions of a mixed conditional-disjunctive syllogism and apply the condition for production (one premise must be universal).

Core Idea
In mixed conditionals and disjunctives, standard syllogistic figures collapse; only the position of the middle term matters.

Classical logic treats syllogisms composed of conditional (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila) premises as a distinct category. The author of this passage explains that when the middle term is a complete part of both premises, the usual four figures become indistinguishable. Instead, the division is based on whether the conditional is the minor or major premise and whether the middle term is its antecedent or consequent. This yields four possible arrangements. Additionally, a key condition for the syllogism to produce a valid conclusion is that one of the two premises must be universal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
In a mixed syllogism, one premise is a conditional (muttasila) and the other is a disjunctive (munfasila). Determine which is minor and which is major.
2
Recognize the middle term
The middle term is a complete part of both premises. In the conditional, it is either the antecedent or the consequent. In the disjunctive, it is one of the disjuncts.
3
Apply the indistinguishability of figures
Because the middle term is a complete part, the standard figures (first through fourth) lose their distinguishing characteristics. Instead, the division is based solely on the position of the conditional (minor or major) and the middle term (antecedent or consequent).
This yields exactly four divisions.
4
Check the condition for production
For the syllogism to yield a valid conclusion, at least one premise must be universal (kulliyya). This is a necessary condition regardless of the division.
The passage also discusses how the quality and quantity of the conclusion depend on the types of disjunction (maniat al-jam' and maniat al-khuluww).

The Middle Term and General Condition in Conditional Syllogisms
Author states general condition and applies to four cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the middle term in a conditional syllogism and state the condition that one premise must be universal for production.

Core Idea
The middle term is always the antecedent or consequent; production requires a universal premise.

In this lesson, we unpack the foundational condition for any conditional syllogism to yield a valid conclusion. The author of Qustas explains that the middle term is either the antecedent or consequent of one premise, and that one of the two premises must be universal. We will apply this to both conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two premise types
A conditional syllogism has two premises: at least one is a conditional proposition (conjunctive if-then or disjunctive either-or). Identify which type each premise belongs to.
The passage uses 'المتصلة' (conjunctive) and 'المنفصلة' (disjunctive).
2
Locate the middle term
The middle term (الحد الأوسط) is the proposition that appears in both premises. In conditional syllogisms, it is always either the antecedent (مقدم) or the consequent (تالي) of the conditional premise.
For example, if the premises are 'If P then Q' and 'If Q then R', the middle term is Q (the consequent of first and antecedent of second).
3
Apply the universality condition
The author states that one of the two premises must be universal (كلية). Universal conditionals are those that hold for all instances (e.g., 'Whenever A is B, C is D'). The other premise can be particular.
If both premises are particular, no conclusion follows.
4
Determine the middle term's role
Based on whether the middle term is the antecedent or consequent, the syllogism falls into one of four divisions. The author then provides rules for each case.
The four divisions arise from the middle term being antecedent of first, consequent of first, etc.

Author explains the third path where the middle term is a complete part of one premise and incomplete in the other.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In the third path, the middle term is complete in one premise – that premise behaves like a categorical.

In the previous paths of conditional syllogisms, the middle term was either partially present or absent. The third path introduces a hybrid: the middle term is fully contained in one premise, making that premise act like a categorical proposition. This classification helps us understand how conditional and disjunctive premises can substitute for categorical ones in syllogisms, streamlining the analysis of compound arguments.

Guided Path
1
Locate the middle term
Identify the term that appears in both premises. This is the middle term (al-awsat) that connects them.
2
Check completeness in each premise
Determine if the middle term is a complete part (juz' tamm) or incomplete (ghayr tamm) in each premise. A complete part means it constitutes an entire antecedent, consequent, or disjunct.
In a conditional premise, the entire antecedent or consequent is a complete part; in a disjunctive, a full disjunct is a complete part.

Identify the premise with the complete part
If the complete part is in the connected (muttasila) premise, the syllogism is like a categorical-disjunctive mix. If it is in the disjunctive (munfasila) premise, the syllogism is like a categorical-connected mix.
The complete premise takes the place of the categorical (hamli) in the hybrid composition.

This falls under the subcase where the complete is in the connected, so the syllogism functions as a categorical-disjunctive hybrid.

Conditions for the Third Path from Two Disjunctions
Author states conditions for the third path

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions (universality, difference in quality, unity in genus) for the third path and identify the resulting conclusion type.

Core Idea
Three conditions turn two disjunctions into a categorical conclusion.

The third path (al-maslak al-thalith) is a method to derive a categorical proposition from two exclusive disjunctions. The author sets out precise conditions that must be met: one premise must be universal, the two premises must differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative), and they must share a genus. Understanding these conditions is essential to apply the method correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disjunctions
The two premises are exclusive disjunctions (munfasilatayn). Look for the phrase 'إما ... وإما' (either ... or).
In the example, the disjunctions are 'Always either every J is B or (H Z)' and 'Not always either (H Z) or some B is A'.
2
Check universality condition
One of the two premises must be universal (kulliyya). The other may be particular. Identify the quantifier: 'Always' indicates universality, 'Not always' indicates particularity.
The first premise is universal, the second is particular negative.
3
Check difference in quality and unity in genus
The premises must differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative) and must be of the same genus (i.e., both are disjunctions of the same type, e.g., both are 'preventers of emptiness' or both 'preventers of conjunction').
In the example, both are 'preventers of emptiness' (mani'at al-khulu).
4
Determine the result
The conclusion is the contradictory (naqid) of the outcome of composing the two shared parts. For 'preventers of emptiness', take the affirmative side for the negative side; for 'preventers of conjunction', do the opposite.
The result is a categorical proposition, e.g., 'No J is A'.

Author gives proof by reductio

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio proof that validates the third path for deriving a categorical from two exclusive disjunctions.

Core Idea
Reductio proves that assuming the opposite leads to a contradiction.

The author provides a proof by reductio ad absurdum (burhan khalaf) to show that the categorical conclusion necessarily follows from the two disjunctive premises under the given conditions. The proof combines a categorical-conditional syllogism with a conditional-disjunctive syllogism. We will walk through the formal structure of this proof using the example given.

Guided Path

Write the two premises in logical form
Premise 1: 'Always either every J is B or (H Z)'. Premise 2: 'Not always either (H Z) or some B is A'. These are inclusive disjunctions (mani'at al-khulu).
The second premise is a negated disjunction; it is equivalent to 'some B is A and (H Z) are both false'? Actually, 'not always either ... or ...' means it is not the case that at least one is always true; it allows both false sometimes. Better to keep as is.
2
State the desired conclusion and assume its contradictory
Desired conclusion: 'No J is A'. Its contradictory is 'Some J is A'. Assume 'Some J is A' for reductio.
3
Show the entailment from the assumption
From 'Some J is A' and the first premise, we derive 'Always (if every J is B then some B is A)'. The proof uses a categorical-conditional composition: from the universal affirmative 'every J is B' and the particular affirmative 'some J is A', we get 'some B is A' (by subalternation and conversion, but here it's direct).
4
Derive a contradiction with the second premise
The entailment 'Whenever every J is B, some B is A' contradicts the second premise 'Not always either (H Z) or some B is A'. Because if 'some B is A' is always true when every J is B, then the disjunction 'either (H Z) or some B is A' would be always true (since one disjunct is true), contradicting 'not always'.
Thus the assumption leads to a contradiction, so the original conclusion 'No J is A' is valid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two disjunctives give a categorical only when they share a part, differ in quality, and one is universal.

This lesson introduces a specialized method in syllogistic logic: from two disjunctive propositions that share a common part, we can derive a simple categorical conclusion under specific conditions. This technique was used by classical logicians to reduce complex disjunctive reasoning to simpler forms. We will analyze the conditions and work through examples.

Locate the two munfasila statements in the argument. Note their type (mani'at al-khuluw or mani'at al-jam') and their constituent parts.

Find the common part
The shared part between the two disjunctives (e.g., 'H Z' in the example). This acts as the middle term connecting them.

If any condition fails, no categorical conclusion follows.

Assume the contradictory of the intended conclusion, then combine with the premises using categorical and conditional syllogisms to derive a contradiction. This confirms the conclusion.
The proof is often structured: first categorical+conditional, then conditional+disjunctive.

Definition of Relational Syllogism and Its Essential Conclusions
Author defines relational syllogism and explains essential conclusions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a relational syllogism and construct its essential conclusion from given premises.

Core Idea
In a relational syllogism, the relation from the minor premise attaches to the major term to form a compound predicate.

This lesson introduces the relational syllogism (qiyas al-nisba), a distinctive form in post-Avicennan Islamic logic. Unlike categorical syllogisms, it operates on relations between terms. The author defines it and then focuses on the first type of conclusion: the essential (bi al-dhat) conclusion. Through examples, you will learn how to combine the relation from the minor premise with the major term to produce a valid inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the minor and major terms
The minor term (al-asghar) is the subject of the desired conclusion. The major term (al-akbar) is the object of the relation in the major premise. For example, in 'Zayd is father of Bakr; Bakr is brother of Khalid', the minor term is Zayd, the major term is Khalid.
The middle term is the relatum that appears in both premises.
2
Locate the relation in the minor premise
Find the relation (nisba) that connects the minor term to the middle term. In the example, the relation is 'father of'. This relation will be preserved in the conclusion.
The relation can be any dyadic predicate, such as kinship, equality, containment, etc.
3
Identify the relation in the major premise
Determine the relation that connects the middle term to the major term. In the example, it is 'brother of'. This relation becomes part of the predicate in the conclusion.
The major premise may also have a non-relational predicate, e.g., 'Bakr is a scholar'.
4
Combine to form the essential conclusion
The essential conclusion has the minor term as subject and a compound predicate: the relation from the minor premise applied to the major term, optionally including the relation from the major premise. For example, 'Zayd is father of (brother of Khalid)' or 'Zayd is father of a scholar'.
The principle is: the relative of a relative is a relative by that same relation.
5
Verify using examples
Test the pattern with other examples: pearl in box and box in chest yields pearl in chest. Man from sperm and sperm from elements yields man from that which is from elements.
Transitive relations allow simplification, but the essential conclusion retains the intermediate step.

Valid Inferences in Disjunctive Conditional Syllogisms
Author lists productive forms of istithna'i syllogisms for conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of disjunctive conditionals (haqiqiyya, mani'at al-jam', mani'at al-khuluw) and identify the valid inference patterns for each.

Core Idea
The type of disjunction determines which direction of inference is valid — not all disjunctions support both affirming and denying.

Disjunctive conditionals ('either...or') come in three varieties, each with its own logical behaviour. This passage outlines the productive forms for each type. Understanding these distinctions is critical when evaluating legal arguments that present alternatives, such as whether an action is permissible or forbidden.

Guided Path
1
Identify a disjunctive conditional
Look for 'منفصلة' or 'either...or'. The disjunction has two parts (tarafan).
Example: 'The water is either pure or impure.'
2
Determine the type of disjunction
Classify as haqiqiyya (exactly one true), mani'at al-jam' (at most one true), or mani'at al-khuluw (at least one true). This depends on the relationship between the parts.
Use the definitions from the key terms.
3
Note the valid inference patterns for each type
For haqiqiyya, affirming one yields denying the other and vice versa. For mani'at al-jam', only affirming one yields denying the other. For mani'at al-khuluw, only denying one yields affirming the other.
Memorize these patterns.
4
Apply to a fiqh example
Given 'This substance is either halal or haram' (mani'at al-jam' if we assume it cannot be both but could be neither). If you know it is halal, conclude it is not haram. But if you know it is not halal, you cannot conclude it is haram because it could be something else (e.g., makruh).
Practice with different types.

Commentary
This passage provides the rules for productive istithna'i syllogisms. For a conjunctive conditional (muttasila), there are two valid forms: (1) affirming the antecedent yields the consequent, and (2) denying the consequent yields the denial of the antecedent. The text notes that this does not reverse (i.e., denying the antecedent does not yield the negation of the consequent). For disjunctive conditionals (munfasila), three types are distinguished: (1) real exclusive disjunction (haqiqiyya), where excepting one yields the negation of the other, and vice versa. (2) Exclusive of conjunction (mani'at al-jam'), where the two parts cannot both be true but may both be false. Here, affirming one yields the negation of the other, but not vice versa (i.e., denying one does not affirm the other because both could be false). (3) Exclusive of emptiness (mani'at al-khuluw), where the two parts cannot both be false but may both be true. Here, denying one yields affirming the other, but not vice versa (i.e., affirming one does not deny the other because both could be true). The author says these are obvious. In Islamic jurisprudence, an example of a real exclusive disjunction: 'This act is either wajib or haram' (cannot be both or neither). Mani'at al-jam': 'This is either a day or a month' (cannot be both but can be neither if night). Mani'at al-khuluw: 'This person is either healthy or sick' (cannot be neither but could be both? Actually in this case, it's likely an example).

Imam al-Razi's Objection on Excepting the Negation in Muttasila
Imam al-Razi argues that when the consequent is absolute universal, excepting its negation fails.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the rule of modus tollens fails in a conditional syllogism due to an absolute universal consequent and explain Imam al-Razi's critique.

Core Idea
Modus tollens fails when the consequent is absolute universal.

In conditional syllogisms, it is generally valid to infer the negation of the antecedent from the negation of the consequent. However, Imam al-Razi alerts us to a critical exception: when the consequent is an absolute universal proposition, this inference no longer holds. This lesson examines his objection and refines our understanding of valid conditional reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the standard rule for muttasila
Recall that in a conditional (muttasila) syllogism, negating the consequent (tali) yields the negation of the antecedent (muqaddam). This is known as modus tollens.
The rule assumes a genuine logical connection between antecedent and consequent.
2
Identify the condition al-Razi critiques
Al-Razi specifies that the rule fails when the consequent is an 'absolute universal' (mutlaqan 'amman), meaning the predicate applies to all subjects without any temporal or qualitative restriction.
Absolute universals are distinguished from conditional universals that may allow exceptions.
3
Examine al-Razi's counterexample
He gives the conditional: 'Whenever this is a human, it is actually laughing' with 'actually laughing' as absolute universal. Even if we assume this conditional is true, negating the consequent ('it is not laughing') does not entail 'it is not a human', because some humans (e.g., a silent person) are not laughing.
The example is chosen to illustrate the failure, not as a real universal statement.
4
Determine why the inference fails
The failure occurs because the conditional connection is not genuine; the consequent being absolute universal means it is not necessarily linked to the antecedent. For modus tollens to be valid, the conditional must be a necessary relationship, not an accidental universal.
Al-Razi refines the condition: the consequent must not be absolute universal in a way that allows exceptions.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection by Imam al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) against the general rule for muttasila. The rule states that negating the consequent yields the negation of the antecedent. However, if the consequent is a universal absolute proposition (i.e., true for all individuals at all times without qualification), then excepting its negation may not yield the negation of the antecedent. The example: 'Whenever this is a human, it is actually laughing' — here 'actually laughing' is claimed to be an absolute universal, meaning that every human is actually laughing, which is false because not all humans are laughing at a given moment. But the example is chosen to illustrate that even if we assume it is true, the negation 'it is not laughing' does not entail 'it is not a human', because there are non-laughing entities that are human (e.g., a silent human). Note: the example itself is problematic as a valid universal, but it serves to show that the rule fails when the consequent is absolute universal because the connection is not genuine. This objection refines the condition for the validity of the inference: the conditional must be genuine and the consequent must not be absolute universal in a way that allows exceptions.
