The Foreign Necessary Premise and Contrapositive
Author defines a syllogism type using a foreign premise and demonstrates via contrapositive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a syllogism with a foreign necessary premise and reconstruct its missing premise using contrapositive.

Core Idea
A foreign premise is unstated but logically necessary; contrapositive reveals it.

In this passage, the author introduces a special kind of syllogism where the conclusion follows from premises that are not fully explicit. One premise is 'foreign' — not stated — but necessarily implied. He shows how the missing premise can be recovered using the logical operation of contrapositive ('aks al-naqīḍ). This technique is crucial for evaluating arguments that appear incomplete but are logically valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two explicit premises
Find the two statements given in the example. One is: 'A part of substance necessitates by its removal the removal of substance.' The other: 'Everything that is not a substance does not necessitate by its removal the removal of substance.'
2
Spot the foreign premise
Notice that the conclusion 'A part of substance is a substance' does not directly follow from these two premises. There is a missing premise. That missing premise is the foreign premise — unstated but necessary.
3
Apply contrapositive to the major premise
The author says the conclusion is derived 'by means of the contrapositive of the major premise'. Take the major premise 'Everything that is not a substance does not necessitate...' and apply contrapositive to get: 'What necessitates by its removal the removal of substance is a substance.'
Contrapositive of 'If not-S then not-N' is 'If N then S'.
4
Reconstruct the full syllogism
Now combine the minor premise 'A part of substance necessitates...' with the contrapositive of the major (as the new major) to get the conclusion 'A part of substance is a substance'. This is a valid first-figure categorical syllogism.
5
Distinguish the two senses of syllogism
The author notes that this is a syllogism in the second sense (with an implicit premise), not the first (all explicit). Understand why the foreign premise makes it permissible under the broader definition.

Commentary
This passage from Qustas discusses a special type of syllogism called 'syllogism with a foreign necessary premise' (al-qiyas bi-muqaddima gharība lāzima). The author begins by distinguishing two cases: either the foreign premise alone produces the conclusion, or it does so together with an omitted premise. He then provides an example: 'A part of substance necessitates by its removal the removal of substance' and 'Everything that is not a substance does not necessitate by its removal the removal of substance' entail 'A part of substance is a substance' through the contrapositive of the major premise. The author then addresses an objection: some might argue that such an inference is not a proper syllogism because it relies on an unstated premise. The response is twofold: first, even if it is not a syllogism in the first sense (explicit premises), it is one in the second sense (implicit premise). Second, the production of the conclusion by an external (foreign) premise does not contradict production by something else; indeed, a reductio ad absurdum (khalaf) shows that the conclusion follows necessarily. If we assume the contradictory, we derive a contradiction. The author further explains that the omitted premise is not missing entirely but is replaced by an equivalent statement that has the same logical force. Thus, the syllogism is valid, albeit with a premise that is not explicitly stated but logically necessary. The passage illustrates the logical principle of conversion by contrapositive ('aks al-naqīḍ) and shows how syllogisms can be reconstructed from incomplete statements.

Defending the Foreign Premise Syllogism: Objections and Reductio
Author defends the foreign premise syllogism against an objection using reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the objection that a syllogism with an unstated premise is invalid by reconstructing its reductio proof.

Core Idea
Even if a premise is unstated, a reductio ad absurdum confirms the conclusion's necessity.

The author anticipates an objection: if a syllogism relies on a premise that is not explicitly stated, is it still a valid syllogism? He responds by showing that the same conclusion can be proven reductively. By assuming the contradictory of the conclusion and combining it with the given premises, a contradiction arises, proving the original conclusion. This lesson unpacks that defense and the logical moves involved.

Guided Path
1
Restate the objection
The critic says: 'Such examples are excluded by your definition of syllogism as composed of propositions.' He means the foreign premise syllogism does not have all premises explicit, so it should be invalid.
2
Identify the author's first response
The author grants it is not a syllogism in the first sense (all explicit), but it is in the second sense (with implicit premise). Understand the distinction: the second sense allows for necessary premises that are not stated.
3
Examine the reductio argument
The author says: assume the contradictory 'a part of substance is not a substance'. Combine with the second premise 'everything not a substance does not necessitate' to derive 'a part of substance does not necessitate', but the first premise says it does necessitate. This is a contradiction.
4
Verify the reductio structure
Check that the assumed premise (contradictory of conclusion) together with the given premises leads to a contradiction. This proves the original conclusion must be true.
5
Understand the author's final point
The omitted premise is not truly missing; its logical force is captured by the given premises. The syllogism is essentially valid because the missing premise is equivalent to the given one.

When Syllogisms Need Hidden Premises: The Lāzima and Ajnabiyya Example
Author gives an example of a productive syllogism with two strange premises and analyzes its mechanism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and supply the missing logical steps (contrapositive conversion and hidden premise) required to make a syllogism with two foreign premises yield its conclusion.

Core Idea
Not all stated premises are directly productive; sometimes you need conversion and hidden premises.

In classical logic, a syllogism is expected to have premises that directly produce the conclusion. But sometimes the premises as given do not share a middle term with the conclusion. In this lesson, we examine an example where the conclusion follows only after converting one premise and adding a hidden premise. This illustrates the concepts of 'lazima' (necessary but unstated) and 'ajnabiyya' (foreign) premises. Understanding this helps you evaluate arguments that seem to skip steps.

Guided Path
1
Identify the stated premises and conclusion
The passage gives two premises: 'This is white' and 'Every Zanj is always black.' The conclusion is 'This is not a Zanj.' Notice that the terms of the conclusion ('this' and 'Zanj') do not both appear together in any premise. 'This' appears in the first premise, 'Zanj' in the second, but there is no direct connection.
This is why the premises are called 'ajnabiyya' (foreign) to the conclusion.
2
Recognize the need for contrapositive conversion
The second premise 'Every Zanj is black' can be contrapositively converted to 'Whatever is not black is not a Zanj.' This is the operation of 'aks al-naqid. Now we have a premise that relates 'not black' to 'not Zanj.'
This conversion is valid only if the original premise is a universal affirmative.
3
Identify the hidden premise (lāzima)
The first premise 'This is white' does not directly connect to 'not black.' We need a hidden premise like 'White is not black' or 'Whatever is white is not black.' This premise links the subject of the conclusion (this) to the converted premise.
The hidden premise is necessary but not stated; it is a necessary truth based on the nature of colors.
4
Reconstruct the full syllogism
Now we have three premises: (1) This is white. (2) White is not black (hidden). (3) Whatever is not black is not a Zanj. Therefore: This is not a Zanj. This is a valid categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara) if we arrange terms appropriately. The middle term is 'white' and 'not black' through the hidden premise.
The author says the syllogism is productive 'by means of the contrapositive conversion and a deleted premise.'

Imam al-Razi's first doubt: the sum of premises cannot simultaneously exist in the mind.

Core Idea
Al-Razi's first doubt argues that none of the possible causes of the syllogistic conclusion—the sum, each premise, or some premises—can actually produce it.

In this lesson, we encounter one of the most famous critiques of the syllogism from the Islamic tradition. Imam al-Razi, in his work al-Mulakhkhas, raises a profound doubt: what exactly causes the conclusion of a syllogism to follow from the premises? He systematically examines three possibilities and finds each impossible. This critique challenges the very foundation of Aristotelian logic and forces us to rethink how reasoning works.

Commentary
This passage presents the first doubt of Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi against the claim that a syllogism produces new knowledge. Al-Razi enumerates three possibilities for what causes the conclusion: (1) the sum of the premises (the entire collection of knowledges), (2) each premise individually, or (3) some premises but not others. Then al-Razi dismisses the second possibility (each premise is a cause) as necessarily false, because we know that a single premise alone does not produce a conclusion. The third possibility (some premises are the cause) fails because then the other premises would be superfluous. Thus the syllogism does not produce new knowledge in the sense of a causal process.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A categorical syllogism is built from two premises sharing a middle term; its structure is defined by figure and mood.

This lesson extracts the key definitions from the Shaykh's text on categorical syllogisms. Guided Path
1

The passage states a syllogism is composed of two premises (muqaddima). One is 'minor' (sughra) containing the subject of the conclusion, the other is 'major' (kubra) containing the predicate. 5
Distinguish matlub from natija
The passage notes that the conclusion is called 'matlub' (desired) when it is the goal of the reasoning, and 'natija' (result) when it is derived. This distinction is procedural: we seek the matlub, then the syllogism gives the natija.

The Fourth Figure and the Reductio Argument
Avicenna's doubt about the first-figure conditions and resolution via the fourth figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourth figure of a categorical syllogism and explain how it can produce valid conclusions even when the first-figure conditions are not met.

Core Idea
A syllogism that seems to violate first-figure conditions may actually be valid in the fourth figure, as revealed by the relation of its terms to the conclusion.

The Shaykh (Avicenna) raises a doubt: the premises 'No J is B' and 'Some B is A' appear to violate the conditions for a first-figure syllogism, yet they yield a conclusion 'Some A is not J' via reductio. The author explains that when the conclusion is considered, the syllogism's figure changes to the fourth. This lesson examines this argument and the concept of the fourth figure in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Premises and Conclusion
The example: 'Nothing of J is B' (No J is B) and 'Some B is A'. The conclusion derived is 'Some A is not J' by reductio ad absurdum.
J, B, A are term variables representing categories.
2
Check First-Figure Conditions
The first figure requires an affirmative minor and a universal major. Here, the minor 'Some B is A' is particular affirmative (I), not universal, and the major 'No J is B' is universal negative (E). So both conditions are violated.
3
Understand Reductio Derivation
The conclusion 'Some A is not J' is derived by assuming the contradictory 'All A is J' and showing it leads to a contradiction with the premises. This technique is valid but does not follow the direct categorical form of the first figure.
4
Recognize the Fourth Figure
When the syllogism is measured against the conclusion 'Some A is not J', the major term is J, the minor term is A, and the middle term is B. In the first figure, the middle is subject of the major and predicate of the minor. Here, the major premise 'No J is B' has middle as predicate; the minor 'Some B is A' has middle as subject. This arrangement corresponds to the fourth figure.
The fourth figure is defined as: middle is predicate of major and subject of minor.
5
Apply by Determining Figure
Given a set of premises and a conclusion, identify the terms and determine the figure. Practise with Islamic examples to see when the figure shifts.

How Indefinite and Determinate Propositions Behave in Syllogisms
Author explains the treatment of indefinite and determinate propositions in syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a proposition as indefinite, determinate, or quantified and determine how it affects the form and conclusion of a categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Indefinite propositions are treated as particulars; determinate propositions are treated as universals, but their conclusions remain determinate.

When building a syllogism, not all propositions have explicit quantifiers like 'all' or 'some'. In this lesson, we examine two types: indefinite propositions (al-muhmalat) with no quantifier, and determinate propositions (al-makhsosat) with a specific subject like a proper name. The author, following Ibn Sina, gives a clear rule for how each functions in a syllogism and shows that the third figure may sometimes produce a universal conclusion, contrary to what earlier logicians thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three proposition types
Distinguish quantified (mahsurat: have explicit quantifiers), indefinite (muhmalat: no quantifier), and determinate (makhsosat: refer to a specific individual, e.g., 'Zayd').
Indefinite like 'Humans are animals' is actually particular; determinate like 'Zayd is standing' is treated as universal because it covers the single instance.
2
Apply the basic rule for premises
Indefinite propositions are treated as particulars, and determinate propositions as universals when checking the validity of syllogistic figures. This allows determinate and indefinite premises to be used as universal or particular in the figure conditions.
For example, a determinate can serve as the major premise in the first or second figure because it is considered universal.
3
Analyze the conclusion rule for determinates
Although determinates are treated as universals regarding premise form, the conclusion from two determinate premises is determinate, not universal. The passage states: 'the conclusion from two determinate premises is mostly determinate (i.e., always in the first two figures).'
Example: From 'The Shaykh al-Ra'is is a philosopher' and 'The Shaykh al-Ra'is is a doctor', we conclude 'The Shaykh al-Ra'is is a doctor-philosopher' (determinate), not 'Every philosopher is a doctor' (universal).
4
Examine the third figure special case
The passage notes that the third figure can yield a universal conclusion when the minor term is a true universal and the major is a true particular concept. This occurs with a negative minor premise, as in the example: 'The Shaykh al-Ra'is is not a sultan' (negative determinate) and 'The Shaykh al-Ra'is is Ibn Sina' (affirmative determinate), concluding 'No sultan is Ibn Sina' (universal negative).
This challenges earlier logicians who thought the third figure could only produce particular conclusions.

Conditions for a Productive Fourth Figure
States the conditions for a valid fourth figure syllogism and identifies sterile combinations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given fourth figure categorical syllogism is productive or sterile by applying the conditions on negation and particularity.

Core Idea
Avoid the deadly combination of negation and particularity in fourth figure premises.

The fourth figure of the categorical syllogism has its own restrictive rules. Unlike the first three figures, it imposes a careful balance between negation and particularity. Mastering these conditions will allow you to quickly spot which fourth figure combinations yield a valid conclusion and which are sterile (عقيم).

Guided Path
1
Confirm the figure
Ensure the syllogism is in the fourth figure: the middle term is the predicate of the minor premise and the subject of the major premise.
2
Catalog premises for negation and particularity
For each premise, note whether it is affirmative or negative (salb) and universal or particular (juz'i). Mark the presence of the two characteristics: negation and particularity.
A universal negative premise has negation but not particularity; a particular affirmative has particularity but not negation.
3
Apply the general condition
Check that the two characteristics (negation and particularity) do not come together in the same premise or across the two premises. If they do, the syllogism is sterile.
For example, a particular negative premise in either position violates the condition.
4
Apply the exception
If the minor premise is affirmative particular (موجبة جزئية), then the major premise must be universal negative (سالبة كلية). Otherwise, even if the general condition is met, the syllogism is sterile.
This exception allows a valid fourth figure mood when the minor is particular affirmative.
5
Declare productivity or sterility
If all conditions are satisfied, the fourth figure syllogism can produce a necessary conclusion. If any condition is violated, it is sterile (عقيم) and yields no valid inference.

Proving Productive Moods: Swapping and Conversion
Explanation of proof methods: swapping and conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply premise swapping and conversion to prove that a syllogistic mood is productive.

Core Idea
Swapping and converting premises are fundamental tools to validate syllogistic moods.

This passage from the Qustas introduces five methods for proving that a syllogistic mood yields a valid conclusion. We begin with the two most straightforward: swapping the premises (tabdīl al-muqaddimatayn) and conversion (aks). These are essential for reducing moods to the first figure or directly deriving the conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mood's premises
List the minor and major premises of the syllogism in standard order (minor then major).
If the order is reversed, note that swapping may be needed.
2
Apply swapping if necessary
If the premises are not in the correct order for the desired figure, swap them. This is equivalent to reordering without changing logical content.
Swapping is always safe and does not affect validity.
3
Apply conversion to a premise
If a premise is of a type that converts (e.g., universal affirmative to particular affirmative), perform the conversion. This may bring the middle term into the required position.
Not all premises convert (e.g., particular negative does not convert).
4
Check if the mood is now productive
After swapping and/or conversion, see if the resulting premises fit a productive mood (e.g., first figure). If they do, the original mood is productive.
The conclusion may also need conversion after deduction.
