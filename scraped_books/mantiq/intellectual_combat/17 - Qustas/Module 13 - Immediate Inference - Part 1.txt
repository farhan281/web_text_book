Al-Farabi's Condition for Contradiction
Author states al-Farabi's view on conditions for contradiction, and the perplexity of later scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Farabi's position that unity of time alone is sufficient for contradiction and why later scholars were perplexed.

Core Idea
Al-Farabi: time alone is the essential condition for contradiction.

Al-Farabi's view on the conditions of contradiction (tanaqud) highlights a key methodological principle: one condition can subsume another. While later logicians required both unity of time and unity of place, al-Farabi argued that place is dependent on time. This seemingly minor point reveals how logical analysis refines our understanding of identity and difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify standard conditions for contradiction
Recall that for two propositions to be contradictory, they must agree in all conditions (subject, predicate, time, place, etc.). The standard list includes unity of time and unity of place as separate conditions.
These conditions ensure that the contradiction is genuine, not merely apparent due to a change in context.
2
Understand al-Farabi's reduction
Al-Farabi asserts that unity of place is already subsumed under unity of time. That is, any change in place necessarily implies a change in time (e.g., being in a different location at the same time is impossible). Thus, requiring time alone captures all relevant variation.
This is a claim about ontological priority: time is more fundamental than place.
3
Analyze later scholars' perplexity
Later logicians struggled because they treated place as independent of time. For them, two propositions could refer to the same time but different places, and they considered such propositions as potentially non-contradictory if the rulings differ by place. Al-Farabi's view forces a re-evaluation of the relationship between time and place.
This perplexity arises from different metaphysical assumptions about the nature of time and place.
4
Evaluate implications for logical analysis
Al-Farabi's approach shows that condition hierarchy matters: a higher-level condition can subsume lower-level ones. This is a tool for simplifying logical analysis and avoiding redundant conditions.
In practice, this means checking time before place, as place variations may be irrelevant if time is already fixed.

This lesson clarifies the concept of 'partial difference' (al-mubayanah al-juz'iyyah), a key relation in immediate inference. Core Idea
The contradictory is pure negation; the modified is not the same.

The doubt against Avicenna's conversion arises from confusing the contradictory of a term with its modified opposite. In Arabic, 'naqīd al-bā' salbuhu' – the contradictory of B is its negation.

Define ma'dūla (modified)
A ma'dūla proposition has a predicate that is the opposite of the original, e.g., 'Zayd is not-stone' (affirming the attribute of 'not-stone'). This is different from simple negation.
The opponents mistakenly took the contradictory as a modified term.

Examine the objection
Opponents said Avicenna 'made the subject itself a predicate in the negative', implying he used the modified term. The author refutes this by showing the conversion of 'No human is a stone'.
The strict conversion yields 'Not everything not a stone is not a human', which has negative terms but is not affirming 'not-stone' as a positive attribute.
4
Trace Avicenna's simplification
Avicenna replaced the strict conversion with 'Some not-stone is a human' for brevity. This is an affirmative with a negative subject, but it does not change the logic.
The simplified form is equivalent to the strict conversion.
5
Apply the distinction to the core doubt
The core doubt is that opponents confused naqīd with ma'dūla. Once we use the correct contradictory, the conversion is valid and consistent.
The conversion of 'Every J is B' uses the contradictory of B (not B) and the contradictory of J (not J), not their modified forms.

Commentary

The Four Logical Relations Between Concepts
Author enumerates the four possible relations between any two concepts with their definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of concepts into one of the four logical relations and justify your classification.

Core Idea
Every pair of concepts has exactly one of four logical relations: equality, absolute generality, partial overlap, or complete disparity.

In logic, when we compare two concepts, they can relate in only four ways. This classification helps us understand definitions, classifications, and arguments. The passage from Qustas enumerates these four relations. Let's explore each one and learn how to identify them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two concepts being compared.
Take any two concepts, e.g., 'human' (insan) and 'animal' (hayawan). Ask: what do they refer to?
2
Check for equality (musāwāh).
If every instance of concept A is an instance of concept B, and every instance of B is an instance of A, they are equal. Example: 'rational animal' and 'human'.
If equal, you are done.
3
If not equal, test for absolute generality.
If one concept applies to everything the other applies to, but not vice versa, then it is absolutely general. Example: 'animal' applies to every 'human', but not every animal is human.
Identify which is the broader (general) and which is the narrower (specific).
4
If neither equality nor absolute generality, check for partial overlap or disparity.
If they share some instances but each also has instances the other does not, it is generality from one aspect. If they share no instances, it is complete disparity.
Example of partial overlap: 'teacher' and 'male' – some teachers are male, some are female, some males are teachers, some are not. Example of disparity: 'stone' and 'living' – no overlap.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a broader term is always narrower, but only if the term is not the broadest possible—unless it is privative.

Core Idea
Contradictories of partially general terms always have partial divergence.

In Islamic logic, understanding the relations between terms is essential for valid reasoning. This lesson focuses on a specific rule: when two terms are partially general (each applies to some but not all of the other's instances), their contradictories are not simply related – they exhibit partial divergence. We will parse the author's concise statement to grasp this important principle.

Identify the two terms under consideration

Form the contradictory of each term
The contradictory of a term is its negation. For 'insan', the contradictory is 'la insan' (non-human). For 'aswad', it is 'la aswad' (non-black).
Contradictories are formed by adding negation to the term.

Now check if 'la insan' is true of some 'la aswad' instances. A non-human non-black thing, e.g., a white horse, is indeed both non-human and non-black, so 'la insan' is true of some 'la aswad'. Condition for Valid Conversion: Agreement in Universality
Author proposes a condition to fix conversion errors by requiring agreement in universality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a conversion between two propositions is valid based on the agreement of subject and predicate terms in universality or particularity.

Core Idea
Valid conversion requires subject and predicate to agree in universality or particularity.

This passage addresses a common error in converting propositions: assuming that if 'Some A is B' is true, then 'Some B is A' is also always true. The author provides a condition to avoid such errors by ensuring that the 'title' (unwan) of the subject and the concept (mafhum) of the predicate match in terms of universality (kulliyya) and particularity (juz'iyya). Understanding this condition is crucial for correct immediate inferences in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand conversion in mantiq
Conversion (aks) swaps subject and predicate of a proposition. The author focuses on quantified propositions used in conversions and syllogisms.
The condition applies especially when conversion is used as part of a syllogistic proof.
2
Identify the condition on subject and predicate
The condition is that the 'title' (unwan) of the subject must not conflict with the concept (mafhum) of the predicate in terms of universality or particularity. For example, if the subject term is universal, the predicate term must also be universal; if the subject is individual, the predicate must also be individual.
This prevents mismatched conversions.
3
Apply the condition to the example
The author gives the example: 'Some human is Zayd' is true but its conversion 'Some Zayd is human' is false. Why? Because 'human' is a universal term while 'Zayd' is an individual term. The condition is violated, so the conversion is invalid.
This shows the condition in action.
4
Generalize the condition's role
This condition repairs corruptions in conversion rules and ensures logical consistency in syllogistic reasoning. It is a check for any immediate inference involving conversion.
Without this condition, many false conversions would be accepted.

Commentary
This passage offers a solution to conversion problems. The author proposes that to fix corruptions in conversion and syllogism rules, we must impose a condition on quantified propositions: the expression used as subject (its 'title') must not conflict with the predicate's concept in terms of universality or particularity. For example, if the subject is a universal term (e.g., 'man'), the predicate must also be universal in the relevant sense; if the subject is a proper name (singular/individual), the predicate should not be a universal. This condition blocks invalid conversions like from 'Some human is Zayd' (true) to 'Some Zayd is human' (false), because 'Zayd' is an individual while 'human' is universal. Thus, the condition ensures logical consistency.

Contradictories of Simple Modal Propositions
Author enumerates contradictories for simple modals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory (naqid) of each simple modal proposition and state the rule for its formation.

Core Idea
Every simple modal has a simple contradictory: necessity contradicted by possibility, permanence by actual negation.

The author begins with the simplest case: each simple modal proposition has a simple contradictory. The rule is systematic: absolute necessary is contradicted by general possible, because possibility is the negation of the opposite necessity. Permanent is contradicted by absolute (actual) negation. Conditional and customary modals have their counterparts involving whenness and possibility. Mastering these pairs is foundational for understanding compound contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the simple modal type
Look at the modal qualifier attached to the proposition. Common types are: necessary (ضرورية), permanent (دائمة), conditional (مشروطة), customary (عرفية). Each has a specific contradictory.
2
Recall the contradictory rule from the passage
For necessary, contradictory is general possible (ممكنة عامة). For permanent, contradictory is general absolute (مطلقة عامة). For general conditional, contradictory is 'possible whenness' (حينية ممكنة). For general customary, contradictory is 'absolute whenness' (حينية مطلقة).
General means without restriction.
3
Apply the rule to a given simple modal
Take a proposition like 'Every human is necessarily rational'. Its contradictory is 'Not every human is necessarily rational', which is equivalent to 'Some human is possibly non-rational' (general possible).
The contradictory must be a simple modal itself.
4
Verify the contradiction
Check that the two cannot both be true and cannot both be false. For necessary and possible, if necessary is true, possible is false; if possible is true, necessary is false. They exhaust all possibilities.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and analytical exposition of the rules for finding the contradictory (naqid) of various modal propositions. The author begins with simple modal propositions, stating that each simple modal has a simple contradictory. For example, the contradictory of absolute necessary is general possible, because possibility is the negation of the opposite necessity. Similarly, the contradictory of permanent is general absolute, since permanent affirmation is contradicted by actual negation. Then the author turns to compound modals, which are either universal or particular. For universal compound modals, the contradictory is not a single proposition but a disjunctive concept between the contradictories of its two parts. The reasoning is that if the original compound is false due to the falsity of one part, each of the two possible contradictories (and even their conjunction) might be false as well. Therefore, the contradictory must be the disjunction of the two contradictories. The author then lists specific examples, such as the special conditional, special customary, temporal, diffuse, existential non-permanent, existential non-necessary, and special possible. For particular compound modals, the author demonstrates that a simple disjunction between the two universal contradictories is insufficient. Using the example of 'human' with respect to 'animal', he shows that if some animals are always humans and some are not, then the original particular proposition is false, but both universal contradictories ('no animal is always human' and 'every animal is always human') are also false. Therefore, one must disjoin between the contradictories of the two parts for each individual subject, resulting in three possible concepts, one of which exactly equals the contradictory. The author then anticipates an objection: if the particular is false, why not simply take the disjunction of the two universals? He replies that the second part (non-permanence) has a restricted subject and is more specific than the universal contradictory; falsity of the specific does not imply truth of the general. Finally, he presents al-Khunaji's method: to obtain a proposition equal to the contradictory of the particular, restrict the subject of one side of the disjunction. This passage is a rigorous logical analysis essential for understanding modal opposition in Aristotelian logic as adapted by Islamic logicians.

بسيطة (basīṭa): A simple proposition without compounding; here, a simple modal proposition. [Note: Consistent usage.]

The negation is formed as the disjunction: 'No J is B always' (لا شيء من ج ب دائماً) OR 'Every J is B not always' (كل ج ب لا دائماً). Notice the subject of the first disjunct is unrestricted, but the second disjunct has the predicate 'not always' attached to the predicate B.
This is the 'restriction' mentioned in the passage.
3
Verify equivalence: original true case
If the original is true, then there is some J that is B, but not always. Therefore, it is false that no J is B always (since some J is B), and it is false that every J is B not always (since the 'not always' condition is not universal). So both disjuncts are false, making the disjunction false. Thus when original true, negation false (consistent).
4
Verify equivalence: original false case
If the original is false, then either no J is B at all, in which case the first disjunct 'No J is B always' is true. Or there is some J that is B, but then it must be that every J is B always (otherwise the original would be true). In that case, the second disjunct 'Every J is B not always' is true. So exactly one disjunct is true, making the disjunction true. Hence the disjunction is equivalent to the negation.
The proof uses a reductio: if both disjuncts were false when original false, a contradiction arises.
5
Recognize the scope limitation
This method works only for 'existential non-persistent' propositions (الوجودية اللادائمة). For other types like 'special customary' (العرفية الخاصة), the equivalence fails because the original can be false while both disjuncts are false.
Example: 'Some body is moving as long as it is a body not always'.

The True Naqīḍ: Bare Negation
Definition of naqīḍ as bare negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the true contradictory (naqīḍ) of any proposition or term based on the author's definition.

Core Idea
The contradictory is bare negation, nothing more.

In this passage, the author corrects a common confusion: what logicians usually call 'contradictory' is actually not the true contradictory, but an equivalent. We first need to understand what the true contradictory is according to the author's precise definition.

Guided Path

2

The author explicitly includes both propositions and terms: 'سواء كان الشيء قضية أو مفرداً' (whether the thing is a proposition or a term). So naqīḍ applies to any logical unit.

The author states: 'فما ذكروا في نقيض القضايا ليس شيء منها نقيضاً، بل مساوياً' (So what they mentioned concerning the contradictory of propositions — none of them is a contradictory, but rather an equivalent). Recognize that standard textbook contradictory forms are not the true naqīḍ.
4

The essence: naqīḍ is simply the negation (رفع) without any extra conditions, quantifier changes, or structural modifications. It is the most direct logical opposite.
This pure negation is often too raw for practical logical operations, which is why the musāwī is introduced.

Core Idea

This rule is essential for transforming propositions in syllogistic reasoning. Let's examine why this must be the case.

Alternative Proofs and Conversion of Special Propositions
Second and third proofs and special propositions rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe two alternative proofs for the conversion rule and convert special (khāṣṣa) propositions into temporal non-perpetual propositions.

Core Idea
Special propositions convert to temporals that are not perpetual, and the contradictory method provides an alternative proof.

The author continues with two more proofs for the conversion of perpetual/general propositions: one by combining the contradictory of the converse with the original to derive a self-negation, and another by converting the contradictory of the converse to get the contradictory of the original. Then he addresses special propositions (khāṣṣatān) which also convert to a temporal that is not perpetual.

Guided Path
1
Understand the second proof
Take the contradictory of the converse (i.e., not-Some B is A) and combine it with the original (Every A is B). This leads to the negation of something by itself, which is impossible, so the contradictory is false and the converse must be true.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.
2
Understand the third proof
Convert the contradictory of the converse to get the contradictory of the original. If that result is false, then the original contradictory is false, so the converse is true.
This uses the conversion rule itself, making it circular? The author presents it as a proof.
3

Special propositions (e.g., 'Some human is always laughing') convert to a temporal that is not perpetual. The converse holds at some time but not always, because the subject may not always have the predicate.
Example: 'Some human is necessarily laughing' converts to 'Some laughing being is human at some time', not always.

Conversion of Perpetual and Universal Affirmatives to Absolute Temporal
Author presents proofs for conversion of perpetual and common universal affirmatives to absolute temporal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert a perpetual or common universal affirmative proposition into its correct absolute temporal converse and identify the logical proof used.

Core Idea
Perpetual and common universal affirmatives convert to particular absolute temporal propositions, not to universal or conditional ones.

Here the author explains the conversion rules for two types of universal affirmative propositions: the perpetual (dā'imatān) and the common ('āmmatān). These do not convert to another universal but rather to a particular absolute temporal (ḥīniyya muṭlaqa). Three logical proofs are given to establish this rule, each demonstrating the impossibility of an invalid conversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition type
Determine if the proposition is perpetual (always true, like 'Every human is necessarily animal') or common (absolute, like 'Every man is laughing'). These are universal affirmatives.
2
Set up the conversion target
The converse should be an absolute temporal particular: 'Some B is J when it is B' (some predicate is subject at the time of being the predicate).
3
Apply the first proof (using example)
Take an individual of J that is B as long as J exists. From the original, D is B as long as J, but D is J in actuality. Hence B and J coexist in D, so some B is J when it is B. This uses third figure reasoning.
The third figure is used to derive the conclusion.
4
Apply the second and third proofs (contradiction method)
In proof 2, combine the contradictory of the desired converse with the original to derive a self-negation, showing impossibility. In proof 3, convert the contradictory of the converse to get the contradictory of the original, validating the converse.
Both proofs rely on the impossibility of a self-contradiction.
5
Recognize the limitation
The converse cannot be more specific (e.g., conditional) because the subject may separate from the predicate's description. Example: 'Every laughing thing is necessarily human' does not convert to 'Some human is always laughing' because laughing is not essential.
The original's subject (laughing) may not always be actual under the description 'human'.

Conversion of Universal Negative Perpetuals
Author specifies conversion rule for universal negative perpetuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of conversion for universal negative perpetual propositions and identify the resulting modal weakening from necessary to absolute perpetual.

Core Idea
Perpetual negatives become absolute perpetual upon conversion, not necessary.

In this passage, the author presents a specific rule for converting universal negative perpetual propositions. While earlier logicians often allowed necessary perpetuals to convert as necessary, this refinement restricts the conversion to absolute perpetual. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly performing immediate inferences in syllogistic reasoning, especially when dealing with modal propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Recognize that the proposition is a universal negative (e.g., 'No S is P') with a perpetual modality (holding for all times). This is a 'dā'ima' (perpetual) proposition.
The passage mentions 'the two perpetuals', likely meaning both necessary perpetual and absolute perpetual negatives.
2
Recall the standard conversion rule
In Aristotelian logic, a universal negative 'No S is P' converts simply to 'No P is S'. This holds regardless of modality at the level of truth.
Standard conversion preserves truth value but not necessarily modality.
3
Apply the modal refinement
The author states that the two perpetuals (necessary and absolute perpetual negatives) convert to the 'absolute perpetual' (dā'ima muṭlaqa). This means the converted proposition is not necessary but holds perpetually without necessity.
This is a refinement by later logicians (e.g., Khūnajī) who distinguished between necessary and absolute perpetual.
4
Verify with an example
Take 'No human is a stone perpetually (necessarily)'. Converting gives 'No stone is a human perpetually (absolutely)', not 'necessarily'. The necessity is lost.
The modality weakens from necessary to absolute, which is important for syllogistic validity.
