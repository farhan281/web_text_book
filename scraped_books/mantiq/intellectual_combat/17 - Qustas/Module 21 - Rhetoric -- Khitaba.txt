Rhetorical Syllogism: Persuasion through Probable Premises
Defines rhetorical syllogism and its permissible forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining features of a rhetorical syllogism and distinguish it from demonstrative syllogism.

Core Idea
Rhetorical syllogisms persuade using probable premises, not certain ones.

In this lesson, we examine the rhetorical syllogism (qiyas khatabi), one of the five arts of logic. Unlike demonstrative syllogisms that yield certainty, rhetorical syllogisms aim at persuasion (iqna') and preponderant opinion (zann ghalib) using premises that are probable or accepted but not necessarily true. The logicians allow even formally invalid forms for rhetorical purposes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus and differentia
The rhetorical syllogism is a type of syllogism (qiyas). Its differentia is that its premises are 'probable opinions and accepted premises not commonly known' (المظنونات والمقبولات التي ليست بمشهورة).
Probable = likely true but not certain; accepted = widely agreed in a community.
2
Determine the purpose
The stated purpose is 'persuasion and preponderant opinion' (الإقناع والظن الغالب). This contrasts with demonstration which aims at certainty.
3
Note permissible forms
The author states: 'it is permissible to use induction, analogy, and sterile moods' (جاز استعمال الاستقراء والتمثيل والضروب العقيمة). These are not valid in demonstrative logic but serve rhetorical ends.
Sterile moods = formally invalid categorical syllogisms.

Commentary
This passage defines two more types of syllogism: rhetorical (khatabi) and poetic (shi'ri). The rhetorical syllogism uses premises that are probable (مظنونات) or accepted (مقبولات) but not widely known (مشهورة). Its purpose is persuasion (إقناع) and producing a preponderant opinion (ظن غالب). Unlike demonstration, it allows induction, analogy, and formally invalid moods. The poetic syllogism uses imaginative premises (مخيلة) that move the soul to desire or fear (ترغيب و ترهيب), regardless of truth. These are the lower forms of argument in the five arts classification. They serve emotional and persuasive ends, not certainty.

Poetic Syllogism: Moving the Soul through Imagination
Defines poetic syllogism and its emotional purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the poetic syllogism and explain how it differs from other syllogistic arts.

Core Idea
Poetic syllogisms use imaginative premises to affect emotions, not to establish truth.

The poetic syllogism (qiyas shi'ri) is the lowest of the five logical arts, designed to move the soul through imagination. Its premises are called 'imaginative' (mukhayyilah) – they produce mental images that encourage or discourage action, regardless of truth value. This lesson unpacks its definition and purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus and differentia
Like the rhetorical syllogism, poetic syllogism is a type of syllogism. Its differentia is that its premises are 'imaginative' (مخيلة) – i.e., they create imagery that affects the soul.
Imaginative premises may be literally false but emotionally effective.
2
Determine the purpose
The purpose is 'to affect the soul by encouragement and intimidation' (لانفعال النفس بالترغيب والترهيب). This is an emotional, not cognitive, aim.
ترغيب = encouragement (desire), ترهيب = intimidation (fear).
3
Note the indifference to truth
The author states the premises may be 'true or false' (صادقة كانت أو كاذبة). This contrasts with demonstration and even rhetoric, which at least aim at probable truth.
