Truth Conditions of the Implicative Connected Conditional
Author states truth conditions for luzumiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether an implicative connected conditional is true or false given the truth values of its antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
An implicative conditional is false only when the antecedent is true and the consequent is false.

Connected conditionals are central to logical inference in Islamic sciences. The implicative type (luzumiyya) expresses a necessary relation: if the antecedent holds, the consequent must follow. Understanding exactly which truth-value combinations make it true or false is essential for evaluating arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
Locate the muqaddam (antecedent) and tali (consequent) in the conditional statement. For example, in 'If the world is temporally originated, then it has a creator', the antecedent is 'the world is temporally originated' and the consequent is 'it has a creator'.
In Arabic, the antecedent follows 'إن' or 'إذا'.
2
Recall the four truth-value combinations
Each part can be true (T) or false (F), giving four pairs: (T,T), (T,F), (F,T), (F,F). We need to check which pairs make the overall conditional true.
These are the standard truth table rows.
3
Apply the rule for implicative conditionals
The author states that the true implicative conditional is composed of two true parts (T,T) or two false parts (F,F). It is also true when the antecedent is false and the consequent is true (F,T). The only false case is (T,F): true antecedent with false consequent.
The reason given: 'the true cannot be implied by the false' – meaning a true consequent cannot follow from a false antecedent in an implicative sense, but that combination still makes the conditional true? Wait: the author says 'its opposite is impossible, for the true cannot be implied by the false'. He is referring to (F,T): a true consequent with false antecedent is possible (false can imply true), but (T,F) is impossible because truth cannot imply falsehood. So (F,T) is allowed, (T,F) is not.
4
Verify with an example
Consider 'If it is daytime, then the sun is risen'. This is false if it is daytime but the sun is not risen (T,F). It is true if both are true (daytime and sun risen), both false (night and sun not risen), or false antecedent with true consequent (night but sun risen? That is actually impossible physically, but logically allowed).
The example shows why only (T,F) is prohibited in luzumiyya.

Truth Conditions of the Accidental Connected Conditional
Author enumerates truth conditions for accidental conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific accidental conditionals and determine their truth conditions.

Core Idea
An accidental conditional is true only when its consequent is true (general) or both parts are true (specific), provided there is no necessary relation.

Accidental conditionals (ittifaqiyya) lack a necessary connection between antecedent and consequent. The author divides them into two types: general, where only the consequent's truth matters, and specific, where both parts must be true. These are important in debate contexts where the opponent uses an accidental connection to make a false inference seem plausible.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish general and specific accidental conditionals
The general accidental conditional is one where the truth of the consequent alone is sufficient for the truth of the whole conditional, provided it does not conflict with the antecedent. The specific accidental conditional requires both parts to be true together.
General: 'If the sun rises, then the earth revolves' – the consequent is true regardless. Specific: 'If the sun rises, then it is daytime' – both are true only at sunrise.
2
Truth conditions for the general accidental conditional
For the general type, the conditional is true only when the consequent is true and compatible with the antecedent. If the consequent is false or conflicts, it is false. Also, if there is a necessary relation ('alāqa), the conditional becomes implicative and its truth conditions change.
Compatibility means the antecedent does not force the consequent to be false.
3
Truth conditions for the specific accidental conditional
For the specific type, the conditional is true only when both antecedent and consequent are true together. If either is false, the conditional is false. Again, if there is a necessary relation, it becomes luzumiyya.
Example: 'If the muezzin calls the adhan, then it is time for prayer' - both true at prayer time.
4
Identify the presence of 'alāqa
If the connection between antecedent and consequent is necessary, then the conditional is not accidental but implicative. The author warns that if there is a 'alāqa (necessary relation), the truth conditions revert to those of luzumiyya.
This is crucial: an apparent accidental conditional may actually be implicative if the connection is necessary.

Core Idea
Accidental conditionals are true by coincidence, not by implication.

This passage distinguishes two types of accidental conditionals: general and specific. The general accidental conditional is true whenever the consequent is true, regardless of the antecedent, as long as there is no contradiction. The specific type requires both parts to be true. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly evaluating conditional propositions in Islamic logic.

For the general type, the truth of the consequent alone is sufficient. The conditional is true when the consequent is true and not contradictory to the antecedent. It is false when the consequent is false, and also false in the first two truth-value combinations if there is an implication relation.

Agreement Disjunctions and Negative Compositions
Explains composition rules for agreement disjunctions and negation criterion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify true and false agreement disjunctions and apply the criterion for negating conditional propositions.

Core Idea
In conditional logic, judge the relation, not the parts — that is the key to affirmation and negation.

This passage addresses a subtle but crucial point in conditional logic: how to compose true and false agreement disjunctions, and how negation interacts with conditionals. You will learn the criterion that governs when a conditional is affirmed or negated — a rule that prevents confusion between affirming the parts and affirming the relation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of agreement disjunctions
Recognize that agreement disjunctions (المنفصلة الاتفاقية) come in three varieties: exclusive (مانعة الجمع), inclusive (مانعة الخلو), and collective (حقيقية). All are 'agreement' because the opposition between parts is accidental.
2
Understand composition of true agreement disjunctions
A true agreement disjunction is composed based on opposition patterns: it is true when the parts are in a state of opposition (like exclusive disjunction), regardless of which type.
3
Understand composition of false agreement disjunctions
A false agreement disjunction is composed from parts that would make the exclusive disjunctions definitely false, and from parts where they would be true if there is a relation.
4
Grasp the rule for negative compositions
For negatives, the rule is the opposite: a negative proposition is true exactly when its corresponding affirmative is false, and false when the affirmative is true.
This is the basic truth-functional relation between affirmation and negation.
5
Apply the criterion for affirming/negating conditionals
The criterion is to affirm or negate the relational judgment (implication, opposition, or agreement) itself, not to affirm or negate the two parts individually.
Example: To negate 'if A then B', you negate the relation of implication, not A or B.

The Genuine Exclusive Disjunction Has Exactly Two Parts
Proves that the genuine exclusive disjunction has exactly two parts via contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the necessary two-part structure of a genuine exclusive disjunction and explain why it cannot have more than two parts using the principle of contradiction.

Core Idea
A genuine exclusive disjunction is always between a proposition and its direct negation.

This passage tackles a subtle but essential point: the genuine exclusive disjunction (al-munfasila al-haqiqiyya) must consist of exactly two parts. Understanding why will sharpen your ability to distinguish true logical alternatives from merely apparent ones. We will walk through the proof that three-part disjunctions collapse into a two-part structure, and see why the relationship between the parts must be contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author claims that a genuine exclusive disjunction can only be composed of two parts, and those two parts must be a proposition and its negation (or logical equivalent).
2
Understand the basis: contradiction
The relationship between the two parts is 'taqabul al-tanaqud' — they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. This is what makes the disjunction exclusive.
If both could be false, the disjunction would be false; if both could be true, it would not be exclusive.
3
Follow the impossibility proof for three parts
Assume there are three parts (P, Q, R). Since P and Q are genuine parts, P implies not-Q. Now consider Q and R. Either not-Q is implied by R or not. If not, then Q and R are not exclusive. If yes, then P implies not-Q and not-Q implies R, so P implies R — again, no genuine disjunction between P and R.
Thus any pair lacking contradiction breaks the exclusivity requirement.
4
Apply the resolution: nested disjunction
Apparent three-part disjunctions, like 'The body is either moving towards the center, away from it, or around it,' are actually a categorical part ('towards the center') and a disjunctive part ('away from it or around it'). The inner disjunction covers the remaining possibilities, preserving the two-part structure.
Always look for a proposition and its denial, possibly expressed as a disjunction of denials.

Exclusive Disjunction (Mani'a al-Jam') and Its Opposition
Defines the exclusive disjunction and identifies its opposition as contrariety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an exclusive disjunction (mani'a al-jam') and distinguish it from a genuine exclusive disjunction (haqiqiyya) based on the opposition of its parts.

Core Idea
The exclusive disjunction prevents both parts being true together, but allows both to be false.

Disjunctions are common in Islamic legal and theological reasoning. Not all disjunctions behave the same way: some require exactly one true, while others only forbid both being true. This lesson clarifies the second type, al-mani'a al-jam', which prevents conjunction but permits both parts to be false. Mastering this distinction is essential for correctly analyzing conditional rulings and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the disjunction
Find the two propositions that are linked by 'or' (أو). They are the disjuncts.

Check if the second part is more specific than the negation of the first
The second part (أخص من نقيضها) is a subset of the negation of the first; it is not the simple negation. For example, 'not in the mosque' includes many possibilities; 'in the market' is one specific alternative.
3
Test whether both parts can be false together
If both can be false (e.g., Zayd is elsewhere), then the opposition is contrariety, not contradiction. This is the defining feature of mani'a al-jam'.
4
Contrast with the genuine exclusive disjunction (haqiqiyya)
In haqiqiyya, the parts are exactly a proposition and its negation, so both cannot be false. Mani'a al-jam' allows both false.

Apply the truth conditions
Check that both parts cannot be true together (imtina' al-ijtima') and that both can be false (jawaz al-irtifa'). For example, if Zayd is either in the house or in the mosque (Mani'at al-Jam'), he cannot be in both, but he could be elsewhere.

Understanding Manee' al-Khuluww and Its Multi-Part Structure
Classification of opposition in manee' al-khuluww and its multi-part composition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a manee' al-khuluww disjunction, distinguish it from exclusive disjunction, and explain its composition from multiple parts with an Islamic legal example.

Core Idea
Manee' al-khuluww requires at least one part true but allows many true; it can have many parts.

We now examine one type of disjunctive proposition: manee' al-khuluww (exclusive of emptiness). Unlike manee' al-jam', here both parts can be true simultaneously. The author also shows how such a disjunction can be composed of more than two parts, a key point for complex legal reasoning. This lesson unpacks these features with a practical fiqh example.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Opposition Type
The passage states the opposition between the two parts of manee' al-khuluww is one where both cannot be false (man' al-khuluww) but both can be true (jawaz al-ijtima'). This is contrary opposition in logical terms.
In contrast, manee' al-jam' forbids both true but allows both false.
2
Understand the Composition from a Proposition and Its Negation
Manee' al-khuluww is composed of a proposition and a more general version of its negation. This ensures the disjunction is exhaustive: at least one must be true. For example, 'This is either khamr or not khamr' covers all possibilities.
The negation 'not khamr' is more general because it includes all non-khamr drinks.
3
Extend to Many Parts
The author permits composing manee' al-khuluww from many parts, provided that no two parts can both be false (man' al-khuluww between every pair). This means if you have three or more options, at least one of every two must be true, ensuring overall at least one is true.
Example: 'Zakat is due on camels, goats, or gold' – you cannot have no zakat on all three simultaneously; but you can have zakat on one, two, or all three.
4
Test the Condition
If we stipulate man' al-khuluww between every two parts, then for any pair, it is impossible that both are false. Otherwise, the two specified parts would both be true, which is impossible in a proper disjunction.
This ensures consistency: if 'camels' and 'goats' were both false, then their negations would be true – but that would contradict the condition.

Imam al-Razi's Argument and Refutation on Three-Part Manee' al-Khuluww
Dialectical argument and refutation on three-part manee' al-khuluww

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze Imam al-Razi's objection to three-part manee' al-khuluww and evaluate the refutation using the concept of lazim a'am.

Core Idea
Three-part exhaustive disjunctions are possible; al-Razi's argument fails because necessary concomitants can be inseparably linked.

In this passage, we encounter a dialectical exchange typical of Islamic logic. Imam al-Razi proposes that a manee' al-khuluww (exhaustive disjunction) cannot have more than two parts. However, the author responds by weakening al-Razi's premise, showing that a counterexample exists. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to evaluate the conditions for disjunctions in logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of manee' al-khuluww
Manee' al-khuluww (exhaustive disjunction) requires that the parts cannot all be false. It is typically expressed with 'either...or' implying at least one is true.
Also called 'exclusive disjunction' in some contexts.
2
State Imam al-Razi's argument
Al-Razi argues that in a three-part manee' al-khuluww, each pair of parts would be necessary concomitants of the negation of the third. This would allow both parts of a pair to be true together or false together, violating the condition that they cannot all be false.
He relies on the notion that if A and B are each lazim a'am of the negation of C, then A and B can both be false.
3
Identify the refutation's key point
The author responds: 'That is weak' because we do not accept that the two necessary concomitants can always be false together. There are cases where the two concomitants cannot both be false simultaneously, as in the example of negations of species.
E.g., 'not human' and 'not horse': If both are false, the entity would be both human and horse, which is impossible.
4
Apply the counterexample
Consider a disjunction of three negations of species under a genus: 'This is either not human, or not horse, or not bird.' Since these three species are mutually exclusive under 'animal', at most one can be true (i.e., the object is one species), so the other two negations are true, and they cannot all be false. Thus, the disjunction is manee' al-khuluww.
This shows three-part manee' al-khuluww is possible, contrary to al-Razi's claim.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In this passage, the author of the Qustas introduces a crucial distinction for conditional propositions: their universality or particularity is not determined by how often the antecedent occurs, but by whether the relationship holds across all possible times and circumstances. This logical insight is essential for evaluating the scope of conditional statements used in Islamic theological and juristic reasoning.

If after checking you find that the consequent holds for every possible time and circumstance under which the antecedent is supposed, the conditional is universal. Otherwise, it is particular or absolute.

What Makes a Conditional Universal According to Ibn Sina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a universal affirmative conditional in Ibn Sina's logic and distinguish it from mere repetition.

Core Idea
Universal conditional requires the connection in every possible state, not frequent repetition.

This lesson unpacks Ibn Sina's criterion for when a conditional proposition (e.g., 'If A then B') is truly universal. Many mistakenly think that a conditional is universal if the antecedent occurs often. Ibn Sina corrects this: universality depends on the consequent following the antecedent in every conceivable situation, across all times and assumptions. This principle is foundational for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Truth Condition of the Partial Affirmative Conditional
Definition of partial affirmative conditional truth condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the truth condition of a partial affirmative conditional proposition and correctly classify a given proposition as true or false under that condition.

Core Idea
A partial affirmative conditional is true if the implication holds for at least one case.

In this lesson, we move from universal conditionals to partial conditionals. The partial affirmative conditional does not require the antecedent and consequent to be connected in every instance; it suffices that they are connected in some cases. This distinction is crucial for understanding many logical arguments in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Locate the phrase 'partial affirmative conditional' (al-mujiba al-juz'iyya). Recognize that it is a conditional proposition that affirms a connection in some cases, not all.
The term 'partial' indicates that the quantifier is 'some' (some cases of the antecedent).
2
Understand the truth condition
The passage states that the partial affirmative is true 'if the implication or opposition holds for some of it' (إذا كان اللزوم و العناد على بعض منها). This means there exists at least one instance where the antecedent and consequent are related by implication (luzum) or opposition ('inad).
The condition is existential: it is enough that one case satisfies the relation.
3
Analyze the example
The example given is 'Sometimes if a thing is an animal, it is a human' (قد يكون إذا كان الشيء حيواناً فهو إنسان). Determine whether there is a case where an animal is a human. Think of humans: they are animals, and they are human. So the proposition is true.
The example uses 'animal' and 'human' to illustrate that the implication holds in some cases (e.g., when the thing is a human).
4
Contrast with the universal
Compare with the universal affirmative conditional 'Always if a thing is an animal, it is a human', which is false because not all animals are human. The partial conditional is weaker and thus easier to satisfy.
This contrast helps solidify the difference between universal and partial conditionals.

Core Idea
A universal affirmative conditional requires necessary entailment in all possible states; without it, absurdities follow.

This lesson examines the author's rigorous defense of the universal affirmative conditional. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to evaluate conditional propositions in Islamic logic.

Understanding the Accidental Conditional
Defines the accidental conditional and its truth condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an accidental conditional and evaluate its truth condition based on the perpetual actual truth of the consequent.

Core Idea
An accidental conditional is true only if the consequent is perpetually true in actual reality.

This lesson tackles a subtle point about universal conditionals: when the connection between antecedent and consequent is not necessary but accidental, what makes the conditional true? The classical logicians discovered that the truth of the consequent must be not just true but perpetually true 'according to actual reality' (bi-ḥasab al-ḥaqīqa). Without this condition, the conditional could be falsified by times when the subject does not exist. Let's explore the reasoning behind this condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conditional
The passage defines 'al-kulliyya al-ittifāqiyya' (accidental universal conditional). Note that it is a conditional proposition where the link between antecedent and consequent is accidental, not necessary. Example: 'Whenever man is rational, the donkey is braying' – there is no necessary connection.
Contrast with 'ḍarūriyya' (necessary conditional) where the connection is essential.
2
State the truth condition given by the author
The author asserts that for a universal accidental conditional, the truth requires that the consequent be true 'according to actual reality' and that this truth be perpetual (dā'im al-ṣidq). For a particular accidental conditional, both sides must be true in actual reality.
'According to actual reality' means true in the external world, not just assumed or hypothetical.
3
Understand the objection from Ibn Sina
Ibn Sina raises an objection: consider 'Whenever every man is an animal, every donkey is braying'. If there is a time when no donkeys exist, one might think the conditional is false because at that time there is no braying donkey. Ibn Sina argues this is a false supposition.
The objection hinges on whether 'every donkey is braying' asserts existence at that time.
4
Grasp Ibn Sina's resolution
Ibn Sina explains that 'every donkey is braying' does not mean 'every donkey exists and is braying now'. It is a universal statement true as long as every donkey that does exist is braying, even if sometimes no donkeys exist. Thus the conditional remains true.
This shows that the truth of the consequent must be perpetual, not dependent on current existence.
5
Apply the condition to new examples
Test other accidental conditionals: e.g., 'Whenever a human is created, the sky is a creation'. Is the consequent 'the sky is a creation' perpetually true in actual reality? Yes, because the sky is always a creation. So the conditional is true.
Use Islamic examples: e.g., 'Whenever the Prophet (PBUH) was sent, the moon split' – the consequent must be perpetually true in actual reality.

Core Idea
Negative conditionals negate a relation across all or some situations.

The Correct Negation of Conditionals

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Negating a conditional is negating the relation, not asserting a conjunction.

Quantifiers of Conditional Propositions in Islamic Logic
Author enumerates quantifier words for muttasila and munfasila conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to correctly identify the quantifier (sawr) of any given conditional proposition in Arabic, distinguishing between universal and particular, affirmative and negative, and between quantified and indiscriminate conditionals.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions have their own quantifier words; knowing them unlocks precise logical analysis of conditional statements.

Conditional propositions (al-shartiyyat) are a key type of proposition in Islamic logic, used heavily in legal and theological reasoning. Like categorical propositions, they require quantifiers to specify the scope of the relationship between antecedent and consequent. This lesson introduces the specific Arabic quantifiers used for conditionals, both connected (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila). Memorizing these quantifiers is essential for correctly interpreting and evaluating conditional arguments, especially when reading classical texts in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two types of conditionals
Remember that conditional propositions are divided into muttasila (connected, e.g., 'if A then B') and munfasila (disjunctive, e.g., 'either A or B'). Each type uses specific quantifier words.
2
Identify universal affirmative quantifiers
For muttasila, the universal affirmative quantifiers are 'kullama' (كلما), 'da'iman' (دائماً), 'hama' (حما), and 'mata' (متى). For munfasila, only 'da'iman' is used. These mean 'whenever' or 'always'.
The word 'hama' is likely a variant of 'haythuma' (wherever).
3
Identify universal negative quantifier
The universal negative quantifier 'laysa al-battata' (ليس البتة) is used for both muttasila and munfasila, meaning 'never at all'.
4
Identify particular quantifiers
Particular affirmative is 'qad yakunu' (قد يكون) and particular negative is 'qad la yakunu' (قد لا يكون). Also, negation can be combined with universal affirmative quantifiers to form 'laysa kullama' (ليس كلما) and 'laysa da'iman' (ليس دائماً).
5
Distinguish indiscriminate (ihmal) conditionals
Words like 'in' (إن), 'idha' (إذا), 'law' (لو) for muttasila, and 'imma' (إما) for munfasila, when used alone without a quantifier, indicate an indiscriminate conditional (muhmala). If they are accompanied by a specific time or place, the proposition becomes particular (shakhsiyya).
Indiscriminate means the quantity is unspecified; it could be universal or particular depending on context.

Author lists modality types for conditionals and states that modality belongs to the consequent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the modality type in a conditional proposition and explain that the modality qualifies the consequent, not the conditional relation.

Core Idea
The modality of a conditional is actually the modality of its consequent.

Just as categorical propositions carry modalities like necessity or possibility, conditional propositions also have modalities. The author lists five types and makes a critical point: the modality belongs to the consequent (tali) of the conditional, not to the linking relation. This insight prevents logical errors when analyzing conditional arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognise that conditionals have modalities
Read the opening sentence: modality and absoluteness may be considered in conditionals as in categoricals. Understand that conditionals, like 'If A then B', can be qualified by necessity, temporality, etc.
This mirrors the modal classification in categorical propositions.

The Necessity of the Extra Condition in Particular Implications - First Proof
First proof that the extra condition must be necessary for the antecedent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the first proof that the extra condition must be necessary for the antecedent in a particular implication and test its validity using examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
If the extra condition is not necessary, the implication collapses.

In this passage, the author presents a proof that the extra condition (أمر زائد) added to the antecedent in a particular conditional must itself be necessary for that antecedent. The proof relies on a defining property of implication: when the consequent is absent, the antecedent must also be absent. If the extra condition were not necessary, it could vanish when the consequent is absent, leaving the antecedent intact and violating the implication. Three illustrative examples from the text show this failure in concrete terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target condition
The passage argues that the extra condition (الأمر الزائد) must be necessary (ضروري) for the antecedent (المقدم) in the state of the implication. First, locate the statement of this condition in the Arabic: 'شرط ضرورة الأمر الزائد للمقدم في تحقق الملازمة الجزئية'.
The extra condition is an additional element that must be attached to the antecedent for the implication to hold.
2
Extract the first proof's logical structure
The proof proceeds by dichotomy: either the extra condition is necessary in that state or it is not. If it is not necessary, then the particular implication does not hold. Why? Because one of the essential properties of any implication is that when the consequent (تالي) is absent, the antecedent must be absent. But if the extra condition is not necessary, it could be absent even when the consequent is absent, leaving the antecedent present — thus the implication fails.
This is a proof by contradiction: assume the extra condition is not necessary, derive a violation of a necessary property of implication, therefore it must be necessary.
3
Analyze the three examples
The author gives three cases where the antecedent is taken with something extra that is not necessary, and no implication results: 1) antecedent with the 'correlate of the consequent' (ملزوم التالي) — stone with human: stone does not imply animal; 2) antecedent with the consequent itself — Zayd with Amr: Zayd's being with Amr does not imply Zayd's implying Amr; 3) antecedent with something that together implies the consequent — three added to seven does not imply ten as a logical implication. Each demonstrates that without necessity, the implication fails.
These examples are heuristic: they show that even if the extra condition happens to be present, the implication does not follow unless that condition is necessary.
4
Formulate the conclusion
Since the assumption of non-necessity leads to the failure of the implication's defining property, the extra condition must be necessary for the antecedent. Write out the conditional proof: If (extra condition not necessary) → (implication fails). Therefore, to have a valid particular implication, the extra condition must be necessary.
This conclusion is a requirement for correct conditional propositions in Islamic logic.

Essential vs. Accidental Necessity in Conditional Syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a bare conditional (mujarrada) and a non-bare conditional (ghayr mujarrada), and determine whether the necessity of an extra matter is essential or accidental to evaluate the validity of the conditional argument.

Core Idea
Essential necessity makes the conditional absolute; accidental necessity leaves it bare and weakens the implication.

This passage refines a key concept in conditional logic: the nature of necessity. The author presents a third argument that stipulating necessity solves previous problems. However, an objection forces a closer look at the source of that necessity. By understanding the distinction between essential and accidental necessity, you will see how a conditional can shift from bare (allowing exceptions) to non-bare (absolute). This is not just a logical nuance — it directly affects the strength of arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem being solved
The author claims that stipulating the extra matter is necessary for the antecedent repels the 'corruptions' (فسادات) previously mentioned. These corruptions arise when the implication is only partial or when contraposition fails. Recognise that the problem is about ensuring the conditional holds universally.
Corruptions here refer to failures in the implication, such as the antecedent being true while the consequent is false.
2

The passage uses a conditional: if antecedent A, then consequent C, with an extra matter M said to be necessary for A. The author argues that if C is negated, either A is negated or M is negated, and in both cases A is negated — this is contraposition. The question is whether the implication of M from A is necessary.
The author assumes a partial implication (ملازمة جزئية) that becomes full when necessity is added.
3

The objector asks: what kind of necessity does M have relative to A? The answer distinguishes: (1) If M is necessary due to A's essence (ذات المقدم), then M always accompanies A, making the conditional absolute (غير مجردة) — the bare conditional becomes non-bare. (2) If M is necessary due to another cause, then that cause must itself be necessary for A, leading to an infinite regress; otherwise, M can separate from A and the corruption returns.
The 'bare conditional' (المجردة) is one where the antecedent and consequent are linked only conditionally, not absolutely.
4

Only essential necessity gives a non-bare conditional, which guarantees the implication. Accidental necessity leaves the conditional bare, failing to solve the original problem. Thus, the third argument succeeds only if the necessity is essential.
This distinction is critical in evaluating proofs in kalam and usul al-fiqh that rely on causal necessity.

Multiplicity of Parts in Disjunctive Propositions
Author states rules for when multiplicity of parts is implied in disjunctive propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a disjunctive proposition implies distinct parts based on its type (exclusive or inclusive) and its quality (affirmative or negative).

Core Idea
Logical behavior of disjunctive parts depends on type and affirmation.

This passage from the Qustas lays out the rules for when a disjunctive proposition forces its parts to be truly distinct. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly analyzing arguments that use 'or' in Islamic legal and theological reasoning. The key is to recognize the different logical properties of exclusive (man'at al-khuluww) and inclusive (man'at al-jam') disjunctions, and how these properties change when the proposition is negative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of disjunction
First, determine whether the disjunction is exclusive (man'at al-khuluww) or inclusive (man'at al-jam'). Exclusive means the parts cannot all be false; inclusive means they cannot all be true.
In many Islamic texts, the terms are given directly. Look for 'مانعة الخلق' or 'مانعة الجمع'.
2
Determine the quality (affirmative or negative)
Check whether the proposition is affirmative (the disjuncts are stated as true) or negative (disjuncts are negated). The rules differ: for affirmative, multiplicity is necessitated in exclusive; for negative, it's necessitated in inclusive.
The passage explicitly treats both cases.
3
Apply the entailment rule for affirmative propositions
For affirmative exclusive: the negation of each part entails the affirmation of the other, and affirming the whole entails each part. Therefore, multiple parts must be distinct. For affirmative inclusive: one part's negation entails the affirmation of the other, but negation of the other does not negate all parts, so multiplicity is not imposed.
This is the core logical property.
4
Apply the entailment rule for negative propositions
For negative exclusive: multiplicity is not necessitated (opposite of affirmative). For negative inclusive: multiplicity is necessitated (opposite of affirmative).
The rule flips for negative propositions.
5
Combine for the true disjunction (haqiqiyya)
The true disjunction (al-ḥaqīqiyya) combines both restrictions: it cannot have all parts true nor all false. Its ruling is composed from the rulings of both exclusive and inclusive types.
Treat haqiqiyya as a composite case.

Commentary
This passage is a response to a critique regarding the definition of 'mulazama' (entailment) in conditional propositions. The author distinguishes two possible interpretations. So the phrase 'if there were no entailment' means that it would be possible for the antecedent to be true without the consequent, which is exactly the contradictory. Therefore, if it is impossible for the antecedent to be true with the contradictory of the consequent, then entailment holds.

Directed vs Absolute General Conditionals
Basic division of conditionals by explicit modality markers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a directed conditional (muwajjaha) and an absolute general conditional (mutlaqa 'amma) based on the presence of explicit modal terms.

Core Idea
A conditional is 'directed' if it states 'luzum', 'inad', or 'ittifaq'; otherwise it is 'absolute general'.

In this lesson, we explore the first major division of conditional propositions: those that explicitly carry a modal indicator (muwajjaha) and those that do not (mutlaqa 'amma). Understanding this distinction is foundational to analyzing the strength and type of connection in conditionals used in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

The Six Modal Types by Dhat and Wasf
Detailed modal classification by dhat and wasf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six modal types of conditional propositions based on the distinction between essence (dhat) and description (wasf) of the antecedent.

Core Idea
The antecedent can be considered in its essence or under a description, leading to six modal types of conditional propositions.

This lesson delves into the sophisticated classification of conditional propositions that emerges when we consider not just the mode (luzum, etc.) but also how the antecedent is taken: in its essence or under a description. This yields a rich taxonomy that was developed by al-Shahrazuri and others.

Guided Path
1
Understand dhat and wasf
Dhat al-muqaddam: the antecedent considered in itself, e.g., 'as long as the essence of A exists'. Wasf al-muqaddam: the antecedent considered under a description, e.g., 'as long as A is characterized by a certain description always'.
The passage gives examples: 'كلما كان آب ج د مادام ذات آب موجودة' and 'كلما كان آب مخ د مادام ذات آب متصفة بوصف آب دائماً'.
2
Classify by necessity under dhat
If the relation is necessary when considered by dhat, it is 'الضرورة الذاتية'. If not necessary, it is 'الدائمة المطلقة'.
3
Classify by necessity under wasf
If necessary by wasf, it is 'المشروطة العامة'. If restricted by non-perpetuity in dhat, it is 'المشروطة الخاصة'. If not necessary by wasf, it is 'العرفية العامة'. If restricted by non-perpetuity in dhat, it is 'العرفية الخاصة'.
The passage lists these sequentially.
4
Apply to an example
Consider: 'إذا كان الإنسان ناطقاً فهو إنسان' (If a human is rational, then he is human). If taken by essence: the relation is necessary (daruriyya dhatiyya). If taken by description: it is also necessary but could be mashruta if restricted.
Practical examples from fiqh: 'إذا كان الخمر مسكراً فهو حرام' (If wine is intoxicating, it is forbidden). By description it is necessary.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed classification of conditional propositions (shartiyyāt) based on a distinction between considering the antecedent in its essence (dhāt) or under a description (waṣf). It begins by quoting al-Imam (Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) and the later scholars, who divide conditionals into conjunctive (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila), then into necessary (luzumiyya), accidental (ittifāqiyya), and incompatible (‘inādiyya). They note that the antecedent may be taken in its essence (e.g., 'Whenever A is B, as long as the essence of A exists') or under a description (e.g., 'Whenever A is B, as long as the essence of A is always characterized by the description of A'). Non-necessity and temporal specifications follow categorical modal propositions. The text then gives a finer classification from al-Shahrazuri: conditional propositions that explicitly mention 'luzum', 'inad', or 'ittifaq' are called 'directed' (muwajjaha); those without these terms are 'absolute general' (mutlaqa ‘āmma). Each of the three may be considered by dhāt or waṣf. When by dhāt: if necessary, 'essential necessary' (ḍarūriyya dhātiyya); otherwise 'absolute perpetual' (dā’ima muṭlaqa). When by waṣf: if necessary, 'general conditioned' (mashrūṭa ‘āmma); if restricted by non-perpetuity in dhāt, 'special conditioned' (mashrūṭa khāṣṣa); if not necessary, 'general conventional' (‘urfiyya ‘āmma); if restricted by non-perpetuity in dhāt, 'special conventional' (‘urfiyya khāṣṣa). The commentary then notes a historical point: al-Shahrazuri’s work dates to 678-680 AH, after al-Tusi and before al-Samarqandi’s 'Qustas'. It observes that al-Samarqandi does not use 'waṣf al-muqaddam', suggesting he only means the literal sense of 'dhāt' as the antecedent itself, not as a technical opposite. This is reinforced by al-Tusi’s response that such considerations are possible but few benefits, so he did not occupy himself with them.

Immediate Inference from Exclusive Disjunctions
Author states the implication rules for real conditionals and for exclusive disjunctions of the same genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive conditional propositions from exclusive disjunctions of the same genus using the rules of negation and equivalence.

Core Idea
An exclusive disjunction 'either A or B' implies 'if not-A then B' and 'if not-B then A'.

This lesson unpacks a concise rule from classical Islamic logic: from an exclusive disjunction of the same genus, we can immediately infer conditionals. The author states that a true exclusive disjunction 'either A or B' (where one and only one holds) entails that the negation of one disjunct implies the other. This rule is frequently used in usul al-fiqh and kalam to convert disjunctive options into testable conditionals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction type
Confirm the disjunction is exclusive (mani'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw) and that both disjuncts belong to the same genus (muttahidat al-jins).
The author specifies 'منفصلات المتحدة الجنس' – disjunctions where the two parts share a category (e.g., two mutually exclusive rulings).
2
Apply the rule 'from the two negations'
Take the negation of the first disjunct as the antecedent, and the second disjunct as the consequent, forming 'If not-A then B'. Similarly, 'If not-B then A'.
This is the core immediate inference: the disjunction is equivalent to a pair of conditionals.
3
Check the converse
The rule also states 'and conversely' – meaning if you have a conditional like 'If not-A then B', you can infer the exclusive disjunction 'Either A or B' (provided exclusivity).
This bidirectional equivalence is valid only for exclusive disjunctions.
4
Use equivalents to negations
If you have a proposition equivalent to 'not-A', you can substitute it in the antecedent. Similarly for the consequent. The rule allows any statement that is equivalent to the negation of a disjunct to trigger the implication.
For example, if 'C' is equivalent to 'not-A', then 'If C then B' also holds.

Entailment Rules of Exclusive Disjunction (مانعة الجمع)
Author states entailment rules for exclusive disjunction propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the entailment relations of an affirmative exclusive-disjunction proposition (مانعة الجمع) from its parts' necessitants and from the contradictories of its parts, and explain why the converse does not hold.

Core Idea
Affirmative exclusive disjunction entails both its parts' necessitants and a negative exclusive disjunction of their contradictories.

Exclusive disjunction (مانعة الجمع) is a key type of compound proposition in Islamic logic. This passage by the author lays out the precise entailment (تلازم) relations that hold for an affirmative exclusive-disjunction. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly drawing inferences from disjunctive statements, which appear frequently in juristic and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
The passage concerns the affirmative exclusive-disjunction (موجبة مانعة الجمع). Note that it is 'affirmative' (موجبة) and 'exclusive' (مانعة الجمع), meaning the two parts cannot both be true.
Affirmative exclusive disjunction is true when exactly one part is true; it is false when both are true or both are false.
2
Parse the first entailment: from necessitants of both parts
The passage says it entails 'its like from the necessary conditions of its two parts' (تستلزم مثلها من ملزومي طرفيها). That means: if the disjunction is true, then for each part, its own necessitant (ملزوم) is also true in a similar disjunction? Actually careful: It entails a proposition of the same type (مثلها) composed of the necessitants (ملزوم) of the original parts. So if A or B exclusively, then (necessitant of A) or (necessitant of B) exclusively.
A 'necessitant' (ملزوم) is the antecedent from which a necessary condition follows. We need to know the relation between each part and its necessitant.
3
Parse the second entailment: from one part's necessitant and the other part itself
It also entails a disjunction formed from the necessitant of one part and the other part itself (العين). And conversely if the entailment between the part and its necessitant is reciprocal. This means the entailment works both ways if the necessitant and the part are mutually entailing.
The phrase 'وبالعكس إن تعاكس اللزوم' indicates the converse holds only when the entailment is reciprocal.
4
Understand the rule about impossibility of conjunction
The passage states: 'وامتناع الجمع بين اللازمين يوجب امتناع الاجتماع بين الملزومين'. This is a general principle: If two necessary conditions cannot be conjoined (both true), then their necessitants cannot coexist (both true).
This principle can be applied to validate the entailments above.
5
Identify the entailment to negative exclusive disjunction
The passage says: 'وتستلزم سالبة مانعة الجمع من نقيضي طرفيها'. So an affirmative exclusive disjunction entails a negative exclusive disjunction formed from the contradictories (نقيض) of its two parts. That is: if (A exclusive-or B) is true, then (not-A exclusive-or not-B) is also true? Actually negative exclusive disjunction means 'not both' but also 'not neither'? Wait: 'سالبة مانعة الجمع' is a negative exclusive disjunction, which is true when the two parts are not both true? Need to check: In Islamic logic, a negative exclusive disjunction (سالبة مانعة الجمع) asserts that the two parts are not together, i.e., it is false that both are true. But that is equivalent to 'not (A and B)'. However the passage says it entails that 'من نقيضي طرفيها' (from the contradictories of its two parts). So it entails a proposition that prevents the conjunction of the contradictories? Actually careful: The passage explains that this entailment holds because it is possible for both parts to be false (i.e., the original exclusive disjunction can be false when both are false), but the converse does not hold because it is possible for two things to coexist while their contradictories also coexist (e.g., both A and B can be false simultaneously, so their contradictories are true, etc.).
The key is that the entailment is one-way: affirmative exclusive disjunction entails the negative one, but not vice versa.

Entailment Between Man'at al-Jam' and Man'at al-Khuluww via Contradictories
Author explains the cross-entailment between exclusive conjunction and exclusive disjunction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive a man'at al-khuluww from a man'at al-jam' (and vice versa) using the contradictories of the two parts.

Core Idea
Preventing conjunction implies preventing emptiness of contradictories.

This passage from the Quṣṭās reveals a fundamental logical equivalence: the conditional proposition that prevents two things from being true together (man'at al-jam') and the one that prevents them from being false together (man'at al-khuluww) entail each other when you replace the parts with their contradictories. This lesson unpacks that transformation, which is essential for reasoning in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditionals
Man'at al-jam' means the two parts cannot both be true; man'at al-khuluww means they cannot both be false. Distinguish them clearly.
In modern terms: man'at al-jam' is exclusive OR (XOR), man'at al-khuluww is inclusive OR (OR).
2
Understand the contradictory operation
The contradictory (naqid) of a proposition has the opposite truth value. For part A, its contradictory is not-A.
3
Apply the entailment rule from man'at al-jam' to man'at al-khuluww
If A and B cannot both be true, then not-A and not-B cannot both be false. Proof: if not-A and not-B were both false, then A and B would both be true, contradicting the original.
The text states: 'منع الجمع بين الشيئين يقتضي منع الخلو بين نقيضيها'.
4
Apply the converse entailment
Similarly, if A and B cannot both be false (man'at al-khuluww), then not-A and not-B cannot both be true (man'at al-jam'). This is also stated as mutual entailment.
The text says: 'وبالعكس' indicating the converse holds.

تلازم المانعات الخلو
وكل موجبة مانعة الخلو:
لأن تستلزم مثلها: من لازمي طرفيها، ومن لازم أحد طرفيها وعين الطرف الآخر، وبالعكس إن تعاكس اللزوم. امتناع الخلو بين الملزومين يوجبه بين اللازمين، وامتناع الخلو بين الشيء وملزوم الغير يوجبه بينه وبين الغير.
وتستلزم سالبة مانعة الخلو: من نقيضي طرفيها لإمكان اجتماع جزئي مانعة الجمع؛ دون العكس.
كل موجبة حقيقية تستلزم:
المبحث الثالث: في تلازم مختلفات الجنس
موجبة مانعة الجمع مثلها في الكم: من ملزومي الجزئين، ومن أحد الجزئين وملزوم الآخر.
وموجبة مانعة الخلو كذلك: من لازمي الجزئين، ومن أحد الجزئين ولازم الآخر.
وكل من مانعة الجمع ومانعة الخلو تستلزم الأخرى من نقيضي جزئيه؛ لأن منع الجمع بين الشيئين يقتضي منع الخلو بين نقيضيها وبالعكس.
وإن توافقتا في الجزئين وتخالفتا في الكيف والكم، وتكون الجزئية سالبة لزمت السالبة الموجبة إلا انقلبت الموجبة حقيقية من غير عكس، ولا تستلزم السالبة الموجبة لإمكان اجتماع الشيئين مع إمكان ارتفاعها.
First chapter: On propositions. Chapter eleven: On entailment and contrariety of conditionals. The possibility of both things together with the possibility of both their contradictories together. Entailment of exclusive disjunctions. Every positive exclusive disjunction entails its like: from the necessary consequences of its two sides, and from the necessary consequence of one of its sides and the same of the other side, and conversely if the entailment is reversed. The impossibility of emptiness between the two implied things necessitates it between the two necessary consequences, and the impossibility of emptiness between a thing and the implied of another necessitates it between it and that other. And it entails a negative exclusive disjunction: from the contradictories of its two sides due to the possibility of conjunction of a part of the exclusive conjunction, but not conversely. Every true positive entails: Third investigation: On entailment of different genera. A positive exclusive conjunction is like it in quantity: from the implied of the two parts, and from one part and the implied of the other. And a positive exclusive disjunction likewise: from the necessary consequences of the two parts, and from one part and the necessary consequence of the other. And each of the exclusive conjunction and exclusive disjunction entails the other from the contradictories of its two parts; because preventing conjunction between two things necessitates preventing emptiness between their contradictories, and conversely. And if they agree in the two parts and differ in quality and quantity, and the particular is negative, then the negative entails the positive, otherwise the positive becomes true with no conversion, and the negative does not entail the positive because of the possibility of both things existing together with the possibility of their removal.

Commentary
This passage presents the rules of entailment (talazum) between two types of conditional propositions: man'at al-jam' (exclusive conjunction, i.e., cannot both be true) and man'at al-khuluww (exclusive disjunction, i.e., cannot both be false). The text begins by defining the possibility of both things being together and the possibility of both their contradictories being together, setting the stage for the entailment relations. It then states that every positive man'at al-khuluww entails its like in three ways: (a) from the necessary consequences (lazim) of both sides, (b) from the necessary consequence of one side and the other side itself, and (c) conversely if the entailment is reversed (i.e., if the sides are reversed). It also states that the impossibility of emptiness between the two implied things (malzum) necessitates the impossibility of emptiness between their necessary consequences, and similarly for a thing and the implied of another. Then it says that a positive man'at al-khuluww entails a negative man'at al-khuluww from the contradictories of its two sides, because it is possible for a part of man'at al-jam' to be together, but not conversely. The second part (mabahith al-thalith) deals with entailment between genera. It states that a positive man'at al-jam' of a certain quantity entails its like from the implied of the two parts or from one part and the implied of the other. Similarly, a positive man'at al-khuluww entails its like from the necessary consequences of the two parts or from one part and the necessary consequence of the other. Then it explains that man'at al-jam' and man'at al-khuluww entail each other from the contradictories of their two parts, because preventing conjunction requires preventing emptiness of the contradictories and vice versa. Finally, it discusses when the two conditionals agree in the two parts but differ in quality and quantity. If the particular is negative, the negative entails the positive; otherwise, the positive becomes true (i.e., universal) with no conversion, and the negative does not entail the positive because the two things can both exist while their contradictories can both be absent.

Checkpoint
According to the passage, if a man'at al-jam' (exclusive conjunction) is true for two propositions, what does it entail concerning a man'at al-khuluww of their contradictories?
A) It entails that the man'at al-khuluww is true.
B) It entails that the man'at al-khuluww is false.
C) It entails nothing about the man'at al-khuluww.
D) It entails that the man'at al-khuluww is only possibly true.

Key Takeaway
Man'at al-jam' (cannot both be true) and man'at al-khuluww (cannot both be false) entail each other when applied to the contradictories of the original parts.
