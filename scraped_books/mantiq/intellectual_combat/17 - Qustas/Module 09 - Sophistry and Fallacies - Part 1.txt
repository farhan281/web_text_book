The Fallacy of Part for Whole: Themistius' Example
Author gives Themistius' fallacy example of substituting part for whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of taking a part for the whole in an argument and distinguish it from valid reasoning.

Core Idea
The fallacy of part for whole replaces a complete premise with a fragment, creating a deceptive argument.

In logical debates, a sophist may substitute a part of a premise for the entire premise, leading to a false conclusion. This fallacy, known as 'taking the part for the whole' (dakhil al-ba'd makan al-kull), is illustrated by Themistius with an example about the shape of the sphere. Understanding this fallacy helps you spot incomplete or distorted reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
Find the main assertion: the sphere is not lentil-shaped (al-falak laysa 'adasiyyan).
This is the conclusion the arguer wants to prove.
2
Examine the Reason Given
The arguer says: 'if it moved on the longest diameter, a vacuum would necessarily occur.' This is a conditional statement (law... la-lazima...).
This conditional is only one part of a potential argument.
3
Spot the Substitution
The arguer treats this conditional premise as if it alone proves the conclusion. But the full argument would require additional premises about what being lentil-shaped entails.
The conditional is a part (ba'd) of the needed reasoning; using it alone is the fallacy.
4
Contrast with Valid Reasoning
A valid argument would need a complete premise linking lentil-shape to motion on the longest diameter and vacuum, not just the conditional.
Think of a valid syllogism: major premise, minor premise, conclusion. Here only a fragment is used.

The Part-for-Whole Fallacy in Conditional Arguments
Author exposes a fallacy in Tamestus' argument about sphere movement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the part-for-whole fallacy in a conditional argument and distinguish it from a valid inference.

Core Idea
Treating a partial condition as the whole condition makes an argument invalid.

This lesson examines a specific fallacy where a part of a premise is substituted for the whole premise, leading to a false conclusion. The author critiques Tamestus' argument about the sphere, showing how a partial condition (moving on the longest diameter) is taken as the entire condition for sphericity. Understanding this fallacy helps you evaluate arguments in Islamic sciences where conditions are often compound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional statement
Locate the conditional in the passage: 'if it moved on the longest diameter, a void would occur'. Note that this is the only condition used to conclude 'the sphere is not spherical'.
2
Spot the part treated as whole
The fallacy is that 'moving on the longest diameter' is only one possible movement of a sphere, not the defining feature of sphericity. The arguer treats this partial condition as if it alone determines sphericity.
In logic, this is akin to the fallacy of accident.
3
Explain why the conclusion does not follow
Even if moving on the longest diameter causes a void, it does not prove the sphere is not spherical, because sphericity involves many other conditions. The argument commits the part-for-whole fallacy by ignoring the rest.
4
Contrast with a valid conditional inference
A valid conditional requires that the antecedent be sufficient for the consequent. Here, the antecedent is only a part of the sufficient condition, so the inference is invalid.
For example, in fiqh: 'If an act causes harm, it is prohibited' is valid only if harm is the complete cause; if it's just one factor, the inference may be partial.

The Liar Paradox: A Self-Referential Fallacy
Author describes the liar paradox and the failure of scholars to solve it convincingly

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the liar paradox as a self-referential fallacy and explain why it perplexed scholars.

Core Idea
The liar paradox traps the mind in a self-contradictory loop because it refers to itself.

Logic is full of puzzles, but few are as stubborn as the liar paradox. The author opens with a classic version: 'All my speech at this hour is false.' If true, it's false; if false, it's true. This self-contradiction baffled scholars for centuries, and despite many attempts, no fully convincing solution emerged. Let's dissect this paradox and see why it resists simple resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement
Write down the exact statement from the passage: 'All my speech at this hour is false.' This is the shubha (sophism).
Note that it covers all speech in that hour, including itself.
2
Test the truth conditions
Assume the statement is true. Then what it says must be the case: all speech is false. But that includes this very statement, so it would be false. Contradiction.
Now assume it is false. Then not all speech is false, meaning some speech is true. But that could be this statement itself, making it true. Again contradiction.
3
Recognize self-reference
The paradox arises because the statement refers to itself. It is a 'self-referential' loop: the truth of the statement depends on itself.
This kind of self-reference is the key feature of the liar paradox.
4
Understand why it perplexed scholars
The passage says scholars were 'perplexed' and wrote many papers but none were convincing. The paradox seems to break the basic law of non-contradiction (a proposition cannot be both true and false).
Many proposed solutions (e.g., banning self-reference, hierarchical truth predicates) have been debated, but none gained universal acceptance.

Detecting Unsound Syllogisms: The Samarqandi Fallacy
Presentation of a fallacious syllogism using a false premise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in a categorical syllogism where a false universal premise leads to an unsound conclusion.

Core Idea
A valid-looking syllogism can be unsound if any premise is false.

This passage from al-Qustas presents a cunning fallacy attributed to Samarqandi. The syllogism appears to follow the correct categorical form, yet the conclusion is false. Understanding why it fails will sharpen your ability to detect sophistry in theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
Find the subject (S: 'one who says man is stone'), predicate (P: 'truthful'), and middle term (M: 'one who says it is a body').
The middle term connects the two premises.
2
Extract the premises
Premise 1: 'Everyone who says man is stone says it is a body.' This is true. Premise 2: 'Everyone who says it is a body is truthful.' This is false.
A false premise makes the argument unsound regardless of form.
3
Check the syllogistic form
The structure is: All S are M, All M are P, therefore All S are P. This is AAA-1 (Barbara), a valid form.
Formal validity does not guarantee a true conclusion.
4
Assess soundness
Since premise 2 is false, the argument is unsound. The conclusion 'Everyone who says man is stone is truthful' is false.
The fallacy is material, not formal.
5
Identify the fallacy type
The fallacy is using a false universal premise (the 'lazim' trick: assuming that a true consequence implies the truth of the antecedent).
In Arabic: مغالطة اللازم (fallacy of necessary consequence).

Commentary
This passage presents a fallacious syllogism. The first premise: 'Everyone who says man is stone says it is a body' (true, since stone is a body). The second premise: 'Everyone who says it is a body is truthful' (false, because not everyone who says something true is always truthful; furthermore, saying 'man is stone' is false, so the premise is problematic). The conclusion: 'Everyone who said man is stone is truthful' is false because the statement 'man is stone' is false, so the speaker is lying. The fallacy arises from the false universal premise that all speakers who say something true are truthful, and from ignoring that the first statement is actually false. This is an example of a logical trick known as 'truth-tellers about lazim' (consequence).

Fallacy of Taking Part for Whole in Conditional Syllogisms
Editor identifies and corrects a fallacy example about the celestial sphere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and correct the fallacy of taking part for whole in a conditional syllogism using an Islamic science example.

Core Idea
A conditional antecedent may have multiple parts; denying the consequent does not allow you to negate only part of it.

In the study of conditional syllogisms, a common error is to treat a compound antecedent as a simple one. The classical fallacy 'taking part for whole' (أخذ البعض مكان الكل) occurs when the antecedent includes multiple conditions, but the argument proceeds as if it were a single condition. This passage from the Qustas corrects an erroneous example about the celestial sphere, showing how to properly analyze such reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional claim
Locate the antecedent and consequent in the Arabic. The antecedent is 'the sphere is lenticular and moves along its longest diameter' (a conjunction). The consequent is 'vacuum would occur'.
The antecedent is compound, consisting of two parts joined by 'and'.
2
Recognize the fallacy
The argument says: if the sphere is lenticular, then vacuum would follow. But the actual antecedent has two parts. The conclusion 'the sphere is not lenticular' only denies one part, not the whole conjunction.
The arguer took a part (lenticular) as the whole antecedent.
3
Understand the correct form
In a valid conditional syllogism (modus tollens), if the antecedent is compound, denying the consequent only tells you the whole antecedent is false, not which part is false.
The correct conclusion is 'not (lenticular and movement)', not 'not lenticular'.
4
Apply to Islamic science
Imagine a fiqh example: 'If the commodity is gold and it is sold for less than its weight, then it is riba.' If we observe no riba, we cannot conclude the commodity is not gold; it might be gold but sold at a fair weight.
This shows the practical danger of the fallacy in Islamic legal reasoning.

The Fourteenth Fallacy: Man is Not an Animal
Author presents a sophistical syllogism and its refutation by identifying a false premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical error in a sophistical syllogism that adds an irrelevant condition to a premise, making it false.

Core Idea
A true premise with an irrelevant restriction becomes false.

This lesson examines the fourteenth fallacy from Qustas al-Afkar, where a seemingly valid syllogism fails because the minor premise is made false by an added condition. You will learn to spot such fallacies and avoid them in your own reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism components
Break down the argument into minor premise, major premise, and conclusion. Write them out in standard categorical form: minor: 'Man is rational insofar as he is rational'; major: 'Nothing that is rational insofar as it is rational is an animal'; conclusion: 'Man is not an animal'.
2
Check the truth of each premise without conditions
Remove the condition 'insofar as he is rational' from the minor premise. The unrestricted statement 'Man is rational' is true. Similarly, remove the condition from the major: 'Nothing rational is an animal' — this is false because man is rational and an animal.
The author focuses only on the minor premise, but the major is also false in reality.
3
Analyze the effect of the condition on the minor premise
With the condition, the minor premise becomes 'Man is rational insofar as he is rational', which implies that man is only rational in that restricted sense. This is false because man has other attributes, including animality. The condition is irrelevant to the predication of rationality.
The condition (qayd) does not belong in the predication (haml) and renders the premise false.
4
Determine why the fallacy is not formal but material
The syllogism's logical form is valid (first figure, EAE mood). The error is in the truth of the minor premise. The fallacy is material, not formal.
This demonstrates that sound arguments require both valid form and true premises.

The Sophism of 'Human is not an Animal' – Identifying Illicit Qualification in Categorical Syllogisms
Author presents a categorical syllogism fallacy and its resolution by preventing the minor premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a categorical syllogism fallacy caused by an irrelevant qualification in the minor premise and formulate the correct solution.

Core Idea
The error is not a false conclusion but an illicit added condition in the premise.

In this lesson, we examine the fourteenth fallacy from the Islamic logical tradition: a syllogism that seemingly proves 'Human is not an animal'. The trick lies in a subtle qualification added to the minor premise. You will learn to spot such irrelevant qualifications and understand why they render a premise false, even if the syllogism's form appears valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The sophist wants to prove 'Human is not an animal' (الإنسان ليس بحيوان). This is the conclusion they attempt to derive.
2
Extract the premises
The minor premise is 'Human is rational insofar as it is rational' (الإنسان ناطق من حيث هو ناطق). The major premise is 'Nothing that is rational insofar as it is rational is an animal' (لا شيء من الناطق من حيث هو الناطق بحيوان).
Notice the qualification 'insofar as it is rational' in both premises.
3
Analyze the qualification
The qualification 'insofar as it is rational' restricts the predicate 'rational' to a specific aspect. However, when we say 'Human is rational' without qualification, it is true. But the qualified version does not assert the same thing – it adds an extra condition that is irrelevant to the predication.
This qualification is not part of the essential meaning of the proposition.
4
Determine the solution
The author states that the minor premise is false because of the added condition. The correct minor premise should be simply 'Human is rational' without the irrelevant qualification. With that correction, the syllogism would not prove the desired conclusion because the major premise would also need adjustment.
The solution is to 'prevent the minor premise' (منع الصغرى) by showing it is false as presented.

In other words, the simple unqualified statement 'Human is rational' is true, but adding 'insofar as it is rational' makes the statement false as a premise because it introduces a restriction that does not belong in the syllogism. The fallacy tricks the listener into accepting a qualified statement as equivalent to an unqualified one, leading to a false conclusion. This example is from the classical tradition of logical sophisms, often used to train students in identifying illicit operations in categorical arguments.

Mastering this distinction protects you from dismissing valid arguments as fallacious.

Guided Path
1
Identify the suspected circle or regress

The passage explicitly mentions al-mutadāyifān as an example of a non-impossible circle.

For an infinite regress to be vicious, each step must be required by the same causal principle. If the first item is self-existent (like an accident's existence being inherent), the regress is only apparent.
The passage illustrates this with the existence of an accident: if its existence required another existence, an infinite regress would follow, but it is self-existent, so no regress.

Fallacy from the Form of the Word
The author describes the second cause of verbal fallacy in single words: the form of the word.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies arising from shared morphological patterns (e.g., active participle form) that cause ambiguity between agent and object.

Core Idea
The same word pattern can indicate different grammatical roles, leading to confusion.

The second type of verbal fallacy in single words concerns the form (صورة) of the word—its morphological pattern. When two words share the same pattern but have different semantic roles (like agent vs. object), one may mistakenly assume the same grammatical function.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared pattern
Look for words that have the same wazn (morphological pattern), such as فاعل (active participle) used for both agent and passive meanings.
Example: 'المختار' can mean 'the one who chooses' or 'the one who is chosen'.
2
Determine the intended role
Check the context to see whether the word is meant as agent or object (or other).
3
Recognize the fallacy
If the argument confuses the two roles, it commits the fallacy of form.
Example: 'القابل' (the receiver) on pattern of الفاعل may be misinterpreted as an action of the subject.

Commentary
This passage classifies the causes of fallacy in reasoning, focusing on the material aspect (i.e., content) rather than form. The author begins by stating that the fundamental cause of fallacy in proof is the inability to distinguish between a thing and what resembles it. Error is then divided into three categories based on whether it arises from matter, form, or both. The simple causes are limited to twelve, starting with the division into error from word (verbal) and error from meaning (conceptual). The passage then details the six verbal fallacies: three from single words and three from compounds. For single words, the error arises from: (1) the matter of the word, i.e., the word has multiple meanings (homonymy, metaphor, equivocation, etc.); (2) the form of the word, e.g., a shared morphological pattern causing ambiguity (like the active participle form used for both agent and object); (3) accidents of the word like declension or dotting that change meaning. Examples are given, including a line of poetry illustrating ambiguity due to diacritics. For compounds, the three types are: (4) ambiguity in the composition itself, where an expression can be parsed in multiple ways (e.g., 'Zaid's beating' could mean the subject or object). Several examples are provided, including ambiguous pronoun reference. (5) The compositor fallacies: thinking a composition exists when it does not, or vice versa. For instance, 'Five is even and odd' is true of five as a person but not as a number, leading to a false inference. Also confusing a composite (like matter and form constituting a body) with its parts. This is called 'analysis of the compound' (tafsīl al-murakkab). (6) Thinking a non-existent composition exists, e.g., combining attributes incorrectly ('Zaid is a poet and a physician, so he is a skillful poet') or confusing collective with distributive (man and horse are animals, but their aggregate is not an animal). This is called 'composition of the distinguished' (tarkīb al-mufaḍḍal). The author concludes that these six divisions are the verbal fallacies. Fallacy from Accidents of the Word
The author describes the third cause of verbal fallacy in single words: accidents of the word.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies caused by changes in declension, dotting, vowel marks, or other accidental features of a word.

Core Idea
A small change in script or vowel can completely alter meaning, leading to a fallacy.

The third type of verbal fallacy in single words comes from the accidents (عوارض) of a word—features like declension (إعراب), indeclinability (بناء), dotting (إعجام), lightening (تخفيف), and doubling (تشديد). A line of poetry is given to illustrate how different diacritical marks can change the meaning of a whole verse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the accidental feature
Look for words where the meaning changes based on i'rab (case endings), dots on letters (e.g., 'ع' vs 'غ'), or shadda vs sukun.
2
Determine the intended reading
Check the context to see which reading is correct. The fallacy occurs when the wrong reading is chosen.
3
Analyze the poetic example
The line 'طعن كفم الزق غداً والزق ملآن' can be read with different diacritics, leading to different meanings (e.g., 'كفم' could be 'كف الم' meaning 'mouth of the wineskin' or something else).
The shift in meaning due to a single dot or vowel is the essence of this fallacy.

Fallacy of Ambiguity in Composition
The author describes the fourth verbal fallacy, which is ambiguity in compound expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies where a compound expression can be parsed in multiple ways, leading to misinterpretation.

Core Idea
The same phrase can have multiple grammatical structures, causing ambiguity.

The first type of verbal fallacy in compounds concerns the composition itself. When a phrase can be analyzed syntactically in more than one way (e.g., different possible attachment of a modifier or pronoun), the meaning becomes ambiguous and a fallacy can occur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous construction
Look for phrases where the grammatical relation between words is not unique, such as 'ضرب زيد' (Zaid's beating / beating Zaid).
The verbal noun can be added to the subject or object.
2
Determine the possible parsings
List the different syntactic analyses that yield different meanings.
Example: 'ضرب زيد' could mean 'the beating that Zaid performed' or 'the beating that Zaid received'.
3
Check if the argument relies on one parsing ambiguously
If the reasoning shifts between parsings, it commits the fallacy.

Fallacy of Analysis of the Compound
The author describes the fifth verbal fallacy, concerning the analysis of compounds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies where one mistakes a composite whole for non-existent when it is actually present, or vice versa.

Core Idea
Treating a true composite as if it were not a composite (or vice versa) causes false inferences.

The fifth type of verbal fallacy occurs when one mistakenly thinks a composition exists when it does not, or when it does not exist when it does. The author gives examples: 'Five is even and odd' (true of a person named Khamsa, but not of the number 5) and 'Matter and form are a body' (the composite is a body, but one might think each part is a body).

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite claim
Look for statements that attribute a property to a whole that may be true only of the parts or vice versa.
2
Determine if the composition actually exists
Check whether the subject is a genuine composite (like a body from matter and form) or a mere aggregate.
3
Spot the fallacy
If the argument treats a non-composite as composite (e.g., 'five is even and odd' applying to both attributes together) or a composite as non-composite, it is a fallacy.
The author calls this 'تفصيل المركب' (analysis of the compound).

The author describes the sixth verbal fallacy, concerning the composition of separate attributes.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies where separate attributes are incorrectly combined into a single description, leading to a false compound.

Fallacy of Division (Tafsīl al-Murakkab)
Second type: thinking an existing composition is non-existent (tafsil al-murakkab).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Treating a unified compound as separate parts can wrongly attribute properties of the whole to its components.

The second type of verbal fallacy occurs when a genuine compound is incorrectly analysed as if it were a mere aggregate, leading to mistaken beliefs about its parts. This is called 'tafsil al-murakkab' (analysis of the compound). Understanding this helps avoid errors in interpreting complex theological or philosophical statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the compound expression

3

Fallacy of Axis Assumption in Celestial Motion
Author critiques Aristotle's argument on motion requiring void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy in Aristotle's argument about oviform/lentiform shapes requiring void by analyzing the unstated assumption about the axis of rotation.

Core Idea
A logical conclusion is fallacious if it depends on an unstated and unjustified condition.

This passage examines a subtle fallacy in natural philosophy concerning the motion of non-spherical celestial bodies. Avicenna shows that Aristotle's argument that oviform or lentiform shapes require void depends on an unstated assumption about which axis they rotate on. By clarifying the geometry, Avicenna refutes the necessity of void and demonstrates the importance of precise conditions in logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify Aristotle's Claim
What does Aristotle assert about oviform and lentiform shapes in circular motion? He claims that if the celestial sphere were oviform or lentiform and rotated not on the longer (for oviform) or shorter (for lentiform) diameter, then a void would be necessary. This is presented as a logical consequence.
2
Locate the Unstated Assumption
The argument implicitly assumes that the axis of rotation is the shorter diameter for the oviform and the longer diameter for the lentiform. However, these shapes are generated by rotating an ellipse about its longer (for oviform) or shorter (for lentiform) diameter. So the proper axes are opposite to what Aristotle assumes.
Note that the oviform is generated by rotation about the longer diameter, and the lentiform about the shorter.
3
Understand the Fallacy
Why is this a fallacy (ghalat)? The fallacy is one of false assumption. Aristotle's conclusion about void follows only if the wrong axis is chosen. If the correct axis is used, the shapes rotate like a sphere without requiring void. The argument is not universally true; it depends on a specific condition that is not justified.
4
Analyze Avicenna's Critique
How does Avicenna expose the fallacy and respond to Biruni's objection? Avicenna explains the geometry of generation, then offers proofs (natural and mathematical) that the celestial sphere cannot be oviform or lentiform. He also addresses Biruni's analogy with internal bodies, distinguishing between the presence of bodies beyond the sphere.
Avicenna's response shows that even if the shape could rotate without void, it does not prove the universe is non-spherical; he provides independent proofs for sphericity.

Author criticizes an argument for the existence of the indivisible part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in arguments about time and indivisible parts and recognize the resulting contradiction of contradictories.

Core Idea
Arguments for atoms often force impossible contradictions about existence and non-existence.

This passage presents a fallacy used to argue for the existence of the indivisible part (atom). The author shows that the argument leads to either the conjunction or absence of contradictories, which are logically impossible. Understanding this fallacy helps you detect sophistical arguments in kalām discussions about atomism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the argument's structure
The argument claims: if a thing becomes nonexistent, its nonexistence either occurs at the same moment as its existence or at another moment.
This sets up a dichotomy.
2
Analyze the first horn
If nonexistence occurs at the same moment as existence, then the thing would both exist and not exist simultaneously, which is a conjunction of contradictories (ijtimā‘ al-naqīḍayn) – impossible.
3
Analyze the second horn
If nonexistence occurs at a different moment, then either there is a time interval between the two moments or not. If there is an interval, then during that interval the thing neither exists nor does not exist – an absence of both contradictories (irtifā‘ al-naqīḍayn) – also impossible.
The argument does not consider the possibility of no time interval, but that would imply the moments are adjacent, which leads to similar issues.
4
Recognize the fallacy
The argument presents a false dilemma and misapplies the laws of non-contradiction and excluded middle. The reasoning is invalid because it assumes only these possibilities and treats them as leading to absurdities, but the absurdities arise from flawed assumptions about time and existence.
This is a classic example of a reductio ad absurdum used incorrectly.

Identifying the Fallacy of Taking Qualified for Absolute: The Atomism Argument
Author presents a proof for the indivisible part and refutes it as a fallacy of taking qualified for absolute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of taking a qualified notion as absolute in a dialectical argument and explain how it undermines the conclusion.

Core Idea
A flawed dichotomy can create an apparent proof; the refutation lies in uncovering a hidden assumption.

In this passage, the author presents a proof for the indivisible part (atomism) using a disjunctive syllogism. The proof seems logically sound until the solution reveals a subtle fallacy: the argument restricts the possibilities by qualifying the notion of non-existence. We will dissect this argument, expose the fallacy, and learn to spot similar errors in theological and philosophical debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall logical structure
The argument is a compound disjunctive syllogism. Determine what is being proved: the existence of the indivisible part (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazza').
2
Map the disjunctions
The first disjunction: non-existence occurs either in the moment of existence or in another moment. The first branch is eliminated because it leads to the conjunction of opposites (impossible). Then the second branch is subdivided: either there is time between the two moments or not. Eliminate the first sub-branch (leads to removal of both opposites). The remaining possibility (no time between) leads to consecutive instants, implying atomic time.
3
Examine the solution
The solution points out that the argument assumed that if non-existence doesn't occur in the moment of existence, it must occur in a later distinct moment. But it could begin at the exact same moment as existence (the beginning of the moment). This is the fallacy: taking ‘non-existence in another moment’ (a qualified version) as the only alternative to ‘non-existence in the same moment’.
4
Identify the fallacy type
This is known as taking the qualified for absolute (أخذ المقيد بدل المطلق). The argument artificially restricts the possibilities by assuming that non-existence must be in a completely separate moment, ignoring the possibility of simultaneous beginning.
5
Generalize the lesson
In any disjunctive argument, ensure that the disjunction is exhaustive and that the branches are not artificially qualified. This fallacy often appears in theological discussions about temporality and attributes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A compound argument can be valid in structure yet invalid if a term is wrongly restricted.

In this passage, the author defends the kalām atomistic view of time against an objection. The argument proceeds stepwise from the absence of time between instants to the atomic composition of time, motion, and distance. Identify the premise chain
