Conversion of the Contradictory of the Temporally Qualified Particular Affirmative
Author proves the conversion rule for the contradictory of a particular affirmative in two special cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof for converting the contradictory of a temporally qualified particular affirmative proposition.

Core Idea
The conversion of the contradictory preserves truth for temporally qualified propositions through a conditional proof.

This passage proves that the two special propositions (temporally qualified particular affirmatives) convert like their counterparts. The proof uses a conditional argument to show that if the original is true, the converted is true. Let's walk through the logical steps.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
The original proposition is 'Some J is B as long as J, not always'. Note the temporal qualifier 'as long as' and the negation 'not always'.
This is a particular affirmative with a temporal condition.
2
Understand the target conversion
The rule claims that the proposition converts to 'Some not-B is not J as long as not-B, not always'. This is the conversion of the contradictory (contraposition) adapted for temporal qualifiers.
3
Follow the proof by contradiction
Assume the original is true. Then there must exist an individual that is J and B, and also something that is not J is not B. If not, B would always be, contradicting 'not always'. From this, it follows that not J is not B as long as not B, not always.
The proof uses the condition 'otherwise contradiction' (khalf).
4
Recognize the conditional syllogism
The argument can be seen as a compound conditional: if original true, then existence condition holds; if existence condition holds, then conversion holds. The conclusion is the conversion.
This is a valid immediate inference.

Ibn Sīnā's Conversion Rule and al-Khūnajī's Objection on Existential Import
Author presents Ibn Sīnā's conversion rule for universal negatives and al-Khūnajī's objection using external existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Sīnā's conversion rule for universal negatives and identify al-Khūnajī's objection regarding existential import in external propositions.

Core Idea
The conversion of the contradictory of a universal negative depends on existential import – al-Khūnajī reveals this subtlety.

This lesson examines a critical refinement in Islamic logic: the conversion of the contradictory of universal negatives. Ibn Sīnā proposed a rule that from 'No J is B' it follows that 'Some not-B is J'. However, al-Khūnajī objected that this fails for external propositions because it assumes the subject term actually exists. We will dissect Ibn Sīnā's proof and al-Khūnajī's counterargument.

Guided Path
1
Understand Ibn Sīnā's proof
Consider the proposition 'Nothing from J is B absolutely' (e.g., 'No void is a dimension'). Ibn Sīnā argues that if it is true, then 'Some of what is not B is J actually' must be true. Otherwise, a contradiction arises: 'Nothing from what is not B is J always' would be true, which converts to 'Nothing from J is not B always', implying 'Every J is B always', contradicting the original.
This proof relies on the principle of contradictory conversion and the law of non-contradiction.
2
Convert using the principle of contradictory
The conversion of the contradictory of a universal negative (E) yields a particular affirmative (I). Formally: from '¬∃x (Jx ∧ Bx)' to '∃x (¬Bx ∧ Jx)'. In Ibn Sīnā's system, this holds as an immediate inference.
This is also known as 'contraposition' in modern logic, but with existential import.
3
Identify al-Khūnajī's objection
Al-Khūnajī points out that in external propositions (those about actually existing entities), 'Nothing from J is not B' may be interpreted in two ways: (1) as double negation of existence ('Negation of negation in external existence') or (2) as negation of an external affirmation of a negative predicate. Only the first would imply 'Every J is B', but the second does not guarantee the existence of Js.
Al-Khūnajī's objection highlights the need to consider whether the terms refer to existent things.
4
Analyze the symbolic distinction
Al-Khūnajī provides symbolic forms: either '¬∃x (Jx ∧ (E!x ∧ ¬Bx))' or '¬∃x (Jx ∧ ¬(E!x ∧ Bx))'. Neither of the resulting conditionals (e.g., ∀x (Jx → (E!x → Bx))) implies the actual existence of Js, because the consequent is conditional on E!x. Thus, the conversion fails for external propositions.
The key insight is that existential import is not automatically guaranteed in external propositions.

The Master's Proof for Conversion by Contradiction
Master's proof for converting universal negatives by contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the chain of immediate inferences the Master uses to prove that 'No J is B' converts to 'Every J is B' by contradiction.

Core Idea
A universal negative converts to a universal affirmative through a chain of contradiction and double negation.

This lesson unpacks the Master's argument that if 'No J is B' is true absolutely, then 'Every J is B' must follow. The proof relies on a series of immediate inferences involving conversion, negation, and contradiction. We will walk through each step to see how the conclusion is derived.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting proposition
The Master begins with the universal negative 'No J is B' (لا شيء من ج ب) taken as absolutely true.
2
Derive 'Some non-J is B'
From the truth of 'No J is B', he asserts that 'Some non-J is B' must be true actually, otherwise a contradiction would follow.
This is an immediate inference (not standard conversion).
3
Express as double negation
'Some non-J is B' is equivalent to 'Not some non-J is not B' (ليس بعض ما ليس ب ج بالفعل), which is a negation of a particular negative.
This step uses the equivalence of 'Some S is P' with 'Not (Some S is not P)'.
4
Convert to a universal negative about non-J
From the previous, the Master infers 'Nothing that is not J is B always' (لا شيء مما ليس ب ج دائماً), a universal negative with a negative subject.
This is a form of conversion by contradictory ( عكس النقيض).
5
Convert again to 'No J is not B'
The universal negative 'Nothing that is not J is B' converts to 'No J is not B' (لا شيء من ج ليس ب دائماً) by another conversion step.
This is a standard conversion of a universal negative.

Al-Khunaji's Objection on External Existential Propositions
Al-Khunaji's objection to the Master regarding external universal negatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why al-Khunaji rejects the Master's conversion when propositions are about external existence and identify the role of double negation.

Core Idea
The Master's conversion fails for external propositions because negating an external affirmation with a negated predicate is not a simple double negation.

Al-Khunaji, reported by the author of Kashf, objects that the Master's proof fails when the propositions are about actual external existence. The issue lies in the interpretation of 'No J is not B externally'. Al-Khunaji argues that this does not entail 'Every J is B externally' unless it is a pure negation of negation. We examine his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the external existential context
The Master's conversion is applied to propositions that assert existence in the external world (الخارجية). Al-Khunaji focuses on 'No J is not B in external existence'.
2
Identify the two possible interpretations
The phrase 'No J is not B externally' could mean either: (a) a negation of a negation (سلب السلب) which would be equivalent to an affirmation, or (b) a negation of an external affirmation with a negated predicate.
Al-Khunaji says that only interpretation (a) would yield 'Every J is B externally'.
3
Analyze interpretation (b)
Under interpretation (b), 'No J is not B externally' means 'There is no J that exists externally with a negation of B from it' – i.e., no J exists that lacks B. This does not assert that any J exists.
This is a universal negative about existing Js that are not-B.
4
See why the conclusion fails
From this interpretation, we cannot conclude 'Every J is B externally' because that would require the existence of at least one J. The proposition could be true even if J has no existence at all.
The possibility of an empty subject (J non-existent) blocks the inference.

Universal vs. Particular Absolute Conditionals
Author summarizes that the major premise's antecedent necessitates the consequent, unlike particular absolute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a universal conditional from a particular absolute conditional and explain why the latter requires an additional condition.

Core Idea
A universal conditional's antecedent alone necessitates the consequent; a particular conditional does not.

In this lesson, we examine the summary of conditional relations. The author clarifies the crucial distinction between universal and particular absolute conditionals. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying conditional syllogisms in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Locate in the Arabic the words 'كلية' and 'جزئية مجردة'. Note that the author contrasts them explicitly.
2
Analyze the universal conditional
The author states that the antecedent of the universal 'by itself' necessitates the consequent. This means the relation is intrinsic and unconditional.
This is the key difference.
3
Analyze the particular absolute
The particular absolute requires an additional condition for the implication to hold. If that condition is absent, then the relation is absent.
So the relation is dependent on an external factor.
4
Apply the distinction
Consider a fiqh example: 'If one consumes alcohol, then they are sinful' — this is universal because alcohol consumption necessarily incurs sin. But 'If one is forced, then they are excused' is particular, because force alone does not always excuse; it depends on degrees of duress.
This shows how the distinction affects legal reasoning.

Why Universal Affirmative Conditionals Do Not Convert
Proves that the universal affirmative conditional does not convert by two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain why a universal affirmative conditional does not convert, using the two proofs from the passage.

Core Idea
Entailment is not symmetric: A implies B does not mean B implies A.

In the previous lesson, you learned about conversion ('aks) of categorical propositions. But what about conditional propositions? Can we swap the antecedent and consequent and expect the conditional to remain true? The author of this passage gives two powerful arguments to show that we cannot. Let us walk through his reasoning, which uses the example of numbers and a clever reductio argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author states that the universal affirmative conditional (such as 'Whenever it is day, it is light') does not convert, meaning the swapped conditional is not necessarily true.
2
Examine the first proof: asymmetry of entailment
The author argues that if A entails B, it does not follow that B entails A, either in essence or by any attribute. He gives the example of ten entailing three (since ten is a sum that includes three as a part), but three does not entail ten.
This shows that entailment is one-way.
3
Examine the second proof: reductio ad absurdum
Assume conversion holds. Then for any two things x and y, their conjunction entails each of them. By conversion, each would entail the conjunction. But what entails a conjunction entails each of its parts, so x would entail y and vice versa. This would mean any two things mutually entail each other, which is false.
This reduces to an absurdity, proving the original assumption false.
4
Understand the consequence
Both proofs show that conversion is invalid for universal affirmative conditionals. This is because conditionals do not have the symmetry required for conversion.
In categorical logic, some propositions convert (e.g., universal negative), but conditionals behave differently.

Entailment is not symmetric: 'A entails B' does not imply 'B entails A'.

We've learned about conversion of categorical propositions. But what about conditionals? The author shows that universal affirmative conditionals (e.g., 'Whenever A, then B') do not convert. In this lesson, we'll explore the first two proofs: a concrete counterexample and a reductio showing absurd consequences if conversion held.

Understand the claim being refuted

Non-Reflection of Conditional Propositions
Author demonstrates that all conditional propositions lack a valid converse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why conditional propositions lack a valid converse and apply the reasoning to both universal/particular and disjunctive conditionals.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions do not convert.

In categorical logic, conversion (عكس) is a valid immediate inference for some proposition types. But conditionals are different: swapping the antecedent and consequent does not preserve truth. This passage shows that no conditional proposition—whether universal or particular, connected or disjunctive—has a true converse. Understanding this limitation is crucial for correct reasoning in Islamic sciences where conditional arguments are common.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition types involved
The author distinguishes universal positive (موجبة كلية) and universal negative (سالبة كلية) conditionals, then extends to particulars (جزئيتين) and disjunctives (منفصلات). Recognize that the argument starts with the universal positive.
A universal positive conditional is like 'Whenever A, then B'.
2

Rules of Entailment Between Exclusive and Inclusive Disjunctions
Author states rules of entailment between exclusive and inclusive disjunctives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the correct disjunctive implications from a given positive necessary proposition and convert between exclusive and inclusive disjunctions by negating their parts.

Core Idea
Exclusive and inclusive disjunctions interconvert through negation of parts.

In this passage, the author establishes key entailment relations between two types of disjunctive propositions: the exclusive (مانعة الجمع) and the inclusive (مانعة الخلق). Understanding these rules is essential for working with compound propositions in Islamic logic, especially in theological and juristic arguments where disjunctions are used to frame options and deduce consequences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two disjunction types
Identify the exclusive disjunction (مانعة الجمع), which allows only one part to be true, and the inclusive disjunction (مانعة الخلق), which allows at least one part to be true. Note that the passage treats them as distinct.
The exclusive is also called 'مانعة الجمع' and the inclusive 'مانعة الخلق' (sometimes misread as 'خلق' for 'خلو').
2
Entailments from a positive necessary proposition
A positive necessary proposition (موجبة حقيقية) entails a positive exclusive disjunction in the same quantity, derived from the two necessary grounds (ملزومي) of its parts, or from one part and the necessary ground of the other. Similarly, it entails a positive inclusive disjunction from the two necessary consequents (لازمي) of its parts, or from one part and the necessary consequent of the other.
The 'necessary ground' (ملزوم) is the antecedent, and 'necessary consequent' (لازم) is the consequent of a conditional implicit in the parts.
3
Interconversion of exclusive and inclusive via negation
Each disjunction type implies the other when we take the negations of their parts. For example, 'not both A and B' (exclusive) implies 'at least one of not-A or not-B is false' (inclusive), and vice versa. The author explains this by noting that preventing conjunction necessitates preventing emptiness of negations.
This is a key transformation: exclusive of A and B is equivalent to inclusive of their negations (or vice versa).
4
Handle differences in quality and quantity
If two disjunctions agree in parts but differ in quality and quantity, and the particular is negative, then the negative implies the positive. Otherwise, the positive is transformed into a necessary proposition without conversion. This rule accounts for cases where the disjunctions are not identical in form.
This covers situations where one disjunction is particular and negative, and the other is universal and positive, etc.
5
Recognize the limitation: negative does not imply positive
The passage states that a negative disjunction does not imply a positive one because it is possible for the two parts to coexist (both true) or both be absent (both false), which would violate the positive form that requires at least one true.
This is a crucial boundary: not all disjunctions are reversible.

Entailment of Conditionals from Categorical Actuals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive conditional propositions from categorical actuals and evaluate whether the entailment is reversible.

Core Idea
A categorical actual entails a conditional, but not reversibly.

In Islamic logic, categorical propositions (haqiqiyyat) are the building blocks of many arguments. But how do we move from a simple categorical like 'Every A is B' to a conditional 'If A then B'? This lesson unpacks the entailment rules from actuals to conditionals, showing the precise patterns and their limitations. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to transform propositions across logical forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of actual proposition
Determine whether the categorical actual is positive (e.g., 'Every A is B') or negative (e.g., 'No A is B'). The entailment rule differs for each.
2
Apply entailment rule for positive actuals
A positive actual entails a positive conditional via two patterns: (a) from the negation of each term or its necessary condition and the other term or its necessary condition, or (b) from one term or its necessary condition and the negation of the other term or the necessary condition of its negation. For example, from 'Every human is rational' and the negation of 'human', we get 'If not a human, then rational' (assuming necessary conditions).
Pattern (a) gives conditionals like 'If not-A then B'; pattern (b) gives 'If A then not-B'.
3
Check reversibility of the entailment
The entailment from a positive actual to a conditional is not reversible. The text states 'ولا تنعكس' because the consequent (tali) may be more general than the antecedent (muqaddam). For instance, 'If something is a human, it is an animal' (true conditional) does not entail the categorical 'Every human is an animal' in a reversible way because the conditional allows for a broader antecedent.
Non-reversibility is a key logical caution.
4
Apply entailment rule for negative actuals and note exception
A negative actual entails a negative conditional from its terms or from the negations of both terms. However, al-Khunaji (in the footnote) objects that the rule claiming impossibility of simultaneous contrariety and entailment is flawed. He gives examples where contradictory propositions entail each other, like 'No proposition is true' and 'Some propositions are true'. This shows that the entailment patterns are not absolute.
Al-Khunaji's objection is a refinement, not a rejection of the basic rule.

Define simple exclusive disjunction (بسيطة)
A simple exclusive disjunction rules that it is impossible for both parts to be true together, but it does not rule on whether both can be false. Example: 'The day is either night or day' (cannot be both, but can it be neither? In reality, no; but logically, the simple sense does not address that).
The key is that only conjunction is prohibited; both false remains possible.
2

A compound exclusive disjunction rules two things: (1) the conjunction is false, and (2) the conjunction of the negations is true (i.e., both false is allowed). In other words, exactly one is true. Example: 'Either the number is even or odd' – one is true, the other false, and not both false.
This is equivalent to an inclusive disjunction plus the condition that at most one is true.

The passage states that the entailment rules apply to the simple sense (بالمعنى البسيط). It warns that most previous rulings apply to the compound sense. Check the footnote: 'هذا الحكم إنما يتم إذا كانت المانعة الجمع بالمعنى البسيط لا المركب'.
Understanding this saves you from misapplying the rules.
4

For a simple exclusive disjunction, the entailment to a conditional is unconditional: if A and B cannot be together, then 'if A then not B' holds. For a compound exclusive disjunction, the same entailment holds, but additionally 'if not A then B' holds (since at most one false). The passage notes that reversal may differ.

The Two Types of Opposition for Conditionals and Disjunctives
Conditions for opposition between conditionals and disjunctives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish full opposition from partial opposition for conditional and disjunctive propositions and identify the truth relations in each case.

Core Idea
Opposition between compound propositions is either full or partial, depending on contraposition.

This passage defines the logical opposition (ta'ānud) between conditional and disjunctive propositions. It provides two cases: full opposition when they are both mutually entailing and contrapositive, and partial opposition when they lack contraposition. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly evaluating arguments that involve conditionals and disjunctives in Islamic disciplines such as usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
The passage mentions two conditions: 'talāzamā' (mutual entailment) and 'ta'ākasā' (contrapositive). Note that both are required for the first case, while only mutual entailment is present in the second.
Mutual entailment means each implies the other; contrapositive means the contrapositive of each equals the other.
2
Understand the first case: full opposition
If both conditions hold, then each proposition is the exact negation of the other in both truth and falsehood. This is full contradiction.
Example: 'If A then B' and 'If not B then not A' (contrapositive) are fully opposed if they also mutually entail each other.
3
Understand the second case: partial opposition
If they are not contrapositive, then the entailing proposition's truth is the negation of the entailed proposition's truth, but the entailing proposition's falsehood is the same as the entailed proposition's truth value.
In other words, truth of the entailing implies falsehood of the entailed, but falsehood of the entailing does not imply truth of the entailed; instead the entailed has the same truth value as the false entailing.
4
Apply to a concrete Islamic example
Consider the conditional 'If the prayer is valid, then purification is required' (A → B) and the disjunctive 'Either the prayer is valid or purification is not required' (A ∨ ¬B). Check if they are mutually entailing and contrapositive.
They are mutually entailing (A→B ⇔ A∨¬B) but not contrapositive in form. Hence this is a case of partial opposition.
