The Connected Conditional Syllogism
Author explains valid inferences in connected conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two valid inference forms in a connected conditional syllogism (modus ponens and modus tollens) and distinguish them from invalid forms.

Core Idea
In a connected conditional, affirming the antecedent forces the consequent; denying the consequent forces denying the antecedent. Nothing else follows.

In this lesson, we examine the first type of conditional syllogism: the connected conditional (muttasil). The author states that from a premise 'If P, then Q', we can validly infer Q from P, and not-P from not-Q. This is the foundation of many theological and juristic arguments. We will break down these rules and see why the reverse forms are invalid.

Guided Path
1
Understand the structure of a connected conditional
A connected conditional (muttasil) is an 'If P, then Q' statement, where P is the antecedent (muqaddam) and Q is the consequent (tali). The entire premise is a conditional, and the syllogism adds an exception (istithna') that affirms or denies one part.
2
Identify valid inference 1: Affirming the antecedent
When we affirm the antecedent (istithna' bi-ayn al-muqaddam), we conclude the consequent. This is modus ponens. The author says: 'taking the same of its antecedent gives the same of its consequent'.
This is valid.
3
Identify valid inference 2: Denying the consequent
When we deny the consequent (istithna' bi-naqid al-tali), we conclude the denial of the antecedent. This is modus tollens. The author says: 'taking the contradictory of its consequent gives the contradictory of its antecedent'.
This is also valid.
4
Recognize invalid reverse forms
The author stresses the rule 'does not reverse'. This means that affirming the consequent does not yield the antecedent, and denying the antecedent does not yield the consequent. These are fallacies.
Common mistake: thinking the converse is valid.

Commentary
This passage classifies conditional syllogisms (qiyas istithna'i). It begins by stating the two main types: connected (muttasil) and disjunctive (munfasil). For the connected, the valid inferences are: affirming the antecedent yields the consequent, and denying the consequent yields denial of the antecedent. The author stresses that this is definitive and does not reverse, meaning denying the antecedent or affirming the consequent are not valid. For the disjunctive, three subtypes are given. The first is true disjunction (haqiqiyya), which excludes both conjunction and emptiness: affirming one yields denying the other, and vice versa. The second is exclusive of conjunction (mani' al-jam'), which allows both to be false but not both true: here, affirming one yields denying the other, but not vice versa (since both could be false). The third is exclusive of emptiness (mani' al-khuluw), which allows both to be true but not both false: here, denying one yields affirming the other, but not vice versa (since both could be true). This classification is standard in Islamic logic for conditional syllogisms.

The Disjunctive Conditional Syllogism
Author classifies disjunctive conditionals and gives inference rules for each type.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the three types of disjunctive conditionals (haqiqiyya, mani' al-jam', mani' al-khuluw) and state which inference forms are valid for each.

Core Idea
The validity of a disjunctive inference depends on whether the disjunction allows both true, both false, or exactly one true.

This lesson covers the second major type of conditional syllogism: the disjunctive (munfasil). The author distinguishes three kinds: true disjunction (haqiqiyya), exclusive of conjunction (mani' al-jam'), and exclusive of emptiness (mani' al-khuluw). Each has different inference rules. Understanding these is essential for correctly applying disjunctive arguments in Islamic law and theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the structure of a disjunctive conditional
A disjunctive conditional (munfasil) is an 'Either A or B' statement. The disjunction can be of three types depending on whether it allows both parts to be true, both false, or exactly one true.
2
Learn the three types
1) Haqiqiyya (true disjunction): one and only one part is true. 2) Mani' al-jam' (exclusive of conjunction): both parts cannot be true, but both can be false. 3) Mani' al-khuluw (exclusive of emptiness): both parts cannot be false, but both can be true.
These correspond to exclusive or, inclusive or, and something like an 'at least one' condition.
3
Identify valid inferences for each type
For haqiqiyya: affirming one part yields denying the other, and denying one yields affirming the other (both directions). For mani' al-jam': only affirming one yields denying the other (not vice versa). For mani' al-khuluw: only denying one yields affirming the other (not vice versa).
The passage uses 'by reversing' to indicate the biconditional.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider 'An action is either obligatory (wajib) or forbidden (haram).' This is mani' al-jam' (cannot be both, but could be neither). If we know it is wajib, we conclude it is not haram. But if we know it is not wajib, we cannot conclude it is haram (it might be makruh or mubah).
Another example: 'The sun has risen or it is daytime.' This is mani' al-khuluw (cannot both be false). If we know the sun has not risen, we can conclude it is daytime. But if we know the sun has risen, we cannot conclude it is not daytime (both are true).

Denying the Consequent in Modal Conditionals: Razi's Objection Refuted
Imam al-Razi's objection and its refutation regarding absolute general consequent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when denying the consequent in a conditional is valid given modal considerations, and explain the author's refutation of Imam al-Razi's objection.

Core Idea
Negation of an absolute general is perpetual; denying the consequent already implies it.

Imam al-Razi raised a challenge to the standard rule of denying the consequent in connected conditionals. He argued that if the consequent is absolute general, the inference fails. The author of this passage refutes him by showing that the contradictory of an absolute general proposition is inherently perpetual, so the rule remains valid. Understanding this resolution sharpens your ability to handle modal conditionals in logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify Razi's Objection
Locate where Imam al-Razi states that if the consequent is 'absolute general' (mutlaq 'amm), then denying it does not produce the negation of the antecedent. Note his example: 'Whenever this is a human, it is actually laughing (absolute general).'
Razi is challenging the standard rule of denying the consequent in a conditional.
2
Understand the Counterexample
Razi gives the case: 'But it is not laughing' does not entail 'It is not a human,' because some humans are not laughing at the moment. Recognize that this counterexample relies on interpreting the consequent as true at some times and false at others.
The absolute general proposition 'he is actually laughing' is true at times when he laughs, false when he doesn't.
3
Analyze the Refutation
The author responds: The contradictory of an absolute general proposition is a perpetual negation (i.e., 'he is always not laughing'). Therefore, to deny the consequent is already to assert that he is perpetually not laughing. Thus the inference becomes: If he is human, he laughs at some time (absolute general). He is perpetually not laughing. Therefore he is not human. This is valid.
The key insight: the negation of 'absolute general' is not 'sometimes not', but 'always not'. The perpetuity is inherent in the negation.
4
Grasp the Modal Principle
The author concludes that antecedent and consequent may have different modalities, so one must consider the modality of both to know how the conclusion should be formed. This means that in modal conditionals, you must check the modality of the consequent's negation to determine whether the inference is valid.
This principle prevents mistaken rejections of modus tollens in modal arguments.

Objection to Conditional Syllogism and Its Refutation
Refutation of an objection to conditional syllogism validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition for the validity of a conditional syllogism and refute the objection that impossibility of the excluded premise undermines the inference.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms require the excluded premise to be true or accepted; otherwise no inference occurs.

Some scholars argued that conditional syllogisms (qiyās istithnāʾī) are invalid because the premise we exclude (either the antecedent or the negation of the consequent) might be impossible, and impossibility may break the implication. The author refutes this by showing that the syllogism's purpose—proving the consequent or negating the antecedent—presupposes the truth of that premise. When that condition holds, the objection is harmless; when it doesn't, the syllogism is not even intended. This lesson unpacks the objection, its logical form, and the correct condition for validity.

Guided Path
1
Recall the structure of a conditional syllogism
Identify the two valid forms: modus ponens (affirming the antecedent to conclude the consequent) and modus tollens (denying the consequent to conclude the negation of the antecedent). The passage refers to both: 'إثبات التالي' (proving the consequent) and 'انتقاء المقدم' (negating the antecedent).
The excluded premise is either the antecedent (in modus ponens) or the contradictory of the consequent (in modus tollens).
2
Understand the objection: impossibility breaks the implication
The objectors claim that if the excluded premise is impossible (ممتنع), then the implication (ملازمة) may not hold, because an impossible proposition can entail another impossible proposition. Therefore, the conclusion cannot be trusted.
This objection hinges on the modality (جهة) of the premises.
3
Analyze the author's refutation: the condition of the syllogism
The author argues that the user of the syllogism intends a specific purpose: to prove the consequent (by affirming the antecedent) or to negate the antecedent (by denying the consequent). This purpose is achieved only if the antecedent is true (in modus ponens) or the consequent is false (in modus tollens), either in reality or in the opponent's belief. The objection only applies if that condition is not met; but if the condition is not met, the syllogism is not being used correctly.
Thus, the validity of the conditional syllogism is conditional upon the truth or acceptance of the excluded premise.
4
Apply the refutation to a concrete example
Take the conditional argument: 'If the world is temporally originated (ḥādith), then it has a cause (muḥdith). The world is temporally originated; therefore it has a cause.' For this modus ponens to be valid, one must assume (or the opponent must accept) that the world is indeed temporally originated. If the opponent denies that, the syllogism does not work, but the objection does not refute the logical form itself.
The condition is either real (fī nafs al-amr) or accepted by the opponent (ʿinda al-khaṣm).

The Simple Syllogism: Exactly Two Premises

Author refutes the claim using the divisive syllogism and second figure conditions

Core Idea
Not every pair of premises yields a conclusion; the second figure requires differing quality.

This passage critiques a blanket claim that any two premises in a syllogism produce a valid conclusion. The author presents a counterexample involving the divisive syllogism, combining a disjunctive premise with two affirmative categoricals in the second figure. Because both categoricals are affirmative, the second figure conditions are violated, and no conclusion follows from them alone. Even when treated as part of a compound syllogism, the attempt leads to a self-contradictory disjunction, revealing deeper constraints on syllogistic validity.

Qiyas al-khalf is not one syllogism but a compound of a categorical and a conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert an indirect proof (qiyas al-khalf) into a direct categorical syllogism by adding the contradictory of the absurd consequent as a premise.

Core Idea
Every indirect proof can be transformed into a direct one by adding one premise.

Proof by contradiction (qiyas al-khalf) is a powerful tool, but logicians prefer direct syllogisms for clarity. The author shows a simple method: take the absurdity that follows from denying the conclusion, assert its contradictory, and combine it with the original true premise. This yields a direct syllogism. Let's walk through the steps.

1
Identify the indirect proof's structure
A qiyas al-khalf assumes the negation of the desired conclusion, derives an impossibility (muhal), and thereby affirms the conclusion. Recognize the true premise (muqaddima sadiqa) and the impossible consequent (al-muhal).
The impossible thing is contradictory to a known truth.
2
Extract the contradictory of the impossible consequent
The impossible thing is a statement that cannot be true. Its contradictory (naqid) must be a necessary truth. Formulate this contradictory explicitly.
If the impossible is 'the world is eternal', its contradictory is 'the world is not eternal'.
3
Add it as a premise to the original true premise
Combine the original true premise with this new premise (the contradictory of the impossible). This forms a direct categorical syllogism.
The original true premise remains unchanged.
4

'Aks al-Khalf: Converting the Opposition
First corollary: conversion of qiyas al-khalf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a qiyas al-'aks by invalidating a premise using the contradictory of the conclusion.

Core Idea
'Aks al-khalf flips the direction of invalidation: assume conclusion false to disprove a premise.

In qiyas al-khalf, we invalidate the contradictory of the conclusion by affirming the premises. The converse of this method, called 'qiyas al-'aks', works the other way: by assuming the contradictory of the conclusion, we can invalidate one of the premises. This technique is useful in dialectical contexts to refute an opponent's assumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original qiyas al-khalf argument.
Find the syllogism where the contradictory of the conclusion is derived from the premises, showing it is false.
e.g., 'If the world is eternal (P), then it changes (Q); but it does not change (not Q); therefore it is not eternal (not P).'
2
Select the premise you want to invalidate.
Choose one premise that you suspect is false. The goal is to show that this premise leads to a contradiction when combined with the opposite of the conclusion.
In the example, we might want to invalidate 'If the world is eternal, then it changes.'
3
Assume the contradictory of the conclusion.
Take the negation of the original conclusion. If the original conclusion was 'not P', assume 'P' (the opposite).
Assume 'The world is eternal' (the opposite of 'not eternal').
4
Combine the assumed conclusion with the other premise.
Add the assumed conclusion to the remaining premise (the one not being invalidated) to derive a contradiction with the target premise.
Take 'The world is eternal' (assumed) and 'the world does not change' (other premise) to derive a contradiction with 'If eternal then changes'.
5
Conclude that the target premise is false.
Since the assumption leads to a contradiction, the target premise must be false. This is the qiyas al-'aks.
Thus, the conditional premise is invalidated.

Qiyas al-Dawr: Circular Syllogism
Second corollary: circular syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a circular syllogism and state the condition for its validity.

Core Idea
In qiyas al-dawr, the conclusion and converse of one premise yield the other premise universally, but only if terms are coextensive.

Normally, a syllogism moves from premises to conclusion. But the author points out a reciprocal relationship: the conclusion, together with the converse of one premise, can produce the other premise. This is called a circular syllogism, and it is only valid when the terms in the affirmative proposition are equal, allowing the converse to be universal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original syllogism.
Take a valid categorical syllogism in the first figure, e.g., 'Every A is B, every B is C, so every A is C.'
Use a kalam example: 'Every contingent has a cause, every created thing is contingent, so every created thing has a cause.'
2
Take the conclusion and the converse of one premise.
The conclusion is 'Every A is C'. The converse of the minor premise 'Every A is B' is 'Some B is A' (conversion per accidens).
For a universal affirmative, conversion yields a particular.
3
Combine to derive the other premise universally.
From 'Every A is C' and 'Some B is A', we can deduce 'Every B is C' only if the terms A and B are equal (coextensive).
If the class of A equals the class of B, then 'Some B is A' becomes 'Every B is A', and using 'Every A is C' we get 'Every B is C'.
4
Check the condition of term equality.
The passage says: 'that is only possible when the two terms of the affirmative [proposition] are equal (musawiyan) so that it converts universally.'
If terms are not equal, the circular syllogism is invalid because the converse is not universal.
5
Conclude whether it is a valid qiyas al-dawr.
If the condition is met, then the syllogism is circular in a valid sense; otherwise, it is not a proper circular syllogism.
This is a technical corollary, not a common fallacy.

Third corollary: begging the question

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism commits the fallacy of musadara (begging the question).

Core Idea
Musadara is using the conclusion as one of the premises, making the argument circular and uninformative.

The third corollary is a warning: if the conclusion itself is placed among the premises, the syllogism becomes viciously circular. This fallacy, called musadara, fails to prove anything because it assumes what it needs to establish. Recognizing this fallacy is crucial for evaluating the soundness of any logical argument.

Musadara occurs when the conclusion appears verbatim or can be trivially deduced from a premise without additional evidence.

The passage says: 'And [the case] where the conclusion is made one of the two premises of the syllogism is called 'musadara'.'
This is a clear definition.
4
Recognize that the argument provides no proof.
Since the conclusion is assumed, the argument is circular and does not advance knowledge.
In dialectics, this is a serious flaw.
5
Suggest how to avoid it.
Ensure that the conclusion is not used as a premise; all premises must be independently established.
Remove the conclusion from the premises and find independent support.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish qiyas al-'aks, qiyas al-dawr, and musadara, and assess their logical validity using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
The syllogism by contradiction has inverse and circular forms, but using the conclusion as a premise is fallacious.

After mastering the basic qiyas al-khalf, the author presents two valid corollaries—inverse and circular syllogisms—and warns against the fallacy of begging the question. Understanding these helps you recognize variations of indirect reasoning and avoid invalid shortcuts in arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inverse syllogism (قياس العكس)
The inverse works in reverse: instead of showing the contradictory of the conclusion falsifies the premise, it shows that the truth of the contradictory of the conclusion (or its opposite) falsifies the premise when combined with one premise. This is like a contrapositive operation.
The Arabic says: 'كما يبطل نقيض النتيجة لصدق المقدمة فكذا تبطل المقدمة لصدق نقيض النتيجة أو ضدها'.
2
Understand the circular syllogism (قياس الدور)
The circular syllogism takes the conclusion and the universal converse of one premise to derive the other premise. This only works if the terms in the affirmative proposition are coextensive; otherwise the conversion is not universal.
The author specifies: 'وذلك إنما يمكن فيما يتساوى طرفا الموجبة لتنعكس كلية'.
3
Recognize begging the question (مصادرة)
Using the conclusion itself as a premise in the syllogism that is supposed to prove it is invalid. This is a common fallacy that destroys the purpose of proof.
The passage says: 'و ما تجعل النتيجة إحدى مقدمتي القياس يسمى مصادرة'.

The Third Path: Finding the Middle Term
Method for discovering syllogistic premises by comparing terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the third path method to discover a middle term for a desired syllogistic conclusion using Islamic theological or jurisprudential examples.

Core Idea
To find the middle term, list what predicates and subjects each term, then look for the overlap.

The third path is a practical method for discovering the premises needed to prove a desired conclusion. Instead of guessing, you systematically collect all subjects and predicates of both terms, then search for a term that links them. This technique is especially useful in kalam and usul al-fiqh for constructing valid syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms of the conclusion
Start with the conclusion you want to prove. Let S be the subject and P be the predicate. For example, to prove 'the world is created', S = 'the world', P = 'created'.
Ensure both terms are stated clearly.
2
List all subjects and predicates of each term
For S, list all terms that are subjects of S (things S is a subject of) and all terms that are predicates of S (things that are affirmed of S). Do the same for P. Include both affirmative and negative relations (what is denied from S or P).
In Arabic, subjects are called 'mawdu'at' and predicates 'mashmulat' (things included).
3
Look for a term that appears on both lists with the right direction
Search for a term M such that M is a predicate of S (i.e., S is predicated with M) and M is a subject of P (i.e., M is a subject for P). If found, M is the middle term, giving a first-figure syllogism: S is M, M is P, so S is P.
The text says: 'if you find among the predicates of the subject that which is the subject of the predicate'.
4
Consider other figures and negative relations
If the desired conclusion is not in the first figure, the method works similarly for other figures by adjusting the relation. Also remember to include negative predicates and subjects (things that are negated).
The text adds: 'and likewise the statement in the remaining figures'.
5
Apply to a concrete Islamic science example
Try: prove that the world is temporally originated (hadith). S = 'the world', P = 'temporally originated'. List predicates of S: e.g., 'composed of bodies and accidents', 'subject to change'. List subjects of P: e.g., 'every temporally originated thing arises after non-existence'. If 'subject to change' is a predicate of the world and also a subject of 'temporally originated', then 'subject to change' is the middle term.
This matches the method in kalam proofs for the existence of God.

Analyzing Syllogisms: From Conclusion to Structure
Method for analyzing syllogisms by working backward from the conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of syllogistic analysis: given a conclusion and a productive statement, identify the premises, middle term, figure, and distinguish simple from compound syllogisms.

Core Idea
Analyze a syllogism backward from its conclusion to uncover its logical skeleton: the middle term is the key.

In this passage, the author teaches how to 'reverse-engineer' a syllogism. Instead of building from premises, we start with the conclusion and examine the argument that claims to produce it. This method is essential for evaluating the validity of arguments and breaking down complex reasoning into its basic components.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conclusion
Begin by clearly stating the desired conclusion (al-matloob). This is the statement the argument is supposed to prove.
2
Check the Relation to the Conclusion
Examine the productive statement. Does it relate to the whole conclusion or only to a part? If to the whole, it is a hypothetical syllogism (qiyas istithna'i). If to a part, proceed to identify which part.
3
Determine the Known Premise
Look at that part: is it the subject (mahkum 'alayhi) or predicate (mahkum bihi)? This tells you whether you have the minor (sughra) or major (kubra) premise.
4
Find the Missing Premise and Middle Term
Join the other part of the known premise to the other part of the conclusion. If a new relation appears, that gives the missing premise. The term common to both premises (and absent from the conclusion) is the middle term (al-wasat), and the figure becomes clear.
5
Handle Compound Syllogisms
If no relation appears, the syllogism is compound (murakkab). Treat the two parts as separate conclusions and apply the same method recursively until all premises and figures are identified.

Determining Syllogism Type and Finding Premises
Author distinguishes conditional from categorical syllogism types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given syllogism is conditional or categorical by analyzing the relation of a premise to the conclusion, and identify the minor or major premise when the relation is partial.

Core Idea
The first step in syllogistic analysis is to see if the premise relates to the whole conclusion or only to a part.

When analyzing a syllogism, we start with the conclusion. The author teaches us to examine the premise that produces it. The nature of the relation to the conclusion determines the type of syllogism and tells us which premise we already have. This lesson guides you through that initial decision point.

Guided Path
1
State the conclusion
Write down the proposition (al-matlab) you want to prove. For example, 'Nabidh is forbidden.'
2
Examine the given premise
Look at the statement that is supposed to prove the conclusion. Does it contain the entire conclusion or just one term from it?
The premise might be 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (contains the predicate 'forbidden') or 'If it is intoxicating, then it is forbidden' (contains the whole conclusion 'it is forbidden').
3
Case 1: Relation to the whole
If the premise contains the entire conclusion (as a part of a conditional), the syllogism is conditional (istithna'i). For example, 'If it is intoxicating, then it is forbidden; it is intoxicating; therefore it is forbidden.' The conditional premise relates to the whole conclusion.
4
Case 2: Relation to a part
If the premise contains only a term (subject or predicate) of the conclusion, the syllogism is categorical. Identify whether that term is the subject or predicate of the conclusion. That tells you whether you have the minor or major premise.
In 'Every intoxicant is forbidden → nabidh is forbidden', the predicate 'forbidden' matches, so it is the major premise.
5
Find the missing premise
Join the other part of the given premise (e.g., 'intoxicant') with the other part of the conclusion (e.g., 'nabidh'). If they form a relation (e.g., 'Nabidh is an intoxicant'), you have the other premise and the middle term ('intoxicant').
The figure becomes clear once both premises are identified.

Resolving Compound Syllogisms
Author explains iterative analysis for compound syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a compound syllogism and apply the iterative method to decompose it into simple syllogisms.

Core Idea
Compound syllogisms are broken down by treating intermediate conclusions as new desired conclusions.

Not every syllogism yields its middle term in one step. When the first pairing fails to produce a deductive link, the author instructs us to treat the two parts as new desired conclusions and repeat the analysis. This iterative process continues until simple syllogisms emerge, each with its own middle term and figure.

Guided Path
1
Attempt the basic analysis
Apply the method from the previous lesson: start with the conclusion, check the relation of the given premise, and try to find the middle term by joining parts.
2
Recognize when it is compound
If no relation occurs between the parts (i.e., no middle term appears), the syllogism is compound (murakkab), not simple (basit).
The author says: 'وإلا فالقياس مركب لا بسيط' (Otherwise, the syllogism is compound, not simple).
3

4
Repeat the analysis for each part
For each new desired conclusion, repeat the analysis: find a premise that relates to it (wholly or partially), identify the other premise, and continue until you have a chain of simple syllogisms.
The author says: 'ضع الجزئين كما وضعت جزئي المطلوب أولاً و اعمل عما كما عملت تهما' (place the two parts just as you placed the two parts of the desired conclusion first, and work with them as you worked with them).
5
Stop when simple syllogisms are reached
Continue until you have a series of simple syllogisms, each with a clear middle term, figure, and conclusion. The final conclusion is the original desired conclusion.
This process is essentially reducing a sorites or poly-syllogism into its components.

The Middle Term's Causality: Explanation vs Existence
Author clarifies that the middle term's causality is relational, not absolute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the middle term being an absolute cause of the major term and being a cause of the predication of the major to the minor.

Core Idea
In demonstration, the middle term explains why the predicate belongs to the subject, not why the predicate exists.

This passage resolves a common confusion: does the middle term need to be a cause of the major term itself? The author shows it only needs to be a cause of the attribution. Understanding this is key to recognizing that demonstrative proofs can include middle terms that are effects, not just causes.

Precision in Proof by Contradiction: Avoiding the Non-Contradictory Trap
Warning about substituting non-contradictory for true contradictory in qiyas al-khalf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error of substituting a non-contradictory for the true contradictory in a proof by contradiction (qiyas al-khalf) and correct it.

Core Idea
The assumed contradictory must be exact; a near-opposite invalidates the entire proof.

Proof by contradiction (qiyas al-khalf) is a powerful logical tool in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. However, it is easy to misuse: one might assume a statement that is not the precise contradictory of the desired claim, leading to an invalid argument. This warning passage alerts us to that error, illustrating it with a metaphysical example about existence and essences. By learning to spot this mistake, you will be able to construct and evaluate proofs by contradiction correctly in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logical form of qiyas al-khalf
Recall that qiyas al-khalf works by assuming the contradictory (naqid) of the conclusion, deriving an absurdity, and thereby proving the original conclusion. The 'contradictory' is a statement that is opposite in truth value: if the conclusion is 'P', the contradictory is 'not-P' (or its logical equivalent).
In Aristotelian logic, the contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative, etc.
2
Recognize the error described in the passage
The passage warns against substituting a 'non-contradictory' (ghayr al-naqid) for the true contradictory. This means using a statement that is not the exact logical opposite but merely different or even contrary. Such an error makes the proof unsound because the assumed premise does not logically force the intended contradiction.
For example, if the claim is 'all A are B', the contradictory is 'some A is not B', not 'no A is B' (which is contrary).
3
Examine the provided example: existence and essences
The claim under debate: 'Existence is not a part of any essence.' The proof by contradiction assumes the opposite, i.e., that existence is a part of essences. The argument then shows that this leads to an infinite regress of genera and differentiae, which is absurd. Therefore, the original claim is proven. The warning is that if one had assumed something else (e.g., 'existence is a part of some essences'), it would not be the exact contradictory, and the proof would fail.
The exact contradictory of 'existence is not a part of any essence' is 'existence is a part of at least one essence', not 'existence is a part of all essences'.
4
Understand the consequence of the error
If a non-contradictory is used, the proof may still appear to work, but it does not actually refute the original claim. The passage emphasizes that this error can slip in, and one must be vigilant, especially in theological and philosophical arguments where precision is crucial.
In Islamic scholastic theology (kalam), such subtle errors can lead to false conclusions about divine attributes.

Author redefines mulāzama to bypass a dialectical objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how redefining a key term can resolve a dialectical objection in conditional syllogisms.

Core Idea
Redefining implication as natural necessitation eliminates the need for external estimation.

In classical logic, a syllogism with two conditional premises faced an objection: it seemed to require an extra estimation to be valid. The author responds by redefining what 'mulāzama' means—shifting from a general logical relation to a natural, inherent implication. This move dissolves the objection because the implication is now intrinsic to the subject itself, requiring no external estimation. Let's see how this works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original objection
The objection claimed that a syllogism with two conditional premises (e.g., 'If A then B, if B then C') does not directly produce a conclusion without an additional estimation that the premises are connected.

The author defines 'mulāzama' as 'iqtiḍāʾ ṭabīʿa' (natural necessitation) of the subject. This means the implication is inherent in the nature of the subject itself, not a relation that requires external support.
This is a stronger, ontological definition of implication compared to a purely logical relation.
3
See how the objection falls away
Once mulāzama is defined as natural implicature, no external estimation is needed. Merely conceptualizing the subject (taṣawwur al-mulzam) suffices to recognize its implication. The syllogism becomes: 'A naturally implies B, B naturally implies C, so A naturally implies C.'

The syllogism is unsound because the minor premise is false. Even though the logical form is valid, a false premise invalidates the argument. The argument fails due to a material fallacy, not a formal one.

This passage presents a reductio ad absurdum (burhan khalaf) to prove a particular implication. The author first assumes the implication is established. He then takes the premise (muqaddam): that the claim (al-mudda'a) does not necessitate the removal of reality (raf' al-waqi'). The reason given is that if the claim's existence were to necessitate the removal of an actual matter, then it would necessitate that implication, which is already assumed. The consequent (tali) is then shown false: if the claim's existence necessitated the implication of removal, then the non-implication would necessitate its non-existence, contradicting an earlier proof that non-implication necessitates existence. Therefore a contradiction (khalaf) arises, proving that the original assumption (that the claim does not necessitate removal) is false, hence the claim does necessitate reality. This is a standard logical technique used in Islamic logic to establish necessary implications by showing that the negation leads to absurdity.

The Fallacy of Compound Negation in Dialectical Exchange
Objection and solution on implication of existence and non-existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical fallacy in an argument that erroneously concludes a claim is necessarily true because it follows from both existence and non-existence of something, and apply the principle that negating a compound does not negate its parts to resolve such dilemmas.

Core Idea
Negating a compound does not negate every part.

This passage presents a common objection in logical debates: if a claim follows from both the existence and non-existence of a thing, then the claim must be true regardless of reality. The solver's response introduces a crucial refinement: the negation of a compound statement does not entail the negation of each conjunct. Understanding this distinction prevents fallacious inferences in Islamic legal reasoning and dialectics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's structure
The objector says: take any thing X such that both X's existence and X's non-existence entail claim C. Then whether X exists or not, C follows. This is a dilemma.
2
Locate the vulnerable point
The objector assumes that if 'if X exists then C' and 'if X does not exist then C' are true, then C is necessarily true. But the solver attacks the interpretation of the antecedent's negation.
3
Understand the solution
The solver says that the statement 'if the thing with this qualification does not exist' is actually a negation of a compound: 'the thing with this qualification exists'. Negating that compound does not force the negation of the part 'the thing exists' because the compound includes the qualification. So the negation may be due to the lack of the qualification, not the thing's existence.
Principle: denial of a conjunction does not deny each conjunct.
4
Apply the principle
Apply to a scenario from Usul al-Fiqh: if a ruling states 'both prayer and fasting are obligatory', denying that compound does not mean that neither is obligatory; one may still be obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange, consisting of an objection (naqd) labeled 'ج' and its answer (jawab) labeled 'حله'. The objector argues: Consider any thing X such that both X's existence and X's non-existence entail a certain claim (al-mudda'a). The objector claims that necessarily, whether X exists in reality or not, the claim follows. The author (or the solver) responds by attacking the inference. The solver says: Your conditional statement 'If X with this qualification does not exist' is in fact a denial of a compound: the compound of 'X with this qualification' together with its existence. Denying a compound does not require denying any specific part of it. Therefore, it is possible that what is denied is not X's existence per se, but rather the qualification 'being with this qualification' itself. In other words, the objector's argument commits a fallacy of composition/division: the negation of the whole does not entail the negation of a part. This is a typical logical refinement found in commentaries on syllogistic theory, where one must distinguish between negating a conjunction and negating each conjunct. The lesson guards against confusing compound negations with simple negations.
