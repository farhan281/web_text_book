Subject of Logic: Avicenna vs Samarqandi on Third/Fourth Intelligibles
Samarqandi's view on the subject matter of logic includes third and fourth intelligibles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Avicenna's and Samarqandi's views on the subject matter of logic and explain the role of third and fourth intelligibles.

Core Idea
Samarqandi expands logic's domain: its problems are about third and fourth intelligibles, not just secondary.

What exactly does logic study? This seemingly simple question was debated among Islamic logicians. While Avicenna held that logic's subject is secondary intelligibles (e.g., 'universal', 'particular'), Samarqandi argued that the problems of logic (its conclusions) involve even higher-order concepts: third and fourth intelligibles. Understanding this distinction clarifies how different scholars conceptualized the scope and abstraction level of logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scholars' positions
Locate in the passage: Avicenna says the subject of logic is secondary intelligibles. Samarqandi says the predicates of the problems of logic are third and fourth intelligibles. Note the difference between 'subject' and 'predicate of problems'.
Subject refers to what the science is about; predicates of its problems are the attributes investigated.
2
Define the levels of intelligibles
Primary intelligibles are concepts of external objects (e.g., 'horse', 'stone'). Secondary intelligibles are mental concepts that apply to primary ones (e.g., 'universal', 'particular'). Third intelligibles are concepts predicated of secondary intelligibles (e.g., 'genus' as a predicate of 'universal'). Fourth intelligibles are even more abstract.
Samarqandi's innovation is to recognize third and fourth intelligibles.
3
Contrast the two accounts
For Avicenna, logic studies secondary intelligibles directly. For Samarqandi, logic's problems (i.e., its conclusions) are about third and fourth intelligibles, meaning logic deals with relations among higher-order concepts.
Samarqandi does not reject Avicenna; he adds that the subject-predicate structure of logical propositions involves higher levels.
4
Apply the distinction to an example
Take the proposition: 'The universal is predicated of many.' Here, 'universal' is a secondary intelligible. But the statement itself—'The universal is predicated'—is a third intelligible because it predicates something of a secondary intelligible.
Samarqandi would say that such propositions are the proper subject of logical analysis.

Partial Opposition and Its Logical Reduction
Author defines partial opposition and reduces it to two particular negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify partial opposition between two concepts and express it as two particular negative propositions.

Core Idea
Partial opposition means each concept applies without the other in some cases.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of partial opposition (al-tabāyun al-juz'ī), a key relation between concepts in Islamic logic. The author not only defines it but also reduces it to its logical form: two particular negatives. This reduction is crucial for precise reasoning in Islamic sciences, where distinguishing degrees of opposition determines correct inferences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definiendum
The passage defines 'partial opposition' (al-tabāyun al-juz'ī). Locate the term and note that it is a type of opposition.
The definiendum is the concept being defined.
2
Extract the Genus and Differentia
The definition states: 'each of two concepts is true without the other in some cases.' The genus is opposition, the differentia is 'partial' (juz'ī), meaning not total. Compare with total opposition.
This is a real definition (hadd haqiqi).
3
Recognize the Reduction to Logical Form
The passage reduces partial opposition to two particular negatives: 'Some A is not B' and 'Some B is not A'. Understand why this captures the definition: if each concept is true without the other in some cases, then there exist instances of A that are not B and instances of B that are not A.
This reduction allows precise logical analysis.
4
Contrast with Total Opposition
Total opposition reduces to two universal negatives: 'No A is B' and 'No B is A'. Partial opposition allows some overlap, while total opposition means no overlap at all.
This contrast highlights the difference in logical strength.
5
Apply to 'More General in One Respect'
The passage shows that a concept which is more general in one respect (al-a'm min wajh) is partially opposed to the contradictory of its counterpart. This is a concrete application of the definition.
For example, 'animal' is more general than 'human' in one respect, so 'animal' is partially opposed to 'non-human'.

Critique of Khunaji: Parts of Essence as Genus or Differentia
Critique of Khunaji's argument on parts of essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate Khunaji's claim that every part of essence is either a genus or a differentia by identifying the conditions for a part to be a genus.

Core Idea
Not every part of the essence is a genus—only those that are essential and predicated.

In this passage, the author critiques Khunaji's assertion that any part of the essence (juz' al-mahiyya) must be either a genus or a differentia. The author argues that this is weak because a part may not be predicated of the species, and also that infinite regress is avoidable. This lesson will guide you through the logical steps of the critique and help you understand the precise conditions for something to be a genus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Khunaji claims that every part of the essence (juz' al-mahiyya) necessarily falls into either genus or differentia. Pinpoint this claim in the passage: it is the view being criticized.
2
Find the first objection
The author objects that even if a part is the 'complete common factor' (tamam al-mushtarak), it is a genus only if it is essential for the species (dhati) and predicated upon it (mahmul). The key point: it is possible that a part is not predicated.
For example, 'animal' is predicated of human, but 'body' is a part of human's essence yet not predicated as a genus.
3
Examine the second objection
The author rejects the claim that infinite regress follows from the requirement of a common factor. He argues that for something to be universal, it suffices that it is found in the entire common factor; no chain of further parts is required.
This blocks the argument that you would need endless parts to define a genus.
4
Synthesize the critique
The author weakens Khunaji's position by showing that (1) a part may not be predicated, so it cannot be a genus, and (2) infinite regress is not a necessary consequence. Therefore, the original claim that all parts of essence are either genus or differentia is not justified.

Method for distinguishing genus and differentia from predicated parts
Author provides a criterion to determine whether a predicated part of essence is genus or differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criterion to determine whether a predicated part of essence is a genus or a differentia.

Core Idea
Genus answers 'what is it?' for essence and another species; differentia does not.

In this passage, the author presents a clear method for distinguishing whether a predicated part of the essence is a genus or a differentia. This is a foundational tool for constructing definitions in Islamic logic and sciences. Understanding this criterion will help you analyze definitions found in fiqh, kalam, and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the predicated part
The passage refers to 'al-juz' al-mahmul' (the predicated part). This is any part of the essence that is predicated of the thing. For example, 'animal' is predicated of 'human'.
2
Check if it answers 'what is it?' for the essence
Determine whether this predicated part can be given as an answer to 'what is it?' (ma huwa) regarding the essence itself. For example, 'animal' answers 'what is it?' for 'human'.
3
Check if it answers 'what is it?' for a different species
See if the same predicated part also answers 'what is it?' for a different type (naw') that is distinct from the original essence. For example, 'animal' also answers 'what is it?' for 'horse'.
The different species must be distinct from the original essence.
4
Apply the criterion
If the predicated part satisfies both conditions (answers 'what is it?' for the essence and for a different species), then it is a genus. Otherwise, it is a differentia. For example, 'animal' is a genus; 'rational' does not answer 'what is it?' for any other species (it is exclusive to humans), so it is a differentia.
The passage adds that the differentia is not a part of all existents because some are simple.

Essential is genus if complete common factor; otherwise differentia.

This passage from al-Qustas presents the most concise formulation for distinguishing genus from differentia. It focuses on the essential (al-dhati) and asks whether it is the complete common factor (tamam al-mushtarak) between the essence and a disparate type. If yes, it is genus; if not, it is differentia. This criterion is powerful because it avoids the complexity of earlier formulations and provides a quick test.

Guided Path

Identify the essential (al-dhati)

Take the definition 'human is a rational animal'. The essential 'animal' is the complete common factor between human and other animal species like horse, so it is genus. The essential 'rational' is not shared by other species, so it is differentia.

Definition and Critique of 'Definiens' (al-Mu‘arraf)
Author states a definition of 'definiens', critiques it, and proposes a corrected definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in a definition of definiens and apply the criterion of exclusivity to assess whether a proposed definition is valid.

Core Idea
A true definition distinguishes; mere conceptual connection is insufficient.

Some later scholars defined 'definiens' (al-mu‘arraf) as 'that whose conception causes the conception of the thing'. At first glance this seems plausible, but the author shows it is too broad: necessary conditions like ceiling for wall or smoke for fire would qualify, yet they are not definitions. In this lesson we will dissect his critique and extract the corrected, precise definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposed definition
Locate the definition given by the later scholars: 'the definiens of a thing is that whose conception is a cause for the conception of the thing or for distinguishing it.'
2
Understand the counterexamples
The author presents ceiling for wall and smoke for fire. In both cases, conceiving the first naturally leads to conceiving the second (wall implies ceiling, fire implies smoke), but neither ceiling nor smoke is a definition of the other.
These are necessary conditions (mulzamat) for their consequents, not definitions.
3
Grasp the logical error
The earlier definition is too broad because it includes any conceptual cause-and-effect relationship, not just statements that uniquely identify a thing. A true definition must exclude everything else, not just be a conceptual trigger.
4
Extract the corrected definition
The author proposes: 'the definiens is the statement which indicates what distinguishes the thing from everything else.' This adds two crucial elements: it must be a statement (qawl) and it must indicate the distinctive feature (tamyiz).
5
Appreciate the methodological note
The author warns against changing established terminology without necessity; doing so is considered erroneous by serious scholars.

Essential predicates cause the subject's essence; concomitants do not.

In logic, not every predicate that is inseparable from its subject is essential. The condition 'biasababihi' (because of it) is crucial: it separates genuine essential predicates from necessary concomitants. This lesson examines that distinction and the classification of accidents.

The Hierarchy of Intelligibles: Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary
Explains the three-tier hierarchy of intelligibles

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intelligibles are ranked: essences, then judgments on essences, then judgments on judgments.

The author next explains the meaning of secondary intelligibles by laying out a three-level hierarchy. Primary intelligibles are essences conceived in themselves (e.g., the concept 'man'). When we attach judgments like 'universal' or 'essential' to those, we get secondary intelligibles. Judgments on secondary intelligibles produce tertiary intelligibles. Define tertiary intelligibles

Logic operates at the secondary and tertiary levels, not at the primary.

This refinement expands the scope of logic to include all cognitive content that can serve as a tool for attaining knowledge.

The Subject of Logic: Second Intelligibles
Definition of logic's subject matter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject matter of logic as second intelligibles (al-ma'qulat al-thaniya) and distinguish it from first intelligibles.

Core Idea
Logic studies the accidents of concepts and propositions, not the things themselves.

In this passage, the author establishes that logic investigates the 'states' or 'accidents' that apply to our conceptual and assertoric information when considered as means to knowledge. This is the standard view of the verified logicians (al-muhaqqiqun).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the subject problem
Every science has a subject (mawdu') whose accidental properties (awarid) it studies. Why must logic have a subject? Because otherwise it would not be a science.
The concept of a science's subject is crucial in Islamic epistemology.
2
Identify the verified view
The verified scholars hold that logic studies the states of propositions and syllogisms in themselves, i.e., the second intelligibles (al-ma'qulat al-thaniya) like 'subject', 'predicate', 'universal', etc.
These are not the first intelligibles (e.g., 'man', 'whiteness') but the attributes that apply to them in thought.
3
Contrast with first intelligibles
Logic does not study 'man' or 'horse' as such; it only considers them insofar as they are subjects or predicates in propositions. This keeps logic distinct from other sciences.
For example, in fiqh, 'wine' is studied for its ruling; in logic, 'wine' is just an example of a concept that can be universal or particular.

Refuting al-Khunji: Why the Subject Cannot Be Information Itself
Refutation of al-Khunji's broader subject view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Khunji's objection and the author's two criticisms, and evaluate their logical force.

Core Idea
Expanding the subject to include first intelligibles collapses logic into the object science.

Al-Khunji argued that because logicians discuss terms like 'universal', 'particular', 'subject', 'predicate', the subject must be broader. The author refutes this by showing these are part of the principles, not the subject, and that making the subject the information itself leads to a corruption.

Guided Path
1
State al-Khunji's claim
Al-Khunji says: The logician investigates universals, particulars, etc., so these are 'problems' (masa'il). Therefore, the subject must be more general than the second intelligibles to include them. He proposes taking the subject as the information itself.
This is a common move: when you see many topics, expand the subject to cover them all.
2
Understand the first criticism
The author responds: Al-Khunji's candidate terms are actually principles (mabadi') of logic, not part of its subject. Principles are presupposed by a science, not investigated within it. For example, 'universal' and 'particular' are foundational conceptual tools that logic uses, not something it studies.
This is a subtle but crucial distinction: the subject is what the science investigates its accidents; principles are used to do the investigation.
3
Understand the second criticism
If the subject were the information itself, then logic would be about that information directly, not its accidents. But every science studies the accidents of its subject. So logic would become the study of the content of knowledge, not the form, blurring the line between logic and other sciences.
This second criticism is a reductio ad absurdum: it would make logic coextensive with all knowledge, which is impossible.

The Two Demands: Conceptual and Assertoric, Proximate and Remote
Classification of logical tools into demands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify logical tools (e.g., explicative statement, argument, proposition) according to the type of demand they fulfill (conceptual/assertoric, proximate/remote).

Core Idea
Every logical operation either provides immediate knowledge (proximate) or serves as a building block (remote) for conceptual or assertoric demands.

After establishing the subject, the author provides a detailed classification of how information leads to demands: proximately (directly) or remotely (as preparatory tools). This organizes the entire discipline of logic into a coherent structure.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish two types of demand
A 'demand' (matlub) is what we aim to acquire through logic: either a concept (tasawwur) or an assent (tasdiq). For example, understanding the meaning of 'iman' is a conceptual demand; proving that iman is necessary for salvation is an assertoric demand.
This distinction is foundational: all logical tools ultimately serve one of these two ends.
2
Proximate vs remote
Proximate (qarib) means the immediate tool that directly satisfies the demand. For a conceptual demand, the proximate tool is an explicative statement (qawl sharh) — a definition that gives the meaning. For an assertoric demand, the proximate tool is an argument (hujja) — a proof that leads to certainty.
Remote (ba'id) tools are components that make the proximate tools possible.
3
Map examples from the passage
The author lists remote conceptual tools: universal, particular, essential, accidental, genus, differentia, property, broad accident. These are building blocks used in definitions. Remote assertoric tools: proposition, converse, contradictory, subject, predicate. These are building blocks for arguments.
Notice that 'proposition' is remote because it is a component of an argument; the argument itself is the proximate assertoric tool.

The Flaw of Defining by Synonymy
Author critiques a proposed definition of kalima via synonymy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw of defining an essence by listing individuals (ta'rif al-mahiyya bi-l-afrad) and explain why it leads to defining the obscure by the more obscure.

Core Idea
A definition must be clearer than the definiendum; defining by synonymy can violate this principle.

In this passage, the author examines a proposed correction to the definition of 'kalima' (word) by the author of al-Kashf. The correction introduces synonymy as a way to encompass non-Arabic words. However, the author identifies two interrelated logical problems: defining the essence by individual instances, and making the definition more obscure than the term being defined. This lesson will help you recognise these issues and why they invalidate the definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original definition being critiqued
The author of al-Kashf proposes: 'kalima is a simple utterance indicating a complete meaning with indication of time by its form and measure, or what is synonymous with this expression.' Pinpoint the addition of 'or what is synonymous' as the problematic element.

Understand the concept of 'definition of the essence by individuals'
The commentator says this definition defines the essence (mahiyya) by listing individuals (afrad). Instead of stating the essential properties (genus and differentia), it gives a list of examples (Arabic words) and then tries to extend to non-Arabic via synonymy.
A proper definition should capture the universal nature, not just instances.

Analyze the alternative interpretation: defining individuals
If we reinterpret it as a definition of individuals (not essence), the definition of a non-Arabic word becomes: 'a simple utterance synonymous with an Arabic word that indicates time by its form.' This requires prior knowledge of the Arabic word.

Detect the principle of clarity (ajla)
The author points out that knowledge of 'the non-Arabic utterance being synonymous with an Arabic kalima' is more obscure than simply knowing it is a kalima in its own language. Definition must be clearer (ajla) than the definiendum.
This is a fundamental rule: the definiens must be better known or more obvious than the definiendum.

Core Idea
Non-necessary attributes are separable from the essence.

Critique of al-Razi's Distinction and the Separation of Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author critiques al-Razi's definition and the separation of tasawwur and tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's critique of al-Razi's separation of necessity by predication and necessity by conjunction, and explain the deeper error of separating tasawwurat from tasdiqat.

Core Idea
Necessity by predication is inseparable from necessity by conjunction; the division of logic into concepts and assents causes many errors.

This passage critiques a distinction made by Imam al-Razi between two types of logical necessity: predication (haml) and conjunction (ittisal). The author argues that the two are not separate and that the very separation stems from a deeper and more harmful division: the Aristotelian separation of the study of concepts (tasawwurat) from propositions (tasdiqat).

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Razi's definition of the external necessary concomitant
Al-Razi defined it as 'what accompanies the quiddity and is not part of it.' This definition is under scrutiny.
The passage quotes from Mantiq al-Mulakhkhas p. 50.
2
Recognize the objection to al-Razi's definition
The objection is that if 'accompany' is taken literally, it includes separable attributes. If interpreted as permanent, it falsely includes accidental permanent co-occurrences (e.g., whenever man is rational, donkey brays).
Thus the definition is either too broad or too narrow.
3
Analyze the author's claim about the unity of the two necessities
The author asserts that necessity by predication must be explained by necessity by conjunction. For example, 'the rational necessitates laughter' means that if something is rational, it is laughing. Thus, predication is a specific case of conjunction.
This refutes al-Razi's distinction according to the author.
4
Connect to the deeper critique of separating tasawwur and tasdiq
The author traces the root error to Aristotle's separation of the study of conceptualizations (tasawwurat) from assents (tasdiqat). This division, though popular, leads to many logical mistakes.
This is a fundamental epistemological critique affecting how logic is structured.

The Logical Genus Does Not Constitute the Species
Author argues logical genus does not constitute natural or logical species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the logical genus does not constitute the natural species or the logical species, and distinguish between relative and real logical species.

Core Idea
Logical genus is a relation, not a constituent of species.

The author opens by asserting that the logical genus does not constitute any species—whether natural or logical. He distinguishes between natural species (essence) and logical species (relative and real), each with its own reason for not being constituted by the logical genus. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping the nature of logical relations in definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of species mentioned
Find in the Arabic: natural species (النوع الطبيعي), relative logical species (النوع المنطقي الإضافي), and real logical species (النوع المنطقي الحقيقي). Note how each is defined.
The first sentence lists all three.
2
Extract the argument for the natural species
The logical genus is a relation between the natural genus and the natural species; as a relation, it is posterior (متأخر) and therefore cannot constitute the species it relates.
Relations are ontologically dependent on their relata.
3
Extract the argument for the relative logical species
The relative logical species (e.g., genus) and the logical genus are correlatives (متضايفان); correlatives do not constitute each other.
Correlation implies mutual implication, not constitution.
4
Extract the argument for the real logical species
The real logical species (e.g., human considered as a species) can be conceived independently of the logical genus, so the genus is not a necessary part of it.
Conceivability indicates that the genus is not constitutive.

Section: On the constitution of the species by the genus. The logical genus does not constitute: the natural species, because it is a relation between it and the natural genus, so it is posterior to it. Nor the logical species: as for the relative, because of their correlation; and as for the real, because it is possible to conceive it without it. And the objection of 'part' and 'whole' does not apply, because what enters is the nature, and the relative is the description. And because not all correlatives are opposites; rather, some correlatives coexist in one thing from one aspect: like love and beloved, for one person can love himself; and like the seer and the seen, for one person can see himself; and like the striker and the struck, for one person can strike himself; and like the killer and the killed, for one person can kill himself. And because many opposites constitute each other, as in contradictories: man and non-man, and possession and privation: the seeing and the blind, for the positive is a constitutive part of the negative. Yes, it seems that correlatives do not constitute each other, and this is self-evident. It can be argued that correlatives are nothing but a composite of a relation and a particular quantifier applied to one of its two positions. For example, consider the relation of love: Lxy (i.e., x loves y). So the two correlatives 'lover' and 'beloved' are nothing but these two predicates: (something loves something) and (something is loved by something). And it is known that one is not a part of the other. Here there is an anticipated objection: that 'part' and 'whole' are correlatives and the former constitutes the latter; so the universal judgment that 'no correlative constitutes its correlative' is false. The author's answer is that these 'part' and 'whole' are two descriptions for two described things; the descriptions are correlative, but the described things constitute each other; so a correlative description does not constitute its correlative. Following the same example, consider the relation of part-hood: Pay (i.e., part). So the correlatives 'part' and 'whole' are nothing but these two predicates: (something is a part of something) and (something has a part). And it is known that one is not a part of the other.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and argumentative section on whether the logical genus constitutes the species. The author opens by stating that the logical genus does not constitute the natural species because it is a relation posterior to the natural genus. Nor does it constitute the logical species, dividing it into two types: relative and real. The relative logical species (like genus) does not constitute because of correlation; the real logical species does not because it can be conceived independently. The author then anticipates an objection: the case of 'part' and 'whole', which are correlatives and yet one (the part) seems to constitute the other. He responds by distinguishing the description from the described object: the descriptions 'part' and 'whole' are correlative, but the objects (like a whole and its part) are not correlatives in that sense; one object constitutes the other, not the description. He provides examples to show that correlatives can coincide in one individual (e.g., one loves oneself) and that opposites can constitute each other (e.g., man and non-man, where 'man' is part of the definition of 'non-man' as its negation). Finally, he offers a formal analysis: correlatives are relational predicates, and one is not a component of the other. The argument is strictly within the Islamic logical tradition, using examples like love, vision, striking, killing, and the logical relations of contradiction and privation.

Responding to the Objection: Part and Whole as Correlatives
Author rebuts objection that 'part' and 'whole' are correlatives constituting each other.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deconstruct the objection that 'part' and 'whole' are correlatives that constitute each other, using the author's distinction between description and described object.

Core Idea
Description and object are not the same: correlative descriptions do not constitute each other.

An objector raises 'part' and 'whole' as a counterexample: they are correlatives and yet the part seems to constitute the whole. The author refutes by distinguishing the descriptive terms from the objects they describe. He also provides examples of correlatives that coexist in one thing and of opposites that constitute each other, strengthening his case. This lesson hones your ability to respond to objections in logical debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the claim: 'part' and 'whole' are correlatives, and the part constitutes the whole. This challenges the principle that no correlative constitutes its correlate.
The objection is introduced with 'و لا يرد عليه «الجزء» و «الكل»' – the author anticipates it.
2
Analyze the author's response
The author distinguishes the descriptions 'part' and 'whole' from the objects that are part and whole. The descriptions are correlative, but the described objects are not; e.g., the description 'part' does not constitute the description 'whole', though the object part may be part of the object whole.
The key phrase: 'الداخل هو الطبيعة، والمضاف هو الوصف' – what enters is the nature, the relative is the description.
3
Apply the formal analysis
The author shows that correlatives are relational predicates: 'x is a part of y' and 'y has a part'. These predicates are not parts of each other, confirming that no description constitutes its correlate.
The example with Pay (part relation) illustrates the point.
