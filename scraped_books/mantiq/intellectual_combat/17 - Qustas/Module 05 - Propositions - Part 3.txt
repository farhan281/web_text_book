The Indefinite Proposition Equals the Particular
Author proves indefinite proposition equals particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an indefinite proposition and convert it to its equivalent particular proposition correctly.

Core Idea
An indefinite proposition always implies its particular, never its universal.

In Islamic logic, propositions without quantifiers (المهملة) are not ambiguous: they have a fixed logical force. This short but powerful rule ensures that when you encounter a statement like 'Man is mortal' without 'all' or 'some', you know it can be treated as 'Some men are mortal'. Understanding this prevents misinterpretation in syllogisms and definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the indefinite proposition
Look for a proposition that lacks a quantifier like 'all' (كل) or 'some' (بعض). It is a simple subject-predicate statement.
Indefinite propositions are common in everyday language and classical texts.
2
Note its quality
Determine whether the proposition is affirmative or negative (الكيف). The rule requires the particular to agree in quality.
Quality refers to affirmation or negation.
3
Apply the rule of equivalence
The indefinite proposition is equivalent to a particular proposition of the same quality. From 'A is B' (indefinite), you can infer 'Some A is B' (particular).
The rule is that the indefinite is in the strength of the particular.
4
Test the reasoning
The author's reasoning: the judgment is on the concept insofar as it is, which applies to particulars. If the indefinite is true, it is true either for all or for some; in both cases, the particular is true.
This reasoning ensures the inference holds regardless of whether the universal is actually true.
5
Verify with an Islamic example
For instance, 'Wine is forbidden' (خمر حرام) is indefinite; it implies 'Some wine is forbidden'. But note: the rule is about logical force in argumentation, not about actual exclusive application.
In Fiqh, such statements are often interpreted as general, but logically they only guarantee the particular.

Therefore, the proposition can be true either as a universal (if it holds for all individuals) or as a particular (if it holds for some). In either case, the corresponding particular proposition is true. For example, the indefinite statement 'Man is mortal' implies 'Some men are mortal'. The Indefinite Proposition: Equivalent to the Particular
Author concludes that the indefinite proposition is equivalent to the particular and rebuts objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the logical force of any indefinite proposition and justify why it is equivalent to a particular proposition, not a universal.

Core Idea
The indefinite proposition is in the strength of the particular, not the universal.

In logic, propositions without explicit quantifiers—called 'indefinite' (muhmala)—often appear in everyday language and in Islamic legal and theological texts. A common mistake is to treat them as if they were universal. This passage clarifies that their logical force is equivalent to a particular proposition that shares the same quality. Why? Because the indefinite is true either as a universal or as a particular, and in either case the particular is true. Let's explore the reasoning and the objection that arises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about the indefinite
The author states that the indefinite (muhmala) is in the strength (qūwa) of the particular (juz'iyya) that agrees in quality (kayf). Note: 'strength' here means logical force—what is entailed by the proposition.
2
Analyze the reasoning: true as universal or particular
The reasoning: an indefinite proposition like 'Man is an animal' is true either as a universal (if every man is an animal) or as a particular (if some men are animals). In both cases, the corresponding particular is true. Therefore, the indefinite is equivalent to the particular.
The indefinite does not distinguish between the two; its truth condition is that at least one instance holds.
3
Understand the subject: 'the concept as such'
The judgment in the indefinite is about 'the concept as such' (al-mafhum min haythu huwa)—the concept considered in itself, not as applying to every instance. This concept is found in the particulars, so the judgment applies to some but not necessarily all.
This notion originates with Ibn Sīnā and is key to the analysis.
4
Resolve the objection: why not universal?
An objector argues: if the judgment is about the concept as such, and the concept exists in all particulars, then the judgment would be universal. The author responds: the judgment is only 'in a general way' (fī al-jumla), not with permanence (dawām) and necessity (luzūm) covering every instance. Example: 'Man is actually a writer' does not entail that every man writes; it only says that some man is a writer, or perhaps it is true in a particular context.
The phrase 'in a general way' means that the indefinite is not quantified over all individuals.
5
Apply the refutation: the particular does not entail the indefinite
The author refutes the claim that the particular entails the indefinite. Example: 'Some animals are not participated in' (i.e., some animals are unique) is true, but 'Animal is not participated in' is false. Thus, the particular does not always imply the indefinite; the indefinite is weaker.
This shows that indefinite and particular are logically equivalent in strength but not identical in meaning.

Author debates whether indefinite proposition equals universal and refutes that particular implies indefinite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An indefinite proposition judges the concept in general, not necessarily every instance.

In this passage, the author engages in a typical Islamic logical debate (munazara) about whether an indefinite proposition (المهملة) carries the same force as a universal (الكلية). Using the objection-response format, he clarifies the precise logical boundaries of indefinite statements. Understanding this distinction prevents a common fallacy in arguments: assuming that a general statement without quantifier applies to all individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (فإن قلت)
The objector argues that an indefinite proposition is equivalent to a universal because its subject is the concept as such, which exists in all particulars. Locate the claim and its reasoning.

The author distinguishes between judging the concept 'in general' (في الجملة) and judging with permanence and necessity (الدوام واللزوم). Indefinite judgments are only about the concept as a whole, not about each and every instance.

3

The example 'Man is actually writing' (الإنسان كاتب بالفعل) illustrates that indefinite statements do not hold universally. It does not mean all men are writing at the moment; it describes the concept in general.
The author uses this to show that even if it applies in some form, that is additional to the indefinite judgment.

It is claimed that a particular proposition (e.g., 'Some animal is not shared in') implies the indefinite ('Animal is not shared in'). The author refutes this by noting that the particular has a specific consideration (باعتبار معين) that the indefinite lacks, so the implication fails.

Assertoric vs Qualified Propositions & the Existence Condition
Definition of assertoric and qualified propositions with the existence condition for affirmatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between assertoric and qualified propositions and explain why affirmative propositions require subject existence.

Core Idea
An affirmative proposition only makes sense if its subject actually exists.

In this lesson, we explore two types of categorical propositions—assertoric (muḥaṣṣala) and qualified (maʿdūla)—based on whether the predicate expresses an existent attribute or its negation. The author then establishes a critical epistemological principle: for an affirmative judgment to be valid, the subject must exist. This principle has far-reaching implications for how we verify statements in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the predicate type
Look at the predicate of the proposition: does it denote an existence (e.g., 'writing') or non-existence (e.g., 'non-writing')? This determines the classification.
An existential predicate affirms a real attribute; a non-existential predicate negates an attribute.
2
Classify the proposition
If the predicate is existential, the proposition is muḥaṣṣala (assertoric). If non-existential, it is maʿdūla (qualified). Each can be affirmative or negative, giving four total types.
The author uses the terms 'simple' (basīṭa) and 'variable' (mutaghayyira) as synonyms.
3
Understand the affirmation requirement
Affirmation is the judgment that the predicate subsists for the subject. This subsistence (wujūd) presupposes the subject's existence. Therefore, you cannot affirm a predicate of a non-existent subject.
This principle applies only to affirmative propositions, not negatives.
4
Apply the condition to examples
Test the principle: 'The phoenix is a bird' is impossible because the subject does not exist. 'The phoenix is not a bird' is allowed because it is a negative.
In Islamic sciences, this ensures we only affirm attributes of existing entities like God or the world.
5
Verify your understanding
Check a set of propositions from fiqh or kalam and determine whether the subject existence condition is satisfied for affirmatives.

Qualified and Unqualified Propositions and Subject Existence
Defines muhassala and ma'dula and existential import

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify categorical propositions as muhassala or ma'dula and determine the existential import conditions for affirmatives and negatives.

Core Idea
Affirmatives require subject existence; negatives can be true even of non-existents.

This passage introduces two types of categorical propositions based on whether the predicate is positive or privative. It then tackles a critical logical issue: does the subject need to exist for a proposition to be meaningful? The answer differs for affirmatives and negatives, and Imam al-Razi raises a subtle doubt that forces a precise distinction in existential import.

Guided Path
1
Identify the predicate's nature
Look at the predicate term: if it is wujudi (positive, e.g., 'seeing'), the proposition is muhassala; if it is 'adami (privative, e.g., 'not-seeing'), it is ma'dula. The classification depends solely on the predicate, not the copula.
Note: ma'dula is sometimes called 'qualified' because the predicate is qualified by negation.
2

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Modification and quality together determine whether two propositions contradict or merely oppose.

In the previous lesson, we distinguished between modified (ma'dula) and simple (muhassala) propositions. Now we see how these interact with affirmation and negation to produce logical relations. Understanding these relations is crucial for valid inference, especially in jadal and fiqh reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dimensions
The passage uses two axes: modification (al-'udul wa al-tahsil) and quality (al-kayf). Modification can be simple (positive predicate) or modified (negated predicate). Quality can be affirmative or negative.

2
Case 1: Agreement in modification, difference in quality
When two propositions have the same modification (both simple or both modified) but opposite quality (one affirmative, one negative), they are contradictories (tanaqud) provided all other conditions of contradiction are met (same subject, predicate, time, etc.).

For affirmatives, they are contraries (cannot both be true); for negatives, they are subcontraries (cannot both be false). This holds if the subject is the same and the predicates are contradictory.
Example: 'Zayd is seeing' (simple affirmative) and 'Zayd is non-seeing' (modified affirmative) cannot both be true.
4

For contradiction, recall the conditions: same subject, predicate, time, place, relation, etc. For mutual opposition, the condition is same subject and contradictory predicates. Ensure these are satisfied before determining the relation.

Two dimensions—modification and quality—determine opposition relations.

In this passage, the author systematizes the relations between propositions by considering two factors: whether the predicate is modified (al-'udul) or simple (al-tahsil), and whether the proposition is positive or negative. Understanding these relations is key to immediate inference in logic. Let's break down the four cases and see how they map onto the square of opposition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two dimensions: 'udul/tahsil and quality
Al-'udul (modification) uses a predicate like 'not-writing' instead of 'writing' (tahsil, simple). Quality (kayf) refers to affirmation or negation. Each proposition can be characterized by these two binary dimensions.

This case is less common but completes the classification.

Square of Opposition for Modified and Simple Propositions
Presents the logical square for personal, universal, and particular modified/simple propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four logical relationships (contrariety, subcontrariety, contradiction, subalternation) in the square of opposition for personal, universal, and particular propositions, and explain how these relationships shift based on quantity.

Core Idea
The square of opposition changes with proposition quantity: personal uses contradiction, universal uses contrariety, particular uses subcontrariety.

The square of opposition is a powerful visual tool that maps the logical relationships between propositions. This passage from the Muqaddimat al-Qustas presents the square for modified (ma'dula) and simple (muhassala) propositions, showing how the relationships of contrariety (tadādd), subcontrariety (taḥt al-taḍādd), contradiction (tanāquḍ), and subalternation (tadākhul) apply differently for personal, universal, and particular propositions. Mastering this tool is essential for analyzing arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four corners of the square for personal propositions
Look at the square for 'Zayd is a writer' and its negations. The top horizontal pair is 'Zayd is a writer' and 'Zayd is not a writer' — they are contraries. The bottom pair is 'Zayd is not not-a-writer' and 'Zayd is not a writer' — they are subcontraries. The diagonals are contradictions. The verticals are subalternation (entailment).
This is the base square; all other variations derive from it.
2
Note how quantity changes the diagonal relationship
For personal propositions, the diagonal is contradiction. For universal propositions (e.g., 'Every human is a writer' and 'No human is a writer'), the same line becomes contrariety. For particular propositions (e.g., 'Some human is a writer' and 'Some human is not a writer'), the diagonal becomes subcontrariety.
This shift is crucial: the logical force of the diagonal depends on whether the subject is individual, all, or some.
3
Apply the square to universal propositions
Examine the universal square: 'Every human is a writer' is a universal affirmative; 'Every human is not a writer' is a universal negative (equivalent to 'No human is a writer'). They are contraries — cannot both be true. The subalternation goes downward: from 'Every human is a writer' to 'Some human is a writer', and from 'Every human is not a writer' to 'Some human is not a writer'.
The universal square replaces contradiction with contrariety on the top horizontal.
4
Apply the square to particular propositions
For particular propositions: 'Some human is a writer' and 'Some human is not a writer' are subcontraries — they cannot both be false but can both be true. The subalternation goes upward: from 'Some human is a writer' to 'Every human is a writer' would be invalid; but from the universal to the particular is valid (the opposite direction is not entailed).
The particular square uses subcontrariety on the bottom horizontal.
5
Recognize the overall pattern

The author argues that modality is inseparable (luzūm) from every proposition because every proposition has a 'matter' (mādda) — the ontological status of the relation: necessity, possibility, or impossibility.

The Two Proportions: Material and Judgmental
Author defines material and judgmental proportions and their associated qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-nisba al-madiyya (material proportion) and al-nisba al-hukmiyya (judgmental proportion), and identify the associated qualities (madda and jiha).

Core Idea
A proposition has an objective reality and a mental judgment; they may differ.

Every proposition contains a relation between subject and predicate. But this relation exists in two realms: the actual state of affairs in reality, and the belief held in the mind. The Arabic logicians distinguished these as the 'material proportion' and the 'judgmental proportion'. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating arguments, because a mismatch between reality and belief can lead to error or sophistry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proportions
In the passage, locate the definitions of al-nisba al-madiyya (the relation in reality of occurrence or non-occurrence) and al-nisba al-hukmiyya (what the intellect believes about that relation). Note that the first is objective, the second is subjective.

This mismatch is the basis for fallacies of modality.

These correspond roughly to necessary, permanent, contingent, and impossible.

Define the quality of the judgmental proportion: jiha
The judgmental proportion has a quality in the intellect – e.g., necessary, permanent, possible. This is called 'modality' (جهة) or 'type' (نوع), whether expressed or understood. Note that it can contradict the actual matter.

The Four Meanings of al-Imkan (Possibility)
Author defines four meanings of possibility: general, special, more special, and future.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of possibility (al-imkan) and identify which type is used in modal claims from Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Possibility is not one concept; logicians distinguish four levels from common to philosophical usage.

In Islamic logic, the concept of 'possible' (al-mumkin) has multiple shades of meaning depending on context. The author here introduces four key types: general possibility used by the common people, specific possibility used by philosophers, a more restrictive version, and a future-oriented one. Understanding these distinctions is essential for accurately evaluating modal propositions in fields like kalam (theology) and usul al-fiqh (legal theory).

Guided Path
1
Identify the common genus
All four types of possibility are defined in relation to necessity (al-darura). Each type negates some kind of necessity. Start by recognizing that possibility is always a negation of necessity.
The genus is 'negation of necessity' (salb al-darura).
2
Analyze general possibility (al-imkan al-'ammi)
This is the broadest type: it negates absolute necessity from the side opposite to the judgment. For example, if the judgment is 'the world exists', then general possibility says 'it is not impossible that it exists' (i.e., non-existence is not necessary). It is the common meaning: possible = not impossible.
Used by the common people (al-jumhur).
3
Analyze specific possibility (al-imkan al-khassi)
This negates necessity from both sides: neither existence nor non-existence is necessary. This is the technical philosophical meaning. For example, a contingent being like a human: it is possible that it exists (since it came into being) and possible that it does not exist (since it perishes).
Used by philosophers (al-hukama').
4

The Two Kinds of Necessity in Modal Propositions
Division of necessity into two kinds with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential necessity (darurat al-dhat) and necessity for the sake of the essence (darurat li-ajl al-dhat) and identify correct examples of each from Islamic discourse.

Core Idea
Necessity attaches either through the subject's essence or through an inseparable attribute.

Modal propositions express not just truth but the mode of truth—whether a statement is necessary, possible, or impossible. In this passage, the author distinguishes two types of necessity: one rooted in the very essence of the subject, and another arising from a permanent attribute. Understanding this distinction is critical for evaluating theological and legal arguments that rely on modal claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and predicate
Look at a modal proposition like 'Every actual writer is necessarily not illiterate.' Separate the subject (writer) from the predicate (illiterate) and note the modality (necessarily).
2
Determine the source of necessity
Ask: Does the necessity come from the subject's essence (e.g., being a writer inherently means not being illiterate) or from an attribute that may not be permanent (e.g., the act of writing)?
3
Test for perpetuity
If the proposition would be false when the subject loses the attribute (e.g., if the writer becomes illiterate after forgetting), then the necessity is not essential but for the sake of the essence.
4
Classify the proposition
Based on your test, classify as essential necessity (darurat al-dhat) if the subject can never lack the predicate, or necessity for the sake of the essence (darurat li-ajl al-dhat) if the necessity depends on a permanent attribute.

The Two Kinds of Possibility and Occurrence Possibility
Division of possibility and introduction of occurrence possibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate essential possibility (al-imkan al-dhati) from occurrence possibility (al-imkan al-wuqu'i) and explain why occurrence possibility ensures that assuming the actual does not lead to impossibility.

Core Idea
Occurrence possibility is a stricter kind of possibility that prevents contradictions.

Just as necessity has two kinds, possibility—as its negation—also divides into two: a general possibility that merely negates essential necessity, and a specific possibility that negates all necessity. The author coins the term 'occurrence possibility' for the latter, which is crucial for the logical principle that 'what is possible does not entail impossibility when assumed to occur.' This distinction appears to be original to Qustas and later adopted by al-Jurjani.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of possibility
Possibility is defined as the negation of necessity. Since necessity has two types, possibility must also have two types corresponding to each.
2
Identify the two types of possibility
Essential possibility (al-imkan al-dhati) means the opposite is not necessary by essence, though it may be necessary by another. Occurrence possibility (al-imkan al-wuqu'i) means the opposite is not necessary at all, neither by essence nor by another.
3
Test with an example
Consider 'Every Iranian could have been born in Iraq.' This is occurrence possibility because the opposite (being born in Iran) is not necessary by any cause. Contrast with 'A writer could be illiterate' which is only essential possibility because writing necessitates literacy by attribute.
4
Apply the principle of possibility and impossibility
If we use essential possibility, the principle 'the possible does not entail impossibility from assuming its occurrence' can fail (as with the writer example). With occurrence possibility, the principle holds: if assuming the actual does not lead to contradiction, then it is truly possible.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of modal propositions, specifically the division of necessity (darura) and possibility (imkan). The author begins by noting that propositions may be considered perpetual with the perpetuity of the essence, regardless of whether the source of necessity is the essence itself or an external attribute. For example, 'Every actual writer is not illiterate' is necessarily true, but the necessity attaches because of the attribute of writing, not the writer's essence. The discussion then divides general possibility into two types: 'for the sake of the essence' (li-ajl al-dhat) and 'at the time of the essence' (hin al-dhat). Since possibility is defined as the negation of necessity, and necessity is divided into two kinds, possibility also has two kinds, each paired with a corresponding necessity. The first kind of necessity is more specific than the second, so the possibility paired with the second necessity is more specific than that paired with the first. The author then critiques two example propositions, showing that they are false if taken as perpetual or necessary, because every writer was once illiterate. However, they can be true as general conditioned propositions. Better examples are provided, such as 'Every Isfahani is Iranian necessarily as long as he exists', because the descriptions are inseparable. Formal notation is given (though garbled in the OCR) to distinguish the two kinds of necessity and the two kinds of possibility. The passage then argues for the priority of the second kind of necessity and possibility. If we use the first kind of essential necessity, the inference that possibility does not entail impossibility fails, as shown by the writer example. But with the second kind, the inference holds. Finally, the author introduces the terms 'occurrence possibility' (al-imkan al-wuqu'i) and 'essential possibility' (al-imkan al-dhati), citing al-Sharif al-Jurjani's definitions. Essential possibility means the contrary is not necessary by essence, but may be necessary by another. Occurrence possibility (also preparatory possibility) means the contrary is not necessary by essence or by another, so assuming the actual side does not lead to impossibility. The author notes a disagreement: in his view, occurrence possibility corresponds to the first kind of essential necessity, while the author of Qustas holds a different view.
