Ghazali's Classification of Signification and the Abandonment of Implicative Signification
Ghazali defines implicative signification and explains why it is abandoned.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conformity, implication, and entailment in conventional signification, and explain why implicative signification is abandoned.

Core Idea
Implicative signification is abandoned because its entailments are infinite and uncontrollable.

Al-Ghazali explains that conventional signification (al-dalala al-wad'iyya) is either direct (conformity) or indirect (implication and entailment). However, implicative signification (dalala iltizamiyya) is considered abandoned (mahjura) because the entailments of any term are unlimited, leading to an infinite number of possible meanings. This makes it impractical for precise communication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two branches of conventional signification
Conventional signification is either direct (ibtida') – called conformity (mutabaqa) – or indirect (bi-wasita) – which includes implication (tadhammun) and entailment (iltizam). Understand that the first gives the core meaning, the latter gives associated meanings.
Implication is when the meaning is part of the definition; entailment is an external necessary accompaniment.
2
Define implicative signification (dalala iltizamiyya)
Implicative signification is the meaning that necessarily accompanies a word due to a self-evident entailment, but is not part of the definition. For example, the word 'ceiling' entails 'wall' because a ceiling cannot exist without walls.
3
Analyze al-Ghazali's example of infinite entailments
Al-Ghazali says: 'the ceiling entails the wall, and the wall entails the pillars (?), and the pillars entail the ground.' This chain shows that a single word like 'ceiling' would have an infinite number of significations if all entailments were considered.
The Arabic word 'الأمس' may be a scribal error; the intended meaning is a chain of physical dependencies.
4
Explain why implicative signification is abandoned
Because the entailments are not limited (ghayr munhasira), a single word would have infinite meanings, making it impossible to fix a specific intended meaning. Therefore, in linguistic practice, only the conventional signification (as per the speaker's intent) is considered, not all logical consequences.
This does not deny that entailments exist; it only denies that they are always intended in communication.

He gives an example: the ceiling entails the wall, the wall entails something else (possibly pillars), and that entails the ground. Thus, a single word would have infinite significations if all implicative relations were considered, making it impractical. Al-Ghazali's point is to argue that only conventional signification (both direct and indirect) as defined by the speaker's intent should be considered, not every logical consequence.

Knowledge is either mere conception or judgment-laden assent.

Distinguishing Knowledge by a Facet from Full Knowledge
Author argues that knowing that something exists does not require knowing its specific identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between knowledge by a facet (al-'ilm bi-wajh) and full knowledge of a thing, and explain why knowing existence does not entail knowing essence.

Core Idea
Knowing that something exists tells you nothing about what it is.

This passage from al-Qustas clarifies a key epistemological distinction: existence is a universal attribute, and knowing that a thing exists is not the same as knowing its specific identity. The commentary emphasizes that we may know something only under a broad description without grasping its particular essence. This distinction is crucial for avoiding confusion in definitions and arguments across Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Find the main thesis: 'The existence of a thing in the external world does not necessitate knowledge of it.' This is the core epistemological point.
2
Understand the objection
The Sharh al-Qustas adds that we do not accept that every existent is known by us as existent or as a thing, because there may be existents our minds have never considered. This shows the limitation of human knowledge.
3
Grasp the key distinction
The Lawami' al-Asrar clarifies: knowledge of the attribute 'existence' (mawjudiyya) or 'thingness' (shay'iyya) is knowledge by a facet (bi wajh), not full knowledge of the thing's essence. This distinction is the logical heart of the passage.
4
Apply the distinction
Recognize that when you know something only as 'existent' or 'a thing', you lack knowledge of its specific nature. This prevents conflating universal attributes with particular identities in reasoning.

Core Idea
Seeking the unknown is possible through known accidents, not impossible.

This answer simply points out that we often have partial knowledge.

The second answer refines: the sought is known in one way (via an accident) and unknown in another (its quiddity). The accident serves as a bridge. Identify the accident mentioned for 'angel' and 'jinn'.

Definition of Knowledge and Its Division into Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author states the two definitions of knowledge and the dispute between muta'akhkhirin and qudama'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two definitions of 'ilm (tasawwur and tasdiq) and identify the points of disagreement between the later scholars and the ancients.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either conception without judgment or assent with judgment, but the ancients equated assent with judgment itself.

This passage introduces the foundational division of knowledge ('ilm) into two types: tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). It presents the common definition and then reveals a significant dispute among scholars about the nature of assent. Understanding this division is crucial for grasping the scope and purpose of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of knowledge
The passage states that knowledge is either tasawwur or tasdiq. Note the condition: tasawwur is apprehension without judgment, tasdiq is with judgment. This is the first formulation.
2
Compare the two formulations
The author offers two similar expressions: one using 'without judgment' (بدون الحكم) and another using 'without considering judgment' (بدون اعتبار الحكم). Recognize they are equivalent definitions.
3
Locate the dissenting view
Some later scholars (muta'akhkhirin) differ. The ancients (qudama') and verifiers among the later scholars hold that tasdiq is merely the judgment (hukm) itself, and tasawwur is the form (surah) explained for it, not a part of the judgment.
This reverses the relationship: in the common view, tasawwur is without judgment; in the ancients' view, tasawwur is separate and tasdiq is just the judgment.
4
Analyze the objection
The passage raises an objection against the common definition: if judgment is considered part of tasawwur, then definitions (hudud) would not be knowledge because they include what is not known; also, a single instance of tasdiq would not count as a single piece of knowledge. This objection challenges the coherence of including judgment in conception.
The objection uses the example of 'hudud' (definitions) to illustrate the problem.
5
Summarize the core disagreement
The key dispute is whether tasdiq includes the entire proposition (apprehension + judgment) or is just the judgment itself, and whether tasawwur is completely separate from judgment or can include it. The ancients and verifiers take the view that tasdiq is only the judgment.

Priority of Conceptualization over Assent and Refutation of the Absolutely Unknown
Author argues for priority of conceptualization and refutes a shubha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that conceptualization precedes assent and refute the objection about the absolutely unknown by identifying its self-defeating nature.

Core Idea
Conceptualization must precede assent, but a minimal grasp suffices; the absolutely unknown cannot be judged.

This passage establishes a foundational epistemological principle: before you can affirm or deny anything, you must have some concept of it. The author then defends this principle against a clever objection that seems to prove it false by pointing to judgments about the unknown. Understanding this argument sharpens your ability to recognize the necessary conditions for valid judgment in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The author asserts that the explicative statement (qawl sharh) that produces conceptualization must precede the argument (hujja) that produces assent. This is because conceptualization naturally comes before assent.
The priority is both by convention (wad'an) and by nature (tab'an).
2
Understand the justification
Every act of assent (tasdiq) is preceded by three conceptualizations: the subject, the predicate, and the relation between them. Without these, judgment is impossible.
This is a necessary condition, not just a psychological observation.
3
Note the qualification
The required conceptualization need only be minimal (bi wajh ma). The example of a body occupying space shows we can judge without knowing its essence.
Full essential knowledge is not required for valid judgment.
4
Reconstruct the objection
The objector argues: If judgment requires even minimal conceptualization, then the statement 'Every absolutely unknown is impossible to judge' would be true. But we do make judgments about things, so the claim seems false.
The objector implicitly denies the consequent (the claim about the absolutely unknown).
5
Analyze the author's response
The author points out that the consequent is actually true: if something is absolutely unknown, it cannot be judged. The objection is self-defeating because if the thing were absolutely unknown, the judgment would be impossible; the fact that a judgment is made shows it was not absolutely unknown.
The objector's attempt at modus tollens fails because the consequent is not false.

Three Types of Conventional Indication: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, and Iltizam
Classifies conventional verbal indication into three types: conformity, containment, and implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam, and explain the condition added by al-Razi to the definition of tadammun.

Core Idea
Words convey meaning through whole, part, or necessary concomitant; al-Razi refines tadammun to exclude homonymy.

In Islamic logic and its allied sciences, understanding how words indicate meanings is foundational. This lesson unpacks the three types of conventional indication—mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam—as defined by Ibn Sina and refined by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. The key nuance is the condition 'min haythu hiya kadhalik' that protects tadammun from confusion with equivocal words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The passage defines divisions of conventional verbal indication (al-dalala al-wad'iyya). Note that the topic is restricted to conventional (wad'i) indication, not natural or rational.
2
Extract the three types and their definitions
Mutabaqa: indication of the entire meaning for which the word was posited. Tadammun: indication of a part of that meaning. Iltizam: indication of something external that necessarily accompanies the meaning.
Memorize these definitions as they are the core of the division.
3
Understand al-Razi's condition for tadammun
The phrase 'min haythu hiya kadhalik' (inasmuch as it is such) is added to exclude equivocal words (mushtarak) that might indicate both a whole and its part by coincidence of homonymy, not by the conventional relation.
This condition ensures tadammun is a genuine logical property, not a linguistic accident.
4
Recognize the historical attribution
The division is attributed to Ibn Sina (al-Shaykh) as an innovation in logic, cited from his al-Shifa'. Al-Razi's refinement appears in his Mantiq al-Mulakhkhas.
This shows the dynamic development of logical concepts.

Evaluating Arguments for Excluding Implicative Signification
Critically examines the claim of exclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the arguments for excluding implicative signification in scientific discourse and identify the logical flaw in each.

Core Idea
Exclusion hinges on variability of clarity, not on rationality or infinitude.

Some logicians argued that implicative signification (دلالة الالتزام) should be excluded from scientific discourse. This passage presents and refutes two common reasons—that it is non-conventional and that it leads to infinite meanings—then gives the real reason: clarity varies between persons. Finally, a counterargument is offered. This lesson dissects the logical structure of each move, showing how critical analysis refines our understanding of signification in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Main Claim
Locate the central assertion: 'Implicative signification is excluded in the sciences.' This is the target of the subsequent analysis.
The Arabic phrase is 'محجورية دلالة الالتزام في العلوم'.
2
Analyze the First Refuted Argument
The first argument claims that implicative signification is excluded because it is rational, not conventional. The refutation: if that were the reason, then inclusion (تضمن) would also be excluded, yet it is accepted. This shows the argument is inconsistent.
The refutation uses a reductio: the same reasoning would exclude a widely accepted type of signification.
3
Analyze the Second Refuted Argument
The second argument claims that because necessary concomitants are infinite, a word would have infinite meanings. The refutation: only clear (بينة) concomitants are considered, and they are finite.
The term 'بينة' refers to obvious, widely evident concomitants, not obscure ones.
4
Examine the Real Reason Given
The passage states the actual reason: if a concomitant is not clear, it fails to convey meaning; but if it is clear, clarity varies between persons, making the meaning unregulated.
This is an epistemological problem: what is clear to one may not be to another.
5
Evaluate the Counterargument
The counterargument proposes considering only what is clear for everyone, like one correlative implying the other (e.g., 'father' implies 'son'). This would avoid variability.
This suggests a possible solution but does not resolve the debate; the passage leaves it open.

The question of whether concomitant signification (dalalat al-iltizam) is restricted in scientific discourse is a classic dialectical exercise in Islamic logic. Identify the main claim

Author presents Ibn Sina's division of knowledge, Razi's denial of summary knowledge, and a defense.

Core Idea
Summary knowledge is a holistic grasp, not ignorance of parts.

This passage presents a foundational epistemological debate. Ibn Sina introduces two types of knowledge: detailed (tafsili), where every part is explicitly known, and summary (ijmali), where the whole is known without analyzing its parts. Al-Razi objects that if parts are known, they must be distinguished in the mind. The author defends Ibn Sina by clarifying that knowledge is the occurrence of the form of a thing in the mind, not the essence itself, thus avoiding the regress objection.

Al-Razi's objection rests on the principle that one must conceive parts before the whole.

The author rejects al-Razi's objection, not because of the infinite regress argument (knowledge of distinction requires further distinction), but because knowledge is defined as 'the occurrence of the form of a thing in the mind, not the occurrence of its essence.' The Regress Problem in Entailment of Conception
Debate on entailment and regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the regress objection against the entailment of conception and evaluate the proposed solution distinguishing real and intellectual regress.

Core Idea
Infinite regress is only vicious in real existence; in mental concepts, it is harmless.

In this passage, the author examines a plausible claim: knowing a subject automatically gives knowledge of its necessary attributes. But this leads to a troubling regress: if knowing the subject gives knowledge of its attribute, then knowing that attribute should give knowledge of its own attribute, and so on. The debate explores whether such a regress is acceptable and introduces key distinctions between real and intellectual matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The claim is that conception of the subject (malzum) necessitates conception of its proximate necessary attribute (lazim qarib).
The reasoning is that the subject's essence is a cause for the attribute, and knowledge of the cause yields knowledge of the effect.
2
Understand the regress objection
If the claim were true, then from the conception of the proximate attribute, we would also necessarily conceive its own necessary attribute, leading to an infinite chain. Consequently, no attribution would ever be unknown, which is clearly false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: the conclusion contradicts observable ignorance.
3
Analyze Imam Razi's doubt
Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi argues that the entailment relation itself is distinct from the subject and attribute. If this relation also entails a further relation, regress; if not, the subject could be conceived without the attribute.
This sharpens the problem: the relational nature of entailment seems to require either regress or separation.
4
Examine the defense using i'tibari matters
The defender answers that such a regress is not impossible in intellectual (i'tibari) matters, as illustrated by the example of 'one' entailing 'half of two' and 'third of three' ad infinitum.
In mental constructs, infinite chains are acceptable because they have no external reality.
5
Evaluate the final resolution
A further objection arises: if the entailment is real, a real regress would occur. The conclusive response is that the entailment of entailment is identical to the original entailment, just as the existence of existence is identical to existence itself.
This principle halts the regress without needing a middle term, appealing to self-evidence.

The Necessity of General Possibility
Author proves necessity of general possibility and addresses objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio proof that general possibility is necessary and explain why its necessity does not transfer to the possible object.

Core Idea
General possibility is necessary, but that does not make the possible thing necessary.

In this passage from Qustas al-Afkar, the author presents a concise modal proof that the concept of general possibility (al-imkan al-'amm) is itself necessary. He then anticipates a common confusion: if possibility is necessary, wouldn't that imply that everything possible is also necessary? The resolution lies in a key distinction—whether possibility is an existential attribute (sifa wujudiyya) or not. This lesson walks through the logical steps and the crucial nuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of the proof
The author aims to prove that 'general possibility'—the broad sense of possibility that includes everything not impossible or necessary—is necessary in itself. Notice he uses the phrase 'imkan al-'amm' and later 'wajib li-dhatihi'.
General possibility is not a contingent property but a conceptual necessity.
2
Trace the reductio argument
Assume the opposite: that general possibility is not necessary. Then it must be either necessary or possible (a dichotomy). In either case, it falls under the scope of general possibility. Now suppose general possibility did not exist—its non-existence would still be possible, leading to its existence, a contradiction. Therefore, the assumption is false, so it is necessary.
This is a standard reductio: assuming non-existence leads to absurdity.
3
Distinguish the objection
One might object: if possibility is necessary, doesn't that make everything that is possible necessary? The author flags this as a potential fallacy.
The objection would collapse the modal categories: possible would become necessary.
4
Analyze the author's resolution
The author clarifies that the necessity of the attribute (general possibility) does not imply the necessity of its subject (the possible thing). It would only follow if the attribute were 'existential' (sifa wujudiyya)—a real property inhering in the subject. But general possibility is not such an attribute; it is a conceptual or negative notion (absence of necessity/impossibility).
The distinction between existential and non-existential attributes is critical in modal metaphysics.
5
Apply to Islamic theology
In kalam, this argument prevents a problematic necessitarianism: the concept of possibility is necessary for the contingency argument, but it does not force the world to be necessary. The world remains contingent even though the concept of possibility is necessary.
This supports the orthodox Ash'ari position that the world is created and contingent.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
According to the passage, why does the necessity of general possibility not imply that its subject is necessary?
A) Because the subject is always contingent.
B) Because possibility is not an existential attribute.
C) Because the subject is defined as necessary.
D) Because the argument only applies to general possibility.

Key Takeaway
General possibility is necessary by a reductio argument, but its necessity does not transfer to its subject unless it is an existential attribute.
