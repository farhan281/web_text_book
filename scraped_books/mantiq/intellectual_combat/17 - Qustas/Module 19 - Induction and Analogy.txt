The Definition and Types of Induction
Author defines induction and distinguishes complete from incomplete induction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete induction from incomplete induction and identify the epistemic status of each.

Core Idea
Complete induction gives certainty; incomplete induction only gives probability.

This passage introduces induction as a path to knowledge in Islamic logic. The author clarifies that the strength of an inductive conclusion depends on whether all particulars are enumerated. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic sciences that move from specific cases to general rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
Read the first sentence: Induction is establishing a universal judgment based on the judgment being established in the particulars. This is the core definition.
Induction moves from specific instances to a general rule.
2
Recognize the condition for certainty
Look for the phrase 'if the enumeration of particulars is known'. When all particulars are counted, the induction is complete and yields certainty.
This is called 'complete induction' or 'divided syllogism'.
3
Understand the consequence of incomplete enumeration
Read the second condition: 'otherwise', meaning if not all particulars are enumerated, the induction yields only probability (zann). The reason is that unobserved cases could differ.
This is the typical induction used in most real-world arguments.
4
Note the alternative terminology
Complete induction is also called 'al-qiyas al-muqassam' (the divided syllogism). Recognize this term for later study.
This term is used by Ibn Sina and later logicians.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') as a method of reasoning that moves from particulars to a universal. The author divides induction into two types. (1) Complete induction (istiqra' tamm): when all particulars are enumerated and known, the universal judgment is certain. This is also called 'the divided syllogism' (al-qiyas al-muqassam), because it effectively partitions the universal into all its species. For example, to prove 'Every act of worship is either obligatory, recommended, prohibited, disliked, or permissible', one would examine each category exhaustively. Since the enumeration is complete, the conclusion is certain. (2) Incomplete induction: when not all particulars are examined, the conclusion is only probable, because the unobserved cases might differ from the observed ones. For instance, inferring 'All jurists are proficient in Arabic grammar' from a sample of jurists is merely probable, as some jurists may lack this proficiency. The author thus clarifies the epistemic distinction between certain and probable induction, a crucial point for dialectical and demonstrative reasoning.

Analogy: Definition and Conditions for Certainty
Definition and conditions of analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of an analogy (root, branch, cause) and list the four conditions required for it to yield certainty.

Core Idea
Analogy only yields certainty if the cause is established, the branch is capable, conditions are met, and obstacles absent.

In this passage, the author introduces the sixth logical path: analogy (al-tamthīl). He breaks it down into its essential parts and then lists the stringent conditions that must hold for analogy to produce certainty. This is a foundational tool in Islamic jurisprudence (uṣūl al-fiqh) and dialectic (jadal), but the author warns that true certainty from analogy is extremely difficult to achieve.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of analogy
Read the Arabic definition: 'إلحاق شيء بشبيه في حكم ثابت له'. Note the three key elements: the thing (branch), the similar (root), and the ruling already established for the root.
The word 'شبيه' emphasizes similarity.
2
Extract the three components: root, branch, unifier
The passage names the two cases: الأصل (root) and الفرع (branch). The shared attribute is called الجامع (unifier) or العلة (cause). Understand that the analogy transfers the ruling from root to branch based on this common attribute.
In fiqh, the unifier is often called 'illah.
3
Analyze the example provided
The author gives an example: likening the sky to a house in being originated (حادث) because both are composed and shaped. This shows a concrete application: the root is house, the branch is sky, the ruling is contingency/huduth, and the unifier is being composed and shaped.
This example is from kalām, not fiqh.
4
List the four conditions for certainty
The passage lists: (1) ثبوت علية المشترك – establishing that the shared attribute is the cause, (2) قابلية الفرع – the branch must be susceptible to the ruling, (3) اجتماع الشرائط – all conditions must be met, (4) ارتفاع الموانع – obstacles must be removed. Recognize that these are prerequisites for certainty, not for a weak analogy.
The author says establishing these premises is very difficult.
5
Assess the author's conclusion
The author states that fulfilling all conditions is 'عسير جداً' (very difficult). This indicates that analogy rarely yields certainty in practice, though it can be a useful probabilistic tool.
This underscores the rigor expected in logical demonstration.

Commentary
This passage defines analogy (التمثيل) as a logical method: joining a thing (the branch/فرع) to something similar (the root/أصل) in a ruling that is already established for the root. The aspect of similarity is called the 'unifier' (جامع) or 'cause' (علة). The author gives the example of likening the sky to a house in being originated (حادث), because the sky is composed and shaped like a house. The passage then lists four conditions for analogy to yield certainty: (1) establishing that the shared attribute is the cause (علية المشترك), (2) confirming that the branch is capable of receiving the ruling (قابلية الفرع), (3) meeting all relevant conditions (اجتماع الشرائط), and (4) absence of obstacles (ارتفاع الموانع). The author concludes that establishing these premises is very difficult (عسير جداً), implying that analogy rarely produces certainty. In Islamic jurisprudence, analogy (قياس) is a central method, but the conditions here mirror those in uṣūl al-fiqh for valid qiyās: the cause must be effective, the branch must share the cause, etc.

Distinguishing Apparent and Actual Infinite Regress
Author explains when infinite regress is merely apparent and when it is real but not impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between apparent and actual infinite regress in Islamic theological arguments, and identify when a regress is harmless.

Core Idea
Not every infinite regress is fallacious — some are merely apparent, some real but not impossible.

In Islamic theology (kalam), the charge of infinite regress (tasalsul) is often used to refute arguments. However, as the author explains, not every chain that appears regressive is actually so. Some regresses are only apparent due to self-referential concepts, while others, like the chain of effects from the side of the effect, are real but not logically impossible. This lesson will equip you to analyze such regress claims critically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about regress
What exactly is being claimed to regress? Is it a causal chain or a conceptual dependency?
2
Analyze the existence-in-a-locus example
The example shows that if existence in a locus were an accident, it would require another existence. The solution is that existence exists by itself. Notice how the apparent regress is resolved by recognizing a self-grounding concept.
3
Recognize other self-referential concepts
The author lists analogous pairs: existence of existence, necessity of necessity, etc. These are not genuine regresses because the second term adds nothing new.
4
Understand the mathematical analogy
One is half of two, half of two is a third of three, etc. This is a conceptual relation, not a real regress.
5
Distinguish actual but harmless regress
The author notes that regress from the side of the effect (e.g., an infinite series of effects without a first cause) is not impossible. In Islamic theology, this relates to the possibility of an eternal world.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of precautions, now addressing infinite regress (tasalsul). The first point is that many apparent infinite regresses are not actual. The example given is about the existence of an accident in a substrate. If we say that existence in a substrate is itself an existential accident, then it would require another existence, leading to regress. The solution is that existence exists by itself, not through another existence. Similarly, phrases like 'the existence of existence' or 'the necessity of necessity' might seem to regress, but they are reflexive or self-referential concepts that do not entail a chain. A mathematical example is the fractional relations of numbers: one is half of two, half of two is a third of three, etc., which appears to regress but is simply a conceptual relation. The second point is that actual infinite regress can occur and is not impossible, specifically when it is from the side of the effect (i.e., a causal series of effects without beginning). In Islamic theology, this is relevant to arguments about the eternity of the world or the chain of created things.
