Distinguishing Hissat al-Naw' from Khas sat al-Naw'
Editor corrects Pahlawan's misreading of 'hissat al-naw'' as 'khas sat al-naw'' in causal statement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'hissat al-naw'' (specific portion) and 'khas sat al-naw'' (specific property) and explain why confusing them undermines a causal argument in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A single misread term can collapse an entire causal argument in Islamic logic.

This lesson examines a critical refinement by an editor who caught a misreading that changed the meaning of a logical claim. Understanding the difference between 'hissat al-naw'' (specific portion of the genus) and 'khas sat al-naw'' (specific property) is essential for evaluating causal relations between genus and species. The editor's correction restores the precise terminology needed for the argument to hold.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two terms
In the Arabic, identify 'حصة النوع' and 'خاصة النوع'. Note the similarity in script: ح vs خ. These are easily confused in manuscripts.
The editor found Pahlawan's edition had 'خاصة' instead of 'حصة'.
2
Define each term
'Hissat al-naw'' is the specific portion of the genus that constitutes the species itself. 'Khas sat al-naw'' is a property peculiar to the species but not its essence. The former is about existence, the latter about accident.
In later texts, 'hissat al-naw' is sometimes used interchangeably with 'naw'' (species) but here it is a precise technical term.
3
Examine the causal claim
The passage says the differentia, in relation to the specific portion, is a cause of its existence. If we read 'specific property' instead, the claim becomes about an accident, not the essence, altering the entailment.
The editor argues that with the wrong term, the logical entailment does not follow.
4
Understand the editor's intervention
The editor restores 'حصة النوع' to preserve the correct causal relationship. This correction is crucial for the logical argument about genus and species.
Without this correction, the argument would be invalid.

Understanding Indication by Correspondence for Composite Words
Editor's correction on composite word correspondence indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the condition for a composite word to indicate its meaning by correspondence and identify whether a given example fulfills it.

Core Idea
Correspondence indication requires the parts of the word to match the parts of the meaning exactly.

In this lesson, we examine a key concept in Islamic logic and uṣūl al-fiqh: how composite words indicate their meanings by correspondence. The passage clarifies that for a phrase to indicate its meaning via muṭābaqah, there must be a direct correspondence between its parts and the parts of the intended meaning. Understanding this distinction helps you analyze definitions and legal terms with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find the term 'دلالة المطابقة للفظ المركب' in the passage. The passage defines this concept: the indication of a composite word by correspondence.
The term appears at the beginning of the passage.
2
Extract the condition
The passage states that for correspondence indication, 'the parts of the word correspond to the parts of the meaning.' This means each part of the phrase points to a corresponding part of the intended meaning.
The condition is explicit: 'تطابق أجزاء اللفظ أجزاء المعنى'.
3
Distinguish from other indications
Contrast this with indication by inclusion (دلالة التضمن), where the whole word indicates a part of the meaning, and implication (دلالة الالتزام), where the word indicates something external. In muṭābaqah, the whole indicates the whole with part-to-part match.
The commentary in the lesson mentions these distinctions.
4
Test with an example from Islamic law
Take the definition of 'sale' (bayʿ) in Islamic law: it is a transaction involving offer (ījāb) and acceptance (qabūl). The word 'bayʿ' as a composite indicates the whole transaction, and its essential parts (offer and acceptance) correspond to the essential parts of the meaning. Verify if the parts match.
This example is from the commentary. Another example: 'human is a rational animal'.

The author clarifies that for a composite word to indicate by correspondence, both the word and its meaning must be composed of parts that match each other exactly. For example, the phrase 'a human is a rational animal' has parts ('human', 'rational animal') that correspond to the parts of the meaning (species and differentia). This is contrasted with indication by inclusion (tḍamun) or implication (iltizam).

Navigating Classical Texts: The Role of Section Headings
Editor discusses the challenges and benefits of adding section headings to classical texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to critically evaluate the structural divisions and added headings in modern editions of classical Islamic texts and distinguish between the original author's organization and editorial interventions.

Core Idea
Classical texts are seamless wholes; modern headings impose subjective structure.

When you open a modern edition of a classical Islamic work, you often see chapter headings, section breaks, and even paragraph divisions that were not present in the original manuscript. How do we know what the author actually intended? This lesson examines the editorial dilemma of adding section headings, equipping you with the awareness to navigate editions critically and use them effectively without being misled by artificial divisions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the original structure of classical texts
Understand that pre-modern Islamic works were typically written as continuous prose with minimal division markers, often only main chapter breaks (فصول) and sections (أبواب). Subtopic headings were absent.
This unity reflects the interconnectedness of arguments in traditional scholarship.
2
Identify editorial additions in a modern edition
Compare a selected passage from a classical text (e.g., al-Ghazālī's Iḥyā') with its modern edition. Note any added headings, paragraph breaks, or numbering that are not present in the manuscript tradition.
Use critical editions that mark editorial interventions.
3
Evaluate the subjective nature of editorial divisions
Consider the editor's criteria: are the divisions based on clear textual signals or the editor's own judgment? Reflect on the editor's warning that different editors will produce different schemes.
Ask yourself: could the original text be read coherently without these divisions?
4
Assess the trade-off between fidelity and accessibility
Weigh the benefit for general readers (finding content quickly) against the potential distortion of the author's intended flow. A scholarly reader can ignore the headings, but headings may subtly influence interpretation.

Section headings: In the writings of the predecessors, the whole book was no more than one paragraph, and apart from the main chapter divisions, no headings for subtopics were considered. In modern editing of these writings, not only are chapters and sections separated, but also many modern editors separate paragraphs according to their own taste, even though they have no clear and specific criteria in this regard. You will obtain the same result when you perform the work with a single editor at different times. The Implication of Future Possibility: A Contradiction
Detailed argument against conditioning future possibility on present non-existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio argument that shows conditioning future possibility on present non-existence leads to a contradiction.

Core Idea
The symmetry of specific possibility forces a future possible to be both non-existent and existent, a contradiction.

The second part of the marginal note presents an argument attributed to Ibn Sina (via al-Razi) against the view that future possibility requires present non-existence. Using the concept of specific possibility (imkan khas), the argument shows that such a condition leads to a logical contradiction. This refines our understanding of possibility in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition under scrutiny
The condition is: 'For X to be possibly existent in the future, X must not be existent in the present.' This is what the proponents of future possibility stipulated.
2
Recognize the symmetry of specific possibility
In 'imkan khas', the possibly existent and the possibly non-existent are identical: whatever is possibly existent is also possibly non-existent, and vice versa. This is because specific possibility means both existence and non-existence are equally possible.
This is a crucial property of the concept.
3
Apply the condition to the possibly non-existent
If the condition applies to the possibly existent, then by symmetry it must also apply to the possibly non-existent: 'If Y is possibly non-existent in the future, then Y must not be non-existent in the present.'
4
Derive the consequence
If something is not non-existent in the present, then it is existent in the present. So the possibly non-existent (which is the same as the possibly existent) must be existent in the present. Thus, the possibly existent is required to be existent in the present.
This contradicts the original condition that it must not be existent in the present.
5
Conclude the contradiction
The original condition leads to the opposite of what it intended. Therefore, the condition is false. Ibn Sina rejects it.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Understanding Manuscript Sigla in Islamic Editions
List of manuscript sigla used in the edition of Qistas al-Afkar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify manuscript sigla commonly used in critical editions of Islamic texts and locate the libraries that hold the manuscripts.

Core Idea
Manuscript sigla are the keys to tracing a text's transmission.

This lesson introduces you to the manuscript abbreviations used in the edition of Qistas al-Afkar. These sigla appear in the apparatus and critical notes of scholarly editions. Knowing what each abbreviation stands for helps you understand the textual basis of the edition and assess its reliability.

Guided Path
1
Locate the list of sigla
Find the list in the front matter of the edition. Typically, it appears as an appendix or in the introduction. In our source, it is Appendix III titled 'List of the Abbreviations of the manuscripts of Qistas al-afkar'.
2
Identify each manuscript entry
Each entry gives a library name and a manuscript number. For example, 'Avasofia library, no. 2565' refers to a manuscript in the Ayasofya collection. Note that the OCR may misread names (e.g., 'Avasofia' should be 'Ayasofya').
3
Understand the order and grouping
The list is divided into two parts: one on the page shown (7 manuscripts: Ayasofya, Atif Efendi, Astan Quds Razavi, Pehlivan's edition) and a continuation on the next page (Sobat, Salim Agha, Al-Zahiriyya, Rampur). The editor also mentions a wish to have accessed more manuscripts.
4
Apply to reading the edition
When you encounter a siglum like 'Ava' or 'Atif' in the footnotes, refer back to this list to know which manuscript is being cited. This lets you evaluate the weight of variant readings.
