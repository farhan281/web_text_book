The Mustanad Rule in Jadal: Answering Denial While the Proof Stands
Dialectical puzzle on the immunity of the mustanad and the problem of answering denial.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rule 'al-mustanad la yujab 'anhu wa la yubhath 'alayh' and analyze the dilemma it creates for responding to a denial in dialectical debate.

Core Idea
The mustanad is immune to attack, yet denial remains possible — how do we resolve this?

In Islamic dialectic (jadal), certain proof texts (mustanad) are considered authoritative and off-limits for direct challenge. Yet a key move in debate is denial (manʿ) of a premise or connection. This passage presents a puzzle: if the mustanad cannot be questioned, how can one answer a denial while the mustanad remains standing? Let's explore this tension and its implications for sound argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Locate the statement of the verifiers: 'al-mustanad la yujab 'anhu wa la yubhath 'alayh'. What does this rule prohibit?
The rule prohibits both answering (jawaab) and questioning (bahth) the mustanad itself.
2
Understand the dilemma
Read the perplexed group's question: If refuting the mustanad is useless and questioning it is forbidden, how can one respond to a denial (manʿ) while the mustanad still stands? What is the logical conflict?
The dilemma arises because a denial typically attacks a part of the proof, but the mustanad as a whole cannot be touched.
3
Formulate the puzzle in your own words
Restate the problem: In a debate, the defender presents a mustanad. The opponent wants to deny a premise. But according to the rule, the mustanad is not open to answer or scrutiny. So how can the opponent's denial be addressed without violating the rule?
This is a classic problem in jadal theory that requires careful distinction between attacking the proof text itself and attacking the inference drawn from it.
4
Propose a resolution (hypothetical)
Think about possible ways out. Could the denial target something other than the mustanad itself? For example, could it challenge the applicability of the mustanad to the case at hand, or the connection (sabr) between the mustanad and the claim?
Later commentators discuss solutions such as distinguishing between dafʿ al-mustanad (refuting the proof) and dafʿ al-istidlāl (refuting the inference).

Responding to the Doubt: Seeking the Unknown from a Known Aspect
Author provides three answers to the doubt that one cannot seek what is completely unknown.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the three dialectical responses to the doubt that one cannot seek the completely unknown.

Core Idea
Partial knowledge enables inquiry: the unknown is sought through its known properties.

The doubt of Manun challenges the possibility of seeking something entirely unknown. This passage presents three dialectical responses, leveraging the distinction between essence and accident. You will learn how Islamic logicians defended the possibility of inquiry (talab) into realities like angels and jinn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt (shubha)
Find the core objection: that one cannot seek what is completely unknown. The passage presumes this doubt and then refutes it.
The doubt is attributed to Manun, a figure in Islamic logical tradition.
2
Extract the first response (Qustas al-Afkar)
The first answer states that what is known from one aspect can be directed towards. The example: seeking the essence of angels and jinn.
This establishes the principle of 'al-ma'lum min wajh' (known from one aspect).
3
Analyze the second response (Sharh al-Qustas)
The sought is known from one aspect but not another. The objector claims the request cannot turn towards the unknown aspect. The response rejects this: the unknown aspect may be the essence itself, while the known aspect is one of its properties (khawass). Through knowledge of that accident ('arid), one seeks the essence. Examples: angels as heavenly, worshipping, good; jinn as capable of transformations.
Note the epistemological role of accidents as bridges to essence.
4
Understand the third response (Lawami' al-Asrar)
The impossibility only applies if the unknown aspect is absolutely unknown from every angle. Here, the unknown aspect is known through the accident that accompanies it. So seeking it is possible.
This deepens the logical point by specifying the condition of absolute ignorance.
5
Compare the three responses
Notice the progression: (1) states the principle, (2) refines it with the essence-accident distinction, (3) clarifies the condition limiting the objection.
All three reinforce that partial knowledge suffices for inquiry.

Defending the Division of Knowledge: Dialectical Refutation of Two Objections
Author establishes necessity of both necessary and theoretical sciences, responds to two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical structure of an argument defending the division of knowledge into necessary and theoretical, by identifying the objections and the author's responses.

Core Idea
The proof that not all knowledge is acquired refutes itself if it were acquired—so it must be necessary.

The author begins by asserting a fundamental epistemic distinction: knowledge is either necessary (self-evident) or theoretical (requiring proof). He then anticipates two powerful objections that threaten this division. The first objection argues that the very proof against all knowledge being acquired would itself be acquired, making it self-defeating. The second challenges the definitions of 'necessary' and the possibility of infinite regress. In this lesson, we will dissect these objections and the author's clever responses, which ultimately ground the distinction in self-evident truths and a pluralistic account of non-acquired knowledge sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's main claim
Locate the initial statement: 'Not all from each is necessary... nor all theoretical.' What is the author trying to prove? He asserts that knowledge is mixed: some is necessary, some theoretical.
The claim avoids extremes: neither pure necessity nor pure acquisition.
2
Paraphrase the first objection
The objector says: 'If all knowledge were acquired, your proof that all-acquired leads to circularity/regress would itself be acquired. How can you use an acquired proof to refute the possibility of all-acquired?' Understand the self-referential problem.
The objection attacks the epistemic status of the proof itself.
3
Paraphrase the second objection
The objector offers two interpretations of 'necessary'. If it means 'self-evident', then a regress could terminate in sensory or empirical knowledge, so no circularity. If it means any kind of necessary, then 'all necessary' does not imply knowing everything because knowledge can depend on sensation, etc.
The objector is trying to show the author's dichotomy is not exclusive.
4
Analyze the response to the first objection
The author argues that the proposition 'not all is acquired' is known in itself (self-evident). If all were acquired, then either this self-evident proposition is unknown (contradiction) or known (which undermines the claim that all is acquired). Hence the assumption that all is acquired is false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: the assumption leads to a contradiction regardless.
5
Analyze the response to the second objection
The author counters: if all knowledge were necessary (in any sense), then acquisition would be unnecessary—but we clearly do acquire knowledge through effort, so the doubt is repelled. He then lists non-acquired sources (intellect, sense, experience, recurrent report, intuition, etc.), showing that some knowledge is indeed not acquired, breaking the regress.
The list provides a concrete foundation for necessary knowledge.

Commentary
This passage addresses the existence of necessary and theoretical sciences and responds to two objections. The author first claims that not all knowledge is necessary (otherwise we would know nothing) nor all theoretical (otherwise we would know nothing), due to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). If not known, that contradicts reality (since it is true), so the assumption fails; if known, the assumption also fails because it admits something known without acquisition. So the assumption is false. For the second, if all were necessary, we would not need acquisition, so the doubt is repelled. He concludes by listing sources of knowledge that are not acquired: intellect, sense, experience, recurrent report, intuition, common knowledge, convention, opinion, error—showing that some knowledge is acquired and some not.

The First Doubt: Can an Acquired Premise Refute Universal Acquiredness?
First doubt: Can an acquired premise refute universal acquiredness?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the first doubt and its refutation, and identify the dialectical move of claiming the premise is known in actuality.

Core Idea
A dialectical refutation must be grounded in necessary knowledge.

The first doubt challenges the very basis of the argument that not everything is acquired. If all knowledge is acquired, then the premise used to prove that would itself be acquired, making the argument self-defeating. The author's response is to assert that this premise is known in actuality (fi nafs al-amr), i.e., it is necessary. This lesson dissects this exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claimant's Position
What is the author trying to prove? The author is arguing that not all knowledge is acquired; some is necessary. The opponent challenges this.
2
Understand the First Doubt
The opponent says: 'On the assumption that all knowledge is acquired, your own statement that "if all were acquired, circularity or regress would follow" would itself be acquired. So how can you use it as a proof?'
This is a meta-argument about the status of the premise.
3
Analyse the Author's Answer
The author replies that this proposition (the conditional) is known in actuality. It is not a matter of acquisition; it is a necessary truth. Therefore, even under the assumption of universal acquiredness, this premise remains necessary and is not undercut.
The key phrase is 'معلومة في نفس الأمر' (known in actuality).
4
Assess the Dialectical Outcome
The objection fails because it relies on the premise being acquired, but the author claims it is necessary. The author shows that in all scenarios, the assumption (that all is acquired) is negated.
This is a classic dialectical move: establishing a premise as necessary to avoid regress.

The Logical Doubt About Man's Composition
Dialectical doubt about man's composition resolved via attachment theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the logical structure of a dialectical doubt (shubha) by identifying its two horns and evaluating the proposed resolution.

Core Idea
A doubt about composition reveals hidden assumptions about predication and substance.

This passage presents a classic doubt about the definition of man. Is man a real composite of body and soul, or a conceptual composite of genus and differentia? The author shows that both options lead to logical problems. Understanding this dialectic sharpens your ability to critique definitions and recognize how theologians resolve such tensions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
The passage presents two possible interpretations of 'man': as 'the animal that is rational' (real composite) or as 'the composite of animal and rational' (conceptual composite). Write them down as a disjunction.
2
Recognize the contradiction in the first horn
If man is the animal that is rational, then animal is described as rational. But rationality is a property of a separate substance (the soul). This creates a contradiction (khalaf).
The term 'khalaf' here means a logical impossibility.
3
Understand the predication problem in the second horn
If man is a composite of animal and rational, then the parts cannot be predicated of the composite univocally. That is, the composite is not truly animal or rational in the same sense as the parts.
Univocal predication means the same term applies in the same manner; here it fails.
4
Examine the proposed resolution
The author suggests that animal becomes rational due to the attachment of the soul. Thus rational is not predicated of animal alone but of the composite. This resolves the first horn.
This resolution is not explicitly found in the philosophers' statements but is close to the truth according to the author.
5
Evaluate the resolution
Consider whether the resolution fully addresses both horns. The second horn's problem of univocal predication remains open. Discuss whether the attachment theory makes the composite a real or conceptual composite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge of the conclusion is caused by the aggregate of premises, not by thought itself.

This passage is the author's response to a skeptical doubt about how knowledge of the conclusion is produced. Responding to the Objection of Impossible Premise
Authors present and answer an objection that the excepted premise might be impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute the man' objection that the excepted premise in a conditional syllogism is impossible.

Core Idea
An impossible premise objection is irrelevant because the syllogism presupposes the premise's truth.

In dialectical logic, a common objection against conditional syllogisms is that the excepted premise might be impossible, thereby breaking the conditional implication. The author of the Qustas shows that this objection is harmless: the user of the syllogism only needs the premise to be actually true or accepted by the opponent. This lesson unpacks that defense and trains you to apply it in disputation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (man')
Read the first part of the passage: the objectors say that the excepted part (either the antecedent or the contradictory of the consequent) might be impossible (mumtani'), and that the implication (mulazamah) may then not hold since impossible things can imply impossible things. Mark the logical structure of the objection.
The objection targets the validity of the conditional syllogism in general.
2
Extract the author's response strategy
The author argues that the syllogism's purpose is to prove the consequent or negate the antecedent. This requires the premise to be true either in reality or according to the opponent. If the premise is truly impossible, then it is not actually true or accepted, so the syllogism does not apply. Thus the objection is irrelevant.
The response relies on the distinction between metaphysical possibility and the practical condition of truth/acceptance.
3
Apply the response to a fresh example
Consider the conditional: 'If this liquid is wine, then it is forbidden. This liquid is wine. Therefore it is forbidden.' The objector says: 'But it might be impossible for this to be wine (e.g., it could be grape juice).' Apply the author's response: the syllogism is used only when the premise 'this is wine' is actually true or accepted by the opponent; if it is impossible, the syllogism is not being used.
This shows the response works even when the premise is factually false, as long as it is accepted for the sake of argument.
4
Evaluate the strength of the defense
The defense is dialectically strong because it shifts the burden: the objector must show that the premise is not just possibly impossible but actually impossible in the context. If the premise is accepted by the opponent, the objection fails. If it is not accepted, there is no syllogism to begin with.
This is a standard move in jadal: a man' that challenges a necessary condition of the argument is neutralized by showing that condition is satisfied.

Author prescribes pre-argument clarification and verification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the steps of tahrir al-mabahith and taqrir al-aqwal to prepare for a theological debate, and detect misattribution errors.

Core Idea
Clarify the issue and record positions before arguing, or your proof will miss the target.

Before any proof can be presented, the dialectician must first do the groundwork: precisely delineating the points of dispute and correctly recording each party's position. This passage from Qustas outlines these prerequisites. In this lesson, you will learn how to apply these steps in a real debate, ensuring that your argument addresses the actual disagreement, not a straw man.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the opponent
Determine what the arguer (mu'allil) is defending and who the specific opponent (sa'il) is. This sets the stage for the debate.
2
Perform tahrir al-mabahith
Delimit the exact topics under dispute. List the points that are agreed and those that are contested. This clarifies the 'surah al-niza'' (form of the dispute).
3
Perform taqrir al-aqwal
Record the precise statements and doctrines of both parties. Verify them with the opponent to ensure accuracy.
The opponent (sa'il) has the right to request verification.
4
Check for misattribution
Ensure you are attributing to the opponent their actual position, not someone else's. If the opponent objects, correct it immediately.
Misattribution can lead to using irrelevant premises, causing confusion (khabat).
5
Present your dalil
Only after the above steps, present your argument. You can now be confident that the dispute is correctly framed and your premises are accepted by the actual opponent.

Types of Objections: Munaqada vs Ghasb
Author classifies objections into three types and explains the first two as legitimate 'munaqada' and the third as prohibited 'ghasb'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any objection in a dialectical exchange as permissible munaqada (simple or with mustanad) or prohibited ghasb, based on the stage of argument and content.

Core Idea
The objector's role is limited: only reject or provide a basis; anything else usurps the arguer's position.

In Islamic dialectic (jadal), the objector (sa'il) and arguer (mu'allil) follow strict rules to ensure productive debate. This passage from al-Qustas defines three types of objections to an arguer's evidence. The first two are permissible forms of opposition (munaqada), while the third is prohibited usurpation (ghasb) because it attacks the arguer's role itself. Understanding this classification is essential for anyone engaged in structured theological or legal debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the stage of objection
Does the objection occur before or after the arguer completes his evidence? The passage focuses on objections before completion, which target a single premise.
Objections after completion have different rules not covered here.
2
Determine the content of the objection
Is it mere rejection (simple 'We do not accept') or does it include additional material? If it includes additional material, does that material provide a basis (mustanad) or something else (e.g., a counter-argument)?
A mustanad can be a question about permissibility, necessity, or applicability.
3
Classify the objection as munaqada or ghasb
If the objection is simple rejection or rejection with a mustanad, it is permissible munaqada. If it includes anything else—especially a counter-argument or alternative causation—it is prohibited ghasb.
The text states ghasb is 'غير مسموع عند المحققين' (not admissible by investigators).
4
Apply the rule to a real debate scenario
Consider a fiqh debate: The arguer claims 'Nabidh is forbidden because it is intoxicating, like wine.' The objector may simply deny the premise ('We do not accept that nabidh is intoxicating') or provide a basis ('Why is it not permissible that the intoxicant from dates differs?'). These are allowed. If the objector instead argues 'Nabidh is not intoxicating because it is made from dates, not grapes,' that would be ghasb, as it introduces a new argument during the arguer's presentation.
Such ghasb leads to confusion and prolongs the debate, as the arguer's role is usurped.

Classification of Objections in Dialectical Debate
Author classifies types of objections in dialectical debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five types of objections (manāqaḍa, ghaṣb, naqḍ ijālī, muʿāraḍa, qalb) and determine which are permissible and which are not based on the rules of debate.

Core Idea
In debate, valid objections are limited to acceptance or denial with a basis; usurping the causal role or baseless denial is invalid.

In formal dialectical debate (al-baḥth wa al-munāẓara), not every response to an opponent's argument is allowed. This passage from the Qusṭās lays out a precise taxonomy of objections, distinguishing between legitimate moves that advance the debate and illegitimate ones that lead to confusion. Understanding this classification equips you to engage in structured theological and jurisprudential argumentation without violating its rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three major sections of objections
The author states there are three sections: the first two together are called 'contradiction' (manāqaḍa), and the third is 'usurpation' (ghaṣb). Note that the first two are permissible, while the third is disallowed.
Manāqaḍa here covers both simple denial and denial with a basis.
2
Understand why ghaṣb is prohibited
Ghaṣb usurps the role of the one giving a cause (muʿallil) by engaging in causation instead of just accepting or denying. It leads to confusion, prolongs debate, and prevents reaching the goal. The passage says it is 'not heard by the verifiers'.
The objector may only accept or deny; proposing new causal arguments is the role of the opponent.
3
Define al-mustanad (the basis)
The basis is the ground on which a denial rests. Examples include questioning possibility ('why is it not permissible?'), necessity ('we do not accept the necessary consequence'), or explanation ('how is this while the situation is such?'). This is the proper way to deny before the proof is complete.
If the denial is baseless, it is mere stubbornness and deserves no response.
4
Distinguish between denial before and after the proof
If denial occurs after the opponent has completed his proof, there are further branches: if the objector does not accept the proof, it is either based on a counter-instance (naqḍ ijālī) or baseless obstinacy. If he accepts the proof but denies the conclusion, it leads to counterargument (muʿāraḍa) if he brings another proof, otherwise it is also obstinacy.
The passage emphasizes that a total refutation (naqḍ ijālī) objects to the proof as a whole by citing a case where the judgment does not apply.
5
Map the subtypes of counterargument
Counterargument (muʿāraḍa) is when the objector brings a different proof that contradicts the conclusion. If his proof is identical in form to the opponent's, it is called reversal (qalb); if similar, 'counterargument by the like'; otherwise, 'counterargument by the other'.
Qalb only occurs when using general proofs (al-adilla al-ʿāmma).

Learning Objective

After learning the types of objections, the author now explains how debates proceed. The proponent starts with a proof; the objector may attack it. Core Idea
The questioner attacks either a single premise or the whole proof, each with a distinct term.

In dialectical debate, the questioner (sa'il) does not just object randomly. He has three precise methods: opposing a premise (mu'arada), summarily refuting (naqd ijmal), and contradicting the entire proof (munakida). This lesson unpacks each of these tools and teaches you to identify them in a real debate.

The Respondent's Defense and Recursive Proofs in Dialectic
Defines the respondent's defense and the recursive nature of proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the respondent must defend a blocked premise and how subsequent proofs are subject to the same objection categories.

Core Idea
The respondent's defense is recursive: each new proof is itself a syllogism open to the same three objections.

When the questioner blocks a premise, the respondent cannot ignore it. He must repel the objection either by providing another proof or by a reminder. And if he brings a second proof, that proof also consists of premises, and the questioner's same three tools apply again—and so on for any further proofs. This recursive structure ensures thoroughness and prevents shallow debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the respondent's initial situation
The respondent presents a proof with premises. The questioner blocks one premise (man').
This is the starting point of the defense.
2
Note the two defense options
The respondent can either bring a new proof (dalil) to support the blocked premise or give a reminder (tanbih) to clarify it.
A reminder is a non-syllogistic clarification.
3
Recognize that a new proof is a syllogism
If he brings a new proof, it must be composed of at least two premises, forming a valid syllogism.
This is standard for qiyas.
4
Understand the recursive application
The questioner can then apply the same three categories (mu'arada, naqd, munakida) to any premise or the whole of the new proof.
The text says: 'فالأقسام المذكورة... بعينها تأتي فيه'.
5
Extend the recursion indefinitely
This process repeats for any third, fourth, etc. proofs, making the dialectic layered and thorough.
No limit is stated; it continues as long as the respondent brings new proofs.

Harmful vs. Harmless Objections and Useful vs. Useless Answers
Defines two types of objection and two types of answer in dialectic.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not every objection hurts your case; some objections actually confirm your conclusion.

In dialectical debate (jadal), the respondent must evaluate whether an objection truly threatens his argument or is harmless. Similarly, his answer must effectively address the objection. This lesson presents the classical classification of objections and answers from the logical tradition, equipping you to assess the strategic value of each move in a debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's effect
First, determine whether the objection undermines the respondent's conclusion or not. If it forces the respondent to abandon his claim, it is harmful. If the conclusion still stands regardless, it is harmless.
The text says the harmful type is self-evident; focus on the harmless subtypes.
2
Recognize harmless objection subtype 1
Check whether the non-existence of the objected premise would actually support the respondent's claim. If the conclusion follows whether the premise exists or not, the objection is harmless.
For example, if the claim is that a ruling applies whether a condition is present or absent, objecting to the condition does nothing.
3
Recognize harmless objection subtype 2
Check whether the answer to the objection was already stated earlier in the argument. If the respondent's initial reasoning already addresses the objection, then the objection is harmless because it adds nothing new.
The text gives the example: respondent says 'X is not a cause because it is posterior'; questioner objects 'but could it be a condition?' – but the respondent already said it is posterior, which also precludes it being a condition.
4
Classify the answer as beneficial or not
An answer is beneficial if it confirms the premise that was objected to. If the answer fails to do that, it is non-beneficial.
The text simply states this dichotomy; applying it requires checking whether the answer actually addresses the specific objection.

Answering an Objection with a Basis (Using Tardid)
Author explains the method of tardid for answering objections with a basis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tardid method to answer an objection that has a basis in a munazara exchange.

Core Idea
Refuting the basis does not remove the objection; you must disjunct the basis with its alternatives.

In a debate, an opponent may support an objection with a proof (mustanad). Directly refuting that proof may seem effective, but the author explains why it fails. Instead, the respondent should use tardid—presenting a disjunction between the basis and its divisions. This lesson walks through that method and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario
Identify when the objection is not a bare denial but is supported by a basis (mustanad). For example, the questioner says 'The premise is false because the Qur'an says X.' Here the basis is the specific interpretation of the verse.
2
Understand why direct refutation fails
The basis is a malzum (necessary condition) for the falsehood of the premise. Negating the basis does not negate the lazim (consequent). So refuting the basis leaves the objection intact; the premise may still be false for another reason.
The author quotes the verifiers: 'The basis is not answered nor is it investigated upon.'
3
Employ tardid: disjunct the basis with its divisions
The respondent should present a disjunction between the basis and its divisions (qasim). For instance, 'Either the verse means X or it means Y, or it is abrogated, etc.' Then, for each alternative, the respondent explains how the original claim remains valid.
The basis must have at least one division (its negation or counterpart).
4
Assess the respondent's ability
There are three cases: (1) Cannot enumerate divisions → answer impossible; (2) Can enumerate but cannot explain each case → answer impossible; (3) Can enumerate and explain → answer is complete. The author notes that case (1) reduces to (2) because everything can be enumerated into two or more divisions (e.g., between the basis and its negation).
5
Apply the principle
In a real debate, first check if you can enumerate all possible divisions of the basis. If you can, then ensure you can explain how your claim holds in each division. If both conditions are met, your answer is dialectically complete.

The Problem of Denial with Basis (Man' ma'a al-Mustanad)
Author explains why refuting the basis fails and proposes tardid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why refuting the mustanad does not eliminate the man' and outline the basic tardid method.

Core Idea
Refuting the basis is futile; tardid tests all alternatives.

When an opponent denies a premise and provides a reason (mustanad), a novice responder might try to refute that reason. This lesson shows why that fails and introduces the correct method of tardid, which systematically examines all possible alternatives to the basis.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish man' i and mustanad
In a debate, man' is the opponent's act of denying a premise (saying 'لا نسلم'). Mustanad is the reason he gives for that denial. Understand that they are logically distinct.
The mustanad is not the denial itself but a supporting claim.
2
Understand the logical relation
The author states that the mustanad is at most a necessary condition for the non-existence of the denied premise. This means: if the mustanad is true, then the premise is false. But refuting the mustanad (showing it false) does not prove the premise is true; the denial could still be correct for other reasons.
Necessary condition: mustanad → ¬premise. Refuting mustanad does not give ¬(mustanad) → premise.
3
Recognize the ineffective strategy
Directly answering the mustanad is ineffective because even if you successfully refute it, the opponent's denial remains unsolved. The verifiers say: 'The basis is neither answered nor investigated.'
This is the core insight of the passage.
4
Introduce the tardid solution
Instead of refuting the mustanad, the responder must use tardid: enumerate all possible counterparts (qasimat) of the mustanad, and show that in every case the desired conclusion holds. This strategy ensures the denial is addressed even if the original basis is set aside.
Tardid works only if the responder can list all counterparts and explain each case.

Commentary
This passage is from a dialectical (jadal) section of the Qustas, discussing the etiquette of debate. It addresses a specific problem: when a denial (man') is accompanied by a basis (mustanad) — i.e., the opponent provides a reason for rejecting the premise — the responder cannot simply refute that basis, because eliminating the basis does not eliminate the denial itself (since the denial could be true for another reason). Instead, the proper method is to use alternation (tardid): the responder must take the basis and enumerate all its possible counterparts (qasimat), then show that in every possible case the desired conclusion still holds. The author categorizes three possibilities: the responder can list all counterparts or not; if he can list them, he can either explain each case appropriately or not. Only if he can both list and explain can he successfully answer. The passage then provides two examples from theological debates. The first example is from the issue of seeing God (ru'ya): a reasoner argued that the cause of vision is either existence or origination, then eliminated origination. If the opponent denies the enumeration by suggesting many other possible causes (such as substantiality, etc.), it becomes impossible to list them all and still show the desired result on each. The second example concerns the temporal origination of the world: if the world is created, God cannot be a necessitating cause (mu'jib), otherwise the world would always exist. If the opponent suggests that God could be necessitating but the world is delayed due to a missing condition, the responder uses alternation: either the delay is due to a missing condition or not. He shows that the first leads to an infinite regress, so the second is established, supporting the original claim. This second example demonstrates successful use of the tardid technique.

Inability to Answer: The Vision Example
Illustrates opponent's inability to answer through open-ended objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an opponent's objection leads to an inability to answer due to an unmanageable list of alternatives, and apply the tripartite division strategy to constrain the debate.

Core Idea
A weak debater leaves gaps for endless objections; a strong debater uses exclusive divisions to close them.

In the first dialectical scenario, the proponent attempts to prove that the cause of vision is existence alone, but the opponent responds by listing many possible causes. This list is potentially infinite, making it impossible to refute point by point. The proponent's tripartite division (existence, origination, or neither) is a powerful tool to simplify the debate, but the opponent cannot accept the third option, revealing their inability to answer properly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proponent's original claim
The proponent asserts that the cause of vision is either existence or origination, then eliminates origination to conclude existence.
2
Spot the opponent's objection
The opponent rejects the exclusive division and lists many other possible causes (e.g., spatial occupation, density). This is an open-ended list.
The list is not exhaustive and can be extended endlessly.
3
Apply the tripartite division method
The proponent can reduce the list to three categories: existence, origination, or neither. This covers all possibilities without enumeration.
The third option ('neither') is problematic for the opponent to explicate.
4
Assess why the opponent fails
The opponent cannot handle the third option because it is vague or leads to absurdity, thus they are unable to provide a satisfactory answer.
Inability here means failure to produce a finite, acceptable counter-proposal.

Ability to Answer: The Temporal Origination Example
Illustrates opponent's ability to answer through infinite regress refutation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a refutation that preempts an opponent's objection by showing it leads to infinite regress.

Core Idea
A strong debater anticipates objections and shows that the opponent's escape route leads to absurdity.

In the second scenario, the proponent argues that if the world is temporally originated, then God cannot be a necessitating cause (otherwise the world would not be delayed). The opponent objects that a missing condition could cause the delay. The proponent refutes this by showing that the missing condition would itself require another missing condition, leading to infinite regress. This demonstrates the ability to answer effectively by closing off the opponent's escape route.

Guided Path
1
Understand the proponent's initial argument
The proponent claims that if the world is temporally originated, then God is not a necessitating cause, because a necessitating cause would have produced the world immediately.
2
Recognize the opponent's objection
The opponent suggests that God could be a necessitating cause, but the world is delayed due to a missing condition.
This is a common escape route in kalam debates.
3
Analyze the response strategy
The proponent splits the possibility: either the delay is due to a missing condition or not. The first option is negated by showing it leads to infinite regress (each condition requires another).
This forces the opponent to accept the second option: the delay is not due to a missing condition.
4
Verify the infinite regress argument
If the delay required a missing condition, then the occurrence of that condition would require yet another missing condition, ad infinitum. This is absurd (tasalsul).
Infinite regress is considered a logical absurdity in Islamic logic.

Verify Your Claim Before Debate
Advice for the mu'allal in dialectical debate to ensure consistent claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two preparatory steps for a mu'allal in a dialectical debate and explain why they prevent self-contradiction.

Core Idea
Verify your claim and know your doctrine before debate test you contradict yourself.

In dialectical debate, the mu'allal (prover) must enter with absolute clarity. This passage from the Qustas gives practical advice: verify the exact claim you intend to prove and specify your doctrinal positions. Why? Because during the heat of debate, you might inadvertently say something that contradicts your own thesis or its necessary implications. The passage warns that even your proof might be invalid while you think it works. Fixing your claims and doctrines beforehand gives you the ability to spot such errors and defend effectively.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two preparatory actions
According to the passage, the mu'allal must 'verify the claims' (تحقيق الدعاوي) and 'specify the positions' (تعيين المذاهب). These are the two steps to take before entering debate.
The positions refer to one's own doctrinal school (madhhab) and its tenets.
2
Understand the three dangers
During debate, the mu'allal may harm himself by saying something that contradicts (1) his own claim, (2) a necessary consequence of his claim, or (3) his doctrine. Also, his proof might be invalid while he thinks it is valid.
These dangers show the importance of clarity and consistency.
3
Recognize the benefit of preparation
If the claim is verified and the doctrines are specified, it becomes easy to spot such contradictions and to have the ability to repel the opponent, whether answering or questioning.
Preparation turns the mu'allal into a strong debater.
4
Apply to a real debate
Consider a debate on whether the world is eternal (qadim). The mu'allal must specify his claim (e.g., 'the world is created in time') and his doctrine (e.g., Ash'arism). He must then ensure that any proofs he uses do not imply eternity accidentally.
This mirrors the classical kalam debate between theologians and philosophers.

Denying the Implication: Using a Contradictory Premise
Dialectical analysis of a contradictory premise's effect on implication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate a dialectical objection that uses a premise contradicting the implication (mulazama) in a conditional argument, and formulate a valid rebuttal.

Core Idea
A contradictory premise does not automatically refute a conditional—it must actually undermine the logical link.

In dialectical argumentation, one strategy to refute a conditional claim is to introduce a premise that contradicts its consequent. However, as the author demonstrates, this only works if the premise genuinely breaks the implication (mulazama). The example considers the claim 'if man is an animal, then he is exclusively either animal or body'. The opponent shows that the conditional itself is unsound because the negation of 'body' does not imply 'animal'. This lesson dissects that move and its proper rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the arguer's strategy
The arguer wants to prove 'man is not an animal' by showing that the conditional leads to a false consequent. He claims that if man is an animal, then he must be exclusively either animal or body (an exclusive disjunction). Since man is both, the disjunction is false, so by modus tollens the antecedent is false.
2
Locate the condition for the implication
The opponent objects that the conditional is not automatically true. It would only hold if the negation of 'body' logically implies 'animal'. That is, the conditional depends on the hidden premise: ¬(body) → animal.
This hidden premise is the real implication that needs to be established.
3
Test the hidden premise
Check whether ¬(body) → animal is valid. In reality, 'animal' implies 'body' (since every animal has a body). By contraposition, ¬(body) → ¬(animal). So the hidden premise is false: from ¬(body) we deduce ¬(animal), not animal.
Thus the original conditional is invalid because its truth requires a false premise.
4
Conclude the dialectical outcome
The opponent successfully denies the mulazama (implication) by showing that the contradictory premise does not actually contradict the intended logical connection—rather it reveals a flaw in the conditional itself. The arguer's attempt to use modus tollens fails.
This is a standard move in adab al-bahth: denying the implication is often more effective than denying the premise.

Man' al-Taqdir: Blocking Conditional Syllogisms
Author explains man' al-taqdir as a dialectical method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain how debaters use man' al-taqdir to block conditional syllogisms by appealing to the impossibility of the hypothesis.

Core Idea
Man' al-taqdir denies a conditional syllogism by declaring its hypothesis impossible.

In dialectical debates, opponents often use conditional syllogisms to force conclusions. A sophisticated counter is man' al-taqdir: you claim the condition (estimation) that makes the syllogism productive is impossible, so the conclusion does not follow. Identify the conditional syllogism structure

The debater denies that the conclusion follows because the hypothesis (e.g., the assumption that the major premise holds under the antecedent) is impossible—either in itself or according to the debater's own constraints.

If the hypothesis is impossible, then the conditional syllogism is not productive. The debater thus 'prevents the estimation' (man' al-taqdir) and the argument fails to prove its conclusion.

Al-Damm: A Flawed Dialectical Response
Author describes and critiques the method of al-damm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flawed dialectical method of al-damm and explain why it fails as a response to challenges about entailment of opposites.

Core Idea
Al-damm is a corrupt dialectical trick that assumes the hypothesis along with the truth of the denied premise.

When debaters deny that a thing can entail its opposite, some respond with 'al-damm' (inclusion). This method simply assumes the hypothesis together with the truth of the denied premise to force the conclusion. The author of Qustas labels this a corrupt answer. This lesson dissects its structure and flaw.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original challenge being addressed
The passage states that some debaters denied the impossibility of a thing entailing its opposite or two opposites. That is, they reject the idea that such entailment is impossible.
2
Understand the response called al-damm
The debaters answer by 'taking the hypothesis along with the truth of the denied premise'. They say: the major premise is true in itself. Then, if we include the hypothesis (the antecedent of the conclusion), the consequent follows.
The passage gives an example: 'the major premise is true in itself. So what is included with it, if the antecedent of the conclusion is true, makes its consequent true.'
3
Analyze why al-damm is corrupt
The method assumes the truth of the major premise that was denied or challenged. It does not provide independent grounds; it simply smuggles in the denied premise via the hypothesis. This is circular and does not genuinely refute the objection.
The author explicitly calls it a 'corrupt answer' (jawaab fâsid).
4
Compare with proper dialectical method
A valid response would need to show that the entailment of opposites is possible without relying on the very premise under dispute. Al-damm fails because it assumes what it needs to prove.

Alternative Dialectical Method: Proving Negation via Conditional Reductio
Author provides an alternative dialectical method for proving negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the alternative dialectical method to prove the non-existence of the minor term or the negation of the contradictory of the consequent in a conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
When an estimation leads to contradiction with reality, that estimation is refuted — achieving the goal.

In dialectical exchanges, the opponent may object by estimating the minor term or the contradictory of the consequent. This lesson presents an alternative response strategy: if the estimation leads to a contradiction with known facts, the estimation collapses, which is precisely the desired conclusion. This method uses a conditional reductio aimed directly at the estimation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objective
Determine whether the opponent's estimation is of the minor term or the contradictory of the consequent. The goal is to prove either that the minor does not exist or that the contradictory of the consequent is false.
2
Assess the estimation
Check whether the estimation, if assumed, necessarily leads to a contradiction with reality (khilāf al-wāqi‘). This is the key logical move.
The contradiction must be with actual facts, not merely with other assumptions.
3
Apply the alternative answer
If the estimation does not lead to contradiction, the original syllogism stands valid. If it does lead to contradiction, the estimation is refuted, and that refutation is itself the desired conclusion.
This is a form of reductio ad absurdum applied to the opponent's estimation.
4
Recognize the second formulation
Understand the alternative wording: assume the major premise under the estimation and the implication under the estimation of the contradictory of the consequent; this entails both the result and its contradictory. Since the consequent is false, the estimation is invalid.
This formulation uses a conditional chain leading to a contradiction.
5
Compare to standard method
Unlike a direct refutation of the syllogism, this method focuses on refuting the estimation itself, turning the opponent's assumption into a liability.
The previous answer (al-munāfi) likely addressed the syllogism directly; this is an alternative.

Refuting the Objection of 'Man‘ al-Taqdīr' in Disjunctive Syllogisms
Author refutes the sophistical objection of preventing estimation in disjunctive reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the two types of estimation (taqdīr) and refute the sophistical objection of man‘ al-taqdīr in disjunctive reasoning.

Core Idea
Estimation in a disjunctive branch is actual, not hypothetical — that's why the objection fails.

In this passage, the author addresses a common dialectical trick: objectors claim that assuming one disjunct is mere estimation and could lead to contradiction. He responds by distinguishing two types of estimation, showing that the objection confuses a hypothetical assumption with a factual one. This is a critical tool for defending conditional arguments in Islamic logic, especially in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (man‘ al-taqdīr)
The objector says that assuming one branch of a disjunction is an estimation, and since reality may differ, it could lead to impossibility. Understand the claim: 'You are just assuming it is so; but it might not be so, so your argument fails.'
This objection is common in dialectical contexts (jadal).
2
Understand the author's distinction of two kinds of estimation
The author divides estimation (taqdīr) into two types: (1) estimation of a thing as merely hypothetical (as in premises of concomitants), where reality may differ; (2) estimation of a thing as actual and existing, where the thing is posited as real.
The author uses the term 'مقدم الملازمات' for the first type.
3
Apply the distinction to the disjunctive branch
The disjunctive branch ('if it is such') is an estimation of the actual situation—it posits a state as real. Therefore, the contrary cannot be assumed, because the estimation itself says 'the actual situation is this'.
Example: 'If the world is originated, then it has a creator.' Here 'the world is originated' is assumed as actual.
4
Recognize the fallacy of the objection
The objector conflates the two types of estimation, treating a factual estimation as if it were hypothetical. Hence the objection is sophistry (muġālaṭa) and pure stubbornness (ʿinād maḥḍ).
This distinction protects disjunctive syllogisms from easy dismissal.
