Porphyry's Definition of Species as a Universal
Author addresses Porphyry's doubt on soul-body composition and his definition of species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Porphyry's definition of species and evaluate its completeness as a logical universal.

Core Idea
Species is not just a class—it is a universal defined by subordination under a genus.

Porphyry, known as 'the theologian' in logic, defined species as that which is arranged under the genus. This definition may seem straightforward, but it raises questions about whether it captures all that species entails, especially when considering real-world composites like the human soul and body. In this lesson, we will analyze the definition and connect it to the broader framework of the five universals.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition in Arabic
Find the phrase 'المُرَتَّب تَحْتَ الجِنْس' in the last sentence. This is Porphyry's description of species.
2
Analyze the components of the definition
The definition has two parts: 'murattab' (arranged/subordinated) and 'taḥt al-jins' (under the genus). This stresses hierarchical relation over intrinsic qualities.
Contrast with the standard Aristotelian definition that includes genus and differentia.
3
Compare with the standard five universals framework
Species is one of the five universals. Here Porphyry gives a relational description (rasm) rather than a complete definition (hadd), because it lacks the specific differentia.
The author uses the verb 'rasamahu' (he described it), indicating this is a rasm.
4
Examine the doubt about soul-body unity
The passage opens with a doubt: how can soul and body form a real unity? This doubt might challenge the very definition of species, because if the human species is a composite, its definition must reflect that composition.
The author offers a corrective: the animal becomes rational due to the soul's attachment—close to truth but not from their speech.
5
Assess the definition's completeness
Does 'arranged under the genus' capture what species is? It distinguishes species from genus but does not provide the differentia that makes it a distinct species. Thus it is a rasm (description) not a hadd (true definition).
In Islamic logic, a complete definition requires genus and differentia.

Classifying Essential and Non-Essential Accidents
Samarqandi's six-fold classification of accidents and Qutb Razi's confirmation of a seventh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an accident as essential (dhati) or non-essential (gharib) based on the mediating cause, and identify the non-essential subtypes including the mubayin addition.

Core Idea
An accident's essentiality depends on whether its cause is intrinsic or extrinsic

In Islamic logic, accidents (a‘rad) are attributes that inhere in a substance. But not all accidents are alike: some follow necessarily from the thing's essence (essential), while others are contingent and arise from external factors (non-essential). This lesson unpacks Samarqandi's refined classification of accidents into six and then seven types, showing how the nature of the mediating cause determines an accident's category. You will learn to apply this schema to distinguish, for example, the essential properties of a species from accidental features due to external agents.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three essential accident types
Essential accidents arise without mediation, through a part of the essence, or through an equal external cause. Identify these as the first three of the six divisions.
The Arabic terms for these subtypes are not given here, but you should remember the threefold division from earlier lessons.
2
Extract the standard non-essential subtypes
Samarqandi mentions that the first two divisions are called 'non-essential accidents' (al-‘arad al-gharib). He adds that a third also belongs to that category, giving three non-essential types. Note that the text does not name the first two subtypes explicitly, but they correspond to accidents mediated by a cause that is more general or more specific than the substance.
The passage says 'they called the first two divisions non-essential' – this refers to the standard classification before Samarqandi's expansion.
3
Identify the new mubayin subtype
Samarqandi introduces a fourth non-essential accident: that which occurs through a completely different (mubayin) cause, such as heat in a body from fire or sunlight. The cause here is neither more general nor more specific but entirely distinct from the substance.
The Arabic word 'mubayin' means 'different, distinct, separate'.
4
Confirm the total count with Qutb al-Razi's endorsement
Qutb al-Razi reports that 'one of the virtuous' (likely Samarqandi himself) added a sixth division (among non-essential types) – namely the mubayin – making three essential and four non-essential for a total of seven. He states that this is correct.
The passage says 'six divisions' initially, then Qutb al-Razi says 'added a sixth division' – careful: the initial six are three essential + three non-essential (without mubayin). The addition makes it three essential + four non-essential = seven.
5
Apply the classification to a new example
Given an accident like 'knowledge' in a human being, determine its type. Knowledge is an essential accident for humans because it arises from the rational soul (a part of the essence). Conversely, 'being burned' in wood is a non-essential accident (gharib) because it comes from fire, a mubayin cause.
Practice distinguishing essential from non-essential using the cause criterion.

Partial Disparity: Two Concepts, Two Negatives
Author defines partial disparity and reduces it to two particular negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define partial disparity between two concepts and reduce it to its logical form as two particular negative propositions.

Core Idea
Partial disparity is when each concept can be true without the other — expressed as two particular negatives.

Passage P0052_L03 defines the relation of partial disparity (مباينة جزئية), one of the five universal relations in Islamic logic. The author states simply: two concepts are partially disparate if each can be true of something without the other in some respect. This relation is then logically reduced to two particular negative propositions. Understanding this reduction is key to distinguishing partial disparity from other relations like total disparity or general-specific.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined: 'مباينة جزئية' (partial disparity). The author begins by stating what it means.
2
Extract the definition's core
The definition is: 'صدق كل منهما بدون الآخر في الجملة' — each of the two concepts is true of something without the other in some respect. That is, there exists at least one instance where A is true and B is not, and at least one instance where B is true and A is not.
Note the phrase 'في الجملة' (in totality/overall), meaning 'in some respect' — it does not require complete separation, just that each can occur without the other in some case.
3
Reduce to propositional form
The author says this relation reduces to two particular negatives: 'Some A is not B' and 'Some B is not A'. Verify: if A is partially disparate from B, then there is an instance where A exists without B (so some A is not B) and an instance where B exists without A (so some B is not A).
Contrast with total disparity, which reduces to two universal negatives: 'No A is B' and 'No B is A'.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Consider 'white' and 'sweet' as concepts. A white object need not be sweet (e.g., snow), and a sweet object need not be white (e.g., honey). So each can be true without the other. This is partial disparity. Logically: 'Some white things are not sweet' and 'Some sweet things are not white'.
This example is commonly used in Islamic logic texts.

Every Composite Has Genus and Species
Author asserts that every composite, including those with non-predicable parts, has genus and species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and species in any composite definition from an Islamic science example.

Core Idea
Every composite, physical or conceptual, must have a genus and a species.

This lesson examines the logical claim that every composite thing, whether a concrete object like a house or an abstract entity like a number, can be defined by its genus and species. The passage asserts that even composites with non-predicable parts still have a place in the hierarchy of genera and species. By the end, you will be able to extract these elements from definitions used in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite (murakkab)
Find the thing that is said to be composite. In the passage, two examples are given: number (al-‘adad) and house (al-bayt).
2
Determine its genus (jins)
Look for the broader category under which the composite falls. For number, it falls under quantity (al-kamm). For house, it falls under substance (al-jawhar) or body (al-jism).
3
Determine its species (fasl) or differentia
Find the characteristic that distinguishes the composite within its genus. For number, it is 'composed of units' (murakkab min al-ahaad). For house, it is 'composed of a ceiling and a wall' (murakkab min al-saqf wal-jidar).
4
Combine genus and species into a definition
A complete definition (hadd) consists of genus and differentia. Verify that the definition includes both and is true of all instances. The passage argues that even physical composites like a house have a conceptual genus and species.
This step confirms that the composite is defined by its genus and differentia.

Commentary
This passage defends the claim that every composite (murakkab) — whether it involves non-predicable parts (like the units of a number or the bricks of a house) or only predicable parts — must have a genus and a species. The author gives two examples: number (al-‘adad) and house (al-bayt). Number is composed of non-predicable units, but it also falls under the category of quantity (al-kamm), so its definition includes 'quantity' as genus and 'composed of units' as species. Distinguishing Essential from Necessary Concomitant
Editor corrects a misprint to show Ibn Sina's definition of essential (ذاتی) includes a condition excluding necessary concomitants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an essential (dhātī) and a necessary concomitant (lāzim) using Ibn Sina's causal condition 'bisababihī'.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina's condition 'because of it' excludes necessary concomitants from essentials.

In this passage, Ibn Sina refines the definition of 'essential' by adding a crucial causal condition. This refinement ensures that only attributes upon which the essence directly depends are considered essential, excluding attributes that merely accompany the essence. Understanding this distinction is vital for accurate definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Find the Arabic definition of dhātī: 'كل كلي إذا فرض معدوماً لشيء من جزئياته لا تبقى ذات ذلك الجزئي بسببه'. Note the key phrase 'بسببه' (because of it).
2
Understand the condition
The condition 'بسببه' means that the loss of the universal directly causes the loss of the particular's essence. This is causal dependence: the essence relies on the universal.
3
Contrast with necessary concomitants
Without the condition, attributes like 'risibility' for humans would also satisfy the definition (if risibility is removed, the human essence is gone). But risibility depends on the essence, not vice versa. The condition excludes such attributes.
The text says: 'وذلك القيد يخرج اللوازم' (that condition excludes necessary concomitants).
4
Apply the distinction
Given an attribute, test whether the essence depends on it (essential) or the attribute depends on the essence (necessary concomitant). Only the former are truly essential.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Sina's definition of 'essential' (ذاتی) as reported by Samarqandi in his Isaghuji commentary. The definition states: 'Every universal such that if it is assumed to be non-existent for something among its particulars, the essence of that particular does not remain because of it.' The key condition is the phrase 'بسببه' (because of it), which indicates that the loss of the universal directly causes the loss of the individual's essence. This condition excludes necessary concomitants (لوازم), which are non-essential attributes that also cause the loss of the individual if they are removed, but not because the essence depends on them; rather, the essence's loss entails the loss of the concomitant. For example, 'risibility' (ability to laugh) is a necessary concomitant of being human. If risibility is removed, the human essence is also removed, but not because the human essence depends on risibility; instead, risibility depends on the essence. Thus, the condition 'بسببه' ensures that only attributes upon which the essence directly depends are considered essential.

Breaking Circularity Through Stipulative Definition
Author resolves circularity by showing conceptualizations can be taught without prior logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how stipulative definitions of logical terms (like genus and differentia) avoid circularity and infinite regress, and apply this to a definition in fiqh.

Core Idea
Stipulative definitions break the cycle: the mind accepts them without prior logic.

One standard objection to logic is that defining its terms requires prior logic, leading to circularity or infinite regress. The author answers this by distinguishing between knowledge that requires proof and knowledge that is accepted via simple stipulation. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows how the five universals are taught without logical prerequisites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The objection is that if logic is needed to define logical terms, you either go in circles (dawr) or regress infinitely (tasalsul). Find where the author mentions this implicitly.
The passage states 'the problem does not return' — so the objection is already assumed.
2
Two Types of Theoretical Sciences
The author divides theoretical sciences into two: those liable to error (need proof) and those not liable (accepted by simple hearing). The latter includes conventional definitions.
The second type is 'such that when heard it is known without effort'.
3
Stipulative Definitions in Action
Take the five universals: 'we mean by 'genus' this meaning, by 'differentia' that'. The mind accepts these without difficulty. This is a stipulative, not discursive, definition.
Stipulation requires no prior logic, only a convention.
4
Apply the Solution
Notice that other concepts like 'proposition', 'contradiction', and 'conversion' are also taught this way. The entire foundation of logic rests on simple stipulations, breaking the circularity.
The author says 'most of the book of the universals is of this kind'.

In reality, the latter pair has absolute generality/specificity, not partial opposition.

This passage is a critical analysis of the threefold division of the universal (kulli) into 'before multiplicity,' 'with multiplicity,' and 'after multiplicity.' The author begins by drawing a sharp distinction between a verbal division (taqsīm lafẓī) and a logical division (taqsīm manṭiqī). In a verbal division, there is no required connection between the meanings of the divided term—it is simply a case of homonymy. The author then demonstrates that the threefold division fails both conditions. These are neither before multiplicity (since they are not caused or emanated) nor with multiplicity (since they are not in external multiplicity) nor after multiplicity (since they are not abstracted from multiplicity). Second, the mutual exclusivity condition fails because many universals can be described as before, with, and after multiplicity simultaneously—for example, artifacts conceived by a maker, realized in production, and remembered later. Thus the threefold division is merely verbal, indicating that 'universal' is a homonym. The author then discusses another traditional division: natural, mental, and logical universal. He compares the two divisions, noting that some scholars like Ibrahim Madkour equate them, but the author proposes alternative mappings. After careful consideration, he concludes that both mappings are weak because the logical universal itself has instances and therefore must fall under one of the three categories. Finally, the author refers to Ibn Sina's text (al-Shifa') and identifies a vagueness in the investigation, concluding with a call for the intelligent to clarify the matter.

Identify the universal term's relation to quiddity

Why 'Indicator of Essence' is Not Simply 'Broader Essential'
Author refutes equating 'indicator of essence' with 'broader essential'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two meanings of 'al-dhātī' and evaluate the claim that 'indicator of the essence' equals 'broader essential'.

Core Idea
One term, two meanings: part of essence vs. essence-destroying universal—misunderstanding them leads to false equivalences.

The ancients claimed that what indicates the essence when answering 'what is it?' is simply the broader essential. But the author shows this fails under both meanings of 'essential'. Let's unpack why this equation is false and what it teaches us about precise definition in logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ancients' claim
The claim is that 'indicator of the essence' is the 'broader essential' (al-dhātī al-aʿamm), which includes species and genus.
This seems plausible at first glance because species and genus do answer 'what is it?'.
2
Distinguish the two meanings of 'essential' (al-dhātī)
First meaning: a part of the essence (juzʾ al-māhiyya). Second meaning (from Avicenna): any universal whose removal would annihilate the subject's essence.
Both are used by logicians, but they are not equivalent.
3

Under the first meaning, 'indicator of essence' (dāll) is more general in some respects, because terms like 'rational' and 'sensitive' are 'broader essential' but do not indicate the essence—they are properties, not parts of the answer to 'what is it?'.
So dāll is not identical to al-dhātī al-aʿamm; it's broader in one way.
4

Under Avicenna's meaning, 'broader essential' includes universals whose absence destroys the subject, but many of these (e.g., relative terms) are not indicators of essence. Here, al-dhātī al-aʿamm is actually broader than dāll.
The equation fails because the two concepts have different extensions.
5
Recognize the required mode of indication
The author emphasizes that indication must be by conformity (muṭābaqa), not implication (iltizām). The indicator must directly answer 'what is it?'.
This ensures only genus and species (properly defined) qualify.

The mental genus works in the intellect, but its constitution is indirect through the logical species.
