Classification of Words into Particular and General
Author categorizes words into particular and non-particular types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between words that refer to particular entities and those that refer to non-particular entities, and identify which non-particular words can be applied to particulars.

Core Idea
Words either name a specific thing or describe an attribute applicable to many.

In logic, words are not all equal—some point to a single individual, while others are general descriptions that can apply to any matching object. This passage from the Qustas al-Mustaqim introduces this foundational classification, which is crucial for correct reasoning in Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Look for the division: words that are placed for a particular thing (mushakhkhas) and those placed for a non-particular thing (ghayr mushakhkhas).
The first type includes proper nouns like 'al-qalam' (the pen).
2
Recognize the nature of particular words
The author notes that most particular words are coined by new convention (bi al-wad' al-jadid). They are assigned to a specific entity.
This contrasts with general terms that are inherited from language roots.
3
Examine the non-particular category
Non-particular words are further divided into those that can be applied to particulars and those that cannot. Focus on the first subcategory.
Examples given: derived words (mushtaqqat), demonstratives (asma' al-isharat), pronouns (mudmarat).
4
Understand why derived words apply to particulars
'Al-mutaharrik' (the moving thing) is placed for something that has motion. Therefore, any object that acquires motion can be correctly described by it.
The meaning is linked to an attribute, not a specific individual.

Simple and Compound Words by Mutabaqa
Author defines compound and simple words based on part-whole signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab) words based on whether their parts signify parts of the meaning by mutabaqa (correspondence).

Core Idea
A single expression can be both simple and compound depending on the meaning considered.

In this lesson, we examine how the author defines simple and compound expressions using the criterion of mutabaqa (correspondence). You will learn that a single expression can be classified differently depending on which meaning is intended, and that many eloquent phrases fall outside the strict condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms
Locate the Arabic terms 'المفرد' (simple) and 'المركب' (compound). Note that the definition depends on whether parts of the expression are intended to signify parts of the meaning by mutabaqa.
Mutabaqa means one-to-one correspondence between the word and its meaning.
2
Understand the condition
The author states: if a part of the expression is intended to signify a part of the meaning by mutabaqa, it is compound; otherwise it is simple. This condition is restricted to mutabaqa, not tadammun (inclusion).
This avoids the case where the same expression could be both simple and compound by different significations.
3
Analyse the example 'al-hayawan al-mashi'
The phrase 'الحيوان الماشي' (the walking animal) is simple when considering the meaning 'animal' because the parts do not separately signify parts of that meaning. It is compound when considering the meaning 'walking body' because the parts ('body' and 'walking') do signify parts of that meaning by mutabaqa.
This shows that classification is relative to the intended meaning.
4
Recognise the exclusion
Expressions like 'تكلم بدر' (Badr spoke) and 'رمى أسد' (a lion threw) are considered compound in normal usage but do not meet the strict condition because the parts signify the meaning by correspondence. The author notes that most eloquent speech is of this type, meaning the condition is more restrictive.
This highlights the technical nature of the logical definition.

Division of Single Words into Noun, Verb, and Particle
Author defines the three types of single words and gives Ibn Sina's definitions of noun and verb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any single word as noun, verb, or particle according to Ibn Sina's definitions and identify the time-indication criterion.

Core Idea
A verb is a word that inherently indicates time; a noun does not.

This passage introduces a foundational classification in Islamic logic: every single word belongs to one of three categories: noun (ism), verb (kalima), or particle (ada). Ibn Sina's definitions from al-Shifa provide the precise criteria: a noun indicates a meaning without time, while a verb indicates time along with its meaning and always functions as a predicate. Understanding this division is essential for constructing sound propositions and arguments, as the time-indication of verbs plays a key role in logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage explicitly states that the single word is either a noun, a verb, or a particle. Write down these three categories.
2
Extract Ibn Sina's definition of noun
Find the definition: 'a single word that indicates a meaning devoid of time.' Note that time is not part of the meaning. For example, 'knowledge' (ʿilm) does not carry any temporal reference.
3
Extract Ibn Sina's definition of verb
Find the definition: 'a single word that indicates, along with what it indicates, time, and it always indicates something that is predicated of something else.' Time is intrinsic. For example, 'he wrote' (kataba) indicates both the action and past time.
4
Understand the particle's place
The passage says particles are 'subordinates of nouns, just as existential words are for verbs.' This means particles function as helpers, often linking or modifying nouns and verbs.
5
Apply the classification
Given any single word (e.g., 'book', 'writes', 'from'), decide whether it is a noun, verb, or particle based on the definitions. Check for inherent time indication and predicate function.

One-Meaning Simple Terms: Proper Noun, Univocal, Ambiguous
Author classifies one-meaning simple terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any simple term that has one meaning into proper noun, univocal, or ambiguous, and explain the criterion for each.

Core Idea
Simple terms with one meaning differ in how meaning relates to individuals: unique, equal, or unequal.

The author begins by dividing simple words that have a single meaning. He distinguishes three types based on how the meaning relates to hypothetical individuals. This classification helps in understanding the logical behavior of terms in definitions and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the term has one meaning.
Check the term's semantic scope. Is it a single concept or multiple? This lesson focuses on terms with one meaning.
2
Determine if the meaning is unified in a specific person.
If the meaning refers to a particular individual (e.g., 'Zayd'), it is a proper noun (علم). Note: pronouns like 'he' are excluded because they are one in species, not individual.
3
If not a proper noun, check equality among hypothetical individuals.
If all imaginary individuals share the meaning equally (e.g., 'human' applies equally to all humans), it is univocal (متواطئ). If not equal (e.g., 'existence' applies primarily to substance, secondarily to accident), it is ambiguous (مشكك).

Multiple-Meaning Simple Terms: Shared, Ambiguous per Meaning, and Innovated
Author classifies multiple-meaning simple terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a shared term (مشترك), explain its relation to the ambiguous term per individual meaning (مجمل), and recognize an innovated term (مرتجل).

Core Idea
A single word can have multiple meanings by original imposition; it is then 'shared' among those meanings and 'ambiguous' when each meaning is taken alone.

The author now turns to simple words that have more than one meaning from their original coinage. These are called 'shared' (مشترك). Each individual meaning is considered 'ambiguous' (مجمل) because it is not specified without context. A special case is the 'innovated' (مرتجل) term, which is transferred from one meaning to another without any connection.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the word has multiple meanings by original linguistic imposition.
If a word was originally coined for more than one meaning (e.g., 'عين' for eye, spring, spy), it is a shared term (مشترك) with respect to all meanings.
2
Consider each individual meaning in isolation.
Without context, each meaning is ambiguous (مجمل) because you do not know which one is intended. The term is 'ambiguous' relative to that particular meaning.
3
Identify if the transfer between meanings was arbitrary.
If a word was originally placed for one meaning and then transferred to another without any connection (e.g., a name given arbitrarily), it is innovated (مرتجل). This is a subtype of shared terms.
4
Distinguish from other types.
Shared terms are different from univocal (which have one meaning) and ambiguous (mushakkik, which have one meaning with varying intensity).

The Four Relations Between Concepts
Author enumerates and defines the four logical relations between two concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four logical relations holds between any two concepts from Islamic sciences (e.g., in usul al-fiqh, kalam).

Core Idea
Every pair of concepts has exactly one of four relations: equality, absolute generality, partial generality, or complete disparity.

In Islamic logic, every two concepts (mafhūmān) have a precise logical relation. This lesson introduces the four possible relations: equality (musāwāh), absolute generality (umūm muṭlaq), partial generality (umūm min wajh), and complete disparity (mubāyanah kulliyyah). Mastering these allows you to see exactly how terms in arguments relate—essential for valid reasoning in fiqh, kalām, and tafsīr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two concepts
Given any two concepts (e.g., 'human' and 'animal', 'wine' and 'intoxicant'), clearly define them. Make sure you know what each applies to.
2
Test equality
Check if each concept applies to everything the other applies to. If yes, they are equal. Example: 'human' and 'rational animal' are equal.
3
Test absolute generality

Sciences relate as overlapping, proportional, or distinct depending on how their subjects connect.

How do we organize the many sciences of the Islamic tradition? One key method is to look at the subjects (mawḍūʿāt) of each science and see how they relate. This lesson unpacks a classical classification that groups sciences into three categories: overlapping, proportional, and distinct. Overlapping sciences occur when one subject is more comprehensive than another. This generality can be real (genus-species) like 'quantity' for geometry, or conceptual (absolute vs. restricted) like 'sphere' vs. 'moving sphere'. Note the two examples.

Proportional sciences either study the same thing under different aspects (e.g., celestial bodies in astronomy vs. physics) or have distinct subjects that share a common inquiry or genus. The example of earth's sphericity shows the same conclusion reached with different proofs (innī vs. limmī).

Commentary
This passage classifies sciences based on the relationship between their subjects. The author, likely following Aristotelian tradition, presents three types: overlapping (mutadākhila), proportional (mutanāsiba), and distinct (mutabāyina).

First, overlapping sciences occur when one subject is more comprehensive than the other. Generality can be real (e.g., 'quantity' as a genus for geometry) or conceptual (e.g., 'sphere' vs. 'moving sphere'). A special case is when the subject of one science considers the subject of another under its accidental properties – for example, music studies notes under numerical proportions, which are accidents of number, the subject of arithmetic. Thus music is subsumed under arithmetic despite distinct subjects.

Another example: medicine is more specific than natural science (physics), which is more specific than first philosophy (metaphysics), whose subject is being as such, the most general. Metaphysics studies essential accidents like one, many, cause, effect, etc.

Second, if subjects are not overlapping but have a unified relation, sciences are 'proportional'. This includes cases where the same thing is studied under different aspects (e.g., celestial bodies as shapes in astronomy, as substances in physics). Hence the same problem, like the earth's sphericity, can be addressed in both sciences but with different types of proof: in astronomy a demonstration 'that it is' (innī), in physics 'why it is' (limmī).

Third, if subjects are distinct but share an inquiry or fall under a common genus, they are also proportional. The passage ends with 'or they are subsumed under a single genus', though the third type (distinct sciences) is only implied as the opposite of overlapping and proportional.

The commentary uses examples from Islamic sciences: mathematics (geometry, arithmetic), physics, metaphysics, and astronomy. The division remains relevant in later Islamic classifications of the sciences (e.g., in the works of al-Fārābī and Ibn Sīnā).

Subject relationship determines whether sciences are proportionate or diverse.

Different Proofs for the Same Problem: Innī and Limmī
Author illustrates how the same problem can be proved by different types of demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between an innī (that) proof and a limmī (why) proof for the same problem across proportionate sciences.

Core Idea
The same problem can be proved by different types of demonstration in proportionate sciences.

In the passage, the author notes that proportionate sciences may share some problems, but each science demonstrates it with its own type of proof. Using the example of the earth's sphericity and central position, he shows that astronomy gives a 'that' proof (innī) while physics gives a 'why' proof (limmī). This illustrates how different scientific perspectives can complement each other.

Guided Path
1
Understand innī and limmī demonstrations
Innī (that) demonstration proves that something is true without showing its cause. Limmī (why) demonstration proves through the cause.
Standard terminology in Aristotelian logic.
2
Identify the shared problem
The earth is spherical and at the centre of the sky. This is a problem (mas'ala) common to astronomy and physics.
3
Determine the proof from astronomy
Astronomy, studying celestial bodies in terms of shape, proves that the earth is spherical (observation of eclipses, etc.) – an innī proof.
It shows the fact without explaining why the earth must be spherical.
4
Determine the proof from physics
Physics, studying the nature of the heavens and earth, proves why earth is spherical and central – a limmī proof from natural motion or gravity.
5
Recognize the complementarity
Both proofs are valid, but they serve different epistemic purposes. One gives certainty of fact, the other gives causal understanding.

Principles in one science can be problems in another, but avoid circularity.

In the hierarchy of Islamic sciences, what counts as an unquestioned principle in a special science (like physics) may be a proven problem in a more general science (like metaphysics). Coextensiveness and Division: A Dialectical Argument
Author resolves a division paradox using coextensiveness insight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical problem of coextensiveness in a division and evaluate whether the division is valid using the author's resolution.

Core Idea
If a whole is coextensive with a part, dividing the whole requires care.

This lesson unpacks a classic logical argument about dividing a term into its parts. The author shows that a seemingly straightforward division can lead to a contradiction if one part is coextensive with the whole. However, he also provides a nuanced resolution that preserves the division. By studying this argument, you will sharpen your ability to detect and resolve such logical pitfalls in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The author initially claims that 'kalima' (word) cannot be divided into noun, verb, and particle. Find this statement in the Arabic and note it.
2
Extract the conditional reasoning
The author supports his claim with a conditional: if the division were valid, then one of the three parts would itself be divided into the same three. This is deemed false. Identify the antecedent and consequent.
This is a modus tollens: if P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
3
Understand coextensiveness (musawi)
The entailment relies on the idea that 'kalima' is coextensive with one of its parts. Define musawi: two terms that apply to exactly the same set of things. Explain why this leads to the division of that part.
If every instance of 'kalima' is also an instance of, say, noun, and vice versa, then dividing 'kalima' into parts would also divide the noun into those same parts.
4
Spot the resolution
The author then notes that the coextensive part is 'not determined' (la 'ala al-ta'yin). This means we do not fix which part (noun, verb, or particle) is coextensive with 'kalima'. Because of this, the division can proceed without contradiction.
The resolution allows the division to be logically sound by not requiring a specific part to be coextensive.
5
Assess the logical structure
The initial argument is a reductio ad absurdum, but the author's later qualification shows that the reduction only works if we assume the coextensive part is fixed. Without that assumption, the division is valid. Evaluate the overall structure as a dialectical move.
This is a common pattern in logical debates: an objection is raised and then answered by refining the premises.
