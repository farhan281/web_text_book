Condition for Necessary Modal Conclusion
Author defines the condition for a necessary conclusion in modal syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a modal categorical syllogism yields a necessary conclusion by checking if the middle term 'enters' the subject of the minor premise.

Core Idea
A necessary conclusion requires the middle term to be intrinsic to the minor subject.

In modal logic, not every combination of necessary and conditioned premises yields a necessary conclusion. The key is whether the description (middle term) is essential to the minor term. This lesson unpacks the condition known as 'entry of the description' and applies it to a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises and their modalities
The passage uses a necessary minor premise (e.g., 'Necessarily, no donkey is a horse ridden by Zayd') and a conditioned major premise (e.g., 'Everything ridden by Zayd is a horse ridden by Zayd, as long as it is ridden by Zayd'). Note the modality type: daruriyya (necessary) and mashruta (conditioned).
The minor is absolute necessity (dhatiyya), the major is temporary (waqtiyya).
2
Check 'entry of the description'
Ask: Is the middle term ('ridden by Zayd') intrinsic to the minor subject ('donkey')? The author states that if the description has entry (i.e., is essential to the subject), the conclusion is necessary. Here, the description does not enter the essence of donkey, so the conclusion is not necessary.
'Entry' means the middle term is part of the essence of the minor term.
3
Determine the modality of the conclusion
When entry is absent, the conclusion cannot be necessary in any sense—essential, descriptive, or temporal. The example 'No donkey is ridden by Zayd' is false in all modalities because the middle term is accidental to the minor.
The falsehood is due to the lack of entry, not the form of the syllogism.
4
Verify with the negative middle variant
If the middle term is made negative (i.e., the minor becomes affirmative and the major negative), the condition may change. This highlights that the entry condition applies specifically to the described configuration.
The passage mentions this as a secondary point for completeness.

Failure of Necessary Syllogisms with Mixed Modalities
Author proves non-productivity of necessary syllogisms when modalities are mixed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when mixing temporal and necessary premises in a categorical syllogism fails to produce a necessary conclusion.

Core Idea
Mixing modalities destroys the necessity of the conclusion.

In modal logic, the necessity of a conclusion depends on the modality of the premises. When we mix a temporal or conditioned premise with a necessary one, the conclusion cannot be necessary. This lesson examines the conditions under which modal syllogisms fail, using a concrete counterexample from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise types
Recognize temporal (waqtiyya) premises, which hold only during a specific time, and necessary (daruriyya) premises, which hold always.
Temporal premises are often expressed with conditions like 'as long as'.
2
Understand the non-productivity condition
The author states that a necessary minor with a temporal major yields no necessary conclusion in any sense (essential, descriptive, or temporal).
3
Examine the counterexample
Consider: Minor (temporal): 'Every black writer is black with moving fingers as long as he is a writer.' Major (necessary): 'Nothing actually white is black with moving fingers.' The apparent conclusion 'No black writer is actually white' is false because a black writer can become white.
The minor is true only temporarily, not always.
4
Generalize the principle
The failure occurs because the temporal premise does not hold absolutely, so the middle term is not essential to the subject, breaking the transfer of necessity.
5
Apply to other cases
Similarly, a conditioned minor with a necessary major, or a temporal minor with an absolute necessary major, also fails to produce a necessary conclusion.
The author also notes that if the middle term is made negative, the quantities change.

Non-Transitivity of Existence in Second Figure Modal Syllogisms
Author explains non-transitivity of existence in second figure with modal example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the existential force (al-wujud) does not extend to the conclusion in a second-figure modal syllogism and evaluate the conditions for production (intaj) regarding quality and modality.

Core Idea
Existence does not transfer when premises differ in modal conditions, even if the conclusion is necessary.

In this passage, the author addresses a key nuance in modal syllogistic: the existential force of the premises does not always carry over to the conclusion. Using a second-figure example with temporal modalities, he shows that a necessary conclusion can follow even when the premises lack the standard conditions for production. Understanding this helps you distinguish between logical validity and existential transitivity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figure and premises
Locate the two premises and the conclusion. Notice the middle term (متحرك) is the predicate in both premises, marking this as second figure. The first premise is 'Every man is moving, not always.' The second premise is 'No resting horse is moving, as long as it is a resting horse, not always.' The conclusion is 'No man is a resting horse necessarily.'
The premises include temporal conditions (لا دائما) that affect their modality.
2
Analyze the modalities
Both premises include 'not always' (لا دائما), indicating they are not necessary but temporal. This means they lack the permanence (دوام) required for a standard necessary syllogism. However, the conclusion is necessary. This indicates that the existential force of the middle term 'moving' does not transfer.
The term 'اللادوام' (non-permanence) is used to describe this temporal modality.
3
Check conditions for production
The author states that the premises lack the conditions of production (شرائط الإنتاج) in quality and modality. In the second figure, one premise must be negative and the quality must align. Here, one premise is affirmative and one negative (A and E), which matches the quality condition. However, the modal condition is violated because the premises are temporal while the conclusion is necessary.
Usually, for the conclusion to be necessary, both premises must be necessary or at least one necessary with appropriate modal rules.
4
Verify the conclusion's necessity
Despite the lack of existential transitivity, the conclusion 'No man is a resting horse necessarily' is true because the subjects 'man' and 'resting horse' are essentially incompatible. This necessity arises from the conceptual relationship, not from the transfer of existence.
The passage thus illustrates that logical entailment does not always require the existential force to carry over.

Non-production of Necessary from Temporal and Necessary

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A temporal premise cannot produce a necessary conclusion.

In modal logic, the modality of the conclusion depends on the weakest premise. When one premise is temporal (true only as long as the condition holds) and the other is necessary (always true), the conclusion cannot be necessary. The author illustrates this with a concrete example and then states the general rule.

Identify the premises and their modalities

Temporal premises are often indicated by phrases like 'mā dāma' (as long as).
2

The author states that the true statement 'Every black writer is actually white possibly' is not necessary. This shows that the conclusion of the syllogism (if one tries to combine the premises) would not be necessary; it is merely possible.
The author uses 'bi-l-imkān' (possibly) to mark the modality of the true statement.
3
Apply the rule: temporal + necessary does not yield necessary
The author explicitly says 'Non-production of necessary from temporal and necessary' (عدم إنتاج الضروريه من الوقتية و الضرورية). This is a general rule: even if the major is necessary, if the minor is temporal, the conclusion cannot be necessary.
The rule applies regardless of the type of necessity (essential, descriptive, temporal).

Existential Import in the Second Figure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether the existential import of premises transfers to the conclusion in a second-figure categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Conclusion can be necessary even when premises are about non-permanent existents.

This lesson examines a subtle property of the second figure: the existential import (that premises refer to existing things) does not necessarily carry over to the conclusion. The author illustrates this with an example involving temporal qualifications ('not always'). Understanding this helps avoid misapplying syllogistic rules in Islamic sciences where existence is often presupposed.

But the premises have temporal modality while the conclusion is necessary. The author notes this violates normal production conditions.
The point is not that the syllogism is invalid, but that existential import does not transfer.

This shows that in the second figure, the conclusion's subject may not have existential import.

Second Condition in the Second Figure: Convertible Negatives and Temporal Exceptions
Author explains the second condition and analyses temporal negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a negative premise in the second figure is convertible and determine if a temporal negative can be used without violating the condition.

Core Idea
The second figure requires convertible negatives; temporal negatives are non-convertible and thus excluded.

The second figure of categorical syllogisms has a strict condition: the negative premise must be convertible. Not all negatives are convertible; temporal negatives, which are true only at a specific time, fail this test. This passage from a classical logic text explains why temporal negatives are excluded and examines rare cases where they might still yield valid conclusions with other premises.

Guided Path
1
Understand the second condition
The second condition of the second figure is that the negative premise must be convertible (i.e., subject and predicate can be swapped while preserving truth). This is necessary because the middle term is the predicate in both premises, and a non-convertible negative would make the syllogism sterile.
Conversion here refers to immediate inference by swapping terms.
2
Identify the temporal negative
A temporal negative is a proposition that is negative only at a specific time, e.g., 'No light is eclipsed during a lunar eclipse' (but light is usually luminant). Such propositions are not convertible because their truth depends on a time restriction.
Temporal negatives are a type of modal proposition.
3
Examine the mixing cases
The author tests temporal negatives with necessary and particular conditioned premises, both as minor and major. When the temporal negative is minor and the major is necessary, a contradiction arises because the differentia cannot be negated from its property. Similar analysis is done for other combinations.
The examples use 'eclipsed' and 'light' to illustrate properties and differentiae.
4
Apply the conversion test
To check if a negative premise is valid in the second figure, try to convert it. If the converted proposition is not equivalent (e.g., a temporal negative becomes false), then the premise fails the condition and cannot be used directly.
Only absolute negatives (always true) and certain conditioned negatives pass the conversion test.

Reduction of Modal Syllogisms and Negative Conclusion Conditions
Explains reduction of two syllogistic types to first figure and negative conclusion conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the modal restrictions (wujud, dawam, darura) that govern the reduction of a categorical syllogism to the first figure and determine when a negative conclusion is permanent or non-permanent.

Core Idea
Modal syllogisms reduce via conversion; the conclusion's modality depends on the major's permanence and minor's negativity.

This passage delves into the mechanics of reducing modal categorical syllogisms to the first figure, a key skill for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. It also clarifies how modal restrictions like 'existence' (al-wujud) and 'permanence' (al-dawam) affect the validity of negative conclusions. Understanding these rules ensures that theological proofs, such as those for the eternity of divine attributes, remain logically sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two syllogistic types being reduced
The passage refers to two types that include the restriction of 'existence' (wujud) in both premises when the major is attributive. Recognize that these types reduce to the first figure by conversion, and the conclusion in the first figure becomes its converse in the original.
The 'two types' likely correspond to moods where the major premise describes a property (wasfiyya).
2
Understand the role of modal restrictions in reduction
The restriction 'existence' (wujud) indicates the judgment applies only while the subject exists. When reducing by conversion, these restrictions carry over or adjust. For instance, if the major has 'existence', the converse retains it under certain conditions.
Conversion here is 'tabdil' (replacement of terms), which may differ from simple 'aks'.
3
Analyze conditions for a negative conclusion
A negative conclusion is 'permanent' (da'ima) if the major premise is truly permanent; otherwise, it follows the converted minor without 'existence' if the minor is affirmative, and without 'necessity' if the major lacks attributive necessity.
The passage distinguishes between permanent and non-permanent negative conclusions based on the major's modal strength.
4

The non-permanence (ladawam) of a negative minor is affirmative, so it can combine with the major to produce a non-permanent conclusion. In contrast, the non-permanence of an affirmative minor is negative, leading to two negatives and no valid syllogism.
This prevents fallacies of two negative premises, which are invalid in categorical logic.

Mixing Proposition Types: The Rule of More Specific
Rule for mixing three proposition types in syllogisms, applied to first figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the modality (external, true, or mental) of the conclusion when premises of different types are mixed in a first-figure categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
When mixing proposition types, the conclusion's modality matches the more specific premise.

In Islamic logic, propositions are classified as external (khārijiyya), true (ḥaqīqiyya), or mental (dhihniyya) based on the scope of their subject. When premises of different types are combined in a syllogism, a clear rule is needed to determine the modality of the conclusion. The author presents a simple yet powerful rule: in affirmative conclusions, the conclusion takes the type of the more specific premise. This lesson unpacks that rule and applies it to the first figure.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three proposition types
Distinguish external (actual existents), true (essential nature), and mental (conceptual) propositions. Note the hierarchy of specificity: external is most specific, then true, then mental.
2
Understand the combinatorial space
With three types, mixing premises yields nine combinations: three homogeneous (same type) and six heterogeneous (mixed types). The homogeneous cases are already known; the rule addresses the six heterogeneous cases.
3
Learn the rule for heterogeneous mixes
For affirmative conclusions, the conclusion's type is the more specific (akhaṣṣ) of the two premise types. That is, external > true > mental. If one is external and the other true, the conclusion is external.
This rule applies only when the conclusion is affirmative; negative conclusions may follow a different pattern (not covered here).
4
Apply to the first figure example
Consider a first-figure syllogism: minor external, major true (affirmative). The major indicates the predicate holds for everything that has the middle; the minor ensures the middle applies to actual individuals. Thus the conclusion applies to actual individuals, making it external.
The same reasoning extends to other figures.

الحقيقية (al-ḥaqīqiyya): A proposition whose subject term is taken to refer to all individuals that would exist if the predicate applies, considering the essence or nature, not only actual existents. [Note: No significant shift; stable across the tradition.]
الذهنية (al-dhihniyya): A proposition whose subject term is taken to refer to mental concepts or essences, irrespective of external existence. [Note: In later texts, 'mental' may be used in epistemological contexts; here it is a technical term for proposition type.]
أخص (akhaṣṣ): More specific; in this context, the proposition type with narrower extension: external is more specific than true, true more specific than mental. [Note: This comparative term is used consistently in modal mixing contexts.]

In categorical syllogisms, premises can describe either external reality (haqiqiyya) or mental concepts (dhihniyya). Their interaction follows specific rules. This lesson unpacks those rules using examples from the tradition, so you can correctly evaluate arguments that cross these domains.

Al-Farabi's Modal Rules for Conversion and Syllogisms
Author summarizes al-Farabi's doctrines on conversion and syllogistic moods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Al-Farabi's three rules for modal conversion and syllogistic figures and apply them to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Al-Farabi refined modal logic: possibility and necessity have specific behaviors in conversion and across syllogistic figures.

This passage summarizes Al-Farabi's key contributions to modal syllogistic: the conversion rules for possibility and necessary negation, and the conditions under which modal premises yield modal conclusions in different figures. These rules are essential for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence that involve modal propositions.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the conversion rule
The passage states: 'انعكست الممكنة ممكنة والسالبة الضرورية سالبة ضرورية'. This means: conversion of a possible proposition remains possible, and conversion of a necessary negative remains necessary negative. For example, 'Every human is possibly writing' converts to 'Some writer is possibly human'.
2
Understand the first/third figure rule
The passage says: in the first and third figures, a possible minor premise 'produces with all (premises) like the actual minor premise without difference'. This means that if the minor premise is possible, the conclusion will also be possible (mumkina), regardless of the major premise. The reason given is that the minor term is subsumed under the subject of the major.
This does not apply to the second figure.
3
Understand the second figure rule
In the second figure, an essential necessary premise (ضرورية ذاتية) combined with any other premise yields a necessary conclusion. For example, 'Every human is necessarily rational (essential necessity) and 'No stone is necessarily rational' (negative necessary) yields 'No human is necessarily a stone' (necessary). Without essential necessity, the conclusion is not necessary.
Essential necessity means the predicate is inseparable from the subject's essence.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Take a first-figure syllogism: 'Every creation is possibly changing' (possible minor) and 'Everything changing is temporally originated' (actual major). According to the rule, the conclusion 'Every creation is possibly temporally originated' is valid and possible. This is relevant in kalam for arguments about the world's origin.

Five Types of Conditional Syllogisms and Three Subtypes of the First
Classifies conditional syllogisms into five types and details the first type's three kinds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the five types of conditional syllogisms and classify any given conditional syllogism into one of the three kinds of the first type.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms have five forms; the first type with two conjunctives has three kinds.

In this passage, the author recalls the classification of conditional syllogisms into five types based on whether premises are conditionals or categoricals. The focus then narrows to the first type—syllogisms composed of two conjunctive conditionals—which itself divides into three kinds depending on whether the shared part is a complete part, incomplete part, or mixed across the two premises.

Guided Path
1
Recall the five types
The author states that conditional syllogisms are composed either of pure conditionals or a mix with categoricals. The possible combinations are: two conjunctives, two disjunctives, categorical + conjunctive, categorical + disjunctive, and conjunctive + disjunctive. Identify each in your mind.
The passage lists them explicitly.
2
Focus on the first type
The first type is composed of two conjunctive conditionals (متصلة). This type has three subdivisions based on the nature of the part common to both premises.
The other four types are not detailed further here.
3
Identify the basis of subdivision
The common part (المشترك) can be a complete part (جزء تام) in both premises, an incomplete part (جزء غير تام) in both, or complete in one and incomplete in the other.
This yields exactly three kinds.
4
Note the author's critique
The author says the last two kinds were attributed to Ibn Sīnā but are actually later inventions; their validity will be refuted later.
This is a warning not to accept them uncritically.

Two Definitions of Complete and Incomplete Parts
Explains two definitions of complete/incomplete part and illustrates with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the two meanings of 'complete part' (جزء تام) and 'incomplete part' (جزء غير تام) as explained in the passage and apply them to analyze an example.

Core Idea
Complete part = full proposition; incomplete part = part of a proposition; but Ibn Sina's phrasing adds a second meaning.

The passage introduces two definitions of 'complete part' and 'incomplete part'. The first definition equates a complete part with a propositional unit (qadiyya) and an incomplete part with a non-propositional component. The second definition, implied by Ibn Sina's wording, treats a complete part as a part that is not itself a part of a part, and an incomplete part as a part of a part. An example illustrates how the same term can be both.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first definition
A complete part (جزء تام) is itself a proposition (qadiyya) upon analysis; an incomplete part is a non-propositional component like a subject or predicate.
This is the straightforward, common definition.
2
Understand the second definition
Derived from Ibn Sina's phrasing: a complete part is a part that is not a part of a part; an incomplete part is a part that is a part of a part (i.e., a subcomponent).
The author says Ibn Sina's expression 'suggests another meaning'.
3
Work through the example
Consider the syllogism: 'Whenever A is B, then whenever C is D, it is H Z' and 'Whenever H is T, then whenever it is H Z, it is F Y'. The middle term is 'H Z'. Under the first definition, 'H Z' is a complete part because it is a proposition ('it is H Z'). Under the second definition, 'H Z' is an incomplete part because it is a part of the consequent ('whenever it is H Z, it is F Y'), which itself is a larger proposition, so it is a part of a part.
This demonstrates the same component can be classified differently.
4
Contrast the definitions
Note that the first definition focuses on atomic propositional status, while the second focuses on hierarchical position (part vs. part of part). Both are used in logical analysis.
The author does not endorse one over the other.

The Claim: Four Figures in Conditional Syllogisms
Author presents the claim that four figures apply to conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how the four figures are assigned to conditional syllogisms based on the position of the middle term (a complete part) and reproduce the claimed mapping.

Core Idea
The four figures of categoricals were extended to conditionals by analogy—but the analogy fails.

In this passage, the author first presents a view that many logicians held: that the four figures of categorical syllogisms can be applied to conditional syllogisms composed of two conjunctive conditionals (luzumiyyat). The key is the 'middle term' understood as a complete part (juz' tāmm) of each conditional. Your task is to understand this mapping before we see how the author refutes it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
The two conditionals (luzumiyyat) each have an antecedent and consequent. The middle term is a complete proposition appearing in both.
A complete part (juz' tāmm) means an entire proposition, not a single term.
2
Define the middle term as a complete part
In conditional syllogisms, the middle term is not a term but a full propositional part (either antecedent or consequent). This differs from categoricals.
3
Map the four figures
According to the advocates: if middle term is consequent in minor and antecedent in major → figure 1; if opposite → figure 4; if consequent in both → figure 2; if antecedent in both → figure 3.
Memorize the mapping: 1: consequent-antecedent; 4: antecedent-consequent; 2: consequent-consequent; 3: antecedent-antecedent.
4
Recall the conditions from categoricals
They claim the conditions of productivity, number of moods, etc. are similar to categoricals. This is the point the author will later refute.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of an attempt to apply the four figures of categorical syllogisms to conditional syllogisms of the conjunctive type (luzumiyyat). The author first states the position of those who claim that when the middle term is a complete part in both premises, the conditional syllogism composed of two conditionals can be classified into the four figures analogously to categoricals. The rule given is: the figure depends on the position of the middle term in the minor and major premises. If the middle term is the consequent (tālī) in the minor and antecedent (muqaddam) in the major, it is figure one; if the opposite, figure four; if consequent in both, figure two; if antecedent in both, figure three. The proponents also assert that all conditions of productivity, number of moods, etc., are similar to categorical syllogisms. Then the author delivers a concise refutation: 'This is what they said. And you have known the invalidity of that from what we explained, that most moods in conditional-luzumiyyat are not productive.' The author does not repeat the earlier argument here but references it. The key point is that the strict analogy between categorical and conditional syllogisms fails because the logical properties of conditionals differ, rendering most of the claimed moods invalid. This critique aligns with a common theme in Islamic logic after Ibn Sina: a rejection of the overextension of categorical analysis to conditionals.
