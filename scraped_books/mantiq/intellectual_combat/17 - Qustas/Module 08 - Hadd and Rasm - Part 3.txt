This passage presents a classical debate between Ibn Sina and Abu al-Barakat on whether definition is difficult. Three Types of Possibility in Modal Logic
Author defines three types of possibility and explains their hierarchical relationship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the three types of possibility (imkan) and identify which negation of necessity defines each.

Core Idea
Possibility is defined by what necessity it negates: general (one side), specific (both sides), most specific (all types of necessity).

In Islamic logic, possibility (imkan) is not a simple concept. The logicians distinguish three levels, each defined by the scope of necessity they exclude. This parsing exercise will help you master the precise technical vocabulary used by philosophers and theologians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
Locate the Arabic names for the three possibilities: الإمكان العامي, الخاصي, الأخص. Notice the root 'imkan' and the suffixes indicating generality.
The terms use comparative/superlative patterns: عامي (general), خاصي (specific), أخص (most specific).
2
Understand the negation
Each possibility negates necessity: general negates absolute necessity from one side (the side opposite the judgment); specific negates it from both sides; most specific negates absolute, descriptive, and temporal necessity from both sides.
The 'side' refers to either affirmation or negation of the proposition.
3
Compare the hierarchy
The author states: الأول أعم من الثاني وهو من الثالث (the first is more general than the second, and the second than the third). This means general possibility includes specific, and specific includes most specific.
Hierarchy: general > specific > most specific.
4
Apply to an example
Take the proposition 'The world is created'. General possibility: creation is not impossible (negates impossibility). Specific possibility: both creation and non-creation are not necessary. Most specific: no type of necessity (absolute, descriptive, temporal) attaches to either side.
This example is common in Kalam debates.

Defining Future Possibility
Definition and explanation of future possibility

Future possibility is possibility that emerges when present obstacles are removed.

How can something be possible if it is impossible right now? Future possibility answers this. In this lesson, we define al-imkan al-istiqbali, give examples, and distinguish it from mental possibility. The passage grounds this concept in Avicenna's Shifa and refines it to avoid logical pitfalls.

After defining future possibility, the author addresses an objection from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. Al-Razi argues that if we require present non-existence for future possibility of existence, then we also implicitly require present existence for future possibility of non-existence, causing a contradiction. The author offers a nuanced response. This dialectical exchange sharpens our understanding of the modal concept.

The author restricts the definition to avoid the symmetry.

Three Differences Between Potentiality and Modal Possibility
Author lists three differences between potentiality and modal possibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Potentiality is more specific and narrower than modal possibility.

In logic, potentiality (al-quwwa) and modal possibility (al-imkān) are often confused, but they have distinct logical properties. The author lists three precise differences that help us separate them. Understanding these differences is crucial for analyzing modal arguments in Islamic sciences such as kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two concepts
Locate the terms 'al-quwwa' (potentiality) and 'al-imkān' (modal possibility) in the passage. Recognize that the author is comparing them.
2
First difference: specificity
The author states that potentiality is 'akhas' (more specific) than modal possibility. That means potentiality applies to a narrower set of subjects. Ask yourself: what kind of subjects possess potentiality?
3
Second difference: application to opposites
For potentiality, the opposite side (e.g., non-existence if potentiality is for existence) is not itself potential. But for modal possibility, both existence and non-existence are possible. Understand that potentiality is directed towards one side, while possibility is neutral.
4
Third difference: negatability
Potentiality can be negated without negating possibility. For example, a substance may not have the potential to be a certain way, yet it is still possible for it to be that way. This is not true for modal possibility.
5
Summarize the distinction
Combine the three points: potentiality is more specific, does not extend to both opposites, and can be negated while possibility remains. This shows that potentiality is a stricter concept.

Defining Genuine Contradiction
Author defines contradiction and condition of 'li dhatihi'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify genuine contradiction by checking the condition of 'li dhatihi' and distinguish it from apparent opposition like salb al-musawi.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires necessity from the propositions themselves, not from external equivalence.

Not every pair of affirmation and negation forms a contradiction. The author sharpens the definition by adding the condition 'li dhatihi' — by itself. This ensures that the truth of one proposition directly forces the falsehood of the other, without relying on an intermediate term. Understanding this condition is crucial for precise logical and theological argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
Read the Arabic definition: اختلاف القضيتين بالإيجاب والسلب بحيث يقتضي لذاته صدق إحداهما وكذب الأخرى. Note the three parts: (1) two propositions differ in affirmation and negation, (2) the relation necessitates truth/falsehood, (3) the necessity is 'by itself'.
2
Extract the condition 'li dhatihi'
Focus on the phrase لذاته. It excludes cases where the necessity comes from an intermediate, such as the negation of a co-extensive term (سلب المساوي).
This is the key differentiator.
3
Analyze the counterexample
The example: 'هذا إنسان' and 'هذا ليس بناطق'. Although the two cannot both be true, the falsehood of the first does not force the truth of the second because 'not a speaker' is not the direct negation of 'human' but of a co-extensive term. The contradiction is indirect.
4
Apply the condition
Given a pair of propositions, test whether the negation is of the exact same predicate in the same respect. If it is, then the pair is a genuine contradiction; if it negates a different predicate or a co-extensive term, it is not.
Use the condition 'li dhatihi' as the criterion.

Necessity for an Individual of a Nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a relation that is universal for an individual and particular for its nature, and explain why no reversal occurs in such cases.

Core Idea
Necessity for an individual does not entail necessity for the nature.

In this passage, the author discusses a subtle but critical distinction: a property may be necessarily true for a specific individual instance of a nature, yet not hold for the nature as a whole. This distinction prevents logical errors where one might incorrectly reverse a conditional into a categorical statement. We will unpack this by analyzing the examples provided and understanding why the relation to the nature is what matters in logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Find the terms 'nature' (طبيعة) and 'individual' (فرد) in the Arabic. Understand that 'nature' refers to the universal essence, while 'individual' refers to a particular instance.
2
Examine the conditional relation
The author uses examples: 'If something is an animal, it is a human' and 'If something is a breather, it is a horse'. Note that these are not universally true for all animals or all breathers, but could be true for a specific individual (e.g., a particular human or horse).
3
Distinguish the two perspectives
Recognize that the relation is 'universal' with respect to that individual (if it is that individual, then it must be human) but 'particular' with respect to the nature (only some animals are humans). 4

Reversal (from conditional to categorical) would require the relation to be universal for the nature. Since it is only particular, reversal is not valid. For example, from 'if something is this animal, it is a human' we cannot conclude 'all animals are humans'.
This is a safeguard against hasty generalization.

Definitions cannot be demonstrated; any attempt leads to circularity or duplication.

This passage refutes the ancient claim, attributed to Xenocrates and others, that definitions can be proven by categorical syllogism. The author systematically shows that such an attempt forces the middle term into impossible roles, leading to logical absurdities. This lesson will walk you through the refutation so you can recognize why definitions must be acquired through other means, such as direct conception or analysis of genus and differentia.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
Find the statement that definitions can be acquired by demonstration (burhan) using categorical syllogism. The author names Xenocrates and some ancients as holding this view.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A definition cannot be proven by demonstration without circularity or presupposing the definition itself.

One of the perennial questions in Islamic logic is whether a definition (hadd) can be acquired through a demonstrative syllogism (burhan). In this passage, the author considers several ways a syllogism might attempt to prove a definition, showing each fails. An objector then proposes a new syllogistic form, but the author's response reveals a fundamental problem: the syllogism already presupposes knowledge of the definition's components. Let's walk through the dialectical exchange step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial problem
The passage begins by considering what happens if the major term (the definition) is taken as a deficient definition of the middle term or as an ordinary predicate. The author shows that in both cases, the conclusion does not yield a complete definition of the minor term. Note the reasoning: if the major is part of the middle, it becomes part of the minor (since part of a part is a part), so only a deficient definition results; if the major is a predicate of the middle, it merely affirms something of the subject without proving it is a definition.
This sets up the logical problem: how can a syllogism prove that something is a definition?
2
Analyze the third estimation
The author then considers a third estimation where the major is related to the middle in four ways: as a complete definition, a deficient definition, a definition of that which the middle is affirmed of, or a predicate of that which the middle is affirmed of. For each, the author evaluates the consequences. Only the second and fourth avoid immediate absurdity, but they still fail to prove the major is a definition of the minor. The third leads to the major premise being identical to the desired conclusion (petitio principii).
This careful breakdown shows the author's method of exhaustion: trying all possible relationships and showing none work.
3
Examine the objector's proposal
The objector concedes that the original framing (defined as minor, definition as major) fails, but proposes a new syllogism: minor premise = 'this sum is genus and differentia of B'; major premise = 'everything that is genus and differentia of B is a definition of B'; conclusion = 'this sum is a definition of B'. This seems to avoid the earlier pitfalls because the definition is now the conclusion, not the major term directly.
The objector's move is clever: it redefines the logical structure to make the definition the conclusion of a valid syllogism.
4
Understand the author's response
The author's response is devastating: 'If genus and differentia are known, what need is there for demonstration?' The objector's minor premise already states that we have identified the genus and differentia of B. But knowing the genus and differentia is precisely what it means to have a definition. Therefore, the syllogism is circular: it presupposes the definition in the premises and then 'proves' it in the conclusion.
This is the key insight: demonstration cannot create new definitions; it can only work with already-known components.
5
Generalize the lesson
The passage teaches that definitions are foundational: they are the starting points of knowledge, not the conclusions of proofs. A syllogism that attempts to prove a definition either fails logically or presupposes what it tries to prove. This is why, in the Islamic logical tradition, definitions are established through other means (e.g., linguistic analysis, division, or immediate intuition), not through demonstrative syllogisms.
This principle protects the integrity of the hierarchy of knowledge: axioms (including definitions) must be prior to proof.

The Method of Division for Constructing Definitions
Author explains the method of division with an example of defining human.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of division (tariq al-qisma) to derive a definition by performing a series of disjunctive eliminations and then synthesizing the components.

Core Idea
Division is a ladder: eliminate false genera step by step until you reach the true genus and differentia.

In this passage, the author demonstrates how to build a definition not by direct description but by systematically dividing a concept into mutually exclusive alternatives and eliminating the inapplicable ones. This method, called tariq al-qisma (method of division), uses a chain of disjunctive syllogisms to isolate the correct genus and differentia. Finally, the parts are combined in a categorical syllogism to confirm the definition. Understanding this method will sharpen your ability to construct precise definitions in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject to be defined
The definiendum is 'human' (al-insan). The method begins with a broad category that includes the subject.
In this example, the initial division is between substance and accident.
2
Apply the first disjunctive division
State that the subject is either A or B. Then eliminate the alternative that does not apply using a disjunctive syllogism (qiyas istithna'i munfasil). For 'human': it is either substance or accident; it is not accident, so it is substance.
Each step uses a 'mani'at al-jam' wal-khuluw' (true exclusive disjunction) to ensure exhaustive and exclusive alternatives.
3
Continue dividing the chosen alternative
Take the result (substance) and divide it again: substance is either animal or non-animal; eliminate non-animal. Then animal is either flying, crawling, or walking; eliminate flying and crawling, leaving walking. Then walking is either rational or non-rational; eliminate non-rational, leaving rational.
The divisions narrow down the genus stepwise: substance → animal → walking → rational. Note that 'walking' may be a sub-genus; the final differentia is 'rational'.
4
Collect the parts and form a compositive syllogism
The parts obtained are the predicates from the conclusions: substance, animal, and rational. Combine them: 'This sum (substance + animal + rational) is a detailed expression indicating a concept; everything that is such is a definition of human; therefore this sum is a definition of human.'
This is a categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara): the conjunction serves as the subject of the minor premise.
5
Verify the definition
The resulting definition is 'rational animal' (or with higher genera). Check that it includes the essence (genus + differentia) and excludes non-humans.
The author does not critique the definition but shows the method of arriving at it. The method is a heuristic, not a proof.

Critique of the Division Method for Defining
Critique of the division method for defining

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the circularity in using division (qisma) to obtain a definition, and explain why the method fails to distinguish essential from accidental properties.

Core Idea
Division cannot define because it presupposes the very knowledge it claims to provide.

The author critiques the division method (qisma) as a way to obtain definitions. He argues that the method is either useless or circular. Let's examine his reasoning and see why division alone cannot yield the essence of a thing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method being critiqued
The division method is described as dividing a whole into its parts or a universal into its particulars. This is a common approach to defining by enumerating components.
For example, defining 'human' by listing body, soul, etc.
2
Understand the condition for its usefulness
The author states that the benefit of this method depends on being able to distinguish essential (dhātī) from accidental (ʻaraḍī) properties. Without that distinction, division yields a mixed list.
Essential properties constitute the essence; accidental ones do not.
3
Identify the critique
Both essential and accidental properties are obtained from division without any inherent distinction. So division alone cannot separate them.
If you divide 'animal', you get rational (essential) and laughing (accidental) – but division itself doesn't label them.
4
Analyze the circularity
If you already know which properties are essential, you don't need division. If you don't know, division cannot tell you. Hence the method is either redundant or insufficient.
This is a classic circularity argument.
5
Acknowledge the conceded benefit
The author admits that division can help detail predicates, whether essential or accidental, but this is only useful after the essence is known.
So division has a subordinate role in organizing descriptions, not in discovering definitions.

Critique of Inference by Opposite for Defining
Critique of inference by opposite for defining

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why inferring a definition by opposite is weak, citing the problems of lacking an opposite and induction's uncertainty.

Core Idea
Inference by opposite is weak because not everything has an opposite, and even if it does, induction cannot guarantee certainty.

The author continues his critique of methods for defining by examining the inference by opposite (istiqlāl bil-ḍidd). This method assumes that from the definition of one thing, the definition of its opposite can be deduced. Let's uncover its flaws.

Guided Path
1
Understand the method
The method is exemplified: if black is known to be a color that contracts vision, then one infers that white is a color that expands vision. The opposite property is assumed to be the logical inversion.
This is a form of reasoning by contraries.
2
Identify the first problem: lack of an opposite
The author points out that not everything has an opposite. Many things (e.g., 'human', 'existence') lack a true opposite. If there is no opposite, the method cannot be applied.
In logic, opposites (ḍidd) are usually pairs like black/white, hot/cold, but many concepts are not part of such pairs.
3
Identify the second problem: unknown universality
Even if an opposite exists, the author argues that the universal validity of the inferred relation is not known. Just because one property has a certain effect, it does not necessarily follow that the opposite property has the opposite effect in all cases.
The example: the contraction of vision due to blackness might not imply that whiteness universally expands vision; other factors may be involved.
4
Note the role of induction
The inference often relies on induction (istiqrāʼ) from observed cases. However, induction does not provide certainty (yaqīn). Even if many instances are observed, absolute certainty is not achieved.
The author is highlighting the epistemological limitation of inductive reasoning.

General conditions and defects of definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate a definition for compliance with the general conditions (coextensiveness, clarity, non-circularity, proper order, no repetition) and identify defects of composition.

Core Idea

These ensure that the definition truly informs about the definiendum and does not confuse or mislead. The passage then describes two categories of defects: those arising from confusing components in specific conditions, and those violating the general conditions.

Guided Path

This step trains practical evaluation.

The Infinite Regress Problem in Defining the Differentia

Learning Objective

This passage presents a critical argument that challenges a common understanding of the differentia (faṣl). Core Idea
Real division requires existence of parts; disjunction does not.

In logic, dividing a whole into parts and stating a disjunction (or-statement) may seem similar, but they have a crucial difference. The author of this passage clarifies that a real division (taqseem) demands that the parts actually exist in reality, whereas a true disjunction does not—one side may be false or even impossible. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in classification and logical analysis.
