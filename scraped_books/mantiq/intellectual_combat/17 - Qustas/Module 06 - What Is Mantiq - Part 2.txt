Definition of Genus and Its Four Levels
Defines genus and its levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of genus and distinguish its four hierarchical levels.

Core Idea
Genus is a universal predicated of many differing in essence, with four levels from highest to lowest.

The passage defines genus and explains its existential definition, then categorizes it into highest, lowest, and intermediate levels. Understanding these levels is crucial for analyzing definitions in Islamic logic, as genus is the first of the five universals and the foundation of definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of genus
Find the phrase that defines genus. The passage states that genus is 'the universal predicated of many differing in realities' (المقول على كثيرين مختلفين بالحقائق). This is the logical definition.
2
Note the existential definitions
The author gives examples: the highest genus is 'the most complete', the lowest is 'the most specific', and the proximate is 'the simple immediate'.
These are relative descriptions, not formal definitions.
3
Recognize the four levels
The levels are: (1) genus of genera (جنس الأجناس), (2) intermediate genera (أجناس متوسطة), (3) the immediate genus (الجنس القريب), and (4) the lowest species (النوع السافل). The passage discusses the chain from species up to 'the relative'.
The four are listed in the chain: genus, predicated of many, predicated, relative.
4
Determine ultimate or intermediate status
Each level can be either an ultimate species (if it has no further species below) or an intermediate species (if it can be further divided). This depends on whether differences of subjects necessitate variety of accidents.
This is a technical point from the discussion.

The Logical Chain Leading to the Genus of Genera
Entailment of treating genus as a genus for its levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument showing that 'the relative' is the genus of genera.

Core Idea
If genus is a genus for its own levels, a hierarchy emerges culminating in 'the relative' as the ultimate genus.

The passage presents a conditional argument: if we treat 'genus' as a genus for its four levels, then those levels become its species. Then, depending on whether differences in subjects necessitate variety of accidents, we either stop at ultimate species or continue a chain until we reach 'the relative' as the genus of genera. This argument challenges the notion of a single highest genus and is crucial for understanding the hierarchy of universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional assumption
The argument begins: 'If we say it is a genus for them, then they would be species for it.' This is the initial conditional that sets up the chain.
The 'it' refers to 'genus' and 'them' refers to the four levels.
2
Consider the first subcase
If differences of subjects necessitate variety of accidents, then each level has ultimate species and the chain stops. This would mean that 'genus of genera' is not an ultimate species.
This corresponds to al-Razi's first scenario.
3
Consider the second subcase
If differences do not necessitate variety, then each level becomes an ultimate species. Then above each we have 'genus', then 'predicated of many', then 'predicated', then 'the relative'. This yields an infinite regress or a highest genus.
This leads to 'the relative' as the genus of genera.
4
Conclude the argument
Thus, regardless of the subcase, 'the relative' emerges as the highest genus (genus of genera). The argument shows that assuming genus as a genus for its levels leads to a definite hierarchy.
The passage then notes that this applies to all logical universals.

Al-Razi's Critique and the Author's Response
Commentary on al-Razi's reductio argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace al-Razi's argument against taking 'genus' as a genus of its levels and the author's defense.

Core Idea
Al-Razi argues that the assumption leads to a contradiction, but the author suggests it is not a corrupt entailment.

The passage includes a quotation from Imam al-Razi's al-Mulakhkhas, which presents a syllogism that seems to show a contradiction: if 'genus' is a genus of its levels, then 'the relative' becomes both genus of genera and species of species. The author argues that al-Razi's conclusion is not necessarily a reductio of the original position, as the author did not transmit the major premise. This highlights how commentary traditions handle logical objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text claim
The Shaykh (likely Ibn Sina) takes 'genus' as a genus for its four levels. This is the claim that al-Razi criticizes.
The passage does not name the Shaykh explicitly, but it refers to 'the Shaykh'.
2
Extract al-Razi's objection
Al-Razi builds a chain: if we make the four levels species of genus, then 'genus of genera' is divided into substance, quantity, etc. Then he presents two scenarios: one where 'genus of genera' is not an ultimate species, and one where it is. In the latter, he adds layers until 'the relative' becomes genus of genera and also species of species (a contradiction).
Al-Razi deduces an unstated conclusion that 'the relative is a species of species', which contradicts its status as genus of genera.
3
Note the transmission status
Al-Khunaji and al-Urmawi transmitted al-Razi's words without accepting or rejecting them. This shows scholarly caution.
4
Understand the author's defense
The author says that al-Razi's words are transmitted as entailments of the Shaykh's words, not as corrupt entailments. The author did not transmit the major premise that would make it a contradiction. Therefore, the original claim may still be valid.
The major premise is: 'The genus of genera is a species of species.' Without it, the contradiction does not arise.

Core Idea

In logic, power (قوة) and specific possibility (إمكان خاص) are often confused because both relate to potentiality. However, this passage clarifies three precise logical differences: power is more specific, its contrary is not itself a power, and power is negated while possibility remains. Understanding these distinctions sharpens your modal reasoning, especially in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three distinctions

The passage states: 'أنها أخص منه، ولا يكون طرفها الآخر كذلك، بخلاف الإمكان فإن طرفه الآخر أيضاً ممكناً، وهي تنتفي دون الإمكان.'

This example is not in the text but is a natural application.

Author defines complete and incomplete syllogisms with examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Complete syllogisms yield the conclusion directly; incomplete ones require a change in term order or conversion.

The passage distinguishes between complete (kamil) and incomplete (ghayr kamil) syllogisms. A complete syllogism shows the conclusion without any change – this includes the first figure of categorical syllogisms and the exceptional (conditional) syllogism. Incomplete syllogisms, like the other three figures, require conversion or a change in the position of terms to make the entailment evident. This classification helps us understand which arguments are self-evidently valid and which require additional steps to display their validity.

Answering the Two Objections to the Definition
Author presents and refutes two objections to the definition of qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the two objections that the conclusion of an exceptional syllogism or a symmetrical pair of premises invalidates the definition of qiyas.

Core Idea
Apparent counterexamples to the definition are resolved by distinguishing actual propositions from embedded statements and by considering premises as combined.

The passage presents two objections that seem to challenge the definition: first, in exceptional syllogisms, the conclusion (the consequent) appears already present in the conditional premise; second, in a pair like 'Every C is B and every B is C', the conclusion is identical to the minor premise. The author answers both: for the first, the consequent is not an actual proposition until it is separated; for the second, the premise is different from the conclusion because it is considered as combined with the other premise. This lesson unpacks these answers and shows how they protect the definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection
Find the example: 'قولنا كلما كان آب ج، لكن آب فـ ج د' (Whenever A is B, C is D; but A is B; so C is D). The objection is that the conclusion 'C is D' is already present in the conditional premise as the consequent, so it seems to follow from that premise alone.
2
Understand the answer to the first objection
The author says that 'qawl akhar' means a statement different from the premises which are actual propositions. The consequent in the conditional is not an actual proposition (it is part of the conditional); it becomes an actual proposition only when the minor premise (A is B) is added and the consequent is extracted. So it is not a repetition of a premise.
Compare: 'If Zaid is a man, then Zaid is mortal' – 'Zaid is mortal' is not a standalone premise; it becomes a conclusion after affirming the antecedent.
3
Identify the second objection
Find the example: 'كل ج ب وكل ب ج فإنه ينتج كل ج ب' (Every C is B and every B is C produces Every C is B). Here the conclusion is exactly the minor premise, so it appears to follow from a part (the minor premise alone).
This is a symmetrical proposition pair.
4
Understand the answer to the second objection
The author answers that the premise is different from the conclusion when it is considered as combined with the other premise. That is, 'Every C is B' as a premise alone is not the same as when it is part of the pair 'Every C is B and every B is C'. The conclusion follows from the combination, not from the premise alone.
The premise by itself does not entail the conclusion; it is the pair that entails it, even though the conclusion looks identical.

Primary and Universal Predicates in Demonstration
Author defines primary and universal predicates in demonstration with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between primary (al-awwali) and universal (al-kulli) predicates in demonstration and identify examples of each from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Primary predicates are immediate; universal predicates cover all times and individuals.

In the science of demonstration (burhan), not all predicates are equal. Some are immediate—they apply directly without a middle term—while others are universal, applying to every instance at all times. The classical logicians defined these precisely to ensure the rigor of demonstrative proofs. In this lesson, we will unpack these definitions and see how they function in actual Islamic scholarly arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms defined
The passage defines two key terms: 'primary' (al-awwali) and 'universal' (al-kulli). Find the Arabic phrases that introduce each definition.
The definitions are introduced with 'بـ«الأولي»' and 'بـ«الكلي»'.
2
Extract the definition of 'primary'
Primary is defined as 'what is predicated without a middle term'. That means the predicate attaches to the subject directly, as genus or differentia. For example, 'human is animal'—the predicate 'animal' is predicated directly without needing an intermediate concept.
The text gives examples: proximate genus, proximate differentia, and real essential accident (e.g., snubness for nose).
3
Extract the definition of 'universal'
Universal is defined as 'the predicate being said of every [member] at all times'. This means the predicate applies to all individuals of the subject category without temporal restriction. Example: 'All humans are mortal' (if the text's example 'sickness' is an error, think of a proper universal like 'body is extended').
Compare with primary: primary is about immediacy of predication; universal is about scope.
4
Understand the distinction in context
In a demonstrative syllogism, premises must be both primary and universal to yield certain knowledge. A primary premise needs no further proof; a universal premise ensures the conclusion covers all cases. This distinction is foundational for evaluating the strength of a proof.
The passage also mentions 'secondary accidents' (al-a'rad al-thaniyya) as examples of essential accidents that are not primary in the strict sense? Actually, the text says 'this kind of essential [predicates] is specified by the name secondary accidents', so they are a subset.
5
Apply the distinction to an Islamic example
Consider the Kalam proof for the existence of God: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' Here, 'temporally originated' is a primary predicate of the world? Actually, it is argued via motion and change, so it may not be primary. A better example: In defining 'salah' as 'specific acts of worship with takbir, qira'a, ruku', sujud, etc.', the predicate 'act of worship' is a primary genus, while 'with takbir...' are differentiae that are primary predicates.

Suppositions and Problems in Scientific Inquiry
Author defines suppositions and problems, and discusses their role in scientific inquiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between suppositions and problems in an Islamic science, identify the three types of suppositions based on where they are proven, and explain why problem predicates must not be constitutive of the subject.

Core Idea
Suppositions are accepted premises; problems are demonstrated conclusions — their predicates cannot be part of the subject's essence.

This passage from the Qistas al-Afkar defines two foundational elements of any science: the suppositions (musadarat) that are taken as given, and the problems (masa'il) that require demonstration. Understanding this distinction is crucial for seeing how sciences like usul al-fiqh build on foundational texts and seek proofs for derived rulings. The author also clarifies a key condition: the predicates of problems must not be constitutive of the subject, lest the demonstration be tautological.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of a supposition
Note how the passage describes suppositions as propositions 'taken with doubt and denial.' They are accepted premises, not demonstrated within the science at that moment.
The Arabic term is 'musadarat'.

Classify the three types of suppositions
The passage lists three possibilities: they may be unproven in any science, proven in the same science, or proven in another science. Trace each type in the text.
Al-Farabi adds that suppositions can be relative to the student's knowledge level.
3
Define a problem (mas'ala)
A problem is a proposition that the science seeks to demonstrate. Its subject is the subject of the science, a species, or an accident.
Problems are what give the science its content and drive inquiry.

The predicate must not be constitutive (muqawwim) of the subject, because the constitutive is known without demonstration. If it were, the problem would be trivial.
An exception: if the subject is conceived only in some respect, not in its essence.

Al-Farabi explains that suppositions are often premises from another science or lengthy premises postponed. They are positions only relative to that student, not absolutely.
