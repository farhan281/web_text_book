Listing of sources referenced in Qustas al-Afkar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key primary sources referenced in Qustas al-Afkar and explain their significance for studying Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the intellectual lineage of a work – the sources that shaped its arguments.

This lesson introduces the bibliography of Qustas al-Afkar, a list of sources that the editor used. Knowing these sources is crucial for understanding the author's methodology and influences. We will learn how to interpret such a list as a scholarly tool.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main authors
Scan the list for recurring names. You will find Ibn Sina (Avicenna), Ibn Rushd (Averroes), and Ibn Nadim. These are central figures in Islamic philosophy and logic.
2
Recognize the types of works
Note that the list includes encyclopedic works (al-Shifa'), summaries (Talkhis), polemical works (Tahafut al-Tahafut), and bibliographies (al-Fihrist). This shows the range of sources used.
3
Understand the significance of al-Shifa'
Al-Shifa' by Ibn Sina is a comprehensive work covering logic, natural sciences, and metaphysics. It is a primary source for Qustas al-Afkar's logical content.
4
Note the modern editions
The bibliography lists 20th-century critical editions with editors and publishers. This indicates that the text of Qustas al-Afkar was prepared using modern scholarly standards.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing the scholarly sources used in the critical edition of Qustas al-Afkar. It begins with a header explaining that the sources include those referenced in the introduction or footnotes of the text. The bibliography then lists works by major Islamic philosophers and historians. The first entries are for Ibn Rushd (Averroes), including his Tahafut al-Tahafut, Talkhis Kitab al-Burhan, and Talkhis Kitab al-Qiyas. These are editions from the late 20th century with introductions and commentaries by various scholars. Next are works of Ibn Sina (Avicenna), such as al-Najat, al-Isharat wa al-Tanbihat, al-Mukhtasar al-Awsat fi al-Mantiq, Rasa'il Ibn Sina, Mantiq al-Mashriqiyyin, and several parts of al-Shifa' (The Cure), including the sections on logic, natural philosophy (Kitab al-Sama' wa al-'Alam), and other logical works. Also included is Ibn Sina's work on definitions, al-Hudud. Finally, the list includes Ibn Nadim's al-Fihrist, a bibliographic work, in two editions. This bibliography shows the range of primary sources used in editing Qustas al-Afkar, emphasizing the reliance on authoritative editions of Ibn Sina and Ibn Rushd, as well as the importance of Ibn Nadim's catalogue for background information.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major logicians and their key works as cited in the Qustas al-Afkar bibliography and trace their significance in the history of Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A bibliography like this is a map: it shows you which thinkers and texts shaped the logical tradition.

This passage lists the primary sources used in studying Qustas al-Afkar. While it may seem like a dry list, each entry represents a node in the network of Islamic logical thought. Understanding who these authors are and what they wrote helps you situate the Qustas within its intellectual context. In this lesson, you will learn to navigate this scholarly apparatus and use it to deepen your reading of the main text.

Guided Path
1
Scan the list for authors and titles
Identify the most frequently mentioned author (Athir al-Din al-Abhari) and list his works (e.g., Tanzil al-Afkar, Muntaha al-Afkar). Note the variety of titles: some are original treatises, others commentaries.
Observe that a single author may have multiple entries across different editions.
2
Categorize the works by type
Classify each entry as (a) a primary logical treatise, (b) a commentary or gloss, (c) a modern critical edition, or (d) a translation. For example, Aristotle's 'Mantiq Aristu' is a translation, while al-Hilli's 'al-Jawhar al-Nadid' is a commentary.
This helps you understand the genre and purpose of each source.

Note the languages and dating conventions
The bibliography uses Persian and Arabic scripts, and dates appear in Solar Hijri (Sh.), Hijri (AH), and Gregorian (CE). Recognizing these conventions prevents confusion when checking publication years.
For instance, '1357 AH' corresponds to the manuscript age, while '1396 Sh.' is a modern edition.

Identify geographical and institutional centers
Look for places like Tehran, Istanbul, Hyderabad, Calcutta, Cairo. These cities were hubs of logical studies and manuscript preservation. This reveals the global reach of Islamic logic across the Ottoman, Indian, and Persian worlds.
Publishing houses like 'Hikmat Publications' indicate ongoing scholarly interest.

Use the bibliography to locate a specific source
