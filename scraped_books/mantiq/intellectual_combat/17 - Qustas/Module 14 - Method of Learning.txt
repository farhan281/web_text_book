First Objection to Khunaji: Principles vs Subject Matter
Objection that discussions of secondary intelligibles are principles, not subject matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between principles (mabādiʾ) and subject matter (masāʾil) in a science and apply this distinction to the debate on logic's subject.

Core Idea
Not everything discussed in a science is its subject; some are principles borrowed from higher sciences.

This passage presents the first objection to Khunājī's proposal to change the subject of logic from secondary intelligibles to tasawwur and tasdiq. The objection argues that concepts like universality and particularity are not part of logic's subject matter but are principles (mabādiʾ) taken from a higher science. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly delimiting any science and for grasping the methodological structure of Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being objected to
Khunājī proposed that logic's subject matter is mental concepts (tasawwur/tasdiq), not secondary intelligibles. This objection challenges that, arguing secondary intelligibles are principles, not subject matter.
The objection is preserved in the commentary chain, not directly from Khunājī.
2
Understand the distinction between principles and subject matter
In any science, principles (mabādiʾ) are foundational truths taken from other sciences, not proven within that science. Subject matter (masāʾil) is what the science investigates. The objection says secondary intelligibles are established in a higher (more general) science, making them principles.
This mirrors the structure of fiqh: usul al-fiqh borrows principles from kalam and Arabic linguistics.
3
Apply the distinction to secondary intelligibles
Concepts like 'universality' and 'particularity' are not proven in logic; they are assumed from metaphysics. Logic only uses them as tools. Hence they are principles, not part of the subject matter.
The passage explicitly says: 'the investigation of secondary intelligibles in logic is only because they are from the principles, not from the subject matter.'
4
Recognize the implication for logic's definition
If secondary intelligibles are principles, the traditional definition of logic's subject (secondary intelligibles) can be retained. The objection thus defends the classical view against Khunājī's revision.
This is a methodological refinement, not a rejection of Khunājī's entire approach.

Why the Subject of Logic Cannot Be Concepts Themselves
If subject is tasawwur and tasdiq, logic becomes about the subject itself, not its accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the structural objection against making tasawwur and tasdiq the subject of logic.

Core Idea
A science studies accidents of its subject, not the subject itself.

This lesson addresses a key objection to Khunājī's view that the subject matter of logic is conceptual and assertoric knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq). The objection argues that if logic's subject were these mental entities, then every logical investigation would be about them directly, not about their accidental properties. This violates the fundamental structure of a science as understood in the Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of a subject in a science
Each science has a defined subject (mawḍūʿ) and studies its essential accidents (ʿawāriḍ). The subject itself is defined but not investigated directly.
2
Identify Khunājī's claim
He proposed that the subject of logic is tasawwur and tasdiq (concepts and assertions).
3
Apply the objection
Since all that logic investigates reduces to either concepts or assertions, if the subject is those, then logic investigates the subject itself, not its accidents.
4
Recognize the consequence
This makes the subject invalid because the investigation is about the same thing as the subject, violating the science's structure.
The commentator adds: 'so the subject would not be a subject.'

The Flaw in Changing Logic's Subject to Concept and Assent
Author states an objection to Khunaji's change of logic's subject

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in Khunaji's redefinition of logic's subject and explain why secondary intelligibles must be principles rather than subject.

Core Idea
Changing logic's subject to concept and assent makes logic study itself, not its accidents.

In this passage, the author critiques Khunaji's proposal to replace secondary intelligibles with concept and assent as the subject of logic. The objection reveals a fundamental principle: a science investigates the accidents of its subject, not the subject itself. By making concept and assent the subject, logic would lose its proper investigative object. Understanding this critique sharpens your grasp of how Islamic logicians defined the scope of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify Khunaji's change
Khunaji replaced the traditional subject of logic, 'secondary intelligibles' (e.g., universality, particularity), with 'concept and assent' (tasawwur and tasdiq). This is the target of the critique.
Secondary intelligibles are meta-cognitive concepts, while concept and assent are the primary products of cognition.
2
Understand the role of a science's subject
In any science, the subject is that whose accidents (properties) are studied. For example, in kalam, the subject is 'the divine attributes', not 'God's essence'. If the subject were the essence, kalam would study the essence itself, which is not its function.
This principle is known from the classification of sciences in the Islamic tradition.
3
Recognize the objection
The objection states: if logic investigates concepts and assents, then these become the subject. But logic would then be studying the subject itself (concepts and assents) rather than their accidents. This violates the proper structure of a science.
The text says 'so the subject would not be a subject' – i.e., it fails to fulfill the role of a subject.
4
Compare with the original view
Originally, secondary intelligibles were considered principles (mabadi') of logic, not its subject. They are established in a higher science (metaphysics) and used by logic as tools. Therefore, logic's investigation of them is legitimate as principles, not as subject.
This distinction between mabadi' and masa'il is crucial in Islamic epistemology.
5
Assess the consequence
The redefinition leads to a corruption (fasād) because the subject-subject relationship is violated. The science would no longer have a proper subject, as it would be studying the very thing it should use as a tool.
The author calls this 'another corruption' (فساد آخر), indicating this is a separate flaw from the earlier point about principles.

Stability of Technical Terminology
Author states that unnecessary change of accepted terminology is considered close to error by scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that changing established technical terminology without necessity is considered close to error.

Core Idea
Stable terminology protects knowledge; unnecessary change invites error.

This lesson addresses a common methodological pitfall: the misuse of the principle 'la mushaha fi al-istilahat' (no quarrel over terminology). While it is true that terminological disagreements are not to be fought over, this does not mean one can arbitrarily change established terms. The verifying scholars (al-muhassilun) regard unnecessary change as close to error. The commentator here reinforces this point, stressing that the term accepted by sound intellects should not be abandoned without necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base principle
Locate the phrase 'la mushaha fi al-istilahat' (no quarrel over terminology) in the Arabic. This is a well-known principle meaning that disagreements that are purely terminological should not be overly contested.
2
Extract the condition
Notice the immediate qualification: 'but leaving the preferable without necessity is ugly, rather it is in the strength of error according to the verifiers.' The principle does not license abandoning the preferred, established term without a pressing need.
3
Understand the commentator's reinforcement
The commentator (the 'I say' speaker) repeats and amplifies the point, adding that the term 'which the intellects have received with acceptance' should not be changed. This indicates that the established term enjoys consensual approval from sound minds.
4
Recognize the practical implication
The conclusion is that corrupting or erring in terminology occurs precisely by leaving the preferable without necessity. In practice, this means that before coining new terms, one must demonstrate a genuine need; otherwise, the change is blameworthy.

Definition of Argument and Indication
Author defines argument and indication, then divides argument into verbal and non-verbal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the definitions of dalil and dalala and classify arguments as verbal or non-verbal using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
An argument is a sign that leads to certain knowledge; indication is the property that makes it a sign.

This lesson establishes the foundational definitions of the entire logical discipline. Before we can evaluate arguments, we must know what an argument is and how it indicates its conclusion. The author gives us a crisp, memorizable definition that applies to both verbal statements and non-verbal signs.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of 'dalil'
Find the sentence that defines 'dalil' in the Arabic passage: 'الدليل هو الذي يلزم من العلم به العلم بوجود المدلول'. Note that it is a relational definition: from knowledge of the dalil, knowledge of the madlul necessarily follows.
The key phrase is 'يلزم من العلم به' – knowledge of it entails knowledge of the indicated.
2
Extract the definition of 'dalala'
Find the definition of 'dalala': 'الدلالة هي كونه بحيث يلزم من العلم به العلم بوجود المدلول'. Observe that it defines the property of being an argument, not the argument itself.
The pronoun 'كونه' refers to the argument; dalala is the attribute of leading to knowledge.
3
Distinguish dalil from dalala
The dalil is the entity (e.g., a verse, smoke), while dalala is its property of indicating. The passage defines two distinct but related concepts.
Memorize: dalil = the sign, dalala = the signification.
4
Identify the division of dalil
The passage states: 'و الدليل إما لفظ أو غيره'. The argument is either verbal (lafz) or non-verbal (ghayr lafz). Find examples for each from Islamic tradition.
A verbal argument: the Qur'anic verse 'أَقِمِ الصَّلَاةَ' is a dalil for the obligation of prayer. A non-verbal argument: the smoke from a fire indicates the presence of fire, which can be used as a sign of Allah's power.

Two Methods to Prove Non-Conversion of Propositions
Author presents two methods: counterexample (naqd) and the possibility of the contrary being true with the premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply two distinct methods (counterexample and compatibility of contradictory) to prove that a proposition does not convert or that a conclusion does not follow from given premises.

Core Idea
Two ways to prove non-entailment: a direct counterexample or showing the contradictory can coexist with the premise.

In logical demonstration, proving that a claimed conversion or conclusion is not necessary is as important as proving that it is. The standard method among logicians is the counterexample (naqd). However, our author introduces a second, more subtle method: verifying that the contradictory of the intended conclusion can be true together with the original premises without causing absurdity. This lesson unpacks both methods and shows their application in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the goal of proving non-conversion
In logical analysis, when a proposition is claimed to convert (e.g., 'Every A is B' converts to 'Some B is A'), one must verify that the conversion is valid. To prove it is not valid, you need to show a counterexample or demonstrate non-entailment.
2
Apply the counterexample method (naqd)
Find a specific instance where the original premise is true but the supposed conversion or conclusion is false. The author notes that this is the only method mentioned by earlier scholars and that he has used it throughout the work.
3
Apply the second method: compatibility of contradictory
Consider the contradictory (naqid) of the intended conclusion. Check whether it can be true simultaneously with the original premise(s) without leading to a logical absurdity (muhal). If possible, then the intended conclusion is not a necessary consequence, because the contradictory of a necessary consequence cannot be true with its necessitating ground.
This method is more abstract but equally rigorous.
4
Compare the two methods
Both methods demonstrate non-entailment. The counterexample is direct and concrete; the second method is indirect and relies on the possibility of coexistence. The author considers both as part of a complete proof.

On the Parts of Research and Guarding Them Against Error. Know that research has three parts: the principles, the middle terms, and the conclusions. The principles are the claims, the formulation of investigations, and the establishment of doctrines. The middle terms are the proofs and arguments by which the claims are proven. The conclusions are that to which the proofs and arguments lead: such as necessary [truths], and such as definitive matters that are settled in the theoretical sciences, and such as circularity, infinite regress, conjunction of two contradictories, predicating a contrary of its contrary, negating a thing of itself, equality of the greater to the smaller, preferring without a preferrer, and what is analogous to them; and in linguistic [matters]: occurrence of metaphor, homonymy, implicitness, specification, abrogation, transference, increase, decrease, applying a word to what it is not permissible to apply, and the like. And in each of these parts, there may occur disturbances and ambiguities from which error in research arises. So the researcher must examine their states to manifest the path of truth and block the door of error. These parts are not specific to the original claim; rather, they are realized with respect to every premise upon which the proof is established. As for the principles, error may occur in them due to ambiguity of claims, doctrines, and technical terms.

Commentary
This passage introduces the three essential components of any research (bahth) in the context of dialectical disputation (al-bahth wa al-munazara). The author defines 'mabadi' (principles) as the initial claims, problem statements, and doctrinal positions set forth for debate. 'Awsat' (middle terms) are the proofs and evidences used to support or attack the claims. 'Maqati' (conclusions) are the endpoints where arguments terminate—these can include self-evident truths (daruriyyat), settled matters in specific sciences, or recognized logical absurdities such as circular argument (dawr), infinite regress (tasalsul), contradiction (ijtima' al-naqidayn), and linguistic fallacies like metaphor or homonymy. The author then emphasizes that each part is susceptible to errors and ambiguities, and it is the researcher's duty to examine them carefully to follow the truth and avoid mistakes. A crucial point: these three parts apply not only to the overarching claim but to every premise within a proof, meaning that every step of a dialectical argument must be scrutinized for correctness in its principles, middle terms, and conclusions. The passage ends by noting that errors in the principles often arise from unclear claims, doctrines, or terminology. This classification provides a systematic framework for analyzing and evaluating arguments in Islamic dialectic (jadal).

Errors in Research: The Need for Vigilance
Warns against errors in each part of research.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of errors that can occur in each part of research and explain why the researcher must examine all three.

Core Idea
Error lurks in every part of research; the truth-seeker must scrutinize principles, proofs, and endpoints.

After defining the three parts, the author warns that each part is vulnerable to disturbances and ambiguities. He lists numerous examples of what can go wrong, especially in the conclusions, and stresses that the researcher must examine all aspects to uncover the truth and block error.

Guided Path
1
Find the author's warning
The text states: 'في كل واحد من هذه الأجزاء قد تقع اختلالات وإيهامات' – in each part disturbances and ambiguities can occur. This is the central warning.
2
List the errors in conclusions
The author gives a long list of maqati' that are problematic: circularity, infinite regress, contradictions, etc. Also linguistic errors like metaphor and homonymy.
These are endpoints that indicate an invalid argument.
3
Understand the extension to every premise
The text says: 'و هذه الأجراء لا تختص بأصل الدعوى بل هي متحققة بالنسبة إلى كل مقدمة' – these parts apply to every premise, not just the main claim. So every step must be checked.
4
Focus on errors in principles
The author specifically mentions that principles can go wrong due to ambiguity of claims, doctrines, and technical terms (إيهام الدعاوي و المذاهب و الاصطلاحات).
Clear definitions are crucial.
