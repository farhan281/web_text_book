Samarqandi's Solution: Essential vs Descriptive Propositions
Samarqandi solves the puzzle of absolute unknown by distinguishing essential and descriptive propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential (dhatiyya) and descriptive (wasfiyya) propositions and explain how this distinction resolves the puzzle of the absolute unknown.

Core Idea
Knowing something under a description is knowing, even if its essence remains unknown.

How can we say anything about something completely unknown? This puzzle, known as the 'absolute unknown,' challenges the very possibility of predication. Samarqandi's elegant solution lies in recognizing two types of propositions: those that speak of a thing's essence and those that speak of a thing under a description. This distinction allows meaningful discourse about unknowns and is fundamental to logic and Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Puzzle
The absolute unknown (majhul mutlaq) is something about which nothing is known at all. The puzzle is: how can any proposition about it be meaningful?
This challenges the very possibility of predication about unknowns.
2
Understand Essential Propositions
An essential proposition (qadiyya dhatiyya) predicates something of the essence (dhat) of a thing. It claims that a property belongs to the thing by virtue of its very essence.
Such propositions require knowledge of the essence.
3
Understand Descriptive Propositions
A descriptive proposition (qadiyya wasfiyya) predicates something of a thing under a description (wasf), not of its essence. It says: 'Everything that is described as X is Y'.
No knowledge of essence is needed; only the description is needed.
4
Apply the Distinction
The puzzle is solved: statements about the absolute unknown are possible as descriptive propositions. E.g., 'The unknown entity is an object of thought' is a descriptive proposition, because it predicates something under the description 'object of thought'.
Thus we can speak about the unknown without claiming to know its essence.

Author defines the term 'موجبة سالبة المحمول' and explains its equivalence to negative propositions.

Core Idea
An affirmative with a negated predicate is equivalent to a negative but presupposes subject existence.

This passage from the Qustas introduces a subtle but important distinction: a proposition like 'Every J is not B' can be interpreted either as a simple negative or as an affirmative with a negative predicate. The author, following Khunaji and Armeni, calls this latter type 'موجبة سالبة المحمول'. The Truth of Negation vs Affirmation in Mental Existence
Author states when negation is true while affirmation is false regarding mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a negative proposition can be true without the subject's mental existence, while an affirmative proposition requires it.

Core Idea
Negation does not require subject existence; affirmation does.

In Islamic logic, the truth conditions of propositions depend on whether the subject has mental existence. This passage from al-Qustas clarifies that negation (salab) can be true even when the subject does not exist in the mind, but affirmation (ijab) presupposes the subject's existence. This rule is critical for statements about non-existent entities, such as 'the partner of God is impossible'—a negative statement that is true without requiring the partner to exist mentally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition types
In the Arabic, find the negative proposition (السلب) and the affirmative proposition (الإيجاب). The negative example: 'ليس المعدوم مطلقاً بمتصوّر'. The affirmative counterpart considered but rejected: 'المعدوم مطلقاً هو لا متصور'.
2
Analyze the existential condition
According to the author, the negative proposition does not require the subject (المعدوم) to have mental existence; it can be true even if the subject is absolutely non-existent. The affirmative proposition, however, would imply the existence of the subject because the predicate 'inconceivable' (لا متصور) is attached to a subject that must exist for the predication to be meaningful.
3
Understand the reason for the rule
The author mentions 'لمر من اقتضائه وجود الموضوع' meaning that affirmative propositions presuppose the existence of the subject. This is a standard logical principle: affirmative propositions imply the subject's existence (at least mentally), while negative propositions do not.
The passage also rejects using 'adl (deflection) to turn the negation into an affirmation, because that would still involve a positive predication.
4
Apply the rule to a theological example
Consider the statement: 'The partner of God (شريك الباري) is non-existent.' This is a negative proposition (the partner is not existent). It can be true without the partner having any mental existence. However, the affirmative 'The partner of God is impossible' would require the partner's existence in the mind for the affirmation to be meaningful.

Coincidental Conditionals: When Non-Contradiction is Not Required
Author clarifies a condition for conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between necessary conditionals and coincidental conditionals (ittifaqiyya) based on whether non-contradiction between antecedent and consequent is required.

Core Idea
Not all conditionals require logical connection; some are merely coincidental.

In Islamic logic, conditional propositions (al-shartiyyat) are often assumed to require a real connection between the antecedent and consequent. However, the author of our passage refines this by stating that 'non-contradiction' is not a condition for what are called al-ittifaqiyya—coincidental conditionals. This distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing conditional statements in Islamic sciences, where some conditionals are merely coincidental and do not imply a necessary causal link.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refined
The passage states that the shaykh (the base author) originally said something about 'non-contradiction' being a condition. Your first step is to note that the author is responding to that claim by clarifying its scope. The refinement applies specifically to al-ittifaqiyya.
The shaykh's original statement is not given; we only have the refinement.
2
Understand what 'non-contradiction' means in this context
Non-contradiction (adam al-munafah) between antecedent and consequent means that if one is true and the other false, there is no logical conflict. In necessary conditionals, such conflict is impossible. Here, the author says this is not required for ittifaqiyya.
Think of it as: in a coincidental conditional, the antecedent and consequent can be unrelated.
3
Define al-ittifaqiyya
Al-ittifaqiyya is a conditional where the connection is merely coincidental—the truth of the antecedent does not cause the truth of the consequent, nor does its falsity cause the falsity of the consequent. It is a degenerate case of conditional.
Example: 'If the sun rises, then I am writing' is coincidental; the two events have no inherent connection.
4
Apply the refinement to a real example
Take a conditional from fiqh: 'If the traveler shortens the prayer, then his journey is not sinful.' This is a necessary conditional because shortening is tied to travel. Now compare: 'If the rooster crows, then the prayer time has entered.' This may be coincidental (the crowing is not the cause of prayer time but occurs around the same time).
In usul al-fiqh, distinguishing necessary from coincidental conditionals affects how legal rulings are derived from conditional statements.

Core Idea
Abu al-Barakat shows that 'dhu' is a relational link, not part of the real predicate.

Ibn Sina treated the entire phrase 'dhū ʿilm' as the predicate, but Abu al-Barakat objects that 'dhū' is a relational particle pointing to possession, and the true predicate is simply 'ʿilm' (knowledge). This distinction matters in logical analysis because it changes how the proposition is broken down for syllogistic use.

Identify the disputed phrase

He argues that 'dhū' indicates a relation (nisba) and is external to the predicate; the real predicate is 'ʿilm' (knowledge).

3
Compare the two analyses
Consider the implications: if 'dhū' is part of the predicate, then the predicate is composite; if not, the predicate is simple. This affects conversion, syllogistic forms, etc.
In later Islamic logic, this debate influenced the treatment of composite predicates.

The truth of a proposition depends on whether the negations differ in meaning, not just in wording.

In the study of logical propositions, the presence of negation can affect truth value. However, not every difference in the number or placement of negation particles changes the meaning. Why Time and Place Are Not Part of the Predicate Unity
Author argues that time and place are not included under the unity of the predicate in contradiction conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Farabi's conditional argument against including time and place under the unity of the predicate, and explain why the infinite regress objection is valid.

Core Idea
Including time in the predicate leads to an infinite regress; therefore time is external.

In determining contradiction, later logicians considered time and place part of the predicate's unity. Al-Farabi (and the author of Lawami' al-Asrar) reject this. Understanding why time and place are external is crucial for correctly assessing contradictory pairs. This lesson walks through the conditional argument and the critical refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being challenged
Find the claim that time and place can be included under the unity of the predicate. In the Arabic, it is 'اندراج الزمان والمكان تحت وحدة المحمول' (inclusion of time and place under the unity of the predicate). This is attributed to 'later people' (قوم من المتأخرين).
2
Extract the conditional reasoning
The author argues: 'If their inclusion in the predicate were considered, it would necessitate that time has another time and place has another place' (لَو اعتُبِرَ دُخولُهُما في المحمول، يَلزمُ أن يكونَ للزمان زمانٌ آخر وللمكان مكانٌ آخر). This is a conditional: if P (inclusion) then Q (regress).
The antecedent is the inclusion; the consequent is the impossible regress.
3
Understand the key concept: adverbiality
Time and place attach to the proposition through the adverbial relation (الظرفية). The relation of predicate to subject occurs in time/place, but the time/place themselves are not part of the predicate. They are external adverbs, just as 'in Ramadan' modifies the verb but is not the verb itself.
The term 'ظرف' means container or adverb; it indicates when/where the relation holds.
4
Trace the objection and response
An objector says: time is external to both terms anyway (الزمان خارج من طرفي القضية). The author responds: even if time were inside the predicate, the regress occurs. The objection fails because adverbiality is realized after the relation, so time cannot be part of the predicate without circularity.
This reinforces the conclusion that time/place must remain external.
5
Apply to contradiction conditions
For two propositions to contradict, they must differ in the predicate itself (e.g., 'wajib' vs. 'not wajib'), not merely in time or place modifiers. For example, 'Zakat is wajib in Ramadan' and 'Zakat is not wajib in Shawwal' do not directly contradict because the time is external; the predicate 'wajib' could be the same. True contradiction requires difference in the core predicate.
This refinement prevents false contradictions in legal reasoning.

Core Idea

Khunaji's Method for Single Contradictories
Khunaji's method for single contradictory of composites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Khunaji's method to derive a single proposition that is the contradictory of any composite proposition.

Core Idea
Every composite proposition reduces to a single affirmative contradictory via subject restriction.

Khunaji developed an elegant method to reduce the contradictory of any composite proposition—whether conditional or disjunctive, universal or particular—to a single affirmative proposition. This simplifies proofs by contradiction in Islamic logic and legal theory. In this lesson, we will break down his procedure step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite and its first part
Determine the type of composite (conditional, disjunctive, etc.) and locate its first part (al-juz' al-awwal). The first part's modality and subject-predicate structure will be used.
For a conditional 'If A then B', the first part is A. For a disjunctive 'Either A or B', the first part is A.
2
Determine the quality of the composite
Is the composite affirmative or negative? Affirmative composites have no negation on the connective; negative composites have a negation. This decides which transformation to apply.
An affirmative conditional is 'If A then B'; a negative conditional is 'It is not the case that if A then B'.
3
Extract subject and predicate from the first part
Take the subject (mawḍūʿ) and predicate (maḥmūl) of the first part. For example, if the first part is 'Zayd stands', subject is 'Zayd', predicate is 'stands'.
4
Apply the affirmative or negative rule
For affirmative composites: new subject = original subject restricted by the contradictory of the original predicate; new predicate = original predicate. For negative composites: new subject = original subject restricted by the original predicate; new predicate = contradictory of the original predicate.
Restriction means adding a condition: e.g., 'Zayd who does not stand' for the contradictory of 'stands'.
5
Form the single affirmative proposition
Combine the new subject and new predicate into a single affirmative categorical proposition. The modality (e.g., necessity, possibility) is taken from the first part's modality.
The resulting proposition is claimed to be equivalent to the contradictory of the original composite.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational relationship in Islamic logic: the two main types of disjunctive propositions—مانعة الجمع (exclusive: not both true) and مانعة الخلو (inclusive: not both false)—are interdefinable. This transformation is crucial for converting propositions in syllogistic arguments, especially in juristic reasoning where disjunctions appear frequently.

Guided Path

The author says each entails the other from the contradictories of its parts: given a مانعة الجمع of A and B, you get a مانعة الخلو of ¬A and ¬B, and vice versa.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic dialectical exchange. Dialectical discussion on a property of nouns

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A claim about language can become self-contradictory if its own subject violates the rule.

This passage presents a dialectical exchange about a core property of nouns in Arabic grammar: that only nouns can be the subject of a report (khabar). This lesson will guide you through unpacking this logical and linguistic nuance.

Essential vs. Derivative Predication
Author defines and contrasts essential and derivative predication with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential (muwata'a) and derivative (ishtiqaq) predication and identify the correct type for given examples.

Core Idea
Essential predication identifies; derivative predication describes.

In Arabic logic, not all predications are equal. Some predicates are identical in meaning to their subjects, while others are derived from a source term and imply a relation. This distinction is fundamental for understanding how universals are predicated of particulars and appears throughout Islamic theology and jurisprudence. The passage you are about to study defines these two types with clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types defined

The phrase 'بـ«هو هو»' literally means 'by is is', indicating identity.
2

Consider 'الإنسان هو حيوان' (The human is an animal). The author says the subject is exactly the predicate. Ask: why is this essential? Because 'animal' is part of the definition of 'human'.
Essential predication does not require numerical identity; it requires identity of meaning.

Derivative predication always involves a relation, here 'possessor of'.

Author explains why some conditional forms are not disjunctive and criticizes al-Razi's view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which conjunctive conditionals are excluded from disjunctives and explain why based on the distinction between direct meaning (muṭābaqa) and implication (iltizām).

Core Idea
A conditional that only negates one side is not a true disjunction.

In this passage, the author refines the definition of disjunctive conditionals. He explains that certain seemingly disjunctive conditionals, such as 'If it is human, it is not a stone' or 'It is not the case that if it is human, it is a stone', are actually not disjunctives of opposition. They only negate one side, not assert mutual exclusion. Even if the mind infers opposition, that inference is by implication (iltizām), not by direct correspondence (muṭābaqa). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly classifying logical propositions.

Refuting al-Razi on Categorical vs Conditional Propositions
Author refutes al-Razi's claim about categoricals resembling conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a categorical judgment of entailment from a conditional proposition based on the criterion of singular vs compound.

Core Idea
Not every statement of entailment is a conditional; the key is whether the relata are propositions or singular terms.

Imam al-Razi claimed that expressions like 'sunrise entails day' are judgments of entailment but not conditional propositions. The author of this passage firmly rejects that view. Understanding why requires you to grasp a foundational distinction in Islamic logic: the difference between singular terms (mufrad) and compound propositions (shartiyya). This lesson will sharpen your ability to classify such statements correctly.

Guided Path
1

2
Extract the author's counter-claim

The author dismisses al-Razi's view as incorrect.
3
Understand 'mufrad' in context
In Islamic logic, 'mufrad' (singular) refers to a term that signifies a single concept, not a compound proposition. A conditional proposition (shartiyya) is a compound consisting of two propositions joined by a condition.
The author implies that 'sunrise entails day' is a categorical judgment about two concepts, not a conditional with a protasis and apodosis.
4
Apply the distinction to the examples
Analyze 'sunrise entails day' – it asserts a necessary relation between the concept of sunrise and the concept of day. Both are singular terms, not full propositions. Therefore, it is a categorical judgment, not a conditional.
In a conditional, both parts would be propositions that can be true or false independently.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Negation is parasitic on affirmation; you cannot negate without first affirming.

The author argues that negation can only be understood as attached to an underlying affirmation, making affirmation logically and epistemologically prior. Parts of a Categorical Proposition and the Copula
Author defines the three parts of categorical proposition and explains the copula.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject, predicate, and relation in any simple categorical proposition, and distinguish between temporal and non-temporal copulas.

Core Idea
A categorical proposition is built from three elements: subject, predicate, and the relation between them.

Every categorical proposition (al-qadiyya al-hamliyya) has a structure that logicians analyze into three essential parts. Understanding these parts — subject, predicate, and the relation (copula) — is the first step in analyzing arguments. This passage defines these components and introduces two types of copula.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject (al-mawḍūʿ)
Look for the term about which something is said. In an Arabic categorical proposition, it is often the first noun or pronoun, e.g., 'al-ʿālam' (the world) in 'al-ʿālamu ḥādithun'.
2
Identify the predicate (al-maḥmūl)
Find the term that is affirmed or denied of the subject. In 'al-ʿālamu ḥādithun', the predicate is 'ḥādith' (temporally originated).
3
Locate the relation (an-nisba al-ḥukmiyya)
Determine whether the proposition is affirmative or negative. The relation is the judgment connecting subject and predicate. In Arabic, it may be implicit in the nominal sentence structure or expressed by a copula.
4
Classify the copula (ar-rābiṭa)
If the relation is explicitly expressed by a word, that word is the copula. Check if it is a verb like 'kāna' (was) or 'wujida' (exists) — then it is temporal. If it is a pronoun like 'huwa' (he/it) or 'hiya' (she/it), it is non-temporal.
The copula is a particle that does not stand alone; it indicates the relation.
