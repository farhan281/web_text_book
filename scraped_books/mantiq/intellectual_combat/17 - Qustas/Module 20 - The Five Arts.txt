The Five Arts of Syllogism
Author classifies the five arts of reasoning based on material and effect on assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given syllogism into one of the five arts based on its material and purpose.

Core Idea
The five arts are distinguished by whether they aim at assent and whether they consider truth.

This passage from al-Qusṭās introduces a fundamental classification in Islamic logic: the five arts or types of qiyās. They are distinguished not by their form but by their material (the kind of premises) and their effect on the listener's assent. Understanding this classification is crucial for evaluating the strength of arguments encountered in kalām, fiqh, and tafsīr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching purpose
Every syllogism must be useful (mufīda). The first division is whether this usefulness is for assent or for something else.
The author starts with 'because they must be useful'.
2
First fork: assent or not?
If the syllogism does not aim at assent (taṣdīq), it is poetry (shiʿr). If it aims at assent, proceed.
Poetry aims at imaginative stimulation (takhyīl).
3
Second fork: decisive or not?
If the assent is not decisive (ghayr jāzim), it is rhetoric (khiṭāba). If decisive, proceed.
Rhetoric uses persuasive premises (muqniʿāt).
4
Third fork: truth considered or not?
If the truth of the premises is not considered, it is either dialectic (jadal) if the premises are generally accepted or come from the opponent, or eristic (shaghab) if they only appear to be accepted. If truth is considered, check if the premises are actually true: if yes, demonstration (burhān); if no, sophistry (safsata).
The author notes that sophistry and eristic are one type.

On the types of syllogisms from the totality of their materials and their effect on assent and other than it. They are five because they must be useful. Their usefulness: either for other than assent, and it is 'poetry'; or for assent. If it is not decisive, it is 'rhetoric'. Otherwise, either its being true is not considered in it: that is 'dialectic' if general acceptance of people or the opponent is also considered and it is so, and 'eristic' if considered and it is not so; and if the assent's being true is considered, then if it is true in itself, it is 'demonstration'; and if not, it is 'sophistry'. And it together with eristic is one type.

Commentary
This passage classifies the five types of qiyas (syllogisms) according to their material (mādda) and their effect on tasdīq (assent). The author states that any syllogism must be useful, and this usefulness is divided into two main categories: either it does not produce assent, which is poetry (shi‘r) – used for imaginative stimulation – or it produces assent. Assent-producing arguments are further divided into non-decisive (ghayr jāzim) which is rhetoric (khiṭāba), and decisive. Decisive arguments are divided into those where the truth of the premises is not considered – here we find dialectic (jadal), which relies on generally accepted premises or the opponent's admissions, and eristic (shaghab), which uses premises that appear accepted but are not. If the truth of the assent is considered, then if the premises are true in themselves, it is demonstration (burhān); otherwise it is sophistry (safsata). The author notes that sophistry and eristic are essentially one type. The classification is based on the epistemic quality of the premises and the purpose of the argument.

Checkpoint
According to the classification, which art uses premises that are not necessarily true but are generally accepted by people?
A) Demonstration (burhān)
B) Rhetoric (khiṭāba)
C) Dialectic (jadal)
D) Poetry (shi‘r)

Key Takeaway
The classification of syllogisms into five arts depends on the epistemic quality of their premises and their intended effect on assent, with demonstration being the only one that yields certain truth.
