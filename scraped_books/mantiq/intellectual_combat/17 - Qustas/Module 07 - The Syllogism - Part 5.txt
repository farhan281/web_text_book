Analyzing a Doubt on the First Figure in Conditional Syllogisms
Author presents a doubt and two solutions on the first figure in implicative conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a doubt against the first figure for implicative conditionals and evaluate the validity of the proposed solutions.

Core Idea
When antecedents are impossible, conditional syllogisms require careful truth evaluation of premises.

One of the most subtle challenges in conditional logic is the problem of impossible antecedents. Al-Shaykh (Ibn Sina) raised a doubt that threatens the validity of the first figure for implicative conditionals, and his own solution—and that of al-Khunaji—reveal deep issues about how we evaluate premises in conditional syllogisms. In this lesson, we will dissect this critical exchange and understand its implications for logical reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Formalize the logical problem
Identify the two conditional premises and the conclusion of the proposed syllogism. Let A = 'two is odd', B = 'two is a number', C = 'two is even'. The syllogism is: if A then B; if B then C; therefore if A then C.
This is a hypothetical syllogism (transitivity of implication).
2
Examine the truth of the premises
Check whether A→B and B→C are true in the ordinary sense. Note that A (two is odd) is impossible. The truth of conditionals with impossible antecedents is debatable: they can be considered vacuously true or false depending on the semantic theory.
Al-Shaykh argues that A→B is false because from impossibility anything can follow, but specifically the consequent 'B' is not entailed.
3
Analyze al-Shaykh's solution
He denies the minor premise (A→B). His reasoning: on the assumption that two is odd, it is not a number because nothing that is odd and two is a number. Therefore the conditional is false, so the syllogism is invalid due to a false premise.
This solution requires a specific interpretation of conditionals with impossible antecedents.
4
Analyze al-Khunaji's solution
He denies that the major premise (B→C) is universally true. His reasoning: among the ways of being a number is the state of being odd (if we entertain that impossible state), and that state contradicts being even. Thus the major premise is not true for all cases of B.
Al-Khunaji's solution is presented as 'the better' approach.
5
Assess the implications for conditional logic
Both solutions show that the simple transitivity of implication fails if we allow impossible antecedents without careful semantic restrictions. This connects to debates about counterfactuals in usul al-fiqh and kalam.
Understanding this nuance is crucial for using conditional arguments in Islamic legal theory.

Ibn Sina's Critique of the First Figure in Conditional Syllogisms and al-Khunaji's Resolution
Ibn Sina critiques the first figure in conditional syllogisms; al-Khunaji offers an alternative solution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyse the validity of a conditional syllogism in the first figure when the antecedent of the minor is impossible, and evaluate Ibn Sina's and al-Khunaji's solutions.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism with an impossible antecedent can appear to break the first figure.

This passage presents a famous critique by Ibn Sina against the first figure of conditional syllogisms. He provides a counterexample that seems to show the first figure yields a false conclusion from true premises. However, his initial solution is contested, and al-Khunaji offers a more refined resolution. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to evaluate conditional arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the alleged counterexample
Ibn Sina constructs two conditionals: 'Whenever two is odd, it is a number' (minor) and 'Whenever two is a number, it is even' (major). These should entail 'Whenever two is odd, it is even', but that conclusion is false. Note that the structure matches the first figure: the antecedent of the major (two is a number) equals the consequent of the minor (it is a number).
2
Analyze Ibn Sina's initial solution
He says the minor is false because its antecedent (two being odd) is impossible; thus the conclusion is not validly drawn. Understand the reasoning: an impossible antecedent can entail anything, so the conditional may be vacuously true but the syllogism fails because the minor's truth is not genuine.
3
Examine the objection to Ibn Sina's solution
A critic points out that this would invalidate all conditionals with impossible antecedents, yet Ibn Sina himself accepts such conditionals as true (e.g., 'Whenever a thing is a void, it is in space'). So his solution is too sweeping.
4
Evaluate al-Khunaji's alternative
He denies the universal truth of the major premise: 'Whenever two is a number, it is even' is not true in all modes because one mode of being a number (odd) contradicts evenness. Thus the major is not universally valid, and the first figure is safe if both premises are properly modalised.
5
Formalize the correct logical analysis
In a valid conditional syllogism of the first figure, the middle term must be taken in the same mode in both premises. Here, the mode 'odd' in the minor is incompatible with the mode required for the major to be true. So the apparent counterexample is resolved by noting the mode shift.

The Solution of al-Atahri: Preserving the First Figure
Author presents the solution to the doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Atahri's solution to the doubt and explain how it resolves the apparent failure of the major premise.

Core Idea
Falsity under assumption implies contrariety, leading to contradiction unless the middle term is repeated.

In this lesson, we delve into al-Atahri's intricate solution to the doubt. He argues that if the major premise were false under the minor's antecedent, then the minor's antecedent is contrary to the implication in the major, leading to either the falsity of the minor or a non-repeated middle term. This preserves the validity of the first figure.

Guided Path
1
Assume the doubt's scenario
Suppose the major premise (true in reality) is false under the assumption of the minor's antecedent. This is the doubt's claim.
2
Derive contrariety
From falsity under assumption, conclude that the minor's antecedent is contrary (munafin) to the implication (luzum) of the major's antecedent for its consequent. In other words, assuming the minor's antecedent negates the implication that naturally holds.
Contrariety here means incompatibility; two propositions cannot both be true.
3
Apply the natural implication principle
The implication is natural to the major's antecedent. Whatever is contrary to the implication is contrary to the major's antecedent itself. So the minor's antecedent is contrary to the major's antecedent.
This step connects contrariety of implication to contrariety of antecedents.
4
Consider two cases
Now, either the major's antecedent is identical to the minor's consequent, or it is not. (Case 1) If identical, then the minor premise would be false because an antecedent cannot be contrary to its own consequent. (Case 2) If not identical, then the middle term is not repeated, and the syllogism collapses.
Both cases lead to a contradiction with the assumption that the doubt is a genuine counterexample.
5
Conclude the contradiction
Thus the doubt's assumption (major false under assumption) is impossible. Therefore the major premise does remain true under the minor's antecedent, and the first figure remains productive.
The solution uses a reductio ad absurdum within the same framework.

Author presents a doubt by al-Khunaji on the third figure of conditional syllogisms and a response.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A conjunction does not imply a conditional without a necessary connection.

Al-Khunaji raised a subtle doubt against the third figure of conditional syllogisms, using two unrelated events: Zaid eating and Amr drinking. While their conjunction implies each individually, the conditional 'if Zaid eats, then Amr drinks' is clearly false. Some scholars responded by arguing that the conditional can hold under certain assumptions—when the antecedent is taken together with the consequent or its necessary condition. Guided Path

Identify the third figure structure for conditionals
In the third figure of categorical syllogisms, the middle term is the subject of both premises. For conditionals, a parallel structure might be: from 'whenever C, then A' and 'whenever C, then B' infer 'whenever A, then B' or similar. Take two unrelated events: Zaid eats (A) and Amr drinks (B). It is true that whenever both occur (A and B), then A occurs, and whenever both occur, then B occurs. The example is deliberately absurd to highlight the lack of logical connection.

Some scholars deny that the conditional is false. They argue: if we assume A together with B (or with what necessitates B), then in that context B does follow from A. For instance, if Zaid's eating is assumed alongside Amr's drinking as a concurrent state, then 'if Zaid eats, then Amr drinks' holds in that particular scenario. This shows that conditionals can be true under specific assumptions even if no absolute connection exists.
The response highlights that conditional truth depends on the circumstances under which the antecedent is considered.

The third figure pattern for conditionals is not universally valid; it only works when the two events are connected by a necessary consequence (luzum). The doubt forces logicians to refine the conditions under which conditional syllogisms are sound, ensuring that the antecedent is a real cause or necessary condition of the consequent.

Straight Proof for Conditional Syllogism (First Form)
Author provides a direct proof for a conditional syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct a straight proof for a conditional syllogism and identify its antecedent, consequent, and middle term.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism is proven by affirming its antecedent to derive its consequent.

In this passage, the author demonstrates a direct proof (burhan mustaqim) for a valid conditional syllogism. The proof uses two premises: a universal conditional and a particular proposition that serves as the antecedent of the conclusion. By following the logical flow, we see how the conclusion necessarily follows. This method is foundational for validating conditional arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion's antecedent and consequent
The conclusion is a conditional: 'It is not the case that whenever W is Z then every A is H.' Its antecedent is the second premise: 'It is not the case that if every A is H then some B is H.' The consequent is the negation of another conditional.
The conclusion is the consequent of the first premise.
2
Locate the first premise
The first premise is a universal conditional: 'Whenever (it is not the case that if every A is H then some B is H), then it is not the case that whenever W is Z then every A is H.' This is of the form 'If P then Q'.
P is the antecedent of the conclusion; Q is the conclusion itself.
3
Locate the second premise
The second premise is: 'It is not the case that if every A is H then some B is H.' This exactly matches the antecedent (P) of the first premise.
This premise affirms the antecedent of the first premise.
4
Apply modus ponens
From the first premise (If P then Q) and the second premise (P), we derive Q by modus ponens. So the conclusion follows directly.
This is the core of the straight proof.

Classification of Disjunctive Compound Syllogisms

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two disjunctives yield valid conclusions only when the middle term is fully or partially shared.

In the previous lesson, we studied simple disjunctive syllogisms. Now we turn to compound ones, where both premises are disjunctive. The structure depends on how the middle term relates to each disjunct. Al-Khunaji divides them into three types. Understanding this classification allows you to systematically derive valid conclusions from two exclusive 'either-or' statements, a crucial skill in Islamic legal reasoning where complex rulings often involve disjunctive conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disjunctive premises
Locate the two munfasila (disjunctive) propositions. Each has two parts (disjuncts). For example: 'Either A is B or A is C' and 'Either A is D or A is E'.
2
Analyze the middle term (al-awsat)
Determine the middle term that appears in both premises. Is it a complete part of each disjunction, an incomplete part, or complete in one and incomplete in the other? 'Complete part' means the entire disjunct itself, not just a part of it.
The middle term could be a term that itself is one of the disjuncts, or a part of it.
3
Assign the division
Based on the middle term's status, assign the syllogism to one of three divisions: (1) middle is complete in both, (2) incomplete in both, (3) complete in one and incomplete in the other.
In division 1, the minor and major premises are distinguished only by position, not by nature.
4
Apply the rule for true disjunctives (haqiqiyyatayn)
If both disjunctives are exclusive (haqiqiyya, meaning exactly one true), then division 1 produces: two affirmative conditionals, two negative conditionals, and two exclusive-disjunctives (mani'at al-jam'). Trace the logic: each disjunct entails the negation of the middle term, which entails the other side.
This step focuses on the first division as given in the passage.

Three Divisions of Compound Disjunctive Syllogisms
Author classifies compound disjunctive syllogisms into three types based on middle term completeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify compound disjunctive syllogisms based on the completeness of the middle term and identify the logical forms that follow from true exclusive disjunctions.

Core Idea
Compound disjunctive syllogisms are classified by whether the middle term is complete, incomplete, or mixed.

Building on simple disjunctive syllogisms, al-Khūnajī now examines compound ones where both premises are disjunctions. His classification by the middle term's completeness prepares the ground for analyzing complex disjunctive chains in theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three divisions
The middle term (al-awsat) can be a complete part of each premise, an incomplete part of each, or complete in one and incomplete in the other. These form the three divisions.
A 'complete part' here means the entire proposition or a whole conjunct of a disjunction.
2
Focus on the first division
When the middle term is a complete part of both premises, the minor and major are not distinguished by nature but by position (bi al-wad'). This division is the most productive.
3
Derive conclusions from true exclusive disjunctions
If both disjunctive premises are true exclusive disjunctions (haqiqiyayn), they yield affirmative conditionals from each side, negative conditionals, and also derived disjunctives (mani'at al-jam' and mani'at al-khuluww).
Each side's negation of the middle term entails the other side, forming conditional chains.
4
Extend to weaker disjunctions
The same derivations hold for exclusive disjunctions of incompatibility (mani'at al-jam') and, when broadly interpreted, for exclusive disjunctions of exhaustiveness (mani'at al-khuluww).
The truth of the original disjunctives follows from the derived ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the correct conditional conclusion when given a categorical premise and a disjunctive premise, including identifying when no conclusion follows.

Core Idea
Affirmative categorical-disjunctive pairs produce conditional conclusions, except when a particular categorical meets an exclusive disjunction.

When one premise is categorical (ḥaqīqiyya) and the other is disjunctive (ghayr al-ḥaqīqiyya), the rules for producing a conditional conclusion are precise. This lesson covers the three main cases from Qustas: both universal affirmatives, mixed quantifiers, and negative disjunctive premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises
Determine which premise is categorical (simple affirmative/negative) and which is disjunctive. Identify the type of disjunction: exclusive (māni‘at al-jam‘) or inclusive (māni‘at al-khuluw).
2
Check if both are universal affirmative
If both premises are universal affirmatives, the conclusion is a universal affirmative conditional. Its antecedent depends on the disjunction type: if the categorical is with an exclusive disjunction, the antecedent is the side of the disjunction; if with an inclusive disjunction, the antecedent is the side of the categorical.
The 'side' refers to one of the disjuncts or the categorical term.
3
Consider mixed quantifiers
If one premise is particular (whether categorical or disjunctive), the conclusion is a particular affirmative conditional, with the antecedent determined as above. However, there is an exception: if the particular premise is categorical and the universal premise is an exclusive disjunction, then no conclusion follows at all.
The author gives an example: 'It may be that a thing is either growing or inanimate' (particular possibility) and 'Always it is either inanimate or growing' (universal exclusive) — here a conditional with implication (luzūm) is valid. But changing the major to 'always it is either inanimate or ensouled' yields only opposition (ʿinād).
4
Handle negative disjunctive
If the disjunctive premise is negative and both premises are universal, a universal negative conditional follows. This is proven by contradiction: assuming the opposite leads to equality between the contradictory of the middle term and the side of the categorical, which falsifies the negative premise.
This rule ensures logical entailment even with a negative disjunctive.
5
Apply to examples
Test the rules on the examples in the passage to see the difference between necessary implication (luzūm) and mere opposition (ʿinād).
The example shows that not all combinations yield a true conditional; sometimes only a weaker relation holds.

Main Rule for Disjunctive Syllogisms with Non-Genuine Negative
Author states the main rule for non-genuine negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid disjunctive syllogism with two universal premises, one non-genuine negative, and verify the conclusion using reduction to the contrary.

Core Idea
When the negative premise is non-genuine and both premises are universal, a universal negative conditional conclusion necessarily follows.

This lesson covers the central rule for disjunctive syllogisms when both premises are universal and one is negative, provided the negative is non-genuine (ghayr haqiqiyya). Understanding this rule allows you to derive valid conclusions in logical arguments involving exclusive or exhaustive disjunctions. The proof relies on reduction to the contrary and the concept of the middle term's contradictory being equal to the term of the genuine premise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises and types
Ensure both premises are universal and one is negative. Determine if the negative is genuine or non-genuine. Non-genuine means the separation is not absolute in reality.
2
Extract middle term and its contradictory
In a disjunctive syllogism, the middle term connects the two premises. Its contradictory is said to equal the term of the genuine (non-negative) premise.
This equality is key for reduction to the contrary.
3
Apply the principle of equality to the necessary
What is equal to the necessary (lazim) is itself necessary, and what is necessary for the equal is necessary. This links the terms.
4
Derive the conclusion
By reduction to the contrary, a universal negative conditional conclusion (e.g., 'Every A is not B') follows. Verify that the negative premise would be falsified if the conclusion were false.
5
Check for exceptions
If the contradictory of the middle is more specific than the term of exclusive disjunction or more general than the term of exhaustive disjunction, the conclusion is affirmative, not negative.

Exception: Genuine Negative Premise in Disjunctive Syllogisms
Author explains exception when negative is genuine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a disjunctive syllogism with two universal premises and one negative yields no conclusion, specifically when the negative is genuine (haqiqiyya).

Core Idea
A genuine negative premise in a disjunctive syllogism with two universal premises produces no valid conclusion, regardless of the disjunction type.

In the previous lesson, we learned the main rule for disjunctive syllogisms with non-genuine negatives. Now we examine the critical exception: if the negative premise is genuine (haqiqiyya), no conclusion follows. This holds for both exclusive (mani'at al-jam') and exhaustive (mani'at al-khuluw) disjunctions. The reason is that the genuine negative can be true in multiple ways, leading to inconsistency. The author also explains why the necessary term is restricted to 'equal' (musawi) to avoid false particular inferences.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the negative is genuine
A genuine negative indicates absolute separation between subject and predicate in reality, without any potential overlap.
2
Consider the type of disjunction
Identify whether the disjunction is exclusive (mani'at al-jam': terms cannot coexist) or exhaustive (mani'at al-khuluw: terms cannot both be absent).
3

The genuine negative can be true between one term and the contradictory of the other term, or between one term and the necessary equal to the other term. Thus two possibilities, so no unique conclusion.
4
For exhaustive disjunction
Similarly, the genuine negative can be true in two ways: between one term and the contradictory of the other, or between one term and the necessary equal to the other. Hence no conclusion.
5
Understand the restriction 'musawi al-lazim'
The necessary (lazim) must be equal in extension to the term to avoid inferring a particular negative that is not validly implied. For example, negating a particular from some individuals of a universal does not justify a universal negative.
