Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the opposing positions of Xenocrates and the author on the acquisition of definition by proof, and explain why definition is epistemologically prior to demonstration.

Core Idea
Definition provides conception; demonstration provides assent, not the other way around.

This passage tackles a fundamental debate: can definition (hadd) be proven through demonstration (burhan)? Xenocrates and some ancients thought so, but the author firmly rejects this, calling their arguments weak. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the structure of Islamic logic, where definition precedes demonstration as the foundation of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Locate the view of Xenocrates and the author. Xenocrates claims definition is acquired by proof; the author states it is not. Note the direct contradiction.
2
Analyze the author's dismissal
The author calls Xenocrates' arguments 'weak' (da'ifa). Consider what makes an argument weak in logical terms—likely circularity or confusion between conception and assent.
3
Recognize the underlying logical distinction
In Islamic logic, definition gives tasawwur (conception) while demonstration gives tasdiq (assent). Conception is needed before assent; proof presupposes definition of terms.
4
Evaluate why definition cannot be proven
If definition were acquired by proof, the proof would already contain the defined term, leading to circularity. Definition is the starting point, not the conclusion.

Refining Ibn Sina's Condition for Complete Definitions
Author cites Ibn Sina's claim and the objection with counterexamples, then Tusi's resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition requires genus and species and when it does not, using the examples of number and house.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina's rule for definitions is not absolute; Tusi's refinement shows exceptions.

Ibn Sina famously asserted that every complete definition must include genus and species. But is this always true? Critics offered counterexamples like 'number' and 'house', whose definitions mention parts that are neither genus nor species. How did later logicians resolve this? In this lesson, we follow the debate and see how Nasir al-Din al-Tusi refined Ibn Sina's position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim
Locate Ibn Sina's statement in the Arabic: 'al-hadd al-tamm la budda wa an yakun yutrakkabu min al-jins wa al-fasl'. He asserts that a complete definition must be composed of genus and species.
This is a strong claim that many commentators found problematic.
2
Find the counterexamples
The objectors used 'number' (al-`adad) and 'house' (al-bayt). Their definitions: number is 'a quantity composed of units'; house is defined by its parts like foundation, walls, roof. None of these parts are genus or species of the whole.
The parts are what logicians call 'non-predicated parts' (al-ajza' al-ghayr al-mahmula).
3
Understand the objection's logic
The parts in these definitions are not predicated of the defined thing: we don't say 'a number is a unit' or 'a house is a wall'. Yet the definition is complete. This shows that Ibn Sina's condition is too restrictive.
The objection assumes that 'complete definition' means 'fully clarifies essence', which can be done by listing parts.
4
Analyze Tusi's resolution
Tusi says: 'What the Shaykh meant by this is some definitions, not all.' This limits the scope: Ibn Sina's condition applies only to definitions that are by genus and differentia (al-hadd al-haqiqi), not to definitions that list parts or properties.
Tusi preserves Ibn Sina's insight while acknowledging valid counterexamples.
5
Apply the resolution to a new example
Consider the definition of 'human' as 'rational animal'. This uses genus (animal) and differentia (rational), so it falls under Ibn Sina's condition. But a definition of 'salah' as 'specific actions and utterances' does not; it lists parts. Tusi's resolution shows both are complete in their own way.
This resolution is important for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences.

A definition must be a compound statement; a single word cannot define.

This lesson tackles a key debate in Islamic logic: can a single differentia (fasl) or a single property (khassa) serve as a complete definition? The author of Qustas al-Afkar says no, because a singular term (mufrad) cannot be a definiens. Yet later scholars permitted it and redefined 'definition' accordingly. The passage mentions definition by the differentia (fasl) alone and by the property (khassa) alone. Note that these are proposals to define something using only one essential or co-extensive attribute.

Distinguishing Genus from Differentia in the Predicated Part
Author explains two formulations to distinguish genus from differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two formulations for determining whether a predicated part of essence is a genus or a differentia.

Core Idea
Genus is the common factor shared between an essence and another species; differentia is what distinguishes them.

When analyzing definitions, it is crucial to know whether a given component is a genus (jins) or a differentia (fasl). This lesson presents two formulations from Islamic logic that give you a clear test. By studying the passage, you will learn how to classify any predicated part of an essence correctly.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of 'predicated part'
The predicated part (al-juz' al-mahmul) refers to a component of the essence that can be predicated of the thing, such as 'animal' for human. In logical definitions, the predicated parts are either genus or differentia.
Essentials (dhatiyyat) are what constitute the quiddity.
2

Take the predicated part and check: Is it predicated of the essence (e.g., human) AND of a different species (e.g., horse) in answer to 'what is it?'? If yes, it is genus (e.g., 'animal' is said of both human and horse). If not, it is differentia.
The differentia is not a full answer to 'what is it?'—it distinguishes one species within a genus.
3

Consider whether the essential part is the entire common factor shared between the essence and a distinct species. If it is the complete commonality, it is genus; otherwise, it is differentia because it cannot be common to all essences and must serve to distinguish.
This formulation is more concise: if the part is completely shared (like animal between human and horse), it is genus; if not, it is differentia.

Take 'human' as the essence. The part 'animal' is predicated of human and also of horse in answer to 'what is it?'—so it is genus. The part 'rational' is not said of horse in answer to 'what is it?', and it is not the entire common factor—so it is differentia.

Do External Composites Have Genus and Differentia?
Qutb al-Din al-Razi refutes the claim that even external composites like a house have genus and differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Qutb al-Din al-Razi's objection to the claim that every composite has genus and differentia, and evaluate the counterargument based on derivation.

Core Idea
External composites are fully defined by their non-predicable parts; predicable parts would duplicate or alter the quiddity.

In this passage, Qutb al-Din al-Razi takes on a widespread claim in logic: that every composite, even a house or a number, must have a genus and a differentia in its definition. He presents the affirmative view with examples, then dismantles it by showing that the sum of non-predicable parts already gives a complete definition, and that adding predicable parts leads to contradiction. Follow the dialectical steps to see how he refutes the counterargument based on derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Thesis and Its Examples
The passage starts by stating the claim: every composite has genus and differentia, whether it has non-predicable parts or not. Note the examples: number (genus: quantity; differentia: composed of units) and house (genus: body; differentia: composed of ceiling and wall). These illustrate the common view.
The examples are key to understanding what is being claimed and what is at stake.
2
Extract the Core Objection
Qutb al-Din argues that if a composite is made of non-predicable parts (e.g., walls and ceiling for a house), those parts in the mind form the complete quiddity. Any definition must express that sum. Predicable parts either duplicate those parts (adding nothing) or introduce an extra element, which either makes the quiddity capable of increase/decrease or brings in an external factor—both impossible.
The objection relies on the idea that the definition must match the quiddity exactly.
3
Analyze the Counterargument via Derivation (Ishtiqaq)
The opponent tries to save the claim by arguing that derivation (ishtiqaq) from the specific part yields a differentia, and from the common part yields a genus. For example, from 'wall' we derive 'wall-ish' (differentia). Thus, even external composites can be seen as having genus and differentia when considered mentally.
Derivation here means creating an adjective or relational term from the part.
4
Evaluate the Response: Derivation Removes the Part
Qutb al-Din replies that derivation adds an external relation (e.g., 'having a wall'), which is not part of the composite's reality. The derived concept is not a part but an attribute; thus, it cannot serve as a part of the definition. Derivation only validates predication (you can predicate 'having a wall' of the house), not composition.
The key point: predication ≠ composition. A part must be constitutive, not just predicable.
5
Synthesize the Conclusion
The passage concludes that a quiddity composed of non-predicable parts is not composed of predicable parts, and vice versa. In other words, the genus-differentia structure belongs to a different type of composition (conceptual) and cannot be imposed on external composites.
This challenges the universality of the genus-differentia model for all definitions.

Mufrad and Murakkab: The Condition of Mutabaqah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An utterance's singleness or composition depends on whether its parts indicate parts of the meaning by correspondence.

The distinction between a single word and a compound expression may seem obvious, but in logic it requires a precise criterion. The author introduces the concept of mutabaqah (correspondence) to define mufrad and murakkab, and shows how the same phrase can be both depending on the type of signification. Understanding this distinction is essential for analyzing the structure of definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three types of signification
Recall that utterances signify meanings by mutabaqah (correspondence), tadhammun (implication), or iltizam (concomitance). Mutabaqah is when the whole utterance indicates the whole meaning and each part indicates a corresponding part of the meaning.
For example, the word 'house' signifies the whole building by mutabaqah, but its parts do not indicate parts of the meaning.
2
Identify the criterion for mufrad and murakkab
If any part of the utterance is intended to indicate a part of the meaning by mutabaqah, it is murakkab; otherwise, it is mufrad. Note that the criterion focuses on the intention of the speaker regarding correspondence.
The condition of mutabaqah is added to avoid ambiguity that arises when considering tadhammun.
3
Analyze the ambiguous example: 'al-hayawan al-mashi'
The phrase 'the walking animal' indicates 'animal' by tadhammun, but its parts do not indicate parts of that meaning by mutabaqah or tadhammun, so it is considered mufrad from that perspective. However, when indicating 'the walking body' by tadhammun, the parts do indicate parts of that meaning, so it is murakkab from another perspective.
This shows how the condition of mutabaqah fixes the classification.
4
Apply the distinction to eloquent Arabic expressions
The author notes that phrases like 'takallama Badr' (Badr spoke) and 'rama Asad' (Asad threw) are excluded from this classification, meaning they are murakkab by the definition, even though they are considered eloquent single expressions in common usage.
This highlights the technical nature of the logical definition.

Words with One Meaning: ʿAlam, Mutawāṭi, Mushakkik

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given term as a proper noun (ʿalam), univocal (mutawāṭi), or equivocal (mushakkik) based on the criteria of meaning unity and individual equivalence.

Core Idea
Words with one meaning are either person-specific (ʿalam) or not; if not, they are either equal (mutawāṭi) or graded (mushakkik).

This lesson focuses on the first branch of the classification: single words that have one meaning. Understanding how logicians distinguish between proper nouns, univocal terms, and equivocal terms will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions in Islamic disciplines such as uṣūl al-fiqh and kalām.

Guided Path

Identify the term's meaning unity

2

3

If the term does not apply equally but with varying degrees (e.g., intensity, priority), it is equivocal (mushakkik). Example: 'wujūd' (existence) applies to God and creation but not equally.

Words with Multiple Meanings: Mushtarak, Manqūl, Ḥaqīqa/Majāz
Classification of words with multiple meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between shared (mushtarak), transferred (manqūl), and literal/metaphorical (ḥaqīqa/majāz) terms based on the nature of their coinage and transfer.

Core Idea
Multiple meanings arise from original coinage (mushtarak) or transfer (manqūl or ḥaqīqa/majāz) depending on predominance and appropriateness.

This lesson covers the second branch: single words with multiple meanings. The logicians classify these based on how the multiple meanings came about—whether by independent coinage or by transfer from one meaning to another. This classification is crucial for interpreting legal and theological texts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of multiplicity
If the term has two or more meanings by original coinage (without transfer), it is 'shared' (mushtarak). Example: 'ʿayn' can mean eye, spring, or spy. Each meaning is called 'ambiguous' (mujmal).
Includes 'murtajal' (innovated) where transfer is arbitrary.
2
Check for transfer (naql)
If the term was originally set for one meaning then transferred to another due to appropriateness, it falls into two subcategories.
3
Determine predominance
If the transferred meaning becomes predominant in usage, it is 'transferred' (manqūl). E.g., 'ṣalāh' originally meant prayer, then transferred to the specific ritual prayer (sharʿī manqūl).
Types: sharʿī (religious), iṣṭilāḥī (technical), ʿurfī (customary).
4
Identify literal vs. metaphorical
If the transferred meaning is not predominant, the original is 'literal' (ḥaqīqa) and the new one is 'metaphorical' (majāz), also called 'borrowed' (mustaʿār) if the transfer is based on sharing some attribute.

Defining Complete Statement: The Dialectical Defense of the Vocative
Author defines compound speech and addresses objections about the vocative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the dialectical exchange about the vocative and identify the logical move from 'khabar' vs 'insha' that resolves the objection.

Core Idea
The vocative refutes the claim that speech is only two nouns or noun+verb, but its performative nature saves the definition.

This passage from Qustas al-Afkar defines different types of compound expressions and then engages in a dialectical discussion about the composition of speech (kalam). You will see how a counterexample (the vocative) is raised, how it is initially defended, and how a further objection is resolved by the crucial distinction between informative and performative speech. This is a classic example of how logical and grammatical analysis intertwine in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim about speech composition
Locate the sentence 'إن الكلام لا يتألف إلا من اسمين أو اسم و فعل'. This is the claim that will be tested by the vocative.
2
Find the counterexample (naqd)
The vocative (نداء) is presented as a refutation because it is a noun preceded by a particle, not two nouns or noun+verb.
3
Trace the first response (jawab)
The response says the vocative is 'في تقدير الفعل' – in estimation of a verb, meaning it implicitly contains a verb, so it still fits the pattern.
4
Analyze the objection (i'tirad) to that response
The objection argues that if the vocative were really in estimation of a verb, it would behave like a proposition – admitting truth/falsehood and allowing third-person address.
This is a conditional argument: if P then Q. The objector claims not-Q, therefore not-P.
5
Examine the final response using the khabar/insha distinction
The author distinguishes between informative (khabar) and performative (insha) speech. The vocative is performative, like the example 'بعث', so it does not admit truth/falsehood even if it contains a verb in estimation.
The conditional only applies to informative utterances, so the objection fails.

Al-Razi's Argument and Refutation on the Natural Universal
Presents al-Razi's proof for existence of natural universal and then refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's argument that the natural universal exists externally and explain the refutation that external existence contradicts universality.

Core Idea
External existence individuates, so the universal cannot be both external and universal.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a proof for the existence of the natural universal in the external world, then immediately refutes it. The dialectic reveals a fundamental tension: if universality requires oneness across individuals, external existence forces individuality. By following the argument closely, you will see how Islamic logicians wrestled with the ontology of universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pro argument's premise
The argument starts with the claim: 'In this existing animal, some animal exists.' This establishes that there is an instance of 'animal' in a concrete individual.
This premise is taken as self-evident.
2
Analyze the case division
The argument divides 'some animal' into two possibilities: it is either the universal 'animal' without restriction or it is animal with a restriction. If it is without restriction, its conception does not prevent sharing, so it is universal. Therefore the universal exists externally.
The key move is that 'without restriction' implies universality.
3
Understand the refutation's dilemma
The refutation argues: if the universal exists externally in Zayd and Bakr, either it is the same entity in both (absurd: one body in two places) or it is different (then not one universal). Both alternatives are impossible, so the universal cannot exist externally.
This is a classic dilemma argument.
4
Grasp the key insight
External existence (al-wujud al-khariji) is itself a restriction that entails individuation. Since universality requires lack of restriction, external existence and universality are incompatible.
This insight is central to later Islamic logic.

Mahiyyah (essence) of a thing is that by which the thing is what it is, and it is synonymous with 'dhat' (self), 'haqiqah' (reality), and 'jawhar' (substance). Sometimes 'dhat' is reserved for existents and 'mahiyyah' for composites. Every haqiqah: either simple, which is not composed of different realities, or composite, which is composed. Parts of a composite: sometimes they are predicated of it, like 'animal' and 'rational' of 'human', and sometimes not, like 'ceiling' and 'wall' of 'house'. There is no clarification of this in the books of the people. Its verification: When the mahiyyah is an expression for a thing composed of several things – which is real composition as commonly understood – then it is impossible to predicate those parts of it by agreement (bi-al-muwata'ah); for it cannot be said that this composite of these different things is the very same as this part; rather it is only correct by derivation (ishtiqaq) or composition. This composition is only in the mind, since externally there is only one thing of which it is true that it is this and that and that; rather the mind composes this mahiyyah from this aggregate. As for how a thing becomes described by these meanings: that a thing: 1. In the example of 'humanity' nothing is possible except composition: 'possessor of humanity' which is in the meaning of 'human' which is the principle of the word 'humanity' and its matter. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition on mahiyyah (essence). It begins by defining mahiyyah as that by which a thing is what it is, noting its synonyms: dhat, haqiqah, and jawhar, with a nuance that dhat is often used for existents and mahiyyah for composites. The author remarks that classical logic books lack clarification on this. This mental composition is in the mind, not externally: externally there is a single thing, and the mind constructs the essence from attributes. Examples illustrate: 'humanity' from 'life' and 'rationality'; 'house' from walls and ceiling; predication by derivation ('rational') or composition ('possessor of rationality').

Predicated and Non-Predicated Parts of Composite Essences
Author discusses parts of composite essences and their predication modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a part of a composite essence is predicated of the whole by synonymy or derivation.

Core Idea
Parts of a composite are not always predicated of it directly; some are predicated by derivation, others not at all.

When we have a composite essence, its parts relate to the whole in different ways. Some parts, like 'animal' in 'human', can be said 'human is animal', while others, like 'wall' in 'house', cannot. This lesson explores why and the modes of predication involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two examples in the Arabic
Locate the examples: 'كالحيوان والناطق على الإنسان' (predicated parts) and 'كالسقف والجدار على البيت' (non-predicated parts).
2
Understand the criterion for predication
A part is predicated by synonymy (al-muwata'ah) if the whole is essentially that part. 'Human is animal' works because 'animal' is part of human's essence. 'House is wall' fails because 'house' is not essentially a wall.
3
Distinguish between real and mental composition
The text explains that if the composite is a real mixture (like a house), parts are not predicated by synonymy but only by derivation. If the composition is mental (like human), parts can be predicated directly.

Author clarifies that composition is mental, not external, and explains how attributes arise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mental composition from real composition and explain how a thing becomes described by attributes.

Core Idea
Real composition is in the external world; mental composition is the mind's construction of an essence from multiple concepts.

The final part of the passage addresses a crucial ontological question: Where does composition actually occur? In external reality or only in the mind? This lesson also unpacks the two-step process by which a thing acquires its mahiyyah and attributes.

Core Idea
The three essence-considerations cannot make all non-predicated parts predicable.

In this passage, the critic takes on the author's claim that Ibn Sina's three modes of considering essence—'with condition', 'without condition', and 'absolutely'—can turn non-predicated parts into predicable ones. The critic argues that this is false, because there are parts that stubbornly refuse predication no matter which mode is used. State the critic's objection

3
Examine the first counterexample
The example of 'house' composed of 'wall' and 'ceiling': these parts are not predicable of 'house' in any way, despite the three considerations.
4
Examine the second and third counterexamples
Negative concepts like 'the infinite' and definitions like 'triangle' show that positive parts remain unpredicated.

Evaluate the critique
Is the critic's argument valid? Yes, because it provides concrete counterexamples that directly contradict the author's claim.

Distinguishing Real and Mental Composition through Predication Modes
Defines 'thing' and distinguishes real vs mental composition based on predication mode

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a composite is real or mental by analyzing whether its parts are predicated by equivalence or derivation.

Core Idea
In real composition, the part is not the whole; in mental composition, the whole is each part.

This passage presents two definitions of 'thing' that reveal a critical distinction: a composite can be a real external entity or a mental construct. The key difference lies in the mode of predication: whether the parts can be predicated of the whole by equivalence (al-muwāṭaʾa) or only by derivation (al-ishtiqāq). Mastering this distinction sharpens your ability to analyze definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two conditional definitions
The passage begins with 'إذا كانت...' and later 'و أما إذا لم تكن كذلك...'. These present two alternative definitions of 'thing': one as a real composite, the other as a mentally composed object.
2
Identify the predication rule for real composition
For real composition, the passage states that it is impossible to predicate the parts by equivalence (المواطأة). Only derivation (الاشتقاق) or composition (التركيب) is permissible.
Example: 'This house is a wall' is invalid, but 'This house has walls' is valid.

Identify the predication rule for mental composition
For mental composition, the parts are predicated of the whole such that each part is true of it (صدق عليها أجزاؤها). This implies equivalence is allowed because the mental whole is just the set of its parts.
Example: In mental composition, one might say 'Man is rational' uses derivation, but the whole 'man' can be described by its parts (e.g., 'Man is animal' could be considered equivalence in a mental framework).
4
Contrast the ontological status
Real composition exists externally (في الخارج) where the whole is a single entity distinct from its parts. Mental composition exists only in the mind (في العقل), where the essence is a mental aggregate.
This distinction affects how we define concepts in logic and fiqh.

Concerning the ranks of genera, and they are four: because genus is either [1] there is no genus above it and there is a genus below it, and it is called the highest genus; or [2] there is no genus below it and there is a genus above it, and it is the lowest genus; or [3] there is no genus below it and no genus above it, and it is the isolated genus, and this division they [?]; or [4] there is a genus above it and a genus below it, and it is the intermediate.

Commentary
This passage defines the four ranks of genera, a classification foundational to the logical analysis of definitions. The author presents the categories systematically: the highest genus (jins al-ajnas) which has no genus above but has species below; the lowest genus (al-jins al-akhir) which has a genus above but no species below; the isolated genus (al-jins al-mufrad) which has neither above nor below, though the author notes that this division is often omitted; and the intermediate genus (al-mutawassit) which has both above and below. This classification is essential for understanding how genus functions in definition, as the genus in a definition must be the nearest genus (intermediate or lowest) for a proper differentia.

Key Takeaway
The four ranks of genera — highest, lowest, isolated, intermediate — are distinguished by the presence or absence of a genus above and species below, and only intermediate or lowest genera can serve as the genus in a proper definition.
