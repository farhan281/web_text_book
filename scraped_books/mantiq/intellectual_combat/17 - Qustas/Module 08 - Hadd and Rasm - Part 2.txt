Causality of the Differentia for the Species Portion
Dialectical exchange on causality of differentia for species portion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's argument that the differentia is the cause of the species-specific portion of the genus and evaluate the objections of al-Khunji and al-Armuwi.

Core Idea
Without causal dependence between genus and differentia, composition fails.

This passage presents a foundational claim in logical ontology: the differentia (fasl) is the cause of the specific portion (hissa) of the genus. The argument is that without such causality, the genus and differentia would be independent and composition would be impossible. Ibn Sina's reasoning is given, followed by two critical responses from later logicians al-Khunji and al-Armuwi. This lesson will walk you through the logical structure of the claim and the dialectical exchange.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim
Identify the claim: the differentia is the cause (illa) of the existence of the species-specific portion of the genus. Note that 'portion' means the genus as restricted to the species.
This is a metaphysical claim about definition.
2
Follow the argument
The argument: if neither the genus nor the differentia caused the other, each would be independent → no composition. Since genus cannot be cause (would imply differentia), the differentia must be cause. Trace the conditional reasoning.
The argument uses a proof by contradiction.
3
Analyze the first response
Al-Khunji and al-Armuwi distinguish two meanings of 'cause': complete cause (tamma) and general cause (a‘amm). Under complete cause, they deny that independence follows from lack of causality. Under general cause, they deny that the genus cannot be cause and that the portion is not implied.
This response refines the notion of causality.
4
Assess the dialectic
Evaluate which side is stronger. Does the objection undermine the original argument? How does the response affect the theory of definition?
This is a live debate in the tradition.

Refuting the Differentia's Causality: Circularity and Ontology
The author and al-Razi refute the differentia's causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the circularity argument against the differentia causing the genus, and evaluate al-Razi's additional ontological objection.

Core Idea
The differentia cannot cause the genus without vicious circularity.

In this passage, the author and Imam al-Razi jointly dismantle the Peripatetic claim that the differentia is the cause of the specific portion of the genus. Using the definitional hierarchy of 'man', they expose a logical circle and then add an ontological argument: an accident cannot cause its substance. This lesson will guide you through both refutations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target thesis
The thesis is that the differentia (e.g., 'rational') is the cause of the specific portion of the genus (e.g., 'animal' in 'rational animal'). The author attributes this to Aristotle, Ibn Sina, and their followers.
The specific portion of the genus is what makes the genus determinate in a species.
2
Reconstruct the circularity argument
Consider the descent: 'body' depends on 'growing body', which depends on 'animal', which depends on 'rational'. If the genus depends on the differentia, then 'animal' depends on 'rational'. But 'rational' itself presupposes 'animal' (since it is defined in relation to it). This creates a circular dependence (dawr).
The hierarchy reflects the successive genera in the definition of man: body, growing body, animal, rational.
3
Examine the counter-objection and rebuttal
An opponent might say the differentia causes the persistence (baqa') of the genus, not its origination (huduth). The author rebuts: if the differentia needed the genus for origination, it likely also needs it for persistence. Moreover, the genus together with the separate intellect (al-mufariq) is a complete cause (sabab tamm), making the differentia superfluous.
The separate intellect is the active intellect in Peripatetic cosmology, causing the soul's origination under the condition of the body.
4
Analyze al-Razi's ontological objection
Al-Razi adds that the differentia can be an accidental quality (sifah), and a quality cannot be a cause of the substance (al-mawsuf) it inheres in. Therefore, the differentia cannot be a cause of the genus, which is substance-like.
The author notes this argument is disputed regarding true quiddity (al-mahiyya al-haqiqiyya).

Critique of the Differentia's Causality Ramifications
Author lists and then refutes five logical consequences of treating differentia as cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the five logical consequences of treating the differentia as a cause, and explain why they fail.

Core Idea
Ramifications from incomplete causality collapse; only complete causes have strict properties.

In this passage, the author examines a common move in Islamic logic: treating the differentia (الفصل) as a cause of the species' share of the genus. From this claim, earlier logicians derived five consequences about the differentia's uniqueness and exclusivity. However, the author shows that these consequences only hold if the differentia were a complete cause (علة تامة), which it is not. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to assess definitions and causal claims in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Find where the author states that others derived consequences from the differentia being a cause. Note the assumption: the differentia is a cause of the species' share of the genus.
2
List the five consequences
The passage enumerates five points: (1) One differentia cannot also be a genus for the same species; (2) It accompanies only one genus; (3) It constitutes only one species; (4) The proximate differentia is one; (5) Al-Imam said it is the perfection of the distinguishing part, so it is one.
Trace each consequence back to the idea that an effect cannot become a cause (1), effect cannot lag (2), etc.
3
Identify the author's critique
The author argues that these consequences are only valid for a complete cause (علة تامة). But the differentia is not a complete cause because it includes parts of the genus and other things. Therefore, lagging and coincidence are possible.
The key distinction is complete vs. incomplete cause.
4
Evaluate the critique's validity
Assess whether the author's objection is sound. Does the differentia indeed not meet the conditions of a complete cause? Consider that the differentia alone does not produce the species without the genus.
This critique is standard in Islamic logic; it prevents overextending causal principles.

When a Differentia Becomes a Genus: Homonymy and 'Natiq'
Debate on whether 'natiq' can be a genus for humans and angels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a term like 'nāṭiq' is used homonymously and explain why that prevents it from serving as a common differentia or genus.

Core Idea
Homonymy breaks the univocal predication required for a term to be a true differentia.

Sometimes a key term like 'nāṭiq' (rational) seems to apply to both humans and angels, but is it the same meaning? Ibn Sina argues that it is not—it is used by verbal homonymy. This lesson walks through the debate and shows how homonymy undermines the claim that 'nāṭiq' could be a genus. You will learn to detect homonymy and assess arguments about definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing positions
First, locate where the passage states: 'It was said: 'Rational' is not a differentia for 'human' absolutely, but a genus for him relative to 'angel'.' Then find Ibn Sina's rejection: 'There is no need for that.' Contrast these two views.
The first position adds 'mortal' to the differentia; Ibn Sina finds that unnecessary.
2
Extract the core logical claim
Ibn Sina says 'nāṭiq' is 'a substance possessing reason', and 'substance' is applied to humans and angels by verbal homonymy (ishtirāk al-lafẓ). That means the term 'substance' does not have a single meaning in both cases.
Homonymy means the same word with different meanings, like 'bank' in English.
3
Understand why homonymy matters for definitions
A differentia must be predicated univocally of all members of the species. If 'nāṭiq' means something different for humans and angels, it cannot be a common differentia, let alone a genus. The passage quotes Aristotle: differentiae indicate 'what sort of thing', not 'what it is'.
This is a classic move: if a term is ambiguous, it cannot serve as a defining property.
4
Analyze Ibn Sina's alternative
Ibn Sina concludes: 'living rational' cannot be a genus for humans and angels, nor can 'body possessing soul' be a genus for plants, angels, and animals. The shared term is only 'reason', but 'reason' is not a differentia because it does not pick out a single essence.
The footnote clarifies that the Shaykh did not say 'substance' is homonymous, but 'nāṭiq' is.

Why a Single Term Cannot Define
Author argues that a single term cannot serve as a definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a single term fails as a definition by analyzing the three types of indication (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam).

Core Idea
A single term either synonymously labels or obscurely hints at an essence; neither gives a clear definition.

This passage from Qustas al-Afkar tackles a central issue in definition theory: can a single word suffice to define something? The author says no, and gives two reasons based on how a word can indicate an essence. Whether the word matches the essence perfectly (mutabaqa) or only partially/implicitly (tadammun/iltizam), it fails to provide a clear, informative definition. Only a composed expression can do that.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
The author states that a single term (al-mufrad) cannot serve as a definiens (muʿarrif). He supports this by considering two ways a term might indicate the essence.
The claim is absolute: no single term, even if it is a differentia or property, can define.
2
Analyze the first case: indication by mutabaqa
If the term indicates the essence by complete correspondence (mutabaqa), then it is simply a synonym. It does not explain the essence; it just renames it. A definition should be more informative than a mere synonym.
Example: 'insan' and 'hayawan natiq' – if 'insan' already fully indicates the essence, then 'hayawan natiq' is just another name.
3
Analyze the second case: indication by tadammun or iltizam
If the term indicates by inclusion (tadammun, e.g., 'animal' for man) or by implication (iltizam, e.g., 'laughter' for man), then the term itself is more obscure than the essence, and the mind moves to its immediate meaning before reaching the essence. This order causes confusion unless more description is added.
The obscurity arises because the term's meaning (e.g., 'animal') is broader or separate from the full essence, so it does not pinpoint the essence clearly.
4
Conclude: composition is necessary
Since both cases fail, the author concludes that a correct definition must be compound (murakkab) – i.e., composed of multiple terms that together clearly indicate the essence, typically genus and differentia.
This aligns with the classical requirement that a hadd (real definition) must include both genus and differentia.

Nominal vs Real Definitions in Islamic Logic

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A definition may clarify a term without requiring its existence; only when existence is verified does it become a real definition.

In this passage, the author classifies definitions into two fundamental types: those that define a term (nominal) and those that define something that exists in reality (real). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences, because a definition of 'ijmaʿ' as 'consensus of scholars' is nominal until consensus is proven to exist as a valid source.

Identify the two types of definitions

Nominal definitions are composed of meanings assigned to a name. Errors are detected if the definition can be increased or decreased while still referring to the same concept. If the definer does not intend the definition to capture the essence but only provides signs (ʿalāmāt), it becomes a description (rasm) according to the name, not a proper definition (ḥadd). This is a key nuance: rasm uses accidental properties.

The first type (nominal) becomes the second (real) when the existence of the definiendum in the external world is verified. For example, a definition of 'wine' as 'intoxicating drink' is nominal unless wine exists; but in reality it does, so it is a real definition.

Manun's Doubt and the Contrapositive Refutation
Presents the trilemma, the contrapositive refutation, and the objection to that refutation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contrapositive arguments can misfire when subject terms differ in generality.

Manun's sophism seems to show that seeking a definition is circular or impossible. Al-Maraghī attempted to use contrapositive logic to refute it, but al-Khunajī exposed a flaw: the contrapositive of one premise does not contradict the other because their subjects are not coextensive. This lesson traces that dialectical move.

Guided Path
1
Restate the trilemma
Write the three cases Manun presents: fully known, fully unknown, and partly known. Note that each is claimed to make seeking impossible.
2
Identify al-Maraghī's response
He claims that the two premises (about fully known and fully unknown) cannot both be true because their contrapositives contradict each other via simple conversion. Write the contrapositives.
Assume standard Aristotelian logic.
3
Examine al-Khunajī's objection
Al-Khunajī restates the premises as about 'concepts' (tasawwur). The contrapositive of 'Every known concept is impossible to seek' is 'Some non-known-concepts are not impossible to seek', which is not the negation of 'Every unknown concept is impossible to seek' because the subject includes non-concepts.
This shows the importance of term scope.
4
Recognise the underlying issue
The failure arises because the contrapositive operation changes the subject to a broader class. Thus the attempted refutation is invalid.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange concerning a famous skeptical puzzle about the possibility of seeking definitions. The chapter opens with a clarification: 'definition' (hadd) means the detailed analysis of the parts of the defined, whether those parts are known through description (rasm) or otherwise. Then the third doubt (shubhat Manun) is presented as a trilemma: (1) if the thing is fully known, its definition cannot be sought; (2) if it is fully unknown, seeking is also impossible because we cannot direct attention toward it; (3) if it is partly known and partly unknown, seeking either aspect is said to be impossible. Al-Maraghī's answer proposes that the two premises (the fully known case and the fully unknown case) cannot both be true because their contrapositives lead to a contradiction via simple conversion. Al-Khunajī objects that the intended premises are specifically about 'concepts' (tasawwur), not things in general. He restates the premises as 'Every known concept is impossible to seek' and 'Every unknown concept is impossible to seek'. The contrapositive of the first is 'Some non-known-concepts are not impossible to seek', which is not the negation of the second because its subject ('non-known-concept') is more general than 'unknown concept' (some non-known-concepts might not be concepts at all). Thus no contradiction arises. A further gloss interprets the subject of the first as 'known in all respects' and the second (implicitly) as 'unknown in all respects', making the subject of the contrapositive more general (since negation of 'known in all respects' is broader than 'unknown in all respects'). However, this explanation is critiqued: if the thing in question is 'unknown in all respects', the second premise would entail its seekability is impossible, but some things unknown in all respects (like nonexistent objects) could be sought? Actually the critique argues that either (i) if it is absolutely unknown, then 'some absolutely unknown things are not impossible to seek' would be true (since some unknown things can be sought by some assumption), invalidating the second premise; or (ii) if it is only partially unknown, then 'some partially unknown things are not impossible to seek' would be true, invalidating the third premise. The author's own final answer is simpler: something can be known in some respect (e.g., its name or effect) and unknown in its essence. This partial knowledge suffices to direct attention toward it, just as we seek the essence of 'angel' or 'jinn' based on some known attributes. Thus the dilemma is dissolved without requiring the complex contrapositive arguments.

The Fourth Doubt on Definition and Its Answer
Author presents the fourth objection to definition and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute the objection that definition is impossible due to circularity or infinite regress.

Core Idea
Definition by parts is not circular; external attributes define without prior knowledge.

The fourth doubt attacks the very possibility of definition, claiming that any attempt to define a thing by itself, its parts, or external attributes leads to circularity or impossibility. The author's refutation carefully distinguishes different senses of 'definition by itself' and shows that external properties can be known without exhaustive detail. This lesson unpacks the objection and the refutation, giving you tools to deflect such skeptical challenges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sub-doubts
The objection lists four ways of defining: by itself, by all parts, by some parts, by external things. For each, it claims impossibility. Note the reasoning for each: the first is circular, the second is defining by itself, the third requires knowing all parts first, and the fourth requires knowing specificity which itself needs prior knowledge.
The fourth sub-doubt has two parts: circularity and impossible detail.
2
Understand the concept of circularity (dawr)
Circularity in definition occurs when the definition presupposes knowledge of the definiendum. For example, if you define 'human' as 'bashar', that is a synonym and considered definition by itself (ta'rīf bi-nafsihi). The objection claims that defining by parts is also circular because knowing the whole requires knowing each part.
The answer will challenge this conflation.
3

The author restricts 'definition by itself' to cases where the definiens has the exact same signification as the definiendum (e.g., 'human is bashar'). Defining by enumerating parts (e.g., 'human is an animal that is rational') is not definition by itself because it provides detail. Therefore, the circularity objection fails.
Also, knowledge of the whole does not require detailed knowledge of every part; the whole may be known through other means.
4
Examine the answer regarding external attributes
The objection claims that defining by external things requires knowing the specificity of that external thing to the definiendum, which itself requires prior knowledge. The author counters that a property can lead to knowledge of the essence without knowing the specificity, or that a vague conceptualization suffices.
Example: knowing that a body occupies a specific space does not require knowing the reality of that body or everything else in detail.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Simple things have no parts and can only be described; composites have parts and can be defined.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational division in definition theory: all that is intended to be defined is either simple or composite. This classification determines whether a proper definition (hadd) is possible or only a description (rasm). Identify the two main categories

Commentary
This passage is a teaching on the classification of definable things and the necessary structure of definitions. It begins by dividing what is intended to be defined into simple and compound. Similarly, a house is a body, so its genus is 'body' and its species is 'composed of a roof and a wall'. Thus, every compound, even those with non-predicable parts, has a genus and species. This defends Ibn Sina's position and shows the universal applicability of the genus-species structure in definitions.

Refuting the Necessity of Genus and Differentia for All Composites
Critique of the claim that every composite has genus and differentia

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's critique of the claim that every composite requires genus and differentia, and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
Not every composite has genus and differentia; non-predicated parts alone can form a complete essence.

This passage from Qutb al-Din al-Razi's Qustas al-Afkar presents a sophisticated critique of a widely held logical doctrine: that every real composite must be definable by genus and differentia. Al-Razi shows that external composites like number and house, composed of non-predicated parts, have a complete reality in the mind and externally without needing genus and differentia. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to evaluate definitions and to see the limits of the genus-differentia model, which is crucial for precise reasoning in Islamic theology and legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Find the claim that every composite (including external ones like number and house) must be composed of genus and differentia. Note that this is attributed to Ibn Sina and others.
The claim is stated in the first paragraph: 'كل مركب حقيقي لا بد و أن يندرج تحت مقولة من العشر اندراج النوع تحت الجنس فلذلك حكم بوجود الجنس و الفصل في كل مركب حقيقي.'
2
Extract al-Razi's objection
Identify the core objection: if a composite consists of non-predicated parts (like units in number, walls in a house), then these parts alone constitute its complete quiddity in the mind. Adding predicated parts (genus and differentia) either duplicates the parts or introduces an extraneous element, both leading to absurdity.
The objection begins with 'و فيه نظر' and uses a reductio ad absurdum.

Al-Razi anticipates a counterargument: that derivation (ishtiqaq) turns specific parts into differentia and common parts into genera. He refutes this by showing that derivation adds an external relation, taking the part out of part-hood, so it does not make the composite genuinely composed of predicated parts.

Synthesize the conclusion
Summarize: a quiddity composed of non-predicated parts is not composed of predicated parts; it is simple in terms of genus and differentia. This refutes the claim that every composite must have genus and differentia.
The final sentence: 'فقد بان أن الماهية المركبة من الأجزاء الغير المحمولة لا تكون مركبة من الأجزاء المحمولة و بالعكس. بل الماهية المركبة من الأجزاء الغير المحمولة لا تكون إلا بسيطة.'

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement from Qutb al-Din al-Razi's Qustas al-Afkar, targeting the claim that every composite—including external composites like number and house—must be composed of genus and differentia. The author first presents the background: some scholars objected to this view using the example of non-predicated parts (ajza' ghayr mahmula), such as the units in number or the roof and wall in a house, which are not predicated of the thing. Others said the Shaykh (Ibn Sina) intended only certain definitions. The Shaykh's position, as presented, is that every real composite falls under one of the ten categories, and therefore has a genus and differentia. The author then mentions the 'chosen doctrine' that composites are only four types, but still each has genus and differentia, giving examples: number under quantity, house under substance.

Then the author presents a stronger claim by 'some people' that even external composites (not just intellectual ones) are composed of genus and differentia because they fall under a genus. The author subjects this to scrutiny (wa fihi nazar). The argument proceeds: a composite of non-predicated parts, when those parts are considered together in the mind, yields a mental quiddity. A definition that expresses that set of parts is complete. The predicated parts (genus and differentia) either include those non-predicated parts or not. If they do not, they fail to match the quiddity. If they do, and they add nothing extra, they are identical to those parts. If they add something extra, that extra either enters the quiddity (making it variable, absurd) or is external (making the definition rely on something external, contradicting its completeness). Thus the non-predicated parts alone constitute the complete reality both mentally and externally. If there were a different set of predicated parts, one thing would have two mental realities, impossible.

The author then responds to an objection: one might say that the composite of non-predicated parts has a specific part (like the final unit of number) and a shared part (like quantity), and derivation (ishtiqaq) turns the specific into a differentia and the shared into a genus. The author refutes: derivation adds a relation external to the whole, thereby taking the part out of part-hood. The part plus an external relation is not the part itself. Derivation only validates predication, not composition.

Conclusion: A quiddity composed of non-predicated parts is not composed of predicated parts; it is simple in terms of genus and differentia. This refutes the claim that every composite requires genus and differentia. The lesson is a sophisticated dialectical exchange, demonstrating the limits of genus-differentia analysis for external composites.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Privations are not mere absences; they are absences tied to specific capacities.

In Islamic theology and jurisprudence, precise definitions of attributes are crucial. For example, when discussing divine attributes or human capacities, one must know whether the term refers to a privation (like 'inability') or a positive quality (like 'ability'). Learning Objective

Core Idea
Privations are absences of a capacity, not absolute nothingness; definitions must include what is lacked.

In Islamic logic, definitions often need to capture whether a term denotes a positive property or a privation. This distinction is crucial in disciplines like fiqh and usul al-fiqh, where terms like 'invalidity' or 'inability' are privatives tied to a specific capacity. Definition fallacies arise from meaning, wording, or both; general fallacies are not restricted to one definition type.

The Non-Coextensive Fallacy (Ghayr al-Musawi)
Author explains the non-coextensive fallacy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a non-coextensive definition and explain whether it is too broad or too narrow.

Core Idea
A definiens must match the definiendum exactly; otherwise it fails to be comprehensive or exclusive.

The first general fallacy the author lists is defining by what is not coextensive in truth. This means the definiens is either too broad (includes things outside the definiendum) or too narrow (excludes things inside). Let's see how this works with Islamic concepts.

Guided Path
1
Understand coextensiveness
The definiens must be exactly equal in extension to the definiendum. If it's broader, it fails 'جامع' (comprehensive); if narrower, it fails 'مانع' (exclusive).
2
Check for broadness
Ask: Does the definiens include anything that is not an instance of the definiendum? E.g., defining 'human' as 'animal' is too broad because animals include non-humans.
3
Check for narrowness
Ask: Does the definiens exclude some genuine instances? E.g., defining 'man' as 'rational animal' is correct, but defining 'man' as 'featherless biped' excludes some humans (e.g., those with hair loss or leg injuries).
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider defining 'zakat' as 'charity'. Charity is broader (includes sadaqah). Defining 'zakat' as 'obligatory charity with conditions' is more coextensive. Check if it covers all zakat types and excludes other charities.

Author details synonym and circular definitions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Defining a thing by itself – via synonym or circularity – provides no new information.

Guided Path
1

The author says each is worse than the previous. Synonymy is bad; circular in one step is worse; multi-step is worst because it hides the circularity more.

The Composition Method for Acquiring Definitions
Author presents the composition method as the strongest way to acquire definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the composition method to extract the genus and differentia of an Islamic legal concept by analyzing multiple individuals.

Core Idea
The composition method extracts definitions by systematically separating common essential attributes from specific ones.

The author introduces the composition method as the strongest way to acquire definitions, rejecting Xenocrates' claim that definitions come from demonstration. This method involves examining multiple individuals of a species, determining their category, extracting essential predicates, and distinguishing the common (genus) from the specific (differentia). Understanding this method is crucial for rigorous definition in any Islamic science, as definitions underpin correct reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its type
Determine whether the term to be defined is a species (e.g., 'prayer' as a type of worship) or a genus (e.g., 'act of worship' encompassing prayer, fasting, etc.). This determines which individuals to sample.
If the definiendum is a genus, take individuals of its species; if it is a species, take individuals of that species.
2
Collect individuals and identify the category
Take several clear instances of the definiendum. For each, ask: to which ontological category (maqūla) does it belong? For example, prayer belongs to the category of action (fi‘l).
The category is the broadest genus of the individual.
3
Extract constitutive predicates
From the category, list all attributes that are essential (dhātiyyāt) to the individuals as instances of the definiendum. These are the predicates that must be present for the thing to be what it is.
Accidental attributes that happen to accompany the individuals in existence are set aside.
4
Separate common and specific
Compare the essential attributes across the individuals. What is shared by all is the genus (jins); what is unique to some and distinguishes them is the differentia (faṣl). The definition combines genus and differentia.
This step yields the complete definition (ḥadd).
