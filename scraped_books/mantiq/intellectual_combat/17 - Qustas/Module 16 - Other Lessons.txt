Partial Opposition of Contradictories of General from a Certain Aspect
Editor corrects a misprint to clarify the relationship between contradictories of two concepts related as 'general from a certain aspect'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the relationship of partial opposition between the contradictories of two concepts that are related as 'general from a certain aspect' (أعم من وجه).

Core Idea
The contradictories of partially overlapping concepts are themselves partially opposed, not totally.

In Islamic logic, understanding the relationships between concepts is fundamental. This passage corrects a common misprint to clarify a precise logical point: when two concepts are related as 'general from a certain aspect' (each is more general in one respect and more specific in another), their contradictories are not totally opposed but partially opposed. This distinction is crucial for accurate classification and definition in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recall the 'general from a certain aspect' relationship
Two concepts are 'general from a certain aspect' (أعم من وجه) if each partially overlaps with the other. For example, 'body' and 'white': some bodies are white, some are not; some white things are bodies, some are not. Recognise that they intersect partially.
This relationship is often contrasted with 'general absolutely' (أعم مطلق) where one concept fully includes the other.
2
Define 'contradictory' in the term sense
The contradictory (نقيض) of a term is a term that denies the original entirely, applying to everything that is not the original. For 'body', the contradictory is 'non-body'; for 'white', it is 'non-white'.
In this context, naqīḍ refers to term negation, not propositional negation.
3
Examine the truth conditions of the contradictories
Take the contradictory of the first concept, e.g., 'non-body'. Is it true with the second concept 'white'? Yes, because some white things are not bodies (e.g., the colour white itself). Now check if 'non-body' is true with 'non-white': some non-bodies are non-white (e.g., sounds), but not all. Similarly, check the contradictory of the second concept against the first.
This shows that the contradictory of each is true with the other concept itself, establishing partial opposition.
4
Determine the overall relationship
If the contradictory of one were also true with the contradictory of the other in all cases, there would be total opposition (مباينة كلية). However, since that is not generally true, the relationship is partial opposition (مباينة جزئية). The corrected text asserts that partial opposition is necessary (لازمة).
Thus, the contradictories of two concepts related as 'general from a certain aspect' are themselves partially opposed.

Reductio and Conversion as Reliable Logical Foundations
Author defends logic by explaining reducio method and conversion as reliable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how conversion is derived from reductio and identify the distinction between terms and relations in logical reasoning.

Core Idea
Reductio is the core method; conversion is built upon it.

This passage addresses an objection that logic is unreliable because logicians err. The author defends logic by clarifying that the core method, reductio ad absurdum, is sound, and that conversion—often seen as merely conventional—is actually derived from reductio and other certain proofs. Understanding this hierarchy of logical methods is key to appreciating logic's epistemic strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection being answered
The objection claims that logicians' mistakes make logic useless. Find the phrase that signals this: 'عدم الفائدة لخطأ المنطقي' means 'lack of benefit due to the logician's error'.
2
Extract the author's core claim
The author states: 'والطريق في الكل هو الخلف' — 'The method in all is reductio'. This means reductio ad absurdum is the foundational proof method for all logical inferences.
Reductio works by assuming the opposite of the desired conclusion and deriving a contradiction.
3
Distinguish terms from relations
The phrase 'التوقف إنما هو للأطراف لا للنسب' means 'stopping is only for the extremities, not for the relations'. Here, 'extremities' (أطراف) refer to the subject and predicate terms and their modalities, while 'relations' (نسب) refer to the copula or judgmental bond. The tawaqquf (inability to proceed) applies only to the terms, not to the logical connection between them.
This clarifies that definitions and term analysis do not hinder the logical relation that ties them together.
4
Trace the derivation of conversion
The author says: 'والعكس - وإن كان كسبياً لكنه - مكتسب من الخلف وغيره من الطرق اليقينية' — 'Conversion – even though it is acquisitive – is acquired from reductio and other certain methods'. So conversion is not primitive; it depends on reductio and other established proofs.
This means conversion (swapping subject and predicate) is valid because it can be justified by reductio.
5
Conclude why this refutes the objection
Since the foundational method (reductio) is sound, and conversion is derived from it, any error by logicians in applying these methods does not undermine the methods themselves. The objection is invalid because it confuses human error with the inherent reliability of the logical tools.

Distinguishing Material Error from Logical Form
Author distinguishes errors due to material content from logical form

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between errors arising from the material content of an argument and errors in its logical form, and explain why logic is not responsible for material errors.

Core Idea
Errors in premises are not errors in logic; logic only governs the form, not the matter.

In this passage, the author addresses a common objection: if logicians themselves make mistakes, then logic is useless. The response is a crucial distinction: errors can come from the material content of an argument (false premises or incorrect facts), not from the logical form. Since logic deals with form, such errors do not indict the discipline. This distinction is foundational for appreciating the scope and limits of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the criticism being raised against logic in the passage. It is the claim that logicians make mistakes, so logic is not beneficial.
2
Extract the author's distinction
The author distinguishes between 'matter' (مادة) and 'form' (صورة). Matter refers to the content or premises, while form is the logical structure.
This distinction is key to defending logic.
3
Apply the distinction
Think of a valid syllogism with a false premise, e.g., 'All mutakallimun are philosophers; All philosophers are infallible; therefore all mutakallimun are infallible.' The form is valid, but the premise is false. The error is in the matter.
4
Conclude why the objection fails
Since logic concerns form, material errors do not invalidate logic. The objector confuses the two sources of error.

Defending the Predicability of Nouns: A Dialectical Analysis
Author argues nouns alone can be predicates and responds to objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the dialectical structure of objections and answers in a logical debate about predication.

Core Idea
Nouns alone can be predicates – but defending this requires handling self-referential paradoxes.

In this passage, the author defends a key logical property of nouns: only nouns can truly be the subject of predication. Opponents raise clever objections that appear to use verbs or particles as subjects. The author responds by clarifying that the subject is never the verb or particle qua verb or particle, but either its wording or a compound. This dialectical exchange sharpens our understanding of what it means for something to be a subject of predication in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
Find the property being argued: that only nouns can be the subject of a true predication (الإخبار عنه). Notice that verbs and particles are excluded by their mere wording.
The claim is about the word type itself, not about meaning.
2
Parse the first objection and answer
The objection uses the example 'ضَرَبَ لا يخبر عن معناه' - this seems to predicate about a verb. The answer says the actual subject is the wording of 'ضَرَبَ', not the verb as a type. The pronoun 'عنه' refers to the word, not the verb's meaning.
This avoids the paradox: the subject is a noun (the word 'ضَرَبَ').
3
Analyze the second objection and answer
The objection 'معنى ضَرَبَ لا يخبر عنه' again predicates about a verb's meaning. The answer says the subject includes the word 'معنى', so it is a compound, not the verb alone.
The addition of 'معنى' makes the subject a noun phrase.
4
Examine the third objection and answer
The objections 'ضَرَبَ فعل ماض' and 'في حرف جر' predicate about a verb and a particle directly. The answer says the subject is the combination of the word and its meaning (مجموع اللفظ والمعنى), not the meaning alone.
This preserves the rule: the subject is not a verb or particle by itself.
5
Synthesize the defense strategy
The author consistently shows that whenever a verb or particle appears to be the subject, the actual subject is either its wording, a compound including it, or the combination of word and meaning. Thus the original claim holds.
The defense relies on distinguishing between the word as a linguistic entity and the word as a type.

Objection Against General Possibility and Answer
Author presents an objection that possibility applied to the Necessary Being leads to contingency, then answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general possibility (al-imkān al-‘āmm) and specific possibility (al-imkān al-khāṣṣ) in Kalam, and explain why applying general possibility to the Necessary Being does not entail contingency.

Core Idea
General possibility negates necessity; it does not imply contingency of non-existence.

Some have objected that if possibility applies to the Necessary Being, then the Necessary would be possibly non-existent or impossible. The author's response hinges on a crucial distinction between general and specific possibility. In this lesson, you will learn to parse this logical distinction and see how it unravels the objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's core dilemma
The objector claims that either possibility applies to the Necessary Being or it does not. If it does, the Necessary becomes possibly non-existent (contingent). If it does not, the Necessary becomes impossible. Both conclusions are unacceptable for the Necessary Being.
This is a classic dilemma used to challenge the concept of possibility in theology.
2
Locate the answer's key move
The author denies that either conclusion follows. The answer turns on the meaning of the term 'possibility'. The objector assumes a single meaning, but the author shows that 'possibility' is ambiguous.
3
Distinguish general possibility from specific possibility
General possibility (الإمكان العام) is simply the negation of necessity: it means 'not necessary'. Specific possibility (الإمكان الخاص) is the negation of both necessity and impossibility, i.e., true contingency. Apply this to the dilemma: if 'possibility' means general, its applying to the Necessary only denies that the Necessary is necessary in a descriptive sense, not that it could be non-existent. If 'possibility' means specific, its failure to apply does not entail impossibility because the Necessary is necessary, not impossible.
General possibility is a broader, more inclusive concept.
4
Evaluate the answer
The author's distinction resolves the dilemma: under the general meaning, the first horn (possibility of non-existence) does not follow; under the specific meaning, the second horn (impossibility) does not follow. The objection commits a fallacy of equivocation.
This distinction is standard in later Kalam works.

Critique of Ibn Sina's Rule on Conditional Entailment
Author presents and critiques a rule about mutual entailment of conditionals with contradictory consequents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Khunaji's objection to Ibn Sina's rule on mutual entailment of conditionals and evaluate whether the rule necessarily holds.

Core Idea
Contradictory consequents do not guarantee mutual entailment — al-Khunaji shows why.

In this passage, the author presents a rule attributed to Ibn Sina (al-Shaykh) concerning the relationship between two conditional propositions that share the same antecedent but have contradictory consequents. According to the rule, such conditionals are mutually entailing. However, al-Khunaji challenges this rule by pointing out logical possibilities that undermine its necessity. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to assess logical relationships in Islamic theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
State Ibn Sina's rule
Identify the exact conditions: two conditionals must agree in quantity (universal/particular) and antecedent, differ in quality (affirmative/negative), and have contradictory consequents. The rule claims they are mutually entailing.
This is essentially a claim about contraposition in conditional logic.
2
Understand the logical relationship
If the rule holds, then from 'If P then Q' we can derive 'If not Q then not P'. The rule extends this to conditionals with the same antecedent but contradictory consequents.
3
Recognize al-Khunaji's objection
Al-Khunaji says the rule is not necessary (ghayr lāzim). He gives two reasons: (a) it is possible for both contradictories to be entailed by one antecedent, (b) it is possible for neither to be entailed. Therefore, the truth of one conditional does not necessarily imply the truth of the other.
If both follow, the antecedent implies a contradiction; if neither follows, the antecedent implies nothing about those consequents.

Evaluate the critique's validity
Consider whether the possibilities al-Khunaji raises are genuinely possible in logical systems. In standard logic, an antecedent cannot entail both a proposition and its negation unless it is impossible. However, the critique highlights that the rule lacks necessity without additional assumptions.
Al-Khunaji's argument is accepted by later logicians as a valid refinement.

When encountering conditionals in Kalam or Fiqh arguments, check whether mutual entailment is assumed. Use al-Khunaji's critique to avoid false equivalences.

Immediate Inferences of Universal Affirmative Conditionals
Author enumerates entailments of universal affirmative conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive valid immediate inferences from a universal affirmative conditional proposition using the rules of entailed and necessary terms.

Core Idea
A universal affirmative conditional entails a family of conditionals by substituting entailing or entailed terms for its parts.

In the previous lesson, you learned about the nature of conditional propositions. Here, the author provides a systematic set of rules for generating new conditionals from a given universal affirmative conditional. These are not arbitrary; they rely on the relationships of entailment (luzūm) between terms. Mastering these rules is essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic legal reasoning and theology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the base form
Identify the original conditional: 'If A then B', where the implication is strict (luzūmiyya). Recognize the parts: antecedent (muqaddam), consequent (tālī), and their respective entailments.
The base conditional is the starting point for all derivations.
2
Apply substitution rules
For each rule, identify what is being substituted: (1) from antecedent and necessary of consequent, (2) from entailed of antecedent and necessary of consequent, (3) from entailed of antecedent and very consequent. Note the conditions for converse when the terms are mutually entailing or reversible.
Rule (1) yields 'If A then C' where B entails C; rule (2) yields 'If D then C' where D entails A; rule (3) yields 'If D then B'.
3
Apply contraposition rules
Recognize that from the original conditional we can also derive conditionals involving contradictories: from the entailed of the contradictory of the consequent and the contradictory of the antecedent, and from the contradictory of the consequent and the necessary of the contradictory of the antecedent.
This is standard contraposition but with substitution of entailments.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Use a fiqh example: 'If it is wine (A), then it is prohibited (B).' Derive: (1) If wine, then impure (since prohibition entails impurity). (2) If a liquid intoxicant withdrawn from sale (which entails wine), then prohibited. (3) If not prohibited, then not wine (contrapositive).
This demonstrates the practical application of the rules.

Commentary
This passage provides rules for the immediate inferences (entailments) of a universal affirmative conditional proposition (muttasila mūjiba kulliyya luzūmiyya). The conditional is of the form 'if A then B' where the implication is strict (luzūmiyya). The author states that such a conditional 'entails its like' meaning that other conditionals can be derived from it under certain conditions. The first set of rules: (1) From the original antecedent (maddam) and the necessary consequent (lāzim tālihā) — meaning that if the original consequent (B) has a necessary implication (C), then we can form 'if A then C'. Conversely, if the two consequents (original and new) are mutually entailing (i.e., each implies the other), then the derivation works both ways. (2) From the entailed of the antecedent (malzūm muqaddamihā) and the necessary of the consequent — meaning if there is something that entails A (called D, i.e., D implies A), then from D and B's necessary we get 'if D then C'. The converse holds if the mutual entailment between antecedent and consequent is reversible (i.e., if A implies B and B implies A). (3) From the entailed of the antecedent and the very consequent (ayn tālīhā) — from D and B we get 'if D then B'. Conversely if the entailment of the antecedent is reversible (i.e., if A and D are equivalent). The author also gives the principle: 'the entailed of the entailed is entailed' (malzūm al-malzūm malzūm), which is the transitivity of entailment. The second set of rules involves contraposition: from the entailed of the contradictory of the consequent (malzūm naqīḍ tālīhā) and the contradictory of the antecedent, and from the contradictory of the consequent and the necessary of the contradictory of the antecedent. These are standard contraposition rules for conditionals. The teacher should illustrate with a fiqh example: e.g., 'If it is wine (A), then it is prohibited (B).' Then (1): 'If it is wine (A), then it is impure (C)' where C is a necessary consequence of B. (2): 'If it is a liquid intoxicant withdrawn from sale (D, which entails A), then it is impure (C).' (3): 'If it is a liquid intoxicant withdrawn from sale (D), then it is prohibited (B).' And contrapositive: 'If it is not prohibited (contradictory of B), then it is not wine (contradictory of A).'

Defining Foreign Premises in Syllogisms
Al-Khūnajī's objection and the author's refinement of 'foreign premise'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the author's refined definition of 'foreign premise' to evaluate syllogisms in Islamic juristic debates.

Core Idea
A foreign premise violates the syllogism's limits entirely, not just one.

In this passage, al-Khūnajī objects that certain conclusions are based on foreign premises, which would invalidate the syllogism. The author responds by refining what 'foreign' means: it is not a mere violation of one condition, but a breach of the entire set of formal limits. This clarification is essential for correctly evaluating arguments in munazara and usul al-fiqh, where the admissibility of premises is often contested.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the two reasons al-Khūnajī gives for calling the premises 'foreign'. First, they violate one limit of the syllogism. Second, the conclusion does not contain the original minor and major terms but one of them and its opposite.
The objection targets the validity of deriving conclusions from disjunctive premises.
2
Extract the author's response
The author redefines 'foreign premise' as one that violates the limits of the syllogism in their entirety (بجدها), not just a single limit. He points to Ibn Sina's practice in al-Shifa' as support.
The Arabic word 'بجدها' likely means 'بحدها' (its limit) but could be read as 'entirety'.
3
Apply the refined definition
Take a sample syllogism and check if a premise violates all conditions (form, number, arrangement) or just one. Only the former qualifies as truly foreign in this technical sense.
For example, in a categorical syllogism, if a premise has the wrong quantity but still fits the figure, it may not be 'foreign' by this definition.
4
Assess the implication for the original argument
Consider whether the premises al-Khūnajī objected to actually violate all limits or just one. If only one, they are not foreign, and the derivation stands.
The passage cuts off, so the full verdict is missing, but the logical move is clear.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection by al-Khūnajī against the earlier derivation of conclusions from disjunctive premises. He raises two points: (1) The premises used are 'foreign' (مقدمة أجنبية) because they violate one of the limits (حدود) of the syllogism—presumably the condition that the conclusion must follow directly from the given premises without introducing new elements. (2) The conclusions are not composed of the originally given minor and major terms but involve one of them and the opposite of the other. The author responds by redefining 'foreign premise': it is one that violates the limits in its entirety (بجدها/بحدها), not just in one limit. He then invokes the authority of Ibn Sina (الشيخ) in al-Shifa' to support his approach. Unfortunately, the passage cuts off, so the full argument is incomplete. In Islamic logic, 'foreign premises' are a key concept in ensuring syllogistic validity. An example: In a debate about the impermissibility of nabidh, if someone uses a premise like 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' which is not part of the original syllogism, it might be considered foreign unless it is a logical necessity.
