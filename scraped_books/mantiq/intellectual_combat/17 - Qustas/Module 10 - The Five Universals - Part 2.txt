Three Considerations of the Lowest Species
Author lists three ways to consider the lowest species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three considerations of the lowest species and apply them to a definition in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
The lowest species is both a real species and a relative one, bridging genus and species.

This passage from the Qustas introduces three angles for viewing the lowest species (al-nawʿ al-sāfil). Understanding these angles is essential when defining terms in fiqh and usul, because it prevents confusion between a species that is truly ultimate and one that is relative to its genus. The three considerations are: real, relative, and the combination called 'species of species' (nawʿ al-anwāʿ).

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'lowest species'
Find the phrase النوع السافل in the passage. Note that it is the species that has no species beneath it.
This is a key concept in the hierarchy of the five universals.
2
List the three considerations
From the passage, extract: (1) real (حقيقياً) because no species below; (2) relative (إضافياً) because the genus is predicated of it; (3) species of species (نوع الأنواع) when both are combined.
The third consideration is a compound of the first two.
3
Apply to an Islamic example
Take a fiqh term like 'wine' (خمر) which is a species under 'intoxicants' (مسكر). It is a real species because no species below 'wine' itself, but relative because 'intoxicant' is said of it. Thus 'wine' is also 'species of species'.
This helps clarify where a term fits in the genus-species tree.

Real vs Relative Species among the Five Universals
Critical refinement on which species is one of the five universals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the real species from the relative species and explain why only the real species is counted among the five universals.

Core Idea
The real species is a predicate; the relative species is a subject—but also a predicate.

When logicians speak of the five universals, they mean the real species (النوع الحقيقي), not the relative species (النوع المضاف). This passage clarifies that distinction by examining predication, then refines it by noting that the relative species can also be predicated. Understanding this nuance is crucial for defining terms correctly in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two candidates
The passage contrasts the real species (النوع الحقيقي) with the relative species (النوع المضاف). The question is which one is truly one of the five universals.
Real species: e.g., 'human' as a species of 'animal'. Relative species: e.g., 'human' relative to 'Zayd'.
2
Extract the criterion
The author states that one of the five must be a predicate (محمول), because the five universals are predicated of many. The universal (الكلي) is by nature a predicate.
The five universals are: genus, species, differentia, property, accident.
3
Apply the criterion to real species
The real species is a predicate: e.g., 'Zayd is a human' predicates 'human' of Zayd. So it satisfies the condition.
4
Consider the objection
The objection (من الإمام) claims that the relative species is a subject (because it has something above it, like genus), not a predicate, so it cannot be one of the five.
The relative species is predicated of individuals but is itself subject to higher genera.
5
Analyze the response
The author responds: although the relative species is a subject, it is also a predicate because it is a universal. It is predicated of its own individuals, so it too can be a predicate.
Thus both are predicates in different respects, but the real species is more basic.

Two Definitions of Differentia by Ibn Sina and the Debate on al-Khunaji's Choice
Author presents two definitions of differentia from Ibn Sina and discusses al-Khunaji's choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Ibn Sina's two definitions of differentia and evaluate al-Khunaji's preference for one over the other.

Core Idea
The differentia is a universal, but its precise definition determines how we analyze quiddities into genus and differentia.

The term 'differentia' (fasl) has a rich history. Ibn Sina provided two nuanced definitions, and later logicians debated which better captures the concept. Al-Khunaji's choice sparked a discussion about whether a quiddity can be composed of two co-extensive parts that defy genus-species analysis. This lesson unpacks both definitions and the arguments for and against each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Definitions
Locate the two definitions of differentia attributed to Ibn Sina. The first is from al-Isharat, the second from al-Shifa. Note the precise wording in Arabic.
The first definition uses 'al-shay'' (the thing), the second uses 'al-naw'' (the species).
2
Compare the Conditions
Each definition has conditions. The first has two: it must be a universal and must answer 'what is it in its essence?'. The second adds a third condition: 'from its genus'. Identify what each condition excludes.
The third condition in al-Shifa restricts the differentia to species that have a genus.
3
Understand al-Khunaji's Objection
Al-Khunaji rejected the second definition because it 'nullifies the confinement of the part to genus and differentia'. The worry: if a quiddity is composed of two co-extensive parts, neither would serve as genus or differentia, making the genus-species breakdown impossible.
This objection assumes quiddities can be composed of co-extensive parts.
4
Evaluate the Defense of the Second Definition
The author counters that any composite quiddity is either a substance (so substance is its genus) or an accident (so a category is its genus). Therefore, even a co-extensive composite has a genus, so the confinement is not broken. The second definition is preserved if we interpret 'differentia' as applying only to species with a genus.
The defense relies on the idea that every quiddity falls under a highest genus.

This passage defines the logical concept of 'differentia' (fasl) and presents two prominent definitions from Ibn Sina (the Shaykh) along with a discussion of which definition is preferable. The Differentia (Fasl) Indicates Quality, Not Essence
Author argues that differentia indicates quality not essence, refuting a prior view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the role of differentia from genus in a definition and identify whether a given differentia indicates essence or quality.

Core Idea
Differentia tells you 'what kind?', not 'what is it?'.

In Islamic logic, definitions are built from genus and differentia. But what exactly does the differentia contribute? Some mistakenly think it reveals the essence of a thing. This passage, quoting Ibn Sina and the Aristotelian tradition, clarifies that differentia indicates only a quality — it tells you 'what sort of thing' it is, not 'what it is'. Understanding this distinction is crucial for constructing precise definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
The passage begins 'And the Sheikh said: There is no need for it because...' The 'it' refers to a prior view that differentia is essential. Identify what the Sheikh (Ibn Sina) rejects.
2
Extract the definition of 'rational'
'Rational' is defined as 'a substance with speech' (jawhar dhū nutq). Note that 'substance' is the genus, but it is applied equivocally to humans and angels.
The common element 'speech' is not a true differentia.
3
Locate the key distinction
The author states: 'not one of the differentiae indicates what the thing is (mā al-shay’) but rather what sort of thing (ayyu shay’).' This is the central thesis.
This directly answers the learning objective.
4
Analyze the example: 'the walker with two legs'
This differentia qualifies a species (e.g., humans) but does not define its essence. It tells you a quality (walking on two legs) not what the thing is.
The example comes from Aristotle's Topics.
5
Connect to the definition of human
The passage ends with: 'Under animal is rational animal, and under this is human.' The differentia 'rational' distinguishes humans from other animals, but does not reveal the essence of 'animal' or 'human'.
This reinforces the distinction between genus (essence) and differentia (quality).

Commentary

The argument is that 'rational' (nāṭiq) is correctly defined as 'a substance with speech' (jawhar dhū nutq). The term 'substance' (jawhar) is said of both humans and angels only by commonality of expression (ishtirāk al-lafẓ), and the common element is 'speech' (nutq), which is not a true differentia because it is not distinctive. The author then asserts that it is clear truth that no differentia indicates 'what the thing is' (mā al-shay’) but rather 'what sort of thing' (ayyu shay’). This distinction is crucial: differentia answers the question 'what kind?' not 'what is it?'. The passage concludes with citations from Aristotle and Porphyry to support this view. In Islamic logic, this is consistent with the standard understanding: differentia, like 'nāṭiq' for humans, distinguishes the species within its genus but does not reveal the core quiddity.

Using Adjective Forms in Definitions: Essential vs. Derivational Predication
Author explains that predicates in definitions must be adjective forms, not abstract nouns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and derivational predication and correctly identify the proper form (adjective vs. abstract noun) for differentia and special properties in definitions.

Core Idea
In definitions, predicates must be adjectives, not abstract nouns, because only essential predication captures identity.

Have you ever written a definition and wondered whether to use 'rational' or 'rationality'? This short note from the classical logic tradition settles the matter: definitions require adjective forms. The reason lies in the type of predication involved: essential predication (al-muwata'ah) demands that the predicate share the same essence as the subject, which happens when we say 'human is rational', not 'human is rationality'. Let's unpack this rule and see why it matters for every definition you encounter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of predication
Essential predication (al-muwata'ah) means the predicate is identical in meaning to the subject, e.g., 'human is rational'. Derivational predication (al-ishtiqaq) means the predicate is derived from the subject and implies a relation, e.g., 'human is a speaker'. Definitions require the first type.
2
Examine the examples from the passage
The author gives two examples: for the species 'human', the differentia is 'rational' (natiq), not 'rationality' (nutq). For the special property, it is 'laughing' (dahik), not 'laughter' (dahk). Notice that each adjective form directly describes the essence of human, whereas the abstract noun would refer to a quality separate from the subject.
3
Explain why adjective form is required
In a definition like 'human is rational', the predicate 'rational' is predicated by essential predication (bi al-muwata'ah). If you used 'rationality', you would be saying 'human is rationality', which would mean human is identical to the quality itself—that is not the intended meaning. The adjective form specifies the type of being of the subject without implying a separate entity.
This applies to all the five universals: genus, species, differentia, property, and accident.
4
Apply the rule to another species
Try applying this to 'horse' (faras). The differentia of horse is 'neighing' (saahil), not 'neigh' (sahil). The special property might be 'galloping' (rakhad) not 'gallop' (rakhad). Check that you are using the adjective form, not the abstract noun.

Rather, whether a universal is a genus, differentia, property, or general accident depends on the actual individuals (afrād ḥaqīqiyya) under consideration. Understanding this relativity prevents the error of treating every universal as a true species.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal in context
Take a universal term like 'white' or a proper name like 'al-Dahhak'. Note what it is being predicated of.
The predication is always of a particular individual or class.
2
Determine the level of the actual individual
Ask: Is the individual a true species (e.g., 'human') or a specific instance (e.g., 'this Dahhak', 'this white perceptible')? The answer determines the role of the universal.
True species are natural kinds under a genus; individuals are concrete particulars.
3
Classify the universal accordingly
If the individual is a true species, the universal may be a property (khāṣṣa) or general accident (ʿaraḍ ʿāmm). If the individual is a specific instance, the same universal may function like a species (nawʿ) for that instance.
Example: 'al-Dahhak' is a property for 'human' but like a species for 'this Dahhak'.

Commentary
This passage clarifies that the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, general accident) are not fixed labels but depend on the level of predication relative to real individuals. The author begins by stating that these universals differ 'relative to their true individuals' – meaning the actual particulars they are predicated of. If this relativity were ignored, every universal, from the highest genus down, would be considered a true species, which is false. The example of 'al-Dahhak' (a proper name) illustrates this: for the specific person 'this Dahhak', the term 'Dahhak' functions like a species (it distinguishes him), but it is not a true species; rather it is a property (خاصة) of the species 'human' and of individual humans. Similarly, the universal 'white' is like a species for a particular white individual (since it delimits its essence in the category of quality), but 'white' as a general accident is predicated of the underlying substance that is the subject of whiteness, not of the whiteness itself. The author distinguishes between the role of 'white' as a property for human and as a general accident for the substrate.

Essential Accidents: Predicates That Flow from Essence
Avicenna's distinction of al-dhati as essential accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish essential accidents from definitional parts and identify them in Islamic scientific definitions.

Core Idea
Essential accidents are inseparable properties that arise from a thing's essence but are not part of its definition.

In logic, the term 'dhati' (essential) carries two distinct meanings, and confusing them leads to errors in definition and demonstration. The first meaning is the constituent part of the definition (genus and differentia). The second meaning, introduced here by Avicenna, is the essential accident ('arad dhati): a predicate that necessarily attaches to the subject from its very substance and quiddity, yet is not included in its definition. This distinction is crucial for understanding how properties are demonstrated in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of dhati in logic.
In the passage, Avicenna refers to 'another place' where dhati means something else. The first meaning (from earlier context) is the constituent part of the definition (e.g., genus and differentia). The second meaning is the essential accident: a predicate that attaches from the subject's substance and quiddity.
Remember that 'dhati' is ambiguous; context determines which meaning is intended.
2
Extract the definition of essential accident.
Avicenna defines it as 'the predicate that attaches to the subject from the substance of the subject and its quiddity'. Paraphrase: it is a property that follows necessarily from the essence without being a part of the definition.
The term 'jawhar al-mawdu' (substance of the subject) refers to the essence, not the Aristotelian category.
3
Analyze the examples given.
Examples: proportionality/equality for quantities, evenness/oddness for numbers, health/sickness for animals, snubness for nose. Notice that these are not part of the definition of the subject (e.g., 'even' is not part of the definition of 'number'), but they are inseparable properties that arise from the essence of the subject.
Contrast with accidental accidents like 'whiteness of a wall', which are not from the essence.
4
Distinguish essential accidents from definitional parts.
Essential accidents (al-a'rad al-dhatiyya) are necessary but not defining; definitional parts (genus and differentia) are what constitute the essence. The test: if the predicate is part of what the thing is, it is definitional; if it is an inseparable consequence, it is an essential accident.
In demonstration, essential accidents are often the middle term in proofs.

Commentary
This passage introduces a second, distinct meaning of the term 'al-dhati' as used in logic, which is the 'essential accident' ('arad dhati). Here, the essential is defined not as a part of the definition, but as a predicate that necessarily attaches to the subject from its substance and quiddity. For example, proportionality and equality attach to quantities as such, evenness and oddness to numbers, health and sickness to animals. These are not part of the definition of the subject, but they flow from the subject's essence. The author calls this kind 'essential accidents' and gives the classic example of 'snubness' (al-futus) for the nose: snubness is not part of the definition of nose, but it is an inseparable property that derives from the nose's essence. This passage clarifies that the term 'dhati' is polysemous, and the context (especially in demonstration) determines which meaning is intended.
