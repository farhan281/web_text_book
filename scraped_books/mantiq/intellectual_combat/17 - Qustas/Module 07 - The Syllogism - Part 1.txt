The Definition of Syllogism and the Middle Term
Author refines the definition of syllogism regarding the middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given syllogism definition implies repetition of the middle term and explain why it does not.

Core Idea
The syllogism definition does not require the middle term to be explicitly repeated.

A common misunderstanding among students of logic is that the definition of syllogism (qiyas) requires the middle term (al-wasat) to appear twice, once in each premise. This lesson clarifies that the standard definition—'a discourse in which, if certain propositions are laid down, something else necessarily follows'—contains no such condition. Understanding this frees us from a needless constraint and allows us to recognize implicit middle terms.

Guided Path
1
Recall the standard definition of syllogism
The standard Aristotelian definition: a syllogism is a discourse in which, certain things being stated, something other than what is stated necessarily follows. No mention of the middle term.
2
Identify what the middle term is
The middle term is the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. It is a logical feature of valid syllogisms, not a syntactic requirement of the definition.
3
Examine the author's claim
The author asserts: 'There is nothing in the definition of syllogism that indicates the necessity of repeating the middle term.' This means the definition itself does not force the middle term to be written twice.
4
Apply to an example
Consider the argument: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; every nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' The middle term 'intoxicant' appears in both premises implicitly (once as subject, once as predicate). The definition does not require it to be repeated as a separate term.
This shows the middle term is a logical relation, not a textual repetition.

Evaluating Conditional Necessity in Historical Logic
Refutation of Ibn Sina's historical claim via conditional logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a conditional claim in a scholarly argument and evaluate whether the implication is necessary or merely possible.

Core Idea
A conditional claim can be false even if its parts are true; necessity is the key.

This passage presents a sharp exchange between two giants of Islamic logic: Ibn Sina and Abu al-Barakat al-Baghdadi. The issue is whether conditional syllogisms (qiyāsāt sharṭīyya) were truly part of Aristotle's corpus. Ibn Sina says Aristotle mentioned them but they weren't translated. Abu al-Barakat objects that if Aristotle had mentioned them, they would have been translated—and that implication is not necessary. This is not just a historical footnote; it is a lesson in how to scrutinize conditional reasoning in any discipline, from kalam to usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional claim
Find the implied 'if...then...' in the text. Ibn Sina's statement implies: If Aristotle mentioned conditional syllogisms, then they would have been translated into Arabic.
The conditional is not explicit but is logically embedded in the claim.
2
Extract the antecedent and consequent
The antecedent (P) is 'Aristotle mentioned them'. The consequent (Q) is 'they were translated into Arabic'. The conditional is P → Q.
In Arabic: إن ذكرها لنقلت.
3
Understand Abu al-Barakat's objection
He denies that P necessarily implies Q. He says 'that is not necessary' (غير لازم), meaning the conditional claim is not valid. He is not denying P or Q individually; he is attacking the implication itself.
This is a critique of the strength of the conditional, not a modus tollens.
4
Apply the tool to test necessity
Ask: Could P be true while Q false? If yes, the conditional is not necessary. In this case, Aristotle might indeed have mentioned them, yet they might have been lost or never translated for other reasons. So the implication fails.
This is the core logical move: challenging the necessity of a consequence.

Refuting Kashshi on Fourth Figure Conclusion Modality
Refutation of Kashshi's claim on fourth figure conclusion modality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate claims about modal conclusions in the fourth figure of categorical syllogisms and identify exceptions based on proposition types.

Core Idea
Not every conclusion in the fourth figure is general or existential; temporal absolutes exist.

In modal syllogistic, determining the conclusion's modality is crucial. A scholar named Kashshi claimed a fixed pattern for the fourth figure. The author here corrects him, showing that the conclusion can also be temporal absolute non-perpetual. This refinement prevents overgeneralization in modal logic.

Guided Path

Identify Kashshi's claim
Locate the exact claim: Kashshi asserts that when the major premise is one of the four descriptive propositions in the second section of the fourth figure, the conclusion is either 'mutlaqa 'amma' (absolute general) or 'wujudiyya la da'ima' (existential non-perpetual).
2
Understand the author's refutation
The author responds 'laysa kathalik' (it is not so) and provides a counterexample: the conclusion may be 'hiniyya mutlaqa la da'ima' (temporal absolute non-perpetual).
3
Distinguish the proposition types
Differentiate 'mutlaqa 'amma' (true at all times), 'wujudiyya la da'ima' (exists but not always), and 'hiniyya mutlaqa la da'ima' (true at a specific time but not always). Understand their truth conditions.
4
Assess the impact
Consider why this matters: it refines the modal possibilities in the fourth figure, affecting the validity of syllogisms and the strength of conclusions in theological and jurisprudential arguments.

Circularity in Using Third Figure for Conversion
Critique of Urmawi's proof of simple conversion using the third figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the circularity in using the third figure to prove simple conversion and explain why it is considered proving the clear by the hidden.

Core Idea
The third figure cannot prove conversion without circularity; conversion is more fundamental.

This lesson examines a logical critique: some logicians attempted to prove simple conversion using the third figure of the categorical syllogism. However, this approach is problematic because it proves what is more evident (conversion) by what is less evident (third figure), and it leads to circular reasoning. We will analyze why the third figure depends on conversion and why the preferred method is to prove conversion directly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim Being Critiqued
Recognize that some logicians, including the author of Matali', used the third figure to prove simple conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi). This is the target of the critique.
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality.
2
Understand the Primary Objection
The passage states that using the third figure for conversion is 'proving what is more manifest by what is more hidden.' Conversion is a simpler, more evident operation than the third figure, so using the latter is logically backward.
3
Recognize the Circularity Argument
The third figure's productivity depends on converting the minor premise to reduce it to the first figure. If conversion itself is being proven by the third figure, then the proof presupposes conversion—making it circular (al-dawr).
This circularity is a key logical flaw.
4
Evaluate the Defense
The response in the passage says that the one who explains conversion via the third figure does not use it to prove the productivity of the third figure, but rather by another method. However, this is still criticized as 'poor ordering' because it explains something by what is not yet explained.
The defense does not fully resolve the issue.
5
Summarize the Preferred Method
The author recommends not referring to the third figure at all for proving conversion. Instead, conversion should be established directly, as done in the Qistas al-Afkar, without relying on more complex syllogistic figures.
This respects the logical order of simplicity.

Necessity of the Extra Condition for Particular Conditionals
Author explains why the extra condition must be necessary for the antecedent in a particular conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given particular conditional is true by checking if the extra condition in the antecedent is necessary.

Core Idea
For a particular conditional, the extra condition must be necessary, or any two things would seem to entail each other.

A particular affirmative conditional (muttaṣila mūjiba juz'iyya) states that sometimes when the antecedent holds, the consequent holds. But if the antecedent includes an extra condition beyond the simple existence of A, that condition must be necessary for A in that state. If not, the conditional becomes false, leading to absurd consequences. In this lesson, we will explore why necessity is required and how to test for it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and the extra condition
Locate the antecedent (muqaddam) and any extra condition (amr zā'id) attached to it. For example, in 'Zayd being together with Bakr', the extra condition is 'being together with Bakr'.
2
Check if the extra condition is necessary for the antecedent in that state
Determine whether the extra condition must be present for the antecedent to exist in the situation described. If it could possibly be absent while the antecedent still exists (albeit in a different state), then it is not necessary.
The author gives examples: 'stone taken with human' – stone can exist without human, so the condition is not necessary.
3
Test the entailment property
If the extra condition is not necessary, then the property of entailment (the necessity of the antecedent's negation when the consequent is negated) fails. This means the conditional 'Sometimes when A exists, B exists' does not hold.
The author shows that without necessity, any two things could be conditionally related, which is absurd.
4
Apply the test to real examples
Take an Islamic science example: 'Sometimes when a person performs wudu (with the intention of prayer), the prayer is valid.' The extra condition 'with the intention of prayer' must be necessary for the act of wudu to entail validity. If wudu without intention could also lead to validity (e.g., for cooling off), the conditional fails.
This demonstrates the logical principle in usul al-fiqh.

Necessary Conclusion from Necessary and Descriptive Conditionals
Author explains how a necessary conclusion follows from a necessary minor and a descriptive conditional major in the second and fourth figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a necessary conclusion follows from a necessary minor and a conditional major premise in the second or fourth figure, and identify the commentator's reservation about the fourth figure.

Core Idea
When the description in the conditional major has entry, the conclusion is necessary because opposition is between essence and description.

In modal syllogistics, combining a necessary minor premise with a conditional major premise that includes a description (waṣf) produces a necessary conclusion, provided the description is operative. This rule applies to the second and fourth figures. The passage also records a critical commentary questioning the fourth figure inference, showing that logical principles require careful scrutiny.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises
The minor premise is necessary (ḍarūriyya), e.g., 'Every A is necessarily B'. The major premise is conditional (mashrūṭa) and includes a description that conditions the predicate, e.g., 'Every B is C as long as it has description D'.
The description (waṣf) is an attribute that must have 'entry' (i.e., be relevant to the inference).
2
Determine the figure
The rule applies specifically to the second figure (middle term is predicate in both premises) and the fourth figure (subject-predicate roles reversed). Identify which figure the syllogism uses.
The passage mentions both figures; the commentary focuses on the fourth.
3
Check if the description has entry
Verify that the description in the major premise is not just incidental but actively conditions the predicate. If it does, the opposition (munāfāh) is between the essence of the minor term and the description of the major term.
If the description has no entry, the conclusion may not be necessary.
4
Derive the conclusion as necessary
Because the opposition is between the minor term's essence and the major term's description, the conclusion is necessary. Form: 'Every A is necessarily C (when condition D holds)'.
This conclusion holds in the second figure and, as per one commentator, also in the fourth.

This step shows that logical rules are sometimes debated among scholars.

Necessary Conclusion from Necessary Minor and Conditional Major
Samarkandi states a rule for modal syllogisms in the second and fourth figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a necessary conclusion follows from a necessary minor and a conditional major (attribute-linked) in a categorical syllogism, and evaluate the fourth figure complication.

Core Idea
When the minor is necessary and the major conditional on an attribute, the conclusion is necessary.

In modal syllogisms, the modality of the conclusion depends on the premises. Samarkandi states a key rule: if the minor premise is necessary (e.g., 'Every human is necessarily an animal') and the major premise is conditional on an attribute (e.g., 'Every walker is a human'), the conclusion becomes necessary. This lesson unpacks the logic and explores a commentator's reservation about the fourth figure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises
Locate the minor and major premises. The minor is a necessary proposition (ḍarūriyya: predicate belongs essentially to subject). The major is conditional (mashrūṭa) with an attribute involved (lil-waṣf madkhal).
Example: minor = 'Every human is necessarily alive'; major = 'Every breather is a human' (conditional on breathing).
2
Understand the attribute condition
The attribute in the major (e.g., 'breather') links to the essence in the minor. The 'opposition' (munāfāh) is between the essence of the minor's subject and the attribute of the major's subject.
This attribute-based link ensures the conclusion inherits necessity.
3
Determine the conclusion's modality
Because the opposition is between essence and attribute, the conclusion is necessary. In the example, from 'Every human is necessarily alive' and 'Every breather is a human', we get 'Every breather is necessarily alive'.
The necessity flows from the minor through the attribute link.
4
Evaluate the fourth figure remark
The commentator notes that the rule also applies to the fourth figure, but adds 'there is something in it' (وفيه ما فيه), hinting at a logical subtlety or exception.

Why Two True Disjunctives Yield No True Conclusion
Defense of Ibn Sina on sterility of two true disjunctives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's argument against producing a true disjunctive conclusion from two true disjunctives and identify the two conditions that lead to invalidity.

Core Idea
Two true disjunctives either contradict themselves or allow a third option, so no valid true disjunctive conclusion follows.

In this passage, the author defends Ibn Sina against critics who misunderstood his doctrine. Ibn Sina argued that a compound syllogism made of two true disjunctives cannot yield a true disjunctive conclusion. The reason is rooted in the relationship between the sides of the disjunctives: if they are identical, self-contradiction results; if different, a third option exists making both false.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being defended
Find the claim that two true disjunctives (الحقيقيتين) do not produce a true disjunctive conclusion (حقيقية).
2
Extract Ibn Sina's two conditions
Note the two cases: (1) the sides of the two disjunctives are identical (اتحدا) → this leads to self-contradiction (عاند الشيء نفسه). (2) they are different (تغايرا) → both disjunctives become false because there is a third possibility outside their sides.
3
Understand why no valid conclusion follows
In case (1), the conclusion would assert a contradiction; in case (2), the premises themselves are false because a third alternative exists. Thus no true disjunctive conclusion can be derived.
4
Reconstruct the formal argument
Let P and Q be the two true disjunctives. If their sides are the same, then the attempted conclusion is a self-contradiction. If their sides differ, then the disjunctives are not exclusive (since a third option exists), so they are not true (ḥaqīqiyya). Therefore no true disjunctive conclusion is possible.

Indeterminate Conclusion from Two True Disjunctives with One Negative
Samarkandi's analysis of disjunctive syllogism with one negative premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a syllogism of two true disjunctives with one negative produces no single definite conditional conclusion and to recognize the indeterminate pair of particular negative conditionals that it does produce.

Core Idea
Two true disjunctives with one negative yield two possible conditionals, not one definite.

In classical Islamic logic, disjunctive syllogisms are powerful but have subtle constraints. Samarkandi here explores a case where a syllogism composed of two true disjunctive premises (exclusive OR), with one premise negative, fails to give a unique conditional conclusion. Instead, it yields an indeterminate pair of particular negative conditionals. Understanding why helps us appreciate the conditions for valid inference in Islamic logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises
You have two true disjunctive propositions (exclusive OR). One of them is negative (either universal or particular). For example: 'Always, either the thing is human or not rational' (positive) and 'Not at all, either the thing is rational or human' (negative).
True disjunctive means exactly one disjunct is true, the other false.
2
Recognize the sought conclusion
The conclusion we look for is a conditional (muttasila) with a definite antecedent. Samarkandi asserts that no such definite conditional follows from these premises.
A definite conditional would be like 'If X then Y' with fixed terms.
3
Examine the two sides
Consider the side of the positive disjunctive and the side of the negative disjunctive. From each side, you can form a syllogism that seems to yield a conditional, but the conditionals differ depending on which side you start from.
The author gives examples: from the positive side, we get 'Always, either the thing is human or not rational' and 'Not at all...'; from the negative side, a different pair.
4
Understand the reductio argument
If you try to force a single definite conditional, you end up with a contradiction or falsehood. The passage shows that assuming a particular conditional leads to equality of terms and a false disjunctive, so the only valid conclusion is an indeterminate pair.
Reductio (khulf) is used to prove the impossibility of a definite conclusion.
5
Grasp the final result
The conclusion is one of two particular negative conditionals, not determined (la ala al-ta'yeen). The antecedent of one is the side of the positive disjunctive and the consequent is the side of the negative; the other is the reverse. Both are possible, and no further determination is justified.
The author gives an example with 'human' and 'not-rational' to illustrate why universal implication does not force a unique direction.

Correcting the Truth Condition for Universal Conditionals
Editor corrects condition for universal quantified conditionals and disjunctives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the correct truth condition for a universal conditional (muttasila) from that for a universal disjunctive (munfasila) and apply it to evaluate statements in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A universal conditional is false if there is even one case where the antecedent holds without the consequent.

This passage from Qustas al-Afkar offers a crucial correction to a phrase that could mislead students about when a universally quantified conditional or disjunctive proposition is considered true. The original text used terms related to incompatibility (inad) in an ambiguous way. The editor clarifies that conditionals and disjunctives have fundamentally different truth conditions. Understanding this correction will sharpen your ability to analyze compound propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ambiguous phrase
The original text mentioned 'عدم المعاندة' (lack of incompatibility) and 'تنافي العناد' (contradicting incompatibility) as conditions for the universal. These terms are appropriate only for disjunctives, not conditionals.
The editor sees the need to separate the two types of propositions.
2
State the correct condition for a universal conditional (muttasila)
For a universal conditional 'Whenever A, then B' to be true, it must be that in every position (wad') where A occurs, B follows; if there is any case where A occurs without B (or without implication luzum), the universal is false.
Implication (luzum) is the key relation; incompatibility is irrelevant here.
3
State the correct condition for a universal disjunctive (munfasila)
For a universal disjunctive 'Either A or B' to be true, whenever A is assumed, B must be absent; the two must be mutually exclusive (mutanadiyyan) in every case. Assuming A with B would violate the incompatibility (inad).
Incompatibility, not implication, is the defining relation.
4
Apply the correction to evaluate propositions
Given any universal conditional or disjunctive, ask: Is there a single counterexample where the antecedent occurs without the consequent (for conditional) or where both disjuncts occur together (for disjunctive)? If yes, the universal is false.
This test preserves the rigorous standard of universality in logic.

Distinguishing Matter and Form in Logical Arguments
Author states that logicians' errors arise from matter, not form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the matter (madda) and form (sura) of a syllogism, and identify which errors logic is responsible for.

Core Idea
Form is logic's domain; matter is the premises' truth.

In this short but crucial passage, the author defends logic against the charge that logicians' errors render logic useless. He makes a sharp distinction: the logical form (sura) of an argument—its structure—is what logic governs. The matter (madda), i.e., the truth or falsehood of the premises, is outside logic's purview. Errors often arise from false premises, not faulty form. Understanding this distinction protects you from blaming logic for mistakes that belong to other disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the form of an argument
Find the logical structure: what figure and mood does the syllogism have? For example, 'Every X is Y; every Y is Z; therefore every X is Z' is a first-figure (Barbara) form.
2
Examine the matter
Look at the content of the premises: are they true? Truth comes from sources outside logic—empirical observation, revelation, consensus, etc.
3
Locate the source of error
If the form is valid but the conclusion is disputed, the error lies in the matter: one or both premises are false or unaccepted.
4
Defend logic against misplaced blame
When you see a syllogism that is formally valid but whose conclusion is rejected, separate form from matter. Logic only guarantees that if the premises are true, the conclusion follows; it does not guarantee the premises' truth.

Immediate Necessary Concomitant and Al-Razi's Proof: A Logical Analysis
Author states immediate necessary is self-evident; Imam al-Razi argues for it; response clarifies logical scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Imam al-Razi's conditional argument for the self-evidence of immediate necessary concomitants and explain the logical response based on the square of opposition.

Core Idea
Immediate necessary concomitants are self-evident; al-Razi's proof uses a conditional, but the response refines the quantifier logic.

This passage tackles a foundational epistemological question: Are immediate necessary concomitants (lāzim qarīb) self-evident? The author asserts they are, and Imam al-Razi offers a powerful conditional proof that if they were not, knowledge would be impossible. The response, however, is a masterclass in logical precision: it shows that denying a universal affirmative does not entail the universal negative, only the particular negative. This subtle distinction saves the possibility of knowledge without infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Define the immediate necessary concomitant
Find where the author defines lāzim qarīb: 'every immediate necessary... is self-evident.' Note the condition 'without a middle' and the definition of self-evident as conceptualization of subject and property sufficing for certainty.
The term 'bayyin' is crucial: it means the relation is known immediately upon conception.
2
Reconstruct Imam al-Razi's conditional argument
Al-Razi argues: If not every immediate necessary were self-evident, then knowledge of the unknown would be impossible (because it would require an infinite chain of middle terms). This is a conditional (istithnā'ī) argument. Identify the antecedent and consequent.
The argument form is modus tollens implicitly: if not P then Q, but Q is impossible (regress), so P must hold.
3
Identify the logical response
The response states: denying the universal affirmative 'Every immediate necessary is self-evident' does not give the universal negative 'No immediate necessary is self-evident', but the particular negative 'Some immediate necessary is not self-evident'. This is a standard rule of the square of opposition.
The response uses immediate inference (subalternation). The conditional proof fails because it assumes the stronger negation.
4
Understand the epistemological upshot
The response allows that some immediate necessary may be self-evident, avoiding the regress. Thus knowledge of the unknown is possible via self-evident intermediates that are themselves immediate necessary concomitants.
This shows how logical precision prevents a philosophical dead end.

Core Idea
A negative minor can still produce a conclusion if the negative relation is repeated in the major premise.

Khūnajī identified a subtle condition for syllogisms with a negative minor premise. Without the repetition of the negative relation, such syllogisms are invalid; with it, they become necessarily valid. This lesson unpacks that condition using the example from the source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the minor premise
Notice that the minor premise is negative: 'Nothing of C is B' (universal negative). This is key because such a minor normally prevents a valid conclusion in standard figures.
2
Examine the major premise for repetition
Look at the major premise. In the first example, it is 'Every B is A' — the term B is not negated. In the second example, it is 'Everything not B is A' — here the subject is negated (not B). This 'repetition' of the negative relation from the minor into the major is the condition.
The negative relation 'not B' appears in the major only in the second example.
3
Determine validity
If the negative is not repeated (first example), no conclusion follows. If it is repeated (second example), the conclusion 'Every C is A' follows necessarily. Thus, the condition is both necessary and sufficient for validity.
The conclusion is derived by converting the minor to 'Every C is not B' (via obversion), then combining with the major.

Why Real Exclusive Disjunction Has Only Two Parts
Proves that the real exclusive disjunction is limited to two parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to prove that a real exclusive disjunction (al-munfasila al-ḥaqīqiyya) must consist of exactly two parts using the condition of contradictory opposition.

Core Idea
A real exclusive disjunction is only possible between a proposition and its contradictory — no third option exists.

This passage presents a logical proof that the real exclusive disjunction cannot have more than two parts. The key insight is that genuine separation (infiṣāl ḥaqīqī) requires the two parts to be contradictories or equivalent to contradictories. Any third part would destroy this relationship. Let's walk through the proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of real exclusive disjunction
A real exclusive disjunction is between two propositions that are mutually exclusive (cannot both be true) and collectively exhaustive (cannot both be false). This is exactly the condition for contradictory opposition (taqābul al-tanāquḍ).
In Arabic logic, al-infiṣāl al-ḥaqīqī is the strongest form of disjunction.
2
Identify the author's initial claim
The author states that the affirmative real disjunction 'is composed of a proposition and its contradictory, or equal to its contradictory.' This means the two parts are contradictories or logical equivalents of contradictories.
'Equal to its contradictory' means a proposition that is logically equivalent to the contradictory.
3
Consider the case of three parts
If you try to form a real disjunction with three parts, pick any two of them. The author argues that then 'the first implies the contradictory of the second.' That is, one part logically entails the negation of another part.
This implication violates genuine separation because if the first is true, the second must be false by implication, not by mutual exclusivity.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since genuine separation requires that no part implies the contradictory of another, three parts cannot satisfy the condition. Therefore, a real exclusive disjunction can only have two parts.
This is a proof by reductio: assuming three parts leads to a contradiction of the definition.

Condition for Universal Conditionals and Anmari's Objection
Author explains why certain positions of the antecedent are necessary for universal conditionals, and responds to an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Anmari's objection to the Shaykh's condition for universal conditionals and evaluate the author's response.

Core Idea
To be certain of a universal conditional, you must check every position of the antecedent—even if unusual cases are possible.

This passage presents a dialectical exchange about the truth conditions of universal conditional propositions. The Shaykh argues that to have a true universal conditional, one must consider all possible positions (awḍāʿ) of the antecedent. Al-Anmari objects using the example of a thing implying both a proposition and its opposite, as in reductio ad absurdum. The author clarifies that the requirement is about obtaining certainty, not about necessary falsity. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to evaluate universal claims in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
State the Shaykh's rule for universal conditionals
Identify the condition he sets: for an implicative conditional (muttaṣila) to be true universally, the antecedent must always imply the consequent; for a disjunctive (munfaṣila), the parts must always be mutually exclusive. In other words, no position of the antecedent should allow the opposite.
2
Identify the 'positions' (awḍāʿ) considered
For the implicative, positions where the antecedent occurs without the consequent or without implication would falsify the universal. For the disjunctive, positions where both parts coexist or mutual exclusion fails would falsify it. The Shaykh says if these positions are not considered, the universal would not be true.
3
Reconstruct al-Anmari's objection
Al-Anmari argues that even if you take the antecedent with the absence of the consequent, the universal affirmative need not be false. Why? Because it is possible for a thing to imply both a proposition and its negation—like in qiyās al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum), where a composite of a thing and its opposite implies both. Thus the objection challenges the necessity of the Shaykh's condition.
4
Explain the author's response
The author says the claim is about obtaining certainty (jazm) of universality, not about necessary falsity. Al-Anmari's scenario is possible but not necessary; therefore it does not undermine the requirement that we must consider all positions to be certain. The condition stands as a sufficient path to certainty.

Analyzing the Source of Necessity in Conditionals

Core Idea
Necessity in a conditional must be intrinsic, or else it leads to regress or breakage.

Conditional syllogisms rely on a necessary connection between antecedent and consequent. But what grounds that necessity? An objector might ask: where does the necessity come from? In this short passage, the author presents a precise two-case answer. By following the logical structure, you will see how he defends the intrinsic necessity of the conditional relation.

Guided Path

Identify the objection
Find the phrase 'فإن قلت' which signals the objection. The objector asks about the source of 'ضرورة ثبوت ذلك الأمر' (the necessity of the existence of that thing) that links the antecedent and consequent.
2
Case 1: Intrinsic necessity
The author responds with two cases. First, if the necessity is due to the antecedent itself ('لذات المقدم'), then the thing always follows from the antecedent. This turns the conditional into a necessary proposition, and the absolute ('مجردة') becomes non-absolute.
This means the conditional is always true by its own nature.

Second, if the necessity is due to another cause ('لأمر آخر'), then that other cause's existence must be considered. Subcase a: if that other cause is itself necessary for the antecedent, then the discussion repeats ('عاد الكلام فيه') – we have a regress.
4
Case 2: External necessity, subcase b
Subcase b: if the external cause is not necessary for the antecedent, then it is possible for the antecedent to exist without that cause, breaking the conditional relation ('يلزم الفساد'). This shows that external necessity leads to instability.

This passage presents a vital rule for converting categorical propositions into conditional (hypothetical) form. The conversion is not merely mechanical; it requires careful attention to the quantity, quality, and modality of the original categorical. Understanding this rule allows you to move smoothly between logical forms, a skill essential for constructing valid arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

1
Identify the categorical proposition

Commentary
This is a dialectical passage where the author defends the definition of qiyas (syllogism) against an objection by al-Khūnajī. The objection is that in qiyas al-musawat (syllogism of equality), the middle term is not repeated, so such arguments cannot be genuine syllogisms. The author systematically refutes this. First, he considers two possible purposes behind the opponent's affectations: either to repel the objection, which fails because the objection remains intact, or to correct the middle term, which also fails because their refutation is unsound. He then examines two claims: that the middle term is not repeated in their syllogisms, and that their argument does not produce a conclusion with qiyas al-musawat per se. Both are false. The second is false because their own reasoning is clear to the intellect. The first is false because the definition of syllogism does not require repetition of the middle term; if it did, qiyas al-musawat would already be excluded without further effort. The Lidhatihi Clause: Excluding Enthymemes from Qiyas
Author explains the role of 'lidhatihi' in excluding enthymemes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a valid syllogism from a simple enthymeme by applying the condition 'lidhatihi' (per se) in the definition of qiyas.

Core Idea
The clause 'lidhatihi' excludes arguments that depend on missing premises.

After refuting al-Khūnajī, the author adds a crucial refinement: the definition of qiyas includes the phrase 'lidhatihi' (for itself) to exclude simple enthymemes. These are arguments like 'He speaks, therefore he is alive' which only produce a conclusion because of an unstated premise. This lesson will teach you to identify such enthymemes and understand why they are not valid syllogisms in the strict sense.

Guided Path
1
Identify the function of 'lidhatihi'
The author explains that 'lidhatihi' serves as a caveat (ihtiraz) against arguments that conclude only by means of omitted premises. Locate the examples given: 'So-and-so speaks, therefore he is alive', 'This is perceptible, therefore it exists', 'Since the sun is risen, daytime exists'.
2
Analyze why these are not valid qiyas
Each of these examples relies on an implicit premise. For instance, 'He speaks, therefore he is alive' depends on the unstated premise 'Everyone who speaks is alive'. Without that premise, the conclusion does not follow necessarily. The definition of qiyas requires that the conclusion follow from the stated premises alone, without hidden assumptions.
3
Distinguish between qiyas and enthymeme
A qiyas is a self-contained argument; an enthymeme leaves out a premise. The 'lidhatihi' clause explicitly excludes enthymemes. Recognize that in Islamic logic, a proper syllogism must be transparent and complete.
4
Address the anticipated objection
Someone might say that these examples are already excluded by the phrase 'composed of propositions' (mu'allaf min qadaya). The author replies that exclusion by one condition does not prevent exclusion by another. Both conditions reinforce the rigor of the definition.

Syllogism with a Necessary Strange Premise
Author explains two types of qiyas using a strange premise and gives an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of syllogism that use a necessary strange premise and apply aks al-naqid (conversion of the contradictory) to derive a conclusion from a given strange premise.

Core Idea
A single strange premise can suffice for a syllogism when combined with aks al-naqid.

The author of al-Qustas introduces a special type of syllogism that employs a 'necessary strange premise' (muqaddima ghariba lazima). Though unusual, this premise is logically indispensable. The passage explains two ways such a premise can produce a conclusion: alone or with a deleted premise. We will focus on the first type, where the strange premise alone suffices after applying the conversion of the contradictory to one of its constituents. The example uses the metaphysical concept of 'substance' (jawhar) and its parts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
Locate the strange premise: 'Part of substance, the removal of which causes the removal of substance' (juz' al-jawhar yujibu irtifā'uhu irtifā'a al-jawhar). The second premise: 'Everything that is not a substance does not cause the removal of substance' (kull mā laysa bi-jawhar lā yujibu irtifā'uhu irtifā'a al-jawhar).
The second premise is the major, the first is the minor.
2
Determine which premise needs conversion
The conclusion 'Part of substance is a substance' is affirmative, but the major premise is negative ('not...does not'). To make it fit a first-figure syllogism, we need an affirmative major. This is achieved by aks al-naqid on the major.
3
Apply aks al-naqid to the major premise
Convert the major by contrapositive: 'Everything that is not a substance does not cause removal of substance' becomes 'Everything that causes removal of substance is a substance'. This is the contrapositive.
The operation: from 'No non-A is B' to 'Every B is A' (after double negation).
4
Form the new syllogism
Now we have: Minor: 'Part of substance causes removal of substance'. Major (converted): 'Everything that causes removal of substance is a substance'. Conclusion: 'Part of substance is a substance'. This is a valid Barbara syllogism.
5
Confirm the logical structure
Check that the middle term ('causes removal of substance') is the same in both premises, and that the conclusion follows necessarily. The strange premise alone, after conversion, suffices.
