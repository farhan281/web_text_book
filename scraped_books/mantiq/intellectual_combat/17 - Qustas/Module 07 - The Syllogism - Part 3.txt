Four Methods to Prove Syllogistic Modes
Lists four proof methods for syllogistic modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe and apply the four proof methods: converting premises, contraposition, reduction to absurdity, and assumption.

Core Idea
Syllogistic validity can be demonstrated by transformation, contradiction, or direct case assumption.

Once a syllogistic mode is proposed, we need to prove it is productive (yields a necessary conclusion). The classical logicians developed four methods: converting both premises, contraposing one or both, reduction to absurdity (al-khalf), and assumption (al-iftirāḍ). Each method provides a rigorous check. This lesson walks through each method with examples from the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand tabdīl al-muqaddimatayn (converting the two premises)
This method swaps the minor and major premises. The truth is preserved because conversion does not change the truth value of categorical propositions in certain cases (e.g., universal negative converts to universal negative). After swapping, the syllogism may reduce to a known productive mode.
The passage mentions this as the first method: 'إما بتبديل المقدمتين'.
2
Understand ʿaks (contraposition)
Contraposition involves converting a proposition by turning it into its contrapositive (e.g., 'All A are B' becomes 'All non-B are non-A'). This method can change the figure and reduce the syllogism to a known valid mode.
The passage distinguishes between contraposing both premises or just one.
3
Understand al-khalf (reduction to absurdity)
Assume the contradictory of the conclusion. Combine this with one of the premises. If a contradiction is derived, then the original conclusion must be true. This method is common in kalam debates.
The passage lists 'بالخلف' as a method.
4
Understand al-iftirāḍ (assumption)
Assume a particular individual that satisfies the premises, and show that the conclusion holds for that individual. Since the premises are about categories, this instantiation demonstrates the conclusion necessarily follows.
This method is less common but mentioned in some commentaries.
5
Apply the methods to an Islamic example
Take the syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. Every nabidh is an intoxicant. Therefore, every nabidh is forbidden.' This is Barbara. Prove it by reduction to absurdity: assume some nabidh is not forbidden, then by the major it would not be intoxicant, contradicting minor.
Try each method on this example to see how they work.

Compound Premises and Barren Modes in Syllogistic Logic
Explains how compound premises can produce conclusions in barren modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify compound premises and determine how they can yield conclusions in barren syllogistic modes via contraposition.

Core Idea
Compound premises hide affirmative components that unlock otherwise barren syllogisms.

In the first, third, and fourth figures, the minor premise must be affirmative for a valid syllogism. But if the minor is a negative compound—like 'No S is P' which contains an implicit affirmative—we can transform it via contraposition to extract an affirmative. This salvages the syllogism. The same applies to the second figure when premises agree in quality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the barren condition
Recall that in figures I, III, IV a minor negative premise makes the syllogism barren. But the text says a negative compound can still yield a conclusion.
Barren mode: no valid conclusion from the direct premises.
2
Recognize a compound premise
A compound premise is a negative proposition (e.g., 'No A is B') that implies an affirmative component. For example, 'No substance is accident' implies 'Every substance is non-accident'.
The implicit affirmative is the contrapositive of the original.
3
Apply contraposition (inʿikās)
Convert the negative compound to an affirmative by contraposition: e.g., 'No S is P' becomes 'Every non-P is non-S' or in this tradition, 'Every non-P is S' (as per the example 'No substance is accident' → 'What is not accident is substance').
The text specifically uses the conversion of negative to affirmative.
4
Construct the valid syllogism
Use the affirmative version as the minor premise and combine with the major. Al-Khunji's example: Minor: 'Substance is not accident' (converted to 'Every substance is non-accident'), Major: 'Everything non-accident is independent of subject', Conclusion: 'Substance is independent of subject'.
This is a first-figure AAA (Barbara) syllogism.
5
Verify the conclusion follows
Check that the middle term 'non-accident' connects the premises. Since both premises are universal affirmative, the conclusion is universal affirmative. The syllogism is valid.
Without the conversion, the original minor would have made the syllogism invalid.

Condition of Actual Existence in the First Figure's Minor Premise
Author states that in the first figure, the minor premise must be actual (bi'l-fi'l) for production.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a minor premise in a first-figure syllogism meets the condition of actuality and explain why a possible minor leads to inconclusive variation.

Core Idea
A possible minor premise in the first figure makes the conclusion uncertain.

In mixed modal syllogisms, the modality of premises affects the certainty of the conclusion. The author focuses on the first figure and establishes a key condition: the minor premise must be actual (fi'liyya). Why is actuality required? Because if the middle term is only possibly attributed to the minor, the subsumption fails and the conclusion becomes unstable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise being discussed
The author talks about the minor premise (sughra) in the first figure. Notice that the condition is about its modality: it must be actual (bi'l-fi'l).

Understand the consequence of non-actuality
If the minor is not actual, the middle term might not be actually affirmed of the minor. Then the minor is not truly subsumed under the middle, so the judgment about the major cannot transfer reliably.
3
Trace the reasoning chain
The author argues: without actuality, it becomes possible that the major is sometimes affirmed and sometimes denied of the minor, leading to variation (ikhtilaf). This is the logical problem.
4
Consider the three perspectives
The condition holds whether we consider external reality, essential attributes, or mental concepts. The need for actuality is universal.

Ibn Sina's Reductio Proofs for the Possible Minor with Necessary Major
Four reductio proofs for a modal syllogistic claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate Ibn Sina's four reductio proofs for the productivity of a possible minor premise with a necessary major premise.

Core Idea
A possible minor with a necessary major yields a necessary conclusion, proven by four elegant reductio arguments.

Ibn Sina (the Shaykh) made a bold claim in modal syllogistics: when the minor premise is contingent (possible) and the major is necessary, the conclusion is necessary. To prove this, he offered four reductio arguments that assume the opposite and derive absurdities. Understanding these proofs deepens your grasp of modal logic and the power of reductio reasoning in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Claim
The minor premise is possible (contingent), the major is necessary. The conclusion must be necessary. Note the apparent tension: how can a possible premise yield a necessary conclusion?
The claim is defended by showing that assuming otherwise leads to absurdity.
2
First Reductio (أ)
Assume the contradictory of the conclusion (the general possible) is true. Actuate it and make it a minor premise. With the necessary major, this yields a necessary conclusion in the second figure, contradicting the original possible minor. The impossibility follows from the assumption, not from the actuation or the major.
The impossible does not follow from actuating a possible, nor from the true major.
3
Second Reductio (ب)
Similarly, arrange the actuated contradictory as a minor premise to produce the contradictory of the major in the third figure, again an impossibility.
This parallel proof reinforces the previous conclusion.
4
Third Reductio (ج)
Assume the original possible minor as actual. This leads to a contradiction of the contradictory of the conclusion, resulting in a conjunction of two contradictories (impossible).
Again, the impossibility is traced to the contradictory assumption.
5
Fourth Reductio (د)
Assume the minor actual; the conclusion necessarily follows because the minor term falls under the middle. If the conclusion were not necessary, the actualization of a possible would convert a non-necessary into necessary, which is impossible.
This proof shows that the conclusion must be necessary in itself, not just under the assumption.

The Rule for Conclusion Modality in First Figure
Author states the rule determining when the conclusion follows the major, minor, or both.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether the conclusion of a first-figure categorical syllogism follows the major or the minor premise based on the type of propositions involved.

Core Idea
The conclusion follows the major unless it is descriptive; then it follows the minor.

In the first figure of categorical syllogisms, the modality (necessity, existence, etc.) of the conclusion is not always inherited from the major premise. This passage from the Qusṭās al-Afkār lays down a precise rule to decide whether the conclusion takes the modality of the major or the minor, depending on the type of propositions involved. We will unpack this rule step by step, using examples from Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major premise type
Check whether the major premise is one of the 'descriptive' propositions (al-waṣfiyyāt): conditional (mashrūṭa) or customary (ʿurfiyya). If it is, the rule for the conclusion changes.
Descriptive propositions include two types of conditionals and two types of customary propositions.
2
Apply the default rule for non-descriptive majors
If the major is NOT descriptive, the conclusion follows the major's modality exactly. For example, if the major is 'Every intoxicant is necessarily forbidden', and the minor is 'Nabidh is an intoxicant', then the conclusion 'Nabidh is necessarily forbidden' follows the major's necessity.
This is the standard dictum de omni.
3
Apply the exception for descriptive majors
If the major is descriptive (conditional or customary), the conclusion follows the minor's modality, except for two restrictions: necessity (ḍarūra) and existence (wujūd). For each restriction, if the major has it, the conclusion takes it from the major; otherwise from the minor.
If the minor lacks an existence restriction, add it from the major.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Consider: Major (customary): 'Usually, wine causes intoxication.' Minor: 'This is wine.' Since the major is customary (descriptive), the conclusion should follow the minor's modality (which is actuality here): 'This causes intoxication.' But if the major had a necessity restriction, it would override.
Practice with different proposition types to internalize the rule.

Why the Conclusion Follows the Major's Modality
Author explains the rationale for the default rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the logical principle (dictum de omni) that justifies why the conclusion inherits the major's modality in non-descriptive first-figure syllogisms.

Core Idea
The major is predicated of every individual of the middle; the minor is one such individual.

The author not only states the rule but also provides its justification: in non-descriptive first-figure syllogisms, the conclusion follows the major because the major term is predicated of every individual of the middle term with a specific modality, and the minor term is included among those individuals. This lesson unpacks that justification using the familiar dictum de omni.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
In a first-figure syllogism, label the major term (al-akbar), middle term (al-awsaṭ), and minor term (al-aṣghar). For example, intoxicant (major), nabidh (middle), forbidden (minor).
The major usually is the predicate of the conclusion; the minor is the subject.
2
State the dictum de omni
The author says: 'If the major is predicated of all individuals of the middle with a specific modality, and the minor is among them, then the major is predicated of the minor with that modality.' This is the core logical principle.

3
Apply to a non-descriptive example
Consider: 'Every intoxicant is necessarily forbidden' (major). 'Nabidh is an intoxicant' (minor). Since 'forbidden' is predicated of every intoxicant with necessity, and nabidh is an intoxicant, it follows 'Nabidh is necessarily forbidden'.
The modality (necessity) is preserved.
4
Contrast with descriptive case
If the major were customary ('Usually, intoxicants are forbidden'), the dictum de omni would not apply straightforwardly because the predicate is not true of every individual in the same way; the rule then switches to the minor.
This shows why the author needed a special rule for descriptive propositions.

Al-Kashshi's Objection on Modality Transfer
Al-Kashshi objects that a necessary minor with a perpetual major yields a necessary conclusion, and the author refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate al-Kashshi's objection regarding the necessity of the conclusion in a mixed modal syllogism and articulate the author's refutation based on man' al-intaj.

Core Idea
Modality of the conclusion follows the weaker premise, not the minor alone.

In modal syllogisms, a key rule states that the conclusion's modality generally follows the major premise when premises are non-descriptive. Al-Kashshi challenged this rule for the specific case of a necessary minor and perpetual negative major, arguing the conclusion must be necessary. The author's response clarifies why this inference fails, reinforcing the original rule. This lesson unpacks the objection and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule under challenge
Al-Kashshi opposes the rule that in a modal syllogism, the conclusion's modality follows the major premise (e.g., if the major is perpetual, the conclusion is perpetual, not necessary). Locate this rule in the passage.
The rule is implicit: the author defends it against al-Kashshi.
2
Understand al-Kashshi's claim
He asserts that when the minor is necessary and the major is perpetual negative, the conclusion should be necessary. Note that he does not deny the perpetual nature of the major; he thinks necessity overrides.
3
Examine al-Kashshi's two proof methods
First, he converts the major (thus turning a perpetual negative into a perpetual negative of the other term) to form a second-figure syllogism that supposedly yields the necessary conclusion. Second, he uses a reductio: joining the contradictory of the conclusion with the major to produce the contradictory of the minor, which is impossible, thus proving the conclusion necessary.
The second figure with a necessary minor and a perpetual major would normally give a possible conclusion if valid.
4
Analyze the author's refutation
The author simply denies the production (man' al-intaj) of a necessary conclusion from a necessary minor and perpetual major in the second figure. Instead, he states the correct result is possible (mumkina). This preserves the rule that the conclusion's modality follows the major.
The refutation is by rejecting the premise that the syllogism is valid for necessity; it is valid only for possibility.

Governing Rule for Conclusion Modality in the Second Figure
General rule for conclusion modality in second figure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for determining the modality of the conclusion in a second-figure syllogism, including the exceptions for 'existence' and 'necessity' restrictions.

Core Idea
The major premise's perpetuity governs the conclusion's modality, with careful exceptions.

The second figure of categorical syllogism requires a clear rule for determining the modality (جهة) of its conclusion. This lesson unpacks the general rule: if the major premise is perpetual (دائم), the conclusion is perpetual; otherwise, it follows the minor premise, but with two important exceptions involving 'existence' and 'necessity' qualifications. Understanding this rule is essential for evaluating modal arguments in Islamic sciences like Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figure
Confirm the syllogism is second figure: middle term is predicate in both premises. This is necessary before applying the rule.
2
Check the major premise for perpetuity (دوام)
Examine the major premise: if it is a perpetual proposition (holds at all times without temporal restriction), then the conclusion is perpetual regardless of the minor.
3
If not perpetual, follow the minor with exceptions
If the major is not perpetual, the conclusion takes the modality of the minor premise, except: (a) ignore any 'existence' restriction (قيد الوجود) in either premise, and (b) ignore any 'necessity' restriction (قيد الضرورة) unless the major has a descriptive necessity (ضرورة وصفية).
These exceptions prevent unwanted modal shifts.
4
Apply to an example
Take a second-figure syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is haram' (perpetual major) and 'Every nabidh is intoxicant' (perpetual minor). Since the major is perpetual, the conclusion 'Every nabidh is haram' is perpetual, not necessary.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical reason why two necessary premises in the second figure do not yield a necessary conclusion, using external propositions as an example.

Core Idea
Necessary premises in the second figure do not guarantee a necessary conclusion because a predicate may hold actually but not necessarily.

In this passage, the author explains a critical limitation of the second figure: even if both premises are necessary, the conclusion may not be necessary. This is because the second figure lacks the middle term's direct link to the extremes that would force necessity. We will examine the author's example and see why this matters for modal logic in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figure and the premises
Recognize that the syllogism is in the second figure (the middle term is the predicate of both premises). The author states that two necessary premises do not produce a necessary conclusion.
Remember the second figure structure: major premise: P is (not) M; minor premise: S is (not) M; conclusion: S is (not) P.
2
Extract the example
The example: 'No horse is a donkey necessarily' (major) and 'Every riding-animal of Zayd is a horse necessarily' (minor). The middle term is 'horse'.
3
Test the supposed necessary conclusion
The expected necessary conclusion would be 'No donkey is a riding-animal of Zayd necessarily'. But the author points out that Zayd might ride a donkey, so 'Some donkey is a riding-animal of Zayd possibly' is true, making the necessary conclusion false.
This shows the conclusion is not necessary because the predicate (riding-animal of Zayd) is accidental, not essential.
4
Generalize the reason
The reason: a quality may actually occur in one species but not in another, even if it is possible for the latter. Thus the first species is necessarily denied of the second, but it is impossible to necessarily deny that quality of the second species.
This applies to external propositions where truth depends on actual existence.
5
Extend to attributive and temporal necessity
The same failure occurs for attributive necessity (darura wasfiyya) and temporal necessity (darura waqtiyya). The example does not produce a necessary conclusion in these modalities either.
This shows the limitation is robust across different types of necessity.

Commentary
This passage first explains why the second figure does not produce a necessary conclusion from two necessary premises in the case of external propositions (qadaya kharijiyya). The reason is that a quality may actually hold for one species but not for another, even though it is possible for the second. For example, 'no horse is a donkey' is necessarily true, and 'every riding-animal of Zayd is a horse' is necessarily true, but the conclusion 'no donkey is a riding-animal of Zayd' is not necessary because Zayd might ride a donkey (even if he doesn't actually). The reasoning: the middle term will be necessarily related to one extreme and necessarily not related to the other, causing a necessary incompatibility between the extremes. Even if the other premise is not necessary, the necessity of the middle term's relation to the necessary extreme forces the conclusion to be necessary. This is a classic debate in Islamic logic about modality in the second figure. Ibn Sina and al-Razi's Defense of Necessary Conclusion
Argument of Ibn Sina and al-Razi for necessary conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina and al-Razi's argument that one necessary premise in the second figure suffices for a necessary conclusion.

Core Idea
If one premise is necessary, the conclusion is necessary due to the necessary incompatibility between the extremes.

In the second part of the passage, Ibn Sina (the Sheikh) and Imam al-Razi counter the earlier claim. They argue that even if only one premise is necessary, the conclusion must be necessary. Their reasoning relies on the concept of essential incompatibility (munafat dhatiyya) between the extremes brought about by the middle term's necessary relation. This lesson will walk through their argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
State the claim
The claim: If one of the two premises is necessary, the conclusion is necessary. The other premise can be necessary, non-necessary, or possibly either.
They consider three cases for the other premise.
2
Case 1: Both premises necessary
If the other premise is also necessary, then the middle term is necessarily related to one extreme and necessarily unrelated to the other. This creates an essential incompatibility (munafat dhatiyya) between the extremes, forcing the conclusion to be necessary.
For example, if 'every B is necessarily A' and 'no C is necessarily A', then B and C are necessarily incompatible, so 'no C is necessarily B'.
3
Case 2: One premise non-necessary
If the other premise is not necessary, the necessity of the middle term's relation to the extreme of the necessary premise still forces a necessary conclusion. The necessity of the middle term's relation (positive or negative) to the necessary extreme is itself necessary. This, combined with the other premise, reduces to a compound of two necessary premises, yielding the same outcome.
The author says 'it reduces to a compound of the two necessary premises'.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The key is that the middle term's relation to the necessary extreme is necessary; this necessity is not affected by the modality of the other premise. Therefore, the extremes are necessarily incompatible (or compatible), producing a necessary conclusion.
This relies on the idea that necessity distributes across the terms.

Necessary Conclusion from One Necessary Premise
Proof that a necessary conclusion follows from at least one necessary premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that a necessary conclusion follows when at least one premise in a categorical syllogism is necessary, regardless of the other premise's modality.

Core Idea
One necessary premise suffices to produce a necessary conclusion — even if the other premise is contingent.

In modal logic, the strength of the conclusion depends on the premises. This passage presents the argument of Avicenna and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi that when at least one premise is necessary (ضرورية), the conclusion must also be necessary. They systematically examine the three possibilities for the other premise—necessary, not necessary, or possible—and show that in each case the conclusion follows necessarily. Understanding this principle is crucial for evaluating theological and legal arguments that rely on necessary truths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author asserts that if one premise is necessary, the conclusion is necessary. This is the thesis to be proved.
2
Consider the other premise's possibilities
The other premise can be either necessary, not necessary (i.e., contingent or impossible), or possible (neither necessary nor impossible). The proof covers all three cases.
3
Analyze the first case: both premises necessary
If both premises are necessary, the middle term is necessarily affirmed of one extreme and necessarily denied of the other. This creates an essential opposition (منافاة ذاتية) between the extremes, forcing the conclusion to be necessary.
Example: 'Every human is necessarily an animal; every animal is necessarily mortal' implies 'Every human is necessarily mortal.'
4
Analyze the second case: one necessary, one not necessary
If the second premise is not necessary, the necessary relation of the middle term to the necessary extreme and its necessary negation of the non-necessary extreme still produce a necessary conclusion. This reduces to the first case because the necessity of the middle term with respect to both extremes is preserved.
5
Analyze the third case: one necessary, one possible
If the other premise is possible (neither necessary nor impossible), it either turns out to be true necessarily or not. In either sub-case, the conclusion remains necessary, completing the proof.
Thus in all scenarios, the conclusion is necessary.

Al-Khunaji's Critique of Necessary Conclusion Proofs
Al-Khunaji refutes the proofs of Shaykh and Razi for necessary conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential opposition (ذات) and descriptive opposition (وصف) and explain why al-Khunaji argues that the conclusion of a modal syllogism is only perpetual (دائمة), not necessarily necessary.

Core Idea
Essential opposition does not force descriptive opposition – a key modal refinement.

Al-Khunaji scrutinizes the proofs of Avicenna (al-Shaykh) and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi for necessary conclusions in modal syllogisms. He uncovers a subtle but critical confusion: the opposition (منافاة) derived from the premises occurs between the essences of terms, but the required conclusion concerns a relation between essence and description. This lesson unpacks his critique and its implications for modal logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two arguments refuted
The passage mentions 'the first [argument]' and 'the second [argument]' by Shaykh and Razi. These arguments attempt to prove that a necessary conclusion follows from certain premises. Identify what each argument claims, though the passage only provides the refutation.
The arguments themselves are not quoted, but the critique assumes they rely on opposition between essences.
2
Extract the key distinction
Al-Khunaji distinguishes between opposition between the essence of the minor and the essence of the major (ذات الأصغر و ذات الأكبر) and opposition between the essence of the minor and the description of the major (ذات الأصغر و وصف الأكبر). The first is what the premises give; the second is what the conclusion requires.
The distinction hinges on the fact that descriptions (أوصاف) can be accidental, not part of essence.
3
Understand the counterexample
The passage refers to a previous counterexample: 'No donkey is a horse' (opposition of essences) but 'Some donkey is Zayd's mount' (possibility between essence of donkey and description of being Zayd's mount). This shows that essential opposition does not entail descriptive opposition.
The counterexample is based on earlier discussion (كما مر من النقض).
4
Analyze the perpetual conclusion
Al-Khunaji grants that if the major premise is universal, the conclusion becomes a perpetual negative (saliba da'ima) – always true, but not necessary in the strict modal sense. The description is not actually established for the essence; otherwise the major premise would lose its universality.
Perpetual (دائمة) means the proposition holds at all times, but not that the opposite is impossible.

When a Necessary-Conditional Syllogism Fails: The Condition of Description Input
Author proves that a necessary-conditional syllogism fails when description has no input.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which a necessary-conditional syllogism fails to produce a necessary conclusion and explain the failure using the provided example.

Core Idea
A conditional major cannot necessitate a conclusion if the description does not apply to the minor's subject.

In modal logic, combining a necessary minor with a conditional major does not always yield a necessary conclusion. The author of the Qustas demonstrates a specific failure: when the description in the conditional premise has no 'input' into the subject of the minor, the syllogism fails to generate a necessary conclusion in any modal sense (essential, descriptive, or temporal). This lesson unpacks that failure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises and their modalities
The minor premise is a necessary universal negative: 'No donkey is a horse that is Zayd's mount' (by essence). The major premise is a conditional universal affirmative: 'Every mount of Zayd is a horse that is Zayd's mount as long as it is Zayd's mount'.
Note that the major is conditional (mashrūṭa), not absolute.
2
Check the description's input
The description in the major is 'being Zayd's mount'. Does this description have an existential input into the subject of the minor (donkey)? No, because donkeys are not necessarily mounts of Zayd; the description does not apply to donkeys in a necessary way.
Without this input, the conditional cannot transfer necessity.
3
Test the conclusion
The apparent conclusion 'No donkey is a mount of Zayd' would be necessary if the syllogism were valid. But this conclusion is false: a donkey could become Zayd's mount. Hence the syllogism is invalid.
The conclusion is false in all three modal senses (essential, descriptive, temporal).
4
Understand why the syllogism fails
The necessity of the minor does not transmit to the conclusion because the major's condition is not necessarily connected to the minor's subject. The description (mount of Zayd) does not inhere in donkeys essentially.
This failure illustrates that modal syllogisms require careful attention to the modalities of terms.
