Signification is either by convention, reason, or nature — each with distinct epistemic implications.

Privative and Positive: Samarqandi's Refinement of the Four Relations
Samarqandi offers a new answer to Katibi's puzzle using 'privative' and 'positive' instead of 'modified' and 'unmodified'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between privative ('adami) and positive (wujudi) terms in the context of the four relations, and explain why Samarqandi rejected the conditions of permanence and actuality.

Core Idea
Samarqandi replaced 'modified' with 'privative' to clarify the four relations beyond existence and non-existence.

The four relations (al-nisab al-arba')—equality, inclusion, exclusion, and overlap—are fundamental to categorical propositions in Islamic logic. Earlier logicians used 'modified' (ma'dul) and 'unmodified' (muhassal) to classify terms, but this created puzzles about how privative terms like 'non-human' relate to positive terms. Following Fakhr al-Razi and Afdal al-Din al-Khunaji, Samarqandi introduced 'privative' ('adami) and 'positive' (wujudi) as clearer alternatives, and he denied that the relations require the terms to be actual or permanent. This lesson examines that refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four relations
List the four standard relations between categorical terms: equality (musawat), inclusion (tadakhul), exclusion (tabayun), and overlap (mubayana min wajh). Note that these are the relations that need to be determined for any two terms.
These relations apply regardless of whether the terms are positive or privative.
2
Understand the old terminology
The earlier scheme used 'ma'dul' (modified) for negative terms like 'non-living' and 'muhassal' (unmodified) for positive terms like 'living'. Consider why this might cause confusion when determining relations—e.g., is 'non-human' a modified term and how does it relate to 'human'?
The puzzle is that 'non-human' seems to be a term but its relation to 'human' is exclusion, yet as a term it could be treated as having its own positive content.
3
Recognize the new terminology
Samarqandi adopts 'adami' (privative) and 'wujudi' (positive) from al-Razi and al-Khunaji. 'Privative' indicates absence of a property, not just logical negation of the entire term. This distinction matters for how terms relate: a privative term like 'non-human' is simply the privation of humanness, not a positive property.
This clarifies that the relation between 'human' and 'non-human' is exclusion, as one denies the other.
4
Analyze the denial of conditions
The passage says Samarqandi denies that 'permanence' and 'actuality' are required for the relations to hold. For example, a privative term like 'non-existent' can still have a determinate relation to 'existent' (exclusion) even if 'non-existent' is not an actual entity. Why is this important? It prevents ontological assumptions from interfering with logical relations.
This streamlines the application of the four relations to all terms, including abstract privatives.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the terms 'wajib al-wujud' (necessary existent) and 'mumkin al-wujud' (contingent). Are these positive or privative? How do they relate? Use the four relations framework. 'Necessary existent' is positive; 'contingent' is also positive but has a dependency. They are related by inclusion (the necessary includes existence fully, the contingent includes existence partially) or overlap? Determine the correct relation.
This connects the refinement to kalam discussions of divine attributes.

The Four-Category View: Relation as Genus
Samarqandi presents the four-category view and its justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the argument that relation (al-nisba) is the genus of the seven remaining categories and evaluate its plausibility within Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Categories reduce to four when relation is seen as the genus of the other seven.

In Islamic philosophy, the number of categories was debated. While Aristotle listed ten, some thinkers argued for just four: substance, quantity, quality, and relation. In this passage, Samarqandi presents and endorses this reduction, arguing that the other seven categories are essentially relations. Let's analyze the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four categories given: substance (jawhar), quantity (kamm), quality (kayf), and relation (nisba). Note that these are considered ultimate genera.
2
Understand the claim about relation
The passage claims that relation is the genus for the seven other categories (e.g., place, time, position, state, possession, action, passion). This means each of those is a species of relation.
The seven are taken from the standard ten minus the first three.

Follow the argument
The reasoning: if you remove the concept of relation from any of the seven, their reality disappears. Therefore, relation must be part of their quiddity (mahiyya). Thus, relation is not just one category but the genus of the others.
The passage uses the phrase 'لو رفعنا مفهوم النسبة عنها لما بقيت حقيقتها'.
4
Evaluate the argument
Consider one of the seven, e.g., 'action' (al-fi'l). Does it inherently involve a relation? In Islamic theology, God's action of creation relates to created beings. Similarly, 'place' involves a relation between an object and location. Assess whether any category might resist this reduction.

The Four-Category Classification and the Role of Relation
Argument for reducing categories to four by making relation a genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument for reducing the ten categories to four and explain why relation is considered a genus for the seven relatives.

Core Idea
Relation is the logical glue holding seven categories together—without it, they dissolve.

The standard Aristotelian list includes ten categories, but some philosophers compressed them into four: substance, quantity, quality, and relation. In this passage, the author endorses this reduction and provides a reasoning based on what happens to the seven relational categories when relation is removed. We will analyse this argument and see how relation becomes a genus for the seven.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two classifications
Note the standard ten categories vs. the four-category reduction presented here. What are the four? Substance, quantity, quality, relation.
The seven relational categories are the remaining ones: when, where, position, state, action, passion, and relation itself or something similar.
2
Extract the core argument
The author says: if we remove the concept of relation from the seven relational categories, their reality would not remain. Therefore relation is part of their essence.
This is a conditional reasoning: if relation is removed, the categories cease to be what they are; hence relation is intrinsic.
3
Assess the role of relation
The author claims relation is a genus (jins) for the seven relatives. What does it mean to be a genus? It means relation is a universal predicable of all of them, and without it they cannot be defined.
A genus is common to multiple species; here relation is the genus, and each of the seven is a species differing in specific difference.
4
Consider the implication
If relation is a genus, then the seven categories are not independent categories but species of relation. The classification becomes four main categories, with relation serving as a supercategory.
This is a significant reinterpretation of the category scheme, compressing the usual ten into four.

Suhrawardi reduces the categories to five, adding motion as an independent category.

Suhrawardi presents a critical note on al-Sawi's categorization of the predicaments. While al-Sawi limits them to four — substance, quantity, quality, and relation — Suhrawardi argues that motion (al-haraka) cannot be subsumed under any of these four. He asserts that motion has its own distinct essence, and thus must be recognized as a separate category. Essential Accidents and Their Three Types
Essential accidents defined and classified into three types based on causal mediation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify essential accidents in a subject based on their causal relation to the essence, and distinguish the three sub-types (without intermediary, with equal intermediary, with part).

Core Idea
Essential accidents are caused directly by the essence, not by external factors.

This passage introduces the foundational concept of essential accidents (عرض ذاتي) in logic. These are the properties that are investigated within a science because they arise from the very essence of the subject. By understanding the three modes of causation—direct, via an equal intermediary, or via a part of the essence—you will be able to identify what truly belongs to the subject as an essential accident. This classification is crucial for defining the scope of any science.

Identify the general principle

Define essential accident

Learn the three sub-types
The author gives three examples: (a) without an intermediary (wonder for man), (b) with an equal intermediary (laughter via wonder), (c) with a part of the essence as intermediary (movement via being a body). For each, trace the causal chain back to the essence.

Core Idea
The term 'dhati' has multiple meanings in logic; confusing them leads to category errors in definition and proof.

This passage from the Qustas presents three distinct meanings of 'al-dhati' (essential). Understanding these meanings is crucial because they appear across Islamic sciences: essential predication in definitions, essential cause in causal reasoning, and essential existence in metaphysics. The Definitions of Genus and Differentia
Author defines genus and differentia

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between genus (jins) and differentia (fasl) and identify them in a definition from an Islamic science text.

Core Idea
Genus is what shares; differentia is what distinguishes – together they define an essence.

In logic, every real definition (hadd) is composed of a genus and a differentia. The genus captures what a thing shares with others, while the differentia captures what is unique to it. This lesson unpacks these two fundamental pillars of definition as stated by the author.

Guided Path
1
Define genus
The author says 'that by which there is sharing is the genus'. Identify the shared essential attribute in a definition.
The genus must be essential, not accidental.
2
Define differentia
The author says 'that by which there is distinction is the differentia'. Identify the distinguishing essential attribute.
The differentia separates the species from others in the same genus.
3
Apply the condition for genus
The author discusses a condition: if the differentia is required to be additional to the shared attributes to form a species, then the commonality is not a genus. Understand this nuance.
This prevents overly broad categories from being considered genera.
4
Identify genus and differentia in an example
For 'human', the genus is 'animal' and differentia is 'rational'. Practise on other Islamic examples like 'salah' or 'zakat'.
Ensure the genus and differentia are essential.

Near and Far Genus and Differentia
Author divides genus and differentia into near and far

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a genus or differentia as near (qareeb) or far (ba'eed) based on the number of steps needed in a definition.

Core Idea
A near genus directly applies; a far genus requires multiple steps. The closer the genus, the more essential attributes it conveys.

The author divides each of the two pillars of definition into near and far. Understanding this division helps you evaluate how precise a definition is. A near genus is the immediate answer to 'what is it?', while a far genus is more abstract. Similarly for differentia. This lesson unpacks these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Define near genus
A near genus is one where the answer to 'what is it?' is the same for the essence and everything that shares with it. For example, 'animal' is the near genus of 'human' because both human and other animals are directly called 'animal'.
The answer is one and immediate.
2
Define far genus
A far genus requires multiple answers to reach the essence. For example, 'body' is a far genus of human because we first say 'animal', then 'living body', etc.
More remote genera have fewer essential answers.
3
Define near differentia
A near differentia distinguishes the essence from everything that shares its genus or existence. For human, 'rational' is near because it separates human from all animals.
It distinguishes from all, not just some.
4
Define far differentia
A far differentia distinguishes only from some. For example, 'sentient' distinguishes living from non-living but not among animals.
It leaves some sharers undistinguished.
5
Apply the classification to an Islamic example
Take 'salah' as an example. Its near genus is 'acts of worship', far genus is 'actions'. Near differentia might be 'specific movements and recitations', far differentia might be 'ritual acts'.
Classify based on the number of steps needed to define the term.

Resolving a Logical Objection: Genus and the Category of Relative
Author presents an objection and answer regarding the categorization of logical genus under the relative category.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional argument structure in a dialectical exchange and apply the technique of distinguishing different conditions to resolve an apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
Resolving contradiction by distinguishing conditions saves genus from being relative more specific.

In this lesson, we examine an objection claiming that if logical genus were from the category of relative, then genus would be more specific than relative, which contradicts the fact that genus is more general. The author of Qistas al-Afkar resolves this by distinguishing two senses of 'relative': unconditional and conditionally absolute. This dialectical move is a prime example of how careful semantic analysis can defuse logical puzzles.

Guided Path
1
Parse the objection's conditional
The objection is a conditional: If genus is from relative (antecedent), then genus is more specific than relative (consequent). The opponent points out that genus is actually more general, so the consequent is false, and by modus tollens, the antecedent must be false.
2
Identify the two senses of 'relative'
The author distinguishes two ways of considering 'relative': (1) without any condition (la bi-shart shay'), which is more general than genus; (2) under the condition of absoluteness (qayd al-itlaq), which is more specific than genus.
3
Apply the distinction to the argument
The premise 'genus is from relative' is ambiguous: if taken as the unconditional relative, genus is indeed more general; if taken as the conditioned relative, genus is more specific. Thus, the contradiction evaporates once the ambiguity is clarified.

Mutual Instancehood vs. Specificity: A Commentator's Critique
Author presents an objection and answer regarding the categorization of logical genus under the relative category.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between instancehood (musadaqiyya) and specificity (akhasiyya) and explain how mutual instantiation challenges the traditional hierarchy of genus and species.

Core Idea
Instancehood is mutual; specificity is one-way: relative and genus can be instances of each other.

The commentator in this passage expresses confusion about the author's distinction between 'without condition' and 'absoluteness' and proposes an alternative view: that 'relative' and 'genus' are not in a hierarchy of generality but rather each is an instance of the other. He then introduces a crucial distinction between 'instancehood' and 'specificity', which is fundamental for understanding predication in logic.

Understand the commentator's confusion
The commentator admits he does not grasp the difference between the two conditions (la bi-shart shay' and qayd al-itlaq). He suggests that perhaps the author's answer is not clear.
2
Extract the alternative proposal
Instead, the commentator proposes that 'relative' and 'genus' are neither more nor less complete; rather, each is an instance (musadaq) of the other. That is, they have mutual instantiation.

Instancehood (musadaqiyya) is a relation that can hold in both directions (e.g., 'human' is an instance of 'animal', and vice versa? Actually, the commentator argues that 'relative' and 'genus' are mutual instances, which is unusual. Specificity (akhasiyya) is one-way and non-reflexive.

This is debatable but illustrates the concept.

Author defines the ten categories and classifies accidents.

The ten categories are the most fundamental classification of all existents. Understanding them is essential for any serious engagement with Islamic philosophy and theology. In this passage, the author defines substance and the nine accidents, giving precise criteria for each. Pay close attention to how the definitions hinge on the concepts of subject, division, and relation.

The Category of Relation: Repeated Relation (Idafa)
Author elaborates on the category of the relative, distinguishing simple relation from repeated relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between a simple relation (nisba) and a repeated relation (idafa) as discussed by Ibn Sina and illustrated by the ceiling-wall example.

Core Idea
Not every relation is an idafa; idafa requires considering both ends of the relation.

The category of 'the relative' (al-mudaf) is one of the most subtle accident categories. The author expands on it by quoting Ibn Sina's al-Shifa', introducing the concept of 'repeated relation' (nisba mutakarrara). This lesson unpacks that distinction, which is crucial for understanding how relational properties are classified in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of relation
The author distinguishes between a simple relation (nisba) and an addition/idafa. A simple relation is one-sided, e.g., the ceiling's relation to the wall. An idafa is a repeated relation, where both ends are considered as having a relation.
The example: ceiling resting on wall is a simple relation if only considering the ceiling; it becomes idafa when considering both ceiling and wall as bearing a relation to each other.
2
Parse the ceiling-wall example
Read the example carefully: The ceiling has a relation to the wall (resting). If you look at the ceiling only from the perspective of its relation, it is a nisba. But if you also consider the wall as that to which it is related (the wall is the support), then it becomes an idafa. Note the difference in perspective.
The key phrase: 'النسبة تكون لطرف واحد والإضافة تكون للطرفين' (the relation is for one side, the addition is for both sides).
3
Apply the definition to other relations
Apply the concept of repeated relation to other examples, such as fatherhood (al-ubuwwa). Fatherhood is idafa because it requires both father and child; the relation is mutual.
Not all relations are idafa; for example, the relation of knowledge to a known object might be considered one-sided in some contexts.
4
Identify the sources and limitation
Note that the author explicitly cites Ibn Sina's al-Shifa' as the source for this distinction. This shows the expansion_probe nature of the text. The author also acknowledges that the enumeration of categories is based on induction.

Reduction of Categories to Four
Author notes that some scholars reduce the ten categories to four: substance, quantity, quality, and relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories in the reduction and explain how the remaining six are subsumed under relation.

Core Idea
Categories reduce to four: substance, quantity, quality, and relation.

Earlier you learned about the ten Aristotelian categories. Some philosophers proposed a simpler classification, reducing them to just four: substance, quantity, quality, and relation. This lesson explores this alternative view and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Recall the standard ten categories
List the ten categories: substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, possession, action, passion. Keep them in mind.
2
Identify the four proposed categories
The reduction keeps only substance, quantity, quality, and relation. Note that 'relation' (nisba) is used broadly here.
3
Understand the subsumption of the remaining six
The categories place, time, position, possession, action, and passion are considered subtypes of relation. For example, 'place' is a relation between a body and its location, 'time' is a relation between events.
This reflects a conceptual simplification: all accidents are modifications of quantity, quality, or relation.
4
Consider the philosophical motivation
This reduction emphasizes that the primary ontological kinds are substance (independent) and accidents (dependent), with accidents further classified into quantity, quality, and relation.
Think of how this might help in analyzing theological concepts like attributes.

Core Idea
A proposition's logical type depends on whether it requires actual truth, possible truth, or no external condition.

In Islamic logic, propositions are classified not only by quantity and quality but also by the nature of the truth required. This classification—external, real, and mental—affects how we assess arguments in kalam and fiqh, especially when dealing with universal claims about existence. For an external proposition, the predicate must be actually true of the subject in external existence. For a real proposition, it is enough that it could be true in the external (even if not realized). A mental proposition requires no condition—truth is assessed purely conceptually.

Positive, Modified, and Negative Terms in Proposition Conversion
Author clarifies the distinction between positive, modified, and negative terms for conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between positive (muḥaṣṣal), modified (ma‘dūl), and negative (salab) terms, and apply the rule that the negation of a negation is affirmation in conversion.

Core Idea
A negative term becomes positive when negated again: salb salb = ījāb.

In the classification of universal affirmatives, the quality of the terms—whether they are positive, modified, or purely negative—determines the conversion rule. The author emphasizes that when taking the negation of each extreme for contraposition, we must take pure negation (salb), not modification (ma‘dūl). This distinction is crucial because the negation of a negative term produces a positive term, affecting the form of the converse.

Guided Path
1
Define the three term qualities
Positive (muḥaṣṣal): a term without any negation, e.g., 'human'. Modified (ma‘dūl): a term with a privative adjective, e.g., 'non-human' (not pure negation). Negative (salab): a term expressing pure absence, e.g., 'not human'.
Modified and negative are not the same; modified is a privative class, negative is logical absence.
2
Understand the conversion rule
To find the converse of a universal affirmative, take the negation of each extreme in the sense of pure negation (salb), not modification. Then the resulting proposition is affirmative with a negative predicate.
The author explicitly says: 'we take the negation of each of the two extremes in the sense of negation, not modification.'
3
Apply the rule to a negative term
If either extreme is negative (salb), when we negate it we get a positive term because 'the negation of a negation is affirmation'. For example, if the subject is 'not human' (negative), its negation is 'human' (positive).
This is a key logical principle: double negation eliminates.

The Conditional Proposition and Its Two Types
Author defines the conditional proposition and divides it into connected and disconnected with sub-types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a conditional proposition as connected or disconnected and identify the two subtypes of disconnected conditionals (haqiqiyya and mani'at al-jam').

Core Idea
The conditional proposition relates two propositions by condition or disjunction.

This lesson introduces the conditional proposition (shartiyya), a core element of Islamic logic used in kalam and usul al-fiqh. You will learn its two main divisions—connected and disconnected—and the distinctions within them. Mastering these types will help you analyse arguments that depend on conditional or disjunctive reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
The author divides the conditional into two: connected (muttasila) and disconnected (munfasila). The connected expresses a conditional dependency (e.g., 'If A then B'), while the disconnected expresses incompatibility between two propositions.
2
Distinguish the connected conditional subtypes
Within connected, the author notes an affirmative form (judging the existence of a proposition given another) and a negative form (judging the negation of the consequent). For example, 'If the prayer is valid, then the ablution is valid' is affirmative; 'If the prayer is valid, then the intention is not absent' is negative.
3
Understand the disconnected conditional types
The disconnected (disjunctive) has two types: haqiqiyya (real) where the propositions cannot be both true and cannot be both false (exclusive disjunction), and mani'at al-jam' (preventive of conjunction) where they cannot be both true but can be both false. Example: 'Either the world is eternal or created' is haqiqiyya; 'Either the act is obligatory or forbidden' could be mani'at al-jam' if there are options in between.
The author mentions that the definition can be interpreted more narrowly (excluding haqiqiyya) or more broadly (including it), depending on whether a condition of non-prevention is added.
4
Apply the classification to real Islamic arguments
In kalam, proofs often use disconnected conditionals. For instance, al-Ash'ari argued: 'Either the world is eternal or originated; it is not eternal; therefore it is originated.' This uses haqiqiyya disjunction. In usul al-fiqh, a jurist might say: 'Either the sale is valid or invalid; it is not valid; therefore it is invalid.'
