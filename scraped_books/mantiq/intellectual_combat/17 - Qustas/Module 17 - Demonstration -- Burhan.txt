Necessary concomitants require both direct and mediated forms to avoid impossible consequences.

The author divides necessary concomitants into existential (like blackness for Ethiopians) and essential ones. Each can be directly attached to the subject or through an intermediary. He then proves that both types—with and without intermediary—must exist. Understanding the Doubt on Proving Entailments
Doubt on entailment proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and restate the doubt raised against the proof of entailments by contradiction in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A doubt questions whether assuming the converse false truly leads to impossibility.

Even rigorous logical methods face scrutiny. This lesson examines a famous doubt (shubha) regarding the proof that a converse or conclusion follows from a premise via contradiction. The doubt challenges the crucial step: does assuming the opposite really produce an impossibility? Understanding this doubt sharpens your grasp of logical proof structures.

Guided Path
1
State the standard method being doubted
The standard method proves that if A implies B, then B must be true when A is true. To prove B, assume not B, derive a contradiction (or impossibility) from A plus not B, so B must follow.
This is reductio ad absurdum (burhān al-‘aks).
2
Parse the doubt statement
The doubt says: 'If the converse is not true on the assumption of the original being true, then its negation would be true, and an impossibility would follow.' Identify the parts: assumption (original true), denial (converse false), negation (converse false -> its negation true), and claimed result (impossibility).
The doubt questions whether the impossibility necessarily follows.
3
Understand the doubt's target
The doubt attacks the 'if...then' link: that if the converse is false, then its negation being true leads to an impossibility. It suggests this link might not hold, thus undermining the proof.
Logicians later clarified why the impossibility does follow, e.g., by converting the original.
4
Consider the broader impact
If valid, this doubt would affect all proofs relying on contradiction in logic, including many in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh. The fact that it was raised and answered shows the self-correcting nature of Islamic logic.

Meanings of 'Necessary' (Daruri) in Demonstration
Author clarifies two meanings of 'necessary' and refutes an infinite regress objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the general and specific meanings of 'necessary' as defined by al-Razi and explain how this distinction refutes the infinite regress objection.

Core Idea
Necessity in premises is conditional on conceptualization, not absolute.

In demonstration, a key objection is that if premises must be necessary, an infinite regress of necessities follows. Al-Razi answers by clarifying that 'necessary' has two senses. The general sense requires conceptualization of terms and relations; the specific sense is stronger. This distinction blocks the regress because the chain can terminate at premises that are necessary in the general sense. Let us unpack this logical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt
The objection claims that if every premise in a demonstration must be 'necessary', then either we have an infinite regress of necessary premises or a circularity. Al-Razi's answer hinges on what 'necessary' means.
2
Extract the general meaning of 'necessary'
Al-Razi says: the two premises are 'necessary' in the general sense if, when you conceptualize their terms and the relation between them, you know that the relation holds. So necessity here depends on an act of conception.
This is conditional — if you conceive, then you know. Not everyone may have conceived.
3
Extract the specific meaning of 'necessary'
In the specific sense, mere conceptualization of the subject (the term alone) gives knowledge of the proposition. This is a stronger, more immediate necessity.
Al-Razi does not fully elaborate here, but contrasts it with the general.
4
Apply the distinction to the regress objection
If the objector means 'necessary' in the general sense, then not everyone shares in it because conceptualization is not universal. If he means the specific sense, the regress is not forced because the chain can end at premises that are necessary in the general sense (which is weaker).
Thus the infinite regress does not arise — the distinction dissolves the objection.

Distinguishing Self-Evident from Nearly Self-Evident in Logical Proofs
Author discusses an objection that the use of mubayina makes proofs redundant and refutes it by distinguishing self-evident from nearly self-evident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between self-evident (bayyin bi-nafsihi) and nearly self-evident (qarib min al-bayyin) propositions, and explain why the mubayina-based argument fails as a self-evident proof.

Core Idea
Self-evidence has degrees; confusing near-self-evidence with true self-evidence invalidates a critique of proofs.

Some critics claimed that the standard syllogistic proofs are unnecessary because the very distinction (mubayina) between the terms makes the conclusion self-evident. The author refutes this by showing that the reasoning involved, though simple, requires a subtle thought, so it is not truly self-evident. This lesson will help you recognize the important difference between what is immediately obvious and what is obvious only after a moment's reflection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objectors' claim
Find the statement: some people said there is no need for demonstrations because when the middle term is affirmed of one extreme and denied of the other, the distinction between extremes necessarily occurs. They think this makes the conclusion self-evident.
The objectors believe that mubayina alone suffices to make the conclusion obvious without a separate proof.
2
Understand the mubayina reasoning
The objectors argue: if A is distinct from B, and J is not A, then J is distinct from A, so J is not A. This is a restatement of the conclusion from the premises. The author says if this is taken as an argument, it is just repeating the claim.
The reasoning is circular if presented as a proof; it only points out the obviousness of the conclusion.
3
See the author's refutation: degrees of self-evidence
The author argues that the mind does arrive at the conclusion naturally, but only with a subtle thought (firk latif) and little reflection. Therefore it is not genuinely self-evident (bayyin bi-nafsihi) but close to it (qarib min al-bayyin). The objectors confused these two categories.
This distinction is crucial: not everything that seems obvious is self-evident; some things require a minimal mental step.
4
Recognize the practical role of this reasoning
The author notes that the Imam (Avicenna) uses this mubayina-based explanation in all figures of the syllogism and calls it 'al-lamiyya' (the why-proof). It is not a dispensible proof but a legitimate type that shows the reason.
Thus, the objection is refuted: the reasoning is not superfluous; it is a useful but non-self-evident proof.

Author begins enumerating the six types of certain premises, defining the first three.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the first three types of certain premises (awwaliyyāt, maḥsūsāt, mujarrabāt) and identify their defining characteristics.

Core Idea
Certain premises are the bedrock of demonstration: self-evident truths, sensory data, and experiential generalizations.

In this passage, the author begins listing the six types of certain premises (yaqīniyyāt) that serve as the foundation for demonstrative knowledge. He defines the first three: primary axioms, sensory propositions, and experiential propositions. Understanding these distinctions is crucial because each type carries different epistemological weight and different requirements for certainty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the category of certain premises
Recognize that yaqīniyyāt are propositions that are absolutely certain and serve as premises for demonstration (burhān). They are the starting points of all demonstrative reasoning.
These are not derived from syllogisms but are themselves the foundations.
2
Identify primary axioms (awwaliyyāt)
Awwaliyyāt are propositions that the mind affirms immediately upon understanding the terms, without needing any external proof. Example: 'The whole is greater than the part.'
They are self-evident, even if the terms themselves are acquired through learning.
3
Identify sensibles (maḥsūsāt)
Maḥsūsāt derive their certainty from the senses—either external senses (mushāhadāt, e.g., sight of the sun) or internal senses (wijdāniyyāt, e.g., hunger). Loss of a sense removes knowledge from that source.
They provide foundational data, not universal opinions.
4
Identify experientials (mujarrabāt)
Mujarrabāt are propositions based on repeated observation of a regularity, together with a hidden syllogism that rules out coincidence. Example: 'Fire burns'—if it were accidental, it would not happen consistently.
The hidden syllogism turns repeated experience into certain knowledge.
5
Compare the three types
Note the differences: awwaliyyāt require no observation; maḥsūsāt require direct sensation; mujarrabāt require repeated observation plus inference. Each has a distinct basis for certainty.

Three Types of Middle Term in Burhan Inni
Author enumerates three types of middle term in Burhan Inni with medical examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three types of middle term (effect, co-effect, correlative) is used in a given Burhan Inni example and explain your reasoning.

Core Idea
The middle term in Burhan Inni is not always an effect; it can also be a co-effect or a correlative.

In demonstration of fact (Burhan Inni), we reason from a known effect to an unknown cause. The middle term is the key link. This lesson explores three possible relationships between the middle term and the major term, as taught in classical Islamic logic. The examples are drawn from medieval medicine, showing how logic was applied to scientific reasoning.

Guided Path
1
1. Understand the structure of Burhan Inni
Burhan Inni infers a cause from an effect that is better known to us. The middle term is the effect or indicator. Familiarise yourself with the example: 'This person has putrefied bile because he has tertian fever.' Here, the middle term 'tertian fever' is the effect of the major 'putrefied bile'.
2
2. First type: middle term as effect (ma'lul)
The middle term is an effect of the relation between major and minor. In the expanded syllogism: 'This person has tertian fever. Everyone with tertian fever has putrefied bile. Therefore, this person has putrefied bile.' The middle term causes the conclusion to follow as an effect.
3
3. Second type: both terms as co-effects
Sometimes both the middle and major are effects of a single hidden cause. Example: 'This person has shivering because he has tertian fever.' Both shivering (middle) and tertian fever (major) are caused by putrefied bile (the hidden cause).
4
4. Third type: correlatives (mutadayifan)
The middle and major can be correlatives—each implies the other. Example: 'This person is a father because he has a son.' Fatherhood and sonship are mutually defining. The middle term 'has a son' indicates the major 'father' without implying causation.
5
5. Apply the classification
Given any Burhan Inni statement, ask: Is the middle term an effect of the major? Is it a co-effect of a common cause? Or is it a correlative? Practice with medical examples to solidify the concepts.

The Condition of Equality between Cause and Effect
Warning about equality of cause and effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the middle term as an effect must be co-extensive with its cause to serve as a valid indicator in a demonstration of fact.

Core Idea
One effect can have many causes — equality ensures certainty.

In demonstrations of fact (burhan inni), the middle term is often an effect. But if a single effect can arise from multiple causes, the inference back to a specific cause becomes uncertain. This lesson unpacks that crucial condition, using the example of death from various causes to illustrate the need for co-extensiveness (tasawi).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem
The author warns that simply knowing the middle term is an effect is insufficient. If multiple causes can produce the same effect, the inference is uncertain.
This is the core of the warning (تنبيه).

Analyze the example
Death can be caused by slaughter, strangulation, or disease. Therefore, observing death does not point uniquely to a specific cause without additional knowledge.
The example illustrates taddud al-‘ilal (multiplicity of causes).
3
Derive the condition
The effect must be co-extensive with its cause — meaning the cause is the only source of that effect. This is called tasawi al-‘illa wa al-ma‘lul.
Without tasawi, the middle term loses its probative force.
4
Apply to Islamic theology
Consider the argument that the world’s change (effect) implies a creator (cause). This requires that change is exclusively caused by a creator, not by other factors.
Used in kalam arguments like huduth al-‘alam.

The Definition of Dalil and the Condition of Equality
Defining dalil with condition of equality in burhan inni.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of dalil in burhan inni and explain why the condition of equality (tasawi) between cause and effect is necessary for certainty.

Core Idea
Dalil is a middle term that is an effect, but only yields certainty when cause and effect are co-extensive.

In this passage, the author defines a special type of middle term called dalil, used in 'that' demonstrations (burhan inni). He emphasizes a crucial condition: the equality between cause and effect, without which the conclusion cannot be certain. We will explore this definition and the logical reasoning behind the condition, using examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'dalil' (proof) in the context of demonstration (burhan). Locate it in the Arabic: 'و الوسط إن كان معلولاً - و هو أعرف - يستى دليلاً'.
Dalil is a technical term in logic, not to be confused with ordinary proof.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'middle term' (wasat). The differentia: it is an effect (ma'lul) and more known (a'raf). Thus dalil is a middle term that is a known effect.
'More known' means more known to us, not necessarily in reality.
3
Understand the condition of equality
The author stresses that for the effect to serve as a certain proof, we must know that the effect is co-extensive with the cause (tasawi). Otherwise, multiple causes could produce the same effect, undermining certainty.
Example: death can be caused by slaughter, strangulation, or disease. So seeing death does not pinpoint the cause without equality.
4
Apply the condition to an Islamic example
Consider the kalam argument: 'The universe is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a creator; therefore the universe has a creator.' The middle term 'temporally originated' is an effect of 'having a creator'. For certainty, we need equality: everything temporally originated has a creator, and every creator creates temporally originated things. Without this, the argument might not yield certainty.
This condition is crucial for distinguishing certain demonstration from probable argument.

Four Causes as Middle Terms in Demonstration
Author lists four causes as middle terms in demonstration with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four causes serves as the middle term in a demonstration and distinguish burhan limmi from burhan inni.

Core Idea
The middle term in demonstration is not always the efficient cause—material, formal, and final causes also serve.

In the Islamic logical tradition, demonstration (burhan) is divided into two types: demonstration of cause (burhan limmi) and demonstration of existence (burhan inni). The key difference lies in the middle term. This passage introduces the four Aristotelian causes as possible middle terms in burhan limmi and their effects as middle terms in burhan inni. Understanding this distinction helps you evaluate the strength of arguments in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of demonstration
Read the Arabic and translation. Note the two forms: burhan limmi (the middle term is the cause of the conclusion) and burhan inni (the middle term is the effect of the conclusion). Understand that the direction of causation between middle and major determines the type.
The author states: 'في هذه الأقسام إذا أخذ الوسط بالأكبر يصير البرهان برهان إن' (if the middle term is taken from the major, the proof becomes burhan inni).

These examples are burhan limmi because the middle term (cause) explains the major (effect).

The Middle Term as Cause of Relation
Author clarifies the meaning of middle term as cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a middle term in a demonstrative syllogism is a cause for the relation or an absolute cause, and correctly interpret the stone syllogism.

Core Idea
The middle term causes the attribution, not the major term itself.

In demonstrative proof (burhan limmi), the middle term must be a cause. But a subtle distinction is often missed: the middle term is a cause for the relation of the major to the minor, not an absolute cause of the major. This lesson unpacks that distinction using the famous stone example and Avicenna's illustration from al-Shifa.

Guided Path
1
Restate the core principle
The author states: when we say the middle term is a cause, we mean it is a cause for the attribution (nisba) of the major to the minor. For example, weight is a cause for the stone seeking the center, not a cause for 'seeking the center' in itself.
2
Analyze the stone syllogism formally
The syllogism: 'The stone is heavy; everything heavy seeks the center; therefore the stone seeks the center.' Identify the middle term (heavy), major (seeker of the center), and minor (stone). Note that 'heavy' is a cause for the major's attribution to the minor because it explains why the stone seeks the center.
The middle term can be an effect of the minor (weight from the stone's nature) yet still cause the relation.
3
Examine Avicenna's example from al-Shifa
Avicenna's example: 'Zayd is human; human is animal; thus Zayd is animal.' He claims 'human' is a cause for Zayd's animality (the relation) but not a cause for animal in itself. The commentator objects that the true cause is Zayd's humanity, not 'human' alone.
This objection introduces the principle of proportionality (tanasub wa sinkhiyya) between cause and effect.
4
Distinguish causality for the relation vs. for the term
Causality for the relation means the middle term is responsible for the major belonging to the minor. Causality for the term would mean the middle causes the major itself (e.g., causing animality in general). The author stresses only the former is required in demonstration.
This distinction prevents confusion in constructing proofs.

Method of Division and Its Critique
Author critiques the method of division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the method of division, explain the critique that it presupposes prior knowledge of essential predicates, and evaluate its limited benefit in definitions.

Core Idea
Division cannot define what it cannot distinguish.

How do you arrive at a definition by repeatedly dividing a genus into species? The method of division (ṭarīq al-qisma) seems straightforward: eliminate alternatives until only the correct differentia remains. But our author stops to ask a crucial question: does this method actually produce new knowledge, or does it only work when you already know what you are looking for? In this lesson, we unpack the method, see the logical structures involved, and understand why this author concludes that division is more of a pedagogical aid than a discovery procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method of division
The passage presents a step-by-step example: to define 'man', start from the highest genus (substance) and divide it by a dichotomy (substance is either animal or non-animal; man is not non-animal, so he is animal). Continue dividing animal into bird/reptile/walker, reject inapplicable ones, and finally add the differentia 'rational'. Recognize that each step is a disjunctive syllogism that eliminates one alternative.
The example uses 'man', a standard Islamic philosophical example.
2
Reconstruct the overall argument as a compound syllogism
After the individual divisions, the author gathers the parts into a final claim: 'This aggregate is a detailed expression indicating a concept; everything that is so is a definition; therefore this is a definition of man.' This final step is a categorical syllogism that uses the results of the divisions as a premise.
3

The author states the method's benefit depends on distinguishing essential (dhātī) from accidental (ʿaraḍī) predicates. But the division itself does not differentiate them; both types appear as alternatives. If you already know which predicates are essential, you have already completed the definition and do not need the division. If you do not know, the division cannot help.
This is a logical circularity: the method presupposes what it aims to provide.

The author admits that division can 'detail' (tafṣīl) the predicates—arranging them clearly—even if it does not discover new essentials. This makes division useful for teaching or organising definitions, but not for original discovery.
The concession preserves a modest role for the method in pedagogical contexts.

The Logical Refutation of Multiple Necessary Beings
Author argues against two necessary beings using conditional reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional disjunctive argument against two necessary beings and identify its logical structure.

Core Idea
Two necessary beings are impossible; correlation or separation both imply dependency.

In this passage, the author presents a crisp logical argument against the possibility of two necessary beings. Using a conditional disjunction, he shows that either the two beings are necessarily correlated or not. Both cases lead to a contradiction with the nature of necessary existence, thereby establishing the oneness of the Necessary Being. This lesson will walk you through the reasoning and help you formalise it in terms of hypothetical syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
The argument begins with a dichotomy: either there is a mulazama (necessary correlation) between the two supposed necessary beings, or there is not. This is an exclusive disjunction covering all possibilities.
2
Analyse the first branch (correlation)
If there is a correlation, then one being (the malzum) depends on the other (the lazim). Dependency implies that the dependent being is not necessary in itself, contradicting its supposed necessity.
A necessary being must be independent in existence.
3
Analyse the second branch (no correlation)
If there is no correlation, then it is possible for one being to exist without the other. That would mean the non-existence of one is possible, again contradicting its necessity.
A necessary being cannot possibly not exist.
4
Conclude the impossibility
Since both branches lead to a contradiction, the original assumption (two necessary beings) is impossible. This proves that the necessary being must be one.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Author refines the definition of absence of correlation.

Disjunctive Proof for the Unicity of the Necessary Being
Author proves the unicity of the Necessary Being via disjunctive syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive proof for the unicity of the Necessary Being and explain the precise meaning of 'lack of relation' (intifā' al-mulāzama) as used in the argument.

Core Idea
Two necessary beings cannot coexist: relation forces dependence, no relation allows separability – both contradict necessity.

This passage presents a concise logical demonstration of tawḥīd used in kalām. The proof is a disjunctive syllogism that exhausts the possibilities between two supposed necessary beings. The author not only proves the conclusion but also refines a key term to prevent a common misunderstanding. Let's walk through the logic step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and disjunctive premise
The claim: 'The Necessary Being cannot be two.' The disjunctive premise: 'Either there is a relation (mulāzama) between them or not.' This covers all possibilities.
The disjunction is exhaustive and exclusive (mani'at al-jam' wal-khuluw).
2
Trace the first horn: relation exists
If there is a relation, one being is dependent on the other (the dependent would be impossible without the other). A dependent being cannot be necessary. Therefore, that horn is impossible.
The author says: 'the dependent is in need of the necessary, since it is impossible upon its removal.'
3
Trace the second horn: no relation
If no relation, they are separable (infikāk): one could cease to exist while the other remains. Then the non-existence of one is possible, contradicting its necessity. Thus this horn is also impossible.
Separation is realized by the non-existence of one, so non-existence is possible.
4
Analyze the refinement of 'no relation'
The author clarifies: 'no relation' means the antecedent does not necessarily entail the consequent (not a logical entailment). It does NOT mean one can exist without the other in reality. The first is more general and does not imply the second. This prevents a mistaken inference.
This nuance guards against equating 'no necessitation' with 'actual independence'.
5
Formalize the disjunctive syllogism
Formally: Either (A) there is a relation or (B) there is no relation. If (A) then one is contingent. If (B) then one is contingent. In both cases, one is contingent, contradicting necessity. Therefore, two necessary beings cannot exist. This is a valid constructive dilemma.
The argument assumes the two beings are distinct and the disjunction covers all possibilities.
