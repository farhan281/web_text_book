Literal vs Metaphorical Naming of Propositions and Order of Discussion
Author distinguishes literal and metaphorical naming and order of discussion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between literal and metaphorical naming of proposition types in Islamic logic and explain why categoricals are discussed first.

Core Idea
Not all 'shartiyya' are conditionals — disjunctives are only metaphorically so.

In this passage, the author clarifies the precise terminology used for proposition types. He explains that the terms 'categorical', 'conditional', and 'disjunctive' are applied literally to affirmative propositions, while negatives are named metaphorically due to shared form. Moreover, the term 'shartiyya' (conditional) is literal for conditionals but metaphorical for disjunctives. This distinction is not merely semantic; it reflects deeper logical structure and guides the order of discussion — categoricals come first because they are naturally prior.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of designation
Recognize that the author distinguishes between literal (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) naming. Literal naming applies when the term fully corresponds to the essence; metaphorical naming applies due to some shared characteristic (musha'a).
2
Apply the distinction to proposition types
Note that affirmatives (positive propositions) are named literally as categorical, conditional, or disjunctive. Negatives are named metaphorically because they share the same form as affirmatives but differ in quality.
3
Analyze the rationale for 'shartiyya'
Conditional propositions are literally called 'shartiyya' because they contain the meaning of condition (shart) and its particle. Disjunctives are metaphorically called 'shartiyya' due to their compositional similarity and because they involve a conditional relation (positing or removal conditional on something).
This prevents a common misunderstanding that all 'shartiyya' are conditionals.
4
Understand the principle of natural precedence
The author states that categoricals precede conditionals by nature (tab'an), so they deserve priority in discussion (wad'an). This is a methodological principle in logical exposition.
5
Connect to the structure of logical texts
Recognize that this passage justifies why many logic textbooks begin with categorical propositions before moving to hypotheticals. This ordering is based on a logical priority, not arbitrary convention.

Literal vs. Metaphorical Meaning: The Principle of Aṣl
Author explains the principle of literal default with conditions for metaphor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that literal meaning is default in interpreting Islamic legal and theological texts, and identify when metaphor is required.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is the default; metaphor requires a clue.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Islamic linguistic theory: the default in language is the literal meaning (ḥaqīqah). This principle governs both how speakers should use language and how hearers should interpret it. Understanding it is essential for correctly interpreting the Qur'an and Sunnah, as well as for engaging in uṣūl al-fiqh discussions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of 'aṣl'
The term 'aṣl' means that something has priority as long as nothing opposes it. In this context, the literal meaning is the default until a countervailing factor appears.
2
Identify the default principle
The principle states: 'The principle in usage is literal meaning, not metaphorical.' This means a word should be taken in its literal sense unless there is a specific reason to do otherwise.
3
Recognize conditions for metaphor
Metaphor (majāz) is only permissible if there is a contextual clue (qarīna) that indicates the non-literal meaning. Without this clue, using metaphor would cause confusion and violate convention.
Contextual clues can be situational (ḥāliyya) or verbal (maqāliyya).
4
Distinguish speaker's and hearer's perspectives
From the speaker's perspective (istiʿmāl), metaphor requires a purpose like eloquence or poetic meter. From the hearer's perspective (istifhām), if a word is encountered without qualification, it must be interpreted literally; only if a clue exists should it be interpreted metaphorically.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle (al-aṣl) in linguistic interpretation. The author states that the default assumption in language is the literal meaning (al-ḥaqīqah), not metaphorical (majāz). This is because metaphor requires a reason or benefit (such as beauty, eloquence, poetic meter) that overrides the literal. Without such a reason, using metaphor would cause misunderstanding and violate convention. This rule applies from the speaker's side (istiʿmāl). From the hearer's side (istifhām), if a word is encountered without qualification, it should be understood literally. However, if there is a contextual clue (qarīna ḥāliyya) or verbal clue (qarīna maqāliyya) indicating metaphorical intent, it must be interpreted accordingly. This is a foundational principle in uṣūl al-fiqh and logic for interpreting texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use an index of names (fihris) to locate discussions of specific scholars in a classical Islamic logic text.

Core Idea
Indexes turn a book into a searchable database of intellectual authorities.

The index you see is not a logical argument, but a powerful tool for research. In a dense work like 'Qustas al-Afkar', the index allows you to quickly find where key figures such as al-Ghazali or al-Tusi are discussed. Learning to read an index is like learning the map of a city: it helps you navigate the text efficiently. Let's walk through how to extract information from this index.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the index
The index is organized alphabetically by name (Arabic script). Each entry includes the name of a scholar or figure and one or more page numbers where they are mentioned. Some entries have multiple numbers separated by spaces or commas.
Note that the index mixes Arabic and Latin names, but the Arabic names follow their own alphabetical order.

Look for a known scholar, for example, 'الغزالي' (al-Ghazali). Run your finger down the index until you find his name. The entry reads: 'الغزالي، أبو حامد محمد، ۱۱۸، ۱۳۳'. This tells you that al-Ghazali is discussed on pages 118 and 133.

3

Open the book to page 118 (or 133) and read the passage. You will find al-Ghazali's views or the author's engagement with al-Ghazali's ideas. The index thus saves time by directing you to relevant passages without reading the entire book.

Author lists primary and secondary sources for the Qustas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major primary and secondary sources used in the critical edition of Qustas al-Afkar and explain the purpose of each type of source.

Core Idea
A critical edition's bibliography reveals the scholarly tradition and textual lineage of a work.

This lesson introduces the bibliography appended to the Qustas al-Afkar fi al-Mantiq. Understanding how to read a scholarly bibliography is essential for anyone working with classical Islamic texts. You will learn to distinguish between primary manuscripts, commentaries, and modern studies, and see how these sources support the editor's work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the bibliography
Notice that the bibliography begins with the work's full title (قسطاس الأفكار في المنطق) and then lists entries by author. Some entries are in Arabic or Persian, others in English. The list includes both medieval and modern sources.
The bibliography is not alphabetized; it appears to follow the order of the editor's research.

Classify each entry by type
Determine whether an entry is a primary source (a manuscript or original Arabic work), a secondary source (a commentary or modern study), or a reference work (like Van Dyck's bibliography). Some entries are manuscripts (e.g., manuscript numbers in Istanbul libraries).

Note the languages and scripts
The bibliography mixes Arabic, Persian, and English. Arabic/Persian entries often contain publication details in Persian (e.g., تهران). English entries follow Western academic conventions. This reflects the international nature of Islamic logic scholarship.
4
Recognize the significance of multiple editions
Some works appear more than once (e.g., Qutb al-Din al-Razi's لوامع الأسرار listed twice with different editors). This indicates variant editions or reprints. Scholars must compare versions for critical work.

Introduction to Qisṭās al-afkār and Its Author
Title page and book outline

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author, title, and overall structure of Qisṭās al-afkār, and explain its place in the Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
Qisṭās al-afkār is a balanced manual of logic for Islamic sciences.

This lesson introduces the work Qisṭās al-afkār fi l-mantiq by Shams al-Din Samarqandi, a key text in the post-classical Islamic logical tradition. Understanding its author and structure helps you appreciate how this book serves as a comprehensive guide to both conceptual analysis (tasawwur) and judgment (tasdiq), the two pillars of logic in Islamic scholarship. The front matter gives you a roadmap of the logical tools you will learn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his era
Find the name 'Shams al-Din Samarqandi' and the date 1322 AD. Note that he was a 14th-century logician from the Islamic East, writing in a period of rich logical commentary.
Samarqandi is known for his concise yet comprehensive logical works.
2
Recognize the book's title and its meaning
The title 'Qisṭās al-afkār fi l-mantiq' means 'The Balance of Thoughts in Logic'. The word 'qisṭās' (balance) implies that logic is a tool for weighing arguments and distinguishing truth from falsehood.
3
Outline the book's structure from the table of contents
The book has an introduction, then two main parts: Part 1 on acquisition of concepts (tasawwur), Part 2 on acquisition of propositions (tasdiq). After that come sections on syllogism and syllogistic appurtenances. This mirrors the classical division of logic into concepts and judgments.
This structure is typical of post-Avicennan logic textbooks.
