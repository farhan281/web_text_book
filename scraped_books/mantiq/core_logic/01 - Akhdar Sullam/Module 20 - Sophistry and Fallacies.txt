Introduction to Error in Proofs: Matter, Form, and Expression Fallacies
Author introduces the chapter on error and divides it into matter and form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify errors in demonstrative proofs as errors of matter or form, and identify the expression fallacies of equivocation and false synonymy.

Core Idea
A proof fails either because its content is corrupt or its structure is flawed—or both.

This passage opens the final chapter of al-Akhdari's logical text, where he warns students about the most common pitfalls in constructing proofs. He divides error into two broad categories—matter and form—then immediately drills into expression errors, giving vivid examples from Arabic that every student would recognise. By learning this taxonomy, you will not only detect errors in the logic of others but also safeguard your own theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching division of error.
Note that the author splits error into two kinds: error in the matter (content) and error in the form (structure). Understand what each comprises.
2
Distinguish matter from form.
Matter refers to the actual terms and propositions used; form refers to the syllogistic arrangement. A valid proof requires both sound matter and correct form.
3
Recognise the two expression fallacies.
The first is equivocation (ishtirak): a single word used in two senses in the same proof, breaking the unity of the middle term. The second is treating two distinct words as if they were synonymous (mutabayina as mutaradifa), which also undermines the middle term.
4
Apply to an Islamic example.
Consider the proof: 'Every nabidh is an intoxicant; every intoxicant is forbidden; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' If 'nabidh' shifts meaning between premises (e.g., date drink in one, grape drink in another), the proof becomes invalid due to equivocation.
This example illustrates ishtirak.

Identifying Iham al-'Aks and Genus-Species Confusion
Author explains two meaning errors: genus-species confusion and iham al-'aks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of iham al-'aks and genus-species confusion in arguments and explain why they are fallacious.

Core Idea
Judging a genus by a species' property confuses particular with universal.

This passage from al-Akhdari turns to the second source of material error: meaning. It explains how one might confuse true premises with false ones by attributing a property of a species to its entire genus. The most famous form of this error is iham al-'aks, the illusion of the converse. Let's break down the examples to see exactly how this fallacy works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy structure
Look for an argument where a property that belongs only to a specific subset (species) is attributed to the whole category (genus). This is a material error because the premise about the genus is false.
2
Test the first example
The argument: 'This is a color; color is black; so this is black.' Why does this fail? The premise 'color is black' is false because black is only one species of color, not a property of the entire genus. The conclusion does not follow despite the syllogistic form.
This is a confusion of genus and species.
3
Test the second example
The argument: 'This is a yellow liquid; yellow liquid is bitter; so this is bitter.' Here the premise 'yellow liquid is bitter' is false because only some yellow liquids (e.g., certain saps) are bitter. The observer commits iham al-'aks by converting a true observation—that every bitter thing is a yellow liquid—into the false universal 'every yellow liquid is bitter.'
The fallacy of illicit conversion.
4
Recognize the general pattern of iham al-'aks
Iham al-'aks occurs when one observes that every A is B and falsely concludes that every B is A. The passage highlights this as the core error: confusing a particular correlation with a universal necessary judgment.

The Fallacy of Absolute vs Qualified Judgments
Author lists further meaning errors: absolute versus qualified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify arguments that improperly apply a qualified judgment to an absolute term.

Core Idea
A judgment true of a qualified term may be false of the absolute term.

This lesson addresses a common material fallacy in reasoning: taking a judgment that is true only under a specific condition or time and applying it without that qualification. The author illustrates with two examples: the case of 'this is a neck; every neck is turbaned' and the poet al-A'sha, called 'sighted' but night-blind. Understanding this fallacy helps you evaluate when a conclusion overreaches its premises.

Guided Path
1
Spot the qualified judgment
Identify the predicate that includes an implicit condition (e.g., 'turbaned' implies a person wearing a turban, not the neck itself).
2
Identify the unqualified term
Find the subject in the conclusion that is taken absolutely (e.g., 'neck' without qualification).
3
Check if the condition is necessary
Determine whether the predicate's truth depends on the condition (e.g., 'sighted' does not guarantee night vision).
4
Verify the conclusion removes the condition
See if the conclusion applies the predicate to the absolute term without the condition. If so, the argument is fallacious.

Commentary
This passage continues the enumeration of meaning-based material errors in proofs. The author first presents the confusion of the absolute (mutlaq) with the qualified (muqayyad): a judgment true only under a certain condition or time is applied without that qualification. He illustrates this with two examples. In the first, one argues about a neck as if the judgment of being turbaned applied to the neck absolutely, when it properly belongs to the qualified whole, namely the person who wears the turban. In the second, referring to the poet al-A'sha—who was called 'the sighted one' yet was night-blind—one falsely concludes that because he is called sighted, he sees at night, ignoring that the predicate 'sighted' was not qualified by nighttime vision. The author then notes another species of error: treating non-decisive premises, such as presumptive propositions (wahmiyyat), as if they were decisive (qati'), which destroys the certainty required of a demonstration. Finally, he gives the example of making the accidental ('aradi) into the essential (dhati): arguing 'this is a human, and human is a writer, therefore this is a writer' falsely assumes that writing belongs to the essence of humanity, when it is merely an accidental property of some humans.

The Fallacy of Treating Non-Decisive Premises as Decisive
Author lists further meaning errors: non-decisive premises treated as decisive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect when a presumptive or non-decisive premise is incorrectly used as if it were certain in a demonstration.

Core Idea
Only decisive premises yield certain conclusions; using presumptive ones destroys demonstrative certainty.

This lesson focuses on the error of treating non-decisive premises (like opinions or presumptions) as if they were certain (qati'). The author warns that such premises, called wahmiyyat, cannot serve as foundations for a valid demonstration (burhan). Recognizing this fallacy is crucial for evaluating arguments that claim certainty but rely on uncertain foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimed conclusion
Determine what the arguer is trying to prove with certainty.
2
Examine the premises
List all premises used in the argument and classify them as certain (qati') or presumptive (wahmi).
Presumptive premises include statements based on common opinion, analogy, or incomplete evidence.
3
Check if any premise is non-decisive
If even one premise is not decisive, the argument cannot produce certain knowledge.
4
Declare the fallacy
If a non-decisive premise is treated as decisive, the argument commits a material error.

The Fallacy of Confusing Accidental with Essential
Author lists further meaning errors: accidental versus essential.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish essential properties from accidental ones and identify arguments that treat accidental properties as part of the essence.

Core Idea
An accidental property does not define the essence of a thing; treating it as essential leads to false universal claims.

The last fallacy in this passage is making the accidental ('aradi) like the essential (dhati). The author gives the example: 'this is a human, and the human is a writer, therefore this is a writer.' While the conclusion may be true, the reasoning is flawed because writing is not an essential property of being human; it is accidental, shared only by some humans. This lesson teaches you to critically evaluate whether a property truly defines the subject's nature or is merely incidental.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property in question
Find the predicate that the argument claims is true of all members of the category (e.g., 'writer' attributed to 'human').
2
Determine if the property is essential or accidental
Ask: Can the subject exist without this property? If yes, the property is accidental. For example, a human can exist without being a writer.
3
Check the argument's quantification
The argument treats 'human is a writer' as a universal affirmative (all humans are writers) when in reality only some are.
4
Identify the fallacy
If the argument uses an accidental property as if it were essential, it commits the fallacy of making accidental like essential.

The Two Divisions of Error in Demonstration
Author classifies error into material and formal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between material error (khaṭa' fī al-mādda) and formal error (khaṭa' fī al-ṣūra) in a demonstration.

Core Idea
Error in demonstration is either in content or form, each with distinct causes.

The passage opens by explaining why this chapter is called 'khatamah' — the seal — because it seals the discussion of demonstration. The author then announces the fundamental division: error can arise from the matter (premises) or the form (syllogistic structure). This classification is critical for diagnosing why a proof fails.

Core Idea
Confusing the logical relations between genus and species, or between absolute and qualified terms, leads to material error.

The author continues with conceptual material errors, which occur in the meaning of the propositions rather than the wording. Begging the Question: Muṣādara 'alā al-Maṭlūb
Author defines and exemplifies the fallacy of begging the question.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of begging the question (muṣādara) in an argument.

Core Idea
Using the conclusion as a premise, even if disguised, makes the argument circular and fails to prove anything.

The final material error the author presents is begging the question (muṣādara 'alā al-maṭlūb), where the result (conclusion) is covertly used as one of the premises. He gives the example: 'This is a moving thing; every moving thing is motion; therefore this is motion.' The middle premise is essentially the same as the conclusion, so no proof is achieved.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Begging the question occurs when the premise assumes the conclusion. The author says it is 'making the result one of the premises of the demonstration by changing it' (بتغييرها).
2
Analyze the example
The example: 'This is a moving thing; every moving thing is motion; so this is motion.' The second premise 'every moving thing is motion' is essentially the same as the conclusion 'this is motion', just phrased differently.
3
Apply to other domains
In kalam, one might argue: 'The world is created because it is temporally originated; everything temporally originated is created; therefore the world is created.' This is transparently circular.

Finally, he discusses begging the question (muṣādara 'alā al-maṭlūb), where the conclusion itself is covertly used as a premise, e.g., 'this is a moving thing, every moving thing is motion, therefore this is motion' — but 'every moving thing is motion' is essentially the same as the conclusion. All examples are drawn from Islamic logical and theological discourse.
