The Division of Proof: Transmitted and Rational
Author classifies proof into two main categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between transmitted (naqli) and rational ('aqli) proof and list the five types of rational proof with their Arabic names.

Core Idea
Proof is either transmitted from revelation or rationally constructed.

Before diving into the five arts, we need to see the big picture: proof (hujja) is first divided by its material. The author presents a clear dichotomy: transmitted proof (naqliyya) relies on revelation, consensus, or tradition; rational proof ('aqliyya) does not. Understanding this division helps you categorise any argument you encounter in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major division
Read the first sentence: 'Proof is two types: transmitted and rational.' This is the primary division of all proof.
2
Memorize the five rational types
The author lists demonstrative, dialectical, rhetorical, poetic, and sophistical. Note the verse that summarises them: 'rhetoric, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, sophistry'.
The verse is a mnemonic aid.
3
Understand the basis of the division
What distinguishes these types is the material (madda) – the epistemic quality of their premises: certain, accepted, likely, imaginative, or fallacious.
The classification is not by form but by the nature of premises.

Rhetorical Proof (Khataba): Accepted and Likely Premises
Author defines rhetorical proof and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify rhetorical proof by its use of accepted and likely premises and construct a simple rhetorical syllogism on an Islamic topic.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades using premises that are accepted or likely, not certain.

The author now zooms in on rhetorical proof (khataba). Unlike demonstration, which demands certainty, rhetoric works with premises that people accept from trustworthy authorities or consider likely. This makes it a powerful tool for da'wa and moral exhortation. The example given is a practical syllogism that a preacher or counselor might use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of rhetori cal proof
The passage says: 'Rhetoric is composed of accepted premises (maqbula) and what is considered likely (maznun).' Note that accepted premises come from a person believed to understand truth but without certainty, or because of virtuous qualities.
2
Analyze the example
The example: 'This one walks at night with a weapon; everyone who walks at night with a weapon is a thief; so this one is a thief.' Identify the major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
This is a categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara form).
3
Determine the purpose
The purpose is 'to make the listener desire what benefits him.' Rhetoric aims at persuasion toward beneficial action, not necessarily at producing certain knowledge.

Poetic Proof (Shi'r): Imaginative Premises for Emotional Effect
Author defines poetic proof with its imaginative premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify poetic proof by its use of imaginative premises and explain how it affects the soul.

Core Idea
Poetry moves the soul through imagination, not logical certainty.

The final type the author illustrates is poetic proof (shi'r). Unlike rhetoric which uses accepted or likely premises, poetry uses imaginative premises (mukhayyala) that work on emotions. The aim is to make the listener desire or detest something. This shows that logic includes even emotional persuasion as a legitimate form of argument.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of poetic proof
The passage says: 'Poetry is composed of imaginative premises (mukhayyala) to make the listener desire or detest something.' Note the contrast with rhetoric.
2
Analyze the example
The example: 'Wine is a flowing ruby, and honey is a bitter gall.' These are not factual claims; they are imaginative comparisons that evoke desire for wine and disgust for honey.
The purpose is not to prove a fact but to shape emotional response.
3
Identify the effect
The purpose is 'the impression of the soul' (ta'aththur al-nafs). Poetic premises aim to produce an emotional state, not belief about reality.

The Two Kinds of Proof: Naqli and Aqli
Author defines the two kinds of proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hujja naqliyya and hujja aqliyya based on their material and give an example of each from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The material of a proof determines its authority: transmitted or rational.

In Islamic logic, not all proofs are equal. The author divides proofs based on where their premises come from. Some rely on revelation, others on reason. Understanding this division helps you classify any argument you encounter in kalam, fiqh, or tafsir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions
Look at the Arabic: الحجة قسمان: نقلية وعقلية. Note that the division is based on 'matter' (مادة).
2
Define naqliyya
It is proof whose premises come from the Book, Sunna, or ijma', explicitly or by inference. Think of a qiyas based on a nas.
3
Define aqliyya
It is proof not relying on transmission. Its premises are purely rational. Examples: the philosophical proof for God's existence.
4
Test the division
Can a proof be both? According to this classification, no – the material is either transmitted or rational.

The Five Rational Arts
Author lists and names the five divisions of rational proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five rational arts and describe the distinguishing feature of each.

Core Idea
Rational proof is not one thing; it spans demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry.

The author tells us that rational proof has five divisions. These five arts correspond to different purposes and levels of certainty. Knowing them helps you identify what kind of argument you are dealing with.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the five names
The five divisions are: برهان (demonstration), جدل (dialectic), خطابة (rhetoric), شعر (poetry), سفسطة (sophistry). Note that sophistry is also called المغالطة (fallacy).
2
Understand the principle of division
These are divisions of rational proof, meaning they do not rely on transmission. They are distinct based on the purpose and material of the argument.
3
Recognize their hierarchy
Demonstration yields certainty; dialectic aims to persuade an opponent; rhetoric moves the emotions; poetry uses imaginative statements; sophistry is deceptive.
This hierarchy is implied by the arrangement in the text.
4
Reflect on contexts
In what contexts would each be used? E.g., demonstration in kalam, dialectic in munazara, rhetoric in preaching.

This lesson unpacks the definition of sophistry (safsata) and its three sub-types as taught in classical Islamic logic. Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching definition
Locate the opening statement that defines sophistry (السفسطة). Notice it is built from premises that resemble truth but are not truth. This is the common core of all three sub-types.

Find the first example: a picture of a horse on a wall. The reasoning 'this is a horse; every horse neighs; therefore this neighs' uses a false minor premise (the picture is not a real horse). This is mughalata – fallacy that resembles truth.

The example may contain an OCR error (مميت vs ميت), but the intended meaning is clear: false imaginary premises.

Reflect on how each type corrupts a different kind of premise: mughalata corrupts truth-like premises, mushaghaba corrupts accepted (dialectical) premises, and wahmiyya kadhiba corrupts purely imaginary premises. This threefold division is essential for diagnosing sophistry in practice.

Sophistry: The Art of Counterfeit Arguments
Author defines sophistry and its three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of sophistry (safsata) and distinguish them from demonstration (burhan).

Core Idea
Sophistry is counterfeit argument: it wears the dress of truth but leads to falsehood.

In the classification of the five arts, demonstration (burhan) stands as the highest, yielding certainty. But along the way, the logician must guard against counterfeit arguments — sophistry (safsata). In this passage, the author defines sophistry and breaks it into three varieties, each with a revealing example. Understanding these types sharpens your ability to detect flawed reasoning in any field, especially when false premises masquerade as truth.

Commentary
This passage defines sophistry (safsata) as the art composed of premises that only appear true. The author distinguishes three types. First, mughalata (fallacy): premises that are like the truth but false, e.g., 'This is a horse' (referring to a picture), 'Every horse neighs,' so 'This neighs'—an erroneous syllogism due to a false premise. Second, mushaghaba (verbal dispute): premises that resemble widely accepted premises but are spurious, e.g., a person who rambles uses academic jargon, so he is deemed a scholar—a false conclusion. Key Terms
السفسطة (al-safsata): Sophistry; an art composed of premises that resemble truth but are not, aiming at deception. [Note: Stable term; corresponds to Aristotle's sophistics.]
مغالطة (mughalata): Fallacy; a type of sophistry with premises resembling truth. [Note: Often used interchangeably with safsata.]
