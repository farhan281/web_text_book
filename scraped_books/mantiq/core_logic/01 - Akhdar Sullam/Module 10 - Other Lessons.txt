The Definition of Knowledge: Why a Real Definition is Impossible
Author discusses the impossibility of a real definition of knowledge and offers a nominal definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a real definition of 'ilm is impossible and identify the nominal definition chosen by the author.

Core Idea
Knowledge cannot be defined essentially; it is self-evident and known by division, example, or as 'conception of the known'.

This passage addresses a foundational issue in Islamic epistemology: can 'ilm (knowledge) be defined? Imam al-Haramayn and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi both argue that a real definition is impossible, but they offer alternative ways to understand it. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and the chosen nominal definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two authorities cited
The passage mentions Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni) and al-Razi. Note what each says about defining knowledge.
Al-Juwayni says definition by essence is impossible; al-Razi says knowledge is self-evident.
2
Understand Juwayni's position
Juwayni says knowledge cannot be defined essentially (bi al-haqiqa) because it is impossible. Instead, we define it by division (qisma) and example (mithal).
Division means breaking it into types; example means pointing to instances.
3
Understand Razi's position
Razi says knowledge is self-evident (daruri) and cannot be defined by something more obvious. So a real definition is impossible.
Daruri means necessary in the sense of axiomatic.
4
Identify the chosen definition
The author chooses the nominal definition: 'conception of the known' (ma'rifa al-ma'lum). This includes both existent and non-existent.
This is a description (rasm) rather than an essential definition (hadd).
5
Address the objection of circularity
Some objected that 'conception' itself implies knowledge, leading to circularity. The author dismisses this because linguistic derivation does not harm here.
The word 'ma'rifa' is not used in the same technical sense.

Commentary
This passage addresses the definition of 'ilm (knowledge). Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni) states that an essential (real) definition is impossible, so we use division (qisma) and example (mithal) as a way to understand it. Al-Razi (Fakhr al-Din) says knowledge is self-evident (daruri) and cannot be defined by something more obvious; the preferred definition is 'conception of the known' (ma'rifa al-ma'lum), which covers both existent and non-existent. A potential objection is that this definition leads to circularity (since it uses 'conception'), but it is dismissed because derivation does not harm here.
