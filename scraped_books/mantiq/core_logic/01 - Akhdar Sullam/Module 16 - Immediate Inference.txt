Immediate Inference (al-'Aks al-Mustawi): Definition and the Universal Affirmative Exception
Author defines immediate inference and states which propositions convert.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of conversion to any categorical proposition and correctly identify the converted form, especially handling the universal affirmative as an exception.

Core Idea
Conversion preserves truth, quality, and quantity—except the universal affirmative becomes particular.

In this lesson, we examine the logical operation known as al-'aks al-mustawi, or immediate inference via conversion. This operation allows us to swap the subject and predicate of a categorical proposition while keeping the proposition true. However, not all propositions convert in the same way: the universal affirmative has a special rule. We will learn the precise definition and the conditions for valid conversion, drawing from the matn and its commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three preserved properties
The definition states that conversion preserves truth (sidq), quality (kayf), and quantity (kam). Find what these mean in the context of categorical propositions.
Truth means the proposition remains true after conversion. Quality means the affirmation or negation remains the same. Quantity means the proposition remains universal or particular, except as noted.
2
Spot the exceptional case
The universal affirmative does not preserve quantity; it converts to a particular affirmative. Why? Because converting a universal affirmative to a universal affirmative would not guarantee truth. For example, 'Every A is B' does not imply 'Every B is A'.
This is the only exception; all other types preserve quantity.
3
Handle the indefinite negative
The commentary adds that the indefinite negative (muhmala salbiyya) behaves like a particular negative because it has the force of a particular. So it converts as a particular negative would.
Indefinite propositions are those without quantifiers; their logical force is determined by context.
4
Distinguish natural vs. conventional arrangement
Conversion applies to propositions with natural arrangement of terms (subject-predicate in the order intended by nature), not to conventional or arbitrary arrangements.
Natural arrangement is based on the inherent predication; conventional arrangement is based on stipulation.
5
Test a conversion on an Islamic example
Take a true universal affirmative proposition from fiqh, e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (kullu muskarin haram). Convert it to 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants' (ba'd al-muharramat muskar). Verify that truth is preserved.
This demonstrates the exception: universal affirmative becomes particular affirmative.

Commentary
This passage defines immediate inference, or conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi), and lays down the conditions governing its validity. The author opens the chapter by announcing that he will treat one of the essential rulings of categorical propositions, namely conversion. He defines it as the transposition of the two terms of a proposition while preserving three things: its truth-value, its quality (affirmation or negation), and its quantity (universality or particularity). The sole exception is the universal affirmative proposition, which does not retain its quantity under conversion; instead, it converts into a particular affirmative proposition. To illustrate with an example drawn from the Islamic sciences: if it is true that 'Every intoxicant is forbidden,' conversion yields 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants,' which remains true but has dropped from universal to particular quantity. The author then cites the versified matn line that summarizes this definition. In his explanatory commentary, he adds that conversion is necessary and valid for all propositions except those characterized by the combination of two features—here the text is unclear but the intended referent is the particular negative proposition, which simultaneously carries negation and particularity—and similar to it is the indefinite negative proposition, because it possesses the logical force (quwwa) of a particular proposition and thus behaves likewise in conversion. He further distinguishes between a natural arrangement of terms (al-tartib al-tabi'i) and a conventional or imposed arrangement (al-tartib al-wad'i), emphasizing that conversion operates according to the natural order of predication, not an artificial order established by stipulation. He begins to clarify that what is meant by conversion is what is necessitated by virtue of that natural arrangement, not what... Definition and Conditions of Simple Conversion
Author defines simple conversion and its necessary conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a valid simple conversion and determine whether a given proposition satisfies them.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth, quality, and quantity — except universal affirmatives, which become particular.

In this passage, the author defines simple conversion as a specific immediate inference. To apply it correctly, you must understand what is preserved and what exceptions exist. This lesson establishes the conceptual boundaries of the operation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operation
Find the definition of 'aks mustawi. What does it mean to 'reverse the two parts' (تحويل جزئي القضية)?
2
The preservations
Note the three things preserved: truth (صدق), quality (كيف), quantity (كم). Why is each important for a valid inference?
3
The exception
Universal affirmative (موجبة كلية) converts to particular affirmative (موجبة جزئية). Understand why converting as universal would lose truth.
Example: 'Every human is an animal' → 'Some animal is a human'.
4
The restrictions
Conversion requires natural order (الترتيب الطبيعي) and does not apply to propositions that combine both deficiencies (الخستين): negative + particular.
Indefinite negatives are included because they are equivalent to particular negatives.
5
The purpose
Conversion is about necessary order-based inference, not accidental agreement. It must hold universally in the logical law.

Chapter on simple conversion. We discuss in this chapter a rule of propositions, namely simple conversion. Simple conversion is: the transformation of the two parts of the proposition while preserving truth, quality, and quantity, except the universal affirmative, which is compensated by the particular affirmative. And to this meaning we alluded in our saying: 'Conversion is the reversal of the two parts of the proposition with preservation of truth, quality, and quantity, except the universal affirmative, so its compensation is the particular affirmative.' And conversion is necessary for other than that in which the two deficiencies are found, so it is restricted. And the indefinite negative is like it because it is in the strength of the particular. And conversion is natural order, not positional order. Know that the purpose of conversion is what is necessary in terms of order, not what is agreeable in some matters even if not necessary in the universal law. And every proposition that has a conversion, its conversion is the transformation of its two terms specifically without changing quality nor quantity, except the universal affirmative, so it converts as a particular affirmative, because if we convert it as its like it would not be true. And the purpose of this chapter is only what is necessary in terms of truth. So we say: In converting 'Every human is an animal' it becomes 'Some animal is a human'. If we converted it as its like and said 'Every animal is a human' it would not be true. Then conversion is necessary for every naturally ordered proposition except that in which the two deficiencies meet, which are the negative and the particular, like 'Some animal is not human', so its conversion is not true. And the indefinite negative is attached to it because it is in its power, as previously. The universal negative converts truly as its like, e.g., 'No human is a stone' becomes 'No stone is a human'. The universal affirmative converts truly as a particular affirmative as above. The particular affirmative converts truly as its like, e.g., 'Some animal is a human' becomes 'Some human is an animal'. The affirmative indefinite converts as the particular affirmative: 'A human is a writer' becomes 'A writer is a human'. And know that conversion only occurs in propositions with natural order, and to that we alluded by our saying: 'Conversion is natural order' as a safeguard from disjunctives, for the transformation of their two parts is not conversion because each of their parts is suitable to be antecedent and consequent, so their order is not determined except by position, unlike categoricals and conditionals whose order is natural, even if their two parts are inverted, they are ordered potentially. And he distinguished 'simple' from contrapositive.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the concept of simple conversion (al-‘aks al-mustawi) in logic. It is a definitional passage that also provides rules for each type of categorical proposition. The author begins by stating that simple conversion is the operation of swapping the subject and predicate of a proposition while maintaining its truth-value, quality (affirmative or negative), and quantity (universal or particular), with one exception: a universal affirmative proposition converts to a particular affirmative, not a universal affirmative. This is because swapping the terms of a universal affirmative like 'Every human is an animal' would yield 'Every animal is a human', which is false; the correct conversion is 'Some animal is a human', which preserves truth. The author then explains that conversion applies only to propositions with 'natural order', where the subject naturally precedes the predicate, as opposed to 'positional order' found in disjunctive conditionals where either term can be first. The passage also identifies the exception to conversion: propositions that combine the two deficiencies (al-khaslatayn) of being negative and particular, like 'Some animal is not human', and indefinite negatives, do not have a valid conversion. Examples are given for each type: universal negative (e.g., 'No human is a stone' converts to 'No stone is a human'), universal affirmative (to particular affirmative), particular affirmative (remains particular affirmative), and indefinite affirmative (treated like particular affirmative). The passage concludes by distinguishing simple conversion from contrapositive (aks al-naqid). This lesson is essential for understanding immediate inferences in categorical logic.

Conversion Rules for Each Categorical Proposition Type
Author enumerates conversion rules for each proposition type with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the simple conversion rule to each of the four categorical proposition types and produce the correct converted form.

Core Idea
Universal negatives and particular affirmatives convert to themselves; universal affirmatives become particular affirmatives; particular negatives have no conversion.

The author provides explicit examples for each proposition type. This lesson practises applying the conversion rules, using the examples given in the passage to reinforce the pattern.

Guided Path
1
Universal negative (E)
The passage says it converts to itself. Verify with example: 'No human is a stone' → 'No stone is a human'. Why does it preserve truth?
Both terms are mutually exclusive.
2
Universal affirmative (A)
Converts to particular affirmative (I). Example: 'Every human is an animal' → 'Some animal is a human'. Understand the necessity of this change.
If converted as A, the conclusion would be false.
3
Particular affirmative (I)
Converts to itself. Example: 'Some animal is a human' → 'Some human is an animal'.
No change needed.
4
Indefinite affirmative
Treated like particular affirmative. Example: 'A human is a writer' → 'A writer is a human'.
Indefinites are equivalent to particulars in conversion rules.
5
Particular negative (O) and indefinite negative
No conversion. The passage says these have the two deficiencies (negative and particular).
Example: 'Some animal is not human' cannot be converted.

Rules of Conversion ('Aks) for Categorical Propositions
Author defines conversion and enumerates which propositions convert validly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of conversion to determine whether a given categorical proposition yields a true converse and identify the correct form of the converse.

Core Idea
Conversion flips subject and predicate; only the universal negative keeps its form; the universal affirmative weakens to particular.

In Islamic logic, conversion ('aks) is an immediate inference where the terms of a categorical proposition are swapped. It is a fundamental tool used in juristic reasoning to test the scope of rulings. This lesson unpacks the rules laid out by the author: which propositions convert validly, and in what form. Understanding these rules ensures you do not incorrectly extend a ruling beyond its proper scope.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of conversion
Conversion is the transposition of the subject and predicate of a proposition without changing its quality (affirmative/negative) or quantity (universal/particular), except for the universal affirmative which becomes particular affirmative.
The author stresses that conversion must preserve truth (lāzimun ʿalā jihat al-ṣidq).
2
Identify the proposition type
Given a categorical proposition, determine its quantity (universal, particular, indefinite) and quality (affirmative, negative). This is essential because the conversion rule varies accordingly.
Indefinite (muhmala) propositions are treated as particular in strength.
3
Apply the conversion rule for each type
Universal negative → converts as itself (e.g., 'No human is stone' → 'No stone is human'). Universal affirmative → converts to particular affirmative (e.g., 'Every human is animal' → 'Some animal is human'). Particular affirmative → converts as itself (e.g., 'Some animal is human' → 'Some human is animal'). Particular negative → does not convert at all (the two deficiencies—negativity and particularity—block it). Indefinite affirmative → converts like particular affirmative. Indefinite negative → does not convert (follows particular negative).
The author uses the Arabic terms: mūjiba kulliyya, mūjiba juziyya, sāliba kulliyya, etc.
4
Check the 'natural arrangement' condition
Conversion is only valid for propositions with natural arrangement (tartīb ṭabīʿī)—namely, predicative (ḥamliyya) and conditional (muttasila) propositions. Disjunctive propositions (munfaṣilāt) are excluded because their terms have no fixed order. Also exclude contradictory conversion (ʿaks al-naqīḍ).
This ensures the operation is genuine conversion, not a different inference.

Commentary
This passage defines the logical operation of conversion ('aks) and enumerates which categorical propositions admit a valid converse. The author begins by defining conversion as the transposition of a proposition's subject and predicate without altering its quality or quantity, with a single exception: the universal affirmative cannot convert as itself, but only to a particular affirmative. He shows this with the example 'Every human is an animal,' which cannot become 'Every animal is a human' without falsity, just as 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' cannot become 'Every forbidden thing is an intoxicant' without falsity since many forbidden things are not intoxicants. He clarifies that his concern is with conversion that preserves truth. He then lays down the general rule that every naturally arranged proposition necessarily admits conversion except the particular negative, where the two deficiencies—negativity and particularity—combine. Thus 'Some animal is not a human' has no true converse, just as 'Some wine is not an intoxicant' would yield no valid converse, and the indefinite negative follows the particular negative in this failure because it is potentially particular negative. Next he enumerates the successful cases: the universal negative converts to itself, as 'Nothing of human is stone' becomes 'Nothing of stone is human,' comparable to 'No dead meat is lawful' becoming 'Nothing lawful is dead meat.' The universal affirmative converts by limitation to a particular affirmative. The particular affirmative converts to itself, as 'Some animal is human' becomes 'Some human is animal,' just as 'Some intoxicant is forbidden' becomes 'Some forbidden thing is an intoxicant.' The indefinite affirmative behaves like the particular affirmative, so 'Human is writer' becomes 'Writer is human.' Finally, the author restricts true conversion to propositions with natural arrangement, namely predicative and conditional propositions, excluding disjunctive propositions because their terms have no fixed natural order but only conventional order, and he excludes contradictory conversion by the expression al-musawi.
