Four Doctrines on the Inferential Link in Kalam
Author reports four theological doctrines on the link between proof and conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four doctrines on the relation between premises and conclusion (rabit) and identify their proponents, assessing which doctrine implies necessary logical entailment.

Core Idea
The link between proof and conclusion is a theological doctrine about how knowledge is caused.

This passage from the commentary on al-Sullam reveals that medieval theologians did not take logical validity for granted. Instead, they debated the metaphysical nature of the inferential bond (rabit) that connects premises to conclusion. Understanding this debate is crucial because it shows that one's view on logical necessity is tied to one's broader worldview—whether one is Ash'ari, Mu'tazili, or a follower of Imam al-Haramayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four doctrines
The passage explicitly names four views: 'aqli (rational), 'adi (habitual), tawallud (generated), and wajib (necessary). Locate each in the Arabic and note the proponent for each.
The fourth doctrine (wajib) is mentioned but not elaborated; the passage focuses on the first three.
2
Understand each doctrine's implications for logical certainty
Imam al-Haramayn's 'aqli view makes the conclusion follow necessarily, so inference yields certainty. Al-Ash'ari's 'adi view makes the link merely customary, so inference yields only probability. The Mu'tazila's tawallud view makes inference a causal process, giving certainty if the cause is unopposed.
The commentator declares the 'aqli view correct, implying that only necessary entailment secures demonstration.
3
Recognize the commentator's stance
The commentator says 'the first, the supported' (al-awwal al-mu'ayyad), glossing it as 'the supported, i.e., the strengthened.' This indicates his own endorsement of the rational view.
4
Connect to Kalam methodology
This dispute shapes how proofs in Kalam are evaluated. For example, the standard proof for God's existence uses a categorical syllogism: if the connection is only habitual, the proof might not be apodictic.
Later Ash'ari theologians leaned toward habituality to preserve divine omnipotence, but this passage shows that even within Ash'arism, there is a spectrum.

Commentary
This passage reports a theological dispute about the nature of the inferential link (rabit) between premises and conclusion. The commentator alludes to four positions he had mentioned earlier in the matn. First is the doctrine of Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni): the link is a rational entailment ('aqli) that is necessary and cannot fail; this is the correct view, and the commentator identifies the matn phrase 'al-awwal al-mu'ayyad' as referring to it, glossing mu'ayyad as strengthened or supported. Second is the doctrine of al-Ash'ari: the link is merely habitual ('adi), so it can in principle fail. The commentator notes that both the first and second doctrines are also attributed to al-Qadi (al-Baqillani). Third is the doctrine of the Mu'tazila: the link is generated (tawallud), meaning that a power (qudra) produced in the mind by reflection causally brings about the conclusion. The fourth doctrine—necessary (wajib)—is mentioned in the opening list but not elaborated here; the passage breaks off after the third.

Key Takeaway
The necessity of logical inference is itself a theological commitment; for the commentator, only the rational ('aqli) view secures certain knowledge.
