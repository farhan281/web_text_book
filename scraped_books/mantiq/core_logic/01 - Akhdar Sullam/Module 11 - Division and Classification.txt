The Three Types of Conventional Signification
Author defines and classifies the three types of conventional signification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of conventional signification (mutabaqah, tadammun, iltizam) and classify examples from Islamic texts correctly.

Core Idea
A word can signify its full meaning, a part of it, or something necessarily attached.

This passage introduces a foundational classification in Islamic logic: the three types of conventional signification (al-dalalah al-wadi'iyyah). Understanding these divisions is critical for precise interpretation of texts, especially when extracting legal rulings or theological doctrines from scriptural language. The author provides concise definitions and examples to anchor each type, and the footnotes expand them with concrete Islamic illustrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three Arabic terms
Find the three key terms in the passage: al-dalalah al-mutabiqah, al-dalalah al-tadammuniyyah, and al-dalalah al-iltizamiyyah. Write them down with their transliterations.
These are the three branches of conventional signification.
2
Understand the criterion for division
Note the author's statement: the word either signifies the entire meaning posited for it, a part of that meaning, or something external but necessarily concomitant. This is the basis for the classification.
The division is exhaustive: every conventional signification falls under one of these three.
3
Analyze the examples for each type
For mutabiqah, the example is 'human' signifying 'rational animal'. For tadammun, 'human' signifying 'animal' alone or 'rational' alone. For iltizam, 'human' signifying 'laughter' or 'acceptance of learning'. Explain why each example fits its category.
Pay attention to the difference between tadammun (part of the meaning itself) and iltizam (something outside the meaning but necessarily connected).
4
Test with Islamic examples from the footnotes
The footnote on mutabiqah gives 'prayer' (al-salat) signifying the complete act (takbir to salam). The footnote on tadammun gives 'prayer' signifying 'prostration' or 'bowing'. Confirm that these are valid instances.
These examples show how the classification applies to Islamic legal terms.
5
Distinguish tadammun from iltizam explicitly
Create a rule: tadammun = part of the definitional meaning of the word; iltizam = something not in the definition but necessary for the meaning to exist. For example, 'animal' is part of what 'human' means; 'laughter' is not part of the definition but follows from being human.
This distinction is crucial to avoid confusion in legal and theological reasoning.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the three types of conventional signification (al-dalalah al-wadi'iyyah). The author begins with a compressed matn: when a word signifies what fully agrees with its posited meaning, that is correspondence signification; when it signifies a part of that meaning, that is inclusive signification; and when it signifies what is externally necessitated by that meaning, that is commitment signification. He then explains that this section treats signification in which human convention has an entrance, and he divides it into three types because a word either signifies the entire meaning posited for it, a part of it, or an external concomitant. Correspondence signification is so called because the signifier corresponds to the signified. The first footnote defines it as the word signifying the complete meaning posited for it, giving the example that the word human signifies rational animal and the word prayer signifies the specific utterances and acts that begin with takbir and end with salam. Inclusive signification, detailed in the second footnote, is the word signifying a part of its meaning within the whole, such as the word human signifying animal or rational, or the word prayer signifying prostration or bowing. Commitment signification, in the third footnote, is the word signifying a matter external to its meaning but necessarily concomitant to it, such as the word human signifying laughter or acceptance of learning.

Compound and Simple Expressions
Author divides expressions into compound and simple, defining each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any expression as compound or simple and distinguish the simple from the singular.

Core Idea
A compound expression has significant parts; a simple expression does not — and 'simple' does not mean 'one'.

Every logical expression falls into one of two fundamental categories: compound or simple. Understanding this division is the first step in analyzing any statement or definition. In this lesson, we explore the precise definitions given by the author and clarify a common confusion between the simple and the singular.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The author states that expressions used in logic are either compound (murakkab) or simple (mufrad). Start by noting this binary division.
This is a foundational classification in mantiq.
2
Understand the compound definition
A compound expression is one where a part of the wording is intended to indicate a part of the meaning. For example, 'Zaid is standing' has 'Zaid' indicating the subject and 'is standing' indicating the predicate.
The key is that parts correspond to parts of the meaning.
3
Understand the simple definition
A simple expression is one where no part of the wording indicates a part of the meaning. For instance, 'man' is simple because its parts (m, a, n) do not indicate parts of the concept of man.
Simplicity here is about signification, not word length.
4
Distinguish simple from singular
The author warns not to confuse simple (mufrad) with one (wahid). The simple can be figurative and applies to many genera, while the one is restricted to the real singular. For example, 'human' is simple but not one, because it applies to many individuals.
This distinction prevents terminological errors in logic and theology.

The Real Universal (al-Kulli al-Haqiqi)
Author defines the real universal and explains its naming.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a real universal term by testing whether its conception permits sharing among multiple individuals, and explain why it is attributed to the whole.

Core Idea
A universal term's meaning does not forbid many from sharing it.

In the previous lesson, we saw that simple expressions are either universal or particular. Now we focus on the universal. The author defines it with a precise condition that separates it from particular terms. This definition is crucial for understanding how definitions and syllogisms work in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition in the Arabic
Look for the phrase 'ما لا يمنع نفس تصوره من وقوع الشركة فيه' in footnote 3.
2
Understand the condition
The definition does not require that the universal actually has many instances; it only requires that the meaning itself does not prevent sharing. For example, 'phoenix' is still a universal if it were to exist.
3
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider 'insān' (man). The concept of man does not prevent it from applying to many individuals; therefore it is a universal.
4
Examine a non-universal
'Zayd' is not universal because the concept of Zayd excludes any other person from being that Zayd.
5
Analyse the naming
The text explains it is called kulli because universality is relative to the particular, and the universal is part of the particular (since particular individuals contain universals). So it is attributed to the whole (the universal applies to all individuals).

Author introduces the initial division of terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given Arabic word into muhmal, musta'mal, murakkab, or mufrad and identify its subtype (ism, fi'l, harf).

Core Idea
Words are either meaningless or meaningful; meaningful words are either simple or compound.

In this lesson, we learn the first classification of lafz (words) presented by the author. This classification is foundational for understanding how terms function in definitions and propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path

Universal and Particular Concepts
Author defines universal and particular and lists types of universals by existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between kulli (universal) and juz'i (particular) and identify instances of each.

Core Idea
A universal does not prevent sharing; a particular does by its very conception.

After classifying simple words, the author introduces the crucial distinction between universal (kulli) and particular (juz'i). This distinction underpins all logical reasoning and is essential for understanding definition and syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Define kulli using the criterion given
The author defines kulli as 'what does not prevent sharing in it by the very conception of its meaning'. Focus on the phrase 'la yamna' nafsu tasawwuri ma'nahu wuqu' al-sharika'. This means that the concept itself allows multiple individuals to be described by it.
2
Explore the existence possibilities of kulli
The author lists cases: impossible existence (e.g., two opposites), possible but not actual (e.g., a sea of mercury), one instance with possible others (e.g., the sun), impossible concept (e.g., a sign), finite many (e.g., human), infinite (e.g., number). Note that existence does not affect universality.
3
Define juz'i and contrast with kulli
Juz'i is defined as 'what prevents sharing by the very conception of its meaning'. The example is 'Zaid' – his essence cannot be shared. Understand that proper names are typical juz'i, but any concept that inherently restricts to one individual is juz'i.
The text calls juz'i 'al-haqiqi' (genuine particular).

Essential, Accidental, and the Five Universals
Author distinguishes dhati, 'aradi, and naw', and names the five universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a universal as dhati, 'aradi, or naw' and list the five universals.

Core Idea
Universals that are part of the essence are essential; those outside are accidental; species combines both.

Building on the concept of kulli, the author now distinguishes between universals that are part of the essence (dhati) and those that are not ('aradi). He also introduces the key idea of naw' (species) and announces the five universals that will be defined in subsequent sections.

Guided Path
1
Understand the criterion for dhati (essential)
A universal is dhati if it is included (mundarij) in the reality of its particulars. The example: 'animal' for Zaid and Amr, because animality is part of their essence. Look for the phrase 'juz' haqiqatiha'—it is a part of their essence.
2
Understand 'aradi (accidental)
An 'aradi universal is not included in the essence but is external to it. Example: 'writer' for a human. Note that a single universal can be dhati for one species and 'aradi for another, but the author focuses on the relation to human.
3
Recognize naw' (species) as the complete essence
The author explains that what expresses the entire essence (majmu' al-haqiqa) is not dhati nor 'aradi, but rather 'wasita' (intermediate) and 'naw''. Example: 'human' combines animality (genus) and rationality (differentia). This is the species.
4
Memorize the list of five universals
The final line states: 'al-kulliyyat khamsa duna intiqas: jins, fasl, 'arad, naw', khassa.' Commit these to memory—they are the tools for definition.

Three Types of Genus: Distant, Close, and Intermediate
Author explains three types of genus and comments on poem's wording.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify a genus as distant, close, or intermediate based on its position in the hierarchy of genera.

Core Idea
Genus is relative: distant above, close below, intermediate in between.

In Islamic logic, genera are not absolute categories; their classification as distant, close, or intermediate depends on their position relative to other genera. The author of the Sullam explains this with clear examples: substance (distant), animal (close for human), and body (intermediate). This lesson will teach you to navigate the Porphyrian tree and correctly identify each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus in question
Locate the genus term in the definition or hierarchy you are examining. For example, in the definition of 'human', the genus is 'animal'.
2
Check for genera above it
If the genus has no higher genus (i.e., it is the highest), it is distant. Example: 'substance' is a distant genus because there is no genus above it.
Also called 'jins ba'id', 'jins 'ali', or 'jins al-ajnas'.
3
Check for genera below it
If the genus has no lower genus (i.e., it is immediate to the species), it is close. Example: 'animal' is a close genus relative to 'human' because there is no intermediate genus between them.
Also called 'jins qarib', 'asfal', or 'akhir'.
4
If both above and below exist, it is intermediate
If the genus has at least one genus above and one below, it is intermediate. Example: 'body' is intermediate because it is below 'substance' and above 'animal'.
Also called 'jins mutawassit'.

Four kalami opinions on the proof-conclusion link
Author classifies four theological opinions on inferential connection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four theological opinions on the nature of the inferential connection between premises and conclusion, and map each to its proponent.

Core Idea
Theologians disagree on whether the link from evidence to conclusion is rational, customary, generative, or necessary.

In this passage, the author presents a key theological debate that underlies the entire project of logical proof in Islam: what is the nature of the connection between premises and conclusion? Four schools of thought offer radically different answers, each with implications for certainty and valid inference. Understanding this debate will help you see why some arguments are considered absolute and others merely probable.

Guided Path
1
List the four opinions
From the Arabic, extract the four types of connection: ʿaqlī (rational), ʿādī (customary), tawallud (generative), and wājib (necessary). Note that the author also calls the first 'al-muʾayyad' (the supported).
The first opinion is declared correct and unbreakable.
2
Match each opinion to its proponent
Identify the scholars: ʿaqlī – Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwaynī; ʿādī – al-Ashʿarī (and also al-Qāḍī); tawallud – the Muʿtazila; wājib – the philosophers.
The author adds that both the rational and customary views are also attributed to al-Qāḍī.
3
Understand the epistemic nature of each connection
Analyze the strength: ʿaqlī is necessary and cannot fail (fa-lā yumkin takhallufuh). ʿādī is merely habitual and can fail (yumkin takhallufuh). Tawallud involves a generated power from reflection. Wājib is necessary but attributed to philosophers.
The distinction determines whether a proof yields certainty (yaqīn) or probability (ẓann).
4
Evaluate the author's preferred opinion
The author explicitly endorses the first (ʿaqlī) as the correct one (huwa al-ṣaḥīḥ). Consider why a rational, inviolable connection would be preferred in kalām for establishing certain theological truths.
The author's phrase 'al-muʾayyad' reinforces this view.

Commentary
This passage classifies four theological doctrines concerning the nature of the inferential link that binds premises to their conclusion. The author begins by quoting his own earlier summary, listing four types of connection: rational (ʿaqlī), customary (ʿādī), generative (tawallud), and necessary (wājib). He then proceeds to map each type to its proponents. The first, identified as the school of Imam al-Haramayn al-Juwaynī, is declared correct and inviolable; the author confirms that his own phrase al-muʾayyad in the matn refers to this fortified, unbreakable rational bond. The second, attributed to al-Ashʿarī, is described as merely customary and thus susceptible to failure; the author adds that both opinions are also ascribed to al-Qāḍī. The third, the Muʿtazilī doctrine of tawallud, is explained through their specific psychology of knowledge: a power generated by the act of reflection produces the conclusion. Finally, the fourth position is assigned to the philosophers, after which the author states that his purpose in rehearsing this disagreement is simply to complete the benefit of the discussion.
