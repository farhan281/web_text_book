Classifying Disjunctive Syllogisms: The Three Divisions
Commentator classifies disjunctive types and proves which is the true hakiki form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of disjunctive syllogism and identify which is the true (haqiqi) form.

Core Idea
The true disjunctive prevents both conjunction and absence; the other two prevent only one.

Disjunctive syllogisms (qiyas istithna'i munfasil) come in three varieties. The commentator on the Sullam al-Murawnaq here classifies them, showing that only one variety is truly exhaustive and exclusive. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in kalam and fiqh that use 'either/or' reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
The passage describes three divisions: mani' al-jam' (prevents conjunction), mani' al-raf' (prevents absence), and mani' al-jam' wal-khuluw (prevents both).
2
Examine the logical condition of each
For mani' al-jam': both can be false but not both true. For mani' al-raf': both can be true but not both false. For the true type: exactly one must be true.
An Islamic example for mani' al-jam': 'Either this drink is khamr or it is permissible.' Both can be false (if not intoxicating). For mani' al-raf': 'Either this act is wajib or it is mandub.' Both can be false (if makruh or haram).
3
Locate the examples in the Arabic
Find the example for mani' al-raf': 'being in the sea and not drowning' (yumkin al-jam'...). For the true type: 'the number is either even or odd'.
4
Understand why the third is called 'true' (haqiqi)
It is more specific because any instance of it also satisfies the conditions of the other two, but not vice versa. The commentator states: 'this division is the true one, and it is more specific than its two divisions.'
5
Apply an Islamic example
Consider the disjunction 'The world is either eternal (qadim) or temporally originated (hadith)'. This is a true disjunctive because it cannot be both eternal and originated, nor neither; every world must be one or the other.
This example is used in kalam to argue for the existence of a Creator.

Commentary
This passage expands on the three divisions of the disjunctive syllogism (qiyas istithna'i munfasil) and demonstrates why the preventer of both conjunction and absence (mani'at al-jam' wal-khuluw) is the true (haqiqi) form. The commentator begins by alluding to the division that prevents only the absence of both alternatives (mani'at al-khuluw), illustrating that both alternatives may be affirmed together—namely, being in the sea and not drowning—while it is impossible for both to be negated together in the example he gives. He then turns to the preventer of both (mani'at al-jam' wal-khuluw), using the classic example of number: every number is either even or odd, for it cannot be both even and odd in one subject, nor can it be neither even nor odd. He then argues that this third division is the true one and more specific (akhass) than the other two, because any instance that falls under it necessarily shares with the preventer of conjunction the impossibility of both alternatives being true, and shares with the preventer of negation the impossibility of both alternatives being false. Yet each of the other two divisions possesses examples that do not fall under the third, which makes them broader (a'amm) in extension while the third remains narrower and more precise.

The Categorical Syllogism: Definition and Theological Example
Author defines the categorical syllogism and illustrates it with a theological example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a categorical syllogism, distinguish the material and formal presence of the conclusion, and apply the definition to Islamic theological arguments.

Core Idea
In a categorical syllogism, the conclusion is already contained in the premises—but only in material, not formal, terms.

The passage introduces the categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani) as a key division of syllogism. It restricts it to predicative propositions and explains that the conclusion is present in potency. The author uses a classic kalam example: the world is changing, and everything changing is temporally originated—implying the world is temporally originated. This lesson unpacks the definition and the crucial distinction between material content (madda) and formal structure (hay'a).

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its genus
The passage defines 'categorical syllogism' as a type of syllogism. The genus is syllogism (qiyas). Locate the definition in the matn: 'ثم القياس عندهم قسمان فمنه ما يدعى بالاقتراني وهو الذي دل على النتيجة بقوة واختص بالحملية.'
2
Extract the specific restriction
The categorical syllogism is restricted to predicative propositions (hamliyya). This means it deals with affirmations or denials of a predicate of a subject, not conditional or disjunctive propositions. Why is this restriction important?
Contrast with conditional syllogisms which use 'if...then' or 'either...or'.
3
Analyze the theological example
The example given is 'العالم متغير وكل متغير حادث'. Identify the two premises. What conclusion is implied? Why does the author say the conclusion is present 'in potency' (bi-l-quwwa) rather than explicitly stated?
The conclusion 'العالم حادث' is not written out but is necessarily implied.
4
Distinguish madda and hay'a
The footnote explains that the conclusion is present in the premises by its material content (madda) i.e., its constituent terms, but not by its compositional form (hay'a). For instance, in 'every human is an animal, every animal is a body, therefore every human is a body', the terms 'human', 'animal', 'body' appear in the premises, but the specific arrangement 'human-body' as a proposition is new.
Hay'a here refers to the syllogistic figure or the propositional structure.
5
Apply to a new Islamic example
Consider: 'The Qur'an is kalam Allah; every kalam Allah is qadim (uncreated).' Is this a categorical syllogism? What is the implied conclusion? How is the conclusion materially present?
The conclusion 'The Qur'an is qadim' is derived from the premises.

The Essential Conditions for Constructing a Valid Syllogism
Conditions for constructing a valid syllogism

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires subsumption of the minor premise under the major.

This passage from al-Akhdari's Sullam al-Munawraq, with its own commentary, condenses the essential structural and epistemic conditions for a valid syllogism. The author stresses that constructing a syllogism is not merely piling up premises; it requires arranging them so that the minor is subsumed under the major, and then verifying their soundness. In this lesson, we unpack these conditions and see how they guarantee a valid conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main phases of construction
The text distinguishes between the act of composing the premises according to the rules (tarkib) and then examining them for soundness (nazar). Notice that composition precedes verification.
The Arabic verb 'فاركب' indicates composition, while 'وانظر' indicates examination.
2
Extract the specific structural requirement
The passage repeatedly insists on subsumption (indiraj) of the minor premise under the major premise. Why is this essential? Because without subsumption, the middle term cannot link the premises to yield a conclusion.
The term 'اندراج' refers to the logical inclusion of the minor in the major.
3
Understand the epistemic link between premises and conclusion
The author states: 'اللازم مبني على المقدمات' – the necessary implication depends on the premises. This means a valid conclusion follows only if the premises are sound and properly structured.
This echoes the principle that the conclusion is implicit in the premises when correctly arranged.

Check the commentary's expansion on the matn
The commentary clarifies that the matn's statement 'فإن لازم المقدمات بحسب المقدمات آت' means the conclusion comes according to the premises. Reflect on how this relates to the condition of subsumption: even if premises are true, without proper arrangement the conclusion may not follow.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between figure (shakl) and mood (darb) in a categorical syllogism and identify which component is defined by the position of the middle term.

Core Idea
Figure is the skeleton without quantifiers; mood adds the flesh of universality and particularity.

In this passage, the author clarifies two technical terms that students often confuse: figure (shakl) and mood (darb). Both describe the two premises of a syllogism, but from different angles. Understanding the distinction is the first step to analysing any syllogism correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
Find the two propositions that serve as premises. The middle term appears in both but not in the conclusion.
2
Locate the middle term
The term that is repeated in both premises. In Islamic logic examples, it is often 'animal', 'changing', or 'originated'.
3
Assign figure
Note whether the middle term is subject or predicate in each premise. That arrangement is the figure, independent of quantifiers.
4
Assign mood
Now consider the quantifiers (universal/particular) and quality (affirmative/negative) of each premise. That combination is the mood.
5
Compare figure and mood
The figure remains the same even if quantifiers change; the mood varies with quantifiers and quality.

The Four Figures of the Categorical Syllogism
Author defines the four figures by middle-term placement and ranks them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four figures of the categorical syllogism by the position of the middle term and rank them correctly.

Core Idea
The figure of a syllogism depends entirely on the middle term's subject-predicate position in each premise.

The categorical syllogism's validity depends not only on mood (quantity and quality of premises) but also on how the terms are arranged across the two premises. This arrangement is called the 'figure' (shakl). Classical logicians recognized exactly four figures and ranked them, with the first being the strongest because all others reduce to it in reality. Understanding these figures is key to constructing sound arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Middle Term
Find the term that appears in both premises but does not appear in the conclusion. This is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat). For example, in 'All humans are animals; All animals are mortal', the middle term is 'animals'.
2
Check the Minor Premise
In the minor premise (the one containing the subject of the conclusion), note whether the middle term is the subject or the predicate. This gives you the first piece of the figure pattern.
3
Check the Major Premise
In the major premise (the one containing the predicate of the conclusion), note whether the middle term is the subject or the predicate. This gives you the second piece.
4
Match to One of Four Patterns
Use the mnemonic from the poem: 'carried in its minor and placed in its major' (middle term is predicate in minor, subject in major) = first figure; 'carried in the whole' (predicate in both) = second; 'placed in the whole' (subject in both) = third; 'reverse of the first' (subject in minor, predicate in major) = fourth.
5
Rank the Figures by Strength
The first figure is the strongest because it is the most natural (the middle term mediates between subject and predicate). The second is next (agrees in the side of predication), then the third, and the fourth is weakest because it is a reversal of the first and agrees in neither side.

The Four Productive Moods of the First Figure
Author enumerates the four valid moods of the first figure and states the rule of the weaker premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the mood of any first-figure categorical syllogism and determine its conclusion using the rule of the weaker premise.

Core Idea
The four moods of the first figure are completely determined by the condition: minor affirmative, major universal.

In the previous lesson, you learned the condition for a productive first-figure syllogism: the minor premise must be affirmative and the major premise must be universal. Here, al-Akhdar enumerates the four specific arrangements of quantity and quality that satisfy this condition, known as the four moods. Each mood yields a distinct type of conclusion, governed by the principle that the conclusion follows the weaker premise. By mastering these moods, you can instantly verify whether any first-figure syllogism is valid and what it proves.

Guided Path
1
Recall the condition of the first figure
For the first figure to be productive, the minor premise must be affirmative and the major premise must be universal. This is because the middle term is the subject of the major and the predicate of the minor.
If either condition fails, the syllogism is invalid.
2
List the four possible combinations of quantity and quality
The four moods arise from the two possible quantities (universal/particular) for the minor, and two possible qualities (affirmative/negative) for the major, while keeping the minor affirmative and major universal.
The minor cannot be negative; the major cannot be particular.
3
Map each combination to its conclusion using the weaker premise rule
The conclusion 'follows the more despicable (weaker) premise': if one premise is particular, the conclusion is particular; if one premise is negative, the conclusion is negative. When both are universal affirmative, the conclusion is universal affirmative.
The weaker premise determines both quantity and quality.

In the passage, ج (jim) is the minor term, ب (ba) is the middle term, and أم (alif-mim) is the major term. For each mood, note the quantifiers on the premises and derive the conclusion.
Example: 'كل ج ب' and 'كل ب أم' gives 'كل ج أم' (first mood).

Omitting Known Premises or Conclusions (Hadhf)
Author permits omission of known premises or conclusion in syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the omitted premise in an enthymeme and justify the permissibility of omission based on the audience's knowledge.

Core Idea
Omitting a known premise or conclusion preserves argument efficiency without loss of validity.

The author next permits the omission (hadhf) of one of the premises or even the conclusion when the omitted element is already known. This practice, common in everyday discourse, makes arguments concise. He provides three examples from Islamic contexts: one from hadd punishments and one from medical knowledge. By analyzing these examples, we learn how to reconstruct the missing part and appreciate the logical principle behind enthymemes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases of omission
Find in the passage the examples of omitting (1) the minor premise, (2) the major premise, and (3) the conclusion.
The author labels them implicitly; you may need to infer which is which based on the structure.
2
Analyze the first example — omitted minor premise
The argument: 'هذا يحد، لأن كل زان يحد' (this one receives the hadd punishment, because every fornicator receives the hadd punishment). The explicit premise is the major (universal rule). The omitted minor is: 'هذا زان' (this one is a fornicator).
This omission works because the listener already knows the minor.
3
Analyze the second example — omitted major premise
The argument: 'هذا يحد لأنه زان' (this one receives the hadd punishment because he is a fornicator). The explicit premise is the minor (particular fact). The omitted major is: 'كل زان يحد' (every fornicator receives the hadd punishment).
4
Analyze the third example — omitted conclusion
The argument: 'هذا رمان وكل رمان يحبس القيء' (this is a pomegranate and every pomegranate restrains vomit). Both premises are present, but the conclusion 'فإذا هذا الرمان يحبس القيء' (so this pomegranate restrains vomit) is omitted.
The listener infers the conclusion automatically.
5
Generalize the rule
Omission is permitted only when the omitted part is known to the audience. This is not a logical error but an efficient presentation.

Termination in Necessary Premises to Avoid Circularity and Infinite Regress
Author insists demonstration must terminate in necessary premises to avoid circularity and infinite regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why demonstration must terminate in necessary premises and identify illegal circularity and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Demonstration must end in necessarily true premises to avoid vicious circularity and infinite regress.

The author concludes the passage with a foundational principle: the chain of premises in any demonstration must ultimately terminate in necessary truths (daruriyyat). This is because two logical defects—circular reasoning (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul)—are impossible. If a proof could go on forever or circle back, no conclusion would be certain. Therefore, all demonstrative knowledge rests on self-evident or axiomatic premises that require no further proof.

3
Recognize the solution: necessary premises
The author states that premises 'must terminate in a decisive necessity' (ضرورة قاطعة). These are self-evident truths that do not require proof.
In Arabic, 'ضرورة' refers to necessary knowledge.

Rules of Conclusion Weakening and Premise Omission
Rules for conclusion weakening and premises/conclusion omission with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that the conclusion follows the weaker premise and to construct syllogisms with omitted premises or conclusions using Islamic legal and medical examples.

Core Idea
The conclusion is always the weaker premise; known parts can be dropped.

In this passage, the author explains two practical rules: first, the conclusion of a syllogism always takes the weaker premise (inferior quantity/quality); second, it is permissible to omit a premise or the conclusion when it is already known. These rules streamline arguments in Islamic sciences, as shown by examples from penal law (hadd) and medicine.

Guided Path
1
Understand 'weaker premise'
The conclusion's quantity and quality are determined by the 'weaker' premise. A particular proposition is weaker than a universal, and a negative is weaker than an affirmative. The weakest is particular negative.

From the text, the inferior (akhass) is the particular negative. The conclusion follows this weakest premise.
3
Apply the rule to a sample syllogism
Given 'Every fornicator is flogged' (universal affirmative) and 'Zayd is a fornicator' (particular affirmative), the conclusion is 'Zayd is flogged' (particular affirmative) because the weaker premise is the minor (particular).

Understand omission conditions
A premise or conclusion may be omitted when it is known (bi al-'ilm). The syllogism remains valid because the omitted part is evident to the audience.
5

Three examples from the text: omit minor premise: 'This one is flogged, because every fornicator is flogged'; omit major: 'This one is flogged, because he is a fornicator'; omit conclusion: 'This is pomegranate, and every pomegranate checks vomiting' (conclusion that it checks vomiting is omitted).

The Necessity of Foundational Premises: Avoiding Circularity and Infinite Regress
Author argues that all premises must ultimately lead to necessary truths to prevent circularity and infinite regress.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every proof must rest on self-evident truths or it collapses into circularity or infinite regress.

In this passage, the commentator explains a fundamental logical axiom: the chain of premises in any demonstration cannot go on forever or loop back on itself. Instead, it must terminate in self-evident necessary truths (darurat). Understanding this principle is crucial for constructing sound arguments in any Islamic science, from kalam to fiqh.

Guided Path

2
Understand why they are impossible
The passage states both are impossible. Circularity makes proof impossible because each premise depends on the other; infinite regress means no starting point for proof.
This is a foundational axiom in logic.
3

The chain of premises must terminate in a self-evident necessity that requires no further proof, cutting off both dawr and tasalsul.
These necessary truths are the bedrock of all demonstrations.

Definition of Conditional Syllogism and Connected Type
Author defines conditional syllogism, its two types, and the connected mood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the conditional syllogism, distinguish its connected and disconnected types, and apply the rule that the antecedent must not be more general than the consequent.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism states its conclusion explicitly, not just potentially.

In this passage from the Sullam al-Munawraq, the author introduces the conditional syllogism (al-istithna'i) as the second major division of syllogism. He immediately splits it into two types: connected (muttasil) and disconnected (munfasil), and then focuses on the connected type. You will learn the definition, the key terminology, and a crucial rule about the relationship between the antecedent and consequent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division of syllogism
Note that the author states the conditional syllogism is the second division of syllogism. The first division is the categorical (iqtirani). This classification is fundamental to Islamic logic.
The conditional syllogism is also called istithna'i because it indicates the conclusion or its contradictory explicitly.
2
Distinguish the two types
The author divides the conditional into connected (muttasil) and disconnected (munfasil). Focus on the connected: it involves a conditional particle and the judgment that the necessity of a second proposition follows from a first.
An example of a conditional particle is 'law' (if) as in the Quranic verse cited.
3
Grasp the terminology
The premise containing the condition is called 'shartiyya'; the other premise is called 'istithna'iyya'. Remember that the istithna'iyya is not the one with the condition.
This is a common point of confusion: the istithna'iyya is the premise that makes the exception or affirmation.
4
Apply the generality rule
The author rules that the antecedent (muqaddam) cannot be more general than the consequent (tali). Reasoning: judging the more general entails judging the more specific, but not vice versa. For example, 'If it is wine, then it is forbidden' is valid, but 'If it is forbidden, then it is wine' is invalid because 'forbidden' is broader.
This rule parallels the categorical syllogism rule that the subject cannot be more general than the predicate.
5
Understand the naming rationale
The conditional syllogism is called 'istithna'i' because it indicates the conclusion or its contradictory explicitly (bil-fi'l), not potentially (bil-quwwa). This contrasts with categorical syllogisms where the conclusion is implicit.
The word 'istithna' literally means exception, but here it refers to the explicit statement of the conclusion.

Explicit vs Potential Mention in Conditional Syllogisms
Commentator explains why the conclusion is said to be present explicitly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish explicit (bil-fi'l) from potential (bil-quwwa) mention of the conclusion in a conditional syllogism, and identify the conclusion's matter and form in the premises.

Core Idea
In a conditional syllogism, the conclusion is not just deduced—its very words are already present in the premises.

This passage from the Sullam al-Mantiq commentary clarifies why the conditional syllogism is called istithna'i: the conclusion or its contradictory is mentioned explicitly in the premises. The commentator uses the terms bil-fi'l and bil-quwwa to contrast literal inclusion with mere potential deduction. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the nature of conditional reasoning in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism type
The passage defines 'al-qiyas al-istithna'i' (conditional syllogism). Note that it is defined by the explicit mention of the conclusion. This contrasts with categorical syllogisms where the conclusion is only implicit.
2
Extract key terms: bil-fi'l and bil-quwwa
Find the Arabic terms. 'Bil-fi'l' means actually, explicitly—the conclusion's wording is present. 'Bil-quwwa' means potentially—the conclusion is only deducible, not literally there.
These terms are stable across mantiq texts.
3
Locate the conclusion in the example
In the example 'if he is a human, then he is an animal', the conclusion 'fa-huwa hayawan' appears verbatim in the major premise. Verify that the same words occur.
The major premise is 'in kana insanan fa-huwa hayawan'.
4
Examine the denial case
The commentator also considers modus tollens: if the minor premise denies the consequent, the conclusion is 'fa-huwa laysa insanan'. Its contradictory 'kana insanan' was already present in the original conditional premise.
5
Contrast with categorical syllogisms
Understand that in categorical syllogisms, the conclusion is never explicitly stated in any premise; it is only potentially there. This distinction is why the conditional syllogism is named istithna'i.

Actual vs. Potential Conclusion in Syllogisms
Commentator explains why the conditional syllogism is called istithnaʾi through the actual mention of its conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the conditional syllogism (al-istithnāʾī) from the categorical (al-iqtirānī) by identifying whether the conclusion is mentioned actually (bil-fiʿl) or potentially (bil-quwwa) in the premises.

Core Idea
The conditional syllogism states its conclusion openly; the categorical merely implies it.

In logic, the way a syllogism relates to its conclusion determines its type. The commentator unpacks why the conditional syllogism is called 'exceptional' (istithnāʾī): because it actually includes the conclusion or its contradictory within its premises. This contrasts with the categorical syllogism, where the conclusion is only latent. Understanding this distinction is key to recognizing the structure of conditional proofs in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two syllogism types
Locate the terms 'istithnāʾī' (exceptional/conditional) and 'iqtirānī' (categorical). The passage explains that the former 'indicates the conclusion or its contradictory actually' while the latter does only potentially.
The term 'bil-quwwa' is used to exclude the categorical.
2
Grasp the meaning of 'bil-fiʿl'
The commentator says 'actually' means the conclusion is present with its very matter and form (māddatuhā wa-hayʾatuhā) in the premises. Look at the example: 'If daytime existed, then the sun would be rising' – the conclusion 'the sun is rising' is explicitly stated in the conditional premise.
Contrast this with a categorical like 'Every human is an animal; Zayd is a human; therefore Zayd is an animal' – here 'Zayd is an animal' is not stated in any premise verbatim.
3
Examine the example with a negative
The passage also gives an example where the contradictory of the conclusion is mentioned: 'If he is a human, he is an animal; but he is not an animal; therefore he is not a human.' Here 'he is not a human' is the contradictory of the conclusion 'he is a human', and it appears in the syllogism.
This shows that the conditional syllogism can contain either the conclusion or its opposite, as long as it is explicit.
4
Apply the distinction to a real argument
Consider a fiqh example: 'If the sale includes gharar, it is invalid; this sale includes gharar; therefore it is invalid.' The conclusion 'it is invalid' is actually present in the first premise. This is a conditional syllogism.
Contrast with a categorical: 'Every invalid contract is void; this sale is invalid; therefore it is void.' The conclusion is not stated in the premises word-for-word.

Valid and Invalid Moods of the Connected Conditional Syllogism
Author enumerates the four moods of the muttaṣil conditional syllogism and identifies the two valid forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four moods of the connected conditional syllogism is valid and explain the logical reason for the invalidity of the other two.

Core Idea
Only affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent yield valid conclusions.

This passage from the Sullam al-Ushul on logic presents the four possible moods of the connected conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i muttasil). The author clearly distinguishes the two valid forms from the two invalid ones, using the famous example of the sun and daytime. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence, where conditional reasoning is common.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four possible exceptions
List the four ways the minor premise can relate to the conditional: excepting the antecedent itself, its contradictory, the contradictory of the consequent, or the consequent itself.
These correspond to the four moods.
2
Recognize the two valid moods
The author states that excepting the antecedent (istithna' 'ayn al-muqaddam) yields the consequent, and excepting the contradictory of the consequent (istithna' naqid al-tali) yields the contradictory of the antecedent.
These are modus ponens and modus tollens.
3
Understand why the other two are invalid
The author explains that excepting the contradictory of the antecedent or the consequent itself does not yield a necessary conclusion because the consequent may be broader in extension than the antecedent.
Establishing the narrower entails the broader, but not conversely; negating the broader entails negating the narrower, but not conversely.
4
Apply to the given example
In the example 'If this is a human, then it is an animal', the consequent 'animal' is broader than 'human'. Thus, 'it is an animal' does not imply 'it is a human' (fallacy of affirming consequent), and 'it is not a human' does not imply 'it is not an animal'.

Valid and Invalid Moods of the Connected Conditional Syllogism
Commentator glosses the matn verse on muttaṣil validity and notes the exception when terms are co-extensive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the valid modus ponens and modus tollens moods, the invalid converse moods, and the co-extension exception in a connected conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
In a connected conditional, modus ponens and modus tollens are valid; converse moods fail unless terms are co-extensive.

This lesson unpacks the commentator's gloss on the matn verse about the connected conditional (muttasil). It distinguishes the two valid forms of inference—modus ponens and modus tollens—from their invalid converses, and then highlights a crucial exception: when the antecedent and consequent are equal in extension, the converse moods also become valid. This distinction between form and matter is essential for sound reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two valid moods
The text states: positing the antecedent yields the consequent (modus ponens) and removing the consequent yields removing the antecedent (modus tollens). Both are valid.
2
Contrast with invalid converse moods
The converse moods—positing the consequent or removing the antecedent—do not produce a conclusion. The text explicitly says 'their converses do not follow'.
The invalid converse of modus ponens is often called the 'fallacy of affirming the consequent', and that of modus tollens is 'denying the antecedent'.
3
Understand why converse moods are generally invalid
The consequent is usually broader in extension than the antecedent, so its affirmation does not guarantee the antecedent, and its negation does not falsify the antecedent.
Example: 'If it is a cat, it is a mammal' – being a mammal does not imply it is a cat.
4
Recognize the co-extension exception
The commentator adds: when the consequent is not broader but equal in extension (mutasawiyan), establishing one establishes the other both ways, making the converse moods valid.
This is a departure from the general rule, but it is grounded in the particular matter (al-khusus al-madda), not in the logical form.
5
Attribute the exception to matter, not form
The co-extension is a property of the specific content, not a structural feature of the syllogistic form. The form alone does not guarantee validity; the material relation matters.
A reminder that logical tools must be applied with awareness of the subject matter.

Valid and Invalid Moods of the Connected Conditional
Commentator explains the connected conditional syllogism's valid moods and the material condition for converse validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish modus ponens and modus tollens from their converses in a connected conditional syllogism, and identify when the converse becomes valid due to co-extensive antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
Form gives two valid moods; matter can make the invalid ones valid.

The connected conditional syllogism (qiyās istithnāʾī muttaṣil) has exactly two formally valid moods: affirming the antecedent yields the consequent, and denying the consequent yields the denial of the antecedent. The converse moods—affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent—are generally invalid. However, the commentator adds a crucial refinement: if the consequent is not broader but equal in extension to the antecedent, then the converse moods become valid. This exception depends on the matter (mādda), not the form (ṣūra). Understanding this distinction prevents logical errors in theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
In a connected conditional (law ṣāra kadhā fa-kadhā), find the part after 'if' (al-muqaddam) and the part after 'then' (al-tālī). For example, 'If the world is created, then it has a Creator' — 'the world is created' is the antecedent, 'it has a Creator' is the consequent.
2
Apply the two valid moods
Modus ponens (waḍʿ al-muqaddam) is when you affirm the antecedent and conclude the consequent. Modus tollens (rafʿ al-tālī) is when you deny the consequent and conclude the denial of the antecedent. Verify these in the text as 'وَضْعُ ذاكَ وَضْعُ التَّالِي' and 'رَفْعُ تالٍ بِرَفْعِ أَوَّلَى'.
3
Identify the invalid converse moods
The converses are: affirming the consequent (to infer the antecedent) and denying the antecedent (to infer the denial of the consequent). The text says these do not necessitate a conclusion ('لا يَلْزَمُ في عَكْسِهِمَا'). Recognize that these are formal fallacies.
4
Examine the material condition for validity
The commentator explains that if the consequent is not broader but equal (mutasāwiyan) to the antecedent, then the converse moods do become valid: affirming the consequent entails the antecedent, and denying the antecedent entails the denial of the consequent. This is due to the matter (al-mādda), not the form.
Co-extensiveness means every instance of the antecedent is an instance of the consequent and vice versa.
5
Distinguish form from matter
The form of the syllogism (ṣūrat al-dalīl) remains the same; the change in validity is due to the material relationship (extension) of the terms. This distinction is critical: the same formal argument can be valid or invalid depending on the matter.

Core Idea
Not every 'either-or' works the same — the type of disjunction dictates which direction of inference is valid.

In this passage, the author introduces the disjunctive syllogism (al-qiyās al-munfaṣil) after briefly defining qiyās al-khalf. 1
Identify the three types of disjunctive propositions
The author lists three kinds: (1) real disjunctive (al-ḥaqīqī) – both prevents conjunction and prevents void; (2) conjunction-preventing (māniʿ al-jamʿ) – only prevents both being true; (3) void-preventing (māniʿ al-khulūw) – only prevents both being false.

Always check whether the alternatives are truly exhaustive and exclusive.

So understand the address, such as making the accidental (al-'aradi) as the essential (al-dhati), or making the conclusion one of the premises, and judging the genus by the judgment of the species, and your making the certain (al-qat'i) the non-certain (ghayr al-qat'i). And the second [kind] is such as departing from its figures and abandoning the condition of production from its completion, that being fulfilled. And his saying 'such as making what has...' is in the language of restriction in the six names; and 'being taken' is a specification (tamiz) like the lām in 'for the genus' (li-l-jins) in the sense of 'ala. And his saying 'such as the certain, the non-certain' contains an added qualifier (fasl mudaf) resembling a verb with the object of the genitive, and it occurs in poetry and prose. As for prose: his saying, upon him be peace, 'Will you leave my companion to me?' (1). And as for poetry: such as the poet's saying: 'You have advanced entirely, for you are accustomed in battle to endurance, by which every one who opposes you is set ablaze with fires' (2). The error. And the pronoun in our saying 'from its completion' refers to the second division, which is form. And peace.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and illustration of the two kinds of error that corrupt a demonstration (burhan). The author has already discussed errors in matter (madda); here he turns to errors in form (sura). An error in form occurs when the syllogism departs from the four figures of the categorical syllogism, either actually or potentially, or when one of the conditions of productive inference fails, as previously explained. The author then returns to the verse he cited earlier—'And the error of the demonstration, wherever the two are found, is in matter or form'—to unpack its two halves. The first half, 'in matter,' covers errors in wording (lafz) and in meaning (ma'na). In wording, this includes equivocation (ishtirak), where a term is used with more than one sense, and taking a word that belongs to a different category, such as treating the rhyme-word (radif) as a logical term (makhduh). In meaning, it includes confusing the false with the true, such as treating an accidental attribute ('aradi) as essential (dhati), or committing the fallacy of petitio principii by making the conclusion one of the premises, or judging the genus by the judgment properly due to the species, or treating a non-certain premise as if it were certain (qati'). The author then adds grammatical notes: the phrase 'ka-j'ali dha' is in the language of restriction among the six declinable nouns, and 'ma'khudhan' functions as a specification (tamiz), like the lām in 'li-l-jins' meaning 'ala. The phrase 'al-qati' ghayr al-qati'' contains an added qualifier (fasl mudaf) that behaves like a verb with a genitive object, and this construction appears in both poetry and prose. For prose he cites the hadith, 'Will you leave my companion to me?' For poetry he cites a line about endurance in battle. Finally, he notes that the pronoun in 'from its completion' refers back to the second division, which is form, not matter. This closes the discussion of formal error.
