Mapping the Principles of Concepts
Author enumerates the chapters of the first section on concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to map the sequential structure of the principles of concepts and explain why each chapter's topic is placed where it is.

Core Idea
The principles of concepts progress from epistemology to language to logical distinctions.

This lesson walks you through the author's table of contents for the first major section on concepts (al-tasawwurat). By seeing the order he chose — from acquired knowledge, through signification, to the universal and particular — you will grasp the logical architecture that underlies the entire study of definition, description, and ultimately judgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main blocks
Read through the chapter headings. Notice that they fall into three groups: epistemology (types of acquired knowledge), language and semantics (conventional signification, discussions of expressions, relation of expressions to meanings), and logical categories (the whole and the part, universality and particularity).
This tripartite division is common in classical logic manuals.
2
Note the two notices (tanbih)
Two 'notice' entries appear after the first chapter. These likely alert the reader to important distinctions or clarifications that are necessary before proceeding to language.
Tanbih often signals a subtle point or warning.
3
Trace the logical progression
Why does the author start with acquired knowledge? Because concepts are the building blocks of all knowledge. Then he moves to how we express concepts in language (signification), then the relation of expressions to meanings, and finally the foundational logical categories (whole/part, universal/particular). This progression shows that definitions rely on understanding parts and universals.
The order is pedagogical: first what we know, then how we say it, then the logical components.
4
Explain the placement of the last chapter
The chapter on 'the whole and the part, universality and particularity' comes last. This is deliberate: after establishing how expressions signify meanings, you need to understand how concepts can be universal or particular, whole or part, because definitions are composed of genus (whole/universal) and differentia (part/particular).
This prepares the ground for the coming sections on definition and description.

Text opens a new chapter on semantics and verbal signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the relation between words and meanings is a prerequisite for logical analysis.

Core Idea
Logic begins with how words signify meanings.

This brief chapter heading marks a critical pivot in the study of logic. Before you can evaluate definitions, arguments, or fallacies, you must understand how verbal expressions (alfāẓ) relate to the concepts (maʿānī) they convey. Without this foundation, the entire logical enterprise rests on unexamined assumptions about language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Chapter Subject
Read the heading: 'فصل في نسبة الألفاظ للمعاني'. The term 'nisba' indicates a relation. Ask yourself: what two things are being related?
The subject is the semantic link between words and meanings.
2
Recall Why This Matters for Logic
Logic operates on propositions, which are composed of words that carry meanings. If the word-meaning relation is ambiguous, logical operations become unreliable. This chapter sets the stage for a clear understanding of signification.
This is a foundational step before any definition or argument.
3
Connect to Islamic Sciences
Think of Usul al-Fiqh, where the correct interpretation of shar'i texts depends on how words signify rulings (e.g., amr vs. nadb). The logician's tools help jurists avoid misinterpretation.
The same relation is at work in Qur'anic exegesis (tafsir).

The Division of Knowledge: Conception and Assent
Author introduces the chapter on conceptions and maps logic's structure around knowledge, definitions, and demonstrations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two divisions of knowledge in Islamic logic and explain why conception precedes assent.

Core Idea
Knowledge is conception or assent; assent always rests on conception.

In the opening of this chapter, the author defines the very foundation of logic: knowledge (al-ilm) is divided into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). He emphasizes that assent is naturally preceded by conception, so the study of conception comes first. This lesson will help you map this fundamental division and understand why it matters for all subsequent logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions of knowledge
Find the sentence where the author says 'al-ilm tasawwur aw tasdiq'. Notice that knowledge is either pure conception or assent that includes conception.
2
Determine the natural order
The author states that tasdiq is 'mashuq bi al-tasawwur tab'an' — naturally preceded by conception. Why? Because you cannot affirm or deny something without first grasping its meaning.
3
Apply to an example
Consider the statement 'Alcohol is forbidden.' The conception of 'alcohol' and 'forbidden' must occur before you can assent to the proposition. This demonstrates the logical priority of tasawwur.

The Instruments of Logic: Definitions and Demonstrations
Author introduces the chapter on conceptions and maps logic's structure around knowledge, definitions, and demonstrations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the instrument of conception (definition) and the instrument of assent (demonstration) and map them to the two parts of knowledge.

Core Idea
Definitions give conception, demonstrations give assent—these are the two essential tools of logical reasoning.

The author draws a clear parallel: just as conception is reached through the 'explaining phrase' (al-qawl al-sharih) which is definitions, assent is reached through 'proof' (al-hujja) which is demonstrations. This lesson will help you see how every logical operation aims at either defining or proving, and why both are necessary.

Guided Path
1
Locate the parallel structure
Find the sentence: 'La yatawasalu ila al-tasawwur illa bi al-qawl al-sharih wa huwa al-hudud, kama annahu la yatawasalu ila al-tasdiq illa bi al-hujja wa hiya al-baraheen.' Notice the symmetry.
2
Identify the instrument for tasawwur
The instrument is al-qawl al-sharih, explicitly identified as al-hudud (definitions). Conception is the product.
3
Identify the instrument for tasdiq
The instrument is al-hujja (proof), identified as al-baraheen (demonstrations). Assent is the product.
4
Contrast the two
Definitions produce understanding of terms; demonstrations produce certainty about propositions. One without the other is incomplete.

The Threefold Analysis of Definitions: Form, Matter, and End
Author introduces the chapter on conceptions and maps logic's structure around knowledge, definitions, and demonstrations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the matter (maddah) and end (ghayah) of definitions as specified in this passage and connect them to the five universals and the goal of knowing definitions.

Core Idea
Definitions have matter (the five universals), end (knowledge of definitions), and form (composition).

After introducing the instruments, the author notes that definitions and demonstrations each have a form, matter, and end. The matter is already known: the five universals. The end is the very knowledge of definitions. The form—how to compose a definition—is the subject of the current chapter. This lesson focuses on understanding this threefold structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three aspects
Find the sentence: 'thumma hinaka al-hudud wa al-baraheen laha surah wa maddah wa ghayah.' These are the three aspects.

Determine the matter (maddah)
The author says: 'famaddatuha ma'rifat al-kulliyyat al-khams' — the matter is knowledge of the five universals. This is the raw material from which definitions are built.
3
Determine the end (ghayah)
The author says: 'wa ghayatuha ma'rifat al-hudud' — the end is knowledge of the definitions themselves. The purpose of studying definitions is to produce correct definitions.
4
Recognize the form (surah)
The form is the composition—how to properly structure a definition. The author says this chapter (this part of the book) is dedicated to the form ('wa ha nahnu natakallam 'ala suratihi wa kayfiyyat tarkibihi').

The Division of Knowledge: Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author explains the purpose of logic and divides knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and identify their respective logical tools.

Core Idea
All knowledge is either conception or assent; conception through definitions, assent through demonstrations.

In this opening passage, the author establishes the fundamental division of knowledge into tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). This is the bedrock of Islamic logic. You will learn that every act of knowing falls into one of these two categories, and that each has a specific tool: definitions for conception, and demonstrations for assent. Understanding this division is crucial before diving into the structure of definitions and proofs.

Guided Path

Commentary
This passage is a prefatory apologia and spiritual lament in which the author frames the composition of his work within a moral and eschatological critique of his own age. He begins by establishing that leniency toward the beginner is a rational obligation, citing the beginner's limited intellect and shallow penetration into knowledge. On that basis, he grants permission to any qualified reader who finds a flaw in the work to correct it after careful reflection. He then inserts a poetic line warning that sound statements are often distorted by unsound understanding, using this to ask his reader for a charitable reading. The target of his real warning, however, is not the beginner but the 'devils among students'—those who, lacking fairness and piety, invert truth by branding what is correct as flawed and what is flawed as correct. To anchor this ethical standard, he cites the Prophetic warning that belittling a Muslim brother is a form of evil sufficient for the believer, couples it with the proverb that a constricted chest leads to a loosened tongue, and reminds the reader that truth is not validated by the status of its speaker; even a shepherd's word must be accepted if it is true. Having set the ethical ground rules, the author turns to a social diagnosis: if beginners deserved excuse in earlier times, the present age is far more deserving of indulgence because it is an age in which the major scholars have perished, leaving only the dregs, while ignorance has overwhelmed hearts and knowledge itself teeters on extinction. He anticipates the objection that he himself is unqualified to write, and answers that his daring springs from optimism and hope in Allah for success in the sciences. At this point he pauses to define the phrase 'the tenth of centuries' as reckoned from the Hijri era. He enumerates the disputed lengths of a century—eight opinions spanning decades, then one hundred years, then one hundred and ten, then up to one hundred and twenty—identifying the hundred-year span as his intended meaning, and identifying this tenth century as his own. He then paints a grim portrait of that century: tribulations, intensifying severity, domination of disbelievers, spread of injustice, multiplication of the wicked, and a populace that has abandoned the paths of guidance. They race after their lusts toward the abyss, neglect death and the afterlife, and labor a lifetime for an hour's worldly benefit while squandering eternal benefit. The author laments that if only such a sleeper would awake and look with the eye of his heart, he would rush to obedience and the Sunna; instead, multiplied sins, hardened hearts, and manifest defects have caused their Lord to forsake them, leaving them impervious to admonition. He contrasts the pre-Islamic worship of idols with the present worship of Satan, seeing in both the spread of evil presaging the Great Signs. The passage then shifts into an extended supplication: he asks Allah for success in what He loves, protection from making desire a god, companionship with the righteous, and help in an age where the suns of truth are eclipsed, the darkness of falsehood spreads, and the smoke of caprice blocks the horizon. He observes that people grieve over losing a grain's weight of worldly matter, their hearts turbulent and changed, yet they ignore the loss of hereafterly good to which the entire world cannot compare. He identifies this as the sign of divine forsakenness and loss. Finally, he anchors the entire description in the hadith that near the Hour nothing will remain of Islam but its name and nothing of the Qur'an but its writing, and closes with a prayer for steadfastness upon the Sunna and eternal blessings upon the Prophet and his companions.

The Tool of Reflecting on Death (Hadim al-Ladhat)
Author delivers a concluding sermon warning against heedlessness and worldly distraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the concept of 'hadim al-ladhat' (the destroyer of pleasures) as a tool to diagnose spiritual heedlessness in yourself and others.

Core Idea
Reflecting on death breaks the illusion of permanence and reorients the soul toward the Aftermath.

The author of this sermon opens by noting that people fail to reflect on 'the destroyer of pleasures' (death). This failure is the root cause of heedlessness. In Islamic ethics, the constant remembrance of death is a trained discipline that cuts through worldly distraction. This lesson examines how the author deploys this tool as a diagnostic for spiritual health.

Guided Path
1
Identify the diagnostic tool
The phrase 'هادِم اللذات' (hadim al-ladhat) is used by the author as a specific reference to death. Note that it is not just any death, but the destroyer of all pleasures—highlighting its universal and definitive nature.
In hadith, the Prophet ﷺ said 'kafā bi-l-mawti wā'iẓan' (sufficient is death as a reminder).
2
Understand the failure
The author says 'ليس له متفكر في هادم اللذات' — meaning the heedless person has no thinker/reflector on death. That is the symptom: absence of reflection on death. This leads to acting as if immortal ('ka'annahum fi al-dunya mukhalledun').
The contrast is with those who 'look with the eye of the heart' and think about the consequence.
3
Apply the diagnosis to oneself
Ask: 'Do I regularly reflect on death? Do I plan my life as if I will live forever?' The author's description of the heedless person serves as a mirror. The tool is self-diagnostic. If you find yourself behaving as if immortal, you are in the category of the heedless.
This is not mere guilt but a call to action.
4
Prescribe the remedy
The author says 'if this sleeper were to awaken and think, he would hasten to obedience.' Regular reflection on death is a spiritual practice that shifts priorities. The author links this to following the Sunnah and the Jama'ah.

Diagnosing Spiritual Disease: Hardness of Heart and Divine Abandonment
Author delivers a concluding sermon warning against heedlessness and worldly distraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the symptoms of spiritual disease (hardening of the heart, divine abandonment) described by the author and use them to self-assess.

Core Idea
Multiplying sins hardens the heart, leading to divine abandonment—a downward spiral that renders admonition ineffective.

The author describes a chain: increased sins → hardened heart → appearance of one's defect → divine abandonment → benefitlessness of advice. This is a clinical description of spiritual pathology. In Islamic intellectual tradition, the heart's hardness is not just a metaphor but a real state that can be diagnosed through specific signs. This lesson will help you map those signs.

Guided Path
1
Locate the chain in the Arabic
Find the sequence: 'كثر ذنبه وقسا قلبه وظهر عيبه فخذله ربه فلم تنفع فيه موعظة'. The author presents five stages: (1) multitude of sins, (2) hardness of heart, (3) appearance of defect, (4) divine abandonment, (5) ineffectiveness of admonition.
Each stage is a symptom and a cause of the next.
2
Understand each stage
Sins accumulate when one neglects self-accounting. The heart becomes hard, losing sensitivity to sins. The inner defect (hypocrisy, pride) becomes visible. Allah withdraws His support, leaving the person to his own devices. Even the best advice no longer moves the person.
This is a progressive disease. The tool is to check if any stage is present in oneself.
3
Self-diagnose using the stages
Ask: 'Do I feel remorse after sinning? Do I find it easy to commit sins? Do I find sermons boring or irritating? Do I feel abandoned in my efforts?' The presence of these signs indicates the need for urgent treatment.
The remedy is sincere repentance (tawba) and seeking Allah's mercy before it is too late.
4
Compare with the culmination: worshipping desires
The author says people of this time are 'abidat al-shaytan (worshippers of Satan). This is the culmination of the disease: one's desires become one's god. The Qur'an says: 'Have you seen the one who takes his desire as his god?' (25:43).
The tool helps to recognize when one has reached this stage.

The Hadith of the End Times: Name of Islam, Drawing of the Quran
Author delivers a concluding sermon warning against heedlessness and worldly distraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the hadith about the persistence of only the name/outward form of Islam and the Quran as a diagnostic tool for the state of the Ummah in any age.

Core Idea
The end times will bring a hollow Islam—the name remains but the substance is lost.

The author cites a well-known hadith: 'There will remain of Islam nothing but its name, and of the Quran nothing but its drawing.' He applies it to his own time. This hadith is a tool for assessing the depth of religious practice in any era. The lesson will show you how to analyze the gap between outward form and inner reality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith in the passage
The author quotes: 'لا يبقى من الإسلام إلا اسمه ولا من القرآن إلا رسمه'. Understand the two parts: Islam reduced to a name (people claim to be Muslims but do not practice its essence), and the Quran reduced to its written form (people recite but do not act or understand).
This hadith is found in various collections, e.g., al-Bayhaqi.
2
Analyze the author's application
The author says 'زماننا هذا هو الذي قال فيه' — our time is that time. He applies the hadith as a diagnostic label, seeing the signs: heedlessness, desire-worship, and outward form without inner spirit.
This is a serious claim; one must be careful not to generalize, but the tool can be used to assess trends.
3
Use the tool for critique
When you see Muslims who call themselves Muslims but whose actions contradict the Shari'a, or who have the Quran in their homes but do not implement it, the hadith provides a framework for understanding this as a prophetic prediction.
It is not a reason for despair but a call to action and a reminder to preserve the essence.
4
Differentiate from fatalism
The hadith warns of a future reality, but it is not a deterministic fate. The tool is to motivate believers to be among those who uphold the substance, not just the name. The author concludes with a prayer for following the Sunnah.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding sermon that frames the work with a warning against spiritual heedlessness and attachment to the ephemeral world. The author begins by describing people who have turned away from the highest ranks of obedience, racing instead toward their base desires. He warns that this race leads only to falling into the depths of destructive pitfalls and the worst evils. He then notes their failure to reflect upon death—the destroyer of pleasures—or to prepare for the standing after death, behaving as if they were immortal in this world despite being witnesses to the extinction of others. The author sharpens the critique by observing that a person may spend his entire life in service to a benefit that lasts but an hour, while wasting the benefit of eternity; he exclaims at the hideousness of such a waste. He then presents the remedy: if such a sleeper were to awaken, look with the eye of his heart, and consider the consequence of his affair, he would rush to obedience and occupy himself with the Sunnah and the Jama'ah. However, the author explains that the multiplication of sins hardens the heart, exposes the defect within the servant, and leads to divine abandonment, such that neither admonition nor counsel benefits him anymore. The author then draws a devastating parallel: if the people of earlier times worshipped idols, the people of the present time worship their own caprice and Satan. He attributes the spread of evil to the nearness of the approach of the Great Signs of the Hour. The sermon then shifts to supplication, asking Allah for success in what He loves, protection from making one's desire a god, and gathering with the allies of Allah. He describes the age as one of eclipsed truth and widespread falsehood, where the smoke of caprice blocks the horizon. He laments that people grieve over losing the weight of a grain from worldly life while remaining utterly indifferent to the loss of Hereafterly good that dwarfs the entire world. He identifies this imbalance as a clear sign of divine abandonment and loss. Finally, he cites the well-known hadith describing a time when nothing remains of Islam but its name and nothing of the Quran but its outward drawing, identifying his own age as that time, and closes with prayers for following the Sunnah and blessings upon the Prophet and his companions.

Distinguishing Editor's Front Matter from Classical Text
Editor lists bibliography and table of contents for the edition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the modern editor’s contributions (introduction, bibliography, methodology) and the classical author’s original text in a printed edition of a mantiq work.

Core Idea
The front matter is the editor's scaffolding, not the author's words.

When you first open a modern critical edition of al-Sullam al-Munawraq, you encounter pages of editorial material: a biography of al-Akhdarī, a list of sources, a description of the editor’s methodology, and a detailed table of contents. This lesson helps you recognize what that material is and why it’s there, so you can quickly locate the actual text of al-Sullam and use the editor’s apparatus responsibly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the editor’s introduction
Look for the section titled 'مقدمة المحقق' (Editor’s Introduction). It typically includes the author’s biography, sources, and methodology. These are not part of the classical text.
2
Locate the table of contents
Find 'مسرد المحتويات' (Table of Contents). Notice that it lists both editorial sections (like the editor’s methodology) and sections of the classical text itself (like 'تعريف علم المنطق').
3
Find where the classical text begins
Search for the heading 'النص' (The Text). Everything after this is the actual work of al-Akhdarī. The preceding pages are the editor’s additions.
Classical manuscripts rarely had such a detailed table of contents; this is a modern editorial convenience.

Navigating the Structure of a Mantiq Text
Critical edition's table of contents outlining the book's structure and topics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two major divisions of a classical mantiq text (tasawwurat and tasdiqat) and locate the main sections within each.

Core Idea
Every mantiq textbook is built from the same blueprint: conceptions, assents, syllogism, five arts.

A table of contents may seem dry, but it reveals the entire logical system in miniature. The critical edition of al-Sullam al-Murawnaq opens with a clear division: everything that belongs to mental conceptions (tasawwurat) is separated from everything that belongs to assents (tasdiqat). Knowing this map will help you find your way through any classical logic text.

Guided Path
1
Spot the two big headings
Look for 'مبادئ التصورات' (Principles of Conceptions) and 'مبادئ التصديقات' (Principles of Assents). These are the two main pillars of the entire science of logic in the classical tradition.
Notice that 'مقاصد التصورات' and 'مقاصد التصديقات' appear later as the objectives under each pillar.
2
Examine the Conceptions section
Under 'مبادئ التصورات', find the chapters on types of acquired knowledge, conventional signification, words (alfaz), and the relation of words to meanings. Then under 'مقاصد التصورات', find chapters on universals (kulliyat) and definitions (mu'arrafat).
This is where terms and concepts are defined before any judgments are made.
3
Examine the Assents section
Under 'مبادئ التصديقات', look for chapters on propositions (qadaya), contradiction (tanaqud), and conversion ('aks). Then under 'مقاصد التصديقات', find the syllogism (qiyas) divided into categorical (iqtirani) and conditional (istithna'i).
This is where truth judgments and arguments are built.
4
Identify the final section on proof
After the syllogism, locate 'أقسام الحجة' (Types of Proof). It subdivides into rational proof (hujja 'aqliyya), rhetoric (khataba), poetry (shi'r), and dialectic (jadal).
These are the five arts of logic, showing how logic branches into different argumentative forms.

Mapping the Sullam: Two Divisions of Logic
Table of contents listing structure of Sullam al-Uluum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two major divisions of logic in the Sullam al-Uluum and list the key chapters under each.

Core Idea
Memorizing the book's map is the first step to mastering logical terrain.

This table of contents is not merely a reference; it is a pedagogical skeleton. The Sullam al-Uluum divides logic into two overarching sections: the purposes of conceptions (maqasid al-tasawwurat) and the purposes of assents (maqasid al-tasdiqat). Before diving into individual arguments, a student must internalize this structure to see how each topic fits into the whole. The following steps will guide you through the blueprint of the text.

Guided Path
1
Find the two main divisions
Look at the source Arabic under 'الموضوع'. You'll see 'مقاصد التصورات' and 'مقاصد التصديقات' clearly marked. These are the two pillars of the entire work.
In earlier texts like Isaghuji, these were simply 'tasawwur' and 'tasdiq'; here they are expanded into sections with multiple chapters.
2
List chapters under conceptions
Under مقاصد التصورات, the table shows chapters on 'الكل والكلية والجزء والجزئية' (the universal, part, etc.) and 'المعرفات' (cognitions/definitions). These deal with simple concepts and the five universals.
Remember: conceptions are about forming ideas, not judging them true or false.
3
List chapters under assents
Under مقاصد التصديقات, you'll find 'القضايا وأحكامها' (propositions and their rulings), 'التناقض' (contradiction), 'العكس المستوي' (conversion), and the detailed sections on 'القياس الاقتراني' (categorical syllogism) with its four figures, then 'الاستثنائي' (exceptive/hypothetical syllogism), and 'أقسام الحجة' (divisions of proof).
Assents are about arriving at certainty through reasoning, hence syllogisms are central here.
4
Note the appendices and indices
After the main chapters, the table lists 'لواحق القياس' (appendages of the syllogism) and 'أقسام الحجة' which includes rational proof, rhetoric, poetry, dialectic, and sophistry. Finally, several analytical indices (glossaries of terms, verses, hadiths, etc.) are provided.
These back matter tools are crucial for reference; they reflect the comprehensive nature of the text.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents and index listing for the latter portion of the Sullam al-Uluum. It enumerates the remaining chapters and their corresponding page numbers, followed by analytical indices. The author organizes the material under the two major divisions of logic: the purposes of conceptions (maqasid al-tasawwurat) and the purposes of assents (maqasid al-tasdiqat). Under conceptions, we find chapters on the five universals (all, universality, part, particularity) and on cognitions (ma'rifat). Under assents, the author lists chapters on propositions and their rulings, contradiction (tanaqud), the contrary conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi), the categorical syllogism (al-qiyas al-iqtirani) and its figures, the conditions for productivity in each of the four figures, the exceptive syllogism (al-istithna'i), appendages of the syllogism (lawahiq al-qiyas), and the divisions of proof (aqsam al-hujja) which include rational proof, rhetoric, poetry, dialectic, and sophistry. The page numbers are heavily corrupted in the OCR, showing mixed Arabic and Latin numerals with many errors. The second page lists the back matter: a conclusion (khatima), followed by analytical indices covering logical terminology, Quranic verses, hadiths, sects and schools, notable figures, sources and references, and a general contents index. This structural overview serves as a memorization anchor for the student to map the entire terrain of the work before and after studying its details.
