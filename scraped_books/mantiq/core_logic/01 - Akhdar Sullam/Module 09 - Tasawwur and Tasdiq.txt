Acquired Knowledge: Conception and Assent
Author divides acquired knowledge into conception and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conception (tasawwur) from assent (tasdiq) as the two species of acquired knowledge, and identify the broader genus of perception (idrak) that contains them.

Core Idea
Acquired knowledge is only two: grasping a single meaning or affirming a relation.

This passage lays the foundational classification of all human knowledge in Islamic logic. Every piece of information we acquire is either a simple concept (like 'prayer' or 'wine') or a judgment that ties concepts together (like 'prayer is obligatory'). Understanding this division is essential before studying definitions, proofs, or fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two species
Find in the Arabic text the phrase 'Idrak mufrad tasawwuran, wa idrak nisba bi-tasdiq'. This directly states the two types of acquired knowledge.
2
Define conception
From the footnote, conception is the occurrence of the image of a thing in the intellect without any judgment. Example: understanding what 'khamr' means without affirming it is forbidden.
The passage notes that conception is prior by nature.
3
Define assent
Assent is perception of a relation with verification—you affirm or deny something about a concept. Example: 'khamr is forbidden' is an assent.
4
Identify the broader genus
The author defines perception (idrak) as the soul's arrival at a meaning, and then splits it into the same two types, showing that both conception and assent are species of perception.
Perception is more general: it includes both simple and relational cognition.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies. The author opens the chapter on the principles of conceptions by dividing acquired knowledge into two fundamental types: conception (taṣawwur) and assent (taṣdīq). Conception is defined as the perception of a single thing, which the footnote clarifies as the occurrence of the image of a thing in the intellect without any judgment of negation or affirmation. For example, when the mind grasps the meaning of “Allah” or “prayer” (ṣalāh) or “wine” (khamr), this is mere conception. Assent, by contrast, is the perception of a relation with verification, such as when the mind affirms that every intoxicant is forbidden or that the world is temporally originated. The author then explains the order: conception is placed first in the text because it is prior by nature; one must first grasp what a thing is before one can affirm or deny anything about it. The specification “acquired” (ḥādith) is added to exclude eternal knowledge, meaning God’s knowledge, which is not subject to necessity or contemplation. Finally, the author defines perception (idrāk) broadly as the soul’s arrival at a meaning in its completeness, whether that arrival occurs through a relation or not, and he mirrors the previous division by splitting perception itself into perception of a single thing and perception of a relation. The footnotes anchor these definitions in the wider tradition, citing al-Jurjani’s al-Taʿrīfāt and the editor’s own manuscript of Saʿīd Qudūra’s commentary.

Key Terms
العلم الحادث (al-ʿilm al-ḥādith): Acquired knowledge that is divided into conception and assent; specified as ḥādith to exclude eternal knowledge, which is neither necessary nor contemplative for its possessor. [Note: In kalām, ḥādith usually means temporally originated; here in mantiq it specifically signals human, acquired cognition as opposed to God’s eternal knowledge.]
التصور (al-taṣawwur): Perception of a single thing; the occurrence of the image of a thing in the intellect and grasping its quiddity without any judgment of negation or affirmation. [Note: Stable across the mantiq tradition since al-Fārābī and Ibn Sīnā; Ibn Taymiyya later critiques the dichotomy polemically but does not alter the standard definition.]
التصديق (al-taṣdīq): Perception of a relation with assent; relating the truth to the informant by testing or verifying the truth of the report. [Note: No significant shift — this term is stable across the tradition as the affirming judgment corresponding to taṣawwur.]
الإدراك (al-idrāk): The soul’s arrival at the meaning in its entirety, whether through a relation or otherwise; the broader genus under which both conception and assent fall. [Note: In later psychological texts idrāk is sometimes restricted to sense-perception, but in mantiq it retains this broader cognitive meaning.]

Conception and Assent: The Two Modes of Acquired Knowledge
Author divides acquired knowledge into conception and assent, defining apprehension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) by applying the definition of apprehension (idrak) and explaining the priority of conception.

Core Idea
Acquired knowledge splits into conception and assent; conception is prior by nature.

This passage lays the foundation for all later logical analysis by dividing acquired knowledge into its two fundamental modes. Understanding the difference between simply grasping a meaning (tasawwur) and affirming a relationship (tasdiq) is essential before studying definitions, arguments, and proofs. The author also clarifies why 'acquired knowledge' excludes eternal knowledge, and why conception is taught first.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions of apprehension
Apprehension (idrak) is divided into apprehension of a singular (conception) and apprehension of a relation (assent). Locate this explicit division in the Arabic.
The term 'idrak' is defined as the soul's arrival at meaning in its completeness.
2
Extract the definition of conception
Conception (tasawwur) is defined as 'the occurrence of the form of the thing in the mind'. Note that it is a singular apprehension without judgment.
Contrast with assent, which involves a relational judgment (e.g., affirmation or negation).
3
Understand the priority of conception
The author states that conception is placed first in the curriculum because it is prior by nature (بِالطَّبْعِ). This means that you must first conceive the meanings of terms before you can make judgments about them.
Pedagogical priority reflects natural epistemic priority.
4
Analyze the scope of 'acquired knowledge'
The text specifies 'acquired knowledge’ (العلم الحادث) to exclude eternal knowledge (العلم القديم), which is not described as necessary or theoretical. This restricts the discussion to human, temporal knowledge.
This exclusion shows the author is concerned with fallible human cognition, not divine knowledge.
5
Apply the distinction to a concrete example
Take the statement 'The world is temporally originated'. The concept 'world' by itself is a conception; the judgment that it is originated is an assent. Practice identifying which part is tasawwur and which is tasdiq.
This example is from Kalam, illustrating the division in a theological context.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the basic modes of acquired knowledge. It opens by naming the two primary divisions: apprehension of a singular, which is called conception (tasawwur), and apprehension of a relation, which is called assent (tasdiq). The author then explains that conception is prior by nature, which justifies its pedagogical priority in the curriculum. Next, he clarifies the qualifier acquired (hadith) attached to knowledge; this is added to exclude eternal knowledge (al-ilm al-qadim), because eternal knowledge is not characterized as either necessary (daruri) or theoretical (nazari). The author then supplies a formal definition of apprehension (idrak): it is the soul’s arrival at a meaning in its totality, whether that meaning involves a relation or not. He notes that apprehension therefore divides into two species. He then defines conception as the occurrence of the form of a thing in the mind, illustrated by our simple grasp of a singular entity. The passage concludes with a phrase naming the first type, though the transmitted wording at this point is unclear.

The Dispute over the Nature of Assent
Author presents the khilaf between al-Imam and the hukama' on whether assent is simple or compound.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of al-Imam and the philosophers on whether assent is simple or compound, and identify the structural difference in their accounts.

Core Idea
The debate over assent is not merely verbal: it concerns whether conception is an internal part of assent or an external condition.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational dispute in Islamic logic and epistemology: the nature of assent (tasdiq). Al-Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi holds that assent includes both the apprehension of a quiddity and a judgment, making it compound. The philosophers argue that assent is merely the apprehension of a relation, with conceptions as external conditions, making it simple. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to parse definitions and recognize structural commitments in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Read the first two sentences of the Arabic passage to find the doctrines of al-Imam and the philosophers. Note the key terms: 'إدراك الماهية مع الحكم' vs 'مجرد إدراك النسبة'.
The Imam adds judgment; the philosophers restrict to relation.
2
Extract the structural elements
For the Imam, assent = apprehension of quiddity + judgment. For the philosophers, assent = mere apprehension of relation; the three conceptions (subject, predicate, relation) are external conditions (شروط), not parts.
This difference is the core of the dispute.
3
Understand the labels 'simple' and 'compound'
The philosophers call assent 'simple' because it is a single act of apprehending a relation. The Imam calls it 'compound' because it includes multiple components: the quiddity, the judgment, and the relation.
Simplicity vs complexity refers to internal composition.
4
Apply the example
Consider the proposition 'العالم حادث' (the world is temporally originated). For the philosophers, assenting to this is simply apprehending the relation of temporal origination to the world. For the Imam, it is a compound act including conceiving 'world', 'temporal origination', 'the relation', and the judgment that the relation holds.
The example makes the abstract dispute concrete.
5
Compare the two accounts
List what each side includes in the act of assent. The Imam includes conceptions of terms and the relation, plus judgment. The philosophers include only the apprehension of the relation, with conceptions as prerequisites but not part of the act itself.
This shows the structural difference is real, not verbal.

Commentary
This passage is dialectical. It turns from conception to the second type, assent (tasdiq), and immediately signals a substantive dispute. The author first presents the position of the Imam—Fakhr al-Din al-Razi in the disciplines of logic and kalam—who holds that assent consists in apprehending a quiddity together with a judgment of negation or affirmation upon it. He then presents the opposing position of the philosophers (hukama'), who hold that assent is nothing but the apprehension of the relation itself; the three conceptions required (of the two terms and the relation) are, for them, merely conditions (shurut), not internal parts. The author clarifies the formula that captures this disagreement: for the philosophers, assent is simple (basit), whereas for the Imam it is compound (murakkab). To make the debate concrete, he uses the proposition The world is temporally originated. For the philosophers, to assent to this is simply to apprehend the relation of temporal origination as belonging to the world. For the Imam, it is a complex act that includes apprehending the relation’s occurrence, conceiving the world, conceiving temporal origination, conceiving the relation itself, and then judging. The passage thus shows that the dispute is not verbal but structural: the Imam builds conception into the very composition of assent, while the philosophers keep it external.

Key Terms
التصديق (al-tasdiq): Assent; the subject of dispute. For the Imam: apprehension of a quiddity with judgment of negation or affirmation. For the philosophers: mere apprehension of a relation, with conceptions as external conditions.
البسيط (al-basit): Simple; the philosophers’ description of assent, because they hold it to be a single apprehension of relation without internal composition from conceptions.
المركب (al-murakkab): Compound; the Imam’s description of assent, because he holds it is composed of apprehending the relation plus conceiving the terms.

Classifying Assent: Certainty, Belief, Opinion, Illusion, Doubt
Author divides assent into certain and uncertain, then subdivides each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given assent as certain ('ilm or i'tiqad) or non-certain (zann, wahm, shakk) based on changeability and probability balance, using criteria from the text.

Core Idea
Certainty is not all alike; even non-certain assent has three distinct species.

In this passage, the author unpacks the concept of assent (tasdiq) by sorting it into certain and non-certain branches. Understanding these divisions is essential for evaluating the epistemic strength of propositions in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main branches
Locate where the author states that assent is either certain (jazim) or non-certain (ghayr jazim). Note that 'certain' here means assent without doubt, not necessarily eternal truth.
2
Distinguish knowledge from belief within certain assent
Look for the criterion: changeability. If the certain assent does not admit change, it is knowledge ('ilm). If it admits change (even if currently held firmly), it is belief (i'tiqad). Examine the examples: 'mountain is stone' (knowledge) vs. imitative tawhid (belief).
Belief can be correct (sahih) or false (fasid) depending on correspondence to reality.
3
Analyze the three types of non-certain assent
Read the description: non-certain assent always involves possibility (ihtimal). Distinguish opinion (zann) where one side outweighs the other, illusion (wahm) which is mere contrariety (muqabala), and doubt (shakk) where the two sides are equal.
The text identifies wahm with 'contrariety' itself, not with a psychological faculty.
4
Apply the classification to examples
Take the examples given: Mu'tazila denying ru'ya is false belief; philosopher's qidam al-alam is false belief. For non-certain, think of a case where evidence slightly favors one view (zann), where two opposing views are both possible (shakk), or where the mere opposition exists (wahm).
Use Islamic examples: zann in fiqh regarding a ruling, shakk in aqida about a non-essential matter, etc.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the three types of conventional signification (al-dalala al-wadi'iyya) recognized in the science of logic. The commentator opens by stating that this chapter treats signification grounded in human coinage (al-wad'), not natural causality, and that it comprises three types because a word either signifies the whole of its coined meaning, a part of it, or something entailed by it. He defines signification by correspondence (dalalat al-mutabaqa) as the word's standing for the complete meaning for which it was coined, illustrating this with the term insan signifying the full definition 'rational animal' and the term salat signifying the entire complex of utterances and acts bounded by takbir and salam with a specific intention. He then defines signification by inclusion (dalalat al-tadammun) as the word's standing for a part of its total meaning within the whole, so that insan may signify merely 'animal' or merely 'rational', and salat may signify 'prostration' or 'bowing' as parts within the greater whole. He emphasizes that entailment need only be mentally necessary to count as true iltizam; it does not require external co-occurrence. To demonstrate this, he offers the critical example of blindness (ama) signifying sight (basar): the mind cannot conceive blindness without reference to sight, yet they are mutually exclusive in external reality, which proves that mental necessity suffices. Finally, he notes that mutabaqa is purely conventional (naqliyya) by agreement, while regarding the other two there are multiple opinions, the third opinion being that iltizam is rational (aqliyya). He concludes that tadammun and iltizam entail mutabaqa but not vice versa, and he explicitly marks this as contrary to the view of Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi.

The Condition of Mental Necessity in Iltizam
Commentator specifies and illustrates the condition of mental necessity for iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a given signifying pair qualifies as dalalat al-iltizam by checking the condition of mental necessity.

Core Idea
Iltizam requires an intellectual bond in the mind, not just a real-world connection.

The commentator emphasizes a crucial condition for iltizam: the necessity must be mental (dhihni). This distinguishes it from mere external correlations. Understanding this sharpens your ability to identify genuine logical entailment in theological and legal reasoning.

External-only necessity: the crow's blackness, but the concept of crow does not mentally entail blackness necessarily.

Classify and confirm
If the connection is mental, it qualifies as iltizam. If not, it is either tadammun (part) or mutabaqa or nothing. Use the text's examples to practice.
The text gives 'four for evenness' (mental and external) and 'opposites' (mental only) as valid, and 'blackness for crow' as invalid.

The Relationship Among the Three Significations
Commentator discusses the entailment relationship among the three significations and mentions al-Razi's disagreement.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The commentator asserts that tadammun and iltizam necessarily imply mutabaqa, but not vice versa. This logical relationship is fundamental for understanding how significations build upon each other. Imam al-Razi disagreed, and knowing this disagreement sharpens your grasp of the issue.

For tadammun: 'human' signifies 'animal' (part). This presupposes that 'human' signifies the whole 'rational animal' (mutabaqa). Without the primary meaning, the part would not be signified.
Similarly, 'human' signifies 'capable of knowledge' (iltizam) only if the meaning of human is already established.
3
Consider the counter-opinion of al-Razi
The commentator notes that Imam al-Razi held the contrary view. Al-Razi might have thought that tadammun and iltizam can operate independently of mutabaqa, perhaps through contextual or secondary signification.
The text does not elaborate al-Razi's argument, but the disagreement highlights the complexity of the issue.
4

The commentator also states that the significations are ordered in strength: mutabaqa is strongest, then tadammun, then iltizam. This ordering relates to their origin: the primary correspondence is foundational.
Strength here refers to epistemic priority, not logical necessity.

Division of Words: Useless, Compound, and Simple
Commentator expands the matn's division with three sub-types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic word using the Mantiq division into mahmal (useless), murakkab (compound) with its two subtypes (taqiydi and khabari), and mufrad (simple) with its three types (ism, fi'l, harf/adah).

Core Idea
A word's logical role hinges on whether it is useless, compound (restrictive or declarative), or simple (noun, verb, particle).

This lesson unpacks the commentator's expansion of the matn's opening classification. Understanding this division is essential because every logical operation—defining, arguing, or judging—begins with identifying the type of word you are dealing with. The commentator divides all words into two main categories and then subdivides them further, showing how each type functions in concept-acquisition or propositional use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
Look for the two main categories: mahmal (useless, e.g., names of alphabet letters) and musta'mal (employed). The commentator first bifurcates 'word' into these two.

Subdivide musta'mal into compound and simple

This step-by-step check ensures correct logical handling.

Essential, Accidental, and Species Universals
Commentator divides the universal into essential, accidental, and intermediate species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal as essential, accidental, or species based on its relation to the essence of its particulars.

Core Idea
Universals relate to essences in three ways: as part (essential), external (accidental), or the whole (species).

The commentator expands on the classification of universals by distinguishing those that are part of the essence, those outside it, and those that equal the whole essence. Understanding this tripartite division is key to precise definitions in Islamic logic, as it clarifies the logical role of each universal in describing an entity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal and its particulars
Take a universal like 'animal' and consider its particulars, e.g., Zayd and Amr. Determine how the universal relates to the essence of these particulars.
The essence (haqiqa) is what makes something what it is.
2
Check if the universal is a part of the essence
If the universal is included as a constituent of the essence, it is essential (dhati). For example, 'animal' is part of Zayd's essence because Zayd is an animal.
Essential universals are like genus or differentia.
3
Check if the universal is external to the essence
If the universal is not part of the essence but attaches to it externally, it is accidental (aradi). For example, 'writer' describes Zayd but is not part of his essence.
Accidental universals include qualities, actions, etc.
4
Check if the universal expresses the totality of the essence
If the universal comprises the entire essence (genus + differentia), it is neither essential nor accidental but intermediate (wasita) or species (naw'). For example, 'man' equals the whole essence of Zayd (animality + rationality).
The species is the complete definition of the individual.

3
Examine tashakkuk and its two subtypes
Tashakkuk is gradation, divided into priority (some individuals more deserving of the label, like existence in the Necessary Being vs. the possible) and intensity (the meaning occurs more strongly in some, again using existence as example).
In kalam, existence is said of God and creation with a priority that reflects ontological dependence.
4
Contrast tabayn (total divergence) with tashakkuk
Tabayn means two meanings are mutually exclusive across all individuals. The example is human and horse: no individual is both. This is unlike tashakkuk, where individuals share the meaning but differ in degree.
In fiqh, 'prayer' and 'fasting' are tabayn: no act is both a prayer and a fasting.
5
Analyze the pair ishtirak and taraduf as opposites
Ishtirak: one word posited for many meanings by many acts of positing, e.g., 'ayn (eye, spring, essence). Taraduf: many words for one meaning, e.g., different words for lion. The author clarifies that 'many' in ishtirak opposes unity, not fewness.
This precision prevents misreading ishtirak as requiring a large number of meanings.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional enumeration in which the author sets out the five semantic relations between words and their meanings. He begins by insisting that these relations are exactly five, neither more nor less, and lists them in order: tawatuʾ, tashakkuk, tabayn, ishtirak, and taraduf. He then turns to each definition in sequence. Tawatuʾ is explained as the equal application of a word’s meaning to every individual in its extension without any variation, using ‘human’ as the example, since every individual instance is equally characterized by the notes of animality and rationality. Next, tashakkuk is divided into two modes: priority, where individuals differ in precedence, as when ‘existence’ is said more completely and more strongly of the Necessary Being than of the merely possible; and intensity, where the meaning occurs more forcefully in some individuals than in others, again using existence as the illustration. Third, total tabayn is defined as a relation between two meanings such that neither meaning extends to any individual falling under the other, exemplified by ‘human’ and ‘horse,’ which are mutually exclusive across their entire extensions. Fourth, the mushtarak is defined as a term posited for many meanings through many distinct acts of positing, like ʿayn in its sharing among meanings; the author adds the precision that ‘many’ here means what opposes unity, not what opposes fewness, thereby blocking the misinterpretation that a mere handful of senses would suffice. Finally, taraduf is defined as the multiplication of words alongside the unification of meaning, using various names for lion as examples; the author stresses that taraduf is the true opposite of ishtirak, because homonymy keeps the word single while multiplying the meaning, whereas synonymy keeps the meaning single while multiplying the words.

The Five Relations of Universal to Meaning
Author enumerates five relations between universal terms and their meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five relations (univocity, analogy, diversity, synonymy, equivocity) in any given term and explain the logical significance of each.

Core Idea
The five relations govern how terms map to meanings, and misidentifying them leads to flawed definitions and invalid arguments.

This foundational lesson introduces the five ways a universal term can relate to its meaning. The author of al-Sullam al-Murawnaq presents them in a clear enumeration, then elaborates with key examples from the Islamic intellectual tradition—such as the term 'existence' (wujūd) applied to God and creation. Mastering these five categories is essential for anyone who wishes to reason precisely about definitions, classifications, and predication in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the fivefold classification
The passage states that the relation of the universal to its meaning is divided into five types. List them: tawāṭuʾ, tashākkuk, takhāluf, tarāduf, ishtirāk. Notice that the first two concern how equally the meaning applies to individuals, while the last three deal with multiplicity of words or meanings.
The author’s order is intentional: from pure equality (tawāṭuʾ) to graded analogy (tashākkuk) to complete diversity (takhāluf), then to the purely word-level relations of synonymy and equivocity.
2
Distinguish tawāṭuʾ from tashākkuk
Tawāṭuʾ applies when all individuals share the meaning equally, e.g., 'man' for Zayd and 'Amr. Tashākkuk applies when some individuals are more primary, e.g., 'whiteness' is more primary in snow than in ivory, or 'existence' is more primary in God than in creation. The passage also notes that a mushakkak (analogical term) can be viewed as univocal from one perspective and not from another.
The example of 'existence' (al-wujūd) is especially important in kalām: it is predicated of God and creatures analogically.
3
Understand takhāluf as complete diversity
Takhāluf occurs when both the word and the meaning are entirely different, e.g., 'man' (insān) and 'horse' (faras). The author equates it with mutual diversity (tabāyun). This is the simplest relation: no overlap in word or concept.
Do not confuse takhāluf with contradiction; here it simply means distinctness.
4
Distinguish tarāduf from ishtirāk
Tarāduf is when multiple words share one meaning, e.g., 'insān' and 'bashar' both mean human. Ishtirāk is when one word has multiple meanings, e.g., 'ʿayn' can mean eye, spring, or gold. The passage uses 'ʿayn' as the classic example of an equivocal term (mushtarak).
In uṣūl al-fiqh, identifying ishtirāk in Quranic terms is crucial for correct interpretation.
5
Apply the framework to a new example
Given a term from Islamic science, classify its relation. For instance, consider 'ṣalāt' (prayer): it is applied equally to all valid prayers? Actually, it is a univocal term (tawāṭuʾ) across instances, unless one considers the prayer of a prophet superior—then it would be tashākkuk. The point is to exercise the classification.
Use real examples from fiqh, kalām, or tafsīr to reinforce the categories.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the five relations that a universal term bears to the meaning it conveys. The author begins by listing the five: tawāṭuʾ, tashākkuk, takhāluf, ishtirāk, and tarāduf. He then explains each in turn. First, tawāṭuʾ is when a term applies equally to all of its instances, such as the word insān applied to every individual human being without any one instance being more entitled to the name than another; the author calls this univocal because the individuals of the meaning agree in it. Second, tashākkuk is when the term applies by gradation, some instances being more primary than others. The author gives two examples from the text itself: whiteness is more primary in snow than in ivory, and existence is prior in the Necessary Being than in the possible being. He then notes that this analogical relation can be viewed in two ways: from the angle of the shared origin of the meaning it resembles tawāṭuʾ, yet from the angle of the difference between instances it is not tawāṭuʾ. Third comes what the author treats under the heading of takhāluf but explains as mutual diversity: both the word and the meaning are entirely distinct, as when we compare insān and faras—one word is diverse from the other because their meanings are diverse. Fourth is tarāduf, when the meaning is one but the words are multiple, such as insān and bashar; the author explains that they are synonymous because they follow one upon the same meaning. Fifth is ishtirāk, when the word is one but the meanings are multiple, such as ʿayn which may mean eye, spring, or gold; this is called equivocal because the meaning is shared in the single word while the referents differ. The passage thus maps out how terms relate to meanings so that the student may distinguish true univocity from mere equivocity when forming definitions or arguments.
