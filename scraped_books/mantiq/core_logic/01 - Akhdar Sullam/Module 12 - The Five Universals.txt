The Five Universals: Definitions and Distinctions
Author enumerates and defines the five universals with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, accident) and explain how their relation to particulars distinguishes them.

Core Idea
Every universal relates to its particulars as totality, inherent, or external — this determines its type.

In this passage from the Sullam al-Ulum, the author lays out the five fundamental categories of universal terms in Islamic logic: genus, species, differentia, property, and accident. Each is defined by a unique pattern of predication. Understanding these five universals is essential for constructing correct definitions in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the threefold relation
The author classifies universals by whether they are the totality of particulars, inherent in them, or external to them. Find this threefold division in the text.
2
Define species (naw')
Species is the totality of its particulars. It is predicated of many differing in number (e.g., 'man' for Zayd, 'Amr). Extract this definition.
3
Distinguish genus and differentia
Both are inherent, but genus answers 'what is it?' in a shared way, while differentia answers 'what thing is it?' and distinguishes the essence. Note examples: animal (genus) and rational (differentia).
4
Contrast property and accident
Both are external to the essence. Property is predicated of many differing in reality and distinguishes among accidents (e.g., risible). Accident does not necessarily distinguish (e.g., moving).
5
Apply to a new example
Given a term like 'can walk', classify it as accident (external, not distinguishing essence) using the criteria.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the five universals (al-kulliyyat al-khams), which are the fundamental categories through which any universal term is analyzed in Islamic logic. The author begins by stating the five: genus (jins), differentia (fasl), accident ('ard), species (naw'), and property (khasa). He then explains that every universal must relate to its particulars in one of three ways: it is either the complete totality of what falls under it, or inherent in them, or external to them. The first relation gives us the species (naw'), which is predicated of many individuals differing only in number — such as 'man' predicated of Zayd, 'Amr, and Bakr — and it answers the question 'what is it?' (ma huwa?). The second relation gives us the genus (jins), which is predicated of many things differing in reality (not merely in number) and answers 'what is it?' in a state of shared commonality; the example given is 'animal' for man. The differentia (fasl) is what distinguishes the quiddity and is inherent in the essentialities; the example is 'rational' (natiq). The property (khasa) is predicated of many differing in reality and distinguishes the thing from among its accidents; the example is 'risible' (dahik). The general accident ('ard 'amm) is external to the essence and is divided into three: necessary accidents like breathing, swiftly passing accidents like the redness of shame, and slow accidents like grey hair and youth. The author thus moves from enumeration to definition to division to example, giving the student both the abstract structure and concrete instances from human nature.

The Three Types of General Accident
Author divides the general accident into three types and distinguishes property from accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between necessary, swift-passing, and slow accidents, and give Islamic examples of each.

Core Idea
Accidents are external to the essence; they are either necessary, swift, or slow — and this classification is crucial for correct definition.

After defining the general accident as one of the five universals, the author of the Sullam divides it into three types according to duration: necessary (like breathing for humans), swiftly passing (like blushing), and slow (like hair greying). This division is crucial for understanding which attributes are truly essential and which are merely accidental. It also clarifies the role of property (khasa) as a distinguishing accident-based attribute.

3
Understand 'necessary accident'
It is always found with the subject (e.g., breathing for humans). Explain why it is still an accident, not essential.
4
Understand 'swift-passing accident'
It changes quickly (e.g., redness of shame). Not persistent.
5
Understand 'slow accident'
It changes slowly (e.g., grey hair). Differentiate from swift.

The Three Ranks of Genus
Author classifies genus into highest, lowest, and intermediate ranks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the remote, proximate, and intermediate genus for any given species and explain the criterion for each rank.

Core Idea
Genus is not one thing; it comes in three ranks defined by what is above or below it.

In this passage, the author distills the hierarchical nature of genus into three clear categories. Understanding these ranks is crucial for constructing precise definitions (hadd) and for tracing the tree of universals used in Islamic logic, kalam, and fiqh. Let's see how to distinguish them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three ranks
The passage lists three divisions: remote (ba'id), proximate (qarib), and intermediate (mutawassit). Note the Arabic terms.
2
Understand the criterion for each rank
Remote: no genus above it. Proximate: no genus beneath it (for the given species). Intermediate: lies between the two.
The rank is relative to the species you are considering.
3
Apply the examples from the text
Substance (jawhar) is remote because nothing is more general. Animal (hayawan) is proximate for human because no genus separates them. Body (jism) is intermediate, between substance and animal.
Check that animal is indeed the last genus before human.
4
Test with a new example
Take 'horse'. Determine its remote, proximate, and intermediate genus using the same logic.
Horse's chain: substance → body → animal → horse. So remote=substance, intermediate=body, proximate=animal.

Five Semantic Relations Overview
Author enumerates and defines five semantic relations between terms and their meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five semantic relations (tawatu', tashakkuk, takhalluf, ishtirak, taraduf) and classify any given pair of terms into the correct relation.

Core Idea
Five relations exhaust how universal terms connect to their instances: equality, priority, difference, synonymy, or homonymy.

When we examine how a term relates to its meaning across instances, we find different patterns. Some terms apply equally to all individuals, others with strength or priority. Some terms share neither word nor meaning, while others share only the word or only the meaning. This lesson introduces the five categories from the Islamic logical tradition, using clear examples to help you recognize each relation.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether the two terms share the same word or same meaning.
First, check if the same word is used for multiple meanings, or if multiple words refer to the same meaning. This initial clue points toward ishtirak or taraduf.
2
Test for ishtirak (homonymy).
If the same word has more than one unrelated meaning (e.g., 'ayn meaning eye or spring), the relation is ishtirak. The word is mushtarak.
In fiqh, such words require contextual interpretation.
3
Test for taraduf (synonymy).
If different words share exactly the same meaning (e.g., 'insan' and 'bashar'), the relation is taraduf. The terms are mutaradifan.
True synonymy is rare; most apparent synonyms have subtle differences.
4
Test for takhalluf (total difference).

Tashakkuk: Graded Priority in Meaning
Author explains tashakkuk with examples of whiteness and existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify tashakkuk (graded priority/strength) in a term and distinguish it from tawatu' and ishtirak using the criteria of shared root meaning and inequality of instances.

Core Idea
Tashakkuk is not ambiguity—it is one meaning applied with priority or strength across instances.

Among the five relations, tashakkuk is the most subtle. At first glance, it resembles tawatu' because all individuals share the same root meaning. But upon closer inspection, some instances exhibit the meaning more strongly, more perfectly, or earlier than others. The author gives two striking examples: whiteness in snow versus ivory, and existence in the Necessary (Allah) versus the contingent. This lesson unpacks the definition and shows how this concept is used in kalam and usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared root meaning.
All instances in tashakkuk share a common meaning. For 'whiteness', both snow and ivory are white; for 'existence', both the Necessary and possible exist.
This is why tashakkuk is sometimes considered a form of tawatu' in the root sense.
2
Recognize the inequality among instances.
The instances are not equal in the degree or perfection of the meaning. Snow is whiter than ivory; the Necessary's existence is prior and more perfect than that of the possible.
Inequality can be in strength (shiddah), priority (taqaddum), or anteriority.
3
Distinguish tashakkuk from ishtirak.
In ishtirak, the same word has multiple distinct meanings that share no common core. In tashakkuk, there is a single meaning with gradation. For example, 'ayn' for eye and spring is ishtirak; 'whiteness' in snow and ivory is tashakkuk.
Do not confuse the two: tashakkuk is a single concept, ishtirak is multiple concepts.
4
Apply the distinction in theological examples.
In kalam, 'existence' (wujud) is mushakkak: Allah's existence is necessary and self-sufficient, while creatures' existence is contingent and derived. Similarly, 'knowledge' ('ilm) is mushakkak: Allah's knowledge is eternal and comprehensive, human knowledge is acquired and partial.
This concept is central to arguments for the ontological difference between Creator and creation.

Whole, Universality, Part, and Particularity
Author opens a new section on whole, universality, part, and particularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the whole (al-kull) and universality (al-kulliyya), and the part (al-juz') and particularity (al-juz'iyya), and explain why these distinctions matter for understanding propositions.

Core Idea
The whole and the part are things; universality and particularity are modes of predication.

The author now shifts from discussing utterances to analyzing the scope of terms in propositions. This section header introduces four key concepts that will ground our understanding of how terms distribute over subjects. Learning to distinguish the whole from universality, and the part from particularity, is essential for grasping the structure of categorical propositions and the logical relations among them.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the four terms
Locate the four Arabic terms in the header: الكل (al-kull), الكلية (al-kulliyya), الجزء (al-juz'), الجزئية (al-juz'iyya). Note their transliterations and that they appear in pairs: the first two relate to 'whole' and its mode, the last two to 'part' and its mode.
These are technical terms with specific logical meanings, not their everyday senses.
2
Distinguish al-kull from al-kulliyya
Al-kull refers to the whole entity itself, considered as a totality (e.g., all humans as a group). Al-kulliyya, on the other hand, is the property of a proposition that applies to every individual of that class (e.g., 'Every human is mortal'). The former is an object, the latter is a mode of predication.
This distinction prevents confusion between the thing and its scope of application.
3
Distinguish al-juz' from al-juz'iyya
Al-juz' is a part or a constituent of a whole (e.g., the hand is a part of the body). Al-juz'iyya is the property of a proposition that applies to some but not all individuals (e.g., 'Some humans are tall'). Again, one is a constituent, the other is a quantificational mode.
Part and particularity are related but not identical: a part is a subset of the whole, while particularity is a logical operator.
4
Connect to the five universals and propositions
These four concepts are foundational for the five universals (genre, species, etc.) and for the quantifiers in categorical propositions. Understanding them allows you to correctly parse statements like 'Every X is Y' (universal) vs 'Some X is Y' (particular) and to know what counts as the whole extension of a term.
This connection will become explicit in the following sections on propositions.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the fiqh ruling: 'Every intoxicant is haram.' Here, 'intoxicant' as a whole (al-kull) encompasses all intoxicating substances. The universal quantifier 'every' indicates that the ruling applies to every member (kulliyya). If one confuses the whole with universality, one might think the ruling applies to the concept rather than to each instance. Correctly distinguishing them ensures sound legal reasoning.
This shows the practical necessity of these distinctions in Islamic jurisprudence.
