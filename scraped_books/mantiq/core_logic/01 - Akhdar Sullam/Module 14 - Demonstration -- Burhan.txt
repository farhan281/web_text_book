Four Things Beyond Demonstrative Proof
Author enumerates four principles beyond demonstrative proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories that do not require demonstrative proof and explain why they are tested by critique and counter-argument instead.

Core Idea
Not every truth in Islam needs a syllogistic proof — some are upheld by convention and tested by dialectic.

In Islamic logic, burhan is the gold standard of certainty. But not every claim demands a demonstrative proof. The commentator lists four kinds of knowledge that are not established by burhan: definitions, customs, consensus, and deeply held beliefs. These are not proven by demonstration; rather, they are subjected to critique and counter-argument (naqd and mu'arada). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying logical tools in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories from the Arabic
Read the Arabic text: four things are listed: الحدود (definitions), العوائد (customs), الإجماع (consensus), and الاعتقادات الكامنة في النفس (beliefs hidden in the soul). Write them down.
2
Understand why they don't require burhan
Reflect on each category: definitions are conventional and not truth-apt in the same way; customs are habitual practices; consensus is based on agreement; beliefs are internal. None are established by syllogistic proof from first principles.
In Islamic epistemology, burhan produces certainty from necessary premises. These four lack that status.
3
Recognize the alternative method: naqd and mu'arada
The text says these are 'رد' (refuted) by النقض (critique) and المعارضة (counter-argument). Instead of demanding a proof, one tests them by showing internal inconsistency, contradictory evidence, or alternative possibilities.
نقض in jadal means to show a flaw or exception; معارضة means to present a competing argument.
4
Apply the framework to an Islamic debate
Pick a real example: e.g., a claimed consensus on a ruling. You would not ask 'prove that this consensus is true in itself' but rather critique it by showing disagreement among scholars (naqd) or counter with a different scholarly opinion (mu'arada).
This shows how the principle operates in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological note on the limits of burhan (demonstrative proof). The commentator presents a received principle: there are four categories of knowledge that are not established by, and do not require, a formal demonstrative proof. These are: definitions (al-hudud), customs or habitual practices (al-'awa'id), consensus (al-ijma'), and deeply held beliefs (al-i'tiqadat al-kamina fi al-nafs). For these, one does not ask for a proof of their correctness in the external reality (fi nafs al-amr), nor does one demand a proof for the correctness of a particular definition. Instead, they are subject to refutation by critique (al-naqd) and counter-argument (al-mu'arada). This reflects the distinction in Islamic epistemology between matters established by demonstration and those established by convention, agreement, or innate conviction, which are tested by dialectical methods rather than syllogistic proof.

Circularity and Infinite Regress: Two Fallacies That Corrupt Demonstration
Author defines circularity, infinite regress, and the lām of causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between circularity and infinite regress and identify each in a given proof structure.

Core Idea
Circularity and infinite regress are structural diseases that make any demonstration invalid.

Every demonstration must rest on an independent ground. If your proof depends on what it is trying to prove, you have fallen into circularity. If it requires an endless chain of prior proofs, you have fallen into infinite regress. This passage defines both exactly and also clarifies that the particle 'lām' in 'lām al-taʿlīl' is a true proposition—a small but important point for sound reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of al-dawr
Find the phrase 'الدور توقف كل واحد من الشيئين على الآخر'. This means circularity is mutual dependence: A depends on B, and B depends on A, with no independent foundation.
Note that this is not mere repetition but strict interdependence.
2
Extract the definition of al-tasalsul
Find 'التسلسل توقف الشيء على أشياء غير متناهية'. Infinite regress is a thing's dependence on an unending series of prior things, so the chain never terminates in a first principle.
This is distinct from circularity: here the dependence goes backwards infinitely, not in a loop.
3
Analyse the opening phrase about two impossible consequences
The passage begins with 'اللازمين لذلك وهما مستحيلان' – likely referring to two impossible outcomes that follow from a flawed proof. Although the subject is missing from this truncated passage, in context these are probably contradiction (al-khilāf) and impossible entailment.
The exact referent requires cross-referencing the full text, but the point is that these fallacies render the proof impossible.
4
Interpret the lām of causation remark
The author says 'واللام في قولنا لام التعليل ... وهو مصدوق ما' – meaning the lām (as in 'li-anna') is a true proposition. This asserts that such causative particles can be used legitimately in demonstrative statements.
This is a brief aside, likely to preface that the lām itself is not a fallacy but a valid logical operator.

This passage defines three interrelated concepts that corrupt any attempted demonstration. The author begins with what appears to be a compressed reference to two impossible necessary consequences — most likely the contradiction (al-khilāf) and the impossible entailment that follow when a proof violates its own conditions. Circularity is defined precisely as the mutual dependence of two things upon one another, so that neither can be established independently. Finally, the author appends a brief note on the particle lām in the expression 'lām al-taʿlīl' and the particle min used to indicate the genus, stating that these are true propositions — meaning they are validly used in demonstrative discourse. The passage is clearly sharḥ-style expansion on a matn that listed these fallacies more tersely.

Demonstration and Certainty
Definition of demonstration and its division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define demonstration (burhan) and certainty (yaqin) as understood in Islamic logic, and distinguish between the two types of demonstration: limi and 'anni.

Core Idea
Demonstration is the strongest proof, built from certain premises.

A demonstration is not just any argument; it is a compound of certain propositions that yield certainty. The footnote defines certainty precisely as assent corresponding to reality with a cause. It also divides demonstration into two kinds: one that shows the cause (limi) and one that shows the existence ('anni). This distinction is crucial for evaluating proofs in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of burhan
Read the first footnote: demonstration is composed of certain propositions. Note the phrase 'ما تركب من القضايا اليقينية'.
Burhan is the highest form of syllogism in the Islamic logical tradition.
2
Define yaqin
The footnote defines certainty as 'الجزم المطابق للواقع عن موجب' (assent that corresponds to reality with a cause). Understand that this includes both truth and the reason for that truth.
Yaqin is not mere opinion; it is the strongest epistemic grade.
3
Distinguish limi and 'anni
The footnote states that burhan is divided into limi (demonstration of the cause) and 'anni (demonstration of the existence). Reflect on the difference: limi explains why something is, while 'anni only proves that it is.
For example, 'The world is contingent' can be proven 'anni, and 'because it changes' is limi.

First Principles and Observations
Definitions of awwaliyyat and mushahadat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the two types of certain premises: awwaliyyat (first principles) and mushahadat (observations), and give Islamic examples of each.

Core Idea
Some certainties are immediate; others come from internal sense.

Not all certain premises are derived from experience. The footnotes identify two types that are directly known: first principles (awwaliyyat) are grasped by pure intellect as soon as the terms are understood, and observations (mushahadat) are known through internal sense. Both serve as foundational premises in demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Define awwaliyyat
Read the second footnote: awwaliyyat are what the intellect perceives at first glance, without needing any inference after understanding the terms.
Example: 'The whole is greater than the part' is self-evident.
2
Define mushahadat
Read the third footnote: mushahadat are what the intellect perceives through internal sense (al-hiss al-batini), such as hunger being painful.
Internal sense is distinguished from external five senses.
3
Distinguish from other premises
Contrast these with mujarrabat (empirical) and mutawatirat (transmitted). Awwaliyyat and mushahadat do not depend on repetition or testimony.
They are the most immediate sources of certainty.

Empirics and Mass Transmission
Definitions of mujarrabat and mutawatirat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define and distinguish mujarrabat (empirical premises) and mutawatirat (multiply-transmitted premises), and identify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Repeated experience and unanimous transmission yield certainty.

Not all certainty comes from pure reason or internal feeling. The footnotes describe two additional sources: mujarrabat, which are known through repeated experience that produces certainty, and mutawatirat, which are known through mass transmission that cannot be false. Both play important roles in medicine, history, and religious sciences.

Guided Path
1
Define mujarrabat
Read the fourth footnote: mujarrabat are propositions known by repetition that yields certainty (يكرر يفيد اليقين). Example: aspirin relieves pain.
Repetition here means multiple observations leading to a universal judgment.
2
Define mutawatirat
Read the fifth footnote: mutawatirat are propositions known from a group whose agreement on a lie is impossible (يؤمن تواطؤهم على الكذب). Example: Mecca has the Ka'ba and the Sacred Mosque.
The group must be large, spread across times and places, and have no reason to collude.
3
Compare epistemic bases
Contrast mujarrabat (empirical generalization) with mutawatirat (testimony). Both yield certainty, but through different mechanisms: personal experience vs. social transmission.
In Islamic law, mutawatir hadith are certain knowledge; in medicine, mujarrabat are used.

Sixth are sensed particulars: direct observation, such as fire being hot. The commentator closes by noting that the matn's phrase 'from first principles' names the genus—certainty—shared by all six, even though only the first type is literally called awwaliyyat.
