Parsing a Textual Correction: al-Marwuq vs. al-Manwarq
Editor verifies the correct title against a manuscript variant and defines its lexical meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the correct title al-Marwuq and the scribal error al-Manwarq by analyzing the lexical evidence provided in the editor's note.

Core Idea
A single letter can change a title's meaning; textual criticism restores the author's intent.

This lesson examines an editor's footnote that corrects the title of al-Akhdari's Sullam. The editor notes that manuscript (b) has al-Manwarq, but the correct form is al-Marwuq, meaning 'adorned' or 'beautiful'. By analyzing lexical roots and metaphorical usage, we learn how careful textual work preserves the precise meaning of a logical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two title variants
In the Arabic, find al-Manwarq and al-Marwuq. Note the difference: the letter nun (ن) in al-Manwarq versus ra' (ر) in al-Marwuq. This one-letter shift is the scribal error.
The manuscript variant is marked as (ب).
2
Extract the editor's lexical definition
The editor defines al-Marwuq as حسن وبهاء (beauty and splendor). He contrasts it with رنق (turbidity), showing that rawnq is the opposite of murkiness. This establishes the semantic field of radiance and freshness.
The root رونق is tied to purity and brilliance.
3
Analyze the metaphorical evidence
The editor cites phrases like 'ذهب رونق شبابه' (the rawnq of his youth departed) and 'رونق الضحى' (the rawnq of the forenoon). These underscore that rawnq means freshness, brightness, and the best part of something.
Metaphorical usage confirms that المرونق implies beauty and luster.
4
Determine why al-Manwarq is an error
Al-Manwarq would derive from نور (light), suggesting 'illuminated'. While logic can be illuminating, the editor's lexical evidence shows the intended meaning is beauty and adornment, not illumination. Hence al-Manwarq is a corruption.
The scribe may have confused the root رونق with نور.

Grammatical Analysis of the Universals Verse
Grammatical and lexical parsing of the five-universals verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the grammatical licenses (tarkhīm, displacement) used in the matn verse and explain how they affect the logical reading of the five universals.

Core Idea
Poetic compression is unpacked by precise grammar; logical definitions depend on correct syntax.

The commentator's grammatical parsing of the verse on the five universals is not mere pedantry. Each poetic license—from the dropped tā' to the displaced negative—serves to preserve the metrical line while conveying exact logical constraints. Understanding these grammatical moves is essential to reading the matn correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim of the verse
The verse states that the universals are five, without deficiency or excess, supported by a previous enumeration. Notice the emphatic claim of exactness.
This sets the logical frame.
2
Parse the word 'jins' (genus)
The commentator analyses 'jins' as the predicate of an omitted mubtada' (subject). The full phrase would be 'and it is genus'. The general term was omitted because it functioned as an adjective for the object of knowledge.
Grammatical ellipsis clarifies the logical role.
3
Examine the dropped tā' of 'al-khāṣṣa' (the particular)
The tā' is dropped for lightening (tarkhīm) due to metrical necessity, even though the word is not a vocative. The commentator cites a line from Imru' al-Qays as a parallel poetic license.
Poetic necessity permits what grammar normally forbids.
4
Analyse the indefinite mubtada' 'awwal' (first)
The word 'first' is an indefinite subject, and its indefiniteness is justified by the detail (tafsīl) that follows in the verse.
Indefiniteness with a reason is a standard grammatical rule.
5
Examine the displaced 'lā' and the meaning of 'shatat'
The negative particle 'lā' is displaced (zahlaqat) from its expected position. 'Shatat' is defined as 'excess' (ziyāda), supported by a hadith on dowry: 'without diminution and without excess'.
The hadith confirms both the meaning and the pair of terms.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and lexical expansion on individual words of the matn's verse concerning the five universals. The commentator begins with the phrase 'and the universals,' explaining that the verse intends to establish exactly five universals, neither more nor less, supported by the previously mentioned enumeration. Throughout, the commentator treats the matn's poetic compression as a problem of Arabic syntax that must be unpacked before the logical content can be grasped.

Grammatical Glosses and Poetic License
Author glosses matn phrases with grammatical analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and justify poetic license (e.g., letter elision) in Mantiq matn verses using Qur'anic parallels.

Core Idea
Poetic elision in logical texts is legitimate, even echoed in the Quran.

The author justifies a metrical elision in the matn verse by citing a Qur'anic example, showing that such license is acceptable in prose and poetry. This reflects the integration of Arabic linguistic norms into logical writing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the elision
In the matn phrase 'wal-thani an yakhtalifa', the word 'al-thani' has its final ya dropped for meter. Locate this in the Arabic.
The elision is marked by the absence of the ya in written form.
2
Find the Qur'anic parallel
The author cites 'al-Kabir al-Muta'al' from the Quran (Q 13:9) as an example where the ya of 'al-Muta'al' is dropped in the basmala or similar. Recognize that this shows the permissibility of such elision even in divine speech.
3
Parse the grammatical structure
'Al-thani' is a mubtada' (subject), and 'an' with its sila (the clause it introduces) is a second mubtada', with the khabar being the condition and its result. Understand the sentence flow.
This parsing helps in understanding the logical condition being stated.
4
Reflect on the significance
The author's use of Qur'anic evidence shows that logical texts are bound by the same linguistic rules as the Quran. This reinforces the idea that Mantiq is rooted in Arabic eloquence.

Mnemonic Verse for Syllogistic Productivity
Mnemonic verse summarizing productive moods of the four figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recall the number of productive moods for each syllogistic figure and apply the principle that the conclusion follows the weaker premise.

Core Idea
First figure: 4, second: 4, third: 6, fourth: 5 – and conclusion follows the weaker premise.

The mnemonic verse at the end of the passage encapsulates the entire doctrine of syllogistic productivity: it gives the count for each figure and the rule that the conclusion follows the weaker premise in quality and quantity. This verse is a powerful tool for memorization.

Guided Path
1
Recall the numbers per figure
The verse says: 'There resulted for the first: four, like the second; then the third: six; and the fourth with five: they have produced.' Memorize these numbers.
Second figure also has four, as indicated by 'like the second'.
2
Identify the exception
The verse continues: 'And other than what I mentioned will not produce.' This means only the listed moods are productive; all others are not.
3
Understand the 'weaker premise' rule
The line 'And follow the conclusion the weaker of those premises' means the conclusion takes the quantity and quality of the weaker premise (i.e., the more restricted one in terms of universal/particular or affirmative/negative).
This rule is crucial for determining the correct conclusion in a valid syllogism.
4
Apply the verse to an example
Take a fourth-figure syllogism: 'Some B is A' (particular affirmative) and 'All B is C' (universal affirmative). According to the verse, this mood is productive (one of the five). The conclusion follows the weaker premise, which is particular and affirmative, so the conclusion is 'Some A is C'.

Navigating the Quranic Verse Index (مسرد الآيات)
Lists Quranic verses with page references from the main text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate a specific Quranic verse in a masrad al-ayat index and use it to find its occurrence in the main text.

Core Idea
The verse index is your roadmap to the Quranic evidence used in Islamic logical arguments.

Classical works on Islamic logic and theology often cite Quranic verses to support their arguments. To help readers find these citations, many books include an index of verses (مسرد الآيات) at the end. Our passage is just such an index. While it contains no logical content itself, understanding how to use it is essential for navigating the text and verifying the contextual evidence. In this lesson, you will learn to read this index and connect its entries to the main exposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the index structure
Notice that the passage begins with the title 'مسرد الآيات'. The list includes a verse excerpt, the word 'الآية', and then the surah and verse number. For example: 'الْكَبِيرُ الْمُتَعَالِ الآية ٩ سورة الرعد' means the excerpt 'The Great, the Exalted' is from verse 9 of Surah Ar-Ra'd.
2
Locate a verse in the index
Choose a verse that interests you, such as 'وَقُل رَّبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا' (Say: My Lord, increase me in knowledge). Find it in the index: it is listed as 'وَقُل رَّبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا الآية ١١٤ سورة طه'.
3
Understand the citation format
The index gives the first few words of the verse, then the verse number, then the surah name. In the printed book, there would also be a page number (not shown in this excerpt) indicating where in the main text that verse is cited.
4
Use the index to cross-reference
When reading the main text and encountering a citation like 'as Allah says...', you can turn to the index to find the exact surah and verse, then look up the full verse in the Quran to see the complete context. This is especially helpful for verifying that the author's interpretation aligns with the verse's meaning.
