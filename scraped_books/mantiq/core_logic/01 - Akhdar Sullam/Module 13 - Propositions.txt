The Definition of al-Khabar (Proposition)
Author defines al-khabar as truth-bearing speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a khabar and explain why it admits truth and falsity in itself.

Core Idea
Khabar is any utterance that can be true or false in itself.

Before we can evaluate arguments, we must distinguish between statements that carry truth-value and those that do not. In this passage, the author defines al-khabar as the kind of speech that is fundamentally truth-bearing. This distinction is the bedrock of logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of khabar
Find the sentence that defines al-khabar. The author gives two equivalent formulations: 'speech susceptible to truth and falsity' and 'that which admits of truth and falsity in itself.'
2
Understand 'in itself' (li-dhatihi)
The phrase 'in itself' means the truth-value belongs to the utterance by its own nature, not by external factors. For example, 'Zayd came' is true if Zayd actually came; no additional context is needed.
This excludes utterances like commands, which cannot be true or false regardless of context.
3
Apply to the given example
The author provides the example 'Zayd came.' Verify that this is a khabar: it can be true (if Zayd indeed came) or false (if he did not).
4
Distinguish from talab
Test a talab utterance, such as a command, against the khabar definition. Notice that a command like 'Stand up!' cannot be true or false, so it is not a khabar.
This contrast solidifies the boundary between the two divisions.

The Definition of al-Talab (Requestive Speech)
Author defines al-talab and lists its subtypes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the nine subtypes of talab and explain why talab does not admit truth-value.

Core Idea
Talab is speech that cannot be true or false — it is performed, not asserted.

While khabar conveys information, talab performs actions like commanding or requesting. This passage defines talab and enumerates its nine categories, which are essential for understanding non-propositional language in logic and Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of talab
The author provides two characterizations: (1) an utterance whose meaning is only understood when spoken, and (2) an utterance that does not admit truth or falsity in itself. Both capture the performative nature of talab.
The first definition emphasizes that talab only exists as a speech act; the second highlights its lack of truth-value.
2
Learn the nine subtypes of talab
Memorize the list: command (amr), prohibition (nahy), supplication (du'a'), interrogation (istifham), offer ('ard), wish (tamanni), hope (tarajji), specification (takhsis), and call (nida'). Understand each category in the Islamic context.
For example, du'a' is a request to Allah, while istifham is a request for information.
3
Verify that no subtype admits truth-value
For each subtype, try to assign a truth-value (true/false). For instance, can a command like 'Pray!' be true? No—it is either given or not. Similarly, a supplication cannot be called true or false.
This reinforces the defining property of talab.
4
Contrast with khabar using an example
Compare a talab, e.g., 'May Allah cure you' (a supplication), with a khabar, e.g., 'Allah cured you' (a statement). Notice the former is talab (non-truth-bearing), the latter is khabar.
This distinction is crucial for analyzing Islamic texts: some utterances are requests for action, others are reports.

Compound Expressions: Ṭalab and Khabar
Author divides compound expressions into ṭalab and khabar and excludes inshāʾ from logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between request (ṭalab) and informative (khabar) in compound expressions, and identify why inshāʾ is excluded from logic.

Core Idea
Logic only studies what can be true or false; requests and performatives are outside its scope.

This lesson clarifies the fundamental division of compound expressions in Islamic logic. The author distinguishes between requests, which cannot be true or false, and informative statements, which are the proper subject of logical analysis. Understanding this boundary is essential for knowing what logic can and cannot analyze.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
The author states that compound expressions are two types: ṭalab (request) and khabar (informative). Locate these terms in the Arabic.
Ṭalab includes commands, supplications, questions, etc. Khabar is what can be true or false.
2
Analyze the subcategories of ṭalab
Notice that ṭalab is further divided. If it is a verb, its type depends on the speaker's stance: superiority (command), equality (supplication), submission (entreaty). If it is not a verb and cannot be true or false, it is a reminder (tanbīh). All else is inshāʾ.
The author's classification uses both grammatical form and speaker-addressee relationship.
3
Recognize the criterion for exclusion
The author explicitly excludes inshāʾ from logic because truth and falsity ('ṣidq' and 'kidhb') do not attach to it. Logic is built on evaluating truth and falsity, so it only deals with khabar.
Commands like 'Establish prayer!' are not true or false; they are performative.

Classifying Composite Utterance: Request vs Report
Author divides composite utterance into talab and khabar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any composite utterance as talab or khabar and further subdivide talab according to the speaker-addressee relationship.

Core Idea
Logic only studies what can be true or false — that is khabar, not talab.

Before we dive into logical arguments, we must first understand what speech the logician actually analyzes. Not every sentence is logical material. In this lesson, we learn the fundamental division of composite utterance into two kinds: request and report. This classification determines which utterances belong to logic and which do not. Understanding this distinction will prevent confusion later when we encounter commands, supplications, and alerts that are not subject to truth-value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite utterance
Recall that a composite utterance is any meaningful combination of words. In logic, we are interested in those that can be evaluated for truth. The author states that composite utterance is divided into two: talab (request) and khabar (report).
2
Examine the request (talab) branch
The author subdivides talab into verbal and non-verbal. If the request is a verb, it further splits based on the relationship: command (superiority), supplication (equality), entreaty (submissiveness). If it is not a verb and does not admit truth/falsity, it is alerting (tanbih). All these are insha'.
Note that talab includes the imperative form (amr) but also extends to non-verbal alerts.
3
Analyze the report (khabar) branch
Khabar is defined as that which in itself admits truth and falsity. This is the only type of utterance the logician studies. Any utterance that cannot be true or false is excluded.
4
Apply the classification to given utterances
Take an utterance like 'Stand up' — it is a request, so talab. 'Zayd is standing' — it can be true or false, so khabar. 'Watch out!' — it is a non-verbal alert, so talab (tanbih). Practice with more examples from the Qur'an.

Why Logic Excludes Insha': The Scope of Truth-Value
Author excludes insha' from logic due to lack of truth-value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why insha' is excluded from logic and identify utterances that count as khabar versus insha' from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Insha' lacks truth-value, so the logician cannot analyze it.

Now that we've distinguished request from report, we need to understand why the logician only focuses on report. The reason is foundational: logic aims to help us distinguish truth from falsehood. Utterances that cannot be true or false (like commands, supplications, and alerts) fall outside this aim. In this lesson, we explore the boundary of logic and see how it directly relates to our goal of reaching certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Recall the purpose of logic
Logic is a tool for knowing the true from the false. Therefore, it only deals with utterances that have a truth-value. The passage says: 'understanding revolves around truth and falsity' (مدار فهم عليهما). This is the key reason.
2
Identify insha' as all talab
Every request, whether verbal (command, supplication, entreaty) or non-verbal (alerting), is insha'. None of these can be said to be true or false. 'Close the door!' is not true or false; it is a demand.
3
Recognize khabar as the only logical utterance
Khabar is defined precisely as that which admits truth and falsity. So any sentence that reports a fact that can be verified or falsified is khabar. For example, 'The sun rises from the east' is khabar; 'Let the sun rise' is insha'.
4
Practice distinguishing insha' and khabar
Take a hadith: 'Do not become angry' (لا تغضب). This is a command, hence insha'. 'Anger comes from Satan' (الغضب من الشيطان) is a report, hence khabar. The logician studies only the second.

Distinguishing the Whole from Universality and the Part from Particularity
Author distinguishes collective wholes from universal propositions and parts from particular judgments.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the collective whole (al-kull) from the universal proposition (al-kulliyya) and the physical part (al-juz') from the particular proposition (al-juz'iyya), and correctly classify judgments from Islamic disciplines.

A collective whole is not a universal; a physical part is not a particular judgment.

The author of al-Sullam devotes this section to four terms that often confuse students: the whole, universality, the part, and particularity. While 'universal' and 'particular' were already covered in the five universals, here they are redefined specifically for propositions and composition. This passage defines four terms that students of logic often confuse: the whole (al-kull), universality (al-kulliyya), particularity (al-juzʾiyya), and the part (al-juzʾ). The author opens with a compressed mnemonic verse — the matn — and then expands each term in prose. He notes that while the universal (al-kullī) and particular (al-juzʾī) were already discussed in the context of the five universals, here the discussion turns to the technical usage of these four related terms in the context of propositions and physical composition.

First, the whole (al-kull) is explained as the aggregate considered as a single entity. The judgment applied to it is collective: when we say, for example, that the congregation for Friday prayer is numerous, we predicate multitude of the group as a single whole, not of every individual worshipper. The author illustrates this with physical examples: a mat is composed of palm fronds and thread, and a house is composed of its constituent parts. The judgment on the whole does not distribute to each part.

Second, universality (al-kulliyya) is defined as a proposition in which the judgment covers every individual member without exception, whether affirmatively — such as ‘Every human is an animal’ or, in the Islamic sciences, ‘Every intoxicant is forbidden’ — or negatively, such as ‘No one is present.’ This is distributive quantification: the predicate must hold for each individual falling under the subject.

Third, particularity (al-juzʾiyya) is defined as a proposition in which the judgment is restricted to some unspecified individuals, as in ‘Some students are present’ or ‘Not some students are present.’ The quantification here is indefinite (mubham): we do not know exactly which individuals are intended, only that the predicate applies to some portion of the subject.

The author gives the example of a palm frond, which together with thread composes a mat, and the materials that compose a house. This is a physical or constitutive part, distinct from the logical particular. The author closes by noting that knowledge of the part is manifest and clear — once you see the frond and the thread, you understand that they are parts of the mat.

Defining the Proposition: Qadiyya and Li-Dhatihi
Author defines proposition with condition li-dhatihi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an utterance is a proposition (qadiyya) by applying the condition of li-dhatihi and distinguish it from non-informative statements.

Core Idea
A proposition is true or false by its own composition, not by external knowledge.

In this lesson, we move from conception to assent. The first step is to understand what a proposition is. The author defines qadiyya as a statement that intrinsically bears truth and falsity. This condition, li-dhatihi, is crucial: it includes even statements we know are false, as long as their composition allows truth-value. We'll walk through the definition and its implications for building proofs in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Look for the definiendum: what is the author defining? In this passage, it is 'qadiyya' (also called khabar). Note that it is part of the second major section on tasdiq (assent).

The genus is 'qawl' (statement) - a composite utterance. The differentia is 'yahtamilu al-sidqa wa al-kadhiba li-dhatihi' (intrinsically bears truth and falsity). So the definition is: a statement that by its own nature can be true or false.

Without 'li-dhatihi', a request (talab) might be included, but requests do not bear truth/falsity. Also, false statements like 'the sky is below us' are included because their composition allows truth/falsity, even if we know they are false by observing the contradictory.

Quantification: Quantified, Unquantified, and the Quantifier
Author explains quantification and the quantifier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between quantified (musawwara) and unquantified (muhmala) propositions, and identify the quantifier (sur) as the word indicating quantity.

Core Idea
A quantified proposition has a quantifier word (sur) like 'every' or 'some'; an unquantified proposition leaves the quantity ambiguous.

The author further explains that universal propositions are either quantified (clearly indicate quantity) or unquantified (no explicit quantity word). The quantifier itself is called sur, likened to a city wall that encloses the individuals. We will learn how to spot these and why they matter.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of musawwara
A quantified proposition is one that includes a quantifier word indicating all or some of the individuals.
2
Understand the definition of muhmala
An unquantified proposition has a universal subject but no explicit quantifier; the quantity is left implicit.
3
Identify the sur
The quantifier (sur) is the word itself, e.g., 'kull' (every) or 'ba'd' (some). It is likened to a wall (sur) that encloses the individuals.
4
Distinguish sur from musawwara
Musawwara is the proposition as a whole; sur is the specific word within it that indicates quantity. Not all propositions are musawwara; those without sur are muhmala.
5
Apply to examples
Identify whether given propositions are quantified or unquantified, and extract the sur if present.

The Definition of Proposition and Its Two Main Divisions
Definition of proposition and its two main divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a proposition by its defining characteristic ('by itself' admits truth/falsehood) and distinguish it from non-propositions, and also classify it as conditional or categorical.

Core Idea
A proposition is what can be true or false by its own composition.

Every statement that can be judged true or false is a proposition, but only when the potential for truth or falsehood is inherent in its structure. The author clarifies this with a surprising example and then divides propositions into two fundamental types: conditional and categorical.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition
Locate the Arabic definition of 'qadiyya' (قضية). It says: 'that which admits truth and falsehood by itself'.
The phrase 'by itself' (لذاته) is crucial.
2
Understand the 'by itself' condition
Why add 'by itself'? To exclude statements that only admit truth/falsehood due to external evidence. The example given is 'the sky is beneath us' – its composition could be true/false, but we know it's false by observing its opposite.
This ensures the definition is purely about the structure of the statement.
3
Identify the primary division
Propositions are divided into conditional (شَرْطِيَّة) and categorical (حَمْلِيَّة). A conditional is composed of two propositions linked by a condition (e.g., 'If the sun rises, then it is day'). A categorical has a subject and predicate (e.g., 'Zayd is a writer').

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory passage from a commentary on al-Sullam. The author begins by defining a proposition (qadiyya) as that which by its very composition can be true or false. He adds the qualification 'by itself' to exclude statements that are obviously false due to external evidence, like 'the sky is beneath us', which in its literal composition could be true or false but we know to be false by observation. Then he divides propositions into two main types: conditional (shartiyya, composed of two propositions) and categorical (hamliyya, with a subject and predicate). The categorical is further divided: it can be personal (shakhsiyya, with a specific individual as subject, e.g., 'Zayd is a writer'), or it can be quantified (musawwara) using a quantifier (sawr). The quantifier indicates the quantity of individuals covered: either universal (like 'every') or particular (like 'some'). The quantifier itself has four types implicitly: universal affirmative ('every'), universal negative ('none'), particular affirmative ('some'), and particular negative ('not some'). The author then states that each of the four kinds of categorical propositions (personal, quantified particular, quantified universal, indefinite) can be either affirmative or negative, thus totaling eight types. He ends by explicitly defining the quantifier as the term indicating quantity and gives examples: 'every human is an animal' (universal affirmative) and 'some humans are an animal' (particular affirmative). This classification is foundational for all subsequent discussions of syllogisms and logical inference.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions are built from two sentences, linked by either coexistence or incompatibility.

In the previous lesson, you learned about categorical propositions (hamliyya). Now we turn to conditional propositions (shartiyya), which are equally fundamental. Unlike categoricals, conditionals embed complete sentences as their parts. This passage from al-Akhdari's Sullam defines them and divides them into connected and disjunctive types, with three subtypes for disjunctives. Mastering this classification is essential for analyzing arguments in Islamic logic, especially in legal theory (usul al-fiqh) and theology (kalam).

Guided Path
1
Contrast with categoricals
Notice that a categorical proposition (hamliyya) resolves into two single terms (mufrad), like subject and predicate. A conditional resolves into two complete sentences (jumlah). This is the key difference.

Conditional Propositions: Conjunctive and Disjunctive Types
Author defines conditional propositions and divides disjunction into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify conditional propositions into conjunctive and disjunctive, and distinguish the three types of disjunctive propositions.

Core Idea
A conditional proposition ties two judgments together; disjunctives vary by whether they exclude conjunction, vacuity, or both.

Conditional propositions form the backbone of logical reasoning in Islamic sciences, from the conditions of contracts in fiqh to the proofs of God's attributes in kalam. This lesson unpacks the definition of a conditional proposition and its two main branches—conjunctive and disjunctive—then delves into the three subtypes of disjunctives that determine how we reason about mutually exclusive alternatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of any conditional
Every conditional proposition has a first part (al-muqaddam) and a second part (al-tali). Locate them in the Arabic text; for example, in 'If the sun is risen, then daytime exists', 'the sun is risen' is the muqaddam and 'daytime exists' is the tali.
2
Determine the relation between parts: talazum or 'inan
If the muqaddam necessarily implies the tali (or its negation), it is a conjunctive conditional (muttasilah). If the parts are mutually exclusive—they cannot both be true—it is a disjunctive conditional (munfasilah). Apply this test to the Quranic example given: 'If there were gods other than Allah, they would both be corrupted.' The parts are connected by necessity, so it is conjunctive.
3
Test a disjunctive for conjunction and vacuity
For a disjunctive, check if its two parts can be true together (jam') and if they can both be false together (khuluw). If they cannot both be true but can both be false, it is mani'at al-jam'. If they cannot both be false but can both be true, it is mani'at al-khuluw. If they can neither be both true nor both false, it is al-haqiqi.
Use the examples: 'equal or greater' (can both be false if less) → mani'at al-jam'; 'in the sea or not drown' (can both be true if in sea and not drown) → mani'at al-khuluw; 'even or odd' (neither both true nor both false) → al-haqiqi.
4
Verify with the author's classification
The author explicitly states that al-haqiqi is more specific (akhass) than the other two types, because every instance of al-haqiqi is also an instance of the other two (since it prevents both jam' and khuluw). Check that your classification matches this hierarchical relation.

This passage defines and classifies conditional propositions (al-qadiyya al-shartiyya). It opens by defining them as propositions in which one judgment is suspended upon another — that is, the existence of one part is made dependent on the existence or negation of the other. The author then divides them into two: the conjunctive (muttasilah) and the disjunctive (munfasilah). In both, the first part is called the antecedent (muqaddam) and the second the consequent (tali). The conjunctive conditional is then defined as the proposition that asserts the necessity (or lack of necessity) of one proposition for another, establishing a necessary connection (talazum) between its two parts. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define contradiction (tanaqud) and derive the contradictory (naqid) of any given proposition by applying the rules of quality and quantifier change.

Core Idea
Contradiction is the relation where two propositions differ in quality and truth-value; its rules let you flip a proposition into its exact opposite.

First, we must understand what contradiction means in logic. The author defines it precisely and then gives you two rules: one for singular/indefinite propositions and one for quantified propositions. These rules are the foundation of all immediate inference in the Aristotelian tradition. You will use them not only in logic but in every Islamic science where you need to test whether a claim is absolutely rejected by evidence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of contradiction
Identify the two conditions: difference in quality (affirmation/negation) and the necessary truth-value relation (one true, one false).
This is a definitive matter (amr qat‘i).
2
Distinguish singular/indefinite from quantified
Singular propositions refer to a specific individual (e.g., Zayd); indefinite have no quantifier (e.g., 'Humans are mortal'); quantified have a quantifier like 'every' or 'some'.
3
Apply the rule for singular/indefinite
Change the quality only: affirmative becomes negative, negative becomes affirmative.
Example: 'Zayd is standing' → 'Zayd is not standing'.
4
Apply the rule for quantified propositions
Change both the quantifier (universal↔particular) and the quality (affirmative↔negative). Universal affirmative → particular negative; universal negative → particular affirmative.
The text says: 'contradict by the opposite of its mentioned forms'.
5
Check that the result is a genuine contradictory
Verify that the two propositions cannot both be true or both false under any circumstance.

Commentary
This passage defines the logical relation of contradiction (tanaqud) and sets out the rules for deriving the contradictory proposition (naqid) from any given proposition. The commentator begins by situating the topic after the study of propositions and their divisions, turning now to the rulings that govern them. He defines contradiction as a difference between two propositions by affirmation and negation such that one must be true and the other false, which means they cannot share the same truth-value under any circumstance. He then presents the versified matn, which states that contradiction is a difference in quality — meaning one proposition is affirmative and the other negative — coupled with a single truth-value, meaning one is true and the other false, a decisive matter. The matn then gives the operational rules. If the proposition is singular (shakhsiyya), such as 'Zayd is standing,' or indefinite (muhammala), such as 'Humans are standing,' you find its contradictory by changing the quality alone: make the affirmative negative or the negative affirmative. If the proposition is quantified (mahsurah) by the quantificational forms, you contradict it by reversing the mentioned form; the general rule is to take the opposite of the quantifier and quality together. Thus, the contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative, and the contradictory of a universal negative is a particular affirmative. The commentator then explains that contradiction, technically speaking, is an expression of the difference of two propositions in truth-value and quality, which is the same meaning as the definition already given. The footnote adds that lexically the word is taken from untying a rope, and it cites the technical definition used by the logicians: a difference in quality such that the truth of one necessarily entails the falsehood of the other by itself (li-dhatihi).

Rules for Forming Contradictory Propositions
Author defines contradiction conditions and rules for quantified and non-quantified propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory of any categorical proposition by applying the rules of quality reversal for unquantified propositions and quantifier reversal for quantified propositions.

Core Idea
Contradiction flips either quality or quantifier—never both—to guarantee one truth and one false.

In Islamic logic, knowing how to properly contradict a proposition is essential for argumentation and refutation. The author lays out the precise conditions: two propositions must differ only in quality (affirmation/negation) or quantifier (universal/particular), and they cannot be simultaneously true or false. This lesson unpacks the method for forming contradictory propositions based on whether the proposition is quantified or not.

Guided Path
1
Classify the proposition as quantified or unquantified
Look for a quantifier (kull, laysa shay', ba'd) at the beginning. If none, it is personal (shakhsiyya) or indefinite (muhmal). Unquantified propositions include singular statements like 'Zayd is standing' or universal statements without quantifier like 'Man is an animal' (treated as unquantified in this context).
In this passage, even universal propositions without a quantifier are considered unquantified for contradiction purposes.
2
Form contradiction by reversing quality for unquantified propositions
If the proposition is unquantified, its contradictory is formed by changing its quality: an affirmative becomes a negative, and a negative becomes an affirmative. Keep the subject and predicate exactly the same. Example: 'Zayd is standing' becomes 'Zayd is not standing'.
This is referred to as contradicting 'by the quality' (al-kayf).
3
Identify the type of quantified proposition
Quantified propositions are one of four types: universal affirmative (A), universal negative (E), particular affirmative (I), particular negative (O). Identify which one you have.
Refer to the classic square of opposition for the standard forms.
4
Form contradiction by reversing the quantifier for quantified propositions
The contradictory of a quantified proposition is formed by substituting the opposite quantifier: A (كل س) ↔ O (ليس بعض س); E (ليس شيء من س) ↔ I (بعض س). Keep the quality (affirmation/negation) the same? Actually, check: For A (universal aff) to O (particular neg) — quality changes from aff to neg and quantifier from universal to particular. For E (universal neg) to I (particular aff) — quality changes from neg to aff and quantifier from universal to particular. So both quality and quantifier change. But the rule as given says 'by the opposite of its quantifier' and examples show quality also flips. This is correct: the rule effectively replaces the quantifier with its contradictory, which also flips the quality. So the student should note that both quantifier and quality change.
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative; of a universal negative is a particular affirmative.
5
Verify the two conditions of contradiction
Check that the two propositions differ only in the prescribed way (quality or quantifier/quality) and that they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. This ensures a genuine contradiction.
For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' and 'Some intoxicant is not forbidden' cannot both be true, and they cannot both be false (if one is false, the other must be true).

Identifying Dropped Apodotic Fa in Conditional Matn
Author identifies conditional structure and dropped fa in matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a conditional proposition in a matn and recognize when the apodotic fa has been dropped for metrical necessity, citing examples from Arabic poetry and hadith.

Core Idea
Dropping the apodotic fa in conditional sentences is a recognized license in Arabic, not a grammatical error.

The commentator points out that the two lines of the matn form a conditional proposition whose apodosis is the contradictory of the protasis. However, the expected fa before the apodosis has been omitted due to the metrical constraints of the verse. This phenomenon is not a mistake; it is attested in classical Arabic poetry and even in prophetic hadith. In this lesson, we will learn how to spot such conditional structures and understand the grammatical license behind the omission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional structure
Look at the two lines of the matn. Recognize that they form a conditional proposition: the protasis (the condition) and the apodosis (the answer). The commentator says the answer is the contradictory of the protasis.
The conditional structure is grammatical and logical.
2
Notice the missing fa
Observe that the apodosis does not begin with the expected fa-connective. In standard Arabic, a conditional sentence typically has fa before the apodosis, but here it is absent.
The fa is the conjunctive particle that signals the apodosis.
3
Understand the reason: darura
The commentator explains that the fa is dropped for 'al-darura' (metrical necessity). The matn is in verse, so the poet omitted the fa to maintain the meter.
Darura here means poetic license, not legal necessity.
4
Examine the evidence from poetry
The commentator cites the line of Hassan ibn Thabit: 'Man yaf‘al al-hasanat Allah yashkurha.' In proper Arabic, it should be 'fa-Allah' but the fa is dropped for the same reason.
This shows the phenomenon is recognized in classical poetry.
5
Examine the evidence from hadith
The commentator also gives a prose example from Sahih: 'fa-in jaa’ sahibuha wa-illa istamta‘.' Here the fa before 'istamta‘' is dropped, but the meaning is clear.
This proves the license exists even in prophetic speech.

Author distinguishes the conjunctive conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two forms of the conjunctive conditional proposition based on whether the particle 'in' or 'law' is used.

Core Idea
In attaches existence to existence; law attaches non-existence to non-existence.

This lesson clarifies the conjunctive conditional proposition as introduced by the author. Understanding the difference between the two particles is crucial for recognizing conditional statements in Islamic logic, which appear frequently in fardh and kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle
Look at the conditional particle: 'in' (إن) indicates attachment of existence to existence; 'law' (لو) indicates attachment of non-existence to non-existence.
The author states that 'in' is laid down to attach existence to existence, while 'law' attaches non-existence to non-existence.
2
Determine the nature of antecedent and consequent
When the particle is 'in', both the antecedent and consequent are affirmative (existence). When the particle is 'law', both are negative (non-existence) – the author calls this the 'raised' conjunctive.
For example: 'If it rains (in), the ground is wet' attaches existence of rain to existence of wetness.
3
Classify the proposition
Based on the particle, classify the conjunctive conditional into: (1) existence-to-existence (with 'in'), or (2) non-existence-to-non-existence (with 'law').
This classification is essential for correctly interpreting conditional statements in Islamic texts, especially in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage explains the conjunctive conditional proposition and defines qiyas al-khalf. The author notes that the particle in is laid down to attach existence to existence—that is, the affirmation of the antecedent is linked to the affirmation of the consequent. When the contradictory of the consequent is at issue, the particle law is used, for it is laid down to attach non-existence to non-existence; this is called the raised conjunctive. The author then defines qiyas al-khalf as proving the sought conclusion by invalidating its contradictory. In the Islamic sciences, this form of reasoning appears frequently in kalam, where a theologian might establish that the world is temporally originated by demonstrating the impossibility of its being eternal, thereby invalidating the contradictory of the thesis and establishing the thesis itself.

Qiyas al-Khalf: Indirect Proof in Islamic Logic
Author defines qiyas al-khalf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a qiyas al-khalf argument to prove a given claim in a kalam context.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-khalf proves the truth by showing its negation leads to absurdity.

This lesson introduces qiyas al-khalf, a powerful method of indirect proof used extensively in kalam and usul al-fiqh. The author defines it as proving the sought conclusion by invalidating its contradictory. This technique is foundational for establishing theological truths.

Guided Path
1
State the claim to be proven (the sought)
Clearly articulate the proposition you want to prove. For example, in kalam: 'The world is temporally originated (hadith).'
The sought is the conclusion you aim to establish.
2
Assume the contradictory of the claim
Take the opposite of the sought. For the example, assume: 'The world is eternal (qadim).'
This assumption is used only to be refuted.
3
Derive a contradiction from the assumption
Using valid reasoning, show that the assumption leads to an impossible consequence. For instance, if the world were eternal, it would be necessary (wajib), but we observe contingency (imkan), so it cannot be eternal.
The contradiction must follow logically from the assumption combined with other accepted premises.
4
Conclude the original claim is true
Since the contradictory leads to a contradiction, the original claim must be true. Thus, the world is temporally originated.
This step is based on the law of non-contradiction: if not-P leads to a contradiction, then P must be true.
