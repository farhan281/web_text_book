Defining Al-Hamd and Distinguishing It from Al-Shukr
Commentator defines al-hamd and distinguishes it from al-shukr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-hamd and al-shukr, and identify the logical relationship between them using genus and differentia.

Core Idea
Hamd praises all beautiful attributes; shukr praises only personal beneficence.

In this opening lesson, we examine how the verifiers define al-hamd and distinguish it from al-shukr. This distinction is not merely lexical; it reveals a precise logical relationship of generality and specificity. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms being defined
Find al-hamd and al-shukr in the Arabic. They are the two primary terms the commentator defines.
2
Extract the genus for each
Both share the genus 'praise' (al-thana'). Confirm that the definition starts with 'praise by speech' etc.
3
Extract the differentiae
For hamd: restricted to speech, cause includes any beautiful attribute. For shukr: includes speech and other means, cause is personal beneficence.
Differentiae distinguish each from the other.
4
Determine the logical relation
The commentator says they are mutually related as general and specific from one aspect (عموم وخصوص من وجه). Note that hamd is broader in cause (sabab) but narrower in locus (mahall), while shukr is the opposite.
This relation is key for understanding when to use each term.

Praise be to Allah who brought forth[؟] the fruits of thought for the people of eloquence absolutely. The verifiers said: al-hamd is praise by speech upon the praised one for his beautiful attributes, whether they be from the perspective of beneficence or perfection; and al-shukr is praise by speech and otherwise upon the benefactor by reason of his beneficence upon the grateful one. Thus it becomes clear from this that between them is a generality and specificity from one aspect: they agree in one form and each class is exclusive to a form. So al-hamd is broader in cause and narrower in locus, and al-shukr is the opposite.

Commentary
This passage is definitional. The commentator begins by citing the opening line of the versified text (matn), then proceeds to define al-hamd and distinguish it from al-shukr. He reports that the verifiers define al-hamd as praising the praised one (al-mahmud) through speech for beautiful attributes, whether those attributes relate to beneficence (ihsan) or to perfection (kamal). By contrast, al-shukr is praising the benefactor (al-mun’im) through speech and through other means—such as action or feeling—by reason of the specific beneficence bestowed upon the grateful one (al-shakir). From this, he draws a precise logical relation of generality and specificity between the two. They share a common form—praise of a deserving party for a noble quality—but they differ in two respects: cause and locus. Al-hamd is broader in cause (sabab) because it can be for any beautiful attribute, whether or not one has received personal benefit; yet it is narrower in locus (mahall) because it is restricted to speech. Al-shukr is the inverse: narrower in cause, because it requires personal beneficence, but broader in locus, because it extends beyond speech to action and feeling. This distinction is foundational for understanding why the text opens with al-hamd rather than al-shukr.

The Definite Article in Al-Hamd: Generic or Particular
Commentator discusses the definite article in al-hamd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the generic (jinsiyya) and particular (ahdiyya) definite articles and identify which one applies to 'al-hamd' according to the commentator.

Core Idea
The al- in al-hamd is generic, not particular, making praise a universal genus.

In this passage, the commentator pauses to resolve a grammatical ambiguity: does 'al-hamd' refer to praise in general or a specific praise? The answer affects the logical scope of the definition. Follow his reasoning to see how he arrives at the stronger view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term in question
Find 'al-hamd' in the Arabic and note the definite article al-. This is the term whose article the commentator is analyzing.
2
Recognize the two possibilities
The commentator lists two grammatical functions for al-: jinsiyya (generic, indicating the genus) and ahdiyya (particular, indicating a specific known instance).
3
Assess the commentator's preference
He states 'the stronger view is that it is generic' (wa-l-asahh annaha jinsiyya). Consider why the generic interpretation is stronger: it gives a broader, more universal scope.
4
Consider the counterargument
Some chose the particular article, but the commentator dismisses that argument as leading away from brevity and concision, his goal in this text.

This passage is explanatory and grammatical. The commentator first justifies his wording: he said 'by speech' (bi-l-kalam) rather than 'by tongue' (bi-l-lisan) so that the definition of al-hamd would encompass the four types of praise (al-mahamid al-arba'a), not merely verbal utterance. He notes that scholars disagree over whether this al- is generic (jinsiyya), referring to the genus of praise in general, or particular (ahdiyya), referring to a specific praise known to speaker and listener. He states that the stronger view is that it is generic, though some prefer the particular with an argument that he sets aside as leading away from the purpose of brevity. He closes by noting that some scholars consider the basmala sufficient in place of the explicit hamdala, though the sentence is cut off in the available text.

Al-Qarafi's Three Exclusions for Valid Definition
Al-Qarafi defines definition and excludes essence, the overly general, and the overly specific.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why al-Qarafi rejects essence, more general, and more specific as definitions, and apply these criteria to evaluate Islamic term definitions.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be epistemically prior and exactly coextensive with the defined.

Al-Qarafi, a Maliki jurist and logician, sets down the essential conditions for a correct definition. He argues that a definition must be known before the thing it defines, and must be neither broader nor narrower than the defined. This lesson walks you through his reasoning and shows how to apply these criteria.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the function of a definition
Al-Qarafi states that a definition must make you conceive the defined and distinguish it from all else. This is the purpose of any definition.
2
Exclude the essence (mahiyya)
The definition cannot be the thing's own essence because the definition must be known before the thing, but a thing cannot be known before itself—that would be circular.
3
Exclude the overly general (a'amm)
A too-broad definition (like a genus alone) fails to give specific knowledge; it does not differentiate the defined from other members of that genus.

Commentary
This passage quotes the Maliki jurist and logician al-Qarafi to establish the boundaries of a correct definition. The author begins by clarifying that by 'definition' is meant a phrase that conveys the meaning of a thing. He then states the essential function of a definition: it is a statement such that if you conceive it, you necessarily conceive the thing defined, and it distinguishes that thing from everything else. Al-Qarafi proceeds to exclude three false candidates. First, the definition cannot be the very essence of the thing itself, because the definition must be known prior to the thing defined, whereas a thing cannot be known before itself; using the essence would be circular. Six Conditions of the Differentia in Definition
Author lists the conditions that every differentia must satisfy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and apply the six conditions that every differentia (fasl) must satisfy in a valid definition.

Core Idea
A valid differentia must be convertible, reversible, evident, not too broad, not equal to the genus, and not metaphorical without indicator.

In the previous lessons, you learned about the five universals and the structure of a definition. Here the author of the Sullam lists the precise conditions that the differentia (fasl) must meet. These six conditions ensure that the differentia truly specifies the species and that the definition is both accurate and informative. Understanding these conditions will allow you to evaluate the quality of any definition you encounter in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being regulated
The passage is about the differentia (fasl). Recall that the differentia is the part of a definition that distinguishes the species from other species under the same genus. Here, conditions are set for its validity.
2
List the positive conditions
The passage states the differentia must be mutaradd (convertible: whenever the definition is present, the defined is present in its preventing capacity), man'aks (reversible: whenever the defined is present, the definition is present in its comprehensive capacity), and zahir (evident, not obscure). These ensure coextensiveness and clarity.
There is a terminological dispute between al-Bajuri and al-Qarafi on the exact labeling of mutaradd and man'aks, but both together guarantee the differentia matches the defined exactly.
3
List the negative conditions
The passage says the differentia must not be ab'ad (more general than the genus, causing overinclusion), musawi (equal to the genus, adding no narrowing), or used tajawwuzan (metaphorically) without a qarina (indication) to avoid ambiguity.
4
Understand the purpose of each condition
For each condition, consider the logical problem if violated. If mutaradd/man'aks are missing, the definition is not coextensive. If not zahir, it is obscure. If ab'ad, it includes non-members. If musawi, it redundant. If metaphorical without qarina, it misleads.

The differentia is 'a set of specific actions and recitations...' Check if it is coextensive with all instances of salah and not too broad or narrow.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A single word cannot define; definitions must be composed to ensure precision.

He presents the permissive view as the basis for his earlier examples, then invokes al-Zarkashi's authority to assert the stronger position that a definition must be a composed statement. Understanding this dispute is essential for anyone who wants to use definitions rigorously in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the practice being questioned
Al-Akhdari mentions 'definition by the differentia alone' and 'definition by the property alone' — these are examples of defining using a single term. Recognize that this is the practice under scrutiny.

Al-Zarkashi's objection rests on the idea that a definition must fully capture the essence and distinguish the definiendum from everything else. In logic, a proper definition (hadd) requires genus and differentia; a description (rasm) typically requires essential properties. Both are composite.
4

If the correct view is that definitions must be composed, then the earlier examples using a single differentia or property are not fully satisfactory. However, they may still be acceptable as shorthand or in contexts where composition is understood implicitly.
Al-Akhdari's note serves as a warning to students not to misinterpret the earlier examples as the final word.
5

This debate highlights the rigorous standards of Islamic logic: definitions are not merely arbitrary labels but carefully constructed statements that convey complete knowledge. Recognizing the requirement for composition prevents sloppy or incomplete definitions in theological and juristic reasoning.
This principle applies directly to fields like usul al-fiqh, where defining terms like 'ribā' or ‘illa’ must be precise to avoid errors in legal analogy.

Four Things Not Requiring Proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of things that do not require proof in Islamic logic and explain why they are accepted as starting points rather than proven.

Core Idea
Some truths are accepted as axioms; they are challenged only by counterexample, not by demanding proof.

Not every claim in a discipline is up for demonstration. Al-Akhdari identifies four foundational categories—definitions, customs, consensus, and inner beliefs—that are taken as given in logical and legal reasoning. This lesson clarifies what they are and how they can still be contested: not by proof, but by showing inconsistency or presenting a counter-argument.

Guided Path
1
List the four categories
From the passage, extract the four things that do not require proof: ḥudūd (definitions/limits), ʿawāʾid (customary practices), ijmāʿ (scholarly consensus), and iʿtiqādāt kāmina fī l-nafs (inner beliefs or foundational convictions).
These are considered axioms or premises that are self-evident or accepted by the community.
2
Understand why they need no proof
The passage states that no one should ask 'what is the evidence for their correctness in themselves?' because these are not derived claims but basic starting points. For example, a definition (ḥadd) defines a term; it is not proven true but accepted as a convention.
In logic, definitions are stipulated, not demonstrated.
3
Recognize how they can be refuted
Even though they do not require proof, they are not immune to challenge. The passage says they can be refuted by naqḍ (pointing out a counterexample or contradiction) and muʿāraḍa (presenting a counter-argument). This is a key principle in disputation (jadal).
This shows that axioms are defeasible by logical inconsistency.
4

In uṣūl al-fiqh, ijmāʿ (consensus) is a source of law. One does not prove that ijmāʿ is authoritative; it is accepted as a foundational principle. However, if someone claims that a particular consensus is not really consensus, they can challenge it by showing that a qualified scholar dissented (naqḍ).
This exemplifies how the four categories operate in practice.

Muṭṭarid and Munʿakis: Coextensiveness and Reciprocity
Al-Akhdari states conditions and explains the terms muṭṭarid and munʿakis with scholarly disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which scholar's convention is used for muṭṭarid and munʿakis and verify that a definition is both comprehensive and exclusive.

Core Idea
Muṭṭarid and munʿakis have opposite meanings; context determines which scholar's definition applies.

This lesson explains the first two conditions for a valid definition: coextensiveness (muṭṭarid) and reciprocity (munʿakis). Al-Qarāfī and al-Ghazālī use these terms differently, so you must know whose terminology is in play. We'll break down their meanings and show how to apply them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and the proposed definition
Locate the term being defined and the words used to define it. For example, if the definition is 'man is a rational animal,' the definiendum is 'man' and the definition is 'rational animal.'
The definition may be a single phrase or a compound.
2
Check which scholar's usage is assumed
If the text follows al-Qarāfī, muṭṭarid means comprehensive (jāmiʿ) and munʿakis means exclusive (māniʿ). If it follows al-Ghazālī or Ibn al-Ḥājib, the meanings are reversed. Look for clues like 'according to al-Qarāfī' or 'according to al-Ghazālī.'
Jurists often follow the Ghazālī convention.
3
Test comprehensiveness (jāmiʿ)
Does the definition include every individual of the defined? For 'rational animal,' every human is rational and animal; no human is left out.
If any instance of the definiendum is not covered, the definition is not comprehensive.
4
Test exclusiveness (māniʿ)
Does the definition exclude everything that is not the definiendum? 'Rational animal' excludes non-rational animals and non-animals. If something else fits, the definition is not exclusive.
Exclusiveness prevents over-inclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the conditions required for valid definitions (ḥudūd) and descriptions (rusūm). It begins by quoting a poetic line (matn) listing conditions: the definition must be muṭṭarid (coextensive), munʿakis (reciprocal), obvious (ẓāhir) but not more remote than the defined, not equal in clarity, and not metaphorical without a contextual clue. The commentary then expands: muṭṭarid means the definition includes all individuals of the defined (jāmiʿ), and munʿakis means it excludes non-individuals (māniʿ). Al-Qarāfī takes this view, while al-Ghazālī and Ibn al-Ḥājib reverse the terms (muṭṭarid = māniʿ, munʿakis = jāmiʿ). The commentator then explains the condition of being more apparent: the definition must be clearer than the defined, not hidden (like defining wheat as 'grain'—too general) and not equal (like 'the moving is what is not stationary'—circular). Next, the definition must avoid obscure (gharība), ambiguous (mushtaraka), and metaphorical (majāziyya) words unless a contextual clue clarifies. Circularity (dawr) is also prohibited: for example, defining 'knowledge' as 'knowledge of the known' is circular because 'known' derives from 'knowledge' and vice versa. However, al-Zarkashī argues that derivation does not necessarily entail circularity if the dependency is different or simultaneous. Finally, definitions must not include rulings (aḥkām) because assent (taṣdīq) depends on conception (taṣawwur), which depends on definition—introducing a ruling would cause circularity. Also, the word 'or' (aw) is prohibited in real definitions (ḥaqīqī) because it would imply that the species has multiple differentiae, but it is allowed in descriptions (rasm). This passage covers all major conditions for sound definitions.

Clarity in Definitions: Avoiding Obscurity
Al-Akhdari explains that definitions must be more apparent and avoid obscure, ambiguous, and metaphorical language.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate a definition for clarity: it must be more apparent than the defined and avoid gharība, mushtaraka, and majāziyya unless clarified by context.

Core Idea
A definition must illuminate, not confuse; obscure words defeat its purpose.

A definition should make the defined term clearer, not more obscure. This lesson covers the condition that the definition must be more apparent than the defined, and must avoid unusual (gharība), ambiguous (mushtaraka), and metaphorical (majāziyya) expressions. Al-Ghazālī allows an exception if a contextual clue (qarīna) clarifies the meaning.

Guided Path
1
Compare the clarity of definition and definiendum
Is the definition easier to understand than the term being defined? For example, defining 'wheat' as 'grain' is too general and not clearer; defining 'moving' as 'not stationary' is equally unclear.
The definition should shed light, not add darkness.
2
Scan for obscure words (gharība, mushtaraka, majāziyya)
Look for rare vocabulary, ambiguous terms with multiple meanings, or figurative language. These can mislead.
If such words are used, check if there is a contextual clue (qarīna) that specifies the intended meaning.
3
Decide if the definition is acceptable
If the definition is clearer than the defined and free of obscure words (or has a clarifying qarīna), it passes. Otherwise, it fails the clarity condition.
The passage explicitly says that obscure words are forbidden unless clarified by a contextual clue.

Avoiding Circularity (Dawr) in Definitions
Al-Akhdari prohibits definitions that depend on knowledge of the defined, citing dawr, and notes al-Zarkashī's nuance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular definitions and apply al-Zarkashī's exception for derivative terms with different dependency directions.

Core Idea
Circularity makes a definition self-referential and useless; but not all dependencies are vicious.

A definition must not rely on knowledge of the defined term itself, as that creates circularity (dawr). The classic example is defining 'knowledge' as 'knowledge of the known,' where 'known' derives from 'knowledge.' However, al-Zarkashī argues that if the dependency direction differs or is simultaneous, circularity does not occur. This lesson dissects the concept of dawr and its exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and the proposed definition
Find the term being defined (e.g., 'ʿilm' = knowledge) and the words used to define it (e.g., 'maʿrifat al-maʿlūm' = knowledge of the known).
The definition may contain a derivative of the definiendum.
2
Check for dependency between definition and definiendum
Does understanding the definition require prior understanding of the definiendum? In 'knowledge of the known,' 'known' is derived from 'knowledge,' so understanding 'known' requires understanding 'knowledge,' and understanding 'knowledge' through this definition requires understanding 'known' — a vicious circle.
Circularity occurs when each depends on the other.
3
Apply al-Zarkashī's exception
If the dependency is one-way (e.g., 'knowledge' is defined by something not derived from it) or if the two are known simultaneously in a different order, circularity is avoided. For example, defining 'fire' as 'that which produces heat' is not circular even though heat is associated with fire.
Al-Zarkashī states: 'Circularity does not necessarily follow from derivation' if the direction of dependency differs.

Rulings and 'Or' in Real Definitions
Al-Akhdari prohibits rulings and 'or' in real definitions, with justification from al-Aṣbahānī.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why rulings (aḥkām) and disjunction (aw) are prohibited in real definitions (ḥadd ḥaqīqī) and why 'or' is allowed in descriptions (rasm).

Core Idea
Real definitions give essence; rulings belong to assent, and 'or' implies multiple differentiae, both forbidden.

This final condition addresses two prohibitions: (1) including a ruling (ḥukm) in a definition, because assent (taṣdīq) depends on conception (taṣawwur), which depends on definition – introducing a ruling would cause circularity; (2) using 'or' (aw) in a real definition, because a species can have only one differentia. In descriptions (rasm), 'or' is permissible. Al-Aṣbahānī explains the reason for prohibiting 'or'.

Guided Path
1
Identify if a definition contains a ruling
A ruling is a legal or theological judgment, e.g., 'wājib,' 'ḥarām.' If the definition says 'Ṣalāh is an obligatory act,' the word 'obligatory' is a ruling, which is prohibited.
Rulings belong to the realm of assent (taṣdīq), not conception (taṣawwur).
2
Understand the circularity caused by rulings
The definition gives conception, which is prerequisite for assent. Adding a ruling (which requires assent) into the definition makes the definition depend on the very thing it is supposed to enable.
The passage explicitly states: 'assent is a branch of conception, and conception is a branch of definition.'
3
Check for the word 'or' in a real definition
If the definition says 'al-insān is a rational animal or a laughing animal,' the 'or' suggests two possible differentiae, which is invalid because a real definition must give the single essence.
In a description (rasm), 'or' is allowed because it lists accidental properties.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the conditions required for valid definitions (ḥudūd) and descriptions (rusūm). It begins by quoting a poetic line (matn) listing conditions: the definition must be muṭṭarid (coextensive), munʿakis (reciprocal), obvious (ẓāhir) but not more remote than the defined, not equal in clarity, and not metaphorical without a contextual clue. The commentary then expands: muṭṭarid means the definition includes all individuals of the defined (jāmiʿ), and munʿakis means it excludes non-individuals (māniʿ). Al-Qarāfī takes this view, while al-Ghazālī and Ibn al-Ḥājib reverse the terms (muṭṭarid = māniʿ, munʿakis = jāmiʿ). The commentator then explains the condition of being more apparent: the definition must be clearer than the defined, not hidden (like defining wheat as 'grain'—too general) and not equal (like 'the moving is what is not stationary'—circular). Next, the definition must avoid obscure (gharība), ambiguous (mushtaraka), and metaphorical (majāziyya) words unless a contextual clue clarifies. Circularity (dawr) is also prohibited: for example, defining 'knowledge' as 'knowledge of the known' is circular because 'known' derives from 'knowledge' and vice versa. However, al-Zarkashī argues that derivation does not necessarily entail circularity if the dependency is different or simultaneous. Finally, definitions must not include rulings (aḥkām) because assent (taṣdīq) depends on conception (taṣawwur), which depends on definition—introducing a ruling would cause circularity. Also, the word 'or' (aw) is prohibited in real definitions (ḥaqīqī) because it would imply that the species has multiple differentiae, but it is allowed in descriptions (rasm). This passage covers all major conditions for sound definitions.

The Two Essential Conditions: Jami' and Mani'
Author lists conditions of definition: jami' and mani' with terminological variation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a definition is comprehensive (jami') and preventive (mani'), and distinguish the terminological variations between al-Qarafi and al-Ghazali/Ibn al-Hajib.

Core Idea
A sound definition must include all instances and exclude all non-instances.

In this passage, the author sets out two fundamental conditions for any definition: it must be comprehensive of all individuals of the defined, and it must prevent outside individuals from entering. However, he notes that the logicians disagree on the labels for these conditions. Understanding this terminological shift is essential for reading later works in logic and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
Read the Arabic: يَشْتَرَطُ فِي كُلِّ وَاحِدٍ مِنَ الْمَعَرِّفَاتِ أَنْ يَكُونَ جَامِعًا لأَفْرَادِ الْمَحْدُودِ وَهُوَ مَعْنًى مُطَرَّدًا وَمَانِعًا مِنْ دُخُولِ غَيْرِهِ فِي الْحُدُودِ وَهُوَ مَعْنًى مُنْعَكِسًا. Note that the conditions are jami' (comprehensive) and mani' (preventive).
Jami' is also called mutarad (pervading) and mani' is called mun'akis (convertible) according to al-Qarafi.
2
Contrast the two schools
Compare al-Qarafi's usage with that of al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Hajib. For the latter, mutarad means mani' and mun'akis means jami'. This reversal can cause confusion if not recognized.
The author notes that the usage of al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Hajib is 'the one current on the tongues of the jurists.'
3

Take the definition of 'salah' as 'a set of specific actions and utterances beginning with takbir and ending with taslim'. Check if it is jami' (includes all valid salah forms) and mani' (excludes non-salah acts like du'a).
If a definition fails either condition, it is defective.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions of a sound definition (hadd) and description (rasm) in Islamic logic. The author begins by stating what must be avoided in a definition: terms that are unknown except through the defined thing itself, and ambiguous shared terms unless the context makes them clear. He then notes that including judgments (ahkam) within definitions is rejected by the logicians, as is the use of the disjunctive particle 'or' in essential definition, though it is permitted in description. The core of the passage sets out the positive conditions. First, a definition must be comprehensive (jami') of all individuals of the defined, which al-Qarafi calls a 'pervading' (mutarad) meaning; it must also be preventive (mani') against including others, which is the 'convertible' (mun'akis) meaning. The author notes a terminological shift: al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Hajib reverse these labels, calling the pervading meaning 'preventive' and the convertible meaning 'comprehensive,' which is the usage current among jurists. Second, the definition must be more evident than the defined, neither more obscure nor equal to it in clarity. The obscure is illustrated by defining al-birr (righteousness) as wheat, which is incomprehensible; the equal is illustrated by defining 'the moving' as 'what is not still,' which is equally unknown. Third, one must avoid obscure, shared, and metaphorical terms, as well as ambiguous expressions, unless a contextual clue removes the ambiguity. Fourth, circularity must be avoided: the definition must not depend for its understanding on prior knowledge of the defined. The example given is that one cannot define knowledge as 'conception of the known,' because 'known' is derived from 'knowledge,' creating mutual dependence. Al-Zarkashi nuances this by noting that derivation (ishtiqaq) or simultaneity (mu'iyya) does not necessarily entail circularity if the dependence is unidirectional. Fifth, judgments must not enter definitions, because assent (tasdiq) depends on conception (tasawwur), which depends on definition, so including a judgment would make conception depend on assent, reversing the epistemological order and causing circularity. Finally, the disjunctive 'or' is prohibited in essential definition because a single species cannot have two differentiae by way of substitution (badal), which would be impossible; it is, however, permitted in description.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions of a sound definition (hadd) and description (rasm) in Islamic logic. The author begins by stating what must be avoided in a definition: terms that are unknown except through the defined thing itself, and ambiguous shared terms unless the context makes them clear. He then notes that including judgments (ahkam) within definitions is rejected by the logicians, as is the use of the disjunctive particle 'or' in essential definition, though it is permitted in description. The core of the passage sets out the positive conditions. First, a definition must be comprehensive (jami') of all individuals of the defined, which al-Qarafi calls a 'pervading' (mutarad) meaning; it must also be preventive (mani') against including others, which is the 'convertible' (mun'akis) meaning. The author notes a terminological shift: al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Hajib reverse these labels, calling the pervading meaning 'preventive' and the convertible meaning 'comprehensive,' which is the usage current among jurists. Second, the definition must be more evident than the defined, neither more obscure nor equal to it in clarity. The obscure is illustrated by defining al-birr (righteousness) as wheat, which is incomprehensible; the equal is illustrated by defining 'the moving' as 'what is not still,' which is equally unknown. Third, one must avoid obscure, shared, and metaphorical terms, as well as ambiguous expressions, unless a contextual clue removes the ambiguity. Fourth, circularity must be avoided: the definition must not depend for its understanding on prior knowledge of the defined. The example given is that one cannot define knowledge as 'conception of the known,' because 'known' is derived from 'knowledge,' creating mutual dependence. Al-Zarkashi nuances this by noting that derivation (ishtiqaq) or simultaneity (mu'iyya) does not necessarily entail circularity if the dependence is unidirectional. Fifth, judgments must not enter definitions, because assent (tasdiq) depends on conception (tasawwur), which depends on definition, so including a judgment would make conception depend on assent, reversing the epistemological order and causing circularity. Finally, the disjunctive 'or' is prohibited in essential definition because a single species cannot have two differentiae by way of substitution (badal), which would be impossible; it is, however, permitted in description.

Avoiding Circularity (Dawr) in Definitions
Author prohibits circular definitions, with example and scholarly nuance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect circular definitions and distinguish genuine circularity from acceptable derivation or simultaneity.

Core Idea
Circularity makes a definition useless; but not every derivation is circular.

One of the most important prohibitions in definition is circularity (dawr), where the knowledge of the defined depends on the definition and vice versa. The passage gives the classic example of defining knowledge as 'conception of the known', and then al-Zarkashi offers a nuance: derivation or simultaneity does not always entail circularity if the dependence is unidirectional.

Guided Path
1
Locate the circularity example
Find the sentence: كَالْعِلْمِ لا يُقَالُ فِيهِ مَعْرِفَةُ الْمَعْلُومِ لأَنَّ الْمَعْلُومَ مُشْتَقٌّ مِنَ الْعِلْمِ... This shows mutual dependence.
2
Trace the dependency
The known (ma'lum) is derived from knowledge ('ilm) and thus requires knowledge for its understanding. But knowledge is defined as 'conception of the known', so knowledge depends on the known. This creates a circle.
Circle: knowledge → known → knowledge.
3
Understand al-Zarkashi's caveat
Al-Zarkashi says circularity does not necessarily follow from derivation (ishtiqaq) or simultaneity (mu'iyya) if the direction of dependence is different. For example, if A is conceptually prior to B but B is not conceptually prior to A, then no circle.
Unidirectional dependence is acceptable.

Prohibition of Judgments and 'Or' in Hadd, Permissibility in Rasm
Author bans judgments and 'or' in hadd, allows in rasm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential definition (hadd haqiqi) and description (rasm) in terms of allowed logical operators.

Core Idea
Hadd prohibits judgments and 'or'; rasm permits them.

The passage concludes with two important prohibitions specific to essential definition (hadd haqiqi): first, no judgments (arguments) can enter because that would cause circularity between tasawwur and tasdiq; second, the disjunctive 'or' is forbidden because a single species cannot have two differentiae. However, both are allowed in description (rasm).

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibition of judgments
Find the sentence: وَيُجْتَنَبُ أَيْضًا دُخُولُ الْحُكْمِ فِي الْحُدُودِ because it leads to circularity between tasawwur and tasdiq.
Tasdiq depends on tasawwur, so including a judgment in a definition reverses the order.
2
Identify the prohibition of 'or'
Find: وَلا يَجُوزُ أَيْضًا دُخُولُ أَوْ فِي الْحَقِيقِيِّ. Al-Asbahani explains that the same species cannot have two differentiae by way of substitution.
A species has only one essential differentia.
3
Differentiate hadd from rasm
Note that the author says 'or' is permitted in rasm. A description can list multiple accidental properties with 'or', e.g., 'man is a laughing or writing animal'.
Rasm does not capture essence, so it can use disjunctions.

Commentary
This passage defines the conditions of a sound definition (hadd) and description (rasm) in Islamic logic. The author begins by stating what must be avoided in a definition: terms that are unknown except through the defined thing itself, and ambiguous shared terms unless the context makes them clear. He then notes that including judgments (ahkam) within definitions is rejected by the logicians, as is the use of the disjunctive particle 'or' in essential definition, though it is permitted in description. The core of the passage sets out the positive conditions. First, a definition must be comprehensive (jami') of all individuals of the defined, which al-Qarafi calls a 'pervading' (mutarad) meaning; it must also be preventive (mani') against including others, which is the 'convertible' (mun'akis) meaning. The author notes a terminological shift: al-Ghazali and Ibn al-Hajib reverse these labels, calling the pervading meaning 'preventive' and the convertible meaning 'comprehensive,' which is the usage current among jurists. Second, the definition must be more evident than the defined, neither more obscure nor equal to it in clarity. The obscure is illustrated by defining al-birr (righteousness) as wheat, which is incomprehensible; the equal is illustrated by defining 'the moving' as 'what is not still,' which is equally unknown. Third, one must avoid obscure, shared, and metaphorical terms, as well as ambiguous expressions, unless a contextual clue removes the ambiguity. Fourth, circularity must be avoided: the definition must not depend for its understanding on prior knowledge of the defined. The example given is that one cannot define knowledge as 'conception of the known,' because 'known' is derived from 'knowledge,' creating mutual dependence. Al-Zarkashi nuances this by noting that derivation (ishtiqaq) or simultaneity (mu'iyya) does not necessarily entail circularity if the dependence is unidirectional. Fifth, judgments must not enter definitions, because assent (tasdiq) depends on conception (tasawwur), which depends on definition, so including a judgment would make conception depend on assent, reversing the epistemological order and causing circularity. Finally, the disjunctive 'or' is prohibited in essential definition because a single species cannot have two differentiae by way of substitution (badal), which would be impossible; it is, however, permitted in description.

This passage provides the grammatical parsing (i'rab) of the matn verses on definition, then adds two further logical distinctions. The commentator begins with the phrase 'wa-shart,' explaining the grammatical construction of the verse: the condition of every definition is a condition functioning as a subject (mubtada'), or the nunation of 'kull' compensates for a noun, and its connector is a predicate. Essential Definition versus Description: The Rule of 'Or'
Commentator explains the logical difference between essential definition and description.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish essential definition (hadd haqiqi) from description (rasm) by applying the rule that a species has only one differentia but many accidental properties.

Core Idea
A species can have many accidental properties, so 'or' works in description; but only one essential differentia, so 'or' destroys the definition.

After parsing the matn, the commentator draws a critical logical distinction: the difference between essential definition and description lies in the uniqueness of the differentia versus the multiplicity of properties. This rule has direct implications for how we define terms in Islamic sciences, from fiqh to kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definition discussed
The commentator contrasts 'haqiqi' (essential) and 'rasmi' (descriptive) definitions. Note which is called 'hadd' and which 'rasm'.
Essential definition gives the genus and differentia; description gives genus and accidental property.

The text states: 'al-naw' al-wahid la yakun lahu faslan' — a single species cannot have two differentiae. But it can have many properties (khawas).

The commentator gives 'al-hayawan al-dahik' (the laughing animal) as a description — note it uses 'or' to list multiple properties. In contrast, 'al-hayawan al-natiq' (the rational animal) is an essential definition — no 'or' is allowed.
The presence of 'or' signals description because it indicates multiple possible properties.
4
Consider the additional condition: part cannot be genus
The commentator adds: it is not allowed to make a part of the defined its genus, as in defining ten as 'five and five'. This ensures the genus is broader, not a part.
This reinforces that the genus must encompass the whole species, not be a component.

The Two Divisions: Categorical and Conditional
Author divides syllogism into categorical and conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between categorical and conditional syllogisms using the potency/act distinction from the passage and correctly classify arguments from Fiqh and Kalam.

Core Idea
The categorical contains the conclusion in potency; the conditional contains it in act.

After defining the syllogism, the author immediately classifies it into two species: categorical and conditional. The key to understanding the difference lies in how the conclusion is present in the premises: either potentially or actually. This distinction determines the structure of proof in each type and the rules for evaluating validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions in the Arabic.
Find the words 'اقترانياً' and 'شرطياً' in the phrase 'وهو قسمان: اقترانياً وشرطياً'. These are the names of the two types.
The word 'اقترانياً' means categorical; 'شرطياً' means conditional.
2
Understand the definition of each division.
The categorical is defined as 'ما يشتمل على نتيجة أو على نقيضها بالقوة' – what contains its conclusion or its contradictory in potency. The conditional is defined as 'ما يشتمل على النتيجة أو نقيضها بالفعل' – what contains its conclusion or its contradictory in act.
Potency (قوة) means the conclusion is implicit; act (فعل) means it is explicit in a conditional premise.
3
Contrast the modal language.
In a categorical syllogism, the conclusion is not stated in the premises but follows from them; it is contained potentially. In a conditional syllogism, the conclusion is explicitly part of the conditional proposition (e.g., 'If P then Q') and is extracted by affirming or denying the antecedent or consequent.
4
Apply the distinction to concrete examples.
Categorical: 'Every A is B; every B is C; therefore every A is C.' The conclusion 'every A is C' is not stated in the premises but is forced from them. Conditional: 'If Zaid is human then he is mortal; Zaid is human; therefore Zaid is mortal.' The conclusion 'Zaid is mortal' is explicitly the consequent of the first premise; the minor premise extracts it in act.
This helps you recognise each type when encountered in Islamic texts.
