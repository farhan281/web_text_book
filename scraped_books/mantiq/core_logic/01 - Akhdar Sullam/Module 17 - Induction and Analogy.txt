Induction: From Particulars to Probability
Author defines induction and states its epistemic limit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an inductive inference and explain why the classical tradition considers it probable rather than certain.

Core Idea
Induction tracks particulars to a universal, but can never guarantee complete coverage.

In daily reasoning and in Islamic sciences, we often argue from specific cases to general rules. The logicians call this induction (istiqrāʾ). But does this give certainty? The classical mantiq tradition says no — induction is only probable. In this lesson, we examine its definition and its limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of inference
The passage says induction moves 'from particular to universal.' Notice how this is the opposite of the categorical syllogism (universal to particular).
2
Extract the definition of induction
The footnote defines istiqrāʾ as 'the examination of the particulars of a universal and tracking them to establish their ruling for the universal.' So it is a survey process.
3
Evaluate the epistemic claim
The text states that induction 'does not yield certainty by proof.' Why? Because you cannot examine every particular of a universal; unexamined instances may contradict the conclusion.
Complete induction (where all particulars are examined) would yield certainty, but such cases are rare and the text implies the ordinary incomplete induction.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
In Usul al-Fiqh, when a jurist collects multiple hadith on a ruling to derive a general principle, that is induction. The result is zann (probability), not qatʿ (certainty).

Analogy (Tamthīl): Transference of a Ruling via a Common Cause
Author defines analogy and states its epistemic limit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an analogical argument (tamthīl) and explain why it yields probability, not certainty.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a ruling from one particular to another via a shared cause, but its conclusion is only probable.

In Islamic legal reasoning, analogy (qiyās fiqhī) is a central tool. But what is its logical structure? The logicians call it tamthīl: carrying one particular to another because they share the cause (ʿilla) of the ruling. The passage gives the classic example of wine and nabidh. In this lesson, we dissect this form and understand its epistemic status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of inference
The passage says analogy moves 'particular to particular.' This is different from induction (particular to universal) and syllogism (universal to particular).
2
Extract the definition of analogy
The footnote defines tamthīl as 'carrying one particular to another in its ruling due to their sharing in the cause of the ruling.' The key element is the common cause (ʿilla).
3
Map the example to the three terms
In the example 'Nabidh is intoxicating like wine, and every wine is forbidden, therefore nabidh is forbidden,' the minor term is nabidh, the major term is 'forbidden,' and the middle term (cause) is intoxication. The commentator explicitly identifies these.
4
Understand the epistemic limit
The text states that analogy 'does not yield certainty by proof.' The footnote adds that jurists accept it because legal rulings need only probability (zann). This is crucial: qiyās fiqhī is probabilistic, not certain.
5
Contrast with the categorical syllogism
The categorical syllogism (universal to particular) can yield certainty if premises are certain. Analogy, by contrast, depends on the strength of the cause (ʿilla) and the assumption that the cause is common, which is always open to doubt.

Induction and Analogy: Non-Demonstrative Reasoning in Islamic Logic
Definitions and epistemic evaluation of induction and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish induction and analogy from categorical syllogism and explain why they yield only probability (zann) not certainty (qat').

Core Idea
Induction and analogy are useful for fiqh but cannot produce certainty.

In this lesson, we examine two argument forms that Islamic logicians annex to the syllogism: induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). The author defines each and evaluates their epistemic status. You will learn why these forms yield only probable opinion (zann) and why jurists nonetheless rely on them for legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three argument forms
The passage distinguishes induction, logical syllogism, and analogy. Induction moves from particular to universal; logical syllogism moves from universal to particular; analogy moves from particular to particular via a common cause.
Note that the logical syllogism is the ideal form yielding certainty.
2
Analyze the definition of induction
Induction is defined as judging a universal based on most of its particulars, e.g., 'every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing' from observing humans, beasts, and predators. The author points out that this may fail due to counterexamples like the crocodile.
The example shows that induction is probabilistic, not certain.
3
Analyze the definition of analogy
Analogy (tamthil) is carrying a ruling from one particular (asl) to another (far') due to a shared cause ('illa). The example is nabidh intoxicating like wine, so nabidh is forbidden. The common cause (intoxication) serves as the middle term.
Jurists call this qiyas fiqhi and rely on it for legal rulings.
4
Evaluate the epistemic status
Both induction and analogy yield only zann (probability), not qat' (certainty). Induction fails because universality is not guaranteed; analogy fails because once the proof is established in the original case, examining another particular is unnecessary.
The text explicitly states they are suitable only for fiqh research, where probability suffices.

Commentary
This passage defines and evaluates two non-demonstrative argument forms annexed to the syllogism: induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). The author begins with the matn verses that set out the distinction: inferring from particular to universal is induction, while carrying one particular over another via a common factor is analogy. He then notes that neither yields certainty (qat'). In the sharh, he expands on induction first. He defines it as judging a universal based on most of its particulars, illustrating it with the example that every animal moves its lower jaw while chewing, inferred from humans, beasts, and predators. He immediately points out the epistemic gap: because universality is not guaranteed—since a crocodile might be an animal that does not move its lower jaw—induction yields only probability, not certainty. He contrasts this with its opposite, the categorical syllogism (al-qiyas al-mantiq), which moves from universal to particular and is productive of certainty. Next, he turns to analogy (tamthil), defined as establishing a ruling for one particular because it shares a meaning with another particular. He explains that this too is weak, because once the proof is established in the original case (al-mustadlal 'alayhi), there is no need to look at another particular; the original case alone suffices. Nevertheless, he allows that analogy has a therapeutic and persuasive function: it heals the soul and secures belief (tathqib al-nafs and tahsil al-i'tiqad), which is why jurists call it qiyas fiqhi and rely on it for legal rulings, since legal rulings need only attain the level of opinion (zann), not certainty. Finally, he returns to the matn verses, glossing each line to show that 'wa-in bi-juz'i' refers to induction and 'wa-haythu al-bayt' refers to analogy, and he reiterates that both are restricted to fiqh research and yield only zann.

In this lesson, we examine al-istiqra' (induction), one of the two non-syllogistic arguments that attach to formal qiyas. The commentator defines it and explains why it can never achieve the certainty of a proper categorical syllogism. Understanding this limitation is crucial for distinguishing between different types of reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage defines and evaluates two non-syllogistic argument forms that the author describes as attaching to qiyas: al-istiqra' (induction) and al-tamthil (analogy). He begins by announcing that the verses of the matn will point out these two types. He defines al-istiqra' as judging a universal based on its presence in most of its particulars, offering the example that every animal moves its lower jaw while chewing because humans, cattle, and beasts of prey do so. He immediately cautions that this does not yield certainty, since universality remains uncertain, citing the crocodile as an excluded particular that breaks the universal claim. He then contrasts this with its opposite: inference from the universal to the particular restricted to certainty, which is al-qiyas al-mantiqiyy proper, the subject of this art, and notes that it has already been discussed. Turning to al-tamthil, he defines it as establishing a ruling in one particular due to another particular sharing a common meaning with it. He judges it weak as well, because once proof is established for the case at hand, examining another particular becomes redundant; still, he grants that it serves to heal the soul and consolidate belief. He then maps these definitions back onto the matn: the phrase 'wa-in bi-juz'i' alludes to inferring from a particular to a universal, which is al-istiqra', while 'wa-haythu al-bayt' alludes to carrying over one particular to another via a common cause, which is al-tamthil. He concludes that both forms are unsuitable for demonstrative logic and are only fit for the investigations of the fuqaha', yielding only zann and not certainty, as the matn verse states.

Analogy (al-Tamthil) in Islamic Logic
Commentator defines al-tamthil, explains its weakness, and notes its practical utility in forming beliefs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-tamthil and explain why it is considered weak compared to formal syllogism.

Core Idea
Analogy is epistemically weak because proof in one case makes another case redundant, yet it serves practical belief formation.

This lesson focuses on al-tamthil (analogy), the second non-syllogistic argument form. The commentator defines it and evaluates its usefulness. Understanding tamthil is important because it is often used in fiqh reasoning, though logicians consider it inferior to deductive proof.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of tamthil
Locate the commentator's statement: 'al-tamthil: establishing a ruling in a particular due to a [another] particular having a shared meaning between them.' Identify the three components: two particulars and a shared meaning (illa).
2
Understand the weakness cited
The commentator says it is weak because if proof is already established for the case under consideration, then considering another particular is unnecessary. In other words, analogy is circular or redundant if the ruling is already known.
This critique applies when the ruling in the primary case is already proven; analogy adds no new evidence.
3
Recognize the positive role of tamthil
Despite its weakness, the commentator says tamthil is suitable for 'healing the soul and acquiring belief' (yuslihu li-tatbib al-nafs wa-tahsil al-i'tiqad). This suggests it has a psychological or pedagogical value, even if not logically compelling.
4
Contrast with formal syllogism and istiqra'
The commentator groups tamthil with istiqra' as both yielding only zann and being suited only for fiqh investigation, not for demonstrative proof. Recognize that tamthil is even weaker than istiqra' because it does not even generalize to a universal.
