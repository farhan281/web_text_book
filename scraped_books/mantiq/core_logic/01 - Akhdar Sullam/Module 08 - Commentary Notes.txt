Muʿtazila and Qadariyya as Warnings in Logic
Text defines Muʿtazila and Qadariyya as historical warnings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the doctrinal positions of the Muʿtazila and Qadariyya and explain why they serve as warnings in a logic manual.

Core Idea
The Muʿtazila and Qadariyya are named as warnings—their corruption of rational premises led them to alter the Sunna.

In studying al-Akhdari's logic manual, you encounter a brief warning about the Muʿtazila and Qadariyya. These notes are not historical trivia; they illustrate what happens when rational inquiry is divorced from revelation. Understanding their positions helps you see why the author insists on grounding syllogisms in sound premises derived from the Qur'an and Sunna.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definitions
Find where the text defines the Muʿtazila (Iʿtizal) and Qadariyya. Note the key characteristics: the Muʿtazila arose as a reaction to Jabr and Irjaʾ, affirming justice and divine unity; the Qadariyya affirm free will and human responsibility, holding that man's actions are not motivated by Allah's will.
The text explicitly counts the Muʿtazila among the Qadariyya.
2
Understand the historical context
The text says the Umayyads nourished Jabr and Irjaʾ for political reasons. Iʿtizal emerged as a backlash. This shows that theological positions can be influenced by political circumstances, a point the author uses to warn against letting extraneous motives corrupt one's method.
3
Relate the two sects
The text states that the Muʿtazila are among the Qadariyya. Both groups prioritized human reason over revealed texts in certain matters, leading to deviations in 'aqida. The author lumps them together as cautionary examples.
Common misunderstanding: that they are entirely separate.
4
Identify the warning function
The author invokes these sects to show that faulty rational premises—here, affirming human free will independently of Allah's will—lead to corrupt conclusions in theology and law. Thus, in logic, one must ensure that premises are sound by revelation.
This is why the notes appear in a logic manual, not a history book.

The Three Significations: Parsing and Conditions
Commentator parses the matn grammatically and orders the three significations by strength.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of signification (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) based on the commentator's parsing and apply the condition of mental necessity to identify true iltizam.

Core Idea
The three significations are ordered by strength; only mental entailment qualifies as iltizam, not mere external accompaniment.

In this lesson, we will walk through the commentator's grammatical and terminological parsing of the matn's verses on the three significations. You will learn not only the definitions of mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam, but also the crucial condition that iltizam requires rational necessity (luzum dhihni), not just any external association.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms being defined
Locate where the commentator names the three types: dalalat al-mutabaqa (correspondence), dalalat al-tadammun (inclusion), and dalalat al-iltizam (entailment). Note that the definitions are grammatically parsed from the matn.
2
Parse the syntax of the matn phrases
Follow the commentator's parsing: 'wa-juz'ihi tadamman' is genitive conjoined to 'ma wafaqahu', and 'wa-ma lazima' is also conjoined. This shows how the matn's poetic structure relates to the logical meanings.
Grammatical parsing helps reveal the intended logical relationships.
3
Distinguish the object of signification for each type
Mutabaqa signifies the entire meaning the word is coined for; tadammun signifies a part of that meaning; iltizam signifies something entailed by that meaning. Understand that the difference lies in what the word refers to directly vs. indirectly.
4
Apply the condition for iltizam
The commentator stipulates that iltizam requires mental necessity (luzum dhihni), not merely external accompaniment. Test examples: 'four' entails 'evenness' both mentally and externally; 'blackness' with 'crow' is only external, so not iltizam.
This condition is critical for defining true iltizam in logical analysis.
5
Order the significations by strength
The commentator states the ordering follows 'al-bada'ah' (immediacy). Mutabaqa is strongest because it directly indicates the entire coined meaning, then tadammun, then iltizam. Understand why: the more immediate the signification, the stronger.
This ordering is used in later arguments about which type of signification applies in various contexts.

Parsing Conditional and Disjunctive Particles in the Matn
Commentator parses matn phrases on conditional inna, amma, and aw huma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the grammatical features of conditional inna, amma, and aw huma in the context of disjunctive syllogisms and explain how they affect logical parsing.

Core Idea
The grammar of particles determines how logical disjunctions are parsed.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the grammatical usage of three key expressions from the matn that are crucial for understanding disjunctive syllogisms. The particles inna (if), amma (as for), and aw huma (or both each other) have specific syntactic properties that affect how the logical relationships are expressed. Understanding these grammatical nuances is essential for correctly interpreting the logical structure of the matn.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish conditional 'inna' from emphatic 'inna'
Note that the inna in the line is the conditional particle (in), not the emphatic. As a conditional, its apodosis must be introduced by fa.
The commentator explicitly says it is 'in shartiyya' and that it is 'qurina bil-fa' wajiban' (coupled with fa necessarily).
2
Recognize the elision of fa after 'amma'
The answer to amma normally requires fa, but it may be deleted in both prose and poetry. The commentator provides examples from the Prophet's khutba and a line of poetry.
This elision is a rhetorical license, not an error.
3
Parse 'aw huma' as a reference to the preventer
The phrase 'aw huma' (or they both) is shorthand for 'aw mani'at al-jam' wal-khuluw' (or the preventer of both conjunction and absence), indicating the true disjunctive preventer.
This shorthand is common in logic texts; the pronoun 'huma' refers to the two preventers taken together.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Poetic imagery is unpacked through precise grammar and astronomy to glorify the Creator.

This passage shows a scholar returning to a line of poetry from his sermon. He does not simply quote it; he breaks down its grammatical structure and fills in the astronomical facts behind the imagery. This method of 'expansion' (bast) is common in Islamic scholarly writing, where a single phrase becomes a gateway to multiple disciplines. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recognize and replicate this scholarly approach.

Commentary
This passage is an astronomical and grammatical expansion explaining the imagery of a poetic line employed in the sermon. The author returns to the line 'ma qata'at shams al-nahar abrajan wa tala'a al-badr al-munir fi al-daja', noting that earlier in the sermon he had already discussed the prayer upon the Prophet. He then turns to the grammatical analysis of the phrase, stating that the 'ma' in 'ma qata'at al-bayt' is masdariyya and zarfiyya. He clarifies that the word 'abraaj', though morphologically a plural of paucity, is intended as a plural of multitude because the zodiacal signs are twelve. He adds that the length of al-malawin and their shortness occurs according to the northern and southern declination, due to the widening and narrowing of the arc in the inclined horizons that possess latitude. Turning to the moon, he notes that it remains in each sign for two nights and a third, completing its orbit in a month. He concludes this reflection with a glorification of the Former of the universes, followed by the customary prayers and closing formulae. The passage thus ties the poetic imagery to precise astronomical data and grammatical terminology, directing the listener from the observable order of the heavens to the power of its Maker.
