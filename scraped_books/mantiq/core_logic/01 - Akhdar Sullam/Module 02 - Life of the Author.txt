Reading an Editor's Biography Introduction
Table of contents for the editor's biography of al-Akhdari.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of an editor's biographical introduction in a critical edition of an Islamic text.

Core Idea
An editor's introduction establishes the author's authority through birth, learning, works, and stature.

When you open a critical edition of a classical Islamic text, the editor often provides an introduction about the author. This passage is a table of contents for such an introduction, listing the topics that establish al-Akhdari's scholarly identity. The editor (muhaqqiq) carefully selects these topics to give you confidence in the author's credentials and the text's reliability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the editor's role
Note that the passage begins with 'مقدمة المحقق' (Introduction of the Investigator). The muhaqqiq is the modern editor who prepared this edition. Understanding that this is the editor's voice, not the author's, is crucial.
The editor is a scholar who compares manuscripts.
2
List the biographical topics
Read through the list: مولده (birth), نسبه (lineage), البيئة والمنشأ (environment and upbringing), تعلمه وشيوخه (learning and teachers), نبوغه في العلم (brilliance in knowledge), مؤلفاته (works), البعد العلمي (scientific stature), وفاته (death). Write them down.
These are standard topics in Islamic biographical dictionaries.
3
Group the topics by function
Understand why each is included: birth and lineage establish identity; environment and upbringing show context; learning and teachers prove transmission; brilliance and works demonstrate contribution; scientific stature shows recognition; death marks completion.
The editor builds a comprehensive picture of authority.
4
Connect to the text being introduced
Relate these topics to the text you are about to study (here, al-Akhdari's al-Sullam). The introduction prepares you to trust that al-Akhdari is a qualified author on logic.
Always ask: how does each topic support the author's authority?

Al-Akhdari's Sullam: Authority and Biography
Editor situates the text in the curriculum and records al-Akhdari's biography.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract from a biographical preface the evidence for a text's scholarly authority and explain how the author's life circumstances enhance its credibility.

Core Idea
A text's institutional reception and its author's precocity both argue for its scholarly value.

In the Islamic tradition, the authority of a text is not only based on its content but also on its reception and the standing of its author. The editor of this edition of al-Akhdari's Sullam marshals both kinds of evidence. He shows that the text became a standard curriculum work at major institutions like al-Azhar and Zaytuna, and that al-Akhdari composed it before reaching legal majority—a sign of remarkable intellectual maturity. Understanding how scholars establish textual authority is essential for evaluating the sources you will study in logic and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the passage's genre
This is an editor's preface. Recognise that its goal is to justify the importance of the text and the edition. The editor is not explaining logic but embedding the text in a scholarly tradition.
Editorial prefaces often contain valuable contextual keys.
2
Extract the evidence of textual authority
The editor points to three signs: (1) the Sullam is a matn muqarrar, a set text in global Islamic institutes; (2) it was translated into French; (3) it generated numerous commentaries (shuruhat) and glosses. List each piece of evidence.
Matn muqarrar indicates curricular endorsement by leading institutions.
3
Analyse the biographical argument
The editor claims al-Akhdari wrote his books before reaching the age of majority (ashudd). This is presented as evidence of his exceptional ability. Note the implied inference: if his youth produced such work, his full maturity would have yielded even more.
Ashudd in fiqh is the age of full legal responsibility; here it measures intellectual precocity.
4
Connect biography to textual value
Reflect: why would the editor include these facts? They build a case that the author was a prodigy, making his text especially worth studying. The institutional recognition confirms that the scholarly community accepted this judgment.
This two‑pronged argument combines external (institutional) and internal (biographical) authority.

Al-Akhdari's Birth Date as a Chronogram in His Poem
Author records al-Akhdari's lifespan, birthplace, and the verse preserving his birth year.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract a scholar's birth year from a poetic chronogram and verify it against manuscript evidence.

Core Idea
A poet's own verses can preserve his birth date as a chronogram.

This passage introduces us to the biographical data of Abd al-Rahman al-Akhdari, the author of the logical poem Sullam al-Ulum. Rather than relying on later historians alone, the editor turns to al-Akhdari's own rajaz poem al-Sullam al-Murawnaq. The final verse contains a hidden date. Understanding how a chronogram works trains us to read sources carefully.

Guided Path
1
Locate the claim about birth year
The passage explicitly states that al-Akhdari was born in 920 AH (1514 CE) and died in 953 AH (1546 CE). Note the corresponding years in both calendars.
2
Identify the documentary source
The editor says the birth date was copied from al-Akhdari's own rajaz poem titled al-Sullam al-Murawnaq. This is an example of using the author's own work as a primary biographical source.
The poem is not his famous logical work but a separate metrical composition.
3
Examine the quoted chronogram line
The line 'ولبني إحدى وعشرين سنة معذرة مقبولة مستحسنة' is a chronogram. Each Arabic letter has a numerical value; the phrase is crafted to sum to the year 920 AH (or he mentions 'twenty-one' as part of the chronogram). Observe how the editor interprets this as a birth date.
Chronograms use abjad numerals; 'twenty-one' may refer to a combination of letters.
4
Reflect on the methodology
This demonstrates a common practice in Islamic biography: using internal evidence from an author's works to verify dates. Consider the reliability: could the poet have used poetic license? Compare with other sources if available.
The editor does not compute the chronogram explicitly here, but it is implied.

Extracting Biography from Internal Evidence
Extracts al-Akhdari's birth year from his poem and traces his lineage to a companion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract an author's birth year from a chronogram in his own work and trace his lineage to a companion.

Core Idea
Biographical data in the Islamic tradition is often derived from internal evidence in the author's own works.

How do we know when a scholar was born, especially when later reference works disagree? This lesson shows how al-Akhdari's own poem provided the key to his birth year, correcting errors in modern biographical dictionaries. We will also examine his full lineage, which connects him to a famous companion of the Prophet (ﷺ), establishing his noble Arab descent.

Guided Path
1
Locate the chronogram in the poem
Find the line in al-Sullam al-Murawniq where al-Akhdari says 'And for my son twenty-one years' (ولبني إحدى وعشرين سنة). This line indicates his age at the time of composition.
The chronogram uses the phrase 'for my son' but actually refers to the author's own age.
2
Identify the composition date
The poem was completed in Muharram 941 AH. This is stated in the same passage. Combining the age (21) with the composition year (941) allows calculation of the birth year.
941 AH minus 21 years equals 920 AH, the correct birth year.
3
Reconstruct the lineage chain
Trace the lineage from al-Akhdari up to the companion 'Abbas ibn Mirdas al-Sulami. The passage gives the full chain: al-Akhdari ibn al-Saghir ibn Muhammad ibn 'Amir ... to 'Abbas. Verify that each link is plausible.
Lineage is a standard way to establish scholarly credentials in Islamic biography.

Core Idea

See his biography in Nuzhat al-Albāʾ fī Ṭabaqāt al-Udabāʾ p. 66 by Abū al-Barakāt Kamāl al-Dīn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Muḥammad al-Anbārī, edited by Muḥammad Abū al-Faḍl Ibrāhīm, al-Maktaba al-ʿAṣriyya, Ṣaydā Beirut.
(2) Al-Naḥḥās is: Aḥmad ibn Muḥammad ibn Ismāʿīl al-Naḥḥās, from the students of al-Zajjāj, and he is called al-Ṣaffār; and he is other than Ibn al-Naḥḥās the grammarian who died in the year 698 AH. (3) Al-Zubaydī is: Abū Bakr Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥasan ibn ʿAbd Allāh ibn Mudḥaj al-Zubaydī al-Ishbīlī, resident of Cordoba, from the students of Abū ʿAlī al-Qālī the linguist. He died, may God have mercy on him, in the year 658 AH. See Bughyat al-Wuʿāt fī Ṭabaqāt al-Lughawiyyīn wa al-Naḥāṭ by al-Ḥāfiẓ Jalāl al-Dīn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Suyūṭī, vol. 2 p. 330, Dār al-Fikr, Beirut Lebanon.

Distinguishing Scholars with Shared Names: Al-Akhfash and Ibn Mālik
Footnotes identifying grammarians and their sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two al-Akhfash scholars and the two Ibn Mālik scholars by their full names, works, and death dates, using the biographical clues in a commentary footnote.

Core Idea
A single name like 'al-Akhfash' can refer to multiple scholars; context and biography unlock the correct reference.

In classical Islamic texts, especially commentaries, scholars are often referred to by short epithets that can be ambiguous. This lesson will train you to unpack such biographical footnotes to identify the precise scholar intended.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The colophon is the silent witness connecting you to the hand that wrote this manuscript centuries ago.

Every Islamic manuscript carries a hidden signature—the colophon. This scribal note records the copyist's name, intention, and the exact moment of completion. More than a signature, it is a witness to the chain of transmission (isnad) that connects you, the reader, to the author via the physical act of copying. Understanding the colophon teaches you to read not just the text, but the human context of its preservation.

1

Find the phrase 'كمل على يد كاتبه' (completed by the hand of its copyist). The scribe names himself as Ahmad ibn Musa al-Shawli al-Sadmi. Note his self-description as 'al-muftaqir ila Mawlahu' (the needy one to his Master) – a common pious epithet.
The copyist (katib) is distinct from the author (mu'allif).
2
Determine the purpose of copying
The phrase 'لنفسه ولمن شاء بعده' (for himself and for whoever wishes after him) reveals the intention: the copyist wrote for his own use and for future readers. This reflects the ethic of knowledge dissemination.
3
Locate the completion date and occasion
The key phrase is 'وكان الفراغ منه بعد صلاة عيد الفطر عام ١١٩٨هـ' (the completion was after the Eid al-Fitr prayer in the year 1198 AH). Note the precise date, including the religious occasion that marks the timing.
The date AH 1198 corresponds to 1784 CE. The occasion (Eid al-Fitr) adds a personal touch.
4
Analyze the supplications and their function
The colophon ends with prayers for forgiveness: for himself, his parents, all readers, and all believers. These supplications (ad'iya) are standard but reinforce the spiritual dimension of copying—an act of worship seeking mercy.
The phrase 'اللهم ارحم من يدعو لنا بالرحمة' (O Allah, have mercy on whoever prays for us with mercy) creates a reciprocal prayer chain.

Understanding the Manuscript Colophon
Author records manuscript completion date and scribe identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a classical Islamic manuscript colophon and explain its function in preserving the chain of transmission.

Core Idea
A colophon is the fingerprint of a manuscript—it tells you who copied it, when, and for whom.

The passage you have just read is not logic per se but the scribe's closing note at the end of a manuscript. Colophons are invaluable for tracing transmission history. In this lesson, we will dissect this colophon to see what information it carries and why it matters.

Guided Path
1
Locate the scribe's name
Look for phrases like 'كمل على يد كاتبه' followed by a name. Here it is 'أحمد أحمد بن موسى الشولي الصدمي' (with a possible duplication).
Scribes often include their full genealogy and epithets.
2
Find the date of completion
Search for phrases indicating time, like 'الفراغ منه بعد'. Here it is 'بعد صلاة عيد الفطر عام ۱۱۹۸هـ'.
Dates are often tied to religious events, not just regnal years.
3
Identify prayers and dedications
Look for supplications (دعاء) for the scribe, his parents, readers, and all believers. This shows the pious context.
Such prayers are common in colophons.
4
Examine the astronomical aside
The scribe notes that 'أبراج' is a plural of paucity (جمع قلة) used for multitude—a grammatical observation reflecting his scholarly interest.
This aside is not part of the logical content but shows the scribe's breadth.

Commentary
This passage is a colophon and completion notice at the end of a manuscript. The author or scribe begins with a poetic line about the sun and moon traversing the zodiacal mansions, then offers a brief astronomical aside explaining that the word 'mansions' (أبراج) is formally a plural of paucity (جمع قلة) but here intends multiplicity, since there are twelve mansions each of thirty degrees. He notes that the sun traverses one degree daily and completes the sphere in a year, while the moon lingers roughly two and one-third nights per mansion and completes the sphere in a month. After this astronomical flourish, the scribe records his name — Ahmad ibn Musa al-Shawli al-Sadmi — and states that he copied the work for himself and for whoever comes after him. He dates the completion to after the Eid al-Fitr prayer in the year 1198 AH, and closes with prayers for mercy and forgiveness upon himself, his parents, the readers, and all believers. This is not a logical teaching but a manuscript closing that preserves the transmission chain and the historical moment of the text's material completion.
