The Nine Introductory Headings of Any Islamic Science
Table of contents previews the definition and divisions of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the nine standard introductory headings (al-umur al-tis'a) for any Islamic science, using the example of logic as given in the passage.

Core Idea
The nine headings are the anatomy of an Islamic science.

Before diving into the technical content of logic, the editor sets out the field's scope through a fixed set of introductory topics. These nine headings—definition, subject, benefit, fruit, relation, founder, name, derivation, and issues—are a standard template used across Islamic sciences. Recognizing this structure helps you orient yourself within any new discipline.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the context
This passage is the beginning of an introduction to logic. It lists the topics that will be covered to define the science before presenting its technical content.
In Islamic scholarship, every science is introduced by a set of preliminary questions.
2
Identify each heading
Read the Arabic list: تعريف، موضوع، فائدة، ثمرة، نسبة، واضع، اسم، استمداد، مسائل. Match each with its English meaning.
There are nine items; note the commas and conjunctions.
3
Understand the purpose of each
Each heading answers a specific question: definition (what is it?), subject (what does it study?), benefit (why should we learn it?), fruit (what immediate result?), relation (how does it relate to other sciences?), founder (who initiated it?), name (what is its proper name?), derivation (from what prior sources?), issues (what problems does it address?).
Distinguish between benefit (fa'ida) and fruit (thamara) — the latter is the specific outcome.
4
Apply the template to another science
Think of usul al-fiqh or nahw. How would its introduction use these nine headings? This shows the structural consistency of Islamic sciences.

Definition of Logic and Its Essential Elements
Definition of logic and its essential attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of logic and list its subject, benefit, fruit, and relation to other sciences as given in al-Akhdari's Sullam.

Core Idea
Logic is the tool that moves from known to unknown, protecting the mind and enabling proof.

This passage from al-Akhdari's Sullam al-Murawnaq lays out the foundational definition of logic. It does not merely state a label but specifies the subject matter, purpose, and relationship logic has to every other science. Understanding this definition is the first step in mastering the entire discipline, because it tells you exactly what logic is and is not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the formal definition of logic
Read the first sentence: 'هو علم يبحث فيه عن المعلومات التصورية والتصديقية من حيث أنها توصل إلى معرفة مجهول تصوري...' Locate the genus (science) and differentia (investigates conceptive and assentive knowables insofar as they lead to the unknown).
The definition is a complete hadd haqiqi.
2
Distinguish the two types of knowables
The text divides knowables into 'تصورية' (conceptive) and 'تصديقية' (assentive). Determine what each one produces: a declarative/defining statement (قول شارح ومعرف) for conceptive, a proof (حجة) for assentive.
These correspond to the two fundamental operations of the mind: grasping a simple meaning and affirming a composed meaning as true.
3
Extract the subject of logic
The subject (موضوعه) is 'المعلوم التصوري والمعلوم التصديقي من هذه الحيثية' — i.e., the conceptive and assentive knowables considered precisely under the aspect of leading to the unknown.
Logic does not study things in themselves but as instruments of inference.
4
Note the benefit, fruit, and relation
The benefit is protecting the mind from error in thought (the text here is corrupt but the intended meaning is clear). The fruit is the ability to establish proofs and defend true doctrines, leading to eternal felicity. The relation to other sciences is that logic clarifies, verifies, and defends their principles.
These three attributes (benefit, fruit, relation) are standard in defining any science.

Commentary
This passage defines the science of logic and lists its essential accidents: subject, benefit, fruit, and relation to other sciences. The author opens with a formal definition: logic is a science that investigates knowables — both conceptive (tasawwur) and assentive (tasdiq) — insofar as they lead to knowledge of something previously unknown. When the unknown is conceptive, the resulting expression is called a qawl sharh or ma'rifa; when the unknown is assentive, it is called a hujja. The subject of logic is therefore the conceptive and assentive knowables considered precisely under this aspect — that is, as instruments for conveying to the unknown. The benefit is stated as protecting the mind from error in thought, though the Arabic here is corrupt and likely intends to say that logic safeguards the intellect from falling into error. The fruit is practical and salvific: the capacity to construct proofs and demonstrations, to defend true beliefs, and thereby to secure eternal felicity. Finally, the relation of logic to other sciences is instrumental and clarificatory: logic does not constitute the substance of those sciences but rather elucidates their contents, verifies their principles, and defends their foundations against objection.

The Instrumental and Scientific Definitions of Logic
Author defines logic and its benefit through poetic allusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the instrumental and scientific definitions of logic as presented in the passage.

Core Idea
Logic is defined either as an instrument that protects thought when observed, or as a science of mental transition.

In the opening lines, the author uses a poetic comparison to allude to the definition and fruit of logic. He then reports a scholarly disagreement on whether logic is an instrument or a science. Understanding these two definitions is essential for grasping the purpose and scope of logic in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Find the exact phrases in the passage that define logic as an instrument and as a science. The instrumental definition calls logic 'a canonical instrument whose observance in thought protects,' while the scientific definition calls it 'a science by which one knows the manner of transition from matters occurring in the mind to matters deduced in it.'
2
Compare the genera
In the instrumental definition, logic is categorized as an 'instrument' (āla). In the scientific definition, it is categorized as a 'science' (ʿilm). Note the difference in the genus term.
3
Examine the differentiae
For the instrumental definition, the differentia is 'whose observance protects thought.' For the scientific definition, it is 'by which one knows the manner of transition...' Recognize that each differentia highlights a different aspect of logic's function.
4
Understand the disagreement
The text does not take sides but reports both views. This disagreement was live among scholars. Reflect on why defining logic as a science or an instrument has different implications for its role in the curriculum and its relation to other disciplines.
Later madrasa texts often favor the scientific definition, while the instrumental view is attributed to earlier or philosophical framings.

The Fruit of Logic: Observance Protects from Error
Author defines logic and its benefit through poetic allusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the observance of logic, not logic itself, prevents error, using the analogy with grammar.

Core Idea
Logic's benefit is realized only through its observance; mere knowledge of logic does not guarantee correctness.

The author's poetic comparison likens logic for the mind to grammar for the tongue. Just as knowing grammar does not prevent a grammarian from making a solecism if he is inattentive, so too knowing logic does not prevent a logician from erring if he fails to observe its rules. This clarifies the true fruit of logic.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the poetic comparison
The first line says logic is to the mind as grammar is to the tongue. What does grammar do for the tongue? It protects from error. Logic does the same for the mind. Recognize the analogy: both disciplines safeguard their respective domains from mistakes.
2
Understand the qualification 'whose observance'
The text explicitly notes that the phrase 'whose observance' is a notification that logic itself does not protect, but rather observing its rules. Highlight the difference between possessing logic and practicing it.
3
Apply the analogy with grammar
A grammarian may err (make a solecism) due to inattention. Similarly, a logician may err due to inattention even if he knows the rules. This shows that knowledge alone is insufficient; active adherence is required.
4
Articulate the fruit of logic
The fruit (thamara) of logic is not automatic correctness but the potential for correctness when the rules are observed. The benefit is conditional on diligent application.
This is a key insight: logic is a tool, not a guarantee.

The Instrument vs Science Dispute in Defining Logic
Author proves mantiq is Arabic and gives two competing definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the instrumental and scientific definitions of logic and explain why the dispute is verbal.

Core Idea
Logic safeguards thought only when its rules are actively observed, not by mere existence.

The commentator opens by establishing that the word 'mantiq' is authentically Arabic, then presents two poetic lines that allude to logic's definition. He records a dispute among scholars: is logic an instrument (āla) or a science (ʿilm)? The answer is not merely academic; it affects how we understand the role of logical rules in protecting the mind from error. In this lesson, you will analyze both definitions and see why the disagreement is ultimately verbal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Find the two definitions the commentator presents. One defines logic as an 'instrument' (āla) and the other as a 'science' (ʿilm). Note the exact phrasing in Arabic.
The instrumental definition: 'al-manṭiq āla qānūniyya taʿṣimu murāʿātuhā fī l-fikr'. The scientific definition: 'al-manṭiq ʿilm yuʿraf bihi kayfiyyat al-intiqāl min umūr ḥāṣila fī l-dhihn ilā umūr mustaḥṣala fīhi'.
2
Extract the key elements of each definition
For the instrumental definition, note that it emphasizes 'observance' (murāʿa) as the active safeguard; logic itself does not automatically protect. For the scientific definition, note the focus on 'transition' (intiqāl) from known (ḥāṣil) to extracted (mustaḥṣal) mental contents.
The instrumental definition has a condition: benefit depends on the logician's attention. The scientific definition is descriptive of logic's function.
3
Compare the two definitions
Both definitions describe the same reality but from different perspectives. The instrumental view treats logic as a tool to be used, while the scientific view treats it as a body of knowledge about correct reasoning.
The commentator states that the dispute is 'verbal' (lafẓī), meaning the difference is in wording, not in the actual subject matter.
4
Understand the conditionality of benefit
The instrumental definition explicitly states that logic's benefit relies on 'murāʿātuhā' (its observance). The commentator gives an analogy: just as a grammarian may lapse into solecism due to inattention, a logician may err if he fails to observe the rules.
This highlights that logic is not a magical shield but a discipline that requires active adherence.

Definition of Contradiction and Rules for Determining the Contradictory
Author defines contradiction and explains how to find the contradictory of singular, indefinite, and quantified propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions of logical contradiction and determine the contradictory of any categorical proposition (singular, indefinite, or quantified) in Islamic arguments.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires two propositions that differ only in quality and necessarily have opposite truth-values.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of contradiction (tanaquḍ) as presented by al-Akhdar in the Sullam. After categorizing propositions, the author turns to their logical relations, beginning with contradiction. We will break down the precise conditions that make two propositions contradictories and learn the rule for finding the contradictory of singular, indefinite, and quantified propositions. This tool is essential for ensuring consistency in theological and legal argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of contradiction
Locate the sentence in the Arabic that defines tanaquḍ. Note the two conditions: the propositions must differ in quality (affirmation vs. negation), and they must necessarily have opposite truth-values (one true, one false).
The definition is: 'اختلاف قضيتين بالإيجاب والسلب بحيث يقتضي أن تكون إحداهما صادقة والأخرى كاذبة'.
2
Extract the essential conditions
The author emphasizes that the only difference allowed is in quality (kayf); subject and predicate must remain the same. Moreover, it is not enough that they merely differ — they must be such that one is true and the other false necessarily.
3
Distinguish between singular/indefinite and quantified propositions
For propositions without a quantifier (singular or indefinite), the contradictory is simply the opposite quality. For quantified propositions (with a universal or particular quantifier), the rule involves both changing the quantifier to its opposite and flipping the quality.
Universal affirmative becomes particular negative; universal negative becomes particular affirmative.
4
Apply the rule to an Islamic example
Take the proposition 'كل عالم حادث' (Every temporally originated thing is created). Its contradictory is 'بعض العالم ليس بحادث' (Some temporally originated thing is not created). Verify that it satisfies the conditions: same subject and predicate, opposite quality (affirmative vs. negative), and opposite quantifier (universal vs. particular).

Commentary
This passage from the Sullam defines the logical concept of contradiction (tanaquḍ) and then provides the rules for determining the contradictory (naqīḍ) of any given proposition. It begins by stating that after discussing the divisions of propositions, we now turn to their rulings, starting with contradiction. The definition given is that contradiction is the opposition of two propositions in quality (affirmative vs. negative) such that it is necessarily the case that one is true and the other false. This is the standard definition in Aristotelian logic. The commentary then emphasizes that the only difference allowed between two contradictory propositions must be in quality; they cannot differ in subject or predicate. The necessity of one being true and the other false is what guarantees the relation of contradiction. The verses (from the matn) lay out the method for finding the contradictory. For singular propositions (e.g., 'Zayd is standing') and indefinite propositions (e.g., 'Man is an animal'), the contradictory is simply obtained by changing the quality: from affirmative to negative or vice versa. For quantified propositions (those with a quantifier like 'every' or 'some'), the rule is to replace the quantifier with its opposite and also change the quality if needed. Specifically, the contradictory of a universal affirmative (every A is B) is a particular negative (some A is not B). The contradictory of a universal negative (no A is B) is a particular affirmative (some A is B). The commentary illustrates with examples: 'Every human is an animal' has its contradictory 'Not some human is an animal' (interpreted as particular negative), and 'Nothing of human is stone' has its contradictory 'Some human is stone'. Finally, it notes a grammatical point in the verse: conditional sentences normally require a 'fa' in the answer, but it was omitted for poetic meter. This lesson is foundational for understanding logical opposition and immediate inferences.

Defining Ummahat al-Mantiq and the Praised Logic
Author concludes and defines ummahat al-mantiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'ummahat al-mantiq' using its literal meaning and explain why logic is praised in Islamic scholarship.

Core Idea
Logic is praised because it safeguards thought from error and distinguishes true knowledge from false.

In this concluding passage, the author clarifies what he means by 'ummahat al-mantiq'—the foundational roots of logic. He also explains why logic is a praiseworthy discipline, linking its virtue to protecting the intellect. Understanding this definition helps us appreciate the role logic plays in all Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'ummahat al-mantiq'
Find this term in the Arabic text. Note that 'ummahat' is the plural of 'umm' (mother). The author defines it as 'usul masa'ilih wa muhimmatih' (the roots of its issues and its important matters).
2
Analyze the etymology of 'umm'
The author explains that 'umm' means origin, using Mecca (Umm al-Qura) as an example. Reflect on how this metaphor applies: just as Mecca is the origin of all lands, the foundational principles are the origin of all logical topics.
3
Extract the definition's components
The definition has two parts: (1) it includes the roots (usul) of the science, and (2) it includes the important matters (muhimmat). The author does not give a strict genus-differentia, but rather a descriptive definition (rasm).
Contrast with a strict logical definition (hadd) which would require a genus and differentia.
4
Examine why logic is praised
The author says logic is 'mamdud' (praised) because it protects thought (fir) from error and distinguishes sound theoretical knowledge from unsound. Note that this is an epistemological justification.
