The Invalid Views: Generated and Necessary Connections
Author rejects the third and fourth views on the nature of entailment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Muʿtazilī doctrine of tawallud and the philosophers' necessary causation, and explain why the author rejects them.

Core Idea
Both the Muʿtazilī claim that the servant's power generates the conclusion and the philosophers' claim that premises are a self-sufficient cause are invalid according to Ashʿarī theology.

Two of the four views are explicitly rejected by the author. The Muʿtazila argued that the servant's created power first acts on the premises, and then 'generates' (tawallud) the conclusion as a second effect. The philosophers held that the premises are a cause that necessarily produces the conclusion by their own essence. Both views attribute causal efficacy to created things, which the Ashʿarīs deny.

Guided Path
1
Locate the third and fourth views
In the passage, after the customary view, find the third (tawallud) and fourth (wājib) views.
2
Understand tawallud (generation)
The created power (al-qudra al-ḥāditha) acts on the premises, and that act generates the conclusion. This implies the servant has causal efficacy.
3
Understand the necessary connection
The premises themselves, without divine intervention, produce the conclusion by their own nature.
4
Identify the refutation
The author says both are invalid because proof (burhān) has been established that the servant has no real effect and that no created cause or nature has true efficacy; only Allah is the free agent.

This is the view of the philosophers, meaning that knowledge or opinion of the two premises is a cause that effectively produces knowledge or opinion of the conclusion by its own essence. Commentary
This passage discusses the nature of the entailment (dalāla) between premises and conclusion in a syllogism. After noting that the connection between proof and result is termed 'indication', the author presents four prominent views on its source. The first view, attributed to Imām al-Ḥaramayn al-Juwaynī, holds that the connection is rational (ʿaqlī) and cannot fail: whenever a person has knowledge (or opinion) of the premises, he must have knowledge (or opinion) of the conclusion, because Allah creates both simultaneously. The relationship is likened to the inseparable concomitance between accident and substance. The second view, from Abū al-Ḥasan al-Ashʿarī, says the connection is merely habitual (ʿādī): it is possible for someone to know the premises yet not know the conclusion if he lacks the acumen to notice that the minor term is subsumed under the middle term. However, the author objects that noticing this subsumption is actually a condition for a valid syllogism, so this scenario is questionable. The third view is the Muʿtazilī theory of generation (tawallud): the servant's created power first acts on the premises, thereby generating the conclusion. The author dismisses this as invalid because, according to Ashʿarī theology, the servant has no real causal efficacy. The fourth view, held by philosophers (falāsifa), posits that the premises are a cause that produces the conclusion by its own nature. This is also rejected because it implies causal efficacy in created things, contra the Ashʿarī doctrine that Allah alone is the true agent. The author (the versifier of the text, al-nāẓim) gives preference to the first (rational) view.

Formal Errors in Demonstration: Two Causes
Author explains the two causes of formal errors in demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two formal causes of error in demonstration: absence of a repeated middle term and violation of figure-specific conditions.

Core Idea
A valid demonstration requires not only true premises but also correct syllogistic form.

A demonstration is not just any argument; it must follow strict formal rules. Even if premises are true, the argument can fail if the form is defective. In this lesson, we learn two key formal errors from the text: first, the absence of a repeated middle term; second, violating conditions like the minor in the first figure being affirmative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two causes
From the passage, list the two causes of error in the form (ṣūra) of demonstration: (1) departing from the four valid figures because the middle term is not repeated, and (2) neglecting a condition required for producing a conclusion in a given figure.
These causes are explicitly named by the author.
2
Understand the first cause: no repeated middle term
The author gives the example: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Every horse is a neighing creature'. These premises have no common term linking them – 'human' and 'horse' are different subjects, 'animal' and 'neighing' are different predicates. Without a repeated middle term, no categorical syllogism can be formed.
A valid categorical syllogism requires a middle term that appears in both premises.
3
Understand the second cause: violating figure conditions
In the first figure, the minor premise must be affirmative and the major premise must be universal. The passage gives two invalid examples: (a) a negative minor: 'Nothing from human is a horse' and 'Every horse is a body' – the minor is negative; (b) a particular major: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Some animals are neighing' – the major is particular.
Each figure has its own conditions (shurūṭ al-intāj). Ignoring them prevents a valid conclusion.
4
Test validity of given premise pairs
Given a pair of premises, first check if a middle term is repeated. If yes, identify the figure and verify whether the conditions for that figure (e.g., minor affirmative in first figure) are satisfied. If either condition fails, the syllogism is formally invalid.
Practice with examples from Islamic sciences to internalize the checks.

Formal Fallacy: No Repeated Middle Term
Author gives an example of disconnected premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a pair of premises lacks a repeated middle term and thus does not form a valid categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
A syllogism without a common middle term is no syllogism at all.

The author introduces the first cause of formal error: the premises do not belong to any of the four figures because the middle term is not repeated. Without a common term, the premises are disconnected and no conclusion follows necessarily. This lesson focuses on recognizing this specific defect.

Identify the two premises
Locate the two statements in the example. They are 'Every human is an animal' and 'Every horse is a neighing thing.'

The middle term must be repeated to connect the premises.
3
Recognise the error
Since no term is common, the premises do not form a valid syllogistic figure. This is the first type of formal fallacy: departure from the four figures.
The author calls it 'error in form' (ghalat fi al-sura).
