Induction: Definition, Types, and Certainty
Defines induction and its types, and explains its limitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define induction, distinguish complete from incomplete induction, and explain why induction does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Induction moves from particular to universal, but its conclusions are never certain.

Induction is a key reasoning tool in Islamic sciences, especially in usul al-fiqh when inferring general rulings from specific cases. However, the author clarifies that it is not a demonstrative proof. This lesson unpacks his definition, the distinction between complete and incomplete induction, and the reason for its lack of certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of inference in induction
Induction moves from particular(s) to a universal conclusion. The author defines it as 'inference from the ruling of the particular to the universal.'
2
Distinguish complete from incomplete induction
Complete induction examines all particulars and yields certainty, but it is rarely achievable. Incomplete induction examines only some particulars and yields probability.
3
Understand why induction lacks certainty
The author states that even in incomplete induction, a counterexample may exist. He gives the example of the crocodile that moves its upper jaw, contradicting the induced generalisation that all animals move their lower jaw.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider intoxicants: observing that khamr, nabidh, and beer are prohibited due to intoxication, one might induce that all intoxicants are prohibited. This induction is incomplete and thus not certain.

Commentary
This passage defines two non-demonstrative forms of inference: induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil), and explains why they do not produce certainty. The poem lines (110-113) introduce them: when a particular is used to infer a universal, it is induction; the reverse (universal to particular) is the logical syllogism; when a particular is applied to another particular due to a commonality, it is analogy. The prose commentary then expands on each. Induction is defined as examining particulars and judging the universal based on their common property. An example is given: observing that humans, horses, and donkeys move their lower jaw when chewing, one concludes that all animals move their lower jaw when chewing. This is incomplete induction because not all animals were examined. Complete induction occurs when all particulars are examined, as in the example of observing that every known animal (whether walking or not) dies, thus concluding 'Every animal dies.' The opposite of induction is the logical syllogism, which moves from universal to particular; the text gives the example of deducing that every human is a body from the premises 'every human is an animal' and 'every animal is a body.' Analogy is then defined: inferring a ruling for a particular based on its similarity to another particular in a relevant attribute. The common example is applying the prohibition of khamr (wine) to nabidh (date wine) due to the shared cause of intoxication. The passage quotes al-Sa'd al-Taftazani's definition, which emphasizes the comparison of two particulars in a shared meaning to transfer the ruling. Finally, the text states the fundamental rule: neither induction nor analogy yields certainty. For induction, an exception may exist (e.g., the crocodile moves its upper jaw, not lower, contradicting the generalization). The implication is that only demonstration (burhan) yields certainty. Islamic examples: in fiqh, analogy is used to extend the prohibition of gambling to similar transactions; in kalam, induction is used to argue that all contingent things require a cause, but it is not certain.

Analogy: Definition and Its Lack of Certainty
Defines analogy and explains its limitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define analogy (tamthil), identify its components, and explain why it does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a ruling from one particular to another, but certainty cannot be guaranteed.

Analogy (tamthil) is widely used in Islamic jurisprudence (qiyas) to extend rulings to new cases. The author defines it here and emphasises its probabilistic nature. This lesson breaks down the definition, the example of khamr and nabidh, and the reason why analogy falls short of certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particulars and the common attribute
The text gives the example of khamr (wine) as the asl (original case) and nabidh (date wine) as the far' (new case), with the common attribute of intoxication (iskan).
2
Recognize the direction of inference in analogy
Analogy moves from one particular to another particular, not from universal to particular. It relies on a shared attribute to transfer the ruling.
3
Understand why analogy lacks certainty
The shared attribute may not be the true cause of the ruling. The author states the general rule that analogy does not yield certainty, and the same reason as induction applies: a counterexample or a difference may exist.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Use the same khamr-nabidh example or another from fiqh, such as analogy between gold and silver in zakah, identifying the asl, far', and 'illah.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') and contrasts it with syllogism. The author gives a clear definition: induction is reasoning from particular cases to a universal judgment. The example uses animals moving their lower jaw when chewing; from observing man, horse, and donkey, one induces that all animals share this trait. Induction is classified into incomplete (nāqiṣ) and complete (tāmm). Incomplete induction surveys most but not all particulars, while complete induction surveys all. The author provides another example: surveying walking and non-walking animals to conclude all animals die. This is a rare case of complete induction, but it is only possible when the number of particulars is limited. The passage ends by contrasting induction with the logical syllogism (qiyas), which moves from universal to particular. For instance, from 'Every man is an animal' and 'Every animal is a body', we derive 'Every man is a body'. This reversal of direction between induction and syllogism is a key distinction.

Understanding Analogical Reasoning (Tamthil) and Its Limits
Author defines analogical reasoning, gives example, and explains why it does not yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify analogical reasoning (tamthil) in an Islamic source, extract the common attribute (jāmi‘), and explain why such reasoning does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Analogy links particulars but does not guarantee certainty.

In this lesson, we explore the definition and limits of analogical reasoning as presented in the text. The author first defines tamthīl as inference from one particular to another based on a shared attribute, then illustrates it with a classic fiqh example: applying wine's prohibition to date wine (nabīdh) due to intoxication. Finally, he states a critical rule: neither induction nor analogy yields certainty. You will learn the structure of analogical argument and why similarity in one attribute does not ensure identical rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tamthīl
Find the author's definition: inference from one particular to another based on a common attribute (jāmi‘), carrying the ruling. Also note al-Taftāzānī's definition cited.
The common attribute is sometimes called 'illah in later usul.
2
Extract the example and the jāmi‘
The example: nabīdh is judged like khamr (wine) in prohibition. The common attribute (jāmi‘) is intoxication (iskār). Identify the source and target.
This qiyas is foundational in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the epistemic rule
The passage states that both istiqrā (induction) and tamthīl (analogy) do not produce qat‘ (certainty). For analogy, the reason is that similarity in one attribute does not necessitate similarity in all rulings.
Contrast with syllogistic reasoning (qiyas iqtirani) which can yield certainty if premises are certain.
4
Apply the rule to the crocodile example
The induction about animal jaw movement was falsified by the crocodile. For analogy, think of a counterexample where two things share one attribute but differ in rulings to internalise the limitation.
This highlights the need for a rigorous 'illah in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines analogical reasoning (tamthīl) and then assesses its epistemic status. The author first gives a clear definition: it is inference from one particular to another based on a shared attribute (jāmi‘). The example is from fiqh: applying the prohibition of wine to nabīdh (date wine) because both share the attribute of intoxication. The definition by al-Sa‘d al-Taftāzānī is quoted, which explicitly mentions the likening of one particular to another in a common meaning. The passage then presents a critical rule: both induction (istiqra’) and analogy (tamthīl) do not yield certainty (qaṭ‘). For induction, the reason is that a counterexample might exist. The author reports that the crocodile was found to move its upper jaw when chewing, contradicting the induced generalisation that all animals move their lower jaw. Thus, the inductive conclusion was not certain. For analogy, the reason is that two things may share one attribute but differ in others; similarity in one aspect does not guarantee identical rulings. This is a fundamental limitation in fiqh analogies: the 'illa must be exactly the same for the ruling to transfer with certainty. The passage underscores that certainty requires a syllogism (qiyas), not induction or analogy.

Why Induction and Analogy Lack Certainty
Epistemological classification of assent methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish induction and analogy from syllogism in terms of certainty, and explain why they fail to produce yaqin.

Core Idea
Induction and analogy are probabilistic; only syllogism yields certain knowledge.

In the previous lesson, you learned that assent-yielding methods are three: induction, analogy, and syllogism. Now the author explains why only one of them—syllogism—leads to certainty (yaqin). Induction and analogy, despite their usefulness, are vulnerable to exceptions that negate certainty. We will unpack the reasoning behind this limitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three methods
The passage states that the methods producing tasdiq (assent) are three: istiqra' (induction), qiyas (syllogism), and tamthil (analogy). Note that qiyas here means categorical syllogism, not legal analogy.
This classification is common in Islamic logic.
2
Examine the induction example
The author gives an example: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing.' This is an inductive generalization from observed animals. But it is not certain because there might be an animal like the crocodile that does not move its lower jaw, meaning a single counterexample refutes the universal claim.
The key point: possibility (ihtimal) of an unexamined individual negates certainty.
3
Understand the analogical flaw
In analogy, two things share one attribute, but that does not guarantee they share all rulings. The cause ('illa) in the original case may differ from the cause in the new case. So the conclusion is only probable.
Even if the cause appears similar, it may not be identical.
4
Conclude that only qiyas yields certainty
The author emphasizes that only syllogistic reasoning (qiyas) is free from such possibility and thus yields certainty. Induction and analogy always leave room for doubt.
This is a foundational epistemological point in Islamic logic.

This passage is an epistemological argument showing why induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil) do not produce certain knowledge. The passage concludes that only syllogism (qiyas) yields certainty.
