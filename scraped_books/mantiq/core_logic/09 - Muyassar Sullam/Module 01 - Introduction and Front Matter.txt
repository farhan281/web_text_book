Why Logic Is the Gateway to All Islamic Sciences
Author introduces the importance of logic and his motivation for writing this commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate why logic is considered the introduction to all Islamic sciences and identify the pedagogical purpose of al-Sullam al-Munawraq in training the mind.

Core Idea
Logic is the key that unlocks every other Islamic science.

This preface sets the stage for the entire course. The author explains that logic is not just another subject—it is the tool that trains the mind to reason correctly, making it indispensable for every student of Islamic knowledge. By understanding his motivation, you will see why mastering logic is essential before delving into deeper studies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rhetorical structure
The preface begins with basmala, praise of Allah, then 'amma ba'd' (to proceed). Find the main sections: the declaration of logic's importance, the description of al-Sullam, and the personal anecdote about the commentary's genesis.
This structure is standard in classical Islamic texts.
2
Extract the key claim about logic
Locate the phrase 'مقدمة العلوم' (introduction to the sciences) and the comparison to mathematics. The author says logic is 'among the most important sciences absolutely' because it teaches correct thinking.
This echoes the classical tradition that logic is a tool (ala) for all sciences.
3
Analyze the description of al-Sullam
The matn is described as 'صغير الحجم ولكنه يحتوي على علم كبير' (small in size but containing great knowledge). Why would scholars value a concise text? It suits beginners while still covering essentials.
Al-Sullam is a versified text, aiding memorization.
4
Understand the author's pedagogical goal
The author wants his commentary to be a bridge between beginner and intermediate books, answering common questions. This shows the intended audience: students who have started but need more guidance.
The commentary aims to fill gaps and clarify ambiguities.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Introduction. Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds, and peace and blessings be upon our master Muhammad, the most honorable of the prophets and messengers. O Allah, teach us what benefits us and benefit us with what You have taught us; indeed You are the All-Knowing, the All-Aware. To proceed: Since the science of logic is among the most important sciences absolutely, because it is the introduction to the sciences, and through it the student of knowledge trains to use his mind and thought correctly, and learns how to distinguish between illusion and reflection, and the great scholars have stipulated the importance of logic, and the verifiers considered it the introduction to the sciences, for it is among the sciences like mathematics among the modern natural sciences - it became necessary for the student of knowledge to learn this art and master it. Among the best concise texts written by later scholars in logic is the poem called al-Sullam al-Munawraq, a small text in size but containing great knowledge. And the interest of scholars in it has become famous, so I loved to write a new commentary on it, in a style close to students of knowledge, so that this commentary may be an aid for them to delve into the books of earlier scholars. Say: 'Praise be to Allah, but most of them do not understand.' And may Allah Almighty bless and greet and bless the most honorable of creation, our master Muhammad, and his family and all his companions. To proceed: Verily the books of my master Sheikh Saeed Foudah - may Allah bless his time and knowledge - are extremely beneficial, and you hardly find in my mind a correct idea except that it has a source from his books or his speech. He is much reading and writing, so he does not finish occupying himself with a book except that I know that he is busy with another, whether commentary, refinement, or authorship. May Allah guide him and benefit us with his knowledge and companionship in both worlds, Ameen. And it was that I visited him about a year ago, and he informed me that he wants to write a commentary on al-Sullam al-Manṭiq by the scholar Abi Zaid Abd al-Rahman ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Amir al-Saghir al-Akhdari, attributed to the Green Mountain in Algeria, born in Biskra there in the year 918 AH and died there also in the year 983 AH. And the Sheikh had placed a structure for the commentary, and he said to me that he intends to add to it and revise it, so that the book becomes a link between beginner books and intermediate books, answering many of the questions of beginners and adding a little in the discussion of what they were lenient in or left out. And after a few days from that, the Sheikh sent me an electronic copy of the book, prefaced by a phrase saying: 'Logic syllabus at the Islamic University of Rotterdam.' And when I asked the Sheikh about that, he said that brother Jalal al-Jihani - may Allah guide him - the lecturer at that university, informed him that he intends to teach logic there, and wants something written by him that is suitable for that. So the Sheikh sent him this book before its revision and completion. And brother Jalal approved of it, and taught it to his students. Then I examined it and found it to be a pleasant and precise book, and I also read it with a small number of students - may Allah guide them - during which I observed areas of their questions, and I annotated the margins of the book with a few words. Then the Sheikh informed me that he wants to publish the book as it is, or perhaps he said after its revision, and he asked the poor servant to record those small comments.

Commentary
This passage is the preface (muqaddima) of the commentary. The author begins with the basmala and praise of Allah, followed by a standard introduction. He emphasizes the importance of logic as the gateway to all sciences, comparing it to mathematics in modern natural sciences. He then introduces the matn, al-Sullam al-Munawraq, as a concise yet comprehensive text, and expresses his desire to write a commentary accessible to students. The author then shifts to praise of his teacher, Sheikh Saeed Foudah, describing his prolific scholarly output. He recounts a personal anecdote: a visit to the sheikh about a year prior, during which the sheikh expressed his intention to write a commentary on al-Sullam. The sheikh had already outlined the structure, aiming to bridge beginner and intermediate levels. The sheikh later sent an electronic copy intended for a logic course at the Islamic University of Rotterdam. The author read the draft with students, noted their questions, and added marginal notes. Finally, the sheikh requested that these comments be included in the publication. The preface thus establishes the context, purpose, and collaborative nature of the work.

He acknowledges that students often fear logic due to its difficulty, and he aims to make it accessible. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a student into beginner, intermediate, or advanced based on their capacity to conceptualize issues and perform deduction.

Core Idea
Three student levels are defined by two cognitive abilities: conceptualization (tasawwur) and deduction (istidlal).

This short passage from the Sullam al-Munawraq classifies students of any science into three ranks. Understanding this classification helps teachers assess readiness and helps students identify their own stage. The criteria are universal across Islamic disciplines, from logic to fiqh.

Guided Path

The passage mentions 'taswir al-masa'il' (conceptualizing issues) and 'istidlal' (deduction). Note that these are the only skills used to distinguish the levels.

A beginner cannot conceptualize the issues. For example, in logic, a beginner cannot picture what a categorical proposition is or how it differs from a conditional.
3
Understand the intermediate (mutawassiṭ)
An intermediate can conceptualize the issues but cannot deduce from them. They know the definitions but cannot draw inferences or solve problems independently.
4
Understand the advanced (muntah)
An advanced student can deduce. Having mastered conceptualization, they derive conclusions through reasoning, such as constructing a valid syllogism.
Deduction here includes both immediate and syllogistic inference.

Take fiqh: a beginner cannot picture 'ghusl' (ritual bath); an intermediate can define ghusl but cannot derive its rulings from texts; an advanced can deduce the obligation of ghusl from primary sources.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the legal rulings (ahkam) concerning learning the science of logic (mantiq). The author, writing a commentary on the Sullam, presents three scholarly opinions and argues for the third. The first view is that logic is absolutely forbidden (haram) because it leads to philosophy, which some consider corrupt. The author refutes this by arguing that not all philosophy is false, and logic is not exclusive to philosophy; it serves many sciences. Therefore, the prohibition lacks basis. The second view is that logic is obligatory (wajib), either individually (ayn) or communally (kifaya). The author mentions Imam al-Ghazali's stance that logic is necessary for reliable knowledge, and elaborates on the conditions for individual obligation, especially when defending the religion. The third view, which the author prefers and attributes to the majority (jumhur), is that learning logic is permissible (ja'iz) for those who have firmly grasped the Islamic sciences from the Quran and Sunnah. For such a person, logic aids in correct reasoning across all fields. The author emphasizes that knowing the rules of logic is not enough; one must also will to apply them. A key condition is 'salamat al-qariha' (soundness of natural intelligence) to prevent doubts. The passage concludes with a critique of those who reject logic and kalam today, arguing that these sciences are needed to defend Islam. The footnotes clarify terms and expand on al-Ghazali's works.

Pure Logic vs. Mixed Philosophy
Author clarifies ruling on studying logic, distinguishing pure from mixed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between pure logic (jā'iz by consensus) and philosophy-mixed logic (disputed) based on the author's criteria.

Core Idea
Pure logic is permissible by consensus; only its philosophical mixture is debated.

Many students of Islamic sciences hear conflicting rulings about studying logic. The author resolves this by showing that the disagreement is not about logic as a pure science but about books that mix logic with philosophical doctrines. This lesson helps you understand that distinction so you can evaluate any logic book's ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of logical study
The author first describes 'logic inasmuch as it is a science, after stripping it of philosophical examples' — this is pure logic. Then he mentions 'books mixed with the beliefs of philosophers' — these are the mixed ones. Find these descriptions in the Arabic.
Pure logic is abstract methodology; mixed books contain philosophical doctrines.
2
Locate the 'pure' examples
The author names his own book, al-Sanusi, al-Taftazani, and al-Katibi as examples of pure logic. Note that these are widely studied in the Islamic tradition.
3
Explain why mixed books cause disagreement
Mixed books include corrupt beliefs (e.g., from al-Farabi and Ibn Sina). Scholars debated whether studying them is forbidden, obligatory, or permissible depending on the student's ability to refute errors.
The disagreement is about the content, not the logical method itself.
4
Determine where the current book falls
The author states that his book is from the pure category. Therefore, studying it is permissible on all opinions and even obligatory on some.
This shows the practical outcome of the distinction.

Classification of Signification and the Scope of Logic
Author classifies signification into non-verbal and verbal, then identifies conventional verbal signification as the focus of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any instance of signification into one of six types and identify which type is relevant to logical analysis.

Core Idea
Logic only deals with conventional verbal signification because it is precise and agreed upon.

Before we can use words as tools for reasoning, we need to understand how they convey meaning. The author of the Sullam begins by mapping out all possible ways something can signify something else, then zeroes in on the one type that matters for logic: conventional language. This classification is the gateway to everything that follows.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish non-verbal from verbal signification.
The passage first divides signification into two broad categories: non-verbal (like pointing or natural events) and verbal (actual words). Write down these two branches as the first level of your classification tree.
2
Identify the three subtypes within each branch.
Both non-verbal and verbal signification are further divided into three types: intellectual (based on natural reasoning), habitual (based on common association), and conventional (based on deliberate agreement). For each subtype, note the example given in the passage.
Non-verbal: change → origination (intellectual), rain → vegetation (habitual), pointing → yes/no (conventional). Verbal: sound of words → speaker behind wall (intellectual), saying 'akh' → chest pain (habitual), word 'lion' → animal (conventional).
3
Pinpoint the type considered in logic.
The passage clearly states that only conventional verbal signification is considered in logic. Underline that sentence in the Arabic. Ask yourself: why would intellectual and habitual verbal signification be excluded?
Intellectual signification (e.g., hearing words indicates a speaker) is not about the meaning of the words themselves, but about the physical fact of their utterance. Habitual signification (e.g., 'akh' for pain) is culture-bound and imprecise.
4
Reflect on the consequence for logical analysis.
Since logic relies on precise meanings, only conventionally assigned meanings (i.e., words defined by linguistic convention) are reliable. This means that when you analyze an argument in logic, you must treat each term according to its conventional definition, not according to personal associations or natural inferences.

Author concludes his work, asks for forgiveness, dates the composition, and offers prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a scholarly conclusion (khātimah) in an Islamic logic text and explain the author's intentions regarding humility, correction, and historical context.

Core Idea
The conclusion of a scholarly work is not just an ending; it is a plea for forgiveness, an invitation for correction, and a marker of historical context.

Every Islamic scholarly work ends with a conclusion (khātimah) that often reveals the author's character and circumstances. In this passage, the commentator explains the closing lines of al-Sullam al-Munawraq, where the author al-Akhdari humbly acknowledges his youth and asks readers to correct any errors gently. Understanding such conclusions helps you appreciate the scholarly tradition and the ethical norms of academic discourse.

Conclusion: With this we have finished explaining this beneficial poetic composition briefly, making meanings accessible for beginners in learning this science. He said: [This is the complete intended purpose from the fundamentals of praised logic. It has ended, by the praise of the Lord of the Dawn, what I aimed for from the art of logic. The devoted servant, in need of the mercy of the great, powerful Lord, al-Akhdari, the slave of the Most Merciful, hoped-for from his bountiful Lord for forgiveness encompassing sins and removing the veil from hearts. And that He reward us with the highest Paradise, for He is the most generous of those who bestow favors.] And [Be, my brother, tolerant towards the beginner, and be a sincere advisor for correcting corruption. Correct corruption through reflection. If it is a mistake, do not change it, for it is said: how many a false coin has been taken as sound due to the ugliness of one's understanding. And say to one who does not achieve my intent: the excuse is a right and obligation for the beginner. And for one of twenty-one years, an excuse is accepted and approved, especially in the tenth century, characterized by ignorance, corruption, and trials. And the composition of this organized rajaz was at the beginning of Muharram of the year 941 after nine hundred.] Prayer and peace perpetually upon the Messenger of God, the best guide, and upon his family and companions walking the paths of salvation, as long as the sun traverses its towers and the bright full moon rises in darkness. It is from it. (1) His saying 'al-ummahat' is the plural of 'umm', and the origin of everything is its source, and the source is the foundation. So the meaning is: this is the complete what I intended to inform you of from the foundations of logic. And 'al-mahmud' means the pure from the corrupt speech of the philosophers and their creeds that oppose the Sharia. And 'al-falaq' is the dawn. And the 'min' in his saying 'min fann ilm al-mantiq' is partitive, so one should not think that this is the entirety of logic; this amount is nothing but a simplified introduction to delve into its intricate issues. Let not our speech discourage you from delving deeper. Whoever understands this amount, he can understand many times its like and more difficult, by effort and perseverance. And his saying 'nazamahu' is from nazm, which is intentionally rhymed and metered speech. And 'al-abd' is the one characterized by servitude, which is the utmost humility and submission, and its goal is the perfection and honor of man appropriate to his reality: to be a servant of God Almighty. And 'al-murtaja' is the hoped-for one. And 'al-mannan' is an intensive form of 'al-mann', which is counting favors; it is praiseworthy from God and blameworthy from creation. And 'yakshif al-ghitaa an al-quloob' means it removes the rust from them. And 'al-thawab' is the reward for an action; acting for it is not blameworthy, though acting out of veneration for God's essence is more complete. And the author asks whoever looks at his book to forgive him for any slips that occurred from him, to advise in correcting it, and to reflect on that without haste, because the hasty person usually misses the mark, and passes off the false as true due to the ugliness of his understanding; if his understanding were good, he would not hasten. His saying 'haqqun lil-mubtadi' means it is strongly recommended and should be sought for him. For he authored this book at the age of twenty-one, and for someone of this age, accepting his excuse is good, especially in the tenth century, whose people are mostly characterized by ignorance and filled with corruption and trials. Then the author dated this rajaz that it was in the beginning of Muharram of the year 941 of the prophetic hijra, and the meaning of prayer was mentioned earlier. And 'al-salaam' is safety from deficiencies. And 'al-sarmad' is perpetual. And his saying 'ma qata'at shams al-nahar' intends generality for all times, as is the custom of the Arabs in such expressions, and not specifying prayer only to this time, as in his earlier saying 'ma dama al-haja'. And 'al-abruj' is the plural of burj, which is the name for one of twelve parts of the eighth sphere. And 'al-duja' is the plural of dujyah, meaning darkness. And God Almighty knows best. So O Allah, O Present One who is not absent, by the truth that You are the Near, the Answerer, accept this work from my shaykh and from me purely for Your noble face, and benefit the Muslims through it, guide us and guide through us, grant us Your protection and do not deprive us of Your care. O Allah, likewise for our parents, our teachers, those who taught us and did good to us, and all Muslims. And send blessings upon our master Muhammad and upon his family and companions and grant them peace, as long as those who remember You remember and those who are heedless of Your remembrance are heedless. And praise be to God, Lord of the worlds.

Commentary
This passage is the conclusion (khātimah) of the Muyassar Sullam commentary. The commentator first states that the explanation of the useful poetic composition has been completed briefly for beginners. Then he quotes the author’s closing lines of the matn (the original poem), which summarize the work: it covers the fundamentals of praised logic, purged from the corruptions of philosophers. The author asks for forgiveness, noting he completed it in his youth at age 21. The commentator then explains key terms from the poem: 'ummahāt' (fundamentals), 'maḥmūd' (pure from philosophical corruptions), 'min' is partitive (so this is not the whole of logic but an introduction), 'naẓm' (intentional poetry), 'ʿabd' (servant), 'murtajā' (hoped for), 'mannān' (bestower of favors). He emphasizes the author’s humility—asking readers to correct errors gently without haste, because the hasty often err. The author was young when writing, so his excuse is accepted, especially in the corrupt tenth century. The commentator also explains the date: beginning of Muharram 941 AH. He details the prayers at the end: perpetual blessings on the Prophet and his family. The commentary concludes with a personal supplication asking Allah to accept the work and benefit all Muslims.
