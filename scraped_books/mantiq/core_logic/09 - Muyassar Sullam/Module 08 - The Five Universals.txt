Division of Knowledge into Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Commentary divides 'ilm into tasawwur and tasdiq based on the nature of the known, excluding Allah's knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq based on the known object and explain why Allah's knowledge is not subject to this division.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either a concept or a judgment, but only for human perception, not Allah's eternal knowledge.

This lesson draws from a classical commentary that divides human knowledge into two fundamental types: conceptualization and assent. Understanding this division is crucial for grasping how Islamic scholars analyze definitions and propositions. The passage also includes a critical theological nuance: this division does not apply to Allah's knowledge, as it is not preceded by ignorance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of division
The author divides knowledge according to the quiddity (māhiyya) of the known object. Notice that the known is either a single form (concept) or a judgmental relation (proposition).
2
Distinguish the two types
Tasawwur is knowledge of a single form without judgment; tasdiq is assent to a proposition. For example, knowing the meaning of 'prayer' is tasawwur, while knowing that prayer is obligatory is tasdiq.
3
Recognize the genus of both
The author states that the 'knowledge' being divided is absolute perception (idrāk), defined as the soul's arrival at a meaning. Both tasawwur and tasdiq are types of perception.
4
Understand the exclusion of divine knowledge
Perception implies prior ignorance. Since Allah's knowledge is eternal and not preceded by ignorance, it cannot be called perception, so it is not divided into tasawwur or tasdiq.
This is a key theological safeguard against anthropomorphism.

Division of knowledge: Knowledge is divided according to the quiddity of the known into conceptualization and assent. That is because the knower either knows a single form, or knows a judgmental relation; by 'judgmental relation' we mean a predicative relation to which psychological assent can attach. So the first division is conceptualization, and the second is assent. Thus knowledge is either conceptualization or assent. Commentary
This passage classifies 'ilm (knowledge) into two types based on the known object. The author states that knowledge is divided taṣawwur (conceptualization) and taṣdīq (assent). The criterion is the nature of what is known: either a single form (a simple concept) or a judgmental relation (a proposition). The first is taṣawwur—knowing the meaning of a term without judgement, like knowing the meaning of 'prayer'. The second is taṣdīq—assenting to a proposition, like knowing that prayer is obligatory. The author clarifies that the 'ilm intended in this division is absolute perception (muṭlaq al-idrāk), which includes both certain and uncertain cognition. Absolute perception is defined as the soul's arrival at a meaning, which presupposes prior ignorance. Consequently, this division does not apply to Allah's knowledge, because His knowledge is eternal and not preceded by ignorance; it is not a 'perception' in this sense. Thus we cannot say Allah's knowledge is taṣawwur or taṣdīq. This guards against anthropomorphizing divine knowledge as composed of concepts and judgments like human knowledge.

Distinguishing the Three Types of Verbal Signification
Author defines the three types of verbal signification with examples and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate between mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam in Arabic logical texts, and apply the condition of mental entailment for iltizam.

Core Idea
Words signify meanings in three ways: whole, part, or necessary associate.

Understanding how words convey meaning is foundational to Islamic logic. This lesson unpacks the three types of verbal signification—mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam—as defined by classical logicians. You'll learn to recognize each type and the crucial condition that distinguishes iltizam from mere association.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types listed
The passage names three types: dalaalat mutabaqa (correspondence), dalaalat tadammun (inclusion), and dalaalat iltizam (commitment). Locate these terms in the Arabic text.
Notice the author orders them differently than the title; the sequence is mutabaqa, mulazama (iltizam), then tadammun.
2
Analyze mutabaqa (correspondence)
Mutabaqa occurs when the word signifies its entire assigned meaning. The example 'asad' (lion) signifying the whole predatory animal is given. Check: does 'human' signifying 'rational animal' fit?
This is the default signification for most words.
3
Analyze tadammun (inclusion)
Tadammun occurs when the word signifies a part of its assigned meaning. For example, 'asad' signifying its tail (a part of the lion). The passage also gives 'house' signifying 'door'.
The part must be intrinsic to the meaning, not merely an external component—some debate exists about 'house' and 'door'.
4
Analyze iltizam (commitment)
Iltizam occurs when the word signifies a meaning that necessarily accompanies the assigned meaning in the mind. Example: 'arbaca' (four) signifying evenness. The key condition is that the transfer from the original meaning to the necessary meaning must be by rational consideration, not mere word association or habit.
The passage illustrates with 'huduth' (origination) leading to 'qidam' (pre-eternity) through mental entailment.
5
Apply the condition for iltizam
Test the example: 'blindness' signifies 'sight' because the mind necessarily infers sight when conceiving blindness. But note: the condition is that the entailment must be mental, not necessarily external. The passage clarifies that even if two things coexist externally (like crows and blackness), that alone does not create iltizam.
The phrase 'bi-mulāḥaẓat al-ʿaql' is key: the transfer must be by intellectual consideration.

Singular vs. Compound Terms
The author defines singular and compound terms with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic term as mufrad (singular) or murakkab (compound) by determining whether any part of the term indicates part of its meaning.

Core Idea
A term's classification depends on whether its parts carry meaning—proper names are singular even if they look compound.

In logic, terms are classified not by grammatical form but by how their parts relate to meaning. This lesson teaches you to distinguish singular from compound terms, a skill essential for analyzing definitions and later for understanding the building blocks of concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term's constituent parts
Look at the term and identify its smallest meaningful components (letters, morphemes, words). For example, 'زيد' has parts 'ز', 'ي', 'د'; 'عبد الله' has 'عبد' and 'الله'.
2
Check if any part indicates part of the term's meaning
Ask: Does any part independently convey a portion of the overall meaning? For 'عبد الله' as a proper name, the parts no longer indicate 'servant' and 'Allah'; they are just labels.
3
Apply the rule
If no part indicates part of the meaning, the term is mufrad (singular). If at least one part does, it is murakkab (compound). For example, 'زيد' is mufrad; 'رجل عالم' (a learned man) is compound.
4
Test with examples from the commentary
The sharh gives 'عبد الله' and 'الحيوان الناطق' when used as proper names as examples of mufrad. Verify that their original meanings are forgotten.

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter on the investigation of terms. The author first reminds the reader that logicians are not primarily concerned with terms themselves, but since meanings are conveyed through terms, some basic rules about them are necessary. The matn verses then present the classification: used terms are either singular or compound. A compound term is one whose part indicates part of its meaning; a singular term is the opposite. The singular is further divided into universal and particular. A universal term conveys by itself the possibility of sharing among its individuals, while a particular does not. The verses also mention the five universals: genus, species, differentia, accident, and property, and that genus can be near, far, or middle. The commentary expands on these definitions. It explains that terms are considered only after they are used, since the goal is communication. The division into singular and compound is clarified: a singular term either has no part (like a preposition) or has parts that do not indicate any part of its meaning (like proper nouns such as 'Zayd'). A compound term must have at least one part that indicates a part of its meaning. Important examples are given: 'Abdullah' and 'Ta'abbata Sharran' when used as proper names are considered singular because their parts no longer indicate their original meanings—they have become mere labels, like the letters in 'Zayd'. The same applies to 'al-Hayawan al-Natiq' if used as a proper name. Then the singular term is divided: if it can be truly predicated of many individuals by virtue of its very conception, it is a universal; otherwise, it is particular. The commentary also notes that universality is not limited to singular words; compound expressions can also be universal if their parts are universal. Finally, the purpose of this entire classification is to establish the five universals, which are the starting points for all concepts and the basis for statements (propositions) in logic.

Universal vs. Particular Terms
The author defines universal and particular terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a singular term is universal (kulli) or particular (juz'i) based on whether its mere conception allows sharing among multiple individuals.

Core Idea
A term is universal if its meaning alone implies it can apply to more than one; otherwise it is particular.

Understanding Simple and Compound Utterances in Logic
Definition of simple and compound utterances with conditions and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an utterance is simple or compound based on whether its parts indicate part of its meaning.

Core Idea
A simple term's parts don't indicate its meaning; a compound's parts do.

In this lesson, we will explore the foundational distinction between simple and compound utterances. The author defines each with precise conditions and addresses a key nuance about proper names that originally had meaning. This distinction is crucial for understanding how terms function in definitions and propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining criterion
The author uses one criterion: whether the part of an utterance indicates any part of its overall meaning. This is the core distinction between simple and compound.
Pay attention to the phrasing 'جزؤه على جزء معناه'.
2
Apply the criterion to simple utterances
Simple includes utterances with no parts (like a single letter) and utterances with parts that do not indicate meaning (like 'Zayd' where letters don't convey 'Zaydness').
Proper names in regular use are typical examples of simple with parts.
3
Apply the criterion to compound utterances
Compound requires that each part indicates a component of the meaning. For example, 'عبد الله' as a common noun means 'servant of Allah', where 'عبد' and 'الله' each contribute.
The parts must be proximate (أجزاء قريبة) and currently indicative.
4
Handle the special case of originally meaningful proper names
Names like 'عبد الله' or 'تأبط شراً' originally had meaning but, when used as proper names, lose that meaning. Their parts no longer indicate part of the new meaning, making them simple.
The author stresses that previous indication is 'نسياً منسياً' (forgotten).

Classifying Universals: Intrinsic vs. Accidental
Author explains why universals are studied and the intrinsic/accidental division, with a nuance about species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal as intrinsic or accidental based on its relation to the essence, and identify the special status of the species.

Core Idea
The classification of a universal as intrinsic or accidental depends on how we define its relation to the essence—and the species is a boundary case.

Why do logicians care about classifying universals? Because universals are the building blocks of definitions and descriptions, and a precise classification prevents errors in reasoning. In this passage, the author explains that universals are studied to regulate knowledge, and then gives the key division: those that are part of the essence (intrinsic) and those that are outside it (accidental). He also highlights a subtlety about the species, which is the entire essence itself. Finally, he clarifies a meta-linguistic point about when we describe utterances or meanings as universal or particular. Understanding this classification is essential for anyone who wants to construct accurate definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essence (mahiyya)
To classify a universal, first determine the essence of the thing you are analyzing. For example, the essence of 'human' is 'rational animal'.
The essence is the definitional core, denoted by the term mahiyya.
2
Check if the universal is part of the essence
Ask: Is this universal included in the definition of the thing? If yes, it is intrinsic (dhati). Examples: 'animal' and 'rational' are intrinsic to 'human' because they are part of its definition.
Intrinsic universals are the genus, species, or differentia.
3
If not part of the essence, it is accidental
If the universal is not part of the definition, it is accidental (arid). Example: 'laughing' is accidental to 'human' because it is not in the definition.
Accidental can be proper (like laughing) or common (like walking).
4
Handle the case of the species
The species (e.g., 'human') is the entire essence. According to the passage, if we define intrinsic as 'included in the essence' and accidental as 'outside the essence', then the species is neither, because it is the whole. But if we define intrinsic as 'not outside', then species would be intrinsic. This shows definitions matter.
This nuance reveals that classification depends on how we define the terms.

Apply the real/metaphorical distinction
The author notes that describing a meaning as universal/particular is real (haqiqa), but describing an utterance as such is metaphorical. Keep this in mind when analyzing concepts.

Divisions of Genus: Proximate, Remote, Intermediate, and Isolated
Author defines three types of genus and introduces a fourth isolated genus, with a note on differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of genus (qarib, baʿīd, mutawassiṭ, munfarid) and identify the proximate and remote differentia in a given definition.

Core Idea
The genus hierarchy determines definition precision; the isolated genus completes the logical division.

In the five universals, genus is a broad category that applies to many species. But not all genera are equal: some are close to the species (proximate), others are overarching (remote), some sit in between (intermediate), and a rare type has no genus above or below (isolated). This lesson clarifies these positions with examples from the logical tradition and also explains how differentia mirrors this hierarchy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the species and its immediate genus
For any species to be defined, find the closest universal that includes it — this is the proximate genus (jins qarīb). Example: for 'human', the proximate genus is 'animal'.
The proximate genus is also called 'lower' (sāfil).
2
Ascend the genus hierarchy
Check if the proximate genus itself has a genus above it. If yes, that higher genus is an intermediate genus (jins mutawassiṭ). Example: above 'animal' is 'body'.
Intermediate genera have both a supergenus and a subgenus.
3
Find the highest genus
Continue ascending until you reach a genus with no genus above it — this is the remote genus (jins baʿīd). Example: above 'body' is 'substance', which has no higher genus.
The remote genus is the highest level in the hierarchy.
4
Recognize the isolated genus
If a purported genus has no genus above it and no genus below it (and no example), it is isolated (jins munfarid). This is a theoretical remainder to complete the division, not something that occurs naturally.
The isolated genus is rarely used but logically necessary.
5
Apply the same logic to differentia
The differentia (fasl) is also divided: proximate if it distinguishes the species within the proximate genus, remote if it distinguishes within the remote genus. Example: 'rational' distinguishes human from other animals (proximate genus 'animal').
This mirrors the division of genus.

Commentary
This is an explanatory passage (sharh) that defines two pairs of terms often confused: al-kull (the whole) vs. al-kulliyya (universality), and al-juz' (the part) vs. al-juz'iyya (particularity). The author begins by giving the occasion for discussing these concepts: they share the same linguistic root as al-kulli (universal) and al-juz'i (particular) but have distinct meanings. He then cites the matn verse: 'Al-kull is our judgment upon the aggregate, like 'all of that did not occur'; and its being upon every single individual as a judgment is known as kulliyya. The judgment for the part is juz'iyya.' The author explains that al-kull refers to a judgment applied to a group collectively, where no single member is independent in bearing that judgment. Example: 'All of Banu Tamim carry the great rock' means they do it together, not each alone. He also cites the Quranic verse about eight angels bearing the throne. The nazim (poet) gave the example of the Prophet's statement to Dhu al-Yadayn: 'All of that did not occur,' in response to a question about whether the prayer was shortened or he forgot. According to the interpretation of al-kull, this would mean the Prophet negated the conjunction of shortening and forgetfulness. However, the author argues that this interpretation is weak (marjuh); the stronger view is that the Prophet negated each individually, making it a universal negation. The author provides a detailed logical critique: the questioner used 'am (or) seeking specification, so the proper answer would be either to specify one or to negate both. The report that Dhu al-Yadayn said 'Rather, some of that has occurred' contradicts a universal negation but not a negation of conjunction. He then states the rule that when 'kull' precedes a negation, it signifies generality of negation, and thus the hadith is a universal negative by supposition (without falsehood). Therefore, the nazim's example is incorrect. The author then gives correct examples of kulliyya: 'Every soul shall taste death' and 'There is no god but Allah' (a universal negative). He explains that the latter is a general negation with an exception (al-Mustathna = Allah), which is a 'connected exception' where the excluded is included in the general term by linguistic convention but excluded by intent. The footnotes further clarify the benefit of the chapter, urging students to make comparative tables, and provide additional analysis of the hadith. The key lesson is to distinguish between judging a group as a whole versus judging each individual separately.

Commentary
This passage is an expanded commentary defining the concepts of part (juz'), whole (kull), and universal (kulli), with a systematic comparison. The author begins by defining the particular proposition as a preliminary, then defines the part (juz') as a component of a whole. Two types of parts are distinguished: the natural part (juz' tabi'i), such as animal relative to man, which is a conceptual component of the quiddity; and the material part (juz' maddi), such as the roof relative to the house, which is a sensible component. The author then shifts to the universal (kulli) and the sensible whole (kull mahsus). The universal is defined as a concept whose meaning does not prevent it being shared by many (e.g., 'animal'); it can have various existential statuses (impossible, possible but non-existent, one individual, many, etc.). The sensible whole, by contrast, cannot be shared by many individually; it must have finite parts and exists externally. A key distinction is that the whole is constituted by its parts and is not predicated of them (e.g., a table is not said of its leg), while the universal is predicated of its particulars and is not constituted by them (e.g., 'man' is said of Zayd but man is not constituted by Zayd). The whole exists externally, whereas the universal exists only in the mind. The parts of a whole are necessarily finite, while the particulars of a universal are infinite (not simultaneously, but successively). The author then presents Imam al-Razi's seven differences between whole and universal: (1) whole exists externally, universal only in mind; (2) whole is counted by its parts, universal by its particulars; (3) universal is constitutive of its particular, whole is constituted by its parts; (4) the nature of the whole does not become a part, but the nature of the universal can become particular (e.g., 'man' becomes 'this man'); (5) the whole is not a whole in each part alone, but the universal is universal for each individual; (6) parts of whole are finite, particulars of universal infinite; (7) whole requires all its parts to be present, universal does not require all its particulars. The passage concludes with a personal reflection on discovering these distinctions. Throughout, the commentary uses examples from everyday objects (table, roof) and logical terms (animal, man) to illustrate the abstract concepts. The distinctions are crucial for understanding composition and predication in logic.

Seven Differences Between Whole (Kull) and Universal (Kulli)
Author enumerates seven differences between whole and universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between whole (kull) and universal (kulli) using the seven criteria articulated by Imam al-Razi.

Core Idea
The whole is a concrete composite with finite parts; the universal is an abstract concept predicable of infinite instances.

The author presents a systematic comparison between the whole (kull) and the universal (kulli), drawing on Imam al-Razi's seven distinctions. These criteria cover existence, counting, constitution, predication, finitude, and presence. Mastering these distinctions is fundamental to avoiding confusion between logical wholes and universal concepts.

And if the judgment is about some, then the proposition is called particular, like our saying 'Some human is a writer' and 'Not every human is a writer'. As for the part, it is clear: it is that from which and from other than it a whole is composed, like animal is a part with respect to man, because man is composed of it and rational, and that is called a natural part; and like the roof with respect to the house, because it is composed of it and the walls, and that is called a material part. Then know that the universal is a concept whose very meaning does not prevent sharing in it, like 'animal', whether it is impossible for any of its individuals to exist in the external world, or possible whether existing or not, like the coexistence of two opposites in one locus, or permissible but non-existent like a mountain of gold, or one individual exists with possibility of others like the sun of our solar system, or one individual exists with impossibility of others like the Necessary Existence (glorified be He), or it is many finite like man, or many infinite like number as we mentioned earlier. The sensible whole prevents its very conception from sharing in it, meaning it is not predicated of many individually, and whether it exists externally or in the mind, it must have finite parts. The whole is constituted by its parts and is not predicated of them, like my table is constituted by its top and legs, and one cannot say of its leg 'table'. Unlike the universal, it is predicated of its particulars and is not constituted by them, like 'man' is said 'Zayd is a man' and 'Amr is a man', and one does not say 'man is constituted by Amr, Bakr, Zayd, etc.' And the whole is susceptible to existence in the external world, and nothing of the universal exists externally qua universal. The parts of the whole are necessarily finite, because the sum of the limited, counted, and finite is limited, counted, and finite; but the particulars of the universal are infinite neither in mind nor externally, not simultaneously. For the mind does not conceive the infinite in detail simultaneously, nor does the infinite exist in existence simultaneously, but successively. Imam al-Razi (may Allah be pleased with him) pointed out in al-Mabahith al-Mashriqiyya, in the fifteenth chapter, on the difference between the whole and the universal, in several aspects: First: the whole as such exists externally, but the universal has no existence except in the mind. Second: the whole is counted by its parts, and the universal is counted by its particulars. Third: the universal is constitutive of the particular, and the whole is constituted by the part. Fourth: the nature of the whole does not become the part, whereas the nature of the universal becomes particular. For example: man when it becomes 'this man' (i.e., pointed to, individuated, specified like Zayd). Fifth: the whole is not a whole in each part alone, and the universal is universal for each part alone because 'man' is true of a single person. [Note: his saying 'each part' means each individual of the universal, i.e., each particular of its particulars, i.e., each instance of its instances.] Sixth: the parts of the whole are finite, and the particulars of the universal are infinite. Seventh: the whole requires the presence of its parts, and the universal does not require the presence of all its particulars. End. And Allah knows best. I have arrived at most of these rulings through my thinking, then I found them scattered in the books, so praise and thanks be to Allah. How delightful it was to see a scholar confirming what I had concluded through my thought and I remained hesitant not to express it until I saw what agrees with it. May Allah grant you the taste of praising Him for that.

Commentary
This passage is an expanded commentary defining the concepts of part (juz'), whole (kull), and universal (kulli), with a systematic comparison. The author begins by defining the particular proposition as a preliminary, then defines the part (juz') as a component of a whole. Two types of parts are distinguished: the natural part (juz' tabi'i), such as animal relative to man, which is a conceptual component of the quiddity; and the material part (juz' maddi), such as the roof relative to the house, which is a sensible component. The author then shifts to the universal (kulli) and the sensible whole (kull mahsus). The universal is defined as a concept whose meaning does not prevent it being shared by many (e.g., 'animal'); it can have various existential statuses (impossible, possible but non-existent, one individual, many, etc.). The sensible whole, by contrast, cannot be shared by many individually; it must have finite parts and exists externally. A key distinction is that the whole is constituted by its parts and is not predicated of them (e.g., a table is not said of its leg), while the universal is predicated of its particulars and is not constituted by them (e.g., 'man' is said of Zayd but man is not constituted by Zayd). The whole exists externally, whereas the universal exists only in the mind. The parts of a whole are necessarily finite, while the particulars of a universal are infinite (not simultaneously, but successively). The author then presents Imam al-Razi's seven differences between whole and universal: (1) whole exists externally, universal only in mind; (2) whole is counted by its parts, universal by its particulars; (3) universal is constitutive of its particular, whole is constituted by its parts; (4) the nature of the whole does not become a part, but the nature of the universal can become particular (e.g., 'man' becomes 'this man'); (5) the whole is not a whole in each part alone, but the universal is universal for each individual; (6) parts of whole are finite, particulars of universal infinite; (7) whole requires all its parts to be present, universal does not require all its particulars. The passage concludes with a personal reflection on discovering these distinctions. Throughout, the commentary uses examples from everyday objects (table, roof) and logical terms (animal, man) to illustrate the abstract concepts. The distinctions are crucial for understanding composition and predication in logic.
