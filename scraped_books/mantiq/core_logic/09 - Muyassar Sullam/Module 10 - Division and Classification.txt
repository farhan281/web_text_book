The Four Types of Lafz Mufrad: Words and Meanings
Author enumerates the four divisions of lafz mufrad based on number of words and meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any single Arabic word into one of the four categories (mutawati', mushtarak, mutaradif, mutabayin) by analyzing the relationship between its word-form and its meaning(s).

Core Idea
Words and meanings align in four ways: one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-one, many-to-many.

In Islamic logic, understanding how words relate to meanings is essential for defining terms correctly. This lesson unpacks the four-way classification of single words (lafz mufrad) based on whether the word is one or many and whether the meaning is one or many. These categories are foundational for both definition (ta'rif) and debate (jadal).

The Three Omitted Types of Word-Meaning Relations
Commentary explains three omitted word-meaning relations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate between the three types of relations between words and meanings: tasawi, umum wa khusus mutlaq, and umum wa khusus min wajh, using their definitions and examples.

Core Idea
The three omitted types are vital for precise definition and classification in Islamic legal theory.

The author of al-Sullam mentioned five types of relations between words and meanings but omitted three crucial ones. This lesson covers those three as explained in the commentary. Understanding these relations is essential for analyzing definitions in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Name the three types in Arabic: التساوي, العموم والخصوص المطلق, العموم والخصوص من وجه. Note their English equivalents: equality, absolute generality-specificity, and generality-specificity from one aspect.
2
Extract the definitional condition for tasawi
For tasawi, the condition is that two words agree in referent (مصداق) but differ in concept (مفهوم). Example: 'writer' and 'laugher' are both properties of man, so every writer is a laugher and vice versa.
Check that the example truly shows co-extension.
3
Extract the definitional condition for umum wa khusus mutlaq
Here, two words intersect in some instances, and each has exclusive instances. Example: 'man' and 'white' meet in white Zaid, but 'man' excludes white in snow, and 'white' excludes black 'Amr.
Note that this definition is non-standard; typically this is 'umum min wajh'.
4
Extract the definitional condition for umum wa khusus min wajh
Here, two words intersect in some instances, but only one has exclusive instances. Example: 'man' and 'animal' meet in every man, but 'animal' has exclusive instances in other species.
This is the standard 'umum mutlaq' relation.
5
Compare and contrast the three
Tasawi is like synonyms in extension but not in meaning. Umum mutlaq is a two-way partial overlap. Umum min wajh is one-way inclusion. Note the shift from standard terminology.
This contrast will help you avoid confusion in later texts.

Commentary
This passage fills a gap left by the author, who had mentioned five types of relations between words and meanings but omitted three very important ones. The commentator explains these three: (1) Tasawi (equality): two words that are co-extensional (same referents) but have different concepts, e.g., 'writer' and 'laugher' as properties of man — every man who is a writer is a laugher and vice versa, but the meanings are different. (2) 'Umum wa khusus mutlaq (absolute generality-specificity): one word's referents are a subset of the other's, e.g., 'man' and 'white' — they intersect (white Zaid) but each has its own exclusive instances (black 'Amr for man, snow for white). (3) 'Umum wa khusus min wajh (generality-specificity from one aspect): they overlap, but one is always a subset of the other? In the example, 'man' and 'animal': all men are animals, but not all animals are men. So it's one-way inclusion — 'animal' is more general, 'man' is more specific, and they intersect in all men. The commentator notes that some say these three can be subsumed under taraduf (for tasawi) and tabāyun (for the other two).

Key Terms
التساوي (al-tasawi): Two terms with identical referents but different concepts.
العموم والخصوص المطلق (al-umum wa al-khusus al-mutlaq): Two terms that intersect in some referents, and each has exclusive referents in others. [Note: Standardly, this is 'umum wa khusus min wajh'. Here it is defined differently.]
العموم والخصوص من وجه (al-umum wa al-khusus min wajh): Two terms where one is fully inclusive of the other, and they intersect in all instances of the narrower term. [Note: This definition is non-standard; typically this would be 'umum wa khusus mutlaq'.]

Al-Kull vs Al-Kulliyya: Collective and Distributive Judgments
Author distinguishes al-kull from al-kulliyya with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a collective judgment (al-kull) and a distributive judgment (al-kulliyya) using examples from the Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Al-kull: judgment on the group as one. Al-kulliyya: judgment on each member separately.

In this lesson, we explore the distinction between two important logical terms: al-kull (the whole) and al-kulliyya (universality). The passage defines them and provides concrete examples from the hadith of Dhul Yadain and the Quranic verse about angels. Understanding this distinction helps avoid confusion in interpreting collective versus distributive statements.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the difference
Determine whether a judgment applies to a group collectively (the whole) or to each individual separately (distributive).
The key is whether each individual independently receives the judgment.
2
Examine the hadith example

This is an example of al-kull.

Al-Juz' and Al-Juz'iyya: Part and Particularity
Author clarifies al-juz' and al-juz'iyya and warns against confusion with juz'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between a part (al-juz') and a judgment on some individuals (al-juz'iyya) using the definitions from the text.

Core Idea
Al-juz' is a part of a whole; al-juz'iyya is a judgment limited to some of the whole.

In this lesson, we examine the terms al-juz' and al-juz'iyya, which are often confused with the similar-sounding juz'i (particular). The passage clarifies that al-juz' refers to a part of something, while al-juz'iyya refers to a judgment that applies to a part (some individuals) of a group. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise logical analysis.

1
Define al-juz'
Al-juz' is simply a part of something, e.g., a subset of a group or a component of a whole.
It is not a judgment; it is an entity.

Al-juz'iyya is a judgment on some individuals of a group, i.e., a particular judgment (like 'some X are Y').
It contrasts with al-kulliyya which is a judgment on each individual.
3
Compare with juz'i
Juz'i (particular) is a term for an individual concept, while al-juz' (part) and al-juz'iyya (judgment on some) are distinct terms that share similar letter forms.
The passage warns beginners about this resemblance.

Practice classification

Commentary
This passage defines four technical terms: al-kull, al-kulliyya, al-juz', and al-juz'iyya, which are distinct from the more familiar kulli (universal) and juz'i (particular). The Two Types of Parts: Natural and Material
Defines part and divides it into natural and material with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a natural part and a material part and classify examples from Islamic sciences correctly.

Core Idea
A part can be conceptual (natural) or physical (material); both are components of a whole.

In logic, the term 'part' (juz') is not limited to physical pieces. Understanding its two meanings—natural (conceptual) and material (sensible)—is essential for analyzing definitions and compositions in Islamic sciences. This passage from al-Muyassar fi Sharh al-Sullam clarifies this division with concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general definition of 'part'
