Why Premises Must End in Certainty
Author proves that premises must be necessary or trace back to necessary premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why premises in a demonstration must either be necessary or trace back to necessary premises, and illustrate this with a kalam argument for the world's origination.

Core Idea
Every valid demonstration rests on a foundation of self-evident truths.

This passage tackles a foundational epistemological requirement for any demonstration (burhan). The author argues that if the premises of an argument are not self-evidently certain, they cannot simply be assumed; they must themselves be proven by premises that eventually lead back to necessary truths. Otherwise, the argument collapses into circular reasoning (dawr) or an infinite regress (tasalsul). Understanding this is crucial for evaluating the strength of theological proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two fallacies
Recognize the two logical problems that arise when premises are not ultimately necessary: circularity (dawr) where a premise depends on the conclusion, and infinite regress (tasalsul) where the chain of proofs never ends.
These are the two invalid forms the author warns against.
2
Understand the requirement
Note the author's claim: every chain of reasoning in a demonstration must terminate in necessary premises. If it ends with a premise that is neither necessary nor conceded, the proof is insufficient.
This is a foundational principle for any valid burhan.
3
Analyze the examples
Look at the simple mathematical example (necessary premises) and the complex kalam example (theoretical premises that reduce to necessary). See how the mathematical premises are immediately certain, while the kalam premises require further justification that eventually reaches necessary truths.
The kalam example shows how a theoretical proof can be valid if its premises are ultimately supported by necessary ones.
4
Trace the kalam argument's regress
Follow the chain of syllogisms in the world's origination proof: from 'attributes are changing' to 'change implies originatedness', etc. Verify that each step ends with a necessary premise like 'the contingent only occurs as originated'.
This exercise demonstrates how a complex theological argument can meet the foundational requirement.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument about the foundational requirements for valid demonstration (burhan). The author states that if the premises of an argument are not themselves necessary (self-evident or intuitively certain), then they must ultimately trace back to necessary premises. The reason is to avoid two logical fallacies: circularity (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul). Circularity occurs when you prove the later (the conclusion) by something that depends on that later. Infinite regress occurs when you rely on an endless chain of proofs. If the chain ends with a premise that is neither necessary nor conceded, the proof is insufficient. The author then provides examples. For necessary premises: 'This number is divisible into two equals, and everything divisible like that is even'—this is a simple mathematical example, which is a necessary truth. For theoretical premises, he uses a kalam argument about the world's attributes being originated, leading to the conclusion that the world is originated. He breaks down the proof into several syllogisms, showing that eventually each chain ends in a necessary premise. He dismisses objections from philosophers as stubbornness. The passage underscores the importance of foundational certainty in Islamic logic, especially in kalam arguments for the world's temporality.

The First Two Sources of Certain Premises
Author lists and begins to explain the six types of certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish awwaliyat from mushahadat and identify which type of certainty each provides.

Core Idea
Certain premises for demonstration come from self-evident truths and inner observation.

Demonstration (burhan) requires premises that are absolutely certain. The author lists six sources, beginning with the two most basic: awwaliyat (self-evident truths) and mushahadat (inner observations). Understanding these sources is essential for constructing sound theological and legal proofs. In this lesson, you will learn to identify each type and see how they function in Islamic arguments.

Guided Path
1
Define awwaliyat
Find the description in the passage: awwaliyat are propositions the intellect judges immediately upon understanding the terms, without sensory aid. Note the examples: 'one is half of two' and 'the whole is greater than the part'.
These are necessary truths, not empirical.
2
Define mushahadat
Find the description: mushahadat require inner sense observation (hiss batini). They are called wijdaniyyat. Examples: knowing you are hungry, angry, in pleasure or pain.
These are certain but depend on internal perception.
3
Distinguish by dependency
Compare the two: awwaliyat need only conceptualization of the terms; mushahadat need inner observation. Awwaliyat are universal and necessary; mushahadat are personal but certain.
4
Apply to Islamic context
Think of an Islamic example for each. Awwaliyat: 'Allah is one' is not awwali because it requires revelation? Actually, the oneness of Allah is known through reasoning and revelation, not self-evident. Better: 'The whole is greater than the part' is awwali. For mushahadat: 'I feel sincere devotion (khushu') in prayer' is known through inner sense.
Not all Islamic truths are awwali; many are theoretical.

The Four Remaining Sources of Certain Premises
Defines the last four of the six certain sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish among المجربات, المتواترات, الحدسيات, and المحسوسات and provide an Islamic example for each.

Core Idea
Certainty in Islamic logic comes from six sources; the last four depend on experience, transmission, intuition, or direct sense.

We have covered the first two sources (أوليات and وجدانيات). Now the author completes the list with four more: تجربيات (based on repeated observation), متواترات (based on mass transmission), حدسيات (based on intuition from signs), and محسوسات (based on external senses). Each has a distinct epistemic basis, and knowing them prevents errors in reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
For each source, locate its name in the Arabic text. The author gives a precise definition and an example.
المجربات, المتواترات, الحدسيات, المحسوسات are the terms.

Determine what epistemic operation yields certainty for each: repeated observation (تجربة) for مجربات; hearing from an incorruptible multitude for متواترات; intuition (حدس) based on a sign for حدسيات; direct sense perception for محسوسات.
The definitions use phrases like 'يحتاج العقل في الجزم بحكمه إلى تكرار المشاهدة'.
3
Examine the example
The author gives an Islamic example for each: scammony for تجربيات, Prophet's prophethood for متواترات, moonlight for حدسيات, sun rising for محسوسات. See how the example illustrates the definition.
The examples are taken from natural science, history, astronomy, and everyday experience.
4
Contrast the sources

Commentary
This passage completes the enumeration of the six sources of certain premises (اليقينيات) by defining the last four: المجربات (experiential), المتواترات (recurrent reports), الحدسيات (intuitive), and المحسوسات (sensory). Another footnote (2) summarizes why certainties are confined to six: either the mind is independent (أوليات), or it needs nothing beyond itself (وجدانيات ومحسوسات), or it needs something else (تجربيات, متواترات, حدسيات). It also notes that knowledge from the latter three cannot prove something to others who lack the same experience.

Linguistic Material Fallacies: Homonymy and False Synonymy
Author explains linguistic material fallacies with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two linguistic causes of material error in demonstration: homonymy (ishtirāk) and misuse of distinct terms as synonyms (tabāyun).

Core Idea
A repeated term must have the same meaning; otherwise, the middle term is lost.

In demonstration, the form may be perfect, but if the matter is flawed, the conclusion fails. One source of material error lies in language: either a single word used in two senses (homonymy) or two different words wrongly treated as synonyms. The author illustrates both with concrete examples from Islamic law and everyday language, showing how these linguistic errors break the required repetition of the middle term.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term
Find the term that appears in both premises and should connect them.
In a valid syllogism, the middle term is the same in meaning in both occurrences.
2
Check for multiple meanings
If the middle term is a single word, verify that it is used in exactly the same sense in both premises. If not, it is homonymy (ishtirāk).
Example: 'qur' in fiqh can mean menstruation or purity.
3
Check for false synonymy
If different words are used in the two premises but treated as if they mean the same, verify whether they are truly synonymous. If not, it is the fallacy of false synonymy (tabāyun).
Example: 'sārim' (sharp sword) and 'sayf' (sword generally) are not synonyms.
4
Confirm that the middle term is repeated truly
In both cases, the middle term fails to be repeated in meaning. Conclude that the argument is invalid due to material error in wording.

And the error in demonstration, wherever it is found, is either in matter or form, so begin. In wording, like homonymy, or like making something with distinct meanings as a synonym. And in meanings, like confusing the false with the true, so understand the address, like making the accidental like the essential or a necessary consequence of one of the premises, and judging the genus by the ruling of the species, and making the non-certain as certain. I say: The error in demonstration is either in the matter of the propositions, or in the form and structure upon which the syllogism is based. Error in matter: The causes of error in matter are either in words or in meanings. As for the causes of error in words, they are: First: homonymy of the term used, like your saying 'this is a qur' (meaning menstrual period), and you intend menstruation, and 'every qur' allows intercourse', and you intend by it purity (non-menstrual state), so the middle term was not repeated, and the conclusion was false. Second: making distinct terms like synonyms, like your saying 'this is a sword' pointing to a non-cutting sword, and 'every sword is a sword', so the reality of 'sword' is distinct from the reality of 'sharp', because 'sword' is whatever is in the specific form, cutting or not, and 'sharp' is the sword with the condition of cutting. So the conclusion was false because 'sharp' in the minor premise was intended for the non-cutting, so the attribution of 'sword' to it in the major premise was invalid; rather it is attributed to the sharp one which is of the kind of sword, so the middle term was not repeated. As for the causes of error in meanings, they are: First: confusing a false proposition with a true one, like making the accidental essential, e.g., our saying 'the person sitting in the ship is moving, and everything moving does not remain in one place'. One of the two premises is false if 'moving' in them has one meaning; but if 'moving' in the first is intended as accidental motion and in the second as essential motion, both are true but not repeated, so the conclusion is not true. Second: making the conclusion one of the premises, like our saying 'this is a movement' and 'every movement is a motion', so 'this is a motion', the conclusion is identical to the minor premise because 'motion' is synonymous with 'movement'. Third: judging the genus by the ruling of the species, like our saying 'every horse is an animal' and 'every animal is rational', so 'every horse is rational', which is false. This is called 'illusion of the converse', because when he saw that every rational is an animal, he imagined that every animal is rational, but it is not so, so error occurred. Fourth: making a non-certain premise certain, e.g., your saying 'this is dead' and 'every dead is inanimate'. The major premise is illusory because the illusion judges the dead to be inanimate due to its being like inanimate in lacking spirit, sensation, and motion; so it was made in this syllogism as certain, and given the status of a certain premise in taking it as a part.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poem (matn) that summarizes the types of errors in demonstration. The author first quotes the poem, which states that error in demonstration occurs either in matter (material) or form. He then explains each cause in detail. The material errors are divided into those in wording and those in meanings. In wording, the first cause is homonymy (اشتراك اللفظ), where the same word is used in two different senses across premises, so the middle term is not truly repeated. The example uses the Arabic word 'qur' (قرء), which can mean either menstruation or purity in Islamic law (fiqh). If one premise uses it for menstruation and the other for purity, the middle term is not actually the same, and the conclusion is false. The second wording error is treating distinct terms as synonyms. The example uses 'sārim' (صارم, sharp sword) and 'sayf' (سيف, sword generally). A non-cutting sword is not truly a 'sārim' in the same sense. In meanings, the first error is confusing a false proposition with a true one, exemplified by accidental motion vs. essential motion: a person sitting in a moving ship moves accidentally, but an object that moves essentially changes place. Comparing them leads to a deceptive syllogism. The second meaning error is making the conclusion identical to a premise, which adds no new knowledge—like 'movement' (naqla) and 'motion' (haraka) being synonyms. The third is judging a genus by the property of a species, such as assuming all animals are rational because all rational beings are animals. This is the 'illusion of the converse' (إيهام العكس). The fourth is treating an uncertain or illusory premise as certain, like assuming a dead body is inanimate because it resembles inanimate objects, while in reality it was once alive. Each fallacy is illustrated with clear examples from logic and Islamic law.

Conceptual Material Fallacies: Accidental, Tautology, Genus-Species, and False Certainty
Author explains conceptual material fallacies with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify four types of conceptual material fallacies: confusing accidental with essential, conclusion as a premise, judging genus by species, and treating an uncertain premise as certain.

Core Idea
Even sound language can hide flawed content; premises must be true and relevant to demonstrate certainty.

Beyond wording, the meanings themselves can be erroneous. The author lists four distinct conceptual pitfalls: taking an accidental property as essential (e.g., motion in a ship), using a premise that is identical to the conclusion (tautology), assuming a property of a species applies to its genus (illusion of the converse), and treating a merely probable or illusory generalization as certain. These fallacies corrupt demonstration even when the syllogistic form is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify accidental vs. essential
Check whether a property used in the syllogism is essential (dhātī) or accidental (ʿaraḍī). If accidental is treated as essential, the premise is false or ambiguous.
Example: 'moving' in 'the person in the ship is moving' is accidental motion; 'everything moving changes place' assumes essential motion.
2
Check if conclusion is a premise
See if the conclusion simply restates one of the premises. If so, it is a tautology and adds no new knowledge.
Example: 'this is a movement, every movement is a motion, therefore this is a motion'—'motion' and 'movement' are synonyms.
3
Avoid judging genus by species
Do not assume that a property of a species (e.g., rationality in humans) applies to the entire genus (animals). This is the 'illusion of the converse' (īhām al-ʿaks).
Example: every horse is an animal, every animal is rational → false.
4
Distinguish certain from uncertain premises
Verify that each premise is certain (yaqīni), not merely assumed due to imagination or illusion (wahm). Treating an uncertain premise as certain leads to false conclusions.
Example: 'every dead is inanimate' is not certain; a dead body was once alive.

رَابِعاً: جَعْلُ الْمُقَدِّمَةِ غَيْرِ الْقَطْعِيَّةِ قَطْعِيَّةً، مِثَالُهُ قَوْلُكَ هَذَا مَيِّتٌ وَكُلُّ مَيِّتٍ جَمَادٌ، فَالْكُبْرَى وَهْمِيَّةٌ لِأَنَّ الْوَهْمَ يَحْكُمُ بِجَمَادِيَّةِ الْمَيِّتِ لِكَوْنِهِ كَالْجَمَادِ فِي عَدَمِ الرُّوحِ وَالْإِحْسَاسِ وَالْحَرَكَةِ فَجُعِلَتْ فِي هَذَا الْقِيَاسِ كَالْقَطْعِيَّةِ، وَنُزِّلَتْ مَنْزِلَتَهَا فِي أَخْذِهَا جُزْءاً لَهَا.
And the error in demonstration, wherever it is found, is either in matter or form, so begin. In wording, like homonymy, or like making something with distinct meanings as a synonym. And in meanings, like confusing the false with the true, so understand the address, like making the accidental like the essential or a necessary consequence of one of the premises, and judging the genus by the ruling of the species, and making the non-certain as certain. I say: The error in demonstration is either in the matter of the propositions, or in the form and structure upon which the syllogism is based. Error in matter: The causes of error in matter are either in words or in meanings. As for the causes of error in words, they are: First: homonymy of the term used, like your saying 'this is a qur' (meaning menstrual period), and you intend menstruation, and 'every qur' allows intercourse', and you intend by it purity (non-menstrual state), so the middle term was not repeated, and the conclusion was false. Second: making distinct terms like synonyms, like your saying 'this is a sword' pointing to a non-cutting sword, and 'every sword is a sword', so the reality of 'sword' is distinct from the reality of 'sharp', because 'sword' is whatever is in the specific form, cutting or not, and 'sharp' is the sword with the condition of cutting. So the conclusion was false because 'sharp' in the minor premise was intended for the non-cutting, so the attribution of 'sword' to it in the major premise was invalid; rather it is attributed to the sharp one which is of the kind of sword, so the middle term was not repeated. As for the causes of error in meanings, they are: First: confusing a false proposition with a true one, like making the accidental essential, e.g., our saying 'the person sitting in the ship is moving, and everything moving does not remain in one place'. One of the two premises is false if 'moving' in them has one meaning; but if 'moving' in the first is intended as accidental motion and in the second as essential motion, both are true but not repeated, so the conclusion is not true. Second: making the conclusion one of the premises, like our saying 'this is a movement' and 'every movement is a motion', so 'this is a motion', the conclusion is identical to the minor premise because 'motion' is synonymous with 'movement'. Third: judging the genus by the ruling of the species, like our saying 'every horse is an animal' and 'every animal is rational', so 'every horse is rational', which is false. This is called 'illusion of the converse', because when he saw that every rational is an animal, he imagined that every animal is rational, but it is not so, so error occurred. Fourth: making a non-certain premise certain, e.g., your saying 'this is dead' and 'every dead is inanimate'. The major premise is illusory because the illusion judges the dead to be inanimate due to its being like inanimate in lacking spirit, sensation, and motion; so it was made in this syllogism as certain, and given the status of a certain premise in taking it as a part.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poem (matn) that summarizes the types of errors in demonstration. The author first quotes the poem, which states that error in demonstration occurs either in matter (material) or form. He then explains each cause in detail. The material errors are divided into those in wording and those in meanings. In wording, the first cause is homonymy (اشتراك اللفظ), where the same word is used in two different senses across premises, so the middle term is not truly repeated. The example uses the Arabic word 'qur' (قرء), which can mean either menstruation or purity in Islamic law (fiqh). If one premise uses it for menstruation and the other for purity, the middle term is not actually the same, and the conclusion is false. The second wording error is treating distinct terms as synonyms. The example uses 'sārim' (صارم, sharp sword) and 'sayf' (سيف, sword generally). A non-cutting sword is not truly a 'sārim' in the same sense. In meanings, the first error is confusing a false proposition with a true one, exemplified by accidental motion vs. essential motion: a person sitting in a moving ship moves accidentally, but an object that moves essentially changes place. Comparing them leads to a deceptive syllogism. The second meaning error is making the conclusion identical to a premise, which adds no new knowledge—like 'movement' (naqla) and 'motion' (haraka) being synonyms. The third is judging a genus by the property of a species, such as assuming all animals are rational because all rational beings are animals. This is the 'illusion of the converse' (إيهام العكس). The fourth is treating an uncertain or illusory premise as certain, like assuming a dead body is inanimate because it resembles inanimate objects, while in reality it was once alive. Each fallacy is illustrated with clear examples from logic and Islamic law.

Material errors in meaning corrupt demonstration even when the form is valid; premises must be true, properly defined, and certain.
