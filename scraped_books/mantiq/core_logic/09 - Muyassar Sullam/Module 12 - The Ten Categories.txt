Classifying Propositions by Quantity
Author classifies propositions by quantity: personal, universal (quantified or indefinite).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given proposition as personal, universal quantified (with universal or particular quantifier), or universal indefinite based on its subject and quantifier.

Core Idea
Propositions are divided by quantity: personal (singular subject) or universal (universal subject, with or without a quantifier).

This passage builds on the distinction between personal and universal subjects by showing how propositions are classified by quantity. The logicians divide all propositions into personal (shakhsiyya) — where the subject is an individual — and universal (kulliyya) — where the subject is a universal concept. Universal propositions are further split depending on whether a quantifier is used and which type. Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing the scope of categorical statements in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject type
Check whether the subject of the proposition refers to a single individual (e.g., 'Zayd') or a universal concept (e.g., 'human', 'intoxicant'). If singular, it is personal (shakhsiyya). If universal, it is a candidate for a universal proposition.
In Arabic, personal propositions often have a proper noun or a definite singular as subject.
2
Check for a quantifier (sur)
For a universal subject, look for a quantifier word like 'every' (kull), 'all' (jami'), or 'some' (ba'd). If present, the proposition is quantified (musawwara). If absent, it is indefinite (muhmala).
The term 'sur' literally means 'wall' – the quantifier encloses the subject's extension.
3
Distinguish quantifier type
If quantified, determine whether the quantifier is universal (covers all individuals) or particular (covers only some). For example, 'Every intoxicant is haram' uses a universal quantifier; 'Some animals are halal' uses a particular quantifier.
Remember: universal quantifier = all; particular quantifier = some.
4
Classify the indefinite proposition
If no quantifier appears, the proposition is indefinite (muhmala). The quantity is left unspecified, but by default it is considered universal in this system. Example: 'Man is honored' (al-insanu mokarram) – no quantifier, so it is indefinite.
Indefinite propositions can sometimes be treated as universal or particular depending on context, but here they are a subtype of universal.

This classification is essential for understanding the four categorical forms in traditional logic.

The Exclusive-and-Exhaustive Disjunctive: Man'iat al-Jam' wa al-Khuluw
Defines the third type of disjunctive conditional with example and clarifies why it represents true opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exclusive-and-exhaustive disjunctive (man'iat al-jam' wa al-khuluw) and distinguish it from the other two types of disjunctive conditionals.

Core Idea
True opposition prevents both conjunction and separation — neither both true nor both false.

In this lesson, we examine the third and most complete type of disjunctive conditional. Unlike previous types which only exclude one possibility, this type excludes both the combination and the simultaneous absence of its parts. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise logical reasoning in Islamic sciences, especially when dealing with contradictions and exclusive categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibited cases
Note that this disjunctive prevents both jam' (conjunction) and khuluw (disjunction). That means: the two parts cannot both be true, and they cannot both be false. This is the key differentiator from the previous two types.
2
Recognize the composition from a thing and its contradictory
The passage states it is composed of a thing and its contradictory (naqīḍ). For example, 'even' and 'odd' are contradictories of each other with respect to an integer.
This ensures that the two parts are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
3
Test with the given example
Consider 'This number is either even or odd.' Can it be both even and odd? No. Can it be neither? No, because every integer is either even or odd. So both conditions are satisfied, confirming it as man'iat al-jam' wa al-khuluw.
4
Contrast with the weaker types
Compare with man'iat al-jam' (prevents only conjunction) and man'iat al-khuluw (prevents only separation). This type is more specific (akhaṣṣ) because it blocks both. Think of a statement like 'The animal is either a horse or a dog' – it prevents being both (jam') but does not prevent being neither (it could be a cat). That is not this type.
The author stresses that this type is the true representative of opposition (ta'ānud).

The third: the exclusive-and-exhaustive disjunctive, which is a proposition that prevents both conjunction and disjunction together, so neither can its two sides be combined nor can both be negated. It is composed of a thing and its contradictory, like our saying: 'This number is either even or odd.' So neither can evenness and oddness be combined in the specific number nor can both be negated from it.
This proposition is the true representative of opposition because opposition is realized between the two sides in truth and falsehood, unlike the preceding ones where it is in only one of them. This proposition is more specific than both preceding types.

So a thing being black and white together is an instance of the negation of the antecedent and consequent of this disjunctive together. But black and white are two opposites that cannot coexist in one subject due to impossibility. Hence he said they cannot both be negated, because their negation means the affirmation of two opposites in one subject, and that is impossible.

Commentary
This passage defines the third type of disjunctive conditional (man'iat al-jam' wa al-khuluw). It begins by stating that this type prevents both the conjunction (jam') and the separation (khuluw) of its two parts. That is, it forbids the simultaneous truth of both parts and also forbids the simultaneous falsehood of both. The example given is a number being either even or odd: a specific number cannot be both even and odd, nor can it be neither (since every integer must be one or the other). The text then explains why this type is the true representative of opposition (ta'ānud): here both truth and falsehood are mutually exclusive between the two sides, whereas in the previous two types (man'iat al-jam' and man'iat al-khuluw) the exclusivity is only in one respect. Consequently, this type is more specific (akhaṣṣ) than the other two. The supplement (faḍl) that follows clarifies why both parts cannot be false simultaneously. It uses the example of black and white: if something were neither black nor white, that would imply it is both black and white (since not non-black is black, not non-white is white), but black and white cannot coexist. Therefore, in a true exclusive-and-exhaustive disjunctive, it is impossible for both parts to be false.

Quantifiers for Conditional Propositions
Lists the quantifier words for universal and particular conditionals (both conjunctive and disjunctive).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct quantifier word for any given type of conditional proposition (conjunctive or disjunctive, affirmative or negative, universal or particular).

Core Idea
Conditional propositions have their own quantifier words, distinct from categoricals.

In this lesson, we complete what the poet omitted: the quantifiers for conditional propositions. While categoricals use 'kull', 'baʿḍ', etc., conditionals require a different set of words. Mastering these quantifiers is essential for handling conditional syllogisms in logic and Islamic sciences like uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two main types of conditionals
Conditionals are either conjunctive (if A then B) or disjunctive (either A or B). Identify which type each quantifier belongs to.
Conjunctive quantifiers include 'kullamā', 'mahmā', 'matā'; disjunctive uses 'dā'imā' for universal affirmative.
2
Distinguish affirmative from negative quantifiers
For universal negative, use 'laysa al-battata' (not at all) for both types. For particular quantities, use 'qad yakūnu' (sometimes) and 'qad lā yakūnu' (sometimes not).
'Qad yakūnu' is a particle indicating possibility, not always temporal.
3
Practice forming partial negation
Partial negation can also be expressed by placing 'laysa' before the universal quantifier, e.g., 'laysa kullamā' (not whenever).
This parallels how categoricals use 'laysa kullu' for particular negative.
4
Categorise the quantifiers by type
Make a mental table with rows for conjunctive/disjunctive and columns for universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, particular negative.
Memorise the specific words: kullamā, dā'imā, laysa al-battata, qad yakūnu, qad lā yakūnu.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining certain terms from the poet's text and then providing a crucial list of quantifier words for conditional propositions (sharṭiyyāt). The commentator first clarifies the wording of the poet: 'what admits truth' without mentioning falsehood is for brevity and politeness. He then explains references in the poem to categorical propositions, universal vs. particular, and the meaning of 'the other' (al-ākhar) as the later in rank. He notes that the poet did not explicitly lay out divisions and quantifiers for conditionals, as he did for categoricals, to keep things simple for beginners. The commentator then supplies the missing information. For conjunctive conditionals (muttaṣila), the universal affirmative quantifiers are 'kullamā' (whenever), 'mahmā' (whatever), and 'matā' (when). For disjunctive (munfaṣila), it is 'dā'imā' (always). Universal negation uses 'laysa al-battata' (not at all). Particular affirmative is expressed with 'qad yakūnu' (sometimes), and particular negative with 'qad lā yakūnu' (sometimes not). Partial negation can also be formed by prefixing the negation to the universal quantifier, e.g., 'laysa kullamā' (not whenever). The commentator encourages the reader to practice with examples. This knowledge is essential for understanding the logical judgments ('aḥkām') that follow.
