Defining Hamd and Shukr in Islamic Discourse
Commentator defines hamd and shukr and contrasts them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hamd and shukr according to their definitions and apply them to Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Hamd is verbal praise for beauty; shukr is specific gratitude using speech, action, or intention.

The commentator begins the logical poem by praising Allah, and then precisely defines the two key terms: hamd (praise) and shukr (thanks). Understanding this distinction is vital for interpreting Islamic texts accurately, especially in theological and jurisprudential contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Locate the Arabic definitions of hamd and shukr in the passage. Note that hamd is defined as verbal commendation of the beautiful, while shukr is the expenditure of blessings for their intended purpose.
The definitions are given in both linguistic and customary terms.
2
Extract genus and differentia
For hamd: genus is 'verbal commendation' (الثناء باللسان), differentia is 'of the beautiful, whether eternal or contingent voluntary'. For shukr: genus is 'expenditure of blessings' (صرف العبد جميع ما أنعم الله به), differentia is 'for the purpose for which they were created'.
3
Compare scope
The commentator explains that shukr is more general in means (speech, action, intention) but more specific in object (only in return for favor), while hamd is specific in means (speech) but general in object.
This shows a partial overlap.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the phrase 'Alhamdulillah' after eating. Is it hamd, shukr, or both? Justify using the definitions.
Try to determine if it is in return for a specific favor or general praise.

He also praised Allah for the blessing of Islam and faith, and sending messengers — that He is not obligated to do at all, but He bestowed it as a favor, so He deserves praise and thanks. Then the author followed with a general introduction on the benefit of logic to bring its concept closer to minds, saying: (1) His saying (Praise be to Allah — praise is verbal commendation of the beautiful, eternal in essence and attributes, and the beautiful, contingent, voluntary, not the necessary by essence and natural. Customarily, it is an act indicating veneration of the benefactor due to His favor absolutely, whether on the one praising or others. Here it is praised with a verbal sentence indicating renewal because favors are renewed. And it is appropriate that (2) His saying (not) — it is not obligatory at all upon Allah because nothing is obligatory upon Him absolutely; if something were obligatory, He would be compelled, not a free agent, and one forced to act is not praised. (3) Thanks linguistically is praise terminologically, with the substitute of 'one praising' with 'one thanking'. Customarily: the servant's expenditure of all that Allah has bestowed upon him for the purpose for which it was created. So thanks is more general than praise in terms of its means because it can be by speech, action, and intention, while praise is only by speech, and more specific in terms of its object because thanks is in return for a favor, while praise does not require that. So between them is generality and specificity from one perspective. (4) And here is what the virtuous one left for teachers of explanation of the verses and other observations. His saying (He brought forth) means He created, better than 'manifested', because manifestation is for something hidden, but results are not so in minds. His saying (results) plural of result, which is the proposition necessarily following from two premises, as will come in the chapter on syllogism. In it is 'bara'at al-istihlal' (good opening), i.e., the author mentions at the beginning of his book what indicates his intention — also called 'ilma'. (Lords of intellect) people of minds. (Removed) meaning removed. (From the sky of intellect) i.e., from the intellect which is like a sky, by the commonality that it is the rising place of ideas just as the sky is the rising place of stars — it is a 'tashbih baligh' (eloquent simile) because the aspect of similarity and the tool are omitted, and the simile is added to the simile. Similarly, his saying (clouds of ignorance) means ignorance like clouds, by the commonality that each conceals and prevents discovery. It is extremely subtle. (Suns of knowledge) i.e., knowledge which is like suns, as before. (The secluded ones) those hidden in their curtains. The meaning: there appeared to them, after removal, the utmost knowledge that was hidden from them due to its subtlety. (From) before 'He specified us' is a substitute for the attached pronoun in 'we praise Him' referring to the Lord, and (the best of those sent) Muhammad (peace be upon him). He is the best who attained stations by striving and acquisition, and states by gift. Mentioning stations reminds of the necessity of striving to attain high things. (Muhammad) is a substituted noun in genitive from 'the best'. The one in charge of the main body of the army is a sayyid (master). He (peace be upon him) is in charge of his community of both weighty things (jinn and men), and 'al-muqtafa' (followed) as a passive participle means the one followed, so he is the master of followers a fortiori. (Lujaj) plural of lujja, which is the main body of water and its difficulty. He likened meanings to a sea; the intellect wades into its depths, i.e., its difficult expanse. He did not intend to restrict prayer to the intellect's wading in those difficulties, but rather to generalize prayer upon him at all times. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the opening lines of the poem (al-Muqaddima). The commentator explains that the author begins with an introduction suitable for logic, praising Allah for intellect, faith, and sending messengers. He then defines 'hamd' (praise) as verbal commendation of the beautiful, whether eternal or contingent voluntary, and notes that it is expressed with a verbal sentence indicating renewal. He clarifies that Allah is not obligated to do anything — if obligated, He would be compelled and not worthy of praise. He distinguishes 'hamd' from 'shukr' (thanks): thanks is more general in means (speech, action, intention) but more specific in object (only in return for favor), while praise is specific in means (speech) but general in object. Then he explains the vocabulary of the poem: 'akhraja' (brought forth) is better than 'azhara' for creating new results, 'nata'ij' (results) are conclusions from two premises, 'arbab al-hija' (people of intellect), 'hatta' (removed), 'sama' al-'aql' (sky of intellect) is an eloquent simile, 'sahab al-jahl' (clouds of ignorance) another simile, 'shumus al-ma'rifa' (suns of knowledge), 'mukhaddarat' (secluded ones). He comments on the substitution in the grammar, and explains that the Prophet is the best of those sent and the master of his community, and the omission of the subject in 'shubbihu' magnifies the Companions, referencing the hadith about them being like stars.

The Opening Metaphor and the Definition of Natija
Commentator explains poetic metaphors and defines natija.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the poetic metaphors in the opening lines and define 'natija' as the conclusion of a syllogism.

Core Idea
The poem's metaphors compare intellect to a sky and ignorance to clouds, highlighting logic's role in uncovering hidden knowledge.

After praising Allah, the author introduces the benefit of logic through vivid imagery: results are brought forth from the sky of intellect like stars, and knowledge shines when ignorance is removed. The commentator explains these metaphors and gives the precise logical definition of 'natija' (result) as the proposition necessarily following from two premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key metaphor
Find the phrase 'أخرجـا نتائج من سماء العقل'. Recognize that intellect is compared to a sky, and results to stars that appear when clouds (ignorance) are removed.
The commentator calls this 'tashbih baligh' (eloquent simile) because the tool and aspect are omitted.
2
Understand the logical term 'natija'
The commentator defines 'natija' as 'al-qadiyya allati talzamu 'an muqaddimatayn' — the proposition necessarily following from two premises. This is the conclusion of a categorical syllogism.
This definition anticipates the chapter on syllogism (qiyas).
3
Analyze the other similes
Examine 'سحاب الجهل' (clouds of ignorance) and 'شموس المعرفة' (suns of knowledge). Note the commonality: clouds conceal, ignorance prevents discovery; suns illuminate, knowledge reveals. The commentator notes the subtlety.
These are also tashbih baligh.
4
Connect to the purpose of logic
The overall metaphor illustrates that logic (embodied in the poem) removes the clouds of ignorance from the intellect, allowing the suns of knowledge (true conclusions) to shine. This is the foundational justification for studying logic.

Then he explains the vocabulary of the poem: 'akhraja' (brought forth) is better than 'azhara' for creating new results, 'nata'ij' (results) are conclusions from two premises, 'arbab al-hija' (people of intellect), 'hatta' (removed), 'sama' al-'aql' (sky of intellect) is an eloquent simile, 'sahab al-jahl' (clouds of ignorance) another simile, 'shumus al-ma'rifa' (suns of knowledge), 'mukhaddarat' (secluded ones). He comments on the substitution in the grammar, and explains that the Prophet is the best of those sent and the master of his community, and the omission of the subject in 'shubbihu' magnifies the Companions, referencing the hadith about them being like stars.
