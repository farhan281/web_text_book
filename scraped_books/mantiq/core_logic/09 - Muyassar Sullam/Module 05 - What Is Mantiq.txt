Logic as a Legal Instrument
Defines logic, its subject, fruit, and founder, and lists its ten principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the definition of logic as a legal instrument (āla qānūniyya) and identify its function of safeguarding the mind from error.

Core Idea
Logic is a legal instrument that safeguards the mind from error in thinking.

In this passage, the commentator defines logic with a precise formulation. This definition is foundational because it tells us what logic is for and how it functions. Let's break it down.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic. It is 'al-mantiq' (logic).
2
Extract the definition's components
The definition has two parts: what logic is (āla qānūniyya) and what it does (ta‘ṣim al-dhihn ‘an al-khaṭa’).
The phrase 'ta‘ṣimu murā‘ātuhā' indicates that by observing its rules, the mind is protected.
3
Interpret 'āla qānūniyya'
This means a set of rules (qawānīn) that function as an instrument to guide reasoning.
Similar to how grammar is an instrument for correct speech, logic is an instrument for correct thought.

The Subject and Fruit of Logic
Defines logic, its subject, fruit, and founder, and lists its ten principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the subject matter of logic (al-ma‘lūmāt al-taṣawwuriyya wa al-taṣdīqiyya) and describe its utility in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Logic studies conceptual and assertive knowledge to lead the mind to new knowledge.

Having defined logic itself, the commentator turns to what logic studies and why it is valuable. This lesson unpacks the subject (al-mawḍū‘) and the fruit (al-thamara) of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of logic
Find the phrase 'al-mawḍū‘' in the Arabic. The subject is 'al-ma‘lūmāt al-taṣawwuriyya wa al-taṣdīqiyya'.
These are two types of cognition: tasawwur (concept) and tasdiq (assent/judgment).
2
Understand the aspect of study
Logic studies these from the perspective of how they lead to other unknown concepts and assents (min ḥaythu ṣiḥḥat īṣālihā ilā taṣawwurāt wa taṣdīqāt ukhrā lam takun ma‘lūma).
Logic is a tool for moving from known to unknown.
3
Identify the fruit (thamara)
The fruit is the benefit: it is useful for proving religious beliefs and refuting false ones, as logic is a principle of kalam and usul al-fiqh.
The commentator emphasizes logic's utility in these sciences.

The Many Names of the Intellect
Lexical analysis of al-jinan and synonyms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five synonyms of al-jinan mentioned in the text and explain the rationale behind each name.

Core Idea
The intellect has many names, each highlighting a distinct function.

The gloss opens with a detailed lexical analysis of al-jinan (the intellect). The author enumerates several synonymous terms—al-lubb, al-haija, al-hijr, al-nuhā—and explains why each is appropriate. This parsing exercise sharpens your understanding of how Islamic scholars defined the seat of reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate 'al-jinan' in the Arabic and note its basic meaning: the heart, i.e., the intellect.
2
Extract the synonyms
Find and list al-lubb, al-haija, al-hijr, al-nuhā as they appear in the passage.
Each is introduced with a reason (li-anna).
3
Analyze the rationale for each synonym
For each synonym, note the root meaning: lubb = core; haija = argument; hijr = restraint; nuhā = intelligence. Understand why these apply to the intellect.
The text gives explicit reasons: lubb is the essence, haija for hitting proofs, hijr for restraining from prohibitions, nuhā for pinnacle of intelligence.
4
Confirm the overall function
The intellect is described as the ultimate good for the servant; note its importance in acquiring rectitude in this world and the next.

Ya'sim: Protection, Not Infallibility
Clarification of ya'sim in the definition of logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the meaning of 'iṣma in the definition of logic from its theological usage.

Core Idea
'Iṣma in logic means protection, not infallibility.

The author clarifies the crucial term ya'sim. It is not the theological 'iṣma (infallibility of prophets) but a linguistic protection that prevents error while allowing its possibility. Understanding this prevents a major misunderstanding of logic's scope.

Guided Path
1
Locate the term ya'sim
Find the word ya'sim in the Arabic passage and note its basic meaning: 'protects'.
2
Identify the negation
Note the phrase 'وليست من العصمة الشرعية' (it is not from legal 'iṣma) and understand that the author is negating theological infallibility.
3
Define the linguistic meaning
Parse 'بل هي لغوية بمعنى أنها تمنع منه مع إمكان الوقوع فيه' – it prevents but does not make error impossible. Error can still occur for a reason known to the observer.
4
Connect to ghayy and da'īq al-fahm
Understand that ghayy is the opposite of guidance, and da'īq al-fahm means precise understanding; these terms relate to the goal of logic.

It is described as acquired for two reasons: First, man is not born knowing; rather, he is born devoid of sciences and knowledges (and the evidence for this is His statement: 'And Allah brought you out from the wombs of your mothers while you knew nothing' [Quran 16:78]). So every knowledge he has afterward occurs in him after not being. Second, most sciences are not necessary; rather, their attainment in man depends on effort and reflection. Therefore, scholars call them, by way of predominance, theoretical sciences. They are also called acquired sciences because they occur in the human soul, and everything that occurs in it is acquisition as long as it depends on will. (Meaning they are not self-evident, obtained by necessity without will followed by reflection and acquisition. Necessity here is the opposite of acquisition, so it is not objected that all sciences are necessarily obtained, because the talk is about the ordinary preparatory causes, not about the actual obtaining. So the intuitive that is spoken about here is not among the causes of theoretical.) The gist is that logic has no entry except into occurring sciences. So it is not said that divine knowledge also depends on it, for this would be confusing logic with occurring knowledge. Divine knowledge is eternal, and the eternal does not depend on anything. Thus, logic is a means for human sciences, not for others. So all the divisions, arrangements, types, and causes that will come have no connection to divine knowledge; rather, they are specific to human knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn verses that open the book. The matn states that the first (i.e., conceptualization or tasawwur) is given precedence in arrangement because it is logically prior. It then defines theoretical knowledge as that requiring reflection, while its opposite is self-evident intuitive knowledge. Finally, it names the statement that leads to conceptualization as 'explanatory statement' (qawl sharh) and that which leads to assent as 'proof' (hujja). The commentator (āqūl) then expands: he defines logic as the science studying the methods that lead to knowledge, emphasizing that knowledge is the goal of logic. He explains that the logician seeks acquired knowledge, which is the knowledge of creatures. He argues that acquired knowledge is not innate, citing the Quranic verse 'you knew nothing,' and that most knowledge requires effort, making it theoretical. He distinguishes intuitive (daruri) knowledge as that which occurs without effort, noting that logic is only relevant to acquired human knowledge, not to divine knowledge which is eternal. The commentary clarifies that the term 'necessary' here refers to the ordinary causes, not the actual occurrence, to avoid the objection that all knowledge is necessary. The lesson establishes the scope of logic: it is a tool for human sciences only.

The Five Relations of Words to Meanings
Defines the five relations between words and meanings with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five relations between words and meanings (tawāṭu', tashākuk, takhāluf, ishtirāk, tarāduf) and correctly classify any given word pair.

Core Idea
A word's relation to its meaning reveals whether it is univocal, ambiguous, synonymous, or exclusive.

In Islamic logic, understanding how words relate to meanings is foundational for precise definition and argumentation. This passage from the Sullam al-Munawraq outlines five essential relations. Mastering them will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions, avoid equivocation fallacies, and engage in rigorous theological and legal discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five relations from the poem
The opening line lists five terms: تواطؤ, تشاكك, تخالف, اشتراك, and its opposite ترادف. Locate them in the Arabic.
The poem compresses the entire classification.
2
Analyze tawāṭu' (univocal)
A word with one meaning applied equally to all individuals. Example: 'human' (insān). Note that differences are external accidents, not in the core meaning.
Univocals are the default for most universal terms.
3
Analyze tashākuk (equivocal by analogy)
One meaning applied with varying intensity. Example: 'light' (nūr) stronger in sun than moon. The observer hesitates between univocality and homonymy.
Also called 'ambiguous' due to graded application.
4
Analyze takhāluf (mutual exclusion)
Two words with meanings that do not overlap at all. Example: 'human' and 'horse'. Condition: both words and meanings differ.
This is the default relation between any two different words.
5
Analyze ishtirāk (homonymy) and tarāduf (synonymy)
Ishtirāk: one word, multiple meanings (e.g., 'ayn for eye/spring). Tarāduf: multiple words, one meaning (e.g., insān and bashar). They are opposites.
Tarāduf is the opposite of ishtirāk as stated in the poem.

Distinguishing Request from Report in Logic
Author classifies utterances into request and report, subdividing request.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any utterance as request or report and, if request, identify it as command, supplication, or entreaty based on the relational display between requester and addressee.

Core Idea
Logic studies only truth-bearing reports; requests, though varied, fall outside its domain.

In order to know what logic investigates, we must first separate utterances that can be true or false from those that seek an action. This lesson unpacks the division of utterances into report (khabar) and request (talab), and the three types of request based on the social dynamic displayed: superiority (command), lowliness (supplication), or equality (entreaty). The author deliberately excludes requests from logical study, focusing our attention on propositions that admit truth and falsehood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance type
Read the utterance and decide if it is asking for an action (like a command or prayer) or if it can be declared true or false. If it can be true/false, it is a report (khabar); if it seeks action, it is a request (talab).
Examples: 'Zayd is standing' is report; 'Strike!' is request.
2
Check for superiority display
If it is a request, look at the relationship between the requester and the one asked. If the requester shows superiority over the addressee, it is command (amr). Superiority is displayed, not necessarily actual rank.
Even a slave can command if he displays superiority, but that would be irregular.
3
Check for lowliness display
If the requester shows lowliness before the addressee, it is supplication (du‘ā’). This is typical in prayers to God or requests from an inferior to a superior.
The text says 'showing lowliness' (khudū‘) and 'display of inferiority'.
4
Check for equality display
If the requester shows equality with the addressee, it is entreaty (iltimās). This occurs when two equals interact, e.g., friend to friend.
The criterion is the displayed stance, not actual social status.
5
Understand the logical purpose
The division is made to clarify the report because logic only studies statements that can be true or false. Requests are not truth-apt and thus not the subject of logical analysis.
The author says: 'The purpose of the division is to clarify the report, because the logician only investigates the report.'

Composing a Complete Definition (Hadd Tamm)
Author explains composition of complete definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compose a complete definition (hadd tamm) by combining the nearest genus and nearest differentia, and distinguish it from incomplete definitions.

Core Idea
A complete definition captures the essence by joining the immediate genus and the unique differentia.

In this lesson, we explore the first type of definition: the complete definition (hadd tamm). The author explains how to construct it using the five universals, specifically the nearest genus and differentia. Understanding this system allows you to define any concept precisely, a skill essential for Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification of definitions
The author divides definitions into three: hadd (definition), rasm (description), and lafzi (verbal). Hadd and rasm each have complete and incomplete forms. Focus on hadd tamm.
2
Recall the five universals
A hadd tamm requires knowledge of the five universals, especially genus (jins) and differentia (fasl). These are the building blocks of definitions.
The five universals are: genus, species, differentia, property, and common accident.
3
Extract the nearest genus
Find the immediate category under which the defined term falls. Example: for 'man', the nearest genus is 'animal' (hayawan).
The genus must be shared by all individuals of the species.
4
Extract the nearest differentia
Identify the unique essential characteristic that distinguishes the species from others in the same genus. Example: for 'man', the differentia is 'rational' (natiq), meaning thinker in potentiality.
Differentia must be essential (dhati), not accidental.
5
Combine genus and differentia
Form the complete definition by joining the nearest genus and nearest differentia. Example: 'Man is a rational animal'.
This combination must include all instances of the defined and exclude all others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compose a complete description (rasm tamm) by combining the nearest genus with a unique property (khassa), and distinguish it from a complete definition.

Core Idea
A complete description identifies the defined term by its genus and a unique accidental property, not its essence.

Not all definitions need to capture the essence. Sometimes, a description (rasm) suffices by using a genus and a unique property. This lesson follows the author's explanation of how to construct a rasm tamm, using 'laughing animal' as an example for man. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise conceptualization in Islamic sciences.

1
Recall the difference between hadd and rasm

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth, quality, and quantity—except universal affirmatives.

Conversion is one of the fundamental rulings on propositions, enabling us to derive new propositions from given ones while preserving truth. This lesson unpacks the poet's definition of simple conversion, highlighting what is preserved and what changes. You will learn to apply this rule to categorical propositions, a skill essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operation described
Find where the poet defines conversion as 'reversing the two parts of the proposition.' In a categorical proposition, the two parts are the subject and predicate. So simple conversion swaps them.
2
Recognize the preserved features
Note the three features preserved: truth (if original is true, converse is true), quality (affirmative/negative stays same), and quantity (universal/particular stays same, except for one case). These ensure the converse is logically equivalent.
3

The verse states: 'except the universal affirmative, for they substituted it with the particular affirmative.' This means the converse of 'All A are B' is 'Some B are A', not 'All B are A'.

The poet advises moderation ('faqtasid') as a reminder not to overstretch the rule.
5
Distinguish natural vs. conventional order
The verse concludes that conversion follows a natural order (the logical order of terms as they appear) not a conventional one (as custom would order them). This reaffirms that the swap is determined by the proposition's structure, not by usage.

Divisions of Qiyas: Conjunctive and Conditional
Divides qiyas into two types and corrects the matn on iqtirani composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive (iqtirani) and conditional (sharti) syllogisms, and explain the corrected view that iqtirani can be composed from conditional propositions.

Core Idea
The conjunctive syllogism links terms directly; the conditional uses exception particles.

After defining qiyas, the author divides it into two types: conjunctive (iqtirani) and conditional (sharti). The key difference is the presence of an exception particle (like 'if' or 'except') in conditional syllogisms. The passage also corrects a common misconception: the original matn claimed iqtirani is only from categorical propositions, but the commentator argues it can also be composed from conditional propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of division
The passage states that qiyas is divided into iqtirani and sharti. The distinction lies in whether the premises are conjoined without an exception particle (iqtirani) or with one (sharti).
Iqtirani means 'conjunctive' because the terms are linked directly.
2
Examine the corrected view
The matn author said iqtirani is only from categorical premises. The commentator rejects this, providing an example with conditionals: 'Whenever the sun is rising, daytime exists; whenever daytime exists, the earth is illuminated; therefore whenever the sun is rising, the earth is illuminated.' This is iqtirani because there is no exception particle separating the premises.
This correction expands the scope of iqtirani.
3
Contrast with sharti (conditional)
A conditional syllogism (sharti) uses an exception particle like 'if' or 'except' to separate a premise that is excepted. For example: 'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists; but the sun is rising; therefore daytime exists.' This is not iqtirani because the exception particle 'if' appears.
The passage does not elaborate on sharti, but it is the counterpart.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Take a Fiqh example: 'If a traveler shortens the prayer, then he is excused from fasting; the traveler shortens the prayer; therefore he is excused from fasting.' This is sharti. Compare with iqtirani: 'Every traveler who shortens the prayer is excused from fasting; this person is a traveler who shortened the prayer; therefore this person is excused from fasting.'
The logical structure differs in the role of the exception.
