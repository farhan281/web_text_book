What is Logical Contradiction (Tanaquḍ)?
Defines logical contradiction and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define logical contradiction between two propositions and identify the conditions for contradictory propositions.

Core Idea
Contradiction is difference in quality with exclusive truth.

In previous lessons, we classified propositions into types. Now we study judgments of propositions, starting with contradiction (tanaquḍ). Contradiction is essential for constructing proofs and verifying truth. The poem defines it as difference in quality such that only one proposition is true. Let's unpack this definition carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Find the two propositions being compared. They must be complete categorical propositions (subject, predicate, copula). Are they affirmative or negative? What is their quantity?
2
Check their quality
Determine if one is affirmative (ījāb) and the other negative (salb). If both are affirmative or both negative, they cannot be contradictories.
Quality is the key differentia in the definition.
3
Check their truth values
Can both propositions be true simultaneously? If yes, not contradictory. Can both be false? If yes, not contradictory. Exactly one must be true.
The definition requires that one and only one is true (luzūm ṣidq wāḥida).
4
Verify the definition against examples
The commentary excludes pairs differing in subject, predicate, or quantity alone. Test a few: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Some human is an animal'? Both true, so not contradictory.

How to Find the Contradictory of Any Proposition
Provides rules for finding contradictories for singular, indefinite, and quantified propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory of any categorical proposition by applying the rules of changing quality and quantity appropriately.

Core Idea
To contradict a quantified proposition, change both quantity and quality.

The poem gave us clear rules: for singular or indefinite propositions, just change the quality. For quantified propositions, change the quantifier to its opposite and also flip the quality. Let's practice these rules step by step with examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Classify the proposition
Is the proposition universal (كل/جميع), particular (بعض), singular (مفرد like 'Zayd'), or indefinite (مهملة, no quantifier)? Identify its quantity and quality.
2
Apply rule for singular/indefinite
If the proposition is about an individual (e.g., 'Zayd is standing') or indefinite (e.g., 'Human is animal'), the contradictory is simply the same proposition with opposite quality: 'Zayd is not standing', 'Some human is not animal'? Wait: indefinite means no quantifier, but it is treated like particular? The text says 'change by quality'—so for indefinite, you change the quality but keep the absence of quantifier? The commentary clarifies: for personal (singular) or unquantified (indefinite), you just change the quality. Example: 'Zayd is standing' => 'Zayd is not standing'.
In indefinite propositions, the contradictory is also indefinite with opposite quality.
3
Apply rule for quantified propositions
For quantified propositions, change the quantifier to its opposite and reverse the quality. The poem gives two cases: universal affirmative => particular negative; universal negative => particular affirmative. For particulars: particular affirmative => universal negative; particular negative => universal affirmative.
Memorize the four conversions: A->O, E->I, I->E, O->A.
4
Verify that exactly one is true
After constructing the contradictory, check that the original and its contradictory cannot both be true and cannot both be false. Use a concrete example: 'Every believer is righteous' and 'Some believer is not righteous' – if one is true, the other must be false.

Definition and Conditions of Contradiction (Tanāquḍ)
Author defines contradiction, excludes other differences, and gives rules for deriving contradictories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two categorical propositions satisfy the conditions for contradiction.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires difference in quality alone — not quantity, subject, or predicate.

In this passage, the author defines tanāquḍ as a judgment on propositions essential for proofs. He distinguishes it from other differences and clarifies that only one proposition can be true. We'll break down the conditions and see how they apply to real fiqh and kalam examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Locate the two statements being compared. Ensure they are complete categorical propositions with the same subject and predicate.
2
Check difference in quality
One proposition must be affirmative, the other negative. If they are both affirmative or both negative, they cannot be contradictories.
3
Verify unity in other aspects
Ensure they do not differ in quantity (universal vs. particular), subject, predicate, time, or place. Any such difference means they are not contradictories.
For example, 'Every human is an animal' and 'Some human is an animal' differ only in quantity, so they are not contradictories.
4
Confirm truth-value exclusivity
It must be impossible for both propositions to be true together or both false together. One must be necessarily true and the other false.
Subcontraries (e.g., some A is B and some A is not B) can both be true; contraries (e.g., every A is B and no A is B) can both be false.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Swap subject and predicate in categoricals; swap antecedent and consequent in conditionals.

Truth and Falsity Entailment in Conversion
Explains the logical entailment between original and converse propositions in conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the truth/falsity entailment between an original categorical proposition and its converse, and apply the broader/narrower rules to validate conversion outcomes.

Core Idea
Truth of original implies truth of converse; falsity of converse implies falsity of original, but not vice versa.

When you convert a proposition, you need to know what the conversion tells you about truth. Does a true original guarantee a true converse? Does a false converse prove the original false? This passage from al-Muyassar lays out exactly these entailments, using the concepts of malzum and lazim, and then connects them to the conversion of universal affirmatives by introducing the broader/narrower relationship between subject and predicate.

Guided Path

The original is the proposition before conversion; the converse is the result.
2

From a true original, the converse must be true. From a false converse, the original must be false. These are necessary implications. Memorize: ṣidq al-malzum yastalzim ṣidq al-lazim, and kidhb al-lazim yastalzim kidhb al-malzum.
These rules are the core of the passage.
3
Recognize non-entailments
The falsity of the original does not force the converse to be false or true—it could be either. Similarly, the truth of the converse does not force the original to be true or false. This asymmetry is crucial for avoiding logical errors.
Example: 'Every animal is human' is false, but its converse 'Some human is animal' is true.
4
Connect broader/narrower rules
The passage then links conversion to the relationship between broader (a'amm) and narrower (akhass) terms. For a universal affirmative, the predicate is broader than the subject. Affirming the broader does not affirm the narrower, so the converse cannot be universal. Only when the narrower is affirmed does the broader necessarily follow, explaining why the converse is particular.
These rules also apply to negations: negating the broader negates the narrower, but not vice versa.

Natural vs Conventional Ordering in Conversion
Author clarifies that conversion requires natural ordering.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish naturally ordered propositions (which convert) from conventionally ordered propositions (which do not) and apply this condition to categoricals and conditionals.

Core Idea
Only propositions where meaning changes with order (natural) are convertible; disjunctives are symmetric and exempt.

The final condition for conversion is that the proposition must be 'naturally ordered' (murattab bi al-tab'). This means the truth conditions depend on the order of terms. Categoricals and conjunct conditionals (if-then) are naturally ordered because swapping terms changes the meaning. But disjunctive conditionals (either-or) are 'conventionally ordered' (murattab bi al-wad')—their meaning is symmetric, so conversion does not apply. This nuance prevents misuse of conversion in complex arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of ordering
Naturally ordered: the meaning changes when terms are swapped. Conventionally ordered: the meaning remains the same when terms are swapped.
The text gives examples: categoricals and conjunct conditionals are natural; disjunctives are conventional.
2
Identify naturally ordered propositions
Categoricals: 'كل إنسان حيوان' vs 'كل حيوان إنسان' have different truth values. Conjunct conditionals: 'إذا كانت الشمس طالعة فالنهار موجود' vs 'إذا كان النهار موجودا فالشمس طالعة' are not equivalent.
These convert by swapping the antecedent and consequent in the converse.
3
Identify conventionally ordered propositions
Disjunctives: 'العدد إما زوج أو فرد' and 'العدد إما فرد أو زوج' mean the same thing. The order is just verbal.
Therefore, disjunctives have no valid conversion in the sense used here.
4
Apply the condition to conversion
Before attempting conversion, check whether the proposition is naturally ordered. If it is conventionally ordered (like disjunctives), conversion is not applicable.
This condition ensures conversion only applies where it preserves truth.

Rules and Conditions of Conversion (al-‘Aks)
Author explains conversion of categorical and conditional propositions with examples and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to correctly convert categorical and singular propositions according to the rules of al-'aks, identifying which types are valid and which are not.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate but only works when the combination of quantity and quality allows it.

Conversion (al-'aks) is a fundamental immediate inference in Islamic logic, used to restate propositions without changing their truth value under specific conditions. This passage from the Sullam al-‘Ulum explains the rules for converting affirmative and negative categorical propositions, including singular and indefinite cases, and highlights the critical exceptions you must recognize to avoid logical errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Determine the quantity (universal, particular, indefinite, singular) and quality (affirmative, negative) of the original proposition. Also check if the predicate is universal or particular.
For example, 'Every human is animal' is universal affirmative with universal predicate.
2
Apply the conversion rule for affirmatives
Universal affirmatives and indefinite affirmatives convert to particular affirmatives. Particular affirmatives remain particular affirmatives.
E.g., 'Every human is animal' becomes 'Some animal is human'.
3
Apply the conversion rule for negatives
Universal negatives convert to themselves (e.g., 'No human is stone' becomes 'No stone is human'). Singular negatives convert to universal negatives if the predicate is universal; if the predicate is particular, the conversion is identical.
For 'Zayd is not stone', the conversion is 'No stone is Zayd'.
4
Check for exceptions
Particular negatives (e.g., 'Some animal is not human') have no valid conversion because they combine negativity and particularity. Indefinite negatives (e.g., 'Animal is not human') also fail because they function as particulars.
These are called 'al-khastān' – the two qualities that prevent conversion.
5
Verify natural order condition
Conversion only applies to propositions with natural order (categoricals and conjunctive conditionals), where swapping terms changes the meaning appropriately. Disjunctive conditionals have a verbal order that does not affect meaning, so conversion is not defined.
E.g., 'The number is either even or odd' and 'either odd or even' are equivalent.

Commentary
This passage explains the rules of conversion (al-‘aks) in logic, an immediate inference that swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth under specific conditions. The author begins with examples of affirmative conversions: universal affirmative ('Every human is animal') converts to particular affirmative ('Some animal is human'); particular affirmative ('Some human is animal') also converts to particular affirmative; and indefinite affirmative ('Human is animal') treated as universal, also converts to particular. For negatives, only universal negative converts to itself ('No human is stone' to 'No stone is human'). Singular negatives convert to universal negatives (e.g., 'Zayd is not stone' to 'No stone is Zayd') because the singular term is treated as universal. If the predicate is particular (like 'Zayd is not Amr'), the conversion is the same ('Amr is not Zayd'). Then the author lists two exceptions: (1) the particular negative (e.g., 'Some animal is not human') has no conversion because it negates a more specific term from some individuals of a more general term, but its conversion would require negating a more general term from some individuals of a more specific term, which is false. Finally, conversion applies only to categorical propositions and conditional conjunctives, not to disjunctives, because disjunctives have a verbal order that does not affect meaning (e.g., 'The number is either even or odd' is equivalent to 'either odd or even'). The key condition is that the proposition must have a 'natural order' where swapping subject and predicate changes the meaning appropriately.

Conversion by Contradiction: The Two Types
Author defines conversion by contradiction and distinguishes its two types: موافق and مخالف.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the concordant (الموافق) and contrary (المخالف) types of conversion by contradiction and apply each to a given proposition.

Core Idea
Conversion by contradiction transforms a proposition by flipping terms and possibly quality.

This passage introduces an important immediate inference known as conversion by contradiction (عكس النقيض). It is not a simple reversal like usual conversion; instead, it replaces terms with their contradictories. The author distinguishes two types based on whether the quality of the original statement is preserved or changed. Understanding these operations is essential for certain logical proofs in Islamic legal reasoning and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Find the subject and predicate of the given statement. For example, in 'Every human is an animal', the subject is 'human' and the predicate is 'animal'.
2
Determine the type
Decide whether the operation should preserve quality (الموافق) or change it (المخالف). The concordant type keeps the quantity and quality; the contrary type changes quality from affirmative to negative or vice versa.
3
Apply the rule
For the concordant type: replace the subject with the contradictory of the predicate and the predicate with the contradictory of the subject. For the contrary type: replace the subject with the contradictory of the predicate and keep the predicate as the original subject, then flip the quality accordingly.
For 'Every A is B', concordant: 'Every non-B is non-A'; contrary: 'No non-B is A'.
4
Verify the result
Check that the resulting proposition matches the definition: the quantity remains universal, and the quality matches (concordant) or differs (contrary) from the original.

Understanding ‘Aks al-Naqīḍ: Conversion of the Contradictory
Author defines 'aks al-naqid with an example showing change in quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply ‘aks al-naqīḍ to a categorical proposition and state the resulting proposition correctly.

Core Idea
‘Aks al-Naqīḍ swaps subject with contradictory predicate and flips quality.

This lesson introduces ‘aks al-naqīḍ, a type of immediate inference that transforms a proposition by exchanging the subject with the contradictory of the predicate and changing the quality (affirmative to negative or vice versa). The author defines it concisely and illustrates with the example 'Every human is an animal' becoming 'Nothing that is not an animal is a human'. Mastering this tool sharpens your ability to derive valid inferences in Islamic logical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's terms and quality
Take the given proposition. For example, 'Every human is an animal' (universal affirmative). The subject is 'human', the predicate is 'animal', and the quality is affirmative.
2
Form the contradictory of the predicate
Negate the predicate to get its contradictory. For 'animal', the contradictory is 'not-animal' (or 'non-animal'). This will become the new subject.
The contradictory of a term is its negation, e.g., 'not-animal'.
3
Swap the original subject with that contradictory predicate
The new proposition's subject is the contradictory of the original predicate, and its predicate is the original subject. So new subject = 'not-animal', new predicate = 'human'.
4
Change the quality
The original was affirmative; the result must be negative (universal negative). Thus the proposition becomes 'No not-animal is a human' or 'Nothing that is not an animal is a human'.
Quality flips: affirmative to negative, negative to affirmative.
5
Verify with the author's example
Check that your result matches the author's: from 'Every human is an animal' (كل إنسان حيوان) to 'Nothing that is not an animal is a human' (لا شيء مما ليس بحيوان إنسان). This confirms correct application.
The operation is called 'mukhalif' (contrary) because the quality differs.
