The Rule of 'Or' in Definitions and Descriptions
Author distinguishes types of 'aw' (or) and states rules for definitions and descriptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the types of 'aw' (or) and apply the rule for using 'or' in definitions and descriptions.

Core Idea
The word 'or' can sabotage a definition; only the divisive 'or' is allowed in descriptions.

The word 'aw' (or) seems simple, but in logical definitions it can cause confusion. The author of al-Sullam distinguishes its types and gives a clear rule: no 'or' is allowed in definitions (hudud) or descriptions (rusum), except for the 'or' of division in descriptions. This lesson breaks down that rule and shows you how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term under discussion
The term is 'aw' (or). Note that the passage distinguishes its uses: alternation (taraddud), doubt (tashkik), and division (taqsim).
Alternation and doubt are grouped together as forbidden in both hadd and rasm.
2
Understand the rule for definitions (hudud)
The passage forbids any use of 'aw' in definitions because a definition must capture a single essence. Using 'or' would suggest multiple possible essences, which is invalid.
3
Understand the rule for descriptions (rusum)
In descriptions, only the 'or' of division is allowed, because a description can list alternative distinguishing features. The 'or' of alternation/doubt remains forbidden.
The example given: 'that whose conception necessitates the conception of the defined or its distinction from others' uses the divisive 'or'.
4
Apply the rule to a new example
Test the rule: e.g., 'A human is a rational animal' is a proper definition. 'A human is a rational or laughing animal' would be invalid in a definition (but might be allowed in a description).
Make sure the 'or' is genuinely divisive and not causing doubt.

Commentary
This passage presents the eighth rule: prohibition of the word 'or' (أو) in definitions and descriptions, with a distinction. The word 'aw' can be used for alternation (taraddud) or doubt (tashkīk), and these uses are completely forbidden in both definitions (ḥudūd) and descriptions (rusūm). The 'aw' of division (taqsīm) is also forbidden in definitions because each division would require its own definition, so using 'aw' in one definition causes confusion (ghalaṭ). However, it is allowed in descriptions. The author gives an example: defining the definer (al-muʿarrif) as 'that whose conception necessitates the conception of the defined or its distinction from others.' Here 'aw' indicates a division between two types of utility, and since it is a description, it is acceptable. The passage concludes by noting the end of the first section on tasawwurāt, covering principles (mabādi') and demands (maṭālib) of tasawwurāt.

Circularity in Definitions (Dawr) When Judgments Are Included
Explanation of circularity (dawr) in definitions using the example of the agent defined as the nominative noun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a circular definition where a judgment depends on the defined, and evaluate whether the circularity is avoided if the judgment falls on a broader genus.

Core Idea
Circularity arises when the definiens presupposes knowledge of the definiendum.

In definitions, it is crucial to avoid circularity (dawr). The author illustrates this with the definition of the agent (fā‘il) as 'the nominative noun.' Since knowing what is nominative requires knowing the agent, the definition is circular. We'll explore al-Ṣabbān's argument that circularity is avoided if the judgment is on the genus, not the defined.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defined term and the judgment
In the example, the defined term is 'al-fā‘il' (agent) and the judgment is 'marfū‘' (nominative). Notice that the judgment is a grammatical property of the agent.
2
Check dependency
Does knowing that something is nominative require knowing that it is an agent? If so, the definition presupposes what it tries to define, creating a circle.
3
Consider al-Ṣabbān's objection
Al-Ṣabbān argues that the judgment 'nominative' falls on 'al-ism' (the noun), not on 'al-fā‘il'. Since 'noun' is a broader genus, knowledge of 'noun' does not require knowledge of 'agent'. Thus no circularity.
4
Assess the strength
The author notes that not all judgments are like this. Some judgments indeed depend on the specific defined, so one must be cautious.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the conditions of proper definitions (hudūd and rusūm). It begins by illustrating circularity (dawr) using the definition of the agent (fā‘il) as 'the nominative noun': the judgment of nominative case depends on knowing the agent, but the definition aims to define the agent, creating a circle. Then it states the eighth condition: the word 'aw' (or) and its equivalent expressing hesitation or doubt are prohibited in both limits and descriptions. However, 'aw' used for division (taqsīm) is prohibited in limits because each division would require a separate limit, but permissible in descriptions, as in defining the defined as 'that which its conception necessitates conception of the defined or its distinction from others'. The passage then notes that including judgments (ahkām) in limits substitutes accidents for essential properties, thereby failing to provide a complete limit. A long discussion follows on al-Ṣabbān's view: he argues that judgments in descriptions do not cause circularity because the judgment is on the genus (e.g., 'noun') not on the specific defined (e.g., 'agent'). The passage distinguishes three types of 'aw': for doubt (tashkīk), for division (taqsīm), and for choice (takhyīr). It notes that some scholars permit 'aw' for choice in descriptions, and al-Anṣārī even permits 'aw' for division in limits, citing the definition of nazar as leading to either knowledge or opinion. The verifying scholar rejects this because it would be two definitions for two things. The commentary ends by clarifying that the author's verse about 'to three' and other lines are well explained by the commentator.

Key Terms
حد (ḥadd): A definition that captures the essence (māhiyya) by stating the genus and differentia; in this passage, the complete limit (ḥadd tāmm) includes all essential properties, while the incomplete limit includes some.
رسم (rasm): A description that defines by accidents or properties; it is permissible to use 'aw' for division in rasm but not in ḥadd.
أو (aw): The word 'or'; its use for hesitation/doubt (tashkīk) is prohibited in all definitions; for division (taqsīm) prohibited in limits but allowed in descriptions; for choice (takhyīr) is debated but some allow in descriptions. [Note: In earlier texts, 'aw' was often treated solely as a disjunction; later commentators refined its uses into three categories.]
دور (dawr): Circular definition or argument; it occurs when knowledge of the defined presupposes knowledge of something that itself presupposes knowledge of the defined.
حكم (ḥukm): A judgment or ruling; in this context, it refers to a grammatical ruling (like nominative case) or a logical property used in definitions.

The Three Types of 'Aw' and Their Use in Definitions
Explanation of the rules of using 'aw' in definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'aw' for doubt, division, and choice, and determine whether each is permissible in hadd or rasm according to different scholars.

The permissibility of 'aw' depends on its type: hesitation is always prohibited, division is allowed only in descriptions, choice is debated.

The word 'aw' (or) has multiple uses in definitions. The author outlines three types: for hesitation (tashkīk), for division (taqsīm), and for choice (takhyīr). The rules vary: hesitation is prohibited everywhere; division is prohibited in limits but allowed in descriptions; choice is debated. We'll examine the reasoning and different scholarly positions.

Choice 'aw' is debated; some scholars like al-Damanhuri and al-Sabban permit it in descriptions, e.g., 'man is a laughing animal or a writing animal.'

Such as your saying in the definition of the agent: it is the nominative noun; so the nominative case is a ruling from the rulings of the agent, and the ruling on a thing depends on its conception. So if the ruling is taken as a part in the definition, the defined depends on it, and circularity occurs, which is the dependence of each of the two things on the other. Eighth: It is not permissible in limits (hudūd) nor in descriptions (rusūm) to mention the word 'aw' (or) or what is in its meaning of hesitation and doubt. As for 'aw' that is for division, it is not permissible to use it in limits, because for every division there exists a different limit, so inserting it in a single limit is a mistake. As for using 'aw' for division in descriptions, it is permissible, an example of that is their saying in the definition of the defined: it is that whose conception necessitates the conception of the defined or its distinction from other than it. And with this, the first section on conceptualizations (taṣawwurāt) has concluded, comprising the principles of conceptualizations and the demands of conceptualizations. (2) Rulings entered the complete limit in place of some essential properties, thus the limit contains accidents, so conception of the defined is not obtained with all its essential properties, which is the meaning of the complete limit. Likewise, if they entered the incomplete limit, then conception of the essence (māhiyya) with all its essential properties or with some of them is not obtained. So the definition by that is neither a complete limit nor an incomplete limit. (1) His saying first: 'dependence of the defined on it' means dependence of knowledge of the defined on knowledge of the ruling. But the ruling on a thing depends on knowledge of the defined; this is circularity. And you now know the necessary and the necessitating, the essential and the accidental, the essence, the part, the limit, and the ruling. So there is no need to explain the commentator's words here. And know that the scholar al-Ṣabbān did not consider that the entry of rulings into descriptions (in which entry of rulings is imagined, unlike limits in which that is not imagined at all, as we explained) leads to circularity, because he held that the thing judged by the mentioned ruling in the definition is not the very defined, but rather the genus taken in the definition. Do you not see that his saying: 'the agent: it is the nominative noun' – that the judgment of nominative case falls on the noun, not on the agent? So the judgment of nominative case only depends on the conception of the absolute noun needed for the conception of the essence, and does not depend on the conception of the particular agent desired to be conceived by the definition. And this is, despite what is in it, sound. So if the rulings that are in descriptions are of this kind such that they do not depend on the conception of the particular defined, then they are permissible without any problem in my view. But not every ruling is of this type. So it is best to guard against them lest some who lack investigation slip into them and fall into circularity, or the judgment depends on the conception of the particular defined through long steps that are not noticed except by precise examination. And one may say: we do not mean by the prohibited rulings except these whose attainment depends on the conception of the particular defined; so there remains no place for the objection of al-Fāḍil al-Ṣabbān. So reflect. (2) He means by 'principles of conceptualizations' the five universals, and by 'their demands' the explanatory statements. And the gist of the discussion about 'aw' is that it is either for doubt, or for division, or for choice. As for that which is for hesitation and doubt, its use is absolutely impermissible in definitions, whether limits or descriptions. However, I saw the scholar al-Damanhūrī permitting its entry into descriptions, like your saying: 'man is a laughing animal or a writing animal', meaning that you are free to distinguish him by the first property or the second. This is what I understood from his statement, and I saw the scholar al-Bājūrī noting this and attributing it to some investigators. And by this meaning, Abū al-Ma‘ārif al-Ṣabbān counted it as another category besides that for doubt, naming it that for choice, and said: its permissibility in descriptions appears. So reflect. As for 'aw' for division, it enters descriptions without dispute, and does not enter limits according to the majority. However, I saw Shaykh al-Islam Zakariyyā al-Anṣārī permitting its entry into the limit, based on their definition of nazar (speculative reasoning) as 'the thought leading to knowledge or preponderant opinion', meaning that nazar is two types: one leads to knowledge, the other to preponderant opinion. And the commentator from al-Muḥaqqiqūn (the verifying scholar) argued against its entry into the limit, because those are in reality two limits for two different things, not a single limit for one thing. And they are here: nazar leading to knowledge, and nazar leading to opinion. So reflect. Then the author's saying 'to three' in one version is 'on three'. And his saying 'nor by what is known by the limited' is what the commentator explained under the fifth. And his saying 'and it is not permissible in limits to mention 'aw', and it is permissible in description' – adherence to what the commentator said requires that 'aw' is for division, because other than it is not permitted to be mentioned in a definition at all, as he chose. And the rest of the text is clear, by Allah's will, and needs no explanation. And Allah is the giver of success.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the conditions of proper definitions (hudūd and rusūm). It begins by illustrating circularity (dawr) using the definition of the agent (fā‘il) as 'the nominative noun': the judgment of nominative case depends on knowing the agent, but the definition aims to define the agent, creating a circle. Then it states the eighth condition: the word 'aw' (or) and its equivalent expressing hesitation or doubt are prohibited in both limits and descriptions. However, 'aw' used for division (taqsīm) is prohibited in limits because each division would require a separate limit, but permissible in descriptions, as in defining the defined as 'that which its conception necessitates conception of the defined or its distinction from others'. The passage then notes that including judgments (ahkām) in limits substitutes accidents for essential properties, thereby failing to provide a complete limit. A long discussion follows on al-Ṣabbān's view: he argues that judgments in descriptions do not cause circularity because the judgment is on the genus (e.g., 'noun') not on the specific defined (e.g., 'agent'). The passage distinguishes three types of 'aw': for doubt (tashkīk), for division (taqsīm), and for choice (takhyīr). It notes that some scholars permit 'aw' for choice in descriptions, and al-Anṣārī even permits 'aw' for division in limits, citing the definition of nazar as leading to either knowledge or opinion. The verifying scholar rejects this because it would be two definitions for two things. The commentary ends by clarifying that the author's verse about 'to three' and other lines are well explained by the commentator.

Key Terms
حد (ḥadd): A definition that captures the essence (māhiyya) by stating the genus and differentia; in this passage, the complete limit (ḥadd tāmm) includes all essential properties, while the incomplete limit includes some.
رسم (rasm): A description that defines by accidents or properties; it is permissible to use 'aw' for division in rasm but not in ḥadd.
أو (aw): The word 'or'; its use for hesitation/doubt (tashkīk) is prohibited in all definitions; for division (taqsīm) prohibited in limits but allowed in descriptions; for choice (takhyīr) is debated but some allow in descriptions. [Note: In earlier texts, 'aw' was often treated solely as a disjunction; later commentators refined its uses into three categories.]
دور (dawr): Circular definition or argument; it occurs when knowledge of the defined presupposes knowledge of something that itself presupposes knowledge of the defined.
حكم (ḥukm): A judgment or ruling; in this context, it refers to a grammatical ruling (like nominative case) or a logical property used in definitions.

Key Takeaway
The permissibility of 'aw' in definitions depends on its type: hesitation is never allowed, division is allowed only in descriptions, and choice is debated for descriptions.
