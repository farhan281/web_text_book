Logic: A Tool for the Mind, Not the Tongue
Commentator expands on the benefit of logic for the mind.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between logic as a mental tool and grammar as a linguistic tool.

Core Idea
Logic trains the mind, not the tongue.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the true benefit of logic. He emphasizes that logic is not a linguistic discipline like grammar, but rather a discipline that sharpens the intellect (jinan). This distinction is crucial for understanding why we study logic in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the key dichotomy
Find the phrase 'logic benefits the mind, not the tongue' in the Arabic. The commentator contrasts two recipients of training: the jinan (mind/heart) and the lisan (tongue).
2
Compare logic to grammar
The commentator says the tongue is trained by grammar, literature, and other linguistic sciences. Grammar corrects speech; what does logic correct? It corrects thought. Identify this analogy.
Logic is to the mind as grammar is to the tongue.
3
Synthesize the commentator's point
Logic is described as a 'language for the mind and thought'. This means logic provides the rules for correct reasoning, just as grammar provides rules for correct speech. Formulate this in your own words.
4
Reflect on the supplication
The commentator ends with a dua asking Allah to help the reader benefit. This shows that mastering logic requires divine aid and that the ultimate goal is spiritual and intellectual growth.

Tasawwur and Tasdiq: The Two Pillars of Knowledge
Author introduces the division of knowledge into conceptualization and assent, and distinguishes necessary from theoretical knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and explain why tasawwur is prior to tasdiq.

Core Idea
Conceptualization precedes assent: you must conceive before you can affirm.

The matn opens by dividing originated knowledge into two types: tasawwur (conceptualization) and tasdiq (assent). This division is fundamental to all subsequent logical operations. Understanding the difference and the order of priority is essential for grasping how definitions and proofs function.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
In the matn, find the Arabic terms for conceptualization and assent. They are 'تصور' and 'تصديق'.
2
Analyze the objects
Tasawwur perceives a single entity (مفرد), while tasdiq perceives a relation (نسبة). Note the difference in what each grasps.
The matn says: إدراك مفرد تصوراً ودرك نسبة بتصديق
3
Understand the priority
The matn states that tasawwur is presented first because it is prior by nature (مقدم بالطبع). Why? You cannot judge a relation without first having the concepts involved.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
To accept the proposition 'God exists' (tasdiq), you must first have a conceptualization of 'God' and 'exists'. Without tasawwur, tasdiq is impossible.
Example from Kalam: the conceptualization of 'contingent being' precedes the assent that it requires a creator.

Chapter on the types of originated knowledge. Perception of an individual is called conceptualization, and perception of a relation is called assent. And the first is presented in the arrangement because it is prior by nature. Theoretical is that which requires reflection, and its opposite is the necessary/evident. That by which one arrives at a conceptualization is called a definition, so be diligent. And that by which one arrives at an assent is called a proof, known among the rational. I say: Logic is a science that investigates the methods by which knowledge is attained. Knowledge is the goal of logic, meaning that knowledge results from logic. Therefore, whoever learns logic aims to attain knowledge. Hence, the concept of knowledge must be clear in the mind of the one who begins to learn logic, so that he can reach his goal and connect the means and the end. Before we delve into the definition of knowledge, we must draw attention to the fact that the goal of the logician is to acquire knowledge for himself. That knowledge which is the goal of the logician is acquired knowledge. Acquired knowledge is the knowledge of created beings. It is described as acquired for two reasons: First, man is not born knowing, but is born stripped of sciences and knowledges. So every knowledge he has after that is originated in him after it was not. Second, most sciences are not necessary; rather their attainment in man depends on effort and reflection. Therefore, scholars, by way of predominance, call them theoretical sciences. They are also called acquired sciences because they occur in the soul, and everything that occurs in it is acquisition as long as it depends on will. The conclusion is that logic has no entry except into originated sciences. So it cannot be said that divine knowledge also depends on it. This is ignorance of logic and of originated knowledge. Divine knowledge is eternal, and the eternal does not depend on anything. Logic is thus a means for human sciences, not for others. So all the divisions, positions, types, and causes that will come have no connection to divine knowledge; rather they are specific to human knowledge. The evidence for this is His statement, the Most High: 'And Allah brought you out from the wombs of your mothers while you know nothing' (Al-Nahl 78). That is, they are not intuitive, occurring necessarily without will followed by deliberation and acquisition. Necessity here is the opposite of acquisition. So it cannot be objected that all sciences are necessarily attained, because the discussion is about the ordinary causes that prepare, not about the attainment itself. The necessary that is spoken of here does not have reflection among its causes.

Commentary
This passage defines the types of originated knowledge. The matn (poem) first gives the definitions: perception of a single entity (mufrad) is called tasawwur (conceptualization), and perception of a relation (nisba) is called tasdiq (assent). It then states that the first is presented before the second because it is prior by nature—conceptualization is necessary before assent. Next, it divides knowledge into two types: theoretical (nazari) which requires reflection, and necessary (daruri) which is self-evident. Finally, it defines the tool for conceptualization as a 'definition' (qawl sharh) and the tool for assent as a 'proof' (hujja). The commentary (أقول) expands on this: Logic aims at acquired knowledge, which is the knowledge of creatures. Human knowledge is originated because humans are born ignorant and most knowledge requires effort. Logic pertains only to created sciences, not to divine knowledge which is eternal. The footnotes provide the Quranic evidence and clarify that 'necessary' here means knowledge that comes without reflection, not that it is inevitable.

Necessary and Theoretical Knowledge
Author introduces the division of knowledge into conceptualization and assent, and distinguishes necessary from theoretical knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari) knowledge and give examples from the Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either self-evident or requires proof; logic deals only with the latter.

After establishing the two types of knowledge, the matn further divides knowledge itself into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari). This division is crucial because logic deals only with theoretical knowledge, which requires reflection and proof. The commentary clarifies that acquired knowledge is the domain of logic.

Guided Path
1
Locate the distinction
Find the line in the matn that defines nazari and daruri. It says: والنظري ما احتاج للتأمل وعكسه هو الضروري الجلي
2
Understand the definitions
Nazari requires reflection (ta'mmul). Daruri is its opposite—self-evident, without need for reflection.
3
Examine the commentary's elaboration
The commentary explains that most human knowledge is nazari because man is born ignorant and must acquire knowledge through effort. This is supported by the Quranic verse.
The verse from Surat an-Nahl is cited as evidence that humans start with no knowledge.
4
Apply to Kalam
For example, the existence of a creator is nazari (requires proof from contingency), while the impossibility of a thing being both existent and non-existent simultaneously is daruri.
5
Reflect on the scope
The commentary stresses that logic is only for nazari sciences, not for divine eternal knowledge.

The Tools of the Logician: Definition and Proof
Author introduces the division of knowledge into conceptualization and assent, and distinguishes necessary from theoretical knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two tools (definition and proof) that correspond to conceptualization and assent, and explain their functions and scope.

Core Idea
Definitions (qawl sharh) serve tasawwur; proofs (hujja) serve tasdiq; both are for human acquired knowledge only.

After dividing knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq, the matn introduces the means to achieve each: definitions for conceptualization and proofs for assent. This lesson examines these tools and clarifies the scope of logic as restricted to acquired human knowledge, not divine knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Locate the lines about the tools
Find the two lines in the matn: one about what leads to tasawwur and one about what leads to tasdiq.
Tasawwur: وما به إلى تصور وصل يدعى بقول شارح; Tasdiq: وما لتصديق به توصلا بحجة
2
Understand the terms
Qawl sharh (explanatory statement) is a definition that clarifies a concept. Hujja (proof) is an argument that produces assent.
The matn adds 'فلتبتهل' (be diligent) for definitions, emphasizing care.
3
Examine the commentary's discussion on scope
The commentary stresses that logic pertains only to originated (human) sciences, not to divine knowledge. This defines the domain of these tools.
Divine knowledge is eternal and does not depend on logical methods.
4
Consider a Fiqh example
Defining 'salah' (qawl sharh) is a conceptualization; proving its obligation from the Quran is a proof (hujja). The definition must precede the proof.

That by which one arrives at a conceptualization is called a definition, so be diligent. And that by which one arrives at an assent is called a proof, known among the rational. Therefore, whoever learns logic aims to attain knowledge. Hence, the concept of knowledge must be clear in the mind of the one who begins to learn logic, so that he can reach his goal and connect the means and the end. Before we delve into the definition of knowledge, we must draw attention to the fact that the goal of the logician is to acquire knowledge for himself. That knowledge which is the goal of the logician is acquired knowledge. Acquired knowledge is the knowledge of created beings. It is described as acquired for two reasons: First, man is not born knowing, but is born stripped of sciences and knowledges. So every knowledge he has after that is originated in him after it was not. Second, most sciences are not necessary; rather their attainment in man depends on effort and reflection. Therefore, scholars, by way of predominance, call them theoretical sciences. They are also called acquired sciences because they occur in the soul, and everything that occurs in it is acquisition as long as it depends on will. The conclusion is that logic has no entry except into originated sciences. So it cannot be said that divine knowledge also depends on it. This is ignorance of logic and of originated knowledge. Divine knowledge is eternal, and the eternal does not depend on anything. Logic is thus a means for human sciences, not for others. So all the divisions, positions, types, and causes that will come have no connection to divine knowledge; rather they are specific to human knowledge. The evidence for this is His statement, the Most High: 'And Allah brought you out from the wombs of your mothers while you know nothing' (Al-Nahl 78). That is, they are not intuitive, occurring necessarily without will followed by deliberation and acquisition. Necessity here is the opposite of acquisition. So it cannot be objected that all sciences are necessarily attained, because the discussion is about the ordinary causes that prepare, not about the attainment itself. The necessary that is spoken of here does not have reflection among its causes.

Commentary
This passage defines the types of originated knowledge. The matn (poem) first gives the definitions: perception of a single entity (mufrad) is called tasawwur (conceptualization), and perception of a relation (nisba) is called tasdiq (assent). It then states that the first is presented before the second because it is prior by nature—conceptualization is necessary before assent. Next, it divides knowledge into two types: theoretical (nazari) which requires reflection, and necessary (daruri) which is self-evident. Finally, it defines the tool for conceptualization as a 'definition' (qawl sharh) and the tool for assent as a 'proof' (hujja). The commentary (أقول) expands on this: Logic aims at acquired knowledge, which is the knowledge of creatures. Human knowledge is originated because humans are born ignorant and most knowledge requires effort. Logic pertains only to created sciences, not to divine knowledge which is eternal. The footnotes provide the Quranic evidence and clarify that 'necessary' here means knowledge that comes without reflection, not that it is inevitable.

The Three Means of Knowledge: Classification and Independence
Author enumerates the three sources of knowledge: senses, intellect, and transmitted report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any piece of human knowledge into one of the three means: senses, intellect, or transmitted report, and identify which are independent and which composite.

Core Idea
Human knowledge has only three sources; senses and intellect are independent, transmitted report combines both.

In this passage, the author establishes the foundational epistemological framework for Islamic sciences. Understanding these three means is essential because all knowledge—from sensory observation to rational deduction to revealed reports—falls into one of these categories. This classification helps us evaluate the reliability of different types of claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three means
Read the opening sentence: the means are three, no fourth. List them as given by the author: senses (al-hawas al-khams), intellect (al-aql), and transmitted report (al-khabar).
The author stresses there are exactly three, no more.
2
Distinguish independent from composite
Notice the author says two are independent (senses and intellect) and one is composite (transmitted report). Determine what 'independent' means: each can function without the other as a source of knowledge.
Independence does not mean they never cooperate; it means each is a primary source.
3
Examine the example of sight
The author uses the example of sight to show how senses and intellect interact. The sense perceives the reflected ray, and the intellect infers properties of the body. This illustrates both independence and cooperation.
Even in sensory perception, the intellect plays a role.
4
Note the special status of transmitted report
Although composite, the author singles out transmitted report due to its immense importance. Reflect on why—because it is the source of religious knowledge that cannot be attained through senses or intellect alone.
This shows that epistemological categories are not just theoretical; they have practical significance.

The Senses and Their Epistemic Limitations
Author enumerates the three sources of knowledge: senses, intellect, and transmitted report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the senses alone do not provide complete knowledge and describe the role of the intellect in processing sensory data.

Core Idea
Senses perceive only the surface; the intellect reaches the essence.

The author delves into the mechanism of sensory perception, using sight as an example to show that what we perceive directly is not the object itself but a reflection. This sets the stage for understanding that sensation is a precondition for, but not the end of, knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand the example of sight
The author presents two views: either sight perceives the body directly via a ray, or it perceives only the reflected ray from the body. The latter is deemed more correct. This shows that even the most immediate sense (sight) involves inference.
The ray itself is a physical intermediary.
2
Recognize the limitation: perception is not complete
In the footnote, the author explains that perception does not reveal the thing 'as it is in reality', but only some of its necessary concomitants. This applies to all senses, whether direct (touch) or indirect (sight).
This is a key epistemological insight: our knowledge of external objects is partial.
3
Identify the intellect's role in inferring characteristics
The intellect uses the qualities of the reflected ray (e.g., color, shape) to infer properties of the body itself, such as its texture or identity. The senses provide raw data; the intellect processes it.
This cooperation shows that senses alone are insufficient.
4
Apply to other senses
The author says 'similarly we say for the other senses'. For example, hearing perceives sound waves, and the intellect interprets them as speech or music. Apply the same pattern to smell, taste, and touch.
This reinforces the general principle.

Transmitted Report (Khabar): The Composite Source of Revelation
Author enumerates the three sources of knowledge: senses, intellect, and transmitted report.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why transmitted report is considered a composite means and why it is given special importance as a source of religious knowledge.

Core Idea
Transmitted report combines senses and intellect; it is the vehicle for divine revelation.

The author singles out transmitted report (khabar) despite it being a composite of sense and intellect because of its immense importance. Through it, we gain knowledge that we could not obtain through senses or intellect alone, especially matters of the unseen and revelation.

Senses give knowledge of material present objects; intellect gives rational truths; transmitted report gives knowledge of the unseen and historical facts. Each has its domain.
Understanding this helps appreciate the role of revelation in Islam.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed explanation of tasawwur (conceptualization). It begins by noting two definitions: some say it is the form occurring in the soul, while others say it occurs at the soul to avoid implying that the soul is a physical container. The author then clarifies that tasawwur is the perception of a single meaning (mufrad). The term 'mufrad' here is carefully defined: it means something that does not involve a judgmental relation (nisba hukmiyya) or its negation. Examples are given: perceiving 'cup' without affirming or denying existence involves no relation. Importantly, even a sentence like 'Zayd is standing' is composed of three conceptualizations: Zayd, standing, and the relation between them. The perception of the relation itself, without assent, is still tasawwur. The footnotes (1) and (2) further refine this: 'mufrad' means a meaning not related to another by a declarative relation; internal composition does not negate its being 'single' in this technical sense. Footnote (2) distinguishes the propositional relation (nisba hukmiyya) as either a linguistic relation (the connection of subject and predicate) or its correspondence to reality. The latter, when assented to, constitutes tasdiq. The key insight is that tasawwur is broader than often assumed—it includes any perception without assent, including relations, as long as there is no judgment of truth or falsehood.

Defining Tasawwur and Tasdiq: The Two Pillars of Knowledge

Core Idea

Why Not All Conceptions Are Self-Evident
First proof: not all conceptions are self-evident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the commentator's proof that not all conceptions are self-evident and identify the three consequences that would follow if they were.

Core Idea
If all conceptions were self-evident, everyone would agree—but they don't, so they aren't.

The commentator begins by establishing an important epistemological principle: not all conceptions (tasawwurat) are self-evident (badihiyya). He uses a simple but powerful reductio argument: if all were self-evident, then everyone would share the same concepts, there would be no disagreement, and no one would need to acquire new concepts. Since all these consequences are contrary to observed reality, the original claim must be false. This argument is foundational in mantiq because it justifies the need for definition and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
The commentator sets out to disprove the idea that all conceptions are self-evident (badihiyya). Locate this claim in the Arabic text. You should spot the phrase 'لا يمكن أن تكون كلها بديهية' at the beginning.
2
State the hypothetical consequence
If all conceptions were self-evident, three things would follow: everyone would have the same conceptions, no one would disagree about concepts, and no one would need to acquire new concepts. The commentator lists these as connected consequences: 'لولا لحصلت للجميع، لما اختلف أحد...، لما احتاج أحد...'
3
Observe the contradiction with reality
The commentator then points out that we know from experience that people do disagree about concepts and do need to acquire new ones. This contradicts the hypothetical scenario. The phrase 'ومعلوم أن هذا كله واقع لا يمكن إنكاره' confirms that these consequences are false in reality.
4
Conclude the impossibility of the original claim
Since the consequences are false, the antecedent (all conceptions are self-evident) must be false. Therefore, not all conceptions are self-evident. This is a valid modus tollens argument: If P then Q; not Q, so not P.

Why Not All Conceptions Are Acquired, and the Same for Assents
Second proof: not all conceptions are acquired; application to assents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why not all conceptions are acquired (kasbiyya) using the infinite regress argument, and extend the same division to assents (tasdiqat).

Core Idea
If every concept required reasoning, we'd never start—proving some concepts must be self-evident.

The commentator now tackles the opposite extreme: all conceptions are acquired (kasbiyya). He offers two objections: first, we clearly have some conceptions without any effort (empirical observation); second, a logical argument—if every conception required reasoning, we would need prior concepts to reason from, leading to an infinite regress or circularity, both impossible. Therefore, some conceptions must be self-evident. He then applies the same logic to assents (judgments): some are self-evident, most are theoretical. This establishes the foundational taxonomy of knowledge in Islamic logic.

The Five Word-Meaning Relations: Tawatu', Tashkuk, Takhaluf, Ishtirak, Taraduf
Classification of word-meaning relations into five types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five types of word-meaning relations (univocal, analogical, different, homonymous, synonymous) in Arabic logical texts.

Core Idea
Words relate to meanings in five distinct ways, and recognizing them is essential for precise definition.

In Islamic logic, before we can define or argue, we must understand how words relate to their meanings. This lesson presents a foundational classification: univocal, analogical, different, homonymous, and synonymous. The commentary explains the first three in detail, showing how a single term like 'light' can be analogical rather than homonymous.

Guided Path
1
Read the matn verses
The opening poem lines list the five relations: univocal, analogical, different, homonymous, and synonymous. Read them carefully to get the overall framework.
The matn also mentions another division (request vs. news), but we focus on the five word-meaning relations.
2
Distinguish univocal from analogical
Univocal (mutawāṭi') applies equally to all individuals (e.g., 'human'). Analogical (mushakkik) applies with variation in intensity (e.g., 'light' for sun vs. moon). Note the commentary's explanation of the observer's hesitation (tashakkuk).
3
Identify the relation of difference
Two terms with no overlap in application (e.g., 'human' and 'horse') are called different (mukhālif/mutabāyin). Recognize this as the default relation for different words and meanings.
4
Understand homonymy and synonymy
Ishtirāk (homonymy) is a single word with multiple unrelated meanings; its opposite is tarāduf (synonymy) where multiple words share the same meaning. Both are mentioned in the matn but not detailed here.
These are opposites: if ishtirāk occurs, tarāduf does not apply to the same words.
5
Apply the classification to examples
Test your understanding by classifying given word-meaning pairs (e.g., 'light' for sun and moonlight is analogical; 'eye' for organ and water source is homonymous).
