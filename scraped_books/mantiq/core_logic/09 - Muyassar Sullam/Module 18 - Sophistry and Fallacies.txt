The Fallacy of Equivocation: Treating Distinct Terms as Synonyms
Author explains the fallacy of equivocation by example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of equivocation in a categorical syllogism where the middle term is used in two different senses.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires the middle term to have the exact same meaning in both premises.

Words can be treacherous: one word may carry multiple meanings. In logic, if a key term shifts meaning between premises, the argument collapses. This lesson dissects a classic fallacy where distinct terms are mistakenly treated as synonyms, leading to an invalid conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises and conclusion
Find the minor premise (e.g., 'This is a sharp sword' pointing to a non-cutting sword), major premise ('Every sharp sword is a sword'), and conclusion ('This is a sword').
2
Locate the middle term
The middle term is 'sharp sword' (ṣārim). Note that it appears in both premises.
3
Determine the meaning in each premise
In the minor premise, 'sharp sword' refers to a non-cutting sword. In the major premise, it refers to a cutting sword. These meanings are different.
4
Check if the meaning is identical
Since the middle term has different meanings, the syllogism commits the fallacy of equivocation. The middle term is repeated only in wording, not in meaning.

Fallacy of Equivocation on the Middle Term
Fallacy of confusing false with true via equivocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and diagnose the fallacy of equivocation on the middle term in a categorical syllogism by detecting shifts in meaning.

Core Idea
Equivocation on a middle term turns a valid form into a fallacy by using two meanings.

This passage from al-Sullam al-Muyassar addresses a common sophistical error: taking an accidental attribute as essential. The example of a person in a ship shows how a syllogism can seem valid but fails because the key term 'moving' shifts meaning between premises. Understanding this fallacy helps you avoid building false conclusions in theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism's components
Read the example: 'The person sitting in the ship is moving, and everything moving does not stay in one place.' Identify the minor premise (person sitting is moving) and major premise (everything moving does not stay in one place). The middle term is 'moving'.
The conclusion is implicit: the person does not stay in one place, which is false if the ship is still.
2
Detect the shift in meaning
Ask: is 'moving' used in the same sense in both premises? In the minor premise, the person moves accidentally (with the ship). In the major premise, 'moving' means essential motion (self-motion). The meaning is different: عرضي (accidental) vs. ذاتي (essential).
The author explicitly contrasts these two senses.
3
Diagnose the fallacy
Because the middle term is not repeated in a single meaning, the syllogism violates the condition of iqtiran (connection). The premises are true only if each is interpreted with its own meaning, but then the middle term is equivocal, so no valid conclusion follows. The fallacy is called 'confusing a false proposition with a true one' because the equivocation makes a false conclusion appear true.
If one meaning is used, one premise becomes false, so the argument is still unsound.

The Fallacy of Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)
Author explains the fallacy of using the conclusion as a premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism commits the fallacy of making the conclusion identical to one of the premises (petitio principii) and explain why it fails to produce new knowledge.

Core Idea
A syllogism may be formally valid but useless if its conclusion merely restates a premise.

The second cause of error in meanings is when the conclusion is made one of the premises. This fallacy, known in Western logic as 'begging the question' or petitio principii, occurs when the conclusion is synonymous with or directly repeated from a premise. Even if the syllogism's form is correct, it yields no new knowledge—the hallmark of a useless argument. In this lesson, we dissect this fallacy using the example from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism's structure
Write out the example: 'This is a transference; every transference is a movement; therefore this is a movement.' Separate into premises (minor: 'This is a transference'; major: 'Every transference is a movement') and conclusion ('This is a movement').
The subject of the minor premise and conclusion is the same: 'this'.
2
Check for synonymy between terms
Notice that the minor premise uses 'transference' (naqla) and the conclusion uses 'movement' (haraka). The text states they are synonyms. Determine if the conclusion's key terms are essentially the same as those in the premises.
In this case, 'movement' is synonymous with 'transference', so the conclusion's predicate is identical in meaning to the minor premise's predicate.
3
Evaluate knowledge gain
Ask: Does the conclusion provide new information beyond the premises? Since the term 'movement' means the same as 'transference,' the conclusion only restates the minor premise in different words. No new knowledge is obtained.
The text explicitly says 'so no additional knowledge is obtained' (فلم يحصل علم زائد عليها).
4
Determine the fallacy's classification
Recognize this as a material fallacy (error in meaning) rather than a formal fallacy. The syllogism is formally valid (first figure, Barbara mood), but materially defective because the terms are synonymous.
Thus, formal validity does not guarantee a useful argument.

Fallacy of Judging Genus by Species
Author explains the fallacy of inferring that a genus has a property of a species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of judging the genus by the species (īhām al-ʿaks) and explain why it occurs.

Core Idea
A property of a species does not automatically extend to the whole genus.

This passage introduces a common logical error: assuming that what is true of a species is true of its entire genus. The author illustrates it with a simple syllogism and names the fallacy 'illusion of conversion'. Understanding this fallacy helps you avoid false generalizations in reasoning, especially in Islamic legal analogies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the false premise
In the example 'Every horse is an animal, every animal is rational, therefore every horse is rational', locate the premise that is actually false. The premise 'every animal is rational' is false because rationality is only a property of humans, not all animals. The other premise 'every horse is an animal' is true.
The false premise is the major premise of the syllogism.
2
Recognize the conversion error
Notice how the error arises from assuming that because 'every rational is an animal' is true, the converse 'every animal is rational' is also true. This is the illusion of conversion (īhām al-ʿaks). Conversion of a universal affirmative (All A are B) yields a particular affirmative (Some B are A), not a universal affirmative.
The fallacy is thinking 'All A are B' implies 'All B are A'.
3
Test with an Islamic example
Consider the true proposition 'every intoxicant is forbidden' (from Islamic law). Its converse 'every forbidden thing is intoxicant' is false because many forbidden things (e.g., pork, blood) are not intoxicants. This fallacy leads to incorrect rulings if applied in fiqh or usul al-fiqh.
Use this test to check for the fallacy in everyday arguments.

Treating a Non-Certain Premise as Certain: The Fallacy of Wahm
Author explains the fallacy of using a merely probable premise as if it were certain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a premise is treated as certain but is actually based on estimation (wahm), and explain why this renders the syllogism unsound.

Core Idea
Certainty in premises is required for certain conclusions; confusion arises when probable premises are treated as certain.

In many arguments, a premise may seem plausible but lacks certainty. The fourth cause of error in meanings is when a non-certain premise is treated as if it were definitive. The author illustrates this with a syllogism where the major premise 'every dead thing is inanimate' is accepted as certain, yet it is only an imaginary judgment from the faculty of wahm. This lesson shows how such an error can be detected and avoided in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise claimed as certain
In the example, the major premise 'every dead thing is inanimate' is presented as if it were certain. Locate the premise that is being elevated to the level of yaqin.
The minor premise 'this is dead' is usually empirical and certain.
2
Assess the epistemic basis of that premise
Determine whether the premise is based on sense perception, reason, or estimation (wahm). The author states it is wahmiyya, meaning it comes from the imagination, which can mistake resemblance for identity.
The dead body may appear inert, but that does not make it inanimate in the same way as a rock.
3
Check the impact on the conclusion
If the major premise is not certain, the conclusion inherits that uncertainty. Even if the syllogistic form is valid, the argument becomes unsound because a premise lacks certainty.
A valid syllogism with a probable premise yields only a probable conclusion.
4
Apply to other Islamic science arguments
Look for similar fallacies in kalam or fiqh proofs where a premise might be taken as certain when it is only probable. For example, in arguments from analogy (qiyas), the 'illa must be established with certainty, not just be an estimation.
