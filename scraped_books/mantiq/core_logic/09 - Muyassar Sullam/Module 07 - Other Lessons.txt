The Senses as Instruments of the Soul
Author explains senses as conditions for intellection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditions and causes in intellectual perception and explain the instrumental role of the senses according to this passage.

Core Idea
Senses are tools, not causes; the soul is the true perceiver.

The author begins by clarifying a fundamental epistemological point: the senses are not the source of intellectual knowledge but merely its prerequisites. This distinction protects us from reducing knowledge to mere sensory input, a mistake that would undermine the soul's central role in intellection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause-condition distinction
Locate the sentence where the author states that senses are not causes (`ilal) but conditions (shurut). Understand the logical difference: a cause necessitates an effect, while a condition merely enables it.
2
Analyze the eye-closing example
The author gives a thought experiment: a person senses something, then closes their eyes. The intellect continues working using stored images. This shows the senses are only needed initially, not continuously.
3
Examine the soul's perception through instruments
The author clarifies that hearing, sight, etc., are perceptions of the soul using instruments. He warns against imagining that hearing is just sound reaching the brain; it is the soul's perception of meaning.
4
Contrast with common misunderstandings
Some people think the intellect is merely a product of the brain or senses. The author's view preserves the soul as the true agent of cognition, with senses as mere tools.

And the intellect depends in its perception on the senses, but the senses are not causes for it, rather they are conditions for it. For as soon as the intellect possesses sensory matters, it begins the work of analysis, composition, and inference by the methods that we will explain in this book. If we suppose that a person sensed a particular thing, then closed his eye, the intellect's work does not stop on the eye remaining open; rather it is satisfied with the images obtained before that and builds upon them relying on the memory of the human soul. In reality, hearing, sight, touch, smell, and taste are perceptions of the soul insofar as it is rational, by means of instruments, and not by the instruments independently. Do not imagine that hearing, for example, is the arrival of sound to the brain through the ear; rather it is the soul's perception of the meaning of those words. Similarly, the talk applies to the rest of the senses. (4) The apparent meaning of the statement is that this work performed by the intellect is automatic without intention and will, and accordingly it belongs to the category of involuntary intellection. As for deliberation (al-nazar), it necessarily requires intention and will. It cannot be said: analysis, composition, and inference are deliberation; how then does he make them occur involuntarily? Because first we understood his statement that the intellect's work here is without will because it is not mentioned in his words, so if we were to assume it, the problem would be resolved. Second, yes, deliberation is analysis, composition, and inference, but it is not necessary that every analysis, composition, and inference be deliberation, because involuntary intellection may occur according to the same law of acquired intellection, but these steps are not perceived. And the truth is that the soul is the cognizer; the intellect is an act of it, not of another. Every act is either voluntary or involuntary, and so is the intellect: from it are voluntary acts, which are the majority, and from it are involuntary acts. Hence people divided the sciences into acquired sciences and necessary sciences. As long as the intellect in its voluntary acts depends on will, and will is a heartfelt desire, the intellect is connected to the heart from this perspective. Therefore some scholars expressed that the intellect's origin is in the heart and its light is in the brain, and scholars differed as to its location: some said it is in the heart like al-Shafi`i, may Allah have mercy on him, and some said it is in the brain like Imam Abu Hanifa al-Nu`man, may Allah have mercy on him. The verified view is what we have mentioned. Hence some people imagine that the rational principles are merely optional matters that a person chooses and then builds upon. Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exploration of the relationship between the intellect and the senses, and the nature of intellectual acts. The author begins by stating that the intellect depends on the senses, but the senses are only conditions (shurut), not causes (`ilal). Once the intellect has sensory data, it automatically begins analysis, composition, and inference. An example is given: a person senses something, closes his eyes, and the intellect continues working using stored images and memory. The author then corrects a misunderstanding: sensory perceptions like hearing and sight are actually perceptions of the soul using instruments, not the instruments themselves; hearing is not merely sound reaching the brain but the soul's perception of meaning. A gloss (4) addresses a potential objection: the automaticity of these processes seems to contradict the fact that deliberation (nazar) is deliberate. The author argues that the initial analysis, composition, and inference can be involuntary, while nazar is always intentional. He responds to an objection that this denies intuitive knowledge (badihi): these processes can be so rapid and imperceptible as to be part of the initial attention. The passage then shifts to the soul as the true agent of intellection; the intellect is an act (fi`l) of the soul, not of the body. Acts are voluntary or involuntary, and sciences are divided into acquired (kasbi) and necessary (daruri). The intellect's voluntary acts depend on will, which is a heartfelt desire, linking the intellect to the heart. The author mentions a scholarly dispute: al-Shafi`i held the intellect is in the heart, while Abu Hanifa held it is in the brain. The author claims the verified view synthesizes both: the origin (asl) is in the heart (intention), and the light (nur) is in the brain. He warns against the misconception that rational principles are merely optional choices.

Voluntary and Involuntary Acts of the Intellect
Author explains voluntary and involuntary intellection and responds to an objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between involuntary intellection (al-ta'aqqul al-idtirari) and deliberative reasoning (al-nazar) and explain the classification of sciences into acquired and necessary.

Core Idea
Involuntary intellection is automatic; deliberative reasoning requires will. Both are acts of the soul.

The author extends the discussion to classify intellectual acts. He addresses an objection about the denial of intuitive knowledge and clarifies that even voluntary reasoning has involuntary underpinnings. This distinction is foundational for understanding how we acquire knowledge—some comes effortlessly, some through effort.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish voluntary and involuntary acts of the soul
Locate the sentence: 'وكل فعل فإما أن يكون إرادياً، أو اضطرارياً'. Note that the intellect, as an act of the soul, shares this division.
2
Understand the objection about intuitive knowledge
A hypothetical objector says that the author's claim about involuntary analysis, composition, and inference denies the existence of intuitive knowledge (badihi). Read the author's response: those processes are so fast and imperceptible that they occur within the initial attention.
3
Connect to the classification of sciences
The author says: 'من هنا قسم الناس العلوم إلى علوم كسبية وعلوم ضرورية'. Acquired sciences rely on voluntary acts; necessary sciences rely on involuntary, self-evident acts.
4
Analyze the dispute on the location of the intellect
The author links voluntary acts to will, which is a heartfelt desire. This leads to the debate: al-Shafi'i said the intellect is in the heart (origin), Abu Hanifa said in the brain. The author synthesizes: origin in heart, light in brain.

Distinguishing Three Views on the Premise-Conclusion Connection
Author presents three theological opinions on the connection between premises and conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the Ash'ari, Mu'tazili, and Philosopher views on the connection between premises and conclusion as presented in this passage.

Core Idea
Three Muslim schools debated whether the conclusion follows by habit, generation, or necessity.

This passage presents three theological positions on the nature of the connection between knowing the premises and knowing the conclusion. Understanding these positions is crucial because they reflect deeper commitments about causality, divine action, and human agency. The author will critique two of them and endorse one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three views
Look at the Arabic: القول الثاني, الثالث, الرابع. What are their core claims? The second is habitual (عادي), the third is generation (تولد), the fourth is necessary (واجب).
2
Extract the key term for each view
Note the terms: عادي, تولد, واجب. What does each imply about the causal relationship?
3
Understand the Ash'ari view in contrast
The text says the habitual view allows for the possibility that someone might not see the conclusion despite knowing premises, due to dullness. Why is that? Because the connection is not necessary.
4
Note the author's preference
The author says الناظم رجح القول الأول. Which view is that? It is the habitual view, though not fully described here.

القول الثالث: الارتباط، تولد، بمعنى أن القدرة الحادثة أثرت في العلم أو الظن بالنتيجة بواسطة تأثيرها في العلم أو الظن بالمقدمتين، إذ التولد أن يوجد فعل لفاعله فعلاً آخر، وهذا القول للمعتزلة وهو باطل لقيام البرهان على أنه لا تأثير للعبد في شيء من الأفعال الاختيارية.
القول الرابع: الارتباط واجب، وهو للفلاسفة، ومعناه أن العلم أو الظن بالمقدمتين علة أثرت بذاتها في العلم أو الظن بالنتيجة، وهو باطل لقيام البرهان على انتفاء تأثير العلة والطبيعة وأنه تعالى هو الفاعل المختار.
وقد رجح الناظم القول الأول على غيره من الأقوال.
The second opinion: The connection is habitual, and this is the saying of Sheikh al-Ash'ari. Its meaning is that it is permissible for knowledge or opinion of the conclusion to lag behind knowledge or opinion of the two premises, such that a person may reach such dullness that he knows the two premises but does not know the conclusion due to his lack of attention to the subsumption of the minor under the major. And in the picture there is consideration, for it is among the conditions to attend to the subsumption of the minor under the major. The third opinion: The connection is generated, meaning that the created power affected knowledge or opinion of the conclusion by means of its effect on knowledge or opinion of the two premises, since generation is that an agent produces an action from his action. This is the opinion of the Mu'tazila, and it is false because of the proof that there is no effect for the servant in any of the voluntary actions. The fourth opinion: The connection is necessary, and it is for the philosophers, meaning that knowledge or opinion of the two premises is a cause that effectively produces by itself knowledge or opinion of the conclusion. It is false because of the proof of the absence of effect of cause and nature, and that He, the Exalted, is the choosing agent. And the poet preferred the first opinion over the other opinions.

Refuting the Philosophers' View of Necessary Connection
Author critiques the Philosophers' view that the connection is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Philosophers' view that premises are a cause of the conclusion and critique it based on the denial of causal efficacy in nature.

Core Idea
The philosophers' claim that premises necessarily cause the conclusion is false because only God is the true agent, not nature.

The Philosophers (falasifa) held that knowledge of premises is a cause that necessarily produces knowledge of the conclusion. The author rejects this as false, invoking the Ash'ari critique of natural causation.

Guided Path
1
State the Philosophers' claim
الارتباط واجب. That the premises are a cause (illa) that effectively produces the conclusion by its own nature (bi-dhatiha).
2
Locate the author's refutation
وهو باطل لقيام البرهان على انتفاء تأثير العلة والطبيعة وأنه تعالى هو الفاعل المختار.
3
Identify the theological principle
The author appeals to the proof (burhan) that causes and nature have no effect; only God acts as the free agent (al-fa'il al-mukhtar).
4
Connect to the broader Ash'ari occasionalism
This is the Ash'ari doctrine: no causal efficacy in created things; God is the direct creator of all effects.
