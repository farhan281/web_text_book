Critical Refinement of Tadammun: Mental Meaning vs External Particularity
Hashiya critiques examples of tadammun, arguing they should be based on mental meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a tadammun example is valid by checking if the part is a necessary component of the mental concept.

Core Idea
Tadammun requires signification upon a part of the mental meaning, not the external object.

The hashia corrects the commentator's examples for tadammun, emphasizing that logicians consider mental meanings. We will learn the criterion for valid tadammun and why external parts are irrelevant in logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commentator's examples
Note that the commentator gave examples like 'lion' signifying 'tail' and 'house' signifying 'door' for tadammun. These are based on external parts of the objects.
Tail is an external part of lion; door is an external part of house.
2
Understand the hashia's critique
The hashia argues that tadammun must be on a part of the mental meaning, not the external object. The examples only work if the part is a necessary component of the mental concept.
The mental concept of house may not necessarily include door; one can conceive house without door.
3
Apply the criterion
For each example, check if the part is necessary for the mental concept. If door is not necessary for the mental concept of house, the example fails as tadammun.
The hashia states: 'if the door is a part of the meaning for which house is assigned such that house cannot be conceived without door, then the example is acceptable.'
4
Distinguish external particularity from mental meaning
External particularity (tashakhkhuṣ khārijī) refers to actual objects; mental meaning (maʿnā dhihnī) is the concept in the mind. Tadammun must be based on the latter.

(1) اعلم أنه لا يتم كون لفظ أربعة دالاً على الزوجية بالالتزام لزوماً بيناً بالمعنى الأخص في الذهن والخارج كمـا سيمثل به، إلا إذا جعلت معنى الأربعة المطابقي هو عدد ذا الزوجين. وعندها فمجرد ذكر الأربعة يستدعى معناهـا المخصوص هذا أى العدد ذا الزوجين، فكما ترى كان مجرد تصور الأربعة بهذا المعنى مستدعياً لمعنى الزوجيــه خارجاً وذهناً.
(5) It is that this contradicts the apparent statements of the logicians, who make the word signify by inclusion upon each part individually of the parts of the corresponding meaning. So what is considered by them is signification upon a part of the meaning in the mind, not by consideration of external particularity. And that is more fitting for the science of logic, which does not consider, as you will know in the signification of commitment, external entailment. The examples given by the commentator are correct by consideration of external particularity, not by consideration of the mental meaning or absolute meaning without the condition of its existence in the mind or in the external world, I mean the meaning of 'date'. Similarly for the signification of 'house' upon 'door': if the door is a part of the meaning for which 'house' is assigned such that 'house' cannot be except by the conception of 'door', then the example is acceptable; otherwise, it is from the type of signification of inclusion by external particularity as well, and although it is fitting for the science of uṣūl, here it is a departure from this art. And it is difficult to accept given the logicians' explicit statement that the word signifies by containing it. It is observed in this signification that the mind transfers from observing the whole to the part. So there is some mental movement in it. (1) Know that the word 'four' does not perfectly signify evenness by commitment with a clear entailment in the specific sense in the mind and externally as will be exemplified, unless the corresponding meaning of 'four' is made to be 'a number that has two pairs'. In that case, the mere mention of 'four' calls to mind its specific meaning, i.e., the number with two pairs, so as you see, the mere conception of 'four' with this meaning calls to mind the meaning of evenness externally and mentally.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) that corrects the commentator's examples for tadammun and iltizam. For tadammun, the hashia argues that logicians consider signification upon a part of the mental meaning, not upon external parts of the actual object. The examples of 'lion' signifying its tail and 'house' signifying the door are problematic because the tail is an external part, not a part of the mental concept of 'lion'. If 'door' is not a necessary part of the mental concept of 'house' (i.e., one can conceive 'house' without 'door'), then the example fails. This critique aligns with the science of logic, which deals with mental concepts. For iltizam, the hashia examines the example of 'four' signifying evenness. For this to be a necessary mental entailment (luzūm bayyin), the mental concept of 'four' must already imply evenness, i.e., 'four' must be conceived as a number with two pairs. The hashia clarifies that without this specific mental meaning, the entailment may not be clear. The overall lesson is that logical signification must be based on mental meanings, not external objects, to maintain precision.

Critical Refinement of Iltizam: The Example of 'Four' and Evenness
Hashiya critiques the example of iltizam, emphasizing the need for the mental meaning to directly imply the signified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a word signifies something by commitment with clear entailment based on the mental meaning.

Core Idea
For iltizam (luzūm bayyin), the mental concept of the signifier must directly imply the signified.

The hashia examines the example of 'four' signifying evenness, clarifying the condition for it to be a clear entailment. We will learn how to check if the mental meaning of a word implies the signified meaning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the example
The commentator proposed that 'four' signifies 'evenness' by commitment with a clear entailment (luzūm bayyin).
The word 'four' is the signifier; 'evenness' is the signified.
2
Recognize the condition
The hashia states that for this to be a clear entailment, the mental meaning of 'four' must be 'a number that has two pairs'.
Without this specific mental meaning, the entailment may not be clear.
3
Check if the entailment is clear
If we conceive 'four' as 'a number with two pairs', then evenness follows necessarily. The mental concept directly implies evenness.
The hashia says: 'مجرد تصور الأربعة بهذا المعنى مستدعياً لمعنى الزوجية' (the mere conception of four with this meaning calls to mind the meaning of evenness).
4
Apply to other examples
For any potential iltizam, ensure the mental meaning of the signifier directly implies the signified. If not, the entailment is not clear.
This ensures that logical signification remains precise and based on mental concepts.

The Author's Closing Prayer and Colophon
Author concludes with prayer and completion date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of an Islamic scholarly colophon and explain its significance in preserving the historical record of a text.

Core Idea
A colophon is more than a date; it is a humble prayer and a mark of scholarly integrity.

The closing lines of a classical Islamic text are not merely formalities. Often they contain a prayer for benefit, a date of completion, and a final plea. In this lesson, we examine such a colophon from the author of the Sullam al-Murawnaq, noting how these details anchor the work in its historical and spiritual context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prayer
Find the invocation to Allah in the Arabic text. What does the author ask for? He asks for benefit in this world and the next through the book, and for the Muslims to benefit from its goodness.
The prayer is 'نسأل الله تعالى العلي العظيم أن يفيدنا دنيا وأخرى بهذا الكتاب، وينفع المسلمين لما فيه خيرهم'
2
Extract the date
Locate the date of completion in both Hijri and Gregorian. The author gives 15 Rabi' al-Thani 1424 AH, corresponding to mid-June 2003 CE. Why are both given? For precision and cross-referencing.
The Hijri date is primary; the Gregorian is added for contemporary readers.
3
Recognize the humility
Notice the author's tone. He does not boast but prays and gives thanks. This reflects the Islamic scholarly tradition of attributing success to Allah and seeking His acceptance.
The phrase 'والحمد لله رب العالمين' shows gratitude.
4
Understand the function
Why do Islamic scholars include colophons? They serve as a historical record, establishing the date of completion, and as a spiritual closing, seeking blessing for the work and its readers.
Colophons help in manuscript studies for dating and authentication.

Commentary

Then the author states the date of completion: 15 Rabi' al-Thani 1424 AH, which corresponds to mid-June 2003 CE. The final word 'رب' may be a remnant of a longer phrase like 'رب العالمين' (Lord of the worlds). This is a standard colophon in Islamic scholarly works, providing a historical record of the work's completion.
