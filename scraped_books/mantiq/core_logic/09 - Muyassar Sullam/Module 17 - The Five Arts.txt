The Two Types of Proof and the Five Rational Arts
Author classifies proof into transmitted and rational, lists five arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given proof as either transmitted (naqliyya) or rational (aqliyya), and list the five rational arts of logic.

Core Idea
Proof is either based on revelation or reason; the five rational arts serve different purposes, from certainty to deception.

In this lesson, we examine the opening of the chapter on proof (hujja). The author defines proof and divides it into two broad categories: transmitted and rational. He then lists the five rational arts that logicians have identified: demonstration, dialectic, oratory, poetry, and sophistry. Understanding this classification is essential for recognising the strength and purpose of any argument.

Guided Path
1
Define hujja
Notice the etymology: 'whoever holds it defeats his opponent'.
2
Identify the two main divisions
Transmitted (naqliyya) relies on revelation; rational (aqliyya) relies on reason alone.
3
Memorise the five rational arts
Burhan (demonstration), Jadal (dialectic), Khitaba (oratory), Shi‘r (poetry), Safsata (sophistry).
4
Understand the hierarchy
Burhan is the noblest because it yields certainty; the others serve persuasion, entertainment, or deception.

Chapter on [Divisions of Proof]. Proof (hujja) is evidence; it is called hujja because whoever holds it defeats his opponent, i.e., overpowers him. He said: [Proof is transmitted or rational. Its divisions are five, clear: Oratory, Poetry, Demonstration, Dialectic, and fifth is Sophistry, you attain the hope. The noblest of them is Demonstration, which is composed of premises connected with certainty: from primary intuitions, observations, experienced facts, mutawatir reports, intuitive judgments, and sensory perceptions. Those are the totality of certainties. And in the implication of premises to the conclusion, there is a difference to come: rational, habitual, generated, or necessary. And the first is supported.] I say: Proof is divided into transmitted (naqliyya) and rational (aqliyya). Transmitted is that which relies on transmission, even if the intellect is the perceiver of it. Rational is attributed to the intellect because the intellect does not depend on transmission in establishing it. Divisions of rational proof: The rational proof is divided into five divisions: (1) You have learned that what benefits the desired assent is three divisions: induction, syllogism, and analogy. The one that benefits certainty among them is syllogism. As for induction and analogy, they do not benefit it. As for induction, because of the possibility that there remains a particular not surveyed, like the crocodile in the mentioned example. As for analogy, because of the possibility that the cause (illa) in the particular being carried over is different from the cause in the particular carried. And the mere occurrence of possibility in both cases negates certainty. So understand.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive classification of proofs. It begins by defining hujja (proof) as that which defeats an opponent. Then a poetic matn (lines 114-120) is quoted, which the author (the commentator) expands. The matn states that proof is either transmitted (naqliyya) or rational (aqliyya), and rational proof has five divisions: oratory (khitaba), poetry (shi’r), demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), and sophistry (safsata). Demonstration is the noblest because it uses premises that are certain (yaqiniyyat). The certain premises are listed in the matn: primary intuitions (awwaliyat), observed facts (mushahadat), experiential facts (mujarrabat), mutawatir reports (mutawatirat), intuitive judgments (hadasiyyat), and sensory perceptions (mahsusat). The matn also notes that there is a difference in how premises imply conclusions: rational (aqli), habitual (adi), generated (tawallud), or necessary (wajib). The commentary then explains that transmitted proof relies on revelation (naql), while rational proof does not. The commentary then adds a crucial epistemological note: only syllogism (qiyas) yields certainty; induction and analogy do not, due to the possibility of counterexamples (crocodile for induction) or different causes (for analogy). This reinforces the earlier lessons on induction and analogy.

Key Terms
حجة (hujja): Proof or evidence that defeats an opponent; divided into transmitted and rational.
برهان (burhan): Demonstration; the noblest of the five rational arts, composed of certain premises yielding certain conclusions.
يقينيات (yaqiniyyat): Certain premises; includes awwaliyat, mushahadat, mujarrabat, mutawatirat, hadasiyyat, and mahsusat.
أوليات (awwaliyat): Primary intuitive knowledge; self-evident propositions like 'the whole is greater than the part'.
مشاهدات (mushahadat): Observed facts; knowledge gained through direct observation, e.g., observing that fire burns.
مجرّبات (mujarrabat): Experiential facts; knowledge gained through repeated experiments, e.g., that scammony purges bile.
متواترات (mutawatirat): Mutawatir reports; knowledge transmitted by so many sources that collusion is impossible.
حدسيات (hadasiyyat): Intuitive judgments derived from powerful insight, e.g., the light of the moon is from the sun.
محسوسات (mahsusat): Sensory perceptions; knowledge via the five senses.
جدل (jadal): Dialectic; rational art using widely accepted premises (mashhurat) to convince an opponent.
خطابة (khitaba): Oratory/rhetoric; uses persuasive premises (muqni‘at) to sway a general audience.
شعر (shi‘r): Poetics; uses imaginary premises to move emotions, not for logical proof.
سفسطة (safsata): Sophistry; uses fallacious premises to deceive.

The Six Certain Premises (Yaqiniyyat) of Demonstration
Author lists six types of certain premises for demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and define the six types of certain premises used in demonstration.

Core Idea
Demonstration uses six absolutely certain types of premises, from self-evident truths to sensory experiences.

The matn lists the specific premises that make demonstration certain: primary intuitions, observations, experiments, mutawatir reports, intuitive judgments, and sensory data. Each type provides knowledge that cannot be doubted. In this lesson, we will memorise these six and understand their source of certainty.

Guided Path
1
Recall that burhan is composed of certain premises
These premises are called yaqiniyyat.
2
Learn the six terms
Awwaliyat (self-evident), mushahadat (observed), mujarrabat (experiential), mutawatirat (mass-transmitted), hadasiyyat (intuitive), mahsusat (sensory).
3
Understand each with an example
Awwaliyat: whole > part; mushahadat: fire burns; mujarrabat: scammony purges bile; mutawatirat: existence of Mecca; hadasiyyat: moonlight from sun; mahsusat: seeing the sun.
These examples are from the commentary.
4
Verify these are the only certain premises
The matn says 'those are the totality of certainties.'

Why Only Syllogism Yields Certainty
Author explains why only syllogism yields certainty, critiquing induction and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why only syllogism (qiyas) yields certainty, while induction and analogy yield only probability.

Core Idea
Certainty comes only from syllogism because induction and analogy always leave room for doubt.

In the commentary, the author explains a crucial point: among the three methods of reasoning (induction, syllogism, analogy), only syllogism produces certainty. This is because induction cannot guarantee all cases have been examined, and analogy cannot guarantee the cause is the same in both cases. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three methods of reasoning
Induction (istiqra'), syllogism (qiyas), and analogy (tamthil).
2
Understand why induction fails
There is always a possibility of a missing case (e.g., crocodile in the example). So it yields only probability.
3
Understand why analogy fails
The 'illa (cause) in the original case might differ from the new case. So it yields only probability.
4
Conclude that only syllogism yields certainty
Given its rigorous structure, syllogism (qiyas iqtirani or istithna’i) can produce certainty.

The Five Rational Proofs: A Classification
Author enumerates and explains the five types of rational argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five types of rational proof (rhetoric, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, sophistry) and distinguish them based on their premises and purpose.

Core Idea
Rational proofs are ranked by certainty: demonstration is certain, dialectic and rhetoric are probable, poetry works on emotion, sophistry deceives.

In this passage, the author classifies all rational arguments into five categories. Understanding this classification is essential because it tells you which type of proof is appropriate for a given context. For example, in theology, only demonstration yields certainty, while dialectic is useful for refuting opponents who accept certain common premises. This lesson will walk you through each type, its defining characteristics, and an example.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish transmitted and rational proofs
Note that hujja (proof) is either transmitted (naqliyya) or rational (aqliyya). This lesson focuses on rational proofs.
Transmitted proofs rely on scripture; rational proofs rely on reason.
2
Identify the five types from the poem
The poem lists: khitaba, shi'r, burhan, jadal, safsata. Memorize this order.
The poem is a mnemonic device.
3
Analyze each type's premises
For each type, determine what kind of premises it uses: certain (yaqiniyya) for burhan, widely accepted (mashhura) for jadal, accepted (maqbula) or probable (maznuna) for khitaba, imaginative for shi'r, false for safsata.
The premises determine the certainty level of the conclusion.
4
Determine the purpose of each
Burhan aims at truth; jadal at winning an argument; khitaba at persuasion; shi'r at emotional effect; safsata at deception.
Purpose distinguishes them even if premises overlap.
5
Recognize the prohibition of sophistry
Safsata is forbidden because it uses falsehood and deception. The external fallacy is a specific case where a debater disturbs the opponent.
This highlights the ethical dimension of logic in Islam.

Core Idea
The power of a syllogism depends on its premises: certain, accepted, probable, emotional, or false.

In this passage, the author presents a foundational classification of logical arguments used in Islamic discourse. From the preacher's persuasive speech to the demonstrative proof of the mutakallim, each art serves a distinct epistemological role. Mastering this classification equips you to assess the strength of arguments in fiqh, kalam, and tafsir.
