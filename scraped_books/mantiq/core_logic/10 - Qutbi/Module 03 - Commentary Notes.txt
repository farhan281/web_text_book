Navigating the Commentarial Tradition of al-Shamsiyya
Survey of major commentaries and glosses on al-Shamsiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the chain of commentaries and glosses on a foundational Islamic logic text, identifying the base text, its major commentaries, and subsequent glosses.

Core Idea
The Shamsiyya commentarial tradition is a layered scholarly conversation spanning centuries.

The passage you are about to read is an editor's introduction surveying the extensive commentarial tradition on al-Shamsiyya (al-Risala al-Shamsiyya) by Najm al-Din al-Katibi. This text was a cornerstone of logic education, and generations of scholars produced commentaries (shuruh) and glosses (hawashi) on it. Understanding this chain helps you locate your own reading within the tradition and appreciate how each scholar built upon or critiqued earlier works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text
The base text is 'al-Shamsiyya' (misspelled as 'al-Khamsiyya' in the source). Recognize it as the foundational work by al-Katibi.
2
List the first-level commentaries
According to al-Jalabi, the major commentaries are by al-Taftazani (753 AH), al-Qaramani (on the introduction), and al-Tahtani (d. 766 AH). Note that al-Tahtani's commentary is 'good and in circulation among students'.
3
Identify the gloss on a commentary
The most famous gloss is by al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani (d. 816 AH) on al-Tahtani's commentary, called 'Hashiya Kuchak'.
The date given (0753) is likely a scribal error; al-Jurjani died much later.
4
Trace the sub-glosses
Al-Jurjani's gloss itself attracted many sub-glosses, by scholars like Qara Dawud, Sidi 'Ali al-'Ajami, and others. This shows a multi-layered tradition.
5
Map the chain
Draw a diagram: matn (al-Shamsiyya) → sharh (e.g., Taftazani, Tahtani) → hashya (Jurjani) → hashya 'ala hashya (Qara Dawud, etc.). This structure repeated across many texts.

Defining a Science by Its Formal Definition (Rasm)
Author argues for defining 'ilm by its rasm to start with insight, addressing an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how a formal definition (rasm) provides a universal premise for recognizing a science's issues and evaluate the author's response to the objection that mere general conceptualization suffices.

Core Idea
A science's formal definition is a key that unlocks its issues, not the treasure itself.

Before embarking on the study of any science, a student must have some conceptualization of what that science is. This passage argues that the most appropriate way to gain such a conceptualization is through a formal definition (rasm). The author defends this choice against an objection and clarifies that the rasm gives a criterion for identifying issues, not detailed knowledge of every issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The author states that it is more appropriate to conceptualize a science by its rasm so that the student has insight (basira). Locate this claim in the Arabic text: 'فالأولى أن يقال: لابد من تصوّر العلم برسمه'.
2
Understand the necessity
Why is a conceptualization necessary? Without any conceptualization, one cannot know that a given issue belongs to that science. The author implies that a rasm provides a universal premise to identify issues.
3
Analyze the objection
The objection is that only a general conceptualization (tasawwur bi wajh ma) is needed, not a specific formal definition. The author responds by arguing that the rasm entails the necessary general conceptualization, so choosing it is valid.
4
Examine the analogy
The author uses an analogy of two roads leading to the same goal. Choosing one does not invalidate the other. Similarly, choosing rasm is valid even though another type of conceptualization would also suffice.
5
Apply the examples
The author gives examples from grammar and logic. For grammar, the rasm 'science of principles by which the states of word endings are known' provides the universal premise that any issue about word endings is from grammar. Practice applying this logic to a new issue.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) that expands on the matn's claim about the necessity of conceptualizing a science (tasawwur al-'ilm) before embarking on its study. The commentator argues that the most appropriate approach is to have a formal definition (rasm) of the science, because it provides a universal premise that allows the student to recognize each issue as belonging to that science. The author anticipates an objection: one might say only a general conceptualization (tasawwur bi wajh ma) is needed, not a specific formal definition. The response uses an analogy: if two roads lead to the same goal, choosing one does not negate the other's validity. Similarly, choosing the formal definition is valid because it entails the necessary general conceptualization, even though another kind of conceptualization would also suffice. The commentator then clarifies that this does not mean the formal definition gives actual knowledge of every individual issue; rather, it equips the student with a criterion (a universal premise) to identify issues when encountered. Examples from grammar (nahw) and logic (mantiq) illustrate how defining a science by its specific property (khassa) enables recognition. The final analogy of a traveler who knows the signs of an unseen road emphasizes that insight (basira) comes from having a guiding framework, not from prior knowledge of every detail.

The Three Types of Signification in Islamic Logic
Author defines three types of signification and justifies the condition 'tawassut al-wad''.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of signification (muṭābaqa, taḍammun, iltizām) and provide an example from Islamic sciences for each.

Core Idea
A word signifies its meaning in three ways: by correspondence, by inclusion, or by implication.

This passage defines the three fundamental types of signification (dalāla) that a word has in relation to its conventionally assigned meaning. Understanding these types is crucial for any analysis of language in Islamic jurisprudence, theology, and logic. The author gives the example of 'human' to illustrate all three, then explains the rationale behind each name.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three significations
In the Arabic text, locate the definitions of muṭābaqa, taḍammun, and iltizām. Note that each is a relationship between a word and a meaning, mediated by the conventional assignment (waḍ‘).
The condition 'bi-tawassuṭ al-waḍ‘' appears in the definitions; we will focus on that in the next lesson.
2
Analyze the example of 'human'
The author uses insān (human) as an example. Its assigned meaning is 'rational animal'. Muṭābaqa: the word signifies the whole meaning (rational animal). Taḍammun: it signifies a part (e.g., animal). Iltizām: it signifies something external but necessary (e.g., capacity for knowledge).
Ensure you see how the same word can have multiple significations simultaneously.
3
Understand the naming of each type
Muṭābaqa comes from tābiq (to match), like two soles matching. Taḍammun means inclusion, because a part is included in the whole. Iltizām means implication, because the external meaning is necessarily implied.
The Arabic etymology reinforces the logical relationship.

The Condition 'Bi-Tawassuṭ al-Waḍ‘' in Definitions of Signification
Author defines three types of signification and justifies the condition 'tawassut al-wad''.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the condition 'bi-tawassuṭ al-waḍ‘' (by mediation of assignment) is necessary in the definitions of the three types of signification, using the homonymy examples provided.

Core Idea
The condition prevents cross-contamination of definitions when a word has multiple assignments due to homonymy.

The author has defined the three significations with the qualification 'bi-tawassuṭ al-waḍ‘'. In this lesson, we explore why this condition is crucial. Without it, a word shared between a whole and its part (like 'imkān') or between an implied and its concomitant (like 'shams') could cause the definition of correspondence to include inclusion or implication. The author demonstrates this with four concrete images and shows how the condition restores exclusivity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem of homonymy
The author notes that a word can be shared (homonymous) between a part and a whole (e.g., 'imkān' assigned to both specific and general possibility) or between an implied and its concomitant (e.g., 'shams' assigned to both the sun's body and its light).
This creates potential for a signification to be classified incorrectly.
2
Examine the four images
The author lists four scenarios: using 'imkān' to mean general possibility, using it to mean specific possibility, using 'shams' to mean the body, and using it to mean the light. Focus on the second image: 'imkān' uttered intending specific possibility.
In this case, the word signifies specific possibility by correspondence and general possibility by inclusion.
3
Trace the violation without the condition
If the definition of correspondence were simply 'signification of the word upon the meaning assigned to it', then the inclusion signification of 'imkān' upon general possibility would also fit, because general possibility is also assigned to the word. Thus, the definition would not be exclusive.
This is the 'violation' (intiqāḍ) the author mentions.
4
Apply the condition to exclude it
The condition 'bi-tawassuṭ al-waḍ‘' means the signification must be directly by virtue of the word's assignment to that meaning. In the example, the signification upon general possibility is not direct; it is mediated by the assignment to specific possibility (which includes general possibility). Therefore, it is excluded from correspondence and correctly classified as inclusion.
The gloss by Sharif clarifies that the word can have two significations on the same meaning from different aspects.

The Relation Between Contradictories of Mutually Exclusive Things
Author refines the claim that contradictories of mutually exclusive things are partially opposed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify that the relation between the contradictories of two mutually exclusive things is partial contrast (tabāyun juz'ī) abstracted from total contrast and commonality from one aspect, and justify this using examples.

Core Idea
The contradictories of mutually exclusive things are partially contrasted because they can be either totally opposed or common from one aspect.

When two terms are mutually exclusive, like 'horse' and 'man', their contradictories ('non-horse' and 'non-man') do not have a fixed relation. Sometimes they coincide, sometimes they do not. This teaches us that the relation is partial contrast, not total contrast or commonality alone.

Recognize the premise
The passage begins by noting that a simple premise that each contradictory coincides with the other is not enough to establish the claim.
2
Identify the two possible sub-relations
The contradictories can either not coincide at all (total contrast) or they can coincide (commonality from one aspect). The partial contrast abstracts from these two.
3
Understand abstraction
The relation 'partial contrast' is not restricted to one sub-relation; it is the general relation that includes both possibilities.
4

Horse and man are mutually exclusive. Their contradictories: non-horse and non-man. A stone is both non-horse and non-man (coincide); a horse is non-man but not non-horse (total contrast). So the overall relation is partial contrast.

And you know that the claim is established merely by the premise saying 'each one of...' (1) The answer to that is that the meaning of their statement 'the two contradictories of two mutually exclusive things are partially opposed' is that the relation between these two contradictories is partial contrast, abstracted from the particularity of each of its two individuals—i.e., total contrast and commonality from one aspect. For if the partial contrast between them in all forms was subsumed under one of the two particularities, like total contrast for example, then the relation between them would be that particularity, since it is not said that the relation between horse and man or between animal and white is partial contrast, despite its certain existence there. Rather it is said: the relation between the first two is total contrast, and between the last two is commonality from one aspect, and from that the existence of partial contrast in both cases is known. And there is no doubt that the claim in this sense is only completed by showing that the contradictories of two mutually exclusive things may not coincide at all and may coincide, so the partial contrast between them is not restricted to the particularity of total contrast in all forms, nor to the particularity of commonality from one aspect in all of them, but rather it is established in some of them under total contrast, and in some of them under commonality from one aspect. So the relation between the contradictories of two mutually exclusive things is partial contrast abstracted from the particularity of each of its two individuals, and that is the desired. And this speech has no doubt in it. It was said: The author showed that the contradictories of two things which have commonality from one aspect may be totally opposed in some forms, and it is apparent that between them there may be commonality from one aspect—like non-animal and non-white. So if that is added to what he mentioned regarding the contradictories of mutually exclusive things, that each one of them coincides with the contradictory of the other, for it applies to them as well—it appears that the relation between them is partial contrast abstracted from the particularity of each of their two individuals. Or we say: He first denied that the relation between them is commonality from one aspect, because the mind immediately leaps to the idea that the relation between the two contradictories is also commonality from one aspect, so he exaggerated in denying it by adding to it the absolute denial of commonality, and he did not address the relation between them there, because it is known from what was mentioned regarding the contradictories of mutually exclusive things itself, because if their two contradictories do not coincide on anything at all—like the contradictory of the general and the exact specific—then between them is total contrast, and if they coincide then between them is commonality from one aspect, necessarily because each of the two meanings coincides with the contradictory of the other. And in any case, there is partial contrast, so it does not follow that the author neglected the relation between them while intending to explain it (Sharif).

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of a previous claim about the relation between the contradictories of two things that are mutually exclusive (mutabāyinān). The author explains that the simple premise that each contradictory of one coincides with the contradictory of the other is insufficient to establish the claim. Rather, one must consider that the partial contrast between these contradictories is not always subsumed under total contrast; it can also be subsumed under commonality from one aspect, depending on whether the contradictories coincide or not. The author then addresses an objection: it is argued that the original author (al-muṣannif) had shown that the contradictories of things with commonality from one aspect may be totally opposed in some forms, and that this leads to the same conclusion of partial contrast. The response clarifies that the original author's denial of commonality from one aspect was meant to counter the natural assumption that the relation is always commonality from one aspect, and that the precise relation is understood from the earlier discussion. The passage emphasizes that the partial contrast is abstracted from both total contrast and commonality from one aspect, and that the original author did not neglect this relation.

Defending the Refinement: Objection and Response
Author refines the claim that contradictories of mutually exclusive things are partially opposed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection against the claim about contradictories of mutually exclusive things and explain how the commentator defends the original author.

Core Idea
The original author did not neglect the relation; it follows from the same principle applied to similar cases.

In the passage, an objector points out that the original author had previously shown that contradictories of things with commonality from one aspect may be totally opposed. The commentator responds that this does not contradict the current claim; rather, it supports it. The key is that the relation is understood to be partial contrast in all cases, whether the sub-relation is total contrast or commonality.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector argues that the author had previously shown that contradictories of things with commonality from one aspect (e.g., animal and white) can be totally opposed in some forms. This seems to conflict with the claim that the relation is partial contrast.
2
Follow the response
The commentator responds by clarifying that the earlier discussion also leads to partial contrast. If the contradictories do not coincide, they have total contrast; if they coincide, they have commonality from one aspect. In either case, the overall relation is partial contrast.
3
Recognize the author's intention
The original author's denial of commonality from one aspect was to counter a natural assumption that the relation is always commonality from one aspect. He did not need to explicitly state the resulting partial contrast because it follows from the logic.
4
Apply the example of animal and white
Animal and white have commonality from one aspect. Their contradictories 'non-animal' and 'non-white' can be totally opposed (e.g., a rock is non-animal and non-white? Actually rock is non-animal, and can be white if it's a white rock. Better: a human who is not white is non-white but not non-animal; while a non-animal like a plant is non-animal but not necessarily non-white. So they sometimes coincide and sometimes not, resulting in partial contrast.
