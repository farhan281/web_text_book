Essential Accidents: Definition and Types
Qutbi defines six types of accidents, focusing on essential ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of essential accidents and explain why they are considered essential based on their attachment to the subject's essence.

Core Idea
Essential accidents trace back to the subject's essence, directly or via a part or equal.

The text begins with examples of essential accidents: amazement attaches to man's essence, voluntary motion attaches via man's animality (a part), and laughter attaches via amazement (an equal external cause). Then it explains the three ways an accident can be essential. We will work through each path and understand the reasoning behind calling them essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three essential attachment paths
From the passage, find the three ways an accident can be essential: attaching to the subject's essence itself, attaching via a part of the essence, or attaching via an external factor equal to the subject. Write them down.
The passage lists them as: 'العارض لذات المعروض، العارض لجزئه، العارض للمساوي'.
2
Understand why each path counts as essential
The passage argues: the part is inside the essence, so its accident is indirectly based on the essence. The equal is based on the essence, and the accident based on the equal is also based on the essence. Confirm this reasoning in the text.
The author provides a chain: 'المستند إلى المستند إلى الشيء مستند إلى ذلك الشيء'.
3
Apply the definition to new examples
Given an accident, determine its attachment path and decide if it is essential. For instance, 'voluntary motion' attaches to man via 'animality' (a part). Therefore it is essential.
Use the criterion that the accident must ultimately be based on the subject's essence.

Strange Accidents and Their Scientific Placement
Qutbi defines the three strange accident types and their scientific placement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the three types of strange accidents and explain why each is not investigated in the science of the subject it attaches to.

Core Idea
Strange accidents attach via external factors not based on the subject's essence; they belong to the science of that external factor.

The text completes the classification of accidents by describing the three types of strange accidents: those attaching via a more general attribute, a more specific attribute, or a separate cause. It then explains the important principle that sciences only investigate essential accidents of their subjects; strange accidents are studied in the science of the external factor that serves as their essential subject. Let's analyze each strange type and apply this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three strange attachment paths
From the passage, find the three ways an accident can be strange: attaching via an external factor that is more general than the subject, more specific, or separate (mubayin). Write down each with the example given.
Examples: motion to white (more general: body), laughter to animal (more specific: human), heat to water (separate: fire).
2
Understand why they are called 'strange'
The passage says they are 'gharib' because they are not based on the essence of the subject. Even though they attach to the subject, their ultimate cause is external. Explain this in your own words.
The strangeness is relative to the subject's essence; the accident is not a proper affection of that subject.
3
Apply the scientific placement principle
The passage states that each accident is studied in the science that investigates its essential subject. For example, motion is a strange accident of white but an essential accident of body, so motion is studied in physics (science of body). Practice by determining where heat should be studied given it is a strange accident of water.
Heat is an essential accident of fire (or of body?), so it would be studied in the science of fire or in physics if fire is a type of body.

Classification of Essential and Strange Accidents
Defines essential and strange accidents with subcategories and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any accident as essential or strange and identify its specific subtype (direct, part, equal, broader external, narrower external, separate).

Core Idea
Accidents are classified by their dependence on the essence; this determines which science studies them.

In Islamic logic, accidents are classified into essential (dhatiyya) and strange (ghariba). This classification is crucial for defining the scope of any science: a science only investigates the essential accidents of its subject. The passage details six types: three essential (accident to the essence itself, to a part of the essence, or to something coextensive with the essence) and three strange (accident to a broader external, a narrower external, or a separate external cause). Understanding this helps you see why, for example, motion is studied in physics (as an essential accident of body) but not in the science of whiteness.

Classifying Simple Words by Predication and Time
Classification of simple words with criteria and objection resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any simple Arabic word as a particle, verb, or noun by applying the criteria of independent predicability and formal time indication, and resolve the counterexample of attached pronouns using the synonym principle.

Core Idea
A word's category depends on its ability to be predicated alone and whether its form signals time.

In logic, simple words are divided into three categories using two clear criteria: can the word stand alone as a predicate? If not, it's a particle. If yes, does its form indicate a specific time? If yes, it's a verb; if no, it's a noun. This lesson explains the criteria and then tackles a subtle counterexample: attached pronouns, which cannot be predicates alone but are not particles. The solution lies in a refined understanding of the particle's definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two criteria
Look for two conditions: (1) whether the word is fit to be predicated alone (yukhbar bihi waḥdahu), and (2) whether its form (hay'a) indicates a specific time (zaman mu'ayyan).
If a word fails the first test, it is a particle. If it passes, check the second test to distinguish verb from noun.
2
Apply to a particle example
Take the preposition 'fī'. Can it be a predicate alone? No. You cannot say 'Zayd fī' meaning 'Zayd is in'. So it is a particle.
3
Apply to a verb example
Take 'ḍaraba' (he hit). Can it be a predicate? Yes: 'Zayd ḍaraba' means 'Zayd hit'. Does its form indicate time? The form fa‘ala indicates past tense. So it is a verb (kalima).
4
Apply to a noun example
Take 'rajul' (man). Can it be a predicate? Yes: 'Zayd rajul' means 'Zayd is a man'. Does its form indicate a specific time? No. So it is a noun.
5
Examine the attached pronoun puzzle
Consider the 'alif' in 'ḍarabā' (they two hit). It cannot be a predicate alone. Does that make it a particle? No, because the criterion for a particle includes that it cannot be predicated even by a synonym. The 'alif' has a synonym 'humā' which can be a predicate, so it is not a particle.
The response to the objection clarifies that the particle's inability to be predicated extends to its synonyms.

Universal Conditionals: Beyond All Times
Author explains universality requires all possible coexisting situations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a conditional proposition is universal by checking its truth in all possible coexisting situations.

Core Idea
A universal conditional must hold even if a donkey brays or the sun rises.

The author begins by clarifying that a universal conditional (sharṭiyya kulliyya) does not merely require implication in all times (azmina) – it must hold in every situation (wad‘) that can possibly coexist with the antecedent. For example, 'If Zayd is human, then he is an animal' must be true whether Zayd is standing, sitting, the sun is rising, or a donkey is braying. This lesson teaches you how to identify whether a conditional meets this stringent requirement, which is essential for valid arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
Locate the part before 'then' (al-muqaddam) and after (al-tālī). In 'If Zayd is human, then he is an animal,' the antecedent is 'Zayd is human' and consequent is 'he is an animal.'
The antecedent is sometimes called the 'condition' (sharṭ).
2
List possible coexisting situations
Imagine all states that can coexist with the antecedent. These include not only temporal moments but also any accidental attributes like Zayd being standing, sitting, the sun rising, or a donkey braying. The list is infinite, but you must verify that the implication holds in every such state.
Consider only states that are logically possible together with the antecedent.
3
Check if the consequent follows in each
For each situation, ask: if the antecedent is true in this situation, is the consequent necessarily true? If there is even one situation where the antecedent is true and the consequent false (or does not follow), the conditional is not universal.
Use the real relation (luzūm) between the two parts to determine necessity.
4
Conclude on universality
If the implication holds in all possible coexisting situations, the conditional is universal. If not, it is particular or accidental.
Universal conditionals are rare; many commonly used 'if-then' statements are only true in certain situations.

Commentary
This passage explains the conditions for a conditional proposition to be universal (kulliyya). The author begins by distinguishing between considering all possible situations (awḍā‘) that can coexist with the antecedent and considering all situations absolutely. For a universal conditional, it is not enough that the implication holds in all times; it must hold in every conceivable state that can coexist with the antecedent, whether that state is a proposition or a non-propositional state. For example, 'If Zayd is human, then he is an animal' must hold even if Zayd is standing, sitting, the sun is rising, or a donkey is braying – any state that can coexist with his humanity. If we considered all absolute situations, including those where the consequent does not exist or where implication fails, the universal conditional would be false. The author then addresses an objection: that states like 'Zayd is standing' are not 'situations' arising from possible coexistence but rather external affairs that happen to coincide. The author refutes this by explaining that such matters, when they coexist with the antecedent, produce states of being concomitant (muqāran) that are different from the matters themselves, just as the act of striking gives rise to the attributes 'striker' and 'struck'. Hence these are genuine situations. Next, the author applies this to disjunctive conditionals: for a universal disjunctive (exclusive), the consequent must oppose the antecedent in all possible situations. If a situation arises where both are true, then the consequent does not oppose the antecedent, breaking universality. Finally, the author restricts this interpretation to implicative conjunctives (al-muttaṣila al-luzūmiyya) and oppositional disjunctives (al-munfaṣila al-‘inādiyya). Accidental conditionals (ittifāqiyya) are different: they lack an intrinsic relation between antecedent and consequent, so we only consider actual states (bi-ḥasab nafs al-amr), not all possible ones. Otherwise, if we considered all possible situations, the accidental conditional would never be universal because there is always a possible situation where the antecedent is true and the consequent false.

What Counts as a Situation? Refuting an Objection
Author refutes objection that coexisting matters are not genuine situations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish genuine situations arising from coexistence from merely accidental coexisting matters.

Core Idea
A situation is a state produced by concomitant coexistence, not just any external fact.

After establishing that universal conditionals require considering all possible coexisting situations, the author addresses an objection: are examples like 'Zayd is standing' really situations arising from possible coexistence, or just external matters that happen to coincide? The author refutes this by analyzing the nature of coexistence itself, using the analogy of Zayd's striking 'Amr – the act of coexistence generates new attributes. This lesson unpacks that refutation, which is crucial for correctly applying the condition of universality.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Someone might argue that 'Zayd is standing' is not a 'situation' (wad‘) arising from the antecedent's coexistence with the sun rising or the donkey braying. Rather, these are external affairs (umūr) that happen to be true alongside the antecedent, not states generated by coexistence.
The objector seems to think situations must be somehow produced by the antecedent, not just any accompanying fact.
2
Analyze the refutation using the striking analogy
The author compares the antecedent to the act of Zayd striking 'Amr. From that act, two attributes emerge: Zayd being a striker (ḍāribiyya) and 'Amr being struck (maḍrūbiyya). These attributes are distinct from the act itself. Similarly, when the antecedent ('Zayd is human') coexists with a matter (e.g., the sun rising), it produces a state of being concomitant (muqārana) – the antecedent's state of being simultaneous with the sun rising. That state is a genuine situation, different from both the antecedent and the sun rising.
This refutes the objection by showing that even external matters, when they coexist, generate new situational attributes.
3
Identify the core insight
The key is that situations (awḍā‘) are not the coexisting matters themselves, but the states (ḥālāt) that accrue to the antecedent by virtue of coexistence. So 'Zayd being standing' is a state of the antecedent (its being concomitant with standing), which is a valid situation to consider.
The Arabic term 'wad‘' is used synonymously with 'ḥāla' (state).
4
Apply to universal conditionals
Thus, for a universal conditional, you must consider all states that arise from the antecedent's coexistence with any possible matter, not just matters that seem 'internal' to the antecedent.
This justifies the inclusion of examples like 'the sun rising'.

Commentary
This passage explains the conditions for a conditional proposition to be universal (kulliyya). The author begins by distinguishing between considering all possible situations (awḍā‘) that can coexist with the antecedent and considering all situations absolutely. For a universal conditional, it is not enough that the implication holds in all times; it must hold in every conceivable state that can coexist with the antecedent, whether that state is a proposition or a non-propositional state. For example, 'If Zayd is human, then he is an animal' must hold even if Zayd is standing, sitting, the sun is rising, or a donkey is braying – any state that can coexist with his humanity. If we considered all absolute situations, including those where the consequent does not exist or where implication fails, the universal conditional would be false. The author then addresses an objection: that states like 'Zayd is standing' are not 'situations' arising from possible coexistence but rather external affairs that happen to coincide. The author refutes this by explaining that such matters, when they coexist with the antecedent, produce states of being concomitant (muqāran) that are different from the matters themselves, just as the act of striking gives rise to the attributes 'striker' and 'struck'. Hence these are genuine situations. Next, the author applies this to disjunctive conditionals: for a universal disjunctive (exclusive), the consequent must oppose the antecedent in all possible situations. If a situation arises where both are true, then the consequent does not oppose the antecedent, breaking universality. Finally, the author restricts this interpretation to implicative conjunctives (al-muttaṣila al-luzūmiyya) and oppositional disjunctives (al-munfaṣila al-‘inādiyya). Otherwise, if we considered all possible situations, the accidental conditional would never be universal because there is always a possible situation where the antecedent is true and the consequent false.

Disjunctive and Accidental Conditionals: Limits of Universality
Author restricts universal condition interpretation to luzūmiyya and ‘inādiyya; accidentals only consider actual situations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify conditionals as implicative, oppositional, or accidental and determine the relevant situations for each when assessing universality.

Core Idea
Accidental conditionals lack real implication, so only actual situations matter for their truth.

The author concludes by restricting his previous analysis to two types of conditionals: implicative conjunctives (al-muttaṣila al-luzūmiyya) and oppositional disjunctives (al-munfaṣila al-‘inādiyya). For these, universality requires checking all possible coexisting situations. But accidental conditionals (ittifāqiyya) are different: they have no intrinsic relation between antecedent and consequent, so considering all possible situations would make them always false. Instead, only actual situations are considered. This lesson helps you recognize which type you are dealing with and what that means for universality.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three types from the passage
The author names: (1) implicative conjunctive (muttaṣila luzūmiyya), where the consequent follows from the antecedent due to a real relation; (2) oppositional disjunctive (munfaṣila ‘inādiyya), where the two parts cannot be true together or false together; (3) accidental conditional (ittifāqiyya), where there is no real connection, just coincidental truth.
In the passage, the author uses 'ittifāqiyya' for accidental conditionals.
2
Understand the rule for the first two types
For luzūmiyya and ‘inādiyya, universality requires the implication (luzūm) or opposition (‘inād) to hold in all possible situations that can coexist with the antecedent. This is the main teaching of the lesson.
The author explains why: if a situation allows the consequent to not follow (conjunctive) or to not oppose (disjunctive), universality fails.
3
Understand the exception for accidentals
Accidental conditionals (e.g., 'If Zayd is human, then the sun is rising') have no intrinsic relation. If we considered all possible coexisting situations, there would always be a situation where the antecedent is true and consequent false (e.g., night time). So they would never be universal. Hence, we only consider actual situations (awḍā‘ bi-ḥasab nafs al-amr). That is, 'If Zayd is human, then the sun is rising' is true only if, in reality, whenever Zayd is human, the sun happens to be rising – but that is only true for particular moments.
This shows that accidental conditionals cannot be universal in the strict sense; they are only true for specific contexts.
4
Practice classification
Given a conditional, first determine if there is a real relation (luzūm) between the two parts. If yes, it is luzūmiyya or ‘inādiyya. If no, it is ittifāqiyya. Then decide which situations to consider for universality testing.
Use the criterion: if the antecedent supports a logical or causal link to the consequent, it is not accidental.

Only conditionals with an intrinsic relation (implication or opposition) require checking all possible coexisting situations for universality; accidental conditionals rely on actual states.
