The Concept of Contradictory and the Use of Equivalent Necessary
Commentator defines nafīḍ as negation, discusses a critique, and introduces equivalent necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a contradictory must be taken as an equivalent necessary rather than direct negation, and apply this to modal propositions in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Not every direct negation is a clear proposition; contradictory is sometimes taken metaphorically via an equivalent necessary.

In this passage, the commentator refines the definition of contradictory (naqīḍ). While a contradictory is commonly understood as the direct negation (rafʿ) of a proposition, a problem arises when that negation lacks a clear conceptual meaning (mafhūm muḥaṣṣal) in the mind. To resolve this, logicians invoke an equivalent necessary (lāzim musāwī) that is easier to grasp. This lesson unpacks that epistemological move and shows how it applies to modal propositions in Islamic logical works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of contradictory
The commentator states: 'the contradictory of everything is its negation (rafʿ)'. This is straightforward: for any proposition, its contradictory is simply the denial of that proposition.
This definition works well for non-modal categorical propositions.
2
Discover the problem with direct negation
When a proposition is negated, the resulting expression may not be a standard proposition with a clear conceptual meaning (mafhūm muḥaṣṣal). For example, negating 'Every human is necessarily an animal' yields 'Not every human is necessarily an animal'—this is not a standard categorical proposition.
The commentator cites a critique by al-Sharif that probing this issue.
3
Learn the solution: the equivalent necessary
Instead of using the direct negation, logicians find a proposition that is logically equivalent to the contradictory and has a clear meaning. This equivalent necessary (lāzim musāwī) is then called the contradictory by metaphorical extension.
For modal propositions, the equivalent necessary is often a possibility statement.
4
Apply to a modal rule
The passage ends by applying this: the contradictory of the absolute necessary (e.g., 'Every A is necessarily B') is the absolute possible ('Some A is possibly not B'), which is the equivalent necessary, not the direct negation.
This rule is central to the logic of modality in Islamic sciences.

I say: Know first that the contradictory of everything is its negation (raf'), and this much is sufficient for taking the contradictory of any proposition, even that every proposition its contradictory is the negation of that proposition. So what is meant by 'contradictory' in this chapter is one of two things: either the actual contradictory or its equivalent necessary. Commentary
This passage is a detailed epistemological discussion about the nature of contradictory (naqīḍ). The commentator begins by defining the contradictory of a proposition as its negation (raf'), which is sufficient for identification. For example, the contradictory of 'Every human is necessarily an animal' is 'It is not the case that every human is necessarily an animal.' However, a problem arises: the direct negation of a proposition may not itself be a clear proposition with a definite conceptualized meaning (mafhūm muḥaṣṣal) in the mind. For instance, negating a universal affirmative results in a statement like 'Not every human is necessarily an animal,' which is not a standard categorical proposition. To resolve this, logicians use an equivalent necessary (lāzim musāwī) that has a definite meaning, such as 'Some human is possibly not an animal.' The commentator then cites a critique (footnote from al-Sharif) that challenges the original definition: negation (salb) is one thing, and its contradictory is affirmation, not negation of negation. The critic suggests it is better to say 'the negation of everything is its contradictory' unless the terms are taken broadly. The commentator then explains that the contradictory in this chapter is taken metaphorically: either the actual contradictory or its equivalent necessary. This allows for practical use in logical reasoning. The passage concludes by reiterating the specific rule from the previous lesson: the contradictory of absolute necessary is absolute possible, but now with this refined understanding.
