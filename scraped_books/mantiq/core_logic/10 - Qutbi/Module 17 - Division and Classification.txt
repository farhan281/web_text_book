Classifying Verbal Indication: Convention, Nature, and Reason
Author classifies verbal indication into three types by source.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given verbal indication as conventional, natural, or rational based on its source and justify the classification.

Core Idea
Verbal indication stems from convention, nature, or rational inference—not from arbitrary choice.

In Islamic logic, understanding how words convey meaning is essential. This passage introduces the three fundamental types of verbal indication: conventional (wad'iyyah), natural (tabi'iyyah), and rational ('aqliyyah). Each type has a distinct basis for connecting a word to its meaning. By mastering this classification, you will be able to analyze how every utterance signals its intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the basis of classification
Verbal indication is classified by the origin of the connection between the word and what it indicates. The author asks: does the connection come from a maker, from nature, or from rational inference?
This is a three-fold division, not a two-fold one.
2
Understand conventional indication (wad'iyyah)
Conventional indication depends on a deliberate assignment (wad') by a maker. For example, the word 'insān' (man) was assigned to indicate 'rational animal'. The connection is arbitrary but fixed by convention.
This is the primary type studied in logic.
3
Understand natural indication (tabi'iyyah)
Natural indication arises from a natural exigency (iqtiḍā' al-ṭab'). For example, the exclamation 'akh' (or 'ah') naturally comes from someone in pain because the speaker's nature forces it.
This type is involuntary and universal across humans.
4
Understand rational indication ('aqliyyah)
Rational indication requires inference. Hearing a voice from behind a wall leads you to conclude rationally that a speaker is present, even if you cannot see them.
The same utterance can provide rational indication in one context but not another (e.g., seeing the speaker directly).
5
Note the methodological justification
The author emphasizes that this threefold division is established by induction (istiqra'), not by a purely rational proof. One might think that if indication is neither conventional nor natural, it must be rational, but the author says that is not logically necessary—we simply observe only these three types.
This shows the author's empirical approach to classification.

And 'wad'' (convention) is making a word correspond to a meaning in the first instance. Or it is according to the exigency of nature, and it is natural (tabi'iyyah), like the indication of 'akh' (ach) for pain, for the nature of the speaker necessitates uttering it when pain occurs. Or it is not (by nature?), and it is rational ('aqliyyah), like the indication of a word heard from behind a wall for the existence of the speaker. And what is intended here is the conventional verbal indication, which is the word being such that... (1) This is the definition of wad' of a word; as for the definition of absolute wad' encompassing this and other things: it is making a thing correspond to another thing such that understanding the first necessitates understanding the second (Sharif). Because of this exigency, this word became indicative of that meaning – namely pain – so the indication is attributed to nature, just as the utterance of the word is attributed to nature (Sharif). But sound heard from behind a wall, the existence of the speaker is known only through rational indication of the word. And the confinement of indication to verbal and non-verbal is a confirmed matter with no doubt. As for the confinement of verbal indication to conventional, natural, and rational, it is by induction (istiqra'), not by rational exhaustive division that alternates between negation and affirmation, for if the indication of a word is not based on convention nor on nature, it does not necessarily follow that it is based on reason. But we have inducted and found only these three divisions (Sharif).

Commentary
This passage classifies verbal indication into three types. The conventional type (wad'iyyah) is based on a deliberate assignment of a word to a meaning, like the word 'insan' meaning 'rational animal'. The natural type (tabi'iyyah) arises from a natural impulse, like the exclamation 'akh' (or 'ah') which is uttered involuntarily when in pain. The rational type ('aqliyyah) involves inference, as when hearing a voice from behind a wall leads one to rationally conclude that a speaker is present. The author then clarifies that the focus is on conventional indication, which is the primary concern in logic. The commentary notes emphasize the definitions and provide linguistic details about the natural exclamation. Finally, the passage justifies the threefold division by stating it is based on induction (istiqra'), not on a purely rational classification that would claim exhaustiveness by implication. This is important because one might think that if indication is not conventional or natural, it must be rational, but the author explains that this is not a necessary logical deduction; rather, empirical observation shows only these three kinds exist.

Distinguishing Natural, Rational, and Conventional Indication
Author classifies dalala into three types and defines conventional indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the three types of verbal indication (natural, rational, conventional) as defined in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Verbal indication is classified by its source: nature, reason, or convention.

In Islamic logic, words convey meaning in three fundamental ways. Some words naturally arise from a state (like 'akh' from pain), some require rational inference (like hearing a voice behind a wall), and most are based on deliberate assignment (wad‘). Understanding these categories helps you pinpoint how a word's meaning is established, which is crucial when analyzing arguments in fiqh, kalam, and tafsir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being classified: verbal indication (dalala lafziyya).
The passage defines dalala as the property of a sign (here a word) to convey meaning. Note that there are also non-verbal indications, but the focus is on verbal.
2
Examine natural indication (tabi‘iyya).
The example is the exclamation 'akh' which naturally arises from pain. The relation is due to human nature, not convention.
3
Examine rational indication (‘aqliyya).
The example is hearing a word from behind a wall; the mind infers the existence of a speaker. It is not based on the word's inherent meaning but on reasoning.
4
Examine conventional indication (wad‘iyya).
This is the primary concern of logic. A word signifies its meaning because of an original assignment (wad‘). The meaning is understood whenever the word is uttered, without contextual clues.
5
Distinguish the types using criteria.
Ask: Is the relation due to nature, reason, or assignment? Natural: involuntary utterance. Rational: inference from cause to effect. Conventional: learned through linguistic training.
Note: The passage emphasizes that conventional indication must be universal (understood whenever uttered), not occasional.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of the types of indication (dalala). The author begins by relating back to the definition of 'wad‘' (positing) as placing a word in correspondence with a meaning. The commentary expands: absolute positing is placing any thing in relation to another. Then the text proceeds to classify indication into two broad kinds: natural (tabi‘iyya) and rational (‘aqliyya). Natural indication is based on the nature of the speaker, as the exclamation 'akh' naturally arises from pain. Rational indication occurs when reason infers the existence of a speaker from hearing a voice behind a wall. The intended focus of logic, however, is conventional (wad‘iyya) verbal indication, where the word's meaning is known due to its conventional assignment. The author notes that the confinement of verbal indication to these three types is based on induction (istiqra'), not a purely rational proof. That is, we do not know a priori that every verbal indication must fall into one of these three; rather, observation shows no other types. This guards against assuming a rational proof that would be circular (between negation and affirmation). The commentary adds that the universal indication (whenever uttered) is what matters in logic, not occasional uses with contextual indicators, which are the concern of linguists.

Key Terms

طبيعي (Tabii): Natural indication: where the relation between word and meaning arises from human nature, not convention. [Note: No significant shift.]

استقراء (Istiqra'): Induction: enumerative observation leading to a general classification. [Note: In logic, often complete induction; here it is the empirical method used to classify dalala.]

The Inductive Basis for the Classification of Indication
Author classifies dalala into three types and defines conventional indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why logicians confine verbal indication to three types through induction rather than rational proof.

Core Idea
The threefold division of indication is empirical, not demonstrable by pure reason.

A key methodological point in Islamic logic: the classification of verbal indication into natural, rational, and conventional is not based on a rational proof that would be circular. Instead, it is based on induction (istiqra‘) – observation that no other types exist. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being justified.
The claim: 'Verbal indication is confined to three types: natural, rational, conventional.'
2
Find the justification given in the text.
The author says: 'it is by induction, not by rational limitation that alternates between negation and affirmation'.
3
Understand why rational limitation is rejected.
A rational proof would require assuming that if indication is not natural or conventional, it must be rational – but that assumption itself needs proof and leads to circularity.
4
Recognize the method: induction (istiqra‘).
The logicians have investigated all known cases of verbal indication and found no other types. This is an empirical, not a priori, conclusion.
5
Assess the certainty of this classification.
Induction, especially incomplete, yields probabilistic certainty (zann), not certainty (yaqin). The passage implies this is sufficient for logical classification.
Note: This is an important epistemological distinction in Islamic thought – many classifications are based on induction.

Author divides conventional indication into mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam with examples.

Core Idea
Every conventional indication is either of the whole meaning, a part, or a necessary concomitant.

This passage introduces a foundational classification in Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh: conventional indication (dalala wad'iyya) divides into three exclusive types. Understanding this division allows you to precisely analyze how words convey meanings in revealed texts and scholarly discourse. Let's unpack each type with the classic example of the word 'man' (insan).

Guided Path
1
Grasp the classification principle
The author states that a word's indication relates to its posited meaning in one of three ways: identity, part, or external concomitant. This is a rational limitation (hasr 'aqli) that exhausts all possibilities.

Define Mutabaqa (Correspondence)
When the signified meaning is exactly the posited meaning, it is mutabaqa. Example: 'man' indicates 'rational animal' because that is what 'man' was coined for.

When the signified is a part of the posited meaning, it is tadammun. Example: 'man' indicates 'animal' because 'animal' is a component of the definition 'rational animal'.

When the signified is external to the posited meaning but necessarily connected (like a necessary property), it is iltizam. Example: 'man' indicates 'capable of laughter' because laughter follows from rationality.
The connection must be necessary; otherwise it is not conventional indication.

Why Only Nouns Are Divided into Particular and Universal
Author justifies restriction of juzʾī/kullī division to nouns

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain, using the criteria of independent meaning (maʿnā mustaqill), why only nouns (ism) can be classified as particular or universal, and why verbs and particles cannot.

Core Idea
Only nouns have independent meanings; verbs and particles are mere tools.

This passage digs deep into the nature of word meanings to answer a foundational question in logic: why do we only divide nouns into particular and universal? The author, al-Quṭb al-Taḥtānī, uses a precise analysis of how meaning functions in nouns, verbs, and particles. By the end of this lesson, you will understand the exact criterion – independence of meaning – and see why it excludes verbs and particles from this classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three parts of speech
Notice that the passage discusses three categories: noun (ism), verb (fiʿl), and particle (ḥarf). The author's goal is to show why only the first can be divided into particular and universal.
2
Distinguish independent vs. instrumental meaning
The key criterion is whether the meaning of a word, considered in itself, is independent (mustaqill) or instrumental. An independent meaning can be a subject (maḥkūm ʿalayh) or predicate (maḥkūm bihī). An instrumental meaning (like that of a particle) is only a relation between other meanings.
For example, 'min' (from) indicates a beginning only in relation to two endpoints.
3
Analyze the verb's composite meaning
A verb (e.g., 'daraba' – he hit) contains two parts: an event (al-ḥadath) and a relation (al-nisba) between the event and its agent. The event alone can be a predicate, but the total meaning (event + relation) is not independent. Therefore, the verb as a whole cannot be a subject or predicate.
The author explains that the verb is intermediate: it is better than a particle because its event part can be predicated, but worse than a noun because its total meaning lacks independence.
4
Apply the practical test
The author gives a simple test: try to express the meaning of a particle or verb by its word and see if you can make it a subject or predicate. For example, can you say 'Min is instrumental'? If you say 'The meaning of min is instrumental', you are using the noun phrase 'meaning of min', not the particle itself, so you have turned it into a noun. This test confirms that only nouns pass.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Insha' statements are classified by whether they request understanding or external action, and by the speaker's relation to the hearer.

The passage divides insha' into those without a request (tanbih) and those with a request. Incomplete Compounds (Murakkab Ghayr Tam)
Author defines and classifies incomplete compound expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between restrictive and non-restrictive incomplete compounds and provide examples from Arabic.

Core Idea
An incomplete compound is either restrictive, where the second part qualifies the first, or non-restrictive, like a noun with a particle.

The passage defines incomplete compound expressions as those that do not form a complete proposition. They are divided into two types: restrictive (taqyidi) and non-restrictive (ghayr taqyidi). The restrictive type has the second part as a restriction of the first, like 'al-hayawan al-natiq' (the rational animal). Non-restrictive includes combinations of a noun with a particle or a verb with a particle. This classification is important for understanding phrase structure in logic and grammar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the compound expression
Look at the expression. Is it a single word or a combination? If it's a combination, proceed.
2
Check if it is a complete proposition
Does it form a full sentence with a subject and predicate that can be true or false? If yes, it is not an incomplete compound.
3
Determine if the second part restricts the first
In a restrictive compound, the second part (e.g., an adjective) qualifies the first (e.g., a noun). Example: 'الحيوان الناطق' where 'الناطق' restricts 'الحيوان'.
4
Classify as restrictive or non-restrictive
If the second part restricts the first, it is taqyidi. Otherwise, it is ghayr taqyidi, like 'زيد في الدار' where the particle connects.
Non-restrictive includes noun+particle or verb+particle compounds.

Why a Real Species Cannot Have Another Real Species Above It
Proof that real species cannot be hierarchical

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that a real species cannot have another real species above or below it and identify the logical contradiction.

Core Idea
A real species cannot have another real species above it because that higher would be a genus, not a species.

In Islamic logic, a real species is defined as the complete essence of its individuals. This passage proves that such species cannot be arranged in a hierarchy. You will walk through the argument step by step, seeing why assuming a higher real species leads to an impossible contradiction, and how this relates to the concept of relative species.

Guided Path
1
Define 'real species'
Understand that a real species (al-naw' al-haqiqi) is the complete essence (tamam al-mahiyya) of all its individuals. Nothing outside its definition is needed to constitute any individual under it.
Example: 'human' is the complete essence of every human being.
2
Assume a higher real species
Suppose there is a universal above this real species that is also claimed to be a real species. For the argument, assume 'animal' is a real species above 'human'.
This assumption is what we will test for contradiction.
3
Derive the contradiction
If the higher is also a real species, it must be the complete essence of the individuals of the lower. But the lower is already the complete essence of those same individuals. Thus each individual would have two different complete essences, which is impossible.
Two complete essences for one thing cannot both be complete; one would be part of the other or neither would be complete.
4
Conclude the higher is a genus
The only way to avoid contradiction is that the higher is not a real species but a genus (common essence). It provides the shared part of the essence, while the lower adds the specific difference.
Thus 'animal' is a genus of 'human', not a real species above it.
5
Apply to relative species
A real species can be below a relative species (like man under animal as a genus) but cannot be above a relative species because a relative species is either a real species or a genus.
This clarifies the hierarchy: real species cannot be ordered above another real species.

The Four Ranks of Relative Species
Author classifies relative species into four ranks and provides examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given relative species into one of the four ranks: high, low, intermediate, or single.

Core Idea
Relative species gain their rank by their position in a hierarchy of universality and specificity.

In this lesson, we explore how relative species are arranged in a hierarchy. The author identifies four ranks based on how universal or specific a species is compared to others. Understanding these ranks is essential for correctly analyzing definitions in Islamic logic, especially in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh where hierarchies of genera and species appear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chain of relative species
In the example, trace the chain: man is a relative species under animal, animal under growing body, growing body under absolute body, and absolute body under substance. Notice how each is a relative species to the one above.
This chain illustrates the hierarchy.
2
Determine the four ranks
Recognize the criteria: the most universal is the 'high species', the most specific is the 'low species', a species that is more universal than some and more specific than others is 'intermediate', and a species with no hierarchical relation is 'single'.
These are the four ranks defined in the passage.
3
Apply the criteria to the chain
Classify each: body (absolute body) is high because it is most universal; man is low because it is most specific; animal and growing body are intermediate because they are more universal than some and more specific than others.
The passage specifically gives these examples.
4
Understand the single species
A single species has no relationship of universality or specificity with any other relative species. The passage notes that no example exists in existence, but it is a theoretical rank.
The single species is considered a rank by considering ordering in its absence.
5
Distinguish between real and relative species
The passage also discusses how real species relate to relative species: a real species compared to another real species is always single; compared to a relative species it can be single or low. A relative species compared to a real species can be single (if no real species under it) or high (if it subsumes a real species).
This helps avoid confusion between different types of species.

Classification of Propositions into Actual and Modified
Author divides propositions into four types and clarifies confusion between two of them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any categorical proposition as actual (muḥaṣṣala) or modified (ma‘dūla) by examining the predicate, and distinguish the actual negative from the modified affirmative using the condition of subject existence.

Core Idea
The negation particle's location determines the proposition type; actual negative and modified affirmative are only separable by checking subject existence.

In this passage, the author presents a fourfold classification of categorical propositions based on whether the negation particle is part of the predicate (modified) or not (actual). He then highlights a common ambiguity: the actual negative and the modified affirmative both contain a single negation particle, making them superficially identical. The resolution lies in a subtle but crucial logical distinction: the modified affirmative requires the subject to exist, whereas the actual negative does not. This lesson will equip you with the tools to identify and distinguish these proposition types in any logical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the predicate and the negation particle
In a proposition, locate the predicate (maḥmūl) and check whether it contains a negation particle (like 'lā' or 'ghayr'). If the negation is part of the predicate, the proposition is modified; otherwise it is actual.
Remember that the copula may also contain negation, but here we focus on the predicate.
2
Recognize the four possibilities
Combine the two dimensions: actual/modified and affirmative/negative. This yields four proposition types. Memorize the examples: actual affirmative, actual negative, modified affirmative, modified negative.
The author lists them explicitly.
3
Spot the ambiguous pair
Compare the actual negative and the modified affirmative. Both have exactly one negation particle. The actual negative has a negation in the copula, while the modified affirmative has a negation inside the predicate. In surface form, they are identical: e.g., 'Zayd is not a writer' could be either.
This confusion is the author's main point.
4
Apply the conceptual distinction
To resolve the ambiguity, test whether the proposition requires the subject to exist. The modified affirmative affirms a negative predicate of the subject, which only makes sense if the subject exists. The actual negative simply denies the predicate, and is true even if the subject does not exist. Thus, the actual negative is more general: if the modified affirmative is true, the actual negative is necessarily true, but not conversely.
Use this test in practice.

As for the 'problems' (masa'il), they are the propositions that are demonstrated in the science, if they are acquired. They have subjects and predicates.

Their subjects: sometimes they are the subject of the science, like our saying: 'Every magnitude is either coincident with another or distinct from it'—magnitude is the subject of geometry.
Sometimes it is the subject of the science along with an essential accident, like our saying: 'Every magnitude that is a middle in a ratio is a side of what is enclosed by the two extremes'—magnitude is the subject of the science, and it is taken in the problem with its being a middle in ratio, which is an essential accident.
Sometimes it is a species of the subject of the science, like our saying: 'Every line can be bisected'—line is a species of magnitude.
Sometimes it is a species of the subject of the science along with an essential accident, like our saying: 'Every line standing upon a line, its two adjacent angles are either right or equal to them'—line is a species of magnitude, and it is taken in the problem with its standing upon another line, which is an essential accident of magnitude.
Sometimes its subject is an essential accident, like our saying: 'Every triangle, its angles are equal to two right angles'—the triangle is an essential accident of magnitude.
Sometimes it is a species of an essential accident, like our saying: 'Every isosceles triangle, its base angles are equal.'
These are the subjects of the problems.
In summary, they are either the subjects of the science, or their parts, or their essential accidents, or their particulars.
As for their predicates, they are the essential accidents of the subject of the science. They must be external to their subjects, because it is impossible for a part of a thing to be sought by demonstration, since parts are evident in their attribution to the thing.

Commentary
This passage classifies the subject matters (mawdu'at) of the problems (masa'il) in a science, and then states the nature of their predicates. The author first defines problems as propositions demonstrated in a science, if they are not self-evident. Every problem has a subject (what it is about) and a predicate (what is attributed to the subject). The author then enumerates six cases for the subject of a problem, illustrated with examples from geometry (since the science in question is geometry; the subject of geometry is magnitude). 

1) The subject can be exactly the subject of the science itself, e.g., 'Every magnitude is either coincident or distinct.' 
2) The subject can be the subject of the science along with an essential accident that modifies it, e.g., 'Every magnitude that is a middle in ratio...' Here magnitude is the subject, and 'being a middle in ratio' is an essential accident added to it. 
3) The subject can be a species of the subject of the science, e.g., 'Every line can be bisected'—line is a species of magnitude. 
4) The subject can be a species of the subject of the science along with an essential accident, e.g., 'Every line standing upon a line...' Here line (species of magnitude) is taken with the accident 'standing upon a line.' 
5) The subject can be an essential accident of the subject of the science, e.g., 'Every triangle...'—triangle is an essential accident (arad dhati) of magnitude. 
6) The subject can be a species of an essential accident, e.g., 'Every isosceles triangle...'—isosceles triangle is a species of triangle, itself an accident of magnitude.

After listing these, the author summarizes that the subjects of problems are either the subject of the science, its parts (i.e., species or individuals), its essential accidents, or its particulars. The predicates of problems are the essential accidents of the subject of the science. They must be external to the subject (not part of its essence), because if the predicate were a part of the subject, then the proposition would be self-evident (since parts are necessarily attributed to the whole), and thus it would not require demonstration; but problems are precisely those propositions that require demonstration.
