The Three Layers of the Qutbi: Matn, Sharh, Hashiya
Editor introduces the book as a collaborative work of three logicians.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the roles of the matn, sharh, and hashiya authors in a composite commentary and trace the chain of scholarship connecting them.

Core Idea
This book is a three-layered masterpiece: the matn by Katibi, expanded by his student's student, and refined by that student's student.

In the Islamic logical tradition, it is common for a foundational text (matn) to receive a commentary (sharh) and then a super-commentary (hāshiya). This lesson introduces you to one of the most celebrated examples: the Qutbi, a collaborative work by three towering logicians. Understanding how these layers build on each other is key to navigating advanced texts in logic and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path

The editor mentions al-Razi was a student of al-Katibi's student (one intermediary), and al-Jurjani was al-Razi's student.
2
2. Understand the chain of transmission
Draw the scholarly chain: al-Katibi taught a student, who taught al-Razi; al-Razi then taught al-Jurjani. This illustrates how knowledge is passed and refined across generations.
The chain is: al-Katibi → his student → al-Razi → al-Jurjani.
3
3. Grasp the significance of the composite work
The editor says the book became 'the focus of teachers' attention and the study of researchers.' Why? Because each layer adds precision and depth, making it a comprehensive reference for later scholars. Composite works like this are common in Islamic logic.
The three authors are described as 'known for precision and subtlety of view.'

And on this commentary there is a gloss by Shaykh Muhammad al-Badakhshi who died in 922, and by al-Mawla Muhammad ibn Hamza al-Fanari who died in 834, mentioned by al-Majdi. And it was commented on by al-Mawla 'Ala' al-Din 'Ali ibn Muhammad known as Musannifak in Persian, who died in 871... and by Jalal al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Mahalli who died in 864 but did not complete it, and Ahmad ibn 'Uthman al-Turkumani al-Juzjani who died in 844, and Abu Muhammad Zayn al-Din 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Abi Bakr ibn al-'Ayni who died in 891. And among its glosses is al-Qamariyya... he named it thus because the text and commentary are encompassed in one reality. And it was commented on by Muhammad ibn Musa al-Busnawi who died in 1045... and it is an integrated commentary. And on al-Qutb's commentary there is a gloss by our master al-Fadil al-Samarqandi, from the scholars of the time of Sultan Husayn - thus in Habib al-Siyar - and by al-Mawlana 'Isam al-Din Dawud who died in the fortress of Shadman...' - end of al-Jalabi's words. And among the commentaries he did not mention is that of al-'Allama al-Hilli, which he named 'al-Qawa'id al-Jaliyya fi Sharh al-Risala al-Shamsiyya'. Method of work in this edition: I relied for printing this book on two editions: the edition of Dar Ihya' al-Kutub al-'Arabiyya in Egypt - Cairo, and the Bulaq edition, organized with several glosses, and therefore it was called 'Shuruh al-Shamsiyya'. Then I added to the book headings to help reviewers reach their goals at the beginning of each chapter, and an index of headings beneficial to viewers, God willing. So I ask the esteemed readers to correct any oversight they see in it, and to pray for themselves and for me, that God grants us success to His pleasure through His bounty and generosity. He is the best supporter and helper.
