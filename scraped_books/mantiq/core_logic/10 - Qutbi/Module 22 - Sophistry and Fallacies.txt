Imaginary Propositions (Wahmiyyāt) in Sophistry
Author defines imaginary premises used in sophistry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginary propositions (wahmiyyāt) and explain why the estimative faculty's judgments are only false in non-sensible matters.

Core Idea
Sophistry thrives not on sense deception but on the wahm's false judgments about the unseen.

In this lesson, we explore the fourth art of logic: sophistry. The author introduces its distinctive premises, called 'imaginary propositions' (wahmiyyāt), which are false judgments made by the estimative faculty about non-sensible matters. This definition may seem surprising because we often think of the imagination as unreliable in general, but the author clarifies that the estimative faculty can correctly judge sensible objects; its error occurs only when it ventures beyond the senses. Understanding this distinction is key to recognizing sophistical arguments in theological and philosophical debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus of wahmiyyāt
Find the broader category to which wahmiyyāt belong. The text says 'قضايا' (propositions). So the genus is proposition (qadiyya).
This is the first step in any definition analysis: locate what kind of thing it is.
2
Extract the differentia
What distinguishes wahmiyyāt from other propositions? The definition states they are false (كاذبة) and judged by the estimative faculty (يحكم بها الوهم) specifically in non-sensible matters (في أمور غير محسوسة).
The differentia has two parts: falsity and the involvement of wahm in non-sensible matters.
3
Understand the restriction to non-sensible matters
The author explicitly restricts the definition to non-sensible matters and gives the reason: judgment of wahm about sensibles is not false. He provides the example of judging a beautiful woman as beautiful – that judgment is correct.
This restriction is crucial: it means wahmiyyāt are not simply any false judgment, but only those about matters beyond sense perception.
4
Examine the nature of wahm
The text describes wahm as a physical power (قوة جسمانية) that perceives particular meanings (الجزئيات المنتزعة) from sensibles. It is subordinate to the senses (تابعة للحس), so its judgments about sensibles follow sense data and are generally correct.
This explanation supports why wahm's judgments on sensibles are reliable, but on non-sensibles they are prone to error.
5
Connect to sophistry
Although the text is cut off, the passage introduces wahmiyyāt as 'among them' (ومنها), referring to the five arts. The syllogisms composed of these imaginary premises constitute sophistry (safsata).
Recognizing wahmiyyāt is essential for identifying sophistical arguments.

Author defines safsata as compound syllogism from wahm and its purpose.

Safsata is a compound syllogism built on false premises from the estimative faculty (wahm), used to deceive and silence.

Definition and Types of Fallacy (Mughalata)
Author defines fallacy as flawed syllogism, listing formal and material types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material corruption in a syllogism and identify examples of material fallacies from the passage.

Core Idea
Fallacy is a corrupt syllogism—either in its form or in its matter.

In this passage, the author defines the term 'mughalata' (fallacy) and gives a systematic classification. Understanding this distinction is crucial before studying more complex fallacies. We will walk through the definition and the key types, focusing on the examples the author provides.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find where the author states: 'والمغالطة قياس يفسد صورته...' Note that the definition explicitly mentions two axes: form and matter.
2
Understand formal corruption
The author says form is corrupted if a condition regarding quantity, quality, or modality is violated. Recall that in a valid syllogism, these conditions must be met for the mood to be productive.
For example, a negative universal major premise with an affirmative universal minor does not produce a valid conclusion if conditions are violated.
3
Analyze material corruption examples
Read each material fallacy example. The first is circularity (petitio principii) through synonymy. The second uses false premise that sounds true due to wording (picture called horse). The third ignores that the subject must exist in affirmative propositions. The fourth confuses natural universal with universal quantifier. The fifth mixes mental and concrete.
Pay attention to the Arabic: 'لكون الألفاظ مترادفة' (synonymous words), 'كاذبة شبيهة بالصادقة' (false resembling truth).
4
Identify the user labels
The last part says the user is called 'sufasta'i' (sophist) when debating a philosopher, and 'mushaghibi' (quarrelsome) when debating a dialectician. This shows that fallacy is context-sensitive.

Commentary
This passage defines 'mughalata' (fallacy) and classifies its types. The definition states that a fallacy is a syllogism that is corrupt either in form or in matter. Formal corruption occurs when a condition regarding quantity (universal/particular), quality (affirmative/negative), or modality (necessity/possibility) is violated. Material corruption includes: (1) circularity (petitio principii) where a premise and the conclusion are synonymous; (2) false premises that resemble truth in wording (e.g., calling a picture a horse); (3) false premises resembling truth in meaning (e.g., ignoring that the subject must exist in an affirmative proposition); (4) confusing the natural universal with the universal quantifier; (5) conflating mental and concrete realities. The author then warns the reader to be careful of these fallacies and distinguishes between the sophist (who debates a philosopher) and the quarrelsome (who debates a dialectician).

Look at the two main categories: corruption from the side of form (sura) and from the side of matter (madda). Formal corruption concerns the structure of the syllogism (quantity, quality, modality). Material corruption concerns the truth or justification of the premises.

The passage opens with a crisp definition: 'al-mughalatah qiyas fasid.' This is the foundation. You must understand that a syllogism can fail in two fundamentally different ways: either the shape of the argument is wrong, or the material (premises) is defective. Commentary
This passage from the Qutbi commentary on the Shamsiyya defines fallacy (mughalatah) and classifies it into formal and material. The passage begins by giving examples of material fallacies that result from ignoring certain conditions: substituting a natural proposition for a universal, taking mental things for external, etc. The author warns the reader to be cautious. Then the formal definition is given: 'Fallacy is a flawed syllogism, either in form or in matter.' Formal fallacies occur when the syllogism violates a condition of productivity concerning quantity, quality, or modality — e.g., in the first figure, the major premise must be universal, the minor affirmative, etc. If the major is particular or the minor negative, the syllogism is invalid. Material fallacies are of two types: (1) begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlub), where the conclusion is the same as a premise, as in 'every man is human, every human is laughling, so every man is laughling' — the premise 'every human is laughling' is essentially the same as the conclusion. (2) Having false premises that resemble true ones. This resemblance can be in form or meaning. Resemblance in form: e.g., applying the syllogism to a picture of a horse: 'This is a horse, every horse neighs, so this neighs' — the error is that the picture is not a real horse. Resemblance in meaning: includes several cases: Lack of existence of the subject in the premises, e.g., 'Every man-and-horse is a man, every man-and-horse is a horse, so some man is a horse' — the subject 'man-and-horse' does not exist. Substituting a natural proposition for a universal, e.g., 'Man is animal, animal is genus' — this is false because 'animal is genus' is not universal. Taking mental things for external, e.g., 'Occurrence is occurring, everything occurring has occurrence, so occurrence has occurrence' — tautology. Taking external for mental, e.g., 'Substance exists in the mind, everything existing in the mind is an accident, so substance is an accident' — substance is not an accident in external reality. The author then notes that the case of substituting natural for universal might be considered a formal fallacy rather than material, because the error is in the lack of universality, which is a formal condition. Finally, those who use fallacies are called 'sophists' if they use them against philosophers, and 'mushaghibi' if they use them against dialecticians.

Material Fallacies — Begging the Question and False Premises
Author details material fallacies: begging the question and false premises resembling true ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify begging the question and material fallacies due to false premises resembling true ones in form or meaning.

Core Idea
Material fallacies deceive by using false premises that look true or by assuming the conclusion in the premises.

After covering formal fallacies, the author explains material fallacies. These are not about the shape of the argument but about the truth or relevance of the premises. They include begging the question (musadara) and using false premises that resemble true ones. The latter has subtypes: resemblance in form (misapplying a syllogism to a metaphorical subject) and resemblance in meaning (non-existent subject, natural vs universal, mental vs external). This lesson unpacks these cases.

Guided Path
1
Recognize begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlub)
Look for the case where the conclusion is essentially identical to one of the premises. The author's example: 'Every man is human, every human is laughing, so every man is laughing.' The premise 'every human is laughing' is not obviously identical to the conclusion, but the author considers it circular because the conclusion is not independent. Actually, the example is weak; a better example from usul al-fiqh: 'The Quran is preserved, because it is the word of Allah, and the word of Allah is preserved' — the second premise may be equivalent to the conclusion.
In classical logic, musadara often refers to assuming the very point to be proven.
2
Identify false premises resembling true ones — resemblance in form
The author gives the example of a picture of a horse: 'This is a horse, every horse neighs, so this neighs.' The form is valid, but the minor premise is false because the picture is not a real horse. The error is in the material (false premise) but it looks like a valid syllogism.
3
Identify false premises resembling true ones — resemblance in meaning
This category includes: (a) non-existent subject: 'Every man-and-horse is a man, every man-and-horse is a horse, so some man is a horse.' The subject 'man-and-horse' does not exist, so the premises are about nothing, leading to a false conclusion. (b) Natural vs universal: 'Man is animal, animal is genus, so man is genus.' The major premise 'animal is genus' is not universal (generically true for some but not all animals), so the conclusion is false. (c) Mental vs external: e.g., 'Substance exists in the mind, everything existing in the mind is an accident, so substance is an accident.' This equivocates between mental existence and external existence.
The author notes that the natural/universal case may actually be a formal fallacy because the error is the lack of universality, which is a formal condition. This shows the interplay between the two types.

Commentary
This passage from the Qutbi commentary on the Shamsiyya defines fallacy (mughalatah) and classifies it into formal and material. The passage begins by giving examples of material fallacies that result from ignoring certain conditions: substituting a natural proposition for a universal, taking mental things for external, etc. The author warns the reader to be cautious. Then the formal definition is given: 'Fallacy is a flawed syllogism, either in form or in matter.' Formal fallacies occur when the syllogism violates a condition of productivity concerning quantity, quality, or modality — e.g., in the first figure, the major premise must be universal, the minor affirmative, etc. If the major is particular or the minor negative, the syllogism is invalid. Material fallacies are of two types: (1) begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlub), where the conclusion is the same as a premise, as in 'every man is human, every human is laughling, so every man is laughling' — the premise 'every human is laughling' is essentially the same as the conclusion. (2) Having false premises that resemble true ones. This resemblance can be in form or meaning. Resemblance in form: e.g., applying the syllogism to a picture of a horse: 'This is a horse, every horse neighs, so this neighs' — the error is that the picture is not a real horse. Resemblance in meaning: includes several cases: Lack of existence of the subject in the premises, e.g., 'Every man-and-horse is a man, every man-and-horse is a horse, so some man is a horse' — the subject 'man-and-horse' does not exist. Substituting a natural proposition for a universal, e.g., 'Man is animal, animal is genus' — this is false because 'animal is genus' is not universal. Taking mental things for external, e.g., 'Occurrence is occurring, everything occurring has occurrence, so occurrence has occurrence' — tautology. Taking external for mental, e.g., 'Substance exists in the mind, everything existing in the mind is an accident, so substance is an accident' — substance is not an accident in external reality. The author then notes that the case of substituting natural for universal might be considered a formal fallacy rather than material, because the error is in the lack of universality, which is a formal condition. Finally, those who use fallacies are called 'sophists' if they use them against philosophers, and 'mushaghibi' if they use them against dialecticians.

Identifying Four Types of Syllogistic Fallacies
Author lists four fallacy types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify fallacies of non‑existent subject, natural‑for‑universal, mental‑for‑external, and external‑for‑mental in categorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires non‑empty terms and correct quantification — confusion here produces fallacy.

The author lists four common fallacies that violate basic conditions for a valid categorical syllogism. Each fallacy arises from a specific error: the subject term has no real existence, a natural proposition is mistaken for a universal one, mental considerations are confused with external realities, or external realities are mistaken for mental ones. By learning to spot these errors, you will strengthen your ability to evaluate syllogistic arguments in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Spot the non‑existent subject fallacy
Look for a syllogism where the subject term of the premises does not refer to anything that actually exists. The example given is 'Every human‑and‑horse is a human; every human‑and‑horse is a horse; therefore some human is a horse'. The error is that 'human‑and‑horse' is not a real thing, so the premises are empty.
This is also known as the fallacy of vacuous terms.
2
Spot the natural‑for‑universal fallacy
Identify when a natural proposition (e.g., 'Human is an animal') is treated as if it were a universal proposition with distributed subject. The conclusion 'Human is a genus' is false because the major premise 'Animal is a genus' is not universal — it would need to be 'Every animal is a genus' (which is false) for the syllogism to be valid.
The natural proposition is not quantified; it states a fact about the essence, not about all individuals.
3
Spot the mental‑for‑external fallacy
Find a syllogism that treats a mental consideration (a concept about a concept) as if it were an external reality. Example: 'Origination is originated; everything originated has origination; therefore origination has origination' — this leads to an infinite regress because 'origination' is a mental attribute, not an external entity.
This fallacy confuses categories: it treats an accident (origination) as if it were a substance.
4
Spot the external‑for‑mental fallacy
Find a syllogism that takes an external reality and treats it as if it existed only in the mind. Example: 'Substance exists in the mind; everything existing in the mind inheres in the mind; what inheres in the mind is an accident; therefore substance is an accident'. The error is that 'existing in the mind' is predicated of substance only in a certain respect, not absolutely.
This shows the importance of qualifying propositions (secundum quid).

The Material vs Formal Classification of Fallacies and the Sophist‑Disputer Distinction
Author classifies the natural-for-universal fallacy as formal and defines sophist/disputer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given fallacy is material or formal based on the author's criteria, and distinguish between a sophist and a disputer by their opponent.

Core Idea
A fallacy's classification depends on whether the error is in matter or form — and the user's intent defines whether they are a sophist or disputer.

The author clarifies that the natural‑for‑universal fallacy, though sometimes considered material, is actually formal because the flaw lies in violating the condition of universality, which is a formal requirement for syllogistic validity. He then defines two types of fallacy users: the sophist, who targets philosophers with deceptive arguments, and the disputer, who targets dialecticians. This lesson sharpens your ability to classify fallacies and understand the motives of those who employ them.

Guided Path
1
Understand the author's point about the natural fallacy
The author states that placing a natural proposition in place of a universal is not a material fallacy (فساد المادة) but a formal one (فساد الصورة). The reason: the only defect is the absence of universality, which is a condition of the syllogism's form. If the major premise were universal, the syllogism would be valid; thus the error is in the form, not the content.
This shows that even seemingly material errors can be traced to formal conditions.
2
Compare material vs formal fallacies
A material fallacy arises from false or meaningless content (e.g., a false premise). A formal fallacy arises from an invalid logical structure (e.g., undistributed middle, violation of figure rules). The author's example is formal because the flaw — lack of universality — is a structural condition.
In Islamic logic, formal fallacies are often considered more fundamental because they violate the very rules of inference.
3
Define sophist vs disputer
The author says that whoever uses a fallacy against a philosopher (حكيم) is called a sophist (سوفسطائي), and whoever uses it against a dialectician (جدلي) is called a disputer (مشاغبي). The distinction is based on the opponent: philosophers seek demonstrative certainty (burhan), while dialecticians aim for dialectical persuasion (jadal). A sophist deliberately deceives the seeker of truth; a disputer may use fallacies to test or challenge in debate.
This classification is important in adab al‑bahth (the ethics of debate) to identify unethical argumentation.
