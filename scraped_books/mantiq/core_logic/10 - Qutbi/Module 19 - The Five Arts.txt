Rhetorical Syllogism: Definition and Purpose
Defines rhetorical syllogism and its purpose

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rhetorical syllogism (khataba) and explain its purpose in motivating moral and religious action.

Core Idea
Rhetorical syllogism persuades toward good, not toward certainty.

In the Islamic logical tradition, rhetoric (khataba) is one of the five arts, distinguished by its material and aim. Unlike demonstrative syllogisms that yield certainty, rhetorical syllogisms use persuasive premises (muqni'at) to move the listener toward moral refinement and religious commitment. This lesson unpacks the concise definition from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The text defines 'khataba' (rhetoric). Note that it is first called a 'syllogism' (qiyas), placing it within the genus of logical arguments.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'syllogism' (qiyas). The differentia has two parts: the premises are persuasive (muqni'at), and the purpose is to make the listener desire moral and religious benefit.
3
Understand the purpose clause
The phrase 'targhibu al-sami'i' indicates an emotional and motivational aim, not intellectual certainty. This distinguishes rhetoric from dialectic (jadal) and demonstration (burhan).
In Islamic preaching (khutbah), the goal is similarly to urge good deeds and piety.

This aligns with the Aristotelian tradition where rhetoric deals with probable arguments and emotional appeal.

Imaginative Premises and Poetic Syllogism
Defines imaginative premises and the poetic syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative premises (mukhayyilat) and explain how poetry functions as a syllogism in the Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
Poetry is not just emotion—it is a logical syllogism built on imaginative premises.

In the Islamic logical tradition, not all premises aim at certainty. Imaginative premises (mukhayyilat) are propositions that affect the soul emotionally—expanding or contracting it—without claiming truth. When such premises are combined into a syllogism, the result is poetry (shi'r). Its purpose is to move the soul toward desire or aversion, often enhanced by meter and sound. This lesson unpacks this definition and its place in the five arts of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate the Arabic terms 'مخيلات' and 'شعر' in the passage. These are the two central concepts. The first is a type of premise, the second a type of syllogism.
2
Understand the effect of imaginative premises
The passage says these premises affect the soul with 'قبض' (contraction) and 'بسط' (expansion). Think of these as emotional responses like fear/anxiety (qabd) or joy/relief (bast). They do not require truth.
Example: 'الخمر ياقوتة سائلة' (Wine is a flowing ruby) creates a feeling of beauty, not a factual claim.
3
Recognise the syllogistic nature of poetry
Poetry (shi'r) is defined as 'القياس المؤلف منها' – the syllogism composed of imaginative premises. This means poetry has a logical structure, not just random verses. The purpose is 'التأثير في النفس بالترغيب والتنفير' (affecting the soul by desire and aversion).
In the five arts, poetry stands alongside demonstrative, dialectical, sophistical, and rhetorical syllogisms.
4
Note the aids: meter and sound
The passage adds 'ويروجه الوزن والصوت الطيب' – meter (wazn) and pleasant sound (sawt tayyib) promote the effect. These are not essential to the logical definition but enhance the emotional impact.
This shows that the logician acknowledges the role of artistic form in achieving the purpose of poetry.

Its goal is psychological: to arouse desire or aversion. Meter (wazn) and pleasant sound (sawt tayyib) enhance the effect. This mirrors the classical classification where poetry is a logical art, not mere entertainment.

Wahmiyyat: The False Premises of the Estimation
Defines wahmi premises and gives examples of their falsity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify wahmi premises and explain their falsity using intellect and religious law.

Core Idea
Wahmi premises feel necessary but contradict revealed reality.

In this passage, Qutb al-Din al-Razi introduces wahmiyyat, a type of false premise that the estimative faculty (wahm) accepts regarding imperceptible matters. These premises often appear self-evident, but reason and revelation expose their falsity. This lesson teaches you to detect such premises and understand why they are not among the primary axioms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate how the author defines wahmiyyat: they are false propositions that the estimation judges about imperceptible things.
2
Recognize the examples
Note the two examples: 'Every existent is pointed to' and 'Beyond the world is infinite space.' These illustrate typical wahmi premises.
3
Understand the epistemic status
The author states that without intellect and religious law, these would be considered primary (awwaliyyat). This highlights their deceptive apparent necessity.
4
See the refutation method
The falsity becomes known when the wahm aligns with intellect in premises that yield a conclusion contradicting the original wahm judgment. This shows how to critically evaluate such premises.

Conceded Premises (Musallamat) and the Art of Dialectic (Jadal)
Defines musallamat and jadal, with an example from fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conceded premises in a dialectical argument and explain how they function in jadal, using the example of zakat on jewelry.

Core Idea
Conceded premises are the backbone of dialectic: accepted for argument, not for truth.

In this passage, the author defines a key type of premise: the musallamah, or conceded premise. These are propositions that the opponent accepts, either by mutual agreement or by the standards of a discipline. The author then shows how such premises form the basis of dialectic (jadal), a syllogistic art aimed at compelling an opponent or convincing a non-specialist. Understanding musallamat and jadal is essential for engaging in formal debates in Islamic sciences, where arguments often rest on premises that are not absolutely certain but are accepted within a field.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of musallamat
Find the sentence: 'وَمِنْهَا الْمُسَلَّمَاتُ، وَهِيَ قَضَايَا تُسَلَّمُ مِنَ الْخَصْمِ وَيُبْنَى عَلَيْهَا الْكَلَامُ لِدَفْعِهِ'. Note that the definition includes a purpose: to rebut the opponent.
2
Classify the types of musallamat
The author mentions two kinds: those conceded between the two debaters, and those conceded among people of knowledge (e.g., within a discipline). Identify these in the text.
Look for 'سَوَاءٌ كَانَتْ مُسَلَّمَةً فِيمَا بَيْنَهُمَا خَاصَّةً أَوْ بَيْنَ أَهْلِ الْعِلْمِ'
3
Examine the example from fiqh
The jurist uses a hadith about zakat on jewelry. The opponent rejects it as a solitary hadith. How does the jurist respond? He appeals to usul al-fiqh as a conceded discipline. Trace how this illustrates a musallamah.
The hadith is taken as conceded in usul al-fiqh, even if not accepted by the opponent initially.
4
Understand the definition of jadal
The syllogism composed of mashhurat and musallamat is called jadal. Note its purpose: إِلْزَامُ الْخَصْمِ وَإِقْنَاعُ مَنْ هُوَ قَاصِرٌ عَنْ إِدْرَاكِ مُقَدِّمَاتِ الْبُرْهَانِ. Compare with demonstration (burhan).
Jadal aims to compel, not to produce certainty.

Conjectural Propositions and the Rhetorical Syllogism
Defines conjectural propositions and rhetorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conjectural propositions and explain how they differ from certain propositions in rhetorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades without certainty; conjectural propositions are probable but not necessary.

In the five arts of logic, rhetoric occupies the realm of persuasion rather than certainty. This passage defines conjectural propositions (maznunat) and shows how they form the backbone of rhetorical syllogisms. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the epistemic strength of arguments used in preaching and oratory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of maznunat

This distinguishes conjectural from certain propositions.
2
Examine the example
The example: 'فلان يطوف بالليل وكل من يطوف بالليل فهو سارق، ففلان سارق'. Recognize that both premises are conjectural: the first is an observation that could be mistaken, the second is a generalization that is not universally true.
3
Understand the composition of rhetorical syllogism
The rhetorical syllogism is made of accepted premises (muqbulaat) and conjectural premises (maznunat). This distinguishes it from demonstrative syllogisms that use certain premises.
4
Grasp the purpose
The purpose is to motivate people (ترغيب) in worldly and religious matters, as done by preachers. This is persuasion, not demonstration.
Rhetoric aims at action, not certainty.

Poetry moves the soul through imagination, not truth.

1
Recall the Five Arts
Remember that the five arts differ in their purpose. Poetics aims at emotional effect (targhib/tarhib), not certainty. This sets the context for understanding imaginative propositions.
2
Define Imaginative Propositions
In the Arabic passage, find the term 'المخيلات'. Note that they are propositions that create an image (takhayyul) in the soul, causing expansion (bast) or contraction (qabd), leading to desire or aversion.
The effect is immediate emotional response, not rational assent.
3
Analyze the Examples
Examine the two examples: 'Wine is flowing ruby' and 'Honey is bitter and vomit-inducing'. Notice how each uses a vivid image—not truth—to move the soul. The first expands the soul (desire), the second contracts it (aversion).
These are not true or false statements; they are effective or ineffective.
4
Understand the Poetic Syllogism
The passage states that a syllogism composed of imaginative premises is called 'poetry' (shi'r). Its purpose is emotional reaction (infi'al al-nafs) of desire or fear. This syllogism is not about logical validity but about producing a psychological effect.
The effect is strengthened by meter (wazn) and beautiful voice (sawt tayyib).
5
Distinguish from Other Arts
Contrast poetic syllogisms with demonstrative (burhan) or dialectical (jadal) arguments. Poetic syllogisms do not aim at truth; they aim at moving the soul. This distinction helps you recognise when an argument is being used for persuasion vs. emotional influence.
In Islamic contexts, poetry and oratory often use imaginative propositions for moral or religious exhortation.

Illusory Propositions and Sophistry
Defines illusory propositions, estimation's errors, and sophistry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify illusory propositions (wahmiyyat) and explain why the faculty of estimation (wahm) errs in non-sensible matters, and define sophistry (safsata).

Core Idea
Estimation (wahm) reliably judges sensibles but dangerously mistakes non-sensibles for realities.

This passage introduces a class of false propositions that arise from the human faculty of estimation (wahm). Unlike deliberate deception, these errors are natural, yet they can be corrected by reason and revelation. Understanding them is crucial for guarding against sophistry in theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the faculty described
Locate where the passage defines 'al-wahm' (estimation). Note its function: it perceives particular meanings abstracted from sensibles. Recognize that it is reliable for sensible judgments but prone to error when applied to non-sensibles.
The passage calls wahm a 'corporeal faculty' (qūwa jismāniyya).
2
Extract examples of wahmiyyat
Find the concrete examples of illusory judgments: 'every existent is pointed-to' and 'beyond the world there is infinite space'. Understand why these are false: they import sensible categories (pointedness, spatial extension) into non-sensible realities.
These examples are taken from kalām discussions about God's attributes and the universe.
3
Understand reliability condition
Notice the restriction: wahm judges correctly only about sensibles. When it extends its judgments to non-sensibles, it becomes false. This is because wahm is derived from sense perception and remains bound to its limitations.
The passage explicitly says 'if it judges sensibles, its judgment is correct'.
4
Analyze the refutation example
Read the syllogism: 'The dead is an inanimate body; an inanimate body is not feared; therefore the dead is not feared.' See how reason (via a categorical syllogism) contradicts the initial wahm judgment (fear the dead). Observe that when the conclusion is reached, wahm itself recoils.
This shows that even wahm can be made to recognize its error when confronted with a valid rational argument.
5
Define sophistry and its purpose
The passage defines 'safsata' (sophistry) as a syllogism composed of illusory premises (wahmiyyat). Its aim is to mislead and silence the opponent. The greatest benefit of studying it is to guard against being deceived.
This aligns with the early Islamic concern to protect the faith from deceptive arguments.

Commentary
This passage defines the art of sophistry. It begins with 'illusory propositions' (wahmiyyat)—false propositions rendered by the faculty of estimation (wahm) concerning non-sensible matters. The estimation is a corporeal faculty that perceives particular meanings extracted from sensibles; it is reliable for sensible judgments but errs when applied to non-sensible matters (e.g., claiming everything that exists is 'pointed to' or that there is infinite space beyond the world). These errors become so ingrained that they may be confused with primary principles. Reason and divine law (shar') together falsify these illusions. The passage then gives a concrete syllogism: estimation says 'Fear the dead' but reason says 'The dead is an inanimate body; an inanimate body is not feared; so the dead is not feared.' When both reach the conclusion, estimation withdraws its judgment. The syllogism composed of such false premises is sophistry, intended to mislead and silence the opponent. Learning sophistry is essential to guard against it.

Definition of Fallacy and Its Types
Defines fallacy and gives examples of formal and material corruption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify causes of fallacy (mughalata) as formal or material corruption based on Qutbi's definition.

Core Idea
Fallacy is a syllogism corrupted in form or material, rendering it unproductive.

In this passage, al-Qutbi defines fallacy (mughalata) as a syllogism whose form or material is corrupted. He provides examples: formal corruption through synonymous terms, and material corruption through false premises resembling truth in wording or meaning. Understanding these categories helps in diagnosing flawed arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find the Arabic sentence that defines mughalata. Note that it says 'قياس يفسد صورته بأن لا يكون على هيأة منتجة لاختلال شرط معتبر بحسب الكمّيّة أو الكيفية أو الجهة أو مادته'.
The definition covers both form and material.
2
Distinguish form vs material
Identify that corruption can be in form (violation of conditions of quantity, quality, modality) or in material (premise content).
The passage gives examples of both.
3
Analyze the synonymy example
Examine the syllogism: 'Every human is a person (basar), and every person is a laugher (dhahhak), so every human is a laugher.' Why is it non-productive? Because the terms are synonymous, making the argument circular – this is a formal corruption.
Synonymous premises mean the syllogism fails to progress.
4
Analyze the horse picture example
The syllogism 'This is a horse, every horse neighs, therefore this neighs' has a false minor premise (it's a picture, not a real horse). This is material corruption – the premise is false but resembles truth in wording.
The form is valid, but the material is unsound.

Commentary
This passage begins the discussion of fallacy (mughalata). It defines fallacy as a syllogism whose form is corrupted—it is not productive because some condition of validity is violated in quantity (kammiyya), quality (kayfiyya), modality (jiha), or material (madda). Three types are mentioned: First, corruption due to synonymous terms: the example 'Every human is a human (basar) and every human (basar) is a laugher' is actually synonymous, so it's circular. Second, corruption due to false premises that look true verbally: the example of a picture of a horse: 'This is a horse' (false) and 'Every horse neighs' (true) yields 'This neighs', which is false. Third, corruption in meaning: failing to consider the existence of the subject in an affirmative proposition. The passage is cut off, but this refers to fallacies like 'affirming the consequent' or assuming the subject exists when it does not.

Key Terms
المغالطة (al-mughalata): A syllogism whose form or material is corrupted so it is not productive. [Note: In some texts, mughalata is synonymous with safsata; here they are distinct arts.]
المادة (al-madda): The material of a syllogism, i.e., the content of premises, as opposed to form. [Note: Stable term across logic texts.]
