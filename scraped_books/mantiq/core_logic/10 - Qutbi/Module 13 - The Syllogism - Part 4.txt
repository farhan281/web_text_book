Valid and Invalid Inferences in Conjunctive Conditional Syllogisms
Explains valid and invalid inferences in conjunctive conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish valid from invalid inferences in a conjunctive conditional syllogism and justify each using the concept of luzūm.

Core Idea
Two valid moves, two invalid: know which, or your reasoning collapses.

In Islamic logic, the conjunctive conditional syllogism (qiyās istithnā'ī muttasil) is a powerful tool for drawing conclusions from conditional statements. The passage before you lays out the two valid inference patterns—affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent—and the two invalid ones—affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent. By the end, you will be able to apply these rules to any conditional argument, whether in kalam or fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
In a conjunctive conditional, find the 'if' part (al-muqaddam/al-malzūm) and the 'then' part (al-tālī/al-lāzim). The passage uses these terms implicitly; for example, 'If A then B' where A is the antecedent and B the consequent.
2
Recognise the two valid inference patterns
The passage states: (1) affirming exactly the antecedent yields exactly the consequent (modus ponens), and (2) denying exactly the consequent yields denial of the antecedent (modus tollens). It justifies each by a reductio: if the consequent were absent when the antecedent holds, the implication (luzūm) would break.
3
Understand the justification via luzūm
The relation of luzūm (necessary implication) means that whenever the antecedent exists, the consequent must exist; and whenever the consequent is absent, the antecedent must be absent. This is the foundation of the two valid forms.
4
Spot the two invalid patterns
The passage explicitly says the converse is not valid: affirming the consequent does not yield the antecedent, and denying the antecedent does not yield the negation of the consequent. This is because the consequent may be broader (a'amm) than the antecedent; there could be other reasons for the consequent to hold.
5
Apply the rule to a real Islamic example
Consider the kalam argument: 'If the world is temporally originated, it has a creator.' Affirming temporality proves a creator (valid). Denying a creator proves the world is not temporally originated (valid). But affirming a creator does not prove temporality, nor does denying temporality disprove a creator (both invalid).

Rules of Disjunctive Conditional Syllogisms
Classifies three types of disjunctive conditionals and states their valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the type of a disjunctive proposition and determine which inferences are valid for that type.

Core Idea
In disjunctive logic, the type of 'or' determines which conclusions follow.

This passage from al-Qutbi classifies disjunctive conditional propositions into three types and specifies the syllogistic rules for each. You will learn to distinguish the exclusive disjunction from man'at al-jam' and man'at al-khuluw, and apply the correct inference patterns.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction type
Examine the relationship between the two disjuncts. Determine if they can both be true, both false, or neither. This corresponds to exclusive (neither both true nor both false), man'at al-jam' (both false allowed, both true not allowed), or man'at al-khuluw (both true allowed, both false not allowed).
The examples in the passage illustrate each type.
2
Apply the first inference rule
For exclusive and man'at al-jam', affirming one disjunct allows you to deny the other. Check the passage: 'استثناء عين أي جزء كان نقيض الآخر' (excluding exactly any part produces the contradiction of the other).
This inference is valid because the two cannot be true together.
3
Apply the second inference rule
For exclusive and man'at al-khuluw, denying one disjunct allows you to affirm the other. The passage states: 'استثناء نقيض أي جزء كان عين الآخر' (excluding the contradiction of any part produces exactly the other).
This inference is valid because the two cannot be false together.
4
Test with the passage examples
Work through the even/odd example for exclusive disjunction, the wood/stone example for man'at al-jam', and the 'not wood not stone' example for man'at al-khuluw. Verify that only the prescribed inferences hold.
Notice the number of valid conclusions: four for exclusive, two for each of the others.
5
Recognize the number of valid conclusions
Exclusive disjunction yields four valid results (two from affirming each part, two from denying each part). Man'at al-jam' yields two (only from affirming). Man'at al-khuluw yields two (only from denying).
This is a quick check: if you count the valid inferences, you identify the type.

The Three Types of Disjunctive Syllogisms and Their Valid Inferences
Author distinguishes three types of disjunctive syllogisms and their valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the type of disjunctive proposition and determine which inferences (affirming or denying a part) are valid.

Core Idea
Not all disjunctions are equal—the logical behavior changes depending on whether the parts can be both true or both false.

In Islamic logic, disjunctive propositions are not all the same. They are classified into three types: exclusive (haqiqiyya), preventing conjunction (mani'at al-jam'), and preventing void (mani'at al-khulu). Each type has its own rules for valid inference. This passage from Quṭb al-Dīn al-Rāzī explains exactly which conclusions follow from each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of disjunction
Read the passage and list the three types: haqiqiyya (exclusive), mani'at al-jam' (preventing conjunction), and mani'at al-khulu (preventing void). Note their definitions in terms of truth possibilities.
2
For each type, determine the valid inferences
For haqiqiyya: both parts cannot be true nor both false → affirming one part yields negation of the other, and denying one yields affirmation of the other (four valid conclusions). For mani'at al-jam': parts cannot be true together but can be false together → only affirming one part yields negation of the other (two conclusions). For mani'at al-khulu: parts cannot be false together but can be true together → only denying one part yields affirmation of the other (two conclusions).
3
Apply to the examples given
Check each example: the even/odd example shows the four inferences for haqiqiyya. The tree/stone example shows the two inferences for mani'at al-jam' (affirming yields negation). The negated tree/stone example shows the two inferences for mani'at al-khulu (denying yields affirmation).
The last example uses negated terms; note the logical form.
4
Memorize the pattern
Create a mental table: haqiqiyya → full four, mani'at al-jam' → only 'affirm to negate', mani'at al-khulu → only 'deny to affirm'.

Compound Syllogisms: Definition and Two Types
Author defines compound syllogism and distinguishes two varieties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of compound syllogisms (mawsul and mafusul al-nata'ij) and construct a compound syllogism using Islamic premises.

Core Idea
A compound syllogism chains simple syllogisms to derive a conclusion from self-evident axioms.

In Islamic logic, not all arguments are single-step syllogisms; complex claims often require a chain of reasoning. This lesson introduces the compound syllogism (al-qiyas al-murakkab), which structures such chains. You will learn its definition, its two forms—connected conclusions (mawsul al-nata'ij) where intermediate steps are explicit, and disconnected conclusions (mafusul al-nata'ij) where they are compressed—and how it ultimately rests on self-evident principles (mabadi' badihiyya).

Guided Path
1
Identify the need for a compound syllogism
Recognize that when the premises of a syllogism are not self-evident, they themselves must be proved by further syllogisms, creating a chain that terminates in axioms.
2
Analyze the mawsul al-nata'ij example
Trace the author's example: from 'Every J is B' and 'Every B is D' conclude 'Every J is D'; then use that conclusion with 'Every D is A' to conclude 'Every J is A'; and so on. Note that each intermediate conclusion is explicitly stated and reused.
This explicit chaining makes the reasoning fully transparent.
3
Analyze the mafusul al-nata'ij example
Observe that in the second type, all premises are listed together ('Every J is B, Every B is D, Every D is A, Every A is H') and the final conclusion ('Every J is H') is drawn directly, without stating intermediate conclusions.
This form is more concise but hides the intermediate steps.
4
Compare the two types
Contrast mawsul (explicit, step-by-step) with mafusul (implicit, compressed). Consider when each is useful: mawsul for teaching or verification, mafusul for brevity.
5
Connect to self-evident principles
Understand that the chain must ultimately rest on axioms (mabadi' badihiyya) to guarantee certainty. Without this foundation, the compound syllogism would lead to an infinite regress.

Qiyas al-Khalf: Proving by Refuting the Contradictory
Author defines and illustrates Qiyas al-Khalf with a logical example.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Qiyas al-Khalf proves a claim by showing its opposite leads to an impossible conclusion.

The qiyas al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum) is a powerful method in Islamic logic, used to prove a proposition by refuting its contradictory. This lesson unpacks its structure through the author's example and explains why it is considered valid despite assuming the opposite. You will learn to recognize and construct such arguments, which appear in kalam and usul al-fiqh debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired conclusion (al-matlub)
In the example, the desired conclusion is 'not every C is B'. This is the proposition we want to prove.
The conclusion is often a universal negative or particular statement.
2
Assume its contradictory (naqid)
Assume the contradictory: 'it is false that not every C is B', which is equivalent to 'every C is B'. This is the assumption that will be shown to lead to absurdity.
The contradictory must be the exact opposite in truth value.
3
Combine with a known true premise
Take a true premise: 'every B is A'. Under the assumption, we have 'every C is B' and 'every B is A', so by categorical syllogism we deduce 'every C is A'.
This step uses a straightforward first-figure syllogism (Barbara).
4
Derive a contradiction
We know (or can prove) that 'not every C is A' is true. This contradicts the deduced 'every C is A'. Thus the assumption leads to an impossibility.
The contradiction is between the deduced proposition and an established truth.
5
Reject the assumption and affirm the desired conclusion
Since the assumption leads to a contradiction, it must be false. Therefore, its contradictory (the desired conclusion) is true: 'not every C is B'.
This is a classic modus tollens: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-Khalf proves a conclusion by showing its negation leads to an absurdity using two linked syllogisms.

This lesson breaks down the structure of qiyas al-khalf, a powerful logical tool used to prove statements by contradiction. The author demonstrates how to combine a conjunctive syllogism (conditional + categorical) with an exceptional syllogism (modus tollens) to derive the desired result. We will follow the step-by-step construction and then apply it to an Islamic theological example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion to be proven
Locate the statement the author wants to prove: 'not every B is C' (ليس كل (ج ب)). This is the desired result (al-matlab).
The conclusion is usually a categorical proposition.
2
Assume the contradictory and a true premise
Assume the negation of the conclusion: 'every B is C' (كل (ج ب)). Also, a true premise is given: 'every B is A' (كل (ب ا)). This premise is taken as known.
The true premise must be consistent with reality.
3
Construct the conjunctive syllogism
Combine the assumption (every B is C) and the true premise (every B is A) into a conditional: 'If (not(not every B is C) then every B is A)'. This is the product of the conjunctive syllogism (iqtirani).
The conjunctive syllogism here is: a conditional (if the contradictory is true, then the contradictory is true) plus a categorical (every B is A) yields a new conditional linking the assumption to a consequence.
4
Apply the exceptional syllogism (modus tollens)
Take the resulting conditional 'If (not(not every B is C)) then every B is A'. Then deny the consequent: 'not every B is A' (because 'every B is A' is impossible). By modus tollens, the antecedent is false: 'not(not every B is C)' is false, meaning 'not every B is C' is true.
The impossibility of the consequent is crucial; it must be established independently.
