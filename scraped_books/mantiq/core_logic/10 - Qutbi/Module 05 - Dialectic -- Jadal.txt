Resolving Circularity: The Omitted Genitive in Dialectic
Dialectical exchange: objector claims preface is part of logic; commentator refutes via omitted genitive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve a circular definition objection by identifying an omitted genitive construction (mudaf mahdhuf) in dialectical reasoning.

Core Idea
An omitted genitive can dissolve a circularity objection by shifting from science to book.

This passage presents a classic dialectical exchange: an objector argues that if the preface contains what must be known before logic, then it becomes part of logic, leading to an impossible circularity. The commentator refutes by positing an omitted genitive ('books of logic'), showing the preface is part of the book, not the science. This technique is common in Islamic logical and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the objector's claim: the preface must be part of logic because it contains what must be known in logic. Locate the Arabic phrase 'إن ما يجب أن يعلم في المنطق يكون جزء منه'.
2
Extract the circularity argument
The objector shows that if the preface is part of logic, then starting the preface is starting logic, but starting logic depends on the preface, leading to self-dependence (مَحال). Trace the conditional reasoning: لو كانت المقدمة جزءا منه لزم الدور.
3
Find the commentator's refutation
Look for the term 'مضاف محذوف' (omitted genitive). The commentator reinterprets 'fi al-mantiq' as 'fi kutub al-mantiq' (in the books of logic). This means the preface is part of the book, not the science, breaking the circle.
4
Understand the supporting evidence
The commentator argues that the matn's purpose is to describe the book's contents (the five universal parts), not the science itself. This shows the intended genitive was 'books'.
5
Assess the resolution
Check if both difficulties are removed: the preface is now part of the book, so it is not part of logic (addressing the objection), and no circularity arises because starting the preface is starting the book, not the science, which does not depend on itself.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange: an objection and its answer. The note at the end by Sharif clarifies that 'the five things' refers to complete compounds, removing any remaining issues.

Refuting the Composite Definition of Tasdiq
Objection and refutation on the definition of tasdiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible interpretations of tasdiq as a composite and evaluate the commentator's refutation of both.

Core Idea
The author's explicit statement does not rescue his division; it leads to absurdities on both interpretations.

In this passage, the commentator engages in a dialectical exchange (jadal) with an objector who points out that the author has explicitly defined tasdiq as a composite of perception and judgment, aligning with al-Razi. The commentator shows that this explicit statement does not salvage the division, because whether tasdiq is interpreted as the second division (perception accompanying judgment) or as the composite of the two, it results in absurd consequences. This lesson unpacks the logical moves in this refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's point
The objector says the author explicitly states tasdiq is the composite of perception and judgment, which matches al-Razi's view. Find this in the first Arabic sentence.
2
Analyze the first horn of the dilemma
The commentator considers tasdiq as the second division (perception accompanying judgment). Note why this fails: it corresponds to neither madhhab and is false in itself.
3
Analyze the second horn of the dilemma
If tasdiq is the composite of perception and judgment, then it is not a division of knowledge but a compound. Also, the conception of the subject together with judgment would also be such a composite, leading to absurdity.
This shows the definition fails under both interpretations.
4
Evaluate the refutation's strength
The commentator uses a dilemma (splitting the definition into two possibilities) and shows each leads to a contradiction or unacceptable consequence. This is a strong dialectical move.
Understand why this refutes the objector's appeal to the author's explicit wording.

Objection and Response on Infinite Regress in Knowledge Acquisition
Objection and response on infinite regress in knowledge acquisition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical exchange about infinite regress in knowledge acquisition and distinguish between preparatory means (mu'add) and efficient causes ('illa) in the context of simultaneous causation.

Core Idea
Not all infinite series are harmless: when prior knowledges coexist with the result, they cannot be merely preparatory.

This passage presents a classic dialectical move: the objector tries to save infinite regress by claiming the infinite steps are merely preparatory means that need not coexist. The response, however, shows that the prior sciences involved in reasoning are not preparatory—they are simultaneous causes or conditions. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments about causality and knowledge in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's challenge
The objector claims that infinite regress does not require all infinite steps to exist at once because they can be preparatory means (mu'addat) occurring over infinite past time. Find where the objector says 'the infinite things are preparatory means... it is not necessary that they coexist all at once.'
The objector's argument hinges on the concept of mu'add as non-simultaneous.
2
Extract the key distinction in the response
The responder distinguishes two types of 'infinite things': the actual sciences/knowledges (e.g., knowledge of premises) and the mental transitions (intiqalat) during reasoning. The sciences coexist with the final knowledge, while the transitions do not.
Coexistence (mujam'a) is the key: preparatory means cannot coexist with the result.
3
Apply the causation framework
The responder argues that prior sciences are either efficient causes (illa mujiba) or conditions (shart) for the result. Both require simultaneous existence. If they were merely preparatory, they would imply potentiality, not actuality.
Efficient cause necessitates the effect; condition is also simultaneous.
4
Assess the outcome of the dialectic
The responder refutes the objector by showing that the infinite regress cannot be dismissed as a series of non-simultaneous preparatory means. The prior sciences are causes that must coexist, so the objector's escape fails.
The objector's position is undermined; the original argument against infinite regress stands.

The Dialectical Refinement of Prior Sciences as Preparatory
Objection and response on the nature of prior sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objector's challenge against prior sciences as preparatory causes and evaluate the commentator's response distinguishing sciences from mental transitions.

Core Idea
Sciences are the battlefield, not the weapons; mental movements are the true preparatory causes.

In the previous lesson, prior sciences were presented as preparatory causes for obtaining knowledge. Now an objector challenges this by noting that prior sciences coexist with the conclusion—if they are preparatory, they should be incompatible with actuality. The commentator responds with a crucial refinement: the sciences themselves are not preparatory; rather, the mental transitions (intiqalat) that occur within them are the true preparatory causes. This dialectical exchange sharpens our understanding of how reasoning unfolds in the mind.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim under attack
The objector challenges the assertion (likely from earlier) that the prior sciences (ulum sabiqa) are preparatory causes (mu'iddat) for the required knowledge. Locate this claim in the objector's opening: 'the prior sciences are not mere preparatory causes.'
2
Analyze the objector's reasoning
The objector argues that if prior sciences were preparatory, they could not coexist with the result—knowledge of parts with the defined, premises with conclusion. Read the passage where he says 'they coexist with it' and 'the preparatory cause imposes readiness... so it is impossible to coexist with actual existence.'
The key principle: a preparatory cause (mu'iddat) implies potentiality, not actuality.
3
Find the objector's alternative conclusion
The objector concludes that the mental transitions (intiqalat) are the true preparatory causes and do not coexist. He then argues that the sciences must be either efficient causes or conditions, requiring all to be present simultaneously, leading to an impossible infinity.
This sets up the impossibility that the objector wants to prove.
4
Trace the commentator's distinction
The commentator agrees that intellectual movements are preparatory and cannot coexist. However, he distinguishes the sciences themselves as the 'loci' of these movements. The sciences can coexist with the result because they are not the preparatory causes—they are the material. He provides empirical evidence from complex syllogisms and geometry where premises are forgotten even while the conclusion is certain.
The commentator uses 'we find in ourselves' as experiential proof.
5
Evaluate the dialectical outcome
The objector's challenge is not dismissed but refined. The commentator admits the objection is pertinent and that the infinite things (sciences) can be called preparatory only insofar as they are the loci of the preparatory movements, allowing for successive rather than simultaneous presence.
This preserves the original claim about infinite things being preparatory while avoiding the impossibility.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical exchange: an objection (qila 'alayhi) against the earlier defense, and a response (wa ujiba). The objector argues that the prior sciences are not simply preparatory causes (mu'iddat) because they coexist with the result—knowledge of parts coexists with knowledge of the whole, premises with conclusion. The Three Types of Objection in Dialectical Debate
Author classifies the three ways an opponent can object to a proof in dialectical debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an opponent's objection in a jadal debate as man', naqd ijmail, or mu'arada by analyzing whether a specific premise is denied, whether the denial is general, and whether a witness (shahid) is required.

Core Idea
Objections in jadal are distinguished by their target: specific premise, overall soundness, or a counter-argument.

In dialectical debates (jadal), the opponent's goal is to undermine the proof. Al-Qutbi's commentary on the Shamsiyya identifies three distinct methods: denying a specific premise (man'), claiming a general flaw without specifics (naqd ijmail), and presenting an alternative proof (mu'arada). Recognizing which method is being used is crucial for crafting an effective response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's target
Determine whether the opponent focuses on a single premise (e.g., 'I deny that every intoxicant is forbidden') or attacks the proof as a whole ('Your proof is unsound'). Also check if the opponent brings a new proof or merely points out a flaw.
This step uses the criteria of premise specificity.
2
Check for requirement of a witness (shahid)
If the opponent makes a general claim without specifying the flawed premise, they must provide a witness (shahid) that shows the proof is defective. If the opponent denies a specific premise, they may optionally strengthen it with support (sanad) but do not require a witness.
The passage explicitly states that naqd ijmail requires a shahid.
3
Classify the objection
Based on the two criteria (specific vs. general attack, need for shahid), classify the objection as man'/naqd tafsili, naqd ijmail, or mu'arada. If the opponent provides a counter-argument without denying premises, it is mu'arada.
Mu'arada does not require denials; it presents a competing proof.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of the types of objections (i'tiradat) in dialectical debate (jadal). The author lists three forms an opponent (khasm) can take when faced with a proof (dalil). The first is 'man'' (denial), also called 'munaqada' or 'naqd tafsili', where the opponent singles out a specific premise (muqaddima mu'ayyana) and declares it false. For example, in a proof that 'every intoxicant is forbidden; every nabidh is intoxicant; therefore nabidh is forbidden', the opponent might deny 'every intoxicant is forbidden' by citing a hadith that permits certain intoxicants in small amounts. The second type is 'naqd ijmail' (total refutation), where the opponent claims that the proof is unsound overall without specifying which premise is wrong. This requires a witness (shahid) to indicate the flaw. The third type is 'mu'arada' (counterargument), where the opponent does not deny any premise but presents a whole new proof that contradicts the original claim. For instance, against the proof that nabidh is forbidden, one might bring a proof that the Prophet (peace be upon him) drank nabidh, thus arguing for its permissibility. The passage ends abruptly with 'wa in mana'a' (and if he denies), possibly introducing further subcategories, but the three main types are clearly delineated.

The Objection That Logic is Unnecessary: A Dialectical Analysis
Objection and response on the need for logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection against the need for logic and evaluate the response, including identification of circular reasoning (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul).

Core Idea
The objection that logic is either intuitive or acquired collapses because it presupposes the very logic it attempts to deny.

This passage presents a classic objection: if logic is intuitive, it is already known and thus unneeded; if acquired, its acquisition requires prior logic, leading to circularity or infinite regress. The response shows that the objection actually proves logic's necessity. In this lesson, we will dissect this dialectical exchange and understand how the commentator refines the arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's core argument
The objection states that logic must be either intuitive (badīhī) or acquired (kasbī). If intuitive, no need to learn it; if acquired, its acquisition requires prior logic, leading to dawr (circularity) or tasalsul (infinite regress). Hence, logic is not needed.
2
Isolate the response's structure
The response refutes by rephrasing: 'Logic is not something that is not needed – otherwise it would be either intuitive or acquired – both are false – so it must be needed.' This turns the objection into a proof of necessity.
The response uses a conditional argument: if logic were not needed, it would be intuitive or acquired; but both are false; therefore logic is needed.
3
Examine the alternative formulation of the objection
The commentator presents a refined objection: logic is acquired, so it is not needed for acquiring theoretical matters that themselves require logic. If logic were needed despite being acquired, dawr or tasalsul would follow in acquiring those theoretical matters.
This formulation shifts focus from logic's own acquisition to its role in acquiring other sciences.
4
Understand the author's intended purpose
The passage clarifies that the author aimed to show that logic is neither completely intuitive (so it needs written codification) nor completely unattainable (so it can be learned). Thus, logic is a necessary, learnable discipline that should be written in books.
The author's purpose is to justify the codification of logic as a science.
5
Identify the commentator's critique
The commentator notes that the author's formulation avoids explicitly mentioning 'theoretical matters' and that the well-known approach in this art is to present the objection to deny the need for logic. The commentator also offers an alternative direction for the author's argument.
The commentator critiques the author's phrasing but ultimately supports the conclusion.

The Four Non-Certain Premises in Jadal
Author enumerates four types of non-certain premises and their role in jadal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of non-certain premises (mashhurat, musallamat, maqbulat, maznunat) and explain their role in jadal (dialectic).

Core Idea
Non-certain premises are the building blocks of dialectic; they vary by community and are used to persuade and compel, not to prove with certainty.

This passage from Qutb al-Din al-Razi classifies premises that fall short of certainty. While demonstration (burhan) relies on yaqiniyyat, other logical arts—especially jadal—depend on premises that are widely accepted, conceded, received from authority, or merely probable. Understanding these will sharpen your ability to analyze arguments in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

Guided Path
1
Locate the four types
In the Arabic, find 'مشهورات', 'مسلمات', 'مقبولات', 'مظنونات'. Read the definitions given for each in the text.
The author says 'six' but only lists four; the other two may be elsewhere.
2
Analyse mashhurat
Note that mashhurat are socially conditioned: people accept them due to common benefit, customs, emotions. Compare to awwaliyat (self-evident): if unaided by society, we wouldn't judge them true. The text gives 'injustice is ugly' as an example of awwaliyat, not mashhurat.
Mashhurat can be true or false; they vary by people and profession.
3
Analyse musallamat
These are premises that an opponent concedes. They form the basis for jadal, which aims to refute the opponent using his own concessions. The example: jurists concede usul al-fiqh principles.
Jadal is defined as the syllogism composed of mashhurat and musallamat.
4
Analyse maqbulat and maznunat
Maqbulat are taken from a trustworthy authority (religious or intellectual). Maznunat are merely probable, like inferring a night-walker is a thief.
These are less systematic; they appear in rhetoric and practical reasoning.

Commentary
This passage classifies non-certain premises into four main types (though it says 'six', but then only mentions four; perhaps the other two are mentioned elsewhere or are implied). The first type is mashhurat (widely accepted opinions). These are judgments that people generally accept due to social benefit, emotion, custom, or law. The author distinguishes them from primary self-evident truths (awwaliyat): if a person were isolated from society and convention, they would not necessarily judge these as true. Mashhurat can be true or false and vary by community and profession. The second type is musallamat (conceded premises) – these are propositions that an opponent accepts in a debate, so they can be used to construct an argument against them. The example: jurists concede principles of usul al-fiqh. The syllogism formed from mashhurat and musallamat is called jadal (dialectic), aimed at persuading those who cannot grasp demonstration and at refuting opponents. The third type is maqbulat (received premises) – propositions taken from a trustworthy authority, especially in religious or intellectual matters. The fourth type is maznunat (probable premises) – judgments based on mere probability, like inferring that a night-wanderer is a thief. This classification is foundational to the Islamic logical theory of the five arts (sana'i' khams), where dialectic, rhetoric, and sophistry use such non-certain premises.

Primary Truths vs. Common Opinions in Dialectic
Author distinguishes common opinions from primary truths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish primary truths (awwaliyyāt) from common opinions (mashhūrāt) based on their epistemic certainty and variability.

Core Idea
Common opinions can be false; primary truths cannot.

In the opening of his discussion on dialectic, the author distinguishes two types of premises: those known necessarily by the intellect alone (awwaliyyāt) and those accepted widely but possibly false (mashhūrāt). This distinction is crucial for understanding the epistemic limits of dialectical arguments, which often rely on the latter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of premises
Locate the terms 'al-awwaliyyāt' (primary truths) and 'al-mashhūrāt' (common opinions) in the Arabic. Note that the author says a person free from external influences judges by awwaliyyāt, not mashhūrāt.
Primary truths are self-evident; common opinions are socially accepted.
2
Compare their certainty
The author states that mashhūrāt 'may be true or false' (قد تكون صادقة وقد تكون كاذبة), unlike awwaliyyāt which are always true. This is the key epistemic difference.
Mashhūrāt lack the necessity of primary truths.
3
Recognize variability
Mashhūrāt vary by community ('كل قوم مشهورات بحسب عاداتهم وآدابهم') and discipline ('كل أهل صناعة أيضاً مشهورات بحسب صناعاتهم'). Awwaliyyāt are universal.
This variability makes mashhūrāt useful in dialectic but not demonstration.
4
Reflect on implication for dialectic
Since dialectic aims to refute an opponent and convince those unable to grasp demonstration, it uses these variable and possibly false premises—mashhūrāt—as building blocks.
Dialectic does not require certain premises, only accepted ones.

Concessions and the Purpose of Dialectic
Author defines concessions and the purpose of dialectic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how concessions (musallamāt) function in dialectical arguments and state the purpose of dialectic (jadal).

Core Idea
Dialectic uses conceded premises to refute opponents and persuade the non-expert.

After introducing common opinions, the author defines concessions (musallamāt) as premises the opponent concedes, even if not universally true. He illustrates this with a fiqh debate about zakāh on jewelry. The syllogism built from these premises is called dialectic (jadal), aimed at refuting the opponent and convincing those who cannot follow demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of musallamāt
Find the definition: 'قضايا تُسلّم من الخصم ويُبنى عليها الكلام لدفعه' – propositions conceded by the opponent to build an argument against him. They are not necessarily true, only accepted for the debate.
Concessions can be mutual or established among scholars.
2
Analyze the fiqh example
The jurist uses a hadith ('في الحلي زكاة') to argue for zakāh on jewelry. The opponent objects that solitary reports are not proof. The jurist responds that accepting solitary reports is a principle of uṣūl al-fiqh, so it must be conceded here.
The concession is the principle that solitary reports are evidence.
3
Identify the dialectical syllogism
The syllogism: (1) Solitary hadiths are evidence (conceded from uṣūl al-fiqh). (2) This hadith is a solitary report. (3) Therefore, it is evidence. This is a dialectical, not demonstrative, argument.
Jadal uses such premises to force the opponent to accept the conclusion.
4
State the purpose of jadal
The author says: 'الغرض منه إلزام الخصم وإقناع من هو قاصر عن إدراك مقدمات البرهان' – to refute the opponent and convince one incapable of grasping demonstration.
Jadal does not produce certainty, but persuasion.
