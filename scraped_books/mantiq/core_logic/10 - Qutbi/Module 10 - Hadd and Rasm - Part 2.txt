The Condition of Inference in Definition
Commentator adds inference condition and defends against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the definiens must lead to conception by way of inference (nazar) and how the commentator defends this condition against two objections.

Core Idea
The definiens must lead to conception through inference, not by any other means of implication.

The commentator al-Sharif al-Jurjani expands on the definition of the definiens by adding the crucial condition that it must lead to conception by way of inference (bi-tariq al-nazar). He then addresses two potential objections that could undermine this condition. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and shows how the definition remains sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the added condition: inference
In the commentary, the definiens is specified as 'that whose conception by way of inference leads to conception of the thing or its distinction.' Note the phrase 'bi-tariq al-nazar'.
This condition aligns with the purpose of logic: to explain methods of acquiring conceptions through reasoning.
2
Understand the first objection
The first objection: the definiens's own conception would also necessitate the conception of its definiendum (reflexively). This would make the definiendum a definiens, causing a regress.
3
Examine the response
The response: this implication is not by way of inference (nazar) but by a different relation; it does not fall under the definition's scope.
4
Understand the second objection
The second objection: the conception of essences necessarily implies their obvious concomitants (lawāzim bayyina). So those concomitants would also be definiens.
5
Examine the second response
Again, this implication is not through inference; it is an immediate mental implication. Thus, the definition of definiens is not refuted.
The commentator concludes that the definition stands with the inference condition.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional passage that defines the definiens (al-mu'arraf) and specifies its essential conditions. It begins by stating the definition: the definiens is that whose conception necessitates the conception of the thing and its distinction from all others. The author then explains three negative conditions: (1) It cannot be the essence itself, because the definiens is known before the definiendum. (2) It cannot be broader (more general), because it would fail to adequately specify the thing. (3) It cannot be narrower (more specific), because it would be more obscure than the definiendum. Therefore, it must be coextensive (musawi) in generality and specificity. The commentator then expands on this, explaining that the clarifying statement (al-qawl al-sharih) is the definiens, and emphasizes that the conception must be by way of inference (bi-tariq al-nazar), not just any conception. He addresses two potential objections to the condition that the definiens must lead to conception through inference: (a) The conception of the definiens itself would require another definition, but this implication is not by inference. (b) The conception of an essence necessarily implies its obvious concomitants, but this implication is also not by inference. Thus the definition remains sound.

Refining the Conditions: Essence vs. Distinction in Definitions
Super-commentator refines the requirement that definition must attain essence or distinguish.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between definitions that give essence and those that merely distinguish, and evaluate whether a definition requires distinction from all others.

Core Idea
A perfect definition gives the essence; an imperfect definition may only distinguish. But even a perfect definition requires each part known by essence.

In this passage, the super-commentator Sharif builds on earlier discussions of definition. He clarifies two key points: first, that a perfect definition (hadd tamm) requires every part to be conceived by essence, and second, that the definiens need not distinguish the definiendum from everything else—only enough to achieve some conception. This refines the overly strict conditions set by later logicians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definition
Distinguish between perfect (hadd tamm) and imperfect definitions. The perfect definition gives conception by essence (al-kunh); the imperfect may or may not. The perfect definition requires all parts known by essence.
The text stresses that if any part is not known by essence, the whole essence cannot be known by essence.
2
Understand the later scholars' condition
Later scholars (al-muta'akhkhirun) required the definiens to either lead to essence or distinguish the definiendum from all others. This led them to reject broader and narrower definitions, which fail to distinguish from all.
This strict condition is the target of the super-commentator's critique.
3
Grasp the super-commentator's critique
The correct view, according to Sharif, is that the definiens need only lead to some conception of the thing, even if it distinguishes it only from some others. Complete distinction from all is not necessary, because a thing cannot be conceived without at least some distinction.
The key is that some distinction is inevitable, but full distinction is not required for conception.
4
Apply to a real Islamic example
For example, the definition 'salat is a set of specific actions beginning with takbir and ending with taslim' gives a conception with distinction from other acts (e.g., fasting) but does not give full essence. According to the super-commentator, this is acceptable.
Later scholars would reject it because it does not distinguish from all possible categories; but the correct view allows it.

The Purpose of Definition and the Validity of Broader/Narrower Terms
Author defines the purpose of definition and argues that broader/narrower terms can be definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the later scholars' condition for definition is too strict and justify the use of broader/narrower terms as definitions.

Core Idea
A definition need not give the essence nor exclude everything; even a general or specific term can yield a useful conceptualization.

In this passage, the author clarifies what a definition (ta'rif) is supposed to achieve. He argues that the later scholars' requirement that a definition must be coextensive with the definiendum is too strict. Instead, a definition can aim at conceptualization (tasawwur) either by essence or in some way, and distinction from some things suffices. This opens the door for broader and narrower terms to function as definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two functions of definition
The passage states that a definition aims either at conceptualization (tasawwur) of the thing or at its distinction (imtiyāz) from others. Find the Arabic phrases that express these two purposes.
The first line says 'أو امتيازه عن كل ماعداه' – or its distinction from everything else.
2
Understand the later scholars' objection
The later scholars insisted that a definer must be coextensive (musāwī) with the definiendum and distinguish it from everything else. They therefore ruled out broader or narrower terms as definitions. Locate their reasoning in the passage.
The phrase 'حكموا بأن الأعم والأخص لا يصلحان للتعريف أصلاً' expresses their position.
3
Unpack the author's critique
The author argues that conceptualization does not require exclusive distinction; only distinction from some things is necessary. Why? Because if a thing is not distinguished from at least some others, it cannot be conceptualized at all. Exclusive distinction is additional.
The author says 'لا يمكن أن يكون الشيء متصوراً مع عدم امتيازه عن بعض ما عداه'.
4
Apply the criterion to broader/narrower terms
If a broader term (like 'animal' for 'human') gives a partial conceptualization (bi wajh mā) of the definiendum, and that conceptualization is acquisitive, then the broader term is a valid definer. The same applies for narrower terms.
The author concludes 'فهما يصلحان للتعريف في الجملة'.
5
Distinguish between essential and non-essential conceptualization
Note that complete definition (hadd tām) requires conceptualization by essence (bi al-kunh), which needs all parts known by essence. But incomplete definitions (like broader terms) give conceptualization by aspect, which is still valid and acquisitive.
The passage contains a section distinguishing between complete and incomplete definitions.

Commentary
This passage defines the purpose of definition (ta'rif) in logic. The author begins by explaining that the aim of a definition is either to produce a conceptualization (tasawwur) of the thing or to distinguish it from everything else. He criticizes the view that a complete definition can be obtained without conceptualizing parts by the essence. The author then contrasts the position of the later scholars (mutā'akhkhirūn) with the correct view: they required the definer to lead to the essence or to exclusive distinction, thus rejecting broader or narrower terms. But the author argues that the definer need only lead to some conceptualization, whether of the essence or not, and whether the distinction is from all or some others. He points out that conceptualization in a broader or narrower way is still an acquired concept, so such terms are suitable for definition. This is a definitional passage (lesson_type_tag: definitional) that refines the conditions of definition, functioning as an expansion probe (textual_operation_tag: expansion_probe) on the original matn's claim about definitional equality.

Relations Between Definer and Defined: Equality is Preferred
Author enumerates four relations of definiens to definiendum and explains preference for equality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the relation between a definiens and definiendum as equal, broader, narrower, or disparate, and explain the logical basis for preferring equality.

Core Idea
A definition must use a different term, and equal scope ensures precise distinction.

When we define something, we use another term to clarify it. But not every relation between the definiens and definiendum is equally useful. In this lesson, we explore the four possible relations—equal, broader, narrower, and disparate—and see why the later scholars insisted on equality.

3

The author argues that specific implies general but not vice versa, and more conditions make it rarer and more obscure.

Examine why 'disparate' fails a fortiori
If even the close relations (more general and more specific) are unsuitable, then a term with no overlap is even worse, being extremely distant from the definiendum.
This is an argument from the lesser to the greater.

Complete vs Incomplete Definitions
Author defines complete and incomplete definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a definition is complete (hadd tam) or incomplete (hadd naqis) by examining the genus and differentia used.

Core Idea
A complete definition uses the nearest genus and nearest differentia; anything else is incomplete.

In this short but crucial passage, the author gives us the key to distinguishing a complete definition from an incomplete one. As you study definitions in logic, you'll need this tool to evaluate whether a definition truly captures the essence of a thing. Let's break down the criteria.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Look at the definiendum (the term being defined). The passage focuses on the definition itself, not a specific term, but you will apply this to any definition you encounter.
For practice, consider the definition of 'salah' in fiqh.
2
Locate the genus used in the definition
Find the broader category the term belongs to. Ask: Is this the immediate genus (al-jins al-qarib) or a more distant one (al-jins al-ba'id)?
Example: If defining 'human', the proximate genus is 'animal'; a distant genus would be 'living body'.
3
Locate the differentia
Find what distinguishes the term within its genus. Is it the immediate differentia (al-fasl al-qarib)?
For 'human', the proximate differentia is 'rational'.
4
Determine completeness
If both the proximate genus and proximate differentia are present, the definition is complete (hadd tam). If only the differentia alone or the differentia with a distant genus is used, it is incomplete (hadd naqis).
The incomplete definition is less precise because it does not capture the immediate kind.

Core Idea

The Four Types of Definitions: Complete and Incomplete Hadd and Rasm
Author defines the four types of definitions: complete/incomplete essential and descriptive definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given definition as complete/incomplete essential definition (hadd) or complete/incomplete descriptive definition (rasm) by identifying the components (genus, differentia, property) and determining whether they are essential or accidental.

Core Idea
A definition's type depends on whether it uses essential attributes or external traces, and on how many parts it includes.

In Islamic logic, definitions are not all alike. Some capture the very essence of a thing (hadd), while others describe it through its unique properties (rasm). Each can be complete or incomplete depending on whether all essential parts are present. This classification, outlined by the author, is crucial for precise thinking in 'ulum al-Islamiyya, especially when defining key terms in fiqh and usul al-fiqh. Let's learn to distinguish these four types using the examples provided by the author.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definiendum and Its Components
Look at the word being defined (e.g., 'insān') and the words used to define it. Note the genus (jins) and differentia (fasl) or property (khāṣṣa) in the definition.
The author uses 'insān' as the ongoing example.
2
Determine if Components Are Essential or Accidental
Essential attributes (dhātiyyāt) constitute the quiddity (māhiyya) of the thing. Accidental attributes (ʿaraḍiyyāt) are not part of the quiddity, but some are necessary concomitants (lawāzim). The differentia is essential; the property (khāṣṣa) is accidental but unique.
Example: 'nāṭiq' (rational) is essential; 'ḍāḥik' (laughing) is a property.
3
Classify as Hadd or Rasm
If the definition uses only essential attributes (genus and/or differentia), it is a hadd (essential definition). If it uses a property (khāṣṣa) instead of the differentia, it is a rasm (descriptive definition). The author explains 'hadd' linguistically means prevention (it prevents foreign things from entering), and 'rasm' means trace (it defines by external traces).
4
Check Completeness
For hadd: if it includes both proximate genus and differentia, it is tāmm (complete); if only the differentia (or with remote genus), it is nāqiṣ (incomplete). For rasm: if it includes proximate genus and property, it is tāmm; if only the property (or with remote genus), it is nāqiṣ.
The author gives examples: 'ḥayawān nāṭiq' (complete hadd), 'nāṭiq' alone (incomplete hadd), 'ḥayawān ḍāḥik' (complete rasm), 'ḍāḥik' alone (incomplete rasm).
5
Apply the Classification to New Examples
Practice by classifying definitions from Islamic sciences. For instance, the definition of 'ṣalāh' as 'specific words and actions begun with takbīr and ended with taslīm' — is it a hadd or rasm? (Answer: rasm, because it describes by actions, not essence.)

Commentary
This passage classifies definitions (al-ta'rifat) into four types. The author begins by stating that a definiendum is defined either by an essential definition (hadd) or a descriptive definition (rasm), and each can be complete (tamm) or incomplete (naqis). Thus there are four divisions: complete hadd, incomplete hadd, complete rasm, incomplete rasm. The complete hadd is composed of the proximate genus and differentia, e.g., 'human' defined as 'rational animal'. The author explains why it is called 'hadd': linguistically it means prevention, and because it includes the essential attributes (dhatiyyat) it prevents foreign things from entering the definition. It is called 'tamm' because all essential attributes are mentioned. The incomplete hadd uses only the proximate differentia or with the remote genus, e.g., 'human' as 'rational' or 'rational body'. It remains a hadd because it includes the distinguishing essential attribute, but it is incomplete because some essential attributes are omitted. The author then discusses 'rasm' (description), so called because 'rasm' literally means a trace, and a description defines by external necessary concomitants (khawarij lazima), which are traces of the thing. The complete rasm consists of the proximate genus and a property (khassa), e.g., 'laughing animal'. It resembles complete hadd in having the proximate genus and a distinguishing property. The incomplete rasm uses only the property or with the remote genus. The author addresses a potential objection: what about definitions using general accident with differentia, etc.? He replies that these reduce to the four mentioned. Finally, he distinguishes defining real entities (haqa'iq 'ayniyya), which is extremely difficult, from defining conventional concepts (mafhumat lughawiyya wa-istilahiyya), which is easy because in conventional definitions the essential attributes are determined by what enters into the concept. He warns that linguists and jurists use 'hadd' in a different sense (meaning the entire definition), so one must be careful about terminology.

Identifying an Incomplete Hadd
Author defines incomplete hadd with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an incomplete hadd and explain why it is a hadd yet incomplete.

Core Idea
An incomplete hadd uses the differentia but omits the genus.

In this lesson, we examine the first type of incomplete definition: al-hadd al-nāqiṣ. The author shows that a definition can be a proper hadd (using essential properties) yet still be incomplete if it omits the genus. We will see how defining 'human' as 'rational' is both a definition and incomplete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic. In this passage, 'al-insān' (human) is the definiendum.
2
Extract the differentia
The differentia is the essential property distinguishing it. Here, 'al-nāṭiq' (rational) is the proximate differentia.
3
Check for the genus
Determine if a genus (jins) is included. If only the differentia is given, or the differentia with a distant genus (like 'body'), the definition is an incomplete hadd.
The author's example 'rational' alone or 'rational body' both count as incomplete hadd.

Author defines complete rasm with example and reasoning.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A complete rasm uses the proximate genus and a special property, resembling a complete hadd.

Here we explore al-rasm al-tāmm, the complete description. The author explains that a rasm defines by an external effect (lāzim) rather than essence, but when it combines the proximate genus with a special property, it achieves completeness. The example 'laughing animal' for human illustrates this.

Guided Path
1
Find the proximate genus
Locate the genus that is closest to the definiendum. For 'human', the proximate genus is 'ḥayawān' (animal).
2
Identify the special property
Find the property that is unique to the definiendum and necessary. Here, 'ḍāḥik' (laughing) is the special property.
The property must be a necessary concomitant (lāzim) of the thing.
3
Verify completeness
Check that both proximate genus and special property are present. If so, the definition is a complete rasm.
The author calls it 'tāmm' because it resembles the complete hadd in structure.

The Four Types of Definition: Hadd and Rasm
Commentary enumerates the four types of definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given definition into one of the four types: complete definition (hadd tamm), deficient definition (hadd naqis), complete description (rasm tamm), or deficient description (rasm naqis).

Core Idea
Definitions are divided into four types based on whether they use all essentials, some essentials, or non-essentials.

The passage concludes with a clear method for enumerating the four types of definition. The key distinction is whether a definition uses only essential parts (dhatiyyat) or not. If using essentials, it can be complete (all essentials) or deficient (some essentials). If not using essentials, it can be a complete description (proximate genus and property) or a deficient description (anything else, like a common accident alone). This taxonomy organizes all possible definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
First, determine if the definition is based solely on the essential parts (al-dhatiyyat) or not. Essentials include genus and differentia.
2
For essential definitions: complete vs deficient
If it uses all essential parts (genus and differentia), it is a complete definition (hadd tamm). If it uses only some (e.g., differentia alone), it is a deficient definition (hadd naqis).
3
For non-essential definitions: complete vs deficient description
If it uses the proximate genus and a property (khassa), it is a complete description (rasm tamm). If it uses something else (e.g., property alone, common accident alone, or common accident with property), it is a deficient description (rasm naqis).
4
Practice classification
Take examples from fiqh and kalam and classify them. For instance, 'human is a rational animal' is a complete definition (genus: animal, differentia: rational). 'Human is a laughing animal' is a complete description (genus: animal, property: laughing).

Commentary

Circular Definitions (Dawr)
Author describes circular definition and gives examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect circular definitions, both direct and in a chain, and explain why they are invalid.

Core Idea
A circular definition leaves you exactly where you started.

The second major defect is circularity (dawr). In a circular definition, the definiens ultimately depends on the definiendum, creating a loop. The passage gives examples: direct circle between 'quality' and 'similarity', and an extended chain starting from 'two'. Understanding circularity helps avoid logical traps in Islamic argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definiens in each statement
For the direct circle: definiendum 'quality', definiens 'that by which similarity occurs'. Then definiendum 'similarity', definiens 'agreement in quality'.
2
Trace the dependencies
See if the definiens uses the definiendum itself. In the direct circle, they are used interchangeably.
3
For a chain, trace step by step
Start from 'two': definiens 'first even number'. Then 'first even number' defined as 'divisible into equal parts'. Then 'equal parts' defined as 'two things not exceeding each other'. Then 'two things' defined as 'two'. Eventually you return to 'two'.
All steps rely on the original term.
4
Conclude circularity
If the chain returns to the starting term, the definition is circular and provides no new knowledge.

The Four Types of Definition

Core Idea
Definitions are built from essentials or accidents; completeness depends on which you use.

In the Islamic logical tradition, definitions are not all alike. Qutbi presents a neat four-way classification that cuts through confusion: some definitions capture the essence fully, others only partially, and some rely on accidental properties. Learning this classification gives you a precise tool for evaluating definitions in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition's components
Look at the definition you are analyzing. Does it mention a broader category (genus) and a distinguishing feature (differentia or property)? List them.
The genus is always an essential property; the differentia is essential, while a property is accidental but distinctive.
2
Check if it uses only essential properties
If the definition uses only essential properties (genus and/or differentia), it is a 'hadd' (essential definition). If it includes accidental properties, it is a 'rasm' (description).
Essential properties are those without which the essence would not exist.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid definition must be more known than the definiendum, non-circular, and use clear terms.

Definitions are the foundation of precise reasoning in the Islamic sciences. But not every definition is sound. In this passage, Qutbi explains three common errors that render definitions useless: defining a term by something equally unknown, falling into circularity, or using obscure language. Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definiens
For any definition you encounter, first clearly pick out the term being defined (definiendum) and the expression used to define it (definiens). This is your starting point for checking pitfalls.
2
Check for equality in knowledge
Ask: Is the definiens equally well-known (or unknown) as the definiendum? Finally, consider the listener: Are the words used in the definiens clear and familiar to the intended audience? If the definiens uses strange or foreign words, the definition fails to communicate. This is a verbal fallacy (الأغاليط اللفظية).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the requirement to avoid certain types of words when formulating definitions or logical statements. The commentator discusses three categories: unusual (وحشية) words, metaphorical (مجازية) words, and homonyms (مشتركة). The author explains that unusual words, like calling fire 'an element above the elements', are generally prohibited because they are not commonly understood. Similarly, metaphorical words are problematic because the mind first jumps to the literal meaning, causing confusion. Homonyms, which have multiple meanings, also impair clarity because the listener hesitates between possible meanings. However, there are exceptions: if the listener already knows the unusual word, or if a context clue (قرينة) indicates the intended meaning, then such words may be used. The commentator then adds a note (attributed to Sharif) that metaphorical words are worse than homonyms: with metaphor, the mind immediately goes to the literal meaning, so without a clue it is completely misled; with homonyms, there is at least hesitation. But homonyms can also be taken wrongly, making them worse than rare words (غريبة) where nothing is understood at all, leading to needless inquiry without benefit. The overall lesson is that definitions should use clear, literal, common words to avoid misunderstanding.

The Three Components of a Science
Author defines the three components of a science: subjects, principles, and problems.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any proposition from an Islamic science as belonging to its subject, principles, or problems, and identify the type of subject of a problem.

Core Idea
Every Islamic science is built on three pillars: subject (mawḍū‘), principles (mabādi’), and problems (masā’il).

Just as a building needs a foundation, walls, and a roof, every science has its own structure. In this passage, the commentator explains the three essential components that make up any science: what it studies, how it studies it, and what it seeks to prove. Understanding this framework will help you navigate any Islamic science text and see how its parts fit together.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
The passage lists three parts: subjects (mawḍū‘āt), principles (mabādi’), and problems (masā’il). Subjects are the primary objects of study. Principles include definitions, axioms, and postulates. Problems are the propositions whose truth is demonstrated within the science.
2
Understand the principles
Principles are further divided: definitions of the subject and its essential accidents, and premises. Premises can be postulates (taken as given, like geometric postulates) or self-evident truths (like 'things equal to the same thing are equal to each other').
Essential accidents are properties that necessarily follow from the essence.
3
Analyze the types of problem subjects
The passage gives five ways the subject of a problem can relate to the subject of the science: it can be the subject itself, the subject with an essential accident, a species of the subject, a species with an essential accident, or just an essential accident.
Study the examples: e.g., 'Every magnitude...' uses the subject itself; 'Every line can be bisected' uses a species.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
Take a problem from Kalam: 'The world is temporally originated.' Its subject is 'the world' which is a species of the subject of Kalam (contingent beings). Classify it using the five types.
This skill helps you see how problems are structured in any discipline.

Predicates as Essential Accidents
Author specifies that predicates must be essential accidents of the science's subject and external to it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine why predicates in demonstrative problems must be essential accidents of the science's subject and cannot be parts of it.

Core Idea
Predicates are proven, parts are self-evident—so predicates must be external to the subject.

Having established what can be a subject, the author turns to predicates. He asserts a crucial constraint: predicates must be essential accidents of the science's subject, and they must be external to the subject. This rule ensures that demonstrations actually prove something new rather than merely unpack a definition.

Guided Path
1
Recall the goal of demonstration
Demonstration (burhān) aims to prove a predicate about a subject. The predicate should be something not immediately known about the subject.
If the predicate were a part of the subject, it would be self-evident and need no proof.
2
Identify the predicate in the example
In 'Every triangle has angles equal to two right angles', the predicate is 'having angles equal to two right angles'. This is not part of the definition of triangle; it is an accident.
Contrast with 'triangle has three sides'—that is a part of its definition.
3
Understand the author's reasoning
The author says: 'it is impossible for a part of a thing to be sought by demonstration, because parts are self-evident for the thing.' This means that if the predicate is part of the subject's definition, it cannot be legitimately proven.
Demonstration presupposes the definition and then seeks additional properties.
4
Apply to a different science
In kalam, the subject is 'the world' (as temporally originated). A predicate like 'has a Creator' is an essential accident (dependent on the world's temporality). A predicate like 'is composed of substances' is a part of the definition of the world (according to mutakallimun).
The latter would not be a demonstrative predicate but a definitional one.
