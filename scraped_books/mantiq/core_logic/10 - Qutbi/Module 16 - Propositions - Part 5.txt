Why Conjunctives Have 9 Types and Disjunctives 6
Explains the counting difference between conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why conjunctive conditionals have 9 compositional subtypes while disjunctive conditionals have only 6, using the distinction between natural and positional order.

Core Idea
Natural distinction in conjunctives doubles the mixed types; positional-only distinction in disjunctives does not.

After listing the six basic types, the author tells us that for conjunctive conditionals, each of the three mixed types (categorical+conjunctive, categorical+disjunctive, conjunctive+disjunctive) splits into two subtypes, giving 9 total for conjunctives. For disjunctives, the same mixed types do not split, leaving 6. The reason lies in how antecedent and consequent are distinguished: in a conjunctive, they are naturally distinct (e.g., cause-effect), so swapping them changes the meaning; in a disjunctive, they differ only by position, so order does not create a new type.

Guided Path
1
Recall the six basic types
The six basic types come from ordered pairs of proposition types. For a mixed type like (categorical, conjunctive), we have two orders: (cat, conj) and (conj, cat). However, in a conjunctive conditional, these two orders produce different meanings because the antecedent is naturally distinguished from the consequent.
Natural distinction means the antecedent might be the cause or condition, while the consequent is the effect.
2
Understand the split for conjunctive conditionals
For a conjunctive conditional, the two orders (cat, conj) and (conj, cat) are considered distinct subtypes because the natural relation (e.g., 'if X then Y') is not symmetric. Thus each mixed type splits into two.
The author says 'each one of these last three, in the conjunctive, divides into two parts'.
3
Contrast with disjunctive conditionals
In a disjunctive conditional (e.g., 'either X or Y'), the antecedent and consequent are distinguished only by their position in the statement, not by nature. Swapping them yields a logically equivalent statement, so the two orders are not counted as separate subtypes.
The author says 'the antecedent is distinguished from its consequent only by position'.
4
Calculate the totals
For conjunctives: the three pure types (two cat, two conj, two disj) count as one each, plus three mixed types each splitting into two gives 3 + 3×2 = 9. For disjunctives: no split, so 3 pure + 3 mixed = 6.
The pure types are symmetric and do not split because they are composed of the same proposition type on both sides.

Commentary
This passage defines the possible compositions of a conditional proposition. The matn (قال) first lists six basic types of composition: from two categoricals, two conjunctives, two disjunctives, a categorical and a conjunctive, a categorical and a disjunctive, and a conjunctive and a disjunctive. Then it explains that for conjunctive conditionals, the last three types (mixed with a categorical or a disjunctive) each split into two, because in a conjunctive, the antecedent and consequent are naturally distinguished (e.g., one may be the cause of the other), so the order matters. In contrast, in a disjunctive, the antecedent and consequent are only distinguished by position (no natural precedence), so no further split occurs. Thus total types: for conjunctive conditionals: the three pure types (two categoricals, two conjunctives, two disjunctives) count as one each, and the three mixed types each split into two, giving 3 + (3×2) = 9. For disjunctive conditionals: the three pure types count as one each, and the three mixed types do not split, giving 3 + 3 = 6. The sharh (أقول) expands by explaining that the minimal composition is from two categoricals, and any non-categorical composition must eventually reduce to categoricals, otherwise infinite regress would occur (since categoricals are composed of simple terms). This emphasizes that all conditionals ultimately rest on categorical foundations. The commentator (al-Sharif al-Jurjani) notes that the student should derive examples themselves.

Fixed vs. Placement Roles in Conditionals
Author distinguishes muttasila from munfasila by role fixation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conjunctive from disjunctive conditionals by identifying whether the roles of antecedent and consequent are fixed by nature or determined by placement.

Core Idea
In conjunctives, parts have fixed roles; in disjunctives, roles are mere placement.

This lesson unpacks the core logical distinction that determines the entire classification of conditional propositions. Understanding why conjunctives have nine types while disjunctives have six begins with grasping the fixed versus interchangeable nature of antecedent and consequent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate the Arabic terms for conjunctive (متصلة), disjunctive (منفصلة), antecedent (مقدم), consequent (تالي), implicans (ملزوم), necessary implication (لازم), and contrary (معاند). Note their definitions.
2
Analyze the fixed relationship in conjunctives
In a muttasila, the antecedent is always the implicans (ملزوم) and the consequent is always the necessary implication (لازم) by nature. This is not accidental but inherent to the logical relation.
3
Analyze the interchangeable relationship in disjunctives
In a munfasila, both parts are contraries (معاند) of each other, and their roles as antecedent or consequent are solely due to speaker's placement, not nature. The parts are logically symmetric.
The contrariety is mutual: each part is contrary to the other.
4
Derive the difference in number of types
Because conjunctives have fixed roles, changing which part is antecedent changes the meaning, leading to two distinct forms for each composition. In disjunctives, swapping parts does not change the logical type, so fewer forms exist.
The passage states: 'the three divisions in the conjunctive split into two parts, unlike the disjunctive.'

Commentary
This passage defines the logical structure of conditional propositions (qadaya shartiyya), distinguishing between conjunctive (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila). The author explains that in the conjunctive conditional, the antecedent (muqaddam) is always the implicans (malzum) and the consequent (tali) is the necessary implication (lazim). This relationship is fixed by nature. In contrast, in the disjunctive conditional, both parts are contraries (mu'anid) of each other, so their roles as antecedent and consequent are interchangeable—determined only by the speaker's placement, not by inherent logical necessity. This leads to a key difference: when constructing compound conditionals from categorical, conjunctive, or disjunctive components, the conjunctive type has two distinct forms depending on whether the antecedent is a categorical or a conjunctive, whereas the disjunctive type does not differentiate. Consequently, the author enumerates nine types of conjunctive conditionals and six types of disjunctive conditionals, then provides examples for each of the nine conjunctive types, illustrating all possible combinations of categorical (حمليّة), conjunctive (متصلة), and disjunctive (منفصلة) propositions as antecedents and consequents. The examples include familiar cases from logic and natural phenomena, such as the relationship between humanity and animality, between evenness and oddness, and between sunrise and daytime. All examples are drawn from the Islamic sciences (e.g., definitions of human as animal, number as even/odd). This classification is crucial for understanding the structure of conditional syllogisms in later discussions.

Nine Types of Conjunctive Conditionals

Conjunctive conditionals have nine types depending on the kind of antecedent and consequent (categorical, conjunctive, disjunctive).

Definition and Conditions of Contradiction in Propositions
Author defines propositional contradiction and defends its restriction to propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a pair of propositions satisfies the definition of contradiction, distinguishing genuine contradiction from mere opposition.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires intrinsic necessity of opposite truth values, not just difference in affirmation and negation.

The concept of contradiction (tanāquḍ) is foundational for evaluating arguments and detecting falsehood. In this passage, the author defines contradiction specifically for propositions and sets forth its precise conditions. You will learn to apply these conditions to distinguish a true logical contradiction from accidental opposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Ensure you have a pair of declarative statements (propositions) that can be true or false. For example, 'Zayd is human' and 'Zayd is not human' are both propositions.
2
Check affirmation and negation
Verify that one proposition affirms a predicate of the subject and the other negates exactly the same predicate. If they differ in any other way (e.g., one categorical and one conditional), it is not a candidate for contradiction.
3
Test intrinsic necessity
Ask: does the difference between the two propositions by itself (li-dhātihi) force one to be true and the other false? If there is any possibility that both could be true or both false, it is not a genuine contradiction. For instance, 'Zayd is sitting' and 'Zayd is not moving' can both be true.
4
Exclude external mediators
Ensure that the necessity is not due to an external assumption or particular matter. The example 'Zayd is human' and 'Zayd is not rational' requires the additional premise that rational is a necessary concomitant of human, so it is not intrinsic contradiction.

Intermediary examples involve a proposition and the negation of its logical equivalent—like 'Zayd is human' and 'Zayd is not rational', as 'rational' is a necessary concomitant (lāzim musāwī) of 'human'. The contradiction here is indirect. Particular matter examples show that two universal propositions or two particular propositions may not be contradictory even if they differ by affirmation/negation, depending on subject matter—e.g., 'Every animal is human' and 'No animal is human' are both false, not contradictory; 'Some animal is human' and 'Some animal is not human' are both true. However, mixed universal and particular propositions (like 'Some animal is human' and 'No animal is human') are always contradictory by their form. The commentator thus sharpens the definition: true contradiction is a difference by affirmation and negation that, by its own form (i.e., quantifier structure), necessarily yields one truth and one falsehood.

التناقض (al-tanāquḍ): Contradiction between two propositions such that they differ by affirmation and negation, and this difference intrinsically requires one to be true and the other false by virtue of their form (not by matter or intermediary). [Note: No significant shift—this is the standard definition in classical logic.]

الاختلاف المقتضي (al-ikhtilāf al-muqtaḍī): Difference that necessitates (requires) the truth of one and falsehood of the other, as opposed to non-requiring difference. [Note: This nuance is specific to this commentary; not always distinguished in earlier logic texts.]

The Eight Conditions for Contradiction (Tanāquḍ)
Listing the eight conditions for contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain each of the eight conditions necessary for two propositions to be contradictory.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires eight unities—subject, predicate, condition, whole/part, time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality.

In this passage from the Sharh al-Risāla al-Shamsiyya, the author enumerates the precise conditions that must hold for two propositions to be genuine contradictories. These conditions ensure that the difference between the propositions is solely in quality (affirmation/negation), not in any other aspect of meaning. Understanding these conditions is essential for evaluating whether two claims truly contradict each other, a skill critical in Islamic legal reasoning and theological debate.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Risāla al-Shamsiyya discusses the conditions required for two propositions to be true contradictories (tanāquḍ). The author first sets the context: for quantified propositions (maḥṣūra), in addition to the basic difference in quality (affirmation/negation), there must also be a difference in quantity (universal/particular) because two particulars can be simultaneously true and two universals simultaneously false when the subject is more general than the predicate. Similarly, for modalized propositions (muwajjaha), there must be a difference in modality (e.g., necessary/possible) because two possibles can be true together in a matter of possibility. The author then narrows the focus to the conditions for contradiction between two particular propositions (makhṣūṣa, i.e., indefinite or particular). He states that contradiction between them requires the realization of eight 'unities' (waḥdāt). He enumerates them: (1) Unity of subject (waḥdat al-mawḍūʿ): the subject must be the same; if it differs, both can be true or both false. Example: 'Zayd is standing' and ''Amr is not standing' are not contradictory. (2) Unity of predicate (waḥdat al-maḥmūl): the predicate must be the same. Example: 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not laughing' are not contradictory because the predicate differs. (3) Unity of condition (waḥdat al-sharṭ): the condition attached to the predicate must be the same. Example: 'The body is light-dispersing' (on condition of being white) and 'The body is not light-dispersing' (on condition of being black) are not contradictory. (4) Unity of whole and part (waḥdat al-kull wa al-juzʾ): the proposition must refer to the same whole or part. Example: 'The Zanjī is black' meaning some of him, and 'The Zanjī is not black' meaning all of him, are not contradictory. (5) Unity of time (waḥdat al-zamān). (6) Unity of place (waḥdat al-makān). (7) Unity of relation (waḥdat al-iḍāfa): the relation must be the same. Example: 'Zayd is a father' to 'Amr and 'Zayd is not a father' to Bakr are not contradictory. (8) Unity of potentiality and actuality (waḥdat al-quwwa wa al-fiʿl): the mode of existence (potential or actual) must be the same. Example: 'Wine in the jar is intoxicating' potentially and 'Wine in the jar is not intoxicating' actually are not contradictory. After listing these eight conditions from the earlier scholars (al-qudamāʾ), the author reports that the later scholars (al-mutaʾakhkhirūn) reduced them to just two: unity of subject and unity of predicate. They argued that unity of condition and unity of whole and part are subsumed under unity of subject. A marginal note (likely from al-Sharīf al-Jurjānī) clarifies that these conditions are necessary but not sufficient; additional conditions like difference in modality or quantity are also required. Another marginal note critiques the reduction, arguing that the assignment of some unities to subject and others to predicate is arbitrary because when a proposition is converted, the subject becomes predicate and vice versa. Therefore, the correct view is to say all unities are subsumed under both subject and predicate without specification. The author seems to accept this critique, noting that the one who specified probably relied on what is apparent.

The Reduction of the Eight Conditions and Its Critique
Reduction to two conditions and critical note.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how later scholars reduced the eight conditions for contradiction to two and analyze the critique that this reduction is arbitrary due to conversion.

Core Idea
The eight conditions reduce to unity of subject and predicate, but a conversion argument shows this reduction is not uniquely determined.

After listing the eight conditions for contradiction, the author reports that later scholars condensed them into just two unities: unity of subject and unity of predicate. However, a marginal note points out a flaw in specifying which conditions belong to which unity: when a proposition is converted, subject and predicate swap, so the classification changes. This lesson explores the reduction and the critical insight that the classification is relative to the proposition's formulation.

Guided Path
1
Review the eight conditions
Recall the eight unities: subject, predicate, condition, whole/part, time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality. The later scholars reduced them to two.
This step sets the foundation.
2
Understand the reduction
The reduction states that unity of condition and unity of whole/part are subsumed under unity of subject. The remaining five unities (time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality, and possibly predicate itself) are subsumed under unity of predicate.
The text says 'under unity of subject are subsumed unity of condition and unity of whole and part.'
3
Grasp the conversion argument
The marginal note argues that specifying which unities belong to subject and which to predicate is arbitrary. For example, convert 'The Zanjī is black' (some) to 'Some black thing is the Zanjī'. The unity of part/whole, originally linked to subject, becomes linked to the predicate 'Zanjī' in the converse. Thus the assignment flips.
This shows the reduction is not absolute.
4
Evaluate the alternative view
The correct view, according to the note, is to say that all eight unities are subsumed under both subject and predicate without specification. The author notes that the specific reduction was based on what is apparent (the original proposition's wording).
This highlights the pragmatic nature of the reduction.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Risāla al-Shamsiyya discusses the conditions required for two propositions to be true contradictories (tanāquḍ). The author first sets the context: for quantified propositions (maḥṣūra), in addition to the basic difference in quality (affirmation/negation), there must also be a difference in quantity (universal/particular) because two particulars can be simultaneously true and two universals simultaneously false when the subject is more general than the predicate. Similarly, for modalized propositions (muwajjaha), there must be a difference in modality (e.g., necessary/possible) because two possibles can be true together in a matter of possibility. The author then narrows the focus to the conditions for contradiction between two particular propositions (makhṣūṣa, i.e., indefinite or particular). He states that contradiction between them requires the realization of eight 'unities' (waḥdāt). He enumerates them: (1) Unity of subject (waḥdat al-mawḍūʿ): the subject must be the same; if it differs, both can be true or both false. Example: 'Zayd is standing' and ''Amr is not standing' are not contradictory. (2) Unity of predicate (waḥdat al-maḥmūl): the predicate must be the same. Example: 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not laughing' are not contradictory because the predicate differs. (3) Unity of condition (waḥdat al-sharṭ): the condition attached to the predicate must be the same. Example: 'The body is light-dispersing' (on condition of being white) and 'The body is not light-dispersing' (on condition of being black) are not contradictory. (4) Unity of whole and part (waḥdat al-kull wa al-juzʾ): the proposition must refer to the same whole or part. Example: 'The Zanjī is black' meaning some of him, and 'The Zanjī is not black' meaning all of him, are not contradictory. (5) Unity of time (waḥdat al-zamān). (6) Unity of place (waḥdat al-makān). (7) Unity of relation (waḥdat al-iḍāfa): the relation must be the same. Example: 'Zayd is a father' to 'Amr and 'Zayd is not a father' to Bakr are not contradictory. (8) Unity of potentiality and actuality (waḥdat al-quwwa wa al-fiʿl): the mode of existence (potential or actual) must be the same. Example: 'Wine in the jar is intoxicating' potentially and 'Wine in the jar is not intoxicating' actually are not contradictory. After listing these eight conditions from the earlier scholars (al-qudamāʾ), the author reports that the later scholars (al-mutaʾakhkhirūn) reduced them to just two: unity of subject and unity of predicate. They argued that unity of condition and unity of whole and part are subsumed under unity of subject. A marginal note (likely from al-Sharīf al-Jurjānī) clarifies that these conditions are necessary but not sufficient; additional conditions like difference in modality or quantity are also required. Another marginal note critiques the reduction, arguing that the assignment of some unities to subject and others to predicate is arbitrary because when a proposition is converted, the subject becomes predicate and vice versa. Therefore, the correct view is to say all unities are subsumed under both subject and predicate without specification. The author seems to accept this critique, noting that the one who specified probably relied on what is apparent.

Eight Conditions for Contradiction and Their Reduction
Lists eight conditions for contradiction and their reduction to two unities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the eight conditions for contradiction and explain how they reduce to unity of subject and unity of predicate.

Core Idea
Contradiction between propositions requires more than opposite truth values; eight conditions must hold, reducible to two unities.

In Islamic logic, two propositions are truly contradictory only if they meet precise conditions. The early logicians listed eight such conditions. Later scholars simplified them into two fundamental unities: unity of subject and unity of predicate. This lesson unpacks those conditions, shows how they collapse, and addresses a subtle objection about the assignment of sub-conditions.

Guided Path

The passage mentions eight conditions for contradiction. Although only the seventh (unity of relation) and eighth (unity of potentiality and actuality) are fully exemplified, the earlier six include unity of subject, predicate, time, place, condition, whole/part. Commit these to memory.
The first six are not fully listed in this window; refer to the full text of al-Qutbi for completeness.
2
Understand the reduction to two unities
Later scholars reduced the eight to just two: unity of subject (waḥdat al-mawḍūʿ) and unity of predicate (waḥdat al-maḥmūl). Some conditions are subsumed under unity of subject, others under unity of predicate.
The reduction simplifies the test for contradiction: verify that subject and predicate are identical in the two propositions.

Analyze the subsumption assignment
The commentary specifies that unity of condition and unity of whole/part are subsumed under unity of subject, while time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality are subsumed under unity of predicate. This assignment is defended by noting the natural affinity: conditions and whole/part pertain to the subject, while time, place, etc. pertain to the predicate.
The assignment is not arbitrary; it mirrors how these elements typically relate to the subject or predicate in a proposition.
4

An objection is raised: if the proposition is converted (by 'aks), the subject becomes predicate and vice versa, so the subsumption should be reversible. The defense is that the assignment is based on what is more apparent in the original form, not on an absolute categorization.
This subtle point shows the importance of context in logical analysis.

Take the example from the passage: 'Zayd is a father of Amr' and 'Zayd is not a father of Bakr'. Here the relation (iḍāfa) differs, violating unity of relation, which falls under unity of predicate. Therefore, these are not contradictory.

Subsumption of Contradiction Conditions and al-Farabi's Reduction
Author details how each condition is subsumed under subject or predicate unity and mentions al-Farabi's reduction to one unity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify each condition for contradiction under subject or predicate unity, and describe al-Farabi's reduction to the unity of the judgmental relation.

Core Idea
Al-Farabi reduces all contradiction conditions to one: unity of the judgmental relation.

In the previous lesson, you learned the eight conditions for contradiction. Here, the commentator shows how each condition falls under either subject or predicate unity, and then al-Farabi proposes a deeper unity that subsumes all others. Understanding this subsumption helps you apply the conditions correctly in real arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories for subsumption
The passage divides conditions into those related to subject unity (condition, whole/part) and those related to predicate unity (time, place, relation, potentiality, actuality). Note the examples given: condition (white/black body), whole/part (Zanjī), time (night/day sleep).
2
Examine the rationale for subject subsumption
For condition: the subject changes with the condition (white vs black body). For whole/part: subject changes from part to whole. So difference in condition or whole/part means the subject is not the same; thus contradiction requires unity.
3
Examine the rationale for predicate subsumption
For time: the predicate 'sleeping' implies different times (night vs day). Similarly for place, etc. So the predicate differs. Thus unity of predicate requires unification of these conditions.
4
Understand al-Farabi's reduction
Al-Farabi says all these are reducible to one unity: the judgmental relation (nisba hukmiyya). The negation must bear on the same relation as the affirmation. This makes contradiction a matter of identical judgments.
This reduction is philosophically deeper but the commentator retains the earlier classification as more apparent.
5
Reflect on the commentator's retention of earlier classification
The commentator notes that assigning condition and part to subject, and time/place to predicate is more appropriate and apparent, even though al-Farabi's unified view is more fundamental.

This passage provides a detailed justification for how each of the conditions is subsumed under either subject or predicate unity. Contradiction: When a Particular and a Universal Can Both Be False
Author explains why a compound particular can be false together with a more specific universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify cases where a particular proposition and a universal proposition can both be false by analyzing the specificity relation between the universal and the contradictory of the particular.

Core Idea
A particular and a more specific universal can both be false, contrary to the usual contradiction rule.

In the square of opposition, a particular and its contradictory universal cannot both be false. However, when we consider a universal that is more specific than the contradictory of the particular, a different relation emerges. The passage from Qutb al-Din al-Razi explores this subtle point, showing that a compound particular can be false together with a universal that is more specific than its contradictory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the compound particular proposition
In the example, 'Some body is animal not always' (بعض الجسم حيوان لادائماً) is a compound particular. Note its quantity (particular) and modality (temporal: 'not always').
The term 'compound particular' may refer to a proposition with a temporal modifier.
2
Determine its contradictory
The contradictory (نقيض) of a particular negative is a universal affirmative: 'All body is animal always'.
Standard contradiction: opposite quality and quantity.
3
Identify the universal that is more specific than the contradictory
One of the two universals mentioned is more specific (أخص) than this contradictory. For example, 'All human is animal always' (assuming 'human' is a species under 'body').
The universal must have a narrower extension than the contradictory of the particular.
4
Analyze truth conditions
Since the universal is more specific, it can be false even when the more general (the contradictory) is true. If the contradictory is true (i.e., all bodies are animals always), then the particular 'Some body is animal not always' is false. But the more specific universal (e.g., 'All human is animal always') might also be false (if some human is not always animal). Hence both the compound particular and that universal are false.
The key is that the universal is not the direct contradictory but a subaltern of the contradictory.
5
Generalize the logical pattern
A particular proposition (P) and a universal (U) can both be false if U is more specific than the contradictory of P. This is an exception to the rule that contradictories cannot both be false.
This occurs only when U is not the direct contradictory of P.

How to Contradict a Universal Conditional Proposition
Author states rule for contradicting universal conditionals and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory of any universal conditional proposition by preserving its genus (اتصال or انفصال) and species (لزوم, عناد, اتفاق) while reversing its quality and quantity.

Core Idea
To contradict a universal conditional, change quantity and quality but keep the type.

In previous lessons, you learned how to contradict simple categorical propositions. Now, conditionals have their own structure—they come in conjunctive and disjunctive types, each with subtypes like implicative or contrary. The rule for contradicting them is similar: the contradictory of a universal is a particular of opposite quality, but you must carefully preserve the 'genus' and 'species' of the original. This lesson shows you exactly how to do that.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conditional
Determine if the conditional is conjunctive (متصل) or disjunctive (منفصل). This is its genus (جنس). The text says: الموافقة لها في الجنس - أي في الاتصال والانفصال.
Conjunctive conditionals use phrases like 'كلّما كان' (whenever), disjunctive use 'إما' (either/or).
2
Identify the subtype
For a conjunctive conditional, note if it's implicative (لزومية), contrary (عنادية), or coincidental (اتفاقية). For disjunctive, note if it's real (حقيقية) or other types. This is the species (نوع). The text mentions النوع - أي في اللزوم والعناد والاتفاق.
The species describes the mode of connection between the antecedent and consequent.
3
Note the quantity and quality
Is the proposition universal or particular? Affirmative or negative? The contradictory will be a particular proposition of opposite quality. For example, the contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative.
This mirrors the rule for simple propositions, but here the type must match.
4
Construct the contradictory
Change the universal quantifier to a particular quantifier (e.g., 'كلّما' becomes 'ليس كلّما', 'دائماً' becomes 'ليس دائماً'), flip the quality, and keep the genus and species exactly the same.
Example from text: 'كلّما كان ا ب فج' becomes 'ليس كلّما كان ا ب فج'.

Ranking Categorical Propositions by Nobility
Author ranks universal/particular and affirmative/negative propositions by nobility, explaining the criteria.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to rank the four categorical propositions by nobility and justify the ranking using the criteria of quality and quantity.

Core Idea
Affirmation and universality define nobility in categorical propositions.

In Islamic logic, not all propositions are equal in nobility (sharaf). This ranking reflects deeper epistemic values: existence (affirmation) is nobler than non-existence (negation), and the precision of universal statements outranks particular ones. This lesson unpacks the criteria and shows how the four quantified propositions are ordered, a tool used later to prioritize syllogistic moods.

The passage introduces two dimensions: quality (kayfiyya: affirmation/negation) and quantity (kammiyya: universal/particular). Understand that each dimension has a 'nobler' value: affirmation over negation, universal over particular.
Affirmation is associated with existence (wujud), negation with non-existence (adam).

Take an Islamic science example: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (universal affirmative) vs 'Some intoxicant is not forbidden' (particular negative). Which is more noble? The universal affirmative, because it provides a precise, all-encompassing rule that is more useful in deriving further rulings.

The passage ends by linking proposition nobility to syllogism arrangement: moods that produce nobler conclusions are presented first. This explains why in standard logic texts, certain moods (e.g., Barbara) come before others (e.g., Ferio).

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition of the types of certain premises (yaqiniyyat) that form the material foundation of syllogistic reasoning in logic. The commentator explains that the logician must examine the universal matter of syllogisms to avoid error in both form and matter. Certainty (yaqin) is defined precisely: it is a firm belief that a thing is such, coupled with the belief that it cannot be otherwise, this belief conforming to reality and being unshakeable. The definition has three restrictions that exclude, respectively, conjecture (zann), compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), and the blind follower's belief (taqlid). Certainties are divided into necessary (daruriyyat) and theoretical (nadhariyyat). The necessary are six, because the judge of the truth of certain propositions is either intellect, sense, or a combination. When the intellect judges, it either does so by mere conception of the two terms (yielding 'primaries' awwaliyyat, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part') or by means that is always present in the mind (yielding 'propositions whose syllogisms are with them', qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha, e.g., 'four is even'). When the senses judge, they yield observations (mushahadat): external senses give sensibles (hissiyyat), internal senses give inner feelings (wijdaniyyat). When sense and intellect combine: if the sense is hearing, we get mutawatirat (recurrent reports), like knowledge of Mecca's existence; the number of reporters is not fixed – certainty itself determines sufficiency. If the sense is other than hearing and repetition is needed, we get experientials (mujarrabat), like knowing that scammony is a laxative. If no repetition is needed, we get intuitives (haddsiyyat), like knowing the moon's light is from the sun. Finally, hads (intuition) is defined as rapid transition from principles to conclusions, contrasted with fikr (thinking) which involves two movements. This passage provides the epistemological foundation for distinguishing degrees of certainty in premises.

Primaries and Propositions with Their Syllogisms
Author distinguishes two types of intellectual necessary propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between primary propositions (awwaliyyat) and propositions whose syllogisms are with them (qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha), and identify examples of each.

Core Idea
Some truths are immediately evident; others are self-evident because the reasoning is always in your mind.

The intellect can judge a proposition in two ways: either without any reasoning, or with a reasoning that comes to mind immediately upon conceiving the terms. These are the two types of intellectual certainties.

Guided Path
1
Read the example of a primary
In the Arabic: 'الكل أعظم من الجزء' (the whole is greater than the part). Note that upon conceiving 'whole' and 'part', the intellect instantly affirms the proposition without any intermediate step.
2
Read the example of a proposition with its syllogism
In the Arabic: 'الأربعة زوج' (four is even). When you conceive 'four' and 'even', the mind immediately brings in the idea of 'divisible into two equal parts', which is a syllogism: four is divisible into two equals, and everything divisible into two equals is even. The syllogism is always present upon conception.
This is called 'qadiya qiyasuha ma'aha' because the proof accompanies the terms.
3
Distinguish the two types
In a primary, the judgment is direct; in a proposition with its syllogism, the judgment requires an implicit syllogism that is always mentally available. The key is whether the intellect needs any 'means' (wāsita) beyond the terms themselves.
If the means can disappear from the mind, it is not a certain premise of this kind.

Observations: External and Internal Senses
Author defines observations (mushahadat) and splits them into hissiyyat and wijdaniyyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish external sense propositions (hissiyyat) from internal sense propositions (wijdaniyyat) and give Islamic examples of each.

Core Idea
Certainty from the senses is immediate but limited to what is perceived.

The senses also provide certain premises. They are of two kinds: external (hissiyyat) based on the five outward senses, and internal (wijdaniyyat) based on inner perception. Understanding the difference helps in evaluating arguments that rely on empirical observations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the judge as the senses
In the Arabic, when the judge of truth is the senses (al-hiss), the propositions are called 'mushahadat' (observations).
2
Check if the sense is external or internal
If the sense is external (e.g., sight, hearing), it is 'hissiyyat' (sensibles). Example: 'the sun is luminous.' If the sense is internal (e.g., inner feelings), it is 'wijdaniyyat' (inner feelings). Example: 'I have fear.'
Internal senses include emotions and bodily sensations like hunger.
3
Apply the distinction to a new example
Given a proposition, determine whether it is based on external observation or internal feeling. For instance, 'the sky is blue' is hissiyya; 'I am angry' is wijdaniyya.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition of the types of certain premises (yaqiniyyat) that form the material foundation of syllogistic reasoning in logic. The commentator explains that the logician must examine the universal matter of syllogisms to avoid error in both form and matter. Certainty (yaqin) is defined precisely: it is a firm belief that a thing is such, coupled with the belief that it cannot be otherwise, this belief conforming to reality and being unshakeable. The definition has three restrictions that exclude, respectively, conjecture (zann), compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), and the blind follower's belief (taqlid). Certainties are divided into necessary (daruriyyat) and theoretical (nadhariyyat). The necessary are six, because the judge of the truth of certain propositions is either intellect, sense, or a combination. When the intellect judges, it either does so by mere conception of the two terms (yielding 'primaries' awwaliyyat, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part') or by means that is always present in the mind (yielding 'propositions whose syllogisms are with them', qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha, e.g., 'four is even'). When the senses judge, they yield observations (mushahadat): external senses give sensibles (hissiyyat), internal senses give inner feelings (wijdaniyyat). When sense and intellect combine: if the sense is hearing, we get mutawatirat (recurrent reports), like knowledge of Mecca's existence; the number of reporters is not fixed – certainty itself determines sufficiency. If the sense is other than hearing and repetition is needed, we get experientials (mujarrabat), like knowing that scammony is a laxative. If no repetition is needed, we get intuitives (haddsiyyat), like knowing the moon's light is from the sun. Finally, hads (intuition) is defined as rapid transition from principles to conclusions, contrasted with fikr (thinking) which involves two movements. This passage provides the epistemological foundation for distinguishing degrees of certainty in premises.

Recurrent Reports, Experiments, and Intuitions
Author describes the three types of propositions from combined sense and intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mutawatirat, mujarrabat, and hadsiyyat based on the role of hearing and repetition.

Core Idea
Some certainties come from a combination of sense and intellect: through widespread reports, repeated experiments, or intuitive insight.

Not all certainties are immediate or purely sensory. Some come from hearing a large number of people (mutawatirat), some from repeated observations (mujarrabat), and some from a single leap of intuition (haddsiyyat). This lesson explains how to tell them apart.

Guided Path
1
Check if the sense involved is hearing
In the Arabic, if the sense is hearing (sam'), the propositions are 'mutawatirat' (recurrent reports). Example: knowledge of Mecca's existence comes from countless people reporting it.
The intellect deems collusion on falsehood impossible due to the multitude.
2
If hearing is not the sense, check if repetition is needed
If the sense is other than hearing (e.g., sight), and the intellect needs repeated observations to reach certainty, it is 'mujarrabat' (experientials). Example: knowing that scammony is a laxative after seeing it many times.
3
If no repetition is needed
If the intellect reaches certainty without repeating the observation, it is 'haddsiyyat' (intuitives). Example: knowing that the moon's light is from the sun by observing its phases relative to the sun.
This requires a single insightful observation.

Intuition (Hads) versus Thinking (Fikr)
Author defines hads and contrasts it with fikr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast hads and fikr in terms of movement from principles to conclusions.

Core Idea
Intuition is a direct leap; thinking is a step-by-step search.

The passage ends with a definition of hads (intuition) in contrast to fikr (thinking). This distinction is important for understanding how some premises are known quickly without deliberate reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Define hads from the Arabic
In the Arabic: 'الحدس هو سرعة الانتقال من المبادئ إلى المطالب'. Hads is the rapid transition from principles to sought conclusions.
Note the word 'سرعة' (speed) – there is no deliberateness.
2
Contrast with fikr
Fikr is the movement of the mind towards the principles and its return from them to the sought conclusions. It involves two movements: going to principles and coming back to conclusions.
In hads, there is no movement at all (لا حركة فيه أصلاً). The transition is instantaneous.
3
Apply the distinction
Consider knowing that the moon's light is from the sun. A person with hads infers this immediately upon observing the changing shapes; a fikr would require consciously considering the relationship.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition of the types of certain premises (yaqiniyyat) that form the material foundation of syllogistic reasoning in logic. The commentator explains that the logician must examine the universal matter of syllogisms to avoid error in both form and matter. Certainty (yaqin) is defined precisely: it is a firm belief that a thing is such, coupled with the belief that it cannot be otherwise, this belief conforming to reality and being unshakeable. The definition has three restrictions that exclude, respectively, conjecture (zann), compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), and the blind follower's belief (taqlid). Certainties are divided into necessary (daruriyyat) and theoretical (nadhariyyat). The necessary are six, because the judge of the truth of certain propositions is either intellect, sense, or a combination. When the intellect judges, it either does so by mere conception of the two terms (yielding 'primaries' awwaliyyat, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part') or by means that is always present in the mind (yielding 'propositions whose syllogisms are with them', qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha, e.g., 'four is even'). When the senses judge, they yield observations (mushahadat): external senses give sensibles (hissiyyat), internal senses give inner feelings (wijdaniyyat). When sense and intellect combine: if the sense is hearing, we get mutawatirat (recurrent reports), like knowledge of Mecca's existence; the number of reporters is not fixed – certainty itself determines sufficiency. If the sense is other than hearing and repetition is needed, we get experientials (mujarrabat), like knowing that scammony is a laxative. If no repetition is needed, we get intuitives (haddsiyyat), like knowing the moon's light is from the sun. Finally, hads (intuition) is defined as rapid transition from principles to conclusions, contrasted with fikr (thinking) which involves two movements. This passage provides the epistemological foundation for distinguishing degrees of certainty in premises.

Maqbulat: Premises of Trust and Authority
Defines maqbulat as premises accepted from authoritative figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify maqbulat and distinguish them from other premise types based on the source of authority.

Core Idea
Maqbulat are premises whose authority comes from trust, not proof.

In logic, not every premise is self-evident or demonstrable. Some premises gain their weight from the trustworthiness of the source. The logicians call these 'maqbulat' – accepted premises. In this lesson, we will examine what makes a premise maqbul and why they play a vital role in religious and ethical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Locate the defined term
Find 'al-maqbulat' in the Arabic passage. It is the term being defined.
2
Identify the genus and differentia
Maqbulat are 'propositions taken from one who is believed in.' The genus is 'propositions' (qadaya). The differentia is the source: either divine (prophets/saints) or intellectual/religious authority (scholars/ascetics).
This is a rasm (description) rather than a strict logical definition.
3
Understand the two categories
The author lists two reasons for acceptance: (1) a celestial matter (amr samawi) involving miracles (mu'jizat) and spiritual gifts (karamat) of prophets and saints, and (2) special endowment of intellect and religion (mazid 'aql wa din) like scholars and ascetics.
Note that both categories rely on trust in the person, not on logical demonstration.
4
Analyze the utility
The passage says maqbulat are 'very beneficial in glorifying the command of God and having compassion for God's creation.' Maqbulat serve practical religious and moral purposes.
They are not used to establish certain knowledge, but to motivate action and reverence.
5
Differentiate from other premise types
Maqbulat are listed among non-certain premises (ghayr yaqiniyyat). They are not like first principles (awwaliyyat) or sense perceptions (hissiyyat); they depend on the reliability of a source.
In some classifications, maqbulat are a subtype of mashhurat (widely accepted), but here they are distinct.
