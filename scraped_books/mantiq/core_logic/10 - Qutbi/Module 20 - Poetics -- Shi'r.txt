Imaginative Premises and the Definition of Poetry
Author defines imaginative premises and poetic syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative premises (mukhayyalāt) and explain how a poetic syllogism aims to influence the soul emotionally through desire or aversion.

Core Idea
Poetry is a syllogism of imaginative premises that moves the soul to desire or aversion.

In this lesson, we explore the third of the five arts in logic: poetry (shiʿr). The author defines it not by meter or rhyme but by its premises and purpose. You will learn what imaginative propositions (mukhayyalāt) are, how they affect the soul, and why this distinguishes poetry from other types of discourse.

Guided Path
1
Understand 'mukhayyalāt'
Imaginative propositions are statements that stimulate the imagination rather than intellectual reasoning. They are not meant to prove truth but to create an emotional response.
2
Observe the soul's reaction
The author describes two reactions: expansion (bast) and contraction (qabḍ), which correspond to desire (raghba) and aversion (nufūr). Note how the examples trigger these reactions.
Example: 'Wine is a flowing ruby' causes expansion and desire.
3
Identify the definition of poetry
The syllogism composed of imaginative premises is called poetry. Its sole purpose is emotional persuasion—moving the soul to desire or aversion.
Poetry here is a logical type, not defined by meter or rhyme.
4
Recognize enhancers
Pleasant meter (wazn laṭīf) and good voice (ṣawt ṭayyib) strengthen the emotional effect, but they are not essential to the definition.

ومنها المخيلات وهي قضايا يخيل بها فتتأثر النفس منها قبضاً وبسطاً، فتنفر أو ترغب، كما إذا قيل: «الخمر ياقوتة سيالة» انبسطت النفس ورغبت في شربها، وإذا قيل: «العسل مرة مهوعة» انقبضت وتنفرت عنه، والقياس المؤلف منها يسمى «شعراً» والغرض منه انفعال النفس بالترغيب والترهيب، وتزيد في ذلك أن يكون الشعر على وزن لطيف أو ينشد بصوت طيب.
Poetry. Among them are 'imaginative propositions' (mukhayyalāt), which are propositions by which imagination is stimulated, so the soul is affected by contraction and expansion, and thus it is repelled or desires. For example, when it is said: 'Wine is a flowing ruby,' the soul expands and desires to drink it. When it is said: 'Honey is bitter and disgusting,' the soul contracts and is repelled from it. The syllogism composed of them is called 'poetry' (shiʿr), and its purpose is the soul's emotion of desire and aversion. This is enhanced if the poetry has a pleasant meter or is recited with a good voice.

Commentary
This passage defines the third of the five arts: poetry (shiʿr). The author describes its premises as 'imaginative propositions' (mukhayyalāt), which are statements that stimulate the imagination and thereby affect the soul emotionally—either causing expansion (bast) and desire (raghba) or contraction (qabḍ) and aversion (nufūr). Two examples are given: saying wine is a flowing ruby makes the soul desire it; saying honey is bitter and disgusting repels it. The syllogism composed of such premises is called poetry, and its purpose is to emotionally move the soul towards desire or aversion. This effect is strengthened if the poetry has a pleasant meter (wazn laṭīf) or is recited with a good voice.
