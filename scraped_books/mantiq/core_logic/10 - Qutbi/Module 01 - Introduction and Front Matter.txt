Identifying the Three Layers of Authors in Classical Logic Works
Title page identifying text, commentary, and gloss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the matn, sharh, and hashiya authors in a classical Islamic logic work and explain their relationships.

Core Idea
The matn-sharh-hashiya structure is a standard framework for authoritative commentary.

When you pick up a classical Islamic text, you often find not one but three layers of authorship: the original author (matn), the commentator (sharh), and the glossator (hashiya). This title page introduces you to all three for the work *Tahrir al-Qawa'id al-Mantiqiyya*. Understanding this structure helps you navigate the text and appreciate how knowledge was transmitted and refined across generations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main title of the work
Look at the first line of Arabic: تحرير القواعد المنطقية. This is the title of the commentary, not the original matn. The commentary is by Qutb al-Din al-Razi on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya.
2
Locate the three author names
The passage lists three names: Najm al-Din al-Katibi, Qutb al-Din al-Razi, and al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani. Notice that each is introduced with a title: الماتن, الشارح, المحشي.
3
Match each name to their role
Al-Katibi is the matn author (original base text), al-Razi is the sharh author (commentator), and al-Jurjani is the hashiya author (glossator). This tells you the hierarchy of the text: the matn is the core, the sharh explains it, and the hashiya refines the commentary.
4
Understand the relationship between layers
The sharh builds upon the matn, and the hashiya builds upon the sharh. This layered structure is common in Islamic scholarship; knowing it helps you read critically, since later layers may critique earlier ones.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the book, identifying the three layers of text: the base text (matn), the commentary (sharh), and the gloss (hashiya). The matn is al-Risala al-Shamsiyya by Najm al-Din al-Katibi, a classic textbook on logic. The sharh is Tahrir al-Qawa'id al-Mantiqiyya by Qutb al-Din al-Razi, which explains and expands the matn. The hashiya is by al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani, a super-commentary that refines or corrects the sharh. The passage then explicitly lists each author with their full names and titles: al-Katibi as the matn author, al-Razi as the sharh author, and al-Jurjani as the hashiya author. This clarifies the hierarchical structure of the work for the reader.

Key Terms
الماتن (al-mātin): The author of the original base text (matn) upon which commentary is written. [Note: No significant shift; consistent across the tradition.]
الشارح (al-shāriḥ): The commentator who writes an explanation (sharh) of the matn. [Note: No significant shift; consistent.]
المحشي (al-muḥashshī): The glossator who writes a super-commentary (hashiya) on the sharh, often correcting or refining it. [Note: In the later Ottoman tradition, 'hashiya' sometimes refers to marginal notes rather than a full super-commentary, but here it is a standalone work.]

Author's Works and Commentator's Lineage: The Scholarly Context of the Qutbi
Editor's preface listing al-Katibi's logical works and Qutb al-Din al-Razi's biography.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key logical works of al-Katibi and explain the significance of the ijaza system in establishing the scholarly authority of a commentary.

Core Idea
The Qutbi commentary rests on a chain of scholarship from al-Katibi to al-Razi, legitimated by al-Hilli's ijaza.

This lesson introduces the editorial preface of the Qutbi commentary on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya. You will learn about al-Katibi's other logical writings and the biography of Qutb al-Din al-Razi, focusing on the ijaza (permission to transmit) he received from al-Allama al-Hilli. Understanding this scholarly lineage is essential for appreciating the authority of the commentary you are about to study.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Katibi's logical works
Scan the passage to find the list of works by al-Katibi beyond the Shamsiyya. Note their characteristics: which is broader, which is a commentary, which is a commentary on another book.
2
Understand the dedication of the Shamsiyya
The treatise is named after Shams al-Din al-Juwayni, the vizier. Note the historical context of the Mongol rulers and the significance of dedicating a work to a patron.
3
Trace the commentator's scholarly lineage
Extract the details of Qutb al-Din al-Razi's education: he was a student of al-Allama al-Hilli and received an ijaza. Read the text of the ijaza to see how al-Hilli praises al-Razi and grants permission.
4
Analyze the function of the ijaza
The ijaza is a formal certification of competence and permission to transmit knowledge. It validates the chain of transmission (isnad) from the author to the commentator. This practice is crucial in Islamic scholarship for establishing authority.
Compare with the science of hadith, where ijaza is a common method of transmission.

This passage is the editor's introduction to the Qutbi, a commentary on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya. The passage serves to establish the authority and scholarly lineage of both the original text and its commentary, essential for the reader's trust.

Why al-Jurjani's Biography Matters for Logic
Editor introduces the life and major works of Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major works and scholarly connections of Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani and explain their relevance to studying his logical commentary.

Core Idea
Al-Jurjani’s biography reveals his role in transmitting and teaching Islamic logic across major seminaries.

When you open a commentary on a foundational logic text like al-Shamsiyya, knowing who the commentator was is not mere background. Al-Jurjani's life shows the scholarly chain that shaped his work and his enduring influence on logic curricula. This lesson walks through the key biographical points that every student of his logic should know.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's full name and honorifics
Start with the first sentence: السيد علي بن محمد بن علي الحسيني أو الحسني الجرجاني الملقب بالسيد شريف الدين. Note the titles and name variants. This establishes his identity as a major figure.
The لقب 'السيد شريف الدين' indicates his noble lineage and honor.
2
Map his scholarly network
Look for his teacher (قطب الدين الرازي), his student (جلال الدين الدواني), and his contemporary (سعد الدين التفتازاني). Understand that he studied under the commentator of al-Shamsiyya and taught a famous philosopher.
He also engaged in debates with al-Taftazani, a key figure in Islamic rhetoric and logic.
3
List his major works and their fields
From the passage, extract the most famous: صرف مير (morphology), الكبرى في المنطق (logic in Persian), شرح المواقف (kalam), and التعريفات (a dictionary of definitions). Each served a different purpose in Islamic education.
Two of these – صرف مير and الكبرى – are explicitly called textbooks for beginners in seminaries.
4
Connect his biography to his logic commentary
The passage ends by mentioning his gloss on شرح الشمسية for his teacher. This shows that the very work you are studying is part of a tradition: al-Jurjani built upon his teacher’s commentary. Knowing this helps you read his gloss with awareness of his teacher's influence.
His other glosses also show his breadth across tafsir, balaghah, and nahw.

Classification of Logical Topics into Articles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given logical topic into the correct article based on the author's division: singulars, compounds not intended essentially, or compounds intended essentially (form only).

Core Idea
Logical inquiry progresses from singulars to compounds, and from accidental to essential compounds.

In this passage, the author outlines the structure of the discipline by dividing its subject matter into three articles. This classification is not arbitrary; it reflects a logical progression from simple components to complex constructions. Understanding this division helps you locate where any logical concept belongs within the broader framework of the science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of subject matter
Determine whether the topic concerns singulary expressions (mufradāt) or compound expressions (murakkabāt). If singular, it belongs to Article 1.
Singulars include terms like 'man' or 'animal' used in definitions.
2
Determine if the compound is intended essentially
If the topic involves compounds, ask: Is the compound the primary object of study (maqṣūd bi-l-dhāt) or merely incidental? If not essential, it belongs to Article 2.

Check if form is considered alone

Syllogistic moods and figures are studied in Article 3 because they focus on logical form irrespective of content.
4
Confirm the article by negation
Use the categories to rule out other articles. For example, if a topic is not singular and not about form-only essential compounds, it must belong to Article 2.
This step sharpens your classification skill.

Resolving the Circularity of the Introduction
Author raises and answers a circularity objection about the introduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the circularity objection against including an introduction in a logical work and explain the author's resolution using the distinction between the science and its books.

Core Idea
The introduction belongs to the books of logic, not to the science itself, breaking the circular dependence.

A common objection in many sciences is that an introduction to a discipline cannot be part of that discipline because it presupposes knowledge of the discipline—leading to circularity. Here, the author presents this objection and then resolves it with a subtle distinction. This dialectical move is not just a quibble; it clarifies the relationship between a textbook and the science it teaches.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's premises
Find the two claims: (1) If the introduction is part of logic, then starting it is starting logic. (2) Starting logic depends on the introduction (since it introduces logic). The conclusion is a vicious circle.
The objection assumes 'part of logic' means part of the science itself.
2
Pinpoint the circularity
Combine the claims: starting the introduction requires starting logic, but starting logic requires starting the introduction. This is a classic circular dependency.
The circle is vicious because it makes beginning impossible.
3
Reinterpret the ambiguous phrase
The author introduces a grammatical device: a deleted annex (muḍāf maḥdhūf). The phrase 'the introduction is part of logic' actually means 'the introduction is part of the books of logic' (or 'books of the art').
The Arabic phrase includes an implicit 'books' that changes the meaning.
4
Apply the reinterpretation to dissolve the circle
Now the introduction is part of the textbook, not the science. Therefore, starting the introduction does not require knowledge of logic (the science) because the book provides that knowledge. The circle is broken.
This resolution preserves the necessity of the introduction as a preliminary to studying the book.

The Three Prerequisites for Studying Any Science
Author explains that sciences are distinguished by subjects and lists three prerequisites for studying any science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the three prerequisites for beginning the study of any Islamic science as presented in this passage.

Core Idea
To start any science, you only need a minimal conception and belief in its benefit—not full knowledge of its subject.

Before diving into logic (or any Islamic science), the author lays down a methodological foundation. He explains that sciences are distinguished by their subject matters, and then specifies what a student must have before even beginning. These are not advanced requirements but minimal conditions to make the study meaningful. Let's examine them closely.

Guided Path
1
Understand why sciences are distinct
The author argues that sciences are distinguished by their subjects. If a set of inquiries pertains to one thing or proportionate things, they form one science. Otherwise, they are separate. This is the background for the prerequisites.
This principle applies to all Islamic sciences: Fiqh has rulings of actions, Kalam has divine attributes, etc.
2
Identify the first prerequisite: tasawwur (conception)
The student must conceive the science at least minimally—either by a general notion or by its definition (rasm). Without any conception, he cannot begin.
The author distinguishes between a minimal conception (enough to start) and a detailed definition (for insight).
3
Identify the second prerequisite: tasdiq (belief in benefit)
The student must believe that the science has a specific benefit. This belief need not be certain or even true—just held to avoid vain effort.
This is a pragmatic condition: if you don't think it's useful, you won't study earnestly.
4
Identify the third component: knowledge of the subject (mawdu')
Knowing the precise subject matter is not obligatory for beginning. It only provides increased insight. The author clarifies that initial distinction and insight come from the conception.
This refines a potential misunderstanding: you don't need to know the full subject before starting.
5
Summarize the three components of the introduction
The author concludes that the introduction to any science (as taught here) consists of: (1) conception of the science, (2) affirmation of its benefit, and (3) affirmation of its subject's subjecthood (i.e., that it has a subject).
Only the first two are strictly necessary at the beginning.

Enumeration of nine preliminaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the nine standard preliminaries that introduce a science in the Islamic tradition.

Core Idea
A proper introduction gives the student the tools to navigate the science.

This passage enumerates the nine matters that should ideally be covered in the introduction of a science. Understanding these helps you appreciate how classical scholars structured their works and prepared students.

Guided Path
1

The passage lists: subject, benefit, rank, nobility, founder, naming, summary of issues, need, and discussion of words.
The need for logic is part of the preliminaries.
2

Eight pertain to the science itself; one (discussion of words) pertains to the method of instruction.
3

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary on the Shamsiyya, explaining the purpose of the introductory matters. It begins by illustrating how sciences are distinguished by their subject matter, using fiqh and usul al-fiqh as examples. Fiqh studies actions of the mukallaf in terms of permissibility, prohibition, etc., while usul al-fiqh studies revealed evidences for deriving rulings. Having different subjects makes them distinct sciences. Then it addresses the need for logic: explaining this need helps define logic by its purpose (ghaya), which is a kind of definition by description (rasm). The commentary lists nine standard preliminaries: eight related to the science itself (subject, benefit, rank, nobility, founder, naming, summary of issues, and the need for it) and one related to the method of communication (discussion of words). While it is best to mention all, some may be omitted. The key point is that explaining the need for logic is comprehensive because it includes defining the science by its end, unlike a mere description which might not refer to the end. Thus, the need for logic serves as an introduction to the science.

The Placement of a Definition in a Dialectical Context
Dialectical exchange on placement of definition after division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical exchange in a logical commentary and identify the pedagogical function of placing a definition after a division.

Core Idea
Order in logical treatises is deliberate: division precedes definition to emphasize priority.

This passage from the commentary on Qutbi's treatise presents a simulated objection (naqd) questioning why the definition of absolute tasawwur is placed after the division of 'ilm rather than before it. The answer reveals that the division is the main point, and the definition serves as a reminder (tanbih). By following this exchange, you will learn how commentators use dialectical structure to clarify pedagogical priorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial objection
Find the mu'tarid's first question: Why can't the definition refer to 'ilm, and if it does, why isn't it placed before the division?
The objector assumes definition should precede what it defines.
2
Extract the first answer
The respondent says placing the definition between the two divisions emphasizes that the division is the main purpose; the definition is secondary because 'ilm is already known in some respect.
The argument is about pedagogical emphasis, not logical necessity.
3
Identify the follow-up objection
The mu'tarid then argues that the division itself already shows the common meaning of tasawwur, making the definition redundant.
This objection targets the necessity of the definition, not just its placement.
4
Understand the final answer
The respondent reiterates that the definition serves as a reminder (tanbih) to prevent heedlessness about what the division implies, and its full benefit will become clear later.
The answer defers resolution, acknowledging the objection but maintaining the placement.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange (naqd wa jawab) regarding why the commentator placed the definition of absolute tasawwur (which is synonymous with 'ilm) between the two divisions of 'ilm rather than before them. The objection argues that if absolute tasawwur is identical to 'ilm, then defining it after dividing 'ilm is redundant, and the definition should have preceded the division. The answer explains that the primary purpose is the division itself, as 'ilm is known in some respect, and the definition is only a reminder (tanbih) to alert that tasawwur is used in two senses: one synonymous with 'ilm (including tasdiq) and one specific (bare conception). The objection continues that the division already indicates the common meaning, so the definition seems unnecessary. The answer reiterates that the definition serves as a reminder, and its benefit will become apparent later.

Why the Logician Studies Words Incidentally
Author explains incidental need for words in logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why words are a secondary concern in logic and distinguish between the two contexts (teaching vs. personal reasoning) where words are needed.

Core Idea
Logic is about meanings, but words are the necessary bridge for teaching and learning.

In this passage, the author clarifies a common misconception: logic is not about words per se, but about the meanings that words convey. However, because we communicate and learn through language, words become an indispensable tool. Let's explore the author's reasoning and see how this applies to Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh, where understanding linguistic signification is crucial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's main thesis
Find where the author states that the logician qua logician has no concern with words. Notice the contrast: logic deals with the 'expository statement' (al-qawl al-sharih) and 'proof' (al-hujja) which are about meanings, not words.
2
Distinguish two scenarios for using words
The author presents two cases: (1) teaching another person, where words are necessary, and (2) reasoning for oneself, where words are not strictly necessary but practically very difficult to dispense with. Identify these two scenarios in the text.
Note the psychological habit of associating meanings with words.
3
Explain the psychological reason for word dependency
Find the part where the author describes how the soul has become accustomed to observing meanings through words. Explain why it is difficult to conceive pure meanings without words, referring to 'intuitive experience' (al-wijdan).
4
Conclude why word studies are included in logic
The author concludes that topics of words are made an introduction to logic because they are needed for teaching and learning, not because logic itself is about words. Summarize this conclusion.
This sets the stage for studying dalala (signification).

It also includes methods for proving the validity of conversion: conversion itself, reductio (khalf), and supposition (iftirad). The third and fourth investigations cover contraposition (aks al-naqid) and the correlation of conditionals. Then follows 'The Third Treatise on Syllogism (qiyas)', which has five chapters. The first chapter defines syllogism and its two main types: exceptive (istithna'i) and conjunctive (iqtirani). It then describes the four figures of the syllogism, their conditions for productivity, and their valid moods. The second chapter deals with mixed syllogisms (mukhtalatat), i.e., those combining propositions of different modalities, and provides tables for each figure. The third chapter covers conjunctive syllogisms composed of conditional propositions (mutasila and munfasila) in five sections. The fourth chapter treats the exceptive syllogism. This table of contents serves as a roadmap for the student, indicating the logical structure of the material to be studied.

Navigating the Qutbi's Table of Contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate and identify the main sections of the Qutbi text using its table of contents.

Core Idea
The Qutbi's table of contents maps the entire logical toolkit from syllogism to the five arts.

This table of contents is not just an index—it is a roadmap of the logical curriculum. By memorising it, you internalise the architecture of Islamic logic. The passage shows how the author organises the appendices of syllogism and then divides the conclusion into two investigations covering both certain and non-certain reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Orient to Chapter Five
Find the heading 'الفصل الخامس في لواحق القياس' (Chapter Five: On Appendices of Syllogism). There are four appendices listed: compound syllogism, syllogism of contradiction, induction, and analogy. These are extensions of simple categorical syllogism.
Memorising these four will help you recall the advanced types of reasoning covered later.
2
Examine the First Investigation of the Conclusion
Look at 'البحث الأول في مواد الأقيسة' (First Investigation: On the Matters of Syllogisms). Note that matters are divided into certainties (يقينيات) and non-certainties (غير اليقينيات). Under certainties is demonstration (برهان) split into two subtypes. Under non-certainties are five arts: dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, sophistry, and fallacy.
This classification tells you that the Qutbi treats both certain and probable reasoning as legitimate logical subjects.
3
Note the Second Investigation
Finally, find 'البحث الثاني في أجزاء العلوم' (Second Investigation: On the Parts of Sciences). This section likely discusses how sciences are structured. It appears at the very end of the book, serving as a capstone.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents of the Qutbi text. It presents the overall structure of the book. The student should note that after covering categorical syllogism in earlier chapters, the author dedicates Chapter Five to four appendices: compound syllogism (a chain of syllogisms), syllogism of contradiction (reductio ad absurdum), induction (complete enumeration), and analogy (reasoning by similarity). Then the conclusion covers the matters of syllogisms—certainties and non-certainties—and the parts of sciences. Under certainties, there is demonstration (burhan) divided into demonstration of why (limmi) and demonstration of fact (inni). Under non-certainties are dialectic (jadal), rhetoric (khataba), poetry (shi'r), sophistry (safsata), and fallacy (mughtalita). This overview establishes that the book will treat both certain and uncertain reasoning, and that the five arts are the major logical applications.
