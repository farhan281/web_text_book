Detecting Scribal Errors in Logic Manuscripts
Sharif al-Jurjani corrects a scribal error in the book's structure statement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a scribal error in a logical text by comparing the base text to the author's later statements.

Core Idea
Even in logic books, scribes err—commentators restore intent via internal cross-referencing.

This passage from Sharif al-Jurjani's commentary shows how scholars in the Islamic tradition corrected textual errors by comparing manuscript readings to the author's own later statements. You will learn how to detect such errors using internal consistency, a key skill in the hashiya tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being corrected
Find the phrase in the base text that Sharif flags: 'And I arranged it upon an introduction, three treatises, and a conclusion.' The word 'three' before 'treatises' is the target.
2
Find the contradictory evidence
Locate the author's later statement: 'As for the treatises, they are three.' This already specifies the number, making the earlier 'three' redundant and likely a scribal insertion.
3
Understand the correction
Sharif argues that 'three' is an extra word that copyists mistakenly inserted. The internal contradiction (the author says the same thing twice) is the proof.
This is a common method in textual criticism (tahqiq) in the Islamic tradition.
4
Apply to other manuscripts
When reading any classical text, note if early statements are contradicted by later ones. This can reveal copyist errors or interpolations.

Commentary
This passage is a critical note by Sharif al-Jurjani, a later commentator, correcting a textual error in the manuscript copies of the Shamsiyya. The author (Qutbi) originally wrote 'arranged it upon an introduction, treatises, and a conclusion' without specifying the number of treatises. However, the copyists mistakenly inserted the word 'three' (ثلاث) before 'treatises'. Sharif points out that this is an error because later in the text, the author explicitly says 'As for the treatises, they are three.' The insertion would create redundancy. This type of textual criticism is common in the hashiya tradition, where super-commentators refine and correct the main text based on manuscript evidence and internal consistency.

Using the Index of Names in a Logic Commentary
Index of names and page references for logicians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use an index of names to locate discussions of specific logicians in a classical logic commentary.

Core Idea
An index transforms a dense text into a searchable map of scholarly authorities.

You will often encounter indexes ('fahras') at the end of classical madrasa texts. They are not just appendices; they are essential tools for efficiently navigating lengthy commentaries. This lesson teaches you how to read such an index and use it to find where key logicians like Ibn Sina or al-Farabi are discussed, saving you hours of random page-flipping.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the index
The index is organized alphabetically or by category. In our passage, entries are listed under group headings (e.g., 'Ancient logicians') followed by names and page numbers. Notice the grouping: 'Qudama' al-mantiqiyyin', 'Ibn Sina', 'al-Qawm al-mantiqiyyun', etc.
Arabic indexes often follow Arabic alphabetical order, not English.
2
Locate a specific scholar
Scan the list for the scholar you need. For example, if you are studying a point made by al-Farabi, find 'Farabi' in the index and note the page numbers: 252, 253, 254, 328, 350, 360, 361, 414. These are the pages where al-Farabi is mentioned in the matn, sharh, or hashiya.
Page numbers may refer to different editions, so be consistent with your own copy.
3
Understand the scope of references
The index may distinguish between mentions in the main text (matn), commentary (sharh), and gloss (hashiya). The passage states 'al-warida fi al-matn wa al-sharh wa al-hashiya' – so a single entry covers all three layers. This helps you see which authorities are cited at which level.
Some page ranges, like '35 to 42' for al-Razi, indicate a sustained discussion.
4
Cross-reference with your reading goal
When reading a specific section of the commentary, consult the index to see which authorities are cited there. For example, if you are on page 150, the index tells you Ibn Sina appears there. This quickly contextualizes the argument.
Use the index as a starting point for further research on a scholar's views.

Commentary
This passage is an index of names (fahras al-a'lam) at the end of the commentary on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya. It lists logicians and philosophers referenced in the main text (matn), commentary (sharh), and gloss (hashiya), along with their page numbers. The index distinguishes groups like 'ancient logicians' (qudama' al-mantiqiyyin), 'the later scholars' (muta'akhkhirun), and 'the earlier scholars' (mutaqaddimun). Key figures include Ibn Sina, Farabi, Athir al-Din al-Abhari (author of the Isaghuji matn), Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, and the commentator himself (al-musannif). Such indexes were common in madrasa texts to facilitate reference. The page numbers allow students to locate discussions of specific authorities.
