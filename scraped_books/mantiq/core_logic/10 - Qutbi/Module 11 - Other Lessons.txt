Refutation of Circular and Infinite Regress in Knowledge Acquisition
Author argues that circularity and infinite regress make knowledge acquisition impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute circular reasoning and infinite regress in epistemological arguments using the transitivity of priority principle.

Core Idea
Circularity and infinite regress make knowledge acquisition impossible by forcing logical contradictions.

In this passage, the author demonstrates that knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq) cannot be acquired through circular reasoning or infinite regress. These are not merely inefficient methods; they are logically impossible. Understanding why will sharpen your ability to detect flawed definitions and arguments in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
The author states that if acquisition of knowledge were by circularity (dawr) or regress (tasalsul), acquisition would be impossible. Locate this claim in the Arabic.
It appears at the beginning: 'لو كان بطريق الدور أو التسلسل لامتنع التحصيل والاكتساب'.
2
Analyze the circularity argument
Notice the dependency chain: A depends on B, B depends on A. The author uses the transitivity of priority to show B must be prior to itself, a contradiction. Try to reconstruct the steps in your own words.
The key logical move is that if X is prior to Y and Y is prior to Z, then X is prior to Z.
3
Analyze the infinite regress argument
Here the dependency is an endless chain. The author argues that bringing to mind an infinite series is impossible, so the desired knowledge (which depends on that chain) is also impossible.
This argument assumes human finitude; it does not apply to God's knowledge.
4
Connect to Islamic epistemology
Understand that this argument is foundational for establishing that knowledge must rest on self-evident or axiomatic foundations (badihiyyat). Without it, all knowledge would be either circular or infinitely regressive.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument demonstrating the impossibility of acquiring knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq) if the process involved either circular reasoning (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). The author first states the claim: if acquisition were by either method, it would be impossible. He then explains each. For circularity, he uses the example of two items, 'A' and 'B', each depending on the other. This leads to a logical contradiction: 'B' would have to be obtained before its own obtaining, which is absurd. The argument uses the principle that priority is transitive: if X is prior to Y, and Y is prior to Z, then X is prior to Z. Here, if 'A' depends on 'B' (so 'B' is prior), and 'B' depends on 'A' (so 'A' is prior), then by transitivity 'B' is prior to itself. For infinite regress, the point is that the desired knowledge would depend on an infinite chain of prior acts of bringing to mind (istihdar), which is impossible for a finite mind. Since the dependant (the desired knowledge) depends on the impossible, it too is impossible. The argument is foundational in Islamic epistemology, showing that knowledge acquisition must rest on certain foundations.

Defusing Infinite Regress with the Mu'iddat Distinction
Author rebuts the objection that iterative acquisition requires infinite simultaneous knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze how the distinction between preparatory (mu'iddat) and efficient causes is used to defend against infinite regress objections in epistemological arguments.

Core Idea
Preparatory causes need not be simultaneous, blocking the requirement of infinite simultaneous knowledge.

This passage presents a defense against a classic objection: if knowledge is acquired through an iterative chain (tasalsul), wouldn't the mind need to hold an infinite number of items at once? The author's response turns on a subtle but powerful distinction between efficient causes (which must be simultaneous with their effect) and preparatory causes (mu'iddat), which merely dispose and can be spread over time. By classifying the infinite items as mu'iddat, the author defuses the objection and maintains the possibility of sequential learning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
Find the opponent's claim about tasalsul. The opponent argues that if knowledge acquisition is through successive steps (tasalsul), then obtaining the required knowledge would require retrieving infinite things at once (دفعة واحدة).
This is a common skeptical charge against any theory involving regress.
2
Extract the Author's First Move
The author does not concede the entailment. He reclassifies the infinite items as 'mu'iddat' (preparatory causes), not efficient causes. Preparatory causes are not required to be simultaneous with their effect.
The key term is 'معدات' – plural of mu'iddah.
3
Understand the Mu'iddat Distinction
In Islamic philosophy, an efficient cause ('illah) must coexist with its effect. A preparatory cause (mu'iddah) only disposes the recipient and can precede the effect. This distinction is crucial for denying simultaneity.
This distinction is debated in kalam and falsafa.
4
Follow the Two Scenarios
The author considers two possibilities: If the opponent claims the infinite things must be retrieved at once or in a finite time, the author denies the entailment. If over infinite time, the author concedes but denies the absurdity, positing an eternal soul that has been acquiring infinite perceptions over infinite past time.
The second scenario allows the required knowledge to arise now based on past infinite perceptions.
5
Assess the Defense Strategy
The author shifts the burden: the opponent assumed efficient causality, but the author reframes as preparatory. This blocks the charge of requiring actual infinity of simultaneous mental contents, preserving the model of sequential learning.
This strategy is typical in kalam debates about causality and knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents a defense against the objection that knowledge acquired through an iterative process (tasalsul) would require the mind to grasp an infinite number of items simultaneously. The author argues that the infinite items are merely preparatory (mu'iddat) and such preparatory causes do not need to be contemporaneous. To strengthen the argument, he considers two scenarios: if the infinite items must be present at once or in a finite time, he denies the necessity. If they occur over infinite time, he allows it, positing the possibility of an eternal soul that has been acquiring perceptions over infinite past time, thus enabling retrieval of the required knowledge now. This defense relies on the distinction between efficient causes (which must be simultaneous) and preparatory causes (which need not be). The author is responding to a skeptical charge that any chain of reasoning leading to a conclusion must involve an actual infinity of premises, which is impossible. By denying the simultaneity requirement and invoking the eternity of the soul, he maintains the possibility of sequential learning.

إدراكات (idrakat): Perceptions or cognitions; the soul's apprehensions. [Note: Stable term.]

The Reality of a Science is Its Issues
Author argues that the reality of every science is its issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the author's thesis that the reality of a science is its issues and distinguish it from the roles of subject and principles.

Core Idea
The reality of a science is its issues; subject and principles are mere scaffolding.

What makes a science what it is? Is it its subject matter, its principles, or the issues it investigates? This passage from a commentary on Qutb al-Din al-Razi's work presents a 'noble benefit' on this question. The author argues that the essence of any science is its issues — the specific propositions it studies — and that the subject and principles are merely necessary aids.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central thesis
Find where the author states that 'the reality of every science is its issues' and note the justification: issues are collected first, then the name is given.
The phrase 'حقيقة كل علم مسائله' is the key claim.
2
Distinguish the two senses of science names
The author notes that names like 'logic' or 'grammar' can refer either to the specific information itself or to the knowledge of that information. Understand how each sense affects the definition of reality.
On the first sense, reality = issues; on the second, reality = affirmations (tasdiqat).
3
Understand the objection about parts
An objector argues that according to the author's own later classification, sciences have three parts: subject, principles, and issues. So why claim only issues are the reality?
The objection is based on a prior statement about the divisions of a science.
4
Grasp the response that only issues are essential
The author answers that the subject and principles are needed but not essential. The subject connects issues into a coherent science, and principles are foundations. Only the issues are the intended by essence (المقصود بالذات).
Some scholars include subject and principles as parts out of tolerance, but the first view is more appropriate.

Three Types of Verbal Signification: مطابقة, تضمن, التزام
Author defines the three types of verbal signification with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three types of verbal signification (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) and correctly classify the signification of any given word-meaning pair from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
A word signifies its full meaning, a part of it, or something necessarily attached to it.

When a word is assigned to a meaning (wad‘), it can convey that meaning in three distinct ways: as a whole, as a part, or as an external necessary concomitant. This foundational concept in Islamic logic (mantiq) underpins how scholars interpret terms in the Quran, Hadith, and fiqh. Understanding these distinctions prevents confusion when analyzing definitions and legal inferences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and the meaning being signified.
Pick a word (lafz) and the meaning (ma‘na) it conveys. For example, the word 'insan' and the meaning 'rational animal'.
The meaning must be related to the word's original placement (wad‘).
2
Check if the meaning is the full, a part, or external.
Ask: Is this meaning exactly what the word was originally placed for? If yes, it is mutabaqa. Is it a part of that meaning? Then it is tadammun. Is it something outside the original meaning but necessarily connected? Then it is iltizam.
The distinction between part and external is crucial: parts are inside the definition; external concomitants are not.
3
Apply the definitions to the given example.
Using 'insan' examples from the passage: 'rational animal' is mutabaqa, 'animal' alone is tadammun, 'capable of writing' is iltizam.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of verbal signification known as dalalat al-mutabaqa, al-tadammun, and al-iltizam. The author begins by explaining that when a word is assigned (wad') to a meaning, its signification of that full meaning is called mutabaqa (conformity). For example, the word 'insan' signifies the composite meaning 'rational animal' in its entirety. Next, tadammun (inclusion) occurs when the word signifies a part of that meaning, such as 'insan' signifying 'animal' alone or 'rational' alone. Finally, iltizam (implication) is when the word signifies something external to the original meaning but necessarily connected to it, like the signification of 'insan' for 'capable of knowledge and writing', which is not part of the definition but is a necessary concomitant. These distinctions are foundational for understanding how terms convey meanings in logic.

Conditions of Implicative Indication: Mental vs External Concomitance
Author explains why the definition of implicative indication requires a qualification and states its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the condition of mental concomitance from external concomitance in implicative indication and apply it to examples from Islamic legal theory.

Core Idea
Implicative indication requires mental necessity, not real-world connection.

The author refines the definition of dalalah iltizamiyyah by adding a crucial restriction to exclude conformity cases. He then clarifies its essential condition: the implied meaning must be mentally inseparable from the primary meaning, even if they are not connected in external reality. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between different types of linguistic indication and is essential for deriving implicit meanings in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its restriction
Find the term 'dalalah iltizamiyyah' in the Arabic and note the phrase 'bi-tawassut al-wad'' (mediation of the positing). Understand that without this restriction, the definition would mistakenly include conformity indication.
The author uses the example of 'sun' indicating 'light' — if not restricted, this would be wrongly classified as implicative.
2
Extract the required condition: mental concomitance
Locate where the author states that the external meaning must be such that conceiving the named necessitates conceiving it in the mind. This is the condition of 'al-luzum al-dhihni'.
3
Identify the non-required condition: external concomitance
Find the phrase 'wa la yushtarat fiha al-luzum al-khariji'. The author explicitly rejects the need for a real-world necessary connection.
The example 'blindness' indicating 'sight' illustrates this: blindness does not always coexist with sight externally, yet the mental link exists.
4
Apply the distinction to a new example
Think of the word 'wine' (khamr) in Islamic law. Its primary meaning is a specific drink. It implicatively indicates intoxication. Check: Does conceiving wine necessitate conceiving intoxication in the mind? Yes. Does external existence of wine always entail existence of intoxication? Yes, but that is not required — only the mental link matters.
This shows how the tool works for deriving rulings on intoxicants beyond wine.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary that expands on the definition and conditions of implicative indication (dalalah iltizamiyyah). It begins by justifying a crucial restriction in the definition: the phrase 'mediation of the positing' (bi-tawassut al-wad'). Without this restriction, the definition would mistakenly include cases of conformity indication (dalalah mutabaqiyyah). For example, if the word 'sun' is used to mean 'light', that is strictly conformity indication because the word 'sun' can be posited for 'light' as one of its meanings. But without the restriction, the description 'indication of the word to what is outside the posited meaning' would also apply here, since 'light' is not the primary posited meaning of 'sun' (the sun itself is). Thus, the restriction excludes such cases. The passage then states the conditions for implicative indication: it requires mental concomitance (luzum dhihni) — that is, whenever the named concept (the primary meaning) is conceptualized, the external meaning must necessarily be conceptualized in the mind. This condition is necessary because the word itself is not placed for that external meaning; its understanding depends entirely on this mental concomitance. Without it, the external meaning would not be understood from the word. However, external concomitance (luzum khariji) — a necessary connection in the external world — is not required. The example given is the word 'blindness' (al-'ama) indicating sight (al-basar). In external reality, blindness does not always entail the existence of sight (a blind person lacks sight, but one can be without sight yet not be blind, e.g., when eyes are closed). Yet the word 'blindness' implies the concept of sight in the mind because we understand blindness as the absence of sight. So the condition is purely mental. The reasoning is that word-meaning relations via positing are either direct positing or necessary mental following. Since the external meaning is not directly posited, only the second route remains, which requires that mental concomitance be present.

Qualified Syllogisms in Islamic Logic: The Case of Tadmin and Iltizam
Dialectical refinement of the argument that tadmin and iltizam imply mutabaqa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the categorical syllogism for tadmin and iltizam's implication of mutabaqa, identify the role of the qualifier 'min haythu', and evaluate the dialectical resolution of the middle term repetition problem.

Core Idea
A qualifier like 'from the perspective of' must be placed carefully to avoid invalidating a syllogism.

This passage from a classical logical commentary explores a subtle point: does the fact that inclusion (tadmin) and necessary implication (iltizam) are 'followers' of correspondence (mutabaqa) guarantee that they imply it? The author argues yes, but only if we correctly handle the qualifier 'from the perspective that it is a follower'. This lesson walks through the syllogistic structure and the dialectical refinement, using an example from usul al-fiqh: the implication of a command (amr) for obligation (wujub).

Guided Path
1
Identify the original syllogism
The author claims: Tadmin and iltizam are followers of mutabaqa. A follower qua follower does not exist without its followed. Therefore, tadmin and iltizam do not exist without mutabaqa. Write this as a categorical syllogism in the first figure.
The middle term is 'follower'. The qualifier 'qua follower' is added to the major premise to block counterexamples like heat existing without fire.
2
Locate the objection about the middle term
The objector says: If we use the qualified 'follower qua follower' in the minor premise (i.e., 'tadmin is a follower qua follower of mutabaqa'), then the middle term in the minor is 'follower qua follower', but in the major it is simply 'follower'. Thus the middle term is not repeated, and the syllogism fails.
The objection hinges on the logical rule that the middle term must be identical in both premises.
3
Analyze the proposed answer: attach qualifier to predicate
The author responds: The qualifier 'from the perspective that it is a follower' should belong to the predicate ('does not exist without its followed'), not to the subject ('follower'). This way, the middle term remains 'follower' unqualified, and the major premise becomes: 'Every follower does not exist without its followed when considered as a follower.' This preserves the syllogism.
But now the conclusion is qualified: tadmin does not exist without mutabaqa only when considered as a follower.
4
Evaluate the resolution and the alternative
The qualified conclusion is weaker than the intended absolute statement. Some argue that the attribute of followership is essential to tadmin and iltizam, so the qualified entails the absolute. The author prefers a different approach: tadmin and iltizam imply the positing (wad') which implies mutabaqa, making the syllogism direct and free of qualifiers.
This alternative avoids the dialectical complications entirely.

Why Only Mutabaqa in Word Classification
Commentator defends restricting classification to mutabaqa against objection via 'Abdullah example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical argument for restricting word classification to dalala al-mutabaqa and explain why the 'Abdullah example does not refute it.

Core Idea
Including tadammun or iltizam makes words both mufrad and murakkab under one convention, causing confusion.

In the science of Arabic linguistics, words are classified as simple (mufrad) or compound (murakkab) based on whether a part of the word indicates a part of the meaning. But there are three types of indication: correspondence (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadammun), and implication (iltizam). Why focus only on mutabaqa? This passage unpacks the logical reasoning behind that choice, addressing a subtle objection that might otherwise undermine the classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of dalala
Recall that dalala al-mutabaqa is whole-word-to-whole-meaning, tadammun is part-to-part, and iltizam is the word to an accompanying meaning. Understand how each relates a word to its meaning.
In mutabaqa, every part of the word corresponds to a part of the meaning; in tadammun, a part of the word indicates a part of the meaning; in iltizam, the whole word indicates an external concomitant.
2
Define tarkib and ifrad under mutabaqa
A word is murakkab if a part of it, under mutabaqa, indicates a part of its meaning; it is mufrad if no part does so. For example, 'Abdullah ('slave of Allah') is murakkab when analyzed as 'abd' + 'Allah' because each part corresponds to a part of the meaning under mutabaqa.
3
Explore the consequences of including tadammun or iltizam
Consider two possibilities: (A) require a part to indicate under all three types simultaneously – this is absurd because almost no word would be compound. (B) be satisfied with indication under any one type – then a word could be mufrad under tadammun but murakkab under mutabaqa, making it both simple and compound at once.
The commentator shows that option A is 'very far-fetched' and option B leads to a contradiction under a single convention.
4
Analyze the 'Abdullah objection
The objection says: In 'Abdullah, the word can be mufrad (as a proper noun) and murakkab (as a descriptive phrase) in different contexts, so why not allow the same here? The response is that in 'Abdullah, the two statuses occur under different conventions (different uses), so no confusion; but here, the same word in the same utterance would be both mufrad and murakkab under the same convention, causing an unacceptable confusion in classification.
The key distinction is that the two states in 'Abdullah are not simultaneous under one convention.
5
Conclude the argument
Only mutabaqa avoids this contradiction because it provides a clear, single criterion: a part of the word either does or does not indicate a part of the meaning under correspondence, with no ambiguity from other indication types.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining why in the classification of words into mufrad (simple) and murakkab (compound), only the indication of correspondence (dalala al-mutabaqa) is considered, not the indication of inclusion (tadammun) or implication (iltizam). The commentator begins by stating the principle: when we classify a word as simple or compound, we look at whether a part of the word indicates a part of its meaning under the correspondence relation. For example, the word 'slave' in 'Abdullah (slave of Allah) under correspondence indicates a part of the meaning? Actually the example of 'Abdullah is discussed later. The commentator then considers what would happen if we were to consider tadammun or iltizam. He presents two possibilities: either we require that a part of the word indicate a part of the meaning under all three types of indication simultaneously, or we are satisfied with indication under any one. The first is absurd because it would make almost no word compound. The second leads to a word being both simple and compound at the same time, depending on which indication we consider. For instance, a compound word like 'slave' in 'Abdullah might be simple under tadammun if the meaning has no parts. The objection raised is that this simultaneity is not a problem; after all, the word 'Abdullah itself can be considered mufrad when it becomes a proper noun and murakkab when considered as a descriptive phrase. The response is that in the case of 'Abdullah, the two statuses occur in different contexts and conventions, so there is no confusion. But in the case of considering two different types of indication for the same word in the same utterance, the word would be both mufrad and murakkab at once, leading to confusion in classification. Therefore, only the correspondence indication is reliable. The commentary ends with a gloss by Sharif emphasizing this.

Tarkib by Tadammun/Iltizam is Derivative of Mutabaqa
Commentator refines the argument, showing that tarkib by tadammun/iltizam entails tarkib by mutabaqa, so only mutabaqa need be considered.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why composition (tarkib) by inclusion (tadammun) or implication (iltizam) necessarily implies composition by correspondence (mutabaqa), and why mutabaqa alone is sufficient for defining tarkib.

Core Idea
Composition by inclusion or implication is derivative; only correspondence directly captures the structure.

This passage addresses a subtle objection: if tadammun and iltizam are valid types of indication, then a word with a simple meaning could be considered compound under tadammun or iltizam. The commentator refines that such composition is only possible when there is also composition by mutabaqa. Understanding this refinement clarifies why logicians focus on mutabaqa alone when classifying words as mufrad or murakkab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection claims that if tadammun and iltizam are considered, then a word with a simple meaning under mutabaqa (e.g., a simple concept) would be classified as murakkab under tadammun/iltizam because part of the word could indicate a part of the meaning under those relations. This would undermine the exclusive focus on mutabaqa.
The objector uses the example of a word composed of two parts but assigned to a single simple meaning.
2
Recognize the analogy with 'Abdullah
The commentator points out that a word can be murakkab under one meaning and mufrad under another, as in 'Abdullah: as a descriptive phrase it is murakkab (by mutabaqa), but as a proper noun it is mufrad (also by mutabaqa). Similarly, a word could be murakkab under mutabaqa but mufrad under tadammun/iltizam.
This shows that the two classifications are not contradictory.
3
Understand the refinement
The commentator argues that tarkib by tadammun or iltizam is only realized if it is realized by mutabaqa. For tadammun: if a part of the word indicates a part of the meaning under tadammun, it must also indicate a part of the meaning under mutabaqa (since the tadammun part is part of the whole meaning). For iltizam: similar reasoning applies because the concomitant meaning is not directly indicated.
Thus, tarkib by other modes is not independent; it always presupposes tarkib by mutabaqa.
4
Apply the conclusion
Since tarkib by tadammun/iltizam is derivative, we can safely focus on mutabaqa when determining if a word is murakkab. The converse (simplicity under mutabaqa implying simplicity under tadammun/iltizam) does not always hold, but the definition of tarkib is about compound structure, which is captured by mutabaqa.
The commentator notes that discussing ifrad here is a digression; the main point is about tarkib.

A term's simplicity or composition is not absolute but depends on the type of signification considered.

As for the implicative, because whenever a part of the word indicates a part of its implicative meaning by implication, it has indicated a part of the conformational meaning by conformation, because it is impossible for implication to exist without conformation. And it was objected that although implicative signification entails conformation, the composition of the word with respect to implication does not entail its composition with respect to conformation, because it is possible that the implicative meaning is compound and a part of the word indicates a part of it, while the conformational meaning is not like that, and there is no absurdity in that, since then implication without conformation would not be entailed, but rather composition of the implicative significand without the conformational significand would be entailed; and there is no proof of the impossibility of that. If you say: when a part of the word indicates a part of the implicative meaning, it is not necessary that this signification be by implication, because although the implicative meaning is external to the conformational meaning, it is not necessary that the parts of the implicative meaning be external to the conformational meaning, because the composite of internal and external is external. I say: its signification of a part of the implicative meaning is either implicative, inclusive, or conformational; and on all three suppositions, that part of the word has a conformational significand, and it is also necessary that the other part of the word has another conformational significand—as we have shown—so composition with respect to conformation is definitely entailed (al-Sharif).

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the definition of simple and compound terms in logic. The commentator first states a common view: a term composed of two words with simple meanings is itself simple, because no part of the compound indicates a part of the meaning by inclusion; similarly, a compound term with a simple mental concomitant is simple. However, he raises an objection: the word's simplicity or composition should be considered relative to different types of meaning—conformational, inclusive, and implicative. For example, 'Abdullah' is compound in its conformational meaning before becoming a proper noun, but simple after. Therefore, it is permissible for a word to be simple with respect to one type of meaning and compound with respect to another. The commentator then offers a better formulation: simplicity and composition relative to inclusive or implicative meaning only occur if they occur relative to conformational meaning. He explains that for inclusive meaning, if a part of the word indicates a part of the inclusive meaning, it ipso facto indicates a part of the conformational meaning because the inclusive is a part of the conformational. For implicative meaning, if a part indicates a part of the implicative meaning by implication, it must also indicate a part of the conformational meaning by conformation, since implication presupposes conformation. An objector argues that composition in implicative meaning does not entail composition in conformational meaning, because the implicative meaning could be compound while the conformational meaning remains simple. The commentator refutes this by showing that if a part of the word indicates a part of the implicative meaning, that part must have a conformational significand; otherwise, implication would lack a foundation. Furthermore, the other part of the word must also have a distinct conformational significand, leading to composition in conformational meaning as well. Finally, he addresses a hypothetical objection: the indication of a part of the implicative meaning might not be by implication but by inclusion or conformation, but in all cases, each part has a conformational significand, so composition in conformational meaning follows.

A) Because they have different meanings: 'speaker' refers to the ability to speak, while 'eloquent' refers to a quality of speech.
B) Because they refer to different objects.
C) Because they are both adjectives.
D) Because one is a noun and the other is an adjective.

Key Takeaway
Synonymy requires identity of meaning (concept), not merely identity of reference; the logical error of assuming converse conversion underlies false claims of synonymy.
