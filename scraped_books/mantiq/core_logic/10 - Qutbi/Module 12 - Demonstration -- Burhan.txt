The Soul's Pre-Eternity and Infinite Knowledge
Objector argues infinite knowledge possible if soul is eternal; commentator responds it relies on creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical argument about the soul's pre-eternity and infinite knowledge, identifying the conditional structure and the summary/detailed distinction.

Core Idea
Infinite knowledge requires infinite time; possibility hinges on the soul's creation or pre-eternity.

This passage presents a key objection in Islamic epistemology: if the soul is created, can it possess infinite knowledge? The objector claims that if the soul is eternal, infinite knowledge is possible. The commentator responds that the proof of the soul's creation is already established, and then distinguishes between summary (mujmal) and detailed (mufaṣṣal) knowledge to resolve the puzzle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
Find the conditional assertion: if the soul is eternal, then it can have infinite sciences over infinite times. The objector denies that infinite recall in infinite time is impossible.
2
Isolate the commentator's response
The commentator points out that the proof of creation is already given; then introduces a distinction: summary grasp of infinite things at once is possible, detailed grasp is not.
3
Distinguish summary vs detailed knowledge
Notice that the soul can hold infinite knowledge in summary form (mujmal) at the moment of seeking, but detailed knowledge unfolds over time. This resolves the apparent impossibility.
4
Analyze the conditional structure
Map the argument: If soul is created → infinite simultaneous detailed knowledge impossible. If soul is eternal → infinite sequential detailed knowledge possible. The objector assumes the soul is eternal; the commentator reaffirms creation.

Self-Evident Derivation: Answering the Regress Objection
Author rejects that deriving acquired from self-evident requires a further law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical objection against deriving acquired logic from self-evident premises and explain the author's reply that the reasoning itself is self-evident.

Core Idea
The mind sees the connection directly; self-evident derivation needs no extra rule.

A common objection in logic is that if we derive acquired rules from self-evident ones, we need a rule for that derivation, leading to an infinite regress. The author responds by asserting that the derivation itself is self-evident—the mind directly perceives the logical connection. This lesson unpacks this dialectical exchange and shows how it avoids the regress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
The objector argues: deriving acquired from self-evident requires nazar (reasoning), and knowing that nazar requires another law, leading to infinite regress. Locate this in the text: 'فإن قيل : استفادة البعض الكسبي من البعض البديهي إنما تكون بطريق النظر، فيحتاج في معرفة ذلك النظر إلى قانون آخر فيعود المحذور.'
This is the classic problem of circularity or regress in logic.
2
Extract the author's reply (jawab)
The author says: 'ذلك النظر أيضاً بديهي'—that reasoning itself is self-evident. So the acquired part of logic is derived from the self-evident part through a self-evident path, stopping the regress.
The author does not deny that nazar occurs, but claims its validity is immediately known.
3
Analyse the second objection
A further objection reformulates the problem: if logic is needed, it must be either self-evident or acquired. Self-evident logic implies no need to learn it (but we do learn it), and acquired logic leads back to the same problem. The passage ends abruptly.
This objection likely forces a more precise definition of what 'logic' means in the original claim.
4
Evaluate the dialectical outcome
Though the text is cut off, the author's first reply stands on its own: some reasoning is foundational and does not require a further law. This is a key move in the foundationalist approach to logic.
In jadal (dialectical debate), the opponent's objection is answered by showing it misconceives the nature of the derivation.

Analyzing Two Proofs on Composite Reality
Author presents two proofs against composition of reality from equal parts and responds to objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two arguments against composition of reality from equal parts and evaluate the refutations provided in the commentary.

Core Idea
The proofs rely on external composition; refutations show mental parts need not cause circularity.

This passage from Qutbi's commentary on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya presents two sophisticated arguments against the possibility of a true reality being composed of two equal essential parts. The commentator then refutes both arguments by carefully distinguishing between external and mental parts and by clarifying the meaning of 'accident'. Understanding this dialectic sharpens your ability to spot fallacious assumptions in metaphysical reasoning, a skill essential for kalam and usul al-fiqh debates.

Guided Path
1
State the initial claim
Identify the position the author is attacking: that a true reality (mahiyya haqiqiyya) can be composed of two equal things (dhatiyan).
The claim is the target of the two proofs.
2
Unpack the first proof
The first proof is a conditional argument. If composed of two equals, either they need each other or not. If not, impossible; if yes, either circularity (both need each other) or preponderance without preponderant (one needs the other). Both lead to absurdity.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum.
3
Unpack the second proof
The second proof considers a highest genus like substance composed of two equal parts. It examines possibilities: if one part is an accident → substance subsists by accident (impossible); if substance, then either identical to whole, included in it, or external—each leads to contradiction.
This uses a disjunctive analysis.
4
Analyze the refutation of the first proof
The commentator denies that parts of a true reality always need each other absolutely. He distinguishes external distinct parts (which do need each other) from mental predicated parts (which do not). He also allows need from different aspects, avoiding circularity.
This shows the proof only applies to external existence.
5
Analyze the refutation of the second proof
The commentator chooses that one part is predicated as substance while substance is external. He then denies that an accident must be entirely external to a thing, using the example of man and 'rational'—man is not identical to rational nor a part, yet not entirely external. He distinguishes two meanings of 'accident'.
This clarifies the notion of accident in logical vs. metaphysical senses.

Distinguishing Claim from Definition in Logical Proofs
Author addresses a misstatement in the claim and defends the use of contrapositive conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a petitio principii (begging the question) fallacy in a logical argument and evaluate whether an apparent circularity is actually a legitimate definition or interpretation.

Core Idea
A statement that appears to beg the question may actually be a valid interpretation of the claim.

In this passage, the author critiques a formulation in a proof involving contrapositive conversion. The critic claims the wording makes the claim part of the proof, a fallacy known as begging the question. The commentator defends the wording by explaining it is an interpretation or definition, not the claim itself. This lesson will help you distinguish between a circular argument and a legitimate explanatory definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criticized statement
Find the phrase being criticized: 'The truth of the negation of the specific on everything of which the negation of the general is true without conversion.' Recognize that this is a technical statement about logical relations.
2
Understand the critic's objection
The critic says this statement makes the claim part of the proof, which is a petitio principii. Paraphrase: the statement IS the claim to be proven, so using it as evidence assumes the conclusion.
Petitio principii is also called circular reasoning or begging the question.

Proof that Real Species Cannot Have Another Real Species Above It
Proof that real species cannot be arranged hierarchically

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof by contradiction that a real species cannot have another real species above it, using the concept of complete essence.

Core Idea
A real species is the complete essence—it cannot be subsumed under another real species without contradiction.

In this passage, the author demonstrates why real species (al-naw' al-haqiqi) cannot be arranged hierarchically. The proof relies on the definition of a real species as the complete essence (tamam al-mahiyya) of its individuals. By assuming the opposite—that a real species could have another real species above it—the author shows that this leads to an impossibility: either the lower species would have two complete essences, or the higher would become a genus. This careful logical argument is essential for understanding the taxonomy of universals in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of real species
A real species is defined as the complete essence (تمام الماهية) of every individual under it; it fully captures what that thing is. For example, 'man' is the complete essence of each individual human.
This is not a relative species, which is defined by predication.
2
Assume the contradictory hypothesis
Suppose there is a real species A and another real species B that is above A in the hierarchy (i.e., B includes A under it).
3
Analyze the consequences of the hypothesis
Since B is supposed to be a real species, it must also be the complete essence of all its individuals, including those of A. Thus for any individual of A, both A and B would be complete essences.
The individual would then have two complete essences, which is impossible.
4
Identify the contradiction
Either A includes B plus an addition, making A not a real species but a subordinate genus; or B is only a common essence (not specific), making B a genus and not a real species. Both contradict the assumption.
The author uses the example of man and animal to illustrate this.
5
Conclude the impossibility
Since the hypothesis leads to contradiction, the hypothesis is false: no real species can have another real species above it. The same logic shows that no real species can have another real species below it either.

Critique of Causal Methods: Dawaran and Sabr wa Taqsim
Author critiques two methods for determining causation: coincidence and division of attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical weaknesses in dawaran (coincidence) and sabr wa taqsim (division of attributes) as methods for establishing causality, and apply this critique to qiyas arguments in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Correlation is not causation: dawaran proves coincidence, not causal necessity.

In Islamic logic and jurisprudence, establishing the cause ('illa) of a ruling is essential for analogical reasoning (qiyas). Two common methods for identifying causes are al-dawaran (observing constant coincidence) and al-sabr wa al-taqsim (exhaustive enumeration and elimination of attributes). However, both methods have serious logical flaws, as this passage from a commentary on al-Qutbi explains. Understanding these flaws is crucial for evaluating the strength of qiyas arguments and avoiding fallacious reasoning in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods under critique
First, locate in the Arabic the mention of al-dawaran and al-sabr wa al-taqsim. Note that the author begins by declaring both methods weak (ضعيفان).
2
Analyze the objection to al-dawaran
The author argues that the 'last part of the complete cause' or the 'co-extensive condition' can coincide with the effect without being its cause. This shows that constant accompaniment does not imply causation. In Islamic examples, day and night coincide but neither causes the other.
This is a classic fallacy of post hoc ergo propter hoc or correlation implying causation.
3
Analyze the two objections to al-sabr wa al-taqsim
First objection: The enumeration of possible attributes may not be exhaustive, so the true cause could be omitted. Second objection: Even if enumeration is complete, a common attribute that is a cause in one case may not be a cause in another due to context-specific conditions or preventers.
In usul al-fiqh, this means the 'illa derived from the asl (original case) may not transfer to the far' (branch) if the asl had a special condition or the far' has a preventer (mani').
4
Evaluate the overall force of the critique
Consider how these objections challenge the reliability of causal inference in qiyas. The critique does not completely invalidate qiyas but shows that extra care is needed to confirm causality beyond mere coincidence or simple enumeration.
This aligns with the broader discipline of al-mantiq, where demonstration (burhan) requires certainty, and these methods yield only probability (zann).

If it is not like that – rather it is a cause for the relation only in the mind – then it is an 'inni burhan' because it gives the fact (inniyya) of the relation externally – without its reason – like our saying: 'This one is feverish, and every feverish one has putrefied humors, so this one has putrefied humors.' So fever, although it is a cause for the affirmation of putrefaction of humors in the mind, is not a cause externally; rather the opposite is the case.

Commentary
This passage defines the two types of demonstrative syllogism (burhan). The author first quotes an earlier statement that a burhan is composed of six certain premises (the five certain types plus the first principles? Actually the 'six' refers to the six types of certain premises mentioned earlier: awaliyat, mushahadat, mujarrabat, hadsiyat, mutawatirat, and fitriyat? The text says 'these six' referring to the preceding enumeration). Then he corrects it: burhan is any syllogism of certain premises, not just the six. The key criterion is the middle term's causal role. In limmi (a priori), the middle term is a cause both in the mind (for inference) and in reality (for the existence of the relation). Example: putrefied humors cause fever, so from 'this has putrefied humors' and 'every putrefied humor case is feverish' we conclude 'this is feverish'. In inni (a posteriori), the middle term is only a cause in the mind for inferring the major from the minor, but not in reality. Example: fever is a sign of putrefied humors, but fever does not cause putrefaction; rather the opposite is true. The author emphasizes that inni gives us only the fact (inniyya) of the relation, not the explanation (limmiyya). This distinction is crucial in Islamic logic for understanding the difference between causal demonstrations (limmi) and evidential demonstrations (inni). The medical examples are typical in this tradition.

Burhan Inni: The Demonstration of Existence
Author defines برهان إني and distinguishes it from برهان لمي

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Burhan Inni from Burhan Limmi by determining whether the middle term is a cause in the mind or in external reality.

Core Idea
Burhan Inni proves that something is, but not why it is — the middle term only explains mentally, not causally.

In this passage, the author defines a crucial type of demonstration in Islamic logic: Burhan Inni (demonstration of existence). Unlike Burhan Limmi, which gives the real cause, Burhan Inni only gives a mental cause. Understanding this distinction is vital for evaluating the strength of arguments in Kalam and Fiqh, where we often reason from observed effects to unseen causes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of demonstration
Find the names Burhan Inni and Burhan Limmi in the passage. Notice that the author contrasts them based on the role of the middle term.
2
Focus on the middle term's role
Determine whether the middle term is a cause in the mind (fi al-dhihn) or in external reality (fi al-kharij). The passage states that for Burhan Inni, the middle term is a cause only in the mind.
3
Analyze the fever example
Break down the example: 'This person has a fever; every person with a fever has putrefied humors; so this person has putrefied humors.' Identify the middle term (fever). Ask: Does fever cause putrefied humors in reality? No, putrefied humors cause fever. So fever is a cause only in the mind for inferring putrefied humors.
4
Apply the distinction to new cases
Given an argument, check if the middle term is a real cause or just a mental sign. If it is a mental cause only, it is Burhan Inni; if it is the real external cause, it is Burhan Limmi.

Commentary
This passage defines the 'برهان إني' (demonstration of existence). The author explains that when the middle term is not the real cause of the predicate's existence in the external world but only in the mind, the proof is called برهان إني. The example given is: 'This person has a fever; everyone with a fever has putrefied humors; therefore this person has putrefied humors.' This distinguishes it from برهان لمي, where the middle term is the real cause.
