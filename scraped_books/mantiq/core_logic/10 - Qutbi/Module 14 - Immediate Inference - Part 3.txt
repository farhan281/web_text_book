Contraposition of Categorical Propositions
Author defines contraposition and explains its rules for categorical propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to produce the contrapositive of any categorical proposition and determine whether the contraposition is valid.

Core Idea
Contraposition swaps negated terms while preserving truth.

In the previous lesson on simple conversion, you learned to swap subject and predicate. Contraposition goes further: it negates both terms and swaps them, while preserving the truth of the proposition. This operation is a powerful tool for logical inference, especially in theological arguments. In this lesson, we will explore the rules of contraposition for categorical propositions as laid out by the classical logicians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's quality and quantity
Determine whether it is affirmative or negative, universal or particular. This determines how contraposition operates.
2
Negate both terms and swap them
For a universal affirmative 'Every J is B', the contrapositive is 'Every non-B is non-J'. Notice that the predicate 'B' is negated to become the subject, and the subject 'J' is negated to become the predicate.
Affirmative propositions become negative in quality after contraposition, and vice versa.
3
Adjust quantity if needed
Universal affirmatives remain universal; universal and particular negatives become particular negatives; particular affirmatives do not have a valid contrapositive.
The particular affirmative fails because its contrapositive would be a false proposition.
4
Verify with a contradiction check
If the contrapositive were false, you would derive a logical contradiction (e.g., 'Some non-B is B'), confirming the validity of contraposition.
The author uses this proof technique for each rule.

He said: The third inquiry: On contraposition. It is the act of making the first part of the proposition the negation of the second, and the second the same as the first, while differing from the original in quality and agreeing in truth.
(1) Statement: The ancient logicians said: Contraposition is making the negation of the second part the first part, and the negation of the first part the second, while preserving the quality and truth. So if we say "Every human is an animal," its contrapositive is "Every non-animal is not human."
The rule of affirmative propositions in it is the same as that of negative propositions in simple conversion, and vice versa, such that the universal affirmative converts into itself. So if our statement "Every J is B" is true, it converts into our statement "Every non-B is non-J"; otherwise, "Some non-B is J" and it converts by simple conversion into our statement "Some J is not B," yet it was "Every J is B" — this is a contradiction.
And it is joined with the original thus: "Some non-B is J" and "Every J is B" yields "Some non-B is B," which is impossible.
The particular affirmative does not convert, because our statement "Some animal is not human" is true, while "Some human is not animal" is false.
The negative — whether universal or particular — converts into a particular negative. So if we say "No J is B" or "Some J is not B," then let "Not some non-B is non-J" be true; otherwise, "Every non-B is non-J" and it converts by contraposition into our statement "Every J is B" while it was "No J is B" or "Some J is not B" — this is a contradiction.
Likewise, the universal affirmative conditional converts into itself, because if "Whenever A is B, then C is D" is true, then "Whenever C is not D, then A is not B" because negation of the consequent necessitates negation of the antecedent; otherwise, it would be possible for the consequent to be negated while the antecedent remains, which destroys the implication between them.
The particular affirmative does not convert, because our statement "Sometimes if a thing is an animal, then it is not human" is true, while our statement "Sometimes if a thing is human, then it is not animal" is false.
The two negatives convert into a particular negative, because if "Absolutely not — or sometimes not — if A is B, then C is D" is true, then "Sometimes if C is not D, then A is not B" must be true; otherwise, "Whenever C is not D, then A is not B" and it converts into "Whenever A is B, then C is D" while it was "Absolutely not — or sometimes not — if A is B, then C is D" — this is a contradiction.

Commentary
This passage defines contraposition (`aks al-naqid) and presents its rules for categorical and conditional propositions. The author first gives the standard definition: making the first part of the proposition the negation of the second, and the second the same as the first, while altering the quality but preserving truth. He then notes that ancient logicians defined it as swapping negated parts while preserving quality and truth. Using the example 'Every human is an animal' converts to 'Every non-animal is not human'.

The author explains that the behavior of affirmative propositions in contraposition mirrors that of negative propositions in simple conversion, and vice versa. For the universal affirmative (A-type), it converts into itself: 'Every J is B' becomes 'Every non-B is non-J'. If this fails, we get 'Some non-B is J', which via simple conversion becomes 'Some J is not B' — contradicting 'Every J is B'. This is a proof by contradiction. Similarly, combining 'Some non-B is J' with the original 'Every J is B' yields a contradiction: 'Some non-B is B'.

The particular affirmative (I-type) does not convert: 'Some animal is not human' is true, but its contrapositive 'Some human is not animal' is false.

Both universal and particular negatives (E and O) convert into a particular negative. If contraposition fails, we derive a contradiction.

The author extends this to conditional propositions. For a universal affirmative conditional, contraposition yields the same form: negating the antecedent and consequent swap. This is valid because if the consequent is absent, the antecedent must also be absent (modus tollens). If not, the implication would be invalid.

The particular affirmative conditional does not convert, as shown with a counterexample.

The two negative conditionals (universal and particular) convert into a particular negative conditional, again explained via contradiction.

The passage demonstrates the logical rigor of classical logicians, using notational variables (J, B, C, D) and proving each rule through reductio ad absurdum.

Contraposition of Conditional Propositions
Author extends contraposition rules to conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to produce the contrapositive of any conditional proposition and understand why it is logically valid using the principle of modus tollens.

Core Idea
Contraposition of conditionals is modus tollens: denying the consequent denies the antecedent.

Just as contraposition works on categorical propositions, it also applies to conditional propositions of the form 'If A then B'. The contrapositive becomes 'If not B then not A'. In this lesson, we will see how the classical logicians extended the same rules to conditionals, using the insight that the implication is destroyed if the consequent can be false while the antecedent is true.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
For a conditional 'If A then B', the antecedent is A, the consequent is B. In contraposition, these are negated and swapped.
2
Form the contrapositive
The contrapositive of 'If A then B' is 'If not B then not A'. This is valid because if B is entailed by A, the absence of B implies the absence of A.
This is equivalent to modus tollens.
3
Determine quality and quantity
The original conditional has a quality (affirmative or negative) and quantity (universal or particular). The rules mirror those for categoricals.
Universal affirmative conditional contrapose to itself; particular does not; negatives contrapose to particular negative.
4
Test for contradictions
If the contrapositive were false, you would derive that the antecedent can exist without the consequent, breaking the implication. The author uses this to prove the rule.

Inversion by Contradictory for Categorical Propositions
Author states rules for inversion by contradictory of categorical propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform contraposition on universal affirmative and negative categorical propositions and identify when contraposition fails for particular affirmatives.

Core Idea
Contraposition flips and negates; it works for universals but not particular affirmatives.

One of the key immediate inferences in logic is inversion by contradictory (عكس النقيض), which transforms a proposition into a new one by swapping the subject and predicate with their contradictories while preserving truth under certain conditions. In this passage, the author explains the rules for categorical propositions, showing which types admit contraposition and which do not, using a reductio argument and a counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition type
Determine whether the categorical proposition is universal affirmative (A), particular affirmative (I), universal negative (E), or particular negative (O). The rule for contraposition depends on the quantity and quality.
For example, 'Every intoxicant (khamr) is forbidden (haram)' is universal affirmative.
2
Apply the rule for universal affirmatives
From a true universal affirmative 'Every B is C', the contrapositive is 'Every non-C is non-B'. Verify this using a conditional proof: if the contrapositive were false, then 'Some non-C is B' would be true, and together with 'Every B is C' yields 'Some non-C is C'—a contradiction.
This is the standard contraposition of an A proposition.
3
Test the rule for particular affirmatives
Take a true particular affirmative like 'Some animal is not human' (i.e., 'Some animal is non-human'). Its attempted contrapositive would be 'Some non-human is not animal', which is false because every human is an animal. So particular affirmatives do not admit contraposition.
The author gives this exact example from the passage.
4
Apply the rule for negative propositions
For universal negative (E) and particular negative (O), the contrapositive is a particular negative. From 'No B is C' or 'Some B is not C', the result is 'Not every non-C is non-B', i.e., 'Some non-C is B'. The proof uses reductio: if the contrapositive were false, then 'Every non-C is non-B' would hold, which contraposes to 'Every B is C', contradicting the original negative.
Thus E and O propositions convert to an O proposition by contraposition.

Inversion by Contradictory for Conditional Propositions
Author states rules for inversion by contradictory of conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform contraposition on universal affirmative conditional propositions and identify the conditions under which negative conditionals invert.

Core Idea
Contraposition of conditionals follows modus tollens: from 'If P then Q' derive 'If not-Q then not-P'.

The same operation of inversion by contradictory applies to conditional propositions (الشرطية). The passage extends the rules from categoricals to conditionals, showing that universal affirmative conditionals invert by modus tollens, particular affirmatives do not, and negatives invert to particular negatives. Understanding this parallel is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence that rely on conditional reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional type
Conditional propositions can be universal affirmative (e.g., 'Whenever A is B, then C is D') or particular affirmative ('Sometimes when...'), or negative (universal or particular). The rule depends on quantity and quality.

2
Apply contraposition to universal affirmatives
From a true universal affirmative conditional 'Whenever A is B, then C is D', the contrapositive is 'Whenever C is not D, then A is not B'. This is valid because the negation of the consequent (C is not D) entails the negation of the antecedent (A is not B) by modus tollens. If the implication holds, it must be truth-preserving.
The author explicitly states: 'because the negation of the consequent entails the negation of the antecedent'.

Take a true particular conditional: 'Sometimes when something is an animal, it is not human'. Its attempted contrapositive would be 'Sometimes when something is not human, it is not animal', which is false because every human is an animal. So particular affirmatives do not invert.

The proof uses reductio: if the contrapositive were false, then 'Whenever C is not D, then A is not B' would be true, which contraposes to 'Whenever A is B, then C is D', contradicting the original negative.

Defending Categorical Conversion: Negation vs Privation
Objection and defense on categorical conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the later scholars' objection against the proof for categorical conversion and reconstruct the author's defense using the distinction between negation (salb) and privation ('adul).

Core Idea
The conversion proof is saved by using negation (salb) instead of privation ('adul) in the complements.

In this passage, later scholars challenge a longstanding proof for the conversion of categorical propositions, arguing that it only yields a privative negative, not the required affirmative. The author responds by clarifying that the proof properly uses negation, not privation, and that this resolves the difficulty. Understanding this exchange is crucial for mastering the logic of immediate inference in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objectors and their claim
The later scholars (المتأخرون) say they do not accept that if the converse is false, then 'some non-B is C' follows. They argue that the most that follows is 'not some non-B is not C' (a double negative), which is a privative negative, more general than the required affirmative.
2
Distinguish privative negative from affirmative with privative predicate
The privative negative (e.g., 'some non-B is not C') is more general than the affirmative (e.g., 'some non-B is C'). The truth of the more general does not imply the truth of the more specific, so the objection stands.
Key terms: السالبة المعدولة, الموجبة المحصلة
3
Understand the author's response: taking complements in the sense of negation
The author insists that they take the contradictory complements in the sense of negation (سلب) not privation (عدول). Thus the proposition 'every non-B is not-C' is an affirmative with both extremes negative, which functions like a negative in not requiring subject existence.
4
See how the proof works with negation
If the universal affirmative 'every non-B is not-C' is false, then 'not every non-B is not-C' is true, which negates the negation of C about some subject of non-B, yielding 'some non-B is C' – the required converse.
This completes the proof for the conversion of the universal affirmative as itself.
5
Extend to the conversion of negatives
The proof for universal affirmative conversion also establishes the conversion of the two negative propositions as particular negative, because they are based on the same method.
The author notes that the response suffices for both proofs.

Objection to Conditional Conversion: The Impossibility of the Negation
Objection to conditional conversion (contraposition)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the objection against contraposition (conversion of conditional propositions) and identify the modal nuance that the objector raises.

Core Idea
Contraposition fails if the negation of the consequent is impossible, because the implication may not hold in that scenario.

The later scholars extend their objection to conditional propositions. They argue that the principle 'if p implies q, then not-q implies not-p' is not universally valid, because the negation of q might be impossible. If q is necessarily true, then not-q is impossible, and assuming it occurs breaks the implication. This is a subtle modal consideration that challenges the unconditional use of contraposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
The objector attacks the conversion of conditionals, which is essentially contraposition: from 'If P then Q' infer 'If not-Q then not-P'.
2
Understand the objection
The objector says: we do not accept that the negation of the consequent (not-Q) entails the negation of the antecedent (not-P). The entailment only holds if the implication (P→Q) remains true under the assumption of not-Q. But that is denied: why can't not-Q be impossible in itself? If not-Q is impossible, then assuming it occurs might make the implication false, thus breaking the inference.
3
Analyze the modal dimension
The objection relies on the idea that if Q is necessary, then not-Q is impossible. In possible-world semantics, if we consider a world where not-Q holds, that world is impossible, so the implication P→Q might not hold there. Hence, contraposition fails when Q is necessary.
4
Note the author's tacit defense
The author does not directly rebut here; he simply reports the objection with a marginal note by al-Sharif. Later logicians respond that contraposition holds only for absolute (non-modal) conditionals, or when the implication is strict.

Conversion by Contraposition ('Aks al-Naqid) – Definition and Structure
Author defines conversion by contraposition and illustrates with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of conversion by contraposition and apply it to any categorical proposition from Islamic discourse.

Core Idea
Conversion by contraposition negates and swaps terms while preserving truth.

We have learned simple conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi). Now we encounter another immediate inference: 'aks al-naqid, or conversion by contraposition. This operation is crucial for proofs in kalam and fiqh where we need to deduce a logically equivalent proposition. In this lesson, we will dissect the definition, understand its mechanism, and see an example applied to an Islamic statement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's parts
Take any categorical proposition. Identify its subject (al-juz' al-awwal) and predicate (al-juz' al-thani). In the example 'Every human is an animal', the subject is 'human' and the predicate is 'animal'.
2
Form the contradictory of the predicate
Take the contradictory (naqid) of the predicate. For 'animal', the contradictory is 'non-animal' (la hayawan). This becomes the subject of the converse.
Contradictory is formed by prefixing 'la' or 'ghayr'.
3
Keep the original subject as the new predicate
The original subject 'human' becomes the predicate of the converse, unchanged.
4
Adjust quality and verify truth preservation
The original universal affirmative (Every S is P) becomes a universal negative (No non-P is S). Truth is preserved: if all humans are animals, then indeed nothing that is not an animal is human.
The quality changes but the truth value remains the same.

Contrapositive of Universal Affirmative Propositions with Modalities
Author states rules for contrapositive of universal affirmatives with various modalities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the contrapositive of any universal affirmative proposition based on its modality (necessary, perpetual, common conditional/customary, or particular) and verify its validity using reductio proofs.

Core Idea
The contrapositive of a universal affirmative depends on its modal type: stronger modalities yield perpetual negatives, weaker modalities yield customary negatives.

In this lesson, we delve into the rules for contrapositive (inverse conversion) of universal affirmative propositions. The author explains different outcomes based on whether the proposition is necessary, perpetual, common conditional/customary, or particular. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correct immediate inference in modal logic.

Guided Path
1
Recall the initial statement
The author notes seven types of universal affirmatives whose negatives do not simply convert; an example is given: 'Every moon is not eclipsed at quadrature' is not perpetually true.
This sets up the need for contrapositive rules.
2
Necessary and perpetual modalities
If the original is 'Every J is B necessarily (or perpetually)', its contrapositive is 'Nothing that is not B is J perpetually.' The proof uses contradiction: assume the contrapositive false, then some non-B is J actually, which with the original yields some non-B is B, absurd.
The result is a perpetual universal negative regardless of the original's modality.
3
Common conditional and customary modalities
If the original is 'Every J is B as long as it is J' (conditional) or by custom, the contrapositive is 'Nothing that is not B is J as long as it is not B' i.e., a common customary universal negative.
The proof is similar but uses temporal condition.
4
Particular (specific) modalities
The two particular types (specific conditional and customary) contraposit to a common customary universal, but not perpetual in some cases. The reasoning involves absolute generality and the absence of perpetual truth.
This shows that weaker modalities yield weaker contrapositives.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider 'Every intoxicant is forbidden necessarily.' Its contrapositive is 'Nothing non-forbidden is an intoxicant perpetually.' This can be used in usul al-fiqh to argue that if something is not forbidden, it is not an intoxicant.
The modal force carries over.

Simple Conversion of Universal Affirmative Modal Propositions
Author states conversion rules for universal affirmative modal propositions with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which universal affirmative modal propositions convert by simple conversion and state the result of the conversion.

Core Idea
Not all universal affirmatives convert to affirmatives; the modality determines the converse.

This passage from Qutbi's commentary details the rules for simple conversion of universal affirmative modal propositions. It covers several types: temporal universals do not convert, while necessary and perpetual convert to perpetual negatives, conditioned and conventional to conventional negatives, and the two particular types to conventional but not perpetual. We will walk through each rule and its proof by contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modal types
The passage mentions seven types of universal affirmatives. Note the categories: necessary (ḍarūriyya), perpetual (dā'ima), common conditioned (sharṭiyya 'āmma), common conventional ('urfiyya 'āmma), and the two particular types (khāṣṣatān).
The temporal (waqtiyya) is the one that does not convert.
2
Understand why temporal universals do not convert
The author gives an example: 'Every moon is not eclipsed at the time of quadrature, but not always.' Its converse would be false because the predicate applies only at a specific time. Simple conversion would change the time condition.
This shows that conversion requires the proposition to hold for all times or under all conditions.
3
Follow the proof for necessary/perpetual conversion
If 'necessarily (or perpetually) every J is B' is true, then 'perpetually, nothing that is not B is J' must be true. The proof assumes otherwise and deduces a contradiction: 'some not-B is actually J' together with the original yields 'some not-B is B' impossibly.
The conversion yields a universal negative, not affirmative.
4
Follow the proofs for conditioned/conventional and particular types
For common conditioned/conventional, the result is a common conventional universal. For the particular types, the result is common conventional but not perpetual; the proof shows that if it were perpetual, a contradiction arises.
The particular types include a time condition that prevents perpetual conversion.

Guided Path

The author claims that if a necessary or perpetual universal affirmative is true, then its contrapositive is a perpetual universal affirmative. For example, 'necessarily every J is B' yields 'perpetually nothing that is not B is J'.
The modality changes from necessary to perpetual.
2
Understand the proof by contradiction
Assume the contrapositive is false. Then there exists something that is not B but is actually J. Conjoin this with the original (every J is B) to derive a contradiction: some not B is actually B (or necessarily B). Since that is impossible, the contrapositive must be true.
The proof uses a syllogistic step: from 'some not B is actually J' and 'every J is B' we get 'some not B is actually B' which is absurd.

Contrapositive of Particular Propositions: The Two Specific Types
Author states and proves the contrapositive of particular affirmatives of the two specific types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which particular affirmative propositions have a valid contrapositive and construct the contrapositive for the two specific types (musharata and 'urfiyya khassa).

Core Idea
Only particular affirmatives with conditional modality yield a valid contrapositive.

In previous lessons, we learned the basic rules of contrapositive ('aks al-naqid) for universal propositions. Now we turn to particular propositions, which are more restrictive. The author of the Shamsiyya states that only two specific types—the musharata (conditional necessity) and the 'urfiyya khassa (specific customary)—have a valid contrapositive. This lesson unpacks the rule, its proof by contradiction, and the counterexamples that show why other particular affirmatives fail. Understanding this distinction is crucial when applying immediate inference in Islamic legal reasoning and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule for particular contrapositive
Locate the sentence stating that only the two specific types (al-khassatan) have a contrapositive. Note that the result is a specific customary ('urfiyya khassa) particular.
The two specific types are defined earlier in modal logic: musharata (e.g., 'Some C is B by necessity as long as C exists') and 'urfiyya khassa (e.g., 'Some C is B habitually as long as C exists').
2
Understand the modal conditions of the premise
The premise must be 'by necessity or perpetually, Some C is B as long as C, not perpetually.' This means the proposition is particular (not always true) and has a conditional modality tying B to C.
The phrase 'لا دائماً' (not perpetually) indicates particularity.
3
Follow the proof by contradiction
The author posits an individual D that is C and actually not B (due to non-perpetuity). If D were not C as long as it is not B, a contradiction arises: D would both be B and not B when it is C. Therefore D must be not C as long as it is not B.
This shows the contrapositive's conditional part: 'Some non-B is not C as long as it is non-B.'
4
Note the two parts of the contrapositive
The contrapositive has two parts: (1) 'Some non-B is not C as long as non-B' (conditional) and (2) 'Some non-B is C in actuality' (non-perpetuity). Together they form 'Some non-B is not C as long as non-B, not perpetually.'
This matches the requirement that the contrapositive is a specific customary particular.
5
Recognize the counterexamples
The author gives two counterexamples: 'Some animal is not human with absolute necessity' (absolute particular) and 'Some moon is not eclipsed with temporal necessity' (temporal particular). These lack conditional modality, so they have no contrapositive.
If these two do not convert, none of the remaining particular affirmatives do.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson examines how negative propositions behave under inversion by contradiction ('aks al-naqīḍ). Commentary
This passage explains the rules of inversion by contradiction ('aks al-naqid) for negative propositions (sawalib) in categorical logic. The author first states the general rule: neither universal nor particular negative propositions can be inverted into a universal affirmative. The reason is that the contradictory of the predicate (naqiḍ al-maḥmūl) might be more general than the subject, and it is impossible to affirm the more specific (the original subject) of every individual of the more general. For example, from 'No human is a stone' we cannot conclude 'Everything that is not a stone is a human', because 'not a stone' applies to many things besides humans.

Then the author classifies negative propositions by modality. Two types are distinguished: 'particular negatives' (al-khāṣṣatān) which are perpetual or necessary negatives, and 'time-specific/existential negatives' (al-waqtiyyatān wa al-wujūdiyyatān). For the particular negatives (e.g., 'Nothing of J is B perpetually/necessarily'), their inverse is an absolute temporal (hīniyya muṭlaqa) negative proposition. The proof: assume the perpetual negative 'No J is B' is true as long as J exists non-perpetually. Choose a D that is J at some time. Since D is J and J is never B, D is not B. Also D is J only at that time (since J is not perpetual). So D is not B and is J at that moment, yielding 'Some non-B is J when it is not B' (i.e., at that moment). This is a temporal particular affirmative.

For time-specific (e.g., 'No moon is eclipsed at quadrature') and existential (e.g., 'No crow is white actually') negatives, the inverse is a general absolute (muṭlaqa 'āmma) particular affirmative. Proof: if the negative holds with such a modality, then there exists a subject D that is J (since the modality implies existence). D is not B (by the original) and is J actually. Hence 'Some non-B is J actually'.

The author then repeats the argument for particular negatives again and then offers a correction: the correct view is that the inverse of the two particular negatives is an absolute temporal (hīniyya muṭlaqa) not a perpetual (dā'ima). The temporal part is as before; the non-perpetual part is justified because D cannot be J perpetually (otherwise the original condition of 'non-perpetual' would be violated, leading to contradiction). Thus the inverse is temporal, not perpetual.

Conversion of Particular Negatives with Temporal Modalities
Author explains conversion rules for particular negative propositions with temporal modalities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conversion rule for particular negatives with temporal modalities (e.g., 'not always') to derive absolute temporal affirmatives.

Core Idea
Particular negatives with temporal modalities convert as absolute temporals, not permanent ones.

This lesson delves into the conversion of particular negative propositions when temporal modalities are involved. The author uses a careful proof involving the assumption of a subject D that satisfies the conditions. We will walk through the reasoning step by step, using logical variables to clarify the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particular negative with temporal modality
The proposition is of the form: 'Not some J is B, as long as J is not always' (i.e., a particular negative with non-permanent modality). The author also considers 'necessarily' or 'always' variants. For this lesson, focus on the non-permanent temporal condition.

From the above, we can conclude: 'Some of what is not B is J, when it is not B.' This is an absolute temporal affirmative (hīniyya muṭlaqa). The author notes that this conversion is not permanent (lā dā'ima); it only holds temporally.

Converting Temporal and Existential Negatives
Author explains conversion of temporal and existential negatives to absolute general affirmatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert temporal and existential negative categorical propositions to absolute general affirmatives and explain why the modal qualification does not carry over.

Core Idea
Temporal and existential negatives convert to unrestricted absolute affirmatives, not preserving the modality.

The previous parts of this logic text covered conversion of simple categorical propositions. Now we encounter a special case: when the proposition is negative and restricted by time (temporal) or existence (existential), the conversion rule changes. Instead of yielding a negative with the same modality, these propositions convert to absolute general affirmatives. This lesson unpacks that rule and the proof behind it, using the concept of non-permanence (al-lā dawām).

Guided Path
1
Understand non-permanence
Identify the concept of non-permanence: if a subject D is not B and not C in actuality, then some non-B is non-C in actuality. This serves as a lemma for the conversion rule.
2
Recognize temporal and existential negatives
Temporal negatives (al-waqtiyyāt) are like 'Nothing of C is B at time t'. Existential negatives (al-wujūdiyyāt) are like 'Nothing of C is B in actual existence'. The key feature is that they assert absence only with a temporal or existential qualification.
3
Apply the conversion rule
The rule: if 'Nothing of C is B' or 'Some C is not B' is true under a temporal or existential mode, then 'Some of what is not B is C' is true with absolute generality (no time/mode restriction). The proof: posit an individual D that is not B (true from the premise) and that is C in actuality (since the negation is temporal/existential, D must exist at some time or actually). Hence some non-B is C absolutely.
4
Check why modality does not transfer
The qualification of non-permanence or non-necessity does not carry over because the converse might be necessarily true. Example: 'Some human is not a writer, not necessarily' is true, but its converse 'Some writer is human, not necessarily' is false because every writer is necessarily human. So the converse becomes absolute, not modalized.
This shows that conversion does not preserve the modal force.

Mutual Implication of Non-True Disjunctives
Author proves mutual implication between man' al-jam' and man' al-khuluw via negations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive man' al-khuluw from man' al-jam' by negating both disjuncts, and vice versa, using the proof by contradiction.

Core Idea
Man' al-jam' and man' al-khuluw are two sides of the same coin via negation.

In the previous lesson, we encountered the two types of non-true disjunctives: man' al-jam' (cannot both be true) and man' al-khuluw (cannot both be false). Now the author proves that they are logically equivalent when the disjuncts are negated. This is a classic example of immediate inference and demonstrates the power of contradiction proofs in logic.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definitions
Man' al-jam' means that two propositions cannot be true together (at least one is false). Man' al-khuluw means they cannot be false together (at least one is true). Write these in your own words.
2
First direction: from man' al-jam' to man' al-khuluw of negations
Assume man' al-jam' holds between A and B (so ¬(A∧B)). Suppose for contradiction that man' al-khuluw does NOT hold between ¬A and ¬B, meaning ¬A and ¬B could both be false (i.e., A and B both true). That would allow A and B to be true together, contradicting man' al-jam'. Therefore man' al-khuluw (¬A,¬B) must hold.
This uses the fact that if both negations are false, the originals are true.
3
Second direction: from man' al-khuluw to man' al-jam' of negations
Now assume man' al-khuluw holds between A and B (so ¬(¬A∧¬B) i.e., at least one is true). Suppose man' al-jam' does NOT hold between ¬A and ¬B, meaning ¬A and ¬B could both be true (i.e., A and B both false). That would allow A and B to be false together, contradicting man' al-khuluw. Therefore man' al-jam' (¬A,¬B) must hold.
This uses the fact that if both negations are true, the originals are false.

Generalise the equivalence
The two proofs show: man' al-jam'(A,B) iff man' al-khuluw(¬A,¬B). Similarly, man' al-khuluw(A,B) iff man' al-jam'(¬A,¬B). So the two types are interdefinable by negating the disjuncts.
Memorise this equivalence: it is a handy transformation.

Commentary
This passage proves the mutual logical equivalence between the two types of non-true disjunctives: preventing conjunction (man' al-jam') and preventing vacuity (man' al-khuluw). The author argues that if preventing conjunction between two propositions is true, then preventing vacuity between their negations is also true. The proof uses contradiction: if both negations could be absent (i.e., both originals true), that would allow the originals to be together, contradicting the initial preventing conjunction. Conversely, if preventing vacuity between two originals is true (they cannot both be absent), then preventing conjunction between their negations follows: if both negations could be true (i.e., both originals false), that would allow the originals to be absent, contradicting the initial preventing vacuity. The passage concludes with the heading 'Mutual implication of conditionals', summarizing the relation. This demonstrates the interdefinability of the two disjunctive forms via negation, a key concept in propositional logic.
