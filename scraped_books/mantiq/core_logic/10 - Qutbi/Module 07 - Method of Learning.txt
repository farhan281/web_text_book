Objection and Refutation: Preface as Part of Logic
Objection that preface becomes part of logic, refuted by consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of an objection and its refutation based on consensus in an Islamic methodological text.

Core Idea
The preface is a tool, not a part of the science.

In this passage, an objection is raised: if something must be known in logic, it must be part of logic. If so, the preface would become part of logic, which is absurd. The refutation relies on scholarly consensus (ittifaq) that the introduction to a science is external. Understanding this distinction helps you recognise how Islamic scholars delineate the boundaries of a discipline.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (mu'tarad)
Locate the claim being challenged: that whatever must be known in logic is part of logic, so the preface would be part of logic.
2
Understand the reasoning behind the objection
The objection uses the principle that anything known within a science is part of it; since the preface contains knowledge required for logic, it would be part of logic.
This is an immediate inference from the principle.
3
Find the refutation (jawab)
The commentator rejects the objection by stating it is false (batil) and provides the reason: scholars unanimously agree that the preface to a science is outside that science.
Consensus (ittifaq) is a strong proof in Islamic scholarship.
4
Recognise the basis of the refutation
The refutation is not based on a logical counter-argument but on the established agreement of scholars. This shows the weight of consensus in Islamic methodology.
Consensus is used as a source of certainty here.

The Three Conditions for a Science's Benefit
Commentary on why the student must know the science's goal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a science's benefit (fa'ida) according to the commentator and explain why each prevents vain pursuit (ʿabath).

Core Idea
A science's benefit must be real, significant, and exactly what the science delivers.

Before embarking on any science, a student must know its goal (ghaya). But not just any goal: the commentator Sharif al-Jurjani unpacks three precise conditions that make the pursuit worthwhile. Understanding these conditions is not just academic—it directly affects your motivation and the very validity of your study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition: knowledge of a benefit
Find where the commentator says the student must know there is some benefit (fa'ida) in the science. Without this, starting is impossible because study is a voluntary act.
This is a logical prerequisite: you cannot choose to pursue something if you see no benefit at all.
2
Identify the second condition: benefit must be considerable
Look for the phrase 'muʿtaddan bihā' (considerable). The commentator says the benefit must outweigh the hardship (mashaqqa) of study; otherwise, the effort is vain by custom.
This prevents frivolous or overly demanding studies whose trivial gain doesn't justify the toil.
3
Identify the third condition: benefit must actually result from the science
Find where the commentator says the expected benefit must be the one that truly comes from that science. If the student expects a different benefit, disillusionment may occur, making effort vain.
This ensures honesty: the science's fruit must match the student's expectation.
4
Synthesize: how these conditions strengthen resolve
Read the final sentence: when all three conditions are met, desire is complete and effort increases. The suitability of issues to the benefit reinforces conviction after starting.
The commentator shows that proper preparation leads to sustained motivation.

Why You Must Know a Science's Purpose and Definition Before Studying It
Author explains why a student must know the fruit and definition of a science before studying it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the logical necessity of knowing the fruit (fā'ida) and description (rasm) of a science before beginning its study, and distinguish between the ability and actual knowledge of its issues.

Core Idea
Knowing a science's benefit and definition is like having a compass: without it, your study is aimless.

The commentator Qutb al-Din al-Razi explains two essential preliminaries for any science: its purpose and its description. He argues that beginning a science is a voluntary act that requires a meaningful goal; otherwise, effort is futile. Moreover, knowing the science's defining characteristic (rasm) gives you the ability to recognize its issues, even if not actual knowledge of every detail. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prerequisites
Read the passage and extract the two things a student must know before starting a science: the fruit (fā'ida/ghāyah) and the description (rasm).
The fruit is the benefit that results from the science; the rasm is a description that captures its specific characteristic.
2
Understand why the benefit is necessary
The author says beginning a science is volitional. If the student does not know a considerable benefit, the effort is futile by custom, and earnestness weakens. The benefit must be significant given the hardship of study.
This is not just any benefit, but one that is worthy of the effort.
3
Understand why the rasm is necessary
Knowing the rasm gives a universal premise about the science's specific characteristic (khāṣṣah). This enables the student to recognize that any issue relating to that characteristic belongs to the science.
The author uses logic as an example: conceiving it as a protective instrument yields the premise that every logical issue contributes to mental protection.
4
Distinguish ability from actual knowledge
The author clarifies that knowing the rasm gives ability (qudra) to identify issues, not actual knowledge of every issue. The objection that this is false (since not everyone who knows the definition knows every issue) is rebutted: it only gives a general ability, not detailed knowledge.
This distinction avoids a common misunderstanding.
5
Apply to an Islamic science
Think of a science like Fiqh. Its fruit is to know halal and haram; its rasm might be 'knowledge of practical Sharī'ah rulings from their detailed evidence'. A student who knows this can recognize that a question about wudū' is from Fiqh, even without knowing the specific ruling.
This shows how the principle works in practice.

The Three Preliminaries for Beginning a Science
Author discusses the subject matter of a science and lists the three prerequisites for beginning its study

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three obligatory preliminaries for beginning a science and explain why knowing the exact subject matter is not among them.

Core Idea
Sciences are distinguished by their subjects; only three things are required to start: conception, belief in benefit, and belief in subject.

Before diving into any discipline, whether Kalam or Fiqh, one must understand what makes a science distinct and what prerequisites are necessary to begin. This passage from the Qutbi commentary clarifies that sciences differ by their subjects, and it outlines exactly what a beginner must have: a conception of the science, a belief in its benefit, and a belief that it has a subject. Knowing the precise subject in detail is not obligatory, only beneficial. Let's walk through the logical steps to grasp this framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle of science differentiation
The author states that sciences are differentiated by their subjects (موضوعات). A science is defined by the thing it studies and its related states and rulings. If two groups of rulings relate to the same thing from the same aspect, they belong to one science; otherwise, they are separate sciences.
For example, Fiqh studies actions of the mukallaf, while Usul al-Fiqh studies evidence – thus two sciences.
2
Recognize the three obligatory preliminaries
The author lists three things obligatory on the beginner: (1) conception of the science (تصور العلم) in some way, or better by its description; (2) belief that it has a specific benefit (تصديق بفائدته); (3) belief that it has a subject (تصديق بموضوعية موضوعه).
These are minimal requirements to avoid futile effort and to begin with insight.
3
Differentiate optional from obligatory prerequisites
The author clarifies that knowing the exact subject matter (معرفة موضوعه) is not obligatory for beginning; it only increases insight. The necessary distinction and insight are already obtained by conceiving the science through its description (رسم).
A common mistake is to think precise subject knowledge is required upfront; the text shows it is not.
4
Consider the suggested fourth preliminary
The author suggests adding discussions of expressions (مباحث الألفاظ) as a fourth preliminary, because deriving benefit from the science depends on understanding terms.
This is an expansion probed by the commentator, not part of the three core preliminaries.

But the author reframes: even if that objection holds, it only shows independence from learning logic, not independence from logic itself. The need for logic as a tool for theoretical sciences remains.
The objection confuses the two stations.
3
Recognise why the objection fails
The author says the objection is not suitable (la yasluhu li al-mu'arada) because it does not oppose the real claim—it addresses learning, not the need for logic. This is a mismatch in the debate: the objector attacks a different claim than the one defended.
This is a common logical fallacy: ignoratio elenchi (missing the point).
4
Extract the pedagogical justification
If logic is self-evident, why study it? The author gives two benefits: (1) to clarify any hiddenness (khafa') that might remain, and (2) to use it as a foundation for acquiring other acquired (kasbi) discussions. This shows the value of systematising even obvious knowledge.
This parallels why we study usul al-fiqh even though basic fiqh is clear.

Author divides the subject of logic into conception and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) and identify the corresponding logical tools: the expository statement and the argument.

Core Idea
Logic's purpose is to obtain unknowns, which are either concepts or judgments.

This passage lays the very foundation of Islamic logic: all unknown knowledge is either a mere concept or a judgment. The logician uses specific tools for each—the expository statement (qawl sharih) for concepts and the argument (hujja) for judgments. Understanding this division is essential before we can study definitions and syllogisms.

Natural Precedence of Conception over Assent
Author proves natural precedence of conception over assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of natural precedence and argue why conception is not a cause of assent but a prerequisite.

Core Idea
Conception naturally precedes assent because assent depends on it, yet conception does not cause assent.

The author argues that conception must be studied before assent because nature itself places conception first. But what kind of precedence is this? It is not causal; if it were, having a concept would force immediate assent. Instead, it is a relation of need: every assent requires three conceptions. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of natural precedence
Find the author's definition: natural precedence means the antecedent (conception) is needed by the consequent (assent), but the antecedent is not a cause of the consequent.
The term 'natural precedence' (al-taqaddum al-tabi'i) is technical.
2
Consider the hypothetical if conception were a cause
The author says: if conception were a cause, then whenever you have a concept, you would necessarily have the corresponding assent. Think of an example: if conceiving 'God' automatically caused you to assent that 'God exists', that would be false. Identify this reasoning in the text.
'Necessity of existence of effect when cause exists' – a key principle.
3
Identify the three conceptions required for any assent
Every judgment involves: the subject (what is judged about), the predicate (what is judged), and the relation (affirmation or negation). Find these in the passage.
E.g., in 'Every human is mortal': subject = human, predicate = mortal, relation = affirmation (of mortality to human).
4
Formulate the argument in your own words
Summarize: Assent needs conception (because it requires concepts), but conception does not cause assent (otherwise all concepts would be automatically believed). Therefore, conception precedes assent naturally but not causally.
This justifies studying definitions before arguments.

Why Logic Studies Universals, Not Particulars
Author argues logic only deals with universals because particulars are variable and unattainable by thought.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three reasons logic excludes particulars and justify the mention of true particular as a clarification tool.

Core Idea
Logic studies universals because particulars are variable, sensory, and unattainable by reasoning.

This passage from the commentary on al-Shamsiyya clarifies why the logician's subject matter is universals. Since logic aims to capture unknowns from knowns, and what is known must apply to many, the focus is on universals. The author distinguishes between relative and true particularity and then provides three compelling reasons why particulars are not investigated in logic. He also addresses a potential objection about mentioning the true particular.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of logic
The author reminds that logic is for capturing conceptual unknowns from knowns, and that captured things must extend to many (universals).
This sets the scope.
2

Relative particular and universal are correlatives like father and son; true particular is opposed to universal as possession vs. privation.

Extract the three reasons particulars are excluded
First, particulars are sensed, not reasoned. Second, they are changeable and do not give lasting perfection. Third, they are too numerous and unregulated.
Each reason supports the conclusion that the logician has no purpose with particulars.

The objection: you mention true particular and its relation, so you are studying it? The response: it is only mentioned to clarify the universal by contrast, not as a subject of investigation.
This refines the scope of logic: only universals as subjects, not as clarificatory tools.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining why logic is concerned only with universals. The commentator begins by reminding the reader that the purpose of logic is to learn how to acquire conceptual unknowns from conceptual knowns, and that what is acquired must be something that applies to a particular and to others. He then distinguishes relative universality/particularity as correlatives, and true particularity as opposed to universality in terms of possession and privation. He then gives the reason for naming the true particular as 'particular' because it is more specific than the relative particular. He then proceeds to argue that particulars are not studied in logic for three reasons: (1) they are perceived only by sensation, not by reasoning; (2) they are changeable and do not provide a lasting perfection for the soul; (3) they are too numerous and unregulated. He then addresses a potential objection: 'You have mentioned the true particular and will mention the relative particular, so why is that not a study of particulars?' The answer: The mention of the true particular is only to clarify the concept of the universal by contrast; it is not a study of the particular itself. Similarly, discussing the relationship between the two is part of that clarification. As for the relative particular, if it is a universal (i.e., the concept of the relative particular is itself universal), then it is studied because it is universal; if it is a true particular, it is not studied. The commentary ends by noting that the goal is to explain the states and rulings of things, not to depict concepts.

The Subject and Principles of a Science
Author defines subjects and principles of a science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the subject of a science and classify its principles into conceptualizations and assents with examples.

Core Idea
The subject unifies a science; principles provide the foundational definitions and assumptions.

This passage from the Qutbi commentary introduces the three-part structure of every science. We will focus on the first two: the subject (mawdu') that the science investigates, and the principles (mabadi') upon which its problems depend. Understanding these is essential for properly setting up any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject (mawdu')
Find the definition: the subject is either a single thing (like number for arithmetic) or multiple things sharing a common aspect (like the subjects of logic sharing the goal of leading to the unknown).

2
Understand the role of principles
Principles are what the problems depend on. They are divided into conceptualizations (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat).
3
Distinguish types of assents
Assents are either self-evident (like 'quantities equal to the same are equal') or non-self-evident. Non-self-evident ones, if accepted on good opinion, are 'postulated principles' (usul mawdu'a); if doubted, they are 'postulates' (musadarat).
Examples from geometry are given.
4
Apply to an Islamic science
Think of the science of usul al-fiqh: its subject is the evidences of fiqh; its principles include definitions of terms and the assent that the Quran is a source of law.
This shows real-world relevance.

As for the 'subject' – you have known its definition at the beginning of the book, and it is either one thing, like number for arithmetic, or multiple things, then they must share in one thing that is observed in all investigations of the science, like the subjects of this art, for they share in leading to an unknown sought object, otherwise it would be permissible that the scattered sciences become one science.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory section from the conclusion of the Qutbi commentary on al-Shamsiyya. The author states that every science has three parts: subjects, principles, and problems. He first defines the subject as that which the science investigates, either a single thing or multiple things united by a common aspect. He gives the example of number for arithmetic and the common aim of leading to unknown sought objects for the subjects of this art (logic). Next, he defines principles as that upon which the problems depend, dividing them into conceptualizations (definitions of subjects, parts, etc.) and assents. Assents are further divided into self-evident (like equality of quantities in geometry) and non-self-evident, which are either accepted on good opinion (postulated principles) or doubted (postulates, like drawing circles). The author then addresses a consideration (nazar) about whether the subject itself is a separate part of science. He explains that if 'subject' means the assent to its existence, it is not a part of science but a preliminary; if it means the conceptualization, it falls under principles, not an independent part. Finally, he defines problems as the sought matters demonstrated in the science, which have subjects and predicates. He illustrates the various ways the subject of a problem can relate to the subject of the science: being identical with the subject, being the subject with an essential accident, being a species of the subject, or a species with an essential accident. He uses geometric examples throughout. The passage ends with a rejection of the view that the subject is an independent part, citing Ibn Sina's al-Shifa' to place it under assertive principles. All examples are from geometry, which is typical of the logical tradition.

As for the 'subject' – you have known its definition at the beginning of the book, and it is either one thing, like number for arithmetic, or multiple things, then they must share in one thing that is observed in all investigations of the science, like the subjects of this art, for they share in leading to an unknown sought object, otherwise it would be permissible that the scattered sciences become one science.
As for the 'principles' – they are that upon which the problems of the science depend; and they are either conceptualizations or assents.
As for the conceptualizations, they are the definitions of the subjects, their parts, their particulars, and their essential accidents.
As for the assents, they are either self-evident – and they are called 'common knowledge' – like our statement in geometry: 'Quantities equal to one thing are equal to each other.'
And they are either not self-evident; if the learner accepts them due to good opinion, they are called 'postulated principles', like our statement: 'We can connect every two points with a straight line.' And if he receives them with denial and doubt, they are called 'postulates', like our statement: 'We can draw a circle with any radius and at any point we wish.'
And concerning the subject being a separate part of the science, there is a consideration, because if by it is meant the assent to subjectivity, then it is not a part of the science – since the science does not depend on it, rather it is among the preliminaries of beginning it as previously mentioned – and if by it is meant the conceptualization of the subject, then it is from the principles and not another independent part.
As for the 'problems' – they are the sought matters that are demonstrated in the science if they are acquired, and they have subjects and predicates.
As for their subjects, they may be the subject of the science, like our statement: 'Every quantity either shares with another or is separate from it', and 'quantity' is the subject of geometry.
And it may be the subject of the science together with an essential accident, like our statement: 'Every quantity that is a mean in a proportion is a side of what is enclosed by the extremes', so 'quantity' is the subject of the science and has been taken in the problem together with being a mean in the proportion, which is an essential accident.
And it may be a species of the subject of the science, like our statement: 'Every line can be bisected', for 'line' is a species of quantity.
And it may be a species of the subject of the science together with an essential accident, like our statement: 'Every line standing on a line, then the two adjacent angles are either right or equal to them'; so 'line' is a subject of the science, part of it – so that this assent is outside the science unanimously, so how can it be considered a part of it? Rather we mean by its being part of the science that the assent to the existence of the subject is part of the science – and this answer is rejected because the Chief Master (Ibn Sina) has stated in al-Shifa' that the assent to the existence of the subject is among the assertive principles, so it is also not an independent part but is included in the assertive principles. And Allah is the granter of success to what is correct; to Him is the return and the refuge (Sharif).

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory section from the conclusion of the Qutbi commentary on al-Shamsiyya. The author states that every science has three parts: subjects, principles, and problems. He first defines the subject as that which the science investigates, either a single thing or multiple things united by a common aspect. He gives the example of number for arithmetic and the common aim of leading to unknown sought objects for the subjects of this art (logic). Next, he defines principles as that upon which the problems depend, dividing them into conceptualizations (definitions of subjects, parts, etc.) and assents. Assents are further divided into self-evident (like equality of quantities in geometry) and non-self-evident, which are either accepted on good opinion (postulated principles) or doubted (postulates, like drawing circles). The author then addresses a consideration (nazar) about whether the subject itself is a separate part of science. He explains that if 'subject' means the assent to its existence, it is not a part of science but a preliminary; if it means the conceptualization, it falls under principles, not an independent part. Finally, he defines problems as the sought matters demonstrated in the science, which have subjects and predicates. He illustrates the various ways the subject of a problem can relate to the subject of the science: being identical with the subject, being the subject with an essential accident, being a species of the subject, or a species with an essential accident. He uses geometric examples throughout. The passage ends with a rejection of the view that the subject is an independent part, citing Ibn Sina's al-Shifa' to place it under assertive principles. All examples are from geometry, which is typical of the logical tradition.
