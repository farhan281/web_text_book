Definition of Induction and Its Distinction from Complete Induction
Author defines induction and contrasts it with complete enumeration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define induction (istiqra') and distinguish it from a divisive syllogism (qiyas muqassam).

Core Idea
Induction is a judgment on a universal based on most particulars, not all.

Induction is a common form of reasoning in Islamic sciences, but logicians carefully distinguish it from complete enumeration. This lesson clarifies the precise definition given by the author and why incomplete enumeration is not demonstrative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'istiqra'' (induction). Locate it in the Arabic text.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'judgment on a universal'. The differentia is 'due to its existence in most of its particulars'. This distinguishes induction from other universal judgments.
3
Contrast with complete induction
The author notes: if the judgment held for all particulars, it would be a 'divisive syllogism' (qiyas muqassam), not induction. Understand why this distinction matters.

Why Induction Does Not Yield Certainty
Author explains the epistemological limitation of induction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why induction yields only probability and identify the logical possibility of a counterexample.

Core Idea
Induction fails to yield certainty because one unexamined particular can overturn the universal claim.

Even though induction is widely used, logicians have pointed out its fundamental weakness: it never reaches certainty. This lesson examines the critique using the crocodile example.

Guided Path
1
Recall the inductive generalization
The example given: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing.' This is based on observing humans, beasts, and predators.
2
Identify the basis
The particulars examined are a subset of all animals. The generalization is made because the property was found in most examined instances.
3
Recognize the logical gap
Not all animals were examined. There is a logical possibility that an unexamined animal does not have the property.
4
Apply the counterexample
The crocodile moves its upper jaw, not the lower, contradicting the generalization. This shows induction cannot produce certainty.

Defining Analogy (Tamthil) in Islamic Logic
Author defines analogy (tamthil) with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of an analogical argument (tamthil) in an Islamic text and assess its strength.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a ruling from one known case to a similar case via a shared attribute.

The fourth art of logic, tamthil, is a common reasoning tool but is considered weaker than categorical syllogism. In the passage below, the author defines tamthil and gives a classic theological example: the world's composition implies its temporal origination, like a house. Understanding tamthil is crucial for evaluating arguments in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Find the Arabic phrase defining tamthil: 'إثبات حكم في جزئي وجد في جزئي آخر لمعنى مشترك بينهما'. What does this tell you about the structure?
The definition includes a particular case, another particular, and a common meaning.
2
Extract the components of the example
The example: 'العالم مؤلّف فهو حادث كالبيت'. Identify the original case (أصل), the new case (فرع), the common meaning (علة), and the ruling (حكم).
The world is the new case, the house is the original.
3
Assess the strength of the analogy
Does the common attribute (composition) truly extend from the house to the world? In kalam, this analogy is used to prove the world's contingency. Consider whether the shared attribute is sufficient for the ruling.
The strength depends on the validity of the shared attribute as the cause.

The Structure of Analogy (Tamthil) and Its Example
Author defines analogy and gives the standard kalam example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the root, branch, and common cause in any example of analogy (tamthil/qiyas) used in Islamic logic and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Analogy links two particulars through a shared cause, but its certainty depends on that cause.

The author defines analogy as the fourth type of argument. It is the tool jurists use daily to extend rulings. Let's dissect its components and see how the classic example of the world and the house illustrates it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim made by the analogy
Locate the conclusion: 'The world is originated because it is composed, like the house.' This is the claim being proven by analogy.
The analogy transfers the judgment of 'originated' from the house to the world.
2
Break down the components
Identify the root (asl) = the house, the branch (far') = the world, the common meaning (illa) = composition, and the judgment (hukm) = originated. The root is the known case, the branch is the unknown case being argued.
3
Check the analogical structure
The argument follows: the root has the judgment due to the cause; the branch shares the cause, therefore the branch has the judgment. Verify that the cause (composition) is truly present in both.
This structure is identical to qiyas in usul al-fiqh, though the terminology differs (tamthil vs. qiyas).
4
Distinguish from categorical syllogism
Notice that analogy moves from particular to particular, not universal to particular. The categorical syllogism uses universal premises, while analogy relies on a single shared attribute between two particulars.
This makes analogy weaker in certainty, as the author will later critique.

Critique of Co-occurrence and Division for Establishing Causality
Critique of the two methods for establishing causality in analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why co-occurrence (dawran) and division (sabr wa taqsim) are insufficient to prove causality according to the logicians' critique.

Core Idea
Co-occurrence does not isolate the true cause, and division cannot guarantee exhaustive enumeration.

The author argues that the two methods used to justify the cause in analogy are weak. Co-occurrence fails because many things co-occur with the effect, and division fails because the list of possibilities may be incomplete. Let's examine each critique closely.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim being critiqued
The logicians claim that the causality of the common meaning (e.g., composition for origination) can be proven by two methods: co-occurrence (dawran) and division (sabr wa taqsim). The author aims to show both are insufficient.
2
Analyze the critique of co-occurrence
The author says: 'the last part of the cause and all the equivalent conditions also co-occur, yet they are not the cause.' This means that many attributes accompany the effect (e.g., composition, extension, shape) but only one may be the cause. Co-occurrence alone does not pinpoint which one.
Co-occurrence is a necessary condition for causation but not sufficient.
3
Analyze the critique of division
The author gives two objections: (1) The exhaustive enumeration might be incomplete—an unmentioned cause could exist. (2) Even if the cause is valid in the root, it may not be valid in the branch due to particularities (e.g., a condition in the root or a preventer in the branch).
Division tries to prove causality by eliminating alternatives, but the elimination process itself may be flawed.
4
Summarize the overall weakness
Both methods fail to provide certainty. Therefore, analogy based on these methods yields only probable (zanni) knowledge, not certain (yaqini) knowledge.
This critique is foundational for understanding the epistemological status of qiyas in usul al-fiqh.

I say: Tamthil is establishing one ruling in a particular thing because that ruling is established in another particular thing due to a shared meaning between them. The jurists call it qiyas. The first particular is called far', the second is called asl, and the shared meaning is called 'illa and jami'. As it is said: "The world is composed (mu'allaf), therefore it is originated (hadith); the house (al-bayt) is originated because it is composed; and this 'illa is present in the world, like the house" meaning the world is originated like the house. And they establish the causality of the shared meaning by two ways: One of them is al-dawaran (correlation), which is the conjunction of a thing with another in existence and non-existence. As it is said: "Origination (al-huduth) is correlated with composition (al-ta'lif) in existence and non-existence." As for existence, in the house; as for non-existence, in the Necessary (al-wajib ta'ala). Correlation is an indication that the correlated thing is the cause of the correlator. Thus composition is a cause of origination. The second is al-sabr wa al-taqsim (elimination and division), which is listing the attributes of the asl and invalidating some of them so that the remaining one is established as the cause. As it is said: "The cause of origination in the house is either composition or possibility (al-imkan). The second is invalid due to counterexample (bi al-takhalluf), because the attributes of the Necessary are possible yet not originated. Thus the first is established." And both ways are weak: As for correlation: because the last part of the complete cause and the equal condition (al-shart al-musawi) are correlated with the effect (madar li al-ma'lul) yet they are not the cause. As for elimination and division: because confining the cause to the mentioned attributes is not conceded (mamnu') because the division is not restricted to negation and affirmation, so it is possible that it is other than what was mentioned. Then, even if the confinement is conceded, we do not concede that if the shared attribute is a cause in the original, it necessarily follows that it is a cause in the branch, because it is possible that the particularity of the original is a condition for causality or the particularity of the branch is an impediment to it.

Commentary
This passage discusses analogical reasoning (tamthil) as used by jurists, which they call qiyas. The author defines tamthil as establishing a ruling (hukm) in one particular case (far') because it is established in another particular case (asl) due to a shared meaning (ma'na mushtarak) which is the 'illa (cause) or jami' (unifying attribute). He gives an example: the world (al-'alam) is composed (mu'allaf), so it is originated (hadith); a house (al-bayt) is also composed and therefore originated. The shared attribute is composition (al-ta'lif), which is posited as the cause of origination. The author then explains two methods used by jurists to prove that the shared attribute is indeed the cause. The first is al-dawaran (correlation), which is the observation that the effect (al-hadath) accompanies the supposed cause (al-ta'lif) in both existence and non-existence. In existence: when composition exists, origination exists (e.g., in the house). In non-existence: when composition is absent (as in the Necessary Being, Allah), origination is absent. This correlation is taken as evidence that composition is the cause of origination. The second method is al-sabr wa al-taqsim (elimination and division), whereby the logician enumerates all possible attributes of the asl that could be the cause, then disproves all but one. The example given: the cause of origination in the house is either composition (al-ta'lif) or possibility (al-imkan). Possibility is eliminated because it is present in the attributes of the Necessary (which are possible) yet those attributes are not originated. So composition remains as the only viable cause. After explaining these methods, the author states that both are weak (da'if). He critiques correlation: the last part of the complete cause (al-juz' al-akhir min al-'illa al-tammah) and the equal condition (al-shart al-musawi) are also correlated with the effect, yet they are not the cause. This means that correlation alone does not distinguish the true cause from other necessary concomitants. He critiques elimination and division on two grounds. First, the confinement (hasr) of possible causes to the mentioned attributes is not accepted because the division is not exhaustive; there may be other attributes not considered. Second, even if we accept the confinement, it does not follow that the shared attribute is a cause in the branch (far'), because there could be a particularity in the original (asl) that is a condition for causality, or a particularity in the branch that prevents causality. Therefore, the method does not yield certainty.
