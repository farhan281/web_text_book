Rhetorical Premises: Accepted Opinions and Conjectural Judgments
Author defines accepted opinions and conjectural judgments as premises of rhetoric.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish accepted opinions (maqbūlāt) from conjectural judgments (maẓnūnāt) and explain how they form the rhetorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Rhetoric convinces with probable truth, not certainty, using trusted authority and plausible inference.

In this passage, the author explains the two types of premises that make up rhetorical arguments: accepted opinions taken from reliable authorities, and conjectural judgments that are probable but not certain. Understanding these distinctions helps you recognise how preachers and exhorters use logic to persuade audiences toward beneficial actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of premises
Locate where the author introduces maqbūlāt and maẓnūnāt. Both are introduced with 'منها' (among them), indicating they are subtypes of the premises used in rhetoric.
2
Examine the definition of maqbūlāt
Note that maqbūlāt are propositions taken from a trusted authority. The authority is trusted either due to divine miracles (prophets/saints) or due to exceptional intellect and piety (scholars/ascetics). Their purpose is to exalt Allah and show compassion for creation.
These are very useful (نافعة جداً) for religious exhortation.
3
Examine the definition of maẓnūnāt
Maẓnūnāt are probable judgments where the intellect preponderates (راجح) but allows the opposite. The example given infers theft from night-walking: 'So-and-so walks at night; everyone who walks at night is a thief; so he is a thief.' This is plausible but not certain.
The example is a categorical syllogism, but its conclusion is only probable.
4
Link premises to the rhetorical syllogism
The syllogism composed of these two types is called khaṭāba (rhetoric). Its purpose is to encourage people in worldly and religious benefits (ترغيب الناس فيما ينفعهم من أمور معاشهم ومعادهم). Think of how preachers (khuṭabā') and exhorters (wuʿʿāẓ) use such arguments to motivate action.
Rhetoric aims at persuasion, not certainty.

Rhetoric. Among them are 'accepted opinions' (maqbūlāt), which are propositions taken from one who is believed in, either because of a celestial matter such as miracles and marvels like prophets and saints, or because of his being distinguished by abundant intellect and religion like the people of knowledge and piety; they are very useful for exalting the command of Allah Almighty and showing compassion for Allah's creation. Among them are 'conjectural judgments' (maẓnūnāt), which are propositions that the intellect judges with a preponderant judgment while allowing its opposite, like our saying: 'So-and-so walks about at night, and everyone who walks about at night is a thief, therefore so-and-so is a thief.' The syllogism composed of accepted opinions and conjectural judgments is called 'rhetoric' (khaṭāba), and its purpose is to encourage people in what benefits them regarding their worldly and religious affairs, as preachers and exhorters do.

Commentary
This passage defines the second of the five arts: rhetoric (khaṭāba). The author first describes two types of premises used in rhetoric. The first is 'accepted opinions' (maqbūlāt), which are propositions taken from an authority believed either due to divine miracles (like prophets and saints) or due to exceptional intellect and piety (like scholars and ascetics). These are useful for glorifying Allah and showing compassion to His creation. The second is 'conjectural judgments' (maẓnūnāt), which are probable judgments where the intellect leans towards one side while allowing the opposite; an example is given of inferring that someone is a thief because he walks at night and every night-walker is a thief (this is a probable but not certain inference). The syllogism composed of these two types is called rhetoric, and its aim is to motivate people towards beneficial actions in both worldly and religious matters, as done by preachers (khuṭabā') and exhorters (wuʿʿāẓ).

Key Takeaway
Rhetoric uses probable premises (accepted opinions and conjectural judgments) to persuade people toward beneficial actions, not to produce certainty.
