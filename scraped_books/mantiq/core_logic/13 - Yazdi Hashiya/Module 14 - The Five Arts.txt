Classification of Syllogisms into the Five Logical Arts
Classifies qiyas into five arts by matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given syllogism into one of the five logical arts (burhan, jadal, khitaba, shi'r, mughalata) based on the epistemic quality of its premises and its intended effect.

Core Idea
The five arts are defined by whether premises produce assent or imagination, and the strength of that assent.

In the previous lesson, we saw that syllogisms can be divided by form. Here, the author introduces a more practical division: by the material, i.e., the epistemic character of the premises. This classification, known as the five arts, is crucial for understanding the purpose and validity of arguments in Islamic scholarship. Whether you are constructing a proof in kalam, engaging in dialectical debate, delivering a sermon, composing religious poetry, or exposing a fallacy, you are working within one of these arts. Let us unpack the criteria that distinguish them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division based on effect
The text first distinguishes between arguments that produce assent (tasdeeq) and those that produce imaginative representation (takhyeel). Arguments producing takhyeel belong to poetry (shi'r). Arguments producing tasdeeq are further subdivided.
Takhyeel is the effect specific to poetic syllogisms; it does not involve belief in truth.
2
Subdivide tasdeeq by the level of certainty
Among arguments producing tasdeeq, some produce only opinion (zann) — these are rhetoric (khitaba). Others produce certainty (jasm). Certainty is then divided into certain certainty (yaqini) — demonstration (burhan) — and non-certain certainty, which includes dialectic (jadal) and sophistry (mughalata).
Certainty here means conviction, but demonstration requires the premises themselves to be certain.
3
Distinguish dialectic from sophistry
Both produce non-certain certainty, but dialectic relies on premises that are generally accepted (mashhurat) or submitted by the opponent, while sophistry uses illusory premises (wahmiyyat). The text also notes terminological nuances: sophistry against a wise person is called 'safsata', against a non-wise person 'mushaghaba'.
The distinction hinges on the source and quality of the premises.
4
Apply the conditions for demonstration
Demonstration requires all premises to be certain. The text emphasizes that a fallacy can occur even if only one premise is illusory, but if a premise is poetic (inferior), the syllogism becomes a different art. This highlights the importance of the weakest premise in classification.
This step reinforces that classification is based on the epistemic character of the premises.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of qiyas (syllogism) according to its material, i.e., the epistemic quality of its premises. The author begins by stating that just as qiyas is divided by form (into conjunctive and conditional), it is also divided by matter into five arts: demonstration (البرهان), dialectic (الجدل), rhetoric (الخطابة), poetry (الشعر), and sophistry (المغالطة), also called سفسطة. The division is based on whether the premises give assent (تصديق) or another effect like imaginative representation (تخييل). The second effect belongs to poetry. The first effect (assent) is then subdivided: if it gives opinion (ظن), it is rhetoric; if it gives certainty (جزم), and that certainty is certain (يقيني), it is demonstration; if certainty is not certain but accepted by the general public or submitted by an opponent, it is dialectic; otherwise, it is sophistry. The author then clarifies terminological nuances: 'sophistry' when used against a wise person is called سفسطة, when against a non-wise person it is مشاغبة. He further notes that demonstration requires all premises to be certain, while a fallacy can arise even if only one premise is illusory (وهمية), provided the other is certain. However, it must not contain what is inferior, like poetic premises, otherwise it would belong to another art. This classification is foundational for understanding the five logical arts and their purposes in argumentation.

Widely Accepted and Admitted Premises
Commentator defines the types of premises used in the five arts beyond demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish widely accepted premises (mashhurat) from admitted premises (musallamat) and identify examples of each in Islamic debates.

Core Idea
Mashhurat are based on common opinion; Musallamat are granted by the opponent or borrowed from another science.

In dialectical and rhetorical arguments, not all premises are certain. Some are widely accepted by the community, while others are conceded by the opponent. This lesson clarifies these two types and their roles in debate.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of mashhurat
Read the definition: propositions with which everyone's opinions agree, like 'beneficence is good', or the opinions of a group, like 'slaughtering animals is ugly' for Indians.
Note the relativity to community.
2
Understand the definition of musallamat
These are propositions conceded by the opponent in debate or demonstrated in one science and taken in another on the basis of acceptance.
Musallamat are not necessarily true, just admitted.
3
Compare the two types
Mashhurat rely on societal agreement; musallamat rely on opponent's agreement or interdisciplinary acceptance.
Both are used outside demonstration.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
In a fiqh debate, 'intoxicants are forbidden' is a mashhura if widely accepted, but if the opponent is a Hanafi who allows nabidh, it may be a musallama only if conceded.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of premises used in different syllogistic arts beyond demonstration. The commentator lists and defines each type. First, al-mashhūrāt (widely accepted): these are opinions held by all or most people, like the moral principle that beneficence is good and aggression is bad. They are relative to a community, as illustrated by the Indian objection to animal slaughter. Second, al-musallamāt (admitted premises): these are propositions granted by an opponent in a debate or taken as established in one science and used in another. Third, al-maqbūlāt (accepted premises): these rely on the authority of trustworthy figures like saints and sages. Then the commentator covers al-maznūnāt (conjectural): these are merely probable, not certain, and contrasted with maqbūlāt as the general versus the specific. Next, al-mukhayyalāt (imaginative): these do not produce assent but arouse emotions like desire or aversion, often enhanced by poetic devices (rhyme and meter). Finally, the sophistical division includes al-wahmiyyāt (estimative): judgments based on the estimative faculty about non-sensibles by analogy to sensibles (e.g., every existent must be material), and al-mushabbihāt (dubitable): false propositions that mimic true or widely accepted ones through verbal or conceptual ambiguity. This taxonomy is essential for recognizing the epistemic status of arguments in different disciplines.

Accepted and Conjectural Premises
Commentator defines the types of premises used in the five arts beyond demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate accepted premises (maqbulat) from conjectural premises (maznunat) and evaluate their reliability.

Core Idea
Maqbulat rely on authority; Maznunat are probable but not certain.

In logical arguments beyond demonstration, premises may be accepted on authority or judged as probable. This lesson distinguishes these two types, which are essential for assessing the strength of dialectical and rhetorical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of maqbulat
Maqbulat are propositions taken from someone trusted, like saints and sages. The reliability depends on the trustworthiness of the source.
Used in arguments when certainty is not required.
2
Understand the definition of maznunat
Maznunat are propositions judged by the intellect as preponderant but not decisive; they are probable (zann).
They are the general category, with maqbulat as a specific type under them.
3
Compare the two types
Maqbulat are a subset of maznunat; the passage says 'their opposition to the accepted is like the general to the specific'.
Both lack certainty, but maqbulat come from authority.
4
Apply to Islamic sciences
In fiqh a fatwa may be based on a maqbula from a companion, or a maznuna from scholarly reasoning.

Imaginative, Estimative, and Dubitable Premises
Commentator defines the types of premises used in the five arts beyond demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative, estimative, and dubitable premises and recognize their roles in poetic and sophistical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Mukhayyalat move emotions; Wahmiyyat and Mushabbihaat lead to fallacious reasoning.

Beyond rational argument, some premises aim to influence emotion (poetic) or deceive (sophistical). This lesson explains the three types used in those arts: imaginative, estimative, and dubitable.

Guided Path
1
Understand mukhayyalat
These are propositions that the soul does not assent to but is emotionally affected by, e.g., 'Wine is a flowing ruby' encourages drinking.
Often enhanced by rhyme and meter.
2
Understand wahmiyyat
Judgments of the wahm (estimation) about non-sensibles based on analogy to sensibles, e.g., 'Every existent is located in space'.
These can be false but are taken as true by the estimative faculty.
3
Understand mushammihaat
False propositions that resemble true primary or widely accepted propositions due to ambiguity in wording or meaning.
They deceive by appearing true.
4
Recognize their use in syllogisms
Poetic syllogisms use mukhayyalat; sophistical syllogisms use wahmiyyat and mushammihaat.

His classification starts with the fundamental distinction between premises that include judgment and those that do not, then further divides based on the purpose of study and the degree of certainty. This provides a clear and systematic way to categorize any syllogism according to its logical nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
The commentator first divides arranged premises into two types: those that include judgment and assent (al-hukm wa al-tasdiq) and those that do not. The latter is the poetic syllogism (al-qiyas al-shi'ri).
Poetic syllogism aims at imaginative representation (takhyil), not conviction.
2
Subdivide premises with judgment
Among premises that include judgment, the commentator distinguishes based on whether the investigation is about guarding against them (al-ihrtizaz) or not. If yes, it is sophistry (al-mughalata/al-sufsata).
Sophistry studies fallacious arguments to avoid them.
3
Further subdivide by certainty
For premises whose investigation is not about guarding, the judgment is either certain (yaqini) or opinionative (maznun). Certain leads to demonstrative syllogism (al-qiyas al-burhani); opinionative leads to dialectical syllogism (al-qiyas al-jadali).
Demonstration produces certainty; dialectic produces strong opinion.
4
List the five arts and note the inductive nature
The resulting five arts are: poetic, sophistical, demonstrative, dialectical, and rhetorical (mentioned as al-khitabi in 'al-qiyas al-khitabi'). The commentator notes this enumeration is inductive (istiqra'i), not rational, as there could be other non-judgmental premises beyond imagination.
The classification is based on observation rather than a priori necessity.

The Five Arts: Why Burhan (Demonstration) Is Supreme
Author states that burhan is the most relied upon among the five arts, yielding certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the epistemological status of demonstration, rhetoric, and dialectic as presented in the Islamic logical tradition and explain the Quranic support for the primacy of burhan.

Core Idea
Only demonstration yields certainty; rhetoric and dialectic convince but do not prove.

In this passage, the author asserts that among the five arts of reasoning, demonstration (burhan) is the most relied upon because it alone produces true beliefs and eliminates false ones. While rhetoric and dialectic are sometimes used, they fall short of certainty. The author even links this hierarchy to a Quranic verse, showing the integration of logic with revelation. Your task is to unpack this epistemological distinction and see why burhan holds the highest rank.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three arts mentioned
The passage explicitly names burhan (demonstration), khataba (rhetoric), and jadal (dialectic). Note that these are three of the five arts; the other two (poetics and sophistry) are not discussed.
2
Extract the epistemological claim
The author says burhan yields certainty (yaqin) and removes false beliefs, while the others are only relied upon sometimes. This sets demonstration apart as the only source of certain knowledge.
3
Analyze the Quranic citation
The verse (16:125) is interpreted so that 'wisdom' equals burhan, 'good exhortation' equals khataba, and 'dispute in the best manner' equals jadal. This shows the Quran itself distinguishes these methods.
4
Evaluate the hierarchy
Why is burhan supreme? Because only it provides certainty, which is necessary for true belief in matters of creed. Rhetoric and dialectic serve persuasive or dialectical purposes but do not guarantee truth.
