Refutation of the Philosophers' Emanation Theory
The author refutes the philosophers' doctrine of the first intellect and the ten intellects emanating from the One.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' emanation theory and identify the author's main objections (lack of rational proof, contradiction with scripture and reason).

Core Idea
The emanation chain from ten intellects is a corrupt foundation lacking proof and contradicting tawhid.

This passage presents the Peripatetic philosophers' doctrine that from the absolutely One God, only one effect (the First Intellect) can proceed, which then multiplies through three aspects into a chain of ten intellects, souls, and celestial spheres. The author, a Shi'i theologian, refutes this doctrine as baseless and opposed to the Quran, Sunnah, and sound reason. Understanding this critique is essential for distinguishing valid theological arguments from philosophical speculations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' foundation
The author states that the philosophers and many Islamic scholars laid a 'corrupt foundation.' From the source, extract what that foundation is: the principle that from one true One only one can proceed.
This principle is the core of the emanation theory.
2
Reconstruct the emanation chain
Follow the description: the First Intellect has three considerations (existence in itself, necessity by another, contingency in itself). From each consideration, an intellect, a soul, and a celestial body proceed. This repeats down to the tenth intellect (Active Intellect).
Make a diagram: First Intellect → second intellect, second soul, second heaven; repeat to tenth intellect (moon sphere).
3
Identify the author's grounds for refutation
The author provides four grounds: (1) the theory lacks rational proof, (2) it contradicts transmitted proofs (Quran, Sunnah, consensus), (3) reason itself declares its falsity, and (4) it relies on deficient intellects polluted by illusions and ignorance of the infallibles' reports.
He also notes that later philosophers reinterpret the intellects as mere instruments, but this is a departure.
4
Assess the author's own stance
The author asserts that no effect exists except from Allah alone, and that the philosophers' reliance on their own intellects leads to error. He defers a full investigation to another place.
This shows the author's commitment to strict tawhid and rejection of intermediary causes.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the philosophers' emanation theory, which posits that from the One God only one thing can proceed, leading to a chain of ten intellects. The author begins by stating that the philosophers and many Islamic scholars have built a corrupt foundation. He explains their argument: because God is absolutely one, He cannot be the source of multiple things directly; therefore, the first effect must be one. That first effect, they claim, has three aspects (existence, necessity by another, contingency in itself), and from each aspect proceeds another being: an intellect, a soul, and a celestial body. This chain continues to the tenth intellect, the active intellect, which governs the sublunar world. The author then refutes this: it lacks rational proof and contradicts the Quran, Sunnah, consensus, and reason. He accuses the philosophers of relying on deficient intellects and ignorance of the reports of the infallible Imams. He notes that later philosophers reinterpret the intellects as mere instruments, but the author remains critical. The passage ends by deferring a full investigation to another occasion.

Definition of 'Alam (Proper Name) Linguistically and Technically
Author defines the term 'alam linguistically and technically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the linguistic and technical definitions of 'alam and identify proper names in Arabic texts.

Core Idea
A proper name (علم) is a signpost for the mind: it points to one thing and excludes all others.

This passage introduces the term 'alam, which in Arabic means a proper name. The author first gives its literal meaning—a physical signpost—then defines it logically as a word assigned to a specific individual. Understanding this distinction helps you recognize why proper names like 'Allah' or 'Muhammad' function differently in arguments than common nouns.

Guided Path
1
Locate the linguistic definition
Find where the author gives the first meaning: 'what is erected on the road to be guided by it.' This is the literal meaning.
2
Identify the technical definition
Now find the technical meaning: 'what is placed for a specific individual, not including others.' This is the logical definition.
3
Compare the two definitions
Notice how the technical definition preserves the idea of pointing ('signpost') but shifts from a physical object to a linguistic term.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the proper name 'Allah'. It is a proper name because it refers specifically to the Necessary Existent and does not apply to anyone else. Test 'Muhammad' as well.

Why 'Allah' is a Proper Name: Linguistic and Theological Arguments
Author argues from derivation and tawhid that 'Allah' is a proper name.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that 'Allah' must be a proper name for the Necessary Existent using the derivation from 'ilah' and the tawhid test.

Core Idea
La ilaha illa Allah proves 'Allah' is a proper name, not a concept.

The author addresses the longstanding debate on the nature of the word 'Allah'. He presents a linguistic derivation from 'ilah' and a key theological argument: if 'Allah' were a common noun for 'the one worshipped', the testimony of tawhid would fail. This demonstrates that 'Allah' must be a proper name for the individual Necessary Existent.

Guided Path
1
Trace the linguistic derivation
Note that 'Allah' is traced to 'ilah' (على فعال) meaning 'worshipped', with the hamza deleted and replaced by the definite article. This shows it originally referred to the worshipped one, but later became a proper name.
The author mentions al-Jawhari's alternative view that the hamza deletion is for ease, not compensation.
2
Identify the problem with the common noun interpretation
If 'Allah' were a common noun meaning 'the one worshipped in truth' or 'the necessary by itself', the testimony 'La ilaha illa Allah' would either be an exception of the thing from itself or false due to many false objects of worship. This forces the conclusion that 'Allah' must be a proper name for the individual.
The author quotes Sharh al-Talkhis to reinforce this point.
3
Grasp the theological implication
As a proper name, 'Allah' uniquely identifies the Creator, making the testimony of tawhid meaningful and not tautological or false.

Analyzing Dialectical Objections on the Meaning of Salat
Author interprets salat in Quran 9:103 as du'a, responds to objections about commonality of homonym and transitivity with 'ala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical arguments regarding the meaning of salat in Quran 9:103 and evaluate the commentator's responses to objections.

Core Idea
Salat is used metaphorically, not homonymously, when attributed to Allah and angels.

In this passage, the commentator defends the interpretation of salat as du'a in Quran 9:103, while addressing two key objections: that a homonym cannot be used in two meanings simultaneously, and that du'a with 'ala' implies harm. We will walk through the logical structure of these objections and the commentator's responses using jadal (dialectical) methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim (mudda'i)
The commentator asserts that 'salat' in the verse means du'a (supplication). This is the central position being defended.
2
Find the first objection (mu'tarid)
The objector claims that since salat is a homonym (common between mercy, forgiveness, and supplication), using it in both meanings simultaneously is impermissible according to the rules of language.
3
Analyze the first response (jawab)
The commentator replies that the verse does not use both meanings at once; instead, it is interpreted separately for Allah and angels. He prefers metaphor over homonymy to avoid the problem.
4
Find the second objection
The objector argues that du'a with the preposition 'ala' implies harm, so 'salat' cannot mean du'a because the verse uses 'ala'.
5
Analyze the second response
The commentator offers multiple answers: (a) when attributed to Allah, it is freed from the connotation of request; (b) the rule that 'ala' implies harm is based on common usage, not absolute; (c) even if it did, the harm is linked to the word du'a, not salat. He also points out that claiming an absolute universal would make the objection invalid.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all logic is theoretical; its axiomatic core escapes the charge of circularity.

A powerful objection claims that logic is either acquired (leading to circularity) or self-evident (making learning it unnecessary). This dilemma threatens the very discipline. The passage provides a nuanced response: logic is partly necessary and partly theoretical, with the theoretical parts derived from the necessary. Understanding this resolution is key to defending the utility of logic in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dilemma's two horns
Find where the objector presents two possibilities: logic is either acquired (kasbi) or self-evident (badihi). Each leads to an unacceptable conclusion.

Analyze the first horn (acquired)
If logic is acquired, then acquiring it requires another logical law, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). Both are impossible.
The key is that each step would require a prior logical law.

If logic is self-evident, then it does not need to be learned, so there is no need to study it. This undermines the very purpose of logic textbooks.
This horn denies the value of learning logic.

The author denies that logic is entirely one or the other: part of it (the first figure) is self-evident, and the rest is derived from it. Thus, no circularity arises, and learning the theoretical parts remains necessary.
The first figure is argued to be axiomatic (badihi).

The passage also presents a variation where the objector claims logic is entirely self-evident, so no learning is needed. The author responds that even if true, this only negates the need to learn it, not the need for logic itself in acquiring other sciences.

The Etymology and Morphology of al-Haqiqah
Etymological and morphological analysis of al-haqiqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the morphological structure of the term 'al-haqiqah' and explain the implications of its being either active or passive in meaning.

Core Idea
'Al-haqiqah' is derived from 'haqqa' meaning 'to be established' or 'haqqaqa' meaning 'to establish', with the ta' indicating either transference or feminization.

The author delves into the etymology of 'haqiqah' to deepen our understanding. He considers two possible morphological origins: either it is fa'il (active participle) meaning 'the one that is established' or maf'ul (passive participle) meaning 'the one that is established'. The ta' at the end serves either to mark the transfer from verb to noun or for feminization. This analysis connects linguistic theory with logical precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the root
The term 'haqiqah' comes from the root H-Q-Q, which carries the meaning of truth and reality. Note that the root appears in Quranic usage like 'bi'l-haqqi'.
2
Determine the morphological pattern
The word is on the pattern fa'ila(t) with a taa' marbuta at the end. The base is 'haqiq' (without ta'), which is fa'il (active or passive).
The pattern fa'il can indicate both active and passive meanings.
3
Analyze the two possibilities
As an active participle (from haqqa yahiqqu), 'haqiqah' means 'that which is established' (the one that is true). As a passive participle (from haqqaqtu), it means 'that which is established' (the one made true). The author mentions both.
The ta' is for 'transfer' (naql) in both interpretations, i.e., to form a noun from a participle.
4
Examine the feminization issue
Sahib al-Miftah argued that the ta' is for feminization, not transfer. The author discusses the implications: if active, fa'il agrees in gender; if passive, it is usually invariable unless not run on its described. This affects whether 'haqiqah' is considered feminine.
The Quranic example 'ramim' (decayed) is used to illustrate the passive fa'il not inflected.
5
Consider the singular vs. compound debate
If ta' is for feminization and the word is passive, then 'haqiqah' must be singular (since it modifies a feminine singular described, like 'kalima'). But the well-known view allows compound expressions to be haqiqah, leading to a tension noted by the author.
The author concludes that this analysis involves affectation and can be dispensed with.

Implicit vs Explicit Knowledge: Resolving Circularity in Syllogisms

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A syllogism is not circular: the conclusion is implicitly known in the premise but explicitly known only after deduction.

When studying logical demonstration, a common objection arises: if the conclusion is already contained in the major premise, doesn't the syllogism merely repeat what is already known? The Consistency of Generality Rules for Predicates in Science and Problem
The commentator presents a dilemma to show inconsistency in al-Ustadh's ruling on predicate generality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dilemma used to critique al-Ustadh's position on predicate generality and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
Inconsistency in logical rules is self-defeating; the critic's dilemma exposes the flaw.

In this passage, the commentator examines a position held by 'the Master' (al-Ustadh) concerning whether the predicate of a science's subject and the predicate of a problem's subject can be more general than their subjects. The Master appears to allow generality for the problem's predicate but not for the science's predicate. The commentator argues this is arbitrary and constructs a dilemma to show that regardless of how one interprets the prohibition, the Master's position is inconsistent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two subjects in question
The passage distinguishes between the subject of the science (موضوع العلم) and the subject of a particular problem (موضوع المسألة). Both have predicates (محمول) that may or may not be general (أعم).
2
Determine al-Ustadh's ruling
The Master prohibited generality for the predicate of the science's subject but permitted it for the predicate of the problem's subject. This is the position under attack.
The commentator calls this differential treatment arbitrary (تحكم).
3
Understand the two possible interpretations of 'not general'
The commentator presents two interpretations: (1) the predicate must not remain general without being restricted by specifying qualifications (قيود مخصصة) to become an essential accident; (2) the predicate must not even appear general, even if it can be restricted in reality.
These exhaust the possibilities for what the prohibition might mean.
4
Apply each interpretation to both predicates
Under interpretation (1), both predicates are prohibited from being general without restriction, so allowing it for the problem's subject is inconsistent. Under interpretation (2), both predicates can be general in appearance if they are essentially accidental, so prohibiting it for the science's subject is inconsistent.
In both cases, the Master's differentiated ruling fails to be coherent.
5
Conclude that the position is unsupported
Since reason (عقل) and tradition (نقل) do not support the distinction, the Master's speech is defective (لا يخلو عن خلل). The commentator demands consistency: treat both alike or provide a valid distinction.

Distinguishing Common Belief from Necessary Truth
Gloss advises emptying the mind of common beliefs to distinguish necessary truths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemological danger of confusing shuhra with daruriyyat and apply the method of mental emptying to achieve certainty.

Core Idea
Common opinion can masquerade as self-evident truth; certainty requires stripping away assumptions.

This gloss from a logical commentary warns that widely held beliefs (shuhra) can become so pervasive that they are mistaken for necessary, self-evident truths (daruriyyat). To reach certainty, one must actively clear the mind of extraneous influences and rely on clear primary principles. This lesson unpacks that warning and its practical implications for seekers of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key concepts
Locate 'shuhra' (widespread opinion) and 'daruriyyat' (necessary truths) in the Arabic. Note that shuhra can be false, while daruriyyat are immediately known and certain.
These are contrasting categories in Islamic epistemology.
2
Understand the danger
The gloss states that shuhra 'may sometimes reach such that it becomes confused with necessary truths.' Reflect on how a common but false belief (e.g., 'the earth is flat') could feel self-evident due to repetition.
This confusion is a major obstacle to certainty.
3
Prescribed method: mental emptying
The gloss says one must 'empty his mind of extraneous affairs' (يخلى الأمور المغايرة لعقله). This means setting aside preconceptions, cultural assumptions, and untested common opinions.
This is an active intellectual discipline, not passive neglect.
4
Goal: distinguishing primary truths
The result of mental emptying is that 'primary truths become distinguished for him from others and are not confused.' One can then identify genuinely self-evident propositions.
Examples of daruriyyat: 'The whole is greater than the part,' 'A thing cannot both exist and not exist at the same time.'
5
Reflect on the supplication
The gloss ends with a prayer for guidance to the truth. This reminds us that achieving certainty also requires divine assistance, not just human effort.
The supplication grounds the intellectual effort in a spiritual context.
