Understanding Conceptual Distinctions: Sadaq vs Haqq
A short note comparing the distinction to a previous one between sadaq and haqq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a distinction in Islamic logic is merely conceptual (i'tibari) rather than real, using the example of al-sadaq and al-haqq.

Core Idea
Distinctions can be purely conceptual, reflecting different aspects of the same reality.

In studying Islamic logic, you will often encounter pairs of terms that are distinguished carefully. This note from a classical commentary reminds us that some distinctions are not about separate entities but about different ways of considering the same thing. Understanding this prevents confusion between logical concepts and metaphysical reality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of distinction
The note explicitly says the distinction is 'merely by consideration and observation' (bi-mujarrad al-i'tibar wa al-mulahadha). This tells us it is a conceptual distinction.
Conceptual distinctions are also called i'tibari or 'aqli.
2
Compare with the sadaq/haqq example
The 'truth' (sadaq) is a property of statements, while 'the true' (haqq) is reality itself. They are not two separate things but two aspects: truth as correspondence and truth as reality.
The commentator uses this analogy to clarify that the new distinction is similar.
3
Recognize the pattern in other distinctions
Similar distinctions in logic (e.g., between essence and existence, or between attribute and essence) are often conceptual. The key is whether the two terms can refer to the same object under different descriptions.
If they always coexist and one cannot exist without the other, the distinction is likely conceptual.

The Problem of Equating Mufhum and Ma'na
Critical note identifying a problem in equating mufhum and ma'na.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical contradiction that arises when mufhum and ma'na are both defined as the mental image (sura dhihniyya).

Core Idea
Confusing the mental image with the essence makes universality an attribute of the wrong thing.

In logic, mufhum (concept) and ma'na (meaning) are often equated. But if both are reduced to the mental image, a problem arises: the division into universal and particular would then apply to the image itself, not to the thing it represents. This lesson walks you through the problem as presented by Sheikh Abd al-Rahim.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definitions
Mufhum is what is understood from a word; ma'na is the mental image for which the word stands. They are said to be identical in essence.
2
Trace the consequence
If both are the mental image, then universality (kulliyya) and particularity (juz'iyya) become attributes of that image.
3
Spot the contradiction
But kulliyya and juz'iyya are attributes of the thing with the image (dhu al-sura), i.e., the known essence, not of the image itself.
4
Understand why earlier attempts fail
Al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif's defense (that 'occurred' means 'apt to occur') is insufficient, as the note indicates.
The problem is a logical inconsistency in the definitions.

The Resolution: Two Senses of al-Sura al-Dhihniyya
Resolution of the problem by distinguishing two senses of mental image.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two meanings of 'mental image' (sura dhihniyya) and explain which one grounds the division into kulli and juz'i.

Core Idea
The mental image splits: one is the mirror, the other is the essence seen through it.

The contradiction from the previous lesson is resolved by recognizing that 'mental image' is used in two ways. Only the second sense — the known essence as it exists mentally — is the proper subject of universality and particularity. This lesson clarifies that distinction as presented by Sheikh Abd al-Rahim.

Guided Path
1
Identify sense one
The mental image as a quality that occurs in the mind, acting as a mirror for observing the external thing (dhu al-sura).
This sense is a mental quality, not the thing itself.
2
Identify sense two
The mental image as the known essence (mahiyya) itself, distinguished by means of that image in the mind, considered in its mental existence.
This sense is the essence as it exists in the mind.
3
See which sense divides
The division into universal and particular applies to sense two, because it is the essence that can be 'true of many' or not.
Sense one (the mirror) is not divisible into kulli/juz'i.
4
Apply the resolution
With this distinction, the earlier problem disappears: kulliyya/juz'iyya are attributes of the essence (sense two), not of the representing quality (sense one).
This resolution is offered by Sheikh Abd al-Rahim after rejecting al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif's attempt.

The Etymology of Ma'na: Linguistic Precision in Logic
Etymological clarification of the term 'ma'na'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible derivations of the word 'ma'na' and explain why the maf'al derivation is preferred in the logical tradition.

Core Idea
Etymology ensures logical terms carry no unnecessary baggage.

The word 'ma'na' (meaning) is a cornerstone of Islamic logic. But where does it come from? This short note examines two possible Arabic derivations and argues for the one that is linguistically cleaner. Understanding this choice sharpens your appreciation for how Islamic scholars handled technical terminology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proposed etymons
The passage says 'ma'na' is either maf'al from 'anā (to intend) or a lightened form of the passive participle maf'ūl from the same root. Find these two in the Arabic text.
The root is 'a-n-y (عني).
2
Compare the linguistic changes required
The second derivation (passive participle) requires claiming deletion of a letter and sound changes (kasra to fatha, ya to alif). The first derivation (maf'al) requires no such changes. Spot these differences in the commentary.
The author prefers the simpler derivation.
3
Connect to logical precision
Why does this matter for logic? A cleaner derivation means the term carries no extra assumptions about its meaning. This reflects the broader Islamic concern for precise definitions in logical and legal reasoning.
In logic, the term 'ma'na' often refers to the intended meaning of a word or proposition.

Understanding 'Farad' in the Definition of the Particular
A clarification that 'farad' means the mind's rational allowance in the definition of juz'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the refined meaning of 'farad' in the definition of juz'i and why it resolves the objection about supposing Zayd applies to many.

Core Idea
Farad means rational allowance, not arbitrary supposition—saving the definition of juz'i.

In this hashya, the author clarifies a subtle but crucial point in the definition of the particular (juz'i). The standard definition says the particular is that for which it is impossible to suppose (farad) its truth upon many. But isn't it possible to suppose Zayd, a particular, applies to many individuals? If so, the definition fails. The author resolves this by distinguishing two senses of 'farad'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard definition of the particular.
The definition states: 'The particular is that which cannot be supposed (farad) to be true of many.' This is a standard definition in logic.
The definition is found in classical texts like al-Shamsiyya.
2
Understand the objection raised.
One might object: We can certainly suppose Zayd (a particular) applies to many individuals, like imagining many Zayds. So the definition seems false.
The objection is based on the ability to form a mental image.
3
Distinguish the two meanings of 'farad'.
The author notes that 'farad' here does not mean any arbitrary mental supposition (like imagining a human with multiple heads). Rather, it means the mind's rational allowance and acceptance (tajwiz al-'aql wa irtida'uhu). The mind does not rationally allow that Zayd could be truly applicable to many distinct actual individuals.
Tajwiz al-'aql is a stronger condition than mere supposition.
4
Apply the refinement to the definition.
With this refined meaning, the definition holds: it is impossible for the mind to rationally allow that a particular like Zayd is true of many individuals. This also excludes universals with no actual instances (like partner of God) from being universals, as their truth of anything is impossible even by rational allowance.

Two Meanings of Clear Concomitant and the Commentator's Refinement
Author explains the two meanings of clear and non-clear concomitant and the commentator's refinement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two meanings of clear concomitant (bayyin bi-l-maʿnā al-akhass and bayyin bi-l-maʿnā al-aʿamm) and explain why the commentator expanded the definition of non-clear.

Core Idea
The clear concomitant has two meanings, and the non-clear includes not only those needing a middle term but also those requiring intuition or experience.

In implicative signification (dalāla iltizāmiyya), a necessary concomitant (lāzim) can be clear (bayyin) or non-clear (ghayr bayyin). But clear and non-clear each have two distinct senses, and the standard interpretation of non-clear as merely requiring a middle term is insufficient. The commentator (al-Muḥashshī) refines the definition to include any need for an external factor beyond the two concepts and their relation, such as intuition, experience, or sense. This lesson unpacks that refinement and its implications for Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of 'clear' (bayyin)
From the passage, extract the definitions of clear in the specific sense (bayyin bi-l-maʿnā al-akhass) and clear in the more general sense (bayyin bi-l-maʿnā al-aʿamm). Note that the specific sense requires only conception of the subject for certainty of concomitance, while the general sense requires conception of both terms and the relation.
The specific sense is a subset of the general sense, but the passage argues they are not simply related as part to whole.
2
Define non-clear (ghayr bayyin) for each sense
For each meaning of clear, derive the corresponding non-clear: non-clear in the specific sense is a concomitant whose conception does not follow from the subject alone; non-clear in the general sense is a concomitant whose certainty does not follow from conception of the terms and the relation.
The author's original wording might confuse these two, so careful parsing is needed.
3
Understand the problem with the standard interpretation of non-clear
The well-known interpretation defined non-clear as requiring a middle term (wāsiṭa) introduced by 'because' (li-annahu). The commentator shows that this excludes cases where certainty depends on intuition (ḥads), experience (tajriba), or sense (ḥiss) without an explicit middle term.
Example: 'The world is originated because it is changing' uses 'changing' as a middle term. But 'fire burns' known by experience has no middle term yet is not self-evident.
4
See the commentator's expanded definition
The commentator redefines non-clear as a concomitant that requires something beyond the two terms and their relation, whether that be a middle term (for theoretical matters) or something else (for self-evident but non-immediate matters like intuitions, experiences, and sensations).
This makes the division exhaustive: the self-evident matters that are immediate are clear in the general sense; everything else is non-clear.
5
Apply to a new example
Take the statement: 'This honey is sweet' based on tasting. The concomitant 'sweetness' is not immediately clear from the concept of honey alone (so not clear in specific sense). Is it clear in the general sense? No, because tasting (experience) is needed beyond the concepts of honey and sweetness and their relation. So it is non-clear according to the expanded definition.
This shows why the commentator's refinement is necessary to account for all types of knowledge.

The Definition of Contradiction and Essential Entailment
Defines contradiction with essential entailment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a pair of propositions meets the condition of essential mutual entailment for genuine contradiction.

Core Idea
For genuine contradiction, mutual entailment must be essential, not accidental.

Contradiction (تناقض) is a cornerstone of logical reasoning, especially in kalam proofs. In this lesson, we unpack the precise definition from the hashiya: contradiction is a difference between two propositions such that the truth of each necessarily entails the falsity of the other, and vice versa, and this entailment must be essential (لذاته), not due to external factors. We will learn to distinguish genuine contradiction from accidental opposition.

Guided Path
1
Locate the formal definition
In the passage, the author states: 'يعنى انه لما اعتبر فى التناقض ان يكون اختلاف القضيتين بحيث يلزم لذاته صدق كل منهما كذب الأخرى وبالعكس'. Identify the two directions of entailment: truth-to-falsity and falsity-to-truth.
Both directions must hold.
2
Understand the essentiality condition
The phrase 'لذاته' (essentially) means the entailment is intrinsic, not due to an external cause or specific content. If the entailment relies on an outside equivalence or a particular matter, it is not a genuine contradiction.
This is the key differentia.
3
Apply to an example from the passage
Consider the pair: 'Zayd is human' (زيد إنسان) and 'Zayd is not laughing' (زيد ليس بضاحك). These appear contradictory, but the entailment is mediated by the equivalence of 'human' and 'laughing' (all humans laugh). Thus it is accidental, not essential.
4
Test a pair from kalam
Take the propositions: 'The world is temporally originated' and 'The world is eternal'. Is their mutual entailment essential? Yes, because the two exhaust the possibilities—if one is true, the other is false by definition, without external mediation.
This satisfies the condition.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary (hashiya) that expands on a previous sharh regarding the definition and scope of contradiction (تناقض). The author begins by addressing the claim that contradiction does not apply to single terms (مفردات). He refutes this by showing that if judgment is considered, the terms are no longer single; if not, negation and affirmation are absent, which are essential to contradiction. However, he notes that the real dispute is verbal: some mean contradiction with negation/affirmation, others mean absolute contradiction. The truth is that absolute contradiction applies to single terms as well, and he uses the author's definition of contrapositive (عكس النقيض) to show that terms of a proposition are not themselves propositions (in conditionals, they are completed by particles; in categoricals, the subject is a single term, and the predicate, though a sentence, is treated as a single word grammatically). Next, the author explains why the science restricts contradiction to propositions: because their purpose is to establish contradiction (الخلف) for proving inverses and syllogisms, which requires propositional contradiction only. The core of the passage is the detailed explanation of the condition that the mutual entailment must be essential (لذاته), not accidental. He gives two examples of accidental entailment: (1) an affirmative with its equivalent necessary consequence negated, where the entailment is mediated by an external equivalence (e.g., Zayd is human vs. Zayd is not laughing, if 'human' implies 'laughing'); (2a) particular affirmative and negative, where the truth entailment is not essential but due to matter (e.g., 'Some humans are animals' and 'Some humans are not animals' – both are true with different subjects, but if the matter changes, they can be false); (2b) universal affirmative and negative, where the falsity entailment is not essential (e.g., 'Nothing of humans is animal' and 'Every human is animal' – both are false if the matter is 'animal' and 'human'). The commentator then notes that he explicitly excludes the first type of accidental difference but not the second, because the second is more severe and its exclusion is already apparent; even if included, it would not be called contradiction in the technical sense.

The Scope of Contradiction: Single Terms
Discusses whether contradiction applies to single terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate the two senses of contradiction and explain why the dispute over single terms is verbal.

Core Idea
The dispute over single terms vanishes once we distinguish absolute contradiction from contradiction requiring negation and affirmation.

Some logicians denied that contradiction applies to single terms (مفردات) like 'exists' and 'does not exist'. In this lesson, we explore the argument and its resolution: the disagreement is verbal. Absolute contradiction does apply to single terms, while contradiction defined with negation and affirmation does not. We will see how the author's later definition of contrapositive supports this.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
One group says contradiction does not apply to single terms because negation and affirmation are absent. Another says it does, meaning absolute contradiction. Recognize that the first group means contradiction in the sense requiring judgment (حكم), the second means mere opposition.
The passage calls this a verbal dispute (نزاع لفظي).
2
Find the supporting argument for single-term contradiction
The author cites the definition of contrapositive (عكس النقيض) which involves replacing the contradictories of terms. Those terms are not propositions; therefore, contradiction can hold between single terms in an absolute sense.
See: 'فان اطراف القضية ليست بقضية'.
3
Apply to a concrete example
Take the single terms 'موجود' (exists) and 'معدوم' (non-existent). In the absolute sense, they are contradictory because affirming one implies denying the other, even without a propositional judgment.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary (hashiya) that expands on a previous sharh regarding the definition and scope of contradiction (تناقض). The author begins by addressing the claim that contradiction does not apply to single terms (مفردات). He refutes this by showing that if judgment is considered, the terms are no longer single; if not, negation and affirmation are absent, which are essential to contradiction. However, he notes that the real dispute is verbal: some mean contradiction with negation/affirmation, others mean absolute contradiction. The truth is that absolute contradiction applies to single terms as well, and he uses the author's definition of contrapositive (عكس النقيض) to show that terms of a proposition are not themselves propositions (in conditionals, they are completed by particles; in categoricals, the subject is a single term, and the predicate, though a sentence, is treated as a single word grammatically). Next, the author explains why the science restricts contradiction to propositions: because their purpose is to establish contradiction (الخلف) for proving inverses and syllogisms, which requires propositional contradiction only. The core of the passage is the detailed explanation of the condition that the mutual entailment must be essential (لذاته), not accidental. He gives two examples of accidental entailment: (1) an affirmative with its equivalent necessary consequence negated, where the entailment is mediated by an external equivalence (e.g., Zayd is human vs. Zayd is not laughing, if 'human' implies 'laughing'); (2a) particular affirmative and negative, where the truth entailment is not essential but due to matter (e.g., 'Some humans are animals' and 'Some humans are not animals' – both are true with different subjects, but if the matter changes, they can be false); (2b) universal affirmative and negative, where the falsity entailment is not essential (e.g., 'Nothing of humans is animal' and 'Every human is animal' – both are false if the matter is 'animal' and 'human'). The commentator then notes that he explicitly excludes the first type of accidental difference but not the second, because the second is more severe and its exclusion is already apparent; even if included, it would not be called contradiction in the technical sense.

Essential vs Non-Essential Contradiction
Defines essential contradiction and excludes non-essential differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and non-essential contradiction and identify the two types of non-essential differences excluded from genuine contradiction.

Core Idea
True contradiction requires each proposition to essentially entail the other's falsehood – not by external cause or particular matter.

The definition of contradiction in Islamic logic is precise: two propositions must be such that the truth of each essentially necessitates the falsehood of the other. Not all pairs that appear contradictory qualify; some are non-essential due to external causes or specific subject matter. This lesson refines your understanding of what counts as a genuine logical contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essential condition
Genuine contradiction requires that each proposition's truth essentially entails the other's falsehood both ways. The entailment must be by the very meaning (bi-l-dhat) of the propositions, not by external factors.
2
Recognize the first excluded type
The first type is due to an external cause: when one proposition is equivalent to a necessary property (lazim musawi) of the other, the entailment is mediated. Example: 'Zayd is a human' and 'Zayd is not laughing' – since 'human' is essentially equivalent to 'laughing', the apparent contradiction is not direct.
3
Recognize the second excluded type
The second type is due to particular matter (madda): either the entailment from truth to falsehood is not essential (as with particular affirmative and negative that can both be true in some matters) or the entailment from falsehood to truth is not essential (as with universal affirmative and negative that can both be false in some matters).
4
Apply the distinction to examples
Given a pair of propositions, determine if the contradiction is essential or non-essential. Check whether the entailment is direct or mediated by an external cause or specific matter.

Why 'mu'allaf' is not redundant in the definition of qiyas
Author defends inclusion of 'mu'allaf' against redundancy charge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the logical distinction between 'murakkab' and 'mu'allaf' and defend the inclusion of 'mu'allaf' in the definition of qiyas.

Core Idea
Mu'allaf adds the crucial condition of suitability and formal arrangement to mere composition.

Logicians define qiyas as 'a composed statement that entails...' But why include 'mu'allaf' (composed) after already saying 'murakkab' (compound)? This passage from al-Yazdi's gloss tackles that objection head-on. By unpacking the relationship between general and specific terms, we see that 'mu'allaf' brings precision by requiring affinity and proper form—essential for a valid syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find where the objector claims that 'mu'allaf' after 'murakkab' is redundant. They say the definition could do with one term only.
The objection is that 'murakkab' already implies composition, so adding 'mu'allaf' is useless repetition.
2
Understand the first defence: specific after general
The author responds that 'murakkab' is more general—any combination—while 'mu'allaf' requires suitability (munasaba) between parts. So 'mu'allaf' is the specific case, and mentioning specific after general is common in definitions.
This is like saying 'animal' then 'rational animal' for man.
3
Follow the second defence: 'qawl' as remote genus
Some commentators reinterpret 'qawl' not as the logical 'compound' but as any utterance (simple or compound). Then 'mu'allaf' restricts it to compound utterances, acting as a proximate genus.
Here 'qawl' becomes a broader category that includes simple words, and 'mu'allaf' excludes them.
4
Analyse the objection to both answers
A later objection says that if 'qawl' is more general and 'mu'allaf' is specific, the concept of the general is included in the specific—which violates the rule that a genus should not be part of its species.
For example, defining man as 'body rational animal' includes 'body' inside 'animal', which is circular.
5
Grasp the final resolution
The author resolves this by noting that the concept of the specific can be stripped from the general concept—that is, we can consider 'mu'allaf' independently of its inclusion in 'qawl'.
Thus, the definition remains valid and precise.

Identifying Essential Accidents Across Sciences
Glosses on essential accidents across physics, grammar, and geometry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject and its essential accident in examples from natural science, grammar, and geometry, and distinguish essential accidents from non-essential properties.

Core Idea
An essential accident is a property that belongs to the subject itself, not through an external cause.

In Islamic logic, the concept of 'arad dhati (essential accident) is crucial for understanding how properties are attributed to subjects in demonstrative sciences. This lesson uses concrete examples from physics, grammar, and geometry—as provided in the glosses—to train you to recognize essential accidents and their role in scientific reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the science
For each example, determine what the subject of that science is. In natural science, the subject is the body (jism). In grammar, it is the word (kalima). In geometry, it is magnitude (miqdar) or line (khatt).
The subject is what the science studies primarily.
2
Find the property that is essential to that subject
In the examples, the property is said to be an 'arad dhati for that subject. For body, movement is essential. For word, declension (i'rab) is essential. For magnitude, being a square or being a mean proportional is essential. For line, producing right angles when intersecting.
Essential accidents are not just any property, but those whose existence is caused by the essence of the subject.
3
Check if the property applies to all instances of the subject
An essential accident must be co-extensive with the subject or at least apply to a class of it. For example, every body is subject to movement (in the sense of potential or actual change), every word has declension (in Arabic grammar), every magnitude can be measured, etc.
This ensures the property is intrinsic, not accidental in the non-technical sense.
4
Contrast with properties that are not essential accidents
The commentary also hints that some properties are taken from other sciences. For instance, a square being an essential accident of magnitude is specific to geometry. If a property depends on an external cause, it is not an essential accident.
This distinction is key to defining the boundaries of a science.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Logic is called 'mantiq' because it actualizes the rational soul's perfections.

In this passage, we see a classic example of how Islamic logicians defended the name of their science. Then, if they are not in need of matter in external and intellectual existence, it is the divine science; if they need matter in both existences, it is the natural science; if their need for matter is only in external existence, not in conceptualization, it is the mathematical science.
