Refining the Definition of Relative Species
Author defends the definition of relative species against an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why accidental aggregates like 'Turkish man' are excluded from the definition of relative species and explain the role of essentiality and universality.

Core Idea
Relative species must be both essential and universal to avoid accidental aggregates.

In this passage, the author addresses a potential flaw in the standard definition of relative species (al-naw' al-idafi). He explains why accidental aggregates like 'Turkish man' and real particulars like 'this hand' are correctly excluded. This refinement ensures that the definition only captures true species relations, which is crucial for precise classification in Islamic logic and sciences like usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection claims that the definition of relative species applies to accidental aggregates (sinaf) and real parts. Find the Arabic phrase: 'ان تعريف النوع الاضافى ليس بمطرد الصدقه على الصنف والجزء الحقيق'.
The objection is that the definition is too broad.
2
Understand the response conditions
The author says mahiyya (essence) must be both universal (so excludes particulars) and essential (so excludes accidental aggregates). These two conditions are necessary for a correct definition.
Universality alone is insufficient; essentiality is also required.
3
Distinguish types of properties
A sub-objection suggests that 'sinaf' is a property and thus already excluded by the phrase 'in answer to what is it?'. The author replies by dividing properties into two types: those that share their genus with others and those that do not. Sinaf belongs to the first type, so it is not excluded by that phrase alone.
This shows why the essentiality condition is not redundant.
4
Apply the test
Check 'Turkish man': it is universal but accidental – fails essentiality. Check 'this hand': it is essential but particular – fails universality. Both are correctly excluded.
Practice checking other examples like 'student' or 'living thing'.

Core Idea
The debate over real vs. relative species hinges on whether simple essences can have a genus.

In this passage, the author presents a foundational disagreement in Islamic logic: Are all real species necessarily also relative species? The earlier scholars said yes, the later scholars said no. Their dispute centers on simple essences—entities with no parts—and whether such essences can be classified under a genus. Understanding this debate sharpens your grasp of the tree of Porphyry and the categories.

Know that there is disagreement about the relationship between real species and relative species: is it absolute generality (the former being always included under the latter) or not? The earlier scholars held the first view. They said: every real species is included under one of the ten categories, because the universals are confined to them, as established in its place; and those categories are genera; and whatever is included under a genus is a relative species. So every real species is a relative species. The later scholars held the second view. They said: we do not concede that every real species is included under one of the ten categories; that would be necessary only if every real species were possible, but it is not. And even if we concede, we do not concede that the possibles are confined to the ten categories; rather, what is confined are the genera of the possibles of the world, as they have stated. Then they argued for their view by [the case of] simple [essences]. They said: they have no part such that a genus could be for them, for genus, as previously, is part of the essence. If they are composite, they inevitably terminate in simples, and the same point returns. This was refuted: simplicity of essence does not necessitate that it is a species, let alone a real one; it could be a highest genus, a genus, a differentia, or something else. (Mirza Muhammad Ali)

Commentary
This passage presents a major debate in logic about the scope of relative species. The earlier scholars (al-mutaqaddimun) argued that every real species (naw' haqiqi) — the lowest species under a genus — is also a relative species (naw' idafi) because it must fall under a category, and categories are genera, so it is relative to that genus. The later scholars (al-muta'akhirun) rejected this. They argued that not every real species is under a category — only possible essences are, and some real species might be necessary (like God?) or other. Even if they were under categories, they are not confined to ten. Their main argument was from simple essences (al-basa'it): entities with no parts cannot have a genus because genus is part. Simple essences would then be real species but not relative. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi argued that all essences must be either simple or composite; if simple, they are real species; if composite, they reduce to simples, so the same applies. The refutation (marking a counter-argument) is that simplicity does not automatically make something a species; it could be a highest genus (like substance) or a differentia (like rational). Thus the debate is unresolved.

The Point as Simple Essence: External vs Mental Parts
Critique of point example, distinguishing part types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish external parts from mental parts and explain why the geometric point can have a genus despite having no physical parts.

Core Idea
Lack of external parts does not mean lack of mental parts, which form the definition.

This passage from the hashiya critiques a standard logical example: the geometric point, often used to illustrate a simple essence with no genus. The critique reveals a vital distinction: the difference between external parts (like a hand in a body) and mental parts (like genus and differentia in a definition). While the point has no physical extension, it still has mental parts (e.g., being an endpoint), allowing it to fall under a genus. Understanding this distinction refines our use of examples and prevents erroneous conclusions about simplicity.

This claim is about the point's simplicity.

The key is the distinction between types of parts.

Core Idea
True species looks down to individuals; relative species looks up to genus.

In the five universals, the distinction between true species and relative species is not merely semantic—it determines how a term functions in a definition. The author clarifies that 'true species' is considered with respect to its individuals below, while 'relative species' is considered with respect to its genus above.

3
Apply to examples from Islamic sciences
For example, 'human' is a true species because it has no lower species; 'animal' is a relative species because it serves as a genus for human and other species.

Can Simple Entities Have a Genus? The Critique of al-Razi's Argument
Critique of the argument that simple essences have no genus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate al-Razi's argument that simple essences have no genus and identify the logical flaw in the example of the point.

Core Idea
Simplicity does not preclude having a mental genus.

The passage critiques the claim that simple entities (البسائط) like the geometric point cannot have a genus because they have no parts. Al-Razi argued that if an essence is simple, it must be a true species. The author rebuts this by showing that the genus is a mental part, not an external one, so simplicity does not rule out a genus.

Infinite Regress in Genera and Species
Author argues against infinite regress in genera and species hierarchies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the hierarchies of genera and species must terminate, using the arguments against infinite regress from the passage.

Core Idea
Infinite regress in genera and species is impossible because it would prevent knowledge and individual existence.

How do we know that the chain of genera and species has a top and a bottom? This passage gives a rigorous argument: if there were no highest genus, an essence would be composed of infinite parts, which the mind cannot grasp; and if there were no lowest species, no individuals could exist. We will walk through these arguments step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two chains
The passage discusses two directions: the ascent from specific to general (genera chain) and the descent from general to specific (species chain). Each must end.
Recall that a genus is higher than its species; the hierarchy goes up to a highest genus and down to a lowest species.
2
Understand the argument for the genera chain
If the ascent from a specific to more general genera went on infinitely, an essence (e.g., human) would have infinitely many parts (genera). But then conceiving that essence would require grasping infinite terms, which is impossible. Since we do conceive essences intuitively, the ascent must stop at a genus with no genus above it.
The key phrase: 'تركب الماهية من الاجزاء الغير المتناهية غير معقول' – composition from infinite parts is unintelligible.
3
Understand the argument for the species chain
If the descent from general to specific species went on infinitely, there would be no lowest species; but individuals exist only as instances of a species. Without a terminal species, no individuals would exist, which contradicts the obvious fact that individuals exist.
The text says: 'والايلزم ان لا يتحقق الاشخاص وهو باطل' – otherwise it would entail that individuals do not come into existence, which is false.
4
Identify the endpoints with examples
The author gives examples: in the genera chain, the highest is substance (جوهر); in the species chain, the lowest is human (إنسان). Intermediate levels are 'what is between' (ما بينها).
Practice: find a middle level – e.g., animal is a genus of human but a species of body.
5
Connect to the theory of definition
The argument ensures that definitions by genus and differentia are possible. Without a highest genus, definition would regress infinitely; without a lowest species, classification would have no bottom. Both are needed for a complete classification system.
This is foundational for the five universals and for logic in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh.

When Hadd Can Substitute for Fasl in Definitional Answers
Author refines the criteria for answering with hadd instead of fasl when the known genus is remote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when it is permissible to answer a definitional question with a complete definition (hadd) instead of just the differentia (fasl), and identify the condition of the known genus being remote that makes this substitution valid.

Core Idea
When the known genus is remote, the definition can serve as the distinguisher, not only the differentia.

In the standard approach to definitional questions (mā huwa), we are taught that after identifying the genus, the proper answer is the differentia. But what if the known genus is remote, meaning it is not the proximate genus of the species? The commentator Mirza Muhammad Ali refines this: under such conditions, the complete definition (hadd) can function just as well as the differentia in distinguishing the species from its co-generates. This lesson unpacks this nuance and its implications for constructing definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the standard rule
The rule states: after we know the genus of a thing, we ask for its differentia to distinguish it from others sharing that genus. This implies the answer should be the differentia alone.
2
Identify the objection
An objector claims that the request for 'what distinguishes it' means distinguishing from all co-generates in that genus, so only the differentia can achieve that. Therefore, no other answer is possible.
3
Recognize the author's counter
The author does not concede that only the differentia can distinguish. He says when the known genus is remote (jins ba'īd), the complete definition (hadd) can also distinguish, because it includes the differentia as a part.
4

The author notes that this only repels the objection regarding the genus (i.e., it shows hadd can replace fasl), but if taken absolutely it would cause other problems like excluding the remote differentia. He goes on to refine further.
5
Apply the refined rule
In practice, when you know a remote genus, you can answer with the full definition (e.g., 'rational animal') rather than just the differentia ('rational'), provided the definition does not include already known components in a redundant way.

When an Essence Has No Genus: The Differentia Problem
Discussion on whether a mahiyya can have two equal parts both serving as differentiae without a genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the condition that every mahiyya must have a genus and differentia, and identify cases where this rule may be challenged.

Core Idea
The differentia always presupposes a prior genus; without a genus, the concept of differentia collapses.

In classical Islamic logic, every essence (mahiyya) is analyzed into a genus (jins) and a differentia (fasl). But what if an essence were composed of two equal parts, each serving as a differentia without any genus? This hypothetical scenario challenges the foundational rule 'no genus, no differentia'. In this passage from Yazdi's Hashiya, the author presents this challenge, critiques the standard definition of differentia from Ibn Sina's al-Shifa, and ultimately rejects the scenario, reaffirming the necessity of a genus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard rule
Recognize that in standard logic, every mahiyya must have a genus (a broader category) and a differentia (a distinguishing characteristic). The differentia distinguishes the species from other species under the same genus.
This rule is axiomatic in Aristotelian logic.
2
Understand the hypothetical challenge
Consider the proposal: a mahiyya composed of two or more equal parts, each part serving as an 'essential distinguisher' but none broader than the mahiyya. Thus, no part can be a genus, yet each acts as a differentia. This challenges the rule that a mahiyya must have a genus.
The example of equal parts is a thought experiment.
3
Analyze the objection to the challenge
The author argues that the definition of the differentia as 'the universal that distinguishes from co-partners in the genus' presupposes a genus. If the mahiyya has no genus, then the parts cannot be differentiae in the standard sense, because there is nothing to share and then distinguish from.
The marginal note by al-Ustadh al-Karmi reinforces this: if the means of sharing (genus) is negated, the differentia is also negated.
4
Assess the resolution
The author concludes that the scenario of a mahiyya composed of equal parts is false (bāṭil). Therefore, the standard rule that every mahiyya has a genus and differentia remains intact. The critique only shows the rule's dependency on the genus concept.
This resolution preserves the logical framework while acknowledging the conceptual dependence.

If there is no genus, then there is no subject of commonality (al-ishtirak). The differentia operates by narrowing down a shared category. If the category is absent, the differentia has no function.
This is a necessary condition.

Ibn Sina's definition of differentia assumes a genus; what if the quiddity has none?

Author divides property into absolute, relative, and composite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Property comes in three flavors: absolute (unique to one thing), relative (unique compared to some), and composite (built from general accidents).

Having established the threefold division based on comprehensiveness and necessity, the commentator further divides property into absolute, relative, and composite. Absolute property is specific to one thing relative to all others; relative property is specific relative to some others; composite property is composed of multiple general accidents that together are unique. 1

Define relative property

3
Define composite property
Composite property is composed of multiple general accidents that together are unique to a species, e.g., 'flying and giving birth' for bats.
There is debate whether composite can be called a universal.

Why Universals Require Derivative Predication (Haml al-Muwata'ah)
Author explains why universals must be represented by derivative terms rather than root terms for proper predication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between predication of correspondence (haml al-muwata'ah) and predication of derivation (haml al-ishtiqaq), and explain why universals must use derivatives for proper representation.

Core Idea
Universals predicate identity, not derivation – that's why we say 'natiq' not 'nutq'.

In Islamic logic, how we predicate a universal of its individuals matters. The text explains a subtle but crucial point: when representing a universal like 'speaker', we must use the derived form (natiq) rather than the root (nutq). This is because the universal is predicated on its particulars by way of identity (haml al-muwata'ah), not by derivation. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in conceptual analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of predication
The text distinguishes haml al-muwata'ah (predication of identity) and haml al-ishtiqaq (predication by derivation). In the former, the subject and predicate are coextensive (e.g., 'Zaid is a human' means Zaid is identical to the universal 'human'). In the latter, the predicate is derived from a root but does not imply identity (e.g., 'Zaid is walking' does not mean Zaid is identical to walking).
The third type, haml al-murakkab (predication of composition), is also mentioned but not the focus.
2
Test with a root vs derivative example
Try to predicate the root 'nutq' (speaking) of Zaid. It fails: we cannot say 'Zaid is nutq' because nutq is a universal that applies to instances of speaking (e.g., Zaid's speech), not to Zaid himself. In contrast, the derivative 'natiq' (speaker) is correctly predicated via haml al-muwata'ah, meaning Zaid is identical to a speaker.
The same applies to 'dahk' (laughing) vs 'dahik' (laughing as a derived form).
3
Understand the reason for derivative representation
The text says universals must be predicated of their particulars by haml al-muwata'ah. Derivatives (like natiq) are suited for this because they are adjectives that directly predicate the universal of the subject. Roots (like nutq) are abstract nouns that predicate something else (the action or property), not the identity of the subject.
This is a reminder (tanbih) for logicians to use the correct form.
4
Examine the lenient view
Some scholars used roots in representation, but they intended by them the end results (al-ghayat), meaning they assumed the root stood for the derived meaning. So it was a shorthand, not a different doctrine.
This shows that even exceptions are interpreted in light of the correct principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between predication of identity and derivative predication, and identify the correct type for a given universal in Islamic logical discourse.

Core Idea
Universals must be predicated by identity, not derivation, or the logical link breaks.

When we say 'Zayd is a human', we are predicating the universal 'human' of an individual by identity. But if we say 'Zayd is speaking', we are using a derivative term. The logicians insist that for a true universal, predication must be by identity. This lesson unpacks that distinction and explains why some examples in the textbooks need careful interpretation.

Identify the predicate term
Look at the predicate in a universal claim. Is it a simple term like 'speech' or a derived term like 'speaking'? The passage teaches that universals are correctly represented by derivatives (e.g., 'speaking', 'laughing') not by simple sources.

Check the kind of predication
Determine whether the predication is identity (haml al-muwata'a) or derivation (haml al-ishtiqaq). Identity means the subject and predicate are essentially the same in meaning; derivation means the predicate is derived from a source that is not identical to the subject.
For example, 'Zayd is a human' is identity; 'Zayd is speaking' is derivation.
3
Apply the condition for universals
The passage states that a universal must be predicated of its particulars by identity. If you try to predicate a simple source like 'speech' of a person, it fails because 'speech' is not identical to the person—it is a different universal applying to utterances.
4
Resolve lenient representations
Some scholars used simple terms (e.g., 'speech') as representations, but they intended the derived properties (e.g., 'speaking'). Understand that this leniency does not change the rule; the universal must still be predicated by identity in reality.

When Does Conceiving the Specific Entail Conceiving the General?
Author analyzes multiple cases for entailment of general from specific.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the cases in which conceiving a specific concept (al-akhass) does or does not yield conception of the more general concept (al-a'amm).

Core Idea
Knowing the specific gives the general only when the general is essential and the specific is known by its essence.

This lesson examines a subtle epistemological question raised in the logic tradition: if you have a full conception of a specific thing, does that automatically give you conception of the more general category it belongs to? The answer, as the commentators show, depends on several factors: whether the general attribute is essential or accidental, and whether the specific is conceived through its essence (kunn) or through a description. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for precise definition in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key variables
The author weighs two factors: (1) whether the general concept is essential (dhati) or accidental ('aradi) to the specific concept, and (2) whether the specific concept is known by its essence (kunn) or by a description (rasm or incomplete definition).
2
Analyze the first case (general essential, specific by kunn)
When the general is part of the essence (e.g., 'animal' for 'human') and you conceive the specific through its complete definition ('rational animal'), you necessarily conceive the general. This is the strongest case.
3
Analyze the second case (general accidental, specific not by kunn)
If the general is accidental (e.g., 'walking' for 'human') and you conceive the specific through a description that includes that accident (e.g., 'man is a walking being'), then you may conceive the general, provided the definiens includes it.

Essential Accidents: Two Schools of Definition
Defines subject of a science and essential accidents with two schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two definitions of essential accident (al-'arad al-dhati) given by the ancients and the moderns, and identify which properties qualify under each definition.

Core Idea
Essential accidents are what make a science more than a collection of facts.

In this passage, the author clarifies two foundational concepts for any science: its subject (mawdu') and the essential accidents (a'rad dhatiyya) studied. The definition of 'essential accident' is not monolithic; the ancients and moderns disagree over whether an essential accident must attach directly or can attach through a coextensive intermediary. Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly identifying the properties that a science investigates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of a science
The passage first explains that the subject (mawdu') of a science can be a single thing (e.g., number for arithmetic) or multiple things (e.g., word and speech for grammar, definition and proof for logic). Note that the subject defines what the science is about.
This sets the context: the essential accidents are the properties of that subject.
2
Extract the ancients' definition of essential accident
The ancients (qudama) define the essential accident as what attaches to a thing either (1) primarily and essentially, like wonder attaching to man, or (2) through a coextensive intermediary, like laughter attaching to man through wonder. Recognize that the coextensive is something equal in extension to the subject.
Example: laughter is an essential accident of man because it is coextensive with wonder, which is essentially attached to man.
3
Extract the moderns' definition of essential accident
The moderns (muta'akhkhirun) define it as what attaches to a thing by its essence, or its part, or an external that equals it. This definition removes the coextensive intermediary; attachment must be direct to the essence, part, or equal external.
Under this definition, laughter would not be an essential accident of man because it attaches via wonder, not directly to man's essence.

Core Idea
