Fallacy of Denying the Antecedent in Conditional Arguments
Critiques the invalid inference that denying the antecedent yields denying the consequent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of denying the antecedent in a conditional argument and distinguish between logical entailment and real-world causation when evaluating such arguments.

Core Idea
Denying the antecedent is fallacious unless the conditional is biconditional.

This passage from the logical tradition critiques a common misunderstanding: that denying the antecedent (raf' al-muqaddam) necessarily leads to denying the consequent. The author uses two examples from classical Arabic poetry to illustrate the misuse, but then clarifies that the poets are speaking about external causation, not logical implication. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly evaluating conditional arguments in Islamic legal reasoning and theology.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the fallacy form
The fallacy of denying the antecedent occurs when you have a conditional 'If P then Q' and you assert 'not P' and conclude 'not Q'. This is invalid unless P and Q are equivalent (biconditional).
In formal terms: ((P → Q) ∧ ¬P) → ¬Q is invalid.
2
Analyze the poetic examples
Read the lines by al-Ma'arri and al-Hamasi. Identify the antecedent and consequent. Note that they deny the antecedent and claim the consequent is false.
The poets assert that because the antecedent did not occur, the consequent did not occur.
3
Understand the author's critique
The author explains that knowledge of denying the antecedent does not entail knowledge of denying the consequent. The poets are not making a logical inference but asserting a real-world causal relationship.
The justification is that the necessary condition (lazim) may be more general than the condition.
4
Distinguish between linguistic and logical usage
Linguists focus on external causal relationships, while logicians focus on the epistemic relationship between premises and conclusion. The poets' usage falls under the linguistic type.
The author cites al-Taftazani's Sharh al-Talkhis for this distinction.

Sophistry and the Estimative Faculty (Wahm)
Author defines sophistry and explains the role of wahm in producing false judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when wahm produces false judgments and explain how sophistry exploits this error.

Core Idea
Wahm judges correctly in sensibles but errs when analogically extended beyond – the basis of sophistry.

This lesson examines a key psychological source of logical error: the estimative faculty (wahm). While wahm accurately judges concrete meanings in physical objects, it can mislead when its judgments are applied to metaphysical or abstract entities. The passage explains how such errors give rise to sophistical arguments that deceive the opponent, and why understanding this helps guard against false reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify wahm's domain of accuracy
The author states wahm does not lie in sensibles (al-mahsusat) – it correctly perceives partial meanings in physical objects. Note that this is its proper function.
2
Extract the example of error
When wahm judges non-sensibles (e.g., every existent must occupy a space) by analogy with sensibles, its judgment becomes false. This is the core mechanism of sophistry: illicit analogy from the physical to the metaphysical.
3
Understand why the soul is misled
The false judgments (wahmiyyat) can be so convincing that they are mistaken for primary intelligibles (awwaliyat). Only reason ('aql) or revelation (shar') can correct them. This shows the need for epistemic checks beyond mere intuition.
4
Recognise the purpose and benefit
Sophistry aims to deceive an opponent by exploiting these natural errors. The greatest benefit of studying it is to guard against it – knowing the types of fallacy helps one avoid being trapped.

Verbal Material Fallacies: al-Musadara and al-Ishtirak al-Lafzi
Author distinguishes verbal material fallacies with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of begging the question and equivocation in logical arguments from the Islamic tradition.

Core Idea
Words can hide circularity or ambiguity, misdirecting the reasoning.

Material fallacies arise from the content of propositions rather than their form. This lesson focuses on two verbal material fallacies: begging the question (musadara), where the conclusion is merely restated, and equivocation (ishtirak lafzi), where a key term shifts meaning between premises.

Guided Path
1
Examine begging the question
In the example from the text, the conclusion 'every human is laughing' is identical to the major premise. This makes the argument circular, as no new information is provided.
Be alert for arguments where a premise is logically equivalent to the conclusion.
2
Detect equivocation
The term 'ayn is used in two senses: gold (minor premise) and eye (major premise). The middle term loses its unity, so the syllogism fails.
Track the middle term across premises to see if its meaning remains constant.
3
Distinguish from valid arguments
In valid Islamic arguments (e.g., 'every intoxicant is forbidden; beer is intoxicant; so beer is forbidden'), terms are unambiguous and premises are not circular.

Commentary
This passage covers material fallacies (al-mughalata al-madiyya), subdividing them into verbal (lafzi) and conceptual (ma'nawi). Verbal fallacies include: (1) Begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlab) – where the conclusion is identical to a premise, e.g., 'Every human is B.' (2) Equivocation (ishtirak lafzi) – middle term used with two meanings, e.g., 'ayn meaning gold in the minor and eye in the major. Conceptual fallacies include: (a) Judging the genus by a species: 'This is a color, and color is black' – but black is a species, not all colors. (b) Judging the absolute by a restricted condition: 'This is a neck, and the neck is believing' – the 'neck' in the major is a believing person, not a literal neck. (c) Putting 'natural' in place of 'universal': 'Man is an animal, animal is a genus' – the statement 'animal is a genus' is true for the natural kind, but not for the universal proposition; the fallacy is formal, since the major premise should be universal but is not.

Conceptual Material Fallacies: Genus, Absolute, and Natural
Author distinguishes conceptual material fallacies with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conceptual material fallacies, such as judging a genus by its species, the absolute by the restricted, and confusing natural with universal.

Core Idea
Mistaking category relationships leads to false conclusions.

Conceptual material fallacies occur when the mistake lies in the meaning or category of the terms, not in the words themselves. This lesson covers three types: judging the genus by a species (e.g., 'color is black'), judging the absolute by a restricted condition (e.g., 'neck is believing'), and confusing the natural with the universal (e.g., 'animal is a genus').

Guided Path
1
Understand the genus-species fallacy
The example 'this is a color, and color is black' falsely assumes that a property of a species (black) applies to the entire genus (color). The major premise should be universal over all colors, which is false.
Check if the major premise holds for every member of the genus.
2
Analyze the absolute-restricted fallacy
The example 'this is a neck, and the neck is believing' uses 'neck' in the minor as a body part, but in the major it refers to a believing person (a slave). The term is taken absolutely in one premise and with a restriction in the other.
Identify if a term is used in a broader sense in one premise and a narrower sense in the other.
3
Examine the natural-universal confusion
Saying 'man is an animal, and animal is a genus, so man is a genus' confuses the natural kind (animal as a natural group) with the universal concept (animal as a genus). The conclusion does not follow.
Distinguish between a natural kind and a logical universal.

(b) Judging the absolute by a restricted condition: 'This is a neck, and the neck is believing' – the 'neck' in the major is a believing person, not a literal neck. (c) Putting 'natural' in place of 'universal': 'Man is an animal, animal is a genus' – the statement 'animal is a genus' is true for the natural kind, but not for the universal proposition; the fallacy is formal, since the major premise should be universal but is not.

Key Terms
المصادرة على المطلوب (al-musadara 'ala al-matlab): Begging the question – a fallacy where the conclusion is already assumed in the premises. [Note: Stable across traditions.]

Core Idea

1
Identify the type of fallacy

2
Check for formal defects
Even material fallacies often have a hidden formal flaw, such as the major premise not being universal. The author notes that many examples actually fail due to form.

Begging the Question (Musadara)
Author defines and exemplifies begging the question.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify begging the question (musadara) in Islamic syllogisms and explain why it is a material fallacy.

Core Idea
Begging the question disguises the conclusion as a premise, making the argument circular.

One of the most common material fallacies is musadara 'ala al-matlub, where the conclusion is explicitly or implicitly stated in one of the premises. This makes the argument formally valid but materially empty, as it assumes what it needs to prove. In Islamic theology, this often appears in attempts to prove divine attributes using premises that already assume them.

Guided Path
1
Locate the conclusion
Identify what the argument is trying to prove. For example, 'Every human is a laugher' in the author's example.
The conclusion is often the last proposition.
2
Examine each premise
Check if any premise is identical in meaning to the conclusion. The author's example: 'Every human is a human' is the same as part of the conclusion? Actually, his example is 'Every human is a human, and every human is a laugher, so every human is a laugher' – the first premise is trivial, but the second is the major. However, the classic musadara is when a premise is the conclusion itself.
The author's example seems odd; usually musadara is like 'A is B because A is B' buried in premises. The standard example: 'The soul is immortal because it is not destroyed'.
3
Check for circularity
If the argument assumes what it sets out to prove, it commits musadara. In Islamic fiqh, this is often disguised.
For instance, arguing that riba is haram because it is forbidden in the Quran—this begs the question if the opponent does not accept Quranic authority.

Equivocation (Ishtirak)
Author gives an example of equivocation using the word 'عين'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify equivocation (ishtirak) in Arabic syllogisms by detecting the ambiguous middle term.

Core Idea
One word, two meanings—the middle term slips between them.

Equivocation occurs when the middle term of a syllogism is used in two different senses, making the argument formally valid but materially unsound. In Islamic sciences, this often appears when a word has both a common and a technical meaning, such as 'wajib' (obligatory vs. necessary).

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term
The middle term is the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. In the author's example, the middle term is 'عين' (eye).
The example: 'هذا عين (gold) وكل عين باكية (weeping eye) فهذا باك'.
2
Check the meaning in each premise
Determine if the middle term has the same meaning in both premises. In the example, 'عين' in the minor means 'gold piece', while in the major it means 'weeping eye'.
This is a clear case of equivocation.
3
Test the conclusion
If the meanings differ, the conclusion does not follow. The syllogism commits the fallacy of equivocation.
The conclusion literally says 'this is weeping' but the subject is gold, not an eye.

Judging Absolute by Qualified (مطلق ومقيد)
Author gives example of judging absolute by qualified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect the fallacy of applying a qualified judgment to an absolute term in Islamic legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Don't confuse the absolute with the qualified—a condition-bound ruling does not apply unconditionally.

One common conceptual fallacy is taking a ruling that applies to a qualified term (e.g., 'a believing slave') and applying it to the absolute term (e.g., 'a neck'). In Islamic fiqh, this is crucial when dealing with slaves, oaths, and expiation. The author gives the example: 'This is a neck; the neck is believing; so this is believing'—which is invalid because 'believing' is a qualification of the slave, not the neck itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute and qualified terms
Look for a term that is used without qualification (mutlaq) and then with a restriction (muqayyad). In the example, 'رقبة' (neck) is absolute in the minor, but 'رقبة مؤمنة' (believing slave) is qualified in the major.

Natural Universal vs. Universal Quantifier
Author explains fallacy of substituting natural for universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the natural universal (tabi'iyya) from the universal quantifier (kulliyya) and identify the fallacy of substituting one for the other.

Core Idea
The natural universal is the essence; the universal quantifier is its application to all individuals.

A subtle yet important conceptual fallacy is confusing the natural universal (al-tabi'iyya), which is the essence considered in itself, with the universal quantifier (al-kulliyya), which applies to all individuals. The author gives the example: 'Man is an animal; animal is a genus; so man is a genus.' This is invalid because the major premise 'animal is a genus' is true only when 'animal' is considered as a universal quantifier (all animals), but the minor 'man is an animal' treats 'animal' as a natural universal (the essence). This confusion led some philosophers to mistakenly attribute generic properties to man.

Guided Path
1
Identify the natural universal
The natural universal (al-tabi'iyya) is the concept itself, e.g., 'animal' as an essence. In the minor premise 'man is an animal', 'animal' is used as a natural universal (predicating the essence).
The author uses 'الطبيعية' to mean the essence considered in itself.
2
Identify the universal quantifier
The universal quantifier (al-kulliyya) applies the predicate to every individual. In the major premise 'animal is a genus', 'animal' is considered as a universal quantifier (i.e., all animals are genera? Actually, it means 'animal' is a genus of its species).
The author says 'الحيوان جنس' which means 'animal is a genus' in the sense of a logical category.
3
Spot the shift
The fallacy occurs because 'animal' in the minor is a natural universal (predicate of man), but in the major it is a universal quantifier (genus). The conclusion 'man is a genus' does not follow.
The author notes that the corruption is from the form—the major premise is not universally true as a natural universal.

The Fallacy of Non-Existent Subject
Author gives example of fallacy due to non-existent subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogism's subject term does not refer to any existing entity, making the argument invalid.

Core Idea
If the subject of the premises is empty, the syllogism collapses into fallacy.

A conceptual fallacy occurs when the subject term of the premises has no actual existence, making the argument irrelevant. The author gives the example: 'Every human and horse is a human, and every human and horse is a horse, so some human is a horse.' The subject 'human and horse' is a compound that does not exist in reality, so the premises are about nothing, and the conclusion is false.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the premises
The subject in both premises is 'كل انسان و فرس' (every human and horse). This is a compound subject that refers to an individual that is both human and horse, which does not exist.
The author says 'موضوع المقدمتين ليس بموجود' (the subject of the two premises does not exist).
2
Check if the subject refers to any real entity
In Islamic logic, the subject must exist for the syllogism to be material sound. Here, there is no entity that is both human and horse.
This is similar to the fallacy of the empty set in modern logic.
3
Determine the outcome
Since the subject is non-existent, the premises are not true of any actual thing, so the conclusion is not supported.
The conclusion 'some human is a horse' is false, but even if it were true, the argument is invalid because the premises do not refer to reality.
