Maznoonat and Maqboolat: Premises of Rhetoric
Author explains khitaba as using plausible opinions (maznoonat) and accepted opinions (maqboolat) to influence people.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between maznoonat and maqboolat and identify their use in rhetorical arguments.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades through plausible opinions and accepted authorities.

In this lesson, we explore the building blocks of rhetorical argument. The logicians distinguish between maznoonat (plausible opinions) and maqboolat (opinions accepted from trustworthy figures). These form the basis of persuasive discourse, as opposed to the certain premises of demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Find the definitions of maznoonat and maqboolat in the passage. Maznoonat are plausible opinions that give probable knowledge. Maqboolat are accepted on authority from trustworthy individuals.
Maznoonat are general; maqboolat are a specific subset.
2
Analyze the relationship
Note that maqboolat are a subset of maznoonat. The passage says maznoonat are absolutely general, while maqboolat are absolutely specific. Conjunction of the two terms implies maznoonat refers to non-maqboolat when both are listed.
3
Examine the sources of authority
Premises are taken from people believed in due to divine matters (prophets, saints) or because of their intellect and religion (scholars, ascetics). These give authority to the premises.
4
Apply to the examples
Look at the rhetorical arguments: the wall example uses a generalization; the night-walker example uses a universal claim. These rely on maznoonat (plausible but not certain).

قوله: تؤخذ عمن يعتقد فيه ... اما لامر سماوى من المعجزات و الكرامات كالانبياء والاولياء و اما لمزيد اختصاصها بمزيد عقل و دين كالحكماء والزهاد وهي نافعة جداً في تعظيم امر الله تعالى والشفقة على خلقه تعالى والغرض من الخطابي ... كما في مثل قولنا هذا الحائط ينتشر منه التراب وكل حائط كذلك، فهو ينهدم و قولنا فلان يطوف بالليل وكل من يطوف بالليل سارق فهو سارق. ای مقابلته المذكورات ، وتذكير الضمير باعتبار القول. یعنی: ان المظنونات اعم مطلقا والمقبولات اخص مطلقا تما الجواز حصول الظن فيها في غيرها کقیام زید و قعود عمرو مثلاً و امتناع حصولها بدون الظن فتأمل. يعني لما كانت المظنونات اعم من المقبولات كما ذكر، فعطفها عليها باعتبار ان المراد منها ماسوى الخاص اى المظنونات الغير المقبولة و هكذا فى كل موضع يقع فيه ذلك . ثم الغرض من الخطابي ترغيب الناس فيما ينفعهم من امور معاشهم ومعاد هم كما يفعله الخطباء والوعاظ .
He said: 'It is taken from one who is believed in...' either due to a divine matter of miracles and wonders like prophets and saints, or due to their possessing more intellect and religion like scholars and ascetics. And they are very beneficial in glorifying the command of Allah and having mercy on His creation. And the purpose of rhetoric is... as in our saying: 'This wall has dust falling from it, and every wall that does so collapses, so it collapses' and our saying: 'So-and-so walks at night, and everyone who walks at night is a thief, so he is a thief.' Meaning: the counterpart of the mentioned items, and the masculine pronoun is due to considering the statement. Meaning: Maznoonat (opinions) are absolutely general, and maqboolat (accepted opinions) are absolutely specific, such that it is possible for opinion to occur in other than them like Zayd's standing and Amr's sitting for example, and it is impossible for them to occur without opinion. Reflect. Meaning: since maznoonat are more general than maqboolat as mentioned, their conjunction to them is by considering that what is meant from them is other than the specific, i.e., non-accepted opinions. And likewise in every place where that occurs. Then the purpose of rhetoric is to make people desire what benefits them in their worldly and religious affairs, as preachers and orators do.

Commentary
This passage discusses khitaba (rhetoric), one of the five arts. It first describes how premises are taken from trustworthy figures (prophets, saints, scholars) due to their spiritual or intellectual authority. These premises are useful for religious exhortation. Then it gives examples of rhetorical arguments: one is about a wall that crumbles, another is a hasty generalization about night-walkers being thieves. The text then clarifies the relationship between maznoonat (plausible opinions) and maqboolat (accepted opinions). Maznoonat are more general, including many opinions that are merely probable, while maqboolat are a subset that are specifically accepted from authority. The conjunction of the two terms in classification implies that when both are listed, maznoonat refers to those that are not maqboolat. Finally, the purpose of rhetoric is stated: to encourage people towards what benefits them in this world and the Hereafter, as done by preachers.

Rhetorical Arguments and Their Purpose
Author explains khitaba as using plausible opinions (maznoonat) and accepted opinions (maqboolat) to influence people.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the logical structure of a rhetorical argument and assess its persuasive aim.

Core Idea
Rhetoric aims to motivate, not to prove.

The passage gives two vivid examples of rhetorical arguments: a wall that is falling apart and a person who walks at night. These illustrate how rhetoric uses premises that are plausible but not certain to convince an audience. We will examine their logical form and the ultimate goal of such arguments.

Guided Path
1
Examine the first example
Identify the premises and conclusion: 'This wall has dust falling from it, and every wall that does so collapses, so it collapses.' The conclusion is that the wall collapses.
2
Examine the second example
Identify the premises and conclusion: 'So-and-so walks at night, and everyone who walks at night is a thief, so he is a thief.' The conclusion is that he is a thief.
3
Assess logical validity
The second argument is valid in form (categorical syllogism) but the major premise is false (not all night-walkers are thieves). This is a rhetorical device, not a demonstrative proof. The first argument also uses a plausible generalization.
Formal validity does not guarantee truth of premises.
4
Determine the purpose of rhetoric
The passage states the goal is to make people desire what benefits them in worldly and religious matters. Rhetoric serves to exhort and motivate, not to establish certain knowledge.
