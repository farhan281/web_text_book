Poetry in Islamic Logic: Purpose and Premises
Author explains poetry's emotional purpose and imaginative premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the purpose of poetry (targhib/tarhib) and classify its premises as imaginative (mukhayyilaat) rather than truth-apt.

Core Idea
Poetry moves the soul through imagination, not truth.

In this passage, the commentator explains that poetry is built on imaginative premises (mukhayyilaat) that depict things not as they are, in order to stir desire or fear. Meter and voice enhance the effect. This distinguishes poetry from the other logical arts, which aim at truth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two emotional goals
Find the terms 'targhib' (desire) and 'tarhib' (fear) in the passage. They are the primary purposes of poetry according to the author.
The examples given are wine for desire and honey for fear.
2
Analyze how imaginative premises work
Notice that the premises (mukhayyilaat) describe things contrary to reality: wine is called 'flowing ruby' (not literally true) and honey is called 'bitter and nauseating' (exaggerated). These are not meant to be true or false but to evoke emotion.
This is the key difference from demonstrative premises.
3
Recognize the role of meter and voice
The author states that meter (wazn) and pleasant voice enhance the effect. Consider how rhythm and recitation amplify emotional impact.
This shows poetry is an art form, not just logical structure.
4
Define poetry based on its components
Synthesize: poetry is a syllogism (qiyas shi'ri) composed of imaginative premises, aimed at emotional response, often with meter. This is how Islamic logic categorizes it.
The term 'qiyas' is used metaphorically here.

He said: 'Desire and fear' – as for the first, like someone saying: 'Wine is flowing ruby', and the second like his saying: 'Honey is bitter and nauseating', for the soul then expands with drinking wine and contracts from drinking honey, as is apparent. And the purpose of poetry is the soul's being affected by desire and fear, and meter and pleasant voice enhance it. It is said: and from poetry is that which is one of the poems as poetry, because when desires are conveyed through it, they have a more powerful effect in souls. He said: 'As is currently customary': meaning that the conjunction of imaginative propositions with meter, even if it was not customary among the ancients, it is customary now. And know that their effect is great when recited with a pleasant voice. And know also that the construction of poetry is upon them, and for this reason the syllogism composed of them is called poetical.

Commentary
This passage discusses poetry (shi'r) as one of the five arts. It begins by illustrating two emotional goals: desire (targhib) and fear (tarhib). To make people desire wine, a poet might describe it as 'flowing ruby'; to make them avoid honey, he might call it 'bitter and nauseating'. The aim is emotional movement of the soul. Meter (wazn) and pleasant voice aid this effect. The author notes that although ancient poets did not always use imaginative propositions with meter, contemporary practice does. Recitation with a good voice increases impact. Importantly, the 'poetical syllogism' (qiyas shi'ri) is built on imaginative premises, giving it its name.
