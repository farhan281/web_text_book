Critique of Induction's Definition
Author defines induction, critiques a common definition, and contrasts it with analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the definition of induction as 'surveying particulars' is logically insufficient and state the correct definition of induction.

Core Idea
Surveying particulars is not a premise; induction requires an inference from particulars to their universal.

In logic, induction is one of three types of proof, alongside syllogism and analogy. However, not every enumeration of examples qualifies as a proof. The author of this passage critiques a common definition of induction attributed to al-Farabi and al-Ghazali, showing why it fails the conditions of a proper argument, and provides the correct definition that makes induction a genuine proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of proof
Read the opening: 'Know that proof is of three divisions...' Find the three types: syllogism (from universal to particulars), induction (from particulars to universal), and analogy (from one particular to another under a common universal).
Remember the direction of inference for each.
2
Locate the two definitions of induction
Find the author's 'correct definition' (induction is the proof that infers from the judgment of particulars to the judgment of their universal). Then locate the definition he critiques: 'surveying the particulars and tracking them to prove a universal judgment.'
The critique is against the wording that includes 'surveying' (tasaffuh).
3
Analyze the critique
The author argues that 'surveying particulars' is not a known assent (tasdiq) that can lead to an unknown assent. In other words, merely listing examples does not constitute a premise that forces acceptance of the universal conclusion. Without complete enumeration, the inference is not certain.
This highlights the epistemological requirement for a proof: premises must be known and yield the conclusion necessarily.
4
Understand the author's motive for the lax definition
The author notes that the lax definition was chosen to indicate that the term 'induction' is not an innovation but a transmitted technical term. This shows sensitivity to the history of logical terminology.
The critique is not rejecting the term but refining its logical status.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the definition of induction (istiqra') in logic. The author begins by stating that induction is 'surveying the particulars to prove a universal judgment' but adds a problematic phrase '[؟] an sari' which is unclear. He then contrasts induction with syllogism and analogy (tamthil). The correct definition, according to the author, is that induction is a proof in which one infers from the judgment of the particulars to the judgment of the universal covering them. This definition is sound and accepted. However, the author critiques a definition extracted by the author of the original text (presumably al-Farabi and al-Ghazali) that describes induction as 'surveying the particulars and tracking them to prove a universal judgment.' The author finds this definition problematic because the mere tracking of particulars does not constitute a premise for a proof (hujja); it is not a known assent (tasdiq) that can lead to an unknown assent. In other words, enumeration alone does not provide logical certainty unless it is complete. The author suggests that the laxity in the definition was intentional: to indicate that the term 'induction' is not improvised but a transmitted technical term. The passage ends with a reference to a forthcoming explanation in the context of analogy, and a note about proving a universal judgment 'by way of description' (tawsif), which likely relates to the distinction between complete and incomplete induction.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from the hashya on the definition of induction (al-istiqra') and its types. The author begins by quoting the matn's statement that induction is 'surveying particulars to establish a universal judgment' but notes that this is not the definition of a proper proof (hujja). He then explains that proofs are three kinds: qiyas (from universal to particulars), istiqra' (from particulars to universal), and tamthil (from one particular to another under a common universal). The correct definition of induction, according to this author, is the one he gives: 'the proof in which one infers from the judgment of particulars to the judgment of their universal.' He criticizes the matn's definition because the act of surveying particulars is not a known tasdiq that leads to an unknown tasdiq; thus it does not fit the definition of a proof. However, the author then suggests a charitable interpretation: the matn's wording was deliberately loose to indicate that the term 'induction' is used by convention, not arbitrarily. Next, he delves into two possible readings of the phrase 'to establish a universal judgment.' The first reading (tawsif) treats 'kulli' as described by a property, implying the desired judgment is universal. The second (idafa) treats 'kulli' as standing for 'kulliha' (its universal), meaning the universal of those particulars. He argues that despite appearances, the desired in induction is always the universal. He then distinguishes two types of induction: complete induction (tamm), which surveys all particulars and reduces to a divisive syllogism, yielding certainty; and incomplete induction (naqis), which surveys most particulars and yields only conjecture. He provides examples: the complete induction example classifies animals into rational and non-rational, both sentient, so all animals are sentient. The incomplete induction example observes that all encountered animals (human, horse, cow) move their lower jaw when chewing, but notes the possibility of a crocodile that moves its upper jaw, so the conclusion is probable. He further clarifies that if the desired judgment is itself particular (e.g., 'some animal moves its lower jaw'), then induction from a few examples can yield certainty, as in the example of concluding that some animals are horses. Finally, he concludes that the tawsif reading of the matn is superior because it avoids ambiguity about whether the judgment is universal or particular.

Complete and Incomplete Induction: Certainty vs. Conjecture
Author distinguishes complete and incomplete induction with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate complete induction (yielding certainty) from incomplete induction (yielding conjecture) and explain why complete induction reduces to a divisive syllogism.

Core Idea
Complete induction surveys all particulars and gives certainty; incomplete induction surveys most and gives only probability.

After defining induction, the author distinguishes its two types. Complete induction (tamm) surveys every particular and reduces to a divisive syllogism (qiyas muqassam), producing certain knowledge. Incomplete induction (naqis) surveys only most particulars, yielding only conjecture (zann), because the unexamined cases might contradict the pattern. The author illustrates each with examples from animal classification.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types
Complete induction examines all individuals; incomplete examines most.
This is the foundational distinction.
2
See how complete induction reduces to a divisive syllogism
The example divides animals into rational and non-rational, both sentient, so all are sentient. This is a qiyas muqassam.
The division ensures complete coverage.
3
Recognize the epistemic status
Complete gives yaqin (certainty); incomplete gives zann (conjecture) because of the possibility of counterexamples (like the crocodile moving upper jaw).
The crocodile example illustrates the limitation.
4
Note the condition for incomplete induction
If the desired conclusion is particular (e.g., some animal moves lower jaw), then incomplete induction can give certainty about that particular claim.
This nuance is crucial for applying induction correctly.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from the hashya on the definition of induction (al-istiqra') and its types. The author begins by quoting the matn's statement that induction is 'surveying particulars to establish a universal judgment' but notes that this is not the definition of a proper proof (hujja). He then explains that proofs are three kinds: qiyas (from universal to particulars), istiqra' (from particulars to universal), and tamthil (from one particular to another under a common universal). The correct definition of induction, according to this author, is the one he gives: 'the proof in which one infers from the judgment of particulars to the judgment of their universal.' He criticizes the matn's definition because the act of surveying particulars is not a known tasdiq that leads to an unknown tasdiq; thus it does not fit the definition of a proof. However, the author then suggests a charitable interpretation: the matn's wording was deliberately loose to indicate that the term 'induction' is used by convention, not arbitrarily. Next, he delves into two possible readings of the phrase 'to establish a universal judgment.' The first reading (tawsif) treats 'kulli' as described by a property, implying the desired judgment is universal. The second (idafa) treats 'kulli' as standing for 'kulliha' (its universal), meaning the universal of those particulars. He argues that despite appearances, the desired in induction is always the universal. He then distinguishes two types of induction: complete induction (tamm), which surveys all particulars and reduces to a divisive syllogism, yielding certainty; and incomplete induction (naqis), which surveys most particulars and yields only conjecture. He provides examples: the complete induction example classifies animals into rational and non-rational, both sentient, so all animals are sentient. He further clarifies that if the desired judgment is itself particular (e.g., 'some animal moves its lower jaw'), then induction from a few examples can yield certainty, as in the example of concluding that some animals are horses. Finally, he concludes that the tawsif reading of the matn is superior because it avoids ambiguity about whether the judgment is universal or particular.

This passage defines tamthil (analogy) as establishing a judgment in a particular (far') because it holds in another (asl) due to a common meaning ('illa). Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive treatment of analogy (tamthil) in logic, including its definition, the distinction between the act of analogizing and the argument itself, and the method of establishing the common cause. The author begins by defining analogy as demonstrating that two particulars share the cause of a judgment, so the judgment applies to both. He notes that jurists call this qiyas, and they term the particulars asl (root) and far' (branch), and the shared attribute as 'illa (cause) or jami' (unifier). A typical example is the analogy from the house to the world: the house is temporally originated (hadith) because it is composed (mu'allaf), and the world is also composed, so it is temporally originated. The logicians have two main ways to prove the common cause: dawaran (co-variation) and tardid (disjunction). Dawaran is the observation that the judgment accompanies the attribute in existence and non-existence—e.g., intoxication and prohibition: whenever intoxication is present, prohibition is present; when it is absent, prohibition is absent. This is a sign that the attribute is the cause. The commentary then discusses a subtlety: the author's definition of tamthil is actually for the infinitive sense (the act of analogizing), whereas the term is also used for the argument (hujja) itself. This is akin to how 'aks (conversion) can mean the act of converting or the resulting converted proposition. The commentator notes that the author deliberately deviated from the common definition to avoid looseness (tasamuh), though he critiques that this may be circular. Finally, the passage lists the three necessary premises for a valid analogy: (1) the judgment holds in the original, (2) the cause of that judgment is the specific attribute, (3) that attribute exists in the branch. The first and third are usually obvious; the difficulty lies in proving the second premise (that the attribute is indeed the cause). The primary method for that is dawaran, which relies on the co-occurrence of attribute and judgment.

The Method of Dawaran (Co-Variation) for Establishing Causality
Explains the method of dawaran for proving causality.

Exclusion of Incomplete Induction and Analogy from Qiyas
Author explains the exclusion of uncertain inductive and analogical arguments from qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete and incomplete induction, and between certain and probable analogy, and determine which are considered qiyas.

Core Idea
Only induction and analogy that yield certainty belong to qiyas.

In this passage, Shaykh Abd al-Rahim clarifies what types of reasoning are excluded from qiyas. He specifies that incomplete induction and non-certain analogy are excluded, but their certain counterparts are actually syllogistic. He cites al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif and gives a nabidh example to illustrate when analogy becomes a certain syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of reasoning mentioned
Look for 'istiqra' (induction) and 'tamthil' (analogy) in the Arabic. Note the two subtypes: incomplete vs complete induction; analogy that yields certainty vs not.

Understand that qiyas is defined as a syllogism that yields certainty. Incomplete induction and non-certain analogy lack certainty and are thus excluded. Complete induction and certain analogy, however, are structurally syllogistic and included.
The passage clarifies the boundaries of qiyas, showing that not all analogies are excluded.

Why Proof is Three, Not Four: Universal-Particular Relations
Author defends the tripartite division of proof against the objection that it should be four.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the possible relations between universals in inference and justify the classical tripartite division of proof into syllogism, induction, and analogy.

Core Idea
Inference from universal to universal is not a fourth type—it either reduces to syllogism, induction, or analogy, or it is impossible.

The classical logicians divided proof (hujja) into three categories: syllogism, induction, and analogy. But a critic might ask: since both the premise and conclusion can be universal or particular, why isn't there a fourth category of universal-to-universal inference? In this passage, the author anticipates this objection and shows that any possible relation between two universals falls into one of the three categories or is logically impossible. By understanding this defense, you will see why the tripartite division is exhaustive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's challenge
The objector claims that pairing universal/particular for the inferring premise (mustadall bihi) and the inferred conclusion (mustadall 'alayh) yields four combinations. Find these four in the Arabic text: (universal→universal, universal→particular, particular→universal, particular→particular). The objector wonders why the first is omitted.
The four are implicit: (كلي على كلي، كلي على جزئي، جزئي على كلي، جزئي على جزئي).
2
Follow the author's analysis of universal-universal pairs
The author examines the case where both are universal. He notes that any two universals either have a hierarchical relation (one subsumes the other) or not. If hierarchical, inference can be from the subsuming to the subsumed (syllogism) or vice versa (induction). If not hierarchical, there may or may not be a proximate universal above them that encompasses both. If yes, that is analogy; if no, inference is impossible.
The author uses terms like 'مندرج تحت' (subsumed under) and 'فوقهما كلي قريب' (proximate universal above them).
3
Map each subcase to a type of proof
The first three subcases correspond exactly to the three divisions: (1) universal subsuming → universal subsumed = syllogism (qiyas), (2) universal subsumed → universal subsuming = induction (istiqra'), (3) two non-hierarchical universals sharing a common proximate universal = analogy (tamthil). The fourth subcase (non-hierarchical, no common universal) yields no inference.
Thus the four combinations reduce to three valid inference types; the fourth is excluded as impossible.
4
Confirm the exhaustive nature of the division
Verify that every possible relation between premise and conclusion (whether universal or particular) falls into one of the three types. For example, universal→particular is syllogism (major premise universal, conclusion particular). Particular→universal is induction. Particular→particular is analogy (since two particulars share a common universal). Universal→universal is covered by the three subcases. Thus the tripartite division is complete.
This shows that the division is not arbitrary but based on logical relations.

Commentary

Core Idea
Tanqih al-manat isolates the common factor by eliminating all distinguishing attributes.

Commentary
This passage defines two methods used in Islamic legal theory and logic to identify the 'illa (cause) for analogical reasoning. The first is munasaba wa ikhala: simply pointing to an appropriate attribute that fits the judgment, without further investigation. The Dawaran Problem: When Co-variation Does Not Prove Causation
Raises an objection against using dawaran as proof of causation, with counterexamples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the counterexamples given in the objection and explain why they show that dawaran is insufficient for causality.

Core Idea
Co-variation can occur without causation, as in definition and defined.

The method of tanqih al-manat relies on the idea that the cause and judgment co-vary. But the text raises a powerful objection: co-variation (dawaran) often exists without a causal relationship. Examples include inseparable pairs like definition and defined, or two effects of the same cause. This lesson unpacks that objection and its implications for logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition of dawaran
Gloss 29 defines it: 'كلما وجد الوصف وجد الحكم وكلما فقد فقد' – whenever the description exists the judgment exists, and whenever it is absent it is absent.
2
Read the objection
The objection (30) states: 'انه كثيراً ما يحصل الدوران ولا يكون المدار علة للدائر' – often circulation occurs but the circulating thing is not the cause.
3
Examine the examples
The examples: (1) definition and defined – they are co-extensive but neither causes the other; (2) two effects of the same cause – they co-vary but are not causes of each other; (3) substance and accident; (4) the final part of the complete cause and the complete cause; (5) cause and effect equal in relation to the effect.
Each example shows a non-causal correlation.
4
Draw the conclusion
Therefore, dawaran alone is not sufficient to prove that the attribute is the cause. Additional evidence is needed, such as elimination of other possibilities or demonstration of a necessary connection.

Critique of Sabr wa Taqsim: Method and Limitations
Critique of sabr wa taqsim method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of sabr wa taqsim and evaluate the two main objections against its certainty.

Core Idea
Exhaustive elimination does not guarantee transference of the cause when branch-specific conditions exist.

The method of sabr wa taqsim is used in usul al-fiqh to identify the 'illa (cause) of a ruling by enumerating all possible attributes and eliminating them one by one. However, this method faces serious logical challenges even when the enumeration seems complete. In this lesson, we examine the method itself and the critiques raised in the tradition, including the possibility of a branch-specific preventive factor and the difficulty of truly exhaustive enumeration.

Guided Path
1
Understand the method of sabr wa taqsim
First, clarify that sabr means 'probing' or 'testing' each attribute, and taqsim means 'division' into exhaustive categories. The method works by listing all possible attributes that could be the cause, then eliminating all except one, which is claimed to be the cause.
Note the etymology from probing a wound.
2
Identify the first objection: branch-specific preventive
The passage raises that even if the common factor is the cause in the root, a specific feature of the branch (khususiyya) might prevent the judgment from applying. This means the method can establish causality in the root but not guarantee it in the branch.
This objection undermines the transfer of the ruling via qiyas.
3
Identify the second objection: incomplete enumeration
Another objection states that the enumeration itself may not be truly exhaustive because the division is not between negation and affirmation (i.e., not dichotomous). Eliminating some attributes does not prove the remaining one is the cause.
This highlights the risk of incomplete induction.
4
Evaluate the commentator's response
The commentator concedes that if exhaustive enumeration is established by definitive proof and all other attributes are eliminated, the claimed attribute is the cause in the root. However, the branch-specific preventive remains a possibility, so the judgment does not necessarily follow.
The response is nuanced: it affirms the method for the root but limits its scope.
5
Apply the critique to a real usuli example
Consider the prohibition of wine due to intoxication ('illa). If we enumerate possible attributes for wine and eliminate all but intoxication, we can conclude intoxication is the cause for wine. But for nabidh (date wine), if there is a specific feature (e.g., it is not made from grapes) that prevents the ruling, the method may not justify the extension.
This shows the practical relevance of the objections.

Defines sabr and discusses the condition of exclusivity for proving causality.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sabr tests each attribute by rotation; its proof of causality depends on exhaustive enumeration.

The method of sabr (exploration) is a key tool in usul al-fiqh for identifying the effective cause ('illa) of a ruling. This passage defines sabr linguistically and technically, and addresses the critical condition that for sabr to yield certainty, the list of possible attributes must be exhaustive and all others negated. Understanding this method equips you to evaluate how jurists derive causes in analogical reasoning (qiyas).

1
Identify the linguistic origin of sabr

Grasp the technical meaning in usul al-fiqh
Technically, sabr is the process of systematically testing each possible attribute by rotation (tardid) to determine which one is the cause of the ruling.
3
Understand the principle of dawaran (rotation)
Dawaran means that if a ruling is present when a certain attribute is present and absent when it is absent, this indicates a causal relationship. The passage distinguishes between causality due to the attribute itself versus due to a particular material context.
4
Analyze the objection regarding exclusivity
An objection is raised: even if attributes are limited and all but one are negated, the remaining attribute may not be the cause in a new case because of conditions or impediments specific to the original or new case.
5
Evaluate the condition for sabr to prove causality
The author responds that if exclusivity is established by decisive proof and all other attributes including particularities are negated, then the claimed attribute is indeed the cause. But if a particularity of the new case acts as an impediment, the ruling does not automatically apply.
This highlights the need for thorough vetting and the possibility of impediments (mawani').

و حاصله ان اقتضاء الدوران تكون المدار علة للدائر اما ان يكون من حيث نفسه وبملاحظته في حد ذاته و اما ان يكون من جهة خصوص المادة المخصوصة والاول غير واقع والا لما امكن التخلف والثاني لا يفيد. اما الاول فلان السبر في الاصل ادخال الجراح الميل فى الجراحة المعرفة غورها يقال سبر يسبر سبراً اذا فعل كذا وقد يطلق على مطلق الامتحان. و هيهنا لما امتحن بالترديد ان اى وصف من الاوصاف هو علة الحكم سموه به تسمية المقيد باسم المطلق كتسمية الانف بالمرسن والشفة بالمشفر على وجه. واما الثانى فلما فيه من تقسيم الاوصاف كما هو ظاهر. ای جمعيها كما هو الظاهر ولو بالادعاء. بتنوين كل المكرر معاً ومراده علية كل واحد من الاوصاف. ختاماً نقول ان التمثيل ساقط الحجية فى الامور التوقيفية و منصوص العلة لايقال له للتصريح بمدار الحكم. تمثيل يعني يستفاد من تفحص الاوصاف و سلب العلية من كل منها ان المدعى كون هذا الوصف المدعى علة للحكم. و قد اورد هنا ايضاً انه على تقدير تسليم كون هذا الحصر عقلياً دائراً بين النفى والاثبات لا تسلم ان المشترك اذا كان علة فى الاصل يلزم ان يكون علة فى الفرع الجواز ان يكون خصوصية الاصل شرطاً للعلة او خصوصية الفرع مانعة عنها. والحق أنه اذا ثبت بالدليل القاطع انحصار الاوصاف في المعدودة وسلب العلية عما عدا الوصف المدعي حتى الخصوصية كما هو المفروض يستفاد من ذلك كونه علة الحكم لكنه اذا فرض جواز كون خصوصية الفرع مانعة لا يلزم من تحققه فى الفرع تحقق الحكم فيه كما لا يخفى. لا يخفى ان حصر العلة فى الاوصاف المذكورة ممنوع لان الترديد ليس بين النفي و الاثبات فبابطال بعضها لا يتعين الباقي للعلية.
Summary: The implication of the rotation that the rotated is the cause of the rotating is either (1) by virtue of itself and observing it in itself, or (2) by virtue of the particularity of the specific matter. The first is not actual, otherwise non-existence would not be possible. The second is not useful. As for the first, because 'sabr' originally means the insertion of the probing tool into a wound to know its depth. It is said: sabbara yasbiru sabran when one does that. It is sometimes used for absolute testing. Here, because they test by going through each attribute to see which is the cause of the ruling, they named it by that, naming the restricted by the name of the unrestricted, like naming the nose 'mirsan' and the lip 'mishfar' in a certain way. As for the second, because it involves dividing the attributes, as is apparent. Meaning: all of them, as is apparent, even if by claim, as stated by a group of investigators. With the double tanwin of 'kull', and his intention is the causality of each one of the attributes. Finally, we say: the example has no proof in matters that are based on revelation, and the explicitly stated cause is not said to have that because the basis of the ruling is explicitly stated. Example: meaning, from examining the attributes and negating causality from each of them, it is understood that the claimed attribute is the cause of the ruling. And it is also objected here that assuming the exclusivity of these attributes is rational and turns between negation and affirmation, it is not granted that if the common attribute is a cause in the original case, it necessarily follows that it is a cause in the branch case, because it is possible that the particularity of the original case is a condition for the cause or the particularity of the branch case is an impediment against it. And the truth is that if it is established by decisive proof that the attributes are confined to those enumerated and causality is negated from everything other than the claimed attribute including particularity, then from that it is understood that it is the cause of the ruling. But if it is assumed that the particularity of the branch case is an impediment, then from its occurrence in the branch case, the occurrence of the ruling does not necessarily follow, as is not hidden. It is not hidden that the confinement of the cause to the mentioned attributes is denied, because the going through is not between negation and affirmation; so if some are invalidated, the remaining ones are not determined for causality.

Commentary
This passage is a technical discussion of the method of sabr (exploration) used in Islamic jurisprudence to identify the 'illa (cause) of a ruling. The author begins by clarifying the principle of rotation (dawaran), stating that the implication of a ruling following an attribute consistently could be due to the attribute itself or to some specific aspect of the matter. He then defines sabr linguistically as probing a wound, and technically as testing attributes by going through them to determine which one is the cause. The passage then addresses an important objection: even if one successfully limits the possible attributes to a finite set, it is not guaranteed that the common attribute in the original case will be the cause in the new case, because there might be a condition in the original or an impediment in the new case. The author responds by stating that if the exclusivity of attributes and negation of causality from all others are established by decisive proof, then the claimed attribute is indeed the cause. However, he also notes that if a particularity of the new case acts as an impediment, the ruling does not apply. The final note observes that the confinement of the cause to enumerated attributes is not valid if the list is not exhaustive, meaning the method of sabr requires a thorough vetting.
