Contradictories of Time-Limited and Spread Absolute Propositions
Author explains the contradictories of time-limited and spread propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the contradictories of time-limited (waqtiyya) and spread (muntashira) absolute propositions using the necessity-possibility relationship.

Core Idea
The contradictory of 'necessarily at a specific time' is 'possible at that time'.

After establishing the general customary's contradictory, the passage turns to the time-limited and spread absolute propositions. These are used in temporal arguments. Their contradictories follow a pattern: necessity contradicts possibility. This lesson shows how to derive them.

Guided Path
1
Recall the contradictory of necessity
Essential necessity's contradictory is essential possibility; descriptive necessity's contradictory is descriptive possibility. This is a known principle.
2
Apply to time-limited necessity
Time-limited necessity (waqtiyya ḍarūriyya) judges a relation necessarily at a specific time (e.g., 'Zayd is standing necessarily at noon'). Its contradictory is possibility at that specific time (ḥīniyya mumkina).
3
Apply to spread necessity
Spread necessity (muntashira ḍarūriyya) judges a relation necessarily at some unspecified time. Its contradictory is possibility at some time (muntashira mumkina).
4
Extend to absolute time-limited and spread
The absolute time-limited (waqtiyya muṭlaqa) and absolute spread (muntashira muṭlaqa) are not necessity propositions; they deny perpetuity. Their contradictories are the temporal possible and spread possible respectively, as stated in the passage.
This is because their negations match the forms derived from necessity disappear.

The lesson clarifies the logical relationships and the reason for omissions.

Why the Contradictory Cannot Be Exactly the Negation of One Part
Author proves why a specific part negation fails as contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in taking a specific part's negation as the contradictory of a composite proposition.

Core Idea
Negating one part of a composite fails when the other part causes falsehood.

In Islamic logic, composite propositions like 'Every human is actually an animal, not perpetually' arise frequently. One might think its contradictory is simply 'Not every human is actually an animal'. The author shows that this leads to both being false, violating the condition for contradictories. We will walk through the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite and its parts
Locate the composite proposition (e.g., 'كل إنسان حيوان بالفعل لا دائماً'). Break it into its two parts: part A: 'كل إنسان حيوان بالفعل' (Every human is actually an animal), part B: 'لا دائماً' (not perpetually). Note that the composite is false if either part is false.
The composite is true only if both parts are true.
2
Try the proposed contradictory as negation of part A
Suppose the contradictory is exactly نقيض الجزء الأول (the negation of part A): 'ليس كل إنسان بحيوان بالفعل' (Not every human is actually an animal).
This is a natural guess.
3
Test truth conditions
The composite is false because part B ('not perpetually') is false? Actually the author says the composite is false due to 'الجزء الاخير'. In the example, the composite is false when the 'not perpetually' part is false (i.e., if it is actually perpetual). Then the negation of part A is also false because it denies that any human is an animal, which is false. Thus both false.
The scenario: composite false because second part false; proposed contradictory also false.
4
See that both false violates contradiction condition
For two propositions to be contradictories, they must always have opposite truth values. Here, in the scenario where the composite is false, the proposed contradictory is also false, so they are both false, which is impossible for contradictories. Therefore, the contradictory cannot be exactly the negation of one part.
Thus the contradictory must remove one part without specification.

Commentary
This passage defines the contradictory of a composite proposition (murakkaba) – a proposition with two parts joined by a conjunction like 'and' but with a temporal nuance. The author argues that the contradictory cannot be exactly the negation of one part, because that would allow both the composite and that negation to be false when the other part is false, violating the law of contradiction. Using a fiqh-style example: 'Every human is actually an animal, not perpetually' – if we take the contradictory as 'Not some human is actually an animal', both would be false since the latter is false and the former's negation of perpetuity is also false. Hence the contradictory must be a disjunction that removes one of the two parts without specification. The appropriate disjunction type is man' al-khuluw (incompatibility in falsehood). The passage defends this against objections: (1) The composite could be false by both parts being false, so the disjunction must allow that both negations hold, which man' al-khuluw does because it permits both to be true? Actually man' al-khuluw only prevents both from being false; it allows both true or one true. But here we need that the contradictory removes the composite, i.e., at least one part is false. The resolution is that man' al-khuluw is used in a broader sense to mean simply 'removal of one without specification' considering only falsehood, not truth. (2) A disjunction of man' al-khuluw is affirmative, so how can it contradict an affirmative composite? The answer: it is not an explicit contradictory but equivalent, so the name is metaphorical. The lesson is a nuanced treatment of how to handle 'and' compounds in logical contradiction, showing that the proper contradictory is not a simple negation of either part.

Why the Contradictory is a Disjunctive of Man' al-Khuluw
Author defends man' al-khuluw as the proper type for the contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the contradictory of a composite must be a disjunctive of the type man' al-khuluw and how it differs from other disjunctions.

Core Idea
The contradictory removes one part without specification, but must allow both parts to be false, which only man' al-khuluw permits.

After showing the contradictory cannot be a specific part's negation, the author determines it must be a disjunction that removes one part without specification. However, there are three types of disjunction: true disjunction (man' al-jam' wal-khuluw), man' al-jam', and man' al-khuluw. The author argues that only man' al-khuluw fits because it allows both disjuncts to be true (i.e., both parts removed) and prevents only both false. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recall the required form: removal of one part without specification
We established that the contradictory must assert that at least one part of the composite is false. That is equivalent to a disjunction: 'Either the first part is false or the second part is false' (or both). The contradictory must be true exactly when the composite is false, and false when the composite is true.
The composite is true only when both parts are true.
2
Test the three types of disjunction
A true disjunction (exclusive or) is true when exactly one part is true, false when both true or both false. If both parts are false, the composite is false, so the contradictory must be true, but the true disjunction would be false. Man' al-jam' (incompatibility in truth) is false when both true, true otherwise. It would be true when both false, but when both true it is false (good), but the author objects that in man' al-jam' it is possible that nothing is removed (both parts true) leading to the composite being true while the disjunction is false? Actually the objection is that in man' al-jam', if nothing is removed (both true), the composite is true but the disjunction is false, which would be correct for a contradictory. However, the author says this is false (هذا خلف), perhaps meaning the assumption that nothing is removed leads to a contradiction with the requirement that the disjunction is the contradictory. The text is ambiguous; the conclusion is that only man' al-khuluw works.
The author's reasoning is highly technical; we follow his conclusion.
3
Understand why man' al-khuluw is appropriate
Man' al-khuluw in the broad sense means the two propositions cannot both be false. In our case, the contradictory should be false when the composite is true (both parts true). But when both parts false, composite false, contradictory must be true. Man' al-khuluw is false when both false? Actually the broad sense judges incompatibility in falsehood: it is false when both false, true otherwise. That matches: if both parts false, composite false, contradictory false? Wait: We need contradictory true when composite false, false when composite true. If composite true only when both true, then contradictory false when both true. Man' al-khuluw in broad sense: true when at least one true, false when both false. So if both parts false, composite false, man' al-khuluw false, which is wrong. But the passage says the contradictory is a disjunction that removes one part without specification, i.e., it is true when either part is false. That is actually the opposite: 'remove one part' means at least one part false. So the contradictory is true when at least one part false, false only when both true. That is exactly the truth table of a disjunction of negations: 'Either not A or not B', which is equivalent to 'not (A and B)'. This is the law of de Morgan. In Arabic logical terminology, the author equates this to man' al-khuluw in a broad sense. The nuance is that the standard man' al-khuluw is about two affirmative propositions: 'Either A is true or B is true' with the condition that they cannot both be false. But here we have negative disjuncts. So the author adapts the term. For the lesson, we simplify: the contradictory is a disjunction that is true when at least one part is false, which is structurally man' al-khuluw in a generalized sense.
The key insight: the contradictory is not a simple disjunction of the parts but a disjunction of their negations, which is equivalent to the negation of the conjunction.
4
Note the broader sense of man' al-khuluw
The author clarifies that man' al-khuluw is used in two senses: a specific sense opposite to true disjunction (incompatibility only in falsehood, not in truth), and a general sense meaning incompatibility in falsehood regardless of truth. The general sense is intended here, so no problem arises.
Thus the objection that man' al-khuluw would not allow both parts to be false is resolved.
5
Address the quality objection
It is objected that the disjunctive is affirmative while the composite is affirmative, so they do not differ in quality (condition for contradiction). The answer: the disjunctive is not an explicit contradictory but is equivalent to the explicit contradictory (which would be a negation of the composite). Calling it a contradictory is metaphorical, justified by equivalence.
This resolves the tension between the logical rule and the linguistic form.

This passage defines the contradictory of a composite proposition (murakkaba) – a proposition with two parts joined by a conjunction like 'and' but with a temporal nuance. The author argues that the contradictory cannot be exactly the negation of one part, because that would allow both the composite and that negation to be false when the other part is false, violating the law of contradiction. This passage dives into a subtle point about compound particular propositions. Negating Compound Particular Propositions
Method for negating compound particular propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the logical negation of any compound particular proposition using the universal subject and disjunction method, and identify its equivalent disjunctive form.

Core Idea
Negating a compound particular requires universal subject and disjunction of negated parts.

Negating propositions in logic is not always straightforward, especially when the original proposition is compound and particular. The classical scholars of mantiq developed a precise method: take the subject as a universal, then for each individual, apply the predicate with the modes that negate each part of the compound, and combine these as a disjunction. This lesson unpacks that method from the commentary, showing how to correctly form such negations and why a simple categorical negation is insufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original compound proposition
Locate the proposition: it must be particular (جزئية) and compound (مركبة), e.g., 'Some animal is always human' (بعض الحيوان إنسان دائمًا). Note its subject, predicate, and the two components (humanity, always).
The compound here means the predicate has a mode (always) attached.
2
Take the subject universally
In the negation, the subject becomes universal (كلي) instead of particular. So 'some animal' becomes 'every animal' (كل حيوان).
This mirrors the quantifier shift in standard negation of particular affirmatives.
3
Apply the predicate to each individual with the two opposite modes
For each individual animal, we consider two possibilities: either it is always human, or it is not always human. But the negation requires the modes that negate the original parts: here, the negation of 'always human' is 'not always human', which itself has two sub-cases: never human, or sometimes human sometimes not. The method uses a disjunction between these two negated modes applied to each individual.
Technically, we disjoin the two negations: 'each individual is either not always human or never human'? Check commentary.
4
Express the result as a disjunction (taraddud)
The final negation is a universal proposition with a disjunctive predicate: 'Every animal is either not always human or never human' (or more precisely, a disjunction of the two negated conditions). This disjunction is what the commentator calls 'taraddud'.
This avoids turning the compound into two separate propositions.
5
Recognize the equivalent disjunctive form
The second gloss shows an alternative: a three-part disjunctive (مانعة الخلو) that is equivalent to the conventional negation but not identical. Understand that this equivalence holds, but the conventional negation is the 'istilahi' one.
The disjunctive with three disjuncts covers all possibilities that contradict the original.

Categorical with Disjunctive Predicate vs. True Disjunctive
Brief note on categorical resembling disjunctive and vice versa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a categorical proposition with a disjunctive predicate (hamaliyya muriddat al-mahmul) and a true disjunctive proposition (munfasila haqiqiyya) based on word order.

Core Idea
Word order determines whether 'either...or' makes a proposition categorical or disjunctive.

In Islamic logic, propositions can cross boundaries. A categorical can resemble a disjunctive when two opposite predicates are attached to one subject. The key is where the particle of opposition (harf al-'inad) appears relative to the subject. This lesson clarifies the distinction using the classic example of number parity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Look for the particle of opposition (imma, aw). If present, note whether the subject precedes or follows it.
If no particle, it is a simple categorical.
2
Locate the subject and particle
If the subject comes before the particle, the proposition is categorical with a disjunctive predicate. If the subject comes after the particle, it is a true disjunctive.
Example: 'al-'adadu imma zawjun wa imma fardun' vs. 'imma an yakuna al-'adadu zawjan aw fardan'.
3
Apply the test to the given example
In 'The number is either even or odd', the subject 'the number' precedes 'either', so it is a categorical resembling a disjunctive.
4
Recognize the logical consequence
True disjunctives imply exclusive or inclusive disjunction, but categorical with disjunctive predicate asserts that the subject has one of two properties without specifying which, and it remains a single predicate.
This affects how the proposition is used in inferences.

A proposition has two contracts: one about the subject’s self-description, the other about the predicate — and Ibn Sina and al-Farabi disagreed on whether the first is actual or potential.

In the analysis of propositions, logicians distinguish two contracts: the contract of positing (عقد الوضع) and the contract of predication (عقد الحمل). The first concerns the subject’s own qualified existence, while the second concerns the predicate’s connection to the subject. When the General Conditional Converts or Not
Explanation of why the general conditional does not convert under two interpretations but does under a third.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three interpretations of the general conditional and determine which ones allow conversion.

Core Idea
Conversion of the general conditional depends on whether the description is merely concomitant or causally effective.

This passage from the logic tradition discusses the conversion (عكس) of the general conditional proposition (المشروطة العامة). The author explains that under the interpretations where necessity holds 'as long as' or 'with the condition of' the description, conversion fails. However, under the interpretation where necessity is 'because of' the description, conversion holds. By studying the example and the logical reasoning, you will learn to identify which type of conditionality is at play.

Guided Path
1
Define the general conditional
Understand that the general conditional proposition has three interpretations: (1) necessity as long as the description lasts, (2) necessity with the condition of the description, (3) necessity because of the description. The author treats each separately.
Interpretation (3) is the causal one.
2
Analyze the non-conversion cases
For interpretations (1) and (2), the incompatibility derived from the original is not of the type required for conversion. The original gives incompatibility between the predicate's description and the subject's essence, but conversion requires incompatibility between the subject's description and the predicate's essence. These are not equivalent.
3
Examine the example
The example of Zayd's mount shows a true statement that does not convert under (1): 'Nothing of the donkey is a mount of Zayd as long as Zayd's mount' is true, but its conversion 'Nothing that is a mount of Zayd is a donkey as long as it is a donkey' is false because a donkey could possibly be Zayd's mount at other times.
4
Analyze the conversion case
Under interpretation (3), because the description is the cause of necessity, the incompatibility is symmetric. Therefore, conversion holds: the same reasoning that shows the predicate's description incompatible with the subject's essence because of the subject's description also shows the subject's description incompatible with the predicate's essence because of the predicate's description, which is exactly what conversion requires.
The author says 'the apparent' (الظاهر) that conversion is valid under this interpretation.

Streamlining Convertible Propositions
Proposes dropping two types to reduce convertible count.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list which modal propositions are convertible and explain why temporal and widespread absolute propositions are excluded from the standard list.

Core Idea
Not all modal propositions convert; the list can be reduced by dropping non-convertible types.

In classical logic, not every proposition type has a valid conversion. Scholars debated the exact count of convertible propositions. This passage proposes dropping two types—temporal (waqtiyya) and widespread absolute (muntashira mutlaqa)—and further reducing the list to five by subsuming the negative possible under the rule that possible propositions do not convert. Understanding this simplification helps you master immediate inference and avoid errors in converting Islamic legal or theological statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original set
Recall that the standard list of convertible propositions includes nine types (e.g., absolute, necessary, possible, etc.). The passage suggests that the temporal and widespread absolute can be dropped because they behave like non-convertible types.
The temporal proposition asserts necessity at a specific time; the widespread absolute asserts truth at some unspecified time.
2
Understand why temporal is dropped
The temporal proposition (waqtiyya) is a modal that applies only at a particular time. Its conversion would yield a proposition about a different time, which may not hold. Hence, it is excluded from the convertible list.
For example, 'Zayd is standing at noon' does not convert to 'Something standing at noon is Zayd' in a temporal sense preserving the same time.
3
Understand why widespread absolute is dropped
The widespread absolute (muntashira mutlaqa) is true at some time but not necessarily all. Its conversion is similarly problematic because the time reference changes. So it is also excluded.
E.g., 'Every human is breathing at some time' does not convert to 'Some breathing thing is human at that same time' without ambiguity.
4
Follow the further reduction to five
The commentator suggests an even more concise list: five types. This is achieved by including the two negative possible propositions under the rule that possible propositions have no conversion. Thus, only affirmative possible and negative possible are already covered by the rule; they are not counted as separate convertible types.
The rule 'لا عكس للممكنتين' means 'no conversion for the two possible propositions', so they are excluded entirely.
5
Apply the simplification to Islamic examples
Consider a fiqh ruling that is temporal: 'Fasting is obligatory on the day of Ramadan.' This temporal proposition does not convert to 'Something obligatory on the day of Ramadan is fasting' in the same temporal modality, because other acts (e.g., charity) might also be obligatory at that time but not fasting. The conversion fails, justifying the exclusion.
This shows the practical relevance: incorrect conversion could lead to false inferences in legal reasoning.

Refuting a Misconception: Trivial Syllogisms and Exclusive Definitions
Author refutes an objection to the definition of istithna'i by analyzing a degenerate example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the objection that the trivial example 'Every J is B, and every B is B, therefore every J is B' blurs the distinction between conditional and conjunctive syllogisms, by applying the condition that the conclusion must not be known before the premises.

Core Idea
A syllogism where the conclusion is already known trivially is not a valid inference.

A potential objection arises: the example 'Every J is B, and every B is B, therefore every J is B' appears to satisfy both definitions, making them non-exclusive. The author provides two responses: such a syllogism is not a valid syllogism because the conclusion is known prior to the premises, and also the matter of the conclusion is not mentioned separately. This lesson examines this refinement of the definition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Read the objection: the example 'Every J is B, and every B is B, therefore every J is B' seems to be a valid categorical syllogism (Barbara) and also appears to have the conclusion mentioned by matter and form (since the conclusion is stated verbatim). This would make the definition of istithnā'ī not exclusive (since it could also be considered iqtirānī).
The example uses Arabic variables (ج، ب) as terms.
2
Analyze the first response: not a genuine syllogism
The author argues that this is not a syllogism at all because the conclusion is already known before the syllogism is constructed. A genuine syllogism must produce new knowledge; here the conclusion is the same as the minor premise, so it is trivial.
This is an epistemological condition: the syllogism must be a source of knowledge, not a mere repetition.
3
Analyze the second response: what is mentioned is the conclusion itself, not its matter
The author clarifies that 'matter being mentioned' means that the subject and predicate of the conclusion are mentioned separately in the premises. In the trivial example, the conclusion itself appears verbatim, but its matter (the terms J and B) are not mentioned as separate extremes of the premises (since the premises already contain them in the same arrangement).
The distinction is between mentioning the conclusion as a whole and mentioning its components.

Conditions for Non-Contradiction Among Conditionals
Author defines non-contradiction and compares proposition types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when two conditional propositions are contradictory based on the condition that the truth of each entails the falsity of the other under the same subject and predicate.

Core Idea
Two conditionals conflict only when sharing subject and predicate and their truth forces the other's falsity; otherwise they can be simultaneously true.

This lesson focuses on the non-contradiction condition (munafaat) explained in the glosses. The passage provides a precise criterion for when two propositions—especially conditionals like 'Every eclipsed thing is dark as long as it is eclipsed' and 'No eclipsed thing is dark at a specific time'—can be considered contradictory. You will learn to apply this condition to identify conflicts and understand relationships like mutual exclusivity and generality from one aspect.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of non-contradiction (munafaat)
The gloss defines it as: 'the truth of each one entails the falsity of the other when subject and predicate are the same.' Note that this requires both subject and predicate to be identical. Contradiction is stronger than mere difference.
This is the standard logical definition of contradictories.
2
Apply to the example of the eclipsed thing
Consider two propositions: (A) 'Every eclipsed thing is dark as long as it is eclipsed, not perpetually' and (B) 'No eclipsed thing is dark at a specific time (when it is illuminated), not perpetually.' Both can be true simultaneously because A applies during eclipse, B applies at another time. Thus they do not contradict.
The example shows that temporal qualification prevents contradiction.
3
Identify relationships between proposition types
The passage states that the restricted conditional (first) is mutually exclusive with both the general conditional and the temporal. Mutual exclusivity means they cannot both be true? Actually, the passage says 'النسبة بين الاولى و بين كل واحدة من الآخريين هي المباينة' – mutual exclusivity (تباين). This likely means they are contradictories? But given the earlier example, they can be true at different times. So 'مباينة' here may mean they have no overlap in subject matter? Or they are different in definition. The gloss clarifies that they do not conflict (لا منافاة). So mutual exclusivity might refer to conceptual distinction, not truth-conditional conflict.
Clarify: 'مباينة' here means the types are distinct categories, not that they always contradict each other in truth value.
4
Determine which is the most specific major premise
The final gloss explains that when the minor is not necessary, it is one of fourteen types. Among these, the restricted conditional is the most specific except for the perpetual, with which it has mutual exclusivity. The most specific minor premise is either the restricted or the perpetual, depending on the definition of the general conditional.
This connects the non-contradiction discussion back to classification.

Uncovering Implicit Syllogisms in Simple Propositions
Author demonstrates that the judgment 'Four is even' depends on an implicit syllogism, and an objection is raised.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the implicit categorical syllogism behind any apparently simple proposition and evaluate whether it challenges axiomatic status.

Core Idea
Even the simplest judgment conceals a syllogism; recognizing this is key to testing foundational claims.

You might think a statement like 'Four is even' is immediately obvious, needing no proof. But the logician reveals that it actually depends on a hidden syllogism: 'Four is divisible into two equal halves; everything divisible into two equal halves is even; therefore four is even.' This lesson teaches you to uncover such implicit syllogisms and to assess the objection that this makes all knowledge dependent on premises, potentially leading to infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the simple proposition
Take a proposition that seems self-evident, like 'Four is even' or 'The whole is greater than the part'. Write it down as a declarative statement.
2
Decompose the subject and predicate
Separate the subject (e.g., 'four') and the predicate (e.g., 'even'). Ask: what must be true for the predicate to apply to the subject? The text says 'four' is divisible into two equal halves, and 'even' means divisible into two equal halves.
3
Form the middle term
The middle term is the concept that connects the subject and predicate. Here, 'divisible into two equal halves' is the middle term. It occurs in both premises.
4
Reconstruct the categorical syllogism
Place the middle term as the subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise. Write: Major premise: Everything divisible into two equal halves is even. Minor premise: Four is divisible into two equal halves. Conclusion: Therefore, four is even.
Check that the syllogism is in the first figure (Barbara mood) if the middle term is subject in major and predicate in minor.
5
Evaluate the objection
The objection states that 'The whole is greater than the part' also depends on a syllogism, so it would not be an axiom. This challenges the notion of self-evident truths. Consider: does the presence of an implicit syllogism negate axiomatic status?
The objection hints at a potential infinite regress if every proposition requires proof.
