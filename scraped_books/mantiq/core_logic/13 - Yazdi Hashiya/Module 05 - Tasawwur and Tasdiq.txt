Definition of Knowledge and Its Omission
Commentary defining knowledge as mental form and three reasons for omitting its definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of knowledge from the commentary and explain why a formal definition may be omitted in logical texts.

Core Idea
Knowledge is the mental form of a thing; its definition can be left out if it is self-evident or well-known.

In this passage, the commentator of a foundational logic text defines knowledge (al-ilm) and then explains why the original author did not provide a formal definition. This illustrates a key principle in Islamic logic: not every term needs explicit definition, especially when it is clear from context or widely understood. Understanding this helps you navigate definitions in classical texts.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of knowledge
Locate the exact phrase in the Arabic that defines knowledge. It is 'هو الصورة الحاصلة من الشيء عند العقل' (it is the form obtained from the thing in the intellect).
2
Analyze the definition's components
Identify the genus (الجنس) and differentia (الفصل). The genus is 'صورة' (form), and the differentia is 'حاصلة من الشيء عند العقل' (obtained from the thing in the intellect). This makes it a complete definition (hadd haqiqi).
Check if the definition covers all instances and excludes non-knowledge.
3
List the three reasons for omission
The commentator gives three reasons for the author not defining knowledge: (1) basic conception suffices for division, (2) the definition is already widespread, (3) knowledge is self-evident in conception.
Each reason is introduced by 'إما' (either).
4
Evaluate the reasons
Consider whether these reasons are convincing. The first reason relates to the purpose of the text (division into tasawwur and tasdiq), the second to common knowledge, and the third to immediate understanding.
This shows that definitions are not always necessary.

The Broad Definition of Tasawwur (Conception)
Commentary defining tasawwur broadly as perception, with or without relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the types of tasawwur defined in this passage and distinguish it from tasdiq.

Core Idea
Tasawwur is any perception without assent — including simple, compound, and non-assertoric awareness.

In Islamic logic, tasawwur is one of the two primary cognitive acts, the other being tasdiq (assent). This passage from the commentary expands on what tasawwur encompasses. It is not merely a mental image of a single object; it includes perceptions of multiple objects, relational concepts, and even imagined or doubted propositions — as long as no judgment is made. Understanding this broad definition is essential for grasping how logicians classify knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a hashiya commentary on a matn regarding the first purpose (al-maqsad al-awwal) concerning tasawwurat (concepts). The author, Yazdi, begins by justifying the inclusion of word studies in logic, noting that although logic is primarily about meanings (concepts and arguments), it is customary to discuss technical terms to facilitate communication. He then defines dalala (indication) as a relationship where knowledge of one thing (the dal) leads to knowledge of another (the madlul). This definition is foundational. Next, he divides dalala into three types based on the cause: wad'iyya (conventional) when a positor assigns a word to a meaning, tabi'iyya (natural) when the indicator naturally accompanies the indicated (like a sigh indicating pain), and 'aqliyya (rational) when the mind infers one thing from another by reasoning (like smoke indicating fire). He specifies that only the first type, verbal conventional indication, is the focus of logic because it is the basis of communication. He then subdivides conventional indication into three: mutabaqa (correspondence) when the word indicates its entire designated meaning, tadammun (inclusion) when it indicates a part of that meaning, and iltizam (implication) when it indicates something external but necessarily connected. For iltizam to be valid, there must be a necessary connection (luzum), either intellectual (like the relation of sight to blindness) or customary (like the relation of Hatim to generosity). Finally, he notes that both tadammun and iltizam presuppose mutabaqa, because one must first know the full meaning to infer a part or a necessary concomitant. The author uses Islamic examples: 'Zayd' as a proper name, 'Ah Ah' as a natural sign of pain, and the famous generosity of Hatim al-Ta'i as a customary association. This classification is essential for understanding how words convey meaning and is a standard topic in introductory logic texts.

The Three Types of Conventional Indication: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, and Iltizam
Author defines indication (dalala), divides it into conventional, natural, and rational, then divides conventional into mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam indication, and verify the condition of necessary connection (luzum) for iltizam.

Core Idea

The Logical Definition of a Verb (Kalam)
Definition of logical verb with conditions and objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given Arabic word qualifies as a logical verb (kalam) by applying the conditions of independence and form-based time indication.

Core Idea
A logical verb must be independent and indicate time through its morphological form.

In logic, words are divided into noun, verb, and particle. The logician's definition of a verb (kalam) is precise: it must be independent in meaning and indicate one of the three times (past, present, future) solely through its form. This precision prevents confusion when analyzing arguments in Islamic texts. The following passage explains the definition and handles potential counterexamples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus and differentia
The definition: 'an independent word signifying one of the three times by its form'. The genus is 'independent word' (mustaqill), and the differentia is 'indicating time by its form' (bi-hay'atihi).
Independence means the word does not need another word to complete its meaning.
2
Test with a clear example
The word 'nasara' (نصر) has the form of three consecutive open letters, which consistently indicates past tense when used in a disposed root. Thus it qualifies as a logical verb.
Note that the root must be disposed (mutasarrif) for the form to reliably indicate time.
3
Apply the condition of 'disposed root'
Words like 'jasaq' (جسق) and 'hajar' (حجر) have similar letter patterns but are not derived from disposed roots; their form does not systematically indicate tense. They are not logical verbs.
This excludes accidental resemblance; only roots that can be conjugated count.
4
Handle participles and verbs of approximation
Active participles (e.g., 'muqatil') indicate time but are nouns because they do not indicate time by original position (al-wad' al-awwal). Verbs of approximation (e.g., 'kada') are considered tools (adawat) by logicians, not verbs, because they do not indicate time independently.
The definition relies on the primary linguistic convention, not derived meanings.

Distinguishing Ṣidq and Ḥaqq and Refining the Definition of Proposition
Refines the definitions of ṣidq and ḥaqq and defends the definition of qaḍiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ṣidq (truth) and ḥaqq (reality) and explain how the definition of proposition avoids circularity by using the abstract sense of ṣidq.

Core Idea
Truth is report-to-reality correspondence; reality is reality-to-report correspondence; the proposition is defined by abstract truth.

The commentator clarifies that ṣidq and ḥaqq are reciprocal relations. Ṣidq is the correspondence of a report to reality, while ḥaqq is the correspondence of reality to the report. This distinction is crucial because in defining a proposition (qaḍiyya) as that which admits truth and falsehood, we must be careful not to commit circularity. The definition of proposition uses ṣidq in its abstract sense (the relation itself), not the concrete report. This lesson will walk through the distinction and the defense against circularity.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish ṣidq and ḥaqq
Identify the two relations: ṣidq is the correspondence of the report to reality; ḥaqq is the correspondence of reality to the report. Both are reciprocal.
The same proposition can be described as ṣidq from one perspective and ḥaqq from another.
2
Recognize the abstract sense of ṣidq
Ṣidq can mean either the concrete report that corresponds or the abstract correspondence relation itself. The abstract sense is crucial for defining 'proposition'.
In the definition of qaḍiyya, ṣidq is taken as the abstract correspondence (al-muṭābaqa).
3
Understand the potential circularity
If ṣidq in the definition is understood as the concrete report, then 'proposition' would be defined as 'a statement that admits a corresponding report' – which is synonymous with 'report', causing circularity.
Circularity renders the definition invalid in logical methodology.
4
See how the abstract sense resolves it
By using ṣidq as the abstract correspondence, the definition becomes: 'a statement that admits of the property of correspondence or non-correspondence with reality' – which is not synonymous with 'report'.
This preserves the logicality of the definition.
5
Handle the homonymy objection
A critic might object that using a word with two meanings (homonym) in a definition is problematic. The response is that the abstract meaning is the primary literal meaning (infinitive), and even if metaphorical, it is so commonly understood that it poses no confusion.
This defense ensures the definition's validity in discourse.

The Eight Epistemic States in Conception of a Judgment
Muhammad Ali classifies types of tasawwur of a judgmental relation into doubt, supposition, delusion, certainty, etc.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any conception of a judgmental relation into one of eight epistemic states by applying the dichotomous criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Every conception of judgment falls into one of eight states, from imagination to certainty.

When you conceive a proposition, your mental state can vary widely. The logician Muhammad Ali provides a precise classification of eight possible states, based on whether the conception affects the soul, whether the sides are equal, whether certainty is reached, and whether the belief corresponds to reality. This taxonomy is essential for evaluating the epistemic value of any claim you encounter in Islamic theology or jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first branching point
Does the conception affect the soul with a wondrous effect (contraction/expansion) even if its opposite is known? If yes, it is takhyil (imagination). If no, proceed.
Example: saying 'Wine is a flowing ruby' to entice, even though one knows wine is not literally a ruby.
2
Check the equality of the relation's sides
If the conception does not have a wondrous effect, then examine the relation's two sides (existence and non-existence). Does the mind see both sides as equally probable? If yes, it is shakk (doubt). If not, the sides are unequal; proceed.
Doubt is a state of equipoise, neither side preponderating.
3
Determine whether certainty is obtained
If the sides are unequal, does the mind have certainty (qat') of one side? If not, then if the preponderant side is considered more likely, it is zann (supposition); if the weaker side is deemed more likely, it is wahm (delusion). If certainty is obtained, proceed.
Zann and wahm both lack certainty but differ in the direction of preponderance.
4
Identify the certain side's existence status
If certainty is obtained, determine whether the certain side is non-existence or existence. If non-existence, it is kadhib (falsehood). If existence, it is jadh (assertion). Then check: does the assertion correspond to reality? If not, it is jahl murakkab (compound ignorance). If it does correspond, then does it admit of doubt? If no, it is yaqin (certainty); if yes, it is taqlid (imitation).
Only assertion that corresponds to reality and eliminates doubt qualifies as certainty (yaqin).

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the different mental states resulting from conceiving a judgmental relation (nisba hukmiyya). This classification is typical of the logical tradition's refinement of epistemology.

Muhammad Ali enumerates eight types: (1) takhyil (imaginative representation) when the soul is affected regardless of truth, (2) shakk (doubt) when both sides are equal, (3) wahm (estimation) when one side is weak, (4) zann (conjecture) when one side is strong, (5) kadhib (falsehood) when the certain side is non-existence, (6) jazm (affirmation) when the certain side is existence, (7) jahl murakkab (compound ignorance) when jazm does not correspond to reality, (8) taqlid (imitative belief) when it is not immune to doubt. He clarifies that four (kadhib, takhyil, shakk, wahm) are not tasdiq because they lack idh'an (assent). The remaining five are tasdiq by consensus. Shaykh Abd al-Rahim adds that idh'an must be broad enough to include zann, which is why the commentator lists it among the tasdiq types. There is a disagreement about takhyil and falsehood: some consider them tasdiq, but the correct view is that they are tasawwur because no judgment (hukm) is involved.

Author compares views on necessary and theoretical assent among al-Hukama, al-Imam, and Sahib al-Kashf.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Necessary assent is narrower if all parts must be necessary.

In this gloss, the author compares two criteria for classifying assent as necessary or theoretical. While the philosophers consider only the judgment itself, al-Imam and Sahib al-Kashf require all components of the proposition to be necessary. Guided Path

Identify the two criteria

Al-Imam (and Sahib al-Kashf) consider the composite whole of the four things—subject, predicate, judgment, and the relation between them. Because the philosophers require only the judgment to be necessary, their category of necessary assent is broader: more propositions qualify. For al-Imam, since all parts must be necessary, fewer propositions are necessary; thus necessary assent is narrower, and theoretical assent is broader.

But if the concept of 'world' or 'created' is not itself necessary (e.g., requires proof), then al-Imam would not classify the overall assent as necessary, because not all parts are necessary. This shows how the same proposition can be classified differently.

The Paradox of Seeking the Unknown
Author discusses the requirement that the object of nazar be unknown, and addresses Imam al-Ghazali's objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the object of nazar must be both known and unknown, and use this to resolve Imam al-Ghazali's objection.

Core Idea
To seek knowledge of something, it must be known in one way and unknown in another – otherwise seeking is impossible.

This passage from the Yazdi Hashiya tackles a foundational puzzle: if nazar aims to obtain an unknown matter, how can we direct our mind toward something we are completely unaware of? And if we already know it, why seek it? The author presents and resolves a famous objection by Imam al-Ghazali, clarifying the precise sense in which the object must be unknown. This lesson will walk you through the problem and its solution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge aimed at by nazar
The author states that nazar can obtain either a concept (tasawwur) or an assent (tasdiq). Note the examples: obtaining the concept of 'man' by combining animal and rational; obtaining the assent that the world is originated by reasoning from change.
This shows that the process of nazar works differently for concepts vs. assents but both require prior known premises.
2
Understand the role of known premises (mabadi')
The premises must already be known and present in the mind. The author says 'مبادى المطلوب لابد ان يكون معلومة' – the premises are known. This is why the definition includes 'toward known matters.'
The premises are the known starting points; the sought conclusion is the unknown.
3
Formulate Ghazali's objection in your own words
If the sought object is already known, seeking it is redundant (tahsil al-hasil). If it is completely unknown, the mind cannot even intend to seek it (since it has never occurred to it). This seems to make nazar impossible.
This is a genuine epistemological paradox.
4
Analyze the solution: known in one respect, unknown in another
The response is that the sought object is known 'بوجه آخر' – in another respect. For example, you may know that 'man' exists but not its definition; or you know that the world is changing (known) but not that it is originated (unknown). So the object is known enough to direct attention, yet unknown enough to require acquisition.
The author also mentions the debate by al-Maraghī but does not elaborate.

This is what makes logic a tool for expanding knowledge. The author also critiques an example from the sharh and shows that even obvious propositions like 'fire is hot' can lead to new unknown conclusions through syllogism. The commentator then clarifies the method: the subject of any science is defined by investigating its essential properties, and for logic, that subject is the known conceptual and assentive.

The Three Types of Verbal Signification
Author defines dalaalat al-lafdh and divides it into mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of verbal signification (muṭābaqah, taḍammun, iltizām) and recognize that muṭābaqah is the primary type.

Core Idea
Every utterance signifies its whole meaning, its parts, and its necessary concomitants.

After defining verbal signification, the author divides it into three kinds: signification by conformity (muṭābaqah), by inclusion (taḍammun), and by implication (iltizām). This division, standard in Islamic logic, is crucial for analyzing how a single term can carry multiple interrelated meanings, especially in legal and theological texts.

Guided Path
1
List the three types
The author explicitly names three: dalaalat al-mutabaqa, al-tadammun, and al-iltizam.
All three are sub-types of conventional verbal signification.
2
Understand each type's scope
Muṭābaqah: the utterance signifies the entirety of what it is posited for. Taḍammun: it signifies a part of that meaning. Iltizām: it signifies something external but necessarily following from the meaning.
The example of 'human' (insan) illustrates: mutabaqa = whole (animal rationale), tadammun = part (animal or rational), iltizam = necessary concomitant (e.g., capacity for learning).
3
Recognize the primacy of muṭābaqah
The text says mutabaqa is 'the origin (asl) of the three significations'. It is the primary meaning for which the word is coined; the other two are derived from it.
For example, the word 'salah' primarily signifies the prayer (mutabaqa); by tadammun it signifies the parts like ruku'; by iltizam it signifies the obligation.

The Verbal Dispute on Word Indication and Intention
Author presents differing views on whether word indication requires speaker's intention and resolves the dispute as verbal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two competing definitions of 'indication' (dalala) and explain how the dispute between Ibn Sina and later logicians is resolved as a verbal disagreement.

Core Idea
The dispute on word indication is verbal, not substantive—it hinges on how you define 'indication'.

In this passage, the author presents a famous epistemological debate: does a word indicate its meaning only when the speaker intends it, or regardless of intention? You will see how Ibn Sina and al-Tusi argue for intention-dependence, and how later scholars counter that indication is purely conventional. The author then shows that the conflict dissolves once we clarify the definition of 'indication' (dalala). This lesson will equip you to recognize when a logical dispute is merely verbal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Note the first claim: when a homonym is uttered, a hearer who knows its multiple meanings conceives all of them even if the speaker intends only one. This implies indication does not require intention. The second claim is that indication depends entirely on the speaker's intention.
2
Trace the arguments of the intention-dependence camp
Ibn Sina argues that if words indicated by themselves, they would have fixed, unchangeable meanings; but since they can indicate different things based on intent, indication follows intention. Al-Tusi adds that because indication is conventional (wad'iyya), it is tied to the speaker's intention—only the intended meaning is indicated.
3
Understand the later scholars' objection
The commentator of al-Matali' argues that indication is by convention, not intention: anyone who knows the word's designation will conceive the meaning upon hearing it, whether intended or not. For homonyms, all meanings are conceived, though the intended one may require a contextual clue.
The key distinction: the word's indication of meaning vs. determining the speaker's intention.
4
See the author's resolution
The author declares the dispute verbal: it depends on whether you define 'indication' as the transition from word to meaning specifically as intended (first definition) or as absolute transition (second definition). Both sides agree on the psychological fact that meanings are conceived; they merely label it differently.
The author advises comparing the scholars' words to confirm the verbal nature.

Commentary
This passage presents a key epistemological dispute in logic: does a word indicate its meaning independently of the speaker's intention, or only when the speaker intends that meaning? The author first reports the view of those who say that when a homonym is heard, a person aware of its multiple meanings conceives all of them even if the speaker intends only one; this is the view that indication does not require intention. Then he mentions the opposing view that indication is dependent on intention, quoting al-Shaykh (Ibn Sina) from al-Shifa' and al-Muhaqqiq al-Tusi from Sharh al-Isharat. Ibn Sina argues that a word does not indicate by itself, because if it did, every word would have a fixed set of meanings that cannot be exceeded. Rather, its indication follows the speaker's intention. Al-Tusi adds that since indication is conventional (wad'iyya), it is tied to the speaker's intention; only the intended meaning is indicated by the word. The later scholars (al-muta'akhkhirun) strongly reject this, and the commentator of al-Matali' argues that indication is by convention, not by intention. He provides an example: anyone who knows the conventional meaning of a word will conceive that meaning upon hearing it, regardless of whether the speaker intends it. For homonyms, the hearer conceives all meanings. He concedes that determining the speaker's intention requires a contextual clue, but the word's indication of a meaning is distinct from the speaker's intention of that meaning. The author then resolves the dispute as verbal: it hinges on how one defines 'indication'. If one defines it as the transition from word to meaning specifically as intended, then it depends on intention. If one defines it as absolute transition, then it is broader. Both sides agree on the psychological phenomenon of conceiving meanings upon hearing words; the disagreement is merely terminological. The author advises the reader to compare the words of the scholars to see that the dispute is not substantive.

This is based on the assumption that the natural universal exists externally. Why Universals Exist Only in the Mind
Author presents and refutes arguments for the mental existence of universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound syllogism proving that universals exist only in the mind, and identify the key refutation based on the distinction between essence with and without particularizing attributes.

Core Idea
The universal cannot exist externally; it is a mental abstraction from particulars.

In this passage, the author presents a crisp compound argument that the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) cannot exist in the external world, because every external existent is a particular individual, and no particular is common to many — yet the universal is common to many. The commentary then raises a subtle refutation: the crucial term 'individual' is ambiguous between the essence-plus-accidents and the essence alone. The discussion unfolds into a deeper division argument that reinforces the mental existence of universals. This lesson unpacks the logic and the critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
The author aims to prove that the natural universal exists only in the mind (in the intellect), not in external reality. The claim is stated at the end: since external existence is false, mental existence is determined.
This claim is foundational for later discussions of predication and abstraction.
2
Extract the first categorical syllogism
The argument begins with two premises: 'Every external existent is a particular individual' and 'No particular individual is common to many.' From these, it follows that no external existent is common to many. This is a valid Celarent syllogism (first figure).
The middle term here is 'particular individual' (شخص معين).
3
Complete the compound inference
A third premise 'Every universal is common to many' is then combined with the previous conclusion. From 'No external existent is common to many' and 'All universals are common to many', it follows that no external existent is a universal (Cesare, second figure).
This two-step compound syllogism is the core logical structure.
4
Unpack the refutation
The commentator challenges the use of 'particular individual': if it means the essence with its individualizing attributes, the argument holds; but if it means the essence itself stripped of those attributes, then it can become common when abstracted in the mind. This exposes an ambiguity.
The refutation does not disprove the conclusion but clarifies the sense in which the universal is mental.
