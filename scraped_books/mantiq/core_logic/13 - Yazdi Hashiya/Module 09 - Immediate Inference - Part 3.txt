Converting Temporal Propositions: Preserving the Time Restriction
Author exemplifies conversion of a temporal proposition and refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to correctly convert a temporal proposition while preserving its time restriction, using the example of moon eclipse.

Core Idea
A temporal proposition's converse must retain its time restriction, otherwise the conversion fails.

In this passage, the author demonstrates the correct conversion of a temporal proposition about the moon's eclipse. The key is to preserve the time restriction ('at quadrature') because it defines the proposition's truth conditions. Let's follow the steps of this conversion and understand why the restriction cannot be ignored.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original temporal proposition
The original is: 'Nothing from the moon is eclipsed necessarily at quadrature not perpetually.' This is a universal negative (E) with a time restriction 'at quadrature'.
The time restriction is part of the predicate's condition.
2
Perform the conversion by swapping subject and predicate
Convert by swapping 'moon' and 'eclipsed', keeping the time restriction: 'Nothing from the eclipsed is a moon at quadrature necessarily not perpetually.'
The quantity and quality remain the same (universal negative).
3
Verify the meaning of the converse
The converse means: the moon under the description of eclipse is not necessarily the moon at quadrature (they may co-occur at other times). This is self-evidently true.
4
Check the necessity of the time restriction
If we drop 'at quadrature', the converse would be false and even contradictory, as the author shows. The restriction is essential for truth.
This step highlights the critical refinement: the restriction must be preserved.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed syllogistic/definitional analysis of converting a temporal proposition, followed by a refutation of an erroneous objection. It begins with an example: 'Nothing from the moon is eclipsed necessarily at quadrature not perpetually.' The author explains that this means the negation of necessary eclipse is not perpetual; eclipse may occur at other times. The converse, disregarding the non-permanence restriction, is then given: 'Nothing from the eclipsed is a moon at quadrature necessarily not perpetually,' meaning moon and eclipse co-occur at other times. This converse is correct, as it states that the moon under the eclipse description is not necessarily the moon at quadrature. The critic's mistake is in ignoring the temporal restriction. The critic claimed that 'some eclipsed not being a moon' is false in the general possible, but the proper formulation must include the time restriction: 'some eclipsed is not a moon at quadrature,' which then gives a necessary meaning. The critic's contradictory – 'every eclipsed is a moon necessarily' – is self-contradictory given the original premise. The author then emphasizes three proofs that the time restriction is essential: (1) it is assumed in the original proposition and must be preserved in the converse, (2) it gives the proposition its name 'temporal', (3) the author himself used it in the example of the negative temporal. Neglecting the restriction leads to contradiction. The passage concludes with a general principle: all simple propositions convert to themselves except possible ones, and composites resolve into simples. An objection is then raised: why must truth of affirmative imply falsity of negative? The response is that in conversion discussions, judgments are about existing individuals, so the negative applies to the same subject. Finally, a note justifies using the particular negative converse rather than the universal: the particular is more general in truth, so if the more general fails, the more specific does too.

The Temporal Restriction Fallacy: Why Neglecting the Time Leads to Contradiction
Author refutes an objection that ignores the temporal restriction in conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of ignoring a temporal restriction in proposition conversion and explain why it leads to self-contradiction.

Core Idea
Ignoring the time restriction in a temporal proposition's converse destroys the proposition and leads to a direct contradiction.

In this passage, the author criticizes a commentator who made the mistake of dropping the time restriction when evaluating the converse of a temporal proposition. The author shows how this neglect creates a blatant contradiction and provides three proofs that the restriction is essential. Let's analyze the error and learn why restrictions in propositions must always be respected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the critic's mistake
The critic said: 'Some eclipsed not being a moon is false in the general possible' without including 'at quadrature'. He evaluates a simple proposition instead of the temporal one.
The original proposition has the restriction 'at quadrature'; the critic omitted it.
2
Recognize the corrected formulation
The correct formulation is: 'Some eclipsed is not a moon at quadrature.' With this restriction, the proposition becomes necessary, not possible.
3
Understand the resulting contradiction
The critic's oversight leads to the claim that 'Every eclipsed is a moon necessarily', which directly contradicts the original 'Nothing from the moon is eclipsed necessarily at quadrature'.
The two statements cannot both be true; they are opposites.
4
Review the author's three proofs
(1) The restriction is assumed in the original proposition, so it must be in the converse. (2) The proposition's name 'temporal' derives from the restriction. (3) The author himself used the restriction in his example of the negative temporal.
These proofs establish that the restriction is not optional but constitutive.
5
Generalize the principle
The author concludes: restrictions and conditions in propositions have complete influence on the attached judgment; neglecting them destroys the proposition and any conclusions built on it.
This is the key takeaway for all logical operations.

Identify the two methods
The ancients' method: take the contradictory of both the subject and predicate of the original proposition. The moderns' method: take the contradictory of the predicate first and the subject second (i.e., swap and negate). Locate these definitions in the Arabic: 'طريقة القدماء' vs 'طريقة المتأخرين'.
The ancients' contrapositive of 'كل ج ب' is 'ليس شيء مما ليس ب بج' (nothing that is not b is not j). The moderns' contrapositive is 'كل ما ليس ب ليس ج' (everything that is not b is not j).
2
Understand the moderns' objection
The moderns argued that the contradictory of the ancients' contrapositive is a particular negative ('ليس بعض ما ليس ب ليس ج'), which cannot serve as a premise in the first figure (since the first figure requires a universal affirmative or a universal negative for the major). This invalidates the proof that the contrapositive holds universally.
The first figure requires the minor to be affirmative and the major to be universal. The particular negative fails both conditions.
3
Examine the existential import issue
The moderns also denied the entailment from the negated form to the affirmative form because a negative with a modified predicate (معدولة المحمول) does not imply the existence of the subject. Without existential import, the ancients' claim that the contrapositive necessarily holds collapses.
In modern logical parlance, the ancients' definition assumed that all terms are non-empty, which the moderns rejected.
4
Analyze the commentator's defense
The commentator offers a twofold defense: (1) logic only deals with existents, so the existential import issue does not arise; (2) even if we include non-existents, the moderns' own definition works because their contrapositive's contradictory forces the required conclusion through a reasoning that avoids the problematic negative.
This defense shows that the moderns' definition is technically sound even under a broader interpretation.
5
Evaluate the refinement
Assess why the moderns' definition became standard. It preserves the logical inference without reliance on existential assumptions and fits neatly into the first figure. This refinement reflects the maturing of Islamic logic.
The moderns' definition is now the standard one taught in logic texts.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement explaining why the moderns (muta'akhirun) changed the definition of contrapositive from the ancients' (qudama) method. The ancients used both contradictories in the contrapositive definition, which allowed a direct proof that truth preservation entails quality preservation. However, the moderns objected: the contradictory of the ancients' contrapositive of a universal affirmative is a particular negative, which cannot serve as a premise in the first figure to derive the required result. Furthermore, the entailment from the negated form to the affirmative is denied because the negative with a modified predicate does not imply an affirmative with a direct predicate if the subject might be non-existent. Therefore, the moderns changed the definition: they made the contradictory of the second part first and the first part second, avoiding the problematic negative. The commentator then offers a possible defense of the ancients: logic is limited to existents, so the objection fails. But even if we include non-existents, the moderns' definition works because their contrapositive of a universal affirmative is an affirmative with negative terms, which does not imply existence. The contradictory of that is 'not some of what is not b is not j', which itself forces the truth of 'some of what is not b is j' by a reasoning involving the falsity of the negation due to truth of j. Thus the moderns' definition is valid.

The Early Method of 'Aks al-Naqid (Conversion of the Contradictory)
Explains the early proof of 'aks al-naqid via contradiction and first-figure syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the early logicians' proof for 'aks al-naqid using simple conversion and a first-figure syllogism.

Core Idea
The early method proves 'aks al-naqid by showing that denying the converse leads to a contradiction.

In this lesson, we examine the early logicians' (qudama') method for converting contradictory propositions. The text presents a proof by contradiction that relies on simple conversion and a categorical syllogism in the first figure. This method was standard in the sciences, as the author notes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its intended converse
Start with a universal affirmative: 'Every J is B'. The early method claims it implies 'Every not-B is not-J'. This is the conversion to be proved.
These are abstract variables; in Islamic sciences, an example would be 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' implying 'Everything not forbidden is not an intoxicant'.
2
Assume the converse is false
If 'Every not-B is not-J' is false, its contradictory is true: 'Not all not-B is not-J', i.e., 'Some not-B is J'.
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative? Actually the contradictory of 'Every X is Y' is 'Some X is not Y'. But here the converse is 'Every not-B is not-J', so its contradictory is 'Some not-B is J' (since 'not-J' negated is J). This matches the text.
3
Apply simple conversion to the resulting proposition
'Some not-B is J' converts by simple conversion to 'Some J is not-B'. Simple conversion of a particular affirmative yields a particular affirmative? Wait: 'Some A is B' converts to 'Some B is A'. So 'Some not-B is J' converts to 'Some J is not-B'. That contradicts the original 'Every J is B' (since 'Every J is B' entails 'No J is not-B'), leading to a contradiction.
Alternatively, the text mentions joining with the original to form a syllogism that produces an impossibility.
4
Derive a contradiction via the first-figure syllogism
The text also gives another route: take 'Some not-J is B' (derived from the false assumption? Actually from 'Some not-B is J' and something else?) and 'Every J is B' to produce 'Some not-B is B', which is impossible because a thing cannot both be B and not be B.
This second route is a first-figure syllogism: 'Some not-J is B, Every J is B, therefore Some not-B is B'? But careful with the terms. The text says: 'Some of what is not J is B' and 'Every J is B' produce 'Some of what is not B is B'. This is valid in the first figure with the middle term 'B'.
5
Conclude the converse holds
Since assuming the falsehood of the converse leads to a contradiction, the converse must be true. Thus 'Every J is B' implies 'Every not-B is not-J'.
This establishes the validity of 'aks al-naqid for universal affirmatives by the early method.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical discussion on the conversion of the contradictory ('aks al-naqid) in logic, focusing on the difference between the method of the early logicians (qudama') and the later logicians (muta'akhkhirun). The author (likely from the hashiya tradition) defends the early method against objections raised by the later logicians.

The passage begins by presenting the early logicians' proof for 'aks al-naqid. In a categorical syllogism of the first figure, they show that the converse must hold; if not, a contradiction arises. The early method is described as the one relied upon in the sciences.

Then the objection of the later logicians is introduced. They argue that the contradictory of the converse (naqid al-'aks) is a particular negative proposition. Particular negatives do not have a simple conversion ('aks mustawi), nor can they serve as a minor premise (because it is not affirmative) or a major premise (because it is not universal) in the first figure. Hence the early logicians' syllogism fails. Moreover, they deny the implication that 'Some of what is not B is J' follows from 'Not some of what is not B is not J' because the latter is a negative with a modified predicate, which does not entail an affirmative with an original predicate due to possible non-existence of the subject.

In response, the author offers an answer: the discussion of conversions should be restricted to existent things, since logic is a tool for philosophy which studies existents. Then, if the generality is conceded, he argues that the contradictory is not modified; rather, it is taken as an affirmative with both terms negated, which in the later logicians' view does not require the existence of the subject. The argument is then unfolded step by step to show that the desired conclusion follows.

The passage also includes notes by commentators (Mirza Muhammad Ali, Muhammad Ali) clarifying interpretations. Finally, it cites al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif stating that the early meaning of 'aks al-naqid is what is used in the sciences, not the later one.

Proof that some not-B is J from premises that J is not B and D is J
Proof and explanation of an inference from two premises to a conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct an immediate inference from a particular negative proposition and an auxiliary instance to derive its existential converse.

Core Idea
An instance bridges the gap between a particular negative and its converse with existential support.

In this passage, the commentator shows how from the statement 'some J is not B' and the supposition 'some J is D', we can derive 'some not-B is J'. The key logical principle is that when two descriptions separate in an individual, they can still be predicated of each other in some respect. We will walk through this proof step by step, formalize the inference, and see how it applies in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the given premises
The first premise is 'some J is not B in actuality' (حكم فيه ان بعض ج ليس ب بالفعل). The second premise is a supposition: 'some J is D' (بعض ج، د). Note that 'D' is an arbitrary term that applies to that same J.
The same individual is J, not B, and D.
2
Recognize the reasoning
The commentator says: 'so if we suppose some J is D, then that (some J) is also not B, and that is the desired conclusion.' Actually, the conclusion is 'some not-B is J', which follows because the individual that is J and D is also not B, providing an example of a not-B that is J.
3
Apply the separation principle
The justification is: 'when two descriptions separate in an individual, it is correct for each to be truthfully predicated of the other in some respect.' Here the descriptions 'being J' and 'being not B' separate in D (since D is J but not B). Therefore, it is correct to say 'some J is not B' (given) and 'some not-B is J' (new).
The separation principle allows the inference without assuming anything else.

We can write: (1) ∃x (Jx ∧ ¬Bx) [some J is not B]. (2) Suppose ∃x (Jx ∧ Dx) [some J is D]. Then there exists x such that Jx, ¬Bx, and Dx. Therefore, ∃x (¬Bx ∧ Jx) [some not-B is J]. This is valid because we are using the same individual.
This is not a standard conversion but an existential inference.
5
Note the qualification 'in some respect'
The principle says the predication holds 'في الجملة' (in some respect), meaning the inference is not universal but particular. The conclusion is a particular proposition, not a universal one.

This principle states that the original state of something (here, that D is not B) continues unless there is evidence to the contrary. Since D is originally not B (as established earlier), we are certain that D currently satisfies the condition 'not B'.
This principle is closely related to istishab in usul al-fiqh: presumption of continuity.
3
Apply instantiation
Because D is a specific thing that is not B (by the permanence of the original), we can instantiate the universal conditional with D. From 'as long as not B, D is not J' and the fact that D is not B, we directly infer that D is not J. But the conclusion is not just about D; it is generalized to 'some not-B is not J' (i.e., there exists at least one thing that is not B and that is not J when not B). Since D is such a thing, the existential claim follows.
The conclusion retains the temporal condition 'as long as not B' because the conditional relation holds for any instance where the condition obtains.
4
State the conclusion
Thus we conclude: 'Some not-B is not J as long as not B' (بعض ما ليس ب ليس ج مادام ليس ب). This is a legitimate particular conditional derived from a universal conditional plus a factual instance based on the permanence principle.
The move from universal to particular is valid here because the existential instantiation is justified by an actual instance (D) whose existence and condition are affirmed by a separate principle.

Reducing the Second Figure to the First by Conversion
Commentators explain conversion of second figure moods to first figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert any valid second figure mood to a first figure mood by applying conversion to the major or minor premise.

Core Idea
The second figure is just a converted first figure in disguise.

The second figure syllogism differs from the first only in the position of the middle term. By converting the major premise, we can transform it into a first figure syllogism while preserving the conclusion. This reduction is a key technique for simplifying syllogistic arguments and ensuring their validity is recognized more directly, as demonstrated by the commentators on this passage from al-Yazdi's Hashiya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second figure syllogism
Locate the major and minor premises. In the second figure, the middle term is predicate in both premises. For example: 'Every human is rational' (minor, middle: rational) and 'Nothing of the horse is rational' (major, middle: rational).
2
Determine which premise to convert
To convert directly to the first figure, convert the major premise. Converting the major swaps the subject and predicate, making the middle term subject in the new major, which matches the first figure requirement.
The minor remains unchanged if it is affirmative.
3
Perform simple conversion on the chosen premise
For a universal negative premise like 'Nothing of the horse is rational', simple conversion yields 'Nothing of the rational is a horse'. For a universal affirmative like 'Every human is rational', conversion yields 'Some rational is human' (particular affirmative).
Simple conversion preserves truth but changes quantity for universal affirmatives.
4
Rearrange the premises to first figure order
After conversion, the converted premise becomes the new major, and the original minor (if affirmative) becomes the new minor. The middle term now appears as subject in the major and predicate in the minor, which is the first figure pattern.
The conclusion may remain the same or require a final conversion to match.
5
Verify the conclusion follows
Check that the new first figure syllogism produces the desired conclusion directly or after converting the conclusion. For example, from 'Every human is rational' and 'Nothing of the rational is a horse', we deduce 'Nothing of the human is a horse', which is the original conclusion.
