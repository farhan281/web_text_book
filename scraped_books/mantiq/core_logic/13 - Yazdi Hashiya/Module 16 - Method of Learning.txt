The Components of a Science: Subject, Parts, Accidents, Premises, and Problems
Commentator explains the parts of a science: subject limits, accident limits, premises, and predicates.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the essential components of any Islamic science as described in the logical tradition.

Core Idea
Every science is built on its subject, its parts, its accidents, and its premises—evident or taken.

Before you can engage with any Islamic science—whether kalam, usul al-fiqh, or tafsir—you must understand what constitutes a science. The logicians have laid out a precise structure: the subject matter, its parts and accidents, the premises from which proofs are built, and the problems (propositions) that are sought. In this lesson, we unpack these components as explained by the commentator on al-Yazdi's Hashiya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four main components listed at the beginning
The passage opens by stating that a science consists of: (1) its parts (ajza'), (2) its accidents (a'rad), (3) premises that are either evident or taken (muqaddimat bayyina aw ma'khudha), and (4) problems (masa'il). Note that 'parts' here refers to the definitions of the parts of the subject when the subject is compound.
These components are the building blocks of any science.
2
Distinguish between self-evident and theoretical premises
The commentator divides assertive principles (mabadi' tasdiqiyya) into two: self-evident (bayyina bi nafsiha) and theoretical (ma'khudha). The self-evident are called 'common sciences' (ulum muta'arifa). Theoretical premises are further divided: if accepted on trust from the teacher, they are 'posited principles' (usul mawdu'a); if contested, they are 'postulates' (musadarat).
One premise may be posited for one person and a postulate for another, depending on prior acceptance.
3
Understand the definition of 'problems' (masa'il)
The passage critiques al-Dawani's view that problems are merely predicates. Instead, problems are propositions (qadaya) that have subjects and predicates. The subjects of problems are either the subject of the science itself or something else (like parts or accidents). The predicates are attributes that attach to these subjects essentially.
This clarifies that problems are full propositions, not just attributes.
4
Recognize the subject of a science and its possible types
The subject of a science (mawdu' al-ilm) can be: (a) the genus of its subjects, (b) an essential accident of them, or (c) a compound of both. An example from natural science: 'Every body has a form' shows the subject as genus. Predicates are external to the subject but attach to it essentially.
This structure ensures that the science's topics are coherently defined.
5
Connect the components to a real science example
Take the science of kalam: its subject is Allah's existence and attributes (a compound), its parts include the definitions of 'world' and 'contingency', its accidents include 'createdness', its premises include self-evident truths (e.g., 'the world is originated') and theoretical ones (e.g., 'every originated thing needs a creator'), and its problems are propositions like 'the world is originated' and 'the creator is one'.
This shows how the abstract components apply concretely.

Commentary
This passage defines the essential components of any science: its subject, its parts (limits of parts), its accidents (limits of accidents), and its premises (evident or taken). The commentator begins by quoting the original text that lists these components. Then he discusses a critique of al-Dawani's definition of 'problems' (masa'il), showing that they are not simply the predicates but propositions with subjects and predicates. He then examines four possible interpretations of the structure, favoring the one that includes assent to subjectivity as a separate part for methodological clarity. The commentary then explains each term: 'its parts' refer to the definitions of the parts when the subjects are compound; 'its accidents' refer to definitions of the accidents; 'premises' are divided into self-evident (axiomatic) and theoretical. Theoretical premises are further divided: if accepted on trust, they are 'posited principles'; if contested, 'postulates'. This classification is important for understanding the foundation of a science. Finally, the subject of a science can be either the genus of its subjects, an essential accident of them, or a compound of both. Predicates are external attributes that attach to the subjects in themselves. Examples from natural science illustrate: 'Every body has a shape' shows the subject as genus; 'Every moving thing has inclination' (from later text) shows essential accident. The passage emphasizes the relationship between subject and predicate in scientific propositions.

Mabadi', Muqaddimat, and the Condition of Essential Accidents
Author defines mabadi' and muqaddimat and critiques the condition that predicates must be essential accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mabadi' and muqaddimat with their conditions, and explain the critique of requiring predicates to be essential accidents in scientific problems.

Core Idea
Definitions in logic are not arbitrary; they must exclude false inclusives and withstand critique from actual scientific practice.

This passage tackles two foundational definitions in the organization of a science: what are mabadi' (principles) and muqaddimat (preliminaries). It then dives into a heated debate: must every predicate in a scientific problem be an 'essential accident' of its subject? By studying this, you will see how logicians refine definitions and respond to objections from fiqh, grammar, and natural science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Find where the author defines mabadi' as what one begins with before the intended subject, and muqaddimat as what the commencement depends upon, with the additions 'with expertise' and 'strong desire'. Note how these additions refine the definition.
The commentator explains that without these additions, many things that are not genuinely preparatory would be included.
2
Understand the essential accident condition
The author states that the predicate of a problem must be an essential accident to its subject, following Ibn Sina. Some commentators interpret 'essential accident' broadly to include anything consequent on the essence.
The condition aims to ensure predicates are intrinsically linked to subjects.
3
Analyze the critique
The master investigator objects that many predicates are general foreign accidents, e.g., 'forbidden' for intoxicants (fiqh), 'nominative' for subject (grammar), 'circular motion' for sphere (natural science). These are not essential accidents.
The examples show the condition fails in practice.
4
Evaluate the rebuttal and author's stand
The response is that predicates only need to be not more general than the subject of the science, and general predicates can be reduced to essential accidents by restrictions. The author adds that even this condition is not necessary because general predicates can be referred back via ambiguous concepts.
This shows the condition is not absolute.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the definitions of mabadi' (principles) and muqaddimat (premises) in the context of introducing a science. It begins by stating a possible definition: mabadi' are what one starts with before the main subject, while muqaddimat are what the commencement of the subject depends upon, provided that this dependence is 'with expertise and strong desire' — the author adds these conditions to exclude things that are not genuinely preparatory. The text then shifts to discuss a related logical condition: that the predicate of a problem (mas'ala) in a science must be an 'essential accident' (ardh dhati) of the subject. The author notes some commentators interpret 'essential accident' broadly to include any attribute that follows from the essence, whether directly or through something equal. This is in line with the position of Ibn Sina (the Shaykh). However, a master teacher objects that many predicates in actual sciences are 'general foreign accidents' (a'rad 'amma ghariba), such as 'every intoxicant is forbidden' (fiqh), 'every subject is nominative' (grammar), and 'every sphere moves circularly' (natural science). The defender responds that it is sufficient that the predicate be not more general than the subject of the science, and that such general predicates can be reduced to essential accidents via restrictions. The author (Yazdi) adds that this condition is not necessary either, because general predicates can be referred back to essential accidents through specialized qualifications or ambiguous concepts. Then a marginal note clarifies that the initial phrase 'wa qad yuqalu' refers to a different terminology for mabadi' used by Ibn al-Hajib in Usul al-Fiqh, where mabadi' are simply what one begins with. Another margin note explains that the author of the main text (the matn) added 'with expertise' to exclude cases where the so-called premise is not genuinely essential for starting, and 'strong desire' to exclude mere mentions that do not constitute dependence. The commentator argues that without these additions, the definition would be too broad. The overall passage demonstrates the careful precision required in defining logical and methodological terms.

The Four Educational Methods in Islamic Logic
Author enumerates the four educational methods and their sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four educational methods (division, analysis, definition, demonstration) and explain their role in teaching and learning Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The four methods teach you how to divide, analyze, define, and prove—the pillars of learning.

This passage introduces the eight educational methods, which are foundational for structuring knowledge in any Islamic science. While commentators have differed on their exact meaning, the author follows the authoritative view from Sharh al-Mutali' and lists four: division, analysis, definition, and demonstration. Understanding these methods will sharpen your ability to organize concepts, dismantle arguments, and produce certain proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term 'anḥāʿ taʿlīmiyya'
The word 'anḥāʿ' is plural of 'nahw', meaning path or method. The author specifies that these are teaching methods (taʿlīmiyya) used across sciences. Note that 'nahw' can have other meanings in grammar, but here it clearly refers to pedagogical ways.
2
Memorize the four methods in order
The author enumerates them as: (1) division (taqsīm), (2) analysis (taḥlīl), (3) definition (taḥdīd), (4) demonstration (burhān). Commit this sequence to memory, as it reflects the logical progression from breaking down to proving.
Some texts reorder or add methods, but this fourfold division is standard.
3
Distinguish between division and analysis
Division (taqsīm) means dividing a genus into species or a whole into parts; it is about classification. Analysis (taḥlīl) means resolving a compound into its simple constituents; it is about decomposition. Both break things down, but division works on categories while analysis works on composition.
Example: Dividing 'animal' into 'rational' and 'non-rational' is division; breaking a sentence into words is analysis.
4
Understand definition and demonstration as synthetic methods
Definition (taḥdīd) provides the precise boundaries of a concept using genus and differentia. Demonstration (burhān) builds certain knowledge through syllogisms. After division and analysis clarify the parts, definition fixes the concept, and proof establishes it with certainty.
The order is logical: you first divide and analyze, then define, then demonstrate.
5
Relate the methods to Islamic sciences
Identify how these methods appear in disciplines like fiqh, usul, kalam, and nahw. For instance, in usul al-fiqh, one divides rulings into wajib, mandub, etc., analyzes the components of qiyas, defines 'illa', and demonstrates a ruling through valid syllogisms.

The Two Aims of Logic: Definition and Demonstration
Author concludes by summarizing the two main tasks of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two aims of logic (definition and demonstration) and apply their methods to Islamic concepts and arguments.

Core Idea
Logic serves two purposes: defining essences correctly and proving truths with certainty.

In this concluding passage of his Hashiya, Mulla Abdullah al-Yazdi summarizes the two main objectives of logic: definition (tahdid) and demonstration (burhan). He explains how to formulate a proper definition by distinguishing essential from accidental attributes, and how to construct a demonstrative proof using necessary premises. This lesson unpacks his instructions so you can apply these logical tools in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aims
The author explicitly states that the parts of logic involve definition (tahdid) and demonstration (burhan). Definition is the act of formulating a hadd, while demonstration is the path to arriving at truth and action.
2
Understand the method of definition
To define a thing, first find all that is more general than it (genus and differentia). Then distinguish essential attributes (dhatiyat) from accidental ones (aradiyat) using the criterion: if the mere removal of an attribute necessitates the removal of the essence, it is essential.
Example: For wine (khamr), intoxication is essential because without it, wine is no longer wine.
3
Understand the method of demonstration
To reach certainty, you must use a proof that has a sound form (valid syllogism) and sound matter (true premises). The reliable premises are the six necessary premises (al-daruriyyat al-sitt) or what is derived from them. Avoid confusing these with dialectical, accepted, or ambiguous premises.
The six necessary premises include primary propositions, observations, experimental data, and concurrent reports.
4
Note the later practice
The author observes that later logicians treat definition separately under 'discussions of the definiendum' (mabahith al-mu'arraf), while other topics are placed under discussions of proof (hujja) and syllogism.
This shows that definition and demonstration are conceptually distinct but complementary.

Author presents first problem and resolutions.

Core Idea
The key to resolving regress is to recognize that the hadith's 'important matters' exclude the opener itself.

The hadith 'If something is important, begin with it' seems to require that even the act of beginning must itself be preceded, leading to infinite regress. Commentary
This passage deals with the well-known problems in the hadith about beginning important matters with basmala or hamd. The first problem is that if every important act must be preceded by either basmala or hamd, then basmala itself would need another basmala, leading to infinite regress or circularity. The second problem is that acting on one hadith seems to negate the other. Two initial answers are given: first, that all derivative existence ends at self-existent existence (like will in the Mu'tazilite view); second, that the general rule is specified by context. However, these only solve the first problem, not the second. A better solution redefines 'important matter' (dhū bāl) as what is intended in itself, not what is important in reality; since basmala and hamd are meant as tools for other actions, they are not themselves the 'important matters' in the hadith. For the second problem, the commentator introduces three types of 'beginning': true (unprecedented), relative (prior only to some), and customary (socially recognized). By applying these to the two hadiths, nine combinations arise, three of which are valid and used by scholars, resolving the apparent conflict.

The Referent of 'Hadha' in Book Introductions
Critical refinement of the referent of 'hadha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine why 'hadha' in a book's introduction can only refer to words or meanings, not to inscriptions or composites.

Core Idea
The demonstrative 'hadha' points to what is mentally present.

In this refined analysis, the commentators examine the phrase 'fahadha hadha'. They argue that the pointing is metaphorical and that the possible referents are limited to words and meanings because speech (kalam) is either verbal or mental.

Guided Path
1
Identify the function of 'fa' and 'hadha' in the phrase
Notice that 'fa' introduces 'hadha' as part of the phrase 'fahadha hadha' from the book's introduction. The 'fa' either follows an explicit or implicit 'amma'.
The phrase sets the stage for the content.
2
Recognize that 'hadha' cannot point to absent or written speech
Since no spoken words have occurred at that point, 'hadha' cannot refer to physically present speech. Instead, it points to mentally present content.
This is a figurative use of deixis.
3
List the seven possible referents
The text enumerates seven possibilities: specific meanings, specific words, specific written marks, composite of two (e.g., meanings and words), composite of three (meanings, words, marks).
Only two are ultimately valid.
4
Apply the definition of kalam to eliminate non-speech referents
Since kalam is defined as either verbal (lafzi) or mental (ma'nawi), written marks (nuqush) are excluded because they are not speech. Composites are also excluded because they either involve non-kalam elements or require using a single term for multiple meanings.
This leaves only meanings and words as valid referents.

Grammatical Note on 'Sawa' in the Book's Introduction
Grammatical refinement of the word 'sawa'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to correct the use of 'aw' (or) to 'waw' (and) when expressing equality.

Core Idea
Equality requires conjunction, not disjunction.

The commentator's note on the word 'sawa' (equal) reveals a subtle grammatical precision. He argues that when saying 'before or after is equal', the correct conjunction should be 'waw' (and) because equality applies to multiple items, not to one of them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word 'sawa' as a masdar meaning equality
Recognize that 'sawa' functions as a verbal noun meaning equality, used descriptively like other masdars.
It is derived from the root s-w-y.
2
Analyze the phrase 'whether before or after is equal'
The original phrasing uses 'aw' (or) to connect two options. The claim is that both options yield equality.
This is the construction being critiqued.
3
Note that 'aw' implies one of two, while equality requires both
The particle 'aw' indicates a choice between alternatives, but equality (taswiya) applies to multiple items simultaneously. Therefore, using 'aw' is semantically inconsistent.
Equality cannot be true of only one of the two.
4
Replace 'aw' with 'waw' and justify
The correct particle to express that both items are equal is 'waw' (and), which conjoins them as a group.
Thus, the phrase should read 'before and after is equal'.

Natural Priority vs. Honor-Based Priority in Mantiq
Author explains why tasdiq is presented before tasawwur despite tasawwur's natural priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between natural priority (al-taqaddum al-tabi'i) and honor-based priority (al-taqaddum al-sharafi) and explain why tasdiq is considered more honorable than tasawwur.

Core Idea
Honor can overturn natural order: tasdiq is nobler than tasawwur.

You may have noticed that in the study of logic, tasawwur (conception) is naturally prior to tasdiq (assent), since you must conceive something before you can affirm or deny it. Yet the author of our text places tasdiq first. Why? The commentator explains that the ordering here is based on honor, not nature. Understanding this distinction—natural priority vs. honor-based priority—is crucial for appreciating how Islamic scholars organized their texts. In this lesson, we will dissect the passage to see how the commentator justifies this arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of priority
The passage mentions taqaddum bi al-tab‘ (natural priority) and taqaddum bi al-sharaf (honor-based priority). Tasawwur is naturally prior to tasdiq, but the text orders tasdiq first due to honor. Recognize that these are different standards of ordering.
Natural priority is about logical or temporal sequence; honor-based priority is about nobility.
2
Recognize the two views on the relation between tasawwur and tasdiq
The commentator notes two positions: (1) tasawwur is a part (juz’) of tasdiq, as claimed by Imam al-Razi; (2) tasawwur is a condition (shart) for tasdiq, as held by the philosophers. In both cases, tasawwur is naturally prior.
This shows that regardless of the precise relationship, natural priority is accepted.
3
Understand why tasdiq's concept is existent and honorable
Tasdiq involves a positive judgment (e.g., affirming or denying), so its concept is considered wujūdī (existent). Tasawwur lacks judgment, so its concept is ʿadamī (non-existent). Existence confers honor, so tasdiq's concept is more honorable.
Honor is derived from the presence of judgment, which is a positive act of the mind.
4
Synthesize: why the text's ordering is by honor
Although tasawwur is naturally prior, the author chose to present tasdiq first because the goal of logic is valid assent, and the more honorable concept deserves precedence in textual arrangement. The commentator explicitly says: 'the consideration here is of the concept, and the concept of tasdiq is more honorable.'
This reflects pedagogical or rhetorical choice, not logical necessity.

Answering the Circularity and Regress Objection Against Logic
Author presents and answers the circularity/regress objection against learning logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the circularity objection against logic and explain the author's solution that logic is partly necessary.

Core Idea
Logic is not purely acquired; it has necessary foundations that prevent circularity.

A classic challenge to the study of logic argues that if logic itself is an acquired science, then it would require another logical law for its acquisition, leading to either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). Alternatively, if logic is entirely self-evident, then there is no need to learn it. This passage presents both horns and then provides a refined answer: logic is not entirely theoretical nor entirely necessary; it has both necessary and theoretical parts. The necessary parts, such as the first figure of the syllogism, are self-evident, and from them the theoretical parts are derived. This resolves the dilemma and shows why studying logic is still necessary.

1
Identify the two horns of the dilemma
The objection presents two possibilities: either logic is acquired (kasbi) or it is necessary (badihi). If acquired, it leads to circularity or regress; if necessary, no need to learn it. Note the logical structure of the objection.
2
Trace the logical necessity behind the regression
If logic is acquired, its acquisition requires another logical law. This law, if also acquired, requires another, leading either to a loop (dawr) or an infinite chain (tasalsul). Both are considered impossible.
3
Find the author's solution
The author breaks the dilemma by stating logic is partly necessary and partly theoretical. The necessary parts are self-evident (e.g., first figure), and the theoretical parts are derived from them, so there is a foundation that stops the regress.

The passage also presents a variant where the objection is that logic is entirely necessary, so no need to learn. The author responds that even if logic were entirely necessary, it would still be needed for acquiring theoretical sciences, but learning it would be redundant; yet the author's actual view is the mixed position.
5
Synthesize the compound resolution
The compound nature of logic (part necessary, part acquired) avoids both circularity and the conclusion that logic is unnecessary to learn. The necessary parts serve as a foundation for acquiring theoretical parts, making the study of logic beneficial.

Why Compound Terms Precede Simple Terms in Logic
The author explains why المركب is placed before المفرد in logical discussions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two reasons logicians present compound terms before simple terms, and relate this to the epistemological principle that privations are known through possessions.

Core Idea
Compound terms are known first because they are positive, and privations are only known through their possessions.

You may wonder why logic textbooks discuss compound terms before simple ones, even though simple terms are naturally prior. The commentator Yazdi provides a clear justification based on epistemological and conceptual priority. This lesson unpacks his two reasons.

Guided Path
1
Identify the natural order
Note that the simple is naturally prior to the compound. The passage acknowledges this but explains why logicians deviate from it.
Natural priority is based on composition: simple parts exist before compound wholes.
2
First reason: privation vs possession
The relationship between compound and simple is like possession (malaka) and privation ('adam). We only know privation after knowing possession.
For example, we understand blindness only after knowing sight.
3
Second reason: positive vs privative concepts
Compound has a positive concept (wujudi) signifying existence, simple has a privative concept ('adami). Existence is nobler.
This conceptual nobility gives compound priority in exposition.
4
Understand the epistemological override
Although simple is naturally prior, epistemological and conceptual priority justifies presenting compound first.
The division is based on the concept, so conventional order follows conceptual priority.

Why Logicians Use Variables ج and ب
Explains why logicians use the letters ج and ب as placeholders for subject and predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two benefits of using variables ج and ب instead of concrete terms in logical propositions, and rewrite a concrete proposition using variable notation.

Core Idea
Variables make logical rules universal, not tied to a single example.

In Islamic logic, you will often see the letters ج and ب replacing subject and predicate. This is not arbitrary—it is a deliberate method to ensure that the rules you learn apply to any content, not just the example used. In this lesson, we uncover the two reasons behind this practice, as explained by the logicians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two letters and their roles
ج represents the subject (الموضوع), and ب represents the predicate (المحمول). This is the standard notation in Islamic logic texts.
Some texts may use other letters, but ج and ب are the most common for subject and predicate respectively.
2
Understand the first benefit: brevity
The expression 'كل ج ب' is shorter than writing out a concrete example like 'كل إنسان حيوان' (Every human is an animal). Brevity makes it easier to focus on the logical structure.
3
Understand the second benefit: avoiding restriction
If logicians always used the same concrete example, students might think the rules only apply to that specific content. By using variables, it becomes clear that the rules hold for any subject and predicate.
This is the key reason—universality of logical principles.

Take an Islamic science statement like 'كل مسكر حرام' (Every intoxicant is forbidden). Rewrite it using variable notation: 'كل ج ب' where ج = مسكر and ب = حرام. Then you can apply logical operations to 'كل ج ب' without being misled.
This shows the power of abstraction.

The Method of Assumption (Ifrad) in Conversion Proofs
Author explains the method of assumption (ifrad) in proof of conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the method of assumption (ifrad) and distinguish its role as a preliminary for reductio ad absurdum in proving conversion rules.

Core Idea
Assumption provides a concrete instance for reductio; it is not an independent proof.

When proving conversion rules, logicians often use the method of assumption (ifrad) to give a concrete example that satisfies the conditions. This method is not a stand-alone proof but a preparatory step for reductio ad absurdum. In this lesson, we will analyze how assumption works and why it is needed to counter an opponent's objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the need for assumption
The opponent may ask: 'How do you know that some J is D?' The assumption provides a concrete instance to answer this challenge.
2
Specify the conditions for assumption
The assumed individual must be suitable to the subject, e.g., an individual or species of its genus, not something unrelated like assuming a man to be a stone.
3
Observe the proof structure
The proof of conversion uses the assumption plus a reductio argument. For example, to prove that 'Some J is not B insofar as J' converts to 'Some B is not J insofar as B', assume some J is D, and D is B, etc., then derive a contradiction if not.
4
Clarify the role of assumption
The assumption is not the proof itself; it merely supplies the material for reductio. The actual logical work is done by reductio ad absurdum (dali al-khalf), which may be explicit or implicit.

A) Because later authors have no purpose
B) Because the same purpose applies to all teachers of that science
C) Because only the first compiler's purpose is valid
D) Because they were lazy

Key Takeaway
The first compiler's purpose represents the universal aim of the science, so it is standard to attribute it to the founder.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
