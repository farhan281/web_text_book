The Implicit Fourfold Division of Knowledge
Commentary on the division of attributes between conception and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to infer the four categories of knowledge implied by the division of the attributes of necessity and acquisition between conception and assent.

Core Idea
Dividing two attributes between two types yields four implicit categories.

The commentator unpacks the phrase 'ويقتسمان' from the original text. He explains that the division of the attributes 'darura' (necessity) and 'iktisab' (acquisition) between tasawwur and tasdiq implicitly yields four distinct types of knowledge: necessary conception, necessary assent, acquired conception, and acquired assent. He highlights the rhetorical superiority of this implicit indication over explicit enumeration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two attributes being divided
The text mentions two attributes: al-darura (necessity), defined as obtaining without reflection, and al-iktisab (acquisition), defined as obtaining by reflection. These are the 'parts' being distributed.
These are epistemic categories describing how knowledge is acquired.
2
Recognize the recipients of the division
The attributes are divided between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). Each of these two types of knowledge can be characterized by either attribute.
The text explicitly says 'each of tasawwur and tasdiq takes from the two attributes'.
3
Infer the resulting four categories
By combining each attribute with each knowledge type, we get four categories: necessary conception, necessary assent, acquired conception, acquired assent. This is a logical combinatorial operation.
The commentator calls this a 'clear implication' (al-mulazama al-bayyina).
4
Understand the eloquence of implicit expression
The commentator notes that indicating the four categories implicitly is 'more eloquent and better than explicit mention' because it engages the reader's intellect and avoids redundancy.
This is a literary and pedagogical observation, not a logical one.

The Four Conditions of Murakkab
Author defines murakkab via four conditions and mufrad as its negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four conditions for a word to be compound (murakkab) and apply them to classify Arabic words as mufrad or murakkab.

Core Idea
Compoundness requires parts in word, meaning, and intention.

The distinction between single (mufrad) and compound (murakkab) words is a foundational concept in the study of linguistic convention (al-wad'). The author presents four precise conditions that must all be satisfied for a word to be considered compound. Mastering these conditions allows you to correctly classify words and understand how their parts contribute to meaning. This lesson will walk you through each condition and how to test them with examples from the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The passage defines compound (murakkab) in terms of four conditions. Note that single (mufrad) is defined as its negation: any word that does not meet all four conditions is mufrad.
This is a positive definition for murakkab, with mufrad being the residual category.
2
Extract the four conditions
List them: (1) the word itself has parts (lafẓahū juz'), (2) its meaning has parts (ma‘nāhu juz'), (3) a part of the word indicates a part of the meaning (juz' lafẓihī yadullu ‘alā juz' ma‘nāhu), (4) this indication is intended (hādhihi al-dalāla murāda).
Condition (4) emphasizes intention: the part's indication must be deliberately part of the word's coinage.
3
Test with examples
Apply the conditions to known words. For ‘Abdullāh, parts (‘Abd and Allāh) each indicate parts of the meaning (slave and God), and this is intended, so it is murakkab. For Zayd, the letters do not independently indicate parts of the meaning, so condition (3) fails, making it mufrad.
Remember that a word can have parts in form (letters) but not in meaning.
4
Analyze failure cases
If any condition is absent, the word is mufrad. For example, a word like malik (king) has letters m-l-k, but they do not each signify a part of 'king' (e.g., m does not mean 'rule'), so condition (3) fails.
Even if the word has a definite article, like al-malik, the article 'al' does indicate definiteness, so if intended, it could be considered murakkab. This is a point of nuance in the tradition.
5
Emphasize the role of intention
Condition (4) ensures that the part's indication is not accidental but intended by the word's creator. This prevents arbitrary interpretations.
For instance, the word ‘ilm (knowledge) has a root that may hint at other meanings, but the compoundness of ‘allāmah (very learned) depends on intended parts.

Commentary
This passage defines the distinction between single (mufrad) and compound (murakkab) words. The commentator explains that a word is compound if part of its verbal form indicates part of its meaning by deliberate intention; otherwise it is single. For a compound to exist, four conditions must be met: (1) the word itself has parts (e.g., 'Abdullah' has 'Abd' and 'Allah'), (2) the meaning also has parts, (3) the verbal part indicates the corresponding meaning part, and (4) this indication is intended. If any condition fails, the word is single. The passage then clarifies that a word is primarily placed for its whole meaning, not for its parts or necessary concomitants. However, when the word is used, sometimes the whole meaning is intended and the part or necessary is understood secondarily. In other cases, due to frequent usage, the word may be used to denote just a part or a necessary concomitant, in which case the conformity indication is not actual but is estimated: if the whole meaning had been intended, the word would have indicated it by conformity. This is similar to the earlier discussion of taqdir in the context of the three types of indication.

Indication and Taqdir in Mufrad and Murakkab
Author discusses the relation of conformity indication when a word is used for a part or necessary concomitant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how conformity indication is estimated (yuqaddar) when a word is used for a part or necessary concomitant of its meaning.

Core Idea
Even when a word stands for a part, the whole-meaning indication is assumed.

After establishing the conditions for compoundness, the passage delves into how words relate to their meanings through the three types of indication: conformity (muṭābaqa), inclusion (taḍammun), and necessity (iltizām). It specifically addresses a nuanced case: what happens when a word is used to denote only a part of its meaning or a necessary concomitant? The commentator explains that even then, the original conformity indication is not lost but is estimated (yuqaddar) – it is as if the word were still primarily indicating the whole. This lesson unpacks that logical move.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three types of indication
Remember that a word can indicate its whole meaning (muṭābaqa), a part (taḍammun), or a necessary concomitant (iltizām). The passage refers to these as the 'two derivative indications' arising from conformity.
If you are not familiar with these terms, review the previous lesson on the types of dalāla.
2
Identify the scenario
The passage describes two scenarios: (a) the word is uttered and its whole meaning is intended, but a part or necessary is also understood (this is straightforward). (b) The word is uttered but only a part or necessary is intended, due to fame (ishtihār). In case (b), the conformity indication is not manifest but is still assumed (yuqaddar).
This estimation (taqdīr) preserves the logical primacy of the whole meaning.
3
Understand the reasoning for taqdir
The justification is that if the whole meaning had been intended, the word would have indicated it primarily. Therefore, even when it is not intended, we still say that the conformity indication exists in potential.
This is similar to the concept of 'original intention' in legal interpretation.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the word 'Allāh'. It primarily indicates the divine essence. But in some contexts, it might be used to refer specifically to the attribute of mercy (e.g., in 'ism al-a‘ẓam'). Even then, we estimate that if the full entity were intended, 'Allāh' would indicate it by conformity.
This principle helps reconcile differing uses in the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Classification of Expressions: Compound and Simple
Author classifies expressions into compound and simple, then details compound types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic expression as simple or compound, and further categorize compound expressions as complete or incomplete, and as declarative, imperative, restrictive, or non-restrictive.

Core Idea
Every expression in logic is either simple or compound; compound expressions are either complete sentences or incomplete phrases.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational distinction in logic between simple and compound expressions. The author begins by dividing all expressions into these two categories, then elaborates on the types of compound expressions: complete (tam) which form a full sentence, and incomplete (naqis) which do not. Complete compounds are further divided into declarative (khabar) which can be true or false, and imperative/performative (insha') which cannot. Incomplete compounds are either restrictive (taqyidi) where the second part modifies the first, or non-restrictive (ghayr taqyidi). Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing any proposition or definition in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division
Notice the author first divides all expressions into simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). Simple means no part is intended to indicate a part of the meaning. Compound means it has parts.
The definition of simple is refined later in the passage.
2
Recognize the subdivision of compound
Compound is split into complete (tam) and incomplete (naqis). Complete allows silence (i.e., it forms a standalone sentence). Incomplete does not.
Examples: 'Zayd is standing' is complete; 'slave of Zayd' is incomplete.
3
Distinguish declarative and imperative
Complete compounds are either declarative (khabar) which can be true/false, or non-declarative (insha') like commands and questions which have no truth value.
The author explicitly mentions 'khabar' and 'insha''.
4
Differentiate restrictive and non-restrictive incomplete
Incomplete compounds are either restrictive (taqyidi) where the second part modifies the first, or non-restrictive (ghayr taqyidi) where it does not.
Example of restrictive: 'ghulam Zayd' (Zayd's slave); non-restrictive: 'fifteen'.

The Four Types of Simple Expressions
Author gives four types of simple expressions based on parts of word and meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four types of simple expression a given term belongs to, based on whether it has parts in word or meaning and whether those parts indicate the meaning.

Core Idea
Simple expressions are not all alike: they differ based on how their parts relate to their meaning.

While simple (mufrad) might seem straightforward—an expression with no compound structure—the author explains that there are four distinct types based on the relationship between the word's parts and the meaning's parts. Some simples have no parts at all (like the hamza), some have no parts in meaning (like the name 'Allah'), some have parts that do not indicate the meaning (like proper names), and some have parts that could indicate the meaning but that intention is dropped (like 'al-hayawan al-natiq' used as a name). This nuanced classification is crucial for understanding how terms signify in logic and for defining concepts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Understand the criterion: parts of word vs parts of meaning
The four types are defined by two axes: does the word have parts? do those parts indicate parts of the meaning? The author considers both aspects.
The negation in 'no part intended to indicate part of meaning' is the key.
2
Examine the first type: no parts in word
Example: the interrogative hamza (همزة الاستفهام). It is a single letter, indivisible in word. Since it has no word parts, it naturally has no part indicating meaning.
This type is the most straightforward.
3
Examine the second type: no parts in meaning
Example: the word 'Allah'. Though the word has letters, its meaning (the Necessary Existent) has no parts—it is simple in meaning. So word parts do not indicate meaning parts.
The word itself may have parts, but the meaning is unitary.
4
Examine the third type: parts exist but do not indicate meaning
Example: proper names like 'Zayd' or 'Abdullah'. Even if originally compound (like 'Abdullah'), when used as a name, the parts do not point to parts of the named person. The name is treated as a single unit.
The author notes that proper names are considered indivisible in their names and their named things.
5
Examine the fourth type: parts could indicate meaning but not intended
Example: 'al-hayawan al-natiq' (the rational animal) used as a proper name for a specific human. The parts 'hayawan' and 'natiq' can each indicate parts of the meaning (animality, rationality), but when used as a name, that indication is not intended; the entire phrase functions like a single label.
This shows that simplicity is about intention, not just structure.

Three Types of Mufrad: 'Alam, Mutawati, Mushakkak
Author defines the three types of mufrad with single meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between 'alam, mutawati, and mushakkak in Arabic logical texts.

Core Idea
Single-meaning words divide based on particularization and equality of application.

In Islamic logic, when a word has a single meaning, it falls into one of three types: proper noun, univocal, or equivocal. This distinction is crucial for understanding definition and argumentation. This lesson will teach you to recognize each type and its defining characteristics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
Examine the passage: meaning is one, then check if the word is particularized by wad' (then 'alam), otherwise check if individuals are equal (mutawati) or differ (mushakkak).
2
Apply the condition of particularization
A word is 'alam if its meaning is particularized by the act of wad' (conventional assignment), not merely by usage.
Demonstrative pronouns are not 'alam because they are universal in assignment.
3
Distinguish univocal from equivocal
For non-particularized words, compare whether the universal meaning applies equally to all individuals (mutawati) or with priority/intensity differences (mushakkak).
4
Use Islamic examples
Test with examples: 'Zaid' ('alam), 'insan' (mutawati), 'wujud' (mushakkak regarding God vs. creatures).

Classifying Words with Multiple Meanings: Mushtarak, Manqul, Haqiqah, and Majaz
Author classifies words with multiple meanings into shared, transferred, literal, and metaphorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any single word with multiple meanings into mushtarak, manqul, haqiqah, or majaz using the criteria of original assignment, fame, and abandonment.

Core Idea
Words with multiple meanings are classified by how they acquired those meanings and which is the original.

In this lesson, we explore how logicians and legal theorists classify single words that carry more than one meaning. The classification depends on whether the word was originally assigned to multiple meanings by separate conventions (mushtarak), whether it was transferred to a new meaning that supplanted the old (manqul), or whether it is used in both its original and metaphorical senses (haqiqah and majaz). Understanding these categories is essential for interpreting Islamic legal texts, Qur'anic language, and technical terminology in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the word has multiple meanings
First, determine whether the single word (mufrad) has more than one meaning in actual usage. If it does, proceed to classify it.
2
Check for separate initial assignments
If the word was originally assigned to each of its meanings by a separate conventional act, it is mushtarak (shared/homonym). Example: 'al-ayn' for the eye, gold, or essence.
Mushtarak words are rare but important in fiqh and tafsir.
3
If not mushtarak, check for transfer (naql)
If the word was not assigned to all its meanings initially, then see if it has become famous in a second meaning such that the first meaning is no longer used. If so, it is manqul (transferred). The word is attributed to a transferrer (naqil): the Lawgiver (manqul shar'i like 'salah'), custom ('urfi), or a discipline (like mantiqi).
Manqul words are common in Islamic technical terminology.
4
If not mushtarak or manqul, distinguish haqiqah and majaz
If the word is used in more than one meaning but neither case applies, then when used in the original assigned meaning it is haqiqah (literal), and when used in a non-original meaning it is majaz (metaphorical). Example: 'asad' for the lion (haqiqah) and for a brave man (majaz).
The distinction between haqiqah and majaz is crucial for interpreting metaphorical language in the Qur'an.
5
Practice with examples from Islamic sciences
Apply the classification to words like 'salat' (manqul shar'i from supplication to ritual prayer), 'zakat' (manqul shar'i from purity to obligatory charity), and 'wajib' (manqul 'urfi in fiqh from 'necessary' to 'obligatory').
Knowing the type helps avoid misinterpretation in legal and theological debates.

The Universal and the Particular: The Role of Abstraction
Author explains abstraction condition for universal/particular division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish universal from particular concepts by applying the condition of abstraction from accidental linguistic and conventional restrictions.

Core Idea
A concept's universality depends on the intellect, not on its grammatical form or convention.

In Islamic logic, the division of concepts into universal (kullī) and particular (juz'ī) is foundational. But this division is not simply read off the surface of words. The author of our passage insists that we must first strip away accidental features like dual/plural forms and conventional agreements that artificially restrict meaning. This lesson will walk you through how to correctly classify a concept by applying this abstraction condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept
Look at the linguistic expression and determine what concept it signifies (e.g., 'Zayd', 'human', 'the two Zayds').
The concept is what the intellect grasps from the word.
2
Check for accidental restrictions
Is the expression in dual or plural form? Is there a conventional agreement (tawāṭī') that fixes its reference to a specific individual or type?
Accidental restrictions are called 'ṭawāri'' – they are external to the essence of the concept.
3
Abstract from those restrictions
Mentally remove the accidental features. Ask: if there were no dual/plural or convention, could this concept apply to many individuals?
This abstraction is necessary to see the concept in its pure intellectual form.
4
Apply the intellect's test
Does the intellect permit the concept (after abstraction) to be true of many? If yes, it is universal; if only one, it is particular.
The intellect's permission (tajwīz al-‘aql) is the criterion.

The Four Logical Relations Between Universals
Author defines and exemplifies the four logical relations between universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four logical relations holds between any two given universals and translate each relation into the correct set of categorical propositions.

Core Idea
Two universals relate by one of four relations: total exclusion, equality, inclusion, or partial overlap.

In this passage, the author introduces the four fundamental ways two universals can relate. Understanding these relations is crucial for forming valid definitions and arguments, as they determine how concepts overlap or differ. We will explore each relation and see how it reduces to specific categorical proposition forms used in logical validation.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the universals share any individuals
Ask: Is there at least one individual that both universals apply to? If none, the relation is complete mutual exclusion (تباين كلى). If yes, proceed to the next step.
Example: human and stone share no individuals, so they are mutually exclusive.
2
Check for complete predication from either side
If they share individuals, examine whether one universal applies to every individual of the other. If predication is complete from both sides, the relation is equality (تساوى). If only from one side, it is absolute generality/specificity (عموم مطلق). If from neither side (partial overlap only), it is partial generality (عموم من وجه).
Equality example: human and rational (all humans are rational, all rational are human). Absolute generality: animal and human (all humans are animals, but not all animals are human). Partial generality: animal and white (some animals are white, some are not; some white things are not animals).
3
Reduce the relation to categorical propositions
For each relation, write the specific set of standard-form categorical propositions (universal affirmative A, particular affirmative I, universal negative E, particular negative O) that characterize it. Equality → two A propositions; mutual exclusion → two E propositions; absolute generality → one A (specific as subject, general as predicate) and one O (general as subject, specific as predicate); partial generality → one I and two O propositions.
These propositional forms are the building blocks for syllogistic reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (hashiya) on an earlier work, defining three types of 'ibtida'' (beginning) and then applying them to the two well-known hadiths about beginning actions with basmala and hamd. The author first defines: (1) Real beginning (ibtida' haqiqi): something that is prior and not itself preceded by anything. (2) Relational beginning (ibtida' idafi): something that is prior with respect to the intended goal, even if it is preceded in relation to something else. (3) Customary beginning (ibtida' 'urfi): what is conventionally considered a beginning, regardless of actual temporal precedence, extending from the start of the activity to the commencement of the main purpose. The distinction between the relational (in its more specific sense) and the customary is purely conventional. The author then explains that by applying these three types to the two hadiths (one about basmala, one about hamd), we get nine possible combinations of how the beginning is interpreted in each hadith. Three of these combinations are sound and considered valid (presumably because they involve a real beginning in both hadiths, or some other criterion). Three are sound but not considered valid, and three are unsound. The author discusses why the last two of the non-considered ones are invalid because they lack the two principles (that the beginning is real and that the type of beginning is consistent). For the first non-considered one, he notes it is obscure because it includes the second principle, and suggests it might be arbitrary unless one appeals to customary usage. Finally, he emphasizes that all this depends on the conventional order of basmala before hamd; if that order changed, the analysis would differ. This passage is typical of the hashiya genre, refining and expanding on points made in the original commentary.

Logical Relations in Idafa and Their Meanings
Classifies idafa meanings by logical relations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical relationship between two terms in an idafa and determine whether the idafa means ownership (lam), containment (fi), origin (min), or is impossible without interpretation.

Core Idea
The meaning of an idafa depends on the logical relationship between its two terms: identity, exclusion, generality, or source.

Arabic construct phrases (idafa) often convey ownership, but they can also indicate containment or origin depending on how the two terms relate logically. This lesson unpacks the classical classification of these relationships, using examples from the Qur'an and Islamic texts. Understanding these rules helps you interpret legal and theological phrases accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms of the idafa
Take the construct phrase and separate the first term (المضاف) from the second term (المضاف إليه). For example, 'salat al-masjid' has 'salat' as first and 'al-masjid' as second.
2
Determine which of the four relations applies
Check whether the two terms are identical (tasawi), mutually exclusive (tabayun), one is absolutely more general than the other (umum mutlaq), or they overlap partially (umum min wajh). Use the definitions given in the commentary.
Identity and exclusion make idafa impossible without a special interpretation.
3
Apply the meaning rule based on the relation and subcase
If the relation is container/contained (genitive contains the first term), idafa means 'fi' (in). If the genitive is the source, it means 'min' (from). Otherwise, it means 'lam' (ownership). Also check if the relation is one of the impossible ones requiring interpretation.
Examples: 'makr al-layl' (night's plot) = plot in the night (fi); 'thawb Zayd' = Zayd's garment (lam); 'shajar al-arak' = arak tree (lam).
4
Test your analysis on a new idafa
Choose a construct phrase from an Islamic text (e.g., 'kitab Allah' or 'hukm al-'alim') and walk through the steps to determine the meaning. Confirm your conclusion matches the context.

The Correct Meaning of 'Iqtisam' and the Refinement of Daruri and Kasbi
Commentator explains the meaning of 'iqtisam' and corrects a misinterpretation of the division of concepts and assents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the intransitive and transitive readings of 'iqtisam' in the division of knowledge, and explain why the definition of daruri was refined to exclude intuitive knowledge.

Core Idea
Direction matters: do concepts and assents divide necessity, or are they divided by it?

The hashya begins by addressing a common misunderstanding: the word 'iqtisam' could be read as intransitive ('to be divided') or transitive ('to divide'). The commentators insist on the transitive reading based on lexical evidence, which changes the logical structure of the division. Furthermore, they refine the definitions of daruri and kasbi to avoid a subtle error involving intuitive (hads) knowledge. In this lesson, we will unpack both moves and see how they protect the division from counterexamples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem with 'iqtisam'
The careless reader interprets 'iqtisam' as intransitive, meaning 'accepting division'. This would make tasawwur and tasdiq the objects of division by daruri and kasbi, rather than the agents.
2
Lexical evidence for transitivity
Mirza Muhammad Ali cites al-Zamakhshari's dictionary to show that 'iqtisam' is transitive (muta'addi). Shaykh al-Karmi gives examples of other ifti'al verbs that are transitive, such as ihtamala and irtaja'a.
This evidence supports the reading that tasawwur and tasdiq are the dividers.
3
Restructure the division
With the transitive reading, tasawwur and tasdiq actively divide darura and iktisab into their respective subcategories (badihi and kasbi). This is analogous to division of noun into definite and indefinite, where the divider (the act of dividing) yields two parts.
This avoids the absurdity that all tasawwurat are daruri and all tasdiqat nazari, or vice versa.
4
Refine the definition of daruri
The standard definition (daruri = does not depend on nazar and kasb) is problematic because intuitive knowledge (hads) would be wrongly classified as daruri. Abd al-Rahim redefines daruri as 'what is acquired without nazar', and kasbi as 'what is acquired with nazar', which excludes intuitive knowledge from being daruri.
This refinement preserves the mutually exclusive categories.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) that clarifies the meaning of 'iqtisam' (division) in the author's statement about tasawwur and tasdiq being divided into daruri and kasbi. Mirza Muhammad Ali begins by noting that the purpose is to repel a careless interpretation that 'iqtisam' is intransitive (lazim) meaning 'accepting division'. He argues that, based on al-Zamakhshari's lexicon, 'iqtisam' is transitive (muta'addi) meaning 'dividing'. Therefore, the correct interpretation is that tasawwur and tasdiq themselves divide darura and iktisab, not that they are divided by them. This means that each of tasawwur and tasdiq is divided into daruri and kasbi, not that all tasawwurat are daruri and all tasdiqat nazari. He analogizes this to divisions like noun into definite/indefinite—where the divider (the division) is a single concept that yields two parts. Then Shaykh Muhammad al-Karmi provides evidence that many ifti'al verbs are transitive by themselves, supporting the transitive reading. Then Abd al-Rahim redefines daruri and kasbi to avoid the problem that through intuition (hads) a nazari could become daruri. Finally, a third commentator (38) summarizes that the author's intended meaning is that tasawwur and tasdiq are the dividers, and the resulting divisions are the badihi (self-evident) and nazari (theoretical) types, which is the second premise for establishing the need for logic.

Why Properties Through Parts Remain Essential
Author defends the essentiality of properties mediated by parts or co-extensives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to defend why accidental properties occurring through a part or a co-extensive attribute are still classified as essential (dhati) rather than extraneous (gharib).

Core Idea
A part or co-extensive of the essence is not extrinsic to the subject.

The passage presents an objection: if essential properties are direct, how can properties like spatialization (through body) be direct? The author's defense hinges on the nature of parts and co-extensives. This lesson dives into that logical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find where the imaginary objector says: 'You defined essential as direct, but properties through part or co-extensive are not direct—they have an intermediary.' This challenges the consistency of the classification.
The objection is introduced with 'فإن قلت'.
2
Extract the author's response
The author replies that the part and co-extensive are not 'outside the subject necessarily' (ليسا خارجين عن الذات بالضرورة). They exist with the subject and perish with it.

Analyze the examples
Man is a body: body is his essence (ذاته). Speech (natiq) is an integral part of his essence. Wonder is an essential characteristic (خاصية ذاتية). These are not separate intermediaries—they are the subject itself or its inseparable concomitants.

Apply the reasoning to a new example

Defining Natural Non-Linguistic Indication
Author defines natural non-linguistic indication with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of natural non-linguistic indication and distinguish it from conventional and rational indication.

Core Idea
Natural indication is based on instinct and habit, not on convention or rational inference.

In logic and rhetoric, signs are divided into linguistic and non-linguistic. Within non-linguistic, some scholars only admit conventional and rational types. But the author argues that a third type—natural indication—exists. Let's examine what this means and how to recognize it.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish linguistic from non-linguistic indication
Linguistic indication uses words (e.g., 'kitab' means book). Non-linguistic uses other signs like gestures, sounds, or physical symptoms.
The author gives the sound 'akh' as a natural linguistic example—it is linguistic but natural, not conventional.
2
Identify the three types of non-linguistic indication
Conventional: based on agreement (e.g., writing). Rational: based on logical connection (e.g., smoke indicates fire). Natural: based on instinct or habit (e.g., redness indicates shame).
The author reports that some only accept conventional and rational for non-linguistic.
3
Recognize examples of natural non-linguistic indication
Rapid pulse for fever, redness for shame, paleness for fear, horse stomping for barley, dancing for pleasant music.
These are not learned but arise from nature or repeated association.
4
Differentiate natural from rational indication
Rational indication requires knowledge of a causal relation. Natural indication works even if the observer does not know the causal link—they infer by habit.
The author emphasizes that if a person does not know the rational connection, the natural indication still holds.

Defending Natural Indication Against Reduction to Rational
Author addresses objection and defends natural non-linguistic indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection that natural non-linguistic indication is merely rational, and explain the author's response that natural indication remains even when a rational relation exists.

Core Idea
Natural indication is not eliminated by the presence of a rational connection; both can coexist.

Some logicians argue that every non-linguistic sign is either conventional or rational. The author encounters the specific objection that signs like redness for shame are actually rational (as effect of cause). In this lesson, we trace how the author defends natural indication as a separate category, not reducible to rational.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing view
The author reports that Sharh al-Mutala' and al-Mutawwal only admit two types of non-linguistic indication: conventional and rational. They would deny natural non-linguistic indication.
This is reported to highlight the need for a defense.
2
Recognize the objection
One could object that examples like redness for shame are really rational indication: the redness is an effect of the internal state of shame, so it is rational (effect indicates cause). The same could be said for sounds like 'akh'.
The objector says: nature compels the sound in pain, so it's also a causal relation.
3
Understand the author's response
The author denies that nature is compelled only in sounds; even in physical symptoms like pulse, compulsion exists. He then offers a verification: if there is a rational entailment (e.g., disease causes specific sound), that yields rational indication, but it does not negate natural indication because a person ignorant of the causal link still infers by habit.
So natural indication is based on habitual association, not on knowing the rational relation.
4
Analyze the coexistence
The author asserts that natural indication exists even if rational indication also applies. The two are not mutually exclusive; they operate on different grounds.
This is a key nuance: one sign can have multiple indication types.

Refining the Division of Universal to Nouns Alone
Author refutes an objection about the division of universal into types failing to cover all parts of speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the validity of a logical division by checking whether its subdivisions actually apply to all members of the divided set, and to identify when a division must be restricted to a subset.

Core Idea
A logical division of words does not require every part of speech to exhibit every type.

You have learned that universals can be divided into types like proper noun, univocal, equivocal. But a critical objection arises: if the divided is the singular word (lafz mufrad), verbs and particles do not fit these types. This lesson walks through the objection, a common reply, and the true refinement: the division applies only to nouns because verbs and particles lack the property of being described by unity or multiplicity of meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the divided and proposed subdivisions

2
Understand the initial objection
The objection states that verbs (fi'l) and particles (harf) are individuals (afrad) of the divided, but none of the subdivisions apply to them. If the division is supposed to be exhaustive, this is a flaw.
3

A common reply says a division does not require each subdivision to appear in every type; it suffices that all subdivisions appear somewhere. The author concedes this logical point but rejects it here because, crucially, *none* of the subdivisions exist in verbs or particles. Thus, the enumeration fails because these categories are absent entirely.
4

The author's refined answer: verbs and particles cannot even be described by the properties required for these subdivisions (e.g., unity or multiplicity of meaning). Clarifying 'Unity of Meaning': Two Interpretations
Author clarifies the meaning of 'ittiḥād al-maʿnā' in the division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between actual unity and considered unity of meaning and explain their implications for classifying proper names.

Core Idea
'Unity of meaning' can mean actual oneness or considered oneness; the latter is necessary to avoid excluding shared proper names.

The commentator's phrase 'if they are unified in meaning' requires careful interpretation. Does it mean having one single meaning in reality, or being considered as one meaning? The author shows that a literal interpretation leads to excluding many proper names, while the considered interpretation preserves the classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
The author presents two possible meanings for 'unity of meaning': (1) actual unity: the word has a single meaning in reality, and (2) considered unity: the meaning is treated as one by consideration or observation.
2
Examine the first interpretation
If actual unity is required, then shared proper names (e.g., 'Zayd' used for multiple persons) would not be proper names because the word is shared among multiple individuals.
This leads to a contradiction: these are paradigmatic proper names.
3
Understand the second interpretation
Considered unity allows that a word may have multiple referents but be considered as having a single meaning in the sense of a common concept.
This saves shared proper names from being excluded.
4
Apply the principle
The author concludes that universality and particularity are attributes of meanings, not words themselves. The division depends on the meaning's qualification, which is determined by consideration.
This aligns with practice in usul al-fiqh.

Clear and non-clear each have two meanings, not one.

Intellectual vs. Inductive Exclusive Division
Author defines two types of exclusive division and their certainty levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between intellectual and inductive exclusive division and assess the certainty of each type.

Core Idea
Intellectual division is self-evident; inductive division is only probable.

In logic, we often claim that a certain classification is exhaustive. But not all such claims are equally certain. This passage from al-Yazdi's glosses introduces two types of exclusive division: one that the intellect recognizes immediately, and another that depends on investigation. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the strength of any claim of exhaustiveness in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of division
The passage defines 'intellectual exclusive division' (al-ḥaṣr al-ʿaqlī) and 'inductive exclusive division' (al-ḥaṣr al-istiqrāʾī). Focus on the key difference: the first is based on pure intellect, the second on empirical investigation.
2
Understand the epistemic basis of intellectual division
Intellectual division is such that the intellect judges it exhaustive merely by observing the concepts involved. For example, dividing a thing into existence and non-existence is immediately seen as covering all possibilities. According to al-Muḥaqqiq al-Sharīf, it is always self-evident (badīhī).
The author notes a nuance: some think intellectual division can be theoretical, but the cited authority limits it to self-evident.
3
Understand the epistemic basis of inductive division
Inductive division relies on investigation (tattabuʿ) and induction (istiqrāʾ). Its exhaustiveness is not certain but probable. The example given is the division of verbal indication into three types (e.g., obligation, prohibition, etc.). Another example is dividing a compound into its constituent parts.
The proof for an inductive division is a 'by-elimination' argument: if there were another division, it would have been found, but it wasn't. This is only probable.
4
Compare the certainty of each type
Intellectual division is certain without proof; inductive division yields only probable knowledge. When evaluating any claim of exhaustive division in Islamic sciences, first determine which type is being employed.

Learning Objective

This dual justification is common in Islamic scholarship, showing harmony between revelation and reason.
