Critique of Imprecise Phrasing on Conclusions
Commentator points out imprecision in the author's statement about the eight moods' conclusions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imprecision in a logician's statement about the conclusions of syllogistic moods and correct it by specifying the exact quantity and quality for each mood.

Core Idea
A logician's vague phrasing can undermine precision; every mood's conclusion must be exactly stated.

In this passage, the commentator al-Yazdi points out that the author's wording about the eight moods' conclusions is imprecise. The author stated that the moods produce a 'particular affirmative' if no premise is negative, otherwise a 'particular negative' – but this masks the fact that the third mood gives a universal negative. Yazdi's correction illustrates the importance of exact terminology in logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
Find the author's original statement: the eight moods produce a particular conclusion – affirmative if no premise is negative, otherwise negative.
2
Notice the apparent meaning
The phrasing suggests that particularity is constant across all moods; only the quality (affirmative/negative) changes.
3
Identify the logical issue
The claim implies all conclusions are particular, but the third mood actually yields a universal negative. This is a factual error.
4
Contrast with actual conclusions
First two moods: particular affirmative. Third mood: universal negative. Last five: particular negative. So quantity varies.
Refer to standard first-figure moods: Barbara (AAA), Celarent (EAE), Darii (AII), Ferio (EIO).
5
Evaluate the commentator's suggestion
Yazdi suggests placing 'affirmative' before 'particular' for clarity, but acknowledges the author's intention to treat all as particular in a broad sense.

Understanding Istiṭrād: Deliberate Digression in Logical Commentary
Definition and application of istiṭrād in logical commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'istiṭrād' terminologically and identify its use in a logical commentary passage.

Core Idea
Istiṭrād is a deliberate rhetorical digression, not a logical error—a tool for drawing unintended implications.

In classical Islamic logic commentaries, authors often use a technique called 'istiṭrād' to draw additional implications from a statement without intending them as the main point. This lesson unpacks the lexical origin, the terminological definition, and a concrete example from a hashya on logic. Understanding istiṭrād helps you read commentaries more carefully and appreciate how commentators expand the text.

Guided Path
1
Trace the lexical root
The term comes from horsemanship: a warrior pretends to flee to lure an opponent into a trap. This gives the sense of a strategic move that appears incidental but has a purpose.
The Arabic phrase 'استطرد الفارس لقرنه' illustrates this.
2
Identify the terminological definition
In logic, 'istiṭrād' is defined as 'arriving by mentioning the intended to mentioning what is not intended'—a shift from the main topic to a side topic.
The source says: 'في الاصطلاح هو ان يتوصل بذكر المقصود الى ذكر غير المقصود'.
3
Analyze the example from the commentary
The commentator's phrase 'with its meeting the minor actually' was meant to state a condition for the first and third figures, but it incidentally also implied that condition for four other moods. This is a case of istiṭrād.
The hashya author calls this 'indicative digression' (الإشارة الاستطرادية).
4
Distinguish from logical fallacy
Istiṭrād is not a mistake; it is a conscious rhetorical device. The commentator deliberately uses it to enrich the text, not to mislead.
The alternative definition of a hunter catching unintended game reinforces this deliberate yet incidental nature.

Rules of Wad' and Raf' in Conditional Syllogisms
A series of brief notes explaining key points in conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of wad' (positing) and raf' (denying) to both connected and disjoined conditional syllogisms, producing the correct conclusion.

Core Idea
Wad' and raf' yield opposite results in connected vs. disjoined conditionals.

These concise notes from the hashiya clarify how the operations of positing and denying function in conditional syllogisms. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly applying the istithna'i (exceptive) syllogism, which is foundational in Islamic logic for theological and juristic reasoning. The text distinguishes between connected and disjoined conditionals and specifies the outcomes of wad' and raf' in each case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditional types
Note (144) states that 'shartiyya' includes both muttasila (connected) and munfasila (disjoined). Recognize which type you are dealing with.
2
Distinguish the operations of wad' and raf'
Wad' (positing) means affirming a part; raf' (denying) means negating a part. These are the two operations in an istithna'i syllogism.
Notes (147) and (148) clarify these terms.
3
Apply rules for connected conditional
In a connected conditional (if A then B), wad' of A gives 'ayn of B; raf' of B gives naqiḍ of A. This is from note (145).
The rule is: wad' al-muqaddam yields 'ayn al-tali; raf' al-tali yields naqiḍ al-muqaddam.
4
Apply rules for disjoined conditional
In a disjoined conditional (either A or B, exclusive), wad' of A gives naqiḍ of B; raf' of A gives 'ayn of B. This is the reverse of the connected case.
Note (145) says: 'if it is disjoined, the same of the other is the contradictory and the contradictory of the other is the same.'
5
Check the condition on specification
Note (146) adds that these rules hold when the exception is not limited to a specific part (e.g., only the antecedent or only the consequent). If it is specified, the rules may differ.
This prevents misinterpretation.

Distinguishing Gharaḍ, Manfa‘a, Fā’ida, and Ghāya in Logic and Theology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between gharaḍ, manfa‘a, fā’ida, and ghāya as defined in the Islamic logical tradition, and explain how these distinctions underpin the Ash‘ari-Mu‘tazili debate on whether God acts for a purpose.

Core Idea
Purpose and benefit are not the same; they can even conflict. Whether God acts for a purpose divides Ash‘aris and Mu‘tazilis.

This passage from a super-commentary on a logic text carefully defines four closely related terms: gharaḍ (purpose), manfa‘a (benefit), fā’ida (utility), and ghāya (end). The commentator not only clarifies their linguistic and customary meanings but also shows how misinterpreting them leads to theological errors. Understanding these distinctions is essential for engaging with classical kalam arguments about divine actions.

Guided Path

Identify the four key terms and their definitions

Note how fā’ida linguistically means acquired knowledge or wealth, but customarily means the interest (maṣlaḥa) resulting from an action. When that interest is considered the end of the action, it is called ghāya.
The passage emphasizes that gharaḍ may or may not involve sequence (tarattub) – it can be unrealized if the agent is mistaken.

The commentator states that gharaḍ is that for the sake of which the agent acts, while manfa‘a is the benefit accruing from the action. They can diverge: a person may act with a purpose that actually brings harm, as in the case of erroneous belief. This shows that gharaḍ is more general than manfa‘a and fā’ida in one respect.

The Ash‘aris deny that God acts for a gharaḍ, arguing that doing so would imply God is deficient and seeks completion. The Ash‘ari position negates purposes but affirms benefits, which the commentator finds inconsistent.

The Mu‘tazila and Imāmiyya affirm that God's actions are purposeful (mu‘allala bi-l-aghraḍ). Without purposes, God would be acting frivolously ('abathan) and doing evil – which is impossible for God. They support this with Quranic verses (e.g., 23:115, 51:56, 38:27).
This position aligns with the second interpretation of gharaḍ as a desired benefit (consequent upon the action and intended by the agent).

This is a classic example of critical refinement (taḥqīq) in the commentary tradition.

Meaning of 'Yasluku bi al-Mantiq': Logic as a Guide for Internal Speech
Lexical explanation of the verb 'salaka' with the science of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the lexical meaning of the verb 'salaka' in the phrase 'yasluku bi al-mantiq' and state what it implies about logic's role.

Core Idea
Logic leads internal speech on the straight path and protects it from error.

The phrase 'yasluku bi al-mantiq' is central to the definition of logic in the Islamic tradition. Understanding the verb 'salaka' and its transitive usage helps us grasp that logic is not a passive description but an active guide for our rational thought. This lesson unpacks the lexical layers of this key expression.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verb 'salaka'
The verb 'salaka' has two main senses: intransitive 'salaka al-tariq' (he entered the path) and transitive 'salakahu ghayruhu' (he made him enter). In the phrase, it is transitive with the preposition 'bi', meaning 'to lead or cause to go'.
The form 'yasluku' is imperfect, indicating ongoing action.
2
Parse the phrase structure
The phrase 'yasluku bi al-mantiq' can be read as 'he goes by means of logic' or 'logic leads'. The commentator clarifies that the verb can be transitive by itself or with the preposition 'bi', here emphasizing instrumentality.
3
Connect to the definition of logic
Logic is that which leads internal speech (al-nutq al-batini) along the path of rectitude and correctness. This shows that logic is not about external utterances but about the inner rational process.
Internal speech is the locus of reasoning; logic safeguards it from error.
4
Appreciate the dual role
Logic both guides (takes internal speech along the straight path) and protects (guards against error). This dual function justifies its necessity in all Islamic sciences.
Without logic, internal reasoning may deviate from sound conclusions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Common beliefs can masquerade as necessary truths.

In this passage, Shaykh 'Abd al-Rahim warns that widely accepted opinions (shuhurat) can become so prevalent that they are mistaken for necessary knowledge (daruriyyat). He provides a crucial epistemological remedy: clearing the mind of extraneous matters. This lesson examines that remedy and its role in attaining certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the epistemological danger

The passage ends with a supplication asking for guidance to the truth. This shows that purification is not merely a rational exercise but also relies on God's help.
