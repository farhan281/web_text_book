Etymology of 'Khair' and 'Sharr'
Etymology of khair and sharr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the original form of 'khair' and 'sharr' and explain why the hamza is usually omitted.

Core Idea
The words 'khair' and 'sharr' originally had a hamza but are almost always used without it.

This brief linguistic note reveals that the common Arabic words for 'good' and 'evil' have a hidden original form with a hamza. Understanding this helps in analyzing Quranic readings and classical texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original forms
Note that the author states the original of 'al-khair' is 'akhyar' and of 'al-sharr' is 'asharr', both with a hamza at the beginning.
This is a standard linguistic observation known as 'al-takhfif' (lightening).
2
Find the examples given
The author provides three instances where the original form appears: 'Bilal khair al-nas', 'ibn al-akhyar', and a Quranic reading 'min al-kadhdhab al-asharr'.
The last example is from a variant qira'a.
3
Apply the rule to other words
Consider other Arabic words that undergo similar hamza omission, such as 'ab' (father) originally 'abw' or 'akh' (brother) originally 'akhw'.
Not all words follow this pattern; it is specific to certain morphological classes.

Commentary
This passage combines several editorial notes. The first note (5) is a grammatical observation on the word 'خير' (good), stating that its original form is 'أخير' with a hamza, but it is usually used without the hamza. Examples are given from Quranic readings. The second note (6) explains why the basmala and hamdala are considered 'the best of speech': because they mention the most noble existents, both celestial and terrestrial. The third note (7) quotes multiple hadiths about beginning actions with praise of God, remembrance of God, and the name of God, and defines 'abtar' (cut off). The fourth note (8) is a lengthy grammatical and hadith-based critique of the practice of omitting the preposition 'ala' (upon) when conjoining 'al' (family) with the Prophet (s). The author argues that the Kufan grammarians allow it but the Basrans require repeating the preposition. The commentator (al-muhashi) is criticized for omitting 'ala' in defiance of common practice, but the author defends the elite, citing many supplications that include 'ala' before 'al'. The note also discusses a hadith about separating between the Prophet and his family, suggesting it may be fabricated or misread. This is a critical refinement passage where the author corrects a prevalent misunderstanding and defends the correct grammatical practice.

Why Basmala and Hamdala are 'Best of Speech'
Justification for basmala/hamdala as best speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the basmala and hamdala are considered the best of speech based on the author's reasoning.

Core Idea
Basmala and hamdala are the best because they mention the most noble existents.

This short note justifies why beginning with the basmala and hamdala is recommended: they contain the mention of the highest beings, both spiritual and physical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author states that each of the basmala and hamdala is from 'khair al-kalam' (the best of speech).
2
Find the reason
The reason given is that they contain the mention of the most noble existents ('ashraf al-mawjudat'), both celestial and terrestrial, abstract and material.
This refers to God and perhaps the Prophet and angels mentioned in the phrases.
3
Reflect on the implication
If these phrases are the best, then starting any important matter with them is highly recommended, which connects to the hadith in note (7).
This is a theological and linguistic justification.

Hadiths on Beginning Actions with God's Name
Collection of hadiths on beginning with God's name

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the different wordings of the hadith about beginning actions and define the term 'abtar'.

Core Idea
Every important matter that does not begin with God's praise, remembrance, or name is cut off (abtar).

This note collects three versions of a well-known hadith stressing the importance of beginning actions with mention of God. It also explains the meaning of 'abtar'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three versions
The author lists three hadiths: one with 'bi-hamdillah' (praise of God), one with 'bi-dhikrillah' (remembrance of God), and one with 'bismillah' (name of God).
The third version is specifically for serious matters ('amr khatir dhi bal').
2
Define 'abtar'
The author quotes Ibn al-Athir who defines it as 'cut off' (aqta'), and notes the root 'b-t-r' means cutting.
This implies the action is without blessing or completion.
3
Connect to the example of Ziyad's sermon
The author mentions Ziyad's 'cut off' sermon, which was called 'batra' because it lacked mention of God and blessings on the Prophet.
This is a historical example illustrating the hadith's application.

The Particle of Preference: Unpacking 'Lāsiyyamā'
Commentator explains the pronunciation, origin, and semantic shift of 'lāsiyyamā'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'lāsiyyamā' (implying preference) and 'siyyamā' (implying equality) and correctly interpret their usage in Islamic texts.

Core Idea
A single lā transforms equality into preference: the power of a particle in logical definitions.

The term 'lāsiyyamā' (especially) frequently appears in Islamic scholarly works, from hadith commentaries to usul al-fiqh. Understanding its precise meaning is crucial for correctly grasping arguments that involve comparison and exception. This lesson unpacks its pronunciation, etymology, and the crucial semantic shift caused by the particle 'lā'.

Guided Path
1
Identify pronunciation variants
Note that 'siyyamā' can be pronounced with tashdid (double 'y') or takhfif (single 'y'). The commentator mentions both possibilities.
Tashdid is the more common form in classical texts.
2
Trace the etymology
The word originates from 'lā' + 'siyyamā'. 'Siiyamā' itself derives from 'siwā' (like/same). Thus, 'lāsiyyamā' literally means 'not like something else', implying preference.
The 'lā' here is not a negation of existence but a negation of equality.
3
Understand the semantic effect of 'lā'
Without 'lā', 'siyyamā' indicates similitude and equality. With 'lā', it breaks that equality and gives preference to the following item over the preceding.
Example: 'Charity is recommended in Ramadan, especially in the last ten days' means the recommendation is stronger in the last ten days.
4
Apply to real examples
Take a sentence like: 'The scholars praise the student, especially for his memorization.' The phrase 'especially' indicates the memorization is the prime reason. If you remove 'lā', it would mean the praise for the student equals praise for his memorization, which may not be intended.
This distinction matters when interpreting texts that weigh evidence or preferences.

The Correct Usage of 'Ghayr' After Negation
Author critiques a common phrase citing grammarians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct grammatical form for 'ghayr' after negation and assess the strength of the linguistic critique against 'la ghayr' in logical texts.

Core Idea
Precision in Arabic grammar safeguards logical arguments from ambiguity.

In logical texts, the phrase 'la ghayr' ('without other', 'only') appears frequently. However, grammarians like Ibn Hisham strongly condemn it as a solecism because 'ghayr' requires a preceding verbal negation 'laysa', not the particle 'la'. This lesson unpacks the grammatical rule and the debate surrounding its application in scholarly works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested phrase
Locate 'la ghayr' in the source passage. Note that it appears in the context 'al-sughra daruriyya la ghayr' (the minor was necessary without other). This phrase is used to mean 'only' or 'no other'.
The phrase is common in later logical works.
2
Understand the grammatical rule for 'ghayr'
According to Ibn Hisham, 'ghayr' is a noun that is permanently annexed (mudaf) in meaning. It can only be cut off verbally if its meaning is understood AND preceded by the word 'laysa'. 'La' is a negation particle, not a verb, and does not permit this deletion.
The rule is from Mughni al-Labib.
3
Examine the authorities pro and con
Ibn Hisham and al-Sirafi forbid 'la ghayr'. Al-Zamakhshari, al-Andalusi, Ibn al-Hajib, and Abu al-'Abbas report or use it without rejection. Ibn Malik disapproves but uses it. Note the variety of opinions.
This shows the rule is not universally accepted.
4
Evaluate the inconsistency of Ibn Hisham
Despite his strong denial, Ibn Hisham himself used 'la ghayr' in multiple places in Mughni al-Labib and other works. The commentator finds this amazing and uses it to qualify the absolute prohibition.
This suggests the critique may be a stylistic preference rather than a strict rule.

In this passage, the commentator delves into the meaning of 'al-mudawwana' (compiled sciences) and unpacks the etymology of 'diwan' (register).
