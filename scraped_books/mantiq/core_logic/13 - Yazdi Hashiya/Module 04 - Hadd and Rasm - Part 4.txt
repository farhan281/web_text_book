Core Idea
Compound property was accepted by early logicians but rejected by most later ones.

In Islamic logic, definitions can be composed of a single property (simple) or multiple properties (compound). However, not all scholars agreed on the validity of compound property. Degrees of Completeness in Definitions and Descriptions
Author argues that complete definitions require adding specific differentia or property to the proximate genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when to add property (khassa) or general accident (arad 'amm) to a definition (hadd) or description (rasm) to achieve maximal comprehension or distinction.

Core Idea
Definition completeness is a spectrum; add property to hadd and accident to rasm for maximal precision.

In this passage from a super-commentary on logic, the author refines the classical understanding of definitions (hadd) and descriptions (rasm). While standard definitions use proximate genus and differentia (for hadd) or proximate genus and property (for rasm), the author introduces the idea that completeness is a matter of degree. When seeking the fullest comprehension or distinction, one should incorporate additional elements: property into hadd and general accident into rasm. This insight, claimed as original, allows logicians to calibrate definitions according to their purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify standard components

The author says 'each of comprehension and distinction occurs in varying degrees' – this is the key insight: the goal of a definition (comprehend the essence or distinguish the thing) can be achieved at different levels of detail.

If you want the most complete (akmal) comprehension or distinction, add the property (khassa) to the hadd, and the general accident ('arad 'amm) to the rasm. This goes beyond the standard minimal definition.

The author gives an example: distinction by property alone is possible, yet they still join the proximate genus to it. This shows that even when property suffices for distinction, adding the genus makes it more complete.
The example illustrates the principle that adding components increases completeness.

The Equality Condition in Complete Definition (Hadd Tamm)
Author explains musawah condition in complete definition, distinguishing views of mutahirun and muhassilun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the views of the manifest (zahirun) and thorough (muhassilun) logicians on the condition of musawah in hadd tamm, and identify whether a given definition satisfies the equality condition.

Core Idea
Musawah (coextensiveness) is required only for complete definition, not for all definitions.

In this passage, the author clarifies an important point about definitions: not every definition needs to be coextensive with the definiendum. The condition of musawah (equality) applies specifically to the complete definition (hadd tamm). Two schools of logicians differ on what constitutes a complete definition, and understanding this distinction is key to evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups of logicians
Locate in the passage the two groups: al-zahirun (manifest logicians) and al-muhassilun (thorough logicians). They have different views on what makes a definition complete.
The term 'zahirun' refers to those who take the apparent meaning, while 'muhassilun' are those who seek thoroughness.
2
Extract their definitions of hadd tamm
According to al-zahirun, any definition by essential attributes is a complete definition. For al-muhassilun, it must include all essential attributes without exception.
The phrase 'والأفليس بنام' (possibly 'والأقل ليس بنام') indicates that a lesser definition is not complete.
3
Understand the role of musawah
The quote from Sharh al-Mutali' states that musawah (coextensiveness) is not a condition for every definition, but only for the complete definition. This means that incomplete definitions (e.g., those by a broader term) are permissible as long as they are not required to be coextensive.
Musawah requires the definiens to cover exactly the same individuals as the definiendum.
4

Consider the definition of 'salat' in fiqh: 'specific actions beginning with takbir and ending with taslim'. Does this include all essential attributes? Does it satisfy musawah? According to al-muhassilun, it would need to include all essential attributes to be a complete definition.

Critique of Circularity in Defining Broader by Narrower
Author refutes claim that defining narrower by accidental is invalid due to circularity, with two objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two objections against the claim that defining a broader term by a narrower term is circular, and evaluate whether the counter-objection succeeds.

Core Idea
Circularity in definition depends on term type (essential/accidental) and definition completeness.

This passage presents a dialectical critique of a common rule in definition theory: that defining a broader term (like 'animal') by a narrower term (like 'human') is invalid because it leads to circularity. The author refutes this with two objections, then considers a counter-objection. By working through this dialectic, you will deepen your understanding of the conditions under which definitions can be circular.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim being critiqued
Find the claim that some hold: definition by the essential narrower (e.g., defining 'animal' by 'human') is impermissible because it entails circularity. The claim is that knowledge of the definiendum depends on knowledge of the definiens, which itself depends on the definiendum.
The key term for circularity is al-dawr (الدور).
2
Extract the first objection (المعارضة الأولى)
The first objection states that knowledge of the narrower term (human) does not necessarily depend on knowledge of the broader term (animal) because one can know human via an incomplete definition (e.g., 'rational body') that does not mention animal.
This challenges the premise that knowledge of human depends on knowledge of animal.
3
Extract the second objection (المعارضة الثانية)
Even if the dependency were granted, the circularity argument only applies to defining the essential broader by the essential narrower, not to defining the accidental broader (like 'walking') by the essential narrower (like 'human').
The distinction between essential (ذاتي) and accidental (عرضي) terms is crucial.
4
Follow the counter-objection (ما قيل وفيه)
The counter-objection says that defining human by an incomplete definition (e.g., rational body) does not give conceptualization of the accidental broader (walking) because that accidental is not included. Only if the accidental broader is included would circularity arise.
This shows the challenge of defining accidental broader by essential narrower without circularity.
5
Assess the author's final position (فتأمل)
The author leaves the matter for reflection, indicating the complexity. The dialectic shows that the original claim is not straightforwardly false; it has conditions.
The final 'ta'ammul' invites the reader to think critically about the argument.

Commentary
This passage critiques the claim that defining the narrower by the accidental (i.e., defining a species by a broader accidental term) is invalid due to circularity. The claim is: if we define 'animal' (a broader essential) by 'human' (a narrower essential), then knowledge of animal depends on knowledge of human, which depends on animal (since human is defined as rational animal) — circular. The Purpose of Verbal Definition (Ta'rif Lafzi)
Author discusses verbal definition's purpose and contrasts it with real definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the purpose of verbal definition and distinguish it from real definition.

Core Idea
Verbal definition gives the meaning itself, not the word-meaning connection.

This lesson explores the nature of verbal definition according to the hashiya authors. Understanding what verbal definition aims to achieve is crucial for properly using definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definition
Recall that definitions are either verbal (lafzi) or real (haqiqi). Verbal definition explains the meaning of a word, often by synonym, without analyzing its essence.
2
Analyze the example of 'void'
In the passage, someone asks 'What is the void?' and is told 'It is an imaginary dimension.' This is a verbal definition. The questioner wants to conceptualize the meaning itself, not to know the word's etymology.
3
Determine the purpose of verbal definition
The passage emphasizes that the purpose is conceptualization of the meaning itself (bi-dhatihi), not conceptualization in that it is the meaning of a specific word.
4
Contrast with real definition
In real definition, the definiendum (e.g., 'human') is composed of parts known individually but unknown as a composite. Verbal definition does not analyze the essence; it simply points to the meaning known already.
The same expression can be real for one person and verbal for another based on prior knowledge.

Core Idea
The definition of qadiyya avoids circularity by distinguishing essential accidents from self-evident concepts.

In Islamic logic, the proposition (qadiyya) is defined as a statement that can be true or false. But critics charge that this definition is circular because truth and falsehood themselves are defined in terms of statements. This passage from the hashiya tradition presents powerful responses that save the definition. Let's walk through the objection and its solutions.

Author discusses linguistic meaning and metaphoricity of 'aks.

'Aks means returning; applying it to conversion is a metaphorical extension of the absolute to the restricted.

Two marginal notes clarifying 'particular' and 'universal' in the context of inference types.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Particularity is relative: a term may be universal in one context and particular in another.

In the division of proof, the terms 'particular' and 'universal' are not always absolute. The marginal notes you are about to read clarify that 'particular' can mean a real individual (like Zaid) or a relative universal (like 'animal' relative to 'body'). Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of particular
Read the first note: it mentions 'real particular' (juz'i haqiqi) and 'relational particular' (juz'i idafi). Real particulars cannot be predicated of many (e.g., 'Zaid'). Relational particulars are universals that are particular only relative to a higher universal (e.g., 'animal' is relational particular relative to 'body').

Apply the distinction to inference examples

The second note clarifies that 'universal' in 'subsumed under a universal' means a proximate universal (al-kulli al-qarib), the immediate genus. For instance, 'animal' is the proximate universal of 'man', but 'body' is a remote universal.
This ensures that the universal in induction or analogy is the one that directly contains the particulars.
4

Take the example: inferring from the states of man and cow to the state of horse. Is this induction or analogy? Since 'horse' is not the universal of man and cow, it is analogy (tamthil), not induction (istiqra'), because the inference is between two species sharing a common property.

Critiquing Circular Definitions of Induction and Analogy
Critiques common definitions of induction and analogy and refines them to avoid circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the circularity problem in defining induction and analogy as 'establishing a judgment' and apply this critique to similar definitions in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Defining a proof as an act of establishing is circular – the definiens confuses tool with use.

This passage from a super-commentary on logic refines the definitions of induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). The common definitions treat them as 'establishing a judgment' (ithbat hukm), but the commentator argues that induction and analogy are themselves types of proof (hujja), while 'establishing' is an act – not a proof. This is a subtle but serious logical error: a definition must not confuse the tool with its application. The commentator carefully dissects the wording to avoid this circularity, yet notes that some formulations still fall into the same trap.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions under critique
Locate the definitions of induction and analogy as they appear in the passage. The common definition says induction is 'establishing a universal judgment because it is affirmed in most particulars', and analogy is 'establishing a judgment in one particular because it is affirmed in another particular due to a shared meaning'.
These are the target definitions that the commentator finds flawed.
2
Understand the critique: why 'establishing' is not a proof
The commentator states that induction and analogy are themselves types of proof (hujja). The act of 'establishing' (ithbat) is an epistemic action, not a logical proof. Defining a proof by an action that is not a proof makes the definition circular or misplaced – it defines a thing by something that is not its genus.
This is a violation of the rule that the definiens must be prior and different in kind from the definiendum.
3
Examine the commentator's solution: the descriptive reading
The commentator suggests reading the author's phrase 'li-ithbat hukm kulli' (to establish a universal judgment) as a description (na't) rather than an annexation (idafa). This restricts the definition to universal judgments only, avoiding the inclusion of particular judgments. However, this does not fully resolve the circularity.
The descriptive reading is a linguistic clarification, not a logical fix.
4
Recognize the persistence of tolerance (musamaha)
Even after the refinement, the commentator notes that the author's own definition still contains tolerance (musamaha) because it defines the proof in terms of 'establishing'. Thus the author fled from one tolerance and fell into another – the definition remains circular at a deeper level.
The key lesson: definitions must avoid any term that presupposes the definiendum.

Core Idea
Burhan limmi explains why, burhan inni confirms that – knowing which you have determines the strength of your proof.

Not all demonstrations are equal. In logic, a proof can either give you the cause of the matter or merely confirm that it exists. His statement: 'That it is said: when you want to define something...' It is not hidden that this thing you want to know is either from the real essences existing in external objects or from conventional essences. If it is from the first, then distinguishing between its essential and accidental properties is extremely difficult because of the confusion of genus with universal accident and differentia with proper accident; so distinguishing between its definitions called real definitions and real descriptions is difficult. If it is from the second, then there is no difficulty, because everything that is inside it is essential to it—either genus if shared, or differentia if not—and everything outside it is an accident, either general or specific. So distinguishing between its definitions called nominal definitions and nominal descriptions is not difficult; so anyone can compose whichever part of the definer he wishes.

Commentary
This gloss treats the difficulty of defining real essences (like substances) versus conventional concepts (like terms of fiqh). For real essences, it is hard to tell whether a property is essential (like genus and differentia) or accidental (like common accident and proper accident). For conventional concepts, the definition is simply a matter of stipulation: everything we include is essential by definition, everything excluded is accidental. Therefore, nominal definitions are easy to construct, while real definitions require deep knowledge.
