Distinction Between Muqaddimat al-Ilm and Muqaddimat al-Kitab
Defines two types of introductions in a scientific work.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between muqaddimat al-ʿilm and muqaddimat al-kitāb and identify which type of introductory material belongs to each category.

Core Idea
The preface of a science is part of the science; the preface of a book is just explanatory.

When you open a logic book, the first few pages contain definitions and explanations. But not all introductory material is part of the science of logic. The author distinguishes two kinds: one that belongs to the science (muqaddimat al-ʿilm) and one that only explains terms (muqaddimat al-kitāb). Learning this distinction helps you focus on what is essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Find the Arabic terms muqaddimat al-ʿilm and muqaddimat al-kitāb in the passage. They are the two types of introductions being defined.
2
Extract the defining features
Muqaddimat al-ʿilm includes discussions integral to the art: definition, need, subject, etc. Muqaddimat al-kitāb includes explanations of technical terms and clarifications.
These features are opposite: one is essential, the other is explanatory.
3
Understand the dependency relationship
The art itself depends on muqaddimat al-ʿilm (it is part of the art). It only depends on muqaddimat al-kitāb for clarifying obscure words and terms.
This shows which introduction is indispensable for the science.
4
Apply the distinction to examples
Given a topic from a logic text, decide whether it is muqaddimat al-ʿilm (e.g., definition of logic) or muqaddimat al-kitāb (e.g., explanation of the word 'mantiq').

Why Logic Focuses on Error in Assents
Author explains focus on error in assents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why classical logicians primarily address error in tasdiq (assent) rather than tasawwur (concept), and identify the two types of mental content.

Core Idea
The main concern of logic is judgment because conceptual errors are less obvious.

This passage from the introduction of a logic text explains a key methodological choice: why the author limits the discussion of error to assents (tasdiq) rather than concepts (tasawwur). Understanding this choice helps you grasp the scope and purpose of logic in the Islamic tradition. The passage also references a famous gloss by al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif and suggests an analogy that the reader is expected to draw.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of mental content
Recognize that the passage distinguishes between tasawwur (concept, grasping a meaning without judgment) and tasdiq (assent, judgment that something is true or false). These are the two fundamental operations of the mind in Islamic logic.
Tasdiq is also called 'belief' in some contexts.
2
Locate the reason for excluding concepts
The phrase 'لعدم ظهور ذلك في التصورات' explicitly states that error is not apparent in concepts. This is the core justification: conceptual errors are hidden or not easily detected, unlike errors in judgment.

The author attributes this explanation to al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif (al-Jurjani) in his gloss on the commentary of the Risala. This shows the chain of commentary: base text → commentary → gloss → this hashia.
Al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif is a major figure in later Islamic logic.

Consider the alternative explanation
The author also suggests that the limitation may rely on the reader's ability to analogize from tasdiq to tasawwur. This implies that the method for detecting conceptual errors is similar to that for judgments, so it is left to the reader's understanding.
This reflects the pedagogical style of relying on the student's reasoning.

The Three Types of Signification and Their Role in Definitions
The commentator explains why discussions of signification types are needed for defining logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which type of signification (implicative, inclusion, correspondence) is used or avoided in a given definition from fiqh or usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Correspondence signification is the only type fully acceptable in complete definitions.

The commentator explains that logicians, when constructing definitions, carefully select which type of signification to use. Implicative signification is avoided entirely or partially, inclusion signification is avoided completely, but correspondence signification is always relied upon. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the precision of definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of signification
In the Arabic, find the terms الدلالة الالتزامية, التضمنية, and المطابقية. Note their definitions: implicative (something implied like ceiling→wall), inclusion (part of meaning like house→room), correspondence (entire meaning like human→rational animal).
These are stable across logic manuals.
2
Understand their status in definitions
The text says implicative is مهجورة كلاً أو بعضاً (abandoned fully or partially), inclusion is مهجورة كلا لا بعضاً (abandoned fully, not partially), and correspondence is معتبرة فيها كلا و بعضاً (considered fully and partially). This means only correspondence is fully allowed; the others are restricted.
This rule ensures definitions are precise and not misleading.
3
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider the definition of 'salah' in fiqh: 'specific words and actions beginning with takbir and ending with taslim'. This definition uses correspondence because it includes the full essence. If one defined it as 'actions that prevent immorality' (implicative), that would be incomplete because it only implies the purpose, not the essence.
Fiqh definitions often rely on correspondence to avoid ambiguity.

The Scope of Logic: Meanings as Tools for Tasawwur and Tasdiq
The commentator defines the specific perspective of logic: studying meanings for tasawwur and tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why logic studies meanings only from the perspective of conceptualization and assent, not as physical entities or metaphysical categories.

Core Idea
Logic examines meanings solely as they serve tasawwur and tasdiq.

The commentator clarifies that when logic discusses meanings, it does not consider them as existent objects, sounds, accidents, or metaphysical kinds. Instead, the focus is exclusively on how meanings relate to conceptualization (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). This demarcation is essential to understand the scope of logic as a tool for knowledge.

Other sciences study these aspects; logic has its own specific viewpoint.

The commentator says البحث عنها في كتب المنطق انما هو من هذه الحيثية (the investigation in logic books is only from this aspect). Earlier, 'this aspect' refers to meanings from the perspective of tasawwur and tasdiq. So logic studies meanings as they are used in defining and reasoning.
This aligns with the definition of logic as the tool for acquiring knowledge.

The passage ends by defining العلم as العلم الحصولى المنقسم الى التصور والتصديق (acquired knowledge divided into conceptualization and assent). Thus, logic's subject is meanings in relation to these two types of knowledge.
Tasawwur is concept formation without judgment; tasdiq is judgment (true/false).

This passage addresses an important methodological question: if logical rules are supposed to be universal, why does the discussion of contradiction focus only on propositions? Placement of the Four Educational Methods in Logic
Commentators discuss whether the four educational methods belong in introductions or goals of the art.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify where the four educational methods (division, analysis, demonstration, and definition) are placed in a classical logic text and explain why later logicians treated definition separately.

Core Idea
The four educational methods belong in the goals, not introductions; definition is placed in the definiendum section.

When you open a classical logic textbook, you'll find some topics introduced early as preliminaries and others reserved for the main goals of the science. The four educational methods—division, analysis, demonstration, and definition—are so central to learning that later logicians placed them in the goals section rather than the introductions. But they didn't all go to the same place: division, analysis, and demonstration are discussed under proof, while definition has its own section on the definiendum. This lesson examines the rationale behind this arrangement and what it tells us about the structure of logical education.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four educational methods
From the passage, list the four methods: division (taqsim), analysis (tahlil), demonstration (burhan), and definition (tahdid). Note that these are the main modes of teaching and procuring knowledge in logic.
Some texts use different terms; here these four are standard.
2
Distinguish introductions from goals
Understand that 'introductions' (muqaddimat) refer to preliminary discussions necessary before entering the core of the science, while 'goals' (maqasid) are the main objectives of the art. The passage argues that the eight matters (including these methods) are split: seven belong to introductions, but the eighth (the four methods) belongs to goals.
The eight matters likely refer to standard topics; the passage singles out the four methods.
3
Locate the placement of each method
According to the second gloss, the later logicians place division, analysis, and demonstration in the discussions of proof (mabahith al-hujja). Definition, however, is placed first in the principles (mabadi') and then in the discussions of the definiendum (mabahith al-mu'arraf).
This shows that definition is treated separately because it is foundational to understanding the things being defined.
4
Analyze the commentator's critique
Mirza Muhammad Ali notes that the author's phrasing might imply that later logicians did not mention definition in the definiendum section, but in fact they did. The reader is urged to reflect (tadammul) on this to understand the true arrangement.
This highlights the importance of precise wording and the need to check actual practice against textual claims.

Navigating a Fihrist in Islamic Logic
Table of contents for the hashiya on tasdiqat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret a fihrist (table of contents) from a classical Islamic logic text to identify the sequence of topics and locate specific sections by page number.

Core Idea
A fihrist is the map that reveals the structure and scope of a scholarly work.

Before diving into the dense arguments of a logic super-commentary, you need to know its landscape. This fihrist from the Hashiya on Tasdiqat lists the major topics covered in the work, from propositions and conditionals to syllogism and induction. By learning to parse such a fihrist, you gain the ability to quickly orient yourself within a text and retrieve the relevant passages for your study. This skill is essential for any serious engagement with the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its scope
First, note that this fihrist is for 'Hawashi al-Tasdiqat' – footnotes on the Tasdiqat (assent) section of a logic text. The term 'tasdiqat' tells you the work deals with judgments and syllogisms, not just concepts. This sets your expectation for the content.
2
List the main topics in order
Scan the fihrist and write down the topics as they appear: aqsam al-qadiyya (sections of the proposition), aqsam al-shartiyya (conditionals), tanaqud (contradiction), 'aks mustawi (simple conversion), 'aks al-naqid (conversion by contrapositive), qiyas (syllogism), istiqra' wa tamthil (induction and analogy), aqsam al-qiyas (types of syllogism), ajza' al-'ulum (parts of the sciences). This sequence mirrors the typical order in post-Avicennan logic manuals.
The order is logical: beginning with basic units (propositions), then relations (contradiction, conversion), then complex reasoning (syllogism, induction).
3
Read the page numbers correctly
The page numbers are given in Arabic-Indic numerals (e.g., ٢٦٣ = 263). Match each topic to its page. For example, 'حواشي العكس المستوى' (simple conversion) is on page 335. Use this to navigate the text quickly.
Be careful of OCR errors: sometimes numbers may look similar (e.g., ٣ = 3, ٥ = 5). Verify against the original if possible.
4
Distinguish content from publisher information
The last few lines after the page numbers are not part of the fihrist but publisher or institutional marks (e.g., 'مرکز تحقیقات کامپیوتری علوم اسلامی'). Recognize these so you don't confuse them with the topics.
The Arabic phrase 'جمعداری اموال' is likely a publisher name, possibly corrupted. Such extraneous lines are common in printed editions.
