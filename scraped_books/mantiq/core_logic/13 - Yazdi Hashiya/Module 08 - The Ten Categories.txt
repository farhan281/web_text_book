The Four Relations between Two Universals
Defines the four logical relations between universals and their propositional forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four logical relations holds between any two universals and correctly reduce that relation to its standard propositional forms.

Core Idea
Every pair of universals falls into one of four relations, each with a distinct pattern of truth across individuals.

In this passage, the author lays out the foundational classification of relations between universals. These four relations are the building blocks for all categorical propositions and syllogisms. Mastering them will allow you to quickly analyse any pair of terms in Islamic logic, whether in Kalam, Fiqh, or Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Map the classification tree
Trace the author's decision tree: first, do any individuals of one universal belong to the other? If none, they are متباينان (divergent). If some, then check for total truth from either side. If no total truth from any side, they are أعم وأخص من وجه (generality from one aspect). If total truth from both sides, they are متساويان (equal). If from one side only, they are أعم وأخص مطلقا (absolute generality).
Draw the tree to visualize the four outcomes.
2
Identify the propositional reduction for each relation
For each relation, the author gives a set of categorical propositions that express it. Write them down in Arabic and English: equality → two universal affirmatives; divergence → two universal negatives; absolute generality → universal affirmative + particular negative; generality from one aspect → particular affirmative + two particular negatives.
Memorize these reductions; they are used in immediate inference.
3
Understand the commentary on contradictories
The commentary clarifies that the relation between the contradictories of two equal universals is also equality. The author notes that this is not a new logical point but a pedagogical aid for students. It helps them determine relations by looking at the contradictories instead of the originals.
Consider why the commentator felt the need to explain this.
4
Test with your own examples from Islamic sciences
Pick two universals from Kalam or Fiqh, e.g., 'mumkin' (possible) and 'mawjud' (existent), or 'wajib' (obligatory) and 'mubah' (permissible). Determine their relation and write the corresponding propositional forms.
Verify your answer by checking the individuals.

Commentary
This passage defines the four relations between two universals: total divergence (تباين), equality (تساوي), absolute generality and specificity (عموم مطلق), and generality from one aspect (عموم من وجه). It begins by stating that every pair of universals must fall into one of these four. The classification is based on whether any individuals of one apply to individuals of the other. If none apply, they are divergent (e.g., man and stone). Otherwise, we check for total truth from either side. If there is no total truth from any side, they are general and specific from one aspect (e.g., animal and white: some animals are white, some not; some white things are not animals). If there is total truth from both sides, they are equal (e.g., man and rational). If from one side only, they are absolutely general and specific (e.g., animal and man: every man is animal, but not every animal is man). The author then reduces each relation to its standard propositional forms: equality → two universal affirmatives; divergence → two universal negatives; absolute generality → universal affirmative (subject = specific) and particular negative (subject = general); generality from one aspect → one particular affirmative and two particular negatives. The commentary then discusses a subtle point: the statement 'and their contradictories are likewise' means that the contradictories of two equal universals are also equal. This is because the relation of equality is symmetric between the universals themselves and their contradictories. The passage argues that it is unnecessary to separately state the relation of the contradictories, since it is the same as the relation of the originals. However, some commentators suggest it is mentioned for pedagogical ease, so students can determine relations by looking at the contradictories.

Necessary vs Separable Accidents (Lazim vs Mufariq)
Author classifies accidents into necessary and separable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a necessary accident (lazim) and a separable accident (mufariq) and identify examples from kalam and physics.

Core Idea
An accident is either inseparable from its subject or can be separated.

In Islamic logic, every accident (arad) whether essential or accidental, is classified based on its relationship to the subject. This classification is foundational for understanding definitions and arguments. In this lesson, we examine the primary division into lazim and mufariq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion for necessary accident
The author states that if it is impossible for the subject to exist without the accident, it is lazim. Recognize that this is a definition of necessity.
2
Apply the criterion to examples
For a given accident-subject pair, ask: can the subject exist without this accident? If not, it's lazim; if yes, it's mufariq.
3
Distinguish subtypes of mufariq
The author notes that mufariq can persist, decay quickly, or slowly. Note the examples: motion of sphere (persists), redness of shyness (quick), youth (slow).
The author also mentions that some restrict mufariq to only quickly/slowly changing.

Commentary
This passage is a classificatory and definitional commentary on the kinds of accidents. It first distinguishes between necessary (lazim) and separable (mufariq) accidents. If it is impossible for the subject to exist without the accident, it is necessary; otherwise, it is separable (and may persist or decay fast/slow). The hashiya then subdivides necessary accidents into three types based on the perspective: (1) by essence (lazim al-mahiyya) — inseparable from the quiddity regardless of existence, e.g., evenness for four; (2) by external existence (lazim al-wujud al-khariji) — required only in actual external existence, e.g., burning for fire; (3) by mental existence (lazim al-wujud al-dhihni) — required only in the mind, e.g., the universality of 'man'. The third type is called 'secondary intelligible'. Next, the passage discusses the division of necessary into bayyin (clear) and ghayr bayyin (non-clear). Bayyin has two senses: specific (al-akhass) — where the accident's concept directly implies the subject's concept (like blindness from blind); and general (al-a'amm) — where certainty of implication arises only after also considering the relation (like evenness as necessary for four). The examples are drawn from logic and physics (motion of the sphere, redness of shyness, youth). Finally, a note notes a differing opinion limiting separable accidents to fast and slow changes.

The Three Types of Necessary Accidents
Author subdivides necessary accidents into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between necessary by essence, by external existence, and by mental existence, and classify examples accordingly.

Core Idea
A necessary accident can be required by the essence itself, by external existence, or by mental existence.

In the previous lesson we distinguished necessary from separable accidents. Now we delve deeper into the necessary itself, discovering that it has three distinct types based on the ontological perspective: essence alone, external existence, or mental existence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basis of division
The division is based on the perspective: by essence (lazim al-mahiyya), by external existence (lazim al-wujud al-khariji), or by mental existence (lazim al-wujud al-dhihni).
2
For each type, identify the scope
Lazim al-mahiyya applies regardless of existence (even in mind). Lazim al-wujud al-khariji applies only in external existence. Lazim al-wujud al-dhihni applies only in mental existence.
3
Classify examples
Use the given examples: evenness of four (essence), burning of fire (external), universality of man (mental). Note that the third type is called 'secondary intelligible'.

Rational Division of Compound Predicates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the six types of compound predicates by systematically combining subject and essential accident at generic and specific levels.

Core Idea
Rational division reveals six compound predicates where the eye sees only two.

The commentator on this passage mentions only two examples of compound predicates: subject with essential accident, and type of subject with essential accident. However, the marginalist argues that a complete rational division (tashqīq 'aqlī) yields six distinct types. This is not mere pedantry; it reflects the precision required to analyze propositions in any science. In this lesson, we will walk through the method of rational division to derive all six types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two fundamental components
The components are the subject (al-mawḍū‘) and the essential accident (al-‘araḍ al-dhātī). Each can be taken in its generic sense or as a specific type (naw‘). So we have four basic forms: subject, type of subject, essential accident, type of essential accident.
These correspond to the first two columns in a 2x2 matrix.
2
Combine the generic subject with each form of accident
Take the subject (generic) and combine it with: (a) the generic accident → type (1), (b) the type of accident → type (2). Also combine the subject with its own type → type (3). That gives three types from the subject side.
The marginalist says 'three from the combination of the subject with the last three' – referring to the three forms other than the subject itself.
3
Combine the type of subject with each form of accident
Now take the type of subject and combine it with: (a) the generic accident → type (4), (b) the type of accident → type (5). That gives two types from the type-of-subject side.
The marginalist says 'two from the combination of the type of the subject with the last two' – the remaining two forms after removing the subject and its type.
4
Combine the generic accident with its own type
Finally, combine the generic essential accident with its own type → type (6). This is the one combination of the accidental side with itself.
The marginalist says 'one from their combination' – i.e., from the two accident forms combined.
5
Verify the six types against examples
The marginalist provides examples for each type: (1) subject+accident: 'magnitude that is a mean in proportion'; (2) subject+type of accident: 'every word raised by a verb is the subject'; (3) subject+its own type: 'every speech devoid of waw...'; (4) type of subject+accident: 'every line standing on another line...'; (5) type of subject+type of accident: 'every noun in the genitive...'; (6) accident+its type: 'i‘rab, if it is raf‘...'.

Composition of the Proposition: Ancient vs. Later Views
Contrast of ancient and later views on proposition composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the ancient and later views on the parts of the proposition and explain the compromise that reconciles them.

Core Idea
The proposition's parts depend on whether judgment is a separate component or a mode of perceiving the relation.

This passage presents a fundamental debate in Islamic logic: how many parts constitute a proposition (qadiyya)? The ancients said three: subject, predicate, and the perfect predicative relation. The later scholars added a fourth: the occurrence or non-occurrence of the relation. The debate turns on whether judgment (the perception of occurrence) is a distinct cognitive act or just a mode of the same perception. A reconciliation is offered by distinguishing the relation as perceived without assent (conceptual) versus with assent (judgment). This lesson unpacks the arguments and shows how the commentator resolves them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ancient view
The ancient philosophers held the proposition has three parts: the subject (mahkum 'alayh), the predicate (mahkum bih), and the perfect predicative relation (al-nisba al-tamma al-khabariyya). Judgment is the perception of occurrence or non-occurrence of that relation.
The relation is considered 'perfect' because it completes the proposition.
2
Identify the later view
The later scholars added a fourth part: the restrictive relation and the occurrence or non-occurrence of that relation. They argued that in doubt, one can conceive the relation without any judgment, so the perception of occurrence is a separate fourth component.
This view is based on examples like doubting whether Zayd is standing.
3
Understand the objection to the later view
An objector argues that the perception in doubt is the same as in judgment—it only differs in mode (non-assentive vs. assentive). Thus the later view does not necessitate a fourth part; the difference is in the intensity, not in the number of perceived items.
The objection relies on the idea that the same relation can be perceived with or without assent.
4
Follow the reconciliation
Some later scholars reconcile by noting that the judgmental relation can be considered in two respects: as perceived without assent (conceptual, called the 'judgmental relation') or with assent (assented, called 'judgment'). The philosopher's school (madhhab al-hakim) holds that both are valid perspectives.
Thus the number of parts depends on the perspective (iʿtibār). The commentator (muhaqqiq) bases his position on this dual consideration.
5
Apply to the commentator's position
The commentator (al-muhaqqiq al-Yazdi) argues that the author (musannif) followed the ancients in both stations: making assent identical to judgment, and making the object of assent the perfect predicative relation, not occurrence. This entails a three-part proposition.
The passage ends by explaining why three parts follow if the relation is the final part.

(73) His statement: The subject of a science is that in which (i.e., in that science) is investigated the essential accidents of the mentioned subject. And the accident is of two types: essential (dhati), which is what occurs to the essence directly without an intermediary between the accidens and the subject in reality, even if knowledge of this occurrence requires proof and recognition; and accidental (gharib), which is what occurs to the essence by the mediation of something else in reality. It is called gharib because it is alien to the essence, and what connects it to the essence is an intermediary between it and the essence. It is said that the total of essential and accidental accidents is five: 1) what occurs primarily and essentially, like wonder occurring to man; 2) what occurs by mediation of a part of the subject, whether that part is more general than the subject, like occupation of space occurring to man because he is a body, or equal to it, like speech occurring to man because he is rational; 3) what occurs by mediation of a coextensive notion, like having extremities occurring to body because it is finite, and like laughter occurring to man because he is wondering. These are essential. Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of accidents (a'rad) into essential (dhati) and accidental (gharib). It begins by defining these terms: an essential accident occurs directly to the essence without an intermediary in reality, while an accidental accident occurs through an intermediary. The commentator then presents a list of five types (later expanded to seven). The first three are essential: (1) occurring primarily and essentially (e.g., wonder for man), (2) occurring via a part of the subject (whether that part is more general or equal to the subject—e.g., occupying space for man via body, or speech via rationality), (3) occurring via a coextensive notion (e.g., having extremities for body via finiteness, or laughter for man via wonder). The author defends why (2) and (3) are still considered essential, arguing that a part or coextensive notion is not external to the essence because it exists with it and ceases with it. The next two are accidental: (4) via a more specific notion (e.g., laughter for animal because it is man), and (5) via a more general notion not part of the subject (e.g., motion for man as a walker). Then others add a sixth: (6) via a disparate thing (e.g., heat for water from fire). Finally, al-Mushkini corrects by adding two more: (7a) genus occurring to species (e.g., animal to rational) and (7b) species occurring to genus (e.g., rational to animal), both essential. Shaykh Muhammad 'Ali then summarizes that there are seven types: three essential by consensus (self, equal part, external equal), three accidental by consensus (external more general, external more specific, disparate), and one disputed (via a more general part). The later scholars consider it essential, the ancients accidental. The passage concludes by explaining why only essential accidents are studied in a science: they are the true states of the subject, while accidental accidents belong to the sciences of those things for which they are essential. For example, motion as an accident of man (via walking) is accidental to man but essential to walking; thus it is studied in the science of walking, not in the science of man.

The Disputed Accident: Through a More General Part
Dispute on accidents via a more general part

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between the earlier and later scholars over accidents occurring through a more general part, and evaluate the arguments for each position.

Core Idea
Whether an accident via a more general part is essential depends on whether that part is considered inherent to the essence.

The passage highlights a key disagreement among logicians: when an accident occurs to a subject through a more general part (e.g., occupying space for man via body), is it essential or accidental? The later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) consider it essential because the part is not external to the essence, while the earlier scholars (qudama') consider it accidental because the accident is mediated by that part. This lesson explores the reasoning behind each view and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed accident
Locate in the passage the accident 'occupying space' for man, which occurs via 'body'. This is the example of an accident via a more general part.
Body is more general than man because man is a body, but not all bodies are men.
2
Understand the earlier scholars' position
The earlier scholars (qudama') held that this accident is accidental because it occurs through an intermediary (body) in reality. Since the intermediary is not the essence itself, the accident is external.
Their criterion: direct occurrence without any intermediary, even if the intermediary is a part of the subject.
3
Understand the later scholars' position
The later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) argued that the accident is essential because the part (body) is not external to the essence. They consider that if the intermediary is a constitutive part of the subject, it is still direct in the sense that the accident inheres in the essence through that part.
They define 'direct' as not requiring an external intermediary; internal parts are acceptable.
4
Evaluate the implications
Consider the implication for the scope of a science: if the accident is essential, it should be studied in the science of that subject; if accidental, it belongs elsewhere. This disagreement affects how sciences define their subject matters.
The passage follows the later scholars' view, as al-Mushkini's correction is endorsed.

Finally, al-Mushkini corrects by adding two more: (7a) genus occurring to species (e.g., animal to rational) and (7b) species occurring to genus (e.g., rational to animal), both essential. Shaykh Muhammad 'Ali then summarizes that there are seven types: three essential by consensus (self, equal part, external equal), three accidental by consensus (external more general, external more specific, disparate), and one disputed (via a more general part). The later scholars consider it essential, the ancients accidental. The passage concludes by explaining why only essential accidents are studied in a science: they are the true states of the subject, while accidental accidents belong to the sciences of those things for which they are essential. For example, motion as an accident of man (via walking) is accidental to man but essential to walking; thus it is studied in the science of walking, not in the science of man.

Core Idea
The six-fold division of indication is empirically grounded, not purely rational.

Importantly, he clarifies that this six-fold enumeration is based on induction (istiqra'), not apriori reasoning, except for the verbal conventional subtype. Guided Path
1
Identify the two classification axes
The author multiplies 'two' (verbal and non-verbal indication) by 'three' (conventional, natural, rational indication). Verbal indication uses words; non-verbal uses any other sign. Core Idea
Quantifiers in logic count individuals under a universal, not parts of a physical object.

Core Idea
Modal restrictions define the precise conditions under which a predicate holds in Islamic logic.

In Islamic modal logic, propositions are not simply true or false; they are qualified by necessity, perpetuity, and conditionality. This gloss from al-Yazdi’s Hashiya clarifies how the 'general conditional' (al-mashruta al-'amma) and 'general conventional' (al-'urfiyya al-'amma) are further restricted by descriptive non-perpetuity (al-ladawam al-wasfi) and descriptive non-necessity (al-ladurura al-wasfi). Understanding these restrictions is essential for accurately interpreting modal arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base propositions
The gloss mentions two base proposition types: the general conditional (al-mashruta al-'amma) and the general conventional (al-'urfiyya al-'amma). The general conditional typically means 'Every A is B at some time' (conditional on time), while the general conventional means 'Every A is B in the customary duration' (according to customary time). Know these definitions.
These are standard modal proposition categories in the tradition of al-Qazwini al-Katibi.
2
Define the restrictions
The restrictions are 'descriptive non-perpetuity' (al-ladawam al-wasfi) and 'descriptive non-necessity' (al-ladurura al-wasfi). Descriptive non-perpetuity means the predicate is not permanently true while the subject exists. Descriptive non-necessity means the predicate is not necessarily true while the subject exists. The term 'descriptive' (wasfi) indicates these are descriptive of the subject's state, not essential.
Do not confuse 'descriptive' with 'essential' (dhati).
3
Apply the restrictions to each base
For the general conditional, both descriptive non-perpetuity and descriptive non-necessity are applied. For the general conventional, only descriptive non-perpetuity is applied (not non-necessity). This means the conventional restricted proposition is not permanent but could still be necessary in some sense.
The gloss explicitly says 'by descriptive non-perpetuity and descriptive non-necessity' for the conditional, and 'by descriptive non-perpetuity' alone for the conventional.
4
Recognize the resulting proposition type
These restricted propositions are part of the eight modal forms commonly analyzed in later Islamic logic. They are used in arguments where precise modal force matters, such as in proving existential dependence or temporal creation.
The context of the gloss (page 296 of Yazdi's Hashiya) suggests these restrictions generate specific modal subtypes.

Non-perpetuity and non-necessity collapse into simpler proposition types: absolute general and possible general.

This gloss expands on a commentator's statement about the first four composite propositions. Condition Application and Multiplicity of Subject
Gloss on condition causing multiple subjects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a condition applied to a set of propositions leads to multiplicity of subject and explain why this is a logical concern.

Core Idea
Multiplicity of subject arises whenever a condition touches even one proposition in a composite.

This short gloss from Mirza Muhammad Ali highlights a subtle but critical point in categorical logic: when a condition is attached to a composite of propositions, whether it applies to some or to all of them, it inevitably leads to a multiplicity of subject (ta'addud al-mawdu'). This means the original unified subject becomes fragmented, complicating logical analysis. Understanding this helps you avoid a common pitfall in classifying propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logical context
The gloss presumes a set of propositions (e.g., the seven composites mentioned elsewhere). The condition is something like 'non-perpetuity' or a modifier that attaches to the subject. Recognize that we are dealing with a categorical framework where each proposition has a single subject.
2
Apply the condition to some propositions
If the condition applies only to a subset of the propositions, those few now have a modified subject (e.g., 'temporarily existing'). The remaining propositions keep their original subject. This creates multiple distinct subjects (one modified, one original), breaking the uniformity.
The gloss says 'if it were on some of them, it would necessitate multiplicity of subject'.
3
Apply the condition to all propositions
If the condition applies to every proposition, each proposition's subject is now modified. But the modification might differ across propositions (e.g., some 'temporarily existing' in one sense, others in another). Even if uniform, the class of subjects is no longer identical to the original whole, again producing multiplicity.
The gloss adds 'if it were on all of them, it would necessitate multiplicity of subject as well'.
4
Reflect on the consequence
In either case (partial or full application), the original single-subject classification becomes ambiguous. The logician can no longer treat the composite as a unified subject. This is why the author urges 'so reflect' (fa-ta'ammal) — it demands careful handling.

Core Idea
Disjunction types depend on the relationship between parts, not just truth values.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the classification of disjunctive conditionals. He distinguishes between disjunctions that are logically necessary (like contradiction) and those that happen to be true in a particular case. This distinction is crucial for understanding how disjunctive syllogisms work in Islamic logic, especially when applied to fiqh and kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two senses of 'man'at al-jam'
Read the opening statement: whenever man'at al-jam' is true in the first sense (logical incompatibility), it is true in the second sense (accidental), but not vice versa. Understand that the first sense is stronger because it is based on an essential relationship between the parts.
The same applies to man'at al-khuluw by analogy.
2
Define accidental disjunction (ittifaqiyya)
Find the definition: accidental disjunction occurs when the disjunction is not due to a logical relationship between the parts, but because they happen to have specific truth values in a given subject. For example, in a person who is black and non-writing, 'either non-black or writer' is an accidental exclusive disjunction.
Accidental disjunction can mimic true disjunction in a specific instance.
3
Examine the examples for each type
The passage provides three examples based on a person who is black and non-writing: (1) true disjunction between blackness and writing, (2) exclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam') between non-blackness and writing, (3) exhaustive disjunction (man'at al-khuluw) between blackness and non-writing. Note the truth conditions for each.
The true disjunction is stable even if the person's attributes change (e.g., if the person becomes a writer and non-black, the same true disjunction holds).
4
Distinguish essential vs accidental incompatibility
The commentator stresses that in true disjunction (haqiqiyya) the incompatibility is essential (e.g., one part being the contradictory of the other). In accidental disjunction, the incompatibility is only material, arising from the specific subject's properties. This difference determines whether the disjunction is universal or context-dependent.
For example, 'either this is non-black or a writer' is not universally true; it relies on the person being black and non-writing.
5
Apply the distinction to a theological example
Consider the statement: 'Either the world is temporally originated or it is eternal.' This is a true disjunction if the two exhaust all possibilities and are mutually exclusive by essence. If a third option were possible, it would be accidental. Verify by checking if there is a logical contradiction between the two parts.
This type of analysis is common in kalam arguments for the world's creation.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies types of disjunctive conditionals (munfasila). It begins by distinguishing two senses of 'man'at al-jam' (mutually exclusive disjunction): the first sense is where the disjuncts are logically incompatible by essence; the second sense is where they are incompatible by accident. The key point is that the first sense entails the second but not vice versa, because the second can occur within a true disjunction (haqiqiyya). Similarly for man'at al-khuluw (exhaustive disjunction).

The passage then defines the accidental disjunction (ittifaqiyya). This is a disjunction where the disjuncts are not related by a logical relationship (such as contradiction, equivalence, or generality) but rather happen to be either both not true together and both not false together (like the true disjunction between blackness and writing in a person described as both black and non-writing), or both not true together but both false together (like the exclusive disjunction between non-blackness and writing in that person), or both not false together but both true together (like the exhaustive disjunction between non-blackness and non-writing in that person). The commentator uses the example of a specific person with blackness and non-writing to illustrate all three cases.

A later gloss explains that the true disjunction (munfasila haqiqiyya) is defined by an essential incompatibility between the two sides, not merely a material one. The example given—'sawad' (blackness) and 'kitaba' (writing) in a specified human—demonstrates a true disjunction because the two cannot be true together nor false together (they are mutually exclusive and exhaustive). The gloss then provides examples of man'at al-jam' and man'at al-khuluw conditioned on the specific assumption of that human's attributes. It notes that if we flip the assumption (a writer who is non-black), the exclusive and exhaustive examples are reversed, but the true disjunction example remains valid in both cases. The pedagogical intent is to clarify that the logical classification depends on the meaning of the disjunction, not merely on the material coincidence of truth values.
