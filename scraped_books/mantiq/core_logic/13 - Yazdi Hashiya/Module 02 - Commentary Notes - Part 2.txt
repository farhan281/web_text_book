Linguistic Definition of Iman as Heartfelt Attestation
Linguistic argument for Iman as inner belief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the linguistic meaning of Iman (taṣdīq) and argue that it is primarily heart belief, not verbal confession, using the principle of no transference.

Core Idea
Iman is inner attestation; the tongue only manifests it.

Many think Islam and Iman are the same because both involve speech. But the author shows that Iman, by its original linguistic meaning, is heartfelt attestation. This lesson walks through the linguistic evidence and the principle that religious terms remain in their original meaning unless proven otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find where the commentator defines 'Iman'. He says: 'معنى الايمان هو التصديق بالقلب' (the meaning of Iman is heartfelt attestation). This is the definiendum.
2
Extract the linguistic evidence
The author cites Quranic verses: 'وما أنت بمؤمن لنا' (you are not a believer for us) meaning 'you are not attesting', and 'يؤمن بالجبت والطاغوت' meaning 'attests'. Also Arab usage: 'فلان يؤمن بكذا' (so-and-so believes in something). All point to taṣdīq (attestation).
3
Apply the principle of no transference
The author invokes the principle: 'والأصل عدم النقل' (the original rule is no transference). So the word Iman remains in its linguistic meaning unless there is evidence it was shifted to a religious-technical meaning. No such evidence exists here.
This principle is common in usul al-fiqh for interpreting Quranic terms.
4
Refute the objection about the mute person
Some argue that heartfelt attestation is only understood through speech, so speech must be part of the concept. The author counters: a mute person can have heartfelt attestation without speech, showing that speech is only necessary for manifestation, not essence.
5
Conclude the reality of Iman
Thus the reality (haqiqa) of Iman is heartfelt attestation; the tongue and limbs are only required to manifest it externally.

Hadith Distinction Between Islam and Iman
Hadith evidence for distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the relationship between Islam and Iman as described in the hadith of Imam Ṣādiq (as), distinguishing between outward submission and inner faith.

Core Idea
Islam is outward submission; Iman is inner faith.

After establishing the linguistic meaning of Iman, the commentator presents a key hadith from Imam Ṣādiq (as) that clearly distinguishes Islam from Iman. This lesson unpacks the hadith and its implications, as well as supporting Quranic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on synonymy
The commentator first notes that some consider Islam and Iman synonymous. The hadith is introduced to refute this. Know the positions: synonymy vs. distinction.
2
Extract the hadith of Samā'a
Locate the hadith: Samā'a asks Imam Ṣādiq about Islam and Iman. The Imam replies: 'Iman shares with Islam but Islam does not share with Iman.' This is the core evidence.
This hadith is from the reliable tradition (muwathaqa).
3
Analyze the definition of Islam in the hadith
The Imam defines Islam as the testimony and outward submission that protects blood, allows marriage, inheritance, and gathers people. It is an outward act.
4
Analyze the definition of Iman in the hadith
Iman is defined as guidance and what is established in the heart (ma thabata fi l-qulub), plus action that appears. It is an inner reality that also manifests outwardly, but its core is inner.
5
Derive the relationship
Since Iman shares in the outward (Islam) but Islam does not share in the inward (Iman), Islam is more general. One can be a Muslim without being a true believer, but a true believer is always a Muslim.
The verse 'The bedouins say we believe...' confirms this: they are Muslims (outward) but not believers (inward).

Resolving the Verse 'Indeed the religion with God is Islam'
Resolving apparent objection from Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve the apparent contradiction between the verse 'al-Islām' and the distinction between Islam and Iman, using the logical tool of general and particular (al-ʿāmm wa al-khāṣṣ).

Core Idea
The verse uses 'Islam' as a general term that includes two types.

Some might object that the Quran says 'the religion with God is Islam' (3:19), implying that Islam alone is sufficient, contradicting the distinction with Iman. The commentator resolves this by showing that 'Islam' here is a general term applied to different individuals, including both mere submission and true faith. This lesson teaches how to handle general verses in light of specific evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The verse 'إن الدين عند الله الإسلام' is often used to argue that Islam and Iman are the same or that Islam alone is acceptable. The commentator acknowledges this verse as a potential counter-evidence.
2
Understand the reply: general term applied to multiple individuals
The commentator states: 'فان العام يستعمل في كل واحد من الافراد' (the general term is used for each of the individuals). So 'Islam' here is a general category that includes various levels, not a single monolithic entity.
This is a key logical concept: al-ʿāmm (general) can be applied to different instances.
3
Identify the two types of Islam
The commentator divides Islam into two types: (1) mere verbal confession without inner belief, which is inferior to Iman; (2) verbal confession accompanied by heartfelt conviction and action, which is equivalent to Iman or includes it.
The verse refers to the second type, as it is a commendable religion.
4
Apply to the commentator's own ambiguity
The commentator notes that the hashia author (al-Muḥashshī) was not uncertain about the meaning of 'Islam', but was specifying which individual (type) is intended in the verse.
5
Conclude the resolution
Thus there is no contradiction: the verse is about true Islam (with faith), while the hadith and other verses distinguish between the broader outward submission and inner faith. The general term can cover both.

Distinguishing Lāmiyya from Miniyya Iḍāfa
Author refutes partitive reading of iḍāfa in 'al-ʿaqāʾid'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between lāmiyya and miniyya iḍāfa and apply the principle of compound-part distinctness to resolve similar cases.

Core Idea
The compound is distinct from its parts, so the iḍāfa must be possessive, not partitive.

In Arabic grammar, iḍāfa (possessive construction) can indicate different relationships. One common confusion is whether an iḍāfa is lāmiyya (possessive) or miniyya (partitive). This lesson examines a key principle: when the annexed and annexed-to form a compound, the compound is distinct from its components, ruling out a partitive reading. We'll see how this applies to the phrase 'Islam is beliefs' and why the iḍāfa must be lāmiyya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the iḍāfa
Locate the annexed (muḍāf) and annexed-to (muḍāf ilayh). In 'Islam is beliefs' (al-ʿaqāʾid), the annexed is implicit but understood as 'Islam', and the annexed-to is 'beliefs'.
The phrase is from the matn: 'Islam is beliefs'.
2
Understand the two possible relationships
An iḍāfa can be lāmiyya (indicating possession or belonging) or miniyya (indicating partitive, e.g., 'some of'). Here, the author considers whether 'beliefs' could be a part of 'Islam', making it miniyya.
Lāmiyya: Islam possesses beliefs. Miniyya: Islam is part of beliefs (i.e., some beliefs).
3
Apply the compound-part distinctness principle
The author argues that the compound ('Islam' as a set of beliefs) is distinct from its parts (individual beliefs), just as a mixture (sakanjabīn) is distinct from its ingredients (vinegar and honey). Therefore, the iḍāfa cannot be partitive.
This principle is crucial for resolving similar cases.
4
Refute the opposing view
Some might think that if the annexed (Islam) is more general, the iḍāfa is partitive. The author dismisses this by emphasizing the distinctness of the compound from its components.
Generality does not imply partitive relationship when the whole is emergent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Figurative attribution enriches language by assigning actions to unintended subjects for emphasis.

In this passage from a hashia on a logic text, the commentator explains a common rhetorical device: figurative attribution (tajwiz fi al-isnad). This occurs when a predicate is linked to an entity that is not its true subject, often for hyperbolic effect. Understanding this device is crucial for correctly interpreting theological and legal texts where such usage abounds.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Figurative attribution means attributing something to other than its true owner (isnad al-shay' ila ghayr ma huwa lahu). The author gives examples to illustrate.
Keep this definition in mind as you examine the examples.
2
Examine the examples
The examples 'he fasted his day', 'the river flowed', and 'the rainwater channel flowed' show actions attributed to time, the riverbed, or the channel, when the real agents are the person, water, and water respectively.
Observe how the grammatical subject is not the actual doer.
3
Apply to the commentary context
In the phrase 'ja'altu tabshiran' (I made it an insight), the attribute 'tabshira' is assigned to the pronoun. Originally, it should be attributed to the book or the author's action. This is figurative.
The same mechanism is at play in the opening 'al-hamdu li-Llah'.
4
Consider the two possibilities
The commentator suggests either figurative attribution for hyperbole or an implied annexed noun (e.g., 'dhāta tabshira' or 'shai'an tabshiratan') to make it literal.
These options show how scholars reconcile rhetorical usage with logical precision.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an idafa construction uses the lam of difference (lam-iyya) or possession, and detect metaphorical attribution in scholarly phrases.

Core Idea
Idafa lam-iyya indicates difference between mudaf and mudaf ilayh; tajawwuz fi al-isnad shifts attribution for emphasis.

This lesson unpacks two important grammatical-logical concepts from the commentator's notes: the nature of lam-iyya idafa and the device of metaphorical attribution. Mastering these helps you read classical mutun and shuruh with awareness of when a phrase is literal or figural, preventing misinterpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the idafa compound
Locate the mudaf and mudaf ilayh. Determine if the relationship is ownership (milkiyya) or a connection of difference (lam-iyya). The commentator stresses that lam-iyya indicates tabayun (difference) between the parts.
The compound (murakkab) is different from its components, like sakanjabin (vinegar+honey drink) is distinct from vinegar and honey alone.
2

If the idafa does not indicate possession (e.g., 'Zayd's horse') but rather a relationship where the mudaf is a property or act distinct from the mudaf ilayh, it is lam-iyya. The lam preposition implies 'related to' or 'pertaining to' with separation.

On the view of non-Sakkaki followers, it is clear: the existence of the malzum necessitates the existence of the lazim, akin to proving a claim with evidence. However, on al-Sakkaki's view, there is a difficulty: the lazim may be more general than the malzum, so the general does not necessarily indicate the specific. The resolution is that al-Sakkaki intended the equal lazim (al-lazim al-musawi), which is co-extensive. The commentator then quotes Shaykh Muhammad al-Karmi, who gives an example: the phrase 'long garment' (tawil al-nijad) explicitly refers to the garment, but the intended meaning is that the person has a long stature. The explicit statement does not give the claim purely, but the indirect expression through 'long garment' implies the stature necessarily because the garment's length is a lazim of the person's stature. Thus, the listener understands the intended meaning without the speaker having to explicitly claim it. This makes kinayah more effective because it provides accepted results without the need for a separate proof.

Learning Objective

Refining the Distinction Between Natural and Rational Signification
Al-Sharif refines the distinction between natural and rational signification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conventional, natural, and rational signification and apply al-Sharif's cause-based differentiator.

Core Idea
Natural and rational signification both bypass convention; only the cause (nature vs. reason) distinguishes them.

In the standard classification, conventional signification is set apart from natural and rational. However, al-Muhaqqiq al-Sharif observed that one interpretation of natural signification—the listener's natural understanding—is also present in rational signification. Therefore, the differentiator cannot be the absence of knowledge of convention; it must be the cause: exigency of nature or rational inference. This lesson unpacks that crucial refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Types of Signification
From the passage, note that wad'iyya (conventional) depends on a positer, tab'iyya (natural) from natural exigency, and 'aqliyya (rational) from inference. Al-Sharif's note focuses on the boundary between tab'iyya and 'aqliyya.
The example of tab'iyya is the groan 'āh' indicating pain.
2
Examine al-Sharif's Critique
Al-Sharif points out that the last interpretation of tab'iyya (listener's natural understanding) is shared by 'aqliyya because both do not rely on knowledge of the conventional positing. Thus, this interpretation cannot differentiate the two.
Refer to footnote (12): 'this last possibility is shared by natural and rational, for understanding in both is not based on knowledge of positing.'
3
Understand the Overlap
The common element is that understanding occurs without prior knowledge of a conventional assignment. This invalidates the simple criterion of 'without convention' as a differentiator.
4
Apply the Correct Differentiator
Al-Sharif implies that the true differentiator is the cause: tab'iyya arises from natural exigency (the groan from pain), while 'aqliyya arises from rational inference (smoke to fire). Focus on the source of the relation.
In usul al-fiqh, this distinction affects how evidence is weighted: natural signs are not legally binding unless recognized by custom.

Three Types of Signification and the Restriction 'From That Aspect'
Author defines مطابقة, تضمن, التزام and critiques definitions for lacking qualification 'from that aspect'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every definition of signification needs the qualifier 'from that aspect' to avoid contradictions in homonymous terms.

In this passage, the commentator explains the three classical types of signification: مطابقة (correspondence), تضمن (inclusion), and التزام (implication). He then identifies a critical flaw in their bare definitions: without the restriction 'from that aspect' (من حيث هو كذلك), these definitions would overlap in cases where a word has multiple meanings. Correcting the Whole-Part Mistake: The Case of 'دعاء'
Criticizes a scholar who incorrectly treated 'دعاء' as whole-part example; true signification is مطابقة.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a term's meaning is a single composite concept and distinguish it from a whole-part relation in the context of signification (dalalat).

Core Idea
'دعاء' is 'request for mercy' as one meaning, not a whole comprising two separate parts.

This lesson corrects a common error in analyzing how words signify meanings. A respected glossator mistakenly treated the use of 'دعاء' for 'mercy' as an example of whole-part signification (اطلاق الكل على الجزء). The commentator shows that 'دعاء' itself means 'request for mercy' – a single composite concept – so using it for 'mercy' alone involves a shift in meaning, not a part-whole relation. Understanding this distinction prevents misclassification in the theory of signification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being corrected
The glossator claimed that using 'دعاء' to mean 'mercy' is an example of whole-part (اطلاق الكل على الجزء). This implies that 'دعاء' is the whole and 'mercy' is a part of that whole.
The glossator's error is not in the response to the illusion but in the example used.
2
Understand the commentator's objection
The commentator asserts that 'دعاء' is defined as 'طلب الرحمة' (request for mercy). This definition is a single composite meaning, not two independently signified parts 'طلب' and 'رحمة'.
A composite meaning is one where the components are inseparable in the signification; the word signifies the entire composite at once.
3
Distinguish composite meaning from whole-part
In a whole-part relation, the whole can be used to mean a part (e.g., using 'house' to mean 'door'). Here, 'دعاء' does not have 'mercy' as a separable part; rather, 'mercy' is an essential constituent of its unitary meaning.
The glossator's example confuses the internal composition of the definition with a part-whole extensional relationship.
4
Recognize the actual type of signification
Using 'دعاء' specifically for 'mercy' is a transfer of meaning (مجاز) or a specification, not a whole-part application. The word originally signifies 'طلب الرحمة', but in context it may be used for the mercy requested.
This is a semantic narrowing, not a part-whole shift.
5
Apply the lesson to other terms
When analyzing any term's signification, first check if its meaning is unitary or composite. If composite, determine whether the components are signified separately or together. Avoid confusing internal composition with external part-whole relations.
This principle applies widely in usul al-fiqh when defining legal terms (e.g., 'البيعة', 'الصلاة').

This lesson explores how the word 'jins' (genus) acquired its technical meaning in logic. Defending the Terminology of Antecedent and Consequent
Gloss addressing objection about calling the condition 'first part' when it comes later.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the condition is still called 'first part' even when it appears after the consequent in speech, using the glossator's arguments.

Core Idea
Terminology in logic is based on original order, not accidental sequence.

When studying conditional propositions, you might wonder: if the condition can come after the result in speech, why call it the 'first part'? This lesson examines a classical objection and the glossator's defense. You will see how logical terms are justified by their original state or common usage, not every exceptional case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection states that in phrases like 'I honor you if you come to me', the condition 'if you come' appears after the consequent, so calling it 'first part' seems incorrect.
This challenges the very naming of antecedent and consequent.
2
Analyze the first response: ellipsis
The glossator argues that the antecedent is not actually the second part; rather, it is omitted and understood from context. The apparent order is due to an implied element.
This response maintains the literal priority of the condition.
3
Analyze the second response: original order
Even if we accept the surface order as grammatically possible, the naming is based on the original or default order where the condition precedes the consequent.
This acknowledges variation but prioritizes the norm.
4
Analyze the third response: majority usage
Alternatively, the naming is based on the majority of instances where the condition does come first. Terminology can reflect what is common.
This is a statistical justification.
5
Evaluate the defense
By offering these multiple responses, the glossator shows that the terminology is not arbitrary but rooted in deeper principles.

Identifying the Subject of the Natural Proposition: A Critique of al-Sharih's Error
Author corrects a misinterpretation about the subject of natural propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error that al-Sharih made regarding the subject of the natural proposition and explain why it is mistaken based on the passage.

Core Idea
The subject of a natural proposition is not a universal concept nor stripped external reality.

In this passage, the author of the hashiya corrects a serious misunderstanding by the commentator (al-sharih) concerning the subject of the natural proposition (al-qadiyya al-tabi'iyya). This correction is crucial because misidentifying the subject affects the logical behavior of such propositions, especially in syllogisms. We will examine the error and the grounds for its refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being corrected
Find al-Sharih's statement: 'As it is the subject of the natural proposition.' What exactly did he think the subject is? The passage indicates he likely considered it either a universal concept or the external reality stripped of individuals.
2
Extract the refutation
The author says 'this is a mistake' and lists two things the subject is NOT: it is not a universal concept that can be predicated of many, nor the external reality stripped of particularities.
Note the two negations: not universal, not bare reality.
3
Understand the reasoning
Why can't the subject be a universal? Because natural propositions do not behave like quantified ones—they do not convert and do not produce conclusions in the first figure's major premise. A universal subject would allow such operations. Why not bare reality? Because that would lose the unity required for a propositional subject.
Logical properties of natural propositions are key evidence.
4
Recognize the incompleteness
The text cuts off before giving the positive definition, but from the negation we can infer the correct subject: the nature considered absolutely, without regard to universality or individuation.
This aligns with standard logical doctrine.

Why Eight Modal Forms Were Omitted
Author explains why only 24 of 32 modal forms are discussed, and the meaning of 'unconsidered'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the commentator's rationale for omitting eight modal proposition forms and define what 'unconsidered' (ghayr mu'tabara) means in this context.

Core Idea
Not every logically sound proposition is considered in logical discourse; some are excluded due to lack of relevance.

In the study of modal propositions, logicians identified 32 possible forms. However, the commentator only discussed 24, omitting eight that arise from restricting 'absolute necessary' and 'absolute perpetual' by four conditions. This passage clarifies that these eight were omitted not because they are invalid, but because they are either unsound or 'sound but not considered'—meaning logicians do not analyze them further in contradiction, conversion, or syllogism. Understanding this selectivity helps us navigate the logical tradition's pedagogical choices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the omitted forms
The eight omitted forms come from restricting each of 'absolute necessary' (al-ḍarūriyya al-muṭlaqa) and 'absolute perpetual' (al-dāʾima al-muṭlaqa) by the four temporal/necessity conditions. Write out these combinations to see what is missing.
The four restrictions are typically the same for all modal propositions.
2
Understand the commentator's purpose
The commentator (al-Muḥashshī) aimed to explain only what the main text (al‑matn) indicated. Since the text did not refer to these eight forms, he left them out. This shows his fidelity to the source.
3
Distinguish unsound from unconsidered
Among the eight, six possibilities from the two groups are 'unsound' (ghayr ṣaḥīḥa) and two are 'sound but not considered' (ṣaḥīḥa ghayr muʿtabara). The passage assumes prior knowledge of which are which.
Unsound propositions violate logical or factual constraints; sound but unconsidered ones are valid but not useful for further analysis.
4
Define 'unconsidered'
The term 'unconsidered' (ghayr muʿtabara) means that logicians did not treat the proposition in the subsequent discussions of contradiction, conversion, and syllogism, even though it is logically sound. The author notes that this meaning aligns with one of two earlier definitions.
Avoid the misunderstanding that 'unconsidered' means invalid.

Core Idea
Omission by analogy is a mark of pedagogical efficiency.

The commentator explained the naming for earlier propositions but omitted it for specific possibility. This gloss by Muhammad Ali justifies the omission by pointing to the obviousness through analogy with general possibility. In this lesson, you will learn how to apply the principle of qiyas (analogy) to infer omitted information in logical works.

Identify the omitted item

قوله: «كانت الصغرى ضرورية لاغير»: قال ابن هشام في «مغني اللبيب»: أن «غيراً» اسم ملازم للإضافة في المعنى ويجوز أن يقطع عنها لفظاً إن فهم معناه وتقدمت عليها كلمة «ليس» وقولهم: «لاغير» لحن يعني لعدم تقدم كلمة «ليس» و شدد الإنكار لمرتكبه في «شرح الشذور». وعن «السيرافي»: الحذف إنما يستعمل إذا كانت «غير» بعد «ليس» ولو كان مكانها غيرها من ألفاظ الجحد لم يجز الحذف ولا يتجاوز بذلك مورد السماع. و: أقول حكى «الزمخشري» في «المفصل»: «لاغير» و «ليس غير و قرره الأندلسي» و حكى ابن الحاجب: لاغير أيضاً ولم يرده واحد من شارحي كلامه، وعن أبي العباس أنه كان يقول: لاغير بالبناء على الضم كـ «قبل» و «بعد»، وعن ابن مالك أنه أنشد عليه في باب القسم من شرح التسهيل قول الشاعر: جواباً به تنجوا اعتمد قربنا لعمل أسلفت لا غير تسئل إنكاره ذلك، استعمله في مغني اللبيب وسائر مصنفاته والعجب أن ابن هشام مع أيضاً في مواضع متعددة. (ميرزا محمد علي)
His statement: 'the minor was necessary without more (la ghayr)' — Ibn Hisham said in Mughni al-Labib: 'Ghayr' is a noun necessarily connected in meaning, but it is permissible to be separated from it in word if its meaning is understood and the word 'laysa' precedes it. And their saying 'la ghayr' is ungrammatical, meaning because of the absence of the preceding 'laysa', and he strongly denounced its user in Sharh al-Shudhur. And from al-Sirafi: The elision is only used when 'ghayr' is after 'laysa'; if another negation word were in its place, elision is not allowed and is not to be extended beyond the heard usage. And I say: al-Zamakhshari narrated in al-Mufassal: 'la ghayr' and 'laysa ghayr' and al-Andalusi confirmed it. And Ibn al-Hajib narrated 'la ghayr' as well, and none of the commentators of his words rejected it. And from Abu al-Abbas that he used to say 'la ghayr' indeclinable with damm like 'qablu' and 'ba'du'. And from Ibn Malik that he recited in the chapter on oaths from Sharh al-Tashil the poet's saying: ... (a line of poetry) His disapproval of that, he used it in Mughni al-Labib and his other works. And it is surprising that Ibn Hisham despite that also in multiple places. (Mirza Muhammad Ali)

Commentary
This is a grammatical note on the phrase 'la ghayr' (without more) used in the earlier logical text. The commentator, Mirza Muhammad Ali, cites Ibn Hisham who strongly rejects 'la ghayr' as ungrammatical because the word 'ghayr' requires the negation 'laysa' preceding it, not 'la'. Al-Sirafi agrees, stating that the elision (of the added part of 'ghayr') is only allowed after 'laysa'. However, several other scholars—al-Zamakhshari, al-Andalusi, Ibn al-Hajib, Abu al-Abbas, and Ibn Malik—accept or use 'la ghayr'. The commentator notes the irony that Ibn Hisham himself uses 'la ghayr' in his own works despite his disapproval. This illustrates a grammatical debate about the permissibility of this construction.
