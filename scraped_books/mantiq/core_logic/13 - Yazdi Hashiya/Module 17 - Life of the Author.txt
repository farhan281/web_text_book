The Chain of Transmission: From Aristotle to Ibn Sina
Author gives a brief historical note on the development of logic from Aristotle to Ibn Sina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical development of logic from Aristotle to Ibn Sina and identify the specific contributions of each key figure as mentioned in the passage.

Core Idea
Logic's journey from Greece to Islam: Aristotle founded, Farabi refined, Ibn Sina perfected.

This passage offers a concise historical sketch of how logic entered the Islamic world. Knowing this chain helps you understand why later scholars like al-Taftazani refer to Farabi and Ibn Sina as authorities. Let's walk through each link in the chain.

And the possessor of the laws of logic and philosophy is the great sage Aristotle; he compiled them by the order of Alexander, therefore he was titled 'the First Teacher', and it was said of logic that it is 'the inheritance of Dhul-Qarnayn'. Then, after the translators transferred those philosophical works from the Greek language into Arabic, the Second Teacher, the sage Abu Nasr al-Farabi, refined, collected, and perfected them. Then, after the loss of Abu Nasr's books, the Chief Master Abu Ali Sina (Ibn Sina) detailed and revised them. May Allah reward their beautiful efforts.

Commentary
This passage provides a brief historical account of the development of logic in the Islamic tradition. It identifies Aristotle as the founder of logic and philosophy, calling him 'the First Teacher' (al-mu‘allim al-awwal). It mentions that his works were written by order of Alexander, and logic is called 'the inheritance of Dhul-Qarnayn' (a reference to Alexander the Great in Islamic tradition). Then the passage describes the translation movement from Greek to Arabic, and highlights al-Farabi as 'the Second Teacher' (al-mu‘allim al-thānī) who refined and perfected these works. Finally, it notes that after the loss of al-Farabi's books, Ibn Sina (the Chief Master, al-shaykh al-ra’īs) detailed and revised them, reorganizing the material. The passage thus presents a chain of transmission: Aristotle → translation → Farabi → Ibn Sina, emphasizing their contributions to preserving and developing logic.

Key Terms
المعلم الأول (al-mu‘allim al-awwal): Aristotle, acknowledged as the founder of logic. [Note: Standard honorific in Islamic philosophy.]
المعلم الثاني (al-mu‘allim al-thānī): Al-Farabi, who systematized and refined Aristotelian logic in the Islamic world. [Note: Standard honorific.]
الشيخ الرئيس (al-shaykh al-ra’īs): Ibn Sina (Avicenna), who further developed and reorganized logic after Farabi. [Note: Standard honorific for Ibn Sina.]

Identifying Dhul-Qarnayn: Alexander the Great in Islamic Tradition
Identification and traditions about Alexander the Great as Dhul-Qarnayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the various Islamic traditions regarding Alexander the Great (Dhul-Qarnayn) and explain the different opinions about his title and status.

Core Idea
Alexander the Great is remembered as a righteous servant of Allah in Islamic tradition.

This passage from the ḥāshiyah (gloss) on the commentary of al-Yazdī presents multiple Islamic traditions about Alexander the Great, whom it identifies as Dhul-Qarnayn. It explores his piety, possible prophethood, and the various explanations for his epithet. Understanding these traditions helps situate a famous historical figure within the Islamic worldview.

Guided Path
1
Identify the figure being discussed
The passage begins by identifying Alexander the Roman (al-Iskandar al-Rūmī) as the subject. Who is he, and why is he important in Islamic tradition?
2
List the four world rulers
The gloss mentions a tradition: two believers (Dhul-Qarnayn and Solomon) and two disbelievers (Nimrod and Nebuchadnezzar) ruled the world. What does this classification imply about Alexander’s status?
3
Examine the opinion on prophethood
Some said he was a prophet. How does that align with the view that he was a righteous servant?
4
Analyze the story from Ali ibn Abi Talib
The narration about being struck on both horns and resurrected. What does this story suggest about the origin of the name 'Dhul-Qarnayn'?
5
Compare the various explanations for the title
There are four explanations: reaching the ends of the earth, two horns on a crown, two braids, and the story of being struck. Which one seems most plausible from an Islamic perspective?

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and traditional note about Alexander the Great (al-Iskandar al-Rūmī), identified as Dhul-Qarnayn. It presents various Islamic traditions: that he was a righteous servant given knowledge and rule; that he was one of the four world rulers (two believers, two disbelievers); opinions on his prophethood; and multiple explanations for his title 'Dhul-Qarnayn' (the two-horned one), including a story from Ali ibn Abi Talib about being struck on both sides of his head in obedience. This enriches the textual reference to Alexander in the main work.
