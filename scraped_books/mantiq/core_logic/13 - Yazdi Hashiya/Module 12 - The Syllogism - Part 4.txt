Why Reduction Fails for the Sixth, Seventh, and Eighth Moods
Author explains why reduction to first figure fails for three moods in second figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a second-figure mood is reducible to the first figure by checking the affirmation and universality of its premises.

Core Idea
Reduction fails when premises lack affirmation or universality.

In the second figure, some moods that produce negative conclusions resist reduction to the first figure. The commentator Mirza Muhammad Ali refutes a claim that all such moods are reducible by showing why the sixth, seventh, and eighth moods fail. Let's examine his reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mood and its premise qualities
For the sixth mood: the minor premise is not affirmative (negative), the major is universal affirmative, and the conclusion is particular negative. Only the second method (using contradictory of conclusion) can be attempted.
2
Apply the second method
Take the contradictory of the conclusion (universal affirmative) and combine it with the universal affirmative major premise in a first-figure syllogism. This yields a universal affirmative, which converts to a particular affirmative.
3
Check against the original minor premise
The resulting particular affirmative does not contradict the original particular negative minor premise, because both can be true for different individuals. Hence the reduction fails.
4
Extend to the seventh and eighth moods
For the seventh mood, the minor is affirmative but the major is not universal; the first method fails similarly. For the eighth, both conditions are absent, so no method works.
The general condition for reducibility is that the premises must be affirmative and universal.

The Rule of the Middle Term's Universality in Syllogisms
Commentator defines the rule involving the middle term's universality and explains its application to the fourth figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify in which syllogistic figures the middle term must be a universal subject and verify whether a given categorical syllogism satisfies this condition.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires the middle term to be subject of a universal premise in at least one premise.

In this lesson, we examine the rule (الضابطة) that ensures a syllogism's productivity: the middle term must be the subject of a universal proposition in at least one premise. The commentator clarifies that this condition applies to the first, third, and fourth figures, and both conditions (generality of middle and major term subjecthood) must be met together. We will unpack this rule and test it against examples from Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
1. Understand the rule's definition
The rule (الضابطة) states that the middle term's subjecthood must be general (universal) in one of the two premises. This means there is a premise where the middle term is the subject of a universal proposition (e.g., 'Every A is B'). The rule is derived from the concept of 'preserving' (ضبط) the syllogism's validity.
The term 'منع الخلو' means prevention of emptiness—both conditions must be present together.
2
2. Identify the two conditions
The 'two matters' are: (1) generality of the middle term's subjecthood, and (2) generality of the major term's subjecthood. The commentator stresses that both are required jointly; one alone is insufficient. Without both, the rule fails and the syllogism may be invalid.
3
3. Apply the condition to the first figure
In the first figure, the middle term is always the subject of the major premise, and universality is a condition of the major premise. Therefore, the rule is automatically satisfied. For example: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (major, universal). The middle term 'intoxicant' is subject of a universal major.
The same holds in the third figure, where the middle term is subject in both premises and one must be universal.
4
4. Extend the rule to the fourth figure
The commentator lists the moods of the fourth figure where the condition holds (moods 1,2,3,4,7,8). In these, the minor's subject is the middle term, and the minor is universal. Moods 5 and 6 fail because their minors are particular. This shows the rule's predictive power.
Mood 5 has affirmative particular minor; mood 6 has negative particular minor.

Core Idea

The third figure is a distinct figure in categorical logic. Its unique structure sets it apart from the first and second figures. In this lesson, we examine the definition given by the author, the necessary condition that at least one premise be universal, and the six mood combinations that yield valid conclusions. Modal Conditions in the Second Figure: Essence vs Accident
Details the modal requirements for the second figure, explaining actuality and necessity in the premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a predicate that is necessary for the essence and one that is only necessary for a description, and identify which premises meet the modal conditions of the second figure.

Core Idea
In the second figure, the predicate's necessity must be tied to the essence, not just to an accidental description.

This lesson explores the detailed modal conditions for premises in the second figure, as expounded by commentators like Mirza Muhammad Ali. You'll learn how to differentiate between predicates that are necessarily true of the essence itself (like animality for man) and those that are only necessary for a particular description (like finger movement for 'writer'). This distinction is vital for determining whether a premise fulfills the strict modal requirements of the second figure, where the major premise must be qualified with necessity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the modal types
From glosses 122-127, identify the six types of premises: permanent (dāʾima), specific (khāṣṣa), and general (ʿāmma). Note that the absolute general (al-muṭlaqa al-ʿāmma) is unconditional actuality, while the other types are qualified by necessity or condition.
These are modal categories, not temporal. The 'general' here refers to unconditional.
2
Grasp the entailment principle
Gloss 124 states: the specific entails the general. If the general is incompatible with something, the specific is also incompatible. Apply this to modal conditions: if an absolute general premise is incompatible with a conclusion, any more specific (qualified) premise is also incompatible.
This is a logical principle of entailment used to reason about modalities.
3
Examine necessity for essence vs description
Gloss 132 gives the critical distinction. If a predicate is necessary for the essence (e.g., animal for man), it is necessary for all essential and accidental descriptions. But if a predicate is only necessary for an accidental description (e.g., finger movement for writer), it is not necessary for the essence. Therefore, in the second figure, only premises where the predicate is necessary for the essence itself can be considered 'necessary' premises.
The key test: can the subject exist without that description? If yes, the predicate is not necessary for the essence.
4
Apply to the second figure
Recall that in the second figure, the major premise must be negative and have a special modal condition. Use the above criteria to evaluate whether a given premise meets the condition. For example, 'Every writer is a finger-mover' is not a necessary premise for the essence, so it cannot be used as a necessary major premise in the second figure.
This ensures that the syllogism's modality is sound.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on modality conditions in the second figure, building on the earlier requirement of opposition and quality difference. The glosses (122–132) come from multiple commentators and together clarify how modalities like absolute actuality and necessity apply. Gloss 122 explains that in the second figure, the major premises (which are negative) are judged with 'actuality qualified by necessity' (like necessary or conditional), while the absolute general (al-muṭlaqa al-ʿāmma) is judged with 'absolute actuality' (unqualified). The absolute is more general than the qualified, so if the absolute is incompatible with something, the qualified is also incompatible (gloss 124). Gloss 125 lists six types of premises: two permanent (dāʾimatān), two specific (khāṣṣatān), two general (ʿāmmatān). Gloss 127 categorizes these into two groups: the first three (absolute necessary, general conditional, specific conditional) and the second three (absolute permanent, general conventional, specific conventional). Gloss 130 clarifies the meaning of 'possible' (mumkin) in terms of necessity absence. Gloss 132 then dives deeply into necessary (ḍarūriyya) premises: if a predicate is necessary for the essence as long as the essence exists (e.g., animality for man), then that predicate is necessary for the essence's essential descriptions (like humanity) and also for accidental descriptions (like writing). This is because the essence is a necessary condition for the description. An example: the predicate 'animal' is true of 'standing', 'sitting', 'writer', etc. However, this does not hold for descriptions that are truly accidental and can be replaced by opposites (like stillness vs. movement). The movement of fingers is necessary for the description 'writer' but not for the essence of the man. So the general rule: predicates that are essential to the essence are necessary for all descriptions of that essence, while predicates that are only necessary for a specific accidental description do not transfer to the essence. This is important for determining modality of premises in the second figure.

This passage begins with a gloss (Mirza Muhammad 'Ali) explaining the statement that in the five divisions of compound syllogisms, the two premises must share a part that becomes the middle term. The share can be complete (the whole antecedent or consequent from each premise), incomplete (a part of the antecedent or consequent from each), or mixed (complete from one, incomplete from the other). Three Conditions for a Productive Exceptive Syllogism
Author states three conditions for the exceptive syllogism to be productive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an exceptive syllogism meets the three conditions for productivity.

Core Idea
An exceptive syllogism requires affirmative, necessary, and universal conditions to be productive.

The exceptive syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) is a powerful tool in Islamic logic, but it has strict conditions. In this passage, the author explains three prerequisites without which the syllogism fails to produce a valid conclusion. Understanding these conditions enables you to test any exceptive argument for soundness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conditional
Determine if the conditional premise is connective (muttasila) or disjunctive (munfasila), and check that it is affirmative (not negative). If negative, no connection is established.
2
Verify necessity of the connection
For a connective conditional, it must be implicative (luzumiyya) – the antecedent necessarily implies the consequent. For a disjunctive conditional, it must be contradictory (ʻinadiyya) – the disjuncts are mutually exclusive. Reject accidental (ittifaqiyya) conditionals.
Accidental conditionals lead to circular reasoning if used to derive the truth of a part.
3
Check universality condition
Ensure that either the conditional is universal, or the exception (istithna') is universal (i.e., the affirmed or denied part covers all instances), or the time of the implication matches the time of the exception. Without this, no inference holds.

Two Syllogisms in Qiyas al-Khalf
Author corrects a misunderstanding of qiyas al-khalf and resolves it into two syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deconstruct a qiyas al-khalf argument into its constituent conditional and categorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-Khalf is not a single syllogism but a compound of two: a conditional and a categorical.

Often, qiyas al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum) is treated as a single argument. This passage refines that understanding by showing that it actually consists of two distinct logical steps: first, a conditional modus tollens that proves the negation of the desired conclusion leads to an absurdity; second, a categorical syllogism that establishes the absolute absurdity of the opposite. Mastering this decomposition allows you to verify the rigor of such proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired proposition (al-matlub)
The proposition the argument aims to prove. In the passage, it is simply called 'the desired'. Recognize what is being proven.
2
Identify the opposite (naqid) and the conditional premise
The argument assumes: 'If the desired were not realized, then its opposite would be realized.' This is the antecedent of the conditional. The consequent is the opposite.
3
Recognize the first syllogism: exceptive connective (modus tollens)
The passage states: 'But the consequent is false, so the antecedent is false.' This is modus tollens: If P then Q; not Q, so not P. Here P = 'the desired is not realized', Q = 'the opposite is realized'. Not Q means the opposite is false, so not P: the desired is realized.
4
Recognize the second syllogism: categorical syllogism proving the absurdity of the opposite
The passage argues: 'The opposite of the desired entails an absurdity; whatever entails an absurdity is absurd; therefore the opposite of the desired is absurd.' This is a categorical syllogism: Major premise: Everything that entails an absurdity is absurd. Minor: The opposite of the desired entails an absurdity. Conclusion: The opposite of the desired is absurd.
5
Combine both syllogisms to see the full proof
The two syllogisms work together: The categorical shows the opposite is absurd (i.e., false), which then serves as the minor premise (negation of consequent) of the conditional modus tollens, yielding the desired conclusion. This compound structure is the essence of qiyas al-khalf.

The threefold division of argument is exhaustive because the fourth case is logically impossible.

This passage from a logical hashya defends the standard tripartite division of argument (hujja) into syllogism (qiyas), induction (istiqra'), and analogy (tamthil) against an objection that a fourfold division is necessary. Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector argues that based on whether the evidence and the inferred are each universal or particular, there should be 2x2 = 4 divisions, including inference from universal to universal (kulli ila kulli). Find the objector's words in the Arabic (fa-in qulta...).
The objector is unnamed but represents a standard logical challenge.

The objector pairs the two terms: mustadall bihi (evidence) and mustadall 'alayhi (thing inferred), each either universal or particular. Crossing these gives four possibilities, which seem to require four types of argument.
This is a simple combinatorial argument.

The author first splits two universals into two cases: one is subsumed under the other, or not. If subsumed, two subtypes (subsuming to subsumed = qiyas; subsumed to subsuming = istiqra'). If not subsumed, either they share a common proximate universal (tamthil) or not (impossible).

Thus, no fourth type is needed because the only missing combination is logically impossible. The three types exhaust all possible valid inferences.

Core Idea
A formal fallacy breaks the productive shape of the syllogism, not the truth of its premises.

In this lesson, we examine the classification of formal fallacies (al-mughalata al-sura) in categorical syllogisms. The author distinguishes three ways a syllogism can fail to be productive: the middle term not being repeated, violation of quantity/quality conditions, and violation of modality. Guided Path

Locate the middle term

Proving the Introduction is External to a Science via Compound Syllogism
Author provides three syllogisms proving that the introductory material is external to the science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound syllogism proving the introduction is not part of the science and assess its validity.

Core Idea
If the introduction were part of the science, beginning it would depend on itself—an impossibility.

In this passage, the commentator constructs a compound argument to prove that the introductory material (muqaddima) of a science lies outside that science. This is a classic demonstration of how logical reasoning prevents circular definitions in Islamic scholarly disciplines. The argument uses three linked syllogisms: a conditional, a categorical, and a final combined syllogism that reveals an absurd self-dependence. By walking through each step, you will see how compound syllogistic reasoning secures a foundational principle of scientific methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the opponent's assumption
The claim to prove is that the introduction (muqaddima) is not part of the science. The opponent assumes it is part. The proof will show that this assumption leads to impossible self-dependence.
2
Analyze the first syllogism (conditional)
The first is a conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i). The antecedent: 'If the introduction is part of the science, then beginning the introduction is beginning the science.' The consequent is affirmed by the opponent's assumption. Therefore it results that beginning the introduction is beginning the science.
This is modus ponens: P→Q, P ⊢ Q.
3
Analyze the second syllogism (categorical)
The second is a categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani). Premises: 'Beginning the science depends on something' and 'The introduction, being theoretical, depends on beginning it.' From these we conclude that beginning the science depends on beginning the introduction.
Identify the middle term: 'beginning the introduction' connects the premises.
4
Combine the two conclusions (third syllogism)
The third syllogism takes the conclusions of the first two as premises: (1) Beginning the introduction is beginning the science. (2) Beginning the science depends on beginning the introduction. Therefore: beginning the introduction depends on beginning the introduction (self-dependence). This is the desired impossibility (muhal) that refutes the opponent's assumption.
This is a compound syllogism: the conclusions of earlier syllogisms become premises for the final one.
5
Evaluate the absurdity and the proof's strength
Self-dependence (tawqif al-shay' 'ala nafsihi) is impossible by self-evident necessity (darura). Therefore the assumption that the introduction is part of the science must be false. The proof is valid and uses only necessary premises.
The commentator also cites Ibn Sina's al-Shifa' as authority.

When you encounter a categorical syllogism, you need to know which premise is minor (sughra) and which is major (kubra). 1

The common part between a premise and the conclusion is the shared term. The minor premise shares the subject; the major premise shares the predicate. The middle term is the term repeated in both premises but absent from the conclusion.

Method of Finding the Middle Term in Categorical Syllogisms
Explains step-by-step method for finding the middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the step-by-step method for identifying the middle term in a categorical syllogism and determine whether the syllogism is simple or compound.

Core Idea
Find the shared term between the subject and predicate of the conclusion, and you have found the middle term.

Constructing a valid categorical syllogism requires a middle term that links the two extremes. This passage from the commentary tradition outlines a systematic method for finding that middle term: list all subjects and predicates of each extreme, then look for a term that appears in both sets. If found, you have a simple first-figure syllogism; if not, you need additional premises, making the syllogism compound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extremes of the desired conclusion
Write down the subject (asghar) and predicate (akbar) of the proposition you want to prove. For example, to prove 'human is animal', the subject is 'human' and the predicate is 'animal'.
2
List all subjects and predicates of each extreme
Gather everything that can be predicated of the subject (e.g., human: rational, laughing, walking) and everything that the predicate can be predicated of (e.g., animal: horse, cow, sensitive). Also note negations if relevant.
This step ensures you have a comprehensive set of potential middle terms.
3
Check for a shared term
Look for a term that appears both as a predicate of the subject and as a subject of the predicate (or vice versa). This term is the middle term. In the example, 'laughing' is a predicate of human and a subject of animal.
The middle term must appear in both premises but not in the conclusion.
4
Form the syllogism and verify the figure
Construct the premises: minor premise (subject – middle), major premise (middle – predicate). Check the figure by the position of the middle term. If the middle is predicate in minor and subject in major, it is the first figure, which naturally yields the conclusion.
If the middle term does not produce a valid figure, you may need to convert a premise or adjust.
5
If no direct middle term, repeat iteratively
If the relation between the two extremes is not immediately clear, introduce an intermediate term and seek a middle for the new pair. Continue until you find a chain that directly produces the conclusion. This results in a compound syllogism.
For example, if the desired is 'every A is T' and you have 'every A is B' and 'every H is T', but no middle between B and H, then find a term D such that 'every B is D' and 'every D is H', and so on.

Determining Syllogism Figures by Middle Term Position
Gloss explains how middle term position determines the four figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the figure of a categorical syllogism by identifying the position of the middle term in the two premises.

Core Idea
The figure of a syllogism is defined solely by the middle term's position in the premises.

This lesson introduces the foundational rule for classifying categorical syllogisms into four figures. The figure depends entirely on whether the middle term is the subject or predicate in each premise. Mastering this classification is essential for evaluating syllogistic validity in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term (wasat)
The middle term appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. For example, in 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; nabidh is an intoxicant,' the term 'intoxicant' is the middle term.
The middle term mediates the connection between the major and minor terms.
2
Check the position in the first premise (kubra)
Look at the first premise (the one stated first in the Arabic order). Is the middle term its subject (mawdu') or its predicate (mahmul)?
Typically the first premise is the major premise, but position order can vary.
3
Check the position in the second premise (sughra)
Look at the second premise. Is the middle term its subject or predicate?
The second premise is usually the minor premise.
4
Apply the figure classification rule
Use the gloss's rule: If middle term is predicate in first premise and subject in second, it is the first figure. If predicate in both, second figure. If subject in both, third figure. If subject in first and predicate in second, fourth figure.
This covers all four possibilities.

Method of Seeking Middle Terms
Gloss on the method of seeking middle terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to systematically find middle terms for any given syllogistic pair by collecting all possible subjects and predicates.

Core Idea
Finding the middle term requires examining all subjects and predicates of both ends.

When constructing a syllogism to prove a desired conclusion, the middle term is the key. This gloss reveals a systematic method: take the subject and predicate of the conclusion, then list every term that can be a subject or predicate of each. This exhaustive search ensures you find a valid middle term, even in complex cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two ends
Take the subject (al-mawḍūʿ) and predicate (al-maḥmūl) of the desired conclusion. These are the two extremeties your syllogism must connect.
For example, in 'The world is temporally originated', the ends are 'the world' and 'temporally originated'.
2
Collect all subjects of each end
For each term, think of every other term that can serve as its subject in a true proposition. This includes the term itself if applicable, and broader categories.
Subjects of 'the world' might include 'every created thing', 'everything temporally originated', etc.
3
Collect all predicates of each end
Similarly, for each term, list every predicate that can be truly asserted of it. This includes its essential and accidental attributes.
Predicates of 'the world' might include 'created', 'changing', 'dependent', etc.
4
Find a common term
Compare the two lists from steps 2 and 3. Any term appearing in both lists is a potential middle term. That term will appear as a predicate in one premise and as a subject in the other.
If 'created' appears in the subjects of 'temporally originated' and in the predicates of 'the world', it can serve as the middle term.
5
Verify the syllogism
Check that the chosen middle term yields valid premises that are true and that the conclusion follows according to the figures.
The middle term must be distributed appropriately and the syllogism must be in a valid mood.

Linking Premises to the Conclusion's Extremes in Compound Syllogisms
Gloss on the necessity of premise-conclusion term linkage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to verify whether each premise in a compound syllogism relates to the subject or predicate of the conclusion, and explain why this condition is necessary for validity.

Core Idea
A premise disconnected from the conclusion's extremes cannot help derive that conclusion.

Yazdi's gloss clarifies a critical condition for compound syllogisms: each intermediate premise must be linked to either the subject or the predicate of the desired conclusion. This follows from the fundamental rule that a valid syllogism must contain the conclusion's two extreme terms. Without this link, the premise is irrelevant and the syllogism fails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion's extremes
Determine the subject (موضوع) and predicate (محمول) of the final conclusion you want to prove.
The conclusion is the desired claim (مطلوب).
2
Examine each premise for these terms
For each premise in the compound syllogism, check if it contains the subject term or the predicate term of the conclusion. It does not need to contain both—just one is enough.
3
Assess the link
If a premise contains neither the subject nor the predicate of the conclusion, that premise cannot contribute to deriving that conclusion. The syllogism will not produce the desired result.
The previous principle stated that a valid syllogism must include both ends of the conclusion; here we apply it to each part.
4
Explain the necessity
The syllogism's middle term must connect the two extremes. If a premise lacks both extremes, it cannot serve as a bridge because it has no connection to the conclusion's terms.

Iterative Search for a Middle Term in Syllogisms
Gloss illustrates step-by-step method to find middle term by repeating the process.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the iterative method of middle term search to construct a chain of categorical syllogisms for a desired conclusion.

Core Idea
Finding the middle term may require repeated attempts until a connecting chain is built.

Constructing a syllogism is not always a single step. Often, after identifying two initial premises, you find no common term between them. This lesson introduces a systematic method: if no middle term is found initially, introduce a new term and repeat the search. This iterative process ensures that you ultimately build a chain of syllogisms that yields the desired conclusion. The gloss below illustrates this method with abstract letters, which we will apply to concrete Islamic examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired conclusion
Start with what you want to prove, e.g., 'Every A is T'. This is your target (matlub).
2
Find two initial premises
Locate the two statements you already have. In the example, they are 'Every A is B' and 'Every H is T'. These are your starting premises.
These premises should be known or accepted.
3
Search for a common term between the premises
Look for a term that appears in one premise and connects to the other. Here, we need a term that links B and H. If found, the syllogism is complete.
The common term becomes the middle term (hadd al-awsat).
4
If no common term, introduce a new term
If B and H have no direct relation, introduce a new term D such that a premise like 'Every D is H' is true. Then you have B and D to connect.
The new premise must be true or accepted.
5
Repeat the process until the chain is complete
Now seek a middle term between B and D. Continue introducing new terms and premises until you form a chain from A through intermediate terms to T. The final chain gives a series of syllogisms producing the desired conclusion.
The process ends when you reach a syllogism that is productive (muntij bil-dhat) without requiring further premises.
