Resolving Contradictions in Hadith: The Case of Basmala and Hamdala
Author raises and answers two objections to a hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical objection-response argument in the Islamic tradition, identifying logical problems and evaluating the strength of the answers.

Core Idea
Refining key terms and classifying actions resolves apparent hadith contradictions.

In this lesson, we examine a classic dialectical passage where the author presents two logical objections to the hadiths urging the basmala and hamda at the start of actions. The objections involve circularity, infinite regress, and contradiction. The author offers several answers, including a nuanced redefinition of 'dhu bal' and a classification of 'ibtida''. This shows how careful logical analysis can resolve tensions between revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two hadiths and the apparent problem
The hadiths command beginning important matters with basmala and hamda. The problem: if both are matters of importance (dhu bal), then each would need another beginning, leading to circularity or regress.
The term 'dhu bal' is key; it can mean 'matter of importance' either literally or as 'what occupies the heart'.
2
Understand the first objection and its two consequences
First objection: If basmala and hamda are dhu bal, they must be begun with something similar, leading to either circularity (return to the same) or infinite regress. Also, one must begin with the other, creating a similar problem.
Circularity (دور) and regress (تسلسل) are standard logical critiques.
3
Examine the first two answers to the first objection
Answer (a): Everything that exists through another must end with something self-existent (analogy of divine will and human will). Answer (b): The universal statement is specified by context. Both only address the first consequence (circularity/regress), not the second (one beginning with the other).
The author notes these answers are insufficient for the full objection.
4
Identify the preferred answer for the first objection
The preferred answer redefines 'dhu bal' in the hadith to mean 'what is intended by itself'. Basmala and hamda are not intended by themselves; they are means, so they are outside the scope.
This resolves both consequences without circularity or regress.
5
Analyze the second objection and its answer
Second objection: Acting on one hadith (e.g., beginning with basmala) annuls the other (hamda). The answer, summarized by the commentator, divides 'ibtida'' (beginning) into three types, though the text cuts off before listing them.
The classification of ibtida' shows the hadiths apply to different contexts, removing contradiction.

It is necessary that their beginning be with something like them, meaning that the beginning of the basmala must be with another similar to it, and the beginning of the hamda with another similar to it, and so on. Also, it necessitates that one of them begins with the other, so one of the two occurs again. The second: that acting on one necessitates annulling the other. Because it is inevitable: either one begins with the basmala, so the hadith of tahmeed is annulled; or with the hamda, so the hadith of tasmiya is annulled. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exploration of two problems (shkal) in understanding the hadiths that encourage beginning actions with the basmala and the hamda. The first problem is that if both basmala and hamda are themselves matters of importance (dhu bal), then they themselves must be begun with something similar, leading to circularity or infinite regress. The second problem is that the two hadiths seem to contradict each other, as acting on one annuls the other. The author presents two answers to the first problem. The first answer is that everything that exists through another must ultimately rest on something self-existent, using the example of the hadith about divine will (mashiyya) and the analogy of human will. The second answer is that the universal statement is specified by context, so the hadith does not apply to its own components. However, these answers only address the first necessary consequence (circularity/regress), not the second necessary consequence (that one must begin with the other). The preferred answer is that 'dhu bal' in the hadith is not meant literally for every important matter, but only for things that are intended by themselves (maqsuda bi-al-dhat). The basmala and hamda are not intended by themselves when reciting them; they are the means, so they are exempt. For the second problem, the answer is summarized by the commentator (al-muhashi) as dividing 'ibtida'' (beginning) into three types, which the text cuts off before fully listing. This is a typical extension of jadal (dialectical) reasoning.

Distinguishing Real and Relative Hasr in Dialectical Defense
Author defends hasr claim against objection about Imams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real (haqiqi) and relative (idafi) hasr and apply this distinction to resolve objections in dialectical arguments.

Core Idea
Hasr is not always absolute; relative hasr helps defend exclusivity claims without contradiction.

In this passage, the author faces a powerful objection: if following the Prophet is exclusively suitable, what about following the Imams? He uses the logical distinction between real and relative hasr to protect the original claim without denying the Imams' role. You will learn how this distinction functions and how to deploy it in your own theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the exact objection: following the Imams is also suitable, so exclusivity is false. Note how the objection targets the universal applicability of the hasr claim.
The objection assumes hasr must be real and universal.
2
Analyze the first response: identity
The author denies a distinction between following the Imams and following the Prophet, arguing they are identical because the Imams manifest the Prophet's Sharia. If identical, exclusivity is preserved.
This response resolves the objection immediately if accepted.
3
Analyze the second response: relative hasr
Even if a difference is conceded, the author introduces the two types of hasr: real (absolute) and relative (comparative). The hasr here is relative to other prophets, not to all people including Imams.
Relative hasr restricts exclusivity to a specific category, avoiding contradiction.
4
Apply the distinction to similar cases
Practice using relative hasr to defend other exclusivity claims, e.g., 'Only the Qur'an is the word of God' — relative to other books, but not excluding hadith qudsi.
Think of categories: relative to what?

His saying: 'And as for following the Imams (peace be upon them) — it is as if it is a rebuttal of an illusion arising from his saying: 'And in that case, fronting the adverb is for the purpose of hasr.' The gist of the question: Following the twelve Imams (peace be upon them) is also becoming to us, as is not hidden, so it is not valid to confine suitability to following the Prophet (peace be upon him). The gist of the rebuttal: We do not concede a difference between them, for following the Imams (peace be upon them) is identical to following the Prophet (peace be upon him), because it is evident that they were the manifestations of his Sharia and the conveyers of his rulings to people. And even if one concedes a difference, we say: the hasr is relative (idafi) with respect to other prophets, not real (haqiqi) with respect to all people. Explanation: hasr is of two kinds: real and relative. The specification of a thing by a thing is either according to reality and fact such that it does not extend to anything else at all — that is real; or according to relation and proportion to another thing such that it does not extend to that other — that is relative. In our case, either the hasr is real with respect to all people considering that following the Imams is following him (as above), or it is relative with respect to other prophets, so the objection does not arise requiring an answer. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical defense of the claim that fronting the adverb in the phrase 'He sent him with guidance' indicates hasr (exclusivity) of suitability in following the Prophet. The objection raised is that following the twelve Imams is also suitable, so how can suitability be exclusive to the Prophet? The author gives two responses. First, he denies the distinction: following the Imams is effectively the same as following the Prophet because the Imams are manifestations of the Prophet's Sharia. So the exclusivity holds for the Prophet, and through him, the Imams are included. Second, even if one insists on a distinction, the hasr can be understood as relative (idafi) rather than absolute (haqiqi). Relative hasr means it is exclusive compared to other categories — here, compared to other prophets. The exclusivity of suitability is only relative to other prophets, not to all people including Imams. Since the Imams are not separate from the Prophet in terms of guidance, the objection is neutralized. This shows the author's skill in applying logical concepts like real vs. relative exclusivity to theological discussions.

Defending an Implicit Division in Commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify implicit textual evidence for a division in a commentary and use it to refute objections about misplaced examples.

Core Idea
Implicit textual evidence can refute objections about misplaced examples.

In this passage, the commentator defends the author's placement of an example by showing that the book's division into logic and theology was implied earlier. The objection was that an example should follow an explicit division, but the commentator finds the division hinted in a previous phrase. This illustrates a key skill in reading commentaries: recognizing that authors often signal structures indirectly.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange defending the author's placement of his example. The commentator begins by analyzing the particle 'lammā', noting its grammatical properties and a debate about its nature. He then focuses on the main objection: someone might claim that the author's example did not fall in its proper place because normally an example is given after a division, but the author had not explicitly divided his book into two parts. The commentator answers that the author did indicate the division implicitly in his statement 'this is the limit of refinement in the editing of logic and theology', which shows the book has two parts: logic (mantiq) and theology (kalam). Thus the example is appropriately placed. The commentator then relates this to the distinction between the first section (defined) and the introduction (indefinite). He notes a disagreement about the commentator's intention, and concludes with a critical note that the commentator's statement about not needing explicit mention is inconsistent with the requirement to replace the branched statement. Finally, a marginal note (131) reiterates that the author did not need to make the division explicit because it is implied.

Dialectic on the Purpose of Definition: Completeness as a Spectrum
Objection and answer about definition completeness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objector's argument and the author's response regarding the purpose of definition, and identify when including additional parts (property, general accident) yields a more complete definition.

Core Idea
Definition aims at essence or distinction, both achieved in degrees – more parts mean more completeness.

In this passage, the author anticipates an objection: if the purpose of definition is simply to grasp the essence or to distinguish the thing from all others, why do we need all those combinations of parts? Couldn't we just use the closest genus and differentia? The author's answer reveals a key insight: completeness is not binary – it is a spectrum, and fuller definitions include additional elements like property or general accident to achieve a more detailed or comprehensive understanding. This refines our understanding of what definitions are for.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two purposes of definition
The objector states that the purpose of definition is either knowledge of the essence (al-ittila' 'ala al-kunh) or distinction from everything else (al-imtiyaz). These are the standard aims.
2
Extract the objector's reasoning
The objector argues that these purposes are achieved by the simplest parts: proximate genus and proximate differentia (for hadd) or proximate genus and property (for rasm). Therefore, there is no need for combinations involving remote genus, general accident, etc.
The objector claims the simpler combinations suffice, rendering the more complex ones redundant.
3
Analyze the author's response
The author counters that each purpose can be achieved in varying aspects (wujuh mutafawwita), some more complete (akmal) than others in terms of detail and generality. So if one wants the most complete form, one includes additional parts like property in a hadd or general accident in a rasm.
The author introduces the idea of gradation in completeness.

The Purpose of Jadal: Compelling the Opponent
Author explains the purpose of dialectic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the purpose of jadal (dialectic) and identify how conceded premises (tasleem) function in dialectical arguments using Islamic science examples.

Core Idea
Jadal compels an opponent using premises he accepts, not absolute truth.

In this passage, the author clarifies that dialectic is not about establishing truth per se, but about forcing an opponent to accept a conclusion based on premises he himself concedes. This distinction is crucial for understanding how debates work in Islamic theology, jurisprudence, and grammar. We will explore the two examples given to see how conceded premises are leveraged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the stated purpose of jadal
Read the first line: the purpose is to compel and convince the opponent, regardless of the truth of the premises. Note that the goal is not certainty but persuasion using what the opponent already accepts.
This contrasts with burhan (demonstration), which uses true premises.
2
Understand the role of tasleem (concession)
Concession (tasleem) is when the opponent accepts a premise, making it binding in the debate. The arguer can then use that premise without proving it anew.
The passage emphasizes that the inference is built 'on the basis of concession' (على سبيل التسليم).
3
Analyze the fiqh example
A jurist uses a khabar al-wahid (single-transmitter report) to support a claim. The opponent cannot object that khabar al-wahid is impermissible because it has already been conceded in usul al-fiqh that acting on it is allowed. Thus the premise is binding.
This shows how premises from one field (usul) are taken as conceded in another (fiqh).
4
Analyze the grammar example
A literary scholar argues that conjunction (العطف) in a line by Abu Tammam is unacceptable because the two items lack a common aspect (جهة جامعة). The opponent must accept the rule about conjunction if it was conceded earlier in al-ma'ani.
The condition for acceptable waw-conjunction is a conceded premise.
5
Distinguish jadal from burhan
The passage ends by noting that jadal also serves to convince those incapable of grasping demonstrative proofs (burhan). So jadal is a tool for broader persuasion, not limited to experts.
This reminds us that jadal is one of the five arts of logic, with its own rules.

Learning Objective

Jadal uses borrowed premises as granted to compel opponents, not to prove certainty.

The example given is fiqh using usul al-fiqh and nahw as premises. Identifying Rhetorical Syllogisms
Author gives examples of dialectical premises and a rhetorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a rhetorical syllogism and distinguish it from a valid categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Rhetorical syllogisms persuade but do not prove — their premises are plausible, not universal.

The author gives examples of premises used in dialectic and rhetoric, taken from authoritative figures like prophets and sages. Then he provides two rhetorical syllogisms that appear categorical but lack universal premises. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic discourse, where rhetoric is used for persuasion and sound qiyas for proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
In the first example, the conclusion is 'this wall is collapsing.' In the second, 'he is a thief.' These are the claims the syllogism tries to establish.
2
Find the minor and major premises
For the wall example: minor = 'This wall has dust spreading from it.' Major = 'Every wall that has dust is collapsing.' For the thief example: minor = 'So-and-so walks at night.' Major = 'Everyone who walks at night is a thief.'
3
Check the universality of the major premise
A valid categorical syllogism requires a universal major premise (e.g., 'All A are B'). Here, 'Every wall that has dust is collapsing' is not universally true—many walls have dust but do not collapse. Similarly, not all night-walkers are thieves. The premises are plausible but not certain.
4
Assess logical validity
The syllogisms are not formally valid because the major premise is not a true universal statement. Validity requires that if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. Here, the premises could be true and the conclusion false.
5
Classify as rhetorical
These are rhetorical syllogisms (قياس خطابي): they use widely accepted or plausible premises to persuade, not to produce certain knowledge. In Islamic logic, they belong to the art of rhetoric (خطابة).

In the study of dialectic (jadal), premises are classified into certain (yaqiniyat) and probable (zanniyat). This short passage from a hashīya clarifies a crucial point: when both maznunat and maqbulat are listed together (as in the five arts), the term 'maznunat' is used in a restricted sense to mean only the non-accepted opinions.
