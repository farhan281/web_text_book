Burhan Limmi vs Burhan Inni: Demonstration by Cause and by Effect
Author distinguishes demonstration by cause (limmi) from demonstration by effect (inni)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish burhan limmi from burhan inni by identifying whether the middle term is a cause in reality or only a cause of knowledge, and give an Islamic example of each.

Core Idea
Demonstration by cause gives the reason 'why'; demonstration by effect only gives 'that it is'.

In demonstration, the middle term is always the cause of our knowledge of the conclusion. But logicians ask: is it also the cause of the truth of the conclusion in reality? This distinction yields two types of demonstration: limmi (giving the 'why') and inni (giving only the 'that'). This lesson unpacks that distinction using classical medical examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term in the syllogism
In the example 'This person has putrefied humors; everyone with putrefied humors has a fever; therefore this person has a fever', the middle term is 'putrefied humors'. It appears in both premises.
2
Determine if the middle term is a cause in reality
In this case, putrefied humors are the real cause of the fever (in medieval humoral theory). So the middle term is both a cause of knowledge and a cause in reality. This makes it a limmi demonstration.
3
Contrast with a case where the middle term is only a cause of knowledge
Consider 'Zayd has a fever; everyone with a fever has putrefied humors; therefore Zayd has putrefied humors'. Here fever is the middle term; it is an effect of the real cause (putrefied humors). So it is inni, not limmi.
This is sometimes called dalil (sign).
4
Recognize the third subtype of inni
If the middle term and the predicate are both effects of a third cause, it is also inni but has no special name. Example: 'This fever intensifies daily; every fever that intensifies daily is burning; therefore this fever is burning' – both intensification and burning are effects of putrefied bile.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes two types of demonstrative syllogisms (burhan) based on the nature of the middle term. In every syllogism, the middle term is the cause of our knowledge of the conclusion (wasita fi al-ithbat). But it may also be a cause in reality (wasita fi al-thubut). If it is both, the demonstration is called 'burhan limmi', which gives the reason 'why' (lima), e.g., putrefied humors cause fever. If the middle term is only a cause of knowledge but not a real cause, it is 'burhan inni', which gives only that the judgment exists (anna). This is further divided: the middle may be an effect of the conclusion (e.g., fever as a sign of putrefied humors, called 'dalil'), or both may be effects of a third cause (e.g., daily intensification of fever and burning fever both caused by putrefied bile). The author uses medical examples from Islamic medicine, which are appropriate. This distinction is crucial for evaluating whether a demonstration gives causal explanation or merely empirical indication.

Understanding Fitriyyat: Intuitive Propositions in Demonstration
Commentator defines fitriyyat using the example of 'four is even'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify fitriyyat propositions and explain their epistemological role in demonstration.

Core Idea
Fitriyyat are so self-evident that the middle term is instantly grasped.

The commentator begins by explaining 'al-fitriyyat' (intuitive propositions) using the simple example 'four is even'. This sets the stage for understanding how demonstrations rely on immediate premises that require no external proof.

Guided Path
1
Examine the Example
The commentator gives the proposition 'four is even' as a fitri proposition. Note that the judgment is immediate.
2
Identify the Hidden Middle Term
Even though the judgment feels immediate, there is a middle term: 'divisible into two equal parts'. This term is present in the mind without reflection.
The middle term 'disappears from the mind' only in the sense of being instantly present, not absent.
3
Understand the Fitri Character
The middle term is inherent (fitri) to the human disposition; it does not require external evidence. This makes fitriyyat a subclass of awwaliyyat (primary principles).
Fitriyyat are distinguished from awwaliyyat by the presence of an immediate middle term.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the original text's classification of syllogisms, specifically concerning the premises of demonstration and the two types of demonstrative syllogisms. The commentator first explains 'al-fitriyyat' (intuitive propositions) using the example 'four is even.' The judgment that four is even relies on an intermediary (the property of being divisible into two equal parts), but this intermediary is so immediately grasped that it does not leave the mind; thus fitriyyat are self-evident premises. Then the commentator turns to the distinction between two types of demonstration, defined by the role of the middle term. The middle term in any syllogism must be a cause of our knowledge of the conclusion (the 'intermediary in proof'). If, in addition, the middle term is a cause of the conclusion in reality (wāsiṭa fī al-thubūt), then the demonstration is called 'burhān limī' — a demonstration of the reason (why). For example, when we prove that someone is feverish because his humors are putrefied, the putrefaction is both the cause in the mind and the real cause. If the middle term is not a real cause, either being an effect of the conclusion or both being effects of a third factor, then the demonstration is called 'burhān innī' — a demonstration of the existence (that). The commentator gives examples for both subcases: the effect-as-middle (fever as evidence of putrefied humors, called a 'dalīl') and the common-effect case (intermittent intensity as a sign of burning fever, both caused by yellow bile). This careful distinction is crucial in Islamic logic for evaluating certainty: limī proofs yield understanding of why something is true, while innī proofs only establish that it is true.

Two Types of Demonstration: Limi and Inni
Commentator distinguishes limi and inni demonstrations with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan limi and burhan inni by analyzing the role of the middle term in reality.

Core Idea
The middle term can be a real cause or only a cognitive cause.

The commentator expands on the text's classification of demonstrations. He explains that the middle term must always be a cause of knowledge, but it may or may not be a cause in reality. This yields two types: limi (why) and inni (that).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Role of the Middle Term
The commentator states that in every syllogism, the middle term is a cause of knowing the conclusion (wāsiṭa fī al-ithbāt). This is universal.
2
Check the Real Causal Connection
If the middle term is also a cause in reality (wāsiṭa fī al-thubūt), the demonstration is limi. If not, it is inni.
Limi = demonstration of the reason; inni = demonstration of existence.
3
Examine the Subcases of Inni
Inni is divided into two: when the middle term is an effect of the conclusion (called dalīl), and when both are effects of a third cause.
The commentator gives examples: fever as effect of putrefied humors, and intermittent intensity as common effect of yellow bile.
4
Apply the Distinction
Practice by classifying given syllogisms. For example, 'Zayd has a fever, so he has putrefied humors' is inni (middle term is effect).

The Impossibility of Infinite Regress and Circularity in Knowledge
Author presents the argument against infinite regress in knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that some knowledge must be self-evident by showing that infinite regress and circularity are impossible.

Core Idea
Without self-evident knowledge, all reasoning collapses into infinite regress or circularity.

In this passage, the commentator refines the classic proof that at least some knowledge must be self-evident. The argument shows that if all knowledge were theoretical (acquired through reasoning), we would face an impossible infinite regress or circularity. Understanding this argument is crucial for any systematic epistemology in the Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two options
The author considers the possibility that all concepts and assents (tasawwurat and tasdiqat) could be either self-evident or theoretical. He argues that they cannot all be self-evident because we clearly need reasoning for some knowledge, nor can they all be theoretical, because that would lead to impossible consequences.
2
Extract the conditional argument
The core argument is: If all knowledge is theoretical, then either infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr) follows. Both consequences are impossible, so the antecedent is false. This is a modus tollens argument.
Formally: (All theoretical → (Regress ∨ Circular)) ∧ ¬(Regress ∨ Circular) → ¬(All theoretical)
3
Analyze the infinite regress case
If knowledge A depends on B, B on C, and so on without end, then the acquisition of knowledge would require an infinite chain of dependencies that is never completed. The text demonstrates this with variables: if A depends on B, B on C, and C on A (circularity), or if the chain never ends, then a thing would be prior to itself, which is impossible.
The text uses a formal demonstration with A, B, C to show the contradiction.
4
Analyze the circularity case
If the chain returns to its starting point (e.g., A depends on B, B depends on A), then A is prior to itself, a direct contradiction. This is also impossible.
Both regress and circularity are shown to be impossible, thus the assumption that all knowledge is theoretical is false.
5
Conclude that some knowledge is self-evident
Since not all knowledge can be theoretical, and not all can be self-evident (as we need reasoning for some), it follows that knowledge is divided into two types: self-evident (badihi) and theoretical (nazari). The argument thus establishes the foundation for all subsequent knowledge acquisition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the classic proof that some knowledge must be self-evident. The commentator begins by restating the argument of the majority: not all concepts and assents can be self-evident because we need reasoning for some; nor can all be theoretical (acquired through reasoning) because that leads to an infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr). The author explains the two cases in detail. For infinite regress, he uses a formal example with variables (A, B, C): if A depends on B, B on C, and C on A, then A is prior to itself, which is impossible. This is a direct contradiction. For circularity, it requires the mind to have an infinite number of prior knowledges, which is impossible. Then the commentator addresses a potential objection from philosophers who believe in an eternal soul: they might argue that infinite regress could occur over infinite time, so it's not impossible. The response is threefold: if the objection means infinite in finite time, it's denied; if it means infinite in infinite time, it's granted but then the actualization of the dependent knowledge now would still require an infinite chain that is never completed, which is impossible. The commentator then adds a note that this entire argument rests on the assumption that concepts cannot be acquired from assents or vice versa; if that assumption fails, the argument might not hold. Finally, he gives concrete examples of self-evident knowledge: the concept of heat and the judgment that fire is hot. These occur spontaneously without effort, demonstrating that not all knowledge is theoretical. The passage is a nuanced defense of foundationalism in epistemology, typical of Islamic logic.

Responding to the Objection of Infinite Regress Over Infinite Time
Author addresses objection of infinite regress over infinite time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the objection that infinite regress in knowledge is possible if the soul is eternal and time is infinite.

Core Idea
Even an eternal soul cannot overcome the logical impossibility of an infinite regress.

Some philosophers argued that an infinite regress in knowledge could be avoided if the soul were eternal and could accumulate knowledge over infinite past time. The commentator addresses this objection by denying the possibility of an eternal soul in the Islamic worldview and by showing that even if it were possible, the regress would still never be actualized. This lesson examines the refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims: 'The only impossibility is bringing to mind infinite things in a finite time. But if the soul is eternal and time is infinite, the soul could have acquired an infinite series of knowledges over infinite past time. Therefore, infinite regress is not impossible.'
2
Understand the two-part response
The commentator responds in two ways: (1) If the objection means that infinite things are brought to mind in one time or finite times, it is denied. (2) If it means in infinite times, it is granted that the series could exist, but the actual acquisition of the dependent knowledge now would still require an infinite chain that is never completed, because the past is not actually infinite in the Islamic view.
The response relies on the Islamic doctrine that the world and souls are created, not eternal.
3
Consider the deeper assumption
The commentator notes that the entire argument rests on the assumption that concepts cannot be acquired from assents and vice versa. If that assumption fails, the argument might not hold. This shows the commentator's intellectual honesty and depth.

The Principle of Causality in Logical Demonstration
Author defends the necessity of lunar eclipse against an objection by establishing a causal relation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A necessary connection arises when the effect is tied to its cause, so removing the cause makes the effect impossible.

1

Core Idea

In Islamic logic, demonstrations are classified by the direction of reasoning. The author defines two fundamental types: al-limmi, which moves from cause to effect, and al-inniy, which moves from effect to cause. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the strength of proofs.
