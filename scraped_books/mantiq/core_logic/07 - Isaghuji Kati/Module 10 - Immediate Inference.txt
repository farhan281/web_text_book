The Five Unities for Contradiction of Particular Propositions
Author enumerates five of the eight unities required for contradiction between two particular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the first five unities required for contradiction between two particular propositions, using the examples from the passage.

Core Idea
Contradiction needs more than affirmation and negation; eight unities must agree.

The author has explained that contradiction between two propositions depends on classification. Now he unpacks the special conditions for particular propositions (mukhṣaṣāt), introducing the eight unities. This lesson covers the first five: subject, predicate, time, place, and relation. You will see how each unity prevents false contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Recall that propositions are particular (mukhṣaṣa), quantified, or unrestricted. The author focuses on particular ones, e.g., 'Zayd is standing'.
2
Understand the first unity: subject
The two propositions must have the same subject. Otherwise, like 'Zayd is standing' vs 'Amr is not standing', there is no contradiction because they are about different individuals.
3
Understand the second unity: predicate
They must have the same predicate. 'Zayd is a writer' vs 'Zayd is not a poet' differ in predicate (writer vs poet), so no contradiction.
4
Understand the third and fourth unities: time and place
Time: 'Zayd is standing at night' vs 'Zayd is not standing during the day' differ in time. Place: 'Zayd is standing in the house' vs 'Zayd is not standing in the market' differ in place. Either difference blocks contradiction.
5
Recognize the fifth unity: relation (iḍāfa)
The propositions must agree in any relational qualification. For example, 'Zayd is father of Amr' vs 'Zayd is not father of Bakr' differ in relation (father to Amr vs not to Bakr).
The passage stops here; the remaining three unities (part/whole, condition, potency/act) are covered elsewhere.

Eight Conditions for Genuine Contradiction in Singular Propositions
Author lists eight conditions for contradiction in singular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two singular propositions are genuinely contradictory by checking all eight unities (subject, predicate, time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality, part/whole, and condition).

Core Idea
Contradiction requires more than opposite truth values; eight agreements must hold.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the matn's statement that for two singular (mukhassasatān) propositions, contradiction is not realized except after agreement in eight aspects. These aspects prevent false contradictions that arise from differing contexts, such as time or place. By learning these conditions, you will be able to distinguish true logical contradictions from mere semantic differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Find the two statements that are claimed to be contradictory. Ensure they are singular (about a specific individual).
2
Check unity of subject and predicate
Verify that both propositions refer to the same individual and have the same predicate (or its negation). If different, they are not contradictory.
3
Check unity of time, place, and relation
Ensure the propositions refer to the same time, location, and relational context. If not, contradiction fails.
4
Check unity of potentiality/actuality, part/whole, and condition
Examine whether one proposition speaks of potentiality and the other of actuality, or one of a part and the other of the whole, or one under one condition and the other under a different condition. If so, no contradiction.
5
Apply all eight simultaneously
Given a pair of singular propositions, go through each unity. If all eight are satisfied, they are contradictories; otherwise, they are not.
For example, 'Zaid is standing at night' and 'Zaid is not standing during the day' fail unity of time, so not contradictory.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the conditions for genuine contradiction (tanāquḍ) between two propositions. The author begins by clarifying that some contradictions are indirect (by means of an intermediate), but the focus is on direct contradiction. The text from the matn states that for two particular (mukhassasatān, i.e., singular or non-quantified) propositions, contradiction requires agreement in eight aspects: subject, predicate, time, place, relation, potentiality/actuality, whole/part, and condition. Each is illustrated with examples from everyday language. For instance, if the subjects differ ('Zaid is standing' vs. 'Amr is not standing'), they are not contradictory because they are about different individuals. The eighth condition, unity of condition, is exemplified by the body causing dispersion of vision under one condition (being white) but not under another (being black). After listing these conditions, the author states the standard rule for quantified propositions: the contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative, and the contradictory of a universal negative is a particular affirmative. This rule is demonstrated with the classic logical example: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Some human is not an animal' are contradictories. The passage thus covers both the special conditions for singular propositions and the general law for universal and particular quantifiers. The examples used are from the Islamic tradition (e.g., wine's potential intoxication, the negro's blackness) to ground the logical principles in familiar contexts.

Contradictory Pairs for Universal and Particular Propositions
Author states the contradictory pairs for universal and particular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the contradictory pairs for universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, and particular negative propositions, and verify that they obey the conditions of contradiction.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is the particular negative; the contradictory of a universal negative is the particular affirmative.

After establishing the conditions for singular propositions, the passage turns to quantified propositions (with 'all' or 'some'). Here the rule is simple: to contradict a universal affirmative, you need a particular negative, and vice versa for the universal negative. This is the classic square of opposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quantity and quality of the proposition
Determine if the proposition is universal (كل / لا شيء) or particular (بعض) and whether it is affirmative or negative.
2
Apply the contradictory rule
If the proposition is universal affirmative (كل س), the contradictory is the particular negative (بعض س ليس ص). If universal negative (لا شيء من س ص), the contradictory is the particular affirmative (بعض س ص).
3
Check the example
Test with the given example: 'Every human is an animal' (universal affirmative) and 'Some human is not an animal' (particular negative). They cannot both be true and cannot both be false.
4
Generalize to all four types
For particular affirmative, the contradictory is universal negative; for particular negative, the contradictory is universal affirmative. This completes the square.

Conversion ('Aks) and Its Conditions
Author defines conversion ('aks) and explains its conditions with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of conversion to any categorical proposition and verify that the converse preserves quality and truth value.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate but not truth and quality.

Conversion is a powerful immediate inference that allows you to derive a new proposition from a given one by swapping its terms. However, the operation is not mechanical—you must adjust the quantity and ensure that the truth value remains unchanged. In this lesson, we will unpack the definition of conversion ('aks) and learn the three key conditions that guarantee a valid converse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's type
Determine quantity (universal/particular) and quality (affirmative/negative) of the given proposition. This is crucial because conversion rules differ per type.
Standard types: A (universal affirmative), E (universal negative), I (particular affirmative), O (particular negative).
2
Apply the conversion rule
Swap subject and predicate. For type A ('Every S is P'), converse is I ('Some P is S'). For type E ('No S is P'), converse is E ('No P is S'). For type I ('Some S is P'), converse is I ('Some P is S'). Type O ('Some S is not P') has no valid converse.
3
Preserve quality
Ensure the converse has the same quality as the original: affirmative remains affirmative, negative remains negative. Otherwise, the conversion is invalid (e.g., 'Every human is rational' cannot become 'Some rational is not human').
4
Preserve truth value
Check that if the original is true, the converse is also true; if the original is false, the converse must be false. This guards against accidental truth (e.g., from a false original like 'Every animal is human' you cannot conclude a true converse 'Some human is animal').
5
Verify the result
Confirm that the converse is a well-formed categorical proposition and that the conversion is logically valid according to the rules above.

Conditions of Valid Conversion: Truth and Quality
Author explains conditions for valid conversion: truth and quality preservation, with a note on falsehood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two necessary conditions for valid conversion and determine whether a given converse satisfies them.

Core Idea
A valid converse preserves truth and quality, but not falsehood.

When we swap the subject and predicate of a proposition, the result is its converse. But not every swap yields a valid inference. The logicians laid down two conditions: the converse must retain the same quality (affirmative or negative) as the original, and it must be true whenever the original is true. Interestingly, they did not require the converse to preserve falsehood – a false original can have a true converse. In this lesson, we unpack these conditions and see why they matter for sound reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original and its converse
Take a given categorical proposition. Reverse its subject and predicate to form the converse. For example, from 'Every A is B' we get 'Some B is A' (in the case of A-propositions). Note that the form changes for different types.
The converse is not a simple swap; the quantifier may change depending on the original's type.
2
Check preservation of quality
Ensure the converse has the same quality (affirmation or negation) as the original. If the original is affirmative, the converse must be affirmative; if negative, the converse must be negative. The passage explains that logicians found most valid converses agree in quality with the original.
3
Check preservation of truth
Verify that whenever the original proposition is true, its converse is also true. The converse is a necessary consequent (lāzim) of the original. If this condition fails, the conversion is invalid. For example, 'Every A is B' (true) must guarantee 'Some B is A' (true).
This is often called 'truth-preservation'.
4
Understand why falsehood is not preserved
Observe that the converse may be true even if the original is false. The example in the passage: 'Every animal is human' (false) has a true converse 'Some human is animal'. Therefore, conversion does not require preserving falsehood. This is because the converse is a necessary consequent of the original only in terms of truth, not falsehood.
A false antecedent can have a true consequent; only truth must flow from antecedent to consequent.

Conversion of Universal and Particular Affirmatives
Author applies conversion rules to universal affirmative and particular affirmative propositions with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conversion rule to universal and particular affirmative propositions and prove the validity of the particular converse using reductio ad absurdum.

Core Idea
Universal affirmative converts to particular affirmative, never to universal.

Correctly transforming a proposition without changing its truth value is essential for valid reasoning in Islamic logic. The universal affirmative (e.g., 'Every human is an animal') might seem to imply a universal converse, but it does not. This lesson explains why and how to derive the valid particular converse, along with a rigorous logical proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition type
Examine the example: 'Every human is an animal' (كل إنسان حيوان). This is a universal affirmative: subject = 'human', predicate = 'animal', quantity = universal, quality = affirmative.
2
Apply the conversion rule
For a universal affirmative, the valid converse is a particular affirmative: 'Some animal is a human' (بعض الحيوان إنسان). Test this with the example: does it hold? Yes, because at least one human exists that is also an animal.
The converse has the subject and predicate swapped, quantity becomes particular.
3
Understand why universal conversion fails
The predicate may be broader than the subject. 'Every human is an animal' is true, but 'Every animal is a human' is false because the predicate 'human' does not apply to all animals (e.g., dogs). Converting universally would require the specific to apply to all instances of the general, which is impossible.
Universal-to-universal conversion is only valid if subject and predicate are coextensive (e.g., 'Every rational animal is a human' converts to 'Every human is a rational animal').
4
Prove the particular converse using reductio (khalf)
Assume the converse is false: 'No animal is a human' (لا شيء من الحيوان بإنسان). Combine this with the original 'Every human is an animal' in a first-figure syllogism: Every human is an animal, No animal is a human → No human is a human. This conclusion is a contradiction (something is not itself), proving the assumption false. Hence the particular converse must be true.
This proof uses the first figure (Barbara-like: all A is B, no B is C → no A is C) but note the mood is actually Celarent (no B is C) if we treat 'no animal is human' as a negative. The formal proof is not essential here.
5
Extend to the particular affirmative
The particular affirmative (e.g., 'Some animal is a human') also converts to a particular affirmative ('Some human is an animal') using the same reductio argument. If the converse were false (No human is an animal), contradiction arises.
The matn states: 'The particular affirmative converts particularly by this same argument.'

The Particular Negative and Its Conversion
Commentator explains why the particular negative does not necessarily convert, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a particular negative proposition can be converted and explain why conversion is not universally necessary.

Core Idea
The particular negative does not convert of necessity, but may convert in some cases.

In this lesson, we explore the conversion of particular negative propositions. The classical example 'Some animal is not human' is true, but its converse 'Some human is not animal' is false, showing that conversion is not universally valid. However, the commentator clarifies that this is a matter of logical necessity, not absolute impossibility. We will examine the conditions under which conversion may or may not hold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Recognize that the given proposition is a particular negative (سالبة جزئية), which denies something of some members of the subject.
Example: 'Some animal is not human'.
2
Perform conversion
Swap the subject and predicate to form the converse. For 'Some animal is not human', the converse is 'Some human is not animal'.
3
Check truth preservation
Determine if the truth of the original guarantees the truth of the converse. In the example, the original is true but the converse is false, so conversion does not preserve truth in this case.
4
Understand the qualification 'luzuman'
The author says conversion is not necessary (لا عكس لها لزوماً), meaning it is not a logical entailment. This does not mean it never converts; it only means it is not universally valid.
The commentator emphasizes 'luzuman kulliyyan' (universal necessity).
5
Analyze the counterexample
Why does the converse fail? Because the subject 'animal' is broader than the predicate 'human'. Every human is animal, so 'some human is not animal' is false. Thus, when the subject is broader, conversion fails.
Conversely, if the subject and predicate are unrelated (e.g., human and stone), both the original and converse can be true.
