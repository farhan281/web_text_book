Threefold Division of Indication
Author divides indication into natural, rational, and conventional types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between natural, rational, and conventional indication and identify the basis for each type.

Core Idea
Indication is classified by its connection source: nature, reason, or convention.

In this passage, the author introduces the three types of indication (dalāla) that form the foundation of how signs convey meaning in Islamic logic. Understanding this classification is essential for grasping how linguistic and non-linguistic signs function in Islamic sciences such as uṣūl al-fiqh and kalām.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall classification
Locate the phrase 'تنقسم إلى أقسام ثلاثة' and notice that the author divides indication into three parts: طبعية, عقلية, and وضعية.
This is a rational division (عقلي) as noted in commentaries.
2
Define natural indication
Read the definition and example: 'الدلالة الطبعية أن تكون بحسب اقتضاء الطَّبع كدلالة أُح أُح على وجع الصدر'. The groan 'ugh ugh' indicates chest pain naturally, because the connection is inherent in nature.
This does not require learning or reasoning.
3
Define rational indication
Read the definition and example: 'الدلالة العقلية أن تكون بحسبِ اقتضاء العقل كدلالة لفظ ديز المسموع من وراء الجدار على وجود اللافظ'. Hearing the word 'dīz' (Persian for 'yesterday') from behind a wall, reason infers the speaker's existence.
This relies on cause-effect reasoning.
4
Understand the third type
Note that conventional indication (وضعية) is mentioned but not exemplified here; it is based on human convention, such as the meaning of words. The next lesson covers it in detail.
The division is exhaustive: every indication is either natural, rational, or conventional.
5
Distinguish the types
Summarize the key difference: Natural indication arises from nature itself, rational indication from rational inference, and conventional indication from social agreement. The same sign can potentially be of more than one type? No, each instance belongs to one type based on the source of the connection.
This classification is foundational for analyzing signs in Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage classifies indication into three types based on the source of the necessary connection. The first, 'tabī'iyya' (natural indication), arises from natural constitution, such as a groan ('uḥ uḥ') being a natural sign of chest pain; the relationship is inherent, not learned. The second, 'ʿaqliyya' (rational indication), arises from a judgment of reason, such as hearing the word 'dīz' (Persian for 'yesterday') from behind a wall—reason infers that someone is present because words require a speaker. The third, 'waḍʿiyya' (conventional/posited indication), is not given an example here but is the focus of the next lesson. The commentary notes (from the hashia) that this division is a rational classification (ʿaqlī) because it is exhaustive (affirmation and negation), unlike the subdivision of verbal indication which is empirical (istiqrāʿī). The examples are taken from everyday experience to illustrate the difference between signs rooted in nature, signs requiring inference, and signs based on convention.

Three Types of Conventional Verbal Indication
Author explains the three types of conventional verbal indication: totality, part, and concomitant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which type of conventional indication (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) a given word-meaning relationship exemplifies, and explain the condition for tadammun regarding the object having parts.

Core Idea
One word can indicate its whole meaning, a part, or a necessary concomitant—but not all objects have parts.

In this lesson, we examine the three subdivisions of conventional verbal indication as defined by the logicians. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for precise definition and argumentation in Islamic sciences. The hashia adds a critical refinement: tadammun only applies when the object actually has parts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Recognise the three categories: mutabaqa (whole), tadammun (part), iltizam (concomitant). Each corresponds to a different relationship between the word and what it indicates.
The word 'house' can indicate the whole (mutabaqa), a wall (tadammun), or the existence of a roof (iltizam).
2
Extract the condition for tadammun
Note that the hashia specifies a necessary condition: the object must have parts. For example, 'Allah' has no parts, so a word for Him cannot indicate a part of Him. This excludes entities like the Necessary Existent and the point.
The commentator omitted this condition, but the hashia clarifies it.
3
Apply the classification to examples
Given a word and its meaning, determine which type of indication is operative. Consider the word 'bayt' (house): the entire building is mutabaqa; a wall is tadammun; the existence of a roof is iltizam.
Tadammun is valid because a house has parts.
4
Distinguish iltizam from tadammun
Iltizam indicates something external that necessarily accompanies, not a part. For example, 'roof' indicates wall as a necessary concomitant, but wall is not a part of roof's definition.
The concomitant is not part of the meaning but necessarily coexists.

And what is meant by indication here is conventional verbal indication, which is by virtue of the positioning of the word indicating the meaning. His statement: 'the entirety of what it was positioned for' – this indication is independent of mentioning 'entirety', but its mention was only for the sake of emphasis and improving the contrast. His statement: 'upon a part of what it was positioned for' – the commentator left out an indispensable condition: that it must have a part, to exclude what has no part, like the Necessary Existent and the point, and thereby co-extensive indication is not actualized in that case.

Commentary
This passage focuses on the third type of indication, the conventional verbal indication (dalāla lafẓiyya waḍʿiyya), which is the primary concern in logic. This type is subdivided into three: (1) mutābaqa (totality) – the word indicates the whole meaning for which it was coined, e.g., the word 'human' indicates the entire concept of human; (2) taḍammun (part/concomitance of part) – the word indicates a part of that meaning, e.g., 'human' indicates 'animal' (a part of its definition); (3) iltizām (concomitance) – the word indicates something that necessarily accompanies the meaning in existence, e.g., 'roof' indicates 'wall' (though not a part of its definition, it is a necessary concomitant). The hashia (4) clarifies that for mutābaqa, the mention of 'entirety' is for emphasis, not because it is needed for definition. The hashia (5) critically notes that the commentator omitted a necessary condition for taḍammun: the object must actually have parts. For example, 'God' (the Necessary Existent) has no parts, so a word for God cannot indicate a part of Him (that would be impossible). Similarly, 'point' (in geometry) has no parts, so taḍammun does not apply. Thus, the subdivision is valid only for things that have compositional structure.

Divining the Simple Utterance: Mufrad vs. Mu'allaf
Defines mufrad and mu'allaf with examples and lists four cases of mufrad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any utterance as simple (mufrad) or compound (mu'allaf) by applying the criterion of whether its parts are intended to indicate parts of its meaning.

Core Idea
An utterance is simple when its parts are not intended to carry meaning, even if they themselves are meaningful elsewhere.

This lesson introduces the foundational division of utterances into mufrad (simple) and mu'allaf (compound). The author defines each type with examples, then expands the definition of mufrad into four cases to cover all possibilities. Understanding this division is essential for later logical analysis, because definitions and arguments are built from simple concepts combined into compound expressions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the defining criterion
The key is intention (يراد): when you say a word, do you intend its individual letters or syllables to indicate parts of the meaning? If not, it's mufrad.
2
Analyze the example of 'insan'
The word 'insan' (human) is mufrad because its letters alif-nun-sin do not each carry a part of the meaning 'rational animal'.
3
Analyze the example of 'ramiyy al-hijarah'
This compound (thrower of stones) is mu'allaf because 'ramiyy' indicates the agent and 'hijarah' indicates the object, each contributing to the whole.
4
Examine the four cases of mufrad
The author lists four cases to handle edge situations: (a) no parts at all (e.g., single letter ق), (b) parts with no meaning (e.g., زيد), (c) parts with meaning but not part of the whole's meaning, (d) parts with meaning that is part of the whole but not intended. Only (a) and (b) are given in the excerpt, but the principle covers all.
The remaining two cases are inferred from the definition's nuance.

He said: 'Then utterance: either it is simple (mufrad), which is that from whose part is not intended indication of a part of its meaning, like 'insan' (human), or it is compound (mu'allaf), which is not like that, like 'ramiyy al-hijarah' (thrower of stones).' Commentary
This passage defines and expands on the division of utterance (لفظ) into simple (مفرد) and compound (مؤلف). The author begins by quoting the original matn: a simple utterance is one whose part is not intended to indicate a part of its meaning, like the word 'human' (إنسان) – its parts (alif, nun, etc.) do not indicate parts of the meaning 'rational animal'. A compound utterance is the opposite, like 'thrower of stones' (رامي الحجارة), where the part 'thrower' indicates the person throwing, and 'stones' indicates the object. The commentary then explains that the definition of simple (لا يراد بالجزء منه دلالة على جزء معناه) applies to four cases: (1) the utterance has no parts at all, e.g., the letter ق (qaf) in the name 'قلم' perhaps? (2) the utterance has parts but they have no meaning, e.g., the name 'زيد' (Zayd) – its letters have no inherent meaning. The remaining two cases are presumably that the part has meaning but that meaning is not part of the whole's meaning, and that the part has meaning that is part of the meaning but not intended. The key is that the definition hinges on intention (يراد) – even if a part has meaning, if it is not intended to indicate a part of the whole's meaning, it is still simple.
