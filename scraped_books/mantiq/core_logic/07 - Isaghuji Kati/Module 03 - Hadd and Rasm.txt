The Three Types of Indication
Author defines 'lafz' and explains the three types of indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam in any word-meaning relationship.

Core Idea
A word signifies three things: its full meaning, a part of it, or a necessary companion.

In logic, words are the vehicles of meaning, but a single word can convey different layers. Understanding these layers is essential for precise definition and argumentation. This lesson unpacks the three modes of indication from the Isaghuji tradition as explained by al-Kati.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word being analyzed
Take the example 'human' from the text. What meaning does the word 'human' convey? Note that it is a complex concept.
2
Determine the complete meaning (mutabaqa)
The whole meaning of 'human' is 'rational animal'. This is the correspondence between the word and its full definition.
3
Identify the partial meanings (tadammun)
'Human' includes 'animal' and 'rational' as parts. These are signified by inclusion because the whole includes them.
4
Find the necessary concomitants (iltizam)
'Human' necessarily implies 'capacity for knowledge' and 'craft of writing' in the mind. These are not part of the definition but accompany it.
The concomitants must be inseparable in the mind, not accidental.

Isaghuji and the Five Universals
Author explains Isaghuji and the five universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five universal concepts introduced by Isaghuji and explain why they depend on the three indications.

Core Idea
Isaghuji (Introduction) teaches the five universals that classify all definable things.

The term 'Isaghuji' appears in logic texts as the title of a foundational work. This lesson explains what it refers to and why the three indications are a prerequisite for understanding the five universals, as highlighted by the commentator.

Guided Path
1
Recognize that 'Isaghuji' means 'Introduction'
In logic, 'Isaghuji' is a Greek term for 'introduction', specifically referring to the study of the five universals. It is the name of al-Abhari's famous treatise.
2
Identify the five universals
The five universals are: genus (jins), species (naw'), differentia (fasl), property (khassa), and common accident ('arad 'amm). They are the categories used to define any concept.
3
Understand the dependence on the three indications
To define a species, you need its genus and differentia. This requires knowing how words indicate the whole (mutabaqa) and parts (tadammun). Properties and accidents are conveyed by iltizam. Thus, the three indications are foundational.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Define 'mal' (property) in fiqh using the five universals. Its genus is 'thing of value', differentia is 'legally transactable', property is 'owned', and common accident could be 'wealthy'. Use mutabaqa for the full definition, tadammun for parts, iltizam for necessary implications.

Essential vs Accidental: The Two Types of Universals
Author divides the universal into essential and accidental based on entering the reality of its particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a universal is essential or accidental based on whether it enters the reality of its particulars.

Core Idea
Essential universals are part of the quiddity; accidental universals are external to it.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational division of universals into essential (dhati) and accidental (aradi). This distinction is key to understanding how definitions work: essential universals are the building blocks of a thing's reality, while accidental universals are extraneous attributes that do not define what something is. The passage clarifies that only the parts of the quiddity (genus and differentia) are essential, while the species itself is considered accidental in this technical sense.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal in question
Look at the universal term being analyzed (e.g., 'animal', 'laughing', 'human'). Determine which term's classification is under discussion.
The text discusses 'animal' and 'laughing' with respect to 'human' and 'horse'.
2
Determine the quiddity (mahiyya) of the particulars
What is the reality of the individuals? For human, the quiddity is composed of animal and rational. For horse, it is animal and equine? The text uses animal and a unique differentia.
The quiddity is the set of essential parts that define the species.
3
Check if the universal is one of the constituents
If the universal is one of the parts that make up the quiddity, it is essential. If it is not a part, it is accidental.
For human, 'animal' is a part; 'laughing' is not a part.
4
Distinguish the species itself
Note that the species itself (e.g., 'human') is not considered essential in this context because it is the whole, not a part. It is classified as accidental.
This nuance prevents confusion: only the genus and differentia are essential.

Core Idea
The differentia is what makes a species unique; it can be proximate or remote.

The passage begins with the standard definition of the differentia: the essence that distinguishes a thing from what shares its genus. Using the example of 'rational' for humans, it then differentiates between proximate differentia, like 'rational', and remote differentia, like 'sensitive', which is a part of the definition but not the ultimate distinguishing feature. Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the definition of the differentia (al-fasl) in logic. It begins by stating the standard definition: the differentia is the essence that distinguishes a thing from what shares its genus, exemplified by 'rational' for humans. The author then notes that if the definition had added 'or in existence' (aw fi al-wujud), it would be more inclusive, covering differentiae of quiddities composed of two or more co-equal parts. Core Idea

Completeness here refers to how close the genus is to the essence, not logical sufficiency.

Commentary
This passage introduces the verbal description (al-qawl al-shāriḥ) which is the genus of definition and description. It begins with a preamble: the earlier discussion of the five universals gave descriptions (rusūm) of them, not essential definitions (ḥudūd), because we do not know whether they have distinct quiddities beyond the concepts we mentioned. Distinguishing Hadd and Rasm within al-Qawl al-Sharih
Definition of hadd and its distinction from rasm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a hadd (definition) from a rasm (description) by checking whether the statement indicates the essence (mahiyya) or uses accidents/properties.

Core Idea
Hadd reveals the essence; rasm describes through properties – both are valid but serve different logical purposes.

In Islamic logic, the way we define things is crucial. This lesson unpacks the first of the two types of knowledge: the explanatory statement (al-qawl al-sharih). You will learn how to distinguish between a definition (hadd) that captures the essence and a description (rasm) that uses accidents or properties. This distinction is vital for every Islamic science that relies on precise definitions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two types of knowledge
The passage states that knowledge (al-ilm) is either conceptual (tasawwur) or assenting (tasdiq). The explanatory statement is the tool for tasawwur.
The other tool, al-hujja, is for tasdiq.
2
Understand the definition of al-qawl al-sharih
It is any statement that leads to a concept without judgment. Its defining property is that it provides conceptual understanding.
3
Identify the defining feature of hadd
A hadd is a statement indicating the essence (mahiyya) of a thing. The phrase 'dall 'ala mahiyya' is the key differentia.
This excludes rasm, which does not indicate the essence.
4
Apply the distinction
Given a statement like 'man is a rational animal', determine whether it is hadd or rasm. Check if it mentions the essence (genus + differentia) or just properties.
Rational animal is a complete hadd.

This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition from the commentary on Isaghuji. It begins by establishing that 'al-qawl al-sharih' (the explanatory statement) is one of the two main types of knowledge—the other being 'al-hujja' (proof). The Complete and Incomplete Hadd
Division of hadd into complete and incomplete with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a complete or incomplete definition (hadd) by correctly selecting the proximate or remote genus and the proximate differentia.

Core Idea
A complete hadd uses the proximate genus and differentia; an incomplete hadd uses a remote genus – both are valid but differ in precision.

In this lesson, we delve into the two types of definition (hadd). The passage distinguishes between a complete definition that gives the immediate genus and differentia, and an incomplete one that uses a more distant genus. Understanding this helps you assess the precision of definitions in Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between proximate and remote genus
The proximate genus is the closest category (e.g., animal for man), while the remote genus is a higher category (e.g., body). In hadd tamm, both genus and differentia are proximate.
The proximate differentia is the immediate distinguishing feature.
2
Analyze the example of complete hadd
'Rational animal' – animal is the proximate genus, rational is the proximate differentia. Together they give the essence of man.
3
Analyze the example of incomplete hadd
'Rational body' – body is the remote genus (since man is also a body), rational is still the proximate differentia. This definition still gives the essence but misses precision because it does not use the most immediate genus.
The incomplete hadd is still a definition (indicates essence), but it is less specific.
4
Practice with an Islamic example
Define 'wudu' using a complete hadd and an incomplete hadd. Complete: 'washing specific limbs with water' (genus: washing, differentia: specific limbs). Incomplete: 'purifying act using water' (genus: act, differentia: purifying using water).
The complete hadd is more precise and prevents overlap with other purifications.

Complete and Incomplete Rasm and the Infinite Regress Objection
Division of rasm into complete and incomplete, and refutation of infinite regress in defining hadd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a complete or incomplete description (rasm), and explain why defining 'hadd' does not lead to infinite regress.

Core Idea
Rasm describes through necessary properties or accidents; the definition of hadd is self-identical, stopping infinite regress.

After covering definitions, the passage turns to descriptions (rasm) and addresses a key objection: does defining 'hadd' lead to infinite regress? This lesson clarifies both topics, showing how rasm provides an alternative to essential definition and how the problem of tasalsul is resolved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of rasm
Complete rasm uses the proximate genus and a necessary property (khassa); incomplete rasm uses a set of accidental features that together uniquely identify the thing.
The necessary property is unique to the thing but not part of its essence.
2
Examine the example of complete rasm
'Laughing animal' – animal is the proximate genus, laughing is a necessary property of man (it is unique to humans). This gives a description, not a definition.
The property must be 'lazima' (inseparable) from the thing.
3
Examine the example of incomplete rasm
The list of physical traits (walking on two feet, broad-nailed, bare-skinned, upright stature, naturally laughing) are accidental (aradiyyat) but collectively specify man uniquely.
This type is less precise and relies on a bundle of accidents.
4
Understand the infinite regress objection and its refutation
Some argue that defining 'hadd' would require a definition of that definition, ad infinitum. The author refutes: the definition of hadd is identical to hadd itself, just as the existence of existence is existence itself. Thus no regress.
This is a logical move: the concept is self-referential in a way that stops the chain.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition from the commentary on Isaghuji. It begins by establishing that 'al-qawl al-sharih' (the explanatory statement) is one of the two main types of knowledge—the other being 'al-hujja' (proof). The distinction is based on whether the concept (tasawwur) is without judgment (leading to a conceptual goal) or with judgment (leading to an assent goal). The author then clarifies that 'al-qawl al-sharih' is a general term that includes both hadd (definition) and rasm (description). The definition of hadd is given: it is a statement indicating the essence of a thing. The commentator analyzes this: 'statement indicating' is the genus that covers both hadd and rasm, while 'essence of a thing' serves to exclude rasm (since rasm does not indicate the essence but accidents or properties). Next, a potential objection is addressed: some argue that defining 'hadd' leads to infinite regress (tasalsul). The answer is that such regress does not occur because the definition of definition (hadd al-hadd) is identical to definition itself, just as the existence of existence is existence itself. Following this, the passage divides hadd into two types: complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis). The complete definition consists of the proximate genus and the proximate differentia, exemplified by 'rational animal' for man. The incomplete definition uses the remote genus (e.g., 'body') along with the proximate differentia ('rational'), resulting in 'rational body'. Similarly, rasm is divided into complete and incomplete. The complete description uses the proximate genus and a necessary property (khassa), like 'laughing animal' for man. The incomplete description relies on a collection of accidents (aradiyyat) that together uniquely identify the thing but do not convey its essence; the example given is a list of physical and behavioral traits for man. Each type is illustrated with standard examples from the Aristotelian tradition, all grounded in the definition of man. The commentary thus systematically unpacks the terminology and shows how the distinction between hadd and rasm, and their subtypes, maps onto the broader concept of qawl sharih.

The Expository Statement: Conception without Judgment
Defines the expository statement and distinguishes it from proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the expository statement (qawl sharh) from the proof (hujja) based on the presence of judgment.

Core Idea
The expository statement gives you what something is; the proof gives you that something is true.

Knowledge in logic is divided into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). The expository statement (qawl sharh) is the tool for conception, while the proof (hujja) is the tool for assent. In this lesson, we examine the definition of the expository statement and its role in logical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of knowledge
Recognize that the passage divides knowledge into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). The expository statement is related to conception without judgment.
Tasawwur: forming a concept; tasdiq: affirming a truth.
2
Identify the expository statement's goal
The expository statement leads to the conceptual demand (matlub tasawwuri). It answers 'what is it?' not 'is it true?'.
3
Distinguish from proof
The proof (hujja) includes judgment and leads to the assertoric demand (matlub tasdiqi). Notice the contrast.
4
Recognize generality
The author states that 'qawl sharh' is more general than hadd and rasm. It covers both definitions and descriptions.
Hadd and rasm are subtypes of the expository statement.

Hadd: The Essence-Revealing Definition
Defines hadd, responds to circularity objection, and divides into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a complete hadd by identifying its proximate genus and proximate differentia, and distinguish it from an incomplete hadd.

Core Idea
A complete hadd captures the full essence of a thing using its closest genus and differentia.

The hadd (definition proper) is a statement that indicates the essence (mahiyya) of a thing. It is composed of essential properties (dhatiyyat). In this lesson, we explore the definition of hadd, the objection about circularity, and the division into complete and incomplete.

Guided Path
1
Recall the meaning of hadd
Hadd in language means 'prevention'. In logic, it prevents non-essentials from being included in the definition.
2
Identify the two components of hadd
A hadd consists of a genus (jins) and a differentia (fasl). The genus answers 'what kind of thing is it?' and the differentia answers 'what distinguishes it?'.
These are both essential properties (dhatiyyat).
3
Distinguish complete from incomplete
A complete hadd uses the proximate genus (e.g., 'animal' for man) and proximate differentia ('rational'). An incomplete hadd uses a remote genus (e.g., 'body') with the proximate differentia.
Example from passage: 'rational animal' vs 'speaking body'.
4
Understand the circularity response
The author argues that defining hadd does not lead to circularity because the definition of hadd is itself a hadd, just as 'existence exists' is self-evident.
This is a metaphysical point about self-identity.

Author defines incomplete description and gives the example of man.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An incomplete description uses a unique combination of accidents, not essential traits, to identify a thing.

In the previous lesson, we learned about complete description (al-rasm al-tamm). Now we turn to its counterpart: the incomplete description (al-rasm al-naqis). This type of description is composed entirely of accidental properties (al-‘araḍiyyāt) that, when taken together, are unique to the defined thing, even though each property alone may be found in other things. The author provides the classic example of man to illustrate this concept. Understanding this helps you recognise how logicians define things by their effects rather than their essences.

Guided Path

Identify the composition from accidentals
Read the definition: an incomplete description is composed of accidental properties (al-‘araḍiyyāt). Note that it does not include any essential differentia (faṣl) or genus (jins).
2
Check the uniqueness condition
The totality of the accidental properties must be specific to one essence, but each individual property may be found in other essences. This is the key difference from a complete description.
3
Examine the example of man
The author lists: walking on two feet, broad nails, bare skin, upright stature, and laughing by nature. Verify that no single property is unique to man (e.g., birds walk on two feet), but the combination is exclusive.
4
Understand why it is called 'rasm' and 'naqis'
'Rasm' because it uses necessary properties (al-khāṣṣa al-lāzima) which are traces of the thing, so it is a definition by effect. 'Naqis' because it does not include all parts of a complete description, analogous to the incomplete definition (had naqis).
5
Compare with incomplete definition (had naqis)
Just as an incomplete definition lacks some essential parts (genus or differentia), an incomplete description lacks some accidental parts that would make it complete. The author notes the parallel between the two.

The author gives the classic example for man: walking on two feet, broad nails, bare skin, upright stature, and laughing by nature. Each of these alone can be found in other creatures (e.g., birds walk on two feet, but have different nails), but together they are exclusive to man. The passage then explains why it is called 'rasm' (description) — because it uses necessary properties (al-khassa al-lazima) which are traces of the thing, so it is a definition by effect. And it is called 'incomplete' (naqis) because it does not contain all the parts of a complete description, analogous to how an incomplete definition (had naqis) lacks some essential parts.

Quantification in Categorical Propositions
Author defines quantified categorical propositions and their quantifiers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify categorical propositions as universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, particular negative, or indefinite, and match the correct Arabic quantifier.

Core Idea
Every categorical proposition is a claim about all or some individuals — indefinite propositions leave quantity unspecified and are excluded from syllogisms.

In logic, the quantity of a proposition determines how widely its judgment applies. A universal proposition covers every individual of the subject; a particular covers some; an indefinite leaves it open. This lesson walks through the classification of 'mahsura' (quantified) propositions and the role of the 'sawr' (quantifier). You will also learn why indefinite propositions are not used in scientific reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Look at the judgment: does it cover all individuals, some individuals, or is no quantity specified?
2
Determine quality
Is the judgment affirmative or negative? This gives you the second axis of classification.
3
Match the quantifier
For universal affirmative: 'kull'; universal negative: 'la shay'' or 'la wahid'; particular affirmative: 'ba'd' or 'wahid faqat'; particular negative: 'laysa kull', 'laysa ba'd', or 'ba'd laysa'.
These quantifiers are standard in Arabic logical texts.
4
Test for indefiniteness
If no quantifier is expressed, the proposition is 'muhmala'. Recall: such propositions are not considered in syllogistic reasoning because they lack productive power.
The natural proposition (tabi'iyya) is also excluded for similar reasons.
5
Apply to Islamic examples
Take a statement from fiqh like 'All intoxicants are forbidden' – classify it as universal affirmative with quantifier 'kull'.
This prepares you for using categorical propositions in usul al-fiqh.

Types of Conditional Propositions
Author divides conditional propositions into conjunctive and disjunctive with subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conjunctive from disjunctive conditionals, and classify conjunctive as implicative (luzumiyya) or accidental (ittifaqiyya), and disjunctive as real (haqiqiyya), preventing conjunction only (mani'at al-jam'), or preventing emptiness only (mani'at al-khuluw).

Core Idea
Conditional propositions are the skeleton of hypothetical reasoning — knowing the type of connection determines whether the inference is valid.

Conditional propositions express relationships between two parts. Conjunctive conditionals (متصلة) state that if one holds, the other follows, either necessarily or accidentally. Disjunctive conditionals (منفصلة) present alternatives that may be mutually exclusive in truth or falsehood. This lesson unpacks these divisions as presented in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main connective
Is it 'if...then' (متصلة) or 'either...or' (منفصلة)? This gives you the primary branch.
2
Classify conjunctive conditionals
Check if the antecedent necessitates the consequent (لزومية) or if the connection is coincidental (اتفاقية). Example: 'If the sun rises, day exists' is necessary; 'If man speaks, the donkey brays' is accidental.
Only necessary conditionals yield valid inferences in syllogisms.
3
Classify disjunctive conditionals by truth conditions
Ask: Can both parts be true together? Can both be false together? If neither → real (حقيقية). If only conjunction impossible → مانعة الجمع. If only emptiness impossible → مانعة الخلو.
Real disjunctives have strict exclusivity.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider: 'If revelation is from Allah, then it is true.' This is a conjunctive implicative. Disjunctive: 'The Quran is either created or uncreated' – real disjunctive.
These appear in kalam arguments.
