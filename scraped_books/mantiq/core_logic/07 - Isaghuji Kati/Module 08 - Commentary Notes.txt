Distinguishing Logical and Customary Necessity in 'Lamma'
Marginal note raises two objections and answers them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between logical necessity (luzum 'aqli) and customary necessity (luzum 'adi) in propositions containing 'lamma'.

Core Idea
Not every 'lamma' implies logical necessity; some connections are merely customary.

In al-Kati's commentary on the Isaghuji, he uses the phrase 'lamma faragha min bayan al-dalalat al-thalath shar' fi taqsim al-lafz', which seems to imply a necessary connection between finishing one topic and starting another. But is it really necessary? A marginal note points out a problem and resolves it by appealing to customary practice. Let's examine the objection and its answer to sharpen your understanding of necessity in logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The first objection states that the proposition with 'lamma' is implicative (لزومية), but finishing the explanation does not logically necessitate beginning the division. Can you see the logical gap?
The gap is that the author could have started a different section or paused.
2
Understand the response
The answer says the necessity is customary (عادة). So the connection is not logical but based on typical authoring practice.
Customary necessity is weaker and context-dependent.
3
Distinguish types of necessity
In logic, we distinguish between 'aqli (intellectual) and 'adi (customary). Customary necessity is not universal but based on norms.
For example, it is customary that when a speaker finishes one topic, they begin the next, but not logically required.
4
Apply the distinction
Try to find another example where a connection is customary rather than logical. For instance, 'when the mu'adh-dhin finishes the adhan, the congregation stands' – is that logical or customary?

قال المحقق المولوي نفعنا الله به ههنا سؤالان مشهوران أحدهما: أن القضية المشتملة على كلمة لما لزومية على ما صرح به، ومن البين أن الشروع في تقسيم اللفظ غير لازم للفراغ من بيان الدلالات الثلاث، والجواب أن اللزوم قد يكون عادياً ولا شك أن من تصدّى تأليف كتاب ففراغه من مبحث يستلزم شروعه في مبحث آخر عادة إلى أن يتم المباحث. وثانيهما: أن كلمة لما إذا دخلت على الماضي تكون ظرفا بمعنى إذا الزمانية فيلزم زمان الفراغ والشروع مع أنه لا يسعهما زمان واحد بل يتعاقبان. وأجيب بوجوه: الأول: أن المراد بالشروع إرادة الشروع وهي ما يمكن أن يسعها زمان الفراغ. الثاني: أن المراد بزمان الفراغ هو الزمان العرفي الممتد الذي يسع الشروع. الثالث: أن كلمة لما قد تستعمل للتقليل مجردة عن معنى الظرفية، والتقليل كاللزوم قد يكون عادياً.
The scholar al-Mawlawi – may Allah benefit us through him – said: Here there are two famous questions. One: that the proposition containing the word 'lamma' is implicative as he stated, but it is clear that beginning the division of utterance is not necessarily entailed by finishing the explanation of the three indications. The answer: that implication may be customary, and there is no doubt that one who undertakes to author a book, his finishing one topic customarily necessitates his beginning another topic until the discussions are completed. Second: that the word 'lamma' when it enters on the past tense is a particle indicating time, meaning 'when', so it necessitates a time for finishing and a time for beginning, whereas they cannot be a single time but rather follow one another. And it was answered with several points: first: that what is meant by 'beginning' is the intention to begin, which can be coterminous with the time of finishing. Second: that what is meant by the time of finishing is the customary extended time that accommodates the beginning. Third: that the word 'lamma' may be used for mere connection (taqlil) devoid of temporal meaning, and connection, like implication, may be customary.

Commentary
This passage is a marginal critique of the commentator's use of the word 'لما' (lamma) in the phrase 'لما فرغ من بيان الدلالات الثلاث شرع في تقسيم اللفظ' (when he finished explaining the three indications, he began the division of utterance). Two objections are raised: (1) The proposition with 'lamma' implies a necessary connection between finishing one topic and starting another, but in reality there is no logical necessity – the author could have started the division elsewhere. The answer is that the necessity here is customary ('ādī), not logical; in a book, finishing a section customarily leads to starting the next. (2) 'Lamma' on a past verb normally means 'when' in a temporal sense, implying that the time of finishing and the time of beginning are simultaneous, but they cannot be a single moment – they are sequential. Three answers are given: first, 'beginning' here means the intention to begin, which can coincide with the finish time; second, the 'time of finishing' refers to a customary extended period that includes the start; third, 'lamma' can be used for mere sequential connection without strict temporal simultaneity, and such connection is customary. This shows the precision of Arabic linguistic analysis in logical commentaries.

Resolving the Temporal Ambiguity of 'Lamma'
Marginal note raises two objections and answers them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three ways logicians resolve the apparent temporal simultaneity problem of 'lamma' with past tense verbs.

Core Idea
'Lamma' can indicate intention, extended time, or mere sequential connection, not strict simultaneity.

The second objection in the marginal note deals with a grammatical nuance: when 'lamma' is used with a past tense verb, it normally means 'when' implying that two events occur at the same time. But finishing one topic and starting another are sequential, not simultaneous. The marginal note offers three clever answers rooted in Arabic usage and logical flexibility. Let's parse them.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
'Lamma' with past tense indicates time, so finishing and beginning would need to be simultaneous, but they cannot be one moment.
The objection assumes a strict temporal reading of 'lamma'.
2
Examine the first answer
The 'beginning' refers to the intention to begin (إرادة الشروع), which can coexist with the time of finishing. So the intention to start a new topic can arise at the same moment as finishing the previous one.
This resolves simultaneity by shifting from action to intention.
3
Examine the second answer
The 'time of finishing' is considered in a customary extended sense (الزمان العرفي الممتد), which allows for the beginning to occur within that broader period.
This appeals to customary practice, where 'time' is not a precise instant.
4
Examine the third answer
'Lamma' can be used for mere sequential connection (تقليل) without temporal meaning, similar to customary implication. So it's not bound to strict time.
This is the most radical, stripping 'lamma' of temporal content.
5
Compare the three answers
Which answer do you find most convincing? Discuss their strength and applicability in different contexts.
First answer is psychological, second is common-sense, third is linguistic.
