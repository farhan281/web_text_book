Three Types of Verbal Indication
Author defines verbal indication and explains its three types with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the three types of verbal indication (dalalat al-mutabaqa, al-tadammun, al-iltizam) in any given word-meaning relationship, using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
A word can signify its full meaning, a part of it, or an associated meaning.

This passage defines the foundational concept of verbal indication (dalalat) in Islamic logic. Understanding how words point to meanings is essential for accurately interpreting the Quran, Hadith, and legal texts. The author presents a clear threefold classification that you can apply to any term.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its primary conventional meaning (al-wad')
Start by determining the word (lafz) and what it was originally placed for. For example, the word 'insan' (human) is placed for the meaning 'rational animal'.
This primary meaning is the full intended meaning by وضع.
2
Check if the indicated meaning matches the whole primary meaning (مطابقة)
If the word indicates its entire defined meaning, that is dalalat al-mutabaqa. Example: 'insan' indicates the entire compound 'rational animal'.
This is the default and most complete indication.
3
Check if the indicated meaning is a part of the primary meaning (تضمن)
If the word points to a part of its meaning (e.g., 'insan' indicates just 'animal' or just 'rational'), that is dalalat al-tadammun. This requires the word to have separable parts.
The word itself does not change; it still signifies the whole, but the listener understands a part.
4
Check if the indicated meaning is mentally associated but not part of the definition (التزام)
If the word indicates something that necessarily comes to mind but is not included in the definition, it is dalalat al-iltizam. Example: 'insan' indicates 'capacity for knowledge' or 'craft of writing'.
The association must be in the mind, not necessarily in reality.

Understanding Indication: Dalāla, Dāl, Madlūl
Author defines dalāla, dāl, madlūl, establishing the foundational relation of indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define indication (dalāla) and distinguish the indicator (dāl/dalīl) from the indicated (madlūl) using the relational definition.

Core Idea
Indication is a bridge from known to unknown.

In Islamic logic, the very first thing you must grasp is how knowledge is transmitted from one thing to another. This passage gives the foundational definition of that transmission—indication (dalāla)—and identifies the two poles: the indicator and the indicated. Understanding this distinction is the key to all subsequent discussions of evidence (dalīl) and proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition of dalāla
Dalāla is defined as a thing being in a state such that knowledge of it necessarily leads to knowledge of another thing. This is a relational property, not a fixed attribute of the thing itself.
The necessity here is logical or customary, not physical.
2
Extract the two relata
The first thing is called al-dāl (the indicator) or al-dalīl (the evidence). The second thing is called al-madlūl (the indicated). Note that the text derives both from the same definition, so dāl and dalīl are equivalent in meaning here.
In later logic, 'dalīl' is often used in argumentation contexts, but in this definition they are synonymous.
3
Understand the epistemological direction
Knowledge flows from the indicator to the indicated. If you know the indicator, you necessarily come to know the indicated. This is the basis for all reasoning: from evidence to that which is evidenced.
Reverse direction (from indicated to indicator) is not necessarily valid.
4
Apply the definition to a simple example
Consider the crescent moon (hilāl) indicating the start of Ramadan. The crescent is the indicator; the knowledge that the month has begun is the indicated. The relational property (dalāla) is the necessary connection between seeing the crescent and knowing the month's start.
This example is commonly used in Usul al-Fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional beginning. It first defines 'dalala' (indication) as a relational property: a thing being in such a state that knowing it necessarily leads to knowing something else. The first thing is called 'al-dal' (the indicator), and the second is 'al-madlul' (the indicated). From this definition, the author immediately derives the definitions of 'dalil' (evidence) and 'madlul' (the evidenced). A dalil is that which, when known, yields knowledge of another thing; madlul is that which becomes known when another thing is known. The shift from 'dal' to 'dalil' is subtle: 'dal' is the thing itself in its indicating state, while 'dalil' is that same thing considered as evidence. This establishes the basic epistemological framework for all subsequent logical discussions: knowledge is linked through indication.

The Three Types of Linguistic Signification
Author defines lafz and explains the three types of signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalala mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam, and identify which type is used in any given example from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Words signify meanings in three ways: whole, part, or necessary mental concomitant.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational classification of linguistic signification (dalala lafziyya) into three types: conformity (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadammun), and commitment (iltizam). This classification is essential for understanding how terms in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam convey meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its meaning
Locate the term 'lafz' and its primary meaning (al-ma'na al-wad'i). Note that the word 'insan' signifies the whole meaning of 'animal rational'.
2
Understand dalala mutabaqa
The word signifies its entire meaning. Example: 'insan' signifies the whole meaning of the rational animal. Also, metaphors are included in mutabaqa when a relation exists.
3
Understand dalala tadammun
The word signifies a part of its meaning. Example: 'insan' signifies 'natiq' (rational) because it is a part of the definition.
4
Understand dalala iltizam
The word signifies something external but necessarily concomitant in the mind. Example: 'insan' signifies 'capable of writing' because that is a mental concomitant.
Mental concomitance is the only valid basis for iltizam in mantiq.
5
Distinguish by checking criteria
For a given word-meaning pair, ask: is the meaning the whole (mutabaqa), a part (tadammun), or an external mental concomitant (iltizam)? Use the condition of mental concomitance for iltizam.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and explanatory discussion of the three types of linguistic signification (dalala lafziyya): conformity (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadammun), and commitment (iltizam). The author begins by defining lafz (word) and then explains the three types. For tadammun, he gives the example of 'man' signifying 'rational' (natiq) because it is a part of the meaning of man. For iltizam, he gives 'man' signifying 'capable of knowledge and the craft of writing' as a necessary concomitant. He then explains that iltizam is called commitment because the word does not signify every external matter, but only the external concomitant that is necessary in the mind. The author then discusses the concept of mulazama (concomitance), dividing it into external (kharijiyya) and mental (dhihniyya). He emphasizes that only mental concomitance is considered here because external concomitance would fail for privations like ignorance (jahl) which signify knowledge ('ilm) by commitment, even though they are opposites in external existence. A possible objection is raised: if ignorance is the absence of knowledge, then knowledge is part of its concept, making it inclusion rather than commitment. The author responds by clarifying that ignorance is defined as the absence attached to knowledge, and when taken as such, knowledge is not part of its meaning; the attachment is internal, but the target (knowledge) is external. This is further elucidated by quoting al-Sayyid al-Sharif on the distinction between taking the attached in terms of its essence versus in terms of being attached. The passage then continues on page 16, restating that iltizam requires mental concomitance, and that without it, there would be no certain signification, and metaphors would not be considered proper verbal signification in this art because they rely on contextual indicators. The annotation explains that external concomitance cannot be a condition because privations like blindness (which is a privation of sight) still signify their opposites by iltizam, as blindness is the absence of sight from that which is capable of seeing. The overall purpose is to delimit the three types of verbal signification and to clarify the condition for iltizam, distinguishing it from both mutabaqa and tadammun.

The Condition of Mental Concomitance for Iltizam
Author explains the division of mulazama and why mental concomitance is the condition for iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why mental concomitance is required for dalala iltizamiyya and why external concomitance is insufficient, using the example of privations.

Core Idea
Only mental concomitance validates iltizam; external concomitance fails for privations like ignorance and blindness.

This lesson delves into the crucial condition for dalala iltizamiyya: mental concomitance (al-mulazama al-dhihniyya). The author argues that external concomitance would exclude many valid cases, such as the signification of 'ignorance' for 'knowledge'.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of concomitance
The author divides mulazama into external (kharijiyya) and mental (dhihniyya). External is like 'three' requiring 'oddness'; mental is like 'ignorance' requiring 'knowledge' in the mind.
2
Identify the problem with external concomitance
If iltizam required external concomitance, then privations like 'blindness' would not signify 'sight' because they are opposites in external reality.
3
Apply to the privation example
Blindness is the absence of sight from that which is capable of seeing. In the mind, blindness necessarily brings to mind sight, so iltizam exists mentally despite external opposition.
4
Conclude the condition
Thus iltizam is based purely on mental concomitance, not on any external causal or necessary relation.

Resolving the Privation Objection: Ignorance and Knowledge
Author addresses an objection about privations and resolves it using the mudaf/idafa distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve the objection that a privation's signification of its opposite might be tadammun rather than iltizam, using the distinction of mudaf and idafa.

Core Idea
A privation signifies its opposite by iltizam, not tadammun, because the opposite is not part of its meaning when taken as a privation.

A potential objection arises: if ignorance is the absence of knowledge, doesn't knowledge become part of its definition, making it tadammun? The author resolves this by clarifying the nature of the attached term (mudaf) and the attachment (idafa), quoting al-Sayyid al-Sharif.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector says: 'Ignorance is defined as the absence of knowledge. Knowledge is thus part of the concept of ignorance, so 'ignorance' signifying 'knowledge' would be tadammun, not iltizam.'
2
Examine the response
The author responds: 'Ignorance is posited for the absence attached to knowledge in terms of its being attached. Knowledge itself is not part of the signified.' The attachment (idafa) is internal, but the target (mudaf ilayh, i.e., knowledge) is external.
3
Apply the distinction of al-Sayyid al-Sharif
If 'the attached' is taken in terms of its essence, both the attachment and the target are external. If taken in terms of being attached, the attachment is internal, but the target is external. In 'absence of knowledge', the privation is taken as attached, so knowledge is external.
4
Conclude the logical classification
Therefore, ignorance signifies knowledge by iltizam (concomitance) because knowledge is a necessary mental concomitant, not a part of the definition.

Division of the Singular into Universal and Particular
Author defines singular and divides it into universal and particular with restriction 'concept itself'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between universal and particular based on the criterion of 'the very conception of the meaning' and explain why the restriction 'nafs tasawwur' is necessary.

Core Idea
A term's universality is determined by the mind, not by external reality.

In Islamic logic, the division of singular terms into universal and particular is foundational for definition and syllogism. The author presents a precise criterion: whether the concept itself prevents being true of many. This lesson unpacks that criterion and the crucial qualification 'concept itself' that excludes tricky cases like Wajib al-Wujud.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basic division
The singular (al-mufrad) is divided into universal (kulli) and particular (juz'i). The criterion is whether the very conception of its meaning (nafs tasawwur mafhumihi) prevents sharing (i.e., being true of many) or not. If it prevents, it is particular; if not, it is universal.
2
Identify the key examples
Particular example: 'Zayd' (as a name) – when its meaning is conceived, the mind deems it impossible to be true of many. Universal example: 'human' – its meaning does not prevent being true of many.
Note that 'Zayd' is considered a particular only as a name for a specific person; the word 'Zayd' can also be used as a universal for anyone named Zayd, but here it is taken as a proper name.
3
Analyze the restriction 'concept itself'
The author adds 'nafs tasawwur' (the very conception) to exclude cases where prevention is due to external evidence rather than the concept itself. For instance, Wajib al-Wujud (Necessary Existence) is unique in reality, but its concept does not prevent being true of many; otherwise, we wouldn't need proofs for oneness. This restriction also excludes hypothetical universals like 'nothing' or 'impossible'.
Without this restriction, the definition of particular would incorrectly include Wajib al-Wujud and other concepts that are unique or undefined.
4
Consider why the restriction matters
The restriction ensures that the definition is purely about the mental concept, not about external reality. This allows logicians to classify terms consistently regardless of empirical facts. It also protects theological arguments: Allah's uniqueness is proven by external evidence, not by the concept itself.
This distinction is crucial in kalam discussions about the attributes of Allah.

المشاهدات الكثيرة، ومنها حدسيات): وهي ما لا يحتاجُ العقل في جزم الحكم فيه إلى واسطة تكرار المشاهدة كقولنا: نور القمر مستفاد من نور الشمس لاختلاف تشكُلاتهِ النُّوريةِ بحسب اختلاف أوضاعه من الشمس قرباً وبُعداً، ومنها مُتواتِراتُ: وهي ما يحكمُ العقل فيه بجزم الحكم بواسطة الشماع من جمع كثير استحال العقل توافقهم على الكذب، كالحكم بأن النبي الله ادعى النبوة وأظهر المعجزة على يده، ومنها قضايا قياساتها معها وهي ما يحكمُ العقلُ فيه بواسطة أمر حاضر لا يغيب عن الذهن عند تصورِ الطرفين كقولنا: الأربعة زوج، بسبب وسط حاضر في الذهن، وهو الانقسام بمُتساويين، والوسط: ما يقترن بقولنا حين يُقَالُ كذا وكذا. (1) قوله (حدسیات) فسره بعضهم بأنه رعة انتقال الذهن من المبادئ إلى المطالب وفيه مسامحة ظاهرة والأصوب أنه عبارة عن الظفر عند الالتفات إلى المطالب بالحدود الوسطى دفعة تمثل المطالب مع الحدود الوسطى في الذهن من غير حركة، أما الفكر فهو حركة في المعاني من المطالب إلى مبادئهم فربما ينقطع وربما يتأدى وإذا تأدى فإنما يتم بحركة أخرى من المبادئ إلى المطالب فهو حينئذ مفتقر إلى حركتين ففيه إمكان عدم التأدي ووجود الحركة أو الحركتين، وفي الحدس امتناع عدم التأدي وعدم الحركة أصلا إذ الانتقال فيه دفعي لا تدريجي فإطلاق السرعة تجوز كما حققه السعد العلامة. كذا استفيد.
The numerous observations, and among them are intuitives [hadsiyat]: they are that which the mind does not need, in the certainty of the judgment, a medium of repetition of observation, like our saying: the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun, due to the variation of its luminous phases according to the variation of its positions relative to the sun, near and far. And among them are mutawatirats [mutawatirat]: they are that which the mind judges with certainty of judgment by means of hearing from a large group such that the mind deems it impossible for them to agree upon falsehood, like the judgment that the Prophet of God claimed prophethood and the miracle appeared at his hand. And among them are propositions whose syllogisms are with them [qadaya qiyasatuhu ma'aha], they are that which the mind judges by means of a present matter that does not disappear from the mind when the two extremes are conceived, like our saying: four is even, due to a middle term present in the mind, which is division into two equals. And the middle term: what accompanies our saying when it is said such and such. (1) His saying (hadsiyat) – some explained it as the quickness of the mind's transition from principles to sought conclusions, and there is an apparent looseness in that. The more correct is that it is expression of hitting upon the middle terms at the moment of turning to the sought conclusions, instantly, so that the sought conclusions are represented with the middle terms in the mind without movement. As for thinking, it is a movement in meanings from sought conclusions to their principles; perhaps it is cut off and perhaps it reaches; and when it reaches, it is only completed by another movement from the principles to the sought conclusions. So it is then in need of two movements, and in it there is possibility of non-reaching and existence of one or two movements. In intuition [hads], there is impossibility of non-reaching and no movement at all, since the transition in it is instantaneous, not gradual, so the application of speed is metaphorical, as verified by al-Sa'd al-'Allamah. Thus it is derived.

Commentary
This passage defines three types of certain premises (al-mushahadat al-kathira). The first is hadsiyat (intuitive premises), where the mind judges without repeated observation, e.g., knowing that moonlight is derived from sunlight by seeing the phases. The second is mutawatirat (mass-transmitted premises), where certainty comes from testimony of a multitude that cannot conspire to lie, e.g., the report of the Prophet's claim and miracle. The third is qadaya qiyasatuhu ma'aha (propositions whose syllogisms are inherent), where the middle term is already present in the mind when conceiving the subject and predicate, e.g., 'four is even' with the middle term 'divisible into two equal parts'. The hashaya clarifies hadsiyat, distinguishing it from discursive thought (fikr): hads is an instantaneous grasp of the middle term without movement, while fikr involves a deliberative movement from conclusion to principles and back, potentially failing. Hadsiyat is thus not simply fast thinking but a non-discursive intuition.
