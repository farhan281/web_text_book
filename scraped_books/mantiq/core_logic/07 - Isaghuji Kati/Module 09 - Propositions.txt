What is a Proposition?
Author defines proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given statement qualifies as a proposition (qadiyya) based on the criterion of truth-aptness.

Core Idea
A proposition is a statement that can be true or false.

In logic, we start with the basic unit of argumentation: the proposition. The author defines it as a statement about which it is meaningful to ask whether it is true or false. This definition sets the foundation for everything that follows. Understanding what counts as a proposition is crucial because only propositions can serve as premises and conclusions in arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'proposition' (al-qadiyya). Find it in the Arabic text.
2
Extract the defining property
The author says it is a statement (qawl) that can be called truthful or false. This gives the genus (statement) and differentia (truth-aptness).
3
Apply the criterion
Test a sentence: 'Zaid is a writer.' Can we say it is true or false? Yes, so it is a proposition.
4
Exclude non-propositions
Commands, questions, exclamations are not truth-apt, so they are not propositions. For example, 'Sit down!' cannot be true or false.

The Three Types of Propositions
Author divides propositions into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given proposition as categorical, conditional conjunctive, or conditional disjunctive.

Core Idea
Propositions are either categorical (subject-predicate) or conditional (if-then or either-or).

After defining what a proposition is, the author divides it into three types. This classification is essential because different types of arguments use different propositional forms. Categorical propositions make a direct assertion, while conditional propositions express a relationship between two statements. Understanding these types is the first step in analyzing syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
The author mentions: categorical (hamliyya), conditional conjunctive (shartiyya muttasila), conditional disjunctive (shartiyya munfasila).
2
Analyze the categorical example
'Zaid is a writer.' This has a subject (Zaid) and predicate (writer). It asserts a property of a subject.
3
Analyze the conditional conjunctive
'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists.' This has an antecedent (sun rising) and consequent (daytime exists). It asserts that if the antecedent holds, the consequent holds.
4
Analyze the conditional disjunctive
'The number is either even or odd.' This presents two alternatives and asserts that one of them is true. Usually exclusive.
5
Contrast the types
Categorical propositions have one subject-predicate pair, while conditionals have two component propositions linked by a conditional or disjunctive operator.

The Proposition (Qadiyya): Its Definition and Essential Features
Author defines qadiyya as a saying that admits truth and falsity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the definition of a proposition and distinguish propositions from other types of speech.

Core Idea
Every logical argument begins with a proposition; know its definition to know what can be true or false.

In this passage, the author lays down the foundational definition of the proposition (qadiyya) — the building block of all logical reasoning. He gives us the genus (saying) and the differentia (admitting truth/falsity). Understanding this definition is essential because it tells us which statements can serve as premises and conclusions in arguments. Let's break down the definition step by step.

Guided Path
1
Locate the complete definition of qadiyya in the Arabic text.
The definition is: 'qaulun yasihhu an yuqāla li-qā’ilihi innahu sādiq fīhi aw kādhib fīhi' — a saying such that it is valid to say of its speaker that he is truthful or false in it. This is the core definition.
Note that the definition includes the condition that the speaker can be called truthful or false with respect to that saying.
2
Identify the genus (jins) in the definition.
The genus is 'qawl' (saying), which the author explains is any compound, whether verbal or mental. This genus includes all types of compound expressions, not just propositions. It is the broader category.
Qawl is also used in other contexts, but here it specifically means a compound.
3
Identify the differentia (fasl) that distinguishes propositions from other compounds.
The differentia is 'yasihhu an yuqāla li-qā’ilihi innahu sādiq fīhi aw kādhib fīhi' — the validity of attributing truth or falsity. This property is what separates propositions from commands, questions, and incomplete phrases.
The author emphasizes that non-assertoric compounds like commands do not admit truth/falsity.
4
Apply the definition to distinguish between a proposition and other types of speech.
Take examples: 'Allah is One' is a proposition because we can ask if it is true or false. 'Be just!' is a command, not a proposition, because truth/falsity does not apply. Use this test to classify statements.
This is the practical test for identifying propositions.

Classification of Propositions: Categorical vs Conditional
Author divides propositions into categorical and conditional, then subdivides conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given proposition as categorical or conditional, and if conditional, identify whether it is conjunctive or disjunctive and whether it is affirmative or negative.

Core Idea
Every proposition is either a simple categorical or a compound conditional built from two propositions.

This lesson introduces the fundamental division of propositions into categorical and conditional types. This classification is essential because the structure of a proposition determines what kind of syllogism can be built from it. In Islamic theology, for example, arguments often rely on conditional statements like 'If the world is temporally originated, then it has a Creator.' Understanding this division will help you analyze such arguments correctly.

Guided Path
1
Check if both subject and predicate are simple terms
Look at the proposition and ask: Are the subject (مَحكوم عليه) and predicate (مَحكوم به) each a single concept (مفرد), like 'Zayd' and 'writer'? If yes, it is categorical.
Even if one part is a phrase, like 'Zayd's father', the commentator says it can be considered as a single unit in force.
2
If not simple, identify as conditional
If the proposition connects two complete statements (e.g., 'If ... then ...' or 'Either ... or ...'), it is conditional (شرطية).
The two parts are called المقدّم (antecedent) and التالي (consequent) for conjunctive, or the two disjuncts for disjunctive.
3
Distinguish conjunctive vs disjunctive
If the conditional asserts that one proposition is true on the condition that another is true, it is conjunctive (متّصلة). If it asserts mutual exclusion (تنافي) between the propositions, it is disjunctive (منفصلة).
Example of conjunctive: 'If the sun rises, then daytime exists.' Example of disjunctive: 'Either the number is even or odd.'
4
Determine affirmative or negative
If the judgment affirms the truth of the second part (or the exclusion), it is affirmative. If it negates it, it is negative.
E.g., 'It is not that if the sun rises then night exists' is a negative conjunctive.

Parts of Categorical and Conditional Propositions
Commentator identifies the parts of categorical and conditional propositions with their names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject, predicate, and assertoric relation in any categorical proposition, and the antecedent and consequent in any conditional proposition.

Core Idea
Every proposition has parts: naming them correctly is the first step to analyzing arguments.

The commentator unpacks the basic anatomy of propositions. Just as a body has limbs, a proposition has components that must be recognized before we can evaluate its truth or role in a syllogism. In this passage, you will learn the technical names for these parts and understand why they are called what they are.

1
Distinguish categorical from conditional
First, look at the proposition: does it assert something directly (categorical) or connect two statements (conditional)? The commentator treats both types separately.

Classification of Propositions by Quantity: Specified, Universal, Particular, Indefinite
Author classifies propositions by quantity with commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the quantity of any categorical proposition (specified, universal, particular, or indefinite) and explain the role of the quantifier (sūr).

Core Idea
The quantifier (sūr) is the 'wall' that surrounds the subject's individuals, defining propositional quantity.

In Islamic logic, every categorical proposition has a subject and predicate. But the quantity of the subject—whether it is a specific individual, all individuals, some, or unspecified—determines the proposition's type. This classification is essential for evaluating syllogisms and arguments in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam. In this lesson, we will explore the four types and understand the function of the quantifier.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish specified from non-specified propositions
Look at the subject term. If it is a specific individual (e.g., Zayd), the proposition is specified (makhṣūṣa). If the subject is a general term, it is not specified.
Specified propositions are also called personal (shakhṣiyya).
2
Check for the presence of a quantifier (sūr)
For non-specified propositions, see if a quantifier word like 'every' (kull) or 'some' (baʿḍ) appears. If yes, it is a quantified proposition (maḥṣūra); if no, it is indefinite (muhmala).
The quantifier indicates the extent of the subject.
3
Classify quantified propositions as universal or particular
If the quantifier indicates all individuals (e.g., 'every'), it is universal (kulliyya). If it indicates some (e.g., 'some'), it is particular (juz'iyya).
Both types are called 'quantified' (maḥṣūra) because the quantifier 'surrounds' the individuals like a wall.
4
Identify indefinite propositions
If the subject is a general term and no quantifier is used, the proposition is indefinite (muhmala). Its exact quantity is ambiguous and must be determined by context.
Example: 'Human is a writer' does not specify how many humans.
5
Apply the classification to Islamic examples
Practice classifying propositions from Islamic texts. For instance, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' is universal; 'Some scholars are jurists' is particular; 'Man is in loss' (Quran 103:2) is indefinite; 'Zayd is righteous' is specified.
This skill is crucial for understanding legal rulings and theological arguments.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Disjunctions vary in strength: only haqiqiyya excludes both truth and falsehood together.

In Islamic logic, disjunctive conditionals (al-shartiyya al-munfasila) are not all equal. Their strength depends on whether they exclude the possibility of both parts being true, both false, or both. This lesson breaks down the three types defined by classical logicians, using the example of even and odd numbers as a clear case of the strongest disjunction.

Identify the two parts (juz'ayn)

Ask: Can both parts be true simultaneously? For even and odd, no: a number cannot be both even and odd at the same time.
3
Check falsehood compatibility
Ask: Can both parts be false simultaneously? For even and odd, no: a number must be either even or odd; it cannot be neither.
4

If both truth and falsehood are impossible together -> haqiqiyya. If only truth is impossible -> mani'at al-jam'. If only falsehood is impossible -> mani'at al-khuluw.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction: exactly one part is true; both cannot be true and both cannot be false.

In Islamic logic, disjunctive propositions are crucial for exhaustive reasoning. Exclusive disjunction (al-munfasila al-haqiqiyya) is the strictest type, requiring that one and only one of its parts holds. Understanding its definition is the foundation for analyzing complex arguments.

The Impossibility of Three-Part Exclusive Disjunction and Its Resolution
Proof of impossibility of three-part exclusive disjunction and its compound resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that an exclusive disjunction cannot have three simple parts and correctly resolve such a statement into a compound proposition.

Core Idea
A three-part exclusive disjunction forces contradictions; the correct form is a two-part exclusive disjunction with a nested disjunctive.

The logicians show that genuine exclusive disjunctions have exactly two parts. When we encounter a statement like 'the number is excessive, defective, or equal,' it seems to be a three-part exclusive disjunction. However, careful analysis reveals a logical contradiction. The resolution is that the statement is actually a compound of a categorical and a disjunctive, preserving exclusivity.

Guided Path
1
Write the three-part exclusive disjunction
Write down the example: 'The number is either excessive, defective, or equal.' Note that it is claimed to be an exclusive disjunction.
Assume that the three parts are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive.
2
Derive the first contradiction using man' al-jam'
From 'excessive' we infer 'not defective' and 'not equal'. But then, using the assumption that the disjunction is exclusive and exhaustive, 'not defective' would imply 'equal', leading to 'excessive implies equal', which contradicts man' al-jam' between excessive and equal. Record this contradiction.
The author's chain: 'excessive implies not defective, not defective implies equal' yields 'excessive implies equal', violation.
3
Derive the second contradiction using man' al-khulu
From 'not excessive' we infer 'defective' (since one must be true), and from 'defective' we infer 'not equal'. So 'not excessive' implies 'not equal'. This means that 'excessive' and 'equal' are both false, which violates man' al-khulu (they cannot both be false). Record this contradiction.
Thus, a three-part exclusive disjunction leads to absurdity (khulf).
4
Discover the resolution
The passage says that the correct interpretation is that the statement is not a simple three-part exclusive disjunction. Instead, it is a compound: a categorical proposition ('the number is equal') and a disjunctive proposition ('or excessive or defective'). The underlying form is a two-part exclusive disjunction: 'the number is either equal or not equal', where 'not equal' is further detailed as 'excessive or defective'.
This preserves the exclusivity because 'equal' and 'not equal' are truly exclusive and exhaustive.
5
Apply the resolution to another example
Consider a legal statement: 'This act is either wajib, haram, or mubah.' Show why it cannot be an exclusive disjunction (mubah can coexist with not wajib and not haram), and then resolve it as a compound: 'The act is either wajib or not wajib; if not wajib, it is either haram or mubah.'
This mirrors the logical structure of the number example.

The True Composition of Exclusive Disjunction
Author refutes three-part exclusive disjunction, showing it reduces to two parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the composition of an exclusive disjunction and refute attempts to extend it beyond two parts using logical deduction.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction has exactly two parts; apparent three-part cases are composites.

You have learned that an exclusive disjunction (al-munfasila al-haqīqiyya) prevents both combination and emptiness. But what if a disjunction seems to have three parts, like 'the number is greater, lesser, or equal'? The author of this passage shows that such a structure is impossible for a true exclusive disjunction and explains its real composition. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing compound propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of exclusive disjunction
Recall that an exclusive disjunction (المنفصلة الحقيقية) requires exactly one part to be true: it prevents the conjunction of any two parts (مانعة الجمع) and also the emptiness of all parts (مانعة الخلو).
This is the starting point for the author's argument.
2
Recognize the three-part claim
The author considers the example: 'The number is either greater, lesser, or equal.' This appears to be a three-part disjunction. The claim is that it could be an exclusive disjunction if the three parts cannot be together nor all false.
The author will show this leads to contradiction.
3
Trace the reductio argument
The author assumes the three-part disjunction is exclusive. Then, he derives entailments: from 'greater' we get 'not lesser', and from 'not lesser' we get 'equal' (since emptiness is prevented). This forces 'greater' to imply 'equal', which violates exclusivity (they should be mutually exclusive). Similarly, from 'not greater' we get 'lesser' and then 'not equal', again a contradiction.
The argument uses the impossibility of both combination and emptiness.
4
Understand the correct composite structure
The author explains that the apparent three-part disjunction is actually a composite: a categorical proposition ('the number is equal or not equal') combined with a disjunction ('if not equal, then greater or lesser'). Thus, true exclusive disjunction reduces to two parts.
This clarifies the logical anatomy of compound propositions.

Understanding Contradiction in Propositions
Author defines contradiction as opposition of affirmation and negation that necessarily entails one truth and one falsity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a valid contradiction between two propositions by checking the conditions of sameness of subject, affirmation vs. negation, and inherent necessity of one truth and one falsehood.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires same subject, opposite qualities, and inherent truth-value opposition.

The concept of contradiction (التناقض) is fundamental in logic because it allows us to detect impossible pairs of statements. A contradictory pair can never both be true or both false — they are exact opposites. This lesson breaks down the definition given by the author, showing you how to parse a logical definition into its genus and differentia, and how to apply the conditions to real examples from Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus in the definition
The definition begins with 'opposition' (اختلاف). This is the broad category: contradiction is a kind of opposition between two things. Notice that the commentator explains that 'opposition' can occur between propositions, between terms, or between a term and a proposition. By restricting to propositions, we narrow down.
The genus is the first part of a definition that places the term in a wider class.
2
Extract the differentia
The differentia is what distinguishes contradiction from other types of opposition: it is opposition 'of two propositions in affirmation and negation' (بالإيجاب والسلب). This means one proposition must affirm and the other must negate the same predicate of the same subject. Also, the opposition must 'inherently necessitate' (لذاته) that one is true and the other false — this means the truth values are determined solely by the logical form, not by external facts.
The differentia contains the essential conditions; a valid contradiction must satisfy all of them.
3
Test with the provided example
The author gives the example: 'Zayd is a writer' and 'Zayd is not a writer'. Check each condition: same subject (Zayd), same predicate (writer) but one affirms and one negates, and inherent necessity: if one is true the other is automatically false, and vice versa. This is a perfect contradiction.
Always verify with an example to solidify understanding.
4
Distinguish contradiction from mere opposition
Not every pair of different propositions is a contradiction. For instance, 'Zayd is a writer' and 'Zayd is a scholar' are not contradictory because they can both be true. The key is the combination of affirmation/negation and the logical impossibility of both being true or both false.
This distinction is crucial for valid inferences in debates and proofs.

Definition of Contradiction and Its Five Conditions
Author defines contradiction and analyses its five conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given pair of propositions forms a contradiction by checking the five conditions taught in this passage.

Core Idea
Contradiction is the difference between two propositions by affirmation and negation necessitating one true, one false – by itself.

Contradiction (tanāquḍ) is a fundamental logical relation used extensively in Islamic theology and jurisprudence to establish proofs and refute falsehood. The classical logicians defined it with precise conditions to avoid confusion with other forms of opposition. This lesson unpacks the definition from the text and shows you how to apply the five conditions to real examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Find the two statements claimed to be contradictory. They must both be propositions (qadiyya) capable of truth and falsity. Check that they are not individuals or concepts.
The text uses 'Zayd is a writer' and 'Zayd is not a writer'.
2
Check that they differ by affirmation and negation
One proposition must affirm something, the other must negate the very same thing. For example, 'Zayd is a writer' (affirmation) vs 'Zayd is not a writer' (negation). If the opposition is not direct affirmation/negation, it is not tanāquḍ.
The text excludes differences by connection/disconnection, universality/particularity, etc.
3
Verify that one must be true and the other false
The difference must be such that it is impossible for both to be true or both false. If both can be true, like 'Zayd is stationary' and 'Zayd is not moving', it fails.
The text explicitly mentions this exclusion.
4
Ensure the necessity is by itself (li-dhātihī)
The necessity of one true and one false must arise from the logical form alone, not from external facts. For example, 'Zayd is a human' and 'Zayd is not a speaker' seem contradictory but are not, because the falsehood of the second depends on the coextensiveness of 'human' and 'speaker', not on the logical form.
This is the most subtle condition; it excludes material contradiction.
5
Apply the conditions to an Islamic example
Test a pair like 'This act is obligatory' and 'This act is not obligatory' from fiqh. Confirm all conditions are met: both propositions, affirmation/negation, one true one false by form alone.
Practice with other examples from kalam or usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines the logical term 'tanāquḍ' (contradiction) and explains its conditions through a careful breakdown of the definition. The author first presents the simple definition: contradiction is the difference between two propositions (qadiyyatayn) in affirmation (ījāb) and negation (salb) such that necessarily by itself one is true and the other false. The example given is 'Zayd is a writer' and 'Zayd is not a writer'. Then the commentary unpacks each part of the definition. Finally, 'by itself' eliminates cases where the necessity arises from an external factor, not the mere form of the propositions. For instance, 'Zayd is a human' and 'Zayd is not a speaker' are not contradictory by virtue of the difference itself because 'human' and 'speaker' are coextensive; the falsehood of 'Zayd is not a speaker' depends on the material fact that all humans are speakers by definition, not on the logical form. Thus, the definition precisely isolates formal logical contradiction.

Three Conditions for Contradiction: Potentiality, Whole-Part, Condition
Author lists the sixth, seventh, and eighth conditions for contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a pair of propositions violates any of the three conditions (potentiality/actuality, whole/part, condition) and therefore are not truly contradictory.

Core Idea
To be contradictory, two claims must match in mode, extent, and circumstance.

The logicians have identified eight conditions for two propositions to be truly contradictory. In this lesson, we examine the sixth, seventh, and eighth conditions: unity of potentiality and actuality, unity of whole and part, and unity of condition. Without these, two statements that appear contradictory may actually both be true or both false. Understanding these conditions sharpens your ability to detect false contradictions in Islamic arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions under scrutiny
Write down the two statements that appear contradictory. For example, 'Wine in the vat is intoxicating' and 'Wine in the vat is not intoxicating'.
2
Check for unity of potentiality and actuality
Ask: Do both statements refer to the same mode? If one says something is potentially the case and the other says it is actually not, they are not contradictory. The wine example: the first means potentially intoxicating, the second actually not.
The mode must be identical for contradiction.
3
Check for unity of whole and part
Ask: Do both statements refer to the same scope? If one refers to part of the subject and the other to the whole, they are not contradictory. The Zanjī example: 'some of him is black' vs 'all of him is not black' differ in scope.
The quantifier or implied scope must match.
4
Check for unity of condition
Ask: Are both statements made under the same condition? If conditions differ, they are not contradictory. The body example: 'dispersing light if white' vs 'not dispersing light if black' are both true under their own conditions.
The condition (sharṭ) must be identical.

Contradiction in Quantified Propositions
Author states the rule for contradiction in quantified categorical propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory of any standard categorical proposition (A, E, I, O) and verify the rule of contradiction.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires opposite quality and opposite quantity—one true, one false, always.

In Islamic logic, establishing a contradiction is essential for proofs and refutations. The rule for categorical propositions is simple: change both quantity and quality. This lesson unpacks that rule from the Isaghuji commentary and applies it to real Islamic examples from fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quality and quantity of the given proposition
Determine if the proposition is affirmative or negative (quality) and universal or particular (quantity). This gives you the type: A (universal affirmative), E (universal negative), I (particular affirmative), O (particular negative).
For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' is universal affirmative (A).
2
Apply the contradictory rule
To find the contradictory, flip both quality and quantity: if universal, make particular; if affirmative, make negative. A becomes O, E becomes I, I becomes E, O becomes A.
The contradictory of A is O, of E is I, of I is E, of O is A.
3
Verify the truth conditions
Ensure that the two propositions cannot be both true or both false. For a valid contradiction, one must be true and the other false, regardless of the subject's existence.
Classical logic assumes existential import; the examples here assume non-empty subject terms.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
Take a real fiqh ruling: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (A). Its contradictory is 'Some intoxicant is not forbidden' (O). If a jurist asserts the first, the opponent can contradict with the second.
This forces the debate to a clear logical opposition.

Author explains the condition for contradiction between quantified propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether two quantified propositions are contradictory by checking their quantity and quality.

Core Idea
For contradiction, propositions must differ in quantity and oppose in quality.

This passage establishes the precise condition for logical contradiction in quantified propositions. You will learn why two universals can both be false and why the contradictory of a universal is a particular, not another universal. This rule is essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify quantity
For each proposition, determine whether it is universal (using 'every' or 'no') or particular (using 'some').
Unquantified propositions are treated as particulars.
2
Check difference in quantity
For contradiction, one proposition must be universal and the other particular. If both are universal or both particular, they cannot be contradictory.
Two universals can both be false; two particulars can both be true.
3
Check opposite quality
One proposition must be affirmative, the other negative. If both are affirmative or both negative, they cannot be contradictory.
Quality is determined by the presence or absence of negation.
4
Confirm same subject and predicate
The subject and predicate terms must be identical in both propositions. If they differ, contradiction is irrelevant.
5
Verify the contradictory relation
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative; of a universal negative is a particular affirmative; of a particular affirmative is a universal negative; of a particular negative is a universal affirmative.
This follows from the conditions above.
