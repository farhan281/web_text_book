Introduction to the Five Universals
Author urges beginners to learn logical terms and introduces the five universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to name the five universals in Arabic and explain their dependency on the three modes of indication.

Core Idea
The five universals are the toolbox for every definition in the Islamic sciences.

This opening passage sets the stage for the entire logical project. The author insists that before you can define anything—whether in fiqh, kalam, or tafsir—you must master the five universals. But he also warns that these universals only make sense if you have already understood how words indicate meanings (the three modes). So, the lesson on indication is your key.

Guided Path
1
Locate the list
In the Arabic, find where the five universals are enumerated. Notice the terms: الجنس، النوع، الفصل، الخاصة، العرض العام.
2
Memorize the sequence
This list has a logical order: from most comprehensive (genus) to most specific (species) to distinguishing (differentia), then property and accident. Commit this order to memory.
3
Connect to prerequisites
The author states that these five depend on the three indications. Review the previous lesson on مطابقة, تضمن, التزام. Ask: Why is this prerequisite necessary? Because universals are abstract concepts indicated by words, and you must know how words indicate to grasp them.
4
Apply to one of the five
Take 'genus' as an example. Its definition as a universal is 'the whole that is predicated of many differing in species.' Without understanding indication, you cannot parse this definition.

Universal and Particular in Singular Terms
Matn defining kulli and juz'i with commentary explicating key terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a universal (kulli) from a particular (juz'i) singular term using the criterion of conceptual prevention of sharing.

Core Idea
The universal can be shared, the particular cannot—and the test is purely conceptual.

This foundational division in logic governs all predication. The matn from Isaghuji gives the precise criterion: the mere concept of the term either allows sharing or prevents it. Mastering this distinction is the key to understanding how terms function in definitions and arguments across the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being classified
Locate the word 'المفرد' (the singular) in the matn. This is the overarching category that is divided into universal and particular.
Remember that 'singular' here means a single word or term, not a grammatical number.
2
Focus on the criterion: 'very conceptualization'
The key phrase is 'نفس تصور مفهومه' (the very conceptualization of its concept). This signals that we consider only the meaning itself, not any external factors like existence or context.
The word 'نفس' excludes cases where prevention comes from something else, and 'تصور' excludes actual existence considerations.
3
Check for prevention of sharing
Ask: Does the concept itself prevent the term from being applied to multiple individuals? For 'الإنسان' (human), the concept does not prevent sharing, so it is universal. For 'زيد' (Zayd), the concept inherently refers to one specific individual, so it is particular.
Sharing (شركة) means the possibility of the term applying to many.
4
Apply the test to new terms
Take any singular term and test: Can you conceive of more than one individual fitting this term? If yes, it is universal; if no, it is particular. Practice with terms like 'كتاب' (book) and 'هذا الكتاب' (this book).
This test is purely conceptual; it does not depend on how many individuals actually exist.

Commentary
This passage defines the division of singular terms (المفرد) into universal (كلي) and particular (جزئي) based on the criterion of whether the mere conception of the term's meaning prevents it from being shared among multiple individuals. The key phrase is 'نفس تصور مفهومه' (the very conceptualization of its concept), meaning that the prevention or lack thereof is inherent in the concept itself, not due to external factors. For example, the concept 'human' (الإنسان) does not prevent being applied to many individuals, so it is a universal. Conversely, the concept 'Zayd' (زيد) as a proper noun inherently refers to a specific individual and thus cannot be applied to multiple persons; it is a particular. The commentary further explains that the qualifier 'نفس' (very) excludes cases where prevention arises from something else, and 'تصور' (conceptualization) excludes actual existence considerations. This division is fundamental to logic as it underlies all predication and reasoning.

Essential vs Accidental Universals
Author defines essential and accidental universals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and accidental universals and correctly classify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The essential universal is part of a thing's reality; the accidental is not.

This lesson introduces the foundational division of universals into essential (dhati) and accidental (aradi). Understanding this distinction is crucial for constructing and evaluating definitions (hadd) in logic and for analyzing concepts in Kalam and Fiqh. The passage defines both types with clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Locate the definitions of essential and accidental in the Arabic text. What is the key criterion that distinguishes them?
2
Understand the criterion
The criterion is whether the universal enters into the quiddity (reality) of its particulars. If it does, it is essential; if not, it is accidental.
3
Apply the criterion to the examples
The author gives 'animal' as essential for human and horse because it is part of their reality. 'Laughter' is accidental because it is not part of the definition of human.
Rationality (natiq) is also essential for human, as it is part of the definition.
4
Test with other examples
Consider whether 'body' is essential or accidental for human. Body is part of human's reality (as a material being), so it is essential. But is it a separate universal? Yes.

The Essence (Mahiyya) as Accidental
Commentator clarifies that mahiyya is not essential

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the mahiyya (essence) of a thing is considered accidental in the technical logical sense and apply this to definitions in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The mahiyya is not essential because it is the complete quiddity, not a component.

This lesson delves into a subtle point from the passage: the essence (mahiyya) itself is considered accidental according to the technical definition of 'essential'. This nuance prevents misunderstanding when analyzing definitions where the whole is mistaken for a part.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of essential
An essential universal enters into the reality of its particulars as a component.
2
Apply to mahiyya
Consider 'human' as a mahiyya. It is the entire reality of Zayd, not a component. Therefore, it does not 'enter into' the quiddity; it is the quiddity itself.
3
Understand the consequence
Since the mahiyya is not a part of the quiddity of its particulars, it fails the condition for being essential. Thus, it is classified as accidental in this technical sense.
This does not mean the mahiyya is unimportant; it means it is not a constituent universal.
4
Contrast with animal
Animal is a part of human's quiddity (human = animal + rational), so it is essential. Mahiyya (human) is the whole, so it is not essential.

The Essential as a Technical Term
Author resolves the paradox of calling mahiyya 'essential'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the mahiyya (essence) is called essential without contradiction, by distinguishing linguistic from terminological usage.

Core Idea
Naming the essence 'essential' is a technical term, not a linguistic claim.

The passage opens by addressing a potential logical problem: if 'essential' means 'attributed to the essence', then the essence itself cannot be called essential. The author resolves this by clarifying that the term 'essential' is used terminologically (istilahi), not linguistically, thus avoiding the paradox. This distinction is crucial for understanding the definitions that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The text states that if 'essential' meant 'attributed to the essence', then the essence itself could not be essential without self-attribution. Locate this problem in the Arabic.
2
Find the solution
The author says the naming is 'terminological' (istilahi), not linguistic (lughawi). Why does that avoid the contradiction? Because terminological terms can be defined arbitrarily without logical entailment.
3
Apply to a new example
Consider the definition of 'wajib' as 'that which the Lawgiver demands'. If we said 'the demand is demanding', would that be a problem? Use the terminological vs linguistic distinction.

Why the Essence Itself is Called Essential
Author resolves a potential contradiction over calling essence itself essential

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between linguistic and terminological usage of the term 'essential' (al-dhātī) and explain why the essence itself can be called essential without contradiction.

Core Idea
In logic, 'essential' is a technical term: calling the essence itself essential is allowed by convention, not by literal language.

The term 'essential' (al-dhātī) is fundamental in the theory of the five universals. However, defining it as 'that which is not accidental' seems to make the essence itself essential, which might appear to be a logical contradiction. In this passage, the author anticipates this confusion and clarifies the distinction between literal and terminological naming. Understanding this subtle point is crucial to avoid misinterpreting the foundational definitions of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of al-dhātī
Find the definition given in the passage: 'al-dhātī is that which is not accidental, i.e., not external to the essence.' Recognize that this is a negative definition.
The definition is simple but leads to a puzzle.
2
Spot the potential contradiction
If al-dhātī means 'not external', then the essence itself (the core nature) qualifies as al-dhātī. Some might object that 'essential' should mean 'related to the essence', and a thing cannot relate to itself.
This objection would make the definition self-refuting.
3
Understand the objection's basis
The objection assumes 'essential' is used in a linguistic sense, implying a relation. Realize that this would make the definition of al-dhātī impossible.
4
Analyze the author's resolution
The author explains that here 'essential' is a terminological (iṣṭilāḥiyya) term, not a literal (lughawiyya) one. So the objection does not apply; the definition stands as a technical convention.
Terminological definitions are agreed upon by specialists and do not require literal consistency.
5
Apply the distinction to future definitions
When you encounter similar terms in logic or other Islamic sciences, always check whether the usage is linguistic or terminological to avoid false objections.
This skill prevents many misunderstandings in 'ilm al-mantiq.

The essential (al-dhātī) is the core of any definition. In this lesson, we examine how the logicians divide the essential into three types—genus, species, and difference—based on how they answer the questions 'what is it?' and 'which thing is it?'. Understanding this division is the first step toward mastering the five universals and constructing precise definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of essential
The author lists genus, species, and difference as the divisions of the essential. Note that each is defined by its relation to the question 'mā huwa' (what is it?) or 'ayyu shayʼin huwa fī dhātihi' (which thing is it in its essence?).
The essential is what constitutes the quiddity.
2
Analyze the scope of genus
Genus is predicated of multiple species in answer to 'what is it?' with pure commonality. Example: 'animal' is said of both human and horse when asking about them together. But it does not fully answer the question for an individual species; the complete essence includes a difference.
Pure commonality means no particularity.
3
Analyze the scope of species
Species is predicated of multiple individuals differing in number but not in reality, answering 'what is it?' with both commonality and particularity. Example: 'human' applies to 'Amr and Zayd equally.
Species is the most specific universal in this context.
4
Analyze the scope of difference
Difference is not predicated in answer to 'what is it?' but rather to 'which thing is it in its essence?'. It distinguishes the species from co-genera. Example: 'rational' distinguishes human from horse.
Difference is the essential differentia (fasl).
5
Understand why these are descriptions not definitions
The predicative aspect is accidental to the quiddity. For instance, being predicated of many is not part of the essence of 'animal'; it is a property that follows from its reality. Hence the text uses 'yurasamu' (it is described) rather than 'yuḥaddu' (it is defined).
Rasm is a description that may include accidents.

Understanding the Species (al-Naw') and Its Rasm
Definition and description of species (al-naw')

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the species (al-naw') in a given universal and distinguish it from genus, differentia, property, and universal accident using the rasm provided.

Core Idea
Species is the universal predicated in answer to 'what is it?' of individuals that share an essence but differ by accidents.

Species is one of the five universals essential for classification in Islamic logic. In this lesson, we examine how the author defines species through an example of Zayd, 'Amr, and Bakr, then provides a precise rasm (description) that excludes all other universals. By parsing each component, you will learn to test any candidate universal for specieshood.

Guided Path
1
Understand the example of Zayd, 'Amr, and Bakr
The author asks 'what is it?' about a group of individuals (Zayd, 'Amr, Bakr) and about Zayd alone. The answer is 'human' in both cases, showing that species is predicated both by commonality (shared essence) and particularity (individual essence).
Note that the same term 'insan' answers the essence-question for both group and individual.
2
Extract the rasm definition from the text
The rasm is: 'a universal predicated of many differing in number without reality in answer to what is it'. Write down each component: universal, predicated, of many, differing in number, without reality, in answer to what is it.
The author notes 'universal' is redundant because 'predicated of many' already implies universality.
3
Analyze what each component excludes
Go component by component: 'predicated' includes both universal and particular; 'of many' excludes particular individuals; 'differing in number' excludes genus, differentia, property, and accident (these differ in reality); 'without reality' excludes differentia, property, and accident; 'in answer to what is it' excludes universal accident (which answers 'which is it?') and other universals.
The phrase 'differing in number' means the individuals differ by accidents and individuations, not by essence.
4
Identify the real species (al-naw' al-haqiqi)
The author states that this rasm defines the real species, which is what is meant when 'species' is used absolutely. Real species are natural kinds like 'human' or 'horse', as opposed to logical species which may be broader.
'Number' refers to individuators (mushakhkhasat) and accidents; individuals of a species differ only in these.
5
Apply the criteria to a new example
Take a candidate universal like 'animal'. Does it fit the rasm? 'Animal' is predicated of many differing in number (e.g., Zayd, a horse) but also differing in reality (humans and horses have different essences). Therefore 'animal' is a genus, not a species. Try 'human' again: it passes all conditions.
Practise with other examples like 'rational' (differentia) or 'risible' (property) to see which component fails.

Necessary and Separating Accidents
First division of accidents: necessary vs. separating

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a necessary accident and a separating accident based on the possibility of separation from the essence.

Core Idea
An accident is necessary if it never leaves its essence; separating if it can.

The first division of accidents in logic is based on whether the accident can be removed without destroying the essence. This distinction is foundational for understanding how accidents relate to the quiddity (mahiyya). The passage gives clear definitions: the necessary accident is inseparable, while the separating accident is not. Let's examine these definitions closely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
In the passage, the author first divides the accidental into two kinds. Find the Arabic terms: العَرَض اللازم (necessary accident) and العرض المُفارِقُ (separating accident). What is the criterion for this division?
The criterion is the possibility of separation from the essence (الماهية).
2
Understand the criterion
The necessary accident is defined as that whose separation from the essence is impossible. The separating accident is defined as that whose separation is not impossible. So the key is whether the accident can ever be absent while the essence remains.
This is an essential distinction in determining which attributes can be used in definitions.
3
Apply to examples
Think of an attribute like 'heat' for fire: Can fire exist without heat? No, so heat is a necessary accident of fire. For a human, 'whiteness' is not necessary because a human can be non-white. Thus whiteness is a separating accident.
Use Islamic examples: In kalam, the attribute 'existence' (wujud) is a necessary accident for God (since He cannot not exist), while 'creating' is a separating accident (He may or may not create at a given time).
4
Test yourself
Given a specific accident, decide if it is necessary or separating. For example, is 'pottery' a necessary accident for a pot? No, because a pot can be broken. So it is separating.
This skill is used in usul al-fiqh when evaluating whether an attribute can be the effective cause ('illah) of a ruling.

Proper Accident and General Accident
Second division: proper vs. general accidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a proper accident (khassa) and a general accident ('araḍ 'āmm) based on their scope of application.

Core Idea
A proper accident is limited to one species; a general accident spans many species.

After the primary division of accidents, the author further classifies each type according to scope. Some accidents are unique to a single reality (like 'capable of laughter' for man), while others are shared across multiple realities (like 'capable of breathing' for man and other animals). This distinction is critical for constructing accurate descriptions (rasm).

Guided Path
1
Find the second classification
The passage states: 'وكلُ واحد منهما، إما يمتنع أن يختص بحقيقة واحدة، وهو الخاصة ... وإما أن يعم حقائق فوق واحدة، وهو العرض العام'. This means: Each of the two (necessary and separating) is either specific to one reality (that is the proper accident) or general across more than one reality (the general accident).
Thus, the proper accident (khassa) and general accident are subdivisions of both necessary and separating accidents.
2
Define proper accident
The proper accident is defined as a universal that is said of things under a single reality accidentally. Example: 'capable of laughter' for man. It is specific only to the species of man.
Note that it is said accidentally, not essentially. It is not part of the definition but a property that follows from it.
3
Define general accident
The general accident is a universal that is said of things under different realities accidentally. Example: 'capable of breathing' for man and other animals. It applies to multiple species.
The rasm defines it as a universal predicated of things under different kinds (حقائق مختلفة) accidentally.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider the attribute 'being a messenger' for Prophets. Is it a proper accident for each prophet? Actually, 'messenger' is a general accident because it applies to many prophets. But 'being the Seal of the Prophets' is a proper accident for Prophet Muhammad (SAW) alone.
In 'ilm al-mantiq, this distinction helps in forming accurate descriptions.

1
Identify the primary division basis
Read the Arabic: 'the accidental is either necessary or contingent, because it is either impossible to separate from the essence, or not impossible.' Recognize that the key property is separability from the essence.
This division is foundational for further classification.
2
Distinguish necessary accident subtypes
Note from the marginal commentary that necessary accidents are of two types: lazim al-mahiyya (inseparable in mind and reality, e.g., oddness for three) and lazim al-wujud (inseparable only in external existence, e.g., blackness for the Abyssinian). Understand that both are necessary but differ in modal scope.
3
Understand contingent accident
A contingent accident can separate from the essence, whether it actually does quickly (redness of shyness), slowly (youth), or not at all (perpetual poverty possible to enrich). The possibility of separation is what matters.
Contingent does not mean 'separated now' but 'separable in principle'.
4
Learn the secondary division: proper vs. common
Each of necessary and contingent is either proper (خاصة) – restricted to one species like the potential to laugh for humans – or common (عرض عام) – shared across multiple species like breathing. The definition: a proper accident is predicated of only one reality accidentally; a common accident of many realities.
The same division applies to both necessary and contingent accidents.
5
Evaluate the division order
The commentator notes that logically one should first divide the accidental universal into proper and common, then divide each into necessary and contingent. But the author's order (necessary/contingent first) is still valid because both necessary and contingent can be subdivided by scope. This shows flexibility in taxonomy.
See al-Kalanbawi's justification: the concept of 'proper' is uniform across necessary and contingent.

Dividing Accidental Universals: Necessary, Separable, Property, Common Accident
Author divides accidental universal into necessary and separable, then into property and common accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an accidental universal as necessary or separable, and as property or common accident, using the criteria from the Isaghuji tradition.

Core Idea
Accidents are either inseparable from the essence or separable; each is either exclusive or shared.

In this passage, the author of Isaghuji refines the classification of accidental universals. He first divides the accidental into what necessarily accompanies the essence and what can separate from it. Then he shows that each of these may be unique to one essence or common to many. Understanding these divisions is essential for precise definition in Islamic logic and sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
Look for the first split: necessary (lāzim) and separable (mufāriq). Note the criterion: impossibility of separation vs possibility.
The passage states: 'I say: The accidental: either necessary or separable, because either it is impossible for it to separate from the essence, or it is not impossible.'
2
Understand the examples
The necessary accident is exemplified by writing in potentiality for man; the separable by writing in actuality. Consider why these fit each category.
Writing in potentiality is inseparable from the essence of man because man is always potentially a writer; actual writing comes and goes.
3
Apply the second bifurcation: property vs common accident
Each of the two types (necessary and separable) is further divided based on exclusivity to a single essence. If it applies to only one essence (e.g., laughing for man), it is a property; if to multiple essences (e.g., moving for man and horse), it is a common accident.
The passage explicitly says: 'And each one of them is either a property or a common accident.'
4
Verify using the definition of property
The passage defines property as 'a universal predicated of what is under a single reality only, accidentally.' Check that this definition excludes genus, species, differentia, and common accident.
This is a formal definition (rasm) of the property.

Author defines the proprium and explains its definition components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the proprium among the five universals and break down its definition using the criteria of essence confinement and accidental predication.

Core Idea
The proprium is an accident exclusive to one essence, either inseparable or separable.

In this lesson, we examine the fourth of the five universals: the proprium (al-khassa). The author distinguishes it from genus, common accident, species, and differentia by two key criteria: confinement to a single essence and accidental predication. Understanding this precision is essential for constructing accurate definitions in Islamic logic and its applied sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for proprium
Look for the phrase 'إن اختص بحقيقة واحدة فقط' (if confined to one essence). This is the defining condition that distinguishes the proprium from other universals.
2
Distinguish inseparable vs separable

4
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider the property 'mujtahid' (one who performs ijtihad) as a proprium for certain qualified scholars. It is accidental (not part of human essence) and exclusive to those who meet the criteria.
This shows the utility of the proprium in disciplines like usul al-fiqh.

Identifying the Common Accident (al-'Arad al-'Amm)
Author defines the common accident as a universal predicated on things under different essences accidentally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the common accident among the five universals and construct its definition (rasm) by its distinguishing conditions.

Core Idea
The common accident binds different essences without being essential.

The common accident ('arad 'amm) is the fifth of the five universals. Unlike the proprium, it is not confined to a single essence; it applies to multiple species. In this lesson, we will dissect the definition given by the author and see how the conditions of 'under different essences' and 'accidentally' exclude the other universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for common accident
The author states: if an accident (inseparable or separable) is not confined to one essence but applies to more than one, it is the common accident. Note the two types: inseparable and separable.
2
Analyze the example
The example is 'potentially or actually breathing' for humans and other animals. Potentially breathing is inseparable (cannot separate from the animal), actually breathing is separable (sometimes not breathing). Both are not confined to one species.
This illustrates that both inseparable and separable accidents can be common.
3
Parse the rasm (definition) components
The rasm is: 'a universal that is said about what is under different essences accidentally.' Break it down: 'universal' (redundant), 'is said' (genus of all universals), 'about what is under different essences' (excludes species, differentia, proprium), 'accidentally' (excludes genus).
The redundancy of 'universal' is noted by the author.
4
Test the rasm against other universals
Genus is excluded because it is essential predication, not accidental. Species, differentia, and proprium are excluded because they are confined to one essence. Verify that the definition includes all common accidents and excludes all others.
5
Apply to a new example
Consider 'white' said of snow, milk, and paper. Is it a common accident? Yes, because it is accidental and applies to multiple essences. Use the rasm conditions to confirm.
This reinforces the concept.

Definition of Accident General (ʿĀraḍ ʿĀmm)
Author defines the accident general and explains its definitional components.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the accident general and explain each component of its definition (rasm).

Core Idea
Accident general is a universal accidental predicate across multiple realities.

The five universals are the tools for forming definitions. Among them, the accident general is the broadest accidental universal. You already know the species, differentia, and property; now we examine the accident general and the precise conditions that separate it from the genus. Master this to avoid confusion in defining terms across the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: it is 'al-ʿaraḍ al-ʿāmm' (accident general). The passage opens with conditions for when a universal qualifies as this.
The Arabic phrase starts 'فهو العرض العام'.
2
Extract the definition components
The author gives a rasm: 'a universal that applies to things under different realities as an accidental predicate.' Break it into 'universal', 'applies to things under different realities', and 'as an accidental predicate'.
Each component is analyzed to exclude other universals.
3
Understand the exclusions
The author explains that 'universal' is redundant; 'applies to things under different realities' excludes species, differentia, and property (they apply under one reality); 'as an accidental predicate' excludes genus (which is essential).
This shows how definition works by inclusion and exclusion.
4
Identify the two subtypes
The passage distinguishes between necessary (lāzim) and separable (mufāriq) accidents, both can be accident general if they cover multiple realities.
Example: 'potential breather' is necessary, 'actual breather' is separable.

Refining the Definition: Why 'Predicated in Answer' Was Omitted
Author defends omission of a potential qualification from the definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the phrase 'predicated in answer to any thing is it in its accident' is omitted from the definition of accident general, and distinguish between distinguishing and non-distinguishing accident generals.

Core Idea
Not every accident general can serve as a predicate in definition; only those that distinguish their subject are used.

You have learned the basic definition of accident general. Now we examine a subtle objection: why didn't the author include the phrase 'predicated in answer to any thing is it in its accident'? This lesson unpacks the response, which hinges on the function of definitions and a key distinction between types of accident generals.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objection argues that since accident general can be predicated in answer to 'any thing is it in its accident', this should be part of its definition.
The omitted phrase would add precision.
2
Identify the two types of accident general
The response divides accident general into two types: those that do not distinguish anything (like 'thing', 'possible') and those that do distinguish their subject (like 'walker').
Non-distinguishing ones cannot define anything.
3
Apply the later logicians' principle
Later logicians (mutāʾakhirūn) ruled that only distinguishing terms can be used in definition. Since many accident generals are non-distinguishing, they cannot be used in the answer, so the phrase is not added to the definition.
The definition only needs to work for distinguishing accident generals.
