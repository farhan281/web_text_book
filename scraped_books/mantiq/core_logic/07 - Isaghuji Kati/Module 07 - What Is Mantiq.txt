Definition of Indication and Its Three Types
Author defines indication and classifies it into natural, rational, and conventional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of indication (natural, rational, conventional) and provide Islamic examples for each.

Core Idea
Indication is the bridge from the known to the unknown, and it comes in three forms.

This passage establishes the foundational concept of indication, which is the basis of all logical reasoning. By defining it and classifying its types, the author gives us the tools to understand how signs work, whether natural, rational, or conventional. This distinction is crucial for grasping how words function in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of indication
Identify the key phrase 'كون الشيء بحالة يلزم من العلم به العلم بشيء آخر'. Note that it is a relational definition: from knowing one thing, you come to know another.
2
Extract the roles of دال and مدلول
Recognize that the indicator (دال) is the first thing known, and the indicated (مدلول) is the second thing learned. This is the basic structure of any sign relationship.
3
Distinguish the three types of indication
Natural indication (طبعية) is based on nature, like a groan for pain. Rational indication (عقلية) is based on inference, like smoke for fire. Conventional indication (وضعية) is based on agreement, like words for meanings. Logical discussion primarily deals with conventional indication.
The text only gives an example of natural indication, but the other two are mentioned by name.

Commentary
This passage defines the general concept of indication (دلالة). The author states that indication is a relation between two things such that knowing one (the indicator) leads to knowing the other (the indicated). He then derives the definitions of 'indicator' (دال) and 'indicated' (مدلول) from this. Next, he divides indication into three types: natural (طبعية), rational (عقلية), and conventional (وضعية). The example given for natural indication is the groan 'Ah Ah' indicating chest pain, which is a natural consequence. This classification is essential for understanding how words (conventional indication) work in logic, as they are a subset of conventional indication.

Key Terms
الدلالة (al-dalala): The state of a thing by which knowledge of another thing follows. [Note: In later texts, additional conditions are added, such as the necessity of the relation.]
الدلالة الطبعية (al-dalala al-tab'iyya): Indication based on natural causation, e.g., a groan indicating pain. [Note: Some logicians limit natural indication to involuntary bodily signs.]
الدلالة العقلية (al-dalala al-'aqliyya): Indication based on rational inference, e.g., smoke indicating fire. [Note: This is often called 'dalalat al-'illa wa al-ma'lul' in later texts.]
الدلالة الوضعية (al-dalala al-wad'iyya): Indication based on conventional assignment, e.g., words. [Note: Most of logic deals with this type.]

The Three Types of Verbal Signification
Author defines and illustrates the three divisions of conventional verbal signification: مطابقة, تضمن, التزام.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given word's indication is by conformity (muṭābaqah), inclusion (taḍammun), or implication (iltizām), and justify your classification using the criteria from this passage.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: full meaning, part, or necessary concomitant.

This passage from the Isāghūjī introduces the three types of conventional verbal signification that are fundamental to all later logical analysis. Understanding these distinctions allows you to clarify how words convey meanings in definitions and arguments. We will walk through the three types with a clear example from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the meaning indicated
Determine what meaning the word is being used to convey in a given context. Ask: is this the complete meaning the word was coined for, a part of that meaning, or something else that necessarily accompanies it in the mind?
2
Apply the criterion of coinage
Check if the indicated meaning matches exactly what the word was originally coined for. If yes, it is muṭābaqah. If it is a part of that meaning, it is taḍammun. If it is something external that always accompanies the meaning (like a necessary property), it is iltizām.
The passage states: اللفظ الدال على المعنى لا يخلو من أن يدل على تمام ما وضع له، أو يدل على جزء ما وضع له، أو يدل على ما يلازمه في الذهن.
3
Test with a concrete example
Take a word like 'insān' (human). By conformity, it signifies 'rational animal'. By inclusion, it signifies just 'animal' (a part). By implication, it signifies 'capable of knowledge' (a necessary concomitant). Apply the same method to an Islamic science term like 'ṣalāt' (prayer).
The passage uses 'insān' for muṭābaqah and taḍammun. For iltizām, a common example is 'khamr' (wine) implying intoxication.

Three Types of Verbal Indication in Logic
Author defines three types of verbal indication with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between the three types of verbal indication (muṭābaqa, taḍammun, iltizām) and apply them to Islamic legal and theological terms.

Core Idea
Words signify meaning in three ways: as a whole, as a part, or by necessity.

Understanding how words convey meaning is foundational to logic and Islamic sciences. The author of the Isāghūjī classifies verbal indication into three categories based on whether the word indicates its entire meaning, a part of it, or a necessary concomitant. This lesson unpacks each type with classical examples and shows their relevance to fiqh, kalām, and uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types in the Arabic text
Locate the terms muṭābaqa, taḍammun, iltizām in the passage. Note the condition that triggers each type: first (correspondence), second (concomitance), third (implication).
The author uses 'if the first, then ... if the second, then ... if the third, then ...' to structure the classification.
2
Examine the example for muṭābaqa
The word 'insān' indicates the full definition 'ḥayawān nāṭiq' by correspondence. This is the entire meaning coined for the word. The term 'muṭābaqa' is derived from the comparison of soles matching perfectly.
Muṭābaqa is like a one-to-one mapping between the word and its complete meaning.
3
Examine the example for taḍammun
The same word 'insān' can indicate just 'animal' or just 'rational' because these parts are implicit (ḍimnan) within the whole. This is indication by concomitance.
Taḍammun focuses on a part of the meaning that is included in the whole, like a subset.
4
Examine the example for iltizām
'Insān' indicates 'capable of knowledge' and 'writing' as necessary concomitants. These are not part of the definition but necessarily follow from it. Ignorance implies knowledge because ignorance is defined as the absence of knowledge in something that ought to be known.
Iltizām involves an external meaning that is inseparable from the essential meaning.
5
Apply to an Islamic term
Consider the word 'ṣalāh' (prayer). It indicates the entire prayer act (muṭābaqa), a part like rukūʿ (taḍammun), or an associated ruling like its obligation (iltizām). Practice identifying these with other Islamic terms.
This analytical skill helps in interpreting Qur'anic and hadith texts accurately.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of verbal indication: al-mutābaqa (correspondence), al-taḍammun (concomitance), and al-iltizām (implication). The author begins by establishing the condition: if the word indicates the entire meaning for which it was coined, it is correspondence. The example of 'insān' (human) is given, which indicates its full definition 'ḥayawān nāṭiq' (rational animal). The term 'mutābaqa' is explained etymologically from 'ṭābaqa al-naʿl' (the sole matches the sole) meaning correspondence. Next, if the word indicates only a part of its meaning, it is taḍammun (concomitance). The same example 'insān' when it indicates only 'animal' or only 'rational' is used. The term 'taḍammun' refers to the part being implicit (ḍimnan) in the whole. Finally, if the word indicates something inseparable from its meaning but not part of it, it is iltizām (implication). The example of 'insān' indicating 'capable of knowledge and writing' is given, as these are necessary concomitants of humanity, not part of the definition. The passage then elaborates on iltizām with the example of 'jahl' (ignorance) implying 'ʿilm' (knowledge), because ignorance is defined as the absence of knowledge in something that ought to be known. This threefold division is crucial in logic for understanding how terms convey meaning and is foundational for definitions and syllogisms. In the Islamic sciences, for instance, the word 'ṣalāt' indicates the entire prayer act (mutābaqa), a part like rukūʿ (taḍammun), or an associated rule like its obligation (iltizām).

Mental Concomitance in Concomitant Indication
Author defines the condition of mental concomitance for concomitant indication, with objections and answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why mental concomitance is required for dalala al-iltizam and distinguish it from external concomitance using the example of blindness.

Core Idea
Concomitant indication requires mental association, not real-world connection.

In this passage, the author clarifies a key condition for concomitant indication: the necessary association between a word's primary meaning and an external meaning must be mental, not external or real. This distinction is crucial for understanding how words like 'blindness' can indicate 'sight' by concomitance, even though blindness is a privation. We will unpack the reasoning behind this condition and see how it resolves potential objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Find the terms 'dalala al-iltizam' (دلالة الالتزام) and 'luzum dhihni' (لزوم ذهني) in the passage. Note that the author emphasizes the restriction 'في الذهن' (in the mind).
2
Understand the rejection of external concomitance
Read the argument: if external concomitance were required, then the indication would fail for non-existent entities like blindness, which indicate their opposites (sight) by mental association. The example shows that blindness is a privation with a 'tinge of existence', not a real connection.
The author uses a conditional proof: if external concomitance were necessary, then dalala al-iltizam would not hold for many cases, which is false; so the condition is false.
3
Address the objection about metaphors
Consider the objection: metaphors seem to indicate external meanings without mental concomitance. The reply distinguishes universal indication from particular: metaphorical understanding relies on contextual indicators, making the whole phrase the indicator, not the word alone. This is not considered valid indication in logic.
The key is that logical indication must be universal (kulliyya) and not dependent on context.
4
Distinguish from inclusive indication
Notice the clarification that blindness indicates sight by concomitance, not by inclusion (tadhammun). Because blindness is a negation added to sight, sight is external to it, not a part. This reinforces that the indication is mental, not part-whole.
The distinction ensures that dalala al-iltizam is not confused with dalala al-tadhammun.

The author explains why: if external concomitance were required, then the indication would fail for non-existent entities like blindness, which is the absence of sight, yet we understand sight from the word 'blindness' by concomitance. The commentary then addresses an objection: metaphorical usage might seem to provide indication without mental concomitance, but the reply is that such understanding relies on a contextual indicator (qarina), making the indicator the whole phrase, not the word alone, and such particular indications are not considered in this art because they are not universal. The passage further clarifies that blindness is a privation (adam) with a 'tinge of existence' (sha'iba al-wujud), as it applies only to what is apt to have sight. It also refutes a potential claim that this is not to be inclusive indication (tadhammuniyya), because blindness is a negation added to sight, so sight is external to it, not included. An Islamic example: the word 'khamr' (wine) indicates its prohibition concomitantly, because the mind associates with it the legal ruling of prohibition, even though the ruling is not part of its definition.

Core Idea
A word's simplicity or composition depends on whether its parts individually indicate aspects of its meaning.

Guided Path

Orderal Delay and Customary Causation in Commentary
Commentary discusses two linguistic/logical issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between orderal and temporal delay, and analyze the two questions about 'lamma' in the commentary.

Core Idea
Logical terms like 'thumma' and 'lamma' require contextual interpretation beyond their literal meanings.

The commentary on the matn raises two subtle issues: the meaning of 'thumma' (then) and the implications of 'lamma' (when). These linguistic nuances are essential for correctly interpreting logical and theological texts, where word choice can imply causation or sequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the use of 'thumma' in the matn
The matn begins 'ثُمَّ اللفظ إما مفرد' (Then the word is either simple...). The commentator clarifies that this 'thumma' indicates orderal delay, not temporal. That is, the topic follows in the logical order of the book, not necessarily in time.
This is related to the concept of 'tartib' in classification.
2
Understand the commentator's distinction
He says 'التراخي المستفاد من ثم رتبي لا زماني' (the delay from thumma is orderal, not temporal). This means the sequence is conceptual, like chapters in a book, not a real-time delay.
Compare to Quranic 'thumma' sometimes indicating temporal sequence, e.g., in creation verses.
3
Analyze the first question about 'lamma'
Al-Mawlawi asks: the word 'lamma' in 'لما فرغ من بيان...' (When he finished explaining...) implies necessary causation, but finishing one topic does not logically necessitate starting another. The answer: causation can be customary ('ādī), based on the typical practice of authors.
Customary causation is weaker than logical necessity but sufficient for discourse.
4
Analyze the second question about 'lamma'
The second question is temporal: 'lamma' with past tense implies a specific time of finishing and beginning, but one time cannot hold both. The answers include interpreting 'beginning' as intention, or taking 'lamma' as approximative ('taqlīl'), not temporal.
This shows how linguistic analysis resolves apparent contradictions.
5
Apply to a new context
When you see 'lamma' in a theological text, consider whether it indicates necessary causation, customary causation, or mere approximation. This prevents misinterpretation of arguments.
Example: In kalam, 'lamma thabata al-huduth' (when temporality was established) may imply customary sequence in reasoning.

Definition of Proposition (al-Qadiyya)
Al-Kati defines the proposition (qadiyya) with genus and differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the definition of a proposition and distinguish propositions from non-propositions.

Core Idea
A proposition is a truth-apt statement; commands and questions are not propositions.

Before we can evaluate arguments, we must know what a proposition is. This lesson unpacks al-Kati's definition of 'qadiyya' — the building block of all logical reasoning. You will learn to separate truth-apt statements from other utterances using the genus (qawl) and differentia (truth/falsehood evaluability).

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus (jins)
The genus in the definition is 'qawl' (statement/utterance). It includes all composites, whether verbal or mental, complete or incomplete. Note that 'qawl' is broader than the proposition itself.
The author notes that qawl includes complete and incomplete utterances, as well as performatives. Only complete composites that are truth-apt become propositions.
2
Extract the differentia (fasl)
The differentia is 'it is valid to say of its utterer that he is truthful or false in it'. This condition excludes performatives (commands, questions, exclamations) that cannot be evaluated as true or false.
The phrase 'yasiH' (it is valid) indicates linguistic appropriateness, not actual truth value. A false statement is still a proposition because it's evaluable.
3
Understand the exclusion of performatives
Performative utterances like 'Do not lie' or 'Is Zayd standing?' do not admit truth or falsehood. They are complete composites but not propositions. This distinction is crucial for logical analysis.
The commentary explicitly states that performatives (insha'iyyat) do not admit truth/falsehood.
4
Apply the definition to a test case
Take a statement such as 'Allah is one'. Check: Is it a qawl (composite)? Yes. Can one validly call the utterer truthful or false in this statement? Yes — it is truth-apt. Therefore it is a proposition. Contrast with 'Be grateful!' which is a command and not truth-apt.
This step reinforces the practical use of the definition.

Categorical and Conditional Propositions
Al-Kati gives examples of categorical and conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given proposition as categorical, conditional conjunctive, or conditional disjunctive based on its logical structure.

Core Idea
Categorical propositions assert directly; conditionals link propositions with 'if' or 'or'.

Al-Kati divides propositions into three types: categorical (hamliyya), conditional conjunctive (shartiyya muttasila), and conditional disjunctive (shartiyya munfasila). Each has a distinct logical structure that determines how it functions in arguments. This lesson will help you recognize and categorize these forms.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the categorical example
The example 'Zayd is a writer' (زيد كاتب) has a subject (Zayd) and a predicate (is a writer). This is the simplest proposition type: it directly affirms or denies a predicate of a subject.
Categorical propositions can be affirmative or negative, universal or particular. The example is affirmative and particular (since Zayd is a specific person).
2
Analyze the conditional conjunctive example
The example 'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists' (إن كانت الشمس طالعة فالنهار موجود) consists of two categorical propositions joined by 'if...then'. The first part (sun rising) is the antecedent, the second (daytime exists) is the consequent. The whole is true unless antecedent is true and consequent false.
This is called conjunctive conditional (mutasila) because it implies a connection. It is not simply an 'and' statement; it reflects implication.
3
Analyze the conditional disjunctive example
The example 'The number is either even or odd' (العدد إما أن يكون زوجاً أو فردا) presents two alternatives joined by 'either...or'. Only one can be true at a time (exclusive disjunction). This disjunctive conditional states that exactly one of the two possibilities holds.
Disjunctive conditionals can be exclusive (mani'at al-jam') or inclusive (mani'at al-khuluw). The commentary later elaborates on these subtypes.
4
Practice classification with new examples
Take a statement like 'If it rains, the ground becomes wet' (conditional conjunctive), 'The world is either eternal or created' (conditional disjunctive), and 'Allah is one' (categorical). Confirm each type by checking its structure.
Use examples from Islamic sciences: 'If the traveler shortens prayer, then the journey is permissible' (conditional), 'Either the Quran is created or uncreated' (disjunctive).

The Definition of Qiyas and Its Four Conditions
Definition of qiyas with four conditions unpacked.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four conditions in the definition of qiyas and distinguish qiyas from non-syllogistic reasoning forms (induction, analogy, qiyas al-musawat) based on those conditions.

Core Idea
A syllogism's conclusion must follow necessarily from its premises alone, without any external assumption.

The passage defines qiyas (syllogism) and explains its four essential conditions. Understanding these conditions is crucial because they separate genuine deductive inference from other forms like induction, analogy, and arguments that require extra premises. This distinction is foundational for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Locate the formal definition of qiyas
Find the Arabic sentence: 'القياس قول مؤلف من أقوال متى سلمت لزم عنها لذاتها قول آخر'. This is the core definition. Break it into parts: the subject (qiyas), the genus (qawl mu'allaf min aqwal), and the differentia (mata sullimat lazima 'anha li-dhatiha qawl akhar).
The definition is introduced with an example from kalam.
2
Understand 'mata sullimat' (when granted)
This condition indicates that the premises need not be actually true; they only need to be hypothetically granted. This allows syllogisms with false premises to still be valid deductions. Note the example: 'Every human is inanimate; every inanimate is a donkey; therefore every human is a donkey' is a valid syllogism in form, though its premises are false.
This condition ensures the definition covers both true and false premises.
3
Understand 'lazima 'anha' (necessarily follows from them)
This condition requires that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises, with no possibility of exception. It excludes induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil) because their conclusions are not necessary – they allow for counterexamples.
Induction observes many instances but cannot guarantee the next; analogy compares attributes but may miss differences.
4
Understand 'li-dhatiha' (by themselves)
The entailment must be from the premises alone, without any extraneous premise. This excludes qiyas al-musawat (syllogism of equality) where transitivity of equality requires an extra premise. Also excludes other cases needing an extra lemma.
The example: 'A equals B; B equals C' does not by itself yield 'A equals C' without the transitivity of equality premise.
5
Understand 'qawl akhar' (another statement)
The conclusion must be different from each premise. If it were the same, it would be begging the question (musadara). Also, without this condition, any two premises would count as a syllogism if they imply one of them, which is absurd.
The author notes an objection regarding the example 'Every human is an animal; every stone is inanimate' implying one of them.

Commentary
This passage is an extended definition of qiyas (syllogism) from Kati's commentary on the Isaghuji. The author first gives the standard definition: 'al-qiyas qawl mu'allaf min aqwal mata sullimat lazima 'anha li-dhatiha qawl akhar' (a syllogism is a statement composed of statements such that when they are granted, another statement necessarily follows from them by themselves). He then unpacks each part. The definition is introduced with an example from theology: the changing world implies it is temporally originated, a typical kalam argument. The commentary then clarifies that 'statement' includes both mental concepts and uttered words. He notes that 'statements' must be more than one to exclude immediate inferences like conversion or contraposition, which follow from a single statement. The condition 'mata sullimat' (when granted) does not require actual truth in reality, only that if they were granted, the conclusion would follow. This allows false premises to still form a valid syllogism, as illustrated by the absurd example: 'Every human is inanimate, every inanimate is a donkey, therefore every human is a donkey.' The condition 'lazima 'anha' (necessarily follows from them) excludes induction and analogy, since their conclusions are not necessary—they allow for exceptions. The condition 'li-dhatiha' (by themselves) excludes syllogisms that require an extraneous premise, like qiyas al-musawat (syllogism of equality), where the transitivity of equality is not inherent in the two given statements but requires an additional premise. The author also gives another example from substance metaphysics: part of substance and removal of substance. Finally, the condition 'qawl akhar' (another statement) ensures the conclusion is different from the premises, preventing circular reasoning. If the conclusion were the same as a premise, it would be begging the question, and any two statements would count as a syllogism if they imply one of them, which is absurd. The passage ends with a note from al-Kalanbawi questioning an example.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A syllogism is not just any two statements; it requires self-contained necessary implication without extraneous premises.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the classical definition of qiyas (syllogism). You will learn what makes an argument a syllogism in the strict logical sense, what conditions it must satisfy, and what kinds of reasoning (like induction and analogy) are excluded. This understanding is essential for evaluating proofs in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition's key components
The definition states: 'qiyas is statements (more than one) that, if accepted, necessarily yield another statement by virtue of themselves.' Note the four key parts: 'statements' (plural), 'if accepted', 'necessarily yield', and 'by virtue of themselves'.
The author uses 'aqwal' (statements) not 'muqaddimat' (premises) to avoid circularity.

Analyze the condition 'if accepted'
This means the premises do not have to be true in reality. They only need to be such that if they were granted, the conclusion would follow. This allows syllogisms with false premises to still be valid in form.

3

The phrase 'follows from them' excludes induction and analogy because their conclusions are not necessary. Even if their premises are accepted, the conclusion may not hold due to exceptions.
Induction and analogy produce probability, not certainty.
4
Recognize the need for self-contained implication
The phrase 'by virtue of themselves' excludes arguments like the syllogism of equality (A=B, B=C implies A=C) because they require an extra premise (e.g., transitivity) not stated in the original two statements.
This ensures the syllogism is self-sufficient.

Core Idea
Burhan is a syllogism built from certain premises to yield certain knowledge.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Certainty in Islamic logic comes from reason, senses, experience, intuition, widespread testimony, or implicit reasoning.

Each type is distinct in how the mind obtains certainty: self-evident truths, sensory observation, repeated experience, intuitive leaps, mass transmission, and propositions with an inherent middle term. Distinguish tricky pairs

First Three Certain Premises: Awwaliyat, Mushahadat, Mujarrabat
Author enumerates first three certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish first principles, sense observations, and experiential premises by identifying the cognitive basis of each.

Core Idea
Certitude arises from immediate reason, direct sense, or repeated experience — each with a distinct epistemological foundation.

After defining certainty, the author lists six types of certain premises. We will focus on the first three, which are foundational: awwaliyat (first principles), mushahadat (observations), and mujarrabat (experiences). Understanding their differences helps us evaluate the strength of arguments in different fields.

Guided Path
1
Identify awwaliyat
These are judgments the intellect makes simply by understanding the terms, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'.
No external aid is needed.
2
Identify mushahadat
Judgments based on direct sense perception, e.g., 'fire is hot' or 'we feel anger'.
Can be external or internal senses.
3
Identify mujarrabat
Judgments that become certain only after repeated observations, e.g., 'scammony purges bile'.
Single observation is not enough.
4
Compare the three
Awwaliyat are a priori; mushahadat are empirical singular; mujarrabat are empirical generalised.

Certainty is: belief of a thing that it cannot be except thus, a belief matching reality, not possible to be removed. His saying 'cannot be except thus' excludes opinion: which is the preponderant. His saying 'matching reality' excludes compound ignorance, for although it is a belief that it cannot be except thus, it does not match reality in itself. His saying 'not possible to be removed' excludes the belief of the blind follower, because the belief therein is not from proof and thus can be removed. The certain things are divisions: from them are first principles: which are what the intellect judges by merely conceptualizing the two extremes, like our saying: one is half of two, and the whole is greater than the part. From them are observations: which are what the intellect judges by sense, whether from the external or internal senses, like our saying: fire is burning, the sun is shining, and our saying: we have anger and fear. From them are experiences: which are what the intellect needs, in judging the ruling therein, to repeat observation time after time, like our saying: drinking scammony is a laxative for bile. This ruling is obtained only by means of many observations. From them are intuitions: which are what the intellect does not need, in judging the ruling therein, to a means of repeating observation, like our saying: the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun because of the variation of its luminous shapes according to the variation of its positions from the sun, proximity and distance. The more correct is that it is expression for obtaining, when attending to objectives, the middle terms instantly, the objectives being represented with the middle terms in the mind without movement. As for discursive thought, it is movement in meanings from objectives to their principles; perhaps it is cut off and perhaps it arrives. When it arrives, it is completed by another movement from principles to objectives. So it is then in need of two movements, thus there is possibility of non-arrival and existence of movement or two movements. In intuition, non-arrival and non-movement are impossible, because the transition therein is instantaneous, not gradual. Thus the expression 'swiftness' is metaphorical, as verified by al-Sa'd al-'Allama. Thus it is understood. From them are recurrent reports: which are what the intellect judges definitively by hearing from a large multitude whose agreement upon falsehood is intellectually impossible, like the judgment that the Prophet, peace be upon him, claimed prophethood and produced a miracle at his hand. Commentary
This passage defines the concept of certainty (yaqin) and enumerates the six types of certain premises (yaqiniyat) that form the basis of demonstration (burhan). The author first defines burhan as a belief composed of certain premises yielding certainty. Then certainty is defined in three parts: it is a belief that a thing cannot be otherwise, it must match reality, and it must be unwavering. Each part excludes a different fallacy: 'cannot be otherwise' excludes mere opinion (zann), 'matching reality' excludes compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), and 'unwavering' excludes the belief of a blind follower (muqallid) who lacks evidence. The six types of certain premises are then listed. (1) Awwaliyat (first principles): judgments the mind makes simply by understanding the terms, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'. (2) Mushahadat (observations): judgments based on external or internal senses, e.g., 'fire is hot' or 'we feel anger'. (3) Mujarrabat (experiences): judgments requiring repeated observations to become certain, e.g., 'scammony purges bile'. (4) Hadsiyat (intuitions): judgments reached by a sudden mental leap without repeated observation, e.g., deducing that moonlight is borrowed from the sun due to its phases. The author includes a lengthy footnote clarifying that intuition is not swift discursive thinking but an instantaneous grasp of the middle term. (5) Mutawatirat (recurrent reports): knowledge acquired from such a large number of transmitters that collusion on falsehood is impossible, e.g., the prophethood of Muhammad (s). (6) Qadaya qiyasatuha ma'ha (propositions whose syllogisms are with them): judgments where the middle term is always present in the mind, e.g., 'four is even', where the middle term 'divisible into equal parts' is immediately present. The passage concludes by defining the middle term (wasat) as what accompanies the statement.

Intuition (Hadsiyat) and Its Distinction from Discursive Thought
Author defines intuitions and corrects a misconception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the instantaneous nature of intuitive knowledge and how it differs from discursive reasoning.

Core Idea
Intuition is an immediate, movement-free grasp of the middle term — unlike discursive thought which requires two movements.

The author lists intuitions (hadsiyat) as a source of certainty, but then adds a corrective commentary. Some define it as swift thinking, but the correct view is that it is an instantaneous apprehension without any mental movement. This distinction is critical for understanding how certain knowledge can be obtained without explicit reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Find the initial definition of hadsiyat
The author says: 'which are what the intellect does not need, in judging the ruling therein, to a means of repeating observation'. Example: moonlight derived from sun.
The key is no need for repetition.
2
Understand the alternative incorrect definition
Some defined it as swift mental transition (suraʿat intiqāl al-dhihn).
This is rejected.
3
Learn the correct definition
Correct: obtaining the middle terms instantly, with the objectives and middles present simultaneously without movement.
No motion, no potential for interruption.
4
Contrast with discursive thought (fikr)
Fikr requires two movements: from objectives to principles, then back. It may be interrupted or fail. In intuition, non-arrival is impossible and there is no movement.

Recurrent Reports and Self-Evident Syllogisms
Author concludes enumeration of certain premises and defines middle term.

Core Idea
Certainty can be achieved through mass testimony or through judgments where the middle term is ever-present in the mind.

The author says: 'The middle term is that which accompanies our saying when it is said such and such.'

ظاهرة والأصوب أنه عبارة عن الظفر عند الالتفات إلى المطالب بالحدود الوسطى دفعة تمثل المطالب مع الحدود الوسطى في الذهن من غير حركة، أما الفكر فهو حركة في المعاني من المطالب إلى مبادئهم فربما ينقطع وربما ادعى النبوة وأظهر المعجزة على يده، ومنها قضايا قياساتها معها وهي ما يحكمُ العقلُ فيه بواسطة أمر حاضر لا يغيب عن الذهن عند تصورِ الطرفين كقولنا: الأربعة زوج، بسبب وسط حاضر في الذهن، وهو الانقسام بمُتساويين، والوسط: ما يقترن بقولنا حين يُقَالُ كذا وكذا.
Demonstration is a belief from certain premises to produce certainty as has passed in examples. Certainty is: belief of a thing that it cannot be except thus, a belief matching reality, not possible to be removed. His saying 'cannot be except thus' excludes opinion: which is the preponderant. His saying 'matching reality' excludes compound ignorance, for although it is a belief that it cannot be except thus, it does not match reality in itself. His saying 'not possible to be removed' excludes the belief of the blind follower, because the belief therein is not from proof and thus can be removed. The certain things are divisions: from them are first principles: which are what the intellect judges by merely conceptualizing the two extremes, like our saying: one is half of two, and the whole is greater than the part. From them are observations: which are what the intellect judges by sense, whether from the external or internal senses, like our saying: fire is burning, the sun is shining, and our saying: we have anger and fear. From them are experiences: which are what the intellect needs, in judging the ruling therein, to repeat observation time after time, like our saying: drinking scammony is a laxative for bile. This ruling is obtained only by means of many observations. From them are intuitions: which are what the intellect does not need, in judging the ruling therein, to a means of repeating observation, like our saying: the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun because of the variation of its luminous shapes according to the variation of its positions from the sun, proximity and distance. (1) His saying 'intuitions' — some explained it as swiftness of the mind's transition from principles to objectives, but this contains apparent imprecision. The more correct is that it is expression for obtaining, when attending to objectives, the middle terms instantly, the objectives being represented with the middle terms in the mind without movement. As for discursive thought, it is movement in meanings from objectives to their principles; perhaps it is cut off and perhaps it arrives. When it arrives, it is completed by another movement from principles to objectives. So it is then in need of two movements, thus there is possibility of non-arrival and existence of movement or two movements. In intuition, non-arrival and non-movement are impossible, because the transition therein is instantaneous, not gradual. Thus the expression 'swiftness' is metaphorical, as verified by al-Sa'd al-'Allama. Thus it is understood. From them are recurrent reports: which are what the intellect judges definitively by hearing from a large multitude whose agreement upon falsehood is intellectually impossible, like the judgment that the Prophet, peace be upon him, claimed prophethood and produced a miracle at his hand. From them are propositions whose syllogisms are with them: which are what the intellect judges by means of something present that does not absent itself from the mind when conceptualizing the two extremes, like our saying: four is even, due to a middle term present in the mind, which is division into two equals. The middle term is that which accompanies our saying when it is said such and such.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of certainty (yaqin) and enumerates the six types of certain premises (yaqiniyat) that form the basis of demonstration (burhan). The author first defines burhan as a belief composed of certain premises yielding certainty. Then certainty is defined in three parts: it is a belief that a thing cannot be otherwise, it must match reality, and it must be unwavering. Each part excludes a different fallacy: 'cannot be otherwise' excludes mere opinion (zann), 'matching reality' excludes compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), and 'unwavering' excludes the belief of a blind follower (muqallid) who lacks evidence. The six types of certain premises are then listed. (1) Awwaliyat (first principles): judgments the mind makes simply by understanding the terms, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'. (2) Mushahadat (observations): judgments based on external or internal senses, e.g., 'fire is hot' or 'we feel anger'. (3) Mujarrabat (experiences): judgments requiring repeated observations to become certain, e.g., 'scammony purges bile'. (4) Hadsiyat (intuitions): judgments reached by a sudden mental leap without repeated observation, e.g., deducing that moonlight is borrowed from the sun due to its phases. The author includes a lengthy footnote clarifying that intuition is not swift discursive thinking but an instantaneous grasp of the middle term. (5) Mutawatirat (recurrent reports): knowledge acquired from such a large number of transmitters that collusion on falsehood is impossible, e.g., the prophethood of Muhammad (s). (6) Qadaya qiyasatuha ma'ha (propositions whose syllogisms are with them): judgments where the middle term is always present in the mind, e.g., 'four is even', where the middle term 'divisible into equal parts' is immediately present. The passage concludes by defining the middle term (wasat) as what accompanies the statement.
