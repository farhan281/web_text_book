The Commentary Tradition: Sharh and Hashiya
Author lists several important commentaries on the Isaghuji and their authors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main commentaries on al-Abhari's Isaghuji and distinguish between a sharh (commentary) and a hashiya (gloss).

Core Idea
The Isaghuji tradition builds layer by layer: matn, then sharh, then hashiya.

The Isaghuji is a foundational logic text, and over centuries scholars wrote commentaries (sharh) and glosses (hashiya) to explain and refine it. This lesson introduces the key works and their relationships, using the author's list as a map of the tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text
The base text is al-Abhari's Isaghuji. All commentaries in this passage are built upon it.
The Isaghuji is a brief introduction to logic (mantiq).
2
List the sharhs (commentaries)
From the passage, extract the names of the authors who wrote direct commentaries on the Isaghuji: al-Kati, al-Fanari, al-Abadi, al-Shirazi, Zakariyya al-Ansari, and al-Mughnisawi.
Each sharh has its own characteristics praised by the author.
3
Identify the hashiyas (glosses)
Notice that al-Kati's sharh attracted further glosses: by al-Siyalkuti, Muhyi al-Din, and al-Mawlawi (al-Qawl al-Ma'qul). The author also mentions his own gloss.
A hashiya is a supercommentary, often written on the margin.
4
Understand the hierarchy
The order of study typically goes: matn → sharh → hashiya. Each layer adds explanation and critical refinement.
Knowing this hierarchy helps a student choose appropriate texts.

Evaluating Commentaries: Criteria for Choosing a Sharh
Author lists several important commentaries on the Isaghuji and their authors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of different commentaries on the Isaghuji based on the author's criteria of conciseness, precision, and practicality.

Core Idea
Not all commentaries are equal; the author praises clarity, conciseness, and the ability to resolve difficulties.

When studying a foundational text like the Isaghuji, choosing the right commentary is crucial. The author of this passage evaluates several commentaries, highlighting their unique benefits. This lesson examines his criteria so you can make an informed choice in your own studies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's praise for each commentary
Read the passage and note what the author praises for each: al-Kati – 'concise clarification and ease of expression'; al-Fanari – 'conciseness and firmness of style' (but almost a riddle); al-Mughnisawi – 'most precise and beneficial' and 'analyzed problematic issues without affectation'.
The author also notes that al-Mughnisawi's commentary is recommended for preparation for advanced books.
2
Extract the criteria used
From the praises, derive criteria: conciseness (ijaz), clarity (wuduh), precision (diqqa), ability to resolve doubts (daf' shukuk), and practicality (ta'min al-isti'dad lil-wi lug fi al-kutub al-sa'ba).
These criteria are common in Islamic scholarly tradition for evaluating commentaries.
3
Apply the criteria to a hypothetical choice
If you are a beginner, which commentary would be most suitable? According to the author, al-Kati's might be easier, while al-Mughnisawi's is more advanced. The author recommends al-Mughnisawi only after a solid foundation and with a teacher.
The author stresses that al-Mughnisawi's commentary requires a teacher expert in education methods.

Parsing an Islamic Manuscript Colophon
Author's colophon with completion date and prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the author, completion date, and prayers from an Islamic manuscript colophon.

Core Idea
A colophon is a scholarly fingerprint: author, date, and prayer.

Colophons are the final words of a manuscript, where the author records his name, the date of completion, and prayers for forgiveness and blessing. They provide crucial historical and bibliographic data. This lesson teaches you how to parse a standard colophon using the example from the commentary of Isaghuji.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's self-reference
Look for phrases like 'al-'abd al-faqir' or 'al-mu'allif' followed by a name. Here, 'al-'abd al-faqir ila mawlahu al-ghani' introduces the author as Qasim ibn Na'im al-Hanafi.
2
Locate the completion date
Find words indicating time: 'wafaqa al-faragh' (coincided with completion) followed by a year and month. The colophon gives 'sanat alf wa arba' mi'a wa sab'a 'ashar' (1417) in 'Rabi' al-Thani'.
The date is written in full words, not numerals.
3
Extract the prayers and invocations
After the name, the author asks forgiveness for himself, parents, teachers, and all Muslims. Note the phrases 'ghafara allah' and 'salla allahu 'alayhi'.
These prayers are formulaic but vary in detail.
4
Note the honorific phrases for the Prophet
The colophon includes a phrase praising the Prophet: 'bi-hurmati man kana nabiyyan wa Adam bayna al-ma' wa al-tin' and 'zannatu la ilaha illa Allah'. These are standard but reflect the author's theological stance.

Commentary
This passage is the colophon of the commentary. It identifies the author as Qasim ibn Na'im al-Hanafi, who humbly refers to himself as a poor servant in need of Allah. The prayer ends with salutations upon the Prophet and his family. The completion date is given as Rabi' al-Thani 1417 AH, which corresponds to approximately August-September 1996 CE. This colophon provides key historical information about the scholar and the timeframe of the work.
