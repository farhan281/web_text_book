Poetic Syllogisms: The Power of Imaginative Premises
Definition and examples of poetic syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a poetic syllogism and distinguish its effect on the soul from demonstrative certainty.

Core Idea
Poetic syllogisms move the soul but do not produce knowledge.

In the conclusion of his commentary, the author introduces poetic syllogisms as a type of qiyas that uses imaginative premises to influence emotions. Unlike demonstrative arguments, they aim at affecting the soul's disposition, not establishing truth. This lesson unpacks their nature and examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'poetic syllogism' in the Arabic
Find the word 'الشعر' in the passage and note how it is defined.
2
Examine the examples given
Read the two examples: wine as a flowing ruby and honey as a bitter emetic. Notice how each premise aims to evoke an emotional response.
3
Analyse the effect on the soul
The author states that the soul expands (انبساط) with desire or contracts (انقباض) with aversion. This is the purpose of poetic syllogisms.
4
Contrast with demonstrative syllogisms
Poetic premises are imaginative and not certain, so they produce no knowledge. Demonstration uses yaqini premises and yields certainty.

الخاتمة
تنقبض كما إذا قيل : الخمرُ ياقوتةٌ سَيَّالة انبسطت النفس ورغبت في شربها، وإذا قيل: العسل مُرَّةً مُهوّعةٌ انقبضت النفس وتنفّرت عن أكلها، ومنها المغالطة: وهي قياس مؤلف من مقدمات كاذبة شبيهة بالحق، أو بالمشهورة، أو من مقدمات وهمية كاذبة، والغلط: إما من جهة الصورة، أو من جهة المعنى، أما ما يكون من جهة الصورة فكقولنا لصورة الفرس المنقوشة على الجدار: إنَّها فرش وكلُّ فرس صَهَّالٌ، ينتج أن تلك الصورة صهالة، وأما ما يكون من جهة المعنى فكقولنا: كلُّ إنسان وفرس فهو إنسان، وكلُّ إنسان وفرس فهو فرش، ينتج بعض الإنسان فرش، اعلم أَنَّ ما عليه الاعتمادُ والتَّعويلُ من هذه القياسات إنما هو البرهان، لكونه مُركَّباً من المقدّمات اليقينيَّة، وليكن هذا آخر ما كتبناه من الأوراق لإيضاح ما في كتاب إيساغوجي.
The conclusion: ... as when it is said: 'Wine is a flowing ruby' the soul expands and desires to drink it, and when it is said: 'Honey is a bitter emetic' the soul contracts and is repelled from eating it. And among them is the sophistical [syllogism]: it is a syllogism composed of false premises resembling truth, or resembling accepted [premises], or composed of false imaginary premises. And the error is either in the form or in the meaning. As for that which is in the form, it is like our saying regarding a picture of a horse painted on a wall: 'It is a horse, and every horse neighs', resulting that that picture neighs. And as for that which is in the meaning, it is like our saying: 'Every man and horse is a man, and every man and horse is a horse', resulting that some man is a horse. Know that the one to rely upon and trust among these syllogisms is only the demonstrative, because it is composed of certain premises. And let this be the last of what we wrote from the papers to clarify what is in the book Isaghuji.

Commentary
This passage from the conclusion of the commentary on Isaghuji discusses two types of non-demonstrative syllogisms: poetic and sophistical. The author first mentions the poetic syllogism (al-qiyas al-shi'ri), which uses imaginative premises to affect the soul's emotions, expanding or contracting it. Examples are given: describing wine as a flowing ruby causes desire, while describing honey as a bitter vomit-inducing substance causes aversion. Then the author discusses the sophistical syllogism (al-mughalata), defined as a syllogism composed of false premises that resemble truth or accepted opinions, or imaginary false premises. The error in sophistical arguments can be formal (sura) or material (ma'na). A formal fallacy example: arguing that a picture of a horse is a horse, and all horses neigh, therefore the picture neighs. A material fallacy example: arguing that every man and horse is a man, and every man and horse is a horse, therefore some man is a horse. The author concludes that the only reliable syllogism is the demonstrative (burhan), because it consists of certain premises. This concludes the commentary.

Sophistical Syllogisms: Errors and the Primacy of Demonstration
Definition of sophistical syllogism and classification of errors; conclusion on demonstration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material errors in sophistical syllogisms and explain why demonstration is the only reliable form of reasoning.

Core Idea
Sophistical syllogisms deceive by using false premises that resemble truth; only demonstrative syllogisms yield certainty.

This passage continues the conclusion by discussing sophistical syllogisms (المغالطة). The author explains that errors in such syllogisms can be either in form (invalid structure) or in meaning (false content). He provides clear examples of each. Finally, he asserts that only demonstrative syllogisms (البرهان) are reliable because they consist of certain premises.

Guided Path
1
Define sophistical syllogism
Find the definition: a qiyas composed of false premises that resemble truth, accepted opinions, or imaginary false premises.
2
Identify the two types of error
The author distinguishes error in form (الصورة) and error in meaning (المعنى). Read the examples carefully.
3
Analyse the formal fallacy example
The example: 'This picture of a horse is a horse; every horse neighs; therefore this neighs.' Note why the error is considered formal — the structure is valid but the minor premise is false (the picture is not a horse). Actually the author classifies it as formal; understand his classification.
According to the text, the error is from the form, perhaps because the term 'horse' is used equivocally between the real and the picture.
4
Analyse the material fallacy example
The example: 'Every man and horse is a man; every man and horse is a horse; therefore some man is a horse.' Here the error is material because the premises themselves are false (men and horses are not a single entity).
5
Understand the conclusion
The author declares that demonstration (burhan) is the only reliable type because it uses yaqini premises. All other forms (poetic, sophistical, rhetorical) lack certainty.
