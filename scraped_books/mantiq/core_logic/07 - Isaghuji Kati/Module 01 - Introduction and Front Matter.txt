The Isaghuji: Gateway to Islamic Logic
Title page presenting book name, author, commentator, and publisher.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author, commentator, and significance of the Isaghuji text in the Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
The Isaghuji is the foundational textbook for Islamic logic, taught for centuries in madrasas.

This lesson introduces the title page of the Isaghuji, a classic commentary on al-Abhari's introductory logic text. Knowing the author and commentator helps you place the work in the Hanafi scholarly tradition. Understanding its role as a memorization anchor will show you why it remains essential for students of Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the book title and subject
Read the Arabic title: 'متن الإيساغوجي في المنطق'. 'الإيساغوجي' is the name of the book, and 'المنطق' indicates the subject is logic.
The title is a commentary (sharh) on the original Isaghuji by Athir al-Din al-Abhari.
2
Identify the author
The phrase 'تأليف العلامة المحقق حسام الدين حسن الكاتيلي' tells you the author is Husam al-Din Hasan al-Kati, a Hanafi scholar and verifier.
Al-Kati lived in the 8th/14th century; his commentary became the standard gloss on al-Abhari's text.
3
Identify the commentator and publisher
The phrase 'علق عليه قاسم بن نعيم الحنفي' shows the annotator is Qasim ibn Nu'aym al-Hanafi. 'دار الكتب العلمية' is the publisher, Dar al-Kutub al-Ilmiyya in Beirut.
The commentator added marginal notes (ta'liqat) to clarify difficult points.
4
Recognize the text's role in madrasa education
The Isaghuji is a memorization anchor: students commit its definitions and rules to memory before advancing to higher logic and other Islamic sciences.
This is why the front matter is important—it establishes the authority and chain of transmission.

The Greek Origins and Translation of Logic
Author recounts the history of logic before Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the origin of logic in Greek philosophy and explain why early Muslim scholars were concerned about its translation.

Core Idea
Logic was born in Greek schools, mixed with kufr, then translated into Arabic.

This lesson traces the birth of logic in the schools of Greek philosophers like Aristotle. It explains how logic remained embedded in Greek language and culture for centuries before reaching the Islamic world. You will see why Muslim scholars viewed this imported knowledge with caution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the birthplace of logic
Find the phrase that tells you where logic first developed. The Arabic mentions 'أحضان مدارس فلاسفة وحكماء اليونان'.
2
Recognize the philosophers named
The text names two variants for Aristotle. Note that the author uses 'كارستطاليس وأرسطو' to refer to the same figure.
3
Assess the state of logic before Islam
The Arabs in Jahiliyya did not know formal logic. Their natural disposition included some rules that did not conflict with any religion. What does this imply about the purity of their reasoning?
4
Trace the translation event
When logic was translated in the Abbasid era, scholars became worried. Why? Because it was mixed with infidel philosophy. Identify the phrase 'خطير على عقائد المسلمين'.
This sets the stage for the debate on permissibility.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the introduction (مقدمة) to the commentary on al-Isaghuji. The author, al-Kati, sets the historical and methodological context for the study of logic. He starts with the basmalah and hamdalah, then states the purpose: to discuss the origin of logic. He explains that logic first developed in Greek philosophical schools, specifically mentioning Aristotle (under two names: Caristatlis and Aristo), and that it was mixed with unbelief (kufr) due to its association with Greek philosophy. It remained in Greek for centuries. The pre-Islamic Arabs did not know this formal logic; their natural disposition and dialogues only contained some logical rules that did not conflict with any religion. When Greek logic was translated into Arabic in the Abbasid period, Muslim scholars became concerned because of the admixture of kufr. Some jurists, like al-Nawawi and Ibn al-Salah, ruled it haram to study. Others, like Ibn Sina, al-Farabi, and Ibn Rushd, allowed it for those with a strong faculty to resist harmful influences, and even considered it commendable. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the point is that the statements of the earlier scholars vary. This introduction is crucial for understanding the author's approach: he will present logic as a useful tool, purified from its Greek origins, in line with those who permitted it.

Scholarly Debate on the Permissibility of Logic
Author presents fatwas on logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two main positions among early Muslim scholars regarding the study of logic and name the key proponents of each side.

Core Idea
Imam al-Nawawi forbade logic; Ibn Sina deemed it good to learn.

The translation of Greek logic sparked a fierce debate among Muslim scholars. Some jurists, concerned about its philosophical baggage, issued fatwas of prohibition. Others, especially the philosophers, argued that logic could be a beneficial tool when studied by someone with a strong intellect. This lesson examines the key figures on both sides.

Commentary
This passage is a preface (muqaddimah) that sets the historical and theological context for the study of Islamic logic. The author begins with the traditional basmalah and hamdalah, then outlines the Greek origins of logic, noting that it was mixed with 'infidel philosophy' (falsafah kafirah). He explains that the pre-Islamic Arabs had a natural logical faculty, not the formal Greek system. When Greek logic was translated in the Abbasid period, scholars were divided: some prohibited it (like al-Nawawi and Ibn al-Salah) due to its corrupting influence, while others like Ibn Sina, al-Farabi, and Ibn Rushd permitted it for those with intellectual immunity. The author clarifies that these early rulings applied only to the original Greek logic. The logic available today, he asserts, is an Islamic science, purified by Imam al-Ghazali who stripped it of philosophical overlays and organized it. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi then expanded and refined it further. The author emphasizes the importance of logic as a tool for protecting the mind from error, essential for dialectic (jadal) against innovators, for usul al-fiqh, and even for hadith terminology. He notes that al-Ghazali used fiqh examples in his 'Mi'yar al-'Ilm' to demonstrate logical rules, showing the integration of logic with Islamic jurisprudence.

The Structure of an Islamic Logic Primer: From Utterances to Demonstration
Lists the main sections of the book from introduction to conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the logical progression of the Isaghuji from simple terms to complex arguments and identify the purpose of each major section.

Core Idea
Islamic logic progresses systematically from words to propositions to syllogisms to proof arts.

This lesson introduces the table of contents of the Isaghuji, a foundational text in Islamic logic. At first glance, it may appear to be just a list of chapters. But these chapters represent a deliberate pedagogical journey from the most basic elements of reasoning—utterances and their classifications—through propositions and contradictions, to the full machinery of syllogism and demonstration. By understanding this structure, you gain a mental map that will guide you through the entire course. The order is not arbitrary: each section builds on the previous one, much like the foundations of a building.

Guided Path
1
Group the sections into three logical stages
The classic division in logic is into simple terms (tasawwur) and judgments (tasdiq). Try to see which sections deal with concepts and which with propositions and arguments.
The first sections (utterance division, universals, explicative statement) are conceptual; the middle (propositions, contradiction, conversion) are propositional; the later (syllogism, demonstration, etc.) are argumentative.
2
Identify the five arts of proof
The sections near the end list demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. These are the five types of logical discourse that utilize syllogisms.
Notice that poetry and sophistry are included even though they are not demonstrative—why do you think they are part of a logic text?
3
Note the placement of the explicative statement
The section on definition (al-qawl al-sharih) appears right after the five universals. This is not coincidence: the five universals are the building blocks of definitions.
The explicative statement shows how to combine them into definitions. This placement helps you understand the role of definitions in logic.
4
Observe the symmetry of the introduction and conclusion
The book begins with two introductions (author and commentator) and ends with a conclusion. In between, it moves from simple to complex.
This framing reinforces the idea that logic is a complete, structured discipline with a clear beginning and end.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents listing the major sections of the book. It follows the standard structure of many Islamic logic texts based on Aristotle's Organon. The book begins with an introduction and a commentator's introduction, then proceeds to define 'utterance' (lafz) and divide it into simple and compound. Next, it covers the universal and particular, then the essential and accidental attributes. The 'five universals' (al-kulliyat al-khams) are a central topic. The 'explicative statement' (al-qawl al-sharih) deals with definition. Propositions (qadaya), contradiction (tanaqud), and conversion ('aks) are discussed before syllogism (qiyas). The final sections cover the five arts of proof: demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal) and rhetoric (khataba), poetry (shi'r) and sophistry (mugalata). The book ends with a conclusion. This structure shows how logic proceeds from terms to propositions to arguments, and then applies logic to different types of discourse.
