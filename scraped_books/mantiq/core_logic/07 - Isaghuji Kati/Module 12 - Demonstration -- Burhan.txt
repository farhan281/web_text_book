Burhan and the Six Certain Premises
Author defines demonstration and enumerates six types of certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six types of certain premises and explain how they underpin demonstrative syllogisms.

Core Idea
Demonstration yields certainty only from certain premises of six kinds.

Here the author defines demonstration (burhan) and then provides the six categories of certain premises from which demonstrative syllogisms must be built. Understanding these categories is essential for evaluating the strength of any proof in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of burhan
Note that burhan is defined as a syllogism (qiyas) composed of certain premises (muqaddamat yaqiniyya) to produce certainty (yaqin). This is the genus and differentia.
Demonstration is not any syllogism, but one with certain premises.
2
List the six categories of certain premises
Enumerate them: primary principles (awwaliyyat), observations (mushahadat), experiments (mujarrabat), intuitive judgments (hadsiyyat), mutawatir reports (tawaturat), and propositions with their syllogisms inherent (qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha).
These are the only types of premises considered certain in this classification.
3
Link each category to its example
For each category, note the concrete example provided: awwaliyyat – 'one is half of two'; mushahadat – 'the sun is risen'; mujarrabat – 'scammony is a laxative'; hadsiyyat – 'moonlight comes from the sun'; tawaturat – 'Muhammad (s) claimed prophethood and showed miracles'; qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha – 'four is even due to divisibility into equal parts'.
These examples illustrate how each type yields certainty in a different way.
4
Understand the epistemological grounding
Reflect on why each type is considered certain: awwaliyyat are self-evident; mushahadat are based on direct sense perception; mujarrabat are from repeated experience; hadsiyyat are strong intuitive inferences; tawaturat are from overwhelming transmission; qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha have the middle term immediately present in the mind.
This shows that certainty can arise from multiple sources, not just one.
5
Connect to the purpose of burhan
Recall that burhan must be composed of premises from these categories. Any syllogism using premises from these types will yield certain knowledge. If a premise is not certain, the syllogism cannot be a demonstrative proof.
This classification is foundational for evaluating arguments in kalam, fiqh, and other Islamic disciplines.

Commentary
This passage defines demonstration (burhān) and lists the types of certain premises (yaqīniyyāt) from which it is composed. Demonstration is a syllogism that yields certain knowledge (yaqīn) when its premises are themselves certain. The author enumerates six categories of certain premises. First, primary principles (awwaliyyāt) are self-evident truths such as 'One is half of two' and 'The whole is greater than the part'. Second, observations (mushāhadāt) are sensory perceptions like 'The sun is risen' or 'Fire is burning'. Third, experiments (mujarrabāt) are conclusions from repeated experience, e.g., 'Scammony is a laxative for yellow bile'. Fourth, intuitive judgments (hadsiyyāt) are immediate inferences from strong intuition, like deducing that the moon's light originates from the sun. Fifth, mutawātir reports (tawāturāt) are widely transmitted reports that yield certainty, e.g., the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him) and his miracles. Sixth, propositions whose syllogisms are with them (qaḍāyā qiyāsātuhā maʿahā) are those where the middle term is instinctively present in the mind, such as 'Four is even' because the mind immediately grasps that it is divisible into two equal parts. These categories provide the foundational certainties for demonstrative reasoning in logic and the Islamic sciences.

The Art of Demonstration (Burhan)
Definition of burhan and its significance as the mainstay of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define burhan and explain why it is considered the primary logical art for establishing certainty in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Burhan is the only syllogism that yields certainty; it is the foundation of true belief and refutation of falsehood.

In this passage, the author concludes the treatise by emphasizing that demonstration (burhan) is the mainstay of logic. While other arts serve persuasion, compulsion, or imagination, burhan alone produces yaqin (certainty). This lesson unpacks the definition and significance of burhan.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of burhan in the Arabic
Find the phrase 'al-'umda huwa al-burhan la ghayr' in the matn. Note that it is presented as the mainstay.
The hashaya clarifies that burhan establishes true beliefs and refutes false ones.
2
Identify the purpose of burhan
According to the hashaya, burhan leads to true beliefs that result in paradise and refutes false beliefs that lead to hellfire.
This shows the soteriological importance of burhan.
3
Contrast burhan with the other four arts
Burhan uses certain premises, while others use well-known, accepted, imaginative, or false premises. Only burhan gives certainty.
4
Reflect on why burhan is essential for Islamic theology and jurisprudence
Without burhan, theological claims would be merely persuasive or dialectical, not certain.

The Art of Dialectic (Jadal)
Definition and purpose of dialectic (jadal) as a syllogism of well-known premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define jadal, identify its premises as well-known (mashhurat), and explain its purpose in debate.

Core Idea
Jadal uses premises that are widely accepted, not necessarily true, to compel an opponent in argument.

The first art defined in the passage is dialectic (jadal). Unlike burhan, jadal does not require certain premises; it only requires that the premises be well-known (mashhurat) among the disputants. This lesson explores how jadal functions in Islamic debate traditions.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition of jadal in the Arabic
Locate 'qiyas mu'allaf min muqaddimat mashhura' in the matn.
2
Note the hashaya's clarification
Well-known premises do not need to be true or certain, only widely agreed upon. Fame can vary by time, place, and custom.
3
Understand the purpose: to compel the opponent (ilzam al-khasm)
The goal is not necessarily to reach truth but to force the opponent to accept the conclusion based on premises they publicly acknowledge.
4
Compare jadal with burhan
Burhan aims at certainty; jadal aims at persuasion in debate. Both are syllogisms, but with different epistemic standards.

The Art of Rhetoric (Khitaba)
Definition and purpose of rhetoric (khitaba) as a syllogism of accepted or probable premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khitaba and distinguish it from other arts by its use of accepted or probable premises for persuasion in worldly affairs.

Core Idea
Rhetoric uses premises from a trusted authority or probable opinions to persuade people in practical matters.

The second art defined is rhetoric (khitaba). It is used by orators and preachers to encourage people in worldly affairs. Unlike jadal, its premises are not necessarily well-known but are accepted from a trusted person or probable. This lesson explores the nature and application of khitaba.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of khitaba in the Arabic
Find 'qiyas mu'allaf min muqaddimat maqbula min shakhs mu'taqad fihi, aw maznuna' in the matn.
2
Identify the two types of premises
Premises are either accepted from a person believed in (mu'taqad fihi) or probable (maznuna).
3
Understand the purpose: to encourage people in worldly affairs
The commentator says the purpose is 'targhib al-nas fi ma yanfa'uhum min umur ma'ashihim' (encouraging people in beneficial worldly matters).
4
Compare with jadal
While jadal compels opponents in debate, khitaba persuades the general public for practical benefit.

The Art of Poetics (Shi'r)
Definition and development of poetic syllogisms (shi'r) with emphasis on imagination and meter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shi'r as a syllogism of imaginative premises that affect the soul and explain the role of meter according to later logicians.

Core Idea
Poetry uses imaginative premises to expand or contract the soul, and is considered a syllogism by logicians.

The third art defined is poetry (shi'r). Unlike the other arts, its premises are imaginative (mutakhayyala) and aim to affect the soul emotionally. The passage provides examples of expansion and contraction. Later logicians also considered meter (wazn) essential for poetry. This lesson examines the definition and evolution of poetic syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition of shi'r in the Arabic
Locate 'qiyas mu'allaf min muqaddimat mutakhayyala' in the matn.
2
Note the effect on the soul
The premises cause the soul to expand (tanbasit) or contract (tanqabid). Examples: 'wine is a flowing ruby' (expansion), 'honey is bitter and emetic' (contraction).
3
Read the hashaya on meter
The ancients considered only imagination; moderns consider both imagination and meter. The majority now consider meter essential. Meter gives pleasure through rhythm.
4
Understand poetry as a syllogism
Even though poetic premises are not literal, logicians classify it as a syllogism because it has a structure that leads to an effect on the soul.

The Art of Sophistry (Mughalata)
Definition of sophistry (mughalata) as a deceptive syllogism using false premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mughalata as a deceptive syllogism using false premises that resemble truth or false estimative premises.

Core Idea
Sophistry uses false premises that look true to deceive, and must be distinguished from valid reasoning.

The fourth art defined is sophistry (mughalata). Its premises are false but resemble truth, or are false estimative premises. The purpose is deception. This lesson explains how to identify and avoid sophistical arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition of mughalata in the Arabic
Locate 'qiyas mu'allaf min muqaddimat kadhiba shabiha bil-haqq, aw muqaddimat wahmiyya kadhiba' in the matn.
2
Identify the two types of false premises
Either false premises that resemble truth (shabiha bil-haqq) or false estimative premises (wahmiyya kadhiba).
3
Understand the purpose: to deceive (taghlit)
The goal is to lead the listener to a false conclusion by making it appear valid.
4
Contrast mughalata with burhan
Burhan uses true certain premises; mughalata uses false premises. A good logician must be able to detect the difference.
