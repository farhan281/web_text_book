The Definition of Syllogism (Qiyas) in Logic
Kati defines qiyas as a compound statement entailing a conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the logical definition of qiyas and test whether any argument satisfies the definition.

Core Idea
A syllogism is a self-contained argument: premises alone force the conclusion.

The definition of qiyas is the cornerstone of Aristotelian logic. It tells us what an argument must have to be logically valid. As you study the Arabic, notice that every word carries weight: 'compound', 'when granted', 'necessarily', 'by themselves'. These conditions separate genuine syllogisms from bad arguments or non-arguments.

Guided Path
1
Find the definiendum
The term being defined is 'al-qiyas'. The definition begins after the colon: 'huwa qawl mu'allaf...'. So the definiendum is the logical syllogism, not legal analogy.
Be careful: in fiqh, qiyas means analogical deduction; here it means deductive syllogism.
2
Extract the genus
The genus is 'qawl mu'allaf min aqwal' — a compound statement made up of other statements. This shows that a syllogism is a composition of propositions, not a single proposition.
The premises are called 'aqwal' (plural of qawl).
3
Identify the differentia
The differentia has two parts: (a) 'mata sullimat' — when the premises are granted or accepted as true, and (b) 'lazima 'anha li dhatiha qawl akhar' — another statement follows from them necessarily in themselves.
The phrase 'li dhatiha' rules out external factors; the entailment must be intrinsic.
4
Check completeness
The definition must include every syllogism and exclude everything that is not a syllogism. Does it? It excludes single propositions and arguments where the conclusion does not follow necessarily. It includes valid categorical and conditional syllogisms.
Later logicians debate whether conditional syllogisms fit this definition exactly, but it is intended to be general.

Commentary
This passage provides the classical definition of qiyas (syllogism) in Aristotelian logic. The matn defines it as a composite statement (qawl mu'allaf) made up of individual statements (aqwal). The condition 'when they are granted' (mata sullimat) indicates that the premises are accepted as true. The defining feature is that another statement (qawl akhar) — the conclusion — follows from them necessarily (lazima) by virtue of the premises themselves (li dhatiha), without any external aid. This distinguishes a valid syllogism from other forms of reasoning where the conclusion may depend on additional assumptions. The definition is foundational for all subsequent discussions of syllogistic inference.

The Central Role of Syllogism in Logic
Commentator explains why syllogism is the highest aim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the syllogism is considered the highest aim of logic and distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq.

Core Idea
The syllogism is the engine that converts concepts into certain assent.

The commentator begins by affirming that the syllogism is the highest goal of logical study. This is not an exaggeration: logic's ultimate purpose is to produce certain knowledge (tasdiq), and the syllogism is the primary tool for that. In this lesson, we will unpack why the syllogism takes this privileged position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the aim of logic
The passage states that the highest aim of logical terminology is the syllogism. This implies that all other discussions (concepts, propositions) are subordinate to producing syllogisms.
The commentator quotes al-Dawwani to explain that assents (tasdiqat) are the ultimate goal, and syllogisms are the main way to reach them.
2
Distinguish tasawwur and tasdiq
Concepts (tasawwur) are needed for assents, but they are not the end; assents are. The syllogism directly produces assents by combining known propositions.
This hierarchy shows logic is purpose-driven: the entire enterprise serves to produce new knowledge.
3
Compare syllogism with other instruments
The logician also uses induction and analogy, but the syllogism is the chief. The passage emphasizes that qiyas is 'the highest aim and furthest goal'.
This ranking sets the stage for detailed study of syllogistic forms.
4
Apply to a real kalam proof
Take the classic argument: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' Identify which parts are tasawwur (concepts like 'world', 'cause') and which are tasdiq (the premises and conclusion as assents).
Recognizing the syllogistic structure helps validate the proof.

Dissecting the Definition of Syllogism
Commentator defines qiyas and explains each part of the definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each component of the definition of qiyas and explain what each part excludes.

Core Idea
A syllogism is not a single statement but a compound of statements that necessitate a conclusion.

The definition of syllogism given in the matn is precise. Every word is chosen to exclude invalid candidates. By dissecting each term, we learn exactly what does and does not count as a valid logical syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Isolate the defining phrase
The definition is: 'A statement composed of statements such that, when granted, necessarily yields another statement by itself.' Break it into parts: (1) qawl (statement), (2) mu'allaf (composed), (3) min aqwal (of statements), (4) the condition of yielding a new statement.
Each part serves to exclude something.
2
Analyze 'qawl'
Qawl means a meaningful expression, either mental (intelligible) or uttered. The passage notes it is literal for mental and metaphorical for uttered. This ensures the definition covers both the internal logical form and its external expression.
The logician cares about the mental proof, not just spoken words.
3
Analyze 'mu'allaf' and 'min aqwal'
'Composed' indicates multiple parts; 'of statements' specifies that these parts are statements (propositions). Together they exclude a single statement from being a syllogism. Kalnabawi adds that even a single statement that implies its converse is not a syllogism.
This is a key distinguishing feature: a syllogism must have at least two premises.
4
Understand the 'luzum' (necessitation) condition
The definition says 'when granted, necessarily yields another statement by itself.' This means the conclusion follows from the premises alone, without external assumption. This excludes arguments that rely on outside information.
The phrase 'by itself' (li-dhatiha) emphasizes the formal validity of the syllogism.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Take: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden (khamr). Nabidh is an intoxicant. Therefore nabidh is forbidden.' This has two premises and a conclusion—it fits the definition. A single statement like 'Nabidh is forbidden' would not be a syllogism.
This demonstrates why the definition requires multiple statements.

The Definition of Qiyas (Syllogism)
Commentary defines qiyas and explains its components and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of the definition of qiyas and explain how the condition 'when conceded' relates to the role of syllogism in acquiring unknown assents.

Core Idea
The syllogism is the primary tool for attaining unknown assents from known ones.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational definition of qiyas (syllogism) as presented in the Isaguji commentary. The passage explains why the syllogism is the highest goal of logic and dissects its definition phrase by phrase. Guided Path
1
Locate the definition phrase
Find the Arabic phrase 'قول مؤلف من أقوال' (a statement composed of statements) in the passage. This is the core definition of qiyas.

Identify the three components
Break down the definition: 'قول' (statement), 'مؤلف' (composed), 'من أقوال' (of statements). Each part is necessary to distinguish a syllogism from a single proposition or from non-composite expressions.

Understand the condition 'when conceded'
The phrase 'متى سُلّمت' (when conceded) indicates that the syllogism does not require actual truth of premises; it only requires that if the premises are accepted, the conclusion logically follows. This includes both true and false premises.
This condition is for inclusion, not exclusion.
4
Relate to the purpose of qiyas
The commentary states that qiyas is the highest goal because it is the primary means to attain unknown assents (tasdiqat). Conception (tasawwur) is a means, but assent is the ultimate aim.

Essential Conditions of Qiyas: Entailment and Non-Circularity
Commentary specifies conditions for a valid syllogism and excludes counterexamples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a valid syllogism (qiyas) and cases that require an extraneous premise (like qiyas al-musawat) and identify the fallacy of circular reasoning (musadara).

Core Idea
A valid syllogism entails its conclusion intrinsically, without needing any outside premise.

The definition of qiyas includes critical conditions: the conclusion must follow necessarily (luzum) and by itself (lidhatiha) from the premises. Without these conditions, the argument may be incomplete or circular. This lesson examines these conditions and their exceptions, using examples from the commentary, such as the syllogism of equality (qiyas al-musawat) and the substance argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the entailment condition (luzum)
Find where the passage says 'لزم' (follows). It specifies that the entailment must be 'بطريق الاكتساب' (by way of acquisition), which excludes incomplete induction and analogy if certainty is required.
2
Understand 'by themselves' (lidhatiha)
The phrase 'لذاتها' (by themselves) means the conclusion must follow from the premises alone, without needing an additional premise. The passage gives the example of qiyas al-musawat (equality syllogism) where an extra premise is needed, so it is not a valid qiyas.
Example: A = B, B = C → A = C requires the transitive property as an extra premise.
3
Analyze the exclusion of qiyas al-musawat
Examine the example: 'A equals B, B equals C' does not entail 'A equals C' by itself; it needs the extraneous premise 'everything equal to what is equal to a thing is equal to that thing'. Hence, it is excluded from being a valid syllogism.
4
Examine the condition of differentness (mughayara)
The passage states that the conclusion must be different from each premise to avoid musadara (circular reasoning). If the conclusion is the same as a premise, it is not a valid syllogism.
This also ensures that not every two premises count as a syllogism.

Distinguishing Conjunctive and Exceptive Syllogisms
Division of qiyas into conjunctive and exceptive

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a syllogism as conjunctive (iqtirani) or exceptive (istithna'i) by checking whether the conclusion or its contradictory is explicitly stated in the premises.

Core Idea
The presence of the conclusion in the premises divides syllogisms into two types.

The author opens with a clear division of qiyas into two fundamental types: conjunctive and exceptive. The key difference lies not in the content but in the formal structure of the premises. Understanding this distinction is essential for analysing any syllogism you encounter in Islamic logic texts.

Guided Path

Identify the conclusion
Locate the natija (conclusion) of the syllogism. It is the statement that the syllogism aims to prove.

2
Check for the conclusion in premises
Look for the exact conclusion or its contradictory (naqid) in the premises. If neither appears, the syllogism is iqtirani; if one appears, it is istithna'i.
The criterion is from the commentator: 'if the exact conclusion or its contradictory is not actually mentioned'.
3
Determine the exceptive type
If it is istithna'i, identify whether it affirms the antecedent (modus ponens) or denies the consequent (modus tollens).
Example: 'but the sun is shining' affirms the antecedent; 'but daytime does not exist' denies the consequent.
4
Apply to examples
Test the classification by applying it to the examples given in the text: the 'every body is composed' example is iqtirani; the conditional examples are istithna'i.

Terms of the Categorical Syllogism: Minor, Major, Middle, Premises, Mood, Figure
Defining terms of categorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the minor term, major term, middle term, minor premise, major premise, mood, and figure in any categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Every term in a syllogism has a named role that determines its function.

The commentator expands on the conjunctive syllogism by defining its constituent parts. Understanding these terms is crucial for analysing the structure of any categorical argument. These definitions are the building blocks for studying the figures and moods.

Guided Path
1
Locate the conclusion
Find the natija (conclusion) of the syllogism. It is the statement that the syllogism aims to prove.
In the example 'Every body is composed, every composed thing is temporally originated, therefore every body is temporally originated', the conclusion is 'every body is temporally originated'.
2
Identify the minor and major terms
The subject of the conclusion is the minor term (al-asghar). The predicate of the conclusion is the major term (al-akbar).
In the example, 'body' is the minor term (subject), 'temporally originated' is the major term (predicate).
3
Find the middle term
The term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat).
Here, 'composed' appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
4
Distinguish the premises
The premise containing the minor term is the minor premise (sughra). The premise containing the major term is the major premise (kubra).
The first premise 'Every body is composed' contains the minor term 'body', so it is the minor premise. The second premise contains the major term 'temporally originated'.
5
Determine the mood and figure
The combination of the two premises (their quantity and quality) is the mood (darb). The arrangement of the middle term relative to the minor and major terms determines the figure (shakl).
This syllogism is in the first figure because the middle term is subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise.

The middle term is the key that unlocks the conclusion in a syllogism.

This lesson introduces the essential vocabulary of categorical syllogisms. In order to construct, analyse, or evaluate a syllogism, you must first master the terms that name its parts. Conditional Exceptional Syllogism: Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens
Author explains the rules of conditional exceptional syllogism with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply modus ponens and modus tollens in a conditional exceptional syllogism and identify valid forms based on necessary implication.

Core Idea
Affirm the antecedent to get the consequent; deny the consequent to deny the antecedent.

The exceptional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) uses a conditional premise and a second premise that affirms or denies one part. In this lesson, we focus on the conditional type, where the connection between antecedent and consequent is necessary. You will learn the two valid forms: affirming the antecedent (modus ponens) and denying the consequent (modus tollens).

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the premise with 'if...then' (in Arabic: إن كان...فإن). This is the major premise. Note the antecedent (المقدم) and consequent (التالي).
Ensure the conditional is necessary (لزومية).
2
Identify the second premise (the exception)
The second premise either affirms (وضع) or denies (رفع) one of the two parts. This is called the 'istithna' (exception).
3
Check the direction of inference
If the second premise affirms the antecedent, then the conclusion affirms the consequent (modus ponens). If it denies the consequent, the conclusion denies the antecedent (modus tollens).
Affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent are invalid.
4
Formulate the conclusion
Write the conclusion that follows necessarily. For example: from 'If it is a human, it is an animal' and 'it is a human', conclude 'it is an animal'.
Using Islamic examples from fiqh or kalam.

Disjunctive Exceptional Syllogism: Exclusive Parts
Author explains the rules of disjunctive exceptional syllogism with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of real disjunctive syllogism: affirming one part negates the other, and negating one part affirms the other.

Core Idea
In a real disjunction, affirming one part denies the other; denying one affirms the other.

The exceptional syllogism also applies to disjunctive premises. When the disjunction is real (exclusive), the parts are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. This lesson teaches the two valid forms: affirming one part yields the negation of the other, and negating one yields affirmation of the other.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunctive premise
Find the premise with 'either...or' (in Arabic: إما أن يكون...أو). Ensure it is a real disjunction (حقيقية), meaning the parts cannot both be true and cannot both be false.
If the disjunction is not exclusive, affirming one does not deny the other.
2
Identify the second premise (the exception)
The second premise either affirms (وضع) or denies (رفع) one of the two parts. This is the 'istithna'.
3
Apply the rule
If the second premise affirms one part, the conclusion denies the other. If it denies one part, the conclusion affirms the other.
For example: 'The number is either even or odd; it is even; therefore it is not odd.'

Formulate the conclusion

Conjunctive Conditional Syllogism (Muttasil)
Author explains rules for conjunctive conditional syllogisms with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply modus ponens and modus tollens to Islamic theological arguments and explain their validity using the necessary connection (mulazama).

Core Idea
In a conjunctive conditional, affirming the antecedent or denying the consequent yields a valid conclusion.

This lesson focuses on one of the two types of conditional syllogisms: the conjunctive (al-shartiyya al-muttasila). You will learn how to draw valid conclusions by either affirming the antecedent (modus ponens) or denying the consequent (modus tollens). The author provides clear examples from everyday reasoning, which we will apply to Islamic sciences such as fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conjunctive conditional premise
Find the 'if...then' statement (e.g., 'If this is a human, then it is an animal'). It must be affirmative and necessary (luzumiyya). Note the antecedent (al-muqaddam) and consequent (al-tali).
The antecedent is the part after 'if' and before 'then'.
2
Apply modus ponens (affirming antecedent)
If the minor premise affirms the antecedent, conclude the consequent. Example: 'If it is wine, then it is haram; it is wine, so it is haram.' This is valid because the necessary connection ensures the consequent follows.
3
Apply modus tollens (denying consequent)
If the minor premise denies the consequent, conclude the denial of the antecedent. Example: 'If it is wine, then it is haram; it is not haram, so it is not wine.' This is valid because denying the consequent forces the antecedent to be false.
Otherwise, a true antecedent would imply a false consequent, breaking the necessary connection.
4
Check for necessary connection
Verify that the conditional expresses a genuine necessary connection (mulazama). If the connection is false, the syllogism fails. The author explicitly states: 'otherwise, the separation of the necessary from that for which it is necessary would be required, which invalidates the necessary connection.'
This is why only affirmative necessary conditionals are used.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In a true exclusive disjunction, affirming one part negates the other; negating one part affirms the other.

This lesson covers the second type of conditional syllogism: the disjunctive (al-shartiyya al-munfasila). The author explains the rules for exclusive disjunctions (haqiqiyya), where the two parts cannot be true together and cannot be false together. Understanding this is crucial for arguments that present two opposing possibilities, such as in kalam debates about divine attributes or in fiqh when determining rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunctive premise
Find the 'either...or' statement that is exclusive (haqiqiyya). In the text, the example is 'Always, either the number is even or odd.' Note that the two parts (juz'an) are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
Exclusive disjunction means the two parts cannot both be true (imtina' al-jam') and cannot both be false (imtina' al-khuluww).

List the two disjuncts
Label the first part A and the second part B. For example: A = 'the number is even', B = 'the number is odd'.
3
Apply the rule of affirmation
If the minor premise affirms one disjunct, conclude the negation of the other. Example: 'This number is even, so it is not odd.' This is valid because they cannot be true together.

4
Apply the rule of negation
If the minor premise negates one disjunct, conclude the affirmation of the other. Example: 'This number is not even, so it is odd.' This is valid because they cannot both be false.
The author says: 'the assertion of the contradictory of one of them yields the exact other, due to the impossibility of their both being absent.'
5
Verify exclusivity
Ensure the disjunction is truly exclusive (haqiqiyya). If it is not, the rules may not apply. The author explicitly limits the discussion to 'true disjunctives' (haqiqiyya).
Later logicians distinguish types like mani'at al-jam' (not both true) and mani'at al-khuluww (not both false).

Key Takeaway
In an exclusive disjunctive syllogism, affirming one part negates the other, and negating one part affirms the other, due to the impossibility of both being true or both being false.

Dialectic and Rhetoric: Syllogisms Defined by Premise Type
Author defines dialectical and rhetorical syllogisms by their premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish dialectical and rhetorical syllogisms based on the types of premises they employ.

Core Idea
Dialectic and rhetoric are syllogisms, not just speech; their difference lies in premise type.

In the classification of the five arts, dialectic and rhetoric occupy a middle ground between demonstration and sophistry. This lesson unpacks their definitions, showing that the difference lies in the kind of premises each uses. You will learn to identify mashhurat, maqbulat, and maznunat as premise types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus
Both dialectic and rhetoric are defined as types of syllogisms (qiyas). What common element do they share?
2
Extract the differentia for dialectic
Dialectic uses mashhurat (widely accepted premises). What does this mean for certainty?
Widely accepted does not guarantee truth.
3
Extract the differentia for rhetoric
Rhetoric uses maqbulat (premises accepted from a trusted person) or maznunat (plausible premises). How does this differ from dialectic?
Rhetoric relies on authority or likelihood.
4
Compare with other arts
How do these definitions place dialectic and rhetoric relative to demonstration (certain) and sophistry (fallacious)?
Dialectic and rhetoric are weaker than demonstration but not necessarily fallacious.

Dialectic is a syllogism composed of accepted premises. Rhetoric is a syllogism composed of premises that are accepted from a person in whom [the listener] has faith, or plausible ones, because (1) His saying 'intuitions' — some explained it as swiftness of the mind's transition from principles to objectives, but this contains apparent imprecision. The more correct is that it is expression for obtaining, when attending to objectives, the middle terms instantly, the objectives being represented with the middle terms in the mind without movement. As for discursive thought, it is movement in meanings from objectives to their principles; perhaps it is cut off and perhaps it arrives. When it arrives, it is completed by another movement from principles to objectives. So it is then in need of two movements, thus there is possibility of non-arrival and existence of movement or two movements. In intuition, non-arrival and non-movement are impossible, because the transition therein is instantaneous, not gradual. Thus the expression 'swiftness' is metaphorical, as verified by al-Sa'd al-'Allama. Thus it is understood.

Commentary
This passage defines two of the five arts: dialectic (jadal) and rhetoric (khataba). Dialectic is a syllogism composed of mashhurat (widely accepted premises). Rhetoric is a syllogism composed of premises that are either maqbulat (accepted from a trusted figure) or maznunat (plausible). The remainder of the text, starting with 'لأنه' and the footnote about hadsiyat, appears to be a misplaced continuation of the earlier footnote on intuition. It discusses the nature of intuition (hads) in contrast to discursive thought (fikr), but it does not belong to the definitions of jadal and khataba. The student should ignore this appended text for the purpose of this lesson, as it is an OCR artifact.
