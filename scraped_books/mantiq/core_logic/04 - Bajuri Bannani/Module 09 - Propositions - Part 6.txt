Understanding Musallamat (Conceded Premises)
Author defines conceded premises (musallamat) with example from usul al-fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define musallamat and explain why a jurist might concede a premise from usul al-fiqh in a debate.

Core Idea
Musallamat are premises accepted for argument, not for truth; they borrow authority from other sciences.

Dialectic often requires opponents to agree on certain premises to advance the argument. These conceded propositions (musallamat) may be drawn from other fields. The author gives the example of a jurist conceding that a single-narrator hadith is a proof because it is established in usul al-fiqh. This shows how debate can cross disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Define musallamat
From the Arabic: 'propositions that one opponent concedes to the other in order to build a judgment upon them'.
The concession is for the sake of argument, not necessarily belief in truth.
2
Note the condition of truth
Musallamat may be true or false in themselves; the opponent accepts them temporarily.
3
Understand the source from another science
The author gives an example: a jurist concedes that 'khabar al-wahid is a proof' because it has been proven in usul al-fiqh.
This shows how premises can be borrowed from established sciences.
4
Differentiate from mashhurat
Musallamat are not necessarily widely accepted; they are accepted by the specific opponents for this debate.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of premises used in dialectic (jadal): mashhurat (widely-accepted) and musallamat (conceded). The author first defines mashhurat as propositions that all or most people agree upon due to their widespread fame, stemming from four causes: (1) general benefit, e.g., 'Justice is good'; (2) natural compassion, e.g., 'Supporting the weak is praiseworthy'; (3) protective honor, e.g., 'Exposing the private parts is blameworthy'; (4) religious emotion, e.g., 'Saying Bismillah is praiseworthy.' The distinction is that primary axioms are known by pure intellect, independent of culture or custom; if one imagines being stripped of all external influences, one would still judge primary axioms but not necessarily the mashhurat. Moreover, mashhurat can be false, while primary axioms are always true. These may come from another science where they have been established, e.g., a jurist conceding that a single-narrator hadith is a proof because it is proven in usul al-fiqh. This clarifies how debate can proceed by accepting premises from the opponent's own field.

Concurrent Reports: Certainty through Overwhelming Testimony
Definition of mutawatirat and discussion of its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutawatirat, explain the hidden syllogism behind them, and argue why no fixed number is required for tawatur.

Core Idea
Mutawatirat produce certainty by removing doubt, not by meeting a specific count.

This passage introduces the fourth type of certain premises: concurrent reports (al-mutawatirat). These are propositions whose truth the mind accepts because so many people report the same observation that collusion is impossible. Behind this acceptance lies a hidden syllogism. The author also warns against insisting on a fixed number of reporters, showing that certainty is flexible and context-dependent.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of mutawatirat
Find the complete definition in the Arabic: 'propositions that the mind judges by means of a multitude of testimony...'. Note the key conditions: the report must be about a possible matter based on sense observation, and the multitude must prevent collusion.
2
Unpack the hidden syllogism
The author says a hidden syllogism is attached: 'if this judgment were not true, this group would not have reported it.' Since they did report, the judgment must be true. Identify this as a conditional syllogism of modus tollens form.
3
Recognize the scope of tawatur
Tawatur only applies to matters based on observation (mushahada), not to purely rational claims. Understand why metaphysical truths cannot be established by tawatur.
4
Analyze the number condition
The author rejects fixed numbers (5, 12, 20, etc.) because certainty can occur with fewer or more, depending on circumstances. The true criterion is 'attainment of certainty and removal of possibility'.
This is a key difference from hadith sciences where specific numbers are sometimes debated.

Defining Intuitive Propositions (al-Hadathiyyat)
Author defines hadathiyyat and gives the example of moonlight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify intuitive propositions (al-hadathiyyat) by their distinguishing features and give the classical example.

Core Idea
Intuitive propositions yield certainty through strong intuition from observed indications.

Among the certain premises that provide the foundation for demonstrative proofs, al-hadathiyyat hold a unique place because they are based not on repeated experience or chains of reasoning, but on a sudden insight after observing clues. In this lesson, we will unpack the definition of al-hadathiyyat and see how logicians applied it to a famous astronomical example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of proposition
Locate the term 'الحَدَسِيَّات' in the Arabic and note that it is being introduced as the fifth type of certain premises.
2
Extract the definition
Look at the description: 'قَضَايَا يَحْكُمُ بِهَا العَقْلُ بِحَدَسِ قَوِيٌّ مِنَ النَّفْسِ يَزُولُ مَعَهُ الشَّكُ وَيَحْصُلُ اليَقِينُ لِمُشَاهَدَةِ القَرَائِنِ'. Identify the key components: the mental act (hads), the result (certainty), and the basis (observation of indications).
The definition does not rely on sense-repetition (tajriba) but on a single strong intuition.
3
Analyze the example
The author provides the judgment that moonlight is derived from sunlight. Observe how this judgment arises from noticing the correlation between the moon's phase and its position relative to the sun.
This is an intuitive inference, not a formal syllogism, because the middle term is grasped instantly.
4
Contrast with other premises
Consider how hadathiyyat differ from tajribiyyat (experiential premises). In tajribiyyat, the hidden syllogism involves repetition; in hadathiyyat, the middle term is seen directly through a single observation.
Some logicians classify hadathiyyat under tajribiyyat, but the author treats them separately.

The fifth: the intuitive propositions, and they are propositions that the mind judges by a strong intuition from the soul, by which doubt ceases and certainty is attained due to observing indications; like the judgment that 'the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun' because of what is seen of the variation in the shapes of its light according to the variation of its positions relative to the sun, near and far. Commentary
This passage defines the fifth type of certain premises, al-hadathiyyat (intuitive propositions). The author starts by describing them as propositions that the mind judges through a strong intuition (hads) from the soul, removing doubt and yielding certainty by observing indications (qara'in). The classic example is that the moon's light is derived from the sun, because we observe the changing phases based on the moon's position relative to the sun—when it is near the sun, its light is dim; when far, it is bright. The mind intuitively infers the cause from these clues. Then the author provides a technical clarification: hads is an instantaneous attainment of the middle term when turning to the demands, without gradual movement. If there were gradual movement, it would be fikr (discursive thought). Thus transition in hads is sudden (def'i), not gradual (tadriji), unlike thought. He criticizes those who define hads as 'speed of transition' as speaking metaphorically, because speed still implies gradual movement, whereas hads is truly instantaneous. This distinguishes it from tajribiyyat (experiential) where the hidden syllogism is present but the cause is known only as a cause, not in its essence; in hadathiyyat, the cause is known both by its causal nature and its essence, but attained by intuition rather than discursive reasoning.

Hads vs Fikr: Instantaneous vs Gradual Transition
Author explains hads as instantaneous and criticizes its interpretation as speed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hads (intuition) and fikr (discursive thought) based on the nature of the transition, and evaluate the metaphorical interpretation of hads as speed.

Core Idea
Hads is instantaneous attainment of the middle term; fikr is gradual movement.

The author not only defines al-hadathiyyat but also provides a technical verification of hads itself, clarifying how it differs from ordinary thinking. This distinction is crucial because it affects how we understand the acquisition of certain knowledge. Let's explore the precise definitions and see why interpreting hads as 'speed' is considered metaphorical.

Guided Path
1
Locate the technical definition of hads
Find the sentence: 'وَالْحَقِيقَةُ : أَنَّ الْحَدَسَ عِبَارَةٌ عَن الظُفْرِ عِنْدَ الْالْتِفَاتِ ...'. Note that hads is defined as 'attaining (the conclusion) upon turning to the demands by means of middle terms instantly.'
2
Compare with the definition of fikr
The author says that if there is movement (haraka) it is fikr. Look at the contrast: hads has no movement (daf'atan), while fikr involves gradual movement (tadriji).
Think of fikr as step-by-step reasoning, such as a syllogism.
3
Analyze the critique of the 'speed' interpretation
The author rejects interpreting hads as mere speed of transition because speed still implies a temporal sequence, even if fast. He calls it metaphorical (tajawwuz). Understand why speed is not instantaneous.
Speed is a measure over time; instantaneous has zero duration.
4
Apply the distinction to the example
Return to the moonlight example. The inference that the moon's light comes from the sun is not reached through a series of steps but in a sudden flash of insight based on the observed correlation. That is hads.
If someone reasoned stepwise, it would be fikr, but the example is meant to be intuitive.

The fifth: the intuitive propositions, and they are propositions that the mind judges by a strong intuition from the soul, by which doubt ceases and certainty is attained due to observing indications; like the judgment that 'the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun' because of what is seen of the variation in the shapes of its light according to the variation of its positions relative to the sun, near and far. And the verification: that hads (intuition) is an expression for attaining (the conclusion) upon turning to the demands by means of middle terms instantly, so there is no movement in it, otherwise it would be thought; so the transition in hads is instantaneous, not gradual, opposite to thought. And whoever interpreted hads as 'speed of transition from principles to demands' has been metaphorical.

Commentary
This passage defines the fifth type of certain premises, al-hadathiyyat (intuitive propositions). The author starts by describing them as propositions that the mind judges through a strong intuition (hads) from the soul, removing doubt and yielding certainty by observing indications (qara'in). The classic example is that the moon's light is derived from the sun, because we observe the changing phases based on the moon's position relative to the sun—when it is near the sun, its light is dim; when far, it is bright. The mind intuitively infers the cause from these clues. Then the author provides a technical clarification: hads is an instantaneous attainment of the middle term when turning to the demands, without gradual movement. If there were gradual movement, it would be fikr (discursive thought). Thus transition in hads is sudden (def'i), not gradual (tadriji), unlike thought. He criticizes those who define hads as 'speed of transition' as speaking metaphorically, because speed still implies gradual movement, whereas hads is truly instantaneous. This distinguishes it from tajribiyyat (experiential) where the hidden syllogism is present but the cause is known only as a cause, not in its essence; in hadathiyyat, the cause is known both by its causal nature and its essence, but attained by intuition rather than discursive reasoning.

Sensory Propositions (al-Mahsusat) as Certain Premises
Author defines the sixth type of certain premises: mahsusat, based on external senses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sensory propositions (mahsusat) and distinguish them from other types of certain premises in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Sensory propositions give certainty through the intellect's judgment via external senses.

After covering self-evident premises (awwaliyyat), the author now introduces the sixth category of certain premises: sensory propositions (mahsusat). These are judgments that, while not self-evident, become certain through the use of the five external senses. The commentator connects this to the previous classification and provides concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find 'المحسوسات' in the Arabic passage and note its explanation. This is the subject of the definition.
2
Extract the definition
The text defines mahsusat as 'قضايا يحكم بها العقل بواسطة الحواس الظاهرة' — propositions that the intellect judges by means of the external senses. Write down this definition.
The definition emphasizes the role of both intellect and senses.
3
Analyze the components
Break down the definition: (1) propositions (قضايا), (2) the intellect judges (يحكم العقل), (3) through external senses (بواسطة الحواس الظاهرة). Why is the intellect mentioned? Because the judgment is not raw sensation but a rational act based on sensory input.
This distinguishes mahsusat from mere sense perception; they are judgments of certainty.
4
Examine the examples
The text gives 'the sun is luminous' (sight) and 'fire is hot' (touch). Consider why these are certain: they are direct, clear observations that the intellect affirms without doubt.
These examples illustrate how external senses lead to certain judgments.
5
Place in the classification
The commentator notes that this definition clarifies the earlier statement about 'premises that are certain and connect.' Mahsusat is part of the list of yaqiniyyat (certain premises) that the author summarizes at the end.
Understanding the context helps see how sensory propositions fit into the overall scheme of certain knowledge.
