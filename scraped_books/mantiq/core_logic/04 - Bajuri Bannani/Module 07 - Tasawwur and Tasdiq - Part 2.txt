Definition of Dillalat al-Mutabaqah and Inclusion of Majaz
Author defines dillalat al-mutabaqah and argues it includes majaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to justify why Bannani's definition of dillalat al-mutabaqah includes majaz, using the concept of wad' with qarina.

Core Idea
Metaphor (majaz) is included in logical definition because wad' covers both direct and qarina-based signification.

Bannani refines the definition of dillalat al-mutabaqah (correspondence signification) to ensure it covers both literal and metaphorical uses. He argues that the expression 'speech' (kalam) is better than 'musamma' used by al-Mukhtasar, because the latter would exclude metaphor. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and shows how wad' (linguistic convention) is broad enough to include majaz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The term is dillalat al-mutabaqah. Notice that Bannani defines it as signification over the entirety of what the word is placed for. He then immediately says this includes both haqiqa and majaz.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'signification of a word over the entirety of what it is placed for'. The differentia is implicit: whether it is placed by itself or with a qarina. This differentia allows majaz to enter.
Definition type: hadd haqiqi? Actually more like rasm because it includes qarina condition.
3
Test the definition's comprehensiveness
Check that it includes all cases: both literal ('four' → 'twice two') and metaphorical ('lion' → 'courageous man' with qarina). Bannani says this definition is comprehensive unlike al-Mukhtasar's use of 'musamma'.
Al-Mukhtasar's definition fails because 'musamma' only covers literal meanings.
4
Link to the concept of wad'
Bannani states that wad' means 'appointing a thing to signify a thing by itself or with a qarina'. This broad definition is what allows majaz to be considered mawdu' (established) and thus included in dillalat al-mutabaqah.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional commentary by Bannani on the definition of dillalat al-mutabaqah (correspondence signification). The author opens by praising the expression 'speech' (kalam) used by the author of al-Sullam because it encompasses both literal (haqiqa) and metaphorical (majaz) usage, making the definition comprehensive. In contrast, al-Mukhtasar's use of 'al-musamma' would exclude majaz and thus fail to be comprehensive. Then he gives the definition of dillalat al-mutabaqah: the signification of a word over the entirety of what it is placed for. He provides examples: the word 'four' signifies 'twice two' (its full meaning), 'human' signifies 'rational animal', and 'lion' signifies 'courageous man' when a qarina (contextual indicator) is present. The inclusion of the last example shows that majaz is considered within the definition because, according to them, convention (wad') includes both direct signification and signification with a qarina. Thus homonyms like 'ayn (which can mean eye or spring) enter under this definition. Then the commentator addresses a potential misunderstanding: al-Mukhtasar used 'al-musamma' for correspondence signification, but actually he used 'al-ma'na'. Bannani clarifies that 'al-ma'na' is broader, including both literal and metaphorical meanings, while 'al-musamma' is only the original literal meaning for which the word was placed to signify by itself. Finally, he distinguishes between 'ma'na' and 'mafhum': the mental form considered as intended by the speaker is 'ma'na', and the same form considered as received by the listener from the word is 'mafhum'. This distinction is conceptual (i'tibari).

Distinguishing Ma'na, Musamma, and Mafhum
Author distinguishes ma'na, musamma, and mafhum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-ma'na, al-musamma, and al-mafhum in the context of signification theory.

Core Idea
Ma'na is speaker-centric intention, musamma is literal assignment, and mafhum is listener-centric cognition.

In the same passage, Bannani refines three key terms that are often conflated: al-ma'na, al-musamma, and al-mafhum. This distinction is critical for understanding how words signify in Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh. While al-musamma is restricted to the original literal meaning, al-ma'na is broader and includes both literal and figurative intentions, and al-mafhum is the mental representation received by the listener.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
The terms are al-ma'na (المعنى), al-musamma (المسمى), and al-mafhum (المفهوم). Notice that Bannani describes each as different aspects of the signified.
2
Analyze al-ma'na vs al-musamma
Al-ma'na is what is meant by the word (مَا يُعْنَى بِاللَّفْظِ), regardless of whether it is literal or metaphorical. Al-musamma is only what the word is placed for to signify by itself (مَا وُضِعَ اللَّفْظُ لِيَدُلَّ عَلَيْهِ بِنَفْسِهِ), i.e., the literal meaning. Therefore, al-ma'na includes al-musamma and also includes majaz.
Example: 'lion' (al-asad) – its musamma is the animal, but its ma'na can be a courageous man when used with a qarina.
3
Analyze al-ma'na vs al-mafhum
Both refer to the same mental form, but from different perspectives. Al-ma'na is considered from the speaker's side: what the speaker intends (قصد باللفظ). Al-mafhum is considered from the listener's side: what is obtained in the mind from the word (حاصل من اللفظ في العقل).
This is an i'tibari (conceptual) distinction, not a real difference in the signified itself.
4
Apply the distinction to a fiqh example
Take the word 'salat' in the Quran. Its musamma is the specific ritual prayer. Its ma'na in a particular verse might include both literal prayer and metaphorical supplication if there is a qarina. The mafhum for the listener is the mental concept of prayer that arises from hearing the word, which may be the literal or metaphorical form depending on context.

Two Views on Majaz and Their Effect on Signification Types
Author discusses two views on majaz being mawdu' and its implications for types of signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two views on whether majaz has a wad' and explain how each view affects the application of the three types of signification (mutabaqah, tadammun, iltizam) to metaphorical meanings.

Core Idea
Whether majaz has a wad' determines whether its signification is by correspondence or by qarina alone.

This passage from Bannani's commentary refines the understanding of majaz in relation to wad'. Two opposing views are presented: the preferred view that majaz is established by type-wad', and the view of the rhetoricians that it is not. The choice between these views has direct consequences for how the three divisions of signification—correspondence, inclusion, and commitment—apply to metaphorical usage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two views
Locate where Bannani states the preferred view (al-majaz mawdu' bi al-naw') and the opposing view (ghayr mawdu' aslan).
The preferred view is attributed to al-Talwih; the opposing view is held by the author of al-Miftah and al-Sayyid.
2
Understand implications of the preferred view
On this view, when a word is used metaphorically for a part or concomitant of its original meaning, the signification on the metaphorical meaning is correspondence (mutabaqah), and inclusion (tadammun) and commitment (iltizam) apply to the parts and concomitants of that metaphorical meaning.
Thus the three divisions operate identically in haqiqa and majaz.
3
Understand implications of the opposing view (rhetoricians)
The rhetoricians deny correspondence for metaphorical meaning. They argue that the listener first understands the literal meaning by correspondence, then via qarina derives the metaphorical meaning, which is not one of the three wad'i significations.
This view maintains that the three significations are based only on wad', and majaz is not wad'.
4
Compare the roles of qarina
In the preferred view, qarina merely disambiguates competing wad's (like in mushtarak); in the opposing view, qarina is essential for grasping the metaphorical meaning, which is not directly signified.
This difference highlights the core of the dispute.

The Verbal Nature of the Dispute: Definitions of Wad'
Author discusses two views on majaz being mawdu' and its implications for types of signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two definitions of wad' that underlie the dispute over majaz and determine which definition leads to which conclusion.

Core Idea
The dispute over majaz being mawdu' reduces to how you define wad'.

Bannani, citing al-Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani, reveals that the entire controversy over majaz is verbal (lafzi). It hinges on whether wad' is defined as the appointment of a word itself for a meaning (which excludes majaz) or as the appointment in exchange for a meaning while considering relationships (which includes type-wad' for majaz).

Guided Path
1
Locate al-Sayyid's analysis
Find where the passage states that the khilaf (dispute) is lafzi and its source is the definition of wad'.
This is a key insight—it shows the dispute is terminological.
2
Extract the first definition of wad'
If wad' is 'appointment of the word itself for the meaning' (ta'yin al-lafz bi nafsihi li al-ma'na), then majaz has no wad' because the word itself is not appointed for the metaphorical meaning.
This is the definition assumed by the rhetoricians.
3
Extract the second definition of wad'
If wad' is 'appointment of the word in exchange for the meaning' (ta'yin al-lafz bi iza' al-ma'na), then majaz necessarily has a type-wad' because the relationship type (like similarity) is considered by the institutor.
This definition allows for type-wad'.
4
See which definition the commentator adopts
Bannani notes that the commentator (al-Sharh) and the author of Jam' al-Jawami' adopt the second definition, thus affirming that majaz has wad'.
This shows the practical implication of choosing a definition.

Multiple Indications from Different Perspectives: Mutabaqa, Tadhammun, Iltizam
Author explains multiple indications from different perspectives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutabaqa, tadhammun, and iltizam indications in a given word, using examples from hadith.

Core Idea
A single word can simultaneously indicate multiple meanings by different logical relations.

When you hear the word 'rak'a' in a hadith, does it indicate just the bowing, or the whole unit? The answer is both—but in different ways. This lesson unpacks how a word can carry multiple indications at once, based on the concepts of mutabaqa, tadhammun, and iltizam, as explained by al-Sayyid al-Sharif.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of indication
Mutabaqa: the word indicates the whole meaning it is placed for. Tadhammun: it indicates a part of that meaning. Iltizam: it indicates a necessary concomitant outside the meaning but linked to it.
These are standard in Islamic logic.
2
Analyze the case of a word shared between part and whole
Suppose a word is placed for both a whole and a part (mushtarak). When used for the whole, it indicates the whole by mutabaqa and the part by tadhammun (since the part is included). The mind has two transfers: explicit to the whole, implicit to the part.
The author describes this as two indications from different perspectives.
3
Extend to the case of necessary and necessary
If a word is placed for a necessary (e.g., the sun disc) and also for its necessary (light), using it for the necessary gives mutabaqa for that necessary and iltizam for the necessary.
The necessary does not have to be part of the meaning; it is an external concomitant.
4
Understand the objection and response
Objection: Two indications in one state is impossible if indication were production. Response: Indication is merely the mind's attention; the mind can attend to multiple things at once.
This distinction is crucial to avoid metaphysical impossibility.
5
Apply to hadith examples
In 'Whoever catches the rak'a has caught the sajda', 'rak'a' indicates the bowing by mutabaqa (since it is placed for that) and the whole unit by tadhammun. In the sun example, 'shams' indicates the rays by iltizam.

Two Definitions of Truth and Falsity: The Objection and Response
Author explains two definitions of truth/falsity and responds to an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the two definitions of truth and falsity presented in the text and analyze how the objection regarding contradiction is resolved through the distinction between signification and its object.

Core Idea
Truth is correspondence — but of what? The object or the representation?

In classical Islamic logic, truth and falsity are defined in terms of correspondence with reality. This passage presents two definitions and an important objection. The first definition is standard: a statement is true if it corresponds to what is in reality. The second definition locates truth in the correspondence of the object of the statement's signification. This subtle shift leads to a problem about contradiction that the commentator resolves. Understanding this resolution is crucial for grasping how logicians maintain the law of contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Find in the Arabic where the first definition ends and the second begins. Note the terms 'الحكاية' (representation) and 'المدلول' (signification). Observe how the second definition shifts from the representation itself to the object of the signification ('مُتَعَلَّق المَدْلُول').
2
Understand the objection
Identify the problem: if truth is defined as correspondence of the object of the signification, then two opposing judgments (e.g., 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not standing') would not contradict each other, because they are separate mental acts. The contradiction would only be between the actual states of affairs, not the judgments.
The objection claims that this destroys the logical relation of contradiction between propositions.
3
Analyze the response
The commentator clarifies that contradiction is between the concepts (meanings) of the propositions, not between the real objects. The distinction between 'مَدْلُول' (signification) and 'مُتَعَلَّق المَدْلُول' (object of signification) preserves the possibility of contradiction.
The response argues that the common view places contradiction at the level of concepts, not real objects.
4
Verify the logical structure
Test the response: if we accept the second definition, do we lose the ability to say that two contradictory statements cannot both be true? According to the response, no, because the concepts themselves are contradictory and the truth or falsity of each depends on the object's correspondence.
This step ensures you see how the distinction maintains logical consistency.

Why Tamthil and Istiqra Yield Only Probability
Author explains that tamthil and istiqra only yield probability, not certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why tamthil and istiqra yield only probability (zann) and contrast them with logical qiyas in terms of certainty.

Core Idea
Tamthil can never yield certainty because the 'illa (cause) might be mistaken.

In Islamic logic, tamthil (analogical inference) is widely used in fields like usul al-fiqh, but it has a fundamental epistemological limitation. This passage from the sharh explains that both tamthil and istiqra (induction) produce only probability, not certainty, because the attribute thought to be the 'illa may not be the real cause. Understanding this limitation is crucial for evaluating the strength of arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inferential methods discussed
Locate the terms 'tamthil' and 'istiqra' in the Arabic. Note that the author groups them together as sharing a limitation.
Tamthil is analogy based on a common attribute; istiqra is induction from particular cases.
2
Find the epistemological claim about them
The passage states that neither tamthil nor istiqra yields 'al-qat'' (certainty). The phrase 'la yufid al-qat'' is key.
Certainty here means categorical, unconditioned truth (yaqin).
3
Extract the reason for the limitation
The author gives the reason: 'because it is possible that the 'illa is other than what is thought to be the 'illa.' This means the attribute used as the basis for analogy may not be the true effective cause.
There may be a hidden condition or particularity in the original case that is required for the ruling.
4
Contrast with logical qiyas
The passage mentions that logical qiyas (categorical syllogism) yields certainty. Why? Because its validity is formal; given true premises, the conclusion follows necessarily. Tamthil depends on empirical identification of the 'illa, which is fallible.
Logical qiyas is deductive and form-based; tamthil is inductive and content-based.

Core Idea
Certain premises are the foundational materials of demonstration, classified by how the mind attains them.

In this passage, the author enumerates the six categories of propositions that are certain (yaqiniyyat). These serve as the ultimate premises for any demonstrative argument (burhan) in the Islamic sciences. Understanding them allows you to identify the epistemological foundation of proofs in Kalam, Fiqh, and Usul al-Fiqh. The passage also addresses a subtle point about what makes a premise self-evident.

Define awwaliyat (self-evident)
Awwaliyat are propositions that the mind judges immediately upon conceiving the two terms, without any middle term. Examples: 'one is half of two', 'the whole is greater than the part'.

Define mushahadat (introspective)
Mushahadat (wijdaniyyat) are propositions judged through internal senses, like awareness of hunger, pleasure, joy, or self-awareness. They are not universal but particular.
This differs from mahsusat which are from external senses.

The hashya addresses an objection: the third example seems to require mediation. The response is that the mind conceives the terms in a specific manner (person present in both places simultaneously), and then the impossibility is immediate. Any delay is due to deficiency in the intellect or contamination of thought, not the nature of the proposition.
This shows that awwaliyat can require a moment's reflection for some minds but remain self-evident in principle.

Commentary
This passage classifies the types of certain premises (yaqiniyyat) that constitute the materials for demonstration (burhan). The author lists six types: (1) awwaliyat (self-evident), (2) mushahadat (introspective, also called wijdaniyyat, judgements from internal senses), (3) mujarrabat (empirical from repeated experience), (4) mutawatirat (recurrently reported), (5) hadasiyyat (intuitive, based on insight), and (6) mahsusat (sensory, from external senses). The passage focuses on defining the first two types. Awwaliyat are propositions the mind affirms immediately upon understanding the subject and predicate without any need for a middle term; examples include 'one is half of two' and 'the whole is greater than its part'. The third example, 'a single person cannot be in two places at once', is challenged by a hypothetical objection: one might object that this requires the mind to conceive the person in each place before judging impossibility, thus needing mediation, so it would not be awwali. The answer is that the mind conceives the situation in a specific manner (i.e., the person being present in both places simultaneously), and upon that complete conception, the judgement of impossibility is immediate without external mediation. Furthermore, any apparent need for reflection is due to deficiency in the intellect (like in children), contamination by false beliefs, or lack of proper conception, not because the proposition itself is not self-evident.

The Limits of Certain Knowledge: When Premises Are Not Proofs for Others
Author explains limits of certain premises and defines sensibles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between subjective and objective certainty and identify which types of premises (hads, tawatur, tajriba, wijdaniyyat, mahsusat) can serve as proofs for others in Islamic logical argumentation.

Core Idea
Certainty is not the same as universal proof; your personal certainty may not bind others.

In Islamic logic, some premises yield certain knowledge, but that certainty is not always transferable. The author explains that knowledge from intuition (hads), concurrent testimony (tawatur), experiment (tajriba), introspection (wijdaniyyat), and even sense-perception (mahsusat) may only be proof for the one who experiences it. This lesson unpacks that limitation and defines sensibles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of knowledge listed
The passage mentions five types: hads (intuition), tawatur (concurrent testimony), tajriba (experiment), wijdaniyyat (introspectives), and mahsusat (sensibles). Write them down.
2
Understand the reason for limitation
The author says 'because it is possible that what occurred to you may not occur to another'. This is why these types are not proofs for others: they are subjective.
The key is the possibility of non-occurrence for the other person.
3
Distinguish subjective from objective certainty
For each type, ask: Is this knowledge accessible to anyone else? For mahsusat, it requires sharing the same sense; for wijdaniyyat, only the experiencer knows. Thus, they are not universal proofs.
4
Define mahsusat precisely
The passage defines mahsusat as 'propositions judged by the intellect through the external senses'. Note the role of the intellect: it is not raw sense data but a judgment.
Example: 'the sun is luminous' is a judgment, not just a sensation.
5
Apply the concept to an Islamic example
Consider a personal experience that a particular herb cures a disease. According to the passage, this is tajriba and is not a proof for others unless it is independently verified or becomes tawatur.
This prevents an individual's experience from being used as a hujja in fiqh or kalam debates.
