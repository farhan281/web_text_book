Ownership of the Composite of Eternal and Temporal
Discusses the conditions for lam al-milk with covenant 'al' and the composite of eternal and temporal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine when lam al-milk is permissible with 'al' for covenant and analyze the theological debate on whether a composite of eternal and temporal is temporal.

Core Idea
Whether praise can be owned depends on its composition: a purely eternal entity cannot be owned, but a composite can.

This passage explores a subtle point in the grammar of praise in 'al-hamdu lillah'. If 'al-hamd' refers only to the eternal praise of Allah, it cannot be owned. But if it includes temporal praises from creation, the composite is temporal and thus ownable. This leads to a deeper dispute about the nature of composites containing both eternal and temporal elements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations of 'al-hamd'
First, note that 'al' in 'al-hamd' can be for covenant. The passage considers two cases: the covenanted is only the eternal praise, or it is the totality of praises (eternal and temporal).
2
Understand why the eternal cannot be owned
Ownership (milk) is control over a thing, which applies only to contingent, possible entities. The eternal has no beginning and is not subject to possession.
Compare with fiqh: property ownership only applies to created things.
3
Analyze the claim that the composite is temporal
If the covenanted is the totality of eternal and temporal praises, it is a composite. The passage states that a composite of eternal and temporal is temporal, and therefore ownable. This is the key logical move.
The justification is that the composite is not the individuals but their assembly.
4
Examine the objections to the composite being temporal
Some object: the eternal individual remains eternal, the temporal individual remains temporal, so how can the composite be temporal? The answer given is that the individuals are temporal by virtue of their meeting/joining, and the judgment is on the resulting form, not the individuals.
Further objection: the form is not the intended subject; the individuals are. Then a final answer: judgment can be on the totality from the perspective of its parts.
5
Synthesize the final position
The resolution is that the composite can be considered temporal for ownership because the judgment applies to the temporal parts. Thus, lam al-milk is permissible when 'al' refers to the composite.
This shows how logical distinctions (whole vs. part, form vs. matter) resolve theological-grammatical problems.

Commentary
This passage critically examines the compatibility of lam al-milk with 'al' for covenant. The main text states that if 'al-hamd' refers specifically to the eternal praise (the praise that has existed eternally), it cannot be owned because the eternal cannot be possessed. However, if 'al-hamd' refers to the totality of praises including both the eternal praise of Allah and the temporal praises of prophets and saints, then the covenanted entity is a composite, which is temporal, and thus can be owned. Al-Anbabi explains that ownership is a type of control that applies only to contingent entities. The discussion then deepens into whether a composite of eternal and temporal is truly temporal. Some argue that the individuals remain as they are (one eternal, one temporal), so how can the composite be temporal? The answer given is that the composite is temporal by virtue of the assembly and the resulting form; the judgment applies to the form, not the individuals. Further objections are raised and addressed. Ultimately, the preferred view is that the totality of individuals can be considered owned from the perspective of the temporal parts, and this is sufficient for the meaning of ownership in the context of 'al-hamdu lillah'.

Hyperbole vs. Reality in Logical Pedagogy
Commentator addresses objection that the poem claims to include all logical principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hyperbolic and literal claims in logical pedagogical texts and apply this distinction to resolve apparent contradictions between an earlier hyperbolic statement and a later precise one.

Core Idea
A hyperbole can be rooted in literal truth without being false.

When a teacher says 'these rules cover everything,' is that a boast or a fact? In this passage, an objector points out that the poem does not literally cover all logical principles. The commentator answers by showing that the claim is hyperbole (mubālagha), but it is based on the fact that these rules are the essential principles, enabling one to derive the rest. This lesson teaches you to recognize when a pedagogical text is using hyperbole for emphasis, and how to reconcile such statements with more precise ones that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (al-i‘tirāḍ)
Find the objector's claim: that the first interpretation forces the poem to include all logical principles, which is false because the poem omits many.
2
Identify the grounds of the objection
The objector's grounds: the literal claim that the poem contains all principles is demonstrably false; many principles are left out.
3
Analyze the answer (al-jawāb)
The commentator answers that the rules given are the majority of the important matters (ghālib muhimmāt al-fann), and because they enable the student to grasp what is left out, they are 'as if' all principles. This is hyperbole.
4
Reconcile with the later line
The commentator explicitly says the earlier statement is hyperbole (mubālagha) and the later line is verification (taḥqīq). They are not contradictory because hyperbole arises from the verification.
5
Assess the strength of the defense
Does the answer satisfy the objection? It acknowledges literal incompleteness but justifies the hyperbole as pedagogical emphasis. The defense is considered valid in this context.

The Logical Structure of Objection and Answer
Objection and Answer on the Title 'Fasl fi Jawaz al-Ishtighal bihi'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the parts of a dialectical exchange (objection and answer) in a classical text and explain how the answer resolves the perceived deficiency.

Core Idea
In Islamic dialectic, an objection about incompleteness can be answered by invoking a recognized scholarly convention.

This passage shows a typical naqd (objection) and jawab (answer) in Islamic scholarship. The objector points out that the title seems incomplete, and the author responds by clarifying that titles often highlight one aspect while the section includes more. This exchange teaches us both the logical form of dialectical reasoning and a practical principle about scholarly writing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the phrase 'فإن قيل' and note what the objector claims: the title is deficient because it mentions only jawaz, but the section includes tahrim and ibaha.
The keyword 'قصور' signals the claim of deficiency.
2
Identify the grounds
The objector's grounds: the title covers only permissibility, yet the text under it discusses three rulings. The title should match the content.
3
Identify the answer
Locate 'أجيب' and see how the author responds. The answer does not deny the extra content; it provides a justification based on scholarly practice.
4
Understand the logical move
The answer shifts the premise: a title need not be exhaustive. Titling a thing and adding upon it is acceptable. This resolves the objection without disputing the facts.
This is a common dialectical strategy: accept the opponent's observation but challenge the underlying assumption.

Commentary

Understanding this principle helps us read classical texts correctly: we should not expect every title to exhaustively list all contents. This reflects a practical wisdom in scholarly writing.

Defending a Chapter Heading in Dialectical Exchange
Objection and two responses on chapter heading completeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical exchange about a chapter heading and distinguish between two types of responses: excusing via convention and reinterpreting the heading's scope.

Core Idea
A chapter heading can be defended by convention or by reinterpretation.

In Islamic scholarly writing, the chapter heading (tarjama) is a crafted art. Critics sometimes object that a heading does not fully cover the content. In this passage, the author presents an objection that the heading 'fi jawaz al-ishtighal bihi' only mentions permissibility, while the chapter also discusses prohibition and appropriateness. Two responses are given: one based on scholarly convention, another on reinterpreting the heading's purpose. This lesson will walk you through the dialectical steps.

1

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In logical commentary, what is left unsaid can be more important than what is said.

Commentaries on logical works often contain dialectical exchanges where an objection is raised against the author's method, an answer is given, and that answer is further critiqued. Understanding this layered discussion is crucial for grasping the final position, which may be implicit. In this passage, the commentator defends the author's choice to include a disputed type, and the resolution hinges on an implicit mention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial objection

The objection is about compositional method, not about the truth of the ruling.
2
Extract the answer (jawab)
Find the response: the author first intended to explain the first type's ruling, but that intention led him to also mention the second type. Note how this answer shifts the focus from the objector's framing to the author's original intention.

Analyze the critique (naqd) of the answer

Commentary
This passage is a commentary by al-Bajuri on the line 'practitioner of the Sunnah and the Book' from the matn of al-Sullam. It begins by defining the phrase as meaning someone who has studied the Quran and Sunnah enough to distinguish true beliefs from false ones. The author clarifies that this does not mean mastering all the detailed sciences of tools (like linguistics, reasons for revelation, abrogation) that are required for an absolute mujtahid; rather, it means the ability to discern sound 'aqida. Then he explains that the author of the matn restricted the permission to study the second part of logic (mixed with philosophy) to those with complete intellect (kamil al-qariha) and such practice. Without these, one should not study such works because they contain errors of the philosophers. He then lists famous books of kalam that contain such confusions: al-Matali', al-Tawali', al-Mawaqif, al-Maqasid. Only those who are grounded and can identify falsehood may engage them. To illustrate the danger, he gives the example of the Mu'tazila, who incorrectly denied the beatific vision (ru'ya). They reasoned: Allah is not a body nor inhering in a body; whatever is not a body nor inhering cannot be seen; therefore Allah cannot be seen. This is a categorical syllogism with a false major premise. The correct refutation, al-Bajuri says, is to point out that existence itself suffices for the possibility of being seen, even without body or inherence. He then provides a correct syllogism: Allah exists; everything that exists can possibly be seen; therefore Allah can possibly be seen. This demonstrates the importance of sound reasoning grounded in revelation and sound intellect.

Building a Valid Syllogism for Ru'ya
Constructs correct syllogism for ru'ya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid categorical syllogism using true premises to prove an Islamic theological claim.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism with true premises yields certain theological knowledge.

After refuting the Mu'tazili syllogism, al-Bajuri presents the correct syllogism to prove that Allah can possibly be seen. This syllogism is valid in form and has true premises according to Sunni theology. This lesson teaches you to construct such arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
Al-Bajuri's conclusion is 'Allah can possibly be seen'. Find it in the Arabic: 'الله تعالى يصح أن يرى'.
2
Identify the minor premise
The minor premise is 'Allah exists' (الله تعالى موجود). This is the subject of the conclusion placed in relation to the middle term.
3
Identify the major premise
The major premise is 'Everything that exists can possibly be seen' (كل موجود يصح أن يرى). It relates the middle term 'exists' to the predicate 'can be seen'.
4
Check the syllogism's figure and mood
Minor: S is M (Allah is existing). Major: All M are P (all existing can be seen). Therefore S is P. This is first figure (Barbara). It is valid.
5
Verify the truth of premises
The minor is uncontroversial. The major is defended by reason (as al-Bajuri says: reason rules that whatever exists can be seen) and supported by revelation.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical instruction on the proper method of correcting errors in scholarly texts. The author, al-Bajuri, commenting on the matn lines of the Sullam al-Munawraq, expands on the etiquette of islah al-fasad (correction of error). The matn first advises the reader to be lenient to the beginner and to be a sincere adviser for correction. The commentary explains that 'nasihatan' (sincere advice) here means correction not done rashly at first glance or with disrespectful language, but after thorough consideration (ta'ammul) and with veneration. Then the matn says 'correct the error with reflection, and if it is obvious (badiha), then do not change it.' The commentary clarifies that this is to explicitly state that correction requires reflection, even if implied. Some scholars distinguished between correction in the text (changing the wording) and correction in the margin (suggesting alternatives), but al-Bajuri finds this forced and prefers the straightforward interpretation. The phrase 'if it is obvious' is interpreted by al-Bajuri as meaning 'even if the error's obviousness is self-evident, do not change it' – i.e., if the error is clearly obvious to all, one should not propose a change because it might be a correct statement misunderstood. Finally, the matn quotes a saying (which al-Bajuri identifies as a line of poetry by al-Mutanabbi): 'How many a critic distorts a correct statement because of his own defective understanding.' The commentary notes that the expression 'qila' (it was said) is not used to weaken the statement but merely to attribute it. Overall, the passage teaches the reader to approach textual criticism with care, reflection, and respect, avoiding hasty corrections that might themselves be erroneous due to the critic's misunderstanding.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange (jadal) about whether a nominal or verbal sentence is more eloquent when praising Allah. The commentator presents al-Fanārī's view: if the praised attribute is permanent (like lordship), use the nominal 'al-Ḥamd'; if it is a renewed action, use the verbal 'naḥmaduka' (we praise You). Then an objector raises a problem: Quranic verses praise Allah for creating, guiding, and bestowing—actions that are renewed—yet they use the nominal 'al-Ḥamd'. The answer is that in those verses, the praise is attached to Allah's essence, which encompasses both His essence and His actions. The commentator then quotes Ibn Hishām, who declares the Quranic formula the most complete because of its rhetorical subtleties: the definite article 'al-' denotes generality, the nominal sentence indicates permanence, the phrase does not restrict praise to a time or agent, the preposition 'lām' implies entitlement, and the silence about the praiser shows humility. The commentator ends by criticizing attempts to alter the formula, giving examples from al-Ḥarīrī and al-Zamakhsharī, and advises following the Quranic precedent.

Reconciling Textual Contradictions: The Case of Basmalah and Hamdalah
Commentator lists four answers to the objection of incompatibility and discusses the usul principle of mutually exclusive restricted texts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Textual contradictions are resolved through nuanced interpretation of key terms and usul principles.

This passage shows how scholars reconcile the apparent contradiction between hadiths commanding to start with basmalah and hamdalah. Four answers are given, leading to a discussion of a key usul principle about handling contradictory restricted commands.

Identify the objection
Find the objection: beginning with one prevents beginning with the other because 'beginning' means being first.

This moves from lexical interpretation to a deeper usul principle.

Normally, an absolute command is carried to a restricted one. But if two restricted commands are mutually exclusive, they are both lifted to the absolute to avoid contradiction.

The two hadiths (one commanding basmalah, the other hamdalah) are like two mutually exclusive restrictions; they are resolved by interpreting 'beginning' as absolute (customary or general), allowing both.
Thus the apparent contradiction is resolved without rejecting either hadith.

Two Syllogisms Distinguishing Qur'anic Relations from Eternal Speech
Dialectical argument proving that the nisba expressed by Qur'anic sentences cannot be identical to the eternal divine speech.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the two second-figure categorical syllogisms used to prove that the relational content of Qur'anic sentences is not the eternal speech, and verify their validity.

Core Idea
The relational content of Qur'anic sentences is not the eternal speech; two second-figure syllogisms prove this.

This passage from a commentary on al-Sanusi addresses a critical Ash'ari question: if Qur'anic words signify the eternal divine speech, what type of signification is that? The commentator first notes a disagreement among later scholars, then provides a decisive argument using categorical syllogisms. By carefully analyzing these syllogisms, you will see how the Ash'ari commitment to the unity and real existence of the eternal speech forces the distinction between the linguistic relations expressed in the Qur'an and the divine attribute itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises of the first syllogism
Find the two statements that serve as premises. The first premise is 'No relation exists in the external world.' The second premise is 'The eternal speech exists in the external world.' These are drawn from the passage's first argument.
The major premise is negative universal; the minor premise is affirmative universal.
2
Determine the conclusion of the first syllogism
From the two premises, the conclusion is 'No relation is the eternal speech.' This is a universal negative proposition.
This syllogism is in the second figure, second mood (Camestres).
3
Extract the premises of the second syllogism
The second syllogism uses: 'These relations expressed by Qur'anic words are multiple in reality' and 'The eternal speech is not multiple in reality.'
Here the major premise is affirmative universal, the minor is negative universal.
4
Derive the conclusion of the second syllogism
The conclusion is 'These relations are not the eternal speech.' This is also universal negative.
This syllogism is said to be from the first mood of the second figure (though it matches Camestres in modern classification).
5
Check the validity of both syllogisms
For the second figure, a valid syllogism must have exactly one negative premise and a negative conclusion. Both syllogisms satisfy this: first has EAE, second has AEE. Both are valid.
No fallacy is committed; the arguments are sound given the premises.

Refuting the Objection Against Isti'la' in Command
Author refutes an objection based on Fir'aun's verse against the condition of isti'la'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the author's refutation of the objection that the Fir'aun verse disproves the condition of isti'la' in command.

Core Idea
The Fir'aun verse is metaphorical; real commands require superiority, but that condition is itself disputed.

This passage presents a critical refinement in the debate over the definition of 'amr' (command) and whether it requires superiority (isti'la'). An objector uses the verse of Fir'aun to argue that command does not require superior status. The author refutes this by showing the verse is not a genuine command but a request for advice. Then, he advances his own position that the condition is actually unnecessary, citing a poetic line as evidence. We will trace the logical steps of this refutation and assess its strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection and its basis
The objector cites Fir'aun's verse 'Then what do you command?' (26:35) as evidence that command does not require superiority. He argues that since Fir'aun was worshipped and fought for, no superiority is imagined; thus isti'la' is not a condition.
The objector relies on a literal reading of the verse.
2
Examine the author's initial refutation
The author reinterprets 'amr' metaphorically as 'mashura' (counsel). He points out that the context of humility and equality (الصِّبْغَةُ فِي التَّضَرُّع وَالسَّسَاوِي) precludes a literal command. Thus the verse does not challenge the condition.
This is a semantic approach: the same word can have different meanings based on context.
3
Analyze the author's own positive doctrine
The author then states that the correct view is that isti'la' is not a condition for command, supported by the poetry of Amr ibn al-As: 'I commanded you a decisive command' said by a commander to his subordinate without real superiority. He argues that interpreting this as counsel is far-fetched due to the emphasis 'amran jaziman'.
The author's own doctrine seems to contradict his earlier refutation, but he distinguishes between the metaphorical reading of the verse and the literal usage in poetry.
4
Evaluate the consistency of the author's argument
The author refutes the objection by showing the verse is metaphorical, but then he argues that even literal commands do not require isti'la'. This is consistent only if he holds that isti'la' is not part of the definition of 'amr' but can be presupposed or not. His refutation targets the objector's misuse of the verse, not the condition itself.
The author's position is actually that the condition is not needed; his refutation of the objection does not commit him to the condition.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i on Conditional Divorce: Logician vs Grammarian
Author applies logician/grammarian distinction to Hanafi vs Shafi'i on conditional divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on conditional divorce and identify which reflects the logician's view and which the grammarian's view.

Core Idea
The same conditional yields opposite rulings based on treating it as logical implication or grammatical condition.

We have seen the theoretical distinction between logicians and grammarians on conditionals. Now, Mawlā Khusraw applies it to a real fiqh dispute: the Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings on conditional divorce. By examining this case, you will see how abstract logical theory translates into concrete legal differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional statement
The example is: 'If I marry you, you are divorced.' Recognize the antecedent (marriage) and consequent (divorce).
2
Understand the Hanafi view
The Hanafi school treats the conditional as a logical implication: the consequent is not a cause of prohibition until the condition occurs. Thus, divorce only takes effect upon marriage.
This matches the logicians' view that the whole conditional is a single judgment.
3
Understand the Shafi'i view
The Shafi'i school treats the conditional as a grammatical condition: the consequent is a qualified statement that takes effect immediately. Thus, divorce occurs at the moment of utterance, even before marriage.
This matches the grammarians' view that the consequent is the main assertion, with the antecedent as a qualifier.
4
Map each view to the framework
Determine which school aligns with the logician's interpretation and which with the grammarian's: Hanafi → logician, Shafi'i → grammarian.
5
Determine the legal outcome
After marriage, according to Hanafi, the divorce occurs, so the woman becomes divorced. According to Shafi'i, the divorce already happened before marriage, so the woman is not divorced upon marriage (since she was already divorced).
This shows practical consequences of the theoretical distinction.

Commentary
This passage is a juristic illustration of the theoretical distinction between logician and grammarian views on conditionals. Mawlā Khusraw argues that the Ḥanafī and Shāfiʿī rulings on conditional divorce (taʿlīq al-ṭalāq) reflect the two interpretations. According to the Ḥanafī school, the conditional suspends the divorce; the consequent ('you are divorced') is not an immediate cause of prohibition (taḥrīm) but becomes a cause only when the condition (marriage) occurs. This matches the logicians' view that the whole conditional is a single judgment (ḥukm) of implication, and the parts are stripped of independent assertoric force. Hence, after marriage, she is already divorced, so she does not become prohibited because he lacks ownership of guardianship (milk al-ʿiṣma) at the time of divorce. The author presents this as evidence for the validity of al-Sa'd's distinction, showing that it has real legal implications.

The Deleted Premise in Quran 21:22 and the Nature of Its Implication
Quranic verse as syllogism with deleted premise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the hidden modus tollens syllogism in Quran 21:22 and evaluate whether the implication between multiple gods and corruption is customary or rational.

Core Idea
The Quranic proof of tawhid relies on a suppressed premise—and the nature of that premise determines whether the argument is demonstrative or rhetorical.

This passage uses Quran 21:22 as a case study in logical analysis. The verse is a conditional: if there were multiple gods, corruption would follow. The author shows that the syllogism is completed by a suppressed premise ('but corruption does not occur'), making it a modus tollens. However, whether the implication (mulazama) is based on empirical custom or rational necessity determines the argument's epistemic force. You will learn to unpack this layered discussion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional in the verse
Read Quran 21:22 carefully. It states: 'Had there been gods besides Allah, both would have been corrupted.' This is a conditional proposition: if multiple gods (antecedent), then corruption (consequent).
2
Reconstruct the missing premise
The author explains that the exceptive premise is deleted: 'but they are not corrupted.' This is the denial of the consequent. With this premise supplied, the conclusion follows: 'therefore, there are no gods besides Allah.' Recognize this as modus tollens.
3
Understand the two interpretations of the implication
The passage presents two views: customary (ʿādiyya) and rational (ʿaqliyya). The customary view relies on observed experience that multiple rulers cause disorder. The rational view treats the implication as necessary.
4
Evaluate the consequences of each interpretation
If customary, the argument is rhetorical (khiṭāba) and persuasive, not certain. If rational, difficulties arise: actual corruption is not necessary, and possible corruption does not disprove the antecedent. The author points out that even on the rational reading, the conclusion may not hold.
5
Synthesize the author's overall point
The logical force of the verse depends on the type of implication. The author uses this example to show how logical analysis clarifies the nature of Quranic arguments.

Definition of Dialectic (Jadal) and Its Purpose
Author defines jadal and explains its goal of silencing opponents and persuading the unqualified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish dialectic from demonstration based on its purpose and its use of widely-accepted or conceded premises.

Core Idea
Dialectic silences opponents using accepted premises, not certain proof.

In this passage, the author defines jadal as one of the five logical arts. Unlike demonstration (burhan), which aims at certainty, dialectic aims to compel an opponent in debate or to persuade a learner who cannot grasp certain proofs. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in theological and legal debates, where different standards apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two purposes of jadal
Locate the Arabic phrases 'qaṭ' al-khuṣūm' (silencing opponents) and 'iqnā' al-qāṣir' (persuading the incapable). These are the explicit goals of dialectic.
2
Contrast with burhan
Notice that jadal does not aim at certainty; it uses premises that are widely-accepted (mashhūrāt) or conceded (musallamāt) by the opponent, even if those premises are actually certain in themselves.
The commentator emphasizes that non-consideration of certainty is not consideration of falsehood.
3
Examine the commentator's expansion
The commentator explains 'silencing opponents' in two scenarios: if the opponent is objecting, bind him; if the opponent is presenting a proof, refute it. The quote from Ibn Sina's al-Shifa illustrates a teacher using a dialectical syllogism for a student who cannot grasp demonstration.
This shows that dialectic is a pedagogical tool.
4
Apply the distinction to a real debate
Consider a theological debate about the createdness of the Qur'an. If the opponent concedes certain premises from consensus, you would use a dialectical argument (relying on those conceded premises) rather than a demonstrative proof that requires self-evident axioms.
This illustrates when dialectic is appropriate.
