Two Types of Formal Fallacies in Syllogisms
Explains the two types of formal fallacy: not following the four figures and violating conditions of productivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of formal fallacy—departing from the four figures and violating conditions of productivity—and identify examples of each.

Core Idea
A formal fallacy arises from structural defects: either no valid figure or broken conditions.

This passage from Bajuri's commentary expands on the second type of error in syllogisms—formal fallacies. He explains that such errors occur in two ways: either the argument does not follow any of the four recognized figures, or it violates the necessary conditions for productivity (like quantity/quality). Understanding these categories helps you spot invalid reasoning in Islamic arguments, whether in fiqh or kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and note the two ways a formal fallacy occurs: departing from the four figures, and leaving a condition of productivity.
These are not mutually exclusive; some arguments may do both.
2
Check for a valid figure
For the first category, see if the argument has a repeated middle term and fits one of the four figures (actually or potentially). If not, it's a formal fallacy of the first type.
The example 'This is an animal, that is inanimate' has no middle term at all.
3
Check conditions of productivity
For the second category, verify the conditions for the specific figure. In the first figure, the major must be universal and the minor affirmative. Violation of these is a formal fallacy.
The text gives the example of a first figure with a particular major or negative minor.
4
Apply to new examples
Given a syllogism, determine if it commits a formal fallacy and which type. Use the criteria from the passage.
For example, 'All scholars are knowledgeable; some knowledgeable people are pious; therefore all scholars are pious' fails as a valid figure.

Commentary
This passage introduces the second main type of fallacy: formal error (fasad al-qiyas min jihat al-sura). The author states that formal fallacies are of two kinds. The first is departing from the four valid figures of the syllogism, meaning the argument does not conform to any recognized figure either actually or potentially. The example given is 'This is an animal, and that is inanimate' — here, there is no middle term that links the two premises, so no syllogism exists. The second kind is violating the conditions of productivity (shurut al-intaj) concerning quantity (kulli/juz'i) or quality (ijab/salb). Specific conditions are given for the first figure: the major premise must be universal and the minor premise affirmative. If the major is particular or the minor negative, the syllogism is invalid. Commentary
This passage is a classification and expansion of formal errors in syllogisms. The author, following a poetic text, states that the second type of error (the first being material) concerns the form (surah) of the syllogism and has two subcategories. First: the syllogism does not conform to any of the four known figures, either actually or potentially. The example 'This is an animal, that is inanimate' lacks a repeated middle term, so it is not a valid syllogism. Second: even if the syllogism belongs to a valid figure, it may violate one of the conditions of production (shurut al-intaj) regarding quantity (kamm) or quality (kayf). For the first figure, the major must be universal and the minor affirmative. Using a particular major (e.g., 'Some animal is a horse') or a negative minor (e.g., 'Nothing of man is a horse') leads to non-sequitur. The commentator then addresses a potential objection: why did the poet include 'producing one of the premises' as an example of material error? He explains that such a syllogism is materially corrupt because its material does not yield a new conclusion. Further, the commentator elaborates on the first subcategory: the middle term missing in two ways—completely absent (as in the given example) or incompletely repeated (e.g., 'man has hair' and 'every hair grows' where the middle is 'has hair' not repeated verbatim). Finally, a refinement: using a natural (tabi'iyyah) proposition in place of a universal (kulliyyah) in the first figure's major is considered a formal error by al-Katibi (because universality is lost) but a material error in al-Shamsiyya (because it affects truth).

Nuances of Formal Errors: Incomplete Middle Term and Natural Propositions
Commentator expands on the first subcategory and discusses the natural proposition debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a middle term is not fully repeated and evaluate the debate on natural propositions in the first figure.

Core Idea
A middle term must be repeated exactly; natural propositions may be considered a formal or material error.

The commentator expands on the first subcategory of formal error, showing that the middle term can be missing in two ways. He also addresses a debate about using natural propositions (tabi'iyyah) in the first figure, highlighting differences among logicians.

Guided Path
1
Understand the incomplete middle term
The commentator explains that the middle term may be completely absent (as in the first example) or present but not repeated exactly. Example: 'Man has hair, every hair grows'—the middle is 'has hair', which is a composite, and it is not repeated verbatim in the major.
A syllogism is valid only if the middle term appears identically in both premises.
2
Examine the valid corrected form
If the major is 'Every hair of man grows', then the middle 'hair of man' is repeated exactly, and the resulting syllogism is valid and true.
3
Analyze the natural proposition case
When a natural proposition (e.g., 'Animal is a genus') is used as the major in the first figure instead of a universal ('Every animal is a genus'), al-Katibi considers it a formal error because the universality condition is lost. However, al-Shamsiyya considers it a material error because the truth value is affected.
This shows that the boundaries between formal and material errors can be debated.
4
Compare the two views
Ask: is the error due to the form (structure) or the matter (content)? The disagreement hinges on whether the natural proposition is considered a universal or not.
In practice, you must know the conditions of each figure and the precise terms used.

(۲) - وَثَانِيهَما : تَرْكُ شَيْءٍ مِنْ شُرُوطِ الإِنْتَاجِ بِحَسَبِ الكَم أَوِ الكَيْفِ ; كَأَنْ تَكُونَ كُبْرَى الشَّكْلِ الأَوَّلِ جُزْئِيَّةٌ أَوْ صُغْرَاهُ سَالِبَةٌ ; كَأَنْ يُقَالَ: كُلُّ إِنْسَانٍ حَيَوَانٌ، وَبَعْضُ الحَيَوَانِ فَرَسٌ لِيُوهِمَ أَنَّ بَعْضَ الإِنْسَانِ فَرَسٌ، أَوْ يُقَالُ: لَا شَيْء مِنَ الإِنْسَانِ بِفَرَسٍ، وَكُلُّ فَرَسٍ حَيَوَانٌ؛ لِيُوهِمَ أَنَّهُ لَا شَيْءٍ مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ بِحَيَوَانِ. قلت : الأولى في الجواب أنَّ النَّاظم لم يجعل إنتاج إحدى المقدِّمتين من جملة القضايا الملتبسة بالصَّادقة حتّى يعترض عليه، وإنما جعله مِنَ الفساد المعنوي الشامل له، ولمَّا إذا كانتِ المقدمة الكاذبة ملتبسة بالصَّادقة، ولا شكّ أنَّ القياس المنتج إحدى المقدمتين فاسد المادة؛ لأنَّه لا بد أن تكون مادَّته التي تركَّب منها غير نتيجة، والشَّارح تبعاً لقدُّورة لَمَّا رأى النَّاظم أتى بقوله : أو ناتج إحدى المقدمتين موالياً لقوله: كمثل جعل العرضي الذي هو مثال التباس الكاذبة بالصادقة؛ توهما أنَّهما مثالان لها. قال النَّاظم : وَالثَّانِ) كَـ الخُرُوجِ ... إلخ) : قوله : (أَنَّ الوَسَطَ لَمْ يَتَكَرَّرْ) صادق بصورتين : : بأن لا يكون هناك حد وسط أصلاً كهذا المثال، أو لم يكرّر بتمامه؛ نحو: «الإنسان له شعر، وكل شعر ينبت فـ: «الإنسان ينبت»، ووجه الغلط : أنَّ الحدَّ الوسط هو مجموع له شعر، وهذا المجموع لم يتكرّر ، ولو وجد بتمامه في الكبرى بأن قيل : الإنسان له شعر، وكل شعر للإنسان ينبت ينتج : «الإنسان له شعر ينبت وهو صادق، فالقياسُ صحيح ونتيجته صادقة . قوله : (كُبْرَى الشَّكْل الأَوَّلِ جُزْنِيَّةٌ مثله : ما إذا كانت طبيعيَّة ; كقولك: «الإنسان حيوان، والحيوان جنس ينتج : الإنسان جنس كذا قاله الكاتبيُّ في «الجامع»، فجعل وضع الطبيعية موضع الكلِّيَّة مِنَ الخطأ الصُّوري ; نظراً لفوات كلّيَّة الكبرى عند التَّعبير عنها بالطَّبيعية، وجعله في «الشمسية» مِنَ الخطأ المادي.
Then he pointed to the error occurring from the aspect of form, and said: (127) And the second [type] is like: exiting from its figures and leaving a condition of production [unfulfilled] from its completion. He means that the second of the two types of error, which is the corruption of the syllogism from the aspect of form, occurs by one of two matters: (1) One of them: exiting from the known four figures of the syllogism, that the fallacious person brings a composition that is not in any of the mentioned figures, neither actually nor potentially; e.g., 'This is an animal, and that is inanimate'; and the aspect of the error is that the middle term is not repeated. (2) And the second of them: leaving something of the conditions of production in terms of quantity or quality; such as if the major premise of the first figure is particular or its minor is negative; e.g., saying: 'Every human is an animal, and some animal is a horse,' to give the impression that some human is a horse; or saying: 'Nothing of the human is a horse, and every horse is an animal,' to give the impression that nothing of the human is an animal. I said: The first [response] is that the poet did not make the production of one of the two premises from among the propositions confused with the true, so that an objection could be made against him; rather, he made it part of the material corruption that encompasses it. And when the false premise is confused with the true, there is no doubt that the syllogism producing one of the two premises is materially corrupt; because its material must be not resulting [from the premises]. And the commentator, following Qaddurah, when he saw that the poet came with his statement: 'or the production of one of the two premises' adjacent to his statement: 'like making the accidental which is an example of the confusion of the false with the true,' mistakenly thought that they are two examples for it. The poet said: And the second [type] is like exiting... etc.: His statement: 'that the middle term is not repeated' is true in two forms: either that there is no middle term at all like this example, or that it is not repeated in its entirety; e.g., 'Man has hair, and every hair grows, so man grows,' and the aspect of error is that the middle term is the composite 'has hair', and this composite is not repeated. And if it were found in its entirety in the major, by saying: 'Man has hair, and every hair of man grows,' it would produce 'Man has growing hair,' which is true; so the syllogism is valid and its conclusion true. His statement: ('The major of the first figure being particular' similar to when it is natural; e.g., saying: 'Man is an animal, and animal is a genus,' produces 'Man is a genus,' as al-Katibi said in al-Jami'; so he made placing the natural in place of the universal a formal error, looking at the loss of the universality of the major when expressed by the natural; and in al-Shamsiyya he made it a material error.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and expansion of formal errors in syllogisms. The author, following a poetic text, states that the second type of error (the first being material) concerns the form (surah) of the syllogism and has two subcategories. First: the syllogism does not conform to any of the four known figures, either actually or potentially. The example 'This is an animal, that is inanimate' lacks a repeated middle term, so it is not a valid syllogism. Second: even if the syllogism belongs to a valid figure, it may violate one of the conditions of production (shurut al-intaj) regarding quantity (kamm) or quality (kayf). For the first figure, the major must be universal and the minor affirmative. Using a particular major (e.g., 'Some animal is a horse') or a negative minor (e.g., 'Nothing of man is a horse') leads to non-sequitur. The commentator then addresses a potential objection: why did the poet include 'producing one of the premises' as an example of material error? He explains that such a syllogism is materially corrupt because its material does not yield a new conclusion. Further, the commentator elaborates on the first subcategory: the middle term missing in two ways—completely absent (as in the given example) or incompletely repeated (e.g., 'man has hair' and 'every hair grows' where the middle is 'has hair' not repeated verbatim). Finally, a refinement: using a natural (tabi'iyyah) proposition in place of a universal (kulliyyah) in the first figure's major is considered a formal error by al-Katibi (because universality is lost) but a material error in al-Shamsiyya (because it affects truth).
