Synonymous Words and the Common-Univocal Distinction
Definition of synonymous words and clarification of common vs. univocal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between synonymous (mutarādif), common (mushtarak), and univocal (mutawāṭi') words by analyzing whether the multiplicity lies in the word, the meaning, or the referent.

Core Idea
The multiplicity of a word can be in its meaning or in its application; this determines its classification.

In Islamic logic, words are classified by their relationship to meanings. This lesson introduces the fourth division: synonymous words (mutarādif), then clarifies how common (mushtarak) differs from univocal (mutawāṭi'). Understanding this distinction is crucial for interpreting legal and theological texts correctly, as it prevents confusion between literal ambiguity and metaphorical extension.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth division
Look for the phrase 'القسم الرابع: أن يتعدد اللفظ ويتحد مسماه'. This is the category of synonyms: multiple words, one meaning.
Examples given: البُر والحنطة والقمح (all meaning wheat), الإنسان والبشر (human), الأسد والسبع (lion).
2
Extract the definition of mutarādif
The author defines it as 'اللفظ المتعدد الذي اتحد معناه' (the multiple word whose meaning is one). Note that the words themselves are multiple, but they share a single referent.
The term mutarādif comes from 'tarāduf' meaning following one another, as if the words ride on the same meaning.
3
Differentiate mushtarak from mutawāṭi'
The passage says: common (mushtarak) has multiplicity of meaning (tadduda ma'nāhu), while univocal (mutawāṭi') like 'insān' has one meaning but multiple referents (ta'adud masdūqihi).
In mushtarak, the word is placed for multiple meanings independently. In mutawāṭi', the word applies equally to all individuals falling under that meaning.
4
Apply to the example of 'asad'
The objector asks: 'Isn't 'asad' used for both the ferocious animal and the courageous man, making it common?' The answer: Its named (musammā) is only the animal; the second use is metaphorical (majāz), not a separate literal placement. Therefore 'asad' is univocal.
This clarifies that metaphorical extension does not create commonality.

The Four Types of Categorical Propositions and the Clarification of 'Specific Individual'
Author divides categorical propositions into four types and explains quantification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any categorical proposition into personal, unspecified, universal, or particular, and explain why a personal proposition requires a specific individual subject, not just any particular term.

Core Idea
Categorical propositions are four by quantity: personal, unspecified, universal, particular—defined by the subject's scope and quantifier.

The passage begins by dividing categorical propositions (al-ḥamliyya) into four types based on the subject's nature and whether a quantifier is present. This division is foundational for understanding how propositions are used in syllogisms (al-qiyās) in Islamic logic. The author then clarifies a critical nuance: what counts as a 'specific individual' for personal propositions includes not only proper nouns but also demonstratives and definite descriptions, refining a narrower view of earlier logicians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the proposition
Look at the proposition and isolate its subject (mawḍūʿ). For example, in 'Zayd is knowledgeable', the subject is 'Zayd'. In 'Every human is rational', the subject is 'human'.
The subject is the term about which something is affirmed or denied.
2
Determine if the subject is a specific individual or universal
If the subject refers to a specific, perceptible individual (e.g., 'Zayd', 'this standing'), then the proposition is personal (shakhṣiyya). If the subject is a universal term (like 'human', 'animal'), proceed to the next step.
The author stresses that 'specific individual' includes definite descriptions like 'I' or 'that one departing', not just proper nouns.
3
Check for a quantifier (sawr)
If the subject is universal and is accompanied by a quantifier, note whether it indicates all individuals (kull) or some individuals (baʿḍ). If it has 'kull', it is universal (kulliyya); if 'baʿḍ', it is particular (juz’iyya). If there is no quantifier, it is unspecified (muhmala).
Quantifiers are called 'sawr' because they surround the subject like a city wall.
4
Classify the proposition
Apply the above: personal if subject is specific individual; universal if subject is universal with universal quantifier; particular if with particular quantifier; unspecified if no quantifier.
Each of these can be affirmative or negative, giving eight total types.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition of categorical propositions (al-ḥamliyya). It begins by stating the primary division of categorical propositions into four types: personal (shakhṣiyya), unspecified (muhmala), universal (kulliyya), and particular (juz’iyya). The author then notes that each type can be affirmative or negative, resulting in eight possibilities (four types times two qualities).

The reason (wajh) for this division is immediately given: the subject of a categorical proposition is either a specific individual (shakhṣ muʿayyan) or a universal (kullī). If it is a specific individual, the proposition is personal; if it is a universal, it may be accompanied by a quantifier (sawr) indicating all or some individuals, leading to universal or particular propositions, or left without quantifier, leading to unspecified.

The commentary (from the hashiya of Bannani) then clarifies the phrase 'specific individual' (shakhṣ muʿayyan). It explains that this means the subject is pointed to as a specific perceptible entity—not that the subject's denotation is only a proper noun (ʿalam). The commentator follows ‘Ṣāḥib al-Jumal’ in using 'muʿayyan', which is better than al-Sanūsī’s term ‘juz’ī’ (particular), because al-Sanūsī restricts true particularity to proper nouns, while the commentator’s wording includes definite descriptions like 'this standing' or 'I am writing', which also refer to specific individuals.

The passage then defines each type with examples:
- Personal (shakhṣiyya), also called makhṣūṣa: e.g., 'Zayd is knowledgeable', 'Zayd is not knowledgeable', also 'I am standing', 'that one is departing'. It is called personal because the judgment is restricted to a specific individual.
- Universal (kulliyya): subject is accompanied by a sign of generality, e.g., 'Every human is rational', 'No rational is a horse'.
- Particular (juz’iyya): subject is accompanied by a sign of partiality, e.g., 'Some animal is human', 'Some human is not Arab'.
- Unspecified (muhmala) is implied but not separately defined here.

Finally, it discusses quantifiers: the universal quantifier (sawr kullī) indicates generality, and the particular quantifier (sawr juz’ī) indicates partiality. A proposition with a quantifier is called 'musawwara' (quantified), derived from the image of a city wall (sūr) that surrounds all or part of the city. It is also called 'maḥṣūra' (bounded) because it bounds the individuals of the subject to all or some.

This passage establishes the foundational classification of categorical propositions in terms of quantity and quality, critical for later discussions of syllogistic logic.

Key Terms
الحمليَّة (al-ḥamliyya): A categorical proposition that predicates something of a subject (e.g., Zayd is knowledgeable). It is contrasted with conditional (sharṭiyya).
الشخصيَّة (al-shakhṣiyya): A proposition whose subject is a specific individual (shakhṣ muʿayyan), like 'Zaid is knowledgeable'. It is also called makhṣūṣa (specified). The text emphasizes that the subject must be a specific individual, not merely a particular concept. [Note: Some logicians equate it with a proposition whose subject is a proper noun. This text expands it to include demonstrative phrases like 'this Zaid' or 'I'.]
الكلِّيَّة (al-kulliyya): A universal proposition where the subject is accompanied by a universal quantifier indicating all individuals, e.g., 'Every human is rational'.
الجزئيَّة (al-juz'iyya): A particular proposition where the subject is accompanied by a particular quantifier indicating some individuals, e.g., 'Some animal is human'.
المهملة (al-muhmala): An unspecified proposition without a quantifier; its quantity is left indeterminate. Mentioned in the division but not further defined in this passage.
السور (al-sawr): A quantifier; a term indicating generality (sawr kullī) or partiality (sawr juz’ī). The proposition is called musawwara (quantified) because of it. [Note: In earlier texts, sawr may refer specifically to the universal quantifier only; here it includes both universal and particular.]
المسوَّرة (al-musawwara): A quantified proposition, i.e., one that includes a quantifier. The term is derived from sūr (city wall) because the quantifier encompasses all or some individuals.
المحصورة (al-maḥṣūra): Synonym for musawwara; a 'bounded' proposition because it delimits the subject's individuals.

Distinguishing the Temporal Absolute from the Common 'Urfiyya
Author defines the third and fourth absolute propositions and summarizes all seven types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the absolute temporal (muṭlaqa ḥīniyya) and the common 'urfiyya, and correctly classify a proposition as one of the seven absolutes.

Core Idea
Temporal absolutes bind the predicate to the moment of the attribute; 'urfiyya binds it to the whole subject's existence.

The commentator continues defining the seven absolute propositions, now focusing on two that are often confused: the existential non-permanent and the absolute temporal. He clarifies the critical difference between the temporal and the common 'urfiyya, and summarizes the entire classification. By working through this passage, you will learn to identify the restriction that defines each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Read the definitions of types 3 and 4. Note the restriction in each: for type 3, negation of permanence; for type 4, restriction to the time of the attribute.
The examples help: 'Every human is living, not permanently' vs 'Every writer is moving his fingers when he is writing'.
2
Compare the restrictions
Compare the restriction in the temporal (ḥīniyya) to that of the common 'urfiyya (defined earlier). The temporal restricts to the exact moment the attribute holds; the 'urfiyya restricts to the entire duration of the subject's existence.
The passage explicitly states: 'restriction to the time of description does not encompass all its times, unlike permanence of description which encompasses all times.'
3
Apply to examples
Apply the distinction by examining the examples. For 'Every writer is moving his fingers when he is writing', the predicate holds only at the time of writing, so it is temporal. For 'Every human is living as long as he exists', the predicate holds for the whole subject existence, so it is common 'urfiyya.
Think of other examples: 'The sun is shining when it is above the horizon' (temporal) vs 'The sun is shining during the day' (common 'urfiyya).
4
Locate in the full list
The author states that the first four absolutes are truly absolute (unrestricted), and the last three are restricted. Types 3 and 4 are among the restricted. Place the temporal and common 'urfiyya in context: they are both absolute but with different restrictions.
The full list: 1. General, 2. Perpetual, 3. Common 'urfiyya, 4. Absolute (unrestricted), 5. Existential non-permanent, 6. Negation of necessity, 7. Absolute temporal.

Commentary
This passage defines two specific types of absolute propositions and then summarizes the complete classification. It is a definitional passage from a commentary. The third type is the existential non-permanent, which resembles the previous type but with the restriction that the predicate does not hold permanently: for example, 'Every human is living, not permanently'. The fourth type is the absolute temporal, where the predicate is affirmed only at the moment the subject actually possesses the attribute described: for example, 'Every writer is moving his fingers when he is writing'. Why Intuitive Knowledge is Not a Universal Proof
Author argues certain knowledge types are not proofs for others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of knowledge cannot serve as a proof for others and explain the epistemological reason based on the condition of shared premises.

Core Idea
Personal certainty is not the same as communicable proof.

In the Islamic logical tradition, a proof (hujja) must be based on premises that the opponent acknowledges. This passage from al-Bajuri’s commentary highlights a key limitation: knowledge obtained through intuition (hads), recurrent testimony (tawatur), experience (tajriba), inner states (wijdaniyyat), or sense perception (mahsusat) cannot be used as a proof against someone who does not share that specific avenue of knowledge. The reason is simple: the other person may not have had the same experience or faculty. We will break down this principle and see it applied to a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
List the knowledge types mentioned
The passage names five types: intuition (hads), recurrent testimony (tawatur), experience (tajriba), inner states (wijdaniyyat), and sense perceptions (mahsusat). Write them down.
2
Identify the condition for a valid proof
The author states that these types are not a proof 'against others' because it is possible that what you attained may not be attained by another. This tells you that a valid proof requires the premises to be accessible to the opponent.
This is a key rule in jadal (dialectical argument): you must use premises your opponent accepts.
3
Examine the example of the blind person
The commentator gives the example of a blind person (al-akmah). You cannot argue that the sun is bright as a proof against a blind person because he lacks the sense of sight. This illustrates that sensory proofs are only valid for those who share the relevant sense.

Generalize the principle
The underlying principle is that a proof must be based on premises that the opponent can verify or already accepts. If the knowledge is private or dependent on a specific faculty, it cannot compel the assent of others.
This is why, in kalam debates, arguments must rely on universal rational premises, not personal experiences.
