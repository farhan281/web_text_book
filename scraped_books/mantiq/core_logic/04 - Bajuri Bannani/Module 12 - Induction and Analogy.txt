Induction and Analogy: Non-Demonstrative Arguments
Author defines induction and analogy, noting they do not yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish induction from analogy and explain why neither produces certainty.

Core Idea
Induction and analogy are useful but fall short of demonstrative certainty.

In this lesson, we examine two types of inference that are common in Islamic legal and theological reasoning but do not produce certain knowledge. The author defines induction (istiqra') as reasoning from particulars to a universal conclusion, and analogy (tamthil) as drawing a conclusion about one particular based on another due to a shared property. Understanding their limitations helps you know when a proof is decisive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inference direction in induction
Induction moves from particular cases (e.g., Zayd is mortal, Amr is mortal) to a universal claim (all humans are mortal). Note that the conclusion is not guaranteed by the premises.
Complete induction (surveying all individuals) yields certainty, but incomplete induction is common.
2
Contrast induction with the logical syllogism
The author calls the syllogism 'the converse' of induction: it goes from universal to particular. The syllogism can yield certainty if its premises are certain.
This is the qiyas mantiqi already covered.
3
Analyze analogy (tamthil)
Analogy applies a ruling from one particular to another because of a shared attribute (jami'). For example, wine is forbidden due to intoxication; nabidh shares intoxication, so it is also forbidden.
In fiqh, this is called qiyas, but here it is contrasted with logical syllogism.
4
Recognize why these do not yield certainty
The text states explicitly that neither induction nor analogy gives certainty. For analogy, the shared property may not be the real cause ('illah) of the ruling. For induction, the sample may not be exhaustive.
Certainty belongs only to demonstrations (burhan) with necessary premises.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of non-demonstrative argument: induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). Induction is reasoning from particular instances to a universal conclusion, e.g., from 'Zayd is mortal' and 'Amr is mortal' to 'All humans are mortal'. Its converse (i.e., deduction from a universal to a particular) is the logical syllogism (qiyas mantiqi) which the author has already covered. Analogy (tamthil) is reasoning from one particular to another particular based on a common property (jami'), e.g., arguing that wine is forbidden because it is intoxicating, and then applying that to nabidh (date-wine) since it shares the same property. The author emphasizes that neither induction nor analogy provides certainty; only demonstration (burhan) using certain premises does.

The Weakness of Induction (Istiqra') in Classical Logic
Author defines and critiques istiqra' as probabilistic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an inductive argument and explain why it yields only probability, not certainty, using the example of animal jaw movement.

Core Idea
Induction is inherently weak: one counterexample suffices to refute a universal claim.

In classical Islamic logic, induction (istiqra') is defined as inferring a universal judgment from particular instances. While it may seem plausible, the logicians regard it as unreliable because it does not guarantee exhaustiveness. In this lesson, we will examine the definition given by the author, the famous example of animal jaw movement, and the critical verdict that both interpretations of induction are weak.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of istiqra'
The author states that arguing from a particular judgment to a universal is called induction. Locate the line 'And if one argues from a particular to a universal, then this is known among them as induction.'
This is the simplest definition; note that it is inference, not exhaustive enumeration.
2
Understand the grammatical parsing
The commentator explains that 'bi-juz'i' and 'ala kulli' are attached to 'ustudilla'. The implicit meaning is inferring a particular judgment to a universal judgment. Recognize that the particular and universal are attributes of an omitted noun (judgment).
The phrase 'bi-juz'i 'ala kulli' is highly condensed; the expansion reveals the logical structure.
3
Examine the example
The text gives an example: observing that humans, donkeys, and mules move their lower jaws when chewing leads to the universal claim that all animals do so. However, the crocodile is a counterexample—it moves its upper jaw. This shows the inductive leap is fallible.
The example uses real animals; the crocodile (al-nimsah) is specifically mentioned as an exception.
4
Grasp the critique
The author concludes that both definitions of induction (from particular to universal, and universal based on most particulars) are weak (ḍa'īf). The reason is that a single counterexample disproves the universal, so induction yields only probability, not certainty.
The critique is epistemological: induction lacks certainty (yaqīn).

Commentary
This passage defines and critiques the logical concept of istiqra' (induction). It begins by citing the matn line: 'And if one argues from a particular to a universal, then this is called induction among them.' The commentary then explains the grammatical parsing: 'bi-juz'i' and 'ala kulli' are both attached to 'ustudilla' (one argues). The implicit meaning is inferring a particular judgment to a universal judgment. The demonstrative 'fa-dha' refers to this type of inference, and 'bi-l-istiqra'' is attached to 'uqila' (is known) implying 'is named'. The author explicitly interprets induction as reasoning from a particular judgment to a universal one. An example is given: observing that humans, donkeys, and mules move their lower jaws when chewing, and generalizing that all animals do so. However, the crocodile is a counterexample—it moves its upper jaw. Thus, induction is not certain. The passage then notes a second interpretation: induction as a universal judgment on a universal based on what is found in most of its particulars. It concludes that both interpretations are weak (ḍa'īf), because induction yields only probability, not certainty.

Understanding the Definition of Induction (Istiqra')
Author defines induction and critiques weak definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define induction (al-istiqra') as per al-Sullam and identify its logical structure in a given example.

Core Idea
Induction infers a universal from observed particulars, but one counterexample destroys certainty.

The Sullam defines induction as inferring a universal judgment from a particular one. This lesson unpacks that definition, walks through the animal example, and highlights the crucial role of counterexamples. You will see how induction differs from deduction and why it yields only probability.

Guided Path
1
Parse the verse on induction
The verse says: 'And if one infers by a particular upon a universal, this is called induction among them.' Identify the key elements: 'particular' (juz'i) and 'universal' (kulli).
The commentary explains that 'particular' and 'universal' are attributes of an omitted noun 'judgment'.
2
Extract the definition
Induction is an inference from a particular judgment to a universal judgment. That is, you observe some instances and then claim that all instances share that property.
This is not deduction; the conclusion goes beyond the premises.
3
Examine the example: animals moving lower jaw
The author gives: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing, because humans, donkeys, mules do.' List the observed particulars and the universal claim.
Premises: particular observations. Conclusion: universal statement about all animals.
4
Identify the counterexample
The crocodile moves its upper jaw, not its lower jaw. This one counterexample shows that the universal is false, so the induction fails.
The author uses this to critique the definition's weakness.
5
Assess the strength of induction
Induction based on most particulars (incomplete induction) does not guarantee truth; it requires complete enumeration for certainty, which is rare.
The author concludes that both definitions given are weak.

Core Idea
Both definitions of induction are weak because they ignore the possibility of exceptions and claim certainty where only probability exists.

The author of the Sullam does not merely define induction—he also critiques two common definitions. You will learn to recognize the logical pitfall of hasty generalization and understand the importance of complete enumeration.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any inductive argument as complete or incomplete induction according to the criteria in al-Ghazali and al-Taftazani.

Core Idea
Induction is not a single method – its strength depends on whether it covers all particulars or only some.

This lesson unpacks the authoritative definition of induction given by Hujjat al-Islam al-Ghazali and refined by al-Sa'd al-Taftazani. Commentary

This guards against confusing the name of the argument form with the reasoning process.

Refining Induction: The Condition of 'Most' and the Opposite Qiyas
Author discusses the condition of 'most' in incomplete induction and critiques it, then clarifies the naming of qiyas mantiqi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to critique the requirement that incomplete induction must examine 'most' particulars, and distinguish induction from qiyas mantiqi.

Core Idea
The 'most' condition for incomplete induction is too strict – a sufficient sample that yields presumption is enough.

This lesson examines a critical refinement of incomplete induction. Ibn Qasim challenges the common condition that incomplete induction must examine most (akthar) particulars, using the example of Imam al-Shafi'i's menstrual rulings. You will also learn why the opposite of induction – reasoning from universal to particular – is not called qiyas mantiqi.

Guided Path
1
Understand the 'most' condition
Many logicians require that incomplete induction examine 'most' of the particulars. This excludes cases where half or fewer are examined, which would not count as induction.
2
Analyze Ibn Qasim's objection
Ibn Qasim points out that jurists, including Imam al-Shafi'i, derived rulings on menstruation durations from induction without examining most women. This shows the condition is too strict.
The example: minimum menstruation one day, maximum fifteen, usual six or seven. Al-Shafi'i based this on induction from some women, not most or all.
3
Consider the proposed alternative
The objection suggests replacing 'most' with 'a portion that yields a presumption (zann) of generality.' This is more flexible and reflects actual juristic practice.
4
Clarify the opposite of induction
The opposite of induction is inference from universal to particular. This is not called 'qiyas mantiqi'; that term refers to the two premises themselves. Understand the terminological distinction.
This prevents confusion between the process of reasoning and the formal structure of the syllogism.

Definition of Induction and Its Types
Definition of induction and its division into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istiqra' (induction) and distinguish between complete and incomplete induction based on the extent of particulars surveyed.

Core Idea
Induction surveys particulars to judge a universal; complete induction examines all, incomplete examines most.

The author defines induction (istiqra') as a survey of particular matters to derive a ruling for a universal encompassing them. This definition, attributed to al-Ghazali and endorsed by al-Taftazani, is fundamental for understanding how general principles are established in Islamic sciences. We will break down the definition and explore the two types of induction.

Guided Path

2
Extract the genus
The genus (jins) is 'tasaffuh umur juz'iyya' (تصفح أمور جزئية), i.e., a survey of particular matters. This places induction in the category of investigative activities.
The genus tells us what kind of activity induction is.

Extract the differentia
The differentia (fasl) is 'li-yahkuma bi-hukmiha 'ala amr yashmalu tilka al-juz'iyyat' (ليحكم بحكمها على أمر يشمل تلك الجزئيات), i.e., to judge by their ruling on a matter that encompasses those particulars. This specifies the purpose that distinguishes induction from other surveys.
The differentia captures the essence of induction as a reasoning tool.
4
Distinguish complete vs incomplete
The passage divides induction based on the extent of the survey: if all particulars are surveyed, it is complete induction (istiqra' tamm); if most are surveyed, it is incomplete induction (istiqra' naqis). Understand the logical implications of this division.

Correction of 'Aks: Logical Syllogism is the Premises, Not the Deduction
Terminological correction of 'aks and identification of logical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the opposite of induction (deduction from universal to particular) is not called logical syllogism, and identify the correct referent as the two premises.

Core Idea
The opposite of induction is deduction, but logical syllogism refers to the premises that produce it, not the act itself.

The author states that the opposite of induction is called logical syllogism, but the commentator corrects this: the proper term refers to the 'producer' (mufid) of that opposite, i.e., the two premises that lead to the deductive conclusion. This fine distinction prevents confusion between the logical structure (premises) and the reasoning process (deduction). It is essential for precise usage in logic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The author says: 'And its opposite is called logical syllogism'. This is the claim that needs correction.
The phrase is in the matn being commented on.
2
Understand the commentator's correction
The commentator explains that the intended meaning is 'the producer of its opposite' (mufid 'aksihi) is called logical syllogism. The 'aksi' (opposite) itself is the deductive inference from universal to particular, but that is not what 'logical syllogism' names; rather, the two premises that produce that inference are named.
This requires assuming a genitive construct (mufid 'aks).
3
Grasp the distinction
The opposite of induction is deduction from the universal to the particular. This act of deducing is not the syllogism; the syllogism is the pair of premises that, when combined, necessitate that deduction. The term 'qiyas mantiqi' applies to those premises.
This aligns with the earlier definition of syllogism as a speech composed of premises.
4
Apply the corrected understanding
When reading similar statements in logic texts, recognize that 'qiyas' refers to the premises, not the conclusion or the inferential step.
This prevents misinterpretation of later passages.

Key Takeaway
Analogy yields only probability because the identification of the cause ('illa) is not certain.
