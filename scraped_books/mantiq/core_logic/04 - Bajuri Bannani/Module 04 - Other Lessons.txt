Definition of Eloquent Simile (Tashbih Baligh)
Author defines the conditions of eloquent simile.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an eloquent simile (tashbih baligh) and distinguish it from other types of simile and metaphor.

Core Idea
In tashbih baligh, the tool of comparison is dropped to assert identity, not similarity.

In Arabic rhetoric, the eloquent simile (tashbih baligh) is a powerful figure where the tool of comparison (kaaf, kamaa, etc.) is omitted. This lesson clarifies its definition and conditions, based on the classical commentary on al-Sullam. Understanding this concept is essential for analyzing figurative language in the Qur'an and poetry.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two sides of the simile
Identify the thing being compared (mushabbah) and the thing it is compared to (mushabbah bihi). Example: 'Zaid is a lion' – Zaid is mushabbah, lion is mushabbah bihi.
The tool (adāt) is omitted.
2
Check for the absence of the tool of comparison
If the particle like kāna or ka- is missing, it may be tashbih baligh. If present, it's a non-eloquent simile (tashbih ghayr baligh).
Some scholars also consider the mushabbah and mushabbah bihi must be in a genitive or predicate relationship.
3
Identify the grammatical construction
Tashbih baligh often appears as a predicate nominative (e.g., 'Zaidun asadun') or as an annexation (idafa). Determine if the comparison is implicit.
In 'Zaid is a lion', the tool omitted, both sides are mentioned without explicit comparison.
4
Distinguish from istiara
In istiara (metaphor), the mushabbah is not mentioned explicitly; only the mushabbah bihi is used. In tashbih baligh, both sides are present.
Example: 'I saw a lion' meaning a brave man is istiara, while 'Zaid is a lion' is tashbih baligh.

The Five Relations Between Words and Meanings
Author enumerates five relations between lafẓ and ma‘nā

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the five types of relations between words and meanings (lafẓ and ma‘nā) and provide an Islamic example for each.

Core Idea
The five relations between words and meanings are the key to analyzing precision and ambiguity in Islamic texts.

In this lesson, we will examine the five categories that describe how a word relates to its meaning. This classification is essential for understanding how Islamic scholars handle ambiguity (ishtirāk) and synonymy (tarāduf) in theological, legal, and linguistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five categories
Read the Arabic passage and list the five relations: tawāṭu‘, tashākul, takhāluf, ishtirāk, and tarāduf. Note that the text explicitly says there are five without deficiency.
The Arabic terms are given in the source.
2
Distinguish tawāṭu‘ from ishtirāk
Tawāṭu‘ (univocal) applies the same word with the same meaning to multiple individuals, like 'insān' for Zayd and 'Amr. Ishtirāk (equivocal) uses one word for multiple distinct meanings, like 'ayn for eye, spring, and spy.
Do not confuse them: tawāṭu‘ has one meaning, ishtirāk has multiple.
3
Understand tashākul and takhāluf
Tashākul refers to similarity between words in meaning or form. Takhāluf is complete difference in both name and meaning.
Later logicians sometimes subsume tashākul under ishtirāk.
4
Recognize tarāduf as the opposite of ishtirāk
Tarāduf means multiple words for the same meaning, such as sayf and muhannad for sword. This is the inverse of ishtirāk.
The passage explicitly states 'its opposite is tarāduf'.
5
Apply the classification to Islamic examples
Think of examples from Islamic sciences: the word 'zakāt' applied to different quantities (tawāṭu‘), the word 'riba' meaning both increase and usury (ishtirāk), and the words 'al-ḥamd' and 'al-thanā’ for praise (tarāduf).
Ensure your examples come from Fiqh, Kalam, or Tafsir.

Khabar and Talab: The Two Categories of Complete Utterances
Author divides complete utterances into two main categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a khabar (report) from a talab (request) by checking whether the utterance can be evaluated as true or false.

Core Idea
Requests demand action; reports can be true or false.

Islamic logic begins by classifying complete utterances. The first major division separates statements that claim something true or false (khabar) from those that seek an action (talab). This distinction is vital for usul al-fiqh, where commands (amr) are treated differently from reports (khabar) about the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance as complete
A complete utterance (lafẓ tāmm) is a sequence of sounds conveying a full meaning. If it's not complete, the division does not apply.
2
Check for truth-evaluability
Ask: 'Can this utterance be described as true or false?' If yes, it is khabar. If no, it is talab.
For example, 'Allah is One' is true or false, so it's khabar. 'Pray!' cannot be true or false; it's talab.
3
Apply the distinction in an Islamic text
Take a verse from the Quran or a hadith. Classify each complete utterance as khabar or talab. For instance, 'Establish prayer' (al-Baqara 43) is a command (talab), while 'Allah is the Creator of all things' (al-Zumar 62) is a report (khabar).
This exercise sharpens the ability to separate propositions from imperatives.

The Three Types of Requests: Amr, Du'a, and Iltimas
Author subdivides request into three types based on hierarchy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a request is a command (amr), supplication (du'a), or plea (iltimas) based on the relative status of the speaker and listener.

Core Idea
Hierarchy determines request type: high-to-low = command, low-to-high = supplication, equal = plea.

After classifying an utterance as a request, Islamic logicians subdivide it based on the social or spiritual hierarchy between speaker and addressee. This classification helps in uṣūl al-fiqh to determine whether a divine command creates obligation, or a prophetic supplication is a model of humility.

Guided Path
1
Identify that the utterance is a request
First confirm it is talab (not khabar) by checking that it cannot be true or false and demands an action.
2
Determine the relative status
Ask: Is the speaker of a higher standing than the listener? Lower? Equal? This determines the subcategory.
For example, Allah speaks to humans as superior; a human praying to Allah is inferior; two humans of equal rank use iltimas.
3
Apply the three categories
If speaker is superior, it is amr (command). If speaker is inferior, it is du'a (supplication). If equal, it is iltimas (plea).
In Quranic analysis, knowing this helps interpret commands from Allah as binding, while prophetic du'as are not commands but invocations.
4
Practice with Islamic examples
Classify: 'O Allah, forgive me' (du'a), 'Observe fasting' (amr from Allah), 'Would you pass the salt?' between two equals (iltimas).

The Three Functions of 'al' in 'al-Hamd'
Grammatical analysis of 'al' in 'al-hamd'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three functions of the definite article 'al' in Arabic and apply them to the phrase 'al-hamd' in the basmalah.

Core Idea
The 'al' in 'al-hamd' can indicate covenant, universality, or genus—each with different theological implications.

In the basmalah, the word 'al-hamd' begins with the definite article 'al'. Scholars of Arabic grammar and theology have identified three possible functions for this 'al': al-'ahd (covenant), al-istighraq (universality), and al-jins (genus). Understanding these distinctions helps clarify the meaning of praise in the phrase. This lesson will guide you through each function and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify 'al' in the passage
Locate the definite article 'al' at the beginning of 'al-hamd' in the Arabic text. Note that it is attached to the noun 'hamd'.
2
Define al-'ahd (covenant)
Al-'ahd refers to a specific, known instance of praise—like the praise that the angels or prophets offer. It presupposes a previously agreed-upon reference.
Example: 'al-rajul' (the man) referring to a particular man already known.
3
Define al-istighraq (universality)
Al-istighraq encompasses all individuals of the genus. It means 'all praise' without exception.
Example: 'al-insan' (mankind) meaning all humans.
4
Define al-jins (genus)
Al-jins indicates the genus itself, not its individuals. It refers to the essence or kind of praise.
Example: 'al-dhahab' (gold) meaning the substance, not all gold objects.
5
Compare the three
Reflect on how the meaning of 'al-hamdu lillah' changes under each interpretation. Which one aligns with the context of the basmalah?

The Three Functions of 'lam' in 'lillah' and the Nine Probabilities
Grammatical analysis of 'lam' in 'lillah' and combinatorial meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three functions of the particle 'lam' in 'lillah' and calculate the total number of meanings from combining the three 'al' and three 'lam' functions.

Core Idea
The 'lam' in 'lillah' can signify worthiness, specification, or ownership, yielding nine possible interpretations when combined with the three 'al' functions.

Just as 'al' in 'al-hamd' has three meanings, the 'lam' in 'lillah' also has three: istihqaq (worthiness), ikhtisas (specification), and milk (ownership). By multiplying the possibilities, we derive nine potential meanings for the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah'. This combinatorial analysis is a classic example of logical parsing in Islamic sciences. This lesson will guide you through each lam function and the combinatorial outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify 'lam' in 'lillah'
Find the particle 'lam' at the beginning of the word 'lillah' in the Arabic text. Note that it is attached to the divine name 'Allah'.
2
Define lam al-istihqaq (worthiness)
Lam al-istihqaq indicates that the object of praise deserves the action. Here, it means Allah deserves the praise.
Example: 'al-madhu li-l-ustadh' (praise is for the teacher) meaning the teacher deserves it.
3
Define lam al-ikhtisas (specification)
Lam al-ikhtisas specifies that the praise is exclusively for Allah, without implying ownership or desert.
Example: 'al-kitabu li-l-maktaba' (the book is for the library) meaning it is assigned to it.
4
Define lam al-milk (ownership)
Lam al-milk denotes possession. Here, it would mean the praise belongs to Allah as property.
Example: 'al-baytu li-l-rajul' (the house is the man's) meaning he owns it.
5
Calculate the total probabilities
Since there are three possibilities for 'al' and three for 'lam', multiply them: 3 × 3 = 9. The passage states this explicitly.
The combinations are exhaustive but not all may be equally sound in context.

Commentary
This passage opens the commentary on the first line of the poem 'al-Sullam al-Muruniyy'. The author defines 'al-hamd' as a voluntary act, whose source is the essence of Allah or His power, and what is necessarily concomitant like His attributes. Then he analyzes the grammatical particles: the definite article 'al' in 'al-hamd' can serve three functions: al-'ahd (covenant/reference to a known instance), al-istighraq (universality encompassing all instances), or al-jins (the genus as a kind). Similarly, the lam in 'lillah' can be for istihqaq (worthiness/desert), ikhtisas (specification/assignment), or milk (ownership). By combining each of the three possibilities of 'al' with each of the three of 'lam', we obtain nine possible meanings for the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah'. This grammatical analysis is foundational for understanding the theological and legal implications of the praise of Allah.

The commentator resolves this by distinguishing between the type of causation involved: temporal efficient causation versus a metaphorical correlation with God's eternal perfections. This distinction is crucial for avoiding anthropomorphism in discussions of divine attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical discussion (naqd wa jawab) concerning the statement that the praise (hamd) of God is 'not caused' (ghayr mu'allal). The commentator identifies a contradiction in the earlier claim that God's praise of Himself is a judgment on a voluntary good, which implies it is caused. He resolves this by distinguishing between temporal causation (where something is an efficient cause of an effect) and eternal 'causation', which is only metaphorical: it means the correlation of the eternal speech with the perfections of God, which are deserving of praise. Thus, divine praise is not caused in the temporal sense. The commentator then provides several possible answers to objections: that the intended meaning is 'not caused by a temporal cause'; that the distinction between eternal and temporal praise is that eternal praise is not considered in terms of its establishment but in terms of deserving; that the author's own praise is temporal but particular, etc. This shows the nuanced handling of theological language about God's attributes and the avoidance of anthropomorphic causation.

His statement: (May Allah bless him) This sentence is declarative in wording, performative in meaning. And some permitted that it could be declarative in meaning also. And an objection was raised against it: that then it would follow that the purpose of the Lawgiver from commanding blessing upon him would not be achieved, as indicated by the apparent meaning of His statement: 'O you who believe, bless him' [Al-Ahzab:56]. His statement: (And in it there is an obvious remoteness) means: because the apparent meaning of the verses and hadith that command blessing is only supplication, not veneration. And some said: the past tense is used in the meaning of the present tense, intended for renewal, due to the indication of his saying 'as long as...', so it is a declarative used for continuity and persistence. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical discussion (jadal) about the grammatical nature of the sentence 'صَلَّى عَلَيْهِ اللهُ' in the poem's opening line. The author, Al-Bajuri, states that this sentence is declarative in wording (صيغة الخبر) but performative in meaning (معنى الإنشاء), i.e., it functions as a prayer even though it has the form of a statement. Some scholars, however, allowed that it could be declarative in meaning as well, meaning it simply reports that Allah blesses the Prophet. Al-Bajuri then records an objection: if it were purely declarative, it would contradict the apparent command in the Quran (وآية الأحزاب) which orders believers to send blessings, implying that the act of blessing must be performed, not merely reported. The response to this objection is that the real purpose of the blessing is its necessary implication, which is the veneration of the Prophet, and this veneration can be achieved by informing about the blessing. But Al-Bajuri finds this answer weak because the apparent meaning of the command is supplication (دعاء), not veneration, and also because claiming that the purpose is veneration implies that the Prophet does not benefit from our blessings, which is problematic.

Then Al-Anbabi adds a further objection: if the sentence were declarative in meaning, it would contradict the perpetual nature implied by the phrase 'مَا دَامَ الحِجَا' (as long as the intellect), because perpetuity only applies to the future, while a declarative past tense reports a past event. Two answers are given: either the past tense is used metaphorically for the future to indicate certainty, similar to the Quranic verse {أَتَى أَمْرُ اللَّهِ} (The command of Allah has come) which refers to a future event, or the permission to consider it declarative applies only when there is no indication of perpetuity; here, there is, so it must be performative. Al-Anbabi then explains Al-Bajuri's remark about the remoteness of the first answer, reiterating that the command is for supplication, not veneration.

Finally, Al-Sharshimi's marginal note explores various opinions: some insist the sentence must be performative due to the perpetuity indicator; some students argue that it can still be declarative if the past tense is understood as expressing continuity (like the nominal sentence in the Quranic example), but this is rejected because it would strip the verb of tense and because the nominal sentence is clearer for that purpose. Another view uses the past tense in the sense of the present to indicate renewal, but this is weak because renewal is typically expressed by the present tense. The overall discussion highlights the subtleties of Arabic syntax and the importance of context in determining sentence type, especially in sacred texts.

Refuting Exaggeration of Prophetic Knowledge via Partitive Min
Al-Bajuri uses the partitive 'min' to refute the claim that the Prophet's knowledge encompasses all things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how a grammatical particle (min al-tab'īḍiyya) functions as a theological refutation in a classical commentary and analyze its implications.

Core Idea
A single partitive preposition distinguishes prophetic knowledge from divine omniscience.

This passage from al-Bajuri's commentary demonstrates that even a single grammatical choice can carry profound theological weight. Al-Bajuri points out that the author's use of the partitive 'min' (مِنْ) in the phrase 'from the sea of meanings' subtly but firmly refutes the deviant claim that the Prophet's knowledge encompasses everything like Allah's. This lesson will walk you through how linguistic analysis serves as a tool for theological refinement in the Islamic scholarly tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
Read the passage and find the statement about those who claim the Prophet's knowledge encompasses everything as an encompassing like Allah's knowledge. What exactly is being claimed?
The claim is that the Prophet's knowledge is all-encompassing in the same way as Allah's knowledge.
2
Locate the linguistic evidence
Find the partitive 'min' in the phrase 'min bahr al-ma'ani'. Understand that 'min' here indicates a part, not the whole. How does this grammatical function oppose the all-encompassing claim?
Partitive 'min' means 'some of' or 'a portion of', implying that the Prophet's knowledge is only a part of the sea of meanings.
3
Understand the implication
Al-Bajuri says this indicates that the Prophet does not encompass all meanings; only Allah does. Why is this an explicit refutation?
If the 'min' is partitive, then the Prophet's knowledge is partial, directly contradicting the claim of all-encompassing knowledge.
4
Connect to broader refutation efforts
Al-Bajuri mentions that al-Yusi wrote a work refuting this claim and declaring its proponents disbelievers. Consider how this linguistic point fits into a larger theological argument.
Al-Yusi used both transmitted and rational evidences, but al-Bajuri's focus on the partitive 'min' shows that even a single preposition can be a strong refutation.

The Necessity of Ta'wil for Mutashabihat
Commentary clarifies that ta'wil is obligatory for mutashabihat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ta'wil in the context of aqeeda and explain why it is necessary for verses that suggest anthropomorphism.

Core Idea
Ta'wil is the agreed-upon method to avoid tashbih while affirming revelation.

When the Quran or Sunnah uses terms like 'face' and 'hand' for Allah, a literal reading would imply a body. Muslim scholars unanimously agree that such texts must be reinterpreted (ta'wil) to protect Allah's transcendence. This lesson unpacks that necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Find the verses or hadith that appear to attribute bodily parts to Allah. The passage mentions 'wajh' (face) and 'yad' (hand). Recognize that a literal reading leads to tashbih (anthropomorphism).
2
Define ta'wil
Ta'wil is defined as 'turning the word from its apparent meaning to a metaphorical meaning' to avoid implying a body for Allah. This is the agreed-upon starting point.
3
Recognize the consensus
The passage states that ta'wil is necessary (la budda min ta'wil) and that the Salaf and Khalaf agree on this point. Note that the disagreement comes later.
4
Distinguish from other approaches
Contrast ta'wil with tafwid (delegation without specifying the meaning) and tashbih (literal interpretation). The passage shows that tashbih is rejected, while both ta'wil and tafwid are forms of ta'wil in a broad sense.

Salaf and Khalaf: Two Methods of Ta'wil
Commentary details the two scholarly positions on ta'wil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Salaf approach of tafwid from the Khalaf approach of ta'wil with specification.

Core Idea
The Salaf delegate the meaning to Allah; the Khalaf specify a metaphorical meaning.

While all scholars agree that ta'wil is necessary, a key difference emerges: Should we specify what the intended meaning is (e.g., 'hand' means power) or leave it to Allah alone? This lesson contrasts the two positions as explained by Imam al-Bajuri.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the area of agreement
Both Salaf and Khalaf agree that the apparent meaning (tashbih) is not intended and that ta'wil (turning from literal sense) is required. The passage says 'this is a point of agreement'.
2
Identify the point of disagreement
The disagreement is in 'ta'yin al-ma'na al-murad' – specifying the intended meaning. The Salaf do not specify; the Khalaf do.
3
Explain the Salaf position (tafwid)
The Salaf say: we negate any likeness to creation (laysa lahu wajh ka-wajhina) and then delegate the true meaning to Allah, saying 'no one knows its meaning except Allah'.
4
Explain the Khalaf position (ta'wil with specification)
The Khalaf also negate likeness but then specify a metaphorical meaning: 'wajh' means Essence (dhat), 'yad' means power (qudra). They base this on linguistic usage and rational necessity.
5
Connect to al-Jawhara's line
Al-Bajuri notes that al-Laqqani's phrase 'awwilihu aw fawwid' encapsulates both methods: 'awwilihu' refers to the Khalaf method, 'fawwid' to the Salaf method.

The Word 'Qalis': From Camel Lips to Spiritual Deficiency
Commentary analyzes the literal and metaphorical meanings of 'qalis' (deficient) and its application to spiritual deficiency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the figurative extensions of 'qalis' from its literal meaning to spiritual deficiency, identifying the type of figurative transfer involved.

Core Idea
A camel's shorter lip becomes a metaphor for spiritual deficiency through careful figurative steps.

This lesson unpacks the word 'qalis' as used in a poem to describe an action that is not deficient. The commentator al-Bajuri reveals the word's origin in camel anatomy and explains three ways it can be used figuratively to mean deficient—especially spiritually. This shows how precise lexical analysis can uncover layers of meaning in Islamic poetry and texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning
Find the original, physical meaning of 'qalis' as described in the passage: it refers to the shorter lip of a camel or similar animal. This is the starting point for all figurative uses.
The source cites al-Mukhtar for this definition.
2
Understand the relation (alāqa) between literal and figurative
The passage mentions two types of relations: generality/specificity (mutlaq/muqayyad) for majāz mursal, and similarity for isti'āra. Note that the figurative meaning 'deficient' is related to the literal 'shorter lip' either by generality (all shortages fall under deficiency) or by resemblance.
Majāz mursal relies on a non-similarity relation; isti'āra relies on similarity.
3
Distinguish the three figurative paths
The passage outlines three scenarios: (1) majāz mursal in one step: from literal lip deficiency to absolute deficiency, then to spiritual deficiency as an instance; (2) majāz mursal in two steps: literal to absolute, then absolute to spiritual; (3) isti'āra: based on similarity, likening spiritual deficiency to a physically deficient lip.
These are different analytical paths, not mutually exclusive interpretations.
4
Apply to the poem's context
Determine which path best fits the poet's intention: to deny deficiency in an action. The context may indicate whether the deficiency is moral (seeking praise) or practical (being neglected). The commentator links the figurative usage to confirming purity of intention or clarifying benefit.
The passage discusses how the poet's words confirm or elucidate the intended meaning.

Core Idea
The transfer from literal to metaphorical meaning can be direct or layered, and commentators refine each step.

This passage not only explains the figurative uses of 'qalis' but includes the commentary of al-Anbabi, who questions whether one type involves 'building a metaphor on a metaphor'. This shows how scholarly commentary deepens understanding of balagha classifications. By analyzing the chain from al-Bajuri to al-Anbabi, we see how each commentator adds nuance.

His concern is about the permissibility of such layered transfers.

Al-Anbabi suggests that the two-step process might actually be a single metaphor after a prior majaz, or that isti'ara could be an alternative. This shows careful analysis of the steps.

Commentary
This passage is an in-depth lexical analysis of the word 'qalis' (deficient). Al-Bajuri explains its origin: the literal meaning is the shorter lip of a camel. Then he describes how it is used figuratively. There are three types: (1) majaz mursal in one step: from the literal lip deficiency to any absolute deficiency, then to spiritual deficiency (since spiritual deficiency is a subset of absolute deficiency). (2) majaz mursal in two steps: first to absolute deficiency, then from absolute to spiritual. (3) isti'ara (metaphor) based on similarity. Al-Anbabi adds that if not restricted, the term would apply to all degrees. He clarifies the possible intended meaning of 'pure for His sake'. Then Al-Bajuri continues: if the intended deficiency is moral (seeking praise), then the poet's line confirms the previous line. If the intended deficiency is lack of benefit (being neglected), then the poet's subsequent line 'and that it be beneficial' clarifies it. Al-Anbabi's notes refine the analysis of the metaphoric steps.

Classification of Verbal Requests: Command, Supplication, and Request
Author defines command, supplication, and request with conditions of superiority and equality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between amr, du'a, and iltimas based on the condition of superiority, and identify the preponderant view regarding the definition of command.

Core Idea
The definition of 'command' depends on whether superiority is required or not — a key dispute in logic and usul al-fiqh.

This passage from al-Sullam's matn delineates three types of verbal requests based on the relative status of requester and addressee. The commentary unpacks the contested term 'isti'la' (superiority) and reveals multiple scholarly views, culminating in the preponderant position that superiority is irrelevant to the linguistic form. Understanding this classification sharpens your ability to analyze commands in Islamic legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of request
Locate in the matn the three terms: amr (command), du'a (supplication), and iltimas (request). Notice how they are defined by the relationship of superiority (isti'la) or equality.
The first line of the matn sets the framework.
2
Analyze 'with superiority' (ma'a isti'la)
The commentary offers two interpretations: (a) displaying superiority (sin and nun for request/display), or (b) actual superiority (sin and nun redundant). These yield three possible views on what is required.
Which interpretation does the author follow? The text shows both possibilities.
3
Extract the four views
From the commentary: (1) both actual and displayed superiority; (2) only displayed; (3) only actual; (4) nothing stipulated. The fourth is described as preponderant (rajih).
The author is said to have followed a weak way by implying conditions.
4
Apply the preponderant view
According to the preponderant view, any verbal request (command) can be called amr regardless of the requester's status. This aligns with usul al-fiqh, where the command form indicates obligation irrespective of the speaker.
Contrast with the view that restricts amr to a superior.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional section that classifies types of verbal requests (talab) based on the relative status of the requester and requested. The matn states that a command (amr) is a request with superiority (isti'la); its opposite, a request with inferiority, is supplication (du'a); and a request between equals is termed iltimas (request). The commentary expands on the meaning of 'with superiority', offering two interpretations: one where the sin and nun in 'استعلا' indicate the act of displaying superiority, and another where they are redundant and 'with superiority' simply means the requester is actually superior. These lead to three possible views: (1) superiority must be both real and displayed, (2) only display of superiority is required even if unreal, (3) only real superiority is required even if not displayed. A fourth view, deemed preponderant, stipulates nothing at all, meaning all three types (amr, du'a, iltimas) are valid without any condition of status. The author (al-Sullam) is criticized for adopting a weak position by implying conditions. The commentary also notes that the same analysis applies to supplication and iltimas. This is a fine-grained logical-linguistic discussion relevant to Islamic jurisprudence (usul al-fiqh) where commands and prohibitions are analyzed.

Why Premises Must End in Necessary Truths
Author explains why premises must terminate in necessary truths to prevent circularity or infinite regress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two unacceptable consequences (dawr and tasalsul) that arise when a chain of premises does not terminate in necessary truths, and explain why they are problematic.

Core Idea
Every valid proof must rest on self-evident truths to avoid circularity or infinite regress.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational principle of demonstrative reasoning: every chain of proof must terminate in self-evident truths. The author argues that without such a foundation, reasoning collapses into either a vicious circle or an infinite regress, both of which make knowledge impossible. Al-Bajuri illustrates this with the classic kalam argument for the temporal origination of the world.

Guided Path
1
State the claim
The passage opens by asserting that premises (in a demonstration) end in necessity (darura). Understand that 'necessity' here means a self-evident truth that requires no further proof.
2
Identify the two consequences
If premises did not end in necessity, either a cycle (dawr) or an infinite regress (tasalsul) would result. Dawr is when premise A depends on B and B depends on A. Tasalsul is an endless chain of dependencies. Both are invalid.
In kalam, these are considered impossible because they undermine the possibility of knowledge.
3
Examine the example
Al-Bajuri provides a concrete example from kalam: the proof that the world is temporally originated. He shows a chain that starts with self-evident truths about attributes and events, and ends with the conclusion. Verify that each step in his chain is either self-evident or derived from self-evident premises.
The example: 'Everyone whose attributes are temporally originated is not free from events... does not precede them... is temporally originated.'
4
Connect to epistemology
Recognize that this principle grounds all demonstrative knowledge. Without necessary truths at the base, no argument can produce certainty. The dismissal of philosophers' objections as 'mere obstinacy' highlights that these foundational truths are undeniable.
The text asserts that objections to such self-evident premises are not valid counterarguments.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological explanation of why a valid proof must terminate in self-evident premises. The text opens by stating that premises in a demonstration end in necessity, meaning that at some point we reach propositions that are self-evident and require no further proof. The reason given is that if we did not stop at such premises, we would fall into either a vicious circle (dawr) or an infinite chain (tasalsul). The commentator, al-Bajuri, provides an example: to prove that the world is temporally originated, one can argue that whatever has changing attributes cannot be free of events, and what is not free of events must have come into being after not existing. Both are impossible because they render knowledge impossible. The lesson thus establishes a foundational principle: every sound demonstration must be grounded in necessary, self-evident truths.

Ash'ari Epistemology: Rational Necessity vs. Divine Habit in the Production of Conclusions
Explaining two Ash'ari views on the connection between reflection and conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the 'aqlan and 'ada views within the Ash'ari school and identify the logical correlation (talazum) between reflection and conclusion as exemplified by a categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Ash'aris agree that Allah creates the conclusion, but differ on whether it follows necessarily by reason or only by habit.

This passage from the Bajuri commentary clarifies a crucial epistemological distinction within the Ash'ari school: how does the conclusion of a syllogism come about after reflection? The author presents two orthodox views – rational correlation and habit – and illustrates the first with a famous syllogistic example. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Ash'ari stance on causality and divine omnipotence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground
Both views affirm that the conclusion is created by Allah, not by the premises themselves. The attribution of the bringing forth is to Allah alone.
2
Extract the first view ('aqlan)
The intellect perceives a necessary coexistence (talazum) between reflection and conclusion – but not causation. The example is given: knowing 'the world is changing' and 'everything changing is originated' necessitates knowing 'the world is originated'.
This correlation is rational (aqlan) but both reflection and conclusion are created by Allah.
3
Extract the second view ('ada)
Allah habitually creates the conclusion after reflection, but can break this habit. Examples: Ibrahim (AS) in the fire and the samand (salamander) being unharmed by fire illustrate that habit is not necessary.
Habit (ada) means Allah's typical but non-obligatory practice.
4
Compare the two views
The 'aqlan view posits a constant conjunction by divine decree, while the 'ada view allows for exceptions. Both avoid Mu'tazilite generated causation and philosophical necessitation.
The commentator notes that the first view is supported by the original poet (النَّاظِم).

Critique of the Simultaneous Cycle Answer
Author critiques the simultaneous cycle answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the simultaneous cycle (al-dawr al-ma'iyy) is not a valid response to the definitional cycle between knowledge and the known.

Core Idea
A simultaneous cycle is not impossible, but the cycle here is prior, not simultaneous.

In this passage, the author rejects two attempted solutions to the problem of circularity in definitions. The claim that the cycle is only simultaneous (ma'iyy) is shown to be invalid because the dependence of a derivative term on its root is necessarily prior in both meaning and wording. The commentator clarifies the concepts of simultaneous cycle and derivative dependency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two answers being critiqued
Locate in the Arabic the phrases 'انفكاك الجهة' (separation of aspect) and 'الدور معي' (the cycle is simultaneous). Understand what each answer claims about the relationship between knowledge and the known.
The first answer tries to avoid circularity by separating the aspect (e.g., conception vs. derivation), the second claims the cycle is simultaneous.
2
Understand why the cycle is prior, not simultaneous
The author gives two reasons: (1) the derivative (mushtaqq) depends on its root (mushtaqq minhu) in both meaning and word, so knowledge of the root must precede. (2) Knowledge of the definer (mu'arraf) is a cause of knowledge of the defined (mu'arraf), implying a causal sequence.
Causal dependence entails priority in knowledge.
3
Distinguish simultaneous cycle from prior cycle
A simultaneous cycle is mutual dependence in existence or mental accompaniment, like substance-accident or fatherhood-sonship. These are not impossible. The cycle in definitions is not like that; it involves a one-way dependence (derivative on root, definer on defined) making it a prior cycle (dawr sabqiyy), which is invalid.
Not all cycles are fallacious; simultaneous cycles are acceptable.
4
Apply the critique to a real definitional example
Take the definition of 'mujtahid' (one who exerts effort to derive rulings). If someone defined 'ijtihad' using 'mujtahid', that would create a prior cycle: you need to know 'ijtihad' to know 'mujtahid', but if the definition of 'ijtihad' requires 'mujtahid', the cycle is fallacious. This example illustrates the author's point.
This is a common pitfall in usul al-fiqh definitions.

Conformity (mutabaqa) means the term indicates its full meaning; implication (tadammun) indicates a part within the whole; committance (iltizam) indicates something external but associated.
2
Apply to the general term: why conformity fails
The general term intends all individuals at once, not each alone, so it cannot indicate a single individual by conformity.
3
Why implication (as initially defined) fails
Implication requires the term to be a whole containing parts, but it was claimed that 'amm is a universality (kulliya), not a whole (kull), so the initial objection stands.
This sets up the later resolution.
4
Why committance fails
The individual is not external to the meaning; if one were excluded, all would be excluded, leaving no meaning.
5
Examine al-Subki's argument and its flaw
Al-Subki argued the general proposition is equivalent to many propositions, so it indicates by conformity. The flaw: the general term indicates all at once, not each separately; its indication is implicit, not explicit like the individual propositions.

It is not by conformity because the general term is not intended for the individual alone, but all individuals at once. Nor by implication because implication is understanding the part within the whole, and the general term is a universality not a whole, as established in usul. Nor by committance because the individual is not outside the meaning of the general term; if a single individual were outside, all individuals would be outside, due to the equality of their relation to the general term, and it would remain without meaning. Al-Subki and those who followed him answered that it is by conformity because the proposition of the general term is in the strength of propositions equal to the number of its individuals. He said: (in the strength of propositions equal to the number of its individuals) its explanation: the proposition of the general term, e.g., 'Kill the polytheists', is like saying 'Kill Zayd the polytheist', 'Amr the polytheist', 'Bakr the polytheist', and so on according to the number of individuals of the general term, and each of these propositions indicates its meaning by conformity. The truth according to the investigators is that it is by implication, necessarily because the general term encompasses all individuals, and the single individual is a part of the whole decisively, so it is understood within the whole decisively. And their statement 'the general term is a universality' is interpreted: i.e., its meaning in terms of the judgment upon it is a universality, or its meaning together with what it is attributed to is a universal proposition. Rather the general term in itself is a whole, and the universality is the proposition that includes it, and there is no contradiction between it being a whole and its proposition being universal, since there is no preventer. He said: (that it is implication) i.e., the saying of al-Qarafi that implication is understanding the part within the whole, we say: that is here, for the general term in terms of its meaning is a whole and its individuals under it are its parts. He said: (i.e., its meaning in terms of the judgment upon it) means because the judgment is directed to each individual, so the proposition is universal. He said: (or its meaning together with what it is attributed to) means because the judgment is attributed to each individual of those individuals, so the proposition then is universal. He said: (and the general term in itself is a whole) i.e., because it encompasses all individuals at once, and each individual is a part of it, so it is a singular not a proposition nor a compound at all; and if its being a proposition is negated and it is established that it is singular, how can it be a universality? And it occurs in the expressions of many usul scholars that the general term is a whole, and in al-Mahalli: 'The name of the general term is one, and it is all the individuals.' So then the indication of the general term upon all its individuals is by conformity, and upon some of them by implication, as is the truth.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the types of indication (dalala) for the general term ('amm). The author argues that the general term, such as 'al-mushrikun' in 'Uqtulu al-mushrikin', does not indicate each individual by conformity (mutabaqa) because the general term means all individuals at once, not each separately. Nor does it indicate by implication (tadammun) according to the initial claim because implication is understanding a part within the whole, and the general term is a universality (kulliya), not a whole (kull). Nor by committance (iltizam) because the individual is part of the meaning; if one individual were excluded, all would be excluded due to equal relation. Al-Subki responds that it is by conformity because the general proposition is equivalent to many propositions each indicating its individual. The author refutes this: the general term's indication is not like that of each individual proposition because it indicates all at once. The correct view is that it is by implication because the general term is a whole (kull) comprising individuals as parts, and each individual is understood within that whole. The author clarifies that 'general term is a universality' means the judgment upon it is universal, but the term itself is a whole (kull), not a proposition. Thus, the indication on all individuals is by conformity, and on a single individual by implication.

Why 'Min' is a Particle: The Criterion of Meaning Independence
Author explains why 'min' is a particle, not a noun, due to its particular meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a word is a particle or a noun based on the criteria of meaning independence, particularity, and predicability.

Core Idea
A particle indicates a particular meaning subsidiary to another; a noun indicates an independent universal.

In Arabic grammar and logic, distinguishing between nouns and particles is not just a matter of morphology but of meaning. This lesson analyzes the particle 'min' to show that its meaning—a specific initiation—is not the main focus but a relation, making it a particle. Understanding this criterion helps you classify words correctly and avoid confusion when analyzing Arabic texts, especially in Qur'anic exegesis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its alleged meaning
The passage uses 'min' as an example. Its meaning is said to be 'a specific beginning' (ibtida' mu'ayyan). Note that this meaning is not absolute but specific to a particular instance.
2
Check if the meaning is independent or subsidiary
The passage states that the specific beginning indicated by 'min' is 'not intended for itself' and is 'subsidiary' to the travel and the house. This shows the meaning is dependent on other elements.
3
Test predicability
The passage says it is not valid to predicate the particle or predicate on it. In Arabic, nouns can be subjects (mubtada') or predicates (khabar), but particles cannot. If a word cannot function as a subject or predicate, it is a particle.
4
Determine particular vs universal
The specific beginning is particular (juz'i) because it refers to a concrete instance, while the absolute beginning (al-ibtida' al-mutlaq) is universal (kulli). Nouns typically indicate universals; particles indicate particulars used for relations.

The derivative metaphor from: that it [the particle] is placed a general placing for every specific intended meaning for other than itself. Commentary
This passage defines the nature of particles (huruf) in contrast to nouns (asma'), focusing on the example of the particle 'min'. The author explains that particles are placed as general tools for specific meanings that are intended only as subsidiary to other things. For instance, 'min' indicates a specific initiation (ibtida'), but this initiation is not the main focus; rather, it is merely a relation between the travel and the house. Because the meaning of a particle is inherently dependent and not independent, it cannot be used as a subject or predicate in a sentence. The specific initiation is a particular (juz'i) meaning because it cannot apply to many, whereas the absolute initiation (al-ibtida' al-mutlaq) is a universal (kulli) and thus a noun. This distinction shows why 'min' is a particle and not a noun.

Are Pronouns Particular or Universal in Wad'?
Author presents two views on particularity of definites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two positions on whether definite expressions are particular or universal in wad', and identify the arguments used by each side.

Core Idea
A word's wad' is not its use; pronouns like 'ana' are universal in wad' but become particular in context.

This passage presents a key debate in Arabic linguistics and logic: Are pronouns, demonstratives, and relative pronouns originally placed for particular meanings (like proper nouns) or universal meanings? The majority of investigators argue they are universal in wad', while most grammarians argue they are particular. Understanding this distinction helps clarify how language can refer to individuals without being tied to them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing positions
The first position (majority of investigators) holds that pronouns and other definites are placed universally and become particular through context. The second position (majority of grammarians) holds that they are placed for particulars.
2
Understand the investigators' argument
The investigators argue that if 'ana' were placed for a specific person, it would be metaphorical for others; if placed for each speaker individually, it would require infinite homonymy. Therefore, it must be placed for the universal concept 'speaker'.
They avoid both metaphor and excessive homonymy.
3
Understand the grammarians' counter-argument
The grammarians argue that if these words were universal in wad', they would never be used for their original meaning (since they are always used for specifics), making them metaphors without a real usage, which is problematic.
The commentator sides with the grammarians.
4
Recognize the broader implication
This debate impacts how we understand reference in divine speech (e.g., Qur'an) and the relationship between wad' and isti'mal.

Classifying Utterances by Meaning: The Nine Types
Author classifies complete and incomplete utterances into nine types based on meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any complete utterrance into one of nine types based on whether it indicates a demand and whether it admits truth or falsehood.

Core Idea
All complete utterances are either demand-based or not, and either truth-apt or not — giving nine logical types.

The passage presents a systematic taxonomy of utterances (alfaz) based on their semantic function. Understanding this classification is essential for distinguishing informative statements from performative and demand-based speech, which is foundational in usul al-fiqh and logic. We will walk through the branching criteria step by step, identifying each of the nine types and their defining characteristics.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish complete from incomplete utterances
First, note that the author divides utterances into 'complete' (tāmm) and 'other' (ghayr tāmm). A complete utterance can stand alone as a sentence conveying a message. Incomplete ones (e.g., single words) are excluded from this classification.
In Arabic grammar, 'tāmm' often refers to a complete sentence with subject and predicate.
2
Identify the first major division: does it indicate a demand?
Among complete utterances, the author asks: by convention (bi al-waḍʿ), does it indicate a demand (ṭalab)? This splits all complete utterances into two main branches: those that do and those that do not.
Demand here is broad: it includes commands, prohibitions, requests, etc.
3
Explore the five types of demand-bearing utterances
If the utterance indicates a demand, it falls into one of five subcategories based on what is demanded and the relationship between speaker and addressee: istifhām (interrogation), amr (command), nahy (prohibition), duʿāʾ (supplication), and iltimās (request). The first three involve superiority or neutrality; duʿāʾ and iltimās involve equality or humility.
The author defines iltimās as 'demand stripped of the two conditions' (superiority and humility), i.e., pure demand.
4
Analyze the non-demand branch: truth-aptitude
For utterances that do not indicate a demand, the author subdivides based on whether they admit truth (ṣidq) or falsehood (kidhb). If they do not admit truth/falsehood, they are called tanbīh (exclamation) or inshāʾ (performative/constructive) — two terms for the same category. If they do admit truth/falsehood, they are khabar (informative statement).
Inshāʾ means 'bringing into existence'—these utterances effect a change in reality upon utterance, like contracts or oaths.
5
Verify the total of nine types and note the inclusion of verbs of approximation
Count the types: 5 demand types + 2 non-demand types that don't admit truth (tanbīh/inshāʾ counted as one) + 1 non-demand type that admits truth (khabar) = 9 distinct categories. The author clarifies that verbs of approximation (afʿāl al-muqāraba, e.g., kāda 'almost') are included in inshāʾ because their meaning is nearness of occurrence, not informing of nearness.
This inclusion is based on the authority of al-Saʿd and al-Rāḍī.

Distinguishing the Whole (al-Kull) from Universality (al-Kulliyya)
Author defines the whole as a judgment on a collective without independence, contrasting with universality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a collective judgment (kull) and a distributive judgment (kulliyya) using the criterion of independence (istiqlāl).

Core Idea
The whole is collective; universality is distributive—the key is independence.

In logic, the terms 'kull' and 'kulliyya' sound similar but refer to fundamentally different types of judgments. This passage from al-Bajuri's commentary clarifies the distinction by focusing on whether each individual independently carries the judgment or only the collective does. Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly interpreting propositions in Islamic legal theory and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'kull'
Locate the author's definition: 'the whole is a judgment on the collective insofar as it is a collective, i.e., without independence of any one concerning it apart from another.' Note the key condition: no individual independence.
2
Examine the first example (Quranic verse)
The verse 'And eight will bear the throne of your Lord' illustrates kull: the eight angels collectively bear the throne; no single angel bears it alone. This shows lack of independence.
3
Examine the second example (Banu Tamim)
The statement 'All of Banu Tamim carry the great rock' is also kull because the carrying is cooperative and not each individual independently. Even if some are absent, the collective part carries it together.
This type is also called 'the collective part' (al-ba'd al-majmu'i).
4
Contrast with 'kulliyya' (universality)
The author contrasts: 'With independence, like 'The ten are affirmed,' then it is universality, not a whole.' Here each of the ten is independently affirmed. Hence kulliyya applies when each individual bears the judgment alone.
5
Apply the criterion of independence
To decide whether a proposition is kull or kulliyya, ask: does each individual independently bear the predicate? If yes, it's kulliyya; if only the collective does, it's kull. Test this on new examples from fiqh or kalam.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'kull' (the whole) as distinct from 'kulliyya' (universality). The author begins by noting that the poet (al-nazim) previously mentioned kulli (universal) and juz'i (particular), but now he discusses these similar-sounding terms to avoid confusion. The definition of kull is given as: a judgment (hukm) upon a collective (majmu) considered as a whole, where no single individual carries the judgment independently. Two examples illustrate: First, the Quranic verse that eight angels will bear the throne — each angel alone does not bear it, but together they do. Second, the example 'All of Banu Tamim carry the great rock' — the carrying is not universal to every individual (some may be absent), and even those present cooperate, so no one carries independently. This second type is called 'the collective part' (al-ba'd al-majmu'i). The author then clarifies that a judgment on a number (e.g., 'ten') is kull only when independence is absent, as in the verse. If independence is present, as in 'The ten are affirmed' (meaning each one individually is affirmed), then it is kulliyya (universality), not kull. This distinction is crucial for understanding collective vs. distributive judgments in logic and fiqh, e.g., zakat obligations or collective punishments.

Key Terms
كل (kull): a judgment on a collective considered as a whole, without any part independently bearing the judgment.
كلية (kulliyya): a judgment that applies to each individual independently (distributive universality).
استقلال (istiqlāl): the independence of an individual in bearing the judgment without reliance on others.

The Four Views on How Premises Entail Conclusion
Author presents four views on the implication from premises to conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four positions on how premises entail conclusion and explain the Ash'ari view of direct divine creation.

Core Idea
The link between premises and conclusion is not natural—it is directly created by Allah.

How do we move from premises to a conclusion in a valid argument? Is it a rational necessity, a habit, or something else? This passage presents four competing views on the nature of that implication, with the Ash'ari position claiming that Allah directly creates the knowledge of the conclusion after the reasoning act. Understanding this debate clarifies the metaphysical foundations of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four positions
The author lists four possible ways the premises indicate the conclusion: rational (عقلي), customary (عادي), generative (تولدي), and obligatory (واجب). Note that the author says the first is supported.
2
Understand the three underlying schools
Though four positions are listed, the commentator explains they reduce to three schools. The first school (Ahl al-Haq) holds that Allah creates knowledge directly; the other two schools likely involve secondary causation or habitual conjunction.
The passage focuses on the first school.
3
Analyze the first school's sub-views
Within Ahl al-Haq, there are two views: one (Imam al-Haramayn) says the implication is rational, like the inseparability of accident from substance; the other view is not fully stated but implied to differ.
The rational implication means God cannot create premises without creating the conclusion, like an accident cannot exist without a substance.
4
Connect to Islamic epistemology
This debate is not about whether logic works, but about the metaphysical cause of the knowledge gained. For Ash'aris, no secondary causation exists; all effects are direct divine acts, including the knowledge from reasoning.
This upholds tawhid by denying any natural necessity besides God's will.

Commentary
This passage discusses a key epistemological question in Islamic logic: what is the nature of the connection between premises and conclusion? The author lists four positions: the implication is rational (عقلي), customary (عادي), generative (تولدي), or obligatory (واجب). He says the first is supported. The commentator explains that this refers to the disagreement on how reasoning (nazar) yields knowledge of the conclusion. Although four positions are listed, they reduce to three schools. The first and primary school is that of Ahl al-Haq (the People of Truth, i.e., Ash'aris): God directly creates knowledge of the conclusion after the reasoning act, without any secondary causation. Then within this school there are two sub-views. The first sub-view, attributed to Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni) and held by most Ash'aris according to al-Ghazali, says that the implication (luzum) is rational, like the inseparable relation between an accident and its substance; God cannot create one without the other. The passage ends mid-sentence, but the point is that the link is necessary, not merely customary.
