Understanding the Role of Bibliographies in Islamic Scholarship
Author lists the sources used in the work, beginning with the Quran and tafsir books.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major tafsir and hadith sources listed in a classical bibliography and evaluate their significance for determining the scholarly authority of a work.

Core Idea
A scholarly bibliography is not a mere list—it is a chain of authority that grounds every argument.

In Islamic logic and other disciplines, the strength of an argument often depends on the sources from which it draws its evidence. This bibliography from al-Bajuri's commentary on al-Bannani's work reveals the foundational texts—Quran, tafsir, hadith—that underpin the logical discussions. By learning to read such bibliographies critically, you can assess the scholarly weight of a work and trace its intellectual lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the bibliography
Notice that it begins with the Quran, then tafsir, then ulum al-Quran, then hadith collections. What does this order tell you about the author's priorities?
The order reflects a hierarchy of authority: the Quran first, then its interpretation, then the prophetic traditions.
2
Examine the specific editions cited
Each source includes an editor, publisher, and edition year. Why would a scholar care about which edition is used? How does this affect scholarly reliability?
Editions can differ in critical apparatus, marginal notes, and corrections. Knowing the editor helps assess the text's accuracy.
3
Map the sources to the author's arguments
In the main text of the commentary, the author likely refers to these sources. Think of how familiarity with these specific tafsir works (Baydawi, Zamakhshari, Baghawi) would help a reader understand the author's exegetical choices.
Each tafsir has a particular theological orientation (e.g., al-Zamakhshari is Mu'tazili), which influences how verses are interpreted.
4
Evaluate the completeness of the list
What major tafsir or hadith works are missing? Does this absence reflect a particular methodological stance?
A selective bibliography may indicate the author's preferred tradition or avoidance of certain schools.

Critical Editions (Tahqiq) in Islamic Scholarship: Reading a Reference Entry
A list of references and sources cited in the text, covering Quran, Tafsir, Hadith, Fiqh, and Aqeedah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the components of a critical edition entry (author, editor, publisher, edition) and assess its authority.

Core Idea
The editor (muhaqqiq) and edition are as important as the author's name for the reliability of a source.

In the bibliography, you see abbreviations like 'تحق' (tahq) for editor and 'ط:' for edition. These details tell you who prepared the text and when. A respected editor and recent edition often mean a more reliable text.

Guided Path
1
Locate a full entry
Find an entry that includes the editor (تحق) and edition (ط:). For example, 'أنوار التنزيل' by al-Baydawi.
2
Parse the editor's name
The editor is 'الشيخ محيي الدين الأصفر'. Note his scholarly title and evaluate his reputation.
3
Check the publisher and location
Publisher: 'دار المعرفة', location: 'بيروت'. This indicates a reputable academic publisher.
4
Note the edition number and year
'ط: ١' (first edition), '٢٠١٣م'. Newer editions may incorporate more corrections.
5
Assess reliability
Consider that a first edition from a known publisher edited by a respected scholar is likely reliable.

The Role of Bibliographies in Islamic Scholarship
Author lists sources and references used in his commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key categories of sources used by a classical commentator and explain why bibliographies are essential for scholarly verification.

Core Idea
A scholar's bibliography is a chain of intellectual accountability.

When we encounter a list of books at the end of a commentary, it is easy to skip over it. But in the Islamic tradition, such bibliographies are vital tools. They tell us which authorities the author relied on, what editions he used, and how his work connects to the broader scholarly conversation. This lesson will help you read a bibliography as a map of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Scan the categories
Notice that the author groups sources under headings like Hadith, Fiqh, 'Aqida, and Logic. This shows the breadth of fields he consulted.
Categories are clues to the interdisciplinary nature of the commentary.
2
Identify key authors
Look for well-known scholars like al-Ghazali, al-Sanusi, al-Taftazani, and Ibn Hajar. Their presence indicates the author's intellectual lineage.
Recognizing these names helps assess the scholarly weight of the bibliography.
3
Note publication details
The author provides edition numbers, dates, and publishers. This precision allows readers to locate exact copies and verify references.
Editions matter because later printings may have corrections or different pagination.
4
Consider the function
Ask why this list exists. It allows readers to trace sources, assess the author's methodology, and continue research. It is a form of scholarly transparency.
Without a bibliography, claims about sources become unverifiable.

3
Distinguish between primary texts and commentaries
Works like al-Sullam al-Murawnaq and al-Risala al-Shamsiyya are primary texts in logic; their commentaries (sharh), glosses (hashiya), etc. are secondary. Note the hierarchy.
4
Understand key terms: شرح (sharh), حاشية (hashiya), تحقيق (tahqeeq), مخطوط (makhṭūṭ)
These terms indicate the nature and scholarly status of the work: sharh is a commentary, hashiya is a gloss, tahqeeq is critical editing, makhṭūṭ is a manuscript.
