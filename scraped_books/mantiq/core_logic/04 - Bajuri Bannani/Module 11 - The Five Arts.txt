The Five Arts of Argument and Demonstration
Author classifies arguments into five arts: rhetoric, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, sophistry; defines demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five arts of argument and identify demonstration by its reliance on certain premises.

Core Idea
Demonstration yields certainty because its premises are drawn from six types of certain knowledge.

In this passage, the author introduces a fundamental classification of arguments into five arts: rhetoric, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, and sophistry. Among these, demonstration holds the highest rank because it alone produces certain knowledge. Understanding this classification is essential for distinguishing the logical status of different types of reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five arts
Locate the five names in the Arabic text: خطابة, شعر, برهان, جدل, سفسطة. Write them down in order.
The order is given by the rhyme: khataba, shi'r, burhan, jadal, safsata.
2
Recognize the unique status of demonstration
The text says 'أجلها البرهان' (the noblest of them is demonstration). Why? Because it is composed of certain premises (مقدمات باليقين تقترن). This is the defining feature that sets it apart.
Certainty is the key: demonstration does not rely on mere opinion or imaginative ideas.
3
Enumerate the six types of certain premises
The text lists: أوليات (primary axioms), مشاهدات (observations), مجربات (experiences), متواترات (recurrent reports), حدسيات (intuitive judgments), محسوسات (sensory perceptions). These are the sources of certainty for demonstration.
Memorize these six; they are the 'yaqiniyyat' (certainties).
4
Note the debate on implication
The text mentions a future disagreement about how premises imply the conclusion: rational, habitual, generative, or necessary. The author supports rational implication.
This is a preview; the details will come later. For now, just be aware of the four views.

Commentary
This passage classifies all arguments into five arts (sana'at khams): oratory (khataba), poetry (shi'r), demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), and sophistry (safsata). Demonstration is the most noble because it yields certainty. Its premises are certain propositions of various types: primary axioms (awwaliyyat), observational propositions (mushahadat), empirical generalizations (mujarrabat), mutawatir (recurrent reports), intuitive (hadasiyyat), and sensory (mahsusat). These are collectively called yaqiniyyat (certainties). The text then hints at a disagreement about how premises imply the conclusion: whether the implication is 'rational' ('aqli), 'habitual' ('adi), 'generative' (tawallud), or 'necessary' (wajib). The author indicates that the first, i.e., rational implication, is the supported view.

Interpreting 'Removing Veils': Cause-Effect or Effect-Cause?
Debate between commentators on whether the phrasing is cause-effect or effect-cause, with preference for the former.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the theological implications of two grammatical constructions in Islamic logic and justify the preference for one over the other.

Core Idea
The grammar of causation in logic reflects deep theological commitments about divine action.

In this passage, commentators debate whether the phrase 'removing veils' should be read as cause-then-effect or effect-then-cause. This is not merely grammatical but touches on whether God's actions can be said to have motivating causes. We'll follow the argument and see why the author prefers one reading.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
The first interpretation is 'conjunction of cause upon effect' (عطف السبب على المسبب), where the cause (removing veils) is mentioned first, then the effect (knowledge emerges). The second is 'conjunction of effect upon cause' (عطف المعلول على العلة), where the effect (knowledge) is mentioned first, then the cause.
2
Find the objection
A commentator (al-Sabban) objects that the apparent meaning is that the effect (or final cause) is the emergence of results (خروج النتائج), not the divine act of bringing them forth (إخراج الله). This challenges the second interpretation.
3
Understand the response
The original commentator (al-Malwi) allows that it can be understood as an effect by considering its result – i.e., the emergence of results is the effect of the act of removing.
4
Analyze the author's verdict
The author (Bajuri) prefers the first interpretation because the second could imply a motivating cause for God's actions, which is theologically problematic. God's actions are not caused by external ends, though they contain wisdom.
5
Reflect on the theological principle
This exchange shows how logical analysis is inseparable from theological commitments. The preference for cause-effect order avoids attributing a motivating cause to God, preserving divine transcendence.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical exchange regarding the interpretation of a line from the Sullam al-Munawraq: 'He removed from them every veil of ignorance from the heaven of intellect.' The initial commentary (from al-Malwi) suggested this is a case of 'conjunction of cause upon effect' (عطف السبب على المسبب) or 'conjunction of effect upon cause' (عطف المعلول على العلة). Someone (identified as al-Sabban) objects: the apparent meaning is that the 'effect' (or 'final cause') is the emergence of results (خروج النتائج), not the divine act of bringing them forth (إخراج الله تعالى إياها). The objector argues that the phrase should be understood as the effect being the results themselves, not God's action. The responder (likely al-Malwi or the commentator) allows that it can be interpreted as effect or final cause by considering its impact, which is the emergence. Because the second interpretation would imply that God's actions (like bringing forth results) are caused or have a final cause in the sense of a motivating cause. But Islamic theology holds that God's actions are not caused by any external purpose (though they contain wisdom). Therefore, avoiding any implication of causation in God's actions, the first interpretation is more sound. Note: The distinction is between a 'motivating cause' (علة باعثة) and a 'final cause' (علة غائية). The objection is about the latter, but the author still prefers the former interpretation to avoid any misconception about God being motivated by an end.

The Division of Hujja: Transmitted and Rational
Author divides hujja and defines transmitted type.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a transmitted argument (naqliyya) from a rational argument (aqliyya) and identify the sources that make a premise transmitted.

Core Idea
Every argument is either based on revelation or reason; knowing the source determines its strength.

In this lesson, we examine the primary division of hujja: that which is based on transmitted sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma') and that which relies solely on rational premises. The commentator clarifies that even rational arguments may include transmitted material, but their classification is based on the nature of their inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main divisions
Find where the author states that hujja divides into naqliyya and aqliyya. Note that the overall count becomes six: one transmitted, five rational.
The commentator al-Bajuri emphasizes the total is six.
2
Define transmitted hujja
Extract the definition: a transmitted hujja is one where both premises or at least one come from the Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma', explicitly or by derivation.
Even if the intellect perceives them, the source is transmission.
3
Understand the objection and reply
Read the objection that burhan (demonstration) is listed as rational but may use transmitted premises. Note the reply: classification as rational does not forbid using transmitted premises; logicians restrict burhan to two rational premises because they focus on rational matters.
This shows that the categories are not exclusive in practice.
4
Apply the definition to a real argument
Take a simple qiyas in fiqh: 'Wine is forbidden because it is an intoxicant; nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' The major premise 'every intoxicant is forbidden' is from hadith (transmitted). So this is a transmitted hujja if the minor is also transmitted or rational? Both premises could be transmitted.
This exercise reinforces the definition.

The Five Rational Arts: Focusing on Khataba
Author lists five rational arts and defines khataba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five rational arts and explain the defining feature of khataba (oratory) as composed of accepted premises.

Core Idea
Rational arguments are not monolithic; they are divided into five arts based on the type of premises.

The author briefly lists the five rational arts: oratory, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, and sophistry. The commentary begins to define the first, khataba, as being composed of 'accepted premises.' The glossator explains what accepted premises are, linking them to the credibility of the speaker.

Guided Path
1
List the five rational arts
From the Arabic, identify the five names: khitaba, shi'r, burhan, jadal, safsata. Note that they are all subdivisions of rational hujja.
The text says 'five clear' – these are well-known in logic.
2
Define khataba (oratory)
Read the definition: khataba is composed of 'accepted premises' (muqaddimat maqbula). Understand that these are premises that people accept due to the perceived trustworthiness of the speaker, not due to logical proof.
The glossator adds that accepted premises come from a speaker whom people hold in high regard.
3
Distinguish from other arts
Compare with shi'r (poetry) which uses imaginative premises, burhan (demonstration) using certain premises, jadal (dialectic) using generally accepted premises, and safsata (sophistry) using deceptive premises.
The passage does not define the others in this section, but the distinction is implied.
4
Apply to a real example
Consider a scholar like Imam al-Shafi'i giving a ruling. People accept his statement because of his trustworthiness, even without evidence – this would be khataba. But if he provides a qiyas with a clear 'illah, that becomes burhan or jadal.
This helps recognize khataba in scholarly discourse.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of argument (hujja). The author first states the overall division: hujja is either naqliyya (transmitted) or aqliyya (rational). The commentator al-Bajuri explains that the total number of types is six: one transmitted and five rational. He clarifies that 'hujja' is so called because it enables one to defeat (hajja) one's opponent. He then defines the transmitted hujja as one where both premises or one of them come from the Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma', either explicitly or derived. A potential objection is raised: demonstration (burhan) is listed under rational arguments, but it can include transmitted premises. The answer is that being classified as rational does not preclude using transmitted premises; moreover, logicians typically restrict burhan to premises known by reason alone, since their focus is rational matters. Finally, the commentator begins explaining the five rational types: oratory (khataba) is defined as composed of accepted premises (muqaddimat maqbula). The glossator al-Anbabi adds that 'accepted premises' are those uttered by a speaker whom people hold in high regard, either due to a divine gift or general trust, so that their statements are accepted as true even without evidence.

The Definition and Purpose of Poetry in Logic
Defining poetry by its psychological effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the definition of poetry (shi'r) and explain its purpose of emotional influence regardless of truth.

Core Idea
Poetry in logic is defined by its effect on the soul, not by meter or truth.

The passage begins by enumerating the five logical arts, then turns to poetry. The author defines poetry as compositions of premises that cause the soul to expand (pleasure) or contract (disgust). The examples show how premises are structured to encourage or discourage, using syllogistic form but with imaginative content. This lesson helps you understand how Islamic logicians classify poetry as a distinct art based on its purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'shi'r' (poetry). Note that it is defined here within the context of logic, not literature.
2

The genus is 'what is composed of premises' (mā rukkiba min muqaddimāt). This places poetry in the category of syllogistic compositions.
3

The differentia is 'that cause the soul to expand or contract' (tanbasitu minhā al-nafsu aw tanqabiḍu). This distinguishes poetry from other logical arts.
4
Analyze the examples
The example of wine and honey shows how premises are chosen for their emotional effect. The conclusion 'this is a flowing ruby' is not true but aims to expand the soul.
5
Understand the purpose
Poetry is named for its purpose: to encourage or discourage, as poets do. Truth is irrelevant.

Commentary
This passage defines four of the five logical arts (omitting oratory) as part of the commentary on the Sullam al-Murawnaq. The author starts by quoting the versified enumeration: oratory, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, and sophistry. Then he explains each term. For poetry (shi'r), he gives the definition: it is composed of premises that cause the soul to expand (pleasure) or contract (disgust). He provides two examples: one encouraging wine and one discouraging honey. The example uses a syllogistic form but the premises are imaginative, not necessarily true. The name 'poetry' comes from its purpose, which is to affect emotions. Next, demonstration (burhan) is defined as composed of certain premises, with an example using 'Zayd is a human, every human is an animal, therefore Zayd is an animal.' The term 'burhan' is derived from 'al-barah' meaning cutting, because it cuts off the opponent's argument. Then dialectic (jadal) is defined as composed of well-known premises (commonly accepted) or conceded premises (accepted by the opponent). The author gives three sub-examples: premises well-known among all people (injustice is ugly), premises well-known in a particular context (beneficence adorns), and premises conceded by a specific opponent (Zayd's statement is from a just person). The name 'jadal' comes from its use in disputation; it can be good or even obligatory when defending the truth against misguidance. Finally, sophistry (safsata) is originally 'embellished wisdom' and is defined as composed of false imaginary premises, or premises that resemble truth without being true, or that resemble well-known premises without being well-known. This art is used to deceive.

Demonstration: The Art of Certain Premises
Defining demonstration by certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristic of demonstration (burhan) as a syllogism from certain premises and explain why it cuts off the opponent.

Core Idea
Demonstration yields certain knowledge because its premises are certain.

The passage defines demonstration as a syllogism composed of certain premises. The example 'Zayd is a human, every human is an animal, therefore Zayd is an animal' shows a simple categorical syllogism. The name 'burhan' is derived from 'al-barah' meaning cutting, because it decisively ends dispute. This lesson helps you understand the foundational role of demonstration in Islamic sciences.

1
Identify the definiendum
The term is 'burhan' (demonstration).
2
Extract the genus
The genus is 'what is composed of premises' (mā rukkiba min muqaddimāt).
3
Extract the differentia
The differentia is 'certain premises' (muqaddimāt yaqīniyya). Certainty here means the premises are known to be true beyond doubt.
4
Analyze the example
The example uses a universal affirmative (every human is an animal) and a particular affirmative (Zayd is a human) to conclude correctly. The premises are certain.
5
Understand the name
The term is from 'al-barah' meaning cutting; it cuts off any further argument.

Dialectic: The Art of Well-Known and Conceded Premises
Defining dialectic by its premises and ethical obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish dialectic (jadal) from demonstration by its use of well-known or conceded premises and recognize when it becomes obligatory.

Core Idea
Dialectic uses premises accepted by the opponent or the public, not necessarily certain.

The passage defines dialectic as a syllogism from well-known (mashhura) or conceded (musallama) premises. Examples show premises like 'injustice is ugly' (common knowledge) or 'Zayd's statement is from a just person' (conceded by opponent). The art is named from 'jadal' (disputation), and it can be obligatory when defending against misguidance.

1

The term is 'jadal' (dialectic).
2

Again, 'composed of premises'.
3

Premises are well-known (mashhura) among people or conceded (musallama) by the opponent.
4

First: universal well-known (injustice is ugly). Second: well-known in context (beneficence adorns). Third: conceded by opponent (Zayd's statement is from a just person).
5

Dialectic can be good or obligatory if the objective is truth and the opponent is misguiding.

Sophistry: The Deceptive Art of False Premises
Defining sophistry by its deceptive premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sophistry (safsata) by its use of false, deceptive premises that resemble truth or well-known premises.

Core Idea
Sophistry is a deceptive syllogism that mimics truth but rests on false or imaginary premises.

The passage defines sophistry as originally 'embellished wisdom.' It is composed of false imaginary premises, or premises that resemble truth but are not, or premises resembling well-known ones but not. This art is used to deceive. The name 'safsata' is derived from the Greek but adapted. This lesson helps you recognize and avoid deceptive arguments in Islamic discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term is 'safsata' (sophistry).
2
Extract the genus
Again, 'composed of premises'.
3
Extract the differentia
Premises are wahmiyya (imaginary/false), or shabiha bil-haqq (resembling truth but not), or shabiha bil-mashhura (resembling well-known but not).
4
Understand the etymology
Originally 'embellished wisdom' (al-hikma al-mumawwaha), implying it appears wise but is false.
5
Recognize its danger
It deceives by appearing like demonstration or dialectic.

Commentary
This passage defines four of the five logical arts (omitting oratory) as part of the commentary on the Sullam al-Murawnaq. The author starts by quoting the versified enumeration: oratory, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, and sophistry. Then he explains each term. For poetry (shi'r), he gives the definition: it is composed of premises that cause the soul to expand (pleasure) or contract (disgust). He provides two examples: one encouraging wine and one discouraging honey. The example uses a syllogistic form but the premises are imaginative, not necessarily true. The name 'poetry' comes from its purpose, which is to affect emotions. Next, demonstration (burhan) is defined as composed of certain premises, with an example using 'Zayd is a human, every human is an animal, therefore Zayd is an animal.' The term 'burhan' is derived from 'al-barah' meaning cutting, because it cuts off the opponent's argument. Then dialectic (jadal) is defined as composed of well-known premises (commonly accepted) or conceded premises (accepted by the opponent). The author gives three sub-examples: premises well-known among all people (injustice is ugly), premises well-known in a particular context (beneficence adorns), and premises conceded by a specific opponent (Zayd's statement is from a just person). The name 'jadal' comes from its use in disputation; it can be good or even obligatory when defending the truth against misguidance. Finally, sophistry (safsata) is originally 'embellished wisdom' and is defined as composed of false imaginary premises, or premises that resemble truth without being true, or that resemble well-known premises without being well-known. This art is used to deceive.

Commentary
This passage defines the art of dialectic (جدل), which is the third of the five logical arts. The author states that dialectic is composed of premises that are either mashhura (generally accepted) or musallama (conceded by the opponent). He then gives three examples: the first is a mashhura premise (oppression is ugly, and everything ugly is disgraceful), the second also seems mashhura (kindness is good, everything good is beautifying), and the third is musallama (Zayd's statement is from a just person, every such statement is acted upon). The author then explains that dialectic is called such because it occurs in debate (jadal), and it is commendable if used for a good purpose, and may even become obligatory to refute those who mislead in religious beliefs. He then provides detailed definitions of mashhura and musallama from the commentators: mashhura are propositions known to all people, whether certain or not, due to general benefit or human nature; musallama are propositions conceded by the two disputants for the sake of argument, regardless of truth. The marginal note clarifies that mashhura implies musallama, but not vice versa, establishing a general-specific relationship. This lesson is essential for understanding the foundation of dialectical argumentation in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
The five arts are defined by the certainty of their premises: certain (burhan), accepted (jadal), persuasive (khitaba), imaginative (shi'r), or false (safsata).

In this passage, al-Bajuri lists the five arts of logic and provides three syllogisms illustrating the first three. By understanding the epistemic quality of the premises—whether they are certain, merely accepted, or false—you can place any argument within this framework. The Epistemic Ranking of the Five Arts and the Definition of Demonstration
Author ranks the five arts by certainty and defines burhan as composed of certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to rank the five arts (burhan, jadal, khitaba, shi'r, safsata) by their epistemic certainty and identify the characteristic premise types of each, and define burhan as a syllogism composed of certain premises.

Core Idea
Burhan is the highest art because it uses certain premises; certainty decreases from dialectic to sophistry.

In this lesson, we examine the epistemic hierarchy of the five arts as presented by al-Akhdari in al-Sullam and expounded by al-Bajuri. Demonstration (burhan) stands at the top because its premises are certain (yaqiniyya). Understanding this ranking helps you recognize the different epistemological levels at which arguments operate in Islamic sciences, from certain proofs to deceptive sophistry.

Guided Path

The ranking is based on the certainty of the premises each art uses. Burhan uses certain premises; jadal uses premises close to certainty (well-known or accepted); khitaba uses probable premises; shi'r uses imaginative premises; safsata uses deceptive premises.

Extract the definition of burhan

4

Notice the descending order: burhan (certain) > jadal (close to certain) > khitaba (probable) > shi'r (imaginative) > safsata (deceptive). Each lower art has a weaker epistemic foundation.

The Hierarchy of Certainty in Argument
Commentator ranks the five arts by certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to rank the five arts of argument from strongest to weakest and justify the ranking based on the certainty of their premises.

Core Idea
Demonstration is highest, then dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry—ranked by premise certainty.

The commentator explains the ranking that the matn implies: the best is burhān, then jadal, then khiṭāba, then shi‘r, then safsaṭa. This hierarchy is based on how reliable their premises are.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ranking from the commentary
Find where al-Bajuri says 'أجلها البرهان' and then lists the order: بعدها الجدل، ثم الخطابة، ثم الشعر، ثم السفسطة.
2
For each art, state the premise type
Burhān: certain (yaqīniyya); jadal: close to certainty (mashhūra or musallama); khiṭāba: probable (maznūna); shi‘r: imaginative (not truth-seeking); safsaṭa: deceptive (no epistemic value).
3
Explain why the ranking is justified
Certainty is the highest epistemic status; therefore arguments with certain premises are strongest. Probable premises yield weaker conclusions; imaginative and deceptive premises yield no knowledge.
The commentator explicitly states 'أقواها' for burhān.
4
Understand that the matn's order differed due to meter
The matn listed khiṭāba before shi‘r, etc., but the commentator says it was for poetic necessity (لضرورة النظم).

What is Burhān? Components of a Demonstrative Argument
Author defines burhān and its premise types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define burhān and identify the types of premises that constitute it.

Core Idea
Burhān is an argument composed solely of certain premises, including necessary/theoretical and rational/transmitted.

The matn defines burhān as 'that which is composed of certain premises.' Al-Bajuri specifies that these premises include all certain types: necessary (ḍarūriyya), theoretical (naẓariyya), rational ('aqliyya), and transmitted (naqliyya). Understanding this is crucial for confirming the soundness of theological proofs.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of burhān from the matn
The matn says: 'أَجَلُّهَا الْبُرْهَانُ: مَا أُلْفَ مِنْ مُقَدِّمَاتٍ بِالْيَقِينِ تَقْتَرِنْ' meaning 'the best is demonstration: that which is composed of certain premises that are combined.'
2
Identify the types of premises mentioned by the commentator
Al-Bajuri says: 'الْمُقَدِّمَاتُ... الضَّرُورِيَّةُ، وَالنَّظَرِيَّةُ، وَالْعَقْلِيَّةُ، وَالنَّقْلِيَّةُ'—necessary, theoretical, rational, transmitted.
These are all subcategories of certain premises.
3
Understand why only certain premises are allowed
Demonstration aims at certainty (yaqīn), so only premises that are themselves certain can be used. This excludes probable, widely accepted, or imaginative premises.
4
Apply the definition to a real argument
Example: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' The first premise is certain by observation, the second by reason—both are certain.
This satisfies the definition of burhān.

Hads and Sense as Certain Premises in Logic

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hads and sense give certain knowledge because they reduce to the necessary.

This lesson unpacks al-Bajuri's commentary on the inclusion of conjectural (ḥadsiyyāt) and sense-perceptual (maḥsūsāt) premises among the certainties (yaqīniyyāt). You will learn how logicians classify these types and why they are considered certain despite objections.

The definition of ḥadsiyyāt is less explicit; it is mentioned in contrast to necessary and theoretical.
2
Understand the classification controversy for ḥadsiyyāt
Al-Bajuri notes that the author of the matn builds on the view that ḥadsiyyāt are necessary certainties. Some consider them theoretical, and the majority view among scholars is that they are probable. This shows a range of opinions on their epistemic status.

The objection is based on Arabic morphology; the answer appeals to usage.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary by al-Bajuri on a line from the matn (al-Sullam) that lists ‘conjectural premises’ (ḥadsiyyāt) and ‘sense-perceptual premises’ (maḥsūsāt) as part of the certainties (yaqīniyyāt). This shows a debate over the epistemological status of intuitive or conjectural propositions. Next, al-Bajuri defines maḥsūsāt as what the mind apprehends through the external senses, e.g., “the sun is shining.” He notes a terminological point: the difference between maḥsūsāt and mushāhadāt (observed premises) is merely conventional; they are essentially the same. An objection is raised against the word “maḥsūsāt” as a plural form: the correct passive participle from the verb aḥassa should be muḥass, not maḥsūs. The answer is that such linguistic flexibility is allowed. Some scholars argue that sense does not yield certainty because of errors in perception, but the matn includes them as certainties. Finally, the commentator addresses an objection to the matn’s claim that the listed items constitute the totality of certainties: since certainties can be theoretical, how can they be restricted to necessary? The answer is that theoretical propositions ultimately reduce to necessary ones, so they are treated as necessary. This lesson focuses on the classification and defense of these premise types within the framework of certain knowledge.

The Five Rational Arts: Classification by Matter and Order of Strength
Author classifies rational proofs into five arts based on matter and discusses their order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify rational proofs into the five arts based on their matter (premise type) and identify the correct order of strength among them.

Core Idea
The five arts of rational proof differ in certainty: burhan is strongest, then khitaba, then jadal, then shi'r, then safsata.

In this passage, al-Bannani explains that syllogisms are evaluated not only by their form but also by their matter—the type of premises they use. He then introduces the five rational arts: demonstration, dialectic, oratory, poetry, and sophistry. While the poem lists them in a convenient order, the commentator clarifies that the true order of strength is different, a point reinforced by Shaykh Hamdun in his 'Kharida'.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish form and matter of qiyas
Understand that a qiyas can be valid in form but unsound in content if its premises are not appropriate. The passage highlights that just as we consider the figure and mood (form), we must also consider the nature of the premises (matter).
2
Identify the two types of proof
Separate transmitted proofs (derived from Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus, and qiyas in fiqh) from rational proofs. The passage notes that transmitted proofs are studied in usul al-fiqh, so they are not elaborated here.
Example of a transmitted proof: 'The resurrection is true, because Allah says: "Yes, by my Lord, you will surely be raised." (Qur'an 64:7)'
3
List the five rational arts and their matter
For each of the five arts, note the type of premise that defines it: burhan uses certain premises (yaqiniyat), jadal uses widely accepted premises (mashhurat), khitaba uses persuasive premises (muqni'at), shi'r uses imaginative premises (mukhayyilat), and safsata uses deceptive premises (mushabbahat).
The passage states that rational proofs either produce assent (tasdiq) or influence (ta'thir). Burhan and jadal produce assent; khitaba, shi'r, and safsata influence in various ways.
4

Recognize the correct order of strength as given by Shaykh Hamdun: burhan (strongest), then khitaba, then jadal, then shi'r, then safsata (weakest). Compare this with the poetic order in the matn (khitaba, shi'r, burhan, jadal, safsata) and note that the poet prioritized ease of composition over logical ranking.
The commentator al-Bannani initially placed jadal before khitaba following Qaddura, but then corrected it to the order from Hamdun.

Commentary
This passage introduces the five arts of rational argument. It begins with a line from the matn (the poem al-Sullam al-Marwani) stating that a proof is either transmitted (nuqliyya) or rational ('aqliyya), and the rational proofs are five clear ones: oratory (khataba), poetry (shi'r), demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), and sophistry (safsata). The commentary explains that qiyas (syllogism) is considered from both form and matter; the form was discussed earlier, now matter is discussed. By matter, qiyas divides into two: transmitted (derived from Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus, and analogy in fiqh) and rational. Rational qiyas then divides into five arts based on whether they produce assent (tasdiq) or influence (ta'thir). The commentator clarifies that transmitted proofs are studied in usul al-fiqh, so they are not elaborated here. He gives an example: 'The resurrection is true' supported by the Qur'anic verse 'Say: Yes, by my Lord, you will surely be raised' (64:7). There is a discussion about the order of the five arts in the poem; the commentator notes that the order in the poem is not by strength, but the correct order of strength is: burhan (demonstration) is strongest, then khitaba (oratory), then jadal (dialectic), then shi'r (poetry), then safsata (sophistry). This correct order is from Shaykh Hamdun's 'Kharida'.

Classification of Proof into the Five Arts
Classification of proof into the five arts with definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the five arts of rational proof (burhan, jadal, khitaba, shi'r, safsata) by the type of assent or effect each aims to produce, and identify which art a given syllogism belongs to based on its premises.

Core Idea
The five arts differ in matter, not form – premises determine the art.

In this passage, the logician moves from the form of syllogisms to their matter—the type of propositions used. He divides proof into transmitted (Quran, Sunna, consensus) and rational, then splits rational proof into five arts. Each art targets a different cognitive outcome: decisive assent, accepted assent, probable assent, emotional effect, or deceptive assent. Understanding this classification is essential for choosing the right type of argument in theological and juristic discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of proof
The passage first divides proof into transmitted (derived from revelation and consensus) and rational. The transmitted proof is studied in usul al-fiqh, not logic. Focus on the rational division.
Transmitted proof includes Quran, Sunna, consensus, and analogy derived from them.
2
Recognize the five arts of rational proof
The rational proof is further divided into five arts: burhan (demonstration), jadal (dialectic), khitaba (rhetoric), shi‘r (poetry), and safsata (sophistry). They are all syllogisms in form, but differ in their premises.
The commentator notes the correct order of strength: burhan > khitaba > jadal > shi‘r > safsata.
3
Understand the basis of division: assent vs. effect
The division is based on whether the syllogism produces tasdiq (assent) or ta’thir (effect). Demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, and sophistry produce assent; poetry produces effect (emotional influence).
Assent can be decisive ( jazim) or non-decisive (ghayr jazim).
4
Differentiate each art by the nature of its assent or effect
Each art is defined by the kind of assent it aims for: burhan yields decisive assent that is true in reality (certain premises); jadal yields assent that is accepted by the opponent regardless of truth (conceded/widely accepted premises); khitaba yields non-decisive assent (persuasive/probable premises); safsata yields apparent assent that is actually false (deceptive premises); shi‘r yields effect (imaginative premises).
The Quranic verse (16:125) illustrates the three highest arts: wisdom (burhan), good exhortation (khitaba), and debate (jadal).
5
Apply the classification to a sample argument
Take the claim 'The resurrection is true' supported by a Quranic verse. This is a transmitted proof. Now consider a rational proof: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' If the premises are certain, it is burhan; if they are merely widely accepted, it is jadal; if they are probable, it is khitaba.
The same syllogistic form can belong to different arts depending on the epistemic status of its premises.

And assent: either decisive or non-decisive. And the decisive: either its truth is considered or not. And that whose truth is considered: either it is true in reality or not. (1) So that which yields decisive assent that is true in reality is demonstration. (2) And that which yields assent in which its being true or not is not considered, but rather absolute acknowledgment of it is considered, is dialectic. (3) And that which yields non-decisive assent is rhetoric. (4) And that which yields effect is poetry. (5) And that which yields assent that is thought to be true in reality but is not true is sophistry. So these are five divisions which in terms of form are united, and they are only diversified by the diversification of their matters [vol. 41]. Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of the types of proof (hujja) in logic. It begins by stating that syllogisms are examined from two perspectives: form and matter. The author has already discussed form, and now turns to matter. By matter, he means the content or type of propositions used. He divides proof into two main kinds: transmitted proof (hujja naqliyya), which derives from the Quran, Sunna, and consensus, and rational proof (hujja aqliyya). The transmitted proof belongs to the science of usul al-fiqh, so the logician does not detail it. The rational proof is then divided into five arts: demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), rhetoric (khitaba), poetry (shi‘r), and sophistry (safsata). This division is based on whether the proof yields assent (tasdiq) or effect (ta’thir). The passage then explains the correct order of the arts by strength. The commentator notes that the author's order was dictated by poetic meter, but the correct order in strength is demonstration, then rhetoric, then dialectic, then poetry, then sophistry. This is supported by the Quranic verse (16:125) where wisdom (demonstration) comes first, then good exhortation (rhetoric), and debate (dialectic) is separate. Each art is then defined by the kind of assent or effect it produces. Demonstration yields decisive assent that is true in reality—it uses certain premises. Dialectic yields assent that is accepted by the opponent, regardless of truth—it uses widely accepted or conceded premises. Rhetoric yields non-decisive assent—it uses persuasive or probable premises. Poetry yields effect (ta’thir), i.e., emotional influence, not assent. Sophistry yields assent that is thought to be true but is false—it uses deceptive premises. The commentary emphasizes that all five arts have the same logical form (they are all syllogisms) but differ in their matter (the type of premises). It also notes that rhetoric is closest to demonstration because it aims at assent, and it is the most beneficial to the public. The passage concludes by stating that the author did not explicitly state the materials for each art, but Sayyid Hamdun did for dialectic (conceded and widely accepted propositions).

Form and Material: The Unity and Diversity of the Logical Arts
Unity of form and diversity of material in five arts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between sura (form) and madda (material) in the five logical arts and justify the Quranic preference for rhetoric over dialectic.

Core Idea
The logical form is one; the material determines the art.

The author emphasizes that the five arts share the same syllogistic structure; they differ only in their premises. He then uses a Quranic verse to explain the order in which he presents them, highlighting the practical wisdom of prioritizing rhetoric. This lesson unpacks the concepts of sura and madda and their significance.

Guided Path

Understand the distinction between sura and madda
In Mantiq, the sura of an argument is its logical structure (e.g., categorical syllogism), while the madda is the specific propositions used. The author claims the five arts are united in sura but diverse in madda.
This is a key principle: the same syllogism can be a demonstration or a sophism depending on the premises.

The premises determine the epistemic value of the conclusion.

Analyze the Quranic citation (16:125)
The author quotes the verse to show that Allah commanded the Prophet to invite with wisdom (burhan) and good admonition (khitaba), and then to argue (jadal) separately. This indicates that jadal is not part of the primary invitation but a subsequent method.

Evaluate the claim that rhetoric is the most beneficial art
Shaykh al-Tayyib states that rhetoric is closest to demonstration and is the most beneficial because it influences the majority of people, who may not be capable of following certain proofs.
This highlights the practical utility of khitaba in da'wa and education.

Based on this, five arts are listed: (1) Burhan (demonstration) yields decisive assent that is true in reality, using yaqiniyyat (certain premises). (2) Jadal (dialectic) yields decisive assent but only requires mutual acceptance (taslim) regardless of actual truth, composed of mashhurat (widely-accepted) and musallamat (conceded) premises. (3) Khitaba (rhetoric) yields non-decisive assent, based on persuasive or probable premises. (4) Shi'r (poetry) produces effect (ta'thir) rather than assent, through imaginative premises. (5) Safsata (sophistry) yields false assent that appears true. The author notes that these arts share the same logical form (sura) but differ in their material (madda). He then prioritizes rhetoric over dialectic, quoting Quran 16:125 to show that the prophetic call uses wisdom (burhan) and good admonition (khitaba), while jadal is commanded separately after the call. A comment from Shaykh al-Tayyib explains that rhetoric is the most beneficial because it influences the majority.

أقسام الحجة ثُمَّ قَالَ : ۷۲۱ (۱۱۷) أَجَلُّهَا «البُرْهَانُ»: مَا أُلْفَ مِنْ مُقَدِّمَاتٍ بِاليَقِيْنِ تَقْتَرِنْ يَعْنِي: أَنَّ البُرْهَانَ هُوَ أَجَلُّ الحُجَجِ المُتَقَدِّمَةِ؛ لِإِفَادَتِهِ مَعْرِفَةِ الحَقِّ وَالقَطْعِ بِهِ، وَهُوَ الحِكْمَةُ فِي آيَة : ادْعُ إِلَى سَبِيلِ رَبِّكَ بِالْحِكْمَةِ وَالْمَوْعِظَةِ الْحَسَنَةِ) [النحل: ١٢٥] . وَأَرْدَؤُهَا : المُغَالَطَةُ وَهِيَ السَّفْسَطَةُ ؛ لإفَادَتِهَا التَّشْكِيكِ وَتَلْبِيسِ الحَقِّ بِالبَاطِلِ وَأَقْرَبُهَا إِلَى البُرْهَانِ : الخَطَابَةُ ؛ لإفَادَتِهَا التَّصْدِيقَ بِالدَّلَائِلِ الإِقْنَاعِيَّةِ وَالمَظْنُونَةِ، وَهِيَ المَوْعِظَةُ الحَسَنَةُ فِى الآيَةِ. ثُمَّ عَرَّفَ البُرْهَانَ بِأَنَّهُ: «مَا أُلْفَ مِنْ مُقَدِّمَاتٍ يَقِينِيَّةٍ». - فَـ «مَا » : وَاقِعَةٌ عَلَى القِيَاسِ ؛ لِأَنَّهُ الجِنْسُ الأَقْرَبُ لِلْحُجَجِ الخَمْسِ، وَيَخْرُجُ عَنْهُ : الاسْتِقْرَاءُ وَالتَّمْثِيلُ . - وَقَوْلُهُ: «أُلّف ... إلخ » فَصْلٌ يُخْرِجُ : مَا عَدَا البُرْهَانِ مِنَ الحُجَجِ . - وَاليَقِينُ هُوَ : الِاعْتِقَادُ الجَازِمُ المُطَابِقُ الثَّابِتُ، فَيَخْرُجُ بِـ «الجَازِمِ»: الظَّنُّ، وَبِـ «المُطَابِقِ» : الجَهْلُ المُرَكَّبُ، وَبِـ «الثَّابِتِ» : التَّقْلِيدُ .
Divisions of the argument. Then he said: (117) The noblest of them is 'al-burhan': that which is composed of premises that are certain. Meaning: that al-burhan is the noblest of the aforementioned arguments, because it gives knowledge of the truth and certainty of it, and it is the wisdom in the verse: 'Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good admonition' [al-Nahl: 125]. And the worst of them is al-mughalata, which is sophistry, because it gives doubt and obscuring truth with falsehood. And the closest of them to al-burhan is al-khataba, because it gives assent through persuasive and probable proofs, and it is the good admonition in the verse. Then he defined al-burhan as 'that which is composed of certain premises.' So 'ma' applies to qiyas, because it is the nearest genus for the five arguments, and it excludes induction and analogy. And his statement 'composed... etc.' is a differentia that excludes all other arguments. And yaqin is: firm belief that conforms to reality and is stable. So by 'firm' he excludes opinion, by 'conforms' he excludes compound ignorance, and by 'stable' he excludes taqlid.

Commentary
This passage divides arguments (hujaj) into five arts, which the author enumerates elsewhere. It begins by stating that the noblest (ajalluha) of these arguments is al-burhan (demonstration), defined as a syllogism (qiyas) composed of certain premises. The author notes that burhan corresponds to 'wisdom' (hikma) in the Quranic verse (16:125). The worst argument is al-mughalata (sophistry), which causes confusion and obscures truth. The closest to burhan is al-khataba (rhetoric), which gives assent to persuasive and probable premises, corresponding to 'good admonition' in the verse. The definition of burhan is then given: 'that which is composed of certain premises.' The word 'ma' (that which) refers to the syllogism (qiyas), as it is the closest genus for the five arts, excluding induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). The phrase 'composed of' is the differentia, excluding all other arguments. Finally, yaqin (certainty) is defined as firm belief (i'tiqad jazim) that conforms to reality (mutabiq) and is stable (thabit). This definition excludes opinion (zann) by 'firm', compound ignorance (jahl murakkab) by 'conforms', and taqlid (blind imitation) by 'stable'.

Definition of Yaqin: Three Conditions
Author defines yaqin with three conditions and the exclusions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions of yaqin and explain what each excludes.

Core Idea
Yaqin requires firm belief (jazim) that conforms to reality (mutabiq) and is stable (thabit).

The passage defines yaqin (certainty) with three conditions: it must be firm, conforming to reality, and stable. Each condition excludes a specific epistemic defect: opinion, compound ignorance, and blind imitation. This is the standard Ash'ari definition.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the three conditions
The passage lists three descriptors: jazim (firm/belief that does not waver), mutabiq (conforming to reality), thabit (stable/unchanging). Commit these three to memory: it is the definition of yaqin.
2
Understand what 'jazim' excludes
By requiring firmness, yaqin excludes zann (opinion) where one is not fully certain. In Islamic theology, matters of creed must be held with firm conviction, not mere probability.
The passage says: 'فَيَخْرُجُ بِـ «الجَازِمِ»: الظَّنُّ'.
3
Understand what 'mutabiq' excludes
Conformity to reality ensures that the belief corresponds to the truth as it is. This excludes jahl murakkab (compound ignorance) where one holds a false belief while being certain of it.
The passage says: 'وَبِـ «المُطَابِقِ» : الجَهْلُ المُرَكَّبُ'.
4
Understand what 'thabit' excludes
Stability means the belief is not subject to change or doubt. This excludes taqlid (blind imitation), where one accepts a belief without proper evidence and may abandon it when challenged.
The passage says: 'وَبِـ «الثَّابِتِ» : التَّقْلِيدُ'.
