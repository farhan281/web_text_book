The Proposition (Qadiyya): Definition, Reality, and Multiple Names
Author defines proposition and distinguishes its names.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of a proposition (qadiyya) in Islamic logic, distinguish the mental from the linguistic proposition, and enumerate the names of a proposition according to different aspects.

Core Idea
The proposition is defined not by its linguistic form but by its mental content and its capacity for truth or falsehood.

The full weight of logical reasoning rests on propositions. This passage gives you the precise logical definition of a proposition (qadiyya) and shows you that the same reality goes by many names depending on how you look at it—whether as a judgment, a statement, a premise, a conclusion, or a problem. Understanding this one concept will unlock the entire system of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and the definition formula
The commentator defines what is capable of truth and falsehood as the proposition. Look for the key phrase 'ما احتمل الصدق والكذب'. This is the core definition.
2
Understand the inclusion of the rational proposition
The use of 'ما' (ma) in the definition is meant to include the mental proposition (al-qadiyya al-'aqliyya), not just the spoken one. This is a crucial expansion by the commentator.
The mental proposition is the primary reality; the linguistic is metaphorical.
3
Analyze the etymology: why 'qadiyya'?
The term comes from 'qada'' (judgment) because it involves a ruling (hukm) about the relation between subject and predicate.
Thus a proposition is something in which a judgment is made.
4
Distinguish the various names of the same reality
The commentator quotes al-Sayyid listing seven names: qadiyya, khabar, ikhbar, muqaddima, matlub, natija, mas'ala. Each name highlights a different function or context.
The essence is one; the names differ by consideration.
5
Grasp the rational possibility of falsehood
The commentator explains that probability of falsehood is rational because linguistic meaning is conventional, not necessary like the relation of effect to cause.
This shows that the definition applies even to false propositions.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a base matn defining the proposition (qadiyya). The commentator begins by stating that all the divisions (of propositions) are capable of truth and falsehood, considered merely from the essence of the khabar (statement), which is the affirmation of something for something. This definition encompasses all types. Then he explains the base text's phrasing: 'what is capable of truth and falsehood' — the word 'ma' (what) is used as a mubtada (subject) to include the rational proposition (al-qadiyya al-'aqliyya), i.e., the mental composite of subject, predicate, and judgment. He notes that the phrase 'al-sidqa' (the truth) involves deletion of the waw and its conjunct (i.e., 'and falsehood'), analogous to the Qur'anic verse 'Taqikum al-harra' (protect you from the heat) meaning 'and the cold'. This is because capability (ihtimal) implies alternation between two possibilities, so the omitted opposite is falsehood. The sentence 'jara' (runs) serves as the khabar of the mubtada, indicating that what is capable of both is called 'qadiyya' due to the judgment it contains, and also 'khabar' due to the information it conveys. The commentator then addresses the rational proposition: the term 'qadiyya' is applied in reality to the mental composite, and its application to the linguistic expression is metaphorical, according to one view; another view holds that it is purely homonymous (ishitrak). He clarifies that 'capability' is only used for alternation between two, so a word with a single meaning is not said to be capable. The omitted opposite is determined by knowledge of the context—the opposite of truth is falsehood. The commentator explains the etymology of 'qadiyya' as derived from 'qada'' (judgment), designating it as a 'maf'ula' (something acted upon), because a judgment is made about the relation between its two terms. Then he quotes al-Sayyid (al-Sharif al-Jurjani) from al-Talwih, who enumerates the names of this same composite according to different considerations: qadiyya (by virtue of containing a judgment), khabar (by virtue of probability of truth/falsehood), ikhbar (by virtue of conveying the judgment), muqaddima (as part of a proof), matlub (as sought by proof), natija (as obtained by proof), and mas'ala (as occurring in knowledge and being asked about). The essence is one; the variety of names reflects different aspects or relations. Finally, the commentator explains that the probability of falsehood in a khabar is a rational matter (amr 'aqli), because the indication of words for meanings is conventional (wad'i) not rational; rational implication (like that of effect from cause) cannot fail, but conventional indication can be false.

Defining the Four Modal Operators in Islamic Logic
Commentary defines the four modal operators individually.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define each of the four modal operators (ḍarūrah, imkān, dawām, iṭlāq) as described in the commentary and distinguish their meanings.

Core Idea
Modal operators qualify how a predicate relates to a subject: necessarily, possibly, permanently, or actually.

In Islamic logic, propositions are not just true or false—they come with modalities. This lesson unpacks the four fundamental modal operators: necessity (ḍarūra), possibility (imkān), permanence (dawām), and absoluteness (iṭlāq). The commentary defines each with precision, setting the stage for understanding how they interact in arguments.

This is without temporal extension or necessity.

Commentary
This commentary expands on the definitions of the four modal operators—necessity (ḍarūra), possibility (imkān), permanence (dawām), and absoluteness (iṭlāq)—and explains their mutual oppositions. The author first defines each term: necessity is the mind's judgment that the relation (of predicate to subject) is obligatory, even if that judgment is theoretical (i.e., known through reasoning). Possibility is that the relation is not impossible (i.e., contingent). Permanence is the continuous duration of the relation for as long as the subject exists. Absoluteness is the actual (bi-l-fi‘l) affirmation or negation of the predicate for the subject, without regard to time or necessity. Then the author addresses a potential confusion: how can possibility be opposed to necessity when possibility is more general (a‘amm) than necessity? The answer is that the opposition is not in terms of the concepts themselves, but in terms of the contradictory of each proposition. For example, 'Every human is an animal by necessity' has a contradictory: 'Some human is not an animal by possibility'—here the possibility of negation opposes the necessity of affirmation. Similarly, absoluteness opposes permanence in the contradictory of its proposition. Otherwise, possibility includes both what is actual and what is not, so it is broader than necessity. The author clarifies that possibility has two aspects: (1) considered merely as a concept, it applies to all modal propositions and does not oppose necessity; (2) considered in terms of its relation to affirmation or negation, it opposes necessity in the contradictory relation. So the possibility of affirmation (imkān al-ījāb) is mutually exclusive with the necessity of negation (ḍarūrat al-salb), and vice versa. The same analysis applies to permanence and absoluteness: the absolute affirmative proposition 'Every human is mortal actually' is contradicted by the permanent negative 'Some human is not mortal permanently'. This careful distinction resolves the apparent conflict between generality and opposition.

Modal Opposition: How Possibility and Necessity Conflict
Commentary explains why possibility opposes necessity despite being more general.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how possibility (imkān) opposes necessity (ḍarūra) in the contradictory proposition, using the two aspects of possibility.

Core Idea
Possibility and necessity oppose in the contradictory of the relation, not in their general concepts.

A key puzzle: if possibility is more general than necessity, how can they be opposites? The commentary resolves this by showing that the opposition is not between the concepts themselves, but between their instantiations in contradictory propositions. This lesson unpacks the two considerations of possibility and the parallel opposition of permanence and absoluteness.

Guided Path
1
Understand the apparent conflict

2

Commentary
This commentary expands on the definitions of the four modal operators—necessity (ḍarūra), possibility (imkān), permanence (dawām), and absoluteness (iṭlāq)—and explains their mutual oppositions. The author first defines each term: necessity is the mind's judgment that the relation (of predicate to subject) is obligatory, even if that judgment is theoretical (i.e., known through reasoning). Possibility is that the relation is not impossible (i.e., contingent). Permanence is the continuous duration of the relation for as long as the subject exists. Absoluteness is the actual (bi-l-fi‘l) affirmation or negation of the predicate for the subject, without regard to time or necessity. Then the author addresses a potential confusion: how can possibility be opposed to necessity when possibility is more general (a‘amm) than necessity? The answer is that the opposition is not in terms of the concepts themselves, but in terms of the contradictory of each proposition. For example, 'Every human is an animal by necessity' has a contradictory: 'Some human is not an animal by possibility'—here the possibility of negation opposes the necessity of affirmation. Similarly, absoluteness opposes permanence in the contradictory of its proposition. Otherwise, possibility includes both what is actual and what is not, so it is broader than necessity. The author clarifies that possibility has two aspects: (1) considered merely as a concept, it applies to all modal propositions and does not oppose necessity; (2) considered in terms of its relation to affirmation or negation, it opposes necessity in the contradictory relation. So the possibility of affirmation (imkān al-ījāb) is mutually exclusive with the necessity of negation (ḍarūrat al-salb), and vice versa. The same analysis applies to permanence and absoluteness: the absolute affirmative proposition 'Every human is mortal actually' is contradicted by the permanent negative 'Some human is not mortal permanently'. This careful distinction resolves the apparent conflict between generality and opposition.

Material Error in Burhan: Non-Certain Premises
Author identifies the error of using non-certain premises in a demonstration requiring certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a premise in a demonstrative syllogism (burhan) is certain or non-certain, and recognize the material fallacy of using a non-certain premise in a demonstration.

Core Idea
A demonstration (burhan) fails if its premises are only probable or commonly accepted, not certain.

In this lesson, we examine a specific material fallacy unique to burhan: using premises that lack certainty. The author distinguishes between certain (qat') premises and those that are merely probable (zann) or widely accepted (shuhra). Understanding this error is crucial for constructing sound theological and legal proofs that yield certainty.
