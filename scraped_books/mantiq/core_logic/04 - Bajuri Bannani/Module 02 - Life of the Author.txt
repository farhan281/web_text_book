Biography of Abd al-Rahman al-Akhdari: The Man Behind al-Sullam al-Murawnaq
Introduces the author and lists his major works with biographical details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key biographical details of al-Akhdari and list his major works across various Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Al-Akhdari was a polymath: his logic work is one part of a broader scholarly output spanning multiple Islamic sciences.

This passage introduces the author of al-Sullam al-Murawnaq, the didactic poem that forms the core of our logic study. Knowing his background helps us appreciate his pedagogical approach and the scope of his expertise across multiple Islamic sciences. By parsing the biographical details, we also learn how classical scholars contextualize an author's works.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the full name and lifespan
Locate the full name 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Muhammad al-Akhdari' and the birth/death years 920-983 AH. Note that these are Hijri dates, approximately 1514-1575 CE.
The death year is given in the header, but the birth year is inferred from the poem mentioned in the footnote.
2
2. Locate geographical origin
Find 'Biskra in Algeria' as his birthplace and 'zawiya of Bantiwus' as his burial place. This situates him in North Africa, a major region of Islamic scholarship.
The zawiya indicates a Sufi lodge, suggesting his connection to a spiritual order.
3
3. List his works by science
Scan the numbered list. You should find works on logic (al-Sullam and its commentary), rhetoric (al-Jawhar al-Maknun), astronomy (Sharh al-Siraj), arithmetic and inheritance (al-Durra al-Bayda), and fiqh (Mukhtasar al-Akhdari).
Note the missing number 5 in the list – this is likely an editorial omission.
4
4. Understand the poetic works
Many of his works are in verse: urjuza for logic, nazm for rhetoric and inheritance. Reflect on why scholars composed didactic poems – they aid memorization and are a hallmark of traditional Islamic pedagogy.
Al-Sullam is an urjuza, a poem in rajaz meter, which was a common medium for teaching logic.
5
5. Interpret the footnote for dating
The footnote extracts lines from al-Sullam itself that give the composition date. The phrase 'year one and forty after nine' likely means 941 AH. He says he was 21 years old, so he composed it early in his scholarly career.
This shows that al-Akhdari was already a scholar at a young age, completing a major logic work at 21.

Abd al-Rahman al-Akhdari (920 AH - 983 AH)
Abd al-Rahman ibn Muhammad al-Akhdari, he is from Biskra in Algeria, and his grave is in the zawiya of Bantiwus from the villages of Biskra.

Three Key Scholars of the Maghribi Logical Tradition
Biographical entries for three scholars: al-Akhdari, al-Bannani, and Qassara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Abd al-Rahman al-Akhdari, Muhammad al-Bannani, and Ali Qassara and describe their contributions to the study of logic in the Islamic West.

Core Idea
These three scholars form the backbone of the Maghribi logical commentary tradition.

Before diving into the actual logical texts, it is important to know the scholars who authored, commented on, and transmitted them. This lesson introduces three key figures from North and West Africa whose works on logic—especially al-Sullam al-Murawnaq and its commentaries—are central to our course. Recognizing their biographies helps you situate the texts historically and understand the chains of transmission that preserve Islamic logical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Meet al-Akhdari: the author of al-Sullam
Locate the entry for Abd al-Rahman al-Akhdari. Note his dates (920–983 AH), his region (Biskra, Algeria), and his major logical work, the poem 'al-Sullam al-Murawnaq'. He also wrote a commentary on it and works on rhetoric, astronomy, inheritance, arithmetic, and a Maliki legal manual.
Al-Sullam is a foundational urjūzah (didactic poem) in logic widely studied in the Maghrib.
2
Meet al-Bannani: the commentator and glossator
Find Muhammad al-Bannani's entry. He was a Maliki jurist from Fez (d. 1194 AH). His key logical work is a commentary on al-Sullam, and he also wrote a gloss correcting al-Zurqani's legal commentary. Note the distinction between 'Bannani' (without al-) for the Moroccan and 'al-Bannani' for the Egyptian.
Al-Bannani's commentary on al-Sullam is a standard reference in the tradition.
3
Meet Qassara: the later glossator
Read the entry for Ali Qassara (d. 1359 AH). He was a Moroccan Maliki jurist whose logical work is a gloss on al-Bannani's commentary on al-Sullam. This shows the ongoing commentary tradition.
Qassara's gloss extends and refines al-Bannani's explanations.
4
Connect the chain
Observe the scholarly chain: al-Akhdari authored the base text, al-Bannani wrote a commentary, and Qassara wrote a gloss on that commentary. This illustrates how Islamic logical knowledge was transmitted and built upon across generations.
Understanding this chain is crucial for situating any commentary or gloss you will study.

Tracing the Commentary Chain of al-Sullam al-Murawnaq
Author lists biographies of logicians who wrote on al-Sullam al-Murawnaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the chain of commentary (sharh and hashiya) around al-Sullam al-Murawnaq using the biographies provided.

Core Idea
Biographies reveal the scholarly lineage that preserves and refines logical texts.

This passage introduces the key scholars whose works form the commentary tradition on al-Sullam al-Murawnaq. Understanding who wrote what—and in what order—is essential for navigating the layered commentaries and appreciating how later scholars built upon earlier ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text (matn)
The foundational text is al-Sullam al-Murawnaq fi al-Mantiq by al-Akhdari. It is the poem on logic being commented upon.
Not explicitly mentioned in this passage, but assumed from the context of 'Sharh al-Sullam al-Murawnaq'.
2
Find the first-level commentary (sharh)
Al-Bannani wrote 'Sharh al-Sullam al-Murawnaq fi al-Mantiq'. This is a direct explanation of the matn.
Al-Bannani's sharh is the primary commentary in this edition.
3
Identify the super-commentaries (hashiya)
Ali Qasara wrote a hashiya on al-Bannani's sharh, and al-Bajuri wrote a hashiya on al-Sanusi's compendium (not directly on al-Sullam). Al-Sharshimi wrote on al-Bajuri's glosses.
A hashiya typically corrects or expands upon the commentary it glosses.
4
Map the chain
Draw a lineage: matn (al-Sullam) → sharh (al-Bannani) → hashiya (Ali Qasara). Also note al-Bajuri's independent gloss on al-Sanusi's work, and al-Sharshimi's further gloss.
This chain shows the cumulative scholarly effort.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical section introducing the scholars whose works are included in this edition of al-Bajuri and al-Bannani on al-Sullam al-Murawnaq. It begins with Muhammad al-Bannani (d. 1194 AH), a Maliki jurist and khatib from Fez. His books include a gloss correcting al-Zarqani, a gloss on al-Sanusi's logic compendium, his commentary on al-Sullam al-Murawnaq (which is the primary text being commented on), and a fahrasa of his teachers. A note explains that he was known as 'Bannani' without the definite article to distinguish him from another scholar with the same nisba in Egypt. Core Idea

This passage is the final note from the author of a commentary on Islamic logic. After recording the date of completion, he turns entirely to supplication, asking forgiveness for himself and all believers, and seeking refuge from spiritual and worldly harms. This colophon reveals the deep humility expected of a scholar, who recognizes that his work is only valuable if Allah accepts it.
