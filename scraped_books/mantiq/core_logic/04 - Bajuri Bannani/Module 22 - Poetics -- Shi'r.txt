Poetry as an Imaginative Syllogism
Definition of poetry with examples of expansion and contraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a poetic syllogism (shi'r) in Islamic logic and distinguish it from demonstrative and dialectical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Poetry is a syllogism that moves the soul, not by truth, but by imagination.

This passage from the Bajuri-Bannani commentary defines poetry within the Aristotelian-Islamic logical tradition. Unlike demonstration (burhan) which seeks certainty, poetry aims at emotional effect through imaginative premises. The author defines it as a syllogism composed of such premises, and gives two concrete examples: one for expansion (bast) and one for contraction (qabd). Understanding this definition helps us locate poetry's place in the classification of syllogisms and appreciate how Islamic logicians integrated literary theory into logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definition
Find the statement: 'poetry is a syllogism composed of imaginative premises' and note the key terms 'shi'r', 'mukhayyala', 'bast', 'qabd'.
The definition explicitly states poetry is a syllogism, not arbitrary speech.
2
Understand the Emotional Effect
The soul expands (bast) with pleasure or contracts (qabd) with aversion. The author generalizes to include generosity, boldness, etc.
Contraction and expansion are psychological responses, not logical truth.
3
Examine the Examples
For expansion: wine → flowing ruby → desirable. For contraction: honey → vomit-inducing bile → disgusting. Both are presented as syllogisms.
The examples show how imaginative premises transform ordinary objects.
4
Recognize the Syllogistic Form
Each example has two premises and a conclusion, fitting a categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara). Identify the major, minor, and middle terms.
The logical form is valid, but the premises are not aimed at truth.
5
Note the Indifference to Truth
The author explicitly says 'whether true or false'. This distinguishes poetic syllogisms from demonstrative ones.
Poetry's goal is emotional effect, not epistemic certainty.

His saying: (As for poetry, it is a syllogism composed of imaginative premises, with which the soul expands or contracts. And imaginative premises are: propositions that, when they come to the soul, move it and produce in it a wondrous effect of contraction or expansion, whether true or false. Example of expansion: their saying about wine: 'This is wine, and every wine is a flowing ruby,' so 'This is a flowing ruby.' Example of contraction: their saying about honey to make the soul contract away from it: 'This is a vomit-inducing bile, and every vomit-inducing bile is disgusting,' so 'This is disgusting.' His saying: (from imaginative premises) with a shadda on the ya. His saying: (of contraction or expansion) i.e., or generosity, or boldness, and the like. His saying: (this is wine... etc.) its parallel: 'This is a flowing ruby, and every flowing ruby is desirable,' so 'This is desirable.'

Commentary
This passage defines poetry (shi'r) as a syllogism composed of imaginative (mukhayyala) premises. The definition emphasizes that these premises affect the soul by causing expansion (bast, i.e., joy, desire) or contraction (qabd, i.e., aversion, fear), regardless of whether they are true or false. The commentator clarifies the pronunciation of 'mukhayyala' and expands 'contraction or expansion' to include related emotional responses like generosity or boldness. Two clear examples are given: for expansion, the poet describes wine as a flowing ruby, making it desirable; for contraction, honey is described as a vomit-inducing bile, making it disgusting. The commentator also provides an additional variant: 'This is a flowing ruby, and every flowing ruby is desirable, so this is desirable.' This shows that poetry uses imaginative comparisons to influence emotions, not to convey truth. In the Islamic context, such examples are used to encourage or discourage actions, but the logician here focuses on the formal structure as a syllogism with imaginative premises.
