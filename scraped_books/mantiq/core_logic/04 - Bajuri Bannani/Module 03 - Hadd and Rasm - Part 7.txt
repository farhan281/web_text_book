Sixth Condition: Avoid Ambiguous Terms Without Context
Author explains the sixth condition for definitions: avoiding ambiguous common words without a clarifying context.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify ambiguous terms in a definition and determine whether a clarifying context is required for the definition to be valid.

Core Idea
A definition must be unambiguous; ambiguous terms need a clarifying context or the definition fails.

In defining terms, clarity is paramount. Just as figurative language is restricted without context, ambiguous words (mushtarak) with multiple meanings are also prevented unless a qarina clarifies which meaning is intended. This condition, the sixth for valid definitions, ensures that definitions do not introduce new ambiguity.

Guided Path
1
Identify ambiguous terms
Look at the definition and find any term that has more than one distinct meaning in its context (e.g., 'ayn' can mean eye, gold, or spring). These are mushtarak.
Remember that ambiguity must be real and relevant to the definition.
2
Check for a clarifying context
See if the definition or surrounding text provides a qarina (clue) that specifies which meaning of the ambiguous term is intended. This could be a verbal qualifier, a technical usage, or a contextual indication.
If the qarina is absent, the definition violates the condition.
3
Assess the definition's validity
If an ambiguous term appears without a clarifying context, the definition is invalid for that condition. If a qarina is present, the definition may proceed.
This condition parallels the earlier one about figurative language (majaz).

Seventh Condition: Excluding Rulings from Definitions
Author explains the seventh condition: definitions must exclude rulings to avoid circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between rulings (ahkam) and specific properties (khawass) in definitions and explain why including rulings leads to circularity.

Core Idea
Rulings in definitions cause a vicious circle; only properties already tied to the essence in the mind are allowed.

In this lesson, we examine the seventh condition for valid definitions: that they must not contain rulings (ahkam). The commentator explains that including a ruling, like 'nominative' in the definition of 'subject', creates circularity because the ruling is only known after the defined thing is known. But specific properties (khawass) that are already recognized as distinguishing the essence are permissible. This distinction is crucial for constructing definitions that actually teach rather than presuppose knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand what 'rulings' mean here
Rulings (ahkam) are accidental properties that attach to an essence. They are known only after conceptualizing the essence. Note the shift: in logic, 'hukm' is used narrowly for such properties, distinct from its broader meaning in usul al-fiqh.
2
Recognize the circularity
If a ruling is included in the definition, the listener needs to know the defined thing to know the ruling, but also needs the ruling to know the defined thing. This is a vicious circle (dawr).
3
Distinguish rulings from specific properties
Specific properties (khawass) are already known to the listener to be tied to the essence; they are used only to distinguish, not to inform of a new ruling. Thus they do not cause circularity.
The difference lies in the listener's prior knowledge: properties are familiar, rulings are not.
4
Apply to the grammar example
The example of 'subject' (fa'il): if the listener already knows nominatives, defining the subject as 'a noun in the nominative by a verb' is fine. But if the listener needs to learn what a subject is, including 'nominative' fails because it presupposes knowledge of the subject.
Ibn Malik's definition of 'hal' with accusative is similarly problematic for beginners.

Commentary
This passage explains the seventh condition for a valid definition: that rulings (ahkam) must not be included in definitions. The commentator clarifies that 'rulings' here means the accidental properties that attach to an essence, and the purpose of defining a thing is to conceptualize that essence so that these rulings can then be affirmed or denied. Including a ruling in the definition leads to circularity (dawr) because the ruling is known only after the defined thing is known; if the ruling is part of the definition, it cannot be known until the defined thing is known, creating a vicious circle. The author then anticipates an objection: if specific rulings (like being nominative for the subject) are not allowed, how can specific properties (khawass) that are used in definitions (like 'dividing the gaze' for an animal) be allowed? The answer is that the specific properties used in definitions are those that the listener already knows are tied to the essence—they serve to distinguish the essence, not to inform of a ruling on it. In contrast, a ruling like 'nominative' for a subject is not known until the subject itself is conceptualized. The example illustrates: if someone already knows nominatives and wants to distinguish the subject, defining it as 'a noun in the nominative by a verb' works because they already know what 'nominative' means. But if someone only knows that every subject is nominative, and they need to conceptualize the subject to apply the nominative, then using 'nominative' in the definition would require them to already know the subject, causing circularity. Finally, the commentator applies this principle to answer an apparent violation by Ibn Malik, who included 'accusative' (nasb) in the definition of the circumstantial accusative (hal).

Why Rulings Cannot Be Part of Definitions: Circularity
Explains the prohibition of rulings in definitions and the circularity it causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when including a ruling in a definition creates circularity and explain why it is prohibited.

Core Idea
A ruling cannot define its subject because the subject must first be known for the ruling to apply.

Definitions aim to clarify a concept. But if a definition includes a ruling that itself requires knowledge of the concept being defined, a vicious circle results. This lesson examines that prohibition, focusing on grammatical examples like the subject (fa'il) and the nominative case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and the ruling
Take the example of 'fa'il' (subject) and the ruling 'marfu'' (nominative). The definiendum is the grammatical subject; the ruling is its case ending.
2
Assess epistemic priority
Can the listener know the definiendum 'fa'il' without first knowing that it is nominative? Yes, because fa'il is defined by its role in the sentence, not by its case. But can the listener know that fa'il is nominative without knowing what fa'il is? No – you need to know that something is a subject to judge it as nominative.
3
Check for circularity
If the definition of 'fa'il' includes 'nominative', then to understand the definiendum, the listener must already grasp that it is nominative. But to know it is nominative, he must already know it is fa'il. This back-and-forth (circularity) makes the definition useless.
4
Apply the test to other examples
Consider any definition. Ask: Does the definition contain a property that could only be known after knowing the definiendum? If yes, that property is a ruling and cannot be part of the definition.

Section on Definitions
And it (the ruling) is common, but it is prohibited in definition because a ruling is not known to be established except after conceptualizing the subject of the ruling. So if it is made a part of its definition, circularity follows.
If you say: What is the difference between specific rulings pertaining to the subject of the ruling and those properties (khawass) by which definition is permitted?
I say: The properties by which definition is valid are those which the listener already knows to be specific to the essence, and the essence is distinguished for him by them. So they are not mentioned to him in the definition from the perspective of the ruling about them on the defined term, such that he would need to conceptualize it before them, but rather to distinguish it for him. Unlike rulings, for they are not known to be established except after conceptualizing the subject of the ruling without them.
For example: One who knows the nominative cases and they are distinguished for him, and asks about what distinguishes the subject among them, it is valid to define for him 'the subject' as: 'a noun in the nominative case due to a preceding verb or what takes its place.' But one who hears that every subject is in the nominative case and needs to conceptualize the subject to utter it as nominative, it is not valid to define it for him as 'the noun in the nominative... etc.' because it is not ruled as nominative until he conceptualizes it, and he only conceptualizes it by conceptualizing the parts of its definition, among which is that it is nominative, so circularity follows. And by what we have mentioned, the mention by Ibn Malik of accusative case in the definition of 'hal' is answered. And Allah knows best.
Marginal note: 'Short.'
His saying: ('And it is common') — al-Sayyid explicitly stated this in the words of 'al-Mutawwal' in the discussion of presenting the predicate as described. His saying: ('If you say: What is the difference between specific rulings... etc.') i.e., those which you have prohibited from entering into definitions and what is permitted... etc. The original question and answer are from al-Hilali, except that this commentator abbreviated his words.
His saying: ('And by what we have mentioned, the mention by Ibn Malik of accusative case... etc.') — Consider this, even though the accusative case is an accident of words, not an accident of the concept which is the subject of definitions. That is because 'hal', for example, is established in grammatical convention for this specific definiendum, namely: 'the redundant description mentioned to clarify the state.' So all these matters are part of the name, unlike the accusative case, which is not of this category; because it is not considered in its concept, as understood from their words. Rather, it is not valid to consider it as included in the concept, and it is not among the evident concomitants of this concept; because the verification of the matters considered in its concept does not necessitate the verification of the accusative case. So the verification is that the accusative case is an accident of external referents, not an accident of the concept.
And the apparent meaning of his statement that rulings enter into definitions is that it is prohibited, whether one intends them to be a part of the definition or not [ / 29] [j/ 20], and it is so, contrary to some commentators, because if they are not part of it, they become a corrupting redundancy in its midst.
Marginal note: 'Short.'
It cannot be said: The accusative case is among the things established for 'hal', so why is it not valid to define it by it? Because we say: The fact that something is established for something does not necessitate it being necessary and evident. The validity of definition requires only the latter.
If it is said: What is the difference between the accusative case and its being a redundant description (fadla)?
We say: The difference between them is like the difference between the ض and the ن. That is, its being a redundant description is among the meaningful, intelligible matters that do not differ with different languages. They are requirements of the mind, not only of conventional usage. Unlike the accusative case, which is among the conventional matters whose reference is to the manner of pronouncing the word. So the validity of definition by the redundant description because it is included in the concept of 'hal' does not necessitate its validity by the accusative case, which is not like that. So understand this.
His saying: ('Whether he intends it to be a part of the definition or not') — because the goal is to conceptualize the defined term so that the ruling can be affirmed or denied of it. If the ruling is made a part of the definiendum, it would not be conceptualized except after the ruling upon it, and the assumption is that the ruling upon it depends on its conceptualization, so circularity arises.
If it is said: The ruling on a thing only depends on its conceptualization in some manner, and that has been achieved by mentioning some parts of the definition. The answer: The ruling depends on conceptualization in some manner when the thing ruled upon is not a part of the definition that produces its conceptualization. But if it is a part of it, then no, because the definer's purpose is to depict the defined for the addressee so that he can rule upon it, conceptualized in a more complete manner. So if the ruling is included in the definition, it is not conceptualized in that more complete manner except after the ruling upon it, so circularity is necessary with respect to his purpose. And even if it is conceded, then the thing being defined by it and ruled upon by it are contradictories in principle; because its being defined by it requires being conveyed for restriction and depiction, while its being ruled upon by it requires being conveyed for assent (tasdiq) and informing about the defined term, so they conflict.
His saying: ('A corrupting redundancy') i.e., and redundancy in definition is not permissible, as the master of the craft indicated in his books.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a section regarding definitions (al-ma'rifat). It addresses the prohibition of including rulings (ahkam) in definitions and clarifies the distinction between rulings and properties (khawass) that are permissible in definitions. The commentator first states that a ruling is common but forbidden in definition because a ruling's existence is only known after conceptualizing the subject of the ruling. Therefore, including the ruling as a part of the definition leads to circularity (dawr). A hypothetical objection is raised: what is the difference between specific rulings and the properties that are allowed in definitions? The answer is that properties are known to the listener as specific to the essence before the definition, so they are used merely to distinguish the essence, not as a ruling on the definiendum. In contrast, a ruling can only be known after conceptualizing the subject without it. An example from Arabic grammar illustrates this: if someone already knows the nominative cases and asks what distinguishes the subject from them, it is valid to define the subject as 'a noun in the nominative due to a preceding verb'. But if someone only knows that every subject is nominative and needs to conceptualize the subject to utter it in the nominative, defining it as 'the noun in the nominative' would cause circularity because the definer is using the ruling (nominative) to define the thing, but the ruling presupposes conceptualization. This explanation also answers the criticism of Ibn Malik's inclusion of accusative (nasb) in the definition of 'hal' (circumstantial accusative). The commentator then elaborates on Ibn Malik's definition: the accusative case is an accident of words, not of the concept (mafhum) that is the subject of definitions. The concept of 'hal' is a redundant description mentioned to clarify the state; all these aspects are part of the name, but the accusative case is not part of the concept. It is not a necessary concomitant (lazim bayyin) because verifying the conceptual parts does not necessitate the accusative case. Hence, the accusative is an accident of external referents (masadiq kharijiyya), not of the concept. The passage then asserts that the prohibition holds whether the ruling is intended as a part of the definition or not; even if it appears incidentally, it is a corrupting redundancy (hashw mufsid). Further objections about the accusative case being established for 'hal' are dismissed by distinguishing between what is merely established and what is necessary and evident. The difference between 'fadla' (redundancy) and 'nasb' is that 'fadla' is a meaningful, intelligible property that does not vary across languages, while 'nasb' is a conventional matter dependent on the manner of utterance. Therefore, the validity of defining 'hal' by 'fadla' does not extend to 'nasb'. The passage concludes by reinforcing the principle that including a ruling in a definition creates a contradiction: the definiendum is both defined by it (which requires restriction and depiction) and ruled upon by it (which requires assent and information). This conflict makes it impossible.

Permissible Properties vs. Prohibited Rulings in Definitions
Distinguishes between permissible properties and prohibited rulings in definitions, using the example of 'hal'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a permissible property (khassa) and a prohibited ruling (hukm) in a definition based on the listener's prior knowledge and the nature of the property.

Core Idea
A property is permissible in a definition only if the listener already knows it is specific to the essence; otherwise it becomes a corrupting ruling.

The commentator builds on the previous principle, now focusing on the difference between properties (khawass) that are allowed in definitions and rulings (ahkam) that are not. The key is whether the listener already knows the property is specific to the definiendum, as illustrated by the grammatical example of 'hal' (circumstantial accusative) and its distinction between 'fadla' (redundant description) and 'nasb' (accusative case).

Guided Path
1
Understand the listener's prior knowledge
The commentator explains that properties are permissible only if the listener already knows they are specific to the essence. This prior knowledge means the property is not functioning as a new ruling but as a distinguisher.
Example: A student who knows the nominative nouns can be told that the subject is a specific kind of nominative noun.
2
Identify the nature of the property: meaningful vs. conventional
The property 'fadla' (redundant description) is a meaningful, intelligible concept that applies across languages, while 'nasb' (accusative case) is a conventional matter specific to Arabic morphology. The meaningful property can be part of the concept; the conventional one is an external accident.
3
Apply the distinction to a concrete case: defining 'hal'
The concept of 'hal' includes its being a redundant description (fadla) that clarifies the state. This is part of the concept. However, the accusative case (nasb) is not part of the concept; it is an accidental property that may or may not accompany it. Therefore, defining 'hal' by 'fadla' is valid, but including 'nasb' would be including a ruling that may not be known without prior conceptualization.
Ibn Malik included 'nasb' in his definition of 'hal', which the commentator criticizes as problematic.
4
Check for circularity and redundancy
If a property is not known to be specific to the essence, including it in the definition creates either circularity (if it is a ruling) or redundancy (hashw) that corrupts the definition. The commentator states that even if the property is not intended as a part of the definition, its presence is corrupting redundancy.

If you say: What is the difference between specific rulings pertaining to the subject of the ruling and those properties (khawass) by which definition is permitted?
I say: The properties by which definition is valid are those which the listener already knows to be specific to the essence, and the essence is distinguished for him by them. So they are not mentioned to him in the definition from the perspective of the ruling about them on the defined term, such that he would need to conceptualize it before them, but rather to distinguish it for him. Unlike rulings, for they are not known to be established except after conceptualizing the subject of the ruling without them.
For example: One who knows the nominative cases and they are distinguished for him, and asks about what distinguishes the subject among them, it is valid to define for him 'the subject' as: 'a noun in the nominative case due to a preceding verb or what takes its place.' So if the ruling is included in the definition, it is not conceptualized in that more complete manner except after the ruling upon it, so circularity is necessary with respect to his purpose. This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a section regarding definitions (al-ma'rifat). It addresses the prohibition of including rulings (ahkam) in definitions and clarifies the distinction between rulings and properties (khawass) that are permissible in definitions. The commentator then elaborates on Ibn Malik's definition: the accusative case is an accident of words, not of the concept (mafhum) that is the subject of definitions. It is not a necessary concomitant (lazim bayyin) because verifying the conceptual parts does not necessitate the accusative case. Hence, the accusative is an accident of external referents (masadiq kharijiyya), not of the concept. Further objections about the accusative case being established for 'hal' are dismissed by distinguishing between what is merely established and what is necessary and evident. Therefore, the validity of defining 'hal' by 'fadla' does not extend to 'nasb'. The passage concludes by reinforcing the principle that including a ruling in a definition creates a contradiction: the definiendum is both defined by it (which requires restriction and depiction) and ruled upon by it (which requires assent and information). This conflict makes it impossible.

The Rule of 'Aw in Definitions and Descriptions
Commentator explains the eighth condition: 'aw for division is prohibited in hadd but allowed in rasm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the permissibility of 'aw for division in true definitions (hadd) and in descriptions (rasm), and identify which types of 'aw are prohibited in all definitions.

Core Idea
A single essence cannot have two differentiae, so 'or' for division is impossible in true definitions.

In this passage, the commentator explains a subtle but crucial condition for definitions: the word 'aw (or) is not allowed in true definitions because a single reality cannot have two differentiae. However, in descriptions, 'aw for division is permissible because a thing may have multiple properties. This lesson will help you apply this rule to distinguish proper definitions from improper ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of 'aw
First, note that the passage discusses 'aw al-taqsimiyya (the 'or' of division), which indicates alternative possibilities. Other types like 'aw for doubt or ambiguity are always prohibited.
The passage explicitly restricts the discussion to 'aw for division.
2
Understand why 'aw is impossible in hadd
A true definition (hadd haqiqi) consists of genus and differentia. A single essence cannot have two differentiae because each differentia would produce a distinct essence. Therefore, 'aw for division cannot appear in a true definition.
The commentator refutes the claim that division is permissible in definitions.
3
Recognize when 'aw is allowed in rasm
In descriptions (rusum), one thing can have multiple properties. 'Aw for division is allowed when two properties together cover all instances, but each alone does not. Example: defining 'man' as 'animal, Arab or non-Arab'.
Using 'wa' (and) is better, as Ibn Malik said, because the two properties together constitute one property.
4
Distinguish 'aw for division from other types
Other types of 'aw (doubt, ambiguity, hesitation, permission) are prohibited in all definitions because they conflict with the purpose of clarification. The passage emphasizes this restriction.
The condition for 'aw of hesitation or permission is a request, which has no place in definition.

He explains that for a single essence, there can only be one perfect definition, though it may be expressed with different levels of detail. However, for descriptions (rusum), multiple specific properties can be combined using division, as long as they collectively cover the definition. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its logical foundation.

Using 'Aw' and 'Waw' in Definitions
Commentary on the use of particles in definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the permissible 'aw' of division and other types of 'aw', and explain when it is preferable to use 'waw' instead.

Core Idea
Only 'aw' of division is allowed in definitions; but even then, 'waw' is better because it combines properties into one.

In this passage, the commentator discusses the correct particle to use when joining two specific properties in a definition. He restricts 'aw' to division (taqsim), forbidding 'aw' of doubt, ambiguity, perplexity, or permission. Furthermore, he suggests that 'waw' is even better than 'aw' of division, as it treats the combined properties as a single property. This lesson examines the rules and the reasoning behind the preference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of 'aw'
From the passage, extract the three categories: 'aw' of division (taqsim), 'aw' of doubt/ambiguity (shakk/ibham), and 'aw' of perplexity/permission (tahyir/ibaha). Underline how each is defined.
Doubt implies uncertainty; permission implies choice; division implies alternatives that cover all possibilities.
2
Why non-division 'aw' is prohibited
The commentator says non-division 'aw' is not permissible because it contradicts the purpose (gharad) of definition, which is clarity. Doubt or ambiguity would defeat that purpose. Also, perplexity and permission require a request (talab), which is not present in a definition.
Consider: a definition should be unambiguous and definitive.
3
When 'waw' is preferred over 'aw' of division
Ibn Malik and the commentator argue that when two specific properties together form a single specific property (khassa wahida), 'waw' is better because it joins them into one unit. The example from grammar: definition of istithna' uses 'aw', but 'waw' would emphasize the combined function.
Not all cases of 'aw' of division are bad; but 'waw' is generally superior for uniting properties.
4
Consider the exception with qarina
A later gloss by Shaykh al-Tayyib states that if a contextual indicator (qarina) clearly shows the intended meaning, even 'aw' of perplexity or permission may be used. This shows that the rule is not absolute.
The qarina removes ambiguity, making the definition clear despite using a potentially ambiguous particle.

Definitions Are Not Proven by Demonstration – Formal and Material Defects
Explains epistemological status of definitions and lists their defects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material defects in a definition and explain why definitions are not proven by demonstration.

Core Idea
Definitions convey essences, not judgments; they are evaluated by defects, not proofs.

In this passage, the author clarifies a crucial epistemological point: a definition (hadd) is not something that can be proven by a syllogistic demonstration (burhan). Instead, it can only be critiqued by pointing out defects in its form or content, or by offering a better definition. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly evaluating definitions in any Islamic science.

Guided Path

Identify the main claim of the tanbih
Read the opening note carefully. The author states that a definition is not acquired by demonstration. This means you cannot demand a proof for a definition; the definer is not required to produce a burhan.

2

The reason given: demonstration seeks to affirm or negate a predicate of a subject, whereas a definition is the very essence (haqiqa) of the defined, merely expressed in detail. Thus a definition is not a predication to be proved.

3

If the statement is intended to convey the quiddity (mahiyya) of human, it is a definition and not subject to proof. If it is intended as a report of linguistic or legal usage, it becomes a judgment (hukm) that can be challenged and requires evidence from tradition.
Thus the same expression can function differently depending on the intention.

The 'Unwan: Title of the Subject in Propositions
Defines 'unwan as the description and concept of the subject, which can be the complete quiddity, part, or external.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the 'unwan of a subject in a proposition and classify it as the complete quiddity, a part, or external to the quiddity.

Core Idea
The title of a subject is the concept through which individuals are known, whether essential or accidental.

In Islamic logic, every proposition has a subject term, but what exactly does that term represent? The 'unwan (title) is the conceptual description that identifies the individuals the proposition is about. This lesson unpacks how the 'unwan can be the full essence, a part, or even an external attribute, using examples from the logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'unwan (العنوان). The text explicitly states: 'والعنوان هو وصف الموضوع ومفهومه'. So 'unwan is the definiendum.
2
Extract the definition
The definition is that it is the description (وصف) and concept (مفهوم) of the subject (الموضوع). The commentator notes that these terms are synonymous and refer to the literal signification (مدلول مطابقي) of the subject term.
The commentator suggests it would be more precise to say 'the concept of the subject is its description and title'.
3
Examine the three types
The 'unwan can be the complete quiddity, part of it, or external. Use the examples: 'human' for complete quiddity (كل إنسان حيوان), 'animal' for part (كل حيوان جسم), 'laughing being' for external (كل ضاحك إنسان). Each shows a different relation to the individuals' essence.
The examples illustrate the flexibility of the 'unwan in predication.
4
Understand the function
The 'unwan identifies individuals just as a book title identifies the book. The ruling in a proposition applies to the individuals that fall under the 'unwan, not to the 'unwan itself. For example, in 'Every laughing being is human', the predicate 'human' applies to individuals that are laughing beings, but the 'unwan 'laughing being' is not the quiddity.
This clarifies why the title is called 'unwan - because individuals are known by it.
5
Distinguish concept from individuals
The 'unwan is the concept, while the individuals are the dhat (essence) of the subject. The text distinguishes between the concept (what is called the title) and the individuals (ذات الموضوع). This is crucial for understanding predication in logic.
The judgment is about the individuals, not about the concept as such.

Defining Tamthil: The Logicians' Framework
Author defines tamthil and explains its components.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the logicians' definition of tamthil from the legal definition of qiyas and identify the core components of the analogical proof.

Core Idea
Tamthil is a proof based on equality of cause, not the act of analogy itself.

In this passage, the author presents the logicians' definition of tamthil (analogical reasoning), citing al-Sa'd and al-Subki. You will learn that tamthil is not merely the act of attaching one particular to another, but rather a proof that relies on the equality of their effective causes ('illa). The author also clarifies that what logicians call 'tamthil' is often called 'qiyas' by legal theorists, but warns against conflating the two.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions given
Find the definitions from al-Sa'd and al-Subki in the Arabic text. Al-Sa'd defines tamthil as establishing a ruling in a particular because it is established in another particular due to a common attribute. Al-Subki defines it as attaching a known thing to a known thing due to equality in the ratio of the ruling.
Both definitions emphasize a shared cause ('illa) between the two particulars.
2
Extract the key components of tamthil
From the definitions, identify: the original case (asl), the new case (far'), the ruling (hukm), and the shared attribute (al-ma'na al-mushtarak). In the example, wine (khamr) is the original, nabidh is the new, the ruling is prohibition (hurma), and the shared attribute is intoxication (iskar).
3
Contrast tamthil with qiyas usuli
Note that legal theorists (usuliyyun) call this 'qiyas,' but the author says this is a lax usage. For logicians, tamthil is the proof (dalil) itself, whereas the act of attaching (ilhaq) is the signification (madlul). The real proof is the equality in the cause, not the attachment.
This distinction prevents confusion between the logical argument and the legal methodology.
4
Understand 'relative particular' (al-juz'i al-idafi)
The passage clarifies that 'particular' here means relative particular, not actual particular. So 'nabidh' can be considered particular relative to the more general 'intoxicant,' even though it is technically a universal (kulli) in itself. This allows tamthil to apply to general terms.
This nuance ensures that analogical reasoning can use general categories as cases.

Mujarrabat and the Hidden Syllogism
Definition of mujarrabat and the hidden syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of mujarrabat and reconstruct the hidden syllogism that converts repeated observation into certain knowledge.

Core Idea
Repeated observation plus a hidden syllogism yields certain causal knowledge, not mere sense perception.

In the study of proofs (hujaj), mujarrabat (experiential propositions) are a key source of certainty. The author explains that sense alone only gives knowledge of particulars; universal judgments require reason aided by repeated observation. This lesson dissects the hidden syllogism that underlies such judgments, showing how we move from 'this fire is hot' to 'every fire is hot' using a causal inference.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sense from rational judgment
Notice that sense only informs about particulars (e.g., 'this fire is hot'). The universal judgment ('every fire is hot') is a rational judgment composed of sense and reason.
This sets up why mujarrabat are not purely empirical.
2
Define mujarrabat
Mujarrabat are propositions that reason judges by combining repeated observation with a hidden syllogism that produces certainty about a cause-effect relationship.
The key is the hidden syllogism, not just repetition.
3
Extract the hidden syllogism
The hidden syllogism is: repeated uniform occurrence implies a cause; whenever the cause exists, the effect exists. This is a causal inference that eliminates coincidence.
The syllogism is 'hidden' because it is not always stated explicitly.
4
Apply to the example of saqamuniya
The judgment 'saqamuniya purges yellow bile' arises from repeated observation of purgation. The hidden syllogism: 'This drink causes purgation; if it were coincidental, it would not be constant; but it is constant, so it is not coincidental but causal.' Then explicitly: 'This is saqamuniya, every saqamuniya is a purgative for yellow bile, therefore this is a purgative.'
This shows how the hidden syllogism becomes a categorical syllogism.

Fallacy of Judging Genus by Species (Ibhām al-‘Aks)
Defines the fallacy of inferring a genus has a property because one species does.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of judging a genus by a property of its species (ibhām al-‘aks) and distinguish it from valid converse inferences.

Core Idea
A property of one species does not automatically belong to the entire genus.

The logicians have identified a subtle fallacy where one incorrectly extends a property from a specific species to the entire genus. This error arises from assuming that because a universal statement is true, its converse is also true. In this lesson, we will examine the definition of ibhām al-‘aks through classical examples and learn how to detect it in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the fallacy
Notice that the argument takes a property true for a specific type (species) and applies it to the whole category (genus). For example, 'the horse is an animal' places the species under the genus, then erroneously attributes rationality (a property of one species) to all animals.
2
Recognize the mistaken converse assumption
The fallacy occurs because the arguer assumes that the converse of a true universal is also true. From 'every rational being is an animal' (true), they infer 'every animal is rational' (false). This is the logical error.
3
Test with an Islamic example
Consider: 'Ali is a human; every human is a Muslim; therefore Ali is a Muslim.' The premise 'every Muslim is a human' is true, but its converse 'every human is a Muslim' is false. The argument commits ibhām al-‘aks by assuming the converse.

Identifying the Fallacy of Musadara (Begging the Question)
Defines musadara via synonym substitution with example and objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of the musadara fallacy where the conclusion is used as a premise via synonym substitution.

Core Idea
Musadara makes the claim part of the evidence by disguising the conclusion as a premise.

The fallacy of musadara, or begging the question, occurs when the arguer assumes what they are trying to prove. In this passage, al-Bannani explains a specific subtype: using a synonym for the conclusion as a premise. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to detect circular reasoning in theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim to be proved
Find the conclusion (natija) in the example: 'every human is laughing'.
2
Examine the premises for circularity
Check if the major premise ('every mortal is laughing') is a synonym of the conclusion. Here 'mortal' (bashar) is said to be synonymous with 'human' (insan).
3
Apply the test of opponent's concession
If the opponent concedes the major premise, he must concede the conclusion; otherwise the argument is circular.
4
Consider the response to synonymy objection
If the opponent denies synonymy, the syllogism fails and one must resort to linguistic evidence, not logic.
This shows that musadara is a material fallacy (error in matter) rather than formal.

Commentary
This passage defines a specific type of logical fallacy called 'musadara' (begging the question or circular reasoning) that occurs in the material of proof. The fallacy is committed when the conclusion is made into one of the premises by simply changing the wording to a synonym. The author gives an example: to prove that every human is laughing (ḍāḥik), one says 'every human is a mortal (bashar)' and 'every mortal is laughing', yielding 'every human is laughing'. Here the conclusion is identical in meaning to the major premise ('every mortal is laughing') because 'human' and 'mortal' are synonyms. If the opponent accepts the major premise, he cannot reject the conclusion. This is called musadara because it makes the claim itself part of the evidence. The text then raises an objection: the opponent might accept the major premise but deny the conclusion due to thinking 'human' and 'mortal' are not synonyms. The answer is that if he concedes the truth of the major premise, he necessarily concedes the conclusion's truth; any further dispute is merely about terminology, not logic. The commentary by al-Bannani (or the hashiya author) then discusses a point about the classification of this fallacy. The author (al-Nazim) considered this as a subtype of 'confusion of false with true', following Ibn al-Hajib. However, the commentator argues that it should be an independent division, as al-Katibi and others did. The hashiya then clarifies that although the literal wording may give the impression of a different statement, the error in matter is real. It also notes that if the opponent denies the synonymy, the syllogism does not help; one must rely on linguistic evidence. The hashiya ends with a criticism of Ibn Mansur's attempted defense, considering it far-fetched.

Core Idea

Core Idea
Burhan demands certainty; a probable or widely accepted premise breaks the proof.

In this short passage, the author identifies a critical material fallacy: using a premise that is not certain in a demonstration (burhan) that aims for certain knowledge. While such premises are acceptable in dialectical or rhetorical arguments, they render a demonstrative proof invalid. Understanding this distinction is essential for rigorous reasoning in Islamic theology and jurisprudence, where certain premises are required for conclusive proofs.
