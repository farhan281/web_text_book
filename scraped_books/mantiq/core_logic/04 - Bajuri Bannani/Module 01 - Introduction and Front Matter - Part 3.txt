Verbal Disagreement on the Ruling of Logic: Khilaf fi al-Hal
Resolving the apparent conflict by identifying it as a verbal disagreement (khilaf fi al-hal).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of 'khilaf fi al-hal' as applied to the ruling on logic and identify the philosophical corruptions that were removed.

Core Idea
The dispute over logic is verbal; prohibitors target corrupted content, permitors target purified form.

How can highly respected scholars disagree on whether studying logic is forbidden or recommended? This passage reveals that the disagreement is not real but verbal—both sides agree on the essence once the corrupted elements are removed. Let's explore this.

Guided Path
1
Locate the key phrase 'فَيَكُونُ الخِلافُ فِي حَالٍ'
This phrase is the commentator's conclusion. It means the disagreement is in form (i.e., verbal), not in reality. Write down this Arabic phrase.
2
Understand the hypothetical scenario
The commentator says: if the prohibitors saw logic today (purified), they would permit it; if the permitors saw the original corrupted logic, they would prohibit it. This shows both sides agree on the substance—disagreement is about which version they refer to.
3
List the corruptions mentioned
The text lists: statements about the separate genus (al-jins al-mufrad), example of the separate intellect (al-'aql al-mufariq) as a simple substance, division into ten intellects, and example of the infinite universal with the movement of the spheres. Identify these.
These are specifically philosophical doctrines from the Neoplatonic tradition.
4
Identify the role of the imams
The imams 'purified' (hadhdhabū) logic, removing these corruptions. Once the cause (these corruptions) is removed, its effect (prohibition) is also removed. So the ruling becomes recommendation.

Commentary
This passage is a front_matter discussion on the legal ruling (hukm) of studying the science of logic (mantiq). It begins by stating that a chapter on the permissibility of engaging in logic, and notes that al-Suyuti attributed the prohibition to most scholars. The commentator then presents a critique from 'one of the researchers' (identified as al-Hilali) who argues that the claim of absolute prohibition is invalid. The researcher gives two scenarios: either those who prohibit logic do so out of ignorance of its nature and benefit, which is a judgment without proper conception and thus false; or they know it well, in which case their statements must be interpreted as referring only to the excessive philosophical elements that are corrupt, not to logic itself as purified by Sunni imams. The passage then refutes two arguments for prohibition: first, that logic is a foreign science — but many foreign sciences like astronomy and medicine have been adopted; second, that logic is innate — but this is a straw man, as what is innate is natural reasoning, not technical terminology. The commentary then explains that al-Suyuti initially prohibited logic but retracted after debate. It also narrates that Ibn al-Salah practiced logic for twenty years with no success and then prohibited it, but this is an exception. The passage then asserts that the real disagreement is verbal, not substantive: if logic is purified of philosophical corruptions (like the separate intellect and ten intellects), even the prohibitors would permit it. The second opinion is that logic is recommended (mandub), supported by Ghazali, Ibn Arafa, al-Sanusi, and others. This is declared the truth because logic is a means to both recommended and obligatory sciences. However, it is not itself obligatory because many scholars acquired obligatory knowledge without formal logic. The claim that acquiring knowledge without logic is a miracle is refuted; it is actually common. The passage ends with al-Ghazali's strong statement that one who does not know logic cannot be trusted in his knowledge, indicating its importance. The commentator clarifies that the apparent disagreement is merely about the form of logic, not its essence.

The Ruling on Studying Logic: Recommended or Obligatory?
Author defends the recommended status of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why studying logic is considered recommended (mandub) rather than obligatory (wajib) and distinguish the verbal disagreement among scholars on this issue.

Core Idea
Logic is a means, not a necessity; obligatory sciences are attainable without it.

In this passage, the author establishes the second opinion regarding the legal ruling on studying logic: it is recommended (mandub), not obligatory. He supports this view with evidence that logic is a means (wasila) to acquire sciences, some of which are obligatory, yet it is not a necessary condition for them. Many scholars have mastered obligatory sciences without studying logic. The author also explains that the apparent disagreement among scholars is merely verbal (lafzi) – if the object of debate were refined, all parties would agree. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of this argument and its implications for Islamic learning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three ruling positions on logic
The author mentions that there are three opinions: forbidden (haram), obligatory (wajib), and recommended (mandub). Locate where each is hinted in the text and note the proponents of the recommended view: al-Ghazali, Ibn Arafa, al-Sanusi, and others.
The passage does not list the forbidden opinion explicitly, but it is referenced in the discussion of verbal disagreement.
2
Analyze the key argument for 'recommended'
The author argues: (1) Logic is a means (wasila) to acquiring sciences. (2) Some of those sciences are obligatory (like beliefs) and some are recommended. (3) However, logic is not obligatory because acquiring obligatory sciences is not dependent on logic – countless scholars attained them without studying logic. Trace this reasoning and note the counterarguments from al-Yusi and Sharh al-Matali'.
The phrase 'laysa mawqufan 'alayhi' (not dependent on it) is crucial – logic is a useful tool but not a prerequisite.
3
Examine the nature of the verbal disagreement (khilaf lafzi)
The author explains that if those who forbid logic today saw it after the Imams purified it from corrupting philosophical elements, they would permit it. Conversely, if it remained in its original state, those who permit it would forbid it. This shows the disagreement is about the state of logic, not its essence.
The corrupting elements include the concept of a separate genus, separate intellects, ten intellects, and using celestial motion as an example of an infinite universal.
4
Apply the principle to contemporary Islamic sciences
Consider a modern debate: some argue that studying logic is necessary for usul al-fiqh or kalam. How would the author's reasoning address this? Would it change if logic were essential for understanding a specific obligatory science today?

Commentary
This passage presents and defends the second opinion regarding the ruling on studying logic: that it is recommended (mandub) rather than obligatory or forbidden. The author begins by listing the proponents: al-Ghazali, Ibn Arafa (as transmitted by al-Abi), al-Sanusi, and others. He asserts that this is the correct view because logic is a means (wasila) to acquiring sciences, some of which are themselves recommended or obligatory. However, it is not obligatory despite leading to obligatory knowledge like beliefs, because the acquisition of obligatory sciences is not dependent on logic. Countless scholars have attained such knowledge without studying logic; therefore, acquiring knowledge without logic is not a miraculous anomaly but a common occurrence. The author then cites al-Sanusi in support, contrasting with al-Yusi's gloss and the claim of obligation in Sharh al-Matali'. The commentary that follows explains that the disagreement among scholars is merely verbal (khilaf lafzi): if those who today forbid logic observed that it is now refined by the Imams, they would permit it; conversely, if it remained in its original form with corrupting philosophical elements (e.g., separate genus, separate intellects, ten intellects, infinite universal exemplified by celestial motion), those who permit it would forbid it. Since the Imams have purified it from such corruptions, the cause of prohibition is removed. The commentary also clarifies that al-Ghazali's statement about logic being necessary for trustworthy knowledge does not imply obligation; rather, al-Ghazali himself considered it recommended. The note at the end quotes al-Ghazali and al-Makari extolling the value of logic.

Logic Permissible Only for the Qualified
Author presents the opinion that logic is permissible only for those with sound intellect and knowledge of Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions for permissibility of studying logic according to al-Subki, and explain why logic may be unnecessary for the intellectually perfect.

Core Idea
Logic is a tool, not a goal; its permissibility hinges on the learner's sound intellect and prior grasp of revelation.

This lesson unpacks a crucial debate in Islamic intellectual history: under what conditions is it permissible to study formal logic? The author presents the view of Imam Taqi al-Din al-Subki, who restricts permissibility to those with sound intellect and existing knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah. Along the way, you'll see how the commentators refine this position and why logic is ultimately seen as a tool, not an end in itself.

Guided Path

The opinion states permissibility for 'one who is confident in the soundness of his intellect and has practiced the Book and the Sunnah'. Extract these two conditions: صحة ذهنه (sound intellect) and ممارسة الكتاب والسنة (practice of Quran and Sunnah).
These conditions show that logic is not for beginners or those with weak reasoning.

The author explains that the claim of individual obligation (وجوب عيني) is very weak. Why? Because it assumes that detailed proof of God is obligatory for everyone and that logic is the only way to it. Both assumptions are rejected. Notice how the commentator dismisses this view.

The passage states that logic is an 'ala (tool) that leads to practicing the Book and Sunnah. The commentator adds that if one has already obtained the goal (understanding revelation) without the tool, then studying logic is useless (تحصيل الحاصل). Why? Because it would be like obtaining what is already obtained.
This reveals a key principle: means are justified only if the end is not yet achieved.
5
Evaluate the overall position
Based on the passage, decide: For whom is logic useful? For the deficient person lacking perfect intellect. For whom is it unnecessary? For the perfect intellect (kamil al-qariha) who already understands revelation, like the Salaf. Does this make logic forbidden? No, only conditionally permissible.
This nuanced position avoids the extremes of absolute permission or prohibition.

The Ruling on Studying Logic: A Critical Refinement
The author discusses the three opinions on the permissibility of studying logic and defends the third opinion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument against the necessity of logic and evaluate its implications for Islamic epistemology.

Core Idea
Logic is a tool, not a condition for knowledge; the Salaf's certainty proves it non-essential.

In this passage, al-Bajuri critically examines the ruling on studying logic. He reviews the three opinions—personal obligation, collective obligation, and conditional permissibility—and systematically deconstructs the strongest claim for its necessity. His argument centers on the example of the Salaf, who achieved complete knowledge without formal logic, and on the instrumental nature of logic itself. This lesson dissects his reasoning and its place in the broader Ash'ari critique of Greek logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three opinions
Find the three distinct rulings on studying logic mentioned in the passage: personal obligation, collective obligation, and permissibility for those with sound mind and prior Shari'a knowledge.
2
Analyze the weakness of personal obligation
The author says it is 'extremely weak' and that its basis (that logic is necessary for detailed knowledge of Allah) is 'not conceded.' What assumption is he rejecting? The assumption that detailed knowledge of Allah requires formal logic.
The author does not engage with the epistemological theory behind the claim; he simply labels it as weak and non-conceded.
3
Understand the refutation of al-Subki's view
The author states that the Salaf had complete innate intelligence (qariha) and were independent of logic. How does this counter the permissibility view? Even permissibility is not based on need; the Salaf's example shows it is unnecessary for the complete person.
The author corrects al-Subki's selection by pointing to the Salaf's practice.
4
Examine the instrument argument
Logic is an instrument to achieve practice of the Book and Sunnah. If one already has them, logic is useless. Why does the author call this 'obtaining the obtained'? Because learning the cause (logic) after the effect (knowledge) is redundant.
This is a core argument against any compulsory tool.

Core Idea
A definition of 'definition' is possible without infinite regress.
