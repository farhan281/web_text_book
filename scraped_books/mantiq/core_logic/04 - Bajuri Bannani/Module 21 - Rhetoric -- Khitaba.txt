Khataba: Rhetorical Syllogism and Its Premises
Author defines khataba as a syllogism from accepted or probable premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of premises in a rhetorical syllogism and distinguish accepted premises from probable ones according to the text.

Core Idea
Rhetoric in Islamic logic is a valid syllogism from accepted or probable premises, not mere emotional speech.

In this passage, al-Bajuri defines rhetoric (khataba) as a legitimate syllogistic art, not as empty eloquence. He classifies its premises into accepted (maqbulat) and probable (maznunat). Understanding this classification is essential for evaluating arguments in sermons, theological debates, and advice from authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined: khataba (rhetoric). Note that it is defined as a type of syllogism.
The Arabic term is الخطابة.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is qiyas (syllogism). The differentia is that its premises are either maqbulat (accepted) or maznunat (probable).
This distinguishes rhetoric from demonstrative (burhani) and dialectical (jadali) syllogisms.
3
Analyze the subtypes of maqbulat
Accepted premises are further divided: those accepted due to a celestial reason (e.g., prophets) and those due to apparent merit (e.g., scholars, righteous people). Even anonymous proverbs count as maqbulat.
Celestial reason implies divine endowment; apparent merit is recognized knowledge or action.
4
Understand the commentary on jadal
The commentary clarifies that mashhurat and musallamat are used not for certainty but to convince or obligate opponents, even if they are based on certain truths.
This shows that dialectic and rhetoric serve different epistemic goals from demonstration.

وحاصله : أنَّ المشهورات والمسلمات لا يعتبر فيها اليقين، وعدم اعتبار الشَّيء ليس اعتبار لعدمه، فقضايا الجدل تؤخذ من حيث إنَّها مشهورة أو مسلّمة، وإن كانت في الواقع يقينية أو أوَّليَّة. قوله : (قَطْعُ الخُصُوم) أي : إلزام الخصم وإفحامه إن كان صاحبه معترضاً، ودفع إلزامه إن كان مستدلا، وإقناع القاصر عن البرهان اليقيني؛ قال في الشفاء : ومنهم المتعلم يقصر عن إدراك مقول معلمه، ويأخذ معه في الحجيج، فيأتي له المعلم بقياس جدلي أو مشهور.
As for rhetoric (khataba), it is a syllogism composed of premises that are either accepted (maqbulah) or probable (maznunah). Al-maqbulah are: propositions that people receive with acceptance, because they originate from a person for whom they have a good opinion, due to a celestial reason, or due to his being distinguished by an apparent merit [1/64] of knowledge or action. The celestial reason: as you see in some people, God Most High adorns them with the ornament of acceptance and love, and people receive what comes from them, whether it is true or not. The apparent merit: like propositions taken from the righteous, the scholars, and the worshippers. And propositions may be accepted even if not attributed to anyone, like many proverbs and widespread sayings; being well-known (mashhura) does not remove them from the category of accepted propositions.
[Commentary:]
The summary: that al-mashhurat and al-musallamat do not involve certainty, and not considering a thing is not a consideration of its non-existence. Thus, the propositions of dialectic are taken insofar as they are mashhura or musallamah, even if in reality they are certain or primary. His saying: 'to silence opponents' means: to obligate the opponent and confound him if he is objecting, and to repel his obligation if he is inferring; and to convince the one incapable of certain demonstration. As stated in al-Shifa: Among them is the learner who falls short of grasping the statement of his teacher and argues with him, so the teacher presents a dialectical or well-known syllogism.

Commentary
This passage defines rhetoric (khataba) as a type of syllogism whose premises are either accepted (maqbulat) or probable (maznunat). The author focuses on maqbulat, explaining that they are propositions people accept due to the source's authority. Two types of sources are distinguished: celestial (like prophets or saints, whom God endows with acceptance) and those with apparent merit (scholars, worshippers). Even anonymous proverbs and widespread sayings are included, and being well-known does not exclude them from being maqbulat. The commentary (hashiya) clarifies that the premises of dialectic (mashhurat and musallamat) are not valued for certainty but for their persuasive function; they may be in reality certain or primary, but they are used as commonplaces or concessions. It then explains the purpose of jadal (dialectic): to obligate the opponent and convince those who cannot grasp certain demonstration, citing Ibn Sina's al-Shifa that a teacher may use dialectical arguments for a learner unable to understand demonstrative proofs.

Rhetoric: A Syllogism of Accepted and Probable Premises
Author defines rhetoric as a syllogism from accepted or probable premises, and gives examples and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a rhetorical syllogism and distinguish between accepted and probable premises in Islamic discourse.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades through accepted authority and probable experience, not certain proof.

Rhetoric (khataba) is one of the five arts of the syllogism in Islamic logic. Unlike demonstration, which yields certainty, rhetoric aims at persuasion in practical matters. This lesson unpacks its definition, the two types of premises it uses, and its purpose, as explained by al-Bajuri in his commentary on al-Sanusi's matn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defiiniendum and genus
Rhetoric is defined as a 'syllogism' (qiyas). What kind of syllogism? One composed of specific premises.
The genus is a syllogistic argument, distinguishing it from other types of discourse.
2
Extract the differentia
The differentia is that the premises are accepted (maqbula) or probable (maznuna). Understood? Let's see what each means.
Accepted premises rely on authority; probable premises rely on widespread experience.
3
Distinguish accepted vs probable with examples
Accepted: 'This is the saying of a righteous person, and all such sayings are true.' Probable: 'This one roams at night with weapons, and everyone like that is a thief.'
The accepted premise example involves a chain of authority; the probable is based on experience.
4
Note the purpose of rhetoric
The goal is 'targhib' (encouraging benefit) and 'tarhib' (discouraging harm) in religion and worldly affairs.
This is a practical, not theoretical, aim.
5
Formalize one example as a categorical syllogism
Take the accepted premise example: 'Everything so-and-so says is true. This saying is from so-and-so. Therefore this saying is true.' This is a valid first-figure syllogism (Barbara).
Formalization helps see the logical structure behind the rhetorical argument.

His saying: (As for rhetoric, it is a syllogism composed...) the author did not mention what it is composed of, and our master Sayyidi Hamdun clarified it by his saying: Then rhetoric, its composition is seen from accepted and probable premises. His saying: (due to a heavenly cause) al-Shirazi interpreted it as miracle and grace with respect to prophets and saints, and al-Sanusi interpreted it as that which is not known, as you see in some people, God beautifies them with the adornment of acceptance without being distinguished by an apparent merit, and this is more apparent, so the commentator relied on it. His saying: (taken from the righteous) means: such as saying: this is the saying of so-and-so, and everything so-and-so says is true, therefore this saying is true. And as for the probable, they are propositions whose truth becomes preponderant in the mind, like our saying: 'This one roams at night with weapons, and everyone who roams at night with weapons is a thief.' And the purpose of rhetoric is: encouraging what is beneficial and discouraging what is harmful in religion and worldly affairs, as is the practice of orators. His saying: (like our saying: this one roams at night... etc.) its parallel: 'This wall has dust falling from it, and every wall that has dust falling from it is about to collapse' yields 'This wall is about to collapse.' His saying: (as is the practice of orators) means: therefore the orator and preacher, as it is said, needs to have various methods to persuade the common people, and various tricks to use to make his speech take hold in their souls, without departing from the path of the rightly-guided guides.

Commentary
This passage defines rhetoric (khataba) as a syllogism composed of accepted (maqbula) or probable (maznuna) premises. The commentator first notes that the author of the matn did not specify the components, so Shaykh Hamdun supplied them: rhetoric is built from accepted and probable premises. The commentator then explains the two types of accepted premises: those accepted due to a heavenly cause (like respect for prophets and saints, interpreted as miracles or an unexplainable grace from God) and those due to an apparent merit (like the sayings of righteous people, scholars, and worshippers). The example given: 'This is the saying of so-and-so, and everything so-and-so says is true, therefore this saying is true.' This shows that rhetorical syllogisms rely on authority. Next, probable premises are defined as propositions whose truth is more likely in the mind, such as 'Whoever roams at night with a weapon is a thief.' Such premises are not certain but are convincing. The purpose of rhetoric is practical: to encourage what is beneficial and discourage what is harmful in both religious and worldly matters, as orators do. The commentator gives an analogous example: a wall with dust falling is likely to collapse. Finally, the commentator notes that an orator or preacher needs various persuasive methods and rhetorical tricks to influence the common people, all within the bounds of Islamic guidance. This highlights the pragmatic ethical use of rhetoric.

Key Terms
خَطَابَة (khaṭāba): Rhetoric; a syllogism composed of accepted or probable premises, aiming to persuade in practical matters.
مَقْبُولَة (maqbūla): Accepted proposition; a premise that people accept due to the authority of the speaker, whether from a heavenly cause or apparent merit.
مَظْنُونَة (maẓnūna): Probable proposition; a premise that is deemed likely true based on general experience, not certain.
