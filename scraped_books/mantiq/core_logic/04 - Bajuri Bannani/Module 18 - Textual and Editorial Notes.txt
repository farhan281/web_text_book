Debating the Conjunction: Cause onto Effect or Effect onto Cause?
Debate on causal vs effectual conjunction with final cause clarification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible grammatical coordinations (cause-effect vs effect-cause) and explain the objection and response regarding the final cause interpretation.

Core Idea
The same phrase can be parsed as cause-effect or effect-cause depending on the perspective on final causation.

In classical commentaries, grammatical analysis often reveals deeper logical issues. This passage examines whether a line coordinates the cause with the effect or the effect with its cause, and a critic challenges the final cause interpretation. Learning to follow such dialectical exchanges sharpens your ability to parse nuanced arguments in Islamic logic and usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two grammatical coordinations mentioned
Find the two expressions: 'atf al-sabab 'ala al-musabbab (cause onto effect) and 'atf al-ma'lul 'ala 'illatihi (effect onto cause). Note that the author presents them as alternatives.
2
Locate the critic's objection
Read the objection by 'some' (al-Sabban): the apparent meaning is that the 'caused' or 'final cause' is the exiting of the results, not God's bringing them out. Identify what exactly is being criticized.
The critic argues that the wording suggests the result itself is the cause/effect, not the act of bringing out.
3
Find the response and note its actual author
The response 'it can be said...' is from al-Sabban himself, not the commentator (al-Anbabi clarifies this). The response reframes the cause as considered by its effect (the exiting).
This is a subtle point about attribution: the answer is not from al-Muhanni but from al-Sabban.
4
Explain how the response resolves the objection
The response argues that the coordination is valid because 'bringing out' can be considered a cause or final cause when looking at its effect, i.e., the exiting of results. Thus the grammatical ambiguity is resolved by perspective.
The resolution hinges on the concept of 'consideration of the effect' (i'tibar al-athar).

His statement: 'And He removed... etc.' is coordinated with His statement: 'He brought out... etc.' either as coordination of cause onto effect, or of effect onto its cause, upon its final cause, as is indicated by the words of al-Mallawi in his great commentary. And some criticized this: that the apparent meaning is that the caused and the final cause is the exiting of the results, not God's bringing them out. And it can be said: the intended meaning is that it is a caused or a final cause with consideration of its effect, which is the exiting. His statement: 'And some criticized this' refers to al-Sabban, and the answer that al-Muhanni mentioned in his words 'And it can be said... etc.' is from al-Sabban's words, not from the commentator's, contrary to what might be imagined from his expression. His statement: '(from) coordination of cause onto effect, or effect onto its cause... etc.' is a choice in expression; i.e., you are free to express it with this or this, but a contradiction may appear between something being cause and effect; because being cause implies ordering another thing from it, and being effect implies its ordering from that other, so how is this? And this is repelled by that this contradiction occurs when it is an effect with an efficient cause existing before the thing's occurrence; like: 'human need for water due to thirst, or the like'; because it is an efficient cause for digging and exists before it, and the emergence of water is a final cause subsequent to it. The efficient cause in our case is: reflection on the syllogism and contemplation of it, for it results in the removal of ignorance. As for the exiting of the result and its knowledge, it is final, as the commentator said. And this is an effect with a final cause; i.e., it is an end and something else is arranged upon it, and this is exactly the cause. Similarly, it is said regarding bringing out being a cause and a cause: identical to the removal. That is, if bringing out is interpreted as manifestation or as creation in the mind of the thinker. And that is if bringing out is interpreted as creating the result in the syllogism; whether it occurs in the thinker's mind so that removal occurs, or not.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and causal analysis of the line 'Wa-hatta...' in the matn. It begins by stating that the line is coordinated (ma'tuf) with the previous line 'Akhraja...' either as cause onto effect ('atf al-sabab 'ala al-musabbab) or effect onto cause ('atf al-ma'lul 'ala 'illatihi). The commentator al-Mallawi is cited as supporting the final cause interpretation. A critic (al-Sabban) objects: the apparent meaning is that the 'caused' or 'final cause' is the exiting of the results, not God's bringing them out. The response (from al-Sabban himself, as clarified by al-Anbabi) is that it is considered a cause or final cause by considering its effect, i.e., the exiting. Further, al-Sharshimi discusses the apparent contradiction of something being both cause and effect, resolving it by distinguishing between efficient cause (al-illa al-ba'itha) and final cause (al-illa al-gha'iyya). The efficient cause is the reflection on the syllogism, which leads to the removal of ignorance; the final cause is the knowledge of the result. Thus, something can be both a cause (efficient) and an effect (final) in different aspects.

Distinguishing Efficient and Final Causes in Logical Reflection
Resolution of dual causality using efficient and final cause distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between efficient cause (al-illa al-ba'itha) and final cause (al-illa al-gha'iyya) to a logical argument and explain how one thing can be both a cause and an effect in different respects.

Core Idea
Reflection on the syllogism is the efficient cause of knowledge; knowledge is the final cause of reflection, resolving the paradox of dual causality.

A key challenge in causal reasoning is when the same entity appears as both cause and effect. This passage from the commentary resolves the apparent contradiction by introducing the robust distinction between efficient and final causes. Understanding this distinction is essential for analyzing causal arguments in Islamic logic and law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Find where the text says something can be both cause and effect. The issue arises because being a cause implies ordering something else, and being an effect implies being ordered from something else. Locate this statement.
The contradiction is only apparent; it is resolved by considering two different types of cause.
2
Extract the two types of cause from the passage
The passage mentions 'al-illa al-ba'itha' (efficient cause) and 'al-illa al-gha'iyya' (final cause). Note their definitions: efficient cause is the motive existing before the thing, final cause is the purpose after it.
The example of water and digging illustrates: thirst (efficient cause) leads to digging, and water (final cause) is the purpose.
3
Apply the water-digging example to understand the distinction
Thirst is the efficient cause (existing before digging), digging is the act, and the emergence of water is the final cause (subsequent). Note how a single act (digging) has both a prior motive and a posterior goal.
This shows that something can be both an effect (of thirst) and a cause (of water) from different angles.
4
Relate to the syllogism in the passage
The passage states: the efficient cause here is 'reflection on the syllogism and contemplation', and the final cause is 'the exiting of the result and its knowledge'. Identify these from the text.
Reflection produces knowledge (removal of ignorance) as its effect, but that knowledge is also the goal (final cause) of reflection.
5
Explain how this resolves the contradiction
The same entity (e.g., the act of bringing out or the exiting of results) can be an efficient cause in one respect and a final cause in another, removing the apparent contradiction of being both cause and effect.
This is a standard solution in causal analysis.

The Author's Use of 'نَحْمَدُ' and Its Two Interpretations
Author defends the use of 'نَحْمَدُ' with two interpretations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two linguistic interpretations of the nūn in 'نَحْمَدُ' and explain why both are consistent with the author's humility.

Core Idea
A single Arabic prefix can carry deep theological humility or gratitude.

In this passage, the commentator Bājūrī addresses an apparent objection: why did the author use the plural form 'نَحْمَدُ' (we praise) rather than the singular 'أَحْمَدُ' (I praise)? The objection is based on the two meanings of the nūn prefix. Bājūrī provides two valid answers, both rooted in Arabic grammar and Islamic ethics. Understanding this helps us appreciate how Islamic scholars used linguistic awareness to defend textual choices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find in the Arabic where the objector says the nūn is inappropriate because it can mean either 'speaker with others' or 'speaker glorifying himself'. Why is each problematic for a humble author?
The objector considers both meanings unfitting.
2
Extract the first interpretation
The commentator says the author chose the nūn for mutual praise (المتكلم مع غيره) out of humility. Explain how this shows modesty: the author feels unable to praise Allah alone.
3
Extract the second interpretation
The commentator says the nūn may be for self-glorification (المتكلم المعظم نفسه) to manifest Allah's favor. Explain how this does not contradict humility, as it is a response to the Qur'anic command to report blessings.
Refer to Surah Ad-Duha 11.
4
Synthesize the dialectic
Understand that the commentator does not reject the objection but offers two plausible readings that remove the objection's force. This is a classic jadal (dialectical) move: accepting the opponent's premise but offering alternative explanations.

Defending a Simile: Jadal in Tashbih Analysis
Author defends the simile by analyzing objection and answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange (objection and answer) in a rhetorical critique of a tashbih and evaluate the validity of the simile based on the rules of jadal and tashbih.

Core Idea
Even poetic similes are tested by jadal and tashbih rules to defend against misinformed objections.

This passage from al-Bajuri's commentary shows how a seemingly simple simile can provoke deep theological and linguistic objections. The poet likened the Companions to stars, but a critic objects that the word 'ihtida' (being guided by) is weaker than 'hidaya' (guidance) and that the Companions' spiritual guidance surpasses the stars' sensory guidance, making the comparison invalid. The author, drawing on al-Malawi, defends the simile by distinguishing the passive meaning of 'ihtida' and the specific perspective of the comparison. By following this exchange, you will see how the rules of tashbih (common element, relative strength) and jadal (objection and answer) operate in a real Islamic scholarly discussion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial objection (mu'tarid)
Find the critic's first point: why should the poet have used 'hidaya' instead of 'ihtida'? The critic argues that 'hidaya' describes both the Companions and the stars, making it a better common element (jami'). The objection is that 'ihtida' improperly describes the guided people, not the guides.
The objection relies on the belief that the common element must be a quality inherent in both subjects.
2
Extract the answer (jawab) from al-Malawi
The answer reinterprets 'ihtida' as a passive verbal noun meaning 'being guided by', so it describes the followers, not the guides. However, it still applies to both: the Companions and the stars are things by which others are guided. Thus the common element is preserved.
This is a linguistic defense based on Arabic morphology (masdar al-mabni lil-maf'ul).
3
Recognize the deeper objection
The critic then raises a stronger theological point: the Companions' guidance saves from Hell, which is infinitely greater than the stars' guidance in navigation. According to the rule of tashbih, the point of similarity should be stronger in the compared-to (stars), but here it seems stronger in the compared (Companions).
The rule: 'wajh al-shabah yakun aqwa fi al-mushabbah bihi' (the point of similarity is stronger in the compared-to).
4
Analyze the final answer: distinguishing perspectives

Core Idea
Precise parsing of 'min' and idafa changes the logical scope of definitions.

In this passage, al-Bajuri and al-Anbabi dissect the first line of the poem to clarify the relationship between 'qawa'id' (rules) and 'mantiq' (logic). Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical elements

2
Define the interpretations of idafa
The idafa can be either dependent-to-depended (idafat al-muta'allaq li al-muta'allaq) when 'mantiq' refers only to universal issues, or explicative (bayaniyya) when 'mantiq' also includes partial branches. The 'min' can be partitive (tab'idiyya), meaning 'some of', indicating a part of a whole. Alternatively, it can be interpreted as part of the idafa itself, not adding a partitive sense. The passage mentions four possibilities in total for each occurrence when combining 'min' and idafa.
The exact non-partitive interpretation is not named but is implied by the context of idafa types.

Al-Anbabi shows that when you multiply the four possibilities at one occurrence by the four at the other, you get sixteen combinations. He then tests some combinations: for example, if both idafa and 'min' are bayaniyya, it implies that rules gather all particulars, which is necessary but trivial. If one is bayaniyya and the other partitive, contradictions arise: logic would then only gather some particulars. He saves one combination metaphorically: partial rules develop the ability to apprehend all particulars.
This step trains you to check the logical coherence of grammatical interpretations.

Glossator discusses the validity of the nisba 'Nawawi' and the limits of poetic necessity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all poetic licenses are analogical; some are transmitted and non-extendable.

This lesson examines a real scholarly debate about the nisba 'Nawawi'. The extra alif in this form was defended either as poetic necessity or as a phonetic phenomenon (ishba'). Understanding this distinction is essential for properly applying rules in Arabic grammar and beyond.

Identify the claim about the extra alif

Qiyasi licenses are those the poet may regularly commit by analogy (e.g., declension changes). Samai'i licenses are irregular forms transmitted from the Arabs but not extendable by analogy.

This illustrates how a specific case is adjudicated by the qiyasi/samai'i framework.

Generalize the principle
The same distinction applies in other Islamic sciences: in fiqh, a ruling may be based on qiyas (analogy) or sam' (text). Mistaking samai'i for qiyasi leads to incorrect extensions.

Parsing Pronoun Reference in 'bi-'aksi ma tala'
Commentator analyzes the pronoun reference in 'bi-'aksi ma tala' and resolves ambiguity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct pronoun reference in the phrase 'bi-'aksi ma tala' and explain how it affects the definition of the compound.

Core Idea
Pronoun reference in logical definitions is determined by conceptual logic, not grammatical proximity.

The phrase 'bi-'aksi ma tala' is a key part of defining the compound (murakkab) in this logical commentary. But what does 'tala' (followed) refer to? The author and the commentator disagree on whether the implicit subject pronoun refers to the relative pronoun 'ma' or the compound. This lesson will guide you through the grammatical analysis and show how logical considerations resolve the ambiguity, reinforcing the importance of precision in definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its context
Locate 'bi-'aksi ma tala' in the definition. It describes the compound as 'the opposite of what preceded it'. Determine what 'ma' (what) refers to and what the verb 'tala' (followed) implies about order.
The definition is about the compound being opposite of the single word (mufrad) that preceded it.
2
Understand the two competing interpretations
The author (in his commentary) claims the implicit subject of 'tala' refers to the compound, and the object to the single word. The commentator argues the opposite: subject refers to 'ma' (what), object to the compound. Note the logical difference: who follows whom?
The phrase 'tala' means 'followed' in sequence. The single word normally follows the compound in the definition order.
3
Analyze the argument for the preferred interpretation
The commentator says the single word is subordinate to the compound, so it makes sense that the single word 'follows' the compound. Therefore the implicit subject (the one that follows) should be the single word, represented by 'ma', and the object (the one that is followed) is the compound.
This aligns with the logical reality: the parts (mufrad) come after the whole (murakkab) in the definition.
4
Examine the objection to the author's interpretation
If the compound is the subject of 'tala', then the compound would be following something else, which contradicts the definition. Also, fearing confusion, the pronoun should be explicit if it refers to the compound. Consider why the commentator finds this objectionable.
The objection highlights the danger of ambiguity in definitions when pronouns are implicit.
5
Evaluate the counter-response and its challenge
A defender answers by reinterpreting 'tala' metaphorically as 'connected' (ittaṣala), so both can be said to 'follow'. But this is challenged because the author himself elsewhere used 'tala' as 'tabi'a' (followed in sequence). Decide which interpretation is more logically consistent.
This shows that even figurative language must be used carefully; the metaphor must not undermine the definition's clarity.

In this passage, the author clarifies a debated point in Arabic grammar: the alif that appears before the lam in nisba words like 'Yamani'. Commentary
This passage is a linguistic commentary that examines the correct derivation and form of the logical term 'dhati' (essential). The author begins by citing al-Qamus's position that the ta (feminine suffix) is extra, and from that the proper form should be 'dhawawi' with the elided letter restored. He then quotes Ibn Hisham's claim that the form 'dhati' with ta marbuta is a grammatical error (lahn). The commentary then delves into the philological debate: the original root is 'dhawi' or 'bajumal'? If 'dhawi', the ta is clearly extra; but the laam is elided and replaced by the ta of feminization. The author favors Abu Hayyan's view that 'dha' is a diptote of two-letter origin like 'ma', where the alif is not derived. He supports this with al-Sirafi and Ibn Ya'ish. The discussion then focuses on restoring the elided laam and the rules of inflection in Arabic grammar, with citations from grammatical maxims. Finally, the author questions Ibn Hisham's absolute ruling of 'lahn', noting that Yunus allows it, though he concedes that in the word 'khalifati' the error is agreed upon. The passage underscores the importance of precise linguistic form in technical terminology within Islamic logic.

The Scholar's Bookshelf: Sources Behind a Logical Commentary
Author lists consulted sources across grammar, rhetoric, poetry, and lexicography.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize scholarly references in an Islamic logical commentary into grammar, rhetoric, poetry, and lexicography, and explain why each category is necessary for logical analysis.

Core Idea
A logician's bibliography reveals the linguistic sciences underpinning every argument.

Even a brief commentary on logic, like al-Bannani's Sharh al-Sullam al-Murawnaq, begins with a detailed list of sources. This is not mere formality: every logical claim depends on precise language. The bibliography shows us the exact works a scholar consulted to ensure that his definitions are lexically accurate, his examples are grammatically sound, his metaphors are rhetorically effective, and his evidence is grounded in classical usage. Understanding this list helps you appreciate the interdisciplinary foundation of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories used in the bibliography
Scan the Arabic text and its translation. Notice how the author groups his sources: first grammar (نحو), then rhetoric (بلاغة), then his own commentary works, then poetry collections (دواوين), then dictionaries (معاجم). Each category serves a distinct purpose in logical exposition.
The categories are not always explicitly labeled, but the groupings are clear from the headings.
2
Understand why grammar works are needed
Logic uses terms like 'subject', 'predicate', and 'proposition'. These are rooted in syntax. A logician must consult works like Sibawayh's al-Kitab or Ibn Hisham's al-Masa'il al-Safariyya to ensure that his linguistic analysis matches the rules of Arabic grammar. Errors in grammar can lead to fallacies.
For example, the distinction between 'ism' and 'fi'l' affects how categorical propositions are formed.
3
Understand why rhetoric works are needed
Rhetoric (balagha) deals with clarity, ambiguity, and figurative language. A logician needs works like al-Sa'd's Mukhtasar al-Ma'ani to distinguish between literal and metaphorical statements — a key skill when interpreting Qur'anic verses or prophetic hadith in theological arguments.
Rhetoric helps avoid fallacies like equivocation (ishtirak al-lafz).
4
Understand why poetry and lexicography are needed
Classical poetry (like Diwan al-Farazdaq) provides evidence for linguistic usage — a standard proof in Arabic lexicography. Dictionaries like al-Qamus al-Muhit and al-Sihah give precise definitions. A logician uses these to confirm the exact meaning of a term before building an argument. Without lexical precision, definitions (hadd) become unreliable.
This practice is called 'istishhad' — citation of authoritative usage.
5
Connect the bibliography to the logical text
Now imagine al-Bannani writing a passage on the five universals. He would open al-Jawhari's dictionary to verify the lexical meaning of 'jins' (genus), then check al-Makudi's grammar to ensure his sentence is correct, then consult al-Mutawwal for examples of clear definitions. The bibliography is the toolkit behind every logical move he makes.
