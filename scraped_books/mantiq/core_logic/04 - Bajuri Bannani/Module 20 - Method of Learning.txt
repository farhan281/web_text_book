Condition for Studying Mixed Logic
Explaining the condition for studying mixed logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify who may study mixed logic (al-qism al-thani) based on the prerequisite of knowing true beliefs from false beliefs through practice of Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Only those grounded in Quran and Sunnah can safely study logic mixed with philosophical errors.

This lesson clarifies the necessary qualification for engaging with the second part of logic—the part contaminated with philosophical distortions. Bajuri explains that not everyone may study these texts; one must be a 'mumarris al-sunna wa al-kitab', meaning a person who has internalized the Quran and Sunnah to the point of being able to distinguish true beliefs from false ones. This condition protects the student from being misled, as happened to the Mu'tazila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key phrase
Find the phrase 'ممارس السنة والكتاب' in the Arabic. Note that the commentator is defining this phrase from the base text.
The phrase is used as a condition for studying the second part of logic.
2
Understand what it does not mean
Bajuri excludes the meaning of being an absolute mujtahid. The condition is not about mastery of linguistics, asbab al-nuzul, or nasikh wa mansukh, but about knowing true beliefs from false ones.
The exclusion is important to avoid overburdening the condition.
3
Recognize the danger of not meeting the condition
Without this condition, one may fall into false beliefs like the Mu'tazila, who denied the vision of Allah due to a flawed syllogism.
The example of the Mu'tazila serves as a warning.
4
Identify the permissible texts
The lesson mentions specific kalam books that contain distortions: al-Matali', al-Tawali', al-Mawaqif, and al-Maqasid. Only those with sound innate ability and practice of Quran and Sunnah may study them.
These are advanced texts that require caution.

One who practices the Sunnah and the Book is guided by it to correctness. His statement: 'One who practices the Sunnah and the Book' means: one who engages them and deals with them, such that he knows the true beliefs from the false beliefs. This is what is intended. It is not intended by 'one who practices them' to merely engage them and deal with them such that he knows what pertains to them of languages, causes of revelation, abrogating and abrogated... and other than that, because this is only needed by the absolute mujtahid, as Ibn Ya'qub said. And the author guarded by restricting with 'perfect innate ability' against the deficient, and with 'one who practices the Sunnah and the Book' against one who does not practice them. So it is not permissible for either of them to engage in it; I mean the second part of this art, which is mixed with the errors of the philosophers. And similar to this part in this detail are the books of kalam that contain distortions, such as al-Matali', al-Tawali', al-Mawaqif, and al-Maqasid. It is permissible to engage in them for one of sound innate ability who practices the Sunnah and the Book, such that he knows the true beliefs from the false beliefs, but not for others. It is not permissible for him to engage in them, lest some false beliefs take root in his heart as happened to the Mu'tazila. For some of those beliefs took root in their hearts, such as their belief that Allah, exalted, is not seen, because they imagined that nothing is seen except what is a body or subsisting in a body. They built upon that a syllogism thus: Allah is not a body nor subsisting in a body. Everything that is like that is not seen. The conclusion states: Allah is not seen. And we refute that syllogism by criticizing its major premise, due to the judgment of the intellect that whatever exists can be seen, even if it is not a body nor subsisting in a body. And we build upon that a syllogism saying: Allah exists. Everything that exists can be seen. The conclusion states: Allah can be seen. And it is the truth. And Allah is the Grantor of success. His statement: 'to be guided by it to correctness' is a cause for his statement: 'its permissibility... etc.' And what was mentioned about it has preceded. And it is not hidden that correctness is the opposite of error. We ask Allah for success to correctness and salvation on the Day of Gathering and Reckoning, and for attainment of the companionship of our master Muhammad, the best of those of intellect.

Commentary
This passage discusses the conditions under which it is permissible to engage in the study of logic, specifically the second part which is mixed with philosophical errors. The author clarifies the phrase 'ممارس السنة والكتاب' (one who practices the Sunnah and the Book) from the text being commented on. He emphasizes that it does not mean a full mujtahid but rather someone who knows the true beliefs from false ones, having engaged with the Quran and Sunnah sufficiently. This is to protect oneself from being influenced by false beliefs, as happened to the Mu'tazila. The author presents the Mu'tazili syllogism that denies the vision of Allah: (Major premise) Everything that is not a body nor subsisting in a body is not seen; (Minor premise) Allah is not a body nor subsisting in a body; (Conclusion) Therefore Allah is not seen. He refutes this syllogism by criticizing its major premise, arguing that the intellect judges that existence alone is sufficient for the possibility of being seen, regardless of being a body or not. He then offers a correct syllogism: (Major) Everything that exists can be seen; (Minor) Allah exists; (Conclusion) Allah can be seen. This shows that study of logic is only beneficial for those with sound innate ability and correct belief derived from Quran and Sunnah.

Refuting a Syllogism by Criticizing the Major Premise
Refuting the Mu'tazili syllogism on ru'yat Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute a categorical syllogism by identifying and criticizing its major premise (naqd al-kubra), using the Mu'tazili argument against seeing Allah as an example.

Core Idea
A syllogism can be refuted by showing its major premise is false, even if the form is valid.

This lesson teaches a key refutation technique: naqd al-kubra (criticizing the major premise). The Mu'tazila argued that Allah cannot be seen because He is not a body, using a categorical syllogism. Bajuri refutes it by challenging the major premise, showing that existence alone is sufficient for visibility. This demonstrates that a validly formed syllogism can still be unsound due to a false premise.

Guided Path
1
Reconstruct the Mu'tazili syllogism
Identify the three terms: middle term (being not a body nor subsisting in a body), minor term (Allah), major term (not seen). Then write the syllogism: Minor: Allah is not a body nor subsisting in a body. Major: Everything that is not a body nor subsisting in a body is not seen. Conclusion: Allah is not seen.
This is a categorical syllogism in the first figure (Celarent?). Actually check: The major premise is universal affirmative? It's 'lam yura' (not seen) but we can treat it as universal. The form is: No non-body is seen; Allah is a non-body; so Allah is not seen. That is valid (Camestres? Actually it's Celarent: No M is P; All S is M; No S is P). So the form is valid.
2
Identify which premise to attack
Bajuri attacks the major premise (al-kubra). He argues that the major premise is false because the intellect judges that existence is sufficient for being visible, regardless of being a body.
This is a direct refutation of the premise, not a counterexample.
3
Construct a counter-syllogism
Bajuri presents a new syllogism with a correct major premise: Everything that exists can be seen; Allah exists; therefore Allah can be seen. This uses existence (wujud) as the middle term.
This alternative syllogism shows the logical conclusion that aligns with Sunni belief.
4
Understand the method of naqd al-kubra
In jadal, naqd al-kubra involves showing that the major premise is not universally true. Here, Bajuri argues from the intellect that existence alone makes something possibly visible, so the Mu'tazili major premise is false.
This technique is common in theological debates.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on the matn line calling for the prioritization of the first (tasawwur) over the second (tasdiq) in presentation. The commentator begins by clarifying why the term 'tasdiq' is used for assent despite its literal meaning favoring truth: it is by taghlib, focusing on the nobler possibility. The report (khabar) denotes truth only; falsehood is merely a rational possibility. Thus the name is fitting. Then the commentator addresses the matn's command 'wa qaddimi al-awwala'—he reads it as imperative, indicating an obligation within the discipline (wujuban sina'iyyan), though a passive reading is possible, implying the scholars' practice. The command is to place matters of tasawwur before those of tasdiq. The phrase 'inda al-wad'i is interpreted broadly as in writing, teaching, or learning. The reason given is natural precedence (taqaddum bi al-tab'): what is naturally prior should be placed first so that the order of presentation mirrors natural order. The commentator then expands this into a classification of five types of precedence, citing a poetic verse. The five types are: (1) precedence by nature (tab'), where one thing is prior in existence without being a cause, e.g., the first child in a family precedes the second in existence but is not a cause; (2) precedence by time (zaman), involving temporal distance; (3) precedence by cause ('illa), where one thing is the cause of another in the mind; (4) precedence by rank (rutba), like the imam preceding the follower; and (5) precedence by honor (sharaf), which is about esteem in people's hearts or closeness to Allah. This enumeration provides a conceptual framework for understanding why tasawwur is placed first: it has natural precedence, being the foundation for tasdiq.

How Subject Matters Distinguish Sciences
The commentator explains how subjects distinguish sciences.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sciences are one genus; their diversity comes from diverse subjects—either essentially or in aspect.

The commentator emphasizes that sciences are not inherently separate; their unity is broken only by the diversity of subject matters. He gives clear examples: grammar and medicine differ essentially, while al-ma'ani and al-bayan differ only in aspect. Understanding this distinction is crucial for classifying Islamic sciences.

Why Both Definition and Subject Are Needed
The commentator resolves a dialectical question about the sufficiency of definition and subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to defend the position that both the definition (hadd) and the subject (mawdu') are necessary to fully characterize a science, using logical arguments from the commentary.

Core Idea
The subject is part of the definition; they are distinct but inseparable—one cannot replace the other.

A question arises: if the definition already tells us what the science is about, why do we also need the subject? The commentator provides a crisp answer: the subject is part of the definition, and the definition is related to the subject, so they are different. This lesson unpacks that dialectical exchange and shows why both are essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the question in the passage: 'Why not rely on the definition alone without the subject, or vice versa?' (هلا استغنى بالحدّ عن الموضوع أو العكس).
The objector assumes that since both serve to distinguish the science, one should suffice.
2
Restate the answer
The commentator answers: 'Each is different from the other, so one cannot be dispensed with by the other; the subject is part of the definition, and the definition is related to it.'
The key point is that the subject (مَوْضُوع) is a component of the definition (تَتِمَّة الحد), not synonymous with it.
3
Analyze the example of logic
The definition of logic is given: 'the science investigating states of conceptual and assensional knowns'. The subject is the knowns themselves. The definition tells us the kind of inquiry; the subject tells us what is inquired into.
4
Apply to the science of fiqh
Think of the definition of fiqh: 'knowledge of practical sharia rulings from detailed evidence'. Its subject is 'actions of mukallaf'. The definition clarifies the type of knowledge (sharia rulings), while the subject specifies the domain (actions of morally responsible persons). Both are needed.
