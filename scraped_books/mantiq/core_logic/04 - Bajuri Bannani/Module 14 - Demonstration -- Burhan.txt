Distinguishing Technical and General Meanings of Burhan
Tablawi specifies that 'clear proof' here means any evidence, not specifically the logical syllogism of certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the technical logical meaning of 'burhan' from its general usage in religious texts and explain the commentator's rationale for the broader interpretation.

Core Idea
The same Arabic term 'burhan' can mean different things depending on context: technical syllogism or any compelling evidence.

In logic texts, 'burhan' is a technical term for a syllogism composed of certain premises that yields certainties. However, in many religious contexts—especially when discussing proofs of prophethood—'burhan' simply means any clear proof or evidence. This lesson examines a commentator's critical refinement to prevent confusion between the two senses and to ensure correct interpretation of theological texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term under discussion
Locate the phrase 'al-burhan al-wadih' in the source. The commentator is clarifying what 'burhan' means in this specific context.
2
Extract the technical definition that is excluded
The commentator explicitly mentions the logical definition: 'a syllogism composed of certain premises to produce certainties'. Recognize that this is a precise technical meaning used in Aristotelian logic texts.
3
Contrast with the broader meaning intended
The commentator states that the intended meaning is 'absolute proof' (mutlaq al-dalil). Observe that this includes any type of evidence—miracles, signs, rational arguments—not just formal syllogisms.
4
Understand the significance of the distinction
Why does this matter? In the context of proving prophethood, the 'clear proof' often refers to miracles (mu'jizat) as evidence. Forcing a syllogistic interpretation would misrepresent the argument. The commentator safeguards against anachronistic application of logical terms.

Tracing Premises to Self-Evident or Accepted Foundations
Author explains that premises in a proof must terminate at necessary or accepted premises to avoid circularity or infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the chain of premises in a demonstrative argument and identify where each premise terminates in a necessary (daruriyya) or accepted (musallama) premise, avoiding circularity and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Every demonstrative chain must bottom out in self-evident or conceded premises—or it is a castle in the air.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the matn line 'and they end at a necessity,' explaining that every premise in a demonstration must ultimately be grounded either in self-evident truths (darura) or in propositions conceded by the opponent (musallama). Without such foundations, the argument would either circle back on itself (dawr) or regress infinitely (tasalsul). You will learn to apply this criterion to any argument by tracing its premises back to their ultimate foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion and initial premises
Take a demonstrative argument, e.g., the kalām argument for the origination of bodies: 'Bodies have originated attributes; everything with originated attributes is originated; therefore bodies are originated.' List the two premises: the minor and the major.
2
Assess each premise's epistemological status
Ask: Is this premise self-evident (necessary by observation or reason) or accepted by the opponent (musallama)? If yes, the chain ends. If not, proceed. For example, 'Bodies have changing attributes' is necessary by observation; but 'Everything changing is originated' is not self-evident.
3
Find the supporting argument for the non-obvious premise
Look in the text for how the author proves the premise that needs support. Here, the author argues for the major premise by analyzing change: If change is from non-existence to existence, it's origination; if from existence to non-existence, it shows contingency and thus origination.
4
Repeat until all premises are necessary or accepted
Each supporting argument introduces new premises. Continue until you reach premises that are either observed, self-evident, or conceded by the opponent. Verify there is no circularity (premise depending on conclusion) and no infinite regress (an endless chain without foundation).

His saying: (and they end... etc.) The pronoun refers to the premises known from context. And his saying: (to a necessity) means to a necessary thing, i.e., to necessary premises, and like them are accepted premises. And the place of what was mentioned - as is apparent - is when the premises themselves are not necessary; as in your saying: 'Four is a number that divides into two equals, and every number that divides into two equals is even', or accepted; as in your saying - pointing to an action without right -: 'This is injustice, and every injustice is ugly'. Otherwise there is no meaning for it and no need for it in the second. So it is summarized: that the premises, if they are not necessary nor accepted, must end at necessary or accepted premises. Commentary
This passage explains the epistemological requirement that a chain of premises in a demonstration must terminate at premises that are self-evident (daruriyya) or accepted (musallama). The author expands on the matn line 'And they end at a necessity' meaning that the premises must ultimately rest on necessary or accepted propositions. This is to avoid circularity (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul). The author gives examples: 'Four is a number that divides into two equals, and every such number is even' is a necessary premise; pointing to an unjust act and saying 'This is injustice, and every injustice is ugly' is an accepted premise. If the premises are not themselves necessary or accepted, they must be supported by further arguments that eventually reach such premises. The detailed example concerns the kalām argument for the origination of bodies: bodies have changing attributes, and everything with changing attributes is originated. The minor premise (bodies have changing attributes) is necessary through observation, but the major premise (everything changing is originated) requires further argument about the nature of change, leading to the conclusion that change implies origination (either from non-existence to existence, or from existence to non-existence indicating contingency, which necessitates origination).

Why Arguments Need Necessary Premises
Commentary on the need for premises to end in necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a chain of reasoning must ultimately rest on necessary or conceded premises, and identify the fallacies of circularity and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Every proof must bottom out in self-evident or conceded premises, else it collapses into circularity or infinite regress.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational principle of Islamic logic: that any argument must eventually reduce to premises that are either self-evident (daruri) or conceded by the opponent (musallam). The commentator unpacks this rule and illustrates it with a classic theological proof for the origination of bodies. Understanding this principle protects our reasoning from two dangerous pitfalls: circular reasoning (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of foundational premises
The passage mentions necessary premises (daruriyya) which are self-evident, and conceded premises (musallama) which are accepted from the opponent without proof. Note that both serve as ultimate stopping points for demonstration.
Examples: 'Four is an even number' (necessary) and 'This act is oppression' (conceded, if opponent agrees).
2
Trace the proof for huduth al-ajram
The commentator gives a detailed example: proving that bodies are originated. The chain includes: bodies have changing accidents (observation) → every changing thing is originated (argued from the meaning of change) → everything whose accidents are originated is never free of originated things → does not precede them → therefore it is originated.
Each premise is either observed or reduced to a necessary truth.
3
Observe how each chain ends in necessity
The minor premise ends in an observational necessity (changing is seen), and the major premise ends in a logical necessity (if not free of originated things, then does not precede them, hence originated). The chain stops at these necessary propositions.
The commentator emphasizes that each sub-proof also terminates in necessity.
4
Explain why dawr and tasalsul are avoided
Without an endpoint, you either return to an earlier premise (dawr: circular dependence) or proceed infinitely (tasalsul: endless chain). Both are invalid because they prevent certainty. The requirement of necessity ensures the argument is grounded.
Dawr and tasalsul are fallacies in demonstration; they make knowledge impossible.

And they (the premises) end in necessity, because otherwise a cycle or infinite regress would necessarily occur. The commentator said: His saying 'end' refers to the premises known from context. His saying 'to necessity' means to a necessary thing, i.e., to necessary premises, and conceded premises are similar. And the place of what is mentioned is when the premises themselves are not necessary, as in your saying: 'Four is a number that divides into two equal parts, and every number that divides into two equal parts is even', or conceded, as in your saying, pointing to an action without right: 'This is oppression, and every oppression is ugly'. To summarize: premises that are not necessary nor conceded must end in necessary or conceded premises. For example, when arguing for the origination of bodies: 'Bodies have accidents that are originated, and everything that has originated accidents is originated', each of the two premises of this proof requires proof until it ends in what was mentioned. So you prove the minor by saying: 'Bodies have changing accidents, and everything changing is originated.' The first of these two premises does not need proof because it is necessary by observation, and the second needs proof by saying: 'If change is from non-existence to existence, then existence is emergent, and that is the meaning of origination; and if it is from existence to non-existence, then existence is contingent, and the contingent is only originated.' And you prove the major by saying: 'Everything whose accidents are originated is never free of originated things, and everything that is never free of originated things does not precede them, and everything that does not precede them is originated.' So each of the minor and major has ended in necessity. And no attention is paid to the philosophers' objections to some of those premises, because they are mere obstinacy. If we return to some of the first ones, a cycle would occur — which is the dependence of a thing on that which itself depends on it. This passage is an explanation of the matn line 'وَتَنْتَهِي إِلَى ضَرُورَةٍ لِمَا مِنْ دَوْرٍ أَوْ تَسَلْسُلٍ' which states that any chain of reasoning must ultimately rest on necessary principles to avoid two logical fallacies: circular reasoning (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul). He provides a detailed example from theology: proving the origination of bodies (huduth al-ajram). The major premise 'Everything whose accidents are originated is itself originated' is proven by showing that such a thing is never devoid of originated accidents, hence does not precede them, and hence is originated. Thus both premises end in necessary truths. The commentator dismisses objections from philosophers as mere obstinacy (mukabarah). Core Idea

Commentary
This passage analyzes the celebrated Quranic argument from Surah al-Anbiya' verse 22, presented as a demonstrative proof by modus tollens (denying the consequent). The author explains that the argument is missing an explicit premise, but the implied universal conditional is: 'If there were multiple gods, the heavens and earth would be corrupted.' Since they are not corrupted, there cannot be multiple gods. The core issue is the nature of the implication (mulazama) between plurality of gods and corruption. Al-Sa'd al-Taftazani considered it a customary (adiyya) implication, based on the empirical observation that multiple rulers habitually produce disorder. Hence the argument is rhetorical (khatabi) and persuasive, not demonstratively certain. In his commentary on al-Nasafiyya, he then considers the alternative that the implication is rational (aqliyya). If 'corruption' means actual departure from the observed order, mere plurality does not logically necessitate that because rational agreement on order is possible. If 'corruption' means possibility of corruption, then the absence of corruption does not disprove that possibility; indeed, Islamic texts foretell the eventual destruction of this cosmos, proving corruption is possible. The more robust rational interpretation, favored by al-Sanusi, takes 'corruption' to mean non-existence: multiple gods would entail inability or contradiction, so a single necessary being (wajib al-wujud) is required. This rational interpretation is held to yield certain knowledge. The discussion illustrates the range of approaches to Quranic arguments within Islamic logic and kalam.

Commentary
This passage defines burhan (demonstration) as the highest form of argument. It begins by stating the division of arguments (hujaj) and ranks burhan as the most honorable, followed by rhetoric (khataba) and the worst as sophistry. Burhan corresponds to 'wisdom' in Quran 16:125, while rhetoric corresponds to 'good exhortation'. Then the formal definition is given: burhan is a syllogism composed of certain premises. The commentator explains that the term 'ma' (what) refers to qiyas (syllogism), which is the nearest genus for the five arts, and the differentia 'composed of certain premises' excludes other arguments like induction and analogy. The key part is the definition of certainty (yaqin) as having three conditions: (1) firm belief (jazim), excluding conjecture (zann); (2) correspondence to reality (mutabiq), excluding compound ignorance (jahl murakkab) – the example given is the Mu'tazilite belief that humans create their actions; (3) stability (thabit), excluding blind imitation (taqlid). Thus burhan is not limited to necessary truths but must ultimately rest on them.

The Six Types of Necessary Certainties and the Nature of Awwaliyat
Author lists six certainties and defines awwaliyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the six types of necessary certain premises and define awwaliyat with its standard examples from this passage.

Core Idea
Awwaliyat are propositions the mind accepts instantly without proof upon understanding the terms.

This passage from Bajuri's Sharh begins by listing the six kinds of certain premises that form the foundation of demonstrative reasoning. The first type, awwaliyat (also called badihiyyat), are the most basic: they are propositions the mind judges as soon as it conceives what the subject and predicate mean. Understanding this definition is critical because all demonstration ultimately rests on such self-evident truths.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and dialectical unit from the Sharh of Bajuri on the poem al-Sullam al-Munawraq. It first introduces the topic of the types of certain premises (yaqiniyyat) that are necessary (daruriyyat). The author cites the poet's line enumerating six types: awwaliyat (first principles/self-evident), mushahadat (observed via inner senses), mujarrabat (experimental), mutawatirat (recurrent reports), hadsiyyat (intuitive), and mahsusat (sensed via outer senses). Then the commentator restricts the necessary certainties to these six and begins defining them one by one. The first type, awwaliyat, also called badihiyyat, are propositions that the mind accepts immediately upon simply understanding the subject and predicate, without needing any intermediate reasoning. Examples given are: 'The one is half of two', 'The whole is greater than the part', and 'A single person cannot be in two places at once'. However, the commentator anticipates an objection: some awwaliyat seem to require reasoning, such as 'the whole is greater than the part' — because if someone says 'This whole has a part, and every whole is greater than its part', that is a compound syllogism, not immediate. The objection specifically targets the third example: to know that a person cannot be in two places, one must first conceive of the person being in each place individually and then infer impossibility, requiring reflection. The answer clarifies that the mind judges upon conceptualizing the terms in a particular way — i.e., imagining the person simultaneously occupying both places — and then immediately sees the impossibility. Additional explanation is given: some awwaliyat may require slight reflection due to mental weakness, opposing beliefs, or incomplete conceptualization, but they remain self-evident in essence.

Key Terms
الأَوَّلِيَّات (al-awwaliyat): Propositions that the mind judges by merely conceptualizing their two terms without any need for a mediator. [Note: In later texts, awwaliyat are sometimes distinguished from badihiyyat by whether they are common to all people; here they are synonymous.]
اليَقِينِيَّات الضَّرُورِيَّات (al-yaqiniyyat al-daruriyyat): Necessary certainties—premises that are known with certainty and are a priori or based on immediate experience. [Note: Stable term across the tradition, though the classification into six may vary slightly in later works.]

Defending Awwaliyat: Objection and Response on the Principle of Spatial Impossibility
Author responds to objection about awwali status of spatial impossibility example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection against the awwali status of 'a person cannot be in two places' and explain the author's response using the concepts of specific conceptualization and deficiency of the knower.

Core Idea
An objection that a proposition requires reflection does not disprove its self-evidence; the need for reflection may be due to the knower's limitation, not the proposition.

This part of the commentary addresses a subtle challenge: the example 'a single person cannot be in two places at once' seems to require conceptualizing the person in each place before judging, which would mean it is not a purely immediate judgment. The author anticipates this objection and provides a rigorous defense, showing that even a small pause does not destroy self-evidence if the judgment eventually occurs without a separate mediator. This dialectical exchange is a classic illustration of how Islamic logicians defended foundational premises.

This passage is a definitional and dialectical unit from the Sharh of Bajuri on the poem al-Sullam al-Munawraq. Author defines wijdaniyyat as inner sense judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define wijdaniyyat and explain why their judgments are particular rather than universal.

Core Idea
Inner sense gives immediate particular judgments, not universal ones.

This lesson covers the second of the six certainties: mushahadat, identified with wijdaniyyat (inner sensations). The author defines them as propositions judged by the mind via inner senses, such as hunger or pleasure. A key point is that sense judgments are inherently particular (juz'iyya), not universal. Understanding this distinction is vital for evaluating how such premises function in demonstrations.

Guided Path

Experimental Certainty (Mujarrabat): The Hidden Syllogism
Author defines mujarrabat and explains the hidden syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the hidden syllogism that makes mujarrabat certain and apply it to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Experimental certainty rests on a hidden syllogism: constant conjunction implies a cause; the cause produces the effect.

This lesson explores the third certainty: mujarrabat (experimentals). The author explains that these are propositions judged by the mind through repeated observation combined with a hidden syllogism that yields certainty. The hidden syllogism asserts that repeated uniform occurrence necessitates a cause, and knowledge of the cause guarantees knowledge of the effect. Understanding this logical structure is essential for evaluating empirical claims in demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Define mujarrabat formally
Locate the definition: 'المُجَرَّبَاتُ ... يَحْكُمُ بِهَا العَقْلُ بِانْضِمَام تَكَرُّرِ المُشَاهَدَةِ إِلَيْهِ، وَالقِيَاسُ الخَفِيُّ' (mujarrabat are propositions the mind judges by the addition of repeated observation and the hidden syllogism).
Note the two components: repeated observation and a hidden syllogism.
2
Extract the hidden syllogism
The hidden syllogism is: repeated uniform occurrence (duplicate) must have a cause (1st premise); if the cause exists, the effect certainly exists (2nd premise); therefore, the effect is not coincidental.
The author states: 'الوُقُوعَ المُتَكَرِّرِ عَلَى نَهْجِ وَاحِدٍ لَا بُدَّ لَهُ مِنْ سَبَبٍ'.
3
Apply to the scammony example
The example: scammony is observed to induce laxation repeatedly. The hidden syllogism shows that this is not accidental but due to a causal property of scammony.
The full explicit syllogism is given at the end: 'This is scammony; every scammony is a laxative for yellow bile; therefore this is a laxative for yellow bile.'
4
Distinguish from non-causal conjunctions
Not all constant conjunctions imply causality. The author contrasts cases with effect (like fire burning) from those without (like blackness of pitch). The hidden syllogism only works when there is genuine causal influence.
This distinguishes mujarrabat from mere accidental regularities.

The second: the observed things (mushahadat), and by them he intended inner sensations (wijdaniyyat), and they are propositions that the mind judges by means of the inner senses, like the judgment that ‘we have hunger, pleasure, and joy’, and among them is what we find in our souls without bodily organs, like our awareness of ourselves and our states. And the judgments of sense are only particular. The third: the experimental things (mujarrabat), and they are propositions that the mind judges by the addition of repeated observation to it, and the hidden syllogism that produces certainty in them, which is: that repeated occurrence on a uniform course necessarily has a cause, even if we do not know its essence, and whenever the existence of the cause is known, the existence of the effect is known certainly. That is like the judgment that ‘scammony is a laxative for yellow bile’, and that ‘hitting with wood causes pain’, and similar things that have an effect, like the burning of fire, contrary to what has no effect like the blackness of pitch. His saying: (for sense only informs that this fire is hot) means: and the pain of this hunger, the benefit of this thirst-quenching, and the pleasure of knowing that matter, and therefore the non-Arab may share in it with the articulate. His saying: (and as for the judgment that every fire is hot) means: and every hunger has pain, and every knowledge has pleasure. His saying: (so it is a rational judgment) so it appears: that the judgment about observed things is composed of sense and reason, not purely rational, so the explicit statement of Ibn al-Hajib that wijdaniyyat do not depend on reason does not mean that they, inasmuch as they are premises of demonstration, are like that. His saying: (repetition of observation is an addition of the attribute to the described; i.e., repeated observation beneficial for certainty by means of a hidden syllogism.) His saying: (like the judgment that scammony is a laxative for yellow bile) so this is a proposition that the mind judges by means of observing the laxative effect when drinking it, and that observation was repeated repeatedly, giving us certainty until we were sure that that laxative effect was not coincidental; i.e., it did not happen coincidentally with this drink, rather that laxative effect is due to a cause, which is this drink. So whenever the cause exists, the effect exists, and therefore the judgment is valid for us. And this certainty from this repetition is by means of a hidden syllogistic structure; i.e., the judgment is not apparent, and that is by saying: This drink loosens the stomach, and if its existence coinciding with the existence of the loosening were coincidental, it would not be constant nor mostly so. But it is constant, so it follows: it is not coincidental, but rather due to a cause that necessitated that. So if it is said, for example: This is scammony, and every scammony is a laxative for yellow bile, therefore this is a laxative for yellow bile — that is a syllogism from the experimental things.

Commentary
This passage continues the definition of the six certainties, now covering the second and third types. The second type, mushahadat, is identified with wijdaniyyat—judgments arising from inner senses, such as feeling hunger, pleasure, or joy. This includes self-awareness without external organs. However, such sense judgments are inherently particular (juz'iyya), not universal. The third type, mujarrabat, are empirical generalizations. The mind judges them by combining repeated observations with a hidden syllogism (qiyas khafi) that yields certainty. The hidden syllogism is: repeated uniform occurrence implies a necessary cause, even if the cause's essence is unknown; whenever the cause exists, the effect certainly follows. Examples given: scammony causes laxation of yellow bile, hitting with wood causes pain, and fire burns—contrasting with mere constant conjunctions like the blackness of pitch (which has no causal efficacy). The commentary elaborates on the poet's word 'idh al-hissu innama yufid...' explaining that sense only gives particular information (this fire is hot), while the universal judgment (every fire is hot) is rational, combining sense and reason. Ibn al-Hajib's claim that wijdaniyyat do not need reason is qualified: they are not purely rational but are composed of sense and reason. Then the commentary unpacks 'takrur al-mushahadah': it means repeated observations leading to certainty via a hidden syllogism. The example of scammony is explained: repeated observation of laxation upon drinking scammony eliminates the possibility of chance, establishing a causal link. The hidden syllogism is made explicit: 'This drink loosens the stomach; if it were coincidental it would not be constant; it is constant; therefore it is not coincidental but due to a cause.' Finally, the example is cast as a formal categorical syllogism: 'This is scammony; every scammony is a laxative for yellow bile; therefore this is a laxative for yellow bile'—showing that mujarrabat can be used as premises in syllogisms.

Refuting Essential Necessitation in Causal Reasoning
Author presents and dismisses the philosophers' claim that premises necessitate the conclusion essentially.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the philosophers' doctrine of essential necessitation and explain why it is rejected in Ash'ari kalām on the basis of tawhid al-af'āl.

Core Idea
The philosophers claim premises force the conclusion by themselves; kalām replies: only Allah creates.

We have examined two views on how premises yield the conclusion: generation (tawallud) and acquisition (kasb). Now the philosophers offer a third: essential necessitation. This lesson unpacks their claim and the decisive kalām refutation, showing why attributing causal power to anything other than Allah is a serious error in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Find the statement that the conclusion follows from the premises by 'essential necessitation' (al-ījāb al-dhātī). What does this mean in terms of causality?
The text says: 'by way of essential necessitation; meaning, the effect of the cause in its effect by its essence.'
2
Understand the causal logic used
The philosophers view reflection (nazar) as an efficient cause (ʿilla muʾaththira) that produces the conclusion necessarily. They hold that it is rationally impossible for the cause not to produce its effect.
This implies that the premises themselves have inherent causal power, independent of divine intervention.
3
Locate the refutation in the passage
The author dismisses this view as 'delusion' and appeals to the established kalām proofs that Allah is the Creator of every originated thing. How does this refute the philosophers?
The refutation is not a detailed argument but a reference to the foundational kalām doctrine of tawhid al-af'āl: only Allah creates.
4
Connect to broader kalām principles
In Ash'ari theology, no created entity has independent causal efficacy. The act of reasoning and the resulting knowledge are both created by Allah directly. This doctrine protects tawhid and excludes secondary causes.
Thus, the philosophers' view is not just wrong but contradictory to the core of Islamic creed.

Commentary
This passage presents the third and final madhab on how premises yield the conclusion: the view of the philosophers (falāsifa). They assert that the conclusion follows from the premises by 'essential necessitation' (al-ījāb al-dhātī): the premises are an efficient cause that necessarily produces its effect by its own essence. Thus, if one reflects on the premises, the conclusion must arise. The author dismisses this as pure delusion (hawas) with no plausible evidence. The refutation is that this contradicts the foundational kalām principle that Allah alone creates every originated thing—no creature or created act possesses inherent causal efficacy. The decisive proofs of kalām are sufficient to falsify this error. The author concludes with a prayer for divine guidance.
