Errors in Demonstration: Material vs Formal Fallacies
Author identifies two types of fallacy in demonstration: in matter and form, with linguistic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish material fallacies from formal fallacies and identify homonymy (ishtirak) as a material fallacy.

Core Idea
Fallacies attack the truth of a conclusion either through bad content (material) or bad structure (formal).

In this passage, the author introduces a foundational classification for identifying why a demonstration fails. He divides errors into two categories: material (madda) and formal (sura). Material errors come from the premises themselves—like using a word with multiple meanings (ishtirak) or confusing different meanings as if they were the same. Formal errors are missteps in the syllogistic structure. This lesson will help you spot the most common material fallacy: equivocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of error
The text says error is either in matter (madda) or form (sura). Write these down as the two overarching types of fallacies in demonstration.
Material = content of premises; Formal = logical structure.
2
Recognize the example of material fallacy: homonymy
The author gives 'ishtirak' (اشتراك) as an example of a material fallacy—using a word that has two or more meanings. This is equivocation.
Ishtirak means a single word with multiple distinct meanings.
3
Understand the second example: confusing synonym with identity
The phrase 'جعل ذا تباين مثل الرديف مأخذا' refers to mistakenly treating a word that has distinct meanings (تباين) as if it were a synonym (رديف), i.e., assuming two different words are identical in meaning when they are not. This is another material fallacy.
رديف means synonym; تباين means different meanings.
4
Distinguish material from formal fallacies
A formal fallacy would be a broken syllogistic structure (e.g., invalid figure or mood), not a problem in the premises. The passage focuses on material fallacies, but note that formal fallacies also exist.
This lesson primarily trains you on material fallacies.

Identifying Logical Fallacies in Syllogistic Reasoning
Author lists examples of fallacies including confusing accidental with essential and errors in syllogistic form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify four material fallacies (confusing accidental with essential, one-premise conclusion, judging genus by species, treating non-certain as certain) and one formal fallacy (violating syllogistic figures) using classical Islamic logic criteria.

Core Idea
Fallacies arise from both material confusion and formal violations of syllogistic conditions.

This passage from the hashiyah teaches us to distinguish valid reasoning from sophistry. The author lists several ways arguments go wrong—whether by misapplying terms or by ignoring the structural rules of syllogism. As students of Islamic logic, you must learn to spot these errors in theological and juristic arguments to defend truth against falsehood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the material fallacies
The author mentions four material errors: (1) treating accidental properties as essential, (2) deriving a conclusion from only one premise, (3) applying a rule of the species to the whole genus, and (4) treating uncertain propositions as certain. For each, think of an example from fiqh or kalam where this error would undermine an argument.
Material fallacies involve the content or meaning of terms, not the form.
2
Recognize the formal fallacy
The second type of error is violating the valid figures (ashkāl) of the syllogism or neglecting the conditions for a valid conclusion (shart al-natj). For instance, a syllogism in the second figure with two affirmative premises produces no necessary conclusion.
Formal fallacies break the logical structure regardless of content.
3
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the argument: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' This is valid. But if someone argues: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore nabidh is permitted'—that is a formal fallacy (wrong conclusion). Or if they say 'Nabidh is intoxicating (accidental) so it is forbidden (essential)'—that confuses accidental with essential.
Always check both material terms and syllogistic structure.

Anbabiy's Terminological Refinement of Compound Ignorance
Anbabiy critiques the previous response, arguing that removing compound ignorance does not ensure knowledge, and explains that 'compound' is terminological.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Anbabiy's argument that 'compound ignorance' is terminological and refute the objection that it requires real composition.

Core Idea
Compound ignorance is a simple belief; its name is conventional, not descriptive.

In this passage, al-Anbabiy refines the concept of 'compound ignorance' (al-jahl al-murakkab) by clarifying that its name is purely terminological. He first addresses a subtle objection: even if compound ignorance is removed, simple ignorance may remain, so the removal does not guarantee knowledge. He then argues that calling it 'compound' does not imply real composition, because ignorance is a belief, which is simple. He refutes the counterargument that if it were compound, its parts would have to be either knowledge or ignorance, both impossible. This logical move safeguards the coherence of the term in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
What is the claim that Anbabiy is critiquing? The claim that removing compound ignorance brings knowledge. He shows that this is not necessarily true because simple ignorance can remain.
The context is a commentary on a poetic line praising the removal of ignorance.
2
Understand the counterexample
Anbabiy points out that removal of compound ignorance can leave simple ignorance, so knowledge is not necessarily achieved. This shows why the term needs clarification.
Simple ignorance is the mere absence of knowledge.
3
Analyze the terminological argument
Anbabiy states that 'compound' is a convention; in reality ignorance is a simple belief. Why would anyone think it is literal compound? Because the name suggests composition.
Belief (i'tiqad) is a simple mental act, not composed of parts.
4
Reconstruct the refutation of the composition objection
The objector says: if it is compound, its parts must be knowledge or ignorance. Anbabiy shows both are impossible: if parts are knowledge, it would be composed of opposites; if parts are compound ignorance, infinite regress. Therefore, it cannot be literally composite.
This uses a disjunctive syllogism.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement by al-Anbabiy of Bajuri's earlier argument. He first raises an objection: if the poet meant compound ignorance, removing it does not necessarily lead to knowledge, because simple ignorance (the absence of knowledge) may remain. This weakens the claim that the poem praises the removal of ignorance as bringing knowledge. However, he suggests that the context of praise implies that removing compound ignorance specifically establishes its opposite (knowledge), thus avoiding the problem. Then he addresses a deeper issue: the term 'compound ignorance' is purely terminological; in reality, ignorance is a single belief, which is simple (non-composite). He refutes the objection that if ignorance is 'compound', it must have parts that are either knowledge or ignorance. If its parts were knowledge, that would be contradictory (a thing cannot be composed of its opposite). If its parts were themselves compound ignorance, that leads to infinite regress. Thus the only coherent understanding is that the term 'compound' is used metaphorically, not literally. This shows sophisticated logical analysis typical of hashiya literature.

الجَهْل المُرَكَّب (al-jahl al-murakkab): Terminologically called 'compound' but is actually a simple belief; the name is conventional. [Note: In other logical works, 'compound ignorance' may be treated as literally composite; here Anbabiy emphasizes it is a terminological convenience.]
تَسَلْسُل (tasalsul): Infinite regress, a logical impossibility that would result if parts of compound ignorance were themselves compound ignorance. [Note: Standard in logic as a fallacy; stable.]

Definition of Sophistry and Its Three Types
Author defines sophistry and enumerates three kinds of fallacious premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of premises that constitute sophistry and distinguish them from valid premises.

Core Idea
Sophistry is falsehood dressed as truth, using imaginary, truth-resembling, or accepted-resembling premises.

Sophistry is the fifth logical art, originally called 'embellished wisdom.' It is composed of premises that deceive either by being purely false imaginative, or by resembling truth without being truth, or by resembling widely accepted statements without being widely accepted. Understanding these categories is crucial to detecting deception in arguments.

Such behavior is forbidden (haram) unless necessary in defense against a disbeliever like a Rafidi or Mu'tazili. The marginal notes further clarify that the 'false imaginary' type is distinct from the others and that the third type resembles valid dialectical syllogisms but is fallacious.

Analysing the Examples of Sophistical Syllogisms
Author illustrates the three types of sophistry with categorical syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which type of sophistry each example belongs to and explain why the conclusion is invalid.

Core Idea
Each sophistical example uses a false or deceptive premise to produce a seemingly valid conclusion.

The author provides three concrete syllogisms to illustrate the three types of sophistry. By breaking down these examples, we can see exactly how each type deceives the listener. This will sharpen your ability to spot fallacious reasoning in real debates.

Guided Path
1
Read the first example
Identify the premises and conclusion of: 'A stone is dead, everything dead is inanimate, therefore the stone is inanimate.'
This is the first type: false imaginary premise.
2
Check the premises
Is the major premise 'everything dead is inanimate' universally true? No, because 'dead' is not typically applied to stones. The minor premise 'a stone is dead' is false. So it uses a false imaginary premise.
3
Analyse the second example
Pointing to a picture of a horse: 'This is a horse, every horse neighs, therefore this neighs.' The minor premise is false (it is a picture), but resembles truth. This is the second type.

Commentary
This passage defines sophistry (سفسطة), the fifth logical art. Sophistry is originally called 'the embellished wisdom' (الحكمة المموهة), meaning it dresses up falsehood as truth. Two Material Fallacies in Wording: Ishtirāk and Making Distinct Terms Synonymous
Author defines homonymy and false synonymy as material fallacies in wording.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between the two material fallacies of wording: homonymy (ishtirāk) and treating distinct terms as synonyms (making dhū tabāyun like radīf).

Core Idea
Material fallacies in wording arise when terms are used equivocally or falsely treated as synonyms, even if premises are individually plausible.

So far we have studied formal validity—whether the form of a syllogism guarantees truth. But even a formally valid syllogism can fail if its material (the meanings of terms) is corrupt. This lesson examines two such material fallacies rooted in wording: homonymy (ishtirāk) and falsely equating distinct terms as synonyms. The source passage defines these errors and illustrates the second with a classic example which we will dissect step by step.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two types of wording fallacies
Read the first line: 'في اللفظ كاشتراك أو كجعل ذي تباين مثل الرديف مأخذا'. Identify the two categories: اشتِراك (ishtirāk) and جعل ذي تباين مثل الرديف (making two distinct things like synonyms). These are the two material fallacies in wording.
2
Parse the key terms
Understand اشتراك (homonymy): one word with multiple meanings. For the second type, break down: ذو تباين = two things that are distinct (not fully different, but partially distinct, like generality/specificity); رديف = synonym; مأخذ = the act of taking them in the premises. So the fallacy is taking two distinct terms as if they were synonyms in the premises.
Radīf typically means true synonym, but here it is used for the fallacy of false synonymy.
3
Examine the example: the 'sword' syllogism
The author gives: 'This is a sword' (minor premise, pointing to a non-cutting object) and 'Every sword is sharp' (major premise, meaning the cutting one). Conclusion: 'This is sharp.' Trace how 'sword' (sayf) is used in the minor as a physical object regardless of cutting ability, but in the major it is restricted to cutting swords. This equivocation makes the syllogism materially invalid.
The error is not in the form (which is valid: Barbara), but in the material due to ambiguous wording.
4
Understand why 'sword' and 'sharp' are partially distinct
The commentary explains that 'sword' (sayf) is a name for an object with a certain form, whether cutting or not, while 'sharp' (ṣārim) is a name for that object with the additional condition of being cutting. Thus between them there is absolute generality and specificity (عموم وخصوص مطلق). They are not synonyms; they are partially distinct (تباين جزئي). Treating them as synonyms in the premises is the fallacy.
5
Recognize the material nature of the error
The author emphasizes that the error is fi māddatihi (in its material), not in its form. Even if the major premise were true when interpreted univocally, the syllogism fails because the middle term (sayf) is not used in the same sense in both premises. This is a material fallacy in wording.
Compare with homonymy: similar but with a single word having multiple meanings; here we have two distinct words falsely equated.

Material Error in Meaning: Definition and Scope
Definition of material error in meaning and response to an objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cause of material error in meaning according to the Mantiq tradition, and explain why this same cause can also lead to error in wording in cases of homonymy.

Core Idea
Confusing false with true is the root of material error, but it can also cause verbal error.

In this passage, the commentator defines the cause of material error in meaning (khata' fi al-ma'na) as 'iltibas al-kadhibah bi-dhati sidq' – that is, a false proposition being confused with a true one because it resembles truth. He then addresses a subtle objection: this same confusion can also cause error in wording (khata' fi al-lafz) when homonymy is involved. Understanding this overlap is crucial for distinguishing types of fallacies in Islamic logical analysis.

An accidental property does not entail the same conclusion as an essential one – confusion leads to false inference.

Author gives the second example: conclusion identical to a premise.

Core Idea

The second example in the passage illustrates a different material error: when the conclusion is identical to one of the premises. This reasoning is circular and provides no new information. The commentator also discusses a nuance involving naming. You will learn to recognize such circular arguments and understand why they are considered fallacious in logic.

Key: the preposition 'min' (from) indicates origin, making it a compound concept.
3
Recognize tafsil al-murakkab (fallacy of composition)
The objection incorrectly treats the restricted compound 'fahm min' (understanding from) as if it were the same as the simple 'fahm' (understanding). This is a classic fallacy of composition: dividing a compound into parts and applying a property of the part to the whole.
The fallacy is also known as 'composition fallacy' in Western logic, but here it is specifically applied to a definition.
4
Apply the resolution to the second objection
The second objection (indicator described by indication before and after understanding) is not answered here, but the method shows how careful attention to the precise sense of terms can avoid fallacious reasoning.
For a full treatment, see the commentator's discussion of al-Sa'd's objection in al-Mutawwal.

Commentary
This passage presents two objections against the definition of dalala (indication) as fahm (understanding) and gives the first answer. The author first states that indication is a cause of understanding, and the cause is distinct from the effect, so defining indication by understanding—which is the effect—is invalid. The second objection argues that the indicator (the word) is described by indication both before and after understanding occurs, so if indication were understanding, then it would have to precede itself (a temporal paradox). The answer to the first objection uses the concept of the fallacy of composition (tafsil al-murakkab). The term 'fahm' in the definition is understood in a restricted sense: 'fahm min al-lafz' (understanding from the word) where the preposition 'min' indicates the origin. This understanding is a relation involving the hearer, the utterance, and the meaning. The author clarifies that 'fahm' can be attributed to the hearer as the one who understands, to the utterance as that from which understanding is derived, and to the meaning as that which is understood. Thus, the earlier objection conflated two different senses of 'fahm'. So equating them is invalid.

The Definition of Sophistry (Safsata) and Its Premises
Author defines safsata and its two types of premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sophistry as a syllogism composed of wahmiyyat or mushabbahat, and distinguish between these two types of fallacious premises.

Core Idea
Sophistry is a deceptive syllogism built on imaginative or false resemblance premises.

In this lesson, we examine the precise definition of sophistry (safsata) given by the author. We will break down the two types of premises that compose it: wahmiyyat (imaginative premises from the estimative faculty) and mushabbahat (false propositions that resemble well-known truths). Understanding these is the first step to recognizing and avoiding fallacious arguments in Islamic scholarly discourse.

As for sophistry (safsata), it is a syllogism composed of imaginative premises (wahmiyyat) or false similarities (mushabbahat). The imaginative premises are false propositions that the estimative faculty (wahm) judges in non-sensible matters. Among the ways to know the falsity of the wahm is that it supports the intellect in premises that produce the opposite of what it judged, as the wahm judges fear from a dead person, while it agrees with the intellect that the dead person is an inanimate object, and an inanimate object is not feared, producing our statement: 'The dead person is not feared,' so when the intellect and wahm reach the conclusion, the wahm recoils and denies it. And by 'mushabbahat' is meant false propositions that resemble well-known (mashhurat) propositions. The purpose of sophistry is to mislead the opponent and silence him. The strongest of its benefits is knowing it, so that one may beware of it, and therefore they likened it to poison, which is known in order to be distinguished, so that the person may be safe from its harm, and this is a good likeness, for in sophistry is the destruction of religion, and in poison is the destruction of the body. His saying: 'As for sophistry'—what is meant here is absolute fallacy, by way of using the restricted term for the unrestricted. So it varies by its user, i.e., the one who uses it as a proof, and the situations in which it is used are of various types: whoever uses it knowingly, giving people the impression that he is a wise man who derives proofs, it is sophistry and he is a sophist (sufista'i). Whoever uses it ignorantly is a self-deceiver, and the syllogism is also called 'fallacy' (mughalatah). Whoever uses it to deceive the people of truth and to confuse them, by setting himself up for argument and using it against the dialectician, aiming for victory, it is 'disputatiousness' (mushaghaba), and he is a mushabbih and mumari. Commentary
This passage defines sophistry (safsata) and explains its nature and categories. The author first states that sophistry is a syllogism composed of either wahmiyyat (premises from the estimative faculty) or mushabbahat (premises that resemble well-known truths). He illustrates wahmiyyat with an example: the wahm fears a dead body, although reason knows the dead body is harmless. This shows the conflict between wahm and intellect. Then the purpose of sophistry is stated: to mislead and silence an opponent. The benefit of studying sophistry is to avoid it, likened to knowing poison to avoid harm. The passage then shifts to the commentary (qawluhu), which expands the definition: safsata here means absolute fallacy, used in various contexts. The commentator distinguishes three types: (1) the educated sophist who misleads deliberately, (2) the ignorant who deceives himself, and (3) the contentious person (mushaghhib) who uses it to confuse truth-seekers. The term mughalatah covers the first two types, while external fallacies (khariji) include ad hominem insults, interrupting speech, or using irrelevant arguments. The commentator notes that such external fallacies are the ugliest and most common in his time due to people's love of victory and ignorance of proper logical rules.

Classification of Fallacies: Material vs Formal and Verbal vs Conceptual
Author classifies fallacies into material and formal, with examples of verbal fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify fallacies as material or formal, and identify verbal material fallacies due to equivocation and literal/figurative usage.

Core Idea
Fallacies arise from matter or form; verbal material fallacies hide behind ambiguous words.

In this conclusion, the author warns against corrupt syllogisms by classifying their errors. You will learn to distinguish between material and formal fallacies, and see how verbal errors like equivocation or mixing literal and figurative meanings can lead to wrong conclusions. Mastering this classification protects you from compound ignorance.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish material vs formal error
Note that formal error is in the syllogism's structure (mood/figure), while material error is in the content of the premises. The passage lists both as the two main types.
2
Recognize verbal material error
Material errors can arise from words or meanings. Here, focus on verbal errors: when the premises contain ambiguous or equivocal language.
3
Spot equivocation
Identify a word with multiple meanings used in different senses in the two premises. Example: 'ayn' meaning both 'gold coin' (eye) and 'spring of water'. The middle term shifts, breaking the syllogism.
4
Spot literal/figurative confusion
When a word is used literally in one premise and figuratively in another, the argument becomes invalid. Example: calling a picture a 'horse' and then concluding it must whinny, since only real horses whinny.

Material Fallacies from Verbal Ambiguity (Homonymy, Literal/Metaphorical, Hidden Inflection)
Classification of material fallacy from verbal causes with three examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify material fallacies caused by verbal ambiguity in syllogisms and explain why the middle term is not univocal.

Core Idea
A syllogism fails materially when its middle term carries different meanings in each premise.

The author warns us that not every syllogism that looks valid is valid. Even if the form is correct, the content—the actual meanings of the terms—can corrupt it. In this passage, he explains three ways that words themselves can make a syllogism fallacious: homonymy, mixing literal and figurative senses, and hidden inflectional differences. These are material fallacies, and they all boil down to one core problem: the middle term is not truly one term across both premises.

Guided Path

Determine the intended meaning in each premise
For each premise, decide which specific meaning of the term is intended based on the context (e.g., pointing to gold vs. water; a real horse vs. a picture; active vs. passive participle).
4
Verify whether the middle term is truly one
If the intended meanings are different, then the middle term is not univocal—it is effectively two different terms. Therefore, the syllogism commits a material fallacy from verbal ambiguity.
The error is that the connection between premises is broken; the conclusion does not logically follow.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of fallacious syllogisms (المغالطة). The author begins by stating the purpose: to warn against invalid qiyas. He divides corruption into two aspects: formal (صورة) and material (مادة). Material error is further divided into origin in the word (لفظ) or meaning (معنى). The verse states this classification, and the commentary corrects a wording: 'al-burhan' should be 'al-qiyas' because fallacy is not exclusive to demonstrative syllogisms. The first type of material fallacy from words is due to homonymy (اشتراك). The example: pointing to gold and saying 'This is a 'ayn (eye/spring), and every 'ayn is flowing' – the error is that 'عين' is homonymous: the flowing 'ayn is water, not the gold, so the middle term is not one. The second sub-type is using a word in its literal and figurative sense: pointing to a picture of a horse and saying 'This is a horse, every horse neighs' – the actual horse neighs, not the picture, so again the middle term is ambiguous. The third sub-type is when the distinction is hidden due to inflectional differences (حركة فارقة) obscured by weak letter or doubling: e.g., 'مختار' as active participle (one who chooses) vs. passive participle (chosen). In the example, pointing to an inanimate object (like a stone) and saying 'This is mukhtar (chosen), every mukhtar (choosing) is alive' – the middle term is not unified because the first is passive, the second active. These examples all illustrate how material fallacy from verbal causes prevents the middle term from being truly one.

Material Fallacy from False Synonymy (Mubayan as Muradif)
Author explains material fallacy from false synonymy with example of 'سيف' and 'صارم'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a material fallacy caused by treating two distinct terms as synonyms in a syllogism, using the example of 'سيف' and 'صارم'.

Core Idea
False synonymy breaks the unity of the middle term, turning a valid form into a sophism.

In logic, distinguishing synonyms from distinct terms is crucial for argument validity. This lesson explores a material fallacy where two terms with different connotations are mistakenly thought identical. Through 'سيف' (sword) and 'صارم' (cutting sword), we see how confusion corrupts the syllogism, producing sophistry. A grammatical note about 'ذو' in the versifier's line clarifies language precision. By understanding this fallacy, you will avoid errors in qiyas and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the fallacious syllogism structure
Look at the example: 'This is a صارم, and every سيف is either cutting or not cutting.' Identify the premises and intended conclusion. The reasoner uses two different terms in the premises, treating them as one.
This is a classic case of material fallacy, not formal.
2
Examine the meanings of the key terms
Define 'سيف' and 'صارم'. 'سيف' is a general term for a sword without specifying cutting; 'صارم' specifies a sword that cuts. They are not synonyms because one includes an additional property.
The author compares it to the real synonymy of 'إنسان' and 'بشر'.
3
Check the unity of the middle term
In the first premise, the middle term is 'صارم' (with cutting); in the second, it is 'سيف' (without specifying cutting). If the reasoner assumes they are identical, the middle term is not truly one term.
A valid syllogism requires a single middle term with uniform meaning.
4
Conclude the material fallacy
The syllogism gives the false impression that the terms are synonymous, but they are not. The argument is sophistry because the material (terms) is inconsistent, even if the form were valid.
The fallacy lies in assuming synonymy where there is only partial overlap.

Fallacy of Hidden Grammatical Change
Author explains fallacy of confusing اسم فاعل and اسم مفعول due to hidden grammatical change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of hidden grammatical change in categorical syllogisms and explain why the middle term is equivocal.

Core Idea
When the active and passive participle sound alike, the middle term becomes ambiguous.

This lesson examines a subtle fallacy where a word like 'mukhtar' can be either the active participle ('chooser') or passive participle ('chosen'). When such a word is used as the middle term in a syllogism, the argument may appear valid but is actually unsound. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to detect equivocation in scholarly arguments, especially in contexts where Arabic morphology matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous word
Locate the word that has identical form for active and passive participle. In the example, it is 'mukhtar'.
Other examples: 'mudtar' (forced / compelling), 'mukhtār' (chooser / chosen).
2
Distinguish the two possible meanings
Determine the intended grammatical role in each premise: is it active (doer) or passive (undergoer)? In the first premise, 'mukhtar' is passive (chosen); in the second, it is active (chooser).
3
Reconstruct the fallacious syllogism
Write the syllogism: This (stone) is mukhtar (passive). Every mukhtar (active) is living. Therefore, this stone is living. The middle term 'mukhtar' is not univocal.
4
Explain why the fallacy occurs
The grammatical vowel (حركة فارقة) that would normally distinguish active from passive has been hidden due to vowel elision (إعلال) or gemination (إدغام). This makes the word sound the same despite different grammatical states.
In standard Arabic, the active participle of 'ikhtāra' is 'mukhtār' (with long ā), while the passive is 'mukhtār' (identical after phonological rules).

Begging the Question (Musadara): Detection and Classification
Author explains the fallacy of making the conclusion a premise (musadara) with example and commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a musadara fallacy in a categorical syllogism and explain why it is invalid, as well as understand the scholarly debate about its classification.

Core Idea
Begging the question occurs when the conclusion is smuggled into the premises via synonymy or equivalence.

In this passage, the author introduces the second type of material fallacy: musadara, or begging the question. Understanding this fallacy is crucial for ensuring that theological and juristic arguments do not assume what they are trying to prove. The author provides a clear example from the Islamic science of logic and then engages with a scholarly debate about whether this fallacy is best classified as material or formal. By the end, you will be able to spot circular reasoning in Islamic scholarly debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion and premises
In the given example, the conclusion is 'Every human is laughing.' The premises are 'Every human is bashar' and 'Every bashar is laughing.' Note the terms involved.
2
Check for synonymy between a premise and the conclusion
The major premise 'Every bashar is laughing' uses 'bashar', which the author states is synonymous with 'insan' (human). Thus the premise is simply restating the conclusion in different words. This makes the argument circular.
Synonymy (taraduf) is key: if a premise and the conclusion have the same meaning, the argument is begging the question.
3
Evaluate whether the argument would convince an opponent
If the opponent does not accept the synonymy between 'insan' and 'bashar', the argument fails. The author notes that if the opponent rejects the synonymy, one must provide linguistic proof from the authorities (ahl al-lugha).
This step highlights that musadara often relies on unacknowledged assumptions about meaning.
4
Analyze the classification debate: material vs. formal
The author argues against the versifier (Ibn al-Hajib) who placed musadara under 'confusion of false with true'. The commentator and al-Katibi treat it as an independent type. The hashia adds that it may be considered false only relative to the opponent's claim.
Understanding this debate deepens your grasp of how scholars categorize logical errors.

Material Fallacy: Ruling for the Genus by a Species Ruling
Author explains the fallacy of applying a species' ruling to the whole genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of 'ibham al-'aks' in arguments from Islamic sciences and distinguish it from valid conversion.

Core Idea
The fallacy of 'ibham al-'aks' occurs when you assume a species' property applies to the whole genus.

In this lesson, we examine the fallacy known as 'ibham al-'aks', where one erroneously applies a ruling of a species to the entire genus. This often arises from confusing the converse of a proposition. We'll see examples from animal classification and humoral medicine, and then connect to Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path

Sophistical Syllogisms with False Premises
Author illustrates a sophistical syllogism using false premises to mislead.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a sophistical syllogism that uses false premises and distinguish it from a formally valid but unsound argument.

Core Idea
False premises can make even a valid syllogism produce a false conclusion and deceive the unwary.

In this concluding section, the author presents two examples of syllogistic reasoning intended to mislead rather than inform. By examining these examples, you will learn how false premises, even in a formally correct argument form, can still lead to false conclusions. This is a material fallacy, not a formal one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises of the first example
The first statement is 'Every human is an animal' (true) and 'Every animal is a horse' (false). Write them down as a categorical syllogism.
The first premise is true; the second is false.
2
Determine the syllogistic form
This is a Barbara syllogism: All A are B, All B are C, so All A are C. The form is valid.
Validity concerns form, not truth of premises.
3
Spot the deception
The speaker uses the valid form to suggest that the conclusion 'Some human is a horse' follows. But because the premise 'Every animal is a horse' is false, the conclusion is not guaranteed. The listener is tricked by the apparent logical structure.
This is a material fallacy: relying on a false premise.
4
Analyze the second example
The statements 'No human is a horse' and 'Every horse is an animal' are said to give the false impression that 'No human is an animal'. Here the premises are true, but the conclusion does not logically follow from them; it is a non sequitur. The deception lies in the suggestive sequence, not a valid syllogism.
The second example is an informal fallacy of false implication.

خاتمة. يُقَالُ: «كُلُّ إِنْسَانٍ حَيَوَانٌ وَكُلُّ حَيَوَانٍ فَرَسٌ» لِيُوهِمَ أَنَّ بَعْضَ الإِنْسَانِ فَرَسٌ، أَوْ يُقَالُ: «لَا شَيْءَ مِنَ الإِنْسَانِ بِفَرَسٍ، وَكُلُّ فَرَسٍ حَيَوَانٌ» لِيُوهِمَ أَنَّهُ لَا شَيْءَ مِنَ الْإِنْسَانِ بِحَيَوَانٍ. وَاللهُ سُبْحَانَهُ أَعْلَمُ بِالصَّوَابِ وَإِلَيْهِ المَرْجِعُ وَالمَآبُ لَا رَبَّ غَيْرَهُ وَلَا مَعْبُودَ بِالحَقِّ سِوَاهُ، وَصَلَّى اللهُ عَلَى سَيِّدِنَا مُحَمَّدٍ وَعَلَى آلِهِ وَصَحْبِهِ وَسَلَّمَ تَسْلِيماً.
Conclusion. It is said: 'Every human is an animal, and every animal is a horse' to give the false impression that some human is a horse; or it is said: 'No human is a horse, and every horse is an animal' to give the false impression that no human is an animal. And Allah, glorified, knows best what is correct, and to Him is the return and refuge. There is no lord besides Him and no deity worthy of worship in truth other than Him. May Allah bless our master Muhammad and his family and companions and grant them peace.

Commentary
This passage presents two examples of sophistical syllogisms that rely on false premises to deceive. The author begins by stating a syllogism: 'Every human is an animal, and every animal is a horse.' Though the form is categorical (Barbara: all A are B, all B are C), the minor premise 'every animal is a horse' is false. The purpose is to mislead someone into thinking that 'some human is a horse,' which is the intended false conclusion. The second example uses a different form: 'No human is a horse, and every horse is an animal' (which in standard categorical logic would be Cesare: No A is B, All C is B, so No A is C). Here the major premise 'no human is a horse' is true, but the minor 'every horse is an animal' is true; however, the conclusion 'no human is an animal' is false because the minor premise is irrelevant—the syllogism is invalid if taken as a valid form? Actually the author's point is that by uttering such statements, one falsely suggests an entailment that is not logically sound. The passage underscores that material truth of premises is essential; even valid forms can mislead if premises are false or if the listener is tricked into accepting a non-sequitur. This is a classic illustration of a material fallacy, not a formal one. The author then ends with a prayer, indicating the seriousness of avoiding such deceit.
