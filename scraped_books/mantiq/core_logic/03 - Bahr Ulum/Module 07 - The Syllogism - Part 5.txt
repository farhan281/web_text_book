Critique of the Causality Requirement for Entailment
Author refutes the Peripatetic claim that entailment requires causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the Peripatetic claim that every entailment requires a causal relationship by providing a counterexample from impossible things.

Core Idea
Entailment can exist without causality, as shown by the concomitance of impossible things.

The Peripatetic logicians insisted that for two things to be necessarily entailed, there must be a causal link. The author of Bahr al-Ulum challenges this by pointing out that impossibilities can entail each other without causation, which shows the Peripatetic condition is false. In this lesson, we will analyze this critique and see how it protects logic from a flawed assumption.

Guided Path
1
State the Peripatetic condition
The Peripatetics claim: 'There must be in entailment a relation of causality; for if not, separation would be valid.' Identify this claim in the Arabic.
2
Find the author's counterexample
The author uses 'تلازم المحالين' (entailment of impossible things) as a counterexample. Recognize that impossible things (e.g., two contradictory propositions) can entail each other without a cause.
Impossibilities are not subject to causation.
3
Explain why it refutes the condition
If the Peripatetic condition were true, then impossible things could not entail each other. But they do, so the condition is false. The author says 'فإنه منقوض بتلازم المحالين'.
4
Recognize the broader implication
This shows that logical necessity does not require causality. The author adds that stripping the intellect from illusions reveals that such conditionals are not necessary.
This insight protects logical proofs from an unnecessary restriction.

الصناعات الخمس
ثم إن هذا القول بزعمهم لم ينقله إلا أربعة أو سبعة، فكيف ينعقد بهم التواتر في مثل هذا الأمر؛ لتَوَفُّر الدواعي على نقل مثل هذا؟ مثال آخر: يحكم العقل بأن الميت جماد، ويساعد عليه الوهم أيضا ، وكل جماد لا يستحق أن يُخاف عنه، وهو أيضا يساعد عليه الوهم العقل ، فإذا استنتج لا يقبله ، بل يحكم باستحقاق الخوف.
The syllogism, such as the saying of the Peripatetics: 'There must be in entailment a relation of causality; for if there were no relation of causality between the two entailed things, separation would be valid, and the consequent is false, and the conditional therein has the strength of the original sought. And perhaps something more hidden than the sought is taken. And the way out of the refutation, if its example is possible, is the saying of the aforementioned Peripatetics; for it is refuted by the entailment of impossible things, so the falsehood of the conditional appears, and the stripping of the intellect from illusions and reflection, meaning it appears that it is not necessary, just as if we strip the consideration in that conditional and find it similar to the sought.'
And regarding when tawatur is claimed, especially observing the generations between the claimant of tawatur and the reported [event], if in every generation the amount of tawatur is found, it is accepted, otherwise not. Its example is the saying of the aforementioned Rafidah; for if we trace the sayings of the Companions and the Followers and those after them, we find for this report neither a trace nor an effect, we judge the falsity of its tawatur.
Another path in distinguishing between the false and the necessary is by collecting pure intellect, untainted by illusion, with necessary premises present to it, concerning which illusion does not dispute the intellect, then it produces from them the opposite of that proposition, so it is known that it is from the errors of illusion, as in the aforementioned example; for the matter of the caliphate was most important to the Companions, and at Ghadir Khumm there were more than one hundred thousand men, and there was no benefit for them in concealing it, and none of them narrated it, despite their need, and whenever [it is] like that, the report about it is definitively judged false by intellectual necessity. So it is known that the report of handing over the caliphate to the Commander of the Faithful is pure fabrication.
The Five Crafts
Then this saying, in their claim, was transmitted by only four or seven, so how could tawatur be established by them in such a matter, given the abundance of motives to transmit such [an event]? Another example: the intellect judges that the dead body is inanimate, and illusion also agrees with it, and every inanimate object does not deserve to be feared, and the intellect's illusion also agrees with it, but when it infers, it does not accept it, rather it judges that fear is deserved.

Commentary
This passage from Bahr Ulum presents several logical methods for evaluating claims of necessity, using the Shia claim about the caliphate as a recurring example. The author begins by critiquing the Peripatetic (Mashshai'in) position that entailment (talazum) requires a causal relationship, showing that this is refuted by cases where impossible things (muhalayn) are concomitant—here the conditional proposition is false, and stripping the intellect of illusions reveals that such conditionals are not necessary. The author then introduces two methods for distinguishing false from necessary propositions. The first method relies on tawatur (mutawatir transmission): if a claim is said to be known by tawatur, one must check whether each generation has sufficient transmitters; if not, the claim is false. Applied to the Shia claim of the Prophet's succession at Ghadir Khumm, no such transmission is found among the Companions or later generations. The second method uses pure intellect (aql sarr) freed from illusion (wahm): by assembling necessary premises that illusion does not contest, the intellect can deduce the opposite of the claim, revealing it as an error of illusion. The author applies this to the same historical example: the caliphate was of utmost importance, there were over a hundred thousand witnesses at Ghadir Khumm, no benefit in concealing, and yet none narrated—thus the claim is necessarily false. Finally, under the heading 'The Five Crafts' (al-sinā‘āt al-khams), the author adds that the number of claimed transmitters (four or seven) is insufficient for tawatur, and gives another example concerning a dead body: intellect and illusion initially agree that a dead body is inanimate and not to be feared, but the intellect eventually corrects the illusion and judges that fear is deserved (perhaps referring to awe of Allah or punishment). This passage illustrates the integration of logical critique, historical verification, and intellectual discipline.

Verifying Claims through Tawatur (Unbroken Transmission)
Author presents tawatur as a method to distinguish false from necessary propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tawatur criterion to evaluate whether a historical claim is necessarily true or false.

Core Idea
Tawatur yields certainty; lacking tawatur across generations signals falsehood.

In the Islamic intellectual tradition, tawatur is a gold standard for verifying reports that yield certain knowledge. The author applies this to the Shia claim about the caliphate at Ghadir Khumm. By checking each generation, we find no mutawatir transmission, so the claim is false. In this lesson, you will learn to use this method to distinguish true from false historical propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim said to be mutawatir
Locate the claim that is presented as mutawatir. In the example, it is the Shia report about the caliphate.
2
Define the generations
Determine the generations from the event to the claimant. The author mentions 'ملاحظة القرون' – observing the centuries/generations.
Generations: Sahaba, Tabi'un, and later.
3
Check sufficiency in each generation
In every generation, there must be a number of transmitters reaching the tawatur threshold (usually more than four or seven). If any generation lacks this, the claim of tawatur fails.
4
Conclude falsity if condition not met
The author finds no trace among Sahaba and later, so judges the tawatur claim false.
Absence of evidence in this context is evidence of absence.

Using Pure Intellect to Distinguish False from Necessary Propositions
Author introduces pure intellect as a method to correct illusions and identify false propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a pure intellectual deduction to refute a false claim by showing it contradicts necessary premises.

Core Idea
Pure intellect, freed from illusion, can deduce the opposite of a false claim from necessary premises.

Illusion (wahm) can cloud our judgment and make us accept false propositions as necessary. The author presents a method to overcome this: collect necessary premises that even illusion does not dispute, and then deduce the opposite of the claim in question. This reveals the claim as an error of illusion. We'll learn this through the example of the caliphate claim and the dead body example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the false proposition
Determine the claim that is being evaluated. In the first example, it is the Shia report about the caliphate. In the second, it is the inference that the dead body should not be feared.
2
Gather necessary premises
Collect premises that are undeniably true and necessary, such as 'the caliphate was important', 'there was a large crowd', 'no benefit in concealment', etc. Ensure these premises are not disputed by illusion.
These premises are accepted by both intellect and illusion.
3
Deduce the opposite proposition
From these premises, derive the opposite of the claim. For the caliphate example, the opposite is that the report is false by intellectual necessity.
The author says: 'ينتج منها خلاف تلك القضية' (produces the opposite).
4
Recognize the error of illusion
Conclude that the original claim is an error of illusion (غلط الوهم). The dead body example shows that even when illusion agrees with premises, the conclusion can be wrong; pure intellect corrects it.
In the dead body case, the intellect eventually rejects the conclusion and judges that fear is deserved.
