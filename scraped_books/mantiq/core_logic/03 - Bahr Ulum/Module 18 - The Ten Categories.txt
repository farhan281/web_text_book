Two Types of Relative Attributes: Insidami and Intizami
Author distinguishes between two types of relative attributes based on whether both relata must exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive and abstractive relative attributes and classify a given relation as either type based on whether both relata must exist externally.

Core Idea
Relative attributes are either conjunctive (both relata exist) or abstractive (only subject exists).

In Islamic logic, relations (nisab) are treated with precision. The author here defines two types of relative attributes: conjunctive (insidami) and abstractive (intizami), distinguished by whether both the subject and attribute must exist externally. Understanding this distinction helps you analyse how relations are ontologically grounded in Kalam and Fiqh reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Find the Arabic terms for the two types: الإِنْصَافُ الإِنْضِمَامِيُّ (conjunctive) and الإِنْصَافُ الاِنْتِزَاعِيُّ (abstractive). Note that the passage presents them as a third point (الثَّالِثَةُ).
These terms are technical; later texts use them consistently.
2
Extract the condition for each
For conjunctive: 'يَسْتَدْعِي تَحَقَّقَ الْحَاشِيَتَيْنِ' (requires realization of both relata). For abstractive: 'يَسْتَدْعِي تُبُوتَ الْمَوْصُوفِ فَقَط' (requires only the subsistence of the subject).
The word 'حَاشِيَة' here means a relatum.
3
Understand the ontological grounding
The author explains that an attribute (الإِنْصَاف) is a relation (نِسْبَة), and every relation's existence is derivative from the relata. In the conjunctive case, both relata exist externally, so the relation is also external. In the abstractive case, only the subject exists externally; the attribute is a mental abstraction.
This follows from the principle: 'كُلُّ نِسْبَةٍ تَحَقَّقُهَا فَرْعُ تَحَقَّقَ الْمُنتَسَبَيْنِ'.
4
Apply to examples
The passage gives two examples: body and white (conjunctive, because both exist externally and are united in the same individual), and sky and above-ness (abstractive, because sky exists externally but above-ness is a mental relation). Practise by classifying other pairs.
In the conjunctive external case, the subject and attribute are 'مُتَحِدًا مَعَ الصِّفَةِ فِي الْأَعْيَانِ'.

الثَّالِثَةُ : الإِنْصَافُ الإِنْضِمَامِيُّ يَسْتَدْعِي تَحَقَّقَ الْحَاشِيَتَيْنِ فِي ظَرْفِ الأَنْصَافِ بِخِلَافِ الاِنْتِزَاعِيُّ، يَسْتَدْعِي تُبُوتَ الْمَوْصُوفِ فَقَط ، فَمُطْلَقُ الإِنْصَافِ لَا يَسْتَدْعِي تُبُوتَ الصَّفَةِ في ظَرْفِهِ. أَمَّا مُطْلَقُ النُّبُوتِ فَضَرُورِيٌّ ؛ فَإِنَّ مَا لَا يَكُونَ مَوْجُودًا في نَفْسِهِ يَسْتَحِيلُ أَنْ يَكُونَ مَوْجُودًا لِشَيْءٍ وَالأَنْصَافُ لَيْسَ مُتَحَقِّفًا في الْخَارج ، حَتَّى يَلْزَمَ تَحَقُّقُ الصَّفَةِ فِيهِ؛ لِأَنَّهُ يَسْبَةٌ، وَكُلُّ نِسْبَةٍ تَحَقَّقُهَا فَرْعُ تَحَقَّقَ الْمُنتَسَبَيْنِ ، بَلْ هُوَ مُتَحَفِّقٌ فِي الذِّهْنِ، وَإِنْ كَانَ فِي الْإِنْضِمَامِيِّ الْخَارِجِيِّ الْمَوْصُوفِ مُتَحِدًا مَعَ الصِّفَةِ فِي الْأَعْيَانِ، كَالْجِسْمِ وَالْأَبْيَضِ، وَفِي الانْتِزَاعِيُّ الْخَارِجِي مُنْحِدًا بِحَسَبِ الْأَعْيَانِ ، كَالسَّمَاءِ وَالْفَوْقِيَّةِ.
The third [point]: The conjunctive attribute (al-insaf al-insidami) requires the reality of both relata in the context of the attributes, contrary to the abstractive attribute (al-insaf al-intizami), which requires only the subsistence of the subject. So absolute attribution does not require the subsistence of the attribute in its context. As for absolute subsistence, it is necessary; for that which is not existent in itself is impossible to be existent for something. And the attribute is not realized in the external, such that the reality of the attribute in it would be necessary; because it is a relation, and every relation's reality is derivative from the reality of the two relata. Rather, it is realized in the mind, although in the external conjunctive, the subject is united with the attribute in individuals, like body and white; and in the external abstractive, it is united according to individuals, like heaven and above-ness.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of relative attributes (insaf) in logic and ontology. The first is 'conjunctive' (insidami), which requires both the subject and the attribute to actually exist in the same circumstance; for example, the relation between body and whiteness: both must exist together. The second is 'abstractive' (intizami), which requires only the subject to exist; for example, the relation between the sky and 'aboveness' — the sky exists, but the attribute 'above-ness' is a mental relation, not an external entity. The author explains that absolute attribution does not require the attribute to exist externally, because attributes are relations, and relations only exist as mental entities upon the existence of the relata. In the external, the subject and attribute may be identical in existence (as in body and whiteness) or merely united in terms of the individuals (as in sky and above-ness). The passage uses examples from physics (body and white) and cosmology (sky and above-ness).

Key Terms
الإِنْصَاف الإِنْضِمَامِي (al-insaf al-insidami): A relative attribute that requires both the subject and the attribute to exist in the same context. [Note: This distinction is common in later logic texts; not prominent in earlier works.]
الإِنْصَاف الاِنْتِزَاعِي (al-insaf al-intizami): A relative attribute that requires only the subject to exist; the attribute is abstracted by the mind.
الحاشية (al-hāshiya): Here meaning the two relata (subject and attribute) involved in a relation. [Note: In other contexts, hāshiya means marginal commentary.]

Nine Types of Modal Propositions
Author enumerates nine types of modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the nine types of modal propositions based on their modal conditions.

Core Idea
Modality qualifies the subject-predicate relation in nine distinct ways.

In Islamic logic, propositions are not merely true or false—they carry a modality that specifies how the relation holds. This lesson dissects the classification of modal propositions (muwajjhat) into nine types, each defined by a unique condition on the relation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modal dimension
Notice that each type is defined by a condition on the relation's separation (infikak) or actuality (fi'liyya) or possibility (imkan). The conditions revolve around time, description, or absolute terms.
The Arabic terms often end with 'mutlaqa' (absolute) or 'amma' (general).
2
Group by necessity, perpetuity, actuality, or possibility
The first four types (daruriyya, mashruta, waqtiyya, muntashira) deal with necessity/conditionality. The next two (da'ima, 'urfiyya) are about perpetuity. Then mutlaqa 'amma is actual, and the final two are possible.
'Mumkina 'amma' negates impossibility of the relation; 'mumkina khassa' negates impossibility of both sides.
3
Parse the condition for each type
For each type, extract the precise condition: e.g., 'istihalat infikak al-nisba mutlaqan' for daruriyya, 'ma dama al-wasf' for mashruta, etc. Write them down in order.
Use the key_terms list for quick reference.
4
Distinguish similar types
Compare mashruta 'amma and 'urfiyya 'amma: both condition on the description, but the latter is about non-separation (perpetuity) while the former is about necessity. Similarly, waqtiyya vs muntashira differ only in time specificity.
The difference between daruriyya and da'ima is that daruriyya is impossibility of separation, da'ima is non-separation.
5
Apply the affirmation/negation remark
The author states that the same modal terms apply to both affirmative and negative propositions; only the wording changes (e.g., 'necessarily is' vs 'necessarily is not'). This means the classification is independent of polarity.
This simplifies memorization: learn nine types, not eighteen.

فَهِيَ إِنْ حُكِمَ فِيهَا بِاسْتِحَالَةِ انْفِكَاكِ النِّسْبَةِ مُطْلَقًا فَضَرُورِيَّةٌ مُطْلَقَةٌ، أَوْ مَا دَامَ الْوَصْفُ فَمَشْرُوطَةٌ عَامَّةٌ ، أَوْ فِي وَقْتٍ مُعَيَّنٍ فَوَقْتِيَّةٌ مُطْلَقَةٌ، أَوْ غَيْرِ مُعَيَّنٍ فَمُنْتَشِرَةٌ مُطْلَقَةٌ، أَوْ بِعَدَمِ انْفِكَاكِهَا مُطْلَقًا فَدَائِمَةٌ مُطْلَقَةٌ، أَوْ مَا دَامَ الْوَصْفُ فَعُرْفِيَّةٌ عَامَّةٌ، أَوْ بِفِعْلِيَّتِهَا فَمُطْلَقَةٌ عَامَّةٌ، أَوْ بِعَدَمِ اسْتِحَالَتِهَا فَمُمْكِنَةٌ عَامَّةٌ، أَوْ بِعَدَمِ اسْتِحَالَةِ الطَّرَفَيْنِ فَمُمْكِنَةٌ خَاصَّةٌ ، وَلَا فَرْقَ بَيْنَ الْإِيجَابِ وَالسَّلْبِ فِيهَا إِلَّا في اللُّفْظِ .
So they [the propositions] are: if the impossibility of the separation of the relation is judged absolutely, then it is absolute necessary; or as long as the description [exists], then it is general conditional; or in a specific time, then it is absolute temporal; or in an unspecified time, then it is absolute diffusive; or by the non-separation of it absolutely, then it is absolute perpetual; or as long as the description [exists], then it is general conventional; or by its actuality, then it is general absolute; or by the impossibility of it, then it is general possible; or by the non-impossibility of both sides, then it is specific possible. And there is no difference between affirmation and negation in them except in wording.
Source pages p. 135

Commentary
This passage is a classification (taqsim) of modal propositions (muwajjhat) based on the modality of the relation between subject and predicate. The author lists nine types, each defined by a specific modal condition. The first four deal with necessity and conditionality: absolute necessary (daruriyya mutlaqa) when the relation cannot be separated at all; general conditional (mashruta 'amma) when the relation holds as long as a description (wasf) exists; absolute temporal (waqtiyya mutlaqa) when the relation holds in a specific time; absolute diffusive (muntashira mutlaqa) when it holds in an unspecified time. The next two deal with perpetuity: absolute perpetual (da'ima mutlaqa) when the relation never separates; general conventional ('urfiyya 'amma) when it holds as long as the description exists (similar to conditional but with perpetuity). Then general absolute (mutlaqa 'amma) when the relation is actual; general possible (mumkina 'amma) when its impossibility is negated; specific possible (mumkina khassa) when both sides are not impossible. The author notes that affirmation and negation do not affect the types; only the wording changes (e.g., 'necessary' vs. 'necessarily not'). This enumeration is a standard taxonomy in Islamic logic, often memorized.

Refining Modal Propositions with Essential Non-Permanence
Author shows how general and temporal modal propositions are restricted by adding essential non-permanence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the refined modal proposition types derived from general and temporal propositions by adding essential non-permanence or non-necessity.

Core Idea
Essential non-permanence refines general and temporal modal propositions into more specific types.

Modal propositions, as we have learned, come in various degrees of necessity and permanence. This passage shows how the basic types—the two general propositions and the two absolute temporal ones—can be further restricted by adding the condition of essential non-permanence. Additionally, the general absolute proposition can be restricted by either non-necessity or essential non-permanence. Understanding these refinements is key to precisely expressing the temporal and essential conditions of a relation in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base types
Locate the four base types being restricted: the two general propositions (mashruta 'amma and 'urfiyya 'amma) and the two absolute temporal ones (waqtiyya mutlaqa and muntashira mutlaqa). Note also the general absolute (mutlaqa 'amma).
These are among the nine basic modal types discussed earlier.
2
Understand the condition-adding operation
The operation is 'tafyiḍ' (restriction). The passage states that the general and temporal types are restricted by 'la-dawam dhati' (essential non-permanence). For the general absolute, the restriction applies either by 'la-darura' (non-necessity) or 'la-dawam dhati'.
This is a refinement to capture more specific temporal or essential conditions.
3
Identify the resulting types
From the first operation, we get four types: mashruta khassa (specific conditional), 'urfiyya khassa (specific conventional), waqtiyya (temporal), and muntashira (diffusive). From the second operation, we get wujudiyya la-daruriyya (existential non-necessary) and wujudiyya la-da'ima (existential non-permanent), the latter also called mutlaqa iskandariyya (Alexandrian absolute).
The names of waqtiyya and muntashira are shortened from their longer forms.
4
Classify and associate
Organize the refined types by their base and condition. For example, mashruta khassa is mashruta 'amma + la-dawam dhati. Wujudiyya la-daruriyya is mutlaqa 'amma + la-darura. Practice by explaining each refined type in terms of its base and restrictive condition.
This classification helps in recognizing the logical force of each proposition.

Commentary
This passage shows how the basic nine modal types can be further refined. The author mentions that the two general propositions (mashruta 'amma and 'urfiyya 'amma) and the two absolute temporal ones (waqtiyya mutlaqa and muntashira mutlaqa) are restricted by adding the condition of 'essential non-permanence' (al-la-dawam al-dhati). This yields four new types: specific conditional (mashruta khassa), specific conventional ('urfiyya khassa), temporal (waqtiyya), and diffusive (muntashira). The names of the last two are shortened. Additionally, the general absolute (mutlaqa 'amma) is restricted either by non-necessity (la-darura) or essential non-permanence (la-dawam dhati), resulting in two types: existential non-necessary (wujudiyya la-daruriyya) and existential non-permanent (wujudiyya la-da'ima). The latter is also called the Alexandrian absolute (al-mutlaqa al-Iskandariyya). This refinement demonstrates how modal logic can be nuanced to capture more precise temporal and essential conditions.

Two Meanings of the General Conditional Proposition
Author explains two interpretations of al-mashruta al-`amma and their relationship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two meanings of al-mashruta al-`amma based on the role of the descriptive attribute.

Core Idea
In the general conditional, the attribute may be a cause of necessity or merely a temporal marker.

The general conditional proposition is a key modal form in Islamic logic, used extensively in usul al-fiqh to express necessary relationships. The author distinguishes two subtly different interpretations: one where the descriptive attribute actively grounds the necessity, and another where it only marks the time during which the necessity holds. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analysing legal arguments that rely on conditional necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate 'al-mashruta al-`amma' in the Arabic and note it is the third type of conditional proposition discussed.
2
Extract the two meanings
First meaning: necessity of the relation conditional upon the descriptive attribute (al-wasf al-`unwani). Second meaning: necessity of the relation in all times of the attribute.
The Arabic uses 'tāratan... wa-ukhrā...' to present the two senses.
3
Compare the role of the attribute
In the first meaning, the attribute must have an 'entry' (madkhal) in the necessity – it contributes causally. In the second, it merely marks the temporal scope.
The text explicitly states: 'fi al-awwal yajibu an yakūna li al-wasfi madkhalun fī al-ḍarūrati, bi-khilāfi al-thānī'.
4
Determine the relationship between the two meanings
They have 'umum min wajh' (generality in some respect) – each is more general in one aspect (one includes cases where the attribute is causal, the other includes cases where it is merely temporal).
This means neither fully includes the other; they overlap partially.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider 'Every mufti is knowledgeable.' In the first meaning, knowledge is necessary because of being a mufti. In the second, knowledge is necessary at all times he is a mufti (but may hold for other reasons). Identify which meaning fits a given context.

الثَّالِثُ : الْمَشْرُوطَةُ الْعَامَّةُ تَارَةٌ تُؤْخَذُ بِمَعْنَى ضَرُورَةِ النِّسْبَةِ بِشَرْطِ الْوَصْفِ الْعِنْوَانِيِّ، وَأُخْرَى بِمَعْنَى ضَرُورَتِهَا فِي جَمِيعِ أَوْقَاتِ الْوَصْفِ، وَفِي الْأَوَّلِ يَجِبُ أَنْ يَكُونَ لِلْوَصْفِ مَدْخَلٌ فِي الضَّرُورَةِ، بِخِلَافِ الثَّانِي، وَبَيْنَهُمَا عُمُومٌ مِنْ وَجْهٍ.
The third: The general conditional is sometimes taken in the sense of the necessity of the relation with the condition of the descriptive attribute, and another time in the sense of its necessity in all times of the attribute. In the first, the attribute must have an entry in the necessity, unlike the second. Between them is generality in some respect.
Source pages p. 137

Commentary
This passage defines the general conditional proposition (al-mashruta al-`amma) and distinguishes two meanings. In the first meaning, the necessity of the judgment is conditional upon the descriptive attribute (al-wasf al-`unwani) itself—meaning the attribute plays a role in the necessity. In the second meaning, the necessity applies at all times the attribute holds, but the attribute does not cause the necessity. The author notes a relation of 'generality in some respect' between these two: each includes cases the other does not. For example, in fiqh, 'Every mufti is knowledgeable' could be read in either sense: either knowledge is necessary because of the attribute 'mufti', or it is necessary at all times he is a mufti—the first is more restrictive.

Author distinguishes application of four relations to singulars vs propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish how the four logical relations apply to singular terms (based on truth about a thing) versus propositions (based on truth value).

Core Idea
The four relations differ for singulars (predication) and propositions (truth value).

The four logical relations — equality, inclusion, overlap, and contrariety — are fundamental tools for comparing concepts and statements. This passage clarifies a crucial distinction: these relations apply differently to singular terms (which can be predicated) and to propositions (which are evaluated by truth value). Understanding this difference prevents confusion when analyzing definitions or arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four relations
Recall the four relations from earlier lessons: tasāwī (equality), tadākhul (inclusion), tabāyun (overlap/disjointness), and tanāquḍ (contradiction). These are the standard set for comparing terms or propositions.
These relations are also called 'al-nisab al-arba'.
2
Understand application to singulars
For singular terms (concepts that can be predicated), the relations are determined by whether they are true of the same thing. For example, 'animal' and 'human' are in a relation of tadākhul because every human is an animal, but not vice versa.
Singulars are evaluated based on predication (al-ḥaml).
3
Understand application to propositions
For propositions, the relations are not about predication because propositions are not subjects of predication. Instead, they are compared based on their truth values in reality. For instance, two propositions are tanāquḍ if one is true and the other false in all circumstances.
The text says 'fī al-qadāyā lā yutāṣawwar' because they are not predicated.
4
Note the methodological remark
The author points out that in this science (logic) the initial consideration is based on apparent meanings, while deeper philosophical justification belongs to a more advanced stage. This highlights the pedagogical sequence: first learn the rules, then later study their foundations.
This is a reminder to stay at the right level of depth for now.

The Second Objection: Can Knowledge Fall Under Two Categories?
Author presents a second objection regarding knowledge's categorical classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the problem of a concept falling under two Aristotelian categories and evaluate the metaphorical solution proposed by al-Dawani.

Core Idea
Knowledge cannot be both substance and quality without violating category exclusivity.

This passage presents a second objection to the classification of knowledge under the category of quality. The problem is that when we conceive an essence like substance, the mental form (knowledge) becomes identical with that essence, but knowledge is also in the category of quality. This would force one thing under two categories, which is impossible. We will examine the proposed answers, including the metaphorical usage of 'quality' by al-Dawani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection states that when we conceive a substance or quantity essence, the mental form (knowledge) unites with that essence. But knowledge itself is from the category of quality. Thus, one thing falls under two categories – substance/quantity and quality – which is impossible.
The core problem is categorical exclusivity.
2
Understand the categorical framework
In Aristotelian categories, each being belongs to exactly one highest genus (category). Substance and quality are distinct and mutually exclusive. A single thing cannot be both a substance and a quality.
This is a fundamental principle in falsafah.

The first answer says 'quality' may be applied broadly to anything existing in a subject not divisible by essence, making it a universal accident that could unite with substance. But this is rejected because when applied to knowledge, its truth in that sense is denied.
This answer fails because knowledge is not accepted as that kind of quality.
4
Examine the metaphorical solution
Al-Dawani proposes using metaphor (tajawuz) in applying the category of quality to knowledge. That is, knowledge is not literally a quality but is called one by extension. This avoids the contradiction of falling under two categories.
Metaphor allows non-literal category assignment.
5
Evaluate the solution
The metaphorical solution is acceptable if we accept tajawuz. It acknowledges that the literal category assignment fails, but a metaphorical one works. The author leaves it as something to ponder ('fa-tammal').
This shows the limits of literal categorical classification for mental concepts.

Existential Verbs: The Relational Role of Defective Verbs
Author defines existential verbs as defective verbs that indicate a relation and are dependent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify existential verbs (al-kalimat al-wujudiyya) and distinguish them from perfect verbs based on their logical function of indicating a relation.

Core Idea
Existential verbs indicate a relation, not an action; they are dependent connectors.

In this passage from the 'Chapter on Words', the author clarifies the nature of existential verbs (al-kalimat al-wujudiyya), which are the defective verbs like 'kana'. Their meaning is purely relational, requiring a subject and predicate to be complete. This distinction is crucial for understanding how propositions are formed in logic, especially in Arabic sentences where the copula is often implied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Look for the term 'al-kalimat al-wujudiyya' in the Arabic. The author states that these are the defective verbs (al-af'al al-naqisa).
The term is specific to Islamic logic; in grammar they are called 'af'al naqisa'.
2
Extract the core meaning
The passage explains that 'kana' means 'the being of something something not yet mentioned'. This indicates a relation (nisba) between a subject and an unspecified predicate.
The relation is not independent; it must be completed by both terms.
3
Understand 'not independent'
The author says these verbs are 'not independent' (ghayr mustaqilla). They cannot form a complete sentence alone; they need a subject and predicate.
This is the logical defect: they indicate a relation but not a complete meaning.

Perfect verbs (al-kalima al-tamma) indicate time by a conventional form (hay'a wad'iyya) independently. Existential verbs also indicate time but only through the relational structure.
Perfect verbs like 'daraba' (he hit) give a complete action and time; existential verbs only link.

Refuting Objections to Gradation in Essence: The Case of Magnitude
Author refutes objections to tashkik in magnitude and blackness by arguing that difference is due to the essence itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument that gradation in magnitude is intrinsic, not due to an external relation.

Core Idea
Gradation in essence is real: magnitude is larger or smaller by itself, not by addition.

In this passage, the Illuminationist author defends the view that gradation (tashkik) can occur in the very essence of things, not merely by external relations. The first objection claims that difference in magnitude arises from a relation (idafah) between two magnitudes. The author refutes this by showing that increase and decrease are inherent in the essence of magnitude itself. By analyzing this refutation, you will learn how to defend a metaphysical claim about gradation against a relational counterargument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector says difference in magnitude is due to a relation (idafah) when comparing large and small.
2
Examine the author's initial response
The essence of magnitude applies equally to both; difference arises only upon comparison, but the relation is not the source of the increase.
3
Analyze the argument from al-Urwa al-Wuthqa
If the relation is conjoined to magnitude, it becomes quantity, which again depends on magnitude; if it is abstracted, it must be abstracted from magnitude itself, making magnitude the source.
4
Understand the concept of hiyyah
The author uses the term al-hiyyah (a privative whatness) to argue that non-quantity cannot be the source of increase/decrease, reinforcing that magnitude itself is the subject.
5
Synthesize the author's conclusion
The increase/decrease is intrinsic to magnitude; the objection fails because it posits an external source where none is needed.

Three Modes of Consideration: Accident and Substance
Al-Dawwani’s three modes of considering accident and substance explained.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three modes of considering a nature (la bi-shart shay', bi-shart shay', bi-shart la shay') and apply them to accident and substance in Islamic ontology.

Core Idea
How we consider a nature changes its logical status—unconditional, conditioned, or negatively conditioned.

The distinctions between accident (al-‘arad) and accidental (al-‘aradi) hinge on how we consider the nature. Al-Dawwani’s analysis reveals that a single nature can be understood in three ways, each with different logical consequences for predication and identity. This lesson unpacks those modes and applies them to substance and accident.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three modes
Memorize the key terms: la bi-shart shay' (without condition), bi-shart shay' (with condition), bi-shart la shay' (with condition of not). Each mode represents a different way of considering a nature in relation to a subject or actualizer.
These modes are used widely in Islamic logic and theology.
2
Apply to accident (al-‘arad)
Take the nature of accident. According to al-Dawwani, when taken la bi-shart shay', it is ‘aradi (accidental)—predicable. When taken bi-shart shay' (with subject), it is identical to that subject. When taken bi-shart la shay' (negatively conditioned), it is the accident opposed to substance.
The example 'the cloth is four cubits' illustrates: length (accident) conditioned with cloth becomes identical to cloth's existence.
3
Generalize to any ambiguous quiddity
Some quiddities are fixed; others are ambiguous and need an actualizer. When taken with condition of actualization, they are identical to that actualizer. When taken with condition of non-actualization, they are distinct and not predicated. When taken unconditionally, they can be predicated because both possibilities exist in potential.
This applies to attributes like ‘ilm (knowledge) in created vs. divine contexts.
4
Reflect on the example
The phrase 'the cloth is four cubits' works because 'four cubits' (a measurement) is an accident. When considered with condition of the cloth, its existence is the cloth's existence; when considered without condition, it is simply an accidental quality; when considered with condition of no cloth, it is an accident existing independently (hypothetically).
The example may be clarified as 'the cloth measures four cubits'.

Three Considerations of Quiddity
Author explains the three ways of considering quiddity and their role in predication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three considerations of quiddity (bi-shart shay', bi-shart la, la bi-shart shay') and explain how they determine predication.

Core Idea
The three considerations of quiddity determine whether it is identical, distinct, or fluctuating in relation to another.

In Islamic logic, quiddities are considered in three ways relative to one another. These considerations are the foundation for understanding how a predicate relates to its subject. This lesson unpacks the three modes and shows how they yield the definition of predication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three Arabic expressions
Find in the passage the phrases 'bi-shart shay'' (with condition of something), 'bi-shart la' (with condition of negation), and 'la bi-shart shay'' (unconditionally).
These are the three modes of considering quiddity.
2
Understand each mode's logical implication
When quiddity A is taken bi-shart shay' in relation to B, it is identical to B. When bi-shart la, it is other than B. When la bi-shart shay', it fluctuates between identity and difference.
This is stated explicitly in the passage.
3
Apply to a concrete example
Consider 'white' and 'garment'. White taken bi-shart shay' is the white garment; taken bi-shart la is the accident whiteness; taken la bi-shart shay' is the accidental (aradi).
This is from al-Dawani's example.
4
Derive the definition of predication
The passage states that predication is the union of two distinct things in one aspect. This means the predicate differs from the subject in one mode (bi-shart la) but is identical in another mode (bi-shart shay').
Thus the three considerations directly explain predication.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Bahr Ulum on the Sullam al-Ulum, dealing with a foundational topic in Islamic logic: the distinction between 'arad (accident) and 'aradi (accidental) through the lens of the three considerations of quiddity (bi-shart shay', bi-shart la, la bi-shart shay'). The author begins by stating that separable accidents have existence in reality, while inseparable ones only have mental existence. He then explains that when a quiddity is taken unconditionally (la bi-shart shay'), it can be united with another and predicated upon it. Union is only possible between two things when one is ambiguous and the other determinate, or both are ambiguous. He then clarifies the three modes: if quiddity A is taken 'with the condition of something' (bi-shart shay') in relation to quiddity B, it is identical to B; if taken 'with the condition of negation' (bi-shart la), it is other than B; and if taken absolutely (la bi-shart shay'), it fluctuates between identity and difference. This leads to the definition of predication: the union of two distinct things in one aspect, meaning the predicate differs from the subject in one aspect (e.g., existence) but is identical in another. The author then presents and critiques the view of al-Muhaqqiq al-Dawani, who uses the example of 'white' (abyad) to illustrate: if taken unconditionally, it is an accidental (aradi); if taken with the condition of a garment, it is a white garment; if taken with the condition of nothing, it is the accident (arad) opposite to substance. Dawani analogizes this to genus/matter and differentia/form. The author argues that while Dawani correctly shows accident and accidental are essentially united, he wrongly extends this to the accident and its locus (like whiteness and the garment). The correct view, according to the author, is that accidental things (like 'white') have realities distinct from their subjects (like 'garment'), but there is a relationship such that the subject's existence is attributed to the accidental. The accidental is essentially united with the accident and accidentally united with the subject. The author then explains why the derivative (mushtaq) does not indicate relation or the described; its meaning is solely the attributive quantity (like 'white' meaning whiteness alone) when taken unconditionally. This entire discussion guards against confusing the accident (arad) as a metaphysical category with the accidental (aradi) as a logical predicate.

Key Terms
بشرط شيء (bi-shart shay'): The quiddity is considered with the condition of being identical to another, used to show essential predication. [Note: No significant shift across the tradition.]
بشرط لا (bi-shart la): The quiddity is considered with the condition of being other than another, used to show difference in existence. [Note: No significant shift.]
لا بشرط شيء (la bi-shart shay'): The quiddity is considered unconditionally, allowing it to be either identical or distinct and often used for the essence of the accident (arad) itself. [Note: No significant shift.]
العرض (al-'arad): An accident in the sense of a real attribute that subsists in a substance, like whiteness; it is a category in metaphysics. [Note: In logic, 'arad often means 'accidental universal' opposed to essential; here it is the real accident.]
العرضي (al-'aradi): The accidental universal, an attribute that can be predicated of a subject accidentally, like 'white' predicated of a garment. [Note: Stable; 'aradi is the logical predicate, not the real accident.]
المشتق (al-mushtaq): A derivative term like 'white' that indicates the attribute itself (al-qadr al-na'it) but not the relation or the subject. [Note: This is a specific claim from al-Dawani; in other works, mushtaq may be said to imply the subject.]

Distinguishing Accident from Accidental

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'arad (accident as real attribute) and 'aradi (accidental predicate) using the three considerations and critique al-Dawani's position.

Core Idea
Accident and accidental are essentially united but conceptually distinct; the accidental is predicated, the accident is not.

One of the most subtle distinctions in Islamic logic is between the accident ('arad) as a real attribute and the accidental ('aradi) as a logical predicate. This lesson uses al-Dawani's example of 'white' to clarify the distinction and shows why the derivative (mushtaq) indicates only the attribute itself.

The Standard Refutation: Disjunctive Proof of Impossibility
Author presents a disjunctive argument proving impossibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive argument that proves the impossibility of two genera in one rank and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
If two genera were possible, either they produce two essences or lead to circularity – both impossible.

After presenting objections, the author returns to the main argument that two genera cannot coexist. This proof uses a disjunctive reasoning form: either each genus is constituted by a differentia (producing two essences) or each is constituted by the other (producing circularity). Both outcomes are impossible, so the premise is false. Let's analyze this logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disjuncts
The argument states: if two genera are possible in one rank, then either each is constituted by a differentia (disjunct 1) or each is constituted by the other (disjunct 2). Locate these two possibilities in the Arabic.
The phrase 'إما أن يتقوم كل منهما بفصل ... وإما يتقوم كل منهما بالآخر' gives the disjunction.
2
Analyze the first disjunct
If each genus is constituted by its own differentia, then from combining the two genera with their differentiae, two distinct essences result. This contradicts the assumption that there is only one essence in the rank.
This shows the first disjunct leads to an impossibility (two essences instead of one).
3
Analyze the second disjunct
If each genus is constituted by the other, then each must exist before itself (because to constitute the other, it must be actualized first, but the other also needs to be actualized first). This is circularity (dawr), which is logically impossible.
The author states 'دور محال' (impossible circularity).
4
Determine the overall logical form
The argument is a conditional: P → (Q ∨ R). Then it shows both Q and R are false (impossible), so P must be false. This is a valid modus tollens or proof by contradiction.
The conclusion is that two genera cannot be in one rank for one essence.
5
Reflect on the author's call for reflection
Despite presenting this as 'clear', the author says 'فتأمل فيه فإن فيه كلاما ظاهرا' (so reflect on it, for there is apparent speech). This suggests that the issue is not fully settled and requires deeper thought.
Consider whether the disjunctive reasoning covers all possibilities or if there is a third option not considered.

The Two Fundamental Types of Disjunctive Conditionals
Defines the two base types of disjunctive conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between مانعة الجمع and مانعة الخلو in any given disjunctive proposition and determine which type is being used.

Core Idea
مانعة الجمع excludes joint truth; مانعة الخلو excludes joint falsehood.

Disjunctive conditionals (الشرطية المنفصلة) are statements of the form 'either A or B'. But not all 'or's are the same. Islamic logicians distinguish two fundamental types: مانعة الجمع, where A and B cannot both be true, and مانعة الخلو, where they cannot both be false. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing the validity of arguments that use disjunctions, such as those in Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
Look for a statement of the form 'either ... or ...' (أو). Note the two parts (disjuncts).
In Arabic, 'أو' is common but can also be implied.
2
Check if both can be true
Ask: Can both disjuncts be true at the same time? If not, it is مانعة الجمع (exclusive or).
Example: 'Either Zayd is in the house or he is in the market' – he cannot be in both places simultaneously.
3
Check if both can be false
Ask: Can both disjuncts be false at the same time? If not, it is مانعة الخلو (exhaustive or).
Example: 'Either the number is even or odd' – it cannot be neither.
4
Determine the type
Based on the two checks, classify the proposition. It could be both مانعة الجمع and مانعة الخلو (i.e., true exclusive or), or only one.
If both conditions hold, it is the 'real disjunction' (الحقيقية).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify conditional propositions as universal, particular, or particularized, and correctly interpret negative conditionals as negation of necessity rather than necessity of negation.

Core Idea
Conditionals require a supposition; quantity is defined by how many suppositions the judgment covers.

This passage from the logic text clarifies the nature of conditional propositions. It distinguishes between negative conditionals (negation of necessity vs. necessity of negation) and explains the essential role of supposition (taqdir) in conditional judgments. Understanding this foundation is crucial for later work on conditional syllogisms in Islamic logic.

Note that every conditional judgment must be based on a supposition (taqdir). Without a supposition, the conditional judgment is unintelligible.
3
Determine quantity from supposition scope
If the judgment applies to all suppositions, it is universal (kulliyya); if to some, particular (juz'iyya); if to a specific supposition, it is 'makhsusa' (particularized) including personal and particular.

Recognize intrinsic necessity
When the antecedent necessarily implies the consequent by essence or cause (without suppositional intervention), the judgment must be universal on all suppositions because the accompaniment is independent of any particular supposition.

Definition of Contradiction (Tanaqud)
Author defines contradiction and explains it as a recurrent relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradictories (both concepts and assents) and explain why contradiction is a recurrent relation.

Core Idea
Contradictories are two things where one is the exact negation of the other; they are mutually dependent for understanding.

This lesson introduces the precise definition of contradiction (tanaqud) in Islamic logic. The author defines it broadly to include both concepts (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat). He then explains that contradiction is a 'recurrent relation' — each contradictory is understood only through the other. This foundational concept is essential for analyzing arguments and avoiding logical fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of contradiction
Note that the definition explicitly includes both concepts and assents. The Arabic says 'سواء كانا تصورين أو تصديقين'. This means the rule applies not only to propositions but also to ideas like 'human' and 'not human'.
Many students mistakenly think contradiction only applies to statements.
2
Understand the defining condition
The condition for two things to be contradictories is that one is the negation (رفع) of the other. The text says 'أحدهما رفع الآخر فهما نقيضان'. So if you have A, its contradictory is 'not A'.
Negation here means complete removal, not just opposition.
3
Recognize the implication as a recurrent relation
The author states that contradiction is a recurrent relation. This means each contradictory is conceived only with its counterpart. You cannot understand 'not A' without understanding A, and vice versa.
Recurrent relations are those where each term implies the other.
4
Apply the definition to examples
Test pairs: 'The world is eternal' and 'The world is not eternal' are contradictories because one is the negation of the other. 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is sitting' are not contradictories because 'not standing' is different from 'sitting'.

Dawaran (Co-variation) and Its Limitations in Proving Causality
Definition and critique of dawaran method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a given co-variation is a valid indicator of 'illiyya by identifying whether it meets the condition of ta'thīr.

Core Idea
Mere co-variation does not prove causation; it must be accompanied by ta'thīr (influence).

In Usul al-Fiqh and logic, one of the main methods to prove that a certain attribute is the cause (‘illa) of a ruling is dawaran (co-variation)—the constant conjunction of the ruling with that attribute. However, logicians themselves acknowledge that dawaran alone can be misleading. The author of this passage warns that mere correlation can occur between two effects of the same cause or between correlatives. Hence, co-variation must be supplemented by ta'thīr, the genuine causal influence of the attribute on the ruling. This lesson unpacks the definition of dawaran and the conditions for its valid use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find dawaran (co-variation) in the Arabic. Note its synonyms: ṭard wa ‘aks. Understand that it is a method for proving causality.
2
Extract the definition of dawaran
The author defines it as the conjunction (iqtirān) of the judgment with the cause in existence and non-existence. In other words, when the ‘illa is present, the ruling is present; when it is absent, the ruling is absent.
3
Recognize the logicians' claim
Logicians assert that dawaran is a sign (āya) that the thing around which the judgment revolves (al-madār) is the cause for that which revolves (al-dā'ir). This is the core claim under scrutiny.
4
Analyze the author's objection
The author provides two counterexamples where co-variation exists without causation: (1) two effects of a single cause (both rise and fall together but are not causes of each other), and (2) correlatives (al-mutaḍāyifayn) like fatherhood and sonship, which necessarily coexist but are not causally related.
This shows that dawaran alone is insufficient to establish causality.
5
Understand the requirement of ta'thīr
The author concludes that more co-variation is not enough; it must be combined with ta'thīr (influence)—the candidate cause must genuinely affect the judgment. This is the key condition for a valid causal proof.
