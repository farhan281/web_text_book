Demonstration (Burhan) and Certainty (Yaqin)
Introduces the five arts and defines demonstration and certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define burhan (demonstration) and yaqin (certainty), and explain the types of premises that can yield certainty.

Core Idea
Burhan is the syllogism whose premises are certain, whether rational or transmitted, yielding firm and stable belief.

The five arts classify all rational discourse. The highest is demonstration (burhan), which produces certainty (yaqin). In this passage, the author defines these key terms, emphasizing that transmitted premises can also yield certainty, as long as they are not 'pure transmission' without indicants.

Guided Path
1
Locate the five arts framework
The passage begins by mentioning the five arts. Understand that this classification orders arguments by their epistemic force. Burhan is the first and highest.
The other four arts (dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, sophistry) are not defined here but are referenced in the broader subject.
2
Extract the definition of burhan
Burhan is defined as 'the certain syllogism' (al-qiyas al-yaqini). Identify its genus: qiyas (syllogism). Its differentia: its premises are certain.
Yaqini here means the premises themselves are known with certainty, not just the conclusion.
3
Understand the premise types
The author explicitly states that premises can be either rational ('aqliyya) or transmitted (naqliyya). Do not assume only rational premises count; revelation can also provide certainty.
This was a key point for Islamic logicians: wahy (revelation) can produce yaqin.
4
Note the nuance on transmission
The phrase 'pure transmission is not like that' clarifies that not every transmitted statement is certain; only those with indicants (qara'in) that yield firm belief qualify.
This guards against blind taqlid.
5
Define yaqin (certainty)
Yaqin is defined as firm belief (i'tiqad jazim) that corresponds to reality (mutabiq) and is stable (thabit). Break down each component: firm excludes doubt; correspondence excludes false beliefs; stability excludes fleeting opinions.
This definition is standard across Islamic epistemology.

Commentary
This passage introduces the five arts (sana'at khams) and defines the first, burhan (demonstration). Burhan is a qiyas (syllogism) that yields certainty (yaqin). Its premises can be either rational ('aqliyya) or transmitted (naqliyya). The author notes that transmitted proofs (e.g., revealed texts) can also yield certainty, but pure transmission (naql sirf, i.e., without indication of certainty) does not. Then yaqin is defined: it is a firm belief (i'tiqad jazim) that is in accordance with reality (mutabiq) and stable (thabit). This definition excludes mere opinion or doubt. The five arts are a core concept in Aristotelian logic: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, and sophistry. This passage focuses on demonstration, which is the highest form of reasoning.

Dialectic: The Art of Widely Accepted Opinions
Definition of dialectic by its premise types and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the premise types used in dialectic (jadal) and explain its purpose in disputation.

Core Idea
Dialectic compels an opponent using widely accepted or mutually conceded premises.

In this passage, the author defines dialectic as one of the five arts, distinguished by its material (premises) and goal. Unlike demonstration, which aims at certainty, dialectic relies on premises that are widely accepted or conceded in a given context. Understanding this helps you recognize when an argument is dialectical rather than demonstrative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composition of dialectic
Dialectic is composed of either widely accepted opinions (mashhurat) or mutually conceded premises (musallamat).
Mashhurat are accepted due to common benefit, emotion, or temperament.
2
Understand mashhurat
Mashhurat are propositions that many people accept, but they may be true or false. They vary by community and can be confused with first principles.
The author notes that temperaments and habits influence beliefs.
3
Understand musallamat
Musallamat are premises that the opponent concedes for the sake of argument, such as a jurist conceding that a command indicates obligation.
These are not necessarily true, but agreed upon between disputants.
4
Identify the purpose of dialectic
The purpose is either to compel the opponent (ilzam al-khasm) or to defend one's own opinion (hifz al-ra'y).
Dialectic aims at persuasion, not certain truth.
5
Distinguish from demonstration
Demonstration uses certain premises (awwaliyyat, mujarrabat), while dialectic uses probable or conceded premises.
This distinction is crucial in evaluating arguments.

الثَّالِثُ: الْخَطَابَةُ، وَهُوَ الْمُؤَلَّفُ مِنَ الْمَقْبُولَاتِ الْمَأْخُودَةِ مِمَّنْ يُحْسَنُ الظَّنُّ فِيهِ، كَالْأَوْلِيَاءِ وَالْحُكَمَاءِ. وَمَنْ عَدَّ الْمَأْخُوذَاتِ مِنَ الْأَنْبِيَاءِ عَلَيْهِمُ السَّلَامُ مِنْهَا فَقَدْ غَلِطَ. أَوْ مِنَ الْمَظْنُونَاتِ الَّتِي يُحْكَمُ فِيهَا بِسَبَبِ الرُّجْحَانِ، وَيَدْخُلُ فِيهَا التَّجَرُّبِيَّاتُ وَالْحَدَسِيَّاتُ وَالْمُتَوَاتِرَاتُ الْغَيْرُ الْوَاصِلَةِ حَدَّ الْجَزْمِ. وَالْغَرَضُ مِنْهَا تَحْصِيلُ أَحْكَامٍ نَافِعَةٍ أَوْ ضَارَّةٍ، فِي الْمَعَاشِ أَوِ الْمَعَادِ، كَمَا يَفْعَلُهُ الْخُطَبَاءُ وَالْوُعَّاظُ.
الرَّابِعُ: الشِّعْرُ، وَهُوَ الْمُؤَلَّفُ مِنَ الْمُخَيَّلَاتِ، وَهِيَ قَضَايَا يُخَيَّلُ بِهَا، فَيَتَذَكَّرُ النَّفْسُ قَبْضًا وَبَسْطًا، فَإِنَّهَا أَطْوَعُ لِلتَّحْيِيلِ مِنَ التَّصْدِيقِ، سِيَّمَا إِذَا كَانَ عَلَى وَزْنٍ لَطِيفٍ، أَوْ أُنْشِدَ بِصَوْتٍ طَيِّبٍ، وَالْغَرَضُ مِنْهُ انْفِعَالُ النَّفْسِ بِالتَّرْغِيبِ أَوِ التَّرْهِيبِ، وَهُوَ كَالنَّتِيجَةِ لَهُ.
الْخَامِسُ: السَّفْسَطَةُ، وَهُوَ الْمُؤَلَّفُ مِنَ الْوَهْمِيَّاتِ، نَحْوُ "كُلُّ مَوْجُودٍ مُشَارٌ إِلَيْهِ"، وَالنَّفْسُ مُسَخَّرَةٌ لِلْوَهْمِ، فَالْوَهْمِيَّاتُ رُبَّمَا لَمْ تَتَمَيَّزْ عِنْدَهَا مِنَ الْأَوَّلِيَّاتِ، وَلَوْلَا دَفْعُ الْعَقْلِ حُكْمَ الْوَهْمِ بَقِيَ الِالْتِبَاسُ دَائِمًا، أَوْ مِنَ الْمُشَبَّهَاتِ بِالصَّادِقَةِ صُورَةً أَوْ مَعْنَى، كَأَخْذِ الْخَارِجِيَّاتِ مَكَانَ الذِّهْنِيَّاتِ، وَبِالْعَكْسِ، وَالْغَرَضُ مِنْهُ تَغْلِيطُ الْخَصْمِ، وَالْمُغَالَطَةُ أَعَمُّ، فَإِنَّهَا الْفَاسِدَةُ صُورَةً أَوْ مَادَّةً. وَالْمُخَالِطُ إِنْ قَابَلَ الْحَكِيمَ فَسُوفِسْطَائِيٌّ، وَإِنْ قَابَلَ الْجَدَلِيَّ فَمُشَاغِبِيٌّ.
هَذَا، وَالْمُؤَلَّفُ مِنَ الرَّاجِحِ وَالْمَرْجُوحِ مَرْجُوحٌ، فَتَدَبَّرْ.
خَاتِمَةٌ: أَجْزَاءُ الْعُلُومِ هِيَ الْمَسَائِلُ، وَالْمَبَادِئُ مِنَ الْوَسَائِلِ.
The second: dialectic (jadal), and it is composed of widely accepted premises (mashhurat) which are judged due to agreement of opinions, either for a common benefit, or tenderness, or zeal, or characterological or temperamental emotions, true or false. And from here it is said: temperaments and habits have an influence on beliefs, and each people have specific widely accepted premises, and perhaps they are confused with first principles and become distinguished upon abstraction. Or (it is composed) of conceded premises (musallamat) between the two disputants, such as the jurist's concession that a command indicates obligation, and the purpose is either to compel the opponent or to defend one's opinion. The third: rhetoric (khitaba), and it is composed of accepted premises (maqbulat) taken from one who is well thought of, like saints and sages. Whoever counts the premises taken from prophets (peace be upon them) among them has erred. Or (it is composed) of likely opinion (maznunat) wherein judgment is made due to preponderance, and it includes experiential, intuitive, and mutawatir reports that do not reach the level of certainty. And the purpose of it is to obtain beneficial or harmful judgments, in worldly or religious life, as is done by orators and preachers. The fourth: poetry (shi'r), and it is composed of imaginative premises (mukhayyalat), which are propositions that are imagined, so the soul recalls contraction and expansion, for they are more amenable to imagination than to assent, especially if they are in a pleasant meter or recited with a good voice. And its purpose is the soul's emotion of desire or aversion, and it is like the conclusion for it. The fifth: sophistry (safsata), and it is composed of estimative premises (wahmiyyat), like 'every existent is pointed to', and the soul is subjugated to estimation (wahm), so estimative premises perhaps are not distinguished by it from first principles, and were it not for the intellect repelling the judgment of estimation, the confusion would remain permanent. Or (it is composed) of premises that resemble the true in form or meaning, like taking external things in place of mental ones, and vice versa. And its purpose is to mislead the opponent, and misleading is more general, for it is corrupt in form or matter. And the one who practices sophistry, if he confronts a philosopher, he is a sophist, and if he confronts a dialectician, he is a babbler. This, and what is composed of the preponderant and the preponderate is preponderate, so reflect. Conclusion: The parts of the sciences are the problems (masa'il), and the principles (mabadi') are from the means (wasai'il).

Commentary
This passage defines the remaining four of the five arts (the first, demonstration, having been covered separately). Each art is defined by the type of premises it uses. Dialectic (jadal) uses widely accepted opinions (mashhurat)—which may vary by culture and temperament—or mutually conceded premises (musallamat) between opponents; its purpose is to compel the opponent or defend one's own view. Rhetoric (khitaba) uses accepted opinions (maqbulat) from trustworthy figures or probabilistic propositions (maznunat) such as empirical generalizations, intuitions, and non-definitive recurrent reports (mutawatirat below certainty); its purpose is to guide action in this world and the next. Poetry (shi'r) uses imaginative premises (mukhayyalat) that evoke emotional responses and are more effective when metric or melodious; its purpose is to stir desire or aversion. Sophistry (safsata) uses estimative premises (wahmiyyat) that confuse the imagination with reality, or fallacious arguments that mimic truth; its purpose is to deceive the opponent. The passage concludes with a reminder that a composition of the preponderant and preponderated is preponderated, i.e., a weak premise weakens the whole argument. Finally, it notes that parts of sciences are the problems (masa'il) and principles are means to them.

Rhetoric: The Art of Persuasion for Action
Definition of rhetoric by its premise types and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the premise types used in rhetoric (khitaba) and distinguish it from demonstration and dialectic.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades using accepted opinions from trusted figures or probable premises.

In this passage, the author defines rhetoric as an art composed of accepted opinions (maqbulat) from trustworthy sources or probabilistic premises (maznunat). Unlike dialectic which aims at compelling an opponent, rhetoric aims at guiding practical action in worldly and religious life. The author also clarifies that prophetic teachings are not part of rhetoric because they are certain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise types of rhetoric
Rhetoric uses two types: accepted opinions (maqbulat) from trusted figures, and probabilistic premises (maznunat) based on preponderance.
Maqbulat come from saints, sages, but not prophets—the author warns against including prophets.
2
Understand maznunat
Maznunat include experiential, intuitive, and mutawatir reports that do not reach certainty. They are judged on preponderance.
These are below the level of certain knowledge.
3
Clarify the exception about prophets
Premises from prophets are not part of rhetoric because they are certain, not probable.
The passage explicitly says counting prophetic teachings as rhetoric is an error.
4
Identify the purpose of rhetoric
The purpose is to obtain beneficial or harmful judgments in worldly or religious matters.
Rhetoric aims at action, not just persuasion.
5
Compare with other arts
Unlike demonstration (certain), dialectic (compelling opponent), poetry (imagination), and sophistry (deception), rhetoric focuses on practical guidance.
Rhetoric is used by orators and preachers.

Poetry: The Art of Imagination and Emotion
Definition of poetry by its premise type and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative premises (mukhayyalat) and explain how poetry affects the soul.

Core Idea
Poetry moves the soul through imaginative premises, especially when metered and melodious.

In this passage, the author defines poetry (shi'r) as an art composed of imaginative premises (mukhayyalat) that evoke emotional responses of desire or aversion. Unlike rhetoric, which aims at action, poetry aims at emotional impact. The author notes that poetry is especially effective when it has a pleasant meter or is recited nicely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise type of poetry
Poetry is composed of imaginative premises (mukhayyalat).
These are propositions that stimulate the imagination.
2
Understand how mukhayyalat work
They cause the soul to recall contraction (qabd) and expansion (bast), i.e., emotional responses.
Imagination is more amenable to (طوغ) than assent (tasdiq).
3
Note the enhancing factors
Pleasant meter (wazn latif) and good voice (sawt tayyib) increase the effect.
This distinguishes poetry from mere imaginative prose.
4
Identify the purpose of poetry
The purpose is to stir the soul's emotion of desire (targhib) or aversion (tarhib).
This is like the conclusion of the artistic process.
5
Compare with other arts
Unlike dialectic (compelling), rhetoric (persuading action), and sophistry (deceiving), poetry aims at emotional purification or stimulation.
The author does not evaluate its truth value.

Sophistry: The Art of Deception and Fallacy
Definition of sophistry by its premise types and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify estimative premises (wahmiyyat) and fallacious arguments in sophistry, and distinguish sophistry from other arts.

Core Idea
Sophistry deceives using premises that appear true but are false or misleading.

In this passage, the author defines sophistry (safsata) as an art composed of estimative premises (wahmiyyat) or arguments that resemble truth in form or meaning. The purpose is to mislead the opponent. The author also distinguishes between sophistry and mere fallacy (mughalatat), noting that sophistry is one type of fallacy practiced in confrontation with philosophers or dialecticians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise types of sophistry
Sophistry uses estimative premises (wahmiyyat) or premises that resemble truth (mushabbahat bi al-sadiqa).
Wahmiyyat are accepted by the estimative faculty (wahm) and may conflict with reason.
2
Understand wahmiyyat
Wahmiyyat are propositions like 'every existent can be pointed to'—naturally accepted but false.
The soul is subjugated to wahm, so these premises can be mistaken for self-evident truths.
3
Understand mushabbahat
Premises that resemble truth in form or meaning, e.g., substituting external things for mental ones or vice versa.
This includes formal fallacies and material fallacies.
4
Identify the purpose of sophistry
The purpose is to mislead the opponent (taghlit al-khasm).
This is more specific than general fallacy (mughalata).
5
Distinguish sophistry from fallacy
Sophistry is a subcategory: it is practiced by a specific type of person. Fallacy is broader (general corruption in form or matter).
A sophist vs. a dialectician vs. a philosopher.

Commentary

Poetry (shi'r) uses imaginative premises (mukhayyalat) that evoke emotional responses and are more effective when metric or melodious; its purpose is to stir desire or aversion. Sophistry (safsata) uses estimative premises (wahmiyyat) that confuse the imagination with reality, or fallacious arguments that mimic truth; its purpose is to deceive the opponent. The passage concludes with a reminder that a composition of the preponderant and preponderated is preponderated, i.e., a weak premise weakens the whole argument. Finally, it notes that parts of sciences are the problems (masa'il) and principles are means to them.

Premises either produce assent (tasdiq) or another effect (imagination, mukhayyila). The imaginative are not further subdivided; they are used in poetry and rhetoric.

Maqbulat, Maznunat, and Mukhayyilat: Authority, Conjecture, and Imagination
Defines maqbulat, maznunat, and mukhayyilat as premises from authority, conjecture, and imagination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify premises into maqbulat, maznunat, and mukhayyilat and identify their use in dialectic, rhetoric, and poetry.

Core Idea
Authority, probability, and imagery each serve a distinct logical art: dialectic, rhetoric, and poetry.

After discussing consensus and estimation, the author turns to premises derived from authority (maqbulat), conjecture (maznunat), and imagination (mukhayyilat). These categories are crucial for understanding arguments in fiqh (jurisprudence), rhetoric, and poetry. Each type has a characteristic epistemic status and typical application.

Guided Path
1
Define maqbulat
Maqbulat are propositions accepted from a trusted authority, such as Imam Abu Hanifa or Abu Yazid al-Bistami. They are used in dialectical and rhetorical arguments when a consensus is not available.
The text gives explicit examples from Islamic scholarship.
2
Define maznunat
Maznunat are conjectural premises based on probability. The example given is 'every agent is nominative', a grammatical rule that is generally true but admits exceptions. They are used in rhetoric.
Conjecture does not yield certainty, only likelihood.
3
Define mukhayyilat
Mukhayyilat are imaginative premises that use figurative language to please or repel, like 'Zayd is a moon'. They are the basis of poetic discourse and aim for emotional effect, not truth.
These are not meant to be taken literally.
4
Identify the art each belongs to
Maqbulat and maznunat are used in rhetoric and dialectic, while mukhayyilat is used in poetry. Recognizing the type helps evaluate the argument's purpose and reliability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of mushabbahat and recognize how they deceive by resemblance.

Core Idea
Sophistical premises are false but resemble true ones; they deceive by similarity to necessary, accepted, authoritative, or conjectural truths.

The final category in this passage is mushabbahat (sophistical premises). These are false propositions that bear a resemblance to valid premises, thus tricking the mind into accepting them. The author identifies four types based on what they resemble: necessary truths, accepted opinions, accepted-from-authorities, and conjectural propositions. Understanding these is key to detecting fallacies.

Identify the four types of mushabbahat
The passage lists four categories: those resembling (1) necessary truths, (2) accepted opinions (mashhurat), (3) accepted-from-authorities (maqbulat), and (4) conjectural propositions (maznunat).

Explains the method of tahrir as a tool to distinguish mushurat from awwaliyat.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tahrir strips the mind of all prior influences to reveal a proposition's true epistemic status.

Learning Objective

The Rule of the Weakest Premise
Author lists the five arts and states the compositional rule for their premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that the conclusion follows the weaker premise to classify the epistemic status of a syllogism.

Core Idea
The conclusion follows the weakest premise; the composite is only as strong as its weakest part.

In Islamic logic, the quality of an argument depends not only on its form but on the strength of its premises. This lesson introduces a key principle: the composite of premises is judged by its weakest component. This rule governs all five arts of logic, from demonstration to sophistry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule in the text
Find the Arabic phrase 'والمؤلف من الراجح والمرجوح مرجوح'. Note that 'راجح' means more likely (certain) and 'مرجوح' means less likely (weak). The composite takes the status of the weaker.
This is a general principle, not limited to specific premise types.
2
Understand the implication
If one premise is certain and the other is conjectural, the conclusion cannot be certain; it is conjectural. The conclusion's epistemic status is always that of the lower premise.
This is why a syllogism with a single weak premise cannot produce certain knowledge.
3
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider a kalam argument: 'The world is temporally originated (certain). Everything temporally originated has a cause (certain). Therefore the world has a cause (certain).' Both premises are certain, so conclusion is certain. Now change the second premise to 'Everything temporally originated probably has a cause (conjectural).' The conclusion becomes conjectural, not certain.
This demonstrates how the rule works in theological reasoning.

(۱) وذلك لأن النتيجة تتبع أخس المقدمتين، كما علم وما أبدع هذه العبارة وأبلغها؛ حيث قلت ودلت وانظر مثلا إن شئت عبارة اليزدي في شرح التذيب (۱۱۱): «يعتبر في البرهان أن يكون مقدماته بأسرها يقينية، بخلاف غيره من الأقسام، مثلا يكفي في كون القياس مغالطة أن يكون إحدى مقدمتيه وهمية، وإن كانت الأخرى يقينية. نعم، يجب أن لا يكون فيها ما هو أدون منها كالشعريات، وإلا تلحق بالأدون، فإن المؤلف من مقدمة مشهورة وأخرى مخيلة لا يسمى جدليا، بل شعريا .
The five arts: (The composite of the more likely and the less likely is less likely, so ponder.) So the composite of what is necessary to accept and the well-known and the admitted is dialectic. And from each of them and from what is accepted or conjectured is rhetoric. And from each of them and the imaginative is poetry. And from what is necessary to accept and the dubious or the illusory, and likewise from one of them and from the well-known or admitted or accepted or conjectured is sophistry. And from one of them and the imaginative is poetry. This, and God knows best what is correct.
(1) That is because the conclusion follows the lower of the two premises, as is known. How excellent and eloquent is this expression! For it is concise and indicative. Look, for example, if you wish, at the expression of al-Yazdi in Sharh al-Tahzeeb (111): 'It is required in demonstration that its premises be entirely certain, unlike other divisions. For example, it suffices for a syllogism to be a fallacy that one of its two premises be illusory, even if the other is certain. Yes, it must not contain what is lower than them like poetical premises, otherwise it joins the lower. For indeed the composite of a well-known premise and another imaginative premise is not called dialectical but poetical.'

Commentary
This passage defines the five arts (الصناعات الخمس) of logic: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. The author begins with a general rule: 'The composite of the more likely and the less likely is less likely' — meaning the conclusion follows the weaker premise. He then applies this rule to define each art by the types of premises it employs. Demonstration (برهان) uses certain premises (واجب القبول). Dialectic (جدل) combines a certain premise with a well-known (مشهور) or admitted (مسلم) premise. Rhetoric (خطابة) uses either of those plus an accepted (مقبول) or conjectured (مظنون) premise. Poetry (شعر) adds imaginative (مخيل) premises. Sophistry (سفسطة) uses dubious (مشبهة) or illusory (وهمية) premises alongside others. The footnote expands on the rule, citing al-Yazdi: a demonstrative premise must be certain; if any premise is weaker, the syllogism falls to the lower art. For instance, a well-known premise mixed with an imaginative one becomes poetical, not dialectical. This classification is crucial for identifying the epistemic value of arguments in different contexts.

The Five Arts
intermediate
10 min
Classification of the Five Arts
Author lists the five arts and states the compositional rule for their premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any syllogism into one of the five arts (demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, sophistry) based on the types of its premises.

Core Idea
The five arts are defined by the weakest premise type they contain, from certain (demonstration) to illusory (sophistry).

Islamic logic divides all syllogisms into five arts according to the epistemic quality of their premises. By identifying the lowest type of premise in an argument, you can determine which art it belongs to. This lesson maps each art to its characteristic premise combinations.

Guided Path
1
List the premise types in order of strength
From highest to lowest: certain (wājib al-qabūl), well-known (mashhūr), admitted (musallam), accepted (maqbūl), conjectural (maẓnūn), imaginative (mukhayyal), dubious (mushabbaha), illusory (wahmiyya).
These are the standard types used in Islamic logic.
2
Identify demonstration (burhān)
All premises must be certain (wājib al-qabūl). No weaker premise is allowed.
Demonstration produces certain (yaqīn) knowledge.
3
Identify dialectic (jadal) and rhetoric (khiṭāba)
Dialectic mixes certain with well-known or admitted. Rhetoric mixes certain or well-known/admitted with accepted or conjectural.
The weaker premise in each case determines the art.
4
Identify poetry (shīr) and sophistry (safsata)
Poetry includes any imaginative premises. Sophistry includes dubious or illusory premises, even if mixed with certain ones.
These are the lowest arts, producing only emotional or deceptive effects.
5
Apply the classification to an example
Take a syllogism: 'The world is temporally originated (certain). Everything temporally originated has a cause (certain). Therefore the world has a cause.' Both premises are certain, so it is demonstration. If the second premise were 'Everything temporally originated probably has a cause (conjectural)', it would be rhetoric.
Practice with different combinations to internalize the hierarchy.
