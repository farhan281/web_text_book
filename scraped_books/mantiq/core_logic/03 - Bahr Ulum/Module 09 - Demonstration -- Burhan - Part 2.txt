The Standard View and Its Refutation
Author summarizes and attacks the standard view that certainty requires cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's standard argument for cause-based certainty and identify its two hidden assumptions that the author refutes.

Core Idea
Certainty does not always need a cause; universal knowledge can come from sense perception.

Ibn Sina's theory of demonstration argues that real certainty must come from knowledge of the cause. The author begins by laying out this argument and then points to its flaws. In this lesson, we will unpack the initial exchange and see why the author finds the premises weak.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two options
The standard argument presents two possibilities for certainty: explanation by a cause or no cause. Note that each leads to different consequences: with a cause, certainty is universal only if the cause is universal; without a cause, only self-evidence or induction remains.
The author summarizes this as 'either by cause or not by cause'.
2
Trace the conclusion
The argument concludes that only syllogistic reasoning via a cause yields universal certainty, dismissing induction as unstable. Recognize that this conclusion rests on two unstated assumptions.
3
Extract the hidden assumptions
The author points out two assumptions: (1) that knowledge of a caused thing only comes from its cause, and (2) that sense perception cannot yield universal knowledge. These are the targets of the refutation.
The text explicitly says 'it is based on the fact that knowledge of the thing with a cause is only from the cause, and we have refuted that, and that the senses do not give universal knowledge'.
4
Assess the refutation
The author claims to have refuted these assumptions (e.g., by showing a way sense perception gives universals). For the sake of this lesson, evaluate whether the author successfully challenges the necessity of cause for certainty.
The full refutation is beyond this passage; the author simply asserts it.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of Ibn Sina's (al-Shaykh) position on demonstration and certainty. The author begins by summarizing the standard argument: certain knowledge aims for universal certainty, but how can something uncertain produce universal certainty? The argument considers two possibilities: explanation by a cause or no cause. If by a cause, the cause must be universal, so the syllogism benefits, not induction. If no cause, it must be self-evident (refuted) or by induction (unstable). The author then points out that this argument is based on two premises he has refuted: that knowledge of caused things only comes from the cause, and that sense perception does not yield universal knowledge. He further attacks the claim that regressive syllogisms provide universal certainty, giving examples of impossibilities (infinite, atom, void, triangle) that are known with certainty but not demonstrated by cause. He critiques Ibn Sina's counterexample as a mere claim without proof. Finally, he quotes Ibn Sina's own words from an earlier chapter, where Ibn Sina seems to allow a certain demonstration without knowledge of the cause, calling it a demonstration of 'that' (inn) not 'why' (limma). The author finds this contradictory, concluding that he still does not understand the Shaykh. The commentary follows the author's argumentative order: summary of standard view, critique of its foundations, objection regarding impossibilities, analysis of Ibn Sina's own example, and final charge of contradiction. All examples are from logic and cosmology; the quiz will use an Islamic example.

Certainty About Impossibilities
Author objects that Ibn Sina's theory cannot account for certainty regarding impossibilities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author considers impossibilities a fatal counterexample to Ibn Sina's theory of demonstration.

Core Idea
Impossibilities like the infinite are known with certainty without demonstrative proof via cause.

The author presses the attack: even if we grant some certainty from cause, there are known impossibilities that are not derived from any cause. This lesson examines that objection.

Guided Path
1
List the impossibilities
The author explicitly names: the impossibility of an infinite regress, the indivisible atom (atomism), the void, and a triangle with angles equal to three right angles. These are known with certainty in philosophy and theology.
The text says: 'the impossibility of the infinite, the indivisible atom, the void, and the triangle whose angles are three right angles.'
2
Recognize their certainty
These impossibilities are known as necessarily false, often through a priori reasoning or contradiction. They do not require empirical cause-based demonstration.
3
Identify the flaw in Ibn Sina's theory
Ibn Sina's theory claims that all universal certainty comes from demonstration via cause. Yet we are certain of these impossibilities without any cause. This is a direct counterexample.
The author says: 'regressive syllogisms do not give universal certainty, and they are not true for impossible things' – meaning Ibn Sina's own method fails for these.
4
Assess the objection's strength
If the theory cannot account for a priori knowledge of impossibilities, it is incomplete. The objection is strong if one accepts that these are known with certainty.

The Contradiction in Ibn Sina's Own Words
Author quotes Ibn Sina to demonstrate an internal inconsistency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Sina's alternative demonstration (burhan inn) and articulate why the author considers it contradictory to his earlier position.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina himself allows certainty without cause in his 'demonstration of that', contradicting his earlier requirement.

The author quotes Ibn Sina's own earlier chapter to show that he permits a type of certain demonstration not based on cause. This lesson unpacks that quote and the charge of contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Locate the quotation
The author quotes Ibn Sina describing a demonstration where the major is predicated of the minor by essence, not by cause, and the middle term is also essentially predicated. The conclusion is that the major is evident for the middle, yielding a certain demonstration of 'that' (inn) without the middle being a cause.
The text: 'if the major is for the minor not by a cause, but by essence... the middle is likewise for the minor... then the major is evident for the middle, so a certain demonstration is formed.'
2
Distinguish burhan inn from burhan limma
Burhan limma ('why') requires the middle term to be the cause; burhan inn ('that') only requires the middle to be a sign or an essential attribute that necessitates the conclusion. The quote describes a burhan inn.
3
Identify the contradiction
Earlier, Ibn Sina insisted that all real certainty comes from knowledge of the cause (as in the standard argument). But here he admits certainty without cause. The author calls this a contradiction.
The author says: 'And is this anything but a contradiction?'
4
Formalize the syllogism
Using a concrete Islamic example: all contingent things require an originator (major premise: B->A), all created things are contingent (minor premise: C->B), therefore all created things require an originator (C->A). Here the middle term (contingency) is not the cause but an essential attribute that makes the conclusion evident.
This is a valid first-figure syllogism (Barbara). The middle term reveals the connection even though it is not the cause.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Only essential accidents yield certain demonstrative knowledge; extrinsic accidents rely on intermediaries and cannot be proven directly.

In the Aristotelian-Islamic theory of demonstration (burhan), not every predicate can be proven about a subject. The predicate must be an essential accident (arad dhati) – an attribute that belongs to the subject either by itself or through an equal intermediary. This lesson unpacks the author's classification of accidents and the conditions for demonstrative predicates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The author divides accidents into essential (arad dhati) and extrinsic (arad gharib). Essential accidents are those that belong to the subject without intermediary or through an equal intermediary. Extrinsic accidents involve non-equal intermediaries and are not truly accidents of the subject.
2
Analyze the intermediary condition for essential accidents
An essential accident can belong: (a) directly without intermediary, e.g., fire is hot by its essence; or (b) through an equal intermediary (musawi), i.e., an attribute coextensive with the subject. The equal intermediary preserves the truth of the predicate to the subject.
An example of equal intermediary: the attribute 'created' for the world, where creation and the world are coextensive.
3
Analyze the intermediary condition for extrinsic accidents
Extrinsic accidents occur through an intermediary that is more specific (akhas), more general absolutely (a'am mutlaq) or in some respect (a'am min wajh), or disparate (mubayin). These do not truly belong to the subject; they belong to the intermediary.
4
Apply the rule to demonstrative problems
Only essential accidents can be predicates in a demonstrative problem (mas'ala). Extrinsic accidents are excluded because certainty about them depends on certainty about the intermediary, so they cannot be proven directly by demonstration. The author emphasizes that the predicate must not be more general than the subject of the science.
This condition ensures that the demonstration remains within the proper subject matter.

Core Idea
Certainty is an epistemic state, not a modal property.

First Three Conditions of Demonstration
Author lists the first three conditions of demonstration with brief justifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first three conditions of demonstration and explain why each is necessary for certain knowledge in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Demonstration requires syllogistic form, epistemic priority, and causal priority for certainty.

In Islamic logic, a demonstration (burhan) is the highest form of proof, yielding certain knowledge (yaqin). The author of Bahr Ulum explains the first three conditions that any demonstration must satisfy. Understanding these conditions helps you distinguish genuine demonstrations from weaker arguments in disciplines like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the purpose of demonstration
Demonstration aims to produce certain knowledge. The conditions ensure that the argument is not merely valid but also yields certainty.
2
Identify the first condition: syllogistic form
The argument must be in the form of a categorical syllogism (qiyas). Only this form guarantees that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises.
Other forms like induction or analogy do not yield certainty.
3
Identify the second condition: epistemic priority
Our knowledge of the premises must be prior to and cause our knowledge of the conclusion. This ensures the conclusion is not assumed.
The premises must be better known than the conclusion.
4
Identify the third condition: causal priority
In a demonstration of why (burhan al-limmi), the premises must be causally prior in reality. They give the cause of the conclusion.
This contrasts with demonstration that (burhan al-inni), where premises may be causally posterior.
5
Distinguish between burhan al-limmi and burhan al-inni
Burhan al-limmi gives the reason (lima) why something is true, while burhan al-inni only proves that it is true (inna). Only burhan al-limmi requires causal priority.
This distinction is crucial in Islamic theology when proving attributes of God.

The Fourth Condition: Essential Predicates
Argument for the fourth condition of demonstration using reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a predicate in a demonstrative premise is essential, a proper accident, or a foreign accident, and explain why foreign accidents invalidate the demonstration.

Core Idea
Only essential or proper-accident predicates yield certain demonstration.

In demonstration, not just any true premise suffices. The predicate of each premise must be either constitutive of the subject's essence (dhatiyya) or a necessary property following from it (arad dhatiyya). This condition ensures the middle term genuinely causes the conclusion. The author uses reductio arguments to show that foreign accidents (arad gharib) lead to contradictions or redundancy.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two allowed predicate types
Essential (dhatiyya) predicates are constitutive of the subject, like genus or differentia. Proper accidents (arad dhatiyya) are necessary properties that follow from the essence but are not part of it, e.g., the capacity to laugh for a human.
Foreign accidents (arad gharib) are accidental in the sense that they do not derive from the essence; they are excluded.
2
Follow the first reductio scenario
Suppose the middle term is a foreign accident for the minor. If the major is equal (coextensive) to the middle, then the major is also foreign to the minor – but the major must be essential, contradiction.
This shows the middle cannot be foreign if the major is equal.
3
Examine the second scenario
If the major is more general than the middle, then the middle is consequent upon the minor via the major. That would make the major's predication more evident than the middle's, contradicting the middle's role as the cause.
The middle must be the explanatory middle; a foreign middle fails this.
4
Apply the criterion from al-Isharat
Ibn Sina's criterion: an essential predicate is one that must be taken in the definition of the subject, or the subject must be taken in its definition. This encapsulates the two types.
Use this test to check any demonstrative premise.

The Necessity Condition for Premises in Demonstration
Author argues for necessity of premises in demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why premises must be necessary if the conclusion is necessary and identify the logical rationale given by the author.

Core Idea
If the conclusion must be certain, the premises must be necessary.

In the fifth condition of demonstration, the author delves into a crucial epistemological requirement: the premises of a demonstrative argument must be necessary if the sought conclusion is to be necessary. He then addresses an objection: it was previously said that an existential minor with a necessary major produces a necessary conclusion. He critiques al-Tusi's view that premises must match the genus of necessity, arguing that if the middle term's affirmation is known by a cause, it yields continuous certainty. Three Conditions for Universal Premises in Demonstration
Author enumerates three conditions for universal premises in demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a universal premise in demonstration and test whether a given premise satisfies them.

Core Idea
Demonstrative universality requires more than just 'all'—it demands permanence and essential predicates.

In the logic of demonstration, not every universal statement qualifies as a premise for certain knowledge. The author distinguishes a special type of universality with three strict conditions. Understanding these conditions is crucial for constructing sound proofs in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the shift in 'al-kulliyya'
Note that the author says universality here is different from earlier logic. Earlier, it simply meant 'all individuals'. Here, it adds temporal and attributive restrictions.
This is a key distinction between general logic and demonstration-specific logic.
2
Memorize the three conditions
The passage gives three: (1) istighraq – the judgment covers all individuals of the subject; (2) dawam – the judgment is permanent in the time of the description; (3) 'arad awwali – the predicate is a primary attribute.
These conditions are jointly necessary; if any is missing, the premise is not universal for demonstration.
3
Understand 'arad awwali'
The author clarifies that 'primary attribute' includes not only attributes that are essential to the subject but also attributes equal to it in extension (co-extensive).
This ensures the predicate is not broader than the subject, which would make the premise accidental.
4
Apply conditions to a premise
Take a premise like 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' and check: does it cover all intoxicants? Is the ruling permanent? Is 'intoxicant' a primary attribute? This exercise builds understanding.
Use examples from fiqh or kalam for practice.
