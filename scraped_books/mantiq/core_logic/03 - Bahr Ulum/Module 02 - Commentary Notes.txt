The Nuanced Ruling on Rational Sciences: Al-Subki's Critique and Reconciliation
Author discusses the ruling on rational sciences and quotes Subki's critique and defense.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Taqi al-Din al-Subki's apparent condemnation of kalam and his actual qualified acceptance when grounded in Sharia.

Core Idea
Al-Subki's criticism of kalam is not a blanket prohibition but a call for adherence to Sharia boundaries.

In debates about the permissibility of rational sciences, scholars often appear to contradict themselves. This lesson explores how Taqi al-Din al-Subki, a staunch Ash'ari, criticized kalam harshly yet was later reconciled by his son, Taj al-Din al-Subki, who showed that the critique targeted only ungrounded kalam. Understanding this nuance prevents oversimplification and equips you to navigate apparent contradictions in scholarly positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two apparent positions
Locate al-Subki's strong condemnation of kalam and philosophy, and then the later response that includes kalam among the Sharia sciences. Recognize the tension.
2
Analyze the objection presented
The objection points out that jurists exclude the mutakallim from the term 'scholar.' Understand the force of this objection based on the traditional classification.
3
Examine the reconciliation
The response cites al-Subki's own work (Sharh al-Minhaj) to show that a mutakallim who follows Sharia laws is indeed a scholar. This resolves the contradiction: the earlier condemnation is about unbridled rationalism, not kalam per se.
The commentator notes that al-Subki's harsh words were a wish for a simpler intellectual environment, not a categorical ruling.
4
Assess the underlying principle
The principle is that the value of a rational science depends on its alignment with Sharia. This is a common tool in Islamic epistemology: the same discipline can be praiseworthy or blameworthy based on its method and purpose.
This principle applies to logic, philosophy, and other 'ulum 'aqliyya.

First Section: The ruling on engaging in the rational sciences
The discussion on this issue is not at all new; rather it is a subject of many scholars and thinkers in research and consideration, from the time of the Salaf until now. People are divided regarding it between prohibitor, permissor, and reconciler. The problem is not so simple that it can be solved with a single word like 'permissible' or 'not permissible'. Commentary
This passage opens the discussion on the ruling of engaging in rational sciences ('ulum 'aqliyya). The author begins by stating that this is an old debate, not a new one, and that scholars have taken various positions: prohibiting, permitting, or reconciling between reason and revelation. The issue is complex and cannot be reduced to a simple yes or no. 

To illustrate the complexity, the author cites Taqi al-Din al-Subki, a prominent Ash'ari scholar, who criticized 'ilm al-kalam and Greek philosophy as harmful to beliefs. Al-Subki divides the mutakallimun into three groups: Mu'tazila, Hashwiyya (anthropomorphists), and Ash'ariyya. He claims that even the Ash'ariyya's approach involves risk (mukhatara), and the safest path is that of the early Muslims (Salaf) who relied on sound nature (fitra). This seems to condemn rational theology entirely. 

However, the author then presents an objection: if the Sharia scholars are those of tafsir, fiqh, and hadith, why has the author (or Taj al-Subki in his later comment) replaced them with usul al-din (kalam) and tasawwuf? The jurists (fuqaha') have even said that a mutakallim is not included among the 'ulama'. 

The response is that the Imam (Taqi al-Din al-Subki) himself, in his Sharh al-Minhaj, denied this exclusion. He held that a mutakallim who speaks according to the laws of Sharia is indeed a scholar, and similarly a Sufi who follows Sharia. The author concludes that al-Taqi's earlier harsh words were merely a wishful thinking for a simpler intellectual environment, not a categorical prohibition. Thus the passage reconciles the apparent contradiction: al-Taqi's critique is contextualized, and his deeper position accommodates rational sciences when properly grounded in Sharia.

Complete Induction and Its Limits: Critiquing Claims of Uniqueness
Author refutes claim by highlighting impossibility of complete induction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify any claim that implicitly relies on complete induction and evaluate its epistemological validity using the istiqra' tamm critique.

Core Idea
Complete induction is impossible for claims about all unknown scholars, so such claims are epistemically invalid.

In this passage, the author responds to a parochial claim that no contemporary scholar produced a work like his. He points out that such a claim requires surveying every scholar of that age—a complete induction that is impossible. This logical critique is a powerful tool for evaluating any claim of uniqueness or universality in Islamic scholarship. Let's see how the author applies it and how you can use it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being examined
Find the universal negative claim in the passage: that no one from the author's time produced a similar work, and later the claim that Egyptian scholars are the most learned.
2
Recognize the implicit logical requirement
The claim implicitly requires knowing the states of all scholars—a complete induction (istiqra' tamm) of every scholar in that time and place.
3
Assess the feasibility of complete induction
The author argues that complete induction of all scholars is 'difficult, nay impossible' because we cannot survey every individual. Therefore, such claims cannot be proven with certainty.
4
Apply the critique to similar claims
The same logic applies to any claim that requires exhaustive knowledge of all members of a class. For example, the claim that Egyptian scholars are the most learned also depends on impossible complete induction.

Commentary
This passage is an example of Bahr Ulum's critical engagement with earlier scholars. The context is the epistemologically crucial division of concepts (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat) into those acquired without effort (badihi) and those requiring thought (nazari). Bahr Ulum's critique presumably corrects al-Dawani's interpretation. The passage demonstrates Bahr Ulum's mastery of the technical problem of the division of knowledge and his willingness to challenge authorities like al-Dawani.

Bahr Ulum's Critique of Dawwani on Badiha and Nazari
Bahr Ulum criticizes Dawwani's view that immediate principles suffice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze Bahr Ulum's critique of Dawwani by identifying the role of 'unwan' in determining immediate vs. theoretical knowledge and evaluate its implications for foundational premises in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Immediate knowledge is relative to how you frame the subject.

Dawwani argued that relying on immediate principles (badiha) is safer than long demonstrations. Bahr Ulum counters that what counts as immediate changes with the title (unwan) of the proposition. In this lesson, we unpack Bahr Ulum's argument and its significance for distinguishing badiha and nazari in the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify Dawwani's original claim
Locate the substance of Dawwani's position as transmitted by Bahr Ulum: that it is safer to rely on immediate (badiha) principles than to labor over proofs.
This is the starting point of the critique.
2
Extract Bahr Ulum's key point
Notice Bahr Ulum's statement that badiha and nazari differ according to the 'unwan' (title/aspect) of the proposition. This means the same proposition can be immediate under one description and theoretical under another.
This is the core logical insight.
3
Analyze the logical consequence
If a proposition's status changes with its title, then there is no absolutely first premise that is always immediate. This refutes the need to designate a specific method as obligatory for a disputant.
Bahr Ulum allows that if one argues that the premises themselves are necessary, that may suffice.
4
Distinguish dialectical from necessary
Bahr Ulum concludes that the whole debate is dialectical (jadali) but the required knowledge is necessary and does not require proof. Understand what this means for the disputant: the goal is conviction, not demonstration.
This highlights the practical aim of dialectical exchange.
5
Appreciate the endorsement
Hasan al-Attar praised Bahr Ulum's commentary, confirming its quality and acceptance.
This shows the later reception of the critique.

The Peripatetic Argument from Simplicity to a Chain of Intellects
Author presents and critiques the Peripatetic and Ishraqi views on emanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Peripatetic argument that multiplicity requires aspects in the First, and trace how the first intellect's multiple aspects generate the second intellect and spheres.

Core Idea
The first effect of the One must be a single intellect; its multiple aspects then enable the emanation of the cosmos.

The author presents the Peripatetic response to the problem of how multiplicity arises from the absolutely simple One. They argue that only one being can proceed directly from the One, but that being — the first intellect — has multiple aspects that serve as sources for the rest of creation. This lesson unpacks the logical steps of that argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
Why can't multiplicity proceed directly from the One? The Peripatetics argue that the One is absolutely simple, so no multiplicity can arise directly. Privations are non-existent and do not multiply the essence; relations depend on the existence of the relatum.
This is the key premise: a simple cause can have only one immediate effect.
2
Extract the core premise
Since the One is simple, only one thing can proceed immediately: the first intellect (al-'aql al-awwal). The body is composite, accidents and souls require a subject or body, so they cannot be direct effects.
Thus the first effect is an intellectual substance.
3
Find the aspects of the first intellect
The first intellect has multiple aspects: quiddity (mahiyya), existence (wujud) from Him, necessity (wujub) from Him, and intellection of itself (ta'aqqul li-dhatihi).
These aspects are real, not merely mental, according to the Peripatetics.
4
Trace the emanation from each aspect
From the aspect of existence, the second intellect emanates. From the remaining aspects: from necessity, the soul of the sphere; from self-intellection, the form; from quiddity, the hyle. This continues: from the second intellect, the third intellect and sphere of fixed stars, and so on down to the tenth intellect and sphere of the moon.
The pattern is 'ashraf fa ashraf' (more noble to more noble).
5
Understand the subsidiary point on acquired knowledge
The text adds that if divine knowledge is acquired (husuli) according to the view of their master (Aristotle), then multiplicity could also proceed directly through cognitive forms. But this is presented as an aside.
This shows an internal tension in the Peripatetic school.

The Ishraqi Critique: Mental Considerations Do Not Sufice for Real Multiplicity
Author presents and critiques the Peripatetic and Ishraqi views on emanation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Ishraqi objection that the aspects of the intellects are merely mental considerations (i'tibariyya) and cannot account for the real diversity of the fixed stars.

Core Idea
The Ishraqis demand that the principles of multiplicity be real, not just mental.

The Ishraqi school (Illuminationists) challenges the Peripatetic emanation scheme. They argue that the multiple aspects attributed to the intellects are merely mental considerations (i'tibariyya), not real distinctions. The evidence is the diversity of the fixed stars themselves, which differ in essence and position. This lesson dissects that critique and its logical force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of the critique
The Ishraqis attack the Peripatetic claim that the first intellect has multiple real aspects from which cosmic diversity arises.
2
Understand the Ishraqi charge
They argue that those aspects are i'tibariyya (mental considerations, not real distinctions). Hence, they cannot be causes of real multiplicity in the world.
The term i'tibariyya is crucial: it means something that exists only in the mind as a product of comparison or analysis, not in external reality.
3
Examine the evidence
The fixed stars manifest real diversity in essence (māhiyya) and spatial position (jihat). This cannot be explained by mere mental aspects.
The concrete example grounds the abstract philosophical objection.
4
Recognize the logical implication
If the Peripatetic scheme fails to account for this observable diversity, then a different metaphysical account is required—perhaps direct emanation from the Light of Lights (as the Ishraqis hold elsewhere).
The passage does not give the Ishraqi alternative here, but the critique is a necessary step in the dialectic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a theological-philosophical critique by identifying the problem, the proposed answers, and the grounds of their rejection.

Core Idea
Even if time is imaginary, the specificity of God's will remains a problem that al-Dawani's answer does not resolve.

This marginal note (hashiyah) refines the debate on why God's will attaches to a specific time for creating the world. The commentator first shows that the simple answer—that time is merely imaginary—does not remove the question. Then he examines al-Dawani's more sophisticated answer and finds it insufficient as well. By following this critique, you will learn how to evaluate the logical force of theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
Why is the question 'why this time?' problematic even if time is imaginary? The commentator argues that even imagined time has parts in re (fī nafs al-amr). The possible could exist in any part, so the will's specification still needs explanation.
This shows that reducing time to an imaginary construct does not dissolve the problem of divine choice.
2
Understand al-Dawani's attempted answer
How does al-Dawani try to block the question? He says the eternal will is attached to this particular pattern from pre-eternity, and such will is an attribute of God, so asking 'why this pattern?' is invalid.
This is a common move – grounding specification in the nature of God's attributes.
3
Analyze the commentator's refutation
What two options does the commentator consider, and why does each lead to a problem? If the will to attach to a different part is possible, then a preponderating factor is needed (regress). If impossible, then God cannot bring the possible into existence at another time (contradiction). Thus al-Dawani's answer fails.
This refutation uses a dilemma – forcing an impossible choice.
4
Conclude the commentator's own position
What is the only answer left? The commentator says there is no answer except the impossibility of the originated event existing before its determined time – a standard Ash'ari appeal to God's decree.
This shows that the logical analysis leads back to a theological doctrine.

The investigator al-Dawani answered that the eternal will is attached in this specific manner from pre-eternity, and such will is an attribute of God, so the question 'why this pattern' is invalid. However, the commentator further rejects al-Dawani's answer as also insufficient. He argues that the will to attach to a different part is either possible or impossible. If possible, a preponderating factor is needed. If impossible, then God would be unable to bring the possible into existence at another time, which contradicts possibility. The commentator concludes that there is no answer except to assert the impossibility of the originated event existing before its determined time, a standard Ashʿarī position.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the impossibility of circularity (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul) in the context of knowledge acquisition. The author begins by stating the division of knowledge into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari), then quickly moves to a refutation of circularity. The argument against circularity is presented using the example of (A) depending on (B) and (B) on (A), leading to (A) depending on itself. This requires a distinction between the dependent and the depend-upon, which would multiply entities infinitely. The author notes an objection: if we assume circularity, we cannot simultaneously require distinction because distinction would nullify the circularity. The answer to this objection is promised later.

The passage then addresses infinite regress. The well-known proof mentioned is that proving the falsity of infinite regress would require the simultaneous presence of infinite items in the mind, which is impossible. However, this proof depends on the soul being temporally originated; if the soul were eternal, it could have acquired infinite knowledges over infinite time without simultaneous presence.

Next, al-Dawani's argument is presented: if the chain of acquisition were infinite, no essence (kunn) would ever be obtained, because obtaining the essence depends on obtaining the aspect (wajh), which is theoretical. If it were obtained from its principles, it would take infinite time, leaving no finite time for actual acquisition. Without the essence, the aspect cannot be obtained either, since every aspect is the essence of something. Thus no conceptualization (tasawwur) would occur.

Later scholars completed this: if no conceptualization occurs, then no assent (tasdiq) occurs, because assent depends on conceptualization. However, the author notes that this only holds if all conceptualizations are acquired; if some conceptualizations are necessary, the argument fails.

Another scholar suggests that the aspect in knowing something by an aspect does not require conceptualizing itself by its essence or another aspect; rather, it serves as a mirror for observing the thing itself. Thus it is possible that the essence of the aspect be obtained over infinite time from its principles. The author criticizes this view for lacking proper premises and for confining knowledge of essences to necessary concepts.

Finally, the author alludes to the demonstration of al-tatbiq (برهان التطبيق), the main proof against infinite regress, which holds that every multitude (whether finite or infinite) is subject to a number. The footnote explains that this is attributed to the philosophers and adopted by theologians, though it is criticized by al-Amidi for containing problems. The passage ends with a note on the shared principles of essence and aspect: what is essential to a thing may be accidental to its aspect, and vice versa.

The overall argument type is not a formal syllogism but a critical refutation of logical doctrines concerning knowledge acquisition.

Al-Dawani's Argument: Essence and Aspect in Infinite Regress
Al-Dawani argues infinite acquisition chain prevents essence acquisition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Dawani's argument that an infinite chain of acquisition prevents obtaining any essence.

Core Idea
If acquiring knowledge of an essence requires an infinite chain of aspects, then no essence can ever be fully known.

Al-Dawani deepens the argument against infinite regress. He focuses on the concepts of essence (kunn) and aspect (wajh). He argues that obtaining the essence depends on obtaining the aspect, which is theoretical. If the chain is infinite, obtaining the aspect takes infinite time, so the essence is never obtained.

Guided Path
1
Understand the terms: essence and aspect

Al-Dawani concludes: 'no essence would be obtained'.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the impossibility of circularity (dawr) and infinite regress (tasalsul) in the context of knowledge acquisition. The author begins by stating the division of knowledge into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari), then quickly moves to a refutation of circularity. The argument against circularity is presented using the example of (A) depending on (B) and (B) on (A), leading to (A) depending on itself. This requires a distinction between the dependent and the depend-upon, which would multiply entities infinitely. The author notes an objection: if we assume circularity, we cannot simultaneously require distinction because distinction would nullify the circularity. The answer to this objection is promised later.

The passage then addresses infinite regress. The well-known proof mentioned is that proving the falsity of infinite regress would require the simultaneous presence of infinite items in the mind, which is impossible. However, this proof depends on the soul being temporally originated; if the soul were eternal, it could have acquired infinite knowledges over infinite time without simultaneous presence.

Next, al-Dawani's argument is presented: if the chain of acquisition were infinite, no essence (kunn) would ever be obtained, because obtaining the essence depends on obtaining the aspect (wajh), which is theoretical. If it were obtained from its principles, it would take infinite time, leaving no finite time for actual acquisition. Without the essence, the aspect cannot be obtained either, since every aspect is the essence of something. Thus no conceptualization (tasawwur) would occur.

Later scholars completed this: if no conceptualization occurs, then no assent (tasdiq) occurs, because assent depends on conceptualization. However, the author notes that this only holds if all conceptualizations are acquired; if some conceptualizations are necessary, the argument fails.

Another scholar suggests that the aspect in knowing something by an aspect does not require conceptualizing itself by its essence or another aspect; rather, it serves as a mirror for observing the thing itself. Thus it is possible that the essence of the aspect be obtained over infinite time from its principles. The author criticizes this view for lacking proper premises and for confining knowledge of essences to necessary concepts.

Core Idea
A preparatory cause does not necessitate its effect; only a true cause does.

In the first rebuttal of his refutation, the author targets the opponent's assumption that the acquirer (the cause of knowledge) is a necessary cause that must coincide with its effect. By showing that the acquirer is merely a preparatory cause, he undermines the claim of a necessary connection. This lesson will train you to spot and apply this crucial distinction.

Apply the distinction to the opponent's thesis

Infinite Regress Argument Against Unified Container
Second rebuttal: infinite regress from container unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct an infinite regress argument to refute the claim that cause and effect must exist in the same ontological container (e.g., both mental).

If every contingent cause requires another cause in the same container, an infinite regress follows.

The author's second rebuttal targets the opponent's assumption that cause and effect must share the same container (here, the mind). By showing that this leads to an infinite regress of causes, he demonstrates the absurdity of the assumption. Extract the opponent's claim

Conclude the regress and reject the original claim
The infinite chain (tasalsul) is impossible in Islamic philosophy (especially in Kalam). Therefore, the premise that cause and effect must share the same container is false. The author says: 'the necessity of the unity of the container is false.'
Tasalsul is generally rejected for causal chains.

The Liar Paradox and Its Resolution via Summary and Detail
Author presents liar paradox, critiques al-Dawani, offers final resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the logical resolution of the liar paradox using the distinction between summary (ijmal) and detailed (tafseel) consideration.

Core Idea
The liar paradox dissolves when we distinguish the proposition as report and reported object at different levels.

The liar paradox has puzzled logicians for centuries. Islamic logicians like al-Dawani and the author of this text offered sophisticated solutions. In this lesson, you will examine their arguments and understand how the paradox is resolved through a nuanced view of self-reference and the distinction between summary and detailed consideration.

Guided Path
1
State the Paradox
Identify the self-referential statement (e.g., 'this speech is false') and the contradiction: if false, it becomes true; if true, it becomes false.

2
Examine al-Dawani's Solution
Al-Dawani argues that such statements are not genuine reports (khabar) because a report requires a distinct object of reference; self-reference is impossible due to correlativity.
He concludes the statements are not reports, thus no paradox.

The hashia (marginalia) later argues that al-Dawani's view is actually correct.

The final resolution considers the proposition with all parts taken on the subject side; the relation is considered summarily (as the reported object) and the act of judging in detail (as the report). Thus the proposition can be true in detail and false in summary without contradiction.
This avoids the self-reference problem by splitting the perspective.

Resolving the Objection of a Single Accident in Two Subjects
Resolution of objections about accident inherence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve the objection that a single accident inheres in two subjects by distinguishing overlapping points.

Core Idea
A point common to two lines is actually two overlapping points, so no single accident inheres in two subjects.

A powerful objection against the theory of accidents is that a point at the intersection of lines would have two existences. The author answers by arguing these are two distinct points that overlap. Let's see the logical move.

1
Identify the objection against Ibn Sina
The gloss raises: if accident existence is relational, then a common point between two lines would exist with two existences (one from each line), which seems absurd.
The objection assumes the point is a single accident.
2
Understand the response based on overlap
The author, following the gloss, says that the objection is overcome by understanding Ibn Sina's meaning. The point is not a single accident; rather, there are two points that overlap and are not distinguished by the senses.
This preserves the theory.
3
Examine the further objection about extraction from two subjects
A second objection asks: if a point is extracted (accident) from two subjects (lines), does that entail a single accident from two subjects? The author replies that the extraction is from two different aspects, so no problem.
4
Evaluate the overall argument
The author concludes that there is no inherence of one accident in two subjects because the point is not one. The overlap explanation resolves the issue without violating the principle that an accident inheres in one subject.

Core Idea

Guided Path

In this passage, the commentator corrects a common misreading of Nasir al-Din al-Tusi's statement that accidents like location and position are 'particularizers' (mushakhkhiṣāt). A true copula indicates the relation as its primary meaning; 'kana' indicates time as well, so it fails the criterion.
