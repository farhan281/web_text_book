Critique of Inconsistent Positions on Dalala Iltizamiyya
Commentator explains two definitions of dalala and critiques author's inconsistent position

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two competing definitions of dalala (logicians vs. Arabic linguists) and evaluate the logical consistency of an author's adoption of elements from each.

Core Idea
Mixing incompatible definitions of dalala creates self-contradiction.

This passage exposes an inconsistency in Bahr Ulum's treatment of denotation (dalāla). He adopts the Arabic linguists' definition for iltizām but the logicians' definition for taḍammun. To grasp the critique, you must first understand the two distinct definitions of dalāla and how they affect the classification of the three types of denotation (muṭābaqa, taḍammun, iltizām). The commentator then shows why mixing them leads to a logical contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two definitions of dalāla
The passage presents two definitions: for logicians, dalāla is a mental transition from the concept of a thing to another concept; for Arabic linguists, it is understanding the meaning from the word as used. Identify how each defines the relation between word and meaning.
2
Map the three types under each definition
Under both definitions, dalāla is divided into muṭābaqa (whole meaning), taḍammun (part of meaning), and iltizām (external concomitant). However, note that for logicians, these three coexist in a single usage; for linguists, they cannot coexist, and merging muṭābaqa and taḍammun is unintelligible.
This difference is the crux of the inconsistency.
3
Identify Bahr Ulum's adopted positions
The commentator states: Bahr Ulum chose the Arabic linguists' view for iltizām (allowing customary connection), but for taḍammun he chose the logicians' view (allowing union of muṭābaqa and taḍammun in composites). Locate these claims in the passage.
These positions are taken from his works al-Hāshiya and al-Muslim.
4
Assess consistency
If the two definitions have incompatible implications, adopting one for one type and the other for another type results in contradiction. The commentator argues that the linguists' view forbids exactly what the logicians' view permits. Determine whether the criticism is valid.
Consider whether Bahr Ulum could resolve this by assuming different contexts or a hybrid definition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the author's (Bahr Ulum's) position on the division of denotation (dalāla). The commentator first notes that the author, in his Hashiya, chose the definition of denotation used by Arabic linguists (ahl al-'arabiyya) over that of the logicians. The commentator then explains both definitions. The logicians define dalāla as a relation such that from the conception of one thing, the conception of another necessarily follows; this excludes metaphorical usage. For a word used in its real meaning, it denotes its whole (muṭābaqa), its part (taḍammun), and its external concomitant (iltizām). The Arabic linguists, by contrast, define dalāla as the understanding of a meaning from a word as used; types are determined by whether the meaning is the original (muṭābaqa), part of the original (taḍammun), or external (iltizām). Critically, these three denotations cannot occur together in one usage, and merging the first two is impossible. The commentator then argues that the author is inconsistent: for iltizām he adopts the Arabic linguists' view (allowing customary concomitance), but in his book al-Muslim he refutes the requirement of mental concomitance by citing metaphorical denotations, and for taḍammun he adopts the logicians' view (allowing union of muṭābaqa and taḍammun in composite terms). The commentator concludes the best solution is to exclude metaphorical denotation entirely from consideration, as suggested by some eminent scholars.

Key Terms
دلالة (dalāla): Denotation: for logicians, a relation causing the mind to move from the concept of the signifier to the concept of something else; for Arabic linguists, understanding the meaning from the word as used. [Note: No significant shift; this term is stable across both traditions, though the definition differs slightly.]
مطابقة (muṭābaqa): Denotation of a word for its entire meaning as posited (e.g., 'human' denotes the whole animal+rational).
تضمن (taḍammun): Denotation of a word for a part of its meaning (e.g., 'human' denotes 'animal').
التزام (iltizām): Denotation of a word for something external to its meaning but necessarily connected, either mentally or customarily (e.g., 'ceiling' denotes 'wall').
علاقة عقلية (ʿalāqa ʿaqliyya): A mental connection: the essential necessary concomitance that prevents conceiving the signified without the concomitant.
علاقة عرفية (ʿalāqa ʿurfiyya): A customary connection: based on common association, not essential (e.g., al-ḥātim with generosity).

Levels of Perception: Sense, Imagination, and Intellect
Explanation of how sense, imagination, and intellect abstract essences from matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three levels of perception (sense, imagination, intellect) based on their capacity to abstract the quiddity from material accidents.

Core Idea
The intellect alone fully abstracts the quiddity; sense and imagination remain tied to accidents.

This passage explains how perception operates at three levels: sense, internal imagination, and intellect. Each level abstracts the essence from matter to a different degree, culminating in the intellect's ability to transform the sensible into an intelligible. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for grasping the Islamic epistemological framework of mental existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three faculties
Find where the author names al-hiss (sense), al-khayal (imagination), and al-'aql (intellect). Note the Arabic terms.
These are given in order of increasing abstraction.
2
Analyze each faculty's relation to material accidents
For sense: it perceives the essence only when submerged in accidents and dependent on a positional relation. For imagination: it can represent the image without the external relation but still with accidents. For intellect: it abstracts the quiddity from all individuating accidents.
The key difference is the degree of tajrid (abstraction).
3
Connect to the quote from al-Shifa
Identify the quoted statement from Ibn Sina's al-Shifa. The author uses it to support the idea that all perception involves abstraction from matter, but the degree varies.
The quote reinforces the hierarchical model.
4
Examine the argument about mental existence
The author critiques a proof that true propositions about non-existent subjects (e.g., 'The mountain is golden') would require the particular itself to exist in the mind. The author argues that only an abstracted form occurs, not the particular itself.
This shows the limits of mental representation.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and epistemological discussion of the levels of perception—sense, imagination, and intellect—and their capacity to abstract the essence from material accidents. The author begins by stating that the sense perceives the essence only while it is submerged in material accidents; it does not abstract it. The internal imagination (khayal) can separate the form from the external relation to matter, but still retains the accidents, so it can represent the image in the absence of the object. Only the intellect (aql) can fully abstract the quiddity (mahiyya) from all individuating accidents, transforming the sensible into an intelligible. The author then quotes from Ibn Sina's al-Shifa, which affirms that all perception involves taking a form abstracted from matter to some degree, but when the relation to matter is lost, sensory perception ceases. Instead, what occurs in the mind is an abstraction from the accidents that would prevent mental representation, while still retaining an identity that prevents external multiplicity. This explains how mental representation differs from external existence.

The Peripatetic Interpretation of Divine Knowledge of Particulars
Introduction to Peripatetic view on divine knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Peripatetic distinction between universal and particular knowledge as applied to God.

Core Idea
God knows particulars universally, not particularly, because particularity requires sensation.

In this section, the author sets the stage for the famous debate on divine knowledge. He explains that mental individuation – the basis for particularity – arises from sensation, not intellection. The Peripatetic philosophers therefore conclude that God, who has no senses, knows particulars only in a universal manner. This lesson unpacks that initial claim and its logical foundation.

Commentary

The Objection from Causal Knowledge
The milliyyun objection and Peripatetic response

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the milliyyun objection against the Peripatetic view and evaluate the responses.

Core Idea
If God is the cause of every particular, He must know each particular as such.

The religious scholars (milliyyun) attack the Peripatetic claim by invoking the principle that knowledge of a cause entails knowledge of its effect. Since God is the cause of all contingent things, He should know each particular in its particularity. The passage presents the objection and then attempts to refute it by distinguishing between knowledge from all aspects and knowledge in general. This lesson will walk through that logical argument.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the principle: 'العلم بالعلة يوجب العلم بالمعلول' (knowledge of the cause necessitates knowledge of the effect). This is a common logical-epistemological principle.
2
Apply the principle to God
The objective: since God is the cause (جاعل) of all contingent things, He should know each particular effect in its particularity.
3
Identify the defense
Some commentators respond that knowledge of the cause only entails knowledge of the effect if the cause is known from all aspects (من جميع الجهات). God knows Himself presentially (علم حضوري) but not from all aspects.
The defense tries to restrict the condition of the principle.
4
Evaluate the defense
The author rejects this defense because the particular as particular is an effect, and God is its maker, so knowledge of it should be particular. The defender's move fails.
5
Consider the alternative
The passage also considers distinguishing between presential and acquired knowledge, but this leads to a dilemma: either God knows His essence with all aspects (so must know things as they are) or He doesn't (which implies ignorance).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali charged the Peripatetics with disbelief; later scholars offered interpretations to exonerate them.

However, the author then provides two tawjihs (interpretations) that, if accepted, show that the philosophers did not actually deny God's knowledge of particulars as they are. The first distinguishes intellectual (universal) from sensory (particular) knowledge; the second invokes God's non-temporality. Reconstructing the Reductio on Existence and Essence
Author presents second argument for identity of existence and essence via universal trilemma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio argument that existence must be identical to the essence of the Necessary Being, and evaluate its logical structure.

Core Idea
If existence were separate from the Necessary Being, its essence would be a universal—leading to contradictions.

This passage presents a subtle argument against separating existence and essence in the Necessary Being. The author uses a reductio ad absurdum: assuming separation leads to three exhaustive possibilities for the individuals of the universal essence, each entailing an absurdity. Mastering this argument sharpens your ability to use disjunctive syllogisms in theological contexts.

Guided Path
1
State the assumption
Assume for reductio that existence (wujud) is other than the Necessary Being (al-wajib). Then the Necessary would have a universal essence (mahiyya kulliyya) and existence.
The assumption is the opposite of what the argument intends to prove.
2
Identify the universal's individuals
Every universal essence has individuals besides the one that exists. Consider these individuals in themselves, without regard to existence.
The individuals are not actual existents but possible instantiations.
3
Enumerate the three possibilities for those individuals
Each individual, in itself, must be either impossible (mumtani'a), necessary (wajiba), or possible (mumkina). These three exhaust all options.
This is a logical trilemma.
4
Show each option leads to contradiction
If impossible, then the existent individuals would also be impossible—contradiction. If necessary, then infinite necessary beings would exist—contradiction. If possible, then the Necessary would be possible—contradiction to its assumed necessity.
Each horn of the trilemma destroys the original assumption.
5
Conclude the identity
Since the assumption leads to contradictions, the assumption is false. Therefore existence is identical to the essence of the Necessary. The argument also implies identity of individuation (ta'ayyun).
The author references his work 'al-'Ajala al-Nafi'a' for further discussion.

Distinguishing Qualified Negation from Negation of the Qualified
Distinction between qualified negation and negation of qualified.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the true contradictory of a qualified proposition and distinguish it from a qualified negation.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a qualified proposition is the negation of the whole, not a qualified negation.

This lesson tackles a subtle logical puzzle: how can the essence in itself be neither existent nor non-existent without violating the law of excluded middle? The answer lies in distinguishing between two types of statements: a qualified negation (e.g., 'not existent in essence') and the negation of a qualified statement (e.g., 'it is not the case that the essence in its essence is existent'). The author shows that only the latter is a genuine contradictory, resolving the apparent paradox.

Guided Path
1
Understand the station of the essence in itself
Recognize that when we consider the essence (mahiyyah) stripped of all accidents including existence, only the essence and its essential properties are present. In this station, any predication of accidental attributes like existence is false.
This station is called 'al-martabah al-mutlaqah' or 'al-martabah al-dhatiyyah'.
2
Identify the two forms of questioning
Notice that the author presents two forms: (1) negation placed before the qualification (e.g., 'not existent in its limits') and (2) negation placed after the qualification (e.g., 'existent in its essence or not existent in its essence'). In form (1), the negation applies to the whole qualified phrase, forming a genuine contradictory pair. In form (2), both the affirmation and negation are qualified, so they are not contradictories.
The key is the position of the negation relative to the qualification.
3
Determine the true contradictory
The true contradictory of a qualified affirmation (e.g., 'the essence is existent in its essence') is the negation of the entire qualified statement: 'It is not the case that the essence is existent in its essence'. This is different from 'the essence is not existent in its essence', which is a qualified negation and not a direct contradictory.
The contradictory must be a simple negation of the whole, without the same qualification.
4
Apply to the apparent violation of excluded middle
Now see why both 'existent in its essence' and 'not existent in its essence' can be false without violating the law of excluded middle. They are not genuine contradictories; the real contradictory (negation of the whole) is not false. Thus, the statements in that station are not a genuine contradictory pair.
The author concludes that the phrase 'the two contradictories are removed' is imprecise; what is removed are the qualified pairs, not true contradictories.

Two Paths to Theoretical Knowledge: Parts or Accidents
Author explains the process of acquiring theoretical knowledge through conception of parts or accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two methods of acquiring theoretical knowledge described by the author and identify the outcomes of each.

Core Idea
Theoretical knowledge is attained either through essential parts (disclosing essence) or through accidental distinctions (sketches).

In this passage, the author explains a fundamental epistemological process: how theoretical knowledge is acquired. He outlines two distinct methods—one by conceiving the essential parts of a thing, and another by conceiving its accidents. Understanding this distinction is key to how definitions (hadd and rasm) are constructed in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. The author describes two ways to obtain theoretical knowledge: by conceiving the parts (ajza') of the thing, or by conceiving its accidents (a'warid). Mark these in the text.
Think of 'parts' as essential components (genus and differentia), not physical parts.
2
Understand the process for essential parts
When one conceives the parts, mental forms (ashbah) of those parts appear in the mind. They are 'observed and qualified' (combined). This yields the mental form of the whole object, disclosing its essence (haqiqah). This corresponds to a complete definition (hadd haqiqi).
Qualifying means specifying how the parts relate to form the whole.
3
Understand the process for accidents
Alternatively, one conceives the accidents (non-essential attributes). Their mental forms appear, and then a resulting mental form 'distinguishes' the sought object. The distinction may be complete (as in equivalent sketches, rusum mutasawiyah) or incomplete (as in general or specific sketches). This yields a definition by accidents (rasm).
Equivalent sketches define all individuals equally; general/specific sketches are broader/narrower.
4
Compare the outcomes
The part-based method gives disclosure of the true nature (haqiqah), while the accident-based method gives only a distinguishing mark. The first yields a hadd, the second a rasm. Ask yourself: which gives deeper knowledge?
The author suggests that accident-based knowledge can still be sufficient in many cases.
5
Note the author's disclaimer
The author admits his view contradicts the majority (jumhur), but suggests that the majority view may not be accurate. This is a meta-cognitive point: don't accept authority blindly in logic.
Consider why the majority might disagree. Does the accident-based method truly yield theoretical knowledge?

Core Idea
Opinion is a simple assent to the dominant side, not a composite of two beliefs.

Indirect Attention to Individuals: Refuting Mirzajan
Author argues that individuals are indirectly attended to in judgment, refuting Mirzajan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the author's argument that individuals can be the subject of judgments despite not being directly conceived.

Core Idea
Individuals are attended to even if not directly known—Mirzajan's objection is a corrupt foundation.

The author engages in a critical refinement of an epistemological issue: can individuals serve as the subject of a judgment if they are not directly conceived? Mirzajan argued no, because they are absolutely unknown. The author counters that indirect attention suffices, and that Mirzajan's definition of 'absolutely unknown' is flawed. This lesson unpacks the logical moves and the precise definitions at stake.

Guided Path

State the author's main claim: individuals, even if not directly conceived (متصورة بالذات), are still attended to by themselves (ملتفت إليها بالذات) through their aspect.
The author uses the distinction between direct conception and direct attention.

Understand Mirzajan's counter-argument
Mirzajan argues that the acquisition of the aspect is distinct from the acquisition of the thing having the aspect, making the thing absolutely unknown (مجهول مطلق) by itself and thus impossible to be the subject of judgment.
Mirzajan defines 'absolutely unknown' as something not acquired by any means.

Analyze the author's refutation
The author rejects Mirzajan's characterization of individuals as absolutely unknown. He distinguishes two meanings: if it means not acquired by any means, individuals are not that; if it means not acquired by itself but acquired by its aspect, judgment is not impossible.
The author asserts that Mirzajan's reasoning is neither necessary nor proven; it is the very point of contention.

The critique hinges on the precise meaning of 'absolutely unknown'.

Primary Predication: Definition and Conditions
Author defines primary predication and analyzes its possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of primary predication and determine whether it satisfies the conditions for valid predication.

Core Idea
Primary predication requires essential identity, but conceptual difference is allowed.

In logic, predication is the relation between subject and predicate in a proposition. The author distinguishes primary predication, where subject and predicate are identical in essence, from common predication. This lesson unpacks the definition and the three scenarios for primary predication, showing when it is impossible and when it is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of primary predication
Find where the author defines primary predication: the subject itself is the predicate, meaning the definition of the subject is exactly the predicate's definition.
The term used is al-haml al-awwali.
2
Understand the three cases for a single thing
The author considers a single thing under one consideration, two considerations without conditions, and two considerations with distinct conditions. Note which cases allow predication.
3
Recognize why predication fails in the first two cases
In the first case, no multiplicity at all means no relation. In the second, two considerations without being taken as conditions do not create a real duality; predication requires a genuine otherness between subject and predicate.
The author rejects the view that mere repeated attention suffices.
4
See the third case and its theoretical example
When one thing is considered under two different conditions, predication is valid. It may be trivial or informative. A theoretical example is the statement 'Existence is the Necessary', where 'existence' and 'the Necessary' refer to the same reality but differ conceptually.
This shows primary predication can be used in metaphysical contexts.

Commentary
This passage classifies the types of predication (al-haml) in logic. It begins by defining two fundamental kinds: primary predication (al-haml al-awwali) and common predication (al-hamal al-sha'i'). In primary predication, the subject and predicate are identical in essence; the subject's definition is exactly the predicate's definition. The author then examines the conditions under which such predication is possible. He explains that if a single thing is considered with one aspect only, no predication is possible because there is no distinction between subject and predicate. Similarly, if it is considered with two aspects but without those aspects being taken as distinct conditions, predication is also impossible, contravening the view of al-Sadr al-Dashtaki. The reason is that predication requires a relation, which demands some difference between the two sides. Repeating attention does not create multiplicity. However, if one thing is considered under two different conditions (whether attentional or not), then predication becomes valid, though it may be trivial or informative. This can even be theoretical, as in the statement of the philosophers: 'Existence is the Necessary' (meaning the Necessary Being), where 'existence' and 'the Necessary' are conceptually different but refer to the same reality. The second main type is common predication, which requires only unity in existence—the subject is either identical to the predicate or an instance of it. This is the predication used in the sciences. The author then divides common predication: first, by whether the predicate is essential (dhati) or accidental (aradi) to the subject, yielding essential predication (haml bi al-dhat) and accidental predication (haml bi al-'arad). Second, by the mode of relation: via 'fi' (inherence), 'dhu' (possession), or 'la' (belonging). These divisions are crucial for analyzing statements in logic and the sciences.

Common Predication and Its Divisions
Author defines common predication and its subdivisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and accidental predication and classify sentences accordingly.

Core Idea
Common predication is based on existential unity, not essential identity, and is the standard in sciences.

While primary predication is rare, common predication is the everyday predication used in all sciences. The author defines it as requiring only unity in existence, then divides it into essential vs accidental, and also by the relational modes 'fi', 'dhu', and 'la'. This lesson helps you apply these categories to real statements.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of common predication
Find where the author defines common predication: it restricts predication to mere unity in existence, meaning the subject either is the predicate or is a member of the predicate's extension.
The term is al-haml al-sha'i' al-muta'arif.
2
Identify the first division: essential vs accidental
The predicate can be essential (dhati) to the subject, like 'human' to 'Zayd', or accidental ('aradi), like 'standing' to 'Zayd'. Look for the author's phrasing 'بِحَسَبِ كَوْنِ الْمَحْمُولِ ذَاتِيَّا أَوْ عَرَضِيًّا'.
3
Identify the second division: the three relational modes
The predicate can relate to the subject via 'fi' (inherence: the attribute inheres in the subject), 'dhu' (possession: the subject possesses the attribute), or 'la' (belonging: the attribute belongs to the subject). The author mentions these but does not elaborate here.
This division is based on the particle used in the relation.
4
Apply to an example
Take a statement like 'Zayd is a human' (زيد إنسان). It is common predication because Zayd and human share existence (Zayd is an individual human). The predicate 'human' is essential to Zayd, so it's essential predication. Also, it can be analyzed via 'fi'? Typically not; the 'fi' mode applies to accidents, e.g., 'Zayd is white' (Zayd abyaḍ) – the whiteness inheres in Zayd.
Practice identifying the type.

Author classifies concepts into necessary, possible, and impossible.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every concept is either necessary, possible, or impossible in existence.

This lesson introduces the foundational modal classification used in Islamic logic and theology. The author divides all concepts into three categories: necessary (wājib), possible (mumkin), and impossible (mumtaniʿ). Understanding this classification is essential for grasping how entailment (talāzum) works between concepts, which is the basis for many arguments in kalām and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three modal categories
The passage states: 'The concept is either necessary, possible, or impossible.' Note that these are mutually exclusive and exhaustive categories for any concept that can be conceived.

A necessary being (wājib) is one whose existence is required and non-existence impossible – e.g., God. He suggests that the aspect itself might be such that it realizes knowledge in the intellect, allowing the intellect to judge the necessity. Thus, the claim that we know it is such but do not know it cannot be otherwise is invalid. This is a subtle argument about the nature of certainty and knowledge.
