Conversion of Conditional Propositions
Author explains conversion rules for conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the correct converse of any universal affirmative conditional proposition and explain the role of the negation of the consequent.

Core Idea
The converse of a universal affirmative conditional is also universal affirmative.

This passage explains the rules for converting conditional propositions. The author provides a proof for universal affirmatives and addresses an objection, then covers particular affirmatives and negatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule for universal affirmative conditionals
The author states that universal affirmative conditionals convert to universal affirmative conditionals. Examine the proof: 'because the negation of the consequent necessitates the negation of the antecedent by necessity.'
This is contraposition.
2
Consider the objection about impossible negation
An objector asks: what if the negation of the consequent is impossible? Then it might not necessitate the negation of the antecedent. The author dismisses this as obstinacy, noting that impossibility can entail even contradictions.
The rebuttal relies on the principle that from impossibility anything follows.
3
Apply the rule for particular affirmatives
The author gives an example: 'Sometimes if something is an animal, it is not a human' (true) but its converse 'Sometimes if something is a human, it is not an animal' (false). Thus particular affirmatives do not convert.
The example shows partial implication does not reverse.
4
Understand the rule for negatives
Negative conditionals (universal or particular) convert only into particular negatives. The author provides a reductio proof using variables (A,B,C,D).
The proof shows that universal conversion would contradict the original.

Commentary
This passage covers the conversion of conditional propositions (al-shartiyya) and then addresses a skeptical doubt. First, the author states that universal affirmative conditionals convert universally because the negation of the consequent necessitates the negation of the antecedent. An objection is raised: what if the negation of the consequent is impossible? The author dismisses this as obstinacy, noting that even the impossible can entail both contradictories, so the conversion holds. Next, particular affirmative conditionals do not convert, as shown by the example 'Sometimes if something is an animal, it is not a human' (true) but its converse 'Sometimes if something is a human, it is not an animal' (false). Negative conditionals (universal or particular) convert only to particular negatives, with a similar reductio proof. Finally, a doubt is presented: the statement 'Every non-contradiction is not a partner to God' is true, but its converse 'Every partner to God is a contradiction' is false because there is no subject (partner to God) and the realities are false. The author suggests one might commit to the truth of the converse as a genuine (haqiqiyya) proposition, but this is inadequate because the original is external (kharijiyya). Taking the genuine as absolute would be obstinate. Thus the doubt remains unresolved.

The Doubt about Conversion with Non-Existent Subjects
Author presents and discusses a skeptical doubt about conversion of conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the problem of converting conditionals when the subject does not exist, and evaluate the proposed solution.

Core Idea
Conversion fails when the subject has no existence unless the proposition is taken as genuine (haqiqiyya).

The author raises a doubt: the proposition 'Every non-contradiction is not a partner to God' is true, but its converse 'Every partner to God is a contradiction' is false because the subject 'partner to God' is non-existent. He considers a potential resolution but finds it lacking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its converse
The original is 'Every non-contradiction is not a partner to God' (true). Its converse is 'Every partner to God is a contradiction' (false).
Non-contradiction refers to something that is not a contradiction.
2
Determine why the converse is false
The author gives two reasons: non-existence of the subject (partner to God does not exist) and disparity of their false realities (the concepts are incompatible and false).
The converse would be about a non-existent subject.
3
Understand the suggestion to treat the converse as genuine (haqiqiyya)
One might claim the converse is true as a genuine proposition, i.e., a proposition that holds conceptually even if the subject does not exist in reality.
A genuine proposition does not require actual existence.
4
Analyze why the suggestion is insufficient
The original is external (kharijiyya), considering actual existence. Treating the converse as genuine does not match. If one insists on absolute genuineness, it borders on obstinacy.
Thus the doubt remains unresolved.

Rules of Contraposition for Categorical Propositions
Author states rules of contraposition for universal and particular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the valid contrapositive form for each categorical proposition type (A, E, I, O) and justify it using the entailment principle.

Core Idea
Contraposition flips and negates both terms, but only some proposition types yield a valid immediate inference.

This passage from a logical work lays down the rules for contraposition (عكس النقيض) in categorical logic. You will learn which propositions can be validly contraposed and in what form, using the principle that the negation of the consequent entails the negation of the antecedent. The author also addresses a subtle objection and provides examples to clarify the rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition types
Recall the four categorical forms: A (universal affirmative), E (universal negative), I (particular affirmative), O (particular negative). The passage gives rules for each.
2
Understand the entailment principle
The author uses the principle that if 'A implies B' is true, then 'not-B implies not-A' is also true. This is the basis for contraposition.
In Arabic: انتفاء اللازم مستلزم لانتفاء الملزوم.
3
Apply to universal affirmative
For 'Every A is B', the contrapositive is 'Every non-B is non-A'. The author argues this is universal affirmative, not particular, by the entailment principle.
The objection about impossible consequent is dismissed as obstinacy.
4
Apply to particular affirmative
For 'Some A is B', there is no valid contrapositive. The example: 'If something is an animal, it may not be a human' is true, but its contrapositive 'If something is a human, it may not be an animal' is false.
This shows the inference does not hold.
5
Apply to negative propositions
For universal negative 'No A is B' and particular negative 'Some A is not B', the contrapositive is only a particular negative: 'Some non-B is not A'. The author uses a reductio to prove it cannot be universal.
Check the example with variables (أ، ب، ج، د) to see the logic.

First Doubt: Contrapositive of an Impossible Subject
Author critiques and resolves a doubt about contraposition with a non-existent subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why contraposition fails when the subject of the original proposition is non-existent and identify the correct resolution.

Core Idea
Contraposition requires real individuals; impossible subjects break the rule.

In this lesson, we tackle a subtle problem: if a proposition is true but its subject cannot exist, does its contrapositive have to be true? The author presents a theological example and guides us through the pitfalls of applying contraposition blindly.

Guided Path
1
Locate the original true proposition
Identify the proposition: 'Every non-conjunction of contradictories is not a partner of Allah.' Note that its subject is impossible (non-conjunction of contradictories cannot exist).
2
Compute the contrapositive
Form the contrapositive: 'Every partner of Allah is a conjunction of contradictories.' This is the result of reversing and negating the terms.
3
Evaluate truth values
The original is true because its subject is impossible (a universal negative with an empty subject is true). The contrapositive is false because its subject 'partner of Allah' does not exist, making a universal affirmative false.
This reveals a counterexample to the claim that contraposition always preserves truth.
4
Examine the attempted fix (haqiqiyya)
One might try to reinterpret the original as a haqiqiyya proposition (allowing hypothetical existence) to save the contrapositive. The author rejects this as it either leads to obstinacy or does not resolve the issue.
5
Understand the author's resolution
The author concludes that the only proper answer is to restrict the rule of contraposition to propositions whose terms have actual individuals in reality. Otherwise, the rule does not apply.
This is a critical refinement of the logical principle.

The author warns that we must restrict it to propositions whose contradictory terms have actual existence. This lesson unpacks that condition and shows how to apply it correctly.

And from here it became possible for you to commit to the truthfulness of all impossible propositions, by taking the contradictories of any two impossibles you wish, and forming an affirmative proposition from them, then it converts by conversion by contraposition, necessitating the truth of each [term] of the other, like: Every indivisible part is not void, so every void is an indivisible part. So it is as if impossibility is a single non-existence with multiple designations: sometimes it is expressed by the conjunction of two contradictories, sometimes by indivisible atoms, sometimes by a partner of the Creator. Just as necessity is a single existence with absolutely no participation, and this is an analogical reasoning without a common [principle]. And the permission is strengthened in the implication of an impossible from an impossible absolutely; because everything implies itself.

Commentary
This passage is a two-part discussion. First, it clarifies the conditions for conversion by contraposition (عكس النقيض). The author states that the rule applies only when the contradictory terms of the subject and predicate actually have individuals in reality. If we rely on hypothetical existence for the affirmative, the falsity of the converse does not harm the original. But if we demand actual existence, then we must restrict the claim to propositions with real referents for their terms. Second, the author shows a startling application: by taking two impossible propositions (like 'an indivisible part is void' and its contradictory), we can form an affirmative that, via contraposition, yields the truth of each about the other. This makes all impossibilities equivalent—they are all 'non-existence' under different names. The author compares this to necessity, which is one unified existence. This leads to the principle that an impossible implies any impossible (since everything implies itself). The commentary here is expanding on the implications of the conversion rule, guarding against the mistake of thinking contraposition applies unrestrictedly.
