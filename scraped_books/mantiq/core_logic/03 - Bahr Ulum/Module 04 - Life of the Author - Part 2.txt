Using Biographical Records for Scholarly Authentication
Records death and praises of Bahr al-Ulum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract key biographical details from a scholarly death notice and explain how such notices function in the Islamic tradition of scholarly authentication (tawthiq).

Core Idea
Biographical records are the backbone of hadith authentication and scholarly authority.

This passage records the death and scholarly legacy of Bahr al-Ulum, a major figure in Indian Islamic scholarship. In the Islamic sciences, biographical notices like this are not merely historical; they are tools for tawthiq (establishing the trustworthiness of transmitters and scholars). By learning to read such notices critically, you gain a skill used in hadith studies (ilm al-rijal) and in assessing the reliability of scholarly opinions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic biographical data
From the passage, extract the date, place of death, and burial details. This is the factual core of any biographical notice.
Date: 12 Rajab 1225 AH (1810 CE), Place: Madras (Chennai), Burial: courtyard of the Wali Jahi Mosque.
2
Analyze the function of the prayer (du'a)
The author includes the phrase 'rahimahu Allah' (may God have mercy on him). In Islamic biographical writing, such prayers express respect and signal that the scholar is considered trustworthy and righteous.
This is not just a religious formula; it is part of the tawthiq process.
3
Evaluate the source and nature of the praise
The praise is quoted from Sayyid Abd al-Hayy al-Hasani's Nuzhat al-Khawatir. Note the chain: the passage cites an authoritative biographical dictionary. In Islamic scholarship, the strength of a testimonial depends on the credibility of the source.
Nuzhat al-Khawatir is a well-known biographical work; citing it adds authority.
4
Extract the criteria for scholarly excellence mentioned
The praise lists specific qualities: unique in many arts, excellent teaching, ability to attract students to follow evidence, unmatched in perfections. These criteria align with what the tradition values in a scholar (knowledge, teaching skill, moral influence).
Such criteria are used in tawthiq to rank scholars.
5
Connect to the function of tawthiq in Islamic sciences
Reflect on how this biographical passage would be used by a hadith scholar or historian. It establishes that Bahr al-Ulum was a reliable authority whose works and teachings can be trusted. The praise serves as a form of tazkiyah (recommendation).
In hadith science, biographical entries like this are essential for evaluating transmitters.

Establishing Authorship: The Case of Sullam al-Ulum and its Commentary
Introduces the book and confirms its attribution to author and commentator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key evidence for attributing a classical Islamic text to its author and commentator, using self-reference and biographical consensus.

Core Idea
Authorial self-reference and unanimous biographical consensus are the strongest proof of a text's attribution.

How do we know for certain who wrote a classical Islamic text? This passage from the introduction to Sharh Sullam al-Ulum demonstrates a rigorous method: the author himself references his earlier work, and all major biographers agree. This lesson will equip you with the tools to verify authorship claims in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary evidence for the matn's attribution
The author of Sullam al-Ulum, Muhibb Allah al-Bahari, mentions his own work in his later book 'Musallam al-Thubut'. This self-reference is considered the strongest proof because it comes directly from the author.
Note the year: al-Sullam was written before 1109 AH, when al-Musallam was composed.
2
Check the secondary evidence: consensus of biographers
The passage lists three major biographers (al-Lakhnawi, al-Baghdadi, al-Zirikli) who all attribute the work to al-Bahari. This unanimity reinforces the primary evidence.
Biographical consensus is a standard tool in Islamic historiography.
3
Apply the same method to the commentary (sharh)
The commentator (Bahr al-Ulum) also attributes his own commentary in his other works, and historians confirm it. This double verification ensures certainty.
Observe how the method is identical for both original text and commentary.
4
Evaluate the role of transmission history
The passage narrates how the book reached Egypt via a judge bringing it from Medina. This physical chain of transmission adds circumstantial evidence, but is weaker than authorial self-reference.
Transmission history is useful but not definitive for attribution.
5
Synthesize the method for a new text
Whenever you encounter a classical Islamic text, look for: (1) author's self-reference in other works, (2) unanimous agreement of biographers, (3) external evidence like transmission history. Rank the strength of each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sharh, hashiyah, ta'liqat, and takmilah based on their function in the commentarial tradition, and identify them in a list of works.

Core Idea
Commentaries are not all equal: sharh explicates, hashiyah glosses, ta'liqat annotate, takmilah completes.

The passage you are about to read is a list of scholars who wrote on Sullam al-'Ulum, a foundational text in Islamic logic. But notice the variety: some wrote shuruh (commentaries), others hawashi (glosses), ta'liqat (notes), or takmilah (completions). Each term indicates a different genre of scholarly engagement. This lesson will equip you to recognize these genres and understand their role in the transmission of knowledge.

Understand ta'liqat and takmilah
Ta'liqat are critical notes or annotations; takmilah is a completion of an unfinished commentary. These are specialized forms of engagement.

The Colophon: Concluding a Scholarly Work
Author concludes the book and asks readers for prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a classical Islamic colophon and explain the purpose behind the author's request for prayer.

Core Idea
A colophon is not just an endnote; it is a supplication and an act of humility before God.

In the Islamic scholarly tradition, authors often conclude their works with a prayerful colophon, acknowledging that all benefit comes from Allah and humbly asking readers to remember them in their du'a. This lesson explores the colophon of Bahr al-'Ulum on Sullam al-'Ulum, a typical example of this beautiful practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the colophon's components
Look at the Arabic text. Find the opening 'شرح بحر العلوم على سلم العلوم' (title), the phrase 'تم بحمد الله' (completed by praise of God), and the supplication 'إنك أنت سميع الدعاء'.
2
Understand the author's request for du'a
The author explicitly asks anyone who benefits from the book not to forget him in their prayers. Reflect on why a scholar would make such a request.
This reflects the belief that knowledge is a trust and the scholar hopes for reward and forgiveness through the prayers of others.
3
Connect to the broader tradition
Recognize that similar colophons appear in works by scholars like al-Ghazali in 'Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din' and al-Suyuti in many of his books. This is a sign of adab (etiquette) in knowledge transmission.
