Induction and the Problem of Probability
Author presents induction and a probabilistic paradox

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why induction yields only probable knowledge and identify the logical paradox that arises when applying probability to a population with known proportions.

Core Idea
Induction from majority yields probability, not certainty, because exceptions like the crocodile are always possible.

In this passage, the author discusses induction (الاستقراء) as a form of inference that moves from the ruling of the majority to the whole class. He emphasizes that induction yields only probable belief (ظن), not certainty, because exceptions are always possible. He then presents a fascinating paradox: if you know that in a room of three people two are Muslim and one is a disbeliever, but you don't know who is who, then any individual you see is probably Muslim based on the majority. Yet, once you become certain of the Islamic identity of two of them, the third must certainly be a disbeliever. This seems to generate a contradiction. Let's unpack this.

Induction yields only probability (ẓann) because exceptions are possible, like the crocodile. Complete enumeration would yield certainty, but that is not required; claiming the majority is sufficient for probability.
The author argues against al-Sayyid's view that complete enumeration is necessary.
3

Consider a house with three persons: two Muslims and one disbeliever, identities unknown. Based on majority, any person you see is probably Muslim. However, if you later become certain that two specific individuals are Muslim, the third must certainly be a disbeliever. The author claims that probability of the premise (that two are Muslim) entails probability of the consequent (that each is Muslim), leading to each individual simultaneously being probably Muslim and probably disbeliever, which is contradictory.
The paradox relies on the principle that if you are certain of the exact identities, you deduce the remaining one's disbelief with certainty, not probability.

He gives a biological example and clarifies that induction only produces probable knowledge (ẓann), not certainty. Understanding this limitation is crucial for evaluating inductive arguments in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Definition of Analogy (Tamthīl) and Its Components
Author defines analogy as inference from a particular to another particular via a common attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of tamthīl (aṣl, far', ʿilla) and distinguish the two primary methods for establishing the ʿilla: al-dawarān and al-sabr wa al-taqsīm.

Core Idea
Analogy moves from one particular to another via a shared cause; jurists call it qiyās.

The passage defines tamthīl (analogy) as an inference from one particular instance to another based on a common attribute. This form of reasoning is central to Islamic jurisprudence, where it is known as qiyās. In this lesson, we will break down the definition, identify its three essential components (aṣl, far', ʿilla), and explore the two main methods jurists use to establish the cause: al-dawarān and al-sabr wa al-taqsīm. Understanding these tools is essential for evaluating legal analogies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tamthīl
The text defines tamthīl as 'an inference from a particular to another particular due to a common matter' (استدلال بجزئي على جزئي لأمر مشترك). This sets it apart from syllogism, which is inference from a universal.
The common matter (أمر مشترك) is the basis of the analogy.
2
Extract the three components: aṣl, far', and ʿilla
The first particular is called the 'aṣl' (original case), the second the 'far'' (branch), and the common matter the 'ʿilla' (cause). In juristic qiyās, the ruling from the aṣl is extended to the far' due to the shared ʿilla.
Logicians call the common matter 'jāmiʿ', but jurists use 'ʿilla'.
3
Understand the primary method: al-dawarān (coextensiveness)
Al-dawarān, also called ṭard wa 'aks, is the observation that the ruling exists when the attribute exists and is absent when the attribute is absent. This indicates causality.
It is considered a strong indicator but yields probability (ẓann).
4
Understand the second method: al-sabr wa al-taqsīm (trial and division)
This method, also called tardīd, involves listing all possible attributes for the ruling, then eliminating those that are invalid until one remains, which is then taken as the ʿilla.
It is a process of elimination and also yields probability.
5
Note the scope and authority
The passage states that both methods yield only probable knowledge (ẓann), and the full details belong to uṣūl al-fiqh. This reminds us that analogy is a tool of juristic reasoning, not a demonstrative proof.

Commentary
This passage defines tamthīl (analogy) as an argument that moves from one particular instance to another based on a shared attribute. The jurists call this qiyās (analogical reasoning in fiqh). The first particular is the 'aṣl' (original case), the second is the 'far' (branch), and the shared attribute is the 'ʿilla' (cause). The author refers to uṣūl al-fiqh for a detailed treatment. This is foundational for Islamic legal reasoning, where analogy is used to extend rulings from known cases (e.g., the prohibition of wine) to new cases (e.g., other intoxicants).

A Doubt About Majority and Its Solution
Author presents a logical doubt about applying the majority principle to individuals, then resolves it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve the apparent contradiction in applying the majority principle to individuals by distinguishing the probability of a combination from the probability of its parts.

Core Idea
Probability of each part does not entail probability of the whole—a logical distinction that dissolves the doubt.

This lesson examines a logical doubt about applying the majority principle to individuals in a room with two Muslims and one disbeliever. You will see how an apparent contradiction arises and how careful analysis of what constitutes the 'entailer' dissolves it. This sharpens your understanding of probabilistic reasoning in Islamic legal and theological contexts.

Guided Path
1
Set up the scenario
Imagine three persons in a house: two Muslims and one kafir, but you do not know who is who. Based on the majority principle, each person you see is probably Muslim because most of the group are Muslims.
The 'majority principle' (qā'idat al-aghlabiyya) assigns probability based on the predominant case.
2
Identify the entailment
Notice that Islam of any two specific individuals (the entailer) necessarily entails the kufr of the third (the entailment), given the fixed composition. Therefore, if you are certain that two are Muslim, you become certain that the third is kafir. Similarly, if you have probability (ẓann) that two are Muslim, you should have probability that the third is kafir.
The relation is a conditional: if two are Muslim then the third is kafir.
3
Spot the contradiction
Apply this reasoning to every individual: pick any two persons, and the third becomes probably kafir. Hence, each person is simultaneously probably Muslim (from the majority principle) and probably kafir (from the entailment argument). This appears to be a contradiction.
The contradiction arises because we treat each individual as part of multiple pairs.
4
Apply the solution
The key is that the true entailer is the combination of two individuals being Muslim, not each individually. To deduce the probability of the third being kafir, you need probability that both together are Muslim. But in the majority principle, we only have probability of each separately; that does not give probability of the combination. Hence, the entailment does not hold, and the contradiction disappears.
This is a classic example of why probability of parts does not entail probability of the whole.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between individual and collective probabilistic judgments based on the rule of majority in induction, and identify when the rule is overridden by stronger evidence.

Core Idea
The rule of majority applies to individuals singly, not to the group as a whole.

This lesson examines a subtle but crucial distinction in probabilistic reasoning: the difference between concluding about each individual versus concluding about a collection. The rule states: the judgment of the majority applies to each single individual of that majority. Note that it does not say the judgment applies to any combination of individuals.

The author uses this to show that the rule of majority is not absolute.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the Sullam al-Ulum, continuing the discussion about the impossibility of certain knowledge when there is no cause in existence. The author first explains that if we try to use a middle term (al-mutawassit) that is not a cause, we cannot achieve certainty; and if we make it a cause, we contradict the assumption that there is no cause. He then considers induction (istiqra') as a possible method. Induction could be explained in two ways: either the relation of predicate to particulars is self-evident without a cause, or it has a cause. If self-evident, the evidence comes either from sense or reason. Sense does not yield permanence, so no certainty. Reason is not permissible because the predicate cannot be essential (dhati) in the constitutive sense; if it were accidental, then the accident must be necessary for something essential, implying a general cause, which contradicts the assumption of no cause. Thus induction fails. The passage ends incomplete but the argument is clear: without a cause, neither immediate knowledge nor induction can give certainty.
