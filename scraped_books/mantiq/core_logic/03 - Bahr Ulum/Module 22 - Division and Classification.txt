The Proper Noun View of Science Names
Author critiques opinions on whether names of sciences are proper or generic nouns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn al-Humam's argument that science names are proper nouns and evaluate the objection using the example of sikanjabin.

Core Idea
Science names as proper nouns hinge on the individuality of their instances.

Ibn al-Humam argued that the name of a science (like 'logic') cannot be applied truthfully to each individual issue within that science. Therefore, it must be a proper noun for the entire discipline. However, critics objected using the example of 'sikanjabin' (vinegar), which is a common noun applying to all its instances. In this lesson, we will examine this debate and see how Ibn al-Humam's defender responded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Ibn al-Humam says names of sciences are proper nouns (a'lam ashkhas).
2
Find the reason
Because the science name does not apply to each individual issue (mas'ala) within that science.
3
Understand the objection
The objector gives the example of 'sikanjabin', which is not a proper noun but a common noun for a specific concoction, yet it applies to all individual servings.
4
See the resolution
The defender resolves that the individuals are the 'issues present in minds', and these are multiple by person, so the name applies to each mental issue individually.
5
Evaluate
Does this resolution work? The text says 'tamal' (reflect), indicating it is not fully convincing.

The Generic Noun View and Its Refutation
Author critiques opinions on whether names of sciences are proper or generic nouns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the author considers the generic noun view 'very flimsy' (sakhif jiddan).

Core Idea
Generic nouns are necessary, but science names are acquired through learning.

Some scholars proposed that names of sciences are generic nouns (a'lam ajnas), which refer to a genus. The author dismisses this as 'very flimsy' because generic nouns are self-evident (daruri), while the referents of science names are known only through theoretical study. In this lesson, we will dissect this critique and see the additional evidence from the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1
Understand what generic nouns are
A'lam ajnas are nouns that refer to a genus and are self-evident (like 'human' or 'animal').
2
Why generic nouns are necessary
The author says generic nouns are necessary (daruriyya), meaning their reference is immediately understood.
3
Contrast with science names
Names like 'logic' are not self-evident; one must learn what the science covers. Therefore, they cannot be generic nouns.
4
Additional evidence from ancient speech
The fact that book names (like 'al-Kitab' in the Qur'an) appear with and without the definite article does not prove they are generic, because the definite article can enter proper nouns.
5
Conclusion
The generic noun view is invalid.

The Author's Solution and the Problem of Growth in Sciences
Author critiques opinions on whether names of sciences are proper or generic nouns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author opts for generic nouns despite the problems and identify the issue of applying a single name to an expanding science.

Core Idea
Science names pose a puzzle: they are applied equally to more and less content over time.

Having rejected both extremes, the author leans toward the view that science names are generic nouns, but then raises a significant problem: sciences grow over time, yet the same name is used for both the larger and smaller body of knowledge. How can a single term cover a changing referent? The author suggests two possible solutions — common usage (ishtirak) or general assignment for specific intended meaning (al-wad' al-'amm li al-mawdu' lahu al-khass) — and leaves the matter open for reflection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's choice
The author (al-musannif) chooses that science names are generic nouns because the other two views are invalid.
2
Notice the problem
Sciences increase day by day, and the same name is applied to the increased and the decreased content equally.
3
Proposed solutions
Either the name is assigned to each stage by common usage (ishtirak), which is against the principle of original single assignment, or it is a case of 'general assignment for specific intended meaning' (al-wad' al-'amm li al-mawdu' lahu al-khass).
4
Reflect
The author says 'tamal fihi' (reflect on it) indicating this is a subtle issue.

The Four Mothers of Demands (Ummahat al-Matalib) in Logic
Author defines the four demands and their subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of demands (ما، أي، هل، لم) and classify each as either conceptual or assertoric.

Core Idea
The four demands are the foundational questions that guide all logical inquiry.

In Islamic logic, knowledge is acquired by asking questions. The great logicians organized these questions into four fundamental types, called the 'mothers of demands'. This lesson breaks down each demand and shows how it leads to conceptualization or assent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Notice that the author divides the four demands into two categories: conceptual (تصوري) and assertoric (تصديقي). 'ما' and 'أي' are conceptual; 'هل' and 'لم' are assertoric.
Conceptual demands seek understanding of what something is; assertoric demands seek confirmation of truth.
2
Analyze 'ما' (what)
'ما' demands conceptualization either by way of explaining the name (شرح الاسم) without regard to existence, answered by description (رسم) or definition (حد) as explanatory, or by way of reality (حقيقة) answered by a complete definition or its equivalent.
The text warns against using description for composites, following the chief master (Ibn Sina).
3
Analyze 'أي' (which)
'أي' demands the distinguisher (مميز) that is not said in answer to 'ما هو' in logical terminology. It is answered either by essentialities (ذاتيات) through the proximate or remote differentia, or by accidents through a real or relative property.
In language, 'أي' can ask for any distinguisher, but in logic it is specific.
4
Analyze 'هل' (whether)
'هل' demands assent about the existence of a thing in itself. The text mentions it is called simple (بسيطة), implying there is also a compound form (not covered here).
This demand is foundational for theological and philosophical proofs of existence.
5
Note on 'لم' (why)
The passage cuts off before fully explaining 'لم', but it is listed as the fourth demand. It is assertoric and demands the cause or reason. In later logic, it is used for causal inquiry.
We will encounter 'لم' more fully in later lessons.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the four types of demands (matlab) in logic. The author begins by stating that the demand is either for conceptualization (tasawwur) or assent (tasdiq). The 'mothers of demands' (ummahat al-matalib) are four: ما (what), أي (which), هل (whether), and لم (why). The first two are conceptual demands; the last two are assertoric demands. For 'ما', it requests conceptualization either by way of explaining the name (sharh al-ism) without concern for existence, answered with a definition or description (termed 'shariha' or explanatory) OR by way of reality (haqiqa), answered with a complete definition (hadd tamm) or its equivalent for simple entities. Allowing description for composites contradicts the chief master. For 'أي', it requests the distinguisher (mumayyiz) that is not said in answer to 'ما هو' in this art, though in language it is for any distinguisher. The answer is by essentialities (dhatiyyat) via the proximate differentia (fasl qarib) or remote differentia, or by accidents (awarid) via the real or relative property (khassa). For 'هل', it requests assent about existence, termed 'simple' (basita). The passage cuts off before explaining 'لم' (why) and the second branch of 'هل' (probably compound). This classification is fundamental to Islamic logic as it organizes the types of questions that lead to knowledge.

The Khabar-Insha' Distinction
Author defines insha' as non-narrative utterance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between khabar (informative) and insha' (performative) utterances based on the criterion of intended narration (hikaya) and truth-value.

Core Idea
Insha' utterances perform an action; they do not report a fact and therefore have no truth-value.

The first step in understanding utterance classification in Islamic logic is to distinguish between statements that inform about something (khabar) and those that perform an act (insha'). The author presents this fundamental division by focusing on the intention to narrate (hikaya). Let's explore this key distinction and see why insha' cannot be evaluated as true or false.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining criterion
Read the opening sentence: 'If it is not intended thereby to narrate about something, then it is insha'.' The criterion is the intended narration (hikaya). If narration is intended, it is khabar; if not, it is insha'.
Note the phrase 'as witnessed by consciousness (wijdan)'—this is an appeal to introspection, not a logical deduction.
2
Recognize the misconception
The author rejects the view that insha' could have a subject but simply not narrate about it. He insists that insha' genuinely has no thing narrated about (muhki 'anhu).
This clarifies that insha' is inherently non-referential.
3
Test with examples
Consider 'Stand up!' (command). It does not describe a situation; it creates a request. Similarly, 'Would that it were rain!' (wish) expresses a desire, not a fact. These are insha'. Contrast with 'Zayd stood up,' which reports an event and can be true or false.
4
Apply the truth-value test
Ask: Can this utterance be labeled true or false? If yes, it is khabar. If not, it is insha'. For example, 'Do not steal' (prohibition) cannot be true or false; it is a directive.
This test is a quick heuristic used by later scholars.

Self-Predicable vs. Non-Self-Predicable Concepts
Author classifies concepts into self-predicable and those having their contradictories predicated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given universal concept is self-predicable or has its contradictory predicated, based on the principle of derivation.

Core Idea
A concept is self-predicable if its principle of derivation recurs across all individuals it applies to.

In Islamic logic, concepts are divided based on whether they can be commonly predicated of themselves. This distinction is crucial for understanding how definitions and descriptions work. The author gives examples like 'concept' (self-predicable) and 'particular' (not self-predicable). Let's examine the reasoning behind this classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups
The passage divides concepts into two groups: those predicated of themselves commonly (حملاً شائعًا) and those not, but rather their contradictories are predicated.
2
Extract the defining property
The key property is whether 'the principle subsists in them' (قيام مبدئها بها). For the first group, the principle of derivation recurs (يتكرر نوع مبدئه). For the second, it does not.
The 'principle of derivation' (مبدأ الاشتقاق) is the base meaning from which a term is derived.
3
Analyze the examples
Consider 'concept' (المفهوم): its principle is 'conceptuality', which applies to all concepts, so it is self-predicable. 'General possible' (الممكن العام): its principle is possibility, which applies to all possible things. 'Particular' (الجزئي): its principle is particularity, which does not apply to the universal term 'particular' itself, so it is not self-predicable; instead its contradictory 'concept' is predicated of it.
The passage explicitly states that 'particular' and 'non-concept' have their contradictories predicated.
4
Apply the rule to a new example
Take the concept 'universal' (الكلي). Its principle is universality. Does universality apply to 'universal' itself? Yes, because the term 'universal' is a universal concept. Therefore, 'universal' is self-predicable: we can say 'the universal is a universal'.
This application is not in the passage but follows from the rule given by later scholars.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If a property occurs to something, it also occurs to that from which the derivative is taken, making the derivative predicable.

The later scholars not only stated the rule but also provided a logical justification. This justification hinges on the relationship between a property and its derivation. Let's unpack the reasoning to strengthen your understanding of self-predication.

Guided Path
1
State the rule again
Every universal whose type of principle recurs is self-predicable; otherwise its contradictory is predicated. This is the rule from the passage.
2
Identify the logical justification
The justification is given: 'because the occurrence of a thing to a thing necessitates its occurrence to that from which it is derived, and the occurrence of the principle of derivation to something necessitates the predication of its derivative upon it.'
3

If a property (like 'conceptuality') occurs to something (like the concept 'concept'), then it also occurs to that from which the derivative comes. Here, 'concept' is derived from 'conceptuality', so conceptuality occurs to the concept itself.

If the principle of derivation (conceptuality) occurs to something, then the derivative (concept) is predicated of that thing. This is because the derivative term signifies the thing in relation to that principle.

Therefore, the derivative 'particular' is not predicated of it; rather, its contradictory 'concept' or 'universal' is predicated.

Core Idea

Three Types of Relations Between Sciences
Author classifies relations between sciences based on subject generality and conditioning perspective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of relations between sciences based on subject generality and perspective, and give an example from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A science can be part of another, conditioned differently, or share a subject from distinct perspectives.

In the classification of sciences, scholars distinguish relations between subjects to determine whether one science falls under another. This lesson introduces the three fundamental relations: one science being part of another (species under genus), one being conditioned relative to another (absolute vs. conditioned), and both having different conditions on the same subject. Understanding these relations helps in mapping the Islamic intellectual disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first relation: part-whole (species under genus)
The author states that one science can be a part of another if its subject is a species of the other's subject. For example, the science of fiqh (jurisprudence) studies actions of the mukallaf from the perspective of rulings, which could be considered a species of the broader science of human actions under sharia. Thus fiqh is under the broader science of sharia ends.
This is the classic hierarchy where the more specific science is 'under' the more general.
2
Identify the second relation: absolute vs. conditioned subject
One subject is taken absolutely (unconditioned) and the other with a condition. Example: the science of 'spheres' (celestial bodies) in general vs. the science of 'moving spheres' (which are conditioned by motion). The conditioned science is more specific and falls under the absolute science.
The conditioning adds a restriction that makes the subject more specific.
3
Identify the third relation: same subject, different perspectives (different conditions)
Two sciences study the same subject but from different perspectives (haythiyya). Example: physics (al-‘ilm al-ṭabī‘ī) studies the celestial sphere as a principle of change, while astronomy (al-hay’a) studies it as a magnitude. Since the perspectives are different, the sciences are distinct and neither is under the other simply by generality.
These different perspectives necessitate separate sciences even though the subject is the same.

The Role of Perspective (Haythiyya) in Defining Scientific Boundaries
Author explains how perspective determines scientific hierarchy and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a science is 'under' another by analyzing the perspective (haythiyya) of its subject and testing if that perspective, when isolated, belongs to another science.

Core Idea
A science is under another if its perspective, when isolated, becomes the subject of that other science.

Not only subject generality but also the specific perspective from which a subject is studied determines a science's place in the hierarchy. The author explains that if the perspective itself can be investigated as a separate subject, then the original science is under that other science. We will explore this with the examples of music and astronomy, and also examine the condition that the perspective must be restrictive (taqyīdiyya) rather than an extrinsic accident (ʿaraḍ gharīb).

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of perspective (haythiyya)
Every science takes its subject under a specific 'haythiyya' or perspective. For example, music studies the note from the perspective of numerical composition; the celestial sphere may be studied from the perspective of change (physics) or magnitude (astronomy). The perspective restricts the subject and defines what attributes are investigated.
2
Test if the perspective can be isolated to become a subject of another science
Ask: If we isolate this perspective (e.g., numerical composition), does it become a subject of another science? If yes, the original science is under that other science. The author gives the example of music: its perspective of numerical composition is part of arithmetic, so music is under arithmetic. Similarly, astronomy's perspective of magnitude, if isolated, is geometry, so astronomy is under geometry.
This rule overrides the simple generality hierarchy: music is not under physics despite notes being physical because the perspective is arithmetical.
3
Distinguish between restrictive and non-restrictive perspectives
The author warns that the perspective must be restrictive (taqyīdiyya) and not an extrinsic accidental attribute (ʿaraḍ gharīb). If the perspective were an extrinsic accident without restriction, the attributes following from it would not be essential to the subject, making investigation unsound. Only when taken restrictively does the conditioned subject become the foundation for essential attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of the relationships between different sciences based on their subjects (موضوعات) and the 'whereness' (حيثية) or perspective from which each subject is considered. The author, in this conclusion of the book, aims to clarify when one science can be said to be 'under' (تحت) another, either as a part or as a subordinate science.

The author first identifies three types of relations. The first is when one science's subject is a part of the other's subject (i.e., one is a species of the other's genus). For example, if one science studies a specific type of thing within a broader category, it is 'under' the broader science. The second relation is when one subject is absolute (unconditioned) and the other is conditioned (محين), such as the science of spheres in general versus the science of moving spheres. The third is when both are conditioned but with different conditions, like the science of the heavens and the world (physics) and astronomy (الهيئة); both study the celestial sphere but from different perspectives: physics looks at it as a principle of change, astronomy as having magnitude. These differences necessitate the sciences being distinct.

Then the author explains when a science is 'under' another based on the perspective. If the perspective itself, if isolated, would be the subject of another science, then the original science is under that other science. For instance, music studies notes from the perspective of numerical composition; if isolated, that perspective is arithmetic, so music is under arithmetic, not under physics. Similarly, astronomy's consideration of magnitude, if isolated, is geometry, so astronomy is under geometry, not physics.

The author summarizes: The difference between sciences does not lie merely in the subject but in the perspective. If a science's subject is a species of another science's subject without an additional perspective, it is a part of that science. If the subject is more specific (أخص) either as a species with an extra perspective or not a species at all, and the perspective does not lead to another science, then it is under that science. Also, if the perspective itself becomes the subject of another science when isolated, it is under that science.

Next, the author clarifies that the perspectives taken in the subjects must not be extrinsic accidental qualities (أعراض غريبة) without being restrictive (تقييدية). If they were not restrictive, they would not belong to the science's investigation because the subject is assumed known. The demonstration in a science relies on its specific perspective. For example, the proposition 'the sky is a sphere' can be proven by both physics and astronomy but with different demonstrations: one considering nature, the other magnitude. If the perspective were an extrinsic accident without restriction, the attributes following from it would not be essential to the subject, making investigation invalid. But if taken restrictively, the conditioned subject becomes the foundation.

The passage ends with a pious phrase acknowledging that true knowledge belongs to God.

The examples from the Islamic sciences would be like: The science of fiqh (jurisprudence) and usul al-fiqh (legal theory) — both study actions of the mukallaf but from different perspectives (fiqh from perspective of ruling, usul from perspective of evidence). Or kalam (theology) and philosophy — both study existence but from different principles.
