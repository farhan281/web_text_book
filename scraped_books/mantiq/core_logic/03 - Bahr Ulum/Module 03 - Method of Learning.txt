Al-Subki's Conditions for Studying Logic
Author presents al-Subki's ruling on logic and defends al-Ghazali and al-Razi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Subki's conditions for studying logic and apply them to evaluate the permissibility of studying logic for a given level of religious knowledge.

Core Idea
Logic is forbidden for the unprepared but permissible for the qualified—under strict conditions to protect faith.

This passage presents Taj al-Din al-Subki's nuanced ruling on the study of logic. He distinguishes between those who are firmly grounded in the Islamic sciences—who may study logic to refute falsehoods—and those who are not, for whom it is forbidden. The author defends al-Ghazali and al-Razi, arguing that their engagement with philosophy was legitimate because of their established religious authority. We will analyze al-Subki's conditions and the rationale behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Subki's ruling on logic
Find the part where al-Subki states 'As for logic... it is forbidden for one whose foundations of Shari'a are not firmly rooted...' and 'But as for one who has reached this station, he may examine it... under two conditions.' Note the absolute prohibition for the unprepared and the conditional permission for the advanced.
2
Extract the prerequisites for studying logic
List what al-Subki requires before studying logic: firm root in Shari'a, filled with prophetic greatness, memorization of Quran and much hadith, knowledge of fiqh to the level of a mufti. This shows the high bar.
3
Identify the two conditions for the advanced scholar
First, confidence in one's faith being unshakable by doubts. Second, not mixing the speech of logicians with Islamic scholarship. Understand why mixing is harmful (example of al-Tusi).
4
Apply al-Subki's rule to al-Ghazali and al-Razi
Note the author's defense: they only engaged after becoming imams. So they meet the conditions. Contrast with a hypothetical student who has not reached that level.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary discussing the classification of Islamic sciences and the permissibility of studying logic. The author first divides the religious sciences into three: fiqh (jurisprudence), usul al-din (principles of religion), and tasawwuf (spiritual purification). He asserts that any other science either reduces to these three or is outside the Shari'a. He then raises an objection: why exclude tafsir and hadith and include usul al-din and tasawwuf, especially since jurists often exclude the mutakallim from the label 'scholar'? The answer is that valid mutakallimin and Sufis are indeed included as scholars if they abide by the laws of Shari'a, as the Shaykh Imam stated in Sharh al-Minhaj. The author then laments the current intellectual climate, noting that the wish of al-Taqi al-Subki to return to the pristine atmosphere of the Salaf is impractical due to the proliferation of sects and innovations. He poses a threefold question: should we ignore the intellectual sciences, open them to everyone, or adopt a middle path? He then quotes Taj al-Din al-Subki's ruling on logic: it is haram for those not firmly grounded in Shari'a, memorization of Quran and hadith, and knowledge of fiqh. However, for those who have reached a high level of religious mastery, it is permissible under two conditions: 1) confidence that one's faith is unshakable by doubts and falsehoods, and 2) strict separation of philosophical discourse from Islamic discourse, avoiding mixing. The author notes that the harm from mixing is great, as seen in the time of Nasir al-Din al-Tusi. Finally, he defends al-Ghazali and al-Razi, arguing that they only engaged with philosophy after becoming undisputed leaders in religion, so their mixing is not a precedent for others.

Gradual Development of Rational Sciences
Historical development of rational sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the two-phase development of rational sciences in Islam (translation vs. production) and identify regional variations.

Core Idea
Islamic rational sciences were not imported wholesale; they developed gradually and regionally.

This passage provides a historical framework for understanding how Muslims engaged with rational sciences. Rather than a sudden adoption, the author describes a process from translation to independent production, varying between East and West.

This underscores that there was no uniform Islamic experience.
3
Reflect on significance

The author defends the authenticity of Islamic intellectual contributions.

Commentary
This passage serves as an introductory historical overview, defending the use of rational sciences in Islam and tracing their development and influence. The author begins by refuting a criticism: that the Prophet's concession at al-Hudaybiyya (erasing his title) and the imams' use of the term 'Proof of the Maker' are not blameworthy, as similar concessions are found in earlier scriptures. Then, the second section explicitly discusses the development of rational sciences. The author states that this development was gradual and varied by region (East vs. West, Arab vs. non-Arab). In the 2nd-3rd centuries AH, Muslims were occupied with translation; in the 4th century, they moved to independent production. Al-Farabi is highlighted as the first systematic philosopher ('Second Teacher'), who viewed logic not just as a tool but as part of philosophy, proceeding from word to proposition to syllogism. The author notes that false attributions to al-Farabi (like belief in the world's eternity) are not universally accepted. The passage then explains how logic influenced linguistics: the Basran grammarians adopted a logical approach, influenced by philosophical schools present in Basra, and this led to debates on the origin of language. Finally, the author extends this to usul al-fiqh, noting that works like al-Ghazali's al-Mustasfa and Ibn al-Hajib's Mukhtasar al-Muntaha are filled with logical discussions. Even fiqh itself relies heavily on qiyas (analogical reasoning), which, as developed by usul scholars, depends on detailed logic and philosophy. The overall argument is that rational sciences are integral to Islam and have deeply shaped its core disciplines.

Al-Farabi's Integration of Logic into Philosophy
Al-Farabi's conception of logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Farabi's view of logic as both a law of expression and part of philosophy, contrasting it with the view of logic as a mere tool.

Core Idea
Al-Farabi elevated logic from a mere tool to an integral part of philosophy.

Al-Farabi, known as the Second Teacher, transformed the status of logic in Islamic thought. He argued that logic is not just a methodological instrument but a part of philosophy itself, governing the progression from words to propositions to syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Extract al-Farabi's definition of logic
Find in the passage the phrase: 'a law for expression in the language of the human intellect among all nations'. This shows logic is universal and foundational.
2
Note the structural progression
Al-Farabi says logic proceeds from the simplest elements to the most complex: from word (kalima) to proposition (qadiyya) to syllogism (qiyas). This is a systematic order.
This mirrors the structure of many later logic texts.
3
Understand the implication
By seeing logic as part of philosophy, not merely a tool, al-Farabi asserts that doing philosophy properly requires mastering logic. Logic is constitutive of philosophical reasoning.
Contrast with earlier Greek-influenced views that treated logic as an organon (tool).

The development in the history of rational sciences in Islam did not occur all at once or by chance; rather, this development occurred in different stages, just as it varied in degrees within the Islamic world; the East differs from the West, and the matter varies between Arabs and non-Arabs. It can be said in general that if Muslims in the second and third centuries AH were occupied with translating foreign sciences and understanding them, then in the fourth century they were studying by themselves and for themselves, and they moved from collection and acquisition to production. And al-Farabi Abu Nasr is considered the first to undertake the task of building the philosophical sciences in the history of Islam, and he is the second teacher. He viewed logic as 'a law for expression in the language of the human intellect among all nations, and it must proceed from the simplest elements of speech to the most complex: from the word to the proposition to the syllogism', and he saw that logic is a part of philosophy, not merely a tool for it. And attributing many false statements—such as the eternity of the world itself—to him is not a matter of consensus; rather, among researchers there are those who see that none of the Muslims reached in knowledge of its origination what was affirmed by Plato and Aristotle, and similarly regarding God's knowledge of particulars. The truth is that intellectual activity was not confined to philosophy and logic only, but it even reached the science of language, and a whole current—the Basran school—became a logical current, distinct from the Kufan school. It is not hidden from the observer that the influence of philosophical schools appeared in Basra before its appearance elsewhere, and among the Basran grammarians were many Shi'a and Mu'tazilites, who paved the way for foreign philosophy. And it is also not hidden from the observer the disagreement about the origin of language: whether it is by divine institution or by convention of a positor—a dispute whose roots extend to philosophical influence. And the case of the science of principles—usul al-fiqh—is no different from what we have mentioned; the volumes of usul books are filled with opinions and discussions related to rational and logical sciences. Among the most prominent examples of that are the introduction of al-Mustasfa by Imam al-Ghazali and the introduction of Mukhtasar al-Muntaha al-Usuli by Ibn al-Hajib. And fiqh also could not escape the grasp of this influence—how could it, when half of fiqh is qiyas, if not all? And qiyas—in the form it developed at the hands of usul scholars—relies on the finest details of logic and philosophy.

Commentary
This passage serves as an introductory historical overview, defending the use of rational sciences in Islam and tracing their development and influence. The author begins by refuting a criticism: that the Prophet's concession at al-Hudaybiyya (erasing his title) and the imams' use of the term 'Proof of the Maker' are not blameworthy, as similar concessions are found in earlier scriptures. Then, the second section explicitly discusses the development of rational sciences. The author states that this development was gradual and varied by region (East vs. West, Arab vs. non-Arab). In the 2nd-3rd centuries AH, Muslims were occupied with translation; in the 4th century, they moved to independent production. Al-Farabi is highlighted as the first systematic philosopher ('Second Teacher'), who viewed logic not just as a tool but as part of philosophy, proceeding from word to proposition to syllogism. The author notes that false attributions to al-Farabi (like belief in the world's eternity) are not universally accepted. The passage then explains how logic influenced linguistics: the Basran grammarians adopted a logical approach, influenced by philosophical schools present in Basra, and this led to debates on the origin of language. Finally, the author extends this to usul al-fiqh, noting that works like al-Ghazali's al-Mustasfa and Ibn al-Hajib's Mukhtasar al-Muntaha are filled with logical discussions. Even fiqh itself relies heavily on qiyas (analogical reasoning), which, as developed by usul scholars, depends on detailed logic and philosophy. The overall argument is that rational sciences are integral to Islam and have deeply shaped its core disciplines.

Logic's Influence on Arabic Grammar
Influence of logic on grammar (Basran vs Kufan)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how logical thinking penetrated Arabic grammar, distinguishing the Basran logical approach from the Kufan traditional approach.

Core Idea
The Basran school of grammar became a logical school, shaped by philosophical influences.

The author reveals that logical influence extended beyond philosophy to linguistics. The Basran grammarians, many of whom were Shi'a or Mu'tazilites, adopted a quasi-logical methodology, differentiating them from the Kufan school. This also gave rise to debates on the origin of language (divine or conventional), a question with deep philosophical roots.

Guided Path

Identify the two grammar schools

The Kufan school was more traditional and relied on direct transmission (sam').

The Basran logical approach likely favored the conventionalist view due to its rationalist underpinnings.

Qiyas as the Logical Backbone of Fiqh
The logical foundations of qiyas in fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how qiyas (analogical reasoning) in fiqh is fundamentally dependent on logical and philosophical principles.

Core Idea
Qiyas is not just a juristic tool; it is half of fiqh and relies on logical theory.

The author argues that fiqh could not escape the influence of logic because qiyas is central to it. The qiyas of usul al-fiqh, as refined by scholars, incorporates the finest details of logic and philosophy. This lesson explores that connection.

Guided Path
1
Note the claim about qiyas in fiqh
The passage states: 'half of fiqh is qiyas, if not all'. This shows the central importance of analogical reasoning in Islamic jurisprudence.
2
Understand the structure of qiyas
Qiyas involves moving from an original case (asl) with a known ruling to a new case (far') based on a shared effective cause ('illa). This process requires logical reasoning to correctly identify the 'illa and apply it.
The logical conditions for a valid qiyas are detailed in usul al-fiqh.
3
Connect to examples from usul texts
The passage names al-Ghazali's al-Mustasfa and Ibn al-Hajib's Mukhtasar al-Muntaha as works that integrate logic into usul. Look up these introductions to see how they use logical concepts to formulate the theory of qiyas.
These texts exemplify the fusion of logic and jurisprudence.

Convention of Using ج and ب in Logical Expressions
Convention of using ج and ب in logical schemata.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the roles of the placeholders ج and ب in logical schemata and explain why logicians abstract from matter.

Core Idea
ج and ب are placeholders for subject and predicate, allowing logic to be applied without distraction.

In Islamic logic, letters are used as symbols to represent terms in propositions. This lesson examines the standard convention of using ج for the subject and ب for the predicate, akin to the disconnected letters of the Quran. Understanding this convention is essential for reading logical forms and applying rules uniformly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the placeholder for subject and predicate
Note that the letter ج (jīm) represents the subject (al-mawḍūʿ) and ب (bāʾ) represents the predicate (al-maḥmūl). These are not words but placeholders.
This is a standard convention across the tradition.
2
Understand the pronunciation as a composite name
Logicians pronounce ج and ب together as a single compound word (e.g., 'ج ب' as 'jīm bā''), similar to the disconnected letters in the Quran like 'الم'.
This helps in reciting logical forms smoothly.
3
Recognize the purpose of abstraction from matter
When analyzing propositions, logicians abstract from specific content (matter) to avoid the illusion that the rule applies only to that case. This allows universal application of logical rules.
For example, instead of saying 'Every human is mortal', they say 'Every ج is ب'.
4
Apply to the universal affirmative proposition
The universal affirmative is expressed as 'Every ج is ب' (كل ج ب). This form is used as a template for analyzing properties like truth conditions and conversion.

Commentary
This passage from the conclusion of Bahr Ulum defines the structure of masail (questions/propositions) that constitute the parts of any science. It begins by noting that this topic properly belongs in the art of demonstration (burhan). The core definition from the Shaykh (Ibn Sina) is that masail are propositions whose predicates are essential accidents (arad dhatiyya) of the subject of that science, or of its types, or of its accidents. The passage then expands on the possible relationships between the subject of a particular mas'ala and the overall subject of the science. The subject of a mas'ala can be (a) the subject of the science itself, (b) a type of that subject, (c) an essential accident of that subject, (d) a type of that essential accident, or (e) an accident of an essential accident. Each scenario is illustrated with examples from natural science: e.g., 'Does the body divide indefinitely?' has the subject of natural science (body) as its subject; 'Does air trapped in water rise?' has a type of body as subject; 'Is this motion contrary to that motion?' involves an essential accident (motion) of body. The passage then quotes Ibn Sina further on how the predicates of masail relate to their subjects depending on whether the subject is from the subject of the science or from its accidents. Finally, the author concludes that research in sciences is not restricted to the essential accidents of the subject; it can also involve 'extraneous accidents' (al-a'rad al-ghariba) provided they are essential accidents of the type, part, or accident of the subject. This clarifies that the scope of scientific inquiry is broader than commonly thought, but still constrained by the subject matter of the science.

The Flexibility of Scientific Research: Beyond Essential Accidents
Author expands the scope of scientific research beyond essential accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that scientific research can involve extraneous accidents as long as they are essential accidents of related items, and identify the conditions under which such research is permissible.

Core Idea
Scientific inquiry is not confined to essential accidents of the subject; it can extend to other items under precise conditions.

After presenting Ibn Sina's classification, the author offers his own reflection, emphasizing that research in the sciences is not limited to the essential accidents of the subject alone. He explains that predicates can be extraneous accidents (al-a'rad al-ghariba) provided they are essential accidents of the type, part, or accident of the subject. This nuanced understanding broadens the scope of what can be studied within a given science.

The author begins with an example: time is an accident of motion, which itself is an essential accident of body. Critique of the Restrictive View
Author critiques attempts to restrict scientific investigation to essential accidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two major attempts to defend the view that sciences only investigate essential accidents and explain why the author finds them artificial.

Core Idea
Defenses of the restrictive view rely on artificial distinctions that do not withstand scrutiny.

After establishing that scientific research is broader, the author addresses attempts to maintain the restrictive view that only essential accidents are investigated. He critiques two main defenses: the distinction between predicate of the problem and predicate of the science, and the idea that the subject is the nature as such. Understanding these critiques sharpens your ability to evaluate logical claims.

Guided Path
1
State the restrictive view being defended
Some logicians insist that sciences only investigate essential accidents of the subject of the science. This is the view the author has already shown to be a simplification.
2
First defense: predicate of the problem vs. predicate of the science
They argue that the predicate of the science is an essential accident of the indeterminate subject (al-mawdu' al-mafhum al-murradad), while problems investigate parts of it. The author finds this artificial because it introduces an unnecessary distinction.
The 'indeterminate subject' is a conceptual construct that unifies the science.
3
Second defense: the subject is the nature pervading individuals
Another group claims the subject is the nature (tabi'a) in terms of pervading individuals, so essential accidents of a species are considered essential accidents of the genus that pervades it. The author notes that this does not hold for all cases and involves artificiality.
This tries to extend the notion of 'essential' from the genus to the species.
4
Third view (weaker): the subject is the nature as such (fourth level)
Some say the subject is the nature as such, without regard to universality (the fourth level, la bi-shart). The author calls this a wonder, implying it is even less plausible.
This level is an invention that is not commonly accepted.
5
Evaluate the author's refutation
The author shows that each attempt has artificiality or inconsistency. The common thread is that all these defenses try to preserve the restrictive claim by redefining concepts, but they do not match the actual practice of sciences.
The author's conclusion reinforces that the original expansive view is correct.

Four Objections to the Fourth Level as Subject of Science
Author critiques the fourth level as subject

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the four objections the author raises against the fourth level of abstraction (la bi-shart) as the subject of a science.

Core Idea
The fourth level of abstraction is too broad and non-actual to ground essential attributes.

In this passage, the author targets a specific position: that the subject of a science is the fourth level of abstraction (la bi-shart). He dismantles this view with a series of incisive objections. Understanding these objections is crucial for grasping the correct definition of a science's subject matter and avoiding conceptual confusion in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the views on the subject of a science and its essential accidents. The author begins by stating that every science deals with essential accidents. He then critiques the position that the obligation is to investigate the essential accidents of the subject, calling it a leniency. He presents two attempts by those who insisted on exclusivity: first, a distinction between the predicate of a problem and the predicate of a science, which involves artificiality. Second, the view that the subject is the nature diffusing among individuals. The author then focuses on the most problematic view: that the subject is the fourth level (la bi-shart), invented by some. He raises four objections: 1) The fourth level does not actually exist. 2) Even if it existed, it is more general than the mixed, so attributes of the specific cannot be essential to it. 3) Lack of exclusivity does not negate essentialness. 4) If the subject itself is an accident or its genus, the predicate could be the genus, contradicting the claim that it is an essential accident. He concludes that if taken as unconditioned, it is constitutive, not accidental.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on how sciences are differentiated by their subjects. The author emphasizes that these three divisions necessitate difference of sciences. The use of examples from the Islamic sciences (geometry, arithmetic, medicine, natural science) shows the practical application of this logical classification.

How Sciences Differ: Complete Difference, Generality, and Aspects
Author classifies the ways sciences differ by their subject matters and aspects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the relationship between any two sciences as differing by complete difference, generality-specificity, or aspect, based on their subject matters.

Core Idea
Sciences are distinguished not just by their subject, but by the aspect under which that subject is studied.

The author of Bahr al-Ulum explains that the differences between sciences arise from differences in their subject matters (mawdu'at). These differences are of three kinds: complete difference (tabayun), generality and specificity (umum wa khhusus), and aspects (haythiyyat). Understanding these distinctions helps us see how disciplines relate hierarchically and why some are considered parts of others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of subject difference
The author enumerates three primary types: tabayun (complete difference), umum/khhusus (general-specific), and haythiyyat (aspectual difference).
2
Analyze the first type: complete difference
When subjects are completely different and do not share an aspect of inquiry, sciences differ absolutely. Example: geometry (subject: magnitude) and arithmetic (subject: number).
3
Analyze the second type: generality-specificity
Cases where one subject is a species of the other, with or without an additional aspect. Note if the aspect is an essential accident (arad dhati) or a foreign accident (arad gharib). Examples: astronomy vs. physics (no aspect), medicine vs. physics (essential accident), optics vs. geometry (foreign accident).
4
Analyze the third type: aspectual difference
Either one subject is taken absolutely and the other qualified by an aspect, or both are qualified by different aspects. Example: astronomy vs. configuration (sphere studied as principle of change vs. having magnitude).

Subordination of Sciences: How Aspects Determine Hierarchies
Author classifies the ways sciences differ by their subject matters and aspects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given science is subordinate to another science based on the aspect of its subject matter.

Core Idea
A science is under another if its subject's aspect, when isolated, falls under that other science's subject.

The passage goes beyond mere classification to explain how sciences can be hierarchically arranged. Every science's subject is taken with a specific aspect. If that aspect, when abstracted, would itself be the subject of another science, then the original science is subordinate to that other one. For example, music falls under arithmetic because its aspect (numerical composition) is a subject of arithmetic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the aspect of the science in question
For a given science, find the aspect (haythiyya) under which its subject is studied. Example: music studies the note under the aspect of numerical composition.
2
Abstract that aspect
Imagine the aspect alone, without the specific subject. For music, the aspect is 'numerical composition'.
3
Check if that aspect is the subject of another science
If the abstracted aspect is itself studied in a higher science, then the original science is under that higher science. For music, numerical composition is studied in arithmetic, so music is under arithmetic.
4
Apply the rule to other examples
Test with optics (moving sphere under aspect of light) and geometry (studies magnitude). The aspect of light is not a subject of geometry, but the moving sphere's magnitude aspect is, so optics is under geometry.
