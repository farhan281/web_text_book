The Logical Variables ج and ب in Quantified Propositions
Lists chapter and section headings for topics on propositions, syllogisms, and logical theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the logical variables ج (subject) and ب (predicate) in quantified propositions as used in Arabic mantiq texts.

Core Idea
ج and ب are shorthand for subject and predicate — the skeleton of every qadiyya.

In the margins of quantified propositions, logicians use ج for the subject term and ب for the predicate term. This notation, introduced in the table of contents, is essential for reading formal logical expressions in Arabic. This lesson unpacks what ج and ب mean and how they are used in quantified propositions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the notation
The table says 'تعبيرهم عن الموضوع بـ (ج) وعن المحمول بـ(ب)'. So ج represents the subject (الموضوع) and ب represents the predicate (المحمول). This is a standard convention in Arabic logic texts.
2
Understand the quantifier scope
In quantified propositions, the quantifier (سور) applies to the subject term ج. For example, 'كل ج ب' means 'Every subject is a predicate' (universal affirmative). The quantifier directly modifies ج.
The fihris later specifies that 'المراد بـ(ج) هو ذات الموضوع' (What is meant by J is the essence of the subject).
3
Note the philosophical nuance
The table indicates a discussion on page 383-384 about whether ج refers to the essence or the individuals (disagreement between al-Farabi and Ibn Sina). This affects how we interpret quantified propositions.
4
Apply to examples
Practice reading propositions in ج/ب notation. For instance, 'لا شيء من ج هو ب' represents a universal negative: 'No subject is a predicate' (e.g., 'No human is stone').
The negation and quantifier can vary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five logical arts and explain the purpose of each in the Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
The five arts—demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, sophistry—define the spectrum of logical discourse from certainty to deception.

This passage from the Bahr Ulum commentary presents the table of contents of the work, outlining the logical arts that will be discussed. Understanding this classification is essential because it shows how Islamic logicians organized the field: from the highest epistemic certainty (demonstration) to the lowest forms of argument (sophistry). Each art has a specific function in reasoning and persuasion, and they are all treated as part of the science of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five arts listed
Look at the Arabic headings: البرهان (demonstration), الجدل (dialectic), الخطابة (rhetoric), الشعر (poetry), السفسطة (sophistry). These are the five logical arts.
2
Understand the two types of demonstration
Note that burhan is split into limmi (a priori) and inni (a posteriori). Limmi proceeds from cause to effect; inni from effect to cause.
3
Recognize additional topics
The passage also mentions causes of error and methods of salvation from these errors, plus a conclusion on parts of sciences. These are supplementary to the five arts.
4
Apply the classification
Think of real examples from Islamic sciences for each art. For instance, a proof for God's existence in kalam is often a demonstration (burhan). Disputes in usul al-fiqh may use dialectic (jadal). A preacher's sermon uses rhetoric (khataba). A poetic verse may use imaginative propositions (shi'r). A fallacious argument in a debate is sophistry (safsata).

How to Read Front Matter in Islamic Manuscripts
Publisher's catalog listing two works related to Ibn al-Hajib.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a classical Islamic manuscript's front matter and determine the author, title, and relationship between multiple works.

Core Idea
Front matter is the doorway to the manuscript's scholarly identity.

This lesson takes you through a brief front matter entry from a publisher's catalog. It lists two works related to the famous grammarian Ibn al-Hajib. By learning to parse such entries, you will gain the skills to quickly orient yourself within the vast landscape of Islamic scholarly literature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main work title
Look for the primary title phrase, usually the first name mentioned. Here it is 'Talbiat al-Talib' which is followed by a description of its subject.
The title often contains 'fī' (on) or 'li' (for) indicating the topic.
2
Identify the author
Find the author's name, usually introduced by 'ta'līf' (authored by). Here it is Muhammad ibn Abd al-Salam al-Amawi, a scholar from the 9th century AH.
Sometimes the author is mentioned after a colon or in the next phrase.
3
Identify any additional work
Look for 'wa yalīhi' (and followed by) to see if another work is bound with this one. Here 'al-I'lam' is mentioned as a second work on the meanings of names and proper nouns in Ibn al-Hajib's writings.
Multiple works are often collected in one manuscript.
4
Note historical context
Pay attention to any date or era mentioned. The text says the author is from the ninth century AH, which helps place the work chronologically.
Centuries are often given as 'qarn al-tāsi' (ninth century).
5
Synthesize the relationship
Combine all information: Both works are about understanding Ibn al-Hajib's terminology. The first is a general lexicon, the second focuses on proper names. The author is later than Ibn al-Hajib, indicating a commentary tradition.
This helps you understand the work's place in the scholarly tradition.
