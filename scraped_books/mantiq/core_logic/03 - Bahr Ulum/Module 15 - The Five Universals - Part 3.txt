Natural vs. Absolute Universal: Conditions of Consideration
Author clarifies that the natural universal is more general than the absolute universal and refutes the division into self and other.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the natural universal from the absolute universal by identifying where the condition (haythiyya) applies — to the quiddity or to the consideration.

Core Idea
The natural universal is more general because its condition restricts the consideration itself, not the quiddity.

In this passage, the author addresses a potential confusion: that the absolute and natural universals are identical, which would make their distinction self-defeating. He clarifies that the natural universal is more general because the condition (haythiyya) differs. We will explore how this distinction is maintained and how al-Qushji resolves the issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two universals
Locate the terms 'al-mutlaqa' (absolute universal) and 'al-tabi'i' (natural universal) in the Arabic text. Note that the author is comparing them.
The absolute universal is the quiddity considered without any condition, while the natural universal involves a condition on the consideration itself.
2
Understand the condition (haythiyya) in each
In the absolute universal, the condition is a restriction of the quiddity within the consideration (al-haythiyya qayd lil-mahiyya fi al-lihaz). In the natural universal, the condition is a restriction of the consideration itself (qayd lil-lihaz).
This means the natural universal applies to the absolute and its subdivisions because the condition is on the act of considering, not on what is considered.
3
Recognize why the natural is more general
The text states 'the natural is more general in consideration than the absolute' (al-tabi'i a'amm bi i'tibar min al-mutlaqa). Because the natural universal's condition is on the consideration, it encompasses the absolute and the two subdivisions (abstract and mixed).
4
Examine the potential misunderstanding
The author presents a hypothetical objection: if absolute and natural were the same, dividing the universal into natural and absolute would be self-division (taqsim al-shay' ila nafsihi wa ila ghayrihi). The clarification avoids this error.
5
Consider al-Qushji's resolution
Al-Qushji asserts that this is not a division (laysa taqsiman) but different considerations (i'tibarat) of one quiddity: abstract (la bi-shart), conditioned (bi-shart shay'), and absolute (mutlaq). This reframes the relationship as aspects, not separate species.

The Nature of the Universal and the Subject of Logic
Author refines the understanding of universals and logic's subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of al-Tusi and the author on the existence of the universal and explain why the author affirms the natural universal externally.

Core Idea
The universal is not merely mental; it exists externally as a common nature.

This passage debates whether the universal exists only in the mind or also in external reality. Al-Tusi argued that universality is a mental construct, while the author insists on the external existence of the natural universal. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying logical concepts to Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim about logic's subject
The author states directly that the subject matter of logic is secondary intelligibles. Note this claim and understand that it means logic studies concepts like universality and particularity that apply to primary concepts.
Secondary intelligibles are concepts about concepts, not about external objects directly.
2
Extract the positions of al-Khwansari and al-Tusi
Al-Khwansari says the mental image is particular and the essence itself participates. Al-Tusi argues humanity in individuals is numerically different, and the universal exists only in the mind. Summarize each view in your own words.
3
Analyze the author's criticism of al-Tusi
The author rejects al-Tusi's claim that the universal is only mental. He argues that humanity exists externally as one in kind, with difference only by individuation. Identify the key phrase: 'That one which is in Zayd is completed in 'Amr.'
The author relies on the concept of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) which is one in kind and exists in individuals.
4
Assess the author's argument against multiplicity only
The author rejects the idea that only multiplicity exists externally. He says if that were so, unity and multiplicity would occur on one subject, which is absurd. Understand why he thinks this entails that one species would become multiple species.
This step requires understanding the relationship between the universal nature and its individuals.

Al-Tusi vs. The Author: Two Views on the Nature of Universals
Author critiques al-Tusi's view on universals and defends the natural universal's external existence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The natural universal exists externally as a specific unity, not merely in the mind.

This passage presents a critical exchange on the nature of universals. Al-Tusi argues that the universal humanness exists only in the mind, while the author insists it is real and external. Understanding this disagreement is crucial for grasping the metaphysical foundations of logic and its application in Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the nature of universals, specifically responding to Nasir al-Din al-Tusi's view. The author begins by quoting al-Tusi's claim that the humanness in Zayd is not the same as in Amr, and that the universal humanness that encompasses both exists only in the intellect. Al-Tusi argues that the form of humanness in the mind is neither universal nor particular in itself; it is particular as an individual mental image and universal in its relation to many individuals. The author then critiques this position. He asserts that humanness, as a specific unity, exists externally with the very existence of individuals, and the proofs for the natural universal confirm this. The 'one' in Zayd is completed in Amr; the difference is only through individuation, which is additional to the reality. The author refutes the notion that only multiplicity exists externally and that the intellect constructs a universal. He argues that if someone says a thing exists in individuals and would be multiple there but one in the mind, that leads to a contradiction of unity and multiplicity on one subject. Alternatively, if they deny external existence of natures, that contradicts the presupposition. The author then defends that one thing by specific unity can exist in multiple individuals without impossible location, since numerical oneness is not required. Finally, he concludes that universality, if defined as commonality among many, is an accident of the essence itself, not extending to individuals. This refutes the claim that the subject of logic is only secondary intelligibles; rather it includes essences and their non-extending concomitants. Commentary
This passage is the beginning of a chapter on the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, accident). The author first explains why the logical universal (kulli mantiqi) is not found in the external world: it is a second intelligible, meaning a mental concept. However, the author notes that the real reason for its non-existence is not that it is a second intelligible, but that it is abstractive (intiza'i). Then he discusses the intellectual universal (kulli 'aqli), which is a composite of the logical and natural universals. If the logical universal does not exist externally, the intellectual universal also does not exist externally, because it is composed of the logical and natural. Then he turns to the natural universal (kulli tabi'i), about which there is disagreement. The position of the verifiers (muhaqqiqin), including Ibn Sina (al-Shaykh al-Ra'is), is that the natural universal exists in the external world identically with the existence of individuals. That is, the universal nature (e.g., humanity) is the same as the individuals' existence, but considered in itself. The author then explains that among those who affirm the natural universal's external existence, there are two views on how individuation relates to the essence: (1) individuation is an additional thing annexed to the essence (which the author rejects as false because it would require the essence to be prior to its determination), and (2) individuation is united with the essence like the union of genus with species (the position of al-Iji, author of al-Mawaqif). The author supports the second view and quotes a statement: 'So existence is one in essence, and the existent is two: essence and determination', and existence is an accident (the passage cuts off). This discussion is foundational for understanding the ontological status of the five universals in Islamic logic.

Three Proofs That Individuation Is Non-Existent
Author presents three proofs for individuation being non-existent and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the three proofs that individuation is non-existent and analyze the objection-response structure.

Core Idea
Individuation is not a real attribute; it is merely relational and non-existent.

In this passage, the author defends the position that individuation (al-ta'ayyun) is non-existent (adami), meaning it is not a real addition to the essence. He provides three proofs to support this, followed by an objection and a response. Understanding these proofs is crucial for grasping how the universal exists in multiple individuals without real difference in the essence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
The author states that the existence of the universal with individuation being non-existent is the truth. This is the thesis to be proven.
Individuation is considered a relation, not a real attribute.
2
Analyze the second proof (reductio ad absurdum)
Assume the opposite: the absolute essence does not exist. Then individuation would be identical to the individuals, making them distinct by themselves. That would imply they are necessary existents, which is absurd. Therefore, individuation must be external, and the universal is abstracted.
This is a classic reductio argument.
3
Analyze the third proof (physical example)
A continuous body, when divided, yields two parts similar in essence. The continuous does not resolve into different essences, so the common essence is fully present in both parts. Individuation must be additional; if it were a real component, division would alter the essence.
This relies on principles of natural science (the indivisible part is non-existent).
4
Examine the objection and response
The objection argues that division annihilates the continuous and creates two new continuous things, so the resulting parts could be different in essence. The response concedes that the continuous does not resolve into different essences, but here there is no resolution—only annihilation and new creation.
The author maintains that the unity of essence is not preserved through division.

The Reductio Proof for the External Existence of the Universal
Author gives the second proof (reductio) for the external existence of the universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio argument against the identity of individuation with essence and explain why the absolute mahiyya must exist externally.

Core Idea
If individuation were essence itself, individuals would be self-necessitating — an impossibility.

This lesson analyzes the second proof presented by the author, which uses the method of reductio ad absurdum (khulf). The argument aims to show that the absolute essence (al-māhiyya al-muṭlaqa) cannot be merely mental; it must have an external basis, because if it didn't, individuation (al-ta‘ayyun) would be the very same as the individual entities, leading to the absurd conclusion that individuals are necessarily existent. Follow the guided path to break down the logical steps.

Guided Path
1
State the supposition to be disproved
The author supposes that the absolute mahiyya (e.g., the essence of 'human') has no external existence. This is the hypothesis to be reduced to absurdity.
The absolute mahiyya is the universal considered without any individuation.
2
Derive the consequence regarding individuation
If the absolute mahiyya does not exist externally, then the only thing that distinguishes individuals is their individuation (ta‘ayyun). But since there is no common essence, the individuation would be identical to the individuals themselves.
That is, the individual's essence would be nothing but its individuation.
3
Show the absurd result
If individuals are distinguished by themselves (their own essence), they must be self-determining and self-existing. This leads to the conclusion that they are necessary existents (wājiba), which is false because all contingent beings require a cause.
The term 'necessary' here means existent by themselves without needing a cause, which is only true of God.
4
Conclude the negation of the supposition
Since the consequence is absurd, the supposition is false. Therefore, the absolute mahiyya must exist externally, and individuation is an additional factor (khārij). This establishes abstraction (intizā‘) of the universal.

When a body divides, the parts share one essence, proving that individuation is external.

Identify the common essence
Since the two parts are similar in essence, there must be a single nature (māhiyya) that is common to both. This common nature is the complete essence of each part, not a part of it.
4

The two parts are distinct individuals, yet they share the same essence. Therefore, what makes them different cannot be the essence itself, but something added—namely, individuation (ta‘ayyun). If this individuation were intrinsic, the parts would not share a common essence. So it must be external, and abstraction (intizā‘) is possible.

They argued for it in two ways. First: If the universal exists, then either it is identical to the individuals, so it is the same as the universal and the same as the other individual, necessitating that there is no distinction among individuals, because the same of the same is the same; or it is a part of them, so that it cannot be predicated of them; or it is external to them, so it does not exist. The answer: First, if he means by it that it is the same as individuals without distinction, then the disjunction is not exhaustive, for it is possible that it is the same as them with a conceptual distinction; and if he means by it that it is the same in essence albeit distinct conceptually, then the worst that follows is that individuals are identical in reality, even if they are distinct conceptually, and the only problem is union in essence and conceptually. Second: If the universal exists, then either it is devoid of any additional attribute, necessitating that one individual exists in many places and is characterized by contradictory attributes; or it is with an additional attribute, and if they exist with one existence, then one accident would subsist in two subjects; and if they exist with two existences, then predication is impossible. Second, by choosing the second alternative, and saying that existence is an accident to both, and they become one, as the author of al-Mawaqif and his followers hold; so ponder this, and God knows best. Would that I knew, if Zayd, for example, is simple in every respect, as this faction holds, and is observed insofar as he is himself without regard to commonalities and distinctions, even to existence and non-existence, how can the extraction of distinct forms such as 'animal,' 'rational,' 'sensitive' and the like be conceived from him? For simplicity does not permit the extraction of multiplicity, while Zayd is certainly amenable to such extraction. So they must hold that the truly simple, at the level of its constitution and realization, has two distinct congruent forms, so that it is suitable for the extraction of multiplicity, and this is a statement of two contradictories: simplicity and composition. It was objected to this that the impossibility of extracting multiplicity from the one is not conceded; how is it that the Exalted Necessary Being, may He be glorified, is simple, yet many attributes are extracted from Him? And perhaps this view is invalidated by the fact that one thing may be extracted from multiple individuals, so there must be one common thing existing between them, because it is impossible to extract the one from multiplicity, as was mentioned earlier in the book; thus the existing thing is not a simple essence. And it may be objected to this that the one, like existence, is extracted from the Necessary Being.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition and refutation of the view that universals have no existence in concrete reality but are merely mental abstractions. The author presents two arguments put forward by the opponents of the existence of universals and then provides responses. The first argument is a trilemma: if the universal exists, it must be either identical to the individuals, a part of them, or separate from them. Each option is shown to lead to absurdities. The author's response clarifies that the disjunction is not exhaustive: the universal can be the same as individuals with a conceptual distinction. Alternatively, it can be a part, which does not prevent predication because it is a conceptual part, as held by authorities like the author of al-Mawaqif and investigators regarding genera and species. The second argument is another dilemma: if the universal exists, it must be either without any additional attribute (leading to one individual in multiple places with contradictory attributes) or with an additional attribute (leading to either one accident in two subjects or impossible predication). The response chooses the first alternative, arguing that the additional attribute is privative (individuation), and the necessity of one individual in multiple places is denied; what results is a specific (not individual) existence. For the second alternative, it accepts that existence is an accident making them one, as per al-Mawaqif. Then a poetic objection is raised: if Zayd is simple in every respect, how can multiple forms be extracted from him? This forces the opponents to say the simple has two forms, contradicting simplicity. But the author counters this objection by pointing out that the Necessary Being is simple yet has many attributes, so extracting multiplicity from a simple entity is not impossible. Furthermore, the need for a common existent thing between individuals is argued from the principle that the one cannot be extracted from the many; thus universals must exist. The passage ends with a counter-objection about existence being extracted from the Necessary Being. The overall argument supports the realist position on universals against nominalism.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical examination of mixed and absolute universals from a commentary on Sullam al-Ulum. The author begins with an objection concerning a single person (e.g., Zayd) described by opposite attributes. The objector argues that if a person exists with one existence, then an accident would subsist in two subjects; if with two existences, predication becomes impossible. The answer is twofold: first, by choosing the first disjunct—the person is stripped of an additional entity (individuation) which is a non-existence, so being in multiple places or having opposite attributes is not necessary; only a specific existence is required, and there is no impossibility. Second, by choosing the second disjunct, following the author of al-Mawaqif, who holds that existence is an accident to the quiddity, making them one. The author then presents another objection: if Zayd is truly simple in every respect, how can multiple forms like animal and rational be abstracted from him? The counter is that we must posit two diverse forms within the simple, leading to a contradiction of simplicity and composition. Then the objector refutes this by noting that even the Necessary, who is simple, has many attributes abstracted from Him, and that one thing can be abstracted from many individuals, so there must be a common entity. Finally, the author transitions to the absolute universal, summarizing that the previous discussion was about mixed and absolute. For the absolute: it is one in essence but multiple accidentally via individuals. Its existence when attributed to itself is one (divine existence); when attributed to individuals it becomes multiple (natural existence). The nature with natural existence is realized when one individual exists and negated when that individual perishes, unlike divine existence which persists as long as any individual exists. The absolute is prior to itself in the order of priority (divine over natural). It has two sets of rulings: essential (from divine existence) and accidental (from natural existence). The essential rulings do not necessarily apply to individuals due to particularity, while the accidental ones do. Thus the absolute can bear contradictories: as a quiddity existing by divine existence, both affirmation and negation can coexist, which is why philosophers say unqualified propositions are not contradictory, and sufis say opposites can coexist. The passage ends with an exhortation to memorize this subtle point.

The Absolute Universal: Divine Existence and Natural Existence
Author explains the two modes of existence of the absolute universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the divine existence and natural existence of the absolute universal and explain how it can be both one and many.

Core Idea
The absolute universal is one in its essence, but multiple in individuals—a duality resolved by its two modes of existence.

The absolute universal, like 'humanity', is considered in itself without regard to particularity. But how can it be both one (as a concept) and many (instantiated in many individuals)? The author introduces the notions of 'divine existence' and 'natural existence' to explain this, and also discusses how the absolute can bear contradictories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute universal
The author defines the absolute as one in essence but many accidentally through individuals. This unity is not individual unity, so it doesn't prevent participation.
2
Understand divine existence
When existence is attributed to the absolute itself, it is absolutely one. This is called 'divine existence' because it is only by God's care. The universal is one entity in this mode.
3
Understand natural existence
When existence is attributed to individuals, it becomes multiple. This is 'natural existence'. For each individual there is a distinct existence.
4
See the priority and rulings
The absolute by divine existence is prior to itself by natural existence (a unique priority). Rulings from divine existence (essential) may not apply to individuals due to particularity; rulings from natural existence (accidental) apply. Thus the absolute can bear contradictories.

Refuting the distinction between absolute and natural universals
Author critiques a claim about absolute and natural universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute the mistaken claim that the absolute and natural universals have different subjects of realization and negation.

Core Idea
The absolute and natural universals are two faces of the same coin, not distinct subjects.

In the commentary on the five universals, some scholars attempted to distinguish between the absolute universal (المطلقة) and the natural universal (الطبيعة) by assigning them different conditions for realization and negation. Our author, Bahr Ulum, challenges this distinction with a penetrating critique that reveals the two are actually equivalent. This lesson will guide you through the steps of that critique and show how to defend the correct understanding of the universals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being critiqued
Locate the statement by 'some of the leading scholars' that the absolute universal can be considered as the subject of the particular universal (المهملة) with realization/negation by one individual, or as the subject of the natural universal (الطبيعة) realized by one individual but negated only by all individuals.
This claim attempts to draw a conceptual distinction between two roles of the absolute universal.
2
Analyze the first objection
The author points out that the subject of the natural universal does not exist externally (لا وجود له فی الخارج), so how can it be realized by a single individual? This reveals a fundamental flaw in the claim.
The objection is ontological: the natural universal is not a concrete entity.
3
Analyze the second objection (conditional)
Even if we accept a figurative interpretation, the author shows that the conditions for negation are identical: both the particular and natural universals are negated by a single individual in general, and only completely negated by the negation of all individuals. Thus they are equivalent.
The author uses a comparative analysis of the logical behavior of the two subjects.
4
Synthesize the refutation
Conclude that the claimed distinction collapses because the two subjects are identical in terms of realization and negation conditions. The absolute universal serves both roles without a real difference.
This is a classic hashiya-style refinement that clarifies a misunderstanding in the main commentary.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and epistemological discussion of the abstracted universal (المجردة), one of the five universals. The author first states that no one holds the abstracted universal exists externally except Plato, but clarifies that the attribution to Plato of believing in separately existing abstract essences is a mistake. The correct interpretation is that Platonic forms refer to lords (ارباب) over species, which are spiritual entities in the world of intellects, not separate universals. The author then presents an alternative interpretation: the forms are the world of archetypes (عالم المثال), an intermediate world. After this, the main question is whether the abstracted universal exists in the mind. Two opinions: some say no because mental existence is particular, others say yes because there is no restriction in concepts. The author sides with 'yes' initially, but then critiques that if the concept is merely a mental representation that we suppose to be abstract, then it is conceded but not beneficial; if it means something else, it is denied. The author points out a contradiction: the author of the matn (Sullam) will later rule that the impossible has no mental image, yet here he rules that the abstracted universal exists in the mind, implying it is possible. So the truth is the first opinion (that it does not exist in the mind). This is a typical super-commentary refinement. For example, the concept of 'human' abstracted from individuals is a mental representation, but not a separate existent.

Commentary
This passage defines the wahm, or imaginative faculty, and then classifies perceptions (mushahadat) into three types. It begins by defining wahm as a power in the brain that perceives particular meanings not directly sensed, such as the enmity one senses in a wolf or the affection of a father. These perceptions are called wahmiyyat. The passage emphasizes that wahm is powerful, often controlling other faculties and even misleading the intellect. It then distinguishes two kinds of wahm-based judgments: those concerning sensed things (which are true, e.g., perceiving enmity in a wolf) and those concerning non-sensed things (which are false, e.g., claiming every existent is in a place). The first type belongs to the wijdaniyyat, or self-perceptions. After this, the passage introduces a classification of all perceptions (mushahadat) into three categories: (1) those from the external senses, (2) those from internal senses (including wahmiyyat), and (3) those perceived directly by the soul without instruments. The latter two are together called wijdaniyyat. This classification is foundational for understanding how different kinds of knowledge are acquired.

Key Terms
الوهم (al-wahm): A power in the brain that perceives particular meanings not sensed, like enmity or affection; it can judge truly about sensed things but falsely about non-sensed things. [Note: In earlier falsafah texts, wahm is often a purely imaginative faculty; later mutakallimun sometimes restrict it to estimation without truth-value.]
المشاهدات (al-mushahadat): Observations; perceptions that are directly witnessed, classified here into three types: external sense, internal sense (including wahmiyyat), and self-perception (wijdaniyyat). [Note: In standard logic, mushahadat are often limited to sense perception; here they include internal and self-perception.]
الوجدانيات (al-wijdaniyyat): Self-perceptions; those perceptions obtained through internal sense or direct self-awareness, not through external instruments. [Note: No significant shift; this term is stable across the tradition.]
