Commonly Accepted and Imaginal Premises
Author defines commonly accepted and imaginal premises, their subtypes, and truth value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between absolute and limited commonly accepted premises and identify imaginal premises, and evaluate their truth value in Islamic arguments.

Core Idea
Not all accepted premises are certain; some rely on consensus or estimation.

In dialectical and rhetorical arguments, not every premise needs to be absolutely certain. The logicians classify premises that are accepted by common consensus (mashhurat) or based on the estimative faculty (wahmiyyat). Understanding these helps you evaluate the strength of arguments in kalam and fiqh debates where certainty is not required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main types
The passage defines mashhurat (commonly accepted) and wahmiyyat (imaginal). Note that mashhurat are further divided into absolute and limited.
Mashhurat are premises accepted by consensus; wahmiyyat are based on estimation.
2
Distinguish absolute vs limited mashhurat
Absolute mashhurat are accepted by all people universally, e.g., 'knowledge is good'. Limited mashhurat are accepted by a specific group, e.g., ancient Indians considering animal slaughter blameworthy.
The same premise could be absolute in one culture but limited in another.
3
Assess truth value of mashhurat
Mashhurat can be true (theoretical like geometry, or necessary like law of non-contradiction) or false (as in the Indian example).
Truth is independent of consensus; a premise can be widely accepted yet false.
4
Understand wahmiyyat
Imaginal premises are based on the estimative faculty (wahm). They can be true in sensory judgments, but the passage is cut off; they are used when immediate judgment is needed.
Wahm is a non-rational faculty; its judgments may conflict with reason.
5
Apply to Islamic arguments
In usul al-fiqh, consensus (ijma') is a source, but not all mashhurat are ijma'; some are mere common opinion. In kalam, wahmiyyat may be used in analogies.
Recognizing premise types helps assess argument strength.

Core Idea
Non-certain premises are not false; they are defined by their epistemic status and resemblance to certain premises.

This passage from a logic commentary expands on four types of premises that fall short of certainty. Understanding these categories is essential for evaluating arguments in dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. Author defines awwaliyyat and refutes the necessity of their intuitiveness.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Self-evident premises are the foundation of demonstration, but their self-evidence can be hidden or forgotten.

In this passage, the author defines awwaliyyat (self-evident premises) as judgments made from the mere conception of the subject and predicate. He then refutes the claim that one can always know whether a premise is intuitive or theoretical without proof. This distinction is crucial for understanding the structure of certain knowledge in Islamic logic.

The refutation is based on the possibility of epistemic obscurity.

Apply the reasoning to necessary concepts
The passage extends the same logic to 'tasawwurat daruriyya' (necessary concepts). Consider how a concept might be acquired without reflection but later appear to require proof.
This strengthens the argument that necessity is not always obvious.

Wealth, even if by plunder, is good. And to the detailed division mentioned, he pointed with his statement: 'true'—those commonplaces are either true or false, and their examples have passed. And from here it is said: 'Temperaments and customs enter into beliefs'; for those with hard, strong temperaments consider sharpness good, and those with soft, delicate temperaments consider softness and pardon good, and people of customs consider what their custom runs on as good, and what is other than it as bad. And every people has specific commonplaces, just as the commonplaces of grammar are 'the subject is in the nominative' and 'Imru' al-Qays is most eloquent', and the commonplaces of the Peripatetics are 'the categories are ten', and so on. And sometimes they (commonplaces) become confused with first principles, as occurred for the Mu'tazila, until they said 'truthfulness saves from the Fire and lying lands in it are two necessary truths', but it is not so; rather, they are only known by revelation. And they differ from first principles upon rigorous analysis, i.e., when the intellect is stripped of everything else, such that it seems as if it were created just now. So commonplaces require demonstration, just as a man who severs consideration of the Shari'a does not know the Fire, let alone that truthfulness saves from it and lying lands in it.

Distinguishing commonplaces from first principles
Author explains commonplaces, their dependence on temperaments and customs, and how they are mistaken for first principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of 'tajrid al-aql' (stripping the intellect) to test whether a proposition is a first principle or a commonplace.

Core Idea
A proposition is a first principle if it is known when the intellect is stripped of all external influences.

The passage introduces a powerful tool to distinguish commonplaces from first principles: rigorous analysis (التحرير) by stripping the intellect of all prior influences, as if one were created just now. This test reveals that commonplaces require demonstration, while first principles are immediately grasped. You will now learn to apply this method to evaluate propositions.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Mu'tazila example
The Mu'tazila claimed that 'truthfulness saves from the Fire and lying lands in it' are necessary truths (ضروريتان). But the author says they are only known from revelation.
This is a classic case of confusing a commonplace with a first principle.
2
Apply the 'stripped intellect' test
Imagine a person who has no knowledge of any custom, temperament, or revelation. The author calls this 'tajrid al-aql' — as if created just now. Would this person know that truthfulness saves from the Fire?
Clearly not, because the idea of the Fire and reward/punishment is only known through revelation.
3
Determine the status of the proposition
Since the proposition fails the stripped intellect test, it is not a first principle; it is a commonplace (derived from revelation and community acceptance).
First principles are known without any external input, e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'.
4
Generalize the method
For any proposition, ask: 'Would a completely fresh intellect, devoid of custom and revelation, accept this as true without proof?' If yes, it is a first principle; if no, it requires demonstration.
This is the standard method in Islamic logic to distinguish certain knowledge (yaqin) from accepted premises (musallamat).
