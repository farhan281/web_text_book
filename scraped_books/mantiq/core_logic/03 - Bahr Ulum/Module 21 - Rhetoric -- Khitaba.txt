Khitaba: Definition and Scope of Maqbulat
Author defines khitaba as composed of maqbulat from trusted figures, correcting a common error.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct definition of khitaba and distinguish its premises from prophetic statements.

Core Idea
Rhetoric rests on human authority, not divine certainty.

In this lesson, we explore the definition of khitaba (rhetoric) from the source text. The author defines it as composed of maqbulat—premises accepted on trust from credible human sources like saints and sages. Crucially, he warns against including prophetic statements in rhetoric, because they belong to a higher epistemic category. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly classifying arguments in the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is khitaba (rhetoric). Find it in the Arabic text: الثَّالِثُ: الْخَطَابَةُ
It is the third of the five arts.
2
Extract the genus
Khitaba is composed of maqbulat (accepted statements). The genus is 'accepted premises' that are not based on sensory or logical evidence but on trust.
The term maqbulat is a technical term for such premises.
3
Identify the differentia
The specific source of these maqbulat: they are taken from those in whom good opinion is placed (مِمَّنْ يُحْسَنُ الظَّنُّ فِيهِ), such as saints (أولياء) and sages (حكماء).
This distinguishes khitaba from other arts that use different types of premises.
4
Understand the exclusion
The author explicitly says that counting statements from the prophets (أنبياء) as part of khitaba is a mistake. Why? Because prophetic statements are based on revelation, which yields certainty, not mere acceptance on trust.
This exclusion is a key correction of a common error.
5
Recognize the implication
Khitaba deals with probable or persuasive arguments, not certain ones. Including prophetic statements would misclassify their epistemic status. The definition is thus refined to maintain the correct classification.
This aligns with the broader purpose of Mantiq: to classify arguments by their certainty.

Defining Rhetoric (Khataba) in Islamic Logic
Author defines rhetorical syllogism and its material as accepted statements from trustworthy figures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the rhetorical syllogism and identify its distinguishing features: producing probable opinion and using accepted statements as material.

Core Idea
Rhetoric is a syllogistic art yielding probable opinion, not certainty.

This lesson introduces rhetoric (khataba), the third of the five logical arts. Unlike demonstration which gives certainty, rhetoric produces zann (probable opinion) from premises that are accepted from trustworthy figures. Understanding this definition is crucial for distinguishing it from other arts like dialectic and sophistry.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find the Arabic sentence defining khataba as a proof (ḥujja) that necessitates probable opinion (ẓann) about the conclusion. Note the two components: the proof's function and its epistemic outcome.
2
Identify the material
The author says it is composed of 'maqbūlāt' – accepted statements taken from individuals of good opinion (saints, sages). Understand why their testimony yields only probable opinion: they are trustworthy but fallible.
3
Distinguish from other arts
Compare with demonstrative certainty (burhān) and dialectic (jadal). Rhetoric occupies the middle ground, useful for persuasion and practical deliberation.

Why Prophetic Reports are Certain, Not Probable
Author defines rhetorical syllogism and its material as accepted statements from trustworthy figures.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prophetic reports produce certainty because the informant is absolutely truthful, making them self-evident.

Rhetoric as a Faculty and Its Purpose
Author defines rhetoric as a faculty and lists its purpose and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of rhetoric as a faculty and state its purpose according to the author.

Core Idea
Rhetoric is a faculty for composing persuasive arguments aimed at beneficial or harmful judgments in this life and the next.

The author begins by defining rhetoric as one of the five logical arts. He describes it as a faculty that enables the composition of rhetorical arguments. Its purpose is to achieve judgments that are beneficial or harmful in worldly or religious matters, so that people act or refrain accordingly. This lesson unpacks the definition and purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus
The author defines rhetoric as a 'faculty' (ملكة). A faculty is an ability or power. What broader category does rhetoric belong to?
2
Extract the differentia
The differentia is 'composing rhetorical arguments' (تأليف حجج خطابية). This distinguishes rhetoric from other faculties like demonstration or dialectic.
3
Identify the purpose
The purpose is to obtain judgments that are beneficial or harmful in worldly life or the afterlife. The goal is action: to act upon or avoid them.
4
Compare with other arts
Rhetoric's purpose is practical, not theoretical. Compare with demonstration which aims at certainty, or dialectic which aims at testing opinions.

Commentary
This passage defines the art of rhetoric (صناعة الخطابة) as one of the five logical arts. The author begins by stating its definition as a faculty enabling the composition of rhetorical arguments. Then the purpose is given: to achieve judgments that are beneficial or harmful in worldly or religious matters, so that people act or refrain accordingly, just as preachers and orators do. The key requirement is that the argument be persuasive to the listeners. It can be induction (استقراء), analogy (تمثيل), or even a flawed syllogism (قياس فاسد) as long as it is thought to yield a conclusion (مظنون الإنتاج). Additionally, the expression must be clear and quickly graspable. The premises must be persuasive and include elements of encouragement (ترغيب) or deterrence (ترهيب). The author warns that using true but unpersuasive premises without these elements is considered sophistry in this context. The details are left to the logician and can be found in Ibn Sina's 'al-Shifa'. This passage explains the nature of rhetoric as a practical, persuasive art distinct from demonstration.

Key Terms
الخطابة (al-khiṭābah): A faculty that enables composing rhetorical arguments aimed at persuasion in practical matters. [Note: Stable term across the tradition; defined consistently as the art of persuasion.]
الحجج الخطابية (al-ḥujaj al-khiṭābiyyah): Arguments used in rhetoric, which can be induction, analogy, or flawed syllogism, provided they are persuasive. [Note: Same meaning in other works; classical definition.]
مظنون الإنتاج (maẓnūn al-intāj): A condition that the argument is thought (but not certain) to produce a conclusion. [Note: Stable concept; used in logic texts to distinguish from demonstrative certainty.]

Conditions of Rhetorical Arguments
Author defines rhetoric as a faculty and lists its purpose and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the conditions a rhetorical argument must satisfy and distinguish rhetorical arguments from sophistry.

Core Idea
Rhetorical arguments can be induction, analogy, or flawed syllogism, but they must be persuasive, thought to produce, clear, and include emotional appeal.

After defining rhetoric, the author specifies the conditions for rhetorical arguments. He allows various types of arguments—induction, analogy, even a flawed syllogism—as long as they are thought to produce a conclusion (مظنون الإنتاج). Additionally, the expression must be clear, and the premises must include inducement or intimidation. Using true but unpersuasive premises without emotional appeal is considered sophistry. This lesson examines these conditions.

Guided Path
1
List the permissible argument types
The author says it may be induction (استقراء), analogy (تمثيل), or a flawed syllogism (قياس فاسد). Identify each in the Arabic.
2
Identify the condition 'thought to produce'
The argument must be 'مظنون الإنتاج' (thought to yield a conclusion). This means it need not be certain, only plausible.
3
Check the clarity condition
The expression must be clear (ظاهرة الدلالة) so that the listeners quickly understand. This is a requirement for persuasive effect.
4
Identify the emotional appeal condition
Premises must include inducement (ترغيب) or intimidation (ترهيب). Without them, even true premises are not rhetorical but sophistry.
5
Distinguish from sophistry
Using true but unpersuasive premises without emotional appeal is considered sophistry (سفسطة) in this context. Rhetoric aims to persuade, sophistry misleads.

As for the art of rhetoric, it is a faculty by which one is able to compose rhetorical arguments. The purpose of it is to obtain beneficial or harmful judgments in worldly life or the afterlife, so that one may act upon them or avoid them, as orators and preachers do. You have known that what is sought from rhetorical arguments are beneficial or harmful judgments. It is necessary that the argument be such that it is persuasive to the listeners. So it may be an induction, an analogy, or a flawed syllogism, provided that it is thought to produce a result. And the expression must be clear in indication, such that the minds of the listeners quickly grasp its meaning. Its explanation is the responsibility of the literary scholars. And its premises must be persuasive, containing inducement or intimidation. Indeed, using true primary premises that are not persuasive and that do not contain one of these two is sophistry here. The explanation of these premises is the responsibility of the logician. The details are in 'al-Shifa', so let it be sought there.

Commentary
This passage defines the art of rhetoric (صناعة الخطابة) as one of the five logical arts. The author begins by stating its definition as a faculty enabling the composition of rhetorical arguments. Then the purpose is given: to achieve judgments that are beneficial or harmful in worldly or religious matters, so that people act or refrain accordingly, just as preachers and orators do. The key requirement is that the argument be persuasive to the listeners. It can be induction (استقراء), analogy (تمثيل), or even a flawed syllogism (قياس فاسد) as long as it is thought to yield a conclusion (مظنون الإنتاج). Additionally, the expression must be clear and quickly graspable. The premises must be persuasive and include elements of encouragement (ترغيب) or deterrence (ترهيب). The author warns that using true but unpersuasive premises without these elements is considered sophistry in this context. The details are left to the logician and can be found in Ibn Sina's 'al-Shifa'. This passage explains the nature of rhetoric as a practical, persuasive art distinct from demonstration.

Key Terms
مظنون الإنتاج (maẓnūn al-intāj): A condition that the argument is thought (but not certain) to produce a conclusion. [Note: Stable concept; used in logic texts to distinguish from demonstrative certainty.]
الترغيب والترهيب (al-targhīb wa al-tarhīb): Inducement and intimidation; emotional appeals necessary in rhetorical premises. [Note: Standard terms in rhetoric and preaching.]
السفسطة (al-safasaṭah): Sophistry; using true but unpersuasive premises without emotional appeal is considered sophistry in rhetoric. [Note: In later texts, sophistry is defined more broadly as fallacious reasoning.]

Key Takeaway
A rhetorical argument must be persuasive, thought to produce a conclusion, clearly expressed, and include emotional appeal; otherwise it falls into sophistry.
