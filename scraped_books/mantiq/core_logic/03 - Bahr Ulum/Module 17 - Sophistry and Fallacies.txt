Implication from Impossibility: A Key Logical Principle
Author explains the logicians' principle of implication from impossibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical principle that an impossibility can imply another impossibility and apply it to resolve paradoxical implications.

Core Idea
An impossibility can imply another impossibility; this resolves many fallacies.

This passage addresses a key logical principle: that an impossible premise can entail an impossible conclusion. This principle is not only valid but necessary for resolving certain paradoxes. We will see how logicians like al-Dawwani use it to dissolve a famous fallacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The author states that logicians allow the implication of something to its contradictory based on allowing an impossibility to imply another impossibility.
The term istilzam (implication) is key.
2
Understand the basis
Logical entailment does not require the truth of the antecedent; if the antecedent is impossible, the conditional holds vacuously. Therefore, an impossibility can imply another impossibility without logical error.
3
Note the application
This principle is used in many places, including answering a common fallacy. We will examine that fallacy in the next lesson.
The common fallacy is about implication to two contradictories.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis and defense of a logical principle. The author begins by stating that when an expression is taken in a broader sense, it may not be matched by the intended meaning, but that is not problematic. Then he discusses the view of logicians, including al-Dawwani, who allow that something can imply both the contradictory and the two contradictories (i.e., a proposition and its contradictory). This is based on the principle that an impossibility can imply another impossibility. They use this to resolve a common fallacy. The fallacy is: The claim is true; otherwise its contradictory is true. But if the contradictory is true, then some other thing is true. Therefore, if the claim is false, that other thing is true. By contraposition, if that other thing is false, the claim is true. This leads to a contradiction. The author then provides a logical analysis: if the condition is taken as a qualification of the predicate in the consequent, then when the antecedent is a necessary condition for both contradictories, it would force a contradiction. But if the judgment in the conditional is about the connection between the two propositions (i.e., the conditional as a whole), then no contradiction arises. The contradictory of the connection is simply its negation, not the existence of another connection. Hence the logicians' position is correct.

Resolving the Common Fallacy of Implication to Two Contradictories
Author resolves a common fallacy using conditional analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the common fallacy of implication to two contradictories and explain how the conditional analysis resolves it.

Core Idea
The fallacy dissolves when we distinguish the condition as a predicate qualifier from the conditional as a connection between relations.

A famous fallacy appears: if the claim is not true, then its contradictory is true, and that leads to a contradiction. Logicians show that when the conditional is understood as a connection between propositions (not as a qualifier of the predicate), the contradiction disappears. We will walk through the fallacy and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
State the fallacy
The fallacy runs: The claim is either true or its contradictory is true. If the contradictory is true, then something else must be true. So if the claim is false, that something else is true. By contraposition, if that something is false, the claim is true. This appears to produce a contradiction.
The author calls this the 'commonly occurring famous fallacy'.
2
Identify the logicians' response
Logicians allow that the claim and its contradictory can both be implied by something impossible. This dissolves the apparent impossibility of both being implied.
The key is the principle from Lesson 1.
3
Analyze the conditional
If the condition (e.g., 'when something is not established') is taken as a qualification of the predicate (e.g., 'Zayd is standing at that time'), then it leads to a contradiction because both 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not standing' would be asserted at the same time. But if the conditional is about the connection between two relational propositions (e.g., 'if something is not established, then the claim is established'), then its contradictory is simply the negation of that connection, not another connection.
The author illustrates with the example of Zayd standing.
4
Apply to the fallacy
The fallacy arises from mixing the two interpretations. The logicians' interpretation treats the conditional as a connection between relations, so no contradiction occurs. The contradictory of 'if A then B' is 'A and not B', not 'if not A then not B'.
Thus the logicians' position is correct.

Resolving Two Doubts About Conversion
Author raises two doubts about conversion and provides resolutions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve sophisms arising from the conversion of impossible propositions in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Conversion of impossibilities can lead to false conclusions unless the essential truth of impossible propositions is understood.

In this passage, the author addresses two perplexing doubts about the logical operation of conversion (ʿaks) when applied to propositions about God and impossibilities. These doubts challenge the reliability of conversion, but each is resolved by careful logical analysis. Understanding these resolutions is crucial for distinguishing valid logical inferences from sophisms in theological argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first doubt
The proposition 'Every non-combination of contradictories is not a partner of the Creator' is true; its converse 'Every partner of the Creator is a combination of contradictories' is false under ordinary conversion rules. The doubt arises because conversion should preserve truth, but here it doesn't.
2
Understand the resolution for the first doubt
The author suggests that the converse can be considered true in an essential sense (ḥaqīqiyya) because the subject 'partner of the Creator' is impossible, and in logic impossible propositions are treated as true for the sake of the inference. This allows all impossibilities to be considered true simultaneously, since impossibility is like a single non-existence.
3
Identify the second doubt
The conditional 'Whenever the originated exists, its existence entails the removal of a non-existence in reality' is true. Its conversion by the method of 'al-ʿaks al-mustawī' yields a statement that contradicts the established premise that only necessary beings are always existent.
4
Apply the resolution for the second doubt
The resolution is that there is no real contradiction between the two positive statements (the original conditional and its converse) even though their consequents are contradictories. This is the sophism of istilwām.

Commentary
This passage raises two doubts about conversion and their resolution. The first doubt concerns a true proposition about God's oneness: 'Every non-combination of contradictories is not a partner of the Creator' (i.e., anything that does not combine contradictories is not a partner of God). Its conversion, 'Every partner of the Creator is a combination of contradictories', is false because a partner would be impossible, not actually combining contradictories. The author suggests that one may accept the conversion as true in an essential sense (ḥaqīqiyya), because the impossible proposition is considered true in logic. This leads to the possibility that all impossible propositions are simultaneously true, since impossibility is a single non-existence. The second doubt involves a conditional: 'Whenever the originated (al-ḥādith) exists, its existence entails the removal of a non-existence in reality.' The resolution is to deny that there is a contradiction between the two necessitating positives, even if their consequents are contradictories. This is known as the sophism of istilwām, which has other tricky formulations.

The Paradox of the Absolute Unknown and Its Resolution
Commentator discusses the paradox of the absolute unknown and presents several objections and responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the self-referential paradox of the absolute unknown and evaluate the three responses presented in Bahr al-'Ulum's commentary.

Core Idea
The absolute unknown cannot be conceived without contradiction.

This passage from Bahr al-'Ulum's commentary on Sullam al-'Ulum tackles a classic logical puzzle: if you try to think about something that is absolutely unknown (al-majhūl al-muṭlaq), you already know it in some respect, creating a self-referential contradiction. The commentator presents several attempts to resolve this, from distinguishing essential vs. accidental knowledge to denying that the concept can be initially formed. Understanding this problem is crucial for Islamic epistemology, especially when discussing the limits of human knowledge about divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial problem
The absolute unknown is that which is not known in any respect. But if we try to make a judgment about it, we must have some concept of it, which would make it known in some way. This creates a paradox: the concept itself seems to make the unknown known.
2
Analyze the first response
The commentator mentions a distinction: something can be 'known in essence' (ma‘lūm bi al-dhāt) while being 'absolute unknown accidentally' (majhūl muṭlaq bi al-‘araḍ). This allows judgment based on the essential knowledge, not the accidental aspect.
This answer works if the distinction is accepted.

This shows the first response is insufficient for this stronger formulation.
4
Evaluate the final response
The commentator suggests that the concept of absolute unknown cannot be initially conceived. Only clear self-evident concepts like 'thingness' and 'existence' appear first. This prevents the paradox from arising.
The commentator invites reflection: 'so reflect, and God knows best'.

Dispute Over Entailment from Impossible Premises
Dispute on entailment from impossible premise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between concessional truth (jihāt al-ilzām) and genuine entailment (fī nafs al-amr) in dialectical arguments involving impossible premises.

Core Idea
An impossible premise does not genuinely entail any consequent, even if conceded in debate.

In dialectical reasoning, opponents may concede impossible premises for argument’s sake, but we must not conflate concessional truth with genuine logical entailment. This lesson examines a classical dispute on whether an impossible antecedent entails any consequent, using the example 'If five is even, it is a number'. The passage presents two positions and analyzes the conditional under concession versus in reality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on entailment from an impossible premise
Find the first view: total denial of entailment regardless of consequent truth value. Then find the second view, associated with Avicenna, which denies entailment only when the consequent is true.
The passage explicitly mentions both.
2
Analyze Avicenna’s argument on the removal of both contradictories
Avicenna argues that if an impossible premise (like both a proposition and its negation being false) were to entail a true consequent, then the absurdity that the removal of both contradictories entails their conjunction would follow. This shows that impossible premises cannot be assumed to entail anything.
This is a dialectical argument to show the problem with unconditional acceptance of impossible premises.
3

Refuting Ibn Kamunah's Sophism on the Eternity of Accidents
Author presents and refutes Ibn Kamunah's sophism on the eternity of accidents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional fallacy in Ibn Kamunah's argument for the eternity of accidents and construct a valid refutation using the distinction between essence and temporality.

Core Idea
The sophism fails because it confuses accidents as accidents with accidents as eternal.

Ibn Kamunah's sophism is a famous objection to the kalam argument for the temporal origination of the world. It uses conditional reasoning to argue that if accidents do not entail the removal of non-existence, they must be eternal. The author of Bahr al-Ulum dissects this argument and shows that the fallacy lies in equivocating over the nature of accidents. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of the sophism and the precise refutation given.

Guided Path
1
Identify the established premise
The author sets up a conditional: anything whose existence does not entail the removal of an actual non-existence exists always (eternally). Understand this as a universal conditional: if not entail removal, then always existent.
This premise is foundational for the sophism.
2
Trace Ibn Kamunah's argument
The sophism claims that all accidents do not entail removal of actual non-existence. Then it uses the premise to conclude that accidents exist always. It also attempts a contraposition to derive a contradiction if accidents were temporally originated.
Follow the chain: premise applied to accidents yields eternity; contraposition yields a conflicting conditional.
3
Locate the equivocation
The sophism treats accidents in an absolute sense. The author points out that accidents insofar as they are accidents (temporal) do entail removal of non-existence. The premise 'accidents do not entail removal' is only true if we consider accidents as eternal, which is question-begging.
Key distinction: accidents as accidents vs. accidents as eternal.
4
Apply the author's solution
The author denies the opposition between the two conditionals because the impossible (that an accident does not entail removal) can entail contradictories. The correct refutation requires specifying the respect (haythiyya).
The solution shows that the sophism's contradiction is not real; it arises from ambiguous subject.

Refuting Ibn Kammuna's Doubt on the Eternity of Events
Author presents and refutes Ibn Kammuna's doubt on the eternity of events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional structure of Ibn Kammuna's doubt and explain the refutation using the principle that an impossible antecedent can imply any consequent.

Core Idea
An impossible premise can imply contradictory conclusions without logical conflict.

This lesson examines a famous doubt raised by Ibn Kammuna against the Ash'ari doctrine of temporal origination (hadith). The argument uses conditional logic to try to prove that events must be eternal. The refutation, rooted in the nature of impossible antecedents, is a masterclass in dialectical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial premise
The doubt begins with the premise: 'All events are such that their existence does not necessitate the removal of a real non-existence.' This is the foundational assumption that the doubt attempts to undermine.
2
Construct the conditional chain
Ibn Kammuna argues: If an event exists and necessitates removal, then the premise is false. If it does not necessitate removal, then the contrapositive yields 'if it does not necessitate removal, it does not exist,' which contradicts the premise that events do exist. This leads to a dilemma.
The contrapositive (aks al-naqid) is used: if A implies B, then not-B implies not-A.
3
Apply the contrapositive
From the original premise, the contrapositive is: 'If the existence of an event does not necessitate removal, then it does not exist.' This conclusion conflicts with the premise that events do exist, forming the core of the doubt.
4
Understand the resolution
The author resolves by showing that the antecedent 'the existence of an event does not necessitate removal' is impossible. An impossible antecedent can imply anything, even contradictories, so the two conditionals do not truly conflict. The original premise remains intact.
This relies on the logical principle ex impossibili quodlibet (from impossibility, anything follows).

And its resolution: Denying the conflict between the two implicational consequents, even if their consequents are contradictories, because the non-necessitation of the event's removal of real non-existence is impossible, and the impossible can necessitate two contradictories. This is the doubt of implication, and it has other articulations that cause slips of the feet. Whoever desires comprehensive investigation should refer to al-'Ujala al-Nafi'a.

Commentary
This passage discusses a famous doubt (shubha) attributed to Ibn Kammuna against the doctrine of the temporal origination of the world (hadith al-alam). The doubt is dialectical: it tries to show that if events exist, they must either necessitate the removal of a real non-existence or not. If they do, then the premise fails; if they do not, then they must exist always, implying eternity. The author first outlines the doubt using conditional logic. Then he presents an answer that distinguishes two aspects: events qua events do necessitate removal, but events qua eternal do not. The final resolution (hal) is to deny that the two conditional propositions conflict, even though their consequents are opposites. The reason is that the antecedent (non-necessitation) is impossible, and an impossible proposition can imply anything, even contradictions. Therefore, the original premise remains intact. The author refers to his earlier work for a full treatment. This passage illustrates a sophisticated use of conditional logic in dialectical theology.

Misunderstanding as a Source of Logical Error
Author defines misunderstanding as a primary cause of error

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of misunderstanding in syllogistic reasoning and distinguish them from other types of logical error.

Core Idea
Misunderstanding mistakes false for true and theoretical for self-evident.

The author begins his classification of logical errors by isolating misunderstanding (sū’ al-fahm) as one of two primary causes. Understanding this distinction is crucial for anyone studying Islamic logic, because many errors in theological and juristic reasoning stem from treating a theoretical proof as if it were axiomatic. Let us explore what misunderstanding is and how it manifests.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of misunderstanding
The author states that misunderstanding occurs when someone takes false propositions as true, or theoretical matters as necessary (self-evident). This leads him to build a syllogism on a false or improperly assumed premise, resulting in error.
The word 'theoretical' (nazariyyāt) refers to propositions that require proof, as opposed to 'necessary' (ḍarūriyyāt) which are self-evident.
2
Identify a real-world example from Islamic reasoning
Consider a student of kalam who mistakenly treats a speculative theological argument (e.g., the proof from temporality) as if it were self-evident, and then builds a syllogism on that basis. This is a clear case of misunderstanding.
3
Contrast misunderstanding with confusion
Misunderstanding is about the cognitive state of the reasoner—he genuinely misjudges the epistemic status of a proposition. Confusion, by contrast, arises from the structure of the language or the argument itself, not from a mistaken epistemic evaluation.
This distinction helps in diagnosing whether an error lies in the mind or in the expression.

Verbal Confusion: Homonymy and Word Form
Author subtypes verbal confusion into homonymy, i‘lāl, and i‘rāb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and correct errors caused by homonymy (ishtirāk al-ism), vowel changes (i‘lāl), and inflection ambiguity (i‘rāb) in Arabic logical discourse.

Core Idea
Verbal confusion arises from words with multiple meanings or ambiguous forms, not from faulty reasoning.

After discussing misunderstanding, the author turns to confusion (ishtibāh) and focuses on verbal confusion. This type of error stems from the language itself—homonymous words, changes in vowel pattern, or lack of case endings. Such errors can mislead even an otherwise careful reasoner. Let us dissect each subtype with the examples given.

Guided Path
1
Understand homonymy (ishtirāk al-ism)
Homonymy occurs when a single word has multiple unrelated meanings. The author says this fallacy is called 'ishtirāk al-ism'. For example, the word 'ʿayn' can mean eye, spring, or spy. In a syllogism, using 'ʿayn' in different senses creates ambiguity.
This is a classic fallacy of equivocation.
2
Analyze the i‘lāl example: al-mukhtar
The word 'al-mukhtar' can be derived from different origins: if from 'mukhtayir' (active participle) it means 'chooser'; if from 'mukhtār' (passive participle) it means 'chosen'. The change in internal vowel structure (i‘lāl) leads to this ambiguity.
I‘lāl is a morphological phenomenon; the author uses it to show how word form can cause confusion.
3
Examine the i‘rāb example: ghulām hasan
Without inflection (i‘rāb), 'ghulām hasan' can be interpreted either as an annexation construct ('the slave of Hasan') or as an attributive phrase ('a good slave'). The lack of case endings allows both readings.
I‘rāb ambiguity often appears in Classical Arabic texts that omit vowels or case endings.

Compositional Confusion: Ambiguity in Compound Expressions
Author subtypes compound confusion into reference ambiguity, combination, and separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to diagnose errors due to ambiguous pronoun reference, improper conjunction (composition fallacy) and improper separation (division fallacy) in compound statements.

Core Idea
Compound expressions hide fallacies in reference, aggregation, and division.

The final category of confusion in the passage deals with compound expressions (murakkabāt). The author identifies three sources: ambiguity in the compound itself (e.g., unclear pronoun reference), combining separate statements that are true individually but false when combined (fallacy of composition), and separating a naturally compound statement that is true as a whole but false when split (fallacy of division). These are common in both everyday debate and scholarly argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Recognize ambiguous pronoun reference
The author gives the example 'What the wise man did, he knows it.' The pronoun 'he' could refer to the wise man (making it true) or to the relative pronoun (making it false). The ambiguity lies in the sentence structure, not in any single word.
This is a classic amphibolous statement.
2
Understand the fallacy of composition (combining separate)
If I say 'Zaid is a physician and Zaid is good and Zaid is truthful', each part is true. But if I combine them into 'Zaid is a good physician', the meaning changes and may be false (e.g., if he is a good man but a bad physician). The error comes from conjoining attributes that are not normally combined.
This is a fallacy of composition, but note: in logic, composition fallacy can also refer to inferring something about a whole from its parts; here it's about combining separate predicates.
3
Understand the fallacy of division (separating compound)
The example: 'The pomelo is sweet and sour' is true as a compound. But if separated into 'The pomelo is sweet' and 'The pomelo is sour', each individual statement may be false (since the fruit is both). The error is dividing a true compound into false parts.
This is the mirror image of the previous fallacy.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed classification of the causes of logical error (ghalat). The author begins by identifying two primary causes: misunderstanding (sū’ al-fahm) and confusion (ishtibāh). Misunderstanding occurs when someone falsely takes false statements as true or theoretical matters as self-evident, then proceeds with a syllogism incorrectly. Confusion is then subdivided into two categories: verbal and non-verbal. The verbal category affects single terms or compound expressions. For single terms, error can arise from homonymy (ishtirāk al-ism) — using a word in multiple meanings — or from incidental changes in the word's form, such as i‘lāl (defective vowel changes) illustrated by 'al-mukhtar' (which can mean 'chooser' or 'chosen' depending on its derivation), or from i‘rāb (inflection) as in 'ghulām hasan' which could be read as annexation (his good slave) or attribution (a good slave) depending on case endings. For compound phrases, error can result from ambiguity in reference, as in 'What the wise man did, he knows it' where the pronoun 'he' could refer back to the wise man (true) or to the relative (false). Other compound errors come from combining separate statements, e.g., saying 'Zaid is a physician and good and truthful' is true, but combining them into 'a good physician' may be false, or conversely, separating a combined expression, e.g., 'the pomelo is sweet and sour' (a true compound statement) but separating to 'sweet and sour' may be false.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sophistry is intentional fallacy; fallacy is any invalid argument.

The classical logicians did not treat all faulty reasoning equally. While every fallacy involves an invalid argument, sophistry adds the dimension of deliberate deception. This lesson unpacks the precise definitions given by the author and shows why this distinction matters for scholarly debate.

Fallacies of Syllogistic Form and Begging the Question
Author introduces form fallacies and begging the question.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify invalid syllogistic figures and the fallacy of begging the question in Islamic legal and theological arguments.

Core Idea
A valid form is necessary for certainty; begging the question proves nothing new.

Not every syllogism that looks valid actually yields a true conclusion. The author begins by warning us about fallacies of form, like the second figure with two affirmative premises, and then warns about the common error of assuming the very thing you are trying to prove. Both errors undermine the power of logical proof in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism's figure and mood
Look at the arrangement of terms. The example 'Time encompasses events; the sphere encompasses them; so time is the sphere' has the middle term 'encompassing them' as predicate in both premises – that is the second figure. Both premises are affirmative (mood: AAA in second figure).
The second figure requires one negative premise to be productive.
2
Check the mood conditions for the figure
In the second figure, a valid syllogism must have one negative premise and the conclusion must be negative. Here both premises are affirmative, so the mood is invalid. No conclusion follows necessarily.
This is a formal fallacy – error in the structure, not the content.
3
Recognize begging the question (musadara)
In the example 'Usama is the lion; the lion is a predatory animal', the conclusion 'Usama is a predatory animal' is not explicitly stated but is already implied in the first premise if 'lion' means 'predatory animal'. The argument assumes what it needs to prove.
Begging the question often hides the conclusion in one of the premises, making the reasoning circular.
4
Apply to an Islamic legal analogy
Consider: 'Wine is forbidden because it is an intoxicant; every intoxicant is forbidden.' If the first premise already assumes the conclusion (the prohibition of wine), the argument is circular. Test by checking whether the first premise can be known independently of the conclusion.
In usul al-fiqh, a valid qiyas must have a clear and independent 'illah (cause).

Non Causa Pro Causa and Illusion of Conversion
Author explains non causa pro causa and illusion of conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a genuine causal link from a false cause and identify when a proposition has been illicitly converted in Islamic reasoning.

Core Idea
Not every middle term is a true cause; converting a proposition does not always preserve truth.

Sometimes a syllogism appears to have a sound premise but the connection between the middle term and the conclusion is spurious. The author gives a fiqh example where touching the penis during urination is said to nullify ablution because of impurity – but the real cause may be different. He also warns about assuming that a proposition and its converse are both true without proper justification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the alleged cause in the syllogism
In the example 'Touching the penis nullifies ablution because it is an impurity at the time of urination; every impurity nullifies ablution', the middle term 'impurity at urination' is presented as the cause (illa).
The fallacy is that the conclusion desired is 'touching the penis nullifies ablution', but the argument only proves that touching the penis during urination (when it is impure) nullifies ablution – a narrower claim.
2
Check if the cause truly applies to all cases
The argument incorrectly assumes that the impurity is caused by the touch itself, but the impurity is due to urination, not touch. So the cause (impurity) is not present in all cases of touching the penis (e.g., dry touch). This is 'taking what is not a cause as a cause'.
This fallacy often occurs when a property is attributed to a subject but actually belongs to a condition or context.
3
Detect the illusion of conversion (iham al-'aks)
The example: 'Every air is not seeing; what is not seeing is not a body nor corporeal.' The first proposition 'every air is not seeing' is a universal negative. Its converse 'every thing that is not seeing is air' is false – conversion of a universal negative gives a universal negative only if the terms are coextensive. Here it is illicit.
The author says that if the major premise were converted (i.e., 'everything not seeing is air') it would be false, but the arguer assumes it true.
4
Apply to an Islamic analogy
Consider: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. This drink is forbidden. Therefore it is an intoxicant.' This is an invalid conversion (affirming the consequent). Valid: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. This is an intoxicant. Therefore it is forbidden.' Recognize the difference.
In fiqh, false conversion leads to incorrect rulings.

The Fallacy of Accident and the Role of Restrictions
Author explains accident fallacy and restriction errors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an accidental property is treated as essential and when adding or deleting a restriction distorts a proposition in Islamic logical reasoning.

Core Idea
Accidental properties are not always transferable; restrictions define the scope of a proposition's truth.

The author now turns to fallacies that involve a single proposition and its parts. He warns against mistaking an accidental attribute for an essential one – for example, thinking that a person sitting in a moving ship is essentially moving. He also shows how adding or removing a restriction (qayd) can turn a true proposition into a false one, or vice versa. These errors are common in legal and theological debates when the scope of a ruling is not carefully defined.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish essential from accidental
In the example 'The person sitting in a ship is moving; everything moving is transferred from place to place', the movement of the person is accidental (because the ship moves), not essential. The fallacy treats the accidental movement as if it were the person's own essential movement, leading to an absurd conclusion.
Essential properties belong to the thing by itself; accidental properties are due to external factors.
2
Analyze the addition of a restriction
The example 'Wine in a jug is intoxicating' is true. If we remove the restriction 'in a jug', the proposition becomes 'Wine is intoxicating', which is also true. But adding a restriction can make a false proposition true. The fallacy occurs when a proposition is taken as true without the restriction that made it true.
Restrictions (qayd) limit the scope; ignoring them leads to overgeneralization.
3
Analyze the deletion of a restriction
The example 'Every angle of the tangent line and the circumference of the circle is smaller than every angle' – the correct statement is 'smaller than any angle between two straight lines'. Deleting the restriction 'between two straight lines' makes the proposition false because an angle of tangent and circumference is not smaller than all angles (e.g., it can be larger than some).
Deleting a necessary restriction makes a true proposition false.
4
Apply to an Islamic ruling
Consider: 'The traveler may shorten prayers.' If we delete the restriction 'traveler', we get 'Everyone may shorten prayers', which is false. If we add a restriction 'while traveling', the original is true. Recognize how restrictions affect the validity of rulings.
In fiqh, many rulings are conditional; ignoring conditions leads to error.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Material fallacies stem from false premises that seem necessary due to the soul's immersion in materiality and the influence of estimation (wahm).

After treating formal fallacies, the author turns to material fallacies—errors in the content of premises. The cause is not logical structure but psychological: the estimative faculty (wahm) overpowers reason, making false propositions appear necessary. The examples illustrate how false primary or mutawatir premises lead to invalid conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish material from formal fallacies

The cause is 'انغماس النفس في الظلمة المادية' (immersion of the soul in material darkness) and the domination of wahm over intellect, making one believe false propositions are necessary.

Author lists seven meaning-related fallacies and their remedies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to name all seven meaning-related fallacies, describe each, and apply the specific remedy to detect and avoid them in Islamic logical reasoning.

Core Idea
Seven meaning-related fallacies have precise cures; mastery of these safeguards logical soundness.

In this passage, the author lists seven fallacies that arise from mishandling the meanings of terms and propositions, rather than from formal structure. Each fallacy has a distinct remedy. Understanding these will help you detect subtle errors in theological and legal arguments, where the logical form may be correct but the content leads to false conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy of combining multiple propositions
If an argument merges distinct claims into one proposition, it becomes hard to evaluate. The remedy is to analyze the proposition into its parts to reveal its true logical structure.
Example: 'The Quran is the word of Allah and is eternal' may conflate two separate claims.
2
Detect begging the question (مصادرة على المطلوب)
Check whether the conclusion is implicitly assumed in the premises. The remedy is to ensure that the premises do not depend on the conclusion.
Common in circular definitions.
3
Test causal claims for non-cause as cause
When a reason is presented as a cause, verify by presenting the premises to the conclusion. If the supposed cause does not necessitate the effect, it is a fallacy.
In Usul al-Fiqh, attributing a ruling to an incidental property rather than the effective cause.
4
Distinguish essential from accidental
Do not confuse an accidental property with an essential definition. The remedy is to compare with the limits (حدود) of the conclusion.
Example: Assuming a physical property of a book is essential to its meaning.
5
Handle the remaining fallacies (illusion of converse, bad predicate, etc.)
For illusion of converse, observe the relation between extremes. For bad consideration of the predicate, preserve restrictions and qualifiers. The remedy for the fifth fallacy is to consider terms abstracted from their contexts; for the sixth, note the relation; for the seventh, preserve restrictions.
Each requires careful attention to the terms and their relationships.

When Illusion Leads Reason Astray: Two Examples
Author gives two examples where sensory illusion contradicts rational judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a syllogistic conclusion is overridden by a higher rational principle, and distinguish between illusion-based and reason-based judgments in Islamic epistemological arguments.

Core Idea
Reason can correct even a conclusion that both reason and illusion initially support.

Even when our reasoning and initial impressions agree, the fallible faculty of wahm (illusion) can lead us to a false conclusion. In this passage, the author gives two vivid examples where reason ultimately overrules what seems logically sound. Understanding this dynamic is crucial for anyone learning to distinguish reliable knowledge from mere appearances in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two examples
Read the Arabic and determine the two scenarios: a dead body and inflated skins.
The first example involves a dead person; the second involves air in inflated skins.
2
Extract the implicit syllogism in each example
For the dead body: Premise 1: The dead is inanimate. Premise 2: Everything inanimate does not deserve to be feared. Conclusion: The dead does not deserve to be feared. For the inflated skins: Premise 1: What is in inflated skins resists touch. Premise 2: Everything that resists touch is a body. Conclusion: What is in inflated skins (air) is a body.
The second example is a classic first-figure categorical syllogism.
3
Notice where illusion (wahm) intervenes
In both cases, wahm initially agrees with the premises but then leads to rejecting the conclusion in the first example, or rejecting the conclusion in the second example (illusion says air is not a body).
Wahm is a pre-rational faculty that makes quick judgments based on appearances.
4
See how reason overrules
In the first example, reason judges that fear is still deserved despite the syllogism, based on a higher principle (perhaps sanctity of the dead). In the second, reason affirms that air is a body, correcting the illusion.
Reason does not automatically accept syllogistic results if they conflict with deeper rational truths.
5
Generalize the epistemological lesson
The relationship between reason and illusion is not always straightforward; reason can override syllogistic conclusions when they contradict deeper rational or normative truths.
This is a key point in Islamic epistemology, especially in kalam.

This passage presents a concrete logical fallacy and then delves into the epistemological challenge of distinguishing necessary truths from falsehoods influenced by imagination. Two Types of Sophists: Sūfisṭā'ī and Mushāghibī
Classification of sophists by opponent and premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the sūfisṭā'ī and mushāghibī types of sophists based on their opponent and the kind of premises they use to deceive.

Core Idea
Sophists tailor deceptive premises to the epistemic standards of their target.

Not all fallacious arguers are the same. The Islamic logical tradition distinguishes between the sophist who targets a demonstrative philosopher (ḥakīm mubarhin) and the one who targets a dialectician (jadlī). Each uses different kinds of premises that mimic the premises their opponent would normally accept. Understanding this distinction helps you recognise the type of deception you face in a debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of al-mughāliṭ
In the Arabic text, two terms are introduced: 'sūfisṭā'ī' and 'mushāghibī'. Note that 'sūfisṭā'ī' is a transliteration of the Greek 'sophist', while 'mushāghibī' is an Arabic word meaning 'contentious' or 'quarrelsome'.
2
Determine the opponent for each type
The sūfisṭā'ī confronts the 'ḥakīm al-mubarhin' (demonstrative philosopher), and the mushāghibī confronts the 'jadlī' (dialectician). This difference in target determines the type of deception.
3
Identify the premises each uses
The sūfisṭā'ī uses premises that resemble 'wājib al-qubūl' (necessary-to-accept), which are certain truths like first principles. The mushāghibī uses premises that resemble 'mashhūr' (well-known) or 'musallam' (accepted) propositions—common opinions or concessions from the opponent.
The deception works because the premises appear to have the same epistemic status as what the opponent normally relies on.
4
Understand why these premises are effective
A demonstrative philosopher only accepts necessary truths, so the sūfisṭā'ī must offer premises that seem necessary. A dialectician accepts well-known or conceded premises, so the mushāghibī uses premises that seem to be of that kind. This makes the fallacy harder to detect for the targeted audience.
In Islamic debates, this classification helps you anticipate the kind of fallacy by knowing your opponent's method.
