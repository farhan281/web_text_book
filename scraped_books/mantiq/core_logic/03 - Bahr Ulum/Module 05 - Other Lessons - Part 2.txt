Avoiding the Extremes of Tanzih and Tashbih
Ibn Arabi's poetic guideline for theological balance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical pitfalls of exclusive tanzih or tashbih and formulate a balanced theological stance using Ibn Arabi's criteria.

Core Idea
God is both transcendent and immanent; denying either is a limitation.

Ibn Arabi's verse from the Fusus al-Hikam warns against two extremes: overemphasizing God's transcendence (tanzih) leads to restriction, and overemphasizing similarity (tashbih) leads to limitation. The middle path affirms both without contradiction. This lesson unpacks the logical structure of this teaching and shows how it grounds the doctrine of wahdat al-wujud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing positions
Find in the verse the references to tanzih-only (the Ash'arite view) and tashbih-only (the Mujassima view). Note that each is criticized as either 'restriction' (taqyid) or 'limitation' (tahdid).
Tanzih without immanence renders God distant and disconnected from creation.
2
Recognise the third option
The verse says: 'If you speak of both you are correct' (In qulta bi-l-amrayni kumta musaddadan). That is the balanced position: affirming both transcendence and immanence.
This is not a logical contradiction but a simultaneous affirmation of complementary attributes.
3
Understand the logical consequence
The verse concludes: 'You are not He, rather you are He' — indicating a distinction without separation. This is the core of wahdat al-wujud: creation is a manifestation, not identical to God, but not utterly separate either.
This avoids both pantheism (identity) and deism (complete separation).

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical defense of the doctrine of wahdat al-wujud (unity of being) as articulated by Ibn Arabi. It begins with a quote from the Fusus al-Hikam in verse form, which warns against both extreme transcendence (tanzih) that denies any relationship between God and creation, and extreme anthropomorphism (tashbih) that limits God. Instead, it advocates a middle path: affirming both transcendence and immanence. The author then presents three rational objections by the deniers: (1) it necessitates uniting the Necessary Being with contingent beings, which is impossible; (2) it unifies all existents in reality; (3) the manifestation of the One in multiplicity is impossible. The response first appeals to higher knowledge: this truth is known through prophetic revelation and mystical unveiling, not through ordinary discursive reason. The intermediate intellect may object, but the luminous intellect (al-'aql al-nurani) is not bound by that. A footnote clarifies the orthodox Ash'ari position that God is transcendent in a way that does not deny His attributes. Next, the author uses a logical analogy: just as the concept of one of two contradictories (e.g., existence/non-existence) is necessary while its individual instances are contingent, so too absolute existence is necessary while particular existences are contingent—this is not absurd. He then argues that the critics themselves believe in the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i), which is a single concept abstracted from many particulars. By the same token, there must be a single shared reality—existence itself—which is necessary and non-contingent. Hence existence is identical with the Necessary Existence, and all things are His manifestations. The second objection from the Shari'a is refuted by citing Quranic verses and a hadith where the Quraysh were astonished at the call to worship one God, showing that the unity of God—and by extension the unity of all existence in Him—is a core Islamic teaching, properly understood. The author implies that apparent polytheistic interpretations are due to misunderstanding the true tawhid.

The Logical Proof of the Unity of Existence
Rational proof of wahdat al-wujud using the natural universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound argument from the concept of the natural universal to demonstrate that existence is one necessary reality.

Core Idea
Just as a universal concept is one and applies to many, existence itself is one reality manifested in many beings.

The deniers of wahdat al-wujud raise three rational objections: it necessitates uniting the necessary with the possible, unifying all existents, and manifesting the one in multiplicity. The author responds first by appealing to higher knowledge, then with a rigorous logical argument. This lesson focuses on the second response, which uses the logical concept of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) to show that a single necessary existence can be shared by many contingent beings. You will learn to break down this compound argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first analogy
The author uses the concept of 'one of two contradictories' (mafhum ahad al-naqidayn). The concept as such is necessary (e.g., the concept of existence is necessary), but its individuals (each contradictory, like existence and non-existence) are possible. This shows that a necessary universal can have contingent individuals.
This refutes the objection that the necessary cannot be related to the possible.
2
Apply the natural universal
The author notes that most philosophers affirm the existence of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i), which is a single concept abstracted from many individuals. For example, 'human' is one universal applied to many humans. By analogy, existence itself must be a single universal reality that is shared by all existents.
This counters the objection that one thing cannot appear in many.
3
Draw the conclusion
Since existence is necessary (wajib) and non-contingent, it must be identical with the Necessary Existence (al-wujud al-wajib). Hence, the whole world is a manifestation of that one reality. This is the logical core of wahdat al-wujud.
The conclusion is that existence is one necessary reality, not many separate substances.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical defense of the doctrine of wahdat al-wujud (unity of being) as articulated by Ibn Arabi. It begins with a quote from the Fusus al-Hikam in verse form, which warns against both extreme transcendence (tanzih) that denies any relationship between God and creation, and extreme anthropomorphism (tashbih) that limits God. Instead, it advocates a middle path: affirming both transcendence and immanence. The author then presents three rational objections by the deniers: (1) it necessitates uniting the Necessary Being with contingent beings, which is impossible; (2) it unifies all existents in reality; (3) the manifestation of the One in multiplicity is impossible. The response first appeals to higher knowledge: this truth is known through prophetic revelation and mystical unveiling, not through ordinary discursive reason. The intermediate intellect may object, but the luminous intellect (al-'aql al-nurani) is not bound by that. A footnote clarifies the orthodox Ash'ari position that God is transcendent in a way that does not deny His attributes. Next, the author uses a logical analogy: just as the concept of one of two contradictories (e.g., existence/non-existence) is necessary while its individual instances are contingent, so too absolute existence is necessary while particular existences are contingent—this is not absurd. He then argues that the critics themselves believe in the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i), which is a single concept abstracted from many particulars. By the same token, there must be a single shared reality—existence itself—which is necessary and non-contingent. Hence existence is identical with the Necessary Existence, and all things are His manifestations. The second objection from the Shari'a is refuted by citing Quranic verses and a hadith where the Quraysh were astonished at the call to worship one God, showing that the unity of God—and by extension the unity of all existence in Him—is a core Islamic teaching, properly understood. Second Doubt on Predication: Infinite Regress and Its Refutation
Author presents a doubt that predication leads to infinite regress and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyse a logical doubt about predication leading to infinite regress and evaluate the refutation by identifying the fallacy in the necessitation claim.

Core Idea
Predication does not cause infinite regress because properties do not transfer from origin to derived.

In this passage, the author presents a second doubt against a particular view on predication: if predication is not as assumed, it would lead to self-predication and infinite regress. The commentator refutes this by showing that the occurrence of a property to the derivative origin does not necessitate its occurrence to the derived thing. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of the doubt and its critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hypothesis being denied
The doubt begins with 'if it were not so' (لَوْ لَمْ يَكُنْ كَذَلِكَ). Determine what exactly is being denied: the proper nature of predication as assumed in the original theory.
This sets up the conditional reasoning.
2

The doubt argues that if predication were not as assumed, then the thing would be predicated of itself, which necessitates the occurrence (عروض) of the derivative origin (مبدأ الاشتقاق) to it, and that in turn necessitates its occurrence to itself, leading to repetition in type – an infinite regress.
Follow the chain: self-predication → occurrence of origin → occurrence to itself → repetition.
3

The commentator responds by denying a key premise: 'the occurrence of the thing to the derivative origin necessitates its occurrence to the derived thing' is not granted. He provides a counterexample: origin-ness (المبدئية) and derivation (الاشتقاق) occur to the origin and the derived respectively without transferring.
This breaks the chain and refutes the regress.
4

Consider the pair 'origin' and 'derived'. The property of being an origin applies only to the origin, and the property of being derived applies only to the derived. They do not cross over. Thus, occurrence to one does not imply occurrence to the other.
The counterexample illustrates the logical error of assuming automatic transfer.

The Famous Doubt on Predication and its Resolution
Author presents a famous doubt that predication is impossible and resolves it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive argument against predication and articulate the solution using the notion of consideration under different aspects.

Core Idea
Subject and predicate are identical in reality but different in conception — that is predication.

One of the most famous doubts in Islamic logic concerns the very possibility of predication: if the predicate is identical to the subject, the proposition is uninformative; if different, it cannot be united. This lesson unpacks the doubt, analyzes the dialectical exchange between the doubter and the logician, and shows how the concept of 'without condition' resolves the paradox. By the end, you will see that predication rests on the ability to consider one thing under two aspects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunctive structure of the doubt
The doubt claims that predication is impossible because the predicate (J) must be either identical to the subject (B) or different; if identical, predication is uninformative; if different, predication is impossible because difference prevents union. Write this as a disjunctive syllogism.
The doubt uses the form: either J=B or J≠B; if J=B then not informative; if J≠B then impossible; therefore predication is impossible.
2
Analyze the initial self-refutation objection
The first answer states that the doubt itself uses predication (it predicates 'impossibility' of predication), so it refutes itself. Consider whether this objection works equally against a sophist and a genuine reasoner.
If the doubter is a sophist (al-mughālīṭ), the answer is valid; but if he is a reasoner (mustadill) sincerely denying predication, a deeper answer is needed.
3
Examine the reasoner's reformulation
The reasoner rephrases the claim as a negative proposition: 'Predication does not exist.' He supports it with an exceptional syllogism (qiyās istithnā'ī). Identify the premises of that syllogism from the text.
The text states: 'if it were possible, then either J is identical to B or different; and in either case it is either uninformative or impossible; the consequent is false, so the antecedent is likewise.' This is a modus tollens argument.
4
Apply the solution: identity in one respect, difference in another
The commentator explains that J and B are the same in quiddity (reality) but different in mental conception. Thus predication is possible: they are identical in essence yet distinct in the mind. The predicate must be considered 'without condition' (lā bi-sharṭ shay') to allow both aspects.
This solution resolves the doubt by showing that the disjunction 'either identical or different' is false when both are considered from different viewpoints.

The Rule of Attribution and Its Refinement
Author refines the logical rule of attribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the logical rule of 'thubut shai' li-shai' and describe the refinement that requires the subject to be actual, citing the counterexample of 'Zaid exists'.

Core Idea
Attribution of a property demands the subject's actual existence, not mere mental existence.

A cornerstone of logic is that a property cannot be ascribed to a non-existent subject. However, the simple rule faced a challenge: when we say 'Zaid exists', the subject's existence seems to precede the attribution itself. This led later scholars like al-Dawani and the author of our text to refine the principle, introducing the condition that the subject must be actual (fi'liyya) in the context of attribution. In this lesson, we'll walk through the original rule, the objection, and the subtle but powerful refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original rule
Read the first part: 'ثبوت شيء لشيء فرع لثبوت المثبت له'. This means that attributing a property to a subject presupposes the subject's existence. For example, 'Zaid is tall' requires Zaid to exist.
This is a foundational principle in logic and metaphysics.
2
Understand the counterexample
The passage mentions a counterexample: 'زيد موجود' (Zaid exists). Here, the property 'exists' is attributed to Zaid, but Zaid's existence is not prior to the attribution—it's the very thing being attributed. This challenges the necessity of the rule.
This counterexample forced a rethinking.
3
Trace al-Dawani's shift
Initially, al-Dawani claimed the rule was necessary. After the counterexample, he recanted and argued for 'istilzam' (entailment) rather than priority. This step shows the critical engagement with logical principles.
Al-Dawani's move preserves the rule by reinterpreting the relationship.
4
Grasp the author's refinement
The author, following the author of al-Ufuq al-Mubin, adds: 'في ظرف' (in a context) and 'فرع فعلية ما ثبت له وتقرره' (branch of the actuality and determination of that to which it is attributed). This means the subject must be actual (fi'liyya), not merely mentally considered. For 'Zaid exists', the subject Zaid is actual, so the rule holds if we consider the actuality of Zaid prior to the attribution.
The refinement avoids the counterexample by qualifying the kind of existence required.
5
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider the divine attribute 'alim' (knowing). In kalam, when we say 'Allah is knowing', the subject Allah must be actual (wujud haqiqi), not merely mental. This principle guards against attributing properties to a purely conceptual entity.
This shows the rule's application in theology.

والخصوص بالذات، فالعارض لأمر أخص عرض ذاتي لهذه المرتبة، وإن لم يكن عرضا ذاتيا للماهية من حيث العموم، بخلاف العارض لأمر أعم؛ فإن الأعم لوحدته المبهمة متحد مع كل خاص، فلا اختصاص له بهذا الأخص. وأنت لا يذهب عليك ما فيه أما :أولا فلما مر من عدم تحقق المرتبة الرابعة، وأما ثانيا: فلأنا سلمنا هذه المرتبة لكنها أعم من المخلوطة، والأخص مخلوط ، فأعراضه عارضة لها بواسطة الأخص، فكيف يكون عرضا ذاتيا ؟ وأما ثالثا: فلأنا سلمنا عدم اختصاص العارض لأمر أعم، لكن لا يلزم منه أن لا يكون عرضا ذاتيا ؛ لأن الاختصاص غير مشروط فيه. وأما رابعا: فلأن موضوع المسألة إذا كان عرضا ذاتيا أو نوعه أو عرضه جاز أن يكون المحمول جنس الموضوع ؛ كما نص عليه الشيخ فكيف يكون عرضا ذاتيا لماهية الموضوع من حيث هي ؟ وإن أخذت لا لا بشرط شيء فإنه ذاتي، بمعنى المقوم لها، فافهم.
Conclusion of the book. And specificity by essence, so what accidentally applies to a more specific thing is an essential accident for that rank, even if it is not an essential accident for the quiddity in terms of generality, unlike what accidentally applies to a more general thing; for the more general, due to its vague oneness, is united with every specific, so it has no exclusivity to this specific. And you should not be oblivious to what is in it: As for first, due to what has passed of the non-realization of the fourth rank; and as for second: because we concede this rank but it is more general than the mixed, and the more specific is mixed, so its accidents are accidental to it via the more specific, so how can it be essential? And as for third: because we concede the lack of exclusivity of what applies to a more general thing, but it does not follow that it is not an essential accident; because exclusivity is not a condition for it. And as for fourth: because if the subject of the question is an essential accident or its species or its accident, it is permissible for the predicate to be the genus of the subject, as the Sheikh has stated; so how can it be an essential accident for the quiddity of the subject as such? And if it is taken as not-conditioned-by-anything, then it is essential, meaning constituting it, so understand.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the standard doctrine of the four ranks (maratib) of essential accidents. The commentator raises four objections against the classification. First, the fourth rank does not actually exist, as previously argued. Second, even if we grant the fourth rank, it is more general than the mixed (makhluṭa) rank, and the accidents of the more specific (the mixed) are only attached to the fourth rank via that specific, so they are not essential to the fourth rank itself. Third, the lack of exclusivity of an accident to the subject does not disqualify it from being essential, because exclusivity is not a condition for essentiality. Fourth, in a scientific problem, the predicate (the accident being investigated) may be the genus of the subject, so it cannot be an essential accident of the subject's quiddity. The commentator then notes that if the subject is taken as not-conditioned (la bi-sharṭ), then the predicate is indeed essential in the sense of being constitutive. This shows the precision of the commentator in examining the foundations of the science.

Key Takeaway
Essential accidents are defined by their relation to the subject's quiddity, not by exclusivity; the four-rank classification must be carefully qualified.
