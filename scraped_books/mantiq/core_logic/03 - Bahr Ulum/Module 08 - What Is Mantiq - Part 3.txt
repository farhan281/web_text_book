Tamthil Yields Certainty: A Defense of Analogical Reasoning
Author defends tamthil's certainty against Ibn Sina and al-Tusi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument that tamthil (juristic qiyas) can yield certainty by reducing to categorical syllogism, and evaluate the critique of Ibn Sina and al-Tusi.

Core Idea
Tamthil yields certainty not despite its analogical form but because it is a disguised categorical syllogism with certain premises.

In this passage, the author defends analogical reasoning (tamthil) against the charge of being merely presumptive. He argues that tamthil reduces to the categorical syllogism, and its epistemic value depends solely on the certainty of its premises. He attacks Ibn Sina and al-Tusi for failing to see this, accusing them of poor understanding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Ibn Sina and al-Tusi claim that tamthil is inherently presumptive (zanni) and weaker than induction. They deny it can yield certainty. Find where the author states this.
Look at the part: 'وزعم ابن سينا والطوسي المتشيع وبعضهم شددوا وقالوا دون الاستقراء'
2
Extract the author's counter-argument
The author asserts that tamthil reduces to categorical syllogism (qiyas mantiqi). Therefore, its certainty depends on its premises. Find the statement 'فإنه راجع إلى القياس'.
3
Analyze the conditional example
The author gives a conditional: if the judgment of a particular (asl) is caused by a certain cause, and that cause is certainly present in another particular (far'), then the judgment applies with certainty. This is essentially a Barbara syllogism: All C are H; A is C; therefore A is H. Identify the terms.
The cause ('illa) serves as the middle term.
4
Evaluate the critique's validity
The author dismisses Ibn Sina and al-Tusi as lacking understanding. Consider whether the reduction to syllogism is always valid. Does the presence of the 'illa guarantee the transfer of the ruling? Are there conditions (like munasaba) that must be met? Reflect on the practical limitations.
In usul al-fiqh, qiyas requires a sound 'illa; not all shared attributes are valid causes.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of analogical reasoning (tamthil) against the criticism of Ibn Sina and Nasir al-Din al-Tusi. The author, commenting on the matn line 'al-tamthil yufid al-qat'', explains that tamthil is reasoning from one particular to another due to a shared attribute. Jurists call this qiyas, using terms 'asl (original), far' (branch), and 'illa (cause). Some scholars, including Ibn Sina and al-Tusi, considered tamthil merely presumptive (zanni) and weaker than induction. The author vehemently rejects this, accusing them of misunderstanding. He argues that tamthil reduces to categorical syllogism (qiyas mantiqi). If its premises are certain, it yields certainty; if presumptive, it yields presumption. The author provides a clear example: if we know with certainty that a judgment on one particular is caused by a cause that is also certainly present in another particular, the judgment must apply with certainty. He further notes that jurists often rule tamthil presumptive only because its premises are usually presumptive, but in principle it can be certain. The passage ends with a strong rebuke of those who weaken tamthil, deeming them unfit for scholarly discourse.

Empirical Science (Tajriba) vs. Induction: The Causal Distinction
Author defines empirical science and distinguishes it from induction and intuition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given observation-based judgment qualifies as empirical science (tajriba) or remains induction (istiqra') by checking for causal transition.

Core Idea
Tajriba leaps to the cause, induction stays on the surface.

In the Islamic logical tradition, empirical knowledge (tajriba) is a foundational source of certainty, but it is often confused with mere induction. This passage from the sharh of Sullam al-'Ulum clarifies the essential difference: tajriba involves a transition (intiqal) to the cause (al-'illa) or its concomitant, while induction merely observes repeated instances without grasping the causal link. Understanding this distinction is critical for evaluating the epistemic strength of arguments about the natural world and the rulings of Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term 'التجربة' in the Arabic passage and note its definition as a type of certain premise. Recognize that it is being defined in contrast to induction.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'observation of most individuals of a universal with a judgment' (مشاهدة أكثر أفراد كلي موصوف بحكم). The differentia is 'transition to the cause or its concomitance' (الانتقال إلى العلة أو ملازمتها).
This is the key distinguishing feature.
3
Contrast with induction (istiqra')
Find the description of الاستقراء: it also observes many individuals but without causal transition. It yields only conjecture (ظن). Notice the example of 'every mover is raised'.
The absence of causal inference makes induction non-certain.
4
Analyze the example of scammony
Trace the reasoning: scammony (السقمونيا) is observed to cause bile expulsion in most cases where the matter is receptive. The mind then moves to the conclusion that its nature is a cause or concomitant, leading to a universal judgment: 'Every scammony in our country is a bile-expellant.'
This illustrates the transition to the cause.

Commentary
This passage defines the empirical science (tajriba) as a type of certain premise, distinguishing it from both induction (istiqra') and intuition (hads). The author begins with an example: scammony (al-saqmuniya), a plant whose consumption is observed to cause bile expulsion in most cases where the matter is receptive. The mind then moves to the conclusion that its nature is either a cause or concomitant of the cause, yielding the judgment that every scammony in our region expels bile. This is empirical knowledge. The author differentiates experiment from induction: induction observes many individuals under a universal without grasping the causal link, leading only to a conjectural judgment (e.g., 'every mover is raised'). Experiment, by contrast, involves a transition to the cause (al-'illa) or its concomitance, producing certain knowledge. Nasir al-Din al-Tusi is quoted equating hads (intuition) and tajriba, except that in experiment the cause is known generally (ijmalan), so it applies to all cases, whereas in intuition the cause is known specifically (bi-khususiha), each requiring a separate syllogism—a view the author rejects. The author then addresses the Ash'ari objection that cutting the neck does not cause death per se, but death is created by Allah at the moment of cutting. The response is that no rational person doubts the co-occurrence (al-iqtiran) of cutting and death; the debate is about the necessity of the link, not the link itself. Doubting this is obsessional. The passage concludes with a note: true knowledge is with Allah. Several scholars disputed whether empirical sciences are certainties, confusing them with induction and intuition. The author asserts that denying empirical sciences is akin to denying necessary truths, and intuition's certainty is known only to those with intuition. This passage is an expansion (sharh) of the matn of Sullam al-'Ulum, explaining the nature of empirical premises and their epistemological status. The example of scammony illustrates medical knowledge derived from repeated observation with causal inference. In fiqh, a similar reasoning applies: observing that wine intoxicates in many instances leads to the rule that all wine is intoxicating due to the 'illa of intoxication.

Why the Predicate of a Demonstrative Problem Cannot Be a Definition
Author defines mas'ala and explains why its predicate cannot be a definition of the subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the predicate of a demonstrative problem cannot be a real definition of its subject and identify the two problematic alternatives for the middle term.

Core Idea
The predicate of a problem cannot be its subject's definition, else the proof fails.

In this lesson, we examine the first investigation of demonstration. The author defines 'mas'ala' as the conclusion sought by demonstration and then lays down a crucial rule: the predicate of a demonstrative problem cannot be a real definition (hadd) of the subject. This is not arbitrary; it follows from the nature of demonstration and the requirement that the middle term be more evident than the conclusion. We will unpack the reasoning behind this rule and see why two alternative candidates for the middle term are both impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The author states that the predicate of a demonstrative problem cannot be a definition of the subject. Locate this statement in the Arabic and note the term 'mas'ala'.
The rule is given immediately after defining mas'ala.
2
Understand the conditional reasoning
If the predicate were a definition, then the middle term (al-awsat) that proves it would have to be either a special property (khassa) or another definition. Why would that be? Because in a categorical syllogism, the middle term must be causally related to the predicate.
The author uses a disjunctive condition: either khassa or another hadd.
3
Refute the first option (khassa)
The special property is less evident than the definition, so it cannot serve as a middle term to prove the definition. The definition should be better known than the property.
The author says 'the opposite is the case' – the definition is more evident.
4
Refute the second option (another definition)
A subject can have only one real definition. If there were multiple definitions, they would be either equivalent (redundant) or contradictory. So a second definition is impossible.
The author calls this 'necessarily false' (bāṭil bi’l-ḍarūra).
5
Consider the qualification
The rule applies when the subject is conceived in its essence (bi’l-kunn). If the subject is not fully understood, the essential may not be evident.
This nuance is referenced from al-Talwīḥāt and al-Shams al-Bāzigha.

If the aspect is accidental, the definition becomes like an accident.

Why can the predicate of a demonstrative problem not be a real definition of the subject?
A) Because definitions are self-evident and need no proof
B) Because the middle term would be either a special property or another definition, both leading to problems
C) Because definitions are always true
D) Because the predicate must be an accident

Key Takeaway
The predicate of a demonstrative problem cannot be its subject's definition, because that would make the middle term either less evident or redundant, breaking the conditions of a valid demonstration.
