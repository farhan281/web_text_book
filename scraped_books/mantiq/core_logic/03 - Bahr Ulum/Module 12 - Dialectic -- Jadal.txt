The Socratic Paradox and Its Resolution
Author presents the Socratic paradox of inquiry and resolves it, leading to the need for logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Socratic paradox, articulate its solution as presented in the text, and explain how it justifies the need for a protecting science of logic.

Core Idea
The Socratic paradox shows that inquiry requires partial knowledge, making a protective canon (logic) indispensable.

You have likely experienced the frustration of studying a subject: how can you seek what you don't yet know? This lesson examines a classic puzzle attributed to Socrates that challenges the very possibility of inquiry. We will see how the author resolves it by introducing the notion of 'aspect' (wajh) and then connects this resolution to the necessity of logic as a protective law against error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Paradox
The text presents a doubt: the sought object is either known (making search pointless) or unknown (making search impossible). Recognize this as a dilemma.
2
Examine the Initial Answer
The initial response: it is known in one respect (wajh) and unknown in another. Understand that this attempts to split the object into aspects.
3
Analyze the Objection
Socrates retorts: the known aspect is known, the unknown unknown, so the split doesn't help. See why this objection seems to reinstate the dilemma.
4
Grasp the Final Resolution
The resolution: the unknown aspect is not absolutely unknown; its known aspect is its aspect. This means we have partial knowledge of the same reality under some considerations. The sought object is the reality known in some respects; we seek further understanding.
5
Connect to the Need for Logic
Because not every arrangement of thought is sound (as seen in conflicting opinions), a protecting canon (logic) is required to guide inquiry safely.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic epistemological paradox attributed to Socrates. The problem is: if we seek something, we either already know it (making the search pointless) or we don't know it (making the search impossible because we wouldn't recognize it). The initial reply—that it is known in one respect and unknown in another—is challenged: the known part is known, the unknown part unknown. The resolution (from the matn) explains that the unknown aspect is not absolutely unknown; the known aspect is its aspect, meaning we have partial knowledge. The sought object is a reality known in some respects, and we seek to know it more fully. The author then transitions to the need for logic: because not every arrangement of thought is sound (as seen in conflicting opinions), a law (qanun) to prevent error is necessary, and that is logic.

Objector's Syllogism on a Partner to God
Author rebuts an objection about a partner to God with a syllogism analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categorical syllogism in a kalam objection and evaluate its validity using the author's rebuttal.

Core Idea
Hypothetical composition does not remove real impossibility.

In kalam arguments about the impossibility of a partner to God, an objector presents a syllogism: the composite of two partners is a partner, that composite is composite, and every composite is possible, so some partner is possible. The author denies the major premise, arguing that hypothetical composition does not negate real impossibility. Let's dissect this dialectical exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's syllogism
Extract the three propositions: (1) The composite of two partners is a partner (minor premise). (2) Some partner (that composite) is composite (implied). (3) Every composite is possible (major premise). Conclusion: Some partner is possible. This contradicts the claim that every partner is impossible.
The syllogism is in Darii mood (first figure): Some partner is composite, all composites are possible → some partner is possible.
2
Find the author's rebuttal premise
The author denies the major premise 'every composite is possible' because the composite's dependence on hypothetical existence does not harm its real impossibility. Thus the premise is false.
3
Evaluate the logical validity
The syllogism is formally valid, but the author shows it is unsound because a premise is false. This highlights the difference between form and truth.
The objector's mistake is treating hypothetical composition as a sign of real possibility.
4
Understand the deeper point
Impossibility (mumtani') is essential; it does not depend on actual existence. The composite partner is impossible in reality (nafs al-amr) even if we assume its hypothetical composition.
This protects tawhid from sophistical arguments.

Resolving Infinite Regress with I'tibari
Author resolves an infinite regress objection using mental consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how infinite regress in mental considerations is resolved by the concept of i'tibari.

Core Idea
Infinite regress stops when we stop considering.

The objector raises a second challenge: if two partners entail a third composite, then adding that composite yields a fourth, ad infinitum. The author resolves this by distinguishing between real things and mental considerations (i'tibari). Let's examine this elegant solution.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objector's infinite regress argument
If the existence of two partners entails a third (the composite), then that third combined with the first two yields a fourth, and so on without end. This would make the original two partners impossible because they produce an infinite chain.
2
Identify the author's distinction
The author says the fourth and subsequent composites are i'tibari (mental considerations), not real things. They exist only in the mind after considering the same thing twice.
I'tibari is a key term in kalam and logic for mental constructs.
3
Analyze the 'i'tibari' concept
The fourth composite is obtained by considering the first composite again along with the original two. It is not a new real entity; it is a mental repetition. This cuts the regress because mental acts do not generate real infinite objects.
4
Apply the cut-off
The infinite regress (tasalsul) in mental considerations is not problematic because it is not an actual infinite chain of real beings. When the act of consideration stops, the regress stops.
This is a standard way to avoid an infinite regress in kalam arguments.

One Thing Having Two Contradictories? Resolving the Doubt
Author addresses a doubt and resolves it by distinguishing types of negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between simple negation and double negation, and resolve the apparent dilemma of one proposition having two contradictories.

Core Idea
A double negation is not a simple negation—it is an affirmative in disguise.

A doubt arises: if affirmation contradicts negation, and negating a negation removes it, doesn’t the original negation have two contradictors? The author resolves this by showing that the negation of negation is actually an affirmative proposition, not a mere negative. This distinction preserves the principle of contradiction and illuminates the logic of double negation in Islamic dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt
The passage states: affirmation (ījāb) is the contradictory of negation (salb), and negation of negation (salb al-salb) removes it. So one thing (the original negation) would have two contradictories: the affirmation and the double negation.
This seems to violate the principle that each proposition has only one contradictory.
2
Spot the error
Some might think the double negation is a second contradictory, but the author warns against identifying the two by the same concept. The concepts differ necessarily.
3
Understand the solution
The author explains that negation properly applies only to existence (in itself or for another). Negating a negation removes the existence of the negation, resulting in an affirmative proposition, not a simple negative.
4
Apply the distinction
Recognize that the double negative yields either an affirmative with a negative subject (e.g., 'Some non-A is B') or an affirmative with a negative predicate (e.g., 'Some A is not not-B'). Thus it is not a simple negative.
The double negative is not a contradictory of the original negation in the same sense.
5
Verify the resolution
Check that the original negation now has only one contradictory (the simple affirmative), not two. The double negative is a different kind of proposition.

Dissecting the Dialectic on Celestial Motion
Dialectical objection and three-chains answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical argument about the cause of motion's renewal and identify how the three chains of imaginings, desires, and revolutions avoid regress.

Core Idea
Motion's renewal is explained by an eternal cycle of imagining, desire, and revolution, not regress.

This passage presents a classic objection-answer sequence from a logic commentary addressing a key problem in Islamic philosophy: how the celestial sphere's motion can be both eternal and continuously renewing. The dialectic moves through several logical steps, culminating in a three-chain solution. By following the moves, you will see how Islamic logicians used structured debate to refine explanations of natural phenomena.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The text begins with an objection: 'What is the cause of motion's renewal (tajaddud)?' The initial answer is that motion is self-renewing—it does not require an external maker. Locate this claim in the Arabic.
The phrase 'تجددها لذاتها' (renewal through itself) is the first thesis.
2
Trace the first objection and answer
The objector argues: parts of motion are temporally originated (ḥāditha), so each needs a cause. If the cause is eternal, parts are eternal; if temporal, infinite regress. The answer: motion parts are non-existent due to continuity, so no need for a cause. Understand the logic: continuous motion has no discrete parts that can be singled out as originated.
Key term: 'أجزاء الحركة غير موجودة لاتصالها' (parts of motion are non-existent due to their continuity).
3
Recognize the second objection
A further objection: motion is non-stable (ghayr qārr), so it cannot be caused by a stable entity. Hence the cause itself must be non-stable, leading to an infinite regress of causes. See how this builds on the first objection.
The phrase 'الحركة غير قارة' (motion is non-stable) is the crux.
4
Understand the three-chains solution
The solution posits three simultaneous series: (1) particular imaginings (takhayyulāt), (2) wills and desires (irādāt wa ashwāq), (3) revolutions (dawrāt). Each imagining causes a desire, which prepares a revolution; that revolution prepares another imagining, and so on. Note how the chains interlock: each level serves a different causal role (efficient cause, preparatory cause).
The text: 'فإن ههنا ثلاث سلاسل: الأولى من التخييلات الجزئية، والثانية من الإرادات والأشواق، والثالثة من الدورات'.
5
Verify how the solution avoids regress
Since the three series are co-eternal and interdependent, they form a circle of causation that is self-sustaining without an external starter. This avoids both an eternal cause for temporal parts and an infinite regress of causes. Ask: why is this not a vicious circle? Because each chain is distinct in nature (mental, volitional, physical).
The solution is essentially a 'non-vicious circular' causation that the philosophers used for eternal motion.

The Mutakallimun on Free Will and Causal Delayed Effect
States the mutakallimun's position on causal delay and defends against objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the mutakallimun's argument that the effect can lag behind the complete cause when the agent is free, and explain their defense against the objection that a preponderating factor is needed for the timing of the effect.

Core Idea
Free will allows the effect to lag without a new preponderating factor – the lag itself is the preponderance.

This passage presents a key dialectical exchange in Islamic kalām: can the effect be delayed when the cause is complete and the agent is free? The philosophers argued that a complete cause necessitates its effect immediately, while the mutakallimun, to preserve divine free will, allowed a temporal gap. The passage explains the mutakallimun's defense, including the concept of possibility as a non-extracted attribute, and answers an objection about the need for a preponderating factor for the specific time. Understanding this argument is essential for grasping the kalām position on divine attributes and human agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core position of the mutakallimun
The passage states that the mutakallimun allow the effect (maʿlūl) to lag behind the complete cause (ʿillah tāmmah) when the agent is a free chooser (fāʿil mukhtār). Note that this directly contradicts the philosophical principle of necessary simultaneity.

The author says the preponderance is only 'a preponderance of existence of the lag' (رجحان الوجود التخلف). This means no separate preponderating factor is needed; the very act of choosing with free will provides the preponderance.
This addresses the philosophers' objection of preponderance without a preponderating factor.

Examine the second defense using possibility (imkān) as iʿtibārī
Some mutakallimun argue that possibility enters into the complete cause. Possibility is an extracted (iʿtibārī) attribute with no external existence, so it does not require an existent cause; otherwise circularity would arise.
This defends the assumption of complete cause even with delayed effect.
4
Analyze the objection and the answer about time
The objection states that for God's will in pre-eternity to specify a particular future time, a preponderating factor is needed. The mutakallimun answer that time is not an existent entity but an imagined progression (amr mutawahham), so no additional factor is needed.
This is a classic kalām rebuttal to the problem of temporal specification.

Commentary

The inquiry is not a repetition because the specific attribute knowledge is new, nor is it impossible because the general concept of God is known.

Using I'tibar to Resolve the Universal Paradox

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve the paradox of universal self-predication using the concept of i'tibar (consideration) as taught in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Differing considerations allow opposite judgments without contradiction — the key to solving many logical and theological puzzles.

The passage presents an objection that seems to show a fatal flaw in the doctrine of universals: if a universal is an individual of itself, it would be both itself and other, leading to a contradiction. Identify the objector's argument
The objector says: The universal is an individual of itself. The individual is different from that of which it is an individual. Therefore the universal is different from itself. Recognize the logical tension
The objector treats the universal as a single entity with a single aspect. The contradiction arises because he conflates two different aspects: the universal as a concept and the universal as an instance.

Essential vs. Accidental Predication in Modal Transfer
Author presents and refutes an objection about the necessity of possibility/impossibility transfer from individual to nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and accidental predication when transferring modal properties from individuals to universal natures, and apply this distinction to resolve apparent contradictions in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Properties of individuals transfer to the nature only accidentally, not essentially.

This passage presents a classic dialectical move in Islamic logic: an objector argues that if impossibility is established for an individual, it must also hold for its universal nature, leading to a contradiction with the nature's essential possibility. The author's reply reveals a crucial distinction between essential and accidental predication—a tool that prevents false inferences in theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
Find the objector's argument: what is established for an individual must be established for the nature. Note that he specifically mentions impossibility (imtina') as the property in question.

2
Locate the author's response
The author concedes the transfer but qualifies it: 'even if accidentally' (wa law bil-'arad). Identify the terms 'bil-'arad' (accidentally) and 'bil-dhat' (essentially).
The author does not deny the transfer; he limits its mode.

The nature has possibility essentially (imkan bil-dhat). The individual has impossibility accidentally (imtina' bil-'arad). Realise that these are not contradictory because they belong to different levels of predication.

The objector assumed that if impossibility transfers, it would conflict with possibility. The author shows that accidental impossibility does not negate essential possibility—they can coexist in the same nature.
This is a standard logical move. The same reasoning is used in Kalam to defend divine attributes.

This is a standard move in Islamic logic to resolve apparent contradictions when properties transfer between levels. The Peripatetic Thesis and Its First Defense
Dialectical exchange on determination and individuality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Peripatetic thesis on determination and analyze the dialectical exchange of objection and answer.

Core Idea
A nature that necessitates determination cannot have multiple individuals.

This passage presents a classic dialectical exercise in Islamic logic: if a nature (e.g., an intellect) necessitates its own determination, can it exist in more than one individual? The Peripatetics say no, an objector challenges this, and the Peripatetics respond. Understanding this exchange sharpens your ability to follow and critique arguments in the Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis
What claim do the Peripatetics make about natures like the intellects? They say that whenever determination (al-taʿayyun) is among the necessitations of a nature, that nature's quiddity is confined to one individual.
The example given is 'intellects', which are simple, immaterial substances.
2
Extract the objection
The objector argues that the only necessity is that the nature and its determination coexist in reality (fī nafs al-amr), not that they be in one individual. So the universal could exist in many individuals, each with its own determination, without being separated from determination.
The objector distinguishes between logical coexistence and numerical identity.

Find the answer
The Peripatetics clarify: they mean only those natures that necessitate the attachment of a specific determination. If such a nature existed in another individual, it would acquire a different determination, and then the first determination would not be necessitated—the necessitator would be separated from the necessitated.
The answer hinges on the specific necessitation: if determination is necessitated, it must be unique.
4
Assess the dialectic
Does the answer successfully rebut the objection? The answer blocks the objection by narrowing the scope to natures that necessitate determination. However, the deeper issue of whether a nature can truly necessitate determination remains unresolved, leading to the next objection.

The Second Objection: Nature Cannot Necessitate Determination
Second objection: commentator challenges the intelligibility of nature necessitating determination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the second objection against the Peripatetic view and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
A nature cannot necessitate determination because necessitation implies priority, leading to circularity.

After the first objection is answered, a more fundamental problem is raised: How can a quiddity (mahiyya) necessitate its own determination? The commentator presents two arguments: a temporal/causal priority issue and the universal's equal relation to all determinations. This objection challenges the very foundation of the Peripatetic thesis.

Guided Path
1
State the new objection
The commentator objects that the quiddity's necessitation of determination is 'unintelligible' (ghayr maʿqūl). He gives two reasons: first, the necessitator must precede the necessitated, which leads to the determination of a thing before its determination; second, the universal relates equally to all determinations, so it cannot necessitate one.
The phrase 'ghayr maʿqūl' is strong: it means rationally impossible or incoherent.
2
Analyze the priority problem
The necessitator (muqtaḍī) must be logically or causally prior to its effect (muqtaḍā). If the quiddity necessitates its own determination, then the quiddity must already have some determination to be prior, leading to an infinite regress or circularity.
This is similar to the problem of the 'first determination' in metaphysics.
3
Analyze the equal relation argument
The universal (al-kullī) has an equal relation to all possible determinations. If it were to necessitate one particular determination, that would break the symmetry, but there is no reason for one over another without an external cause.
This is a version of the principle of sufficient reason.
4
Overall assessment
This second objection strikes at the core assumption that natures can necessitate their own individuals. It suggests that a nature requires an external cause to receive a particular determination. Thus, the Peripatetic position is left without a clear defense in this passage.
The passage ends with 'wa-Allāhu aʿlam', leaving the issue open.

Doubt, Entailment, and Collective Sums in Dialectic
Author analyzes a jadal exchange on doubt and entailment in collective sums.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a jadal exchange on whether doubt about individuals entails doubt about the collective, identifying the key moves and distinctions.

Core Idea
Doubt about individuals does not entail doubt about the collective; only collective doubt entails the consequent.

This passage from Bahr Ulum on Sullam Ulum presents a dialectical debate about whether doubt regarding multiple individuals transfers to the collective sum. The author expands on a matn, navigating objections and refining the distinction between certainty and doubt. By following this exchange, you will learn how careful logical distinctions resolve apparent contradictions in entailment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Initial Claim
Find the claim that doubt about the Muslimness of two individuals entails doubt about the disbelief of the third. Note that this is based on the assumption that the Muslimness of two is the entailer (malzum) and the disbelief of the third is the entailment (lazim).
The proponent argues that doubt about the entailer must transfer to the entailment.
2
Recognize the First Solution
The solution distinguishes between doubt about each individual separately and doubt about the combination of the two. The true entailer is the sum (majmu') of the two, not each individually. Since we only have doubt about each individual, not about the combination, no entailment follows.
This hinges on the idea that the parts do not constitute the whole for the purpose of entailment.
3
Follow the Objection
An objection is raised: if each individual exists, then the sum exists. So doubt about each should entail doubt about the sum. This challenges the solution.
The objector assumes existence and doubt transfer similarly.
4
Analyze the Refinement with Sum Types
The refinement distinguishes between a distributed sum (مجموع انتشاري) and a collective sum (مجموع اجتماعي). Only the collective observation of individuals together serves as the entailer. In this case, the doubt is about individuals distributively, not collectively, so the entailment does not hold.
This distinction is crucial to resolve the objection.
5
Evaluate Certainty vs. Doubt
The author contrasts certainty (yaqin) with doubt (zann). For certainty, even distributed certainty of the two entails certainty of the third's disbelief. However, for doubt, only doubt about the collective sum entails doubt about the entailment. This asymmetry is justified by the principle of majority (qa'idat al-aghlabiyya) applied to individuals, not sums.
The principle of majority applies to each individual, not the collective whole.

Commentary
This passage presents a jadal (dialectical) exchange regarding the logical relationship between doubt (ظن) and certainty (يقين) in a scenario involving three individuals, where the Muslimness of two is believed to entail the disbelief of the third. The author expands on a matn from Sullam al-Ulum.

The passage begins by stating the problem: If you have doubt that two individuals are Muslims, then you would also have doubt that the remaining one is a disbeliever, because the Muslimness of the two (the entailer) is assumed to entail the disbelief of the third (the entailment). The proponent argues that since we have doubt about the entailer, we must also have doubt about the entailment, which leads to each individual being suspected of disbelief, contradicting the initial assumption.

The first solution (حل) points out that when the entailer consists of two things, doubt about the entailment requires doubt that both are actualized together (مجموع), not doubt about each individually. Because the true entailer is the combination of the two, not each separately. Here, only doubt about each individual is present, not doubt about their combination. Therefore, no problem arises.

An objection (أقول) follows: The existence of the third is necessary for the existence of the two. Since the respondent conceded the existence of each of the two individually, the combination exists as well, so doubt about the combination is also present.

Then a further objection (فإن قلت) distinguishes between two types of sums: distributed (انتشار) and collective (اجتماع). It claims that only the collective observation of the individuals together entails the disbelief of the third. In this case, we only have distributed observation (looking at each individually), so the entailment does not hold.

The respondent replies (قلت) that certainty of the third (the disbelief of the rest) is entailed by certainty of the Muslimness of the two, regardless of whether the observation is distributed or collective. Both types of certainty entail the disbelief of the rest. However, the passage then points out a crucial difference between certainty and doubt. The alleged difference between the two forms (distributed vs. collective) is only in consideration (اعتبار), but in the case of doubt, the situation is different: doubt about the entailer (the Muslimness of two) does not entail doubt about the entailment (the disbelief of the rest) because doubt about the combination requires doubt about both together, which is not present.

The author then invokes the principle of majority (قاعدة الأغلبية), which states that the ruling of each individual follows the majority. However, this principle does not apply to the collective whole; the individual whole and the collective whole are separate in ruling. In the given example, one doubts the Muslimness of each individual separately, not the sum of two. Therefore, the entailer for doubt of the third's disbelief is the individual doubt, not the collective doubt, so the doubt is resolved.

Finally, the author suggests a descent (تنزل): If the weaker (doubt) disappears in the presence of the stronger (certainty), then the determination of disbelief of one invalidates the doubt about the Muslimness of all, analogous to how definitive text (نص قاطع) invalidates analogy (قياس). This shows that the principle of majority is overridden in some cases.

This passage illustrates the careful logical analysis of entailment in contexts of certainty and doubt, a key feature of Islamic dialectic.

The Three Conditions for a Universal Premise
Author enumerates three conditions for a premise to be universal when the conclusion is universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a universal premise in Islamic logic and explain how each condition is necessary.

Core Idea
A universal premise must cover all individuals, at all times, with a primary accident.

In the previous lesson, we learned about universality in a general sense. Now, the author specifies three precise conditions that a premise must meet to be truly universal. These conditions ensure that the judgment applies to the entire subject permanently and that the predicate is not accidental in a way that would break universality. Understanding these conditions is crucial for constructing valid syllogisms, especially in demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
From the Arabic, extract the three conditions: (1) the judgment encompasses all individuals of the subject, (2) the judgment is permanent during the time of the description, (3) the predicates are primary accidents.
The text explicitly lists these three.
2
Understand the first condition: coverage of individuals
The judgment must apply to every single individual of the subject. If it applies to only some, the premise is not universal. Example: 'Every human is mortal' covers all humans, but 'Some humans are scholars' does not.
3
Understand the second condition: permanence in time
The judgment must hold true for the entire duration of the time that the subject is described. If it only holds for part of the time, universality fails. Example: 'Every lunar eclipse occurs when the earth is between the sun and moon' is always true, but 'Every eclipse is followed by a clear sky' is not permanent.
This is related to the 'time of the description' – the attribute being predicated.
4
Understand the third condition: primary accident
The predicate must be a primary accident (arad awwali). This means the predicate is co-extensive with the subject or equal to it, not a broader or accidental attribute. The Shaykh clarifies that it includes both what is established by itself and what equals the subject. Example: 'Every human is rational' – 'rational' is co-extensive with 'human'.
If the predicate is accidentally true only through a more general concept, universality is invalidated. E.g., 'Every human is breathing' is not a primary accident because 'breathing' is not co-extensive with 'human' (it can be negated).
5
Apply the conditions to a sample premise
Take a premise like 'Every Muslim prays'. Does it satisfy all three conditions? (1) All Muslims? Yes. (2) Permanently? No, Muslims pray only at certain times. (3) Is 'prays' a primary accident? No, it is not co-extensive with 'Muslim' (some Muslims may not pray). So it fails universality.
This exercise reinforces the need for all three conditions to be met.

Core Idea

Commentary
This passage defines the art of dialectic (صناعة الجدل). It begins by distinguishing the dialectical syllogism (قياس جدلي) from other syllogisms: its premises can be either commonplaces or postulates, illustrated by the jurist's acceptance that 'the command indicates obligation' as a postulate. The art itself is a faculty for composing such syllogisms. Its purpose is twofold: either to force the opponent to accept a conclusion (إلزام الخصم) or to preserve one's own opinion (حفظ الرأي), even if that opinion aims to demolish another. The sought result is called a 'position' (وضع), distinct from a 'problem' in demonstration. The predicate of the position can be a definition, essential (genus/differentia), essential accident, or strange accident. Unlike demonstration, dialectic has no fixed subject matter; it deals with any topic. Crucially, the form must produce a conclusion that is acceptable to the opponent (commonplace or postulate), not necessarily true in reality. Similarly, premises must be accepted by the opponent, not necessarily genuine. This explains dialectic's nature as an art of persuasion rather than proof. The commentary uses an Islamic example: the jurist accepting a usul al-fiqh principle as a postulate.

Checkpoint
According to the passage, what is one necessary condition for the premises of a dialectical syllogism?
A) They must be self-evident.
B) They must be commonplaces or postulates accepted by the opponent.
C) They must be essential accidents.
D) They must be derived from first principles.

Key Takeaway
Dialectic is defined by its reliance on premises accepted by the opponent, not by their truth, and its purpose is either to force the opponent or preserve an opinion.
