Classifying Categorical Propositions by Subject
Author defines the four types of categorical propositions based on the nature of the subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any categorical proposition as personal, natural, quantified, or neglected based on its subject and the scope of the judgment.

Core Idea
The type of subject determines how a categorical proposition relates to individuals.

Categorical propositions are used in many Islamic sciences, from fiqh to kalam, to make claims about subjects. Understanding the exact nature of the subject is essential for valid reasoning. This lesson breaks down the four types according to the classical Islamic logicians, as explained in the Taḥzīb commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject (mawḍū‘)
Read the proposition and isolate the subject term. For example, in 'Zaid is standing', the subject is 'Zaid'. In 'Man is a species', the subject is 'Man'.
2
Check if the subject is a particular individual (shakhṣ) or a universal (kullī)
If the subject points to a specific entity (a person, this object), it is a particular. If it is a general concept (like 'man', 'knowledge'), it is a universal.
Personal propositions are also called 'particular' (makhṣūṣa).
3
For universal subjects, determine if the judgment is on the nature itself (ṭabī‘a) or on the individuals (afrād)
If the predicate describes the essence itself without reference to instances (e.g., 'Man is a species'), it is nature (ṭabī‘iyya). If the judgment applies to individuals (e.g., 'Man is animal'), it concerns individuals.
The judgment in ṭabī‘iyya is on the essence as such, not on its instances.
4
If judgment is on individuals, check if quantity is specified or neglected
If terms like 'every', 'some', 'none' (or their equivalents) are present, the proposition is quantified (maḥṣūra). If no quantity is indicated, it is neglected (muhmala).
Quantified propositions are further divided into universal (kulliyya) and particular (juz’iyya).

Divisions of categorical propositions in consideration of the subject: either [the subject is] particular or [the subject is] universal. What indicates it is a quantifier, otherwise it is neglected. The second is natural, the third is quantified, and the fourth is neglected. Then the quantified: if it is made clear that the judgment is on all individuals of the subject, it is universal; if it is made clear that the judgment is on some of its individuals, it is particular. Each of them is either affirmative or negative. In each of those four quantified [propositions] there must be something that specifies the quantity of the individuals of the subject; that thing is called the quantifier, taken from 'wall of the city'; for just as the wall of the city surrounds it, so this thing surrounds what is judged about from the individuals of the subject. So the quantifier of the universal affirmative is 'every' and the lam of generality and whatever conveys their meaning in any language; and the quantifier of the particular affirmative is 'some' and 'one' and whatever conveys their meaning; and the quantifier of the universal negative is 'no one' and 'not one' and their equivalents; and the quantifier of the particular negative is 'not every' and 'not some' and 'some are not' and what equals them. [It is called] natural because the judgment in it is on the very nature of the subject without its individuals. [It is called] neglected because the clarification of the quantity of the individuals of its subject is neglected and left out. Know that his statement according to his choice is that the judgment in the natural is on the very nature of the subject, and in the quantified and neglected on its individuals. The earlier investigators, when they saw that the thing judged about in reality is not the mental form, said: the subject in both the natural and the quantified is the mental thing, i.e., the essence itself, and the difference is that the essence taken in terms of being judged about in a way that does not extend to its individuals is the subject of the natural; and in terms of being judged about in a way that extends to its individuals is the subject of the quantified; and in terms of being taken without an added condition is the subject of the neglected according to the ancients. [It is called] neglected due to neglecting the quantity of the individuals of the subject. The four: i.e., universal affirmative, particular affirmative, universal negative, and particular negative. 'From' etc.: this thing is general, whether it is a word like 'every' or 'some' or others, or not, like the occurrence of a generic noun under negation; for it is a quantifier for universal negation even though it is not a word. 'Wall of the city' means that the naming of that thing as a quantifier is taken from the wall of the city, i.e., what surrounds it, considering that this thing surrounds either all individuals of the subject or some of them, just as the wall of the city surrounds it.
Divisions of categorical propositions in consideration of the subject: the first is antecedent, the second is consequent. The subject: if it is a specific person, the proposition is called personal and particular; if it is the essence itself, then natural; otherwise, if the quantity of its individuals is specified, either universal or particular, it is quantified; and if not, it is neglected. His saying 'antecedent': because it precedes in mention. His saying 'consequent': because it follows the first part. His saying 'and the subject': this is the division of the categorical proposition in consideration of the subject, and therefore in naming the divisions the state of the subject is observed. So what has a personal subject is called personal, and likewise the rest. The summary of the division is that the subject is either a real particular, like our saying 'this is a man', or a universal. In the second case, either the judgment is on the very essence of this universal and its nature as such, or on its individuals. In the second case, either the quantity of the individuals judged about is specified, by making clear that the judgment is on all of them or on some of them, or it is not specified but neglected. So the first is personal. And the subject: i.e., the subject that is in the proposition; if it is specific due to the specification of the personal, whether it exists in the mind or in the external world, and he did not say 'particular' to avoid the misconception that it is restricted to what is in the mind based on the fact that particularity is a mental accident, and he did not say 'proper noun' to avoid the exclusion of something like 'this animal'. 'Real': this includes proper nouns, pronouns, demonstrative pronouns and others, like 'I am knowledgeable' and 'Zaid is ignorant'. 'On the very essence of this universal': i.e., observed with the qualification of absoluteness and generality in concept and title, not the observed and denoted, like 'Man is a species'; thus it is not objected that what is judged about on the very essence of the universal as such, even if generality is not considered, is a neglected proposition, which is more general than the natural. So the first, i.e., what has a real particular as its subject, is called personal because the subject in it is a person, and it is also called particular because it is specific.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the division of categorical propositions based on their subject. The author begins by listing the four types: when the subject is a particular individual (شخصية), when it is the nature itself (طبعية), when the quantity of individuals is specified (محصورة, which includes universal and particular), and when quantity is neglected (مهملة). He then explains the quantifiers (سور) used for quantified propositions, giving specific examples from Arabic: 'كل' for universal affirmative, 'بعض' for particular affirmative, 'لا شيء' for universal negative, and 'ليس كل' for particular negative. The author clarifies that 'طبعية' means the judgment is on the essence itself without regard to individuals, while 'مهملة' means the quantity of individuals is left unstated. He then delves into a more advanced point: earlier logicians held that the subject in both natural and quantified propositions is the mental essence, but they differ in whether the judgment extends to individuals. The quantifier can be a word or a grammatical context (like a generic noun under negation). Finally, he re-summarizes the division: if the subject is a real particular (e.g., 'this Zaid'), it is personal; if it is a universal nature judged as such, it is natural; if the quantity of its individuals is specified (universal or particular), it is quantified; if not specified, it is neglected. All examples are consistent with the Islamic logical tradition, such as 'هذا إنسان' for personal or 'الإنسان نوع' for natural.

Quantifiers in Categorical Propositions (al-Sūr)
Author explains the four quantifiers and their linguistic forms, including implicit quantifiers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the quantifier (sūr) in a quantified proposition and determine the correct type (universal/particular, affirmative/negative).

Core Idea
Quantifiers specify the extent of the judgment: all, some, or none.

Once a proposition is identified as quantified (maḥṣūra), the next step is to determine its quantity and quality using its quantifier. This lesson covers the four standard quantifiers in Arabic logic and their equivalents, as well as implicit quantifiers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition's quantity and quality
First, decide if the proposition is universal or particular, and affirmative or negative. This determines which quantifier to expect.
2
Locate the quantifier word (sūr)
Look for words like كل (for universal affirmative), بعض (for particular affirmative), لا شيء (for universal negative), or ليس كل (for particular negative).
The quantifier may be a phrase or even implied by context.
3
Match the quantifier to the standard patterns
Universal affirmative: 'كل' or the lām of generality (لام الاستغراق). Particular affirmative: 'بعض' or 'واحد'. Universal negative: 'لا شيء' or 'لا واحد'. Particular negative: 'ليس كل' or 'ليس بعض' or 'بعض ليس'.
These are the standard Arabic forms; other languages have equivalents.
4
Consider implicit quantifiers
Sometimes the quantifier is not a word but a grammatical construction, e.g., a generic noun in the scope of negation (ما جاءني رجل) implies 'no man came', which is universal negative.
The author notes that such constructions count as quantifiers even without a specific word.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the division of categorical propositions based on their subject. The author begins by listing the four types: when the subject is a particular individual (شخصية), when it is the nature itself (طبعية), when the quantity of individuals is specified (محصورة, which includes universal and particular), and when quantity is neglected (مهملة). He then explains the quantifiers (سور) used for quantified propositions, giving specific examples from Arabic: 'كل' for universal affirmative, 'بعض' for particular affirmative, 'لا شيء' for universal negative, and 'ليس كل' for particular negative. The author clarifies that 'طبعية' means the judgment is on the essence itself without regard to individuals, while 'مهملة' means the quantity of individuals is left unstated. He then delves into a more advanced point: earlier logicians held that the subject in both natural and quantified propositions is the mental essence, but they differ in whether the judgment extends to individuals. The quantifier can be a word or a grammatical context (like a generic noun under negation). Finally, he re-summarizes the division: if the subject is a real particular (e.g., 'this Zaid'), it is personal; if it is a universal nature judged as such, it is natural; if the quantity of its individuals is specified (universal or particular), it is quantified; if not specified, it is neglected. All examples are consistent with the Islamic logical tradition, such as 'هذا إنسان' for personal or 'الإنسان نوع' for natural.

The Sixteen Proposition-Qualifier Combinations
Enumeration of 16 proposition-qualifier combinations with classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the 16 possible combinations of four proposition types with four qualifiers and classify each as invalid, valid-and-considered, or valid-but-not-considered.

Core Idea
Only 4 out of 16 proposition-qualifier combinations are actively used in Islamic logic.

In this passage, the author completes the classification of propositions by showing how each of the four types (general conditional, general customary, temporal, extended) can be restricted by four qualifiers (essential non-permanence, essential non-necessity, descriptive non-permanence, descriptive non-necessity). This yields 16 possibilities. Most are either logically invalid or practically unnecessary for the science of logic. Understanding this enumeration helps you grasp which proposition forms are actually employed in syllogistic reasoning.

2
Identify the four qualifiers
The four restrictions: al-la-dawam al-dhati (essential non-permanence), al-la-darura al-dhatiyya (essential non-necessity), al-la-dawam al-wasfi (descriptive non-permanence), al-la-darura al-wasfiyya (descriptive non-necessity). These modify the mode of the proposition.
3
Calculate the 16 theoretical combinations
Multiply the four propositions by four qualifiers to get 16. Understand that each proposition can be paired with each qualifier, but not all are logically coherent.
4
Identify the three invalid combinations
The passage states three are invalid. The most obvious is the general conditional (which inherently has descriptive permanence) combined with descriptive non-permanence, as this contradicts its nature. The other two involve similar contradictions.

Identify the four considered valid combinations
These are the two general propositions (general conditional and general customary) and the two temporal propositions, each restricted by essential non-permanence. They are the ones actively used in logical arguments.
