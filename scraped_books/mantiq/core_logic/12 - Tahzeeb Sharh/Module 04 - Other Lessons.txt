The Homonymy of Hidayah: Two Meanings, One Word
Definition of hidayah as homonym and refutation of objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two meanings of 'hidayah' and explain how verbal homonymy resolves the apparent contradiction between Q. 28:56 and Q. 41:17.

Core Idea
Hidayah is a homonym: showing the way vs. delivering the goal — one word, two distinct senses.

In this passage, the author addresses a theological objection against a particular interpretation by appealing to the linguistic concept of verbal homonymy. The word 'hidayah' (guidance) is shown to have two distinct meanings: simply showing the way (irā'at al-ṭarīq) and actually delivering someone to the desired goal (īṣāl ilā al-maṭlūb). By recognizing this, verses that seem to contradict each other can be harmonized. This lesson will teach you how to identify homonyms and use them as a tool for resolving interpretive issues in the Quran and other Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings
Look for the two meanings explicitly listed: 'irā'at al-ṭarīq' (showing the way) and 'īṣāl ilā al-maṭlūb' (delivering to the goal). Note that the author claims 'hidayah' is a verbal homonym (mushtarak lafẓī) between these two.
These meanings are distinct and not reducible to one another.
2
Find the objection
The objection is from Q. 28:56: 'Indeed, you do not guide whom you love.' The objector assumes 'guidance' here includes both meanings, so the verse would negate the Prophet's ability to even show the way. But the author notes that the Prophet's role was only showing the way.
The author uses the phrase 'كان شأنه إراءة الطريق' to describe the Prophet's function.
3
Apply the homonymy solution
By recognizing that 'hidayah' has two meanings, the verse can be interpreted as using the second meaning (delivering). In that case, the verse only negates the Prophet's ability to deliver, not his ability to show the way. Other verses like Q. 41:17 use the first meaning (showing). There is no contradiction.
Context (qarina) determines which meaning is intended.
4
Evaluate the analogy with throwing
The author brings an analogy with Q. 8:17 ('You did not throw when you threw, but Allah threw'). He compares the Prophet's apparent guidance to his apparent throwing. But he then notes that analogy is weak because the throwing had a miraculous effect, while guidance does not.
This critique shows that the author is critically assessing the argument.

Deciding Between Literal, Metaphorical, and Homonymous Meanings: The Case of Hidayah
Four possibilities for hidayah and the method to choose the correct interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze four possible interpretations of a word (literal, metaphorical, verbal homonymy, conceptual homonymy) and apply the rule that when a word is ambiguous between literal and metaphorical meaning, it should be taken as literal for one and metaphorical for the other rather than as a homonym.

Core Idea
When a word has multiple possible interpretations, lexical evidence and preference rules (literal > metaphorical > homonym) guide correct exegesis.

In the latter part of the passage, the author presents four possible interpretations of the word 'hidayah': metaphor in the first meaning, metaphor in the second, verbal homonymy, and conceptual homonymy. He argues that the correct view is that 'hidayah' is literal for delivering to the goal and metaphorical for showing the way, based on lexical evidence and a rule of preferring literal over metaphorical interpretation over homonymy. This lesson will teach you how to evaluate such cases using the tools of Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
List the four possibilities
Identify the four possibilities as given by al-Sayyid al-Zahid: (1) metaphor in the first meaning (showing the way) and literal in the second (delivering); (2) literal in the first, metaphor in the second; (3) verbal homonymy; (4) conceptual homonymy (a single universal meaning with many individuals).
The author focuses on these to narrow down the correct interpretation.
2
Locate the lexical evidence
The author cites Persian lexicons where 'hidayah' is defined as 'showing the path' (rah numudan). He uses this to support that the second meaning (delivering) is the literal meaning. (Note: There is a scholarly debate here, but follow the author's assertion.)
Lexical evidence is crucial in determining the literal meaning.
3
Apply the rule of preference
The author states that when a word is ambiguous between literal and metaphorical and between homonymy, the preferred interpretation is literal and metaphorical (i.e., one meaning literal, the other metaphorical) rather than homonymy. This rule eliminates the homonymy possibilities.
This is a methodological principle from usul al-fiqh: al-ḥamlu 'alā al-ḥaqīqah aw al-majāz awlā min al-ishtirāk.
4
Select the correct possibility
Based on lexical evidence and the preference rule, the author chooses the first possibility: metaphor in the first meaning (showing the way) and literal in the second (delivering). Thus 'hidayah' is a case of literal-metaphorical usage, not homonymy.
The choice determines that the apparent contradiction is resolved not by homonymy but by distinguishing literal from metaphorical usage.
5
Address the meta-objection
An objector says that this third opinion (literal-metaphorical) does not resolve the original dispute because it is a third position. The author responds that the truth is manifested, and the earlier disputants were mistaken due to lack of depth. Thus the dispute is effectively resolved by revealing the correct interpretation.
This shows how logical analysis can settle debates even when new positions are introduced.

Core Idea
Kinayah uses necessary concomitance (luzum) to imply meaning without direct naming.

In this passage, the author explains that 'sawa al-tariq' is not a literal description but a metaphor (kinayah) for the straight path. The Metaphorical Layers of Salah from Allah
Commentator explains the metaphorical usage of salah from Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the metaphorical shifts from salah as supplication to mercy to beneficence when attributed to Allah.

Core Idea
Salah from Allah is a double metaphor: from supplication to mercy, then from mercy to beneficence.

The commentator explains that the word 'salah' literally means supplication (du'a), but when attributed to Allah it is used metaphorically for mercy, and then mercy itself is metaphorical for beneficence. This lesson unpacks the logical necessity of these metaphorical shifts to preserve divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning of salah
The commentator states that the well-known view is that salah is literally supplication (du'a).
2
First metaphor: salah as mercy
When Allah is the subject, salah cannot mean supplication (since Allah does not supplicate), so it is metaphorically mercy.
3
Second metaphor: mercy as beneficence
Mercy literally implies tenderness of heart, which cannot be attributed to Allah, so it must be metaphorical for its end, which is beneficence (ihsan).
This is a metaphor within a metaphor.
4
Appreciate the theological precision
The commentator cites al-Baydawi to show that this layered metaphor is an established interpretation.

The Wisdom of Indirect Reference to the Prophet
Commentator explains why the author used an indirect reference to the Prophet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the reasons for using a descriptive phrase instead of naming the Prophet, and evaluate the objections and response.

Core Idea
The author's indirect reference 'on whom He sent' conveys reverence and exclusivity, following the Qur'anic pattern.

The commentator discusses why the author used the description 'على من أرسله' instead of explicitly naming the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم). He presents two reasons: reverence and exclusivity of the description. Then he addresses objections and provides an answer rooted in the Qur'anic usage. This lesson explores the rhetorical and theological wisdom behind this choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two reasons given
First, not naming implies exaltation (تعظيم). Second, the description is so specific that only the Prophet comes to mind.
2
Analyze the first objection
If not naming implies exaltation, then why does Allah explicitly name Himself in the Qur'an? The objector argues that Allah's greatness is greater, so He should avoid naming Himself a fortiori.
3
Analyze the second objection
If the description is exclusive, then many of Allah's attributes are also exclusive, so why not use them?
4
Understand the answer
The commentator answers that the choice follows the Qur'anic pattern: in the salah verse (33:56), Allah does not name the Prophet explicitly, but in the hamd verse (1:2), He does name Himself. The author is imitating the Qur'an.

Key Takeaway
The extension of the three considerations to particulars is possible only by analogy (tabi'iyya) and is not as straightforward as for universals.

Conclusion of the topic of concepts. The logical, natural, and mental universal. For the negation of the part necessitates the negation of the whole. But the dispute is concerning the natural universal, like 'human', inasmuch as it is human which is subject to universality in the mind: does it exist in the external world within its individuals or not? The answer: The whole does not remain insofar as it is a whole when a part of its parts is negated. There is no doubt that the four insofar as it is four has been negated by the negation of one of them, just as it is negated by the negation of every one of its parts. How so? If the four remained after the negation of one part, it would necessitate that it is composed of three units, just as the three is composed of them; then the difference between it and the three would not remain, and that is self-evidently false (Ismaʻil). And the dispute etc. The detail of the matter is that they differed concerning whether the natural universal exists in the external world or not. It was said: It exists in the external world, not with a separate existence, but with the existence of its individuals united with it in essence; this is the doctrine of the Chief Master (Ibn Sina), but it is not sensible, and what is observed are only individuations. And it was said: Rather, it is also sensible, due to the absence of determinations in the external world according to this proponent. And it was said: The natural universal does not exist in the external world; rather, what exists therein are only the individuals, which are the simple identities, i.e., the individuations, and the universals are abstracted from them. The proofs of the difference are elaborated in the extended works. 'Inasmuch as it is human' means the essence that is the subject of universality; because the essence with the occurrence of universality is not necessarily existent, for every existent in the external world is a particular, and no particular is common among many in the external world. (ʻAbd) 'Within its individuals' means existing by the very existence of its individuals; for the reality of man, for example, when accompanied by external accidents, exists in the external world, so that reality, insofar as it is its essential attributes which are united with it, exists in the external world, otherwise its separation would be necessary. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and dialectical discussion of the three types of universal: logical, natural, and mental. The author begins by stating a logical principle: the negation of a part necessitates the negation of the whole. He then identifies the key dispute: whether the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi‘i), such as the essence 'human' considered in itself as subject to universality in the mind, exists in the external world within its individuals or not. The majority of philosophers (jumhur al-hukama') hold that it does exist in the external world, albeit not independently but through the existence of its individuals. Some later scholars (ba‘d al-muta’akhkhirin) deny this, asserting that only the individuals exist externally. The author then presents an objection and answer concerning the principle of part and whole. The objection argues that if a part is removed, the whole may still remain conceptually. The answer clarifies that the 'whole' in question is the whole as a whole, which is negated when any part is removed, using the example of four: if one unit is removed, the 'fourness' is negated. The author then elaborates on two positions regarding the natural universal: (1) Ibn Sina's view that it exists externally with the existence of its individuals but is not sensible, and (2) an alternative view that it is sensible due to the lack of external determinations, and (3) a third view that it does not exist externally at all. The text further explains the meaning of 'inasmuch as it is human' as the essence that is the subject of universality, emphasizing that the essence with universality is not necessarily externally existent. The final part critiques an argument for the existence of the natural universal externally: the claim that 'animal is a part of this animal' is examined. The author points out that either 'this animal' refers to an individual like Zayd, in which case animal is not a real part (Zayd may be a simple essence), or it refers to the composite concept, which is not externally existent. Thus, the passage thoroughly defines the three universals and the debate around the natural universal's ontological status.

Key Terms
الكلي المنطقي (al-kullī al-manṭiqī): The logical universal: the universal considered in the mind as predicate of many, e.g., 'human' as a term applying to many individuals. [Note: Stable across the tradition; consistently used in logic texts.]
الكلي الطبعي (al-kullī al-ṭabī‘ī): The natural universal: the essence itself, such as 'human' inasmuch as it is human, which is the subject of universality in the mind; its external existence is disputed. [Note: In earlier Peripatetic philosophy, the natural universal is often considered to exist in the external world as a common nature; later thinkers like Ibn Taymiyya critique this notion.]
الكلي العقلي (al-kullī al-‘aqlī): The mental universal: the universal as a concept in the mind that is abstracted from particulars. In this passage, it is one of the three types alongside logical and natural. [Note: Often synonymous with logical universal in some texts; here distinguished as the mental concept.]
