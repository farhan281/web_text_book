The Three Types of Universal: Logical, Natural, and Mental
Definition of the three types of universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish among the logical, natural, and mental universal and identify the existence status of each.

Core Idea
Every universal exists in three modes: as concept, as external reality, and as mental composite.

In this lesson, we encounter a foundational classification in Islamic logic: every universal concept can be viewed in three ways. The logician studies the concept itself, which exists only in the mind. That concept refers to real individuals in the external world. Combining the two gives a mental construct. This tripartite division is essential for understanding how definitions work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Read the first few lines where the author defines 'logical universal', 'natural universal', and 'mental universal'. Match each with its description.
2
Distinguish existence status
Note that the logical universal exists only in the mind as a second intention; the natural universal exists through its individuals externally; the mental universal is a mental composite.
3
Recognize the 'subject' (معروض)
Understand that the natural universal is the subject of the logical universal, i.e., the external reality that the concept applies to.
4
Apply to an example
Take 'human' — its concept is logical universal, its external individuals are natural universal, the composite 'universal human' is mental universal.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and explanatory section from a commentary (sharh) on a matn concerning the three types of universal: logical, natural, and mental. The author begins by defining the three: the concept of universal as studied by logic (logical universal), the external reality that the concept applies to (natural universal), and the combination of the two as it exists in the mind (mental universal). He then extends this tripartite classification to the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, accident), showing that each can be considered logically, naturally, and mentally. The commentary then addresses a potential objection: if a thing (like human) is a subject for both universality and species-ness, how can it be both a natural universal and a natural species without contradiction? The answer is that the aspects are different: it is a natural universal as a subject of universality, and a natural species as a subject of species-ness. This distinction by aspect (jiha) resolves the apparent conflict. The passage emphasizes that the logical universal exists only in the mind (as a second intention), while the natural universal exists through its individuals in the external world. The mental universal is a mental construct combining the two.

Applying the Three Types to the Five Universals and Resolving Aspectual Objections
Extension of three-type classification to five universals and resolution of aspectual objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify each of the five universals into logical, natural, and mental aspects, and resolve the apparent conflict of a thing having multiple natural universal attributes.

Core Idea
The same entity can be a natural universal, a natural species, and a natural genus by considering different aspects.

In this lesson, the author extends the tripartite classification to the five predicables. Then he addresses a potential objection: if a thing (like human) is a subject for universality and for species-ness, how can it be both a natural universal and a natural species at the same time? The answer lies in the difference of aspect. This principle is widely used in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five universals
Recall from previous lessons the five predicables: جنس، نوع، فصل، خاصة، عرض عام.
2
Apply the three types to each
For each, state its logical form (concept), its natural subject (the external reality it applies to), and its mental composite.
3
Understand the objection
The objector says one thing (e.g., human) is both a subject for universality and for species-ness, so it cannot be both a natural universal and a natural species without being two things.
4
Analyze the response
The commentator distinguishes the aspects (جهة العروض). Human is a natural universal inasmuch as it is a subject for universality, and a natural species inasmuch as it is a subject for species-ness.
5
Check the resolution
The author concedes identity in reference but says conceptual distinction suffices because rulings differ with different aspects.

The Four Types of Definition: Complete and Incomplete Hadd and Rasm
Author defines the four types of definition based on essential and accidental attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given definition as a complete or incomplete hadd or rasm based on the presence of essential and accidental attributes.

Core Idea
A definition's completeness depends on including the proximate genus, not just any genus.

In this passage, the author builds on the condition that a definition must be coextensive (musāwī) with its definiendum. The defining element is either essential (a proximate differentia) or accidental (a special property), which determines whether the definition is a ḥadd or rasm. The presence or absence of the proximate genus then decides completeness. This classification is a cornerstone for evaluating definitions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining element
Look at the definition: does it use an essential attribute (like rational for human) or an accidental property (like laughing)? This tells you if it is a ḥadd (essential) or rasm (accidental).
The defining element must be specific to the definiendum.
2
Check for the proximate genus
Does the definition include the immediate genus (e.g., animal for human)? If yes, it is complete; if not, incomplete. The proximate genus is the closest higher category.
Including a remote genus alone still makes it incomplete.
3
Apply the fourfold scheme
Combine the two criteria: (1) defining element type (hadd vs rasm) and (2) completeness. You get four types: complete ḥadd, incomplete ḥadd, complete rasm, incomplete rasm.
A complete ḥadd includes both proximate genus and proximate differentia; complete rasm includes proximate genus and special property.
4
Test with an example
Take a definition like 'human is the rational animal'. The defining element is rational (essential), and it includes animal (proximate genus) → complete ḥadd. Try 'human is the laughing animal': laughing is accidental, but still includes animal → complete rasm.
If a definition only says 'rational' without genus, it is incomplete ḥadd.

This passage defines and classifies definitions (ta‘rif) in the tradition of Islamic logic. The Six Types of Self-Evident Premises
Author classifies self-evident premises into six types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the six types of self-evident premises and identify examples of each.

Core Idea
Self-evident premises are not all the same; they vary by how certainty is obtained.

In the previous lesson, we learned that certain knowledge is either self-evident (badihi) or theoretical (nazari). Now we dive deeper: the badihiyyat themselves are not monolithic. The author, based on induction, classifies them into six types according to the source of their self-evidence. Understanding this classification is crucial for evaluating the epistemic strength of premises in any argument, especially in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main division
Notice that the author first divides badihiyyat into those known by mere conception of terms (awwaliyat) and those that require something else. This is the foundational split.
2
Distinguish observation types
If the premise requires sensory observation, it is mushahadat. Note these are further divided into external senses (hissiyat) and internal feelings (wijdaniyyat).
For example, 'the sun is bright' is hissi, while 'I am hungry' is wijdani.
3
Find the mediating factor
For premises that are not sensory, see if the mediating factor (the reason for certainty) is always present with the terms (fitriyat) or not. If not, it falls into either hadsiyat or mutawatirat.
Fitriyat: 'four is even' — the mediating factor (divisibility) is always present.
4
Recognize intuition vs testimony
If the premise is reached by a quick intuitive leap (hads) from principles, it is hadsiyat. If it is based on widespread reports that preclude collusion, it is mutawatirat.
Example of hadsiyat: 'the moon's light is borrowed from the sun'. Example of mutawatirat: 'Mecca exists'.
5
Apply the classification
Test each type with an example from the text or your own knowledge to internalize the distinctions. This will help you recognize the epistemic status of premises in Islamic arguments.
