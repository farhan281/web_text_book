Defining Sidq, Haqq, and Tasdiq in the Companions' Virtue
Author defines key terms and explains the virtues of the Prophet's companions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sidq and haqq as used in the commentary and explain the condition for a person to be considered a companion of the Prophet.

Core Idea
Truthfulness (sidq) is a two-way correspondence; assent (tasdiq) is the cause of the companions' happiness.

The commentary unpacks a single line praising the companions by defining the key terms 'sidq' (truthfulness), 'haqq' (truth), and 'tasdiq' (assent). These are not just abstract concepts; they have a reciprocal nature and are directly linked to the companions' virtue. Understanding these definitions helps us grasp the deeper meaning of the praise and the criteria for companionship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate the terms الصدق, الحق, التصديق in the Arabic passage. Note that the commentary provides specific definitions for each.
These terms are foundational in both logic and theology.
2
Analyze the reciprocal definition of sidq
The commentary states that sidq involves mutual correspondence: when news/belief matches reality, reality also matches them. This is highlighted by the phrase المفاعلة من الطرفين.
This is a nuanced definition that goes beyond simple correspondence.
3
Distinguish between sidq and haqq
The commentary explains that from one angle (the entity matching reality) it is called sidq, and from the other angle (reality matching the entity) it is called haqq. Also, haqq can refer to the matching itself.
Note the vowel variation: al-haqq (with kasr) for the matching, and al-haqq (with fath) for reality.
4
Understand the role of tasdiq
The phrase بالتصديق is attached to سعدوا (they were happy), indicating that their happiness was due to their assent and belief in what the Prophet brought.
Tasdiq here is faith-based, distinct from the logical concept of assent to a proposition.
5
Grasp the condition for companionship
The commentary argues that the phrase 'they are believers' implies that faith is the cause of companionship, and therefore must persist until death. This is supported by the glossator's objection and clarification.
This refines the common understanding that mere physical meeting suffices.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the description of the Prophet and his family and companions from the preface of the book. It begins by explaining the phrase 'And his companions who were happy in the paths of truthfulness with assent, and ascended in the ladders of truth with verification.' The commentator defines key terms: 'sidq' (truthfulness) applies to speech and belief matching reality, with a reciprocal relationship; 'haqq' (truth) is the matching itself or the reality. 'Tasdiq' (assent) is the cause of their happiness. 'Tahqiq' (verification) is the means of ascent. Then he defines 'infallibles' as those protected from sin, citing a verse, and notes that this view is from the Imami school. He then discusses various opinions on who constitutes the 'Al' (family) of the Prophet, concluding with the preferred view of the pious. Next, he clarifies the condition of companionship: it requires faith (belief) at the time of companionship and continuation until death, and argues that the phrasing 'believers' implies causality. He also defines 'i'tiqad' (belief) as the heart's connection to something, whether true or false. Finally, he explains the phrase 'ascended in the ladders of truth' as implying ascent over all ranks of truth, using the concept of 'al-jam' al-mudaf' to indicate totality, and notes that the author used the necessary (ascent) to imply the necessary (reaching the utmost) because it is more fitting for praise.

The Principle of Truth by Content
Commentary on the fifth part and the principle of truth by content.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that verifiers evaluate statements by truth, not speaker reputation, to Islamic scholarly practice.

Core Idea
Verifiers know men by truth, not truth by men.

This passage from a commentary on the Tahdhib introduces a foundational methodological principle: true scholars assess the truth of a statement independently of who said it. The commentator illustrates this through a historical sketch of logic's transmission from Aristotle to al-Farabi to Ibn Sina, showing that the content's validity is what matters, not the speaker's fame.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Locate the Arabic phrase that expresses the principle: 'يعرفون الرجال بالحق لا الحق بالرجال'. This is the core methodological stance.
2
Distinguish the two approaches
Contrast the verifiers (who judge speakers by truth) with the ignorant (who judge truth by speakers). The verifiers accept a statement if it is true regardless of the speaker's reputation; the ignorant accept a statement only if the speaker is famous for truthfulness.
The commentator explicitly describes both groups and their consequences.
3
Apply to a historical example
Notice how the commentator uses the history of logic to exemplify: Aristotle's work was transmitted and refined by al-Farabi and Ibn Sina based on its truth, not their authority. The transmission chain is valued for the content, not the personalities.
This shows the principle in action across generations.
4
Evaluate an Islamic case
Consider a scenario in hadith criticism: a weak narrator reports a sound hadith. How would a verifier treat it? They would accept the hadith based on its content (if it matches other authentic chains), not reject it solely because of the narrator's weakness.
This demonstrates the real-world application of the principle.

Transmission of Logic: From Aristotle to Ibn Sina
Author recounts the development of logic from Aristotle to Farabi and Ibn Sina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key figures in the transmission of logic from Aristotle to Ibn Sina and explain their contributions using their honorary titles.

Core Idea
Logic was codified by Aristotle, refined by al-Farabi, and perfected by Ibn Sina.

This passage gives a concise history of logic's journey from Greek origins to Islamic scholarship. Understanding this chain of transmission helps you appreciate the authority and development of the logical tools you are about to master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the originator
Locate Aristotle in the passage. Note his role as the codifier of logic and philosophy, and the title 'First Teacher' given to him because he compiled it by Alexander's order.
2
Recognize the translation movement
The passage mentions that translators transferred Greek philosophical works into Arabic. This step is crucial for the transmission of logic into the Islamic world.
3
Understand al-Farabi's contribution
Al-Farabi is called the 'Second Teacher' because he refined, organized, and perfected logic after translation. Identify his role in systematizing the subject.
4
Note the loss and recovery
After al-Farabi's books were lost, Ibn Sina (the 'Chief Master') detailed and edited them. This shows how Ibn Sina preserved and elaborated on the logical tradition.
5

Each title (First Teacher, Second Teacher, Chief Master) reflects the scholar's unique contribution to logic. Trace how the passage links each title to specific actions.
