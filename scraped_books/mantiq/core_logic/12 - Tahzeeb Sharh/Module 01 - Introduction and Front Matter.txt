The Foundational Importance of Logic: Imam Ghazali's Epigraph
Title page and epigraph emphasizing logic's necessity for all sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Imam al-Ghazali considered logic essential for all sciences and locate the quote in its context.

Core Idea
Without logic, no science can be trusted.

This lesson unpacks the opening epigraph of our coursebook: a powerful statement by Imam al-Ghazali that links the study of logic to the reliability of all sciences. As we begin our journey into 'Ilm al-Mantiq, this quote sets the stage for why we must take this discipline seriously. The student is invited to reflect on the relationship between correct reasoning and the pursuit of certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its author
The title page tells us this is 'Sharh Tahdhib' by 'Abdullah Yazdi (d. 1015 AH). Note the addition of a hashia (margin commentary). This helps us understand the tradition of logic study.
The author is a later commentator, building on the foundational text of Tahdhib al-Mantiq.
2
Locate and read the Ghazali quote
At the top of the excerpt, you see: 'من لم يعرف المنطق فلا ثقة له في العلوم أصلا' – Whoever does not know logic has no confidence in the sciences at all. Read it carefully. What does 'no confidence' imply?
This quote is famous and repeated in many logic books.
3
Understand the meaning of 'no confidence'
Ghazali is not saying that a person without logic cannot have true beliefs; rather, he means that logical reasoning is the tool that gives certainty (yaqin) to knowledge claims. Without it, one cannot be sure that a conclusion follows from premises.
This is a strong epistemological claim about the necessity of method.
4
Relate to the Islamic sciences
Consider how logic functions in disciplines like kalam (theology) or usul al-fiqh (jurisprudence). For example, the common argument for God's existence from temporal origination (huduth) depends on logical inference.
This illustrates why logic is considered a prerequisite for advanced study.

The Three Layers of a Classical Commentary Edition
Publisher describes the three-layer structure of the edition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the matn, sharh, and hashiyah in a classical commentary edition and explain their functions.

Core Idea
Every commentary edition has three layers: the base text (matn), the commentary (sharh), and the gloss (hashiyah).

When you open a classical Islamic text, you often find a layered structure: a core text surrounded by commentary and further notes. This lesson shows you how to identify these layers using the publisher's own description of their edition of Sharh al-Tahdhib. Recognizing these layers helps you navigate scholarly works efficiently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (base text)
In the edition described, the publisher states: 'جعلنا كتاب تهذيب المنطق كالمتن' – they made Tahdhib al-Mantiq the matn. The matn is the primary work being commented upon. In this case, it is a classical logic text by al-Taftazani.
The matn is usually printed in a distinct style (e.g., red color) to stand out.
2
Identify the sharh (commentary)
The publisher says: 'جعلنا شرحه شرح التهذيب بين الخطين' – they placed the explanation (Sharh al-Tahdhib) between the lines. The sharh is a commentary that explains the matn. It often surrounds the matn or is interlinear.
In this edition, the sharh is printed in black, and the matn in red.
3
Identify the hashiyah (gloss)
The publisher selected 'تحفه شاهجهاني' as the hashiyah – a gloss that explains important points in the commentary. The hashiyah is a secondary commentary on the sharh, often placed in the margins.
The publisher uses red color for words from the matn or sharh that are explained in the hashiyah.
4
Analyze the editorial choices
The publisher also made other decisions: correcting spelling, adding modern punctuation, vocalizing difficult words, and adding page headings. These help you use the edition effectively. Note that the edition is designed for students of logic in South Asia.
Understanding the editorial methodology helps you evaluate the reliability and user-friendliness of the edition.

Author opens the sharh with praise of Allah and explains the significance of beginning with the basmala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three key components of a traditional diyabah in a sharh and explain their purpose.

Core Idea
The diyabah is a scholarly sunnah — a blessing-seeking opening that embeds the text in prophetic tradition.

Classical Islamic texts almost always begin with the basmala and hamdala. This is not mere formality; it is a conscious emulation of the Quran and Prophetic practice. In this lesson, we examine the opening of the Tahzeeb al-Mantiq's commentary to understand the structure and rationale of the diyabah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of the diyabah
From the passage, locate the tasmiya (بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم), the hamdala (الحمد لله), and the section heading (مبحث الحمد). These are the standard opening elements.
The tasmiya always precedes the hamdala in this tradition.
2
Understand the Prophetic basis
The commentator says he opened with praise 'following the best of speech, and emulating the hadith of the best of mankind.' This refers to the Prophetic hadith that every important matter not begun with praise of Allah is deficient.
The 'best of speech' is the Quran; the 'best of mankind' is the Prophet (peace be upon him).
3
Analyze the commentator's motivational narrative
The commentator describes a progressive strengthening: first the basmala protects from deficiency, then the attribute 'Rahman' gives worldly blessings, then 'Rahim' gives eternal blessings. This progression compels him to begin with it.
This shows a spiritual rather than merely formal motive.
4
Connect to the broader scholarly tradition
This diyabah establishes the author's humility and reliance on Allah, a sunnah echoed by scholars like al-Ghazali and Ibn Hajar. Without it, the text would lack the proper adab of knowledge-seeking.
Recognizing the diyabah helps the reader understand the intellectual context.

Commentator analyzes the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah' as a statement by a speaker and discusses its grammar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the implicit speaker and hidden referent in an Arabic declarative statement such as 'al-hamdu lillah'.

Core Idea
Every statement implies a speaker; find it to unlock the meaning.

The opening phrase of the matn, 'al-hamdu lillah', is not a mere string of words. The commentator explains that it is a statement (qawl) by a speaker (qa'il). Recognizing this implicit speaker is essential for analyzing any proposition in Islamic texts. This lesson guides you through the grammatical cues that reveal the hidden speaker and referent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit words

2
Find the implied speaker
The commentator says speech requires a substrate (mahall), which is the speaker. Here, the speaker is implied in the structure: the hidden third-person pronoun (in the verb or construction) refers back to the speaker.
In Arabic, the speaker is often implied in the verb conjugation or the context.
3
Trace the hidden pronoun's referent
The hidden pronoun needs an antecedent. In this phrase, the speaker is the referent, mentioned implicitly (bi ma'na). The commentator explains that the referent can be verbally explicit, implicitly understood, or by ruling.
4
Apply to other Islamic statements
Practice with other common Islamic phrases, e.g., the basmala: who is the implied speaker? The one reciting it. This skill is crucial in tafsir and fiqh when analyzing attributions.
For instance, in the basmala, the speaker is the servant (reciter), not Allah, even though the phrase is about Allah.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed treatment of the term 'hidayah' (guidance) from the opening praise section of the book. The author begins by presenting two definitions: (1) hidayah is the indication that leads to the goal (al-dalalat al-musila), i.e., actually bringing to the desired end; (2) hidayah is showing the path that leads to the goal (ira'at al-tariq al-musil), i.e., merely pointing out the way. The key difference is that the first definition implies arrival at the goal, whereas the second does not necessarily entail arrival; it only means indicating the path, which may or may not be followed. The author then notes that the first definition faces an objection from the Quranic verse about Thamud (41:17): Allah says He guided them but they chose misguidance. If guidance meant actual leading to the goal, they could not have remained in misguidance after reaching the truth. The response is that in that verse, guidance means showing the path, not causing arrival. A second objection comes from the verse 'Indeed, you do not guide whom you love' (28:56), which seems to deny that the Prophet could guide even by showing the path, since he did show it. The response, based on commentary on al-Kashshaf, suggests that 'hidayah' is a homonym (mushtarak) with two meanings—showing the path (in the Thamud verse) and causing arrival (in the verse about the Prophet). The author of the hashiah argues that the word's transitivity (with 'ila' or bi-nafsihi) indicates the meaning. However, a later commentator (al-Sayyid al-Zahid) holds that the correct view is that hidayah is used figuratively in the sense of causing arrival and literally in the sense of showing the path, based on lexicons where hidayah is defined as 'showing the way.' The discussion concludes that this resolves the apparent conflict between the verses.

Verbal and Mental Speech in the Book's Title
Author explains the book's title and the two senses of 'الكلام'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between verbal and mental speech as used in logical and theological contexts, and apply this distinction to interpret the title of a scholarly work.

Core Idea
One word can mean two things: uttered words or inner meanings.

When you first see the title 'غاية تهذيب الكلام', what does 'الكلام' mean? The author shows that it can refer either to the actual words we speak or to the concepts in our minds. This distinction is central to logic and theology, where 'speech' about Allah (كلام الله) is debated as either eternal mental speech or created verbal speech. Let's unpack how the author uses the preface's demonstrative 'هذا' to decide between the two.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of 'الكلام'
From the passage, extract the two definitions: verbal speech (الكلام اللفظي) is the actual uttered words; mental speech (الكلام النفسي) is the meanings present in the mind. Notice the author says both are possible depending on context.
In logic, 'الكلام' often includes both, but for definitions you must specify which.
2
Examine the role of the demonstrative 'هذا'
The author links the meaning of 'الكلام' to what the demonstrative 'هذا' in the preface points to: if it points to words, then 'الكلام' is verbal; if to meanings, then it is mental. This shows how context determines meaning in Arabic.
This is similar to how in usul al-fiqh, the word 'أمر' can mean command or thing depending on context.
3
Analyze the metaphorical justification for the title
The author addresses an objection: how can 'تهذيب' (refinement) be predicated on 'الكلام'? He explains via metaphor (مجاز), either hyperbole (المبالغة) or deletion (الحذف). This shows that even a title can have layered meanings.
Understanding metaphor is crucial for interpreting dense scholarly texts.
4
Apply the distinction to a theological example
Consider the debate over whether the Quran is eternal (mental speech of Allah) or created (verbal speech in time). The distinction between الكلام اللفظي and الكلام النفسي is at the heart of this theological issue.
This passage does not go into theology, but the logical distinction underpins such debates.

Parsing the Opening Phrase: Wa Ba'd Fa Hadha
Author explains grammatical and rhetorical aspects of the opening phrase 'wa ba'd fa hadha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the grammatical justification of the 'fa' in 'fa hadha' and distinguish between the grammatical concepts of mustaqarr and laghw as used in Arabic logic texts.

Core Idea
Every grammatical choice in logical texts has a reasoned basis — even the 'fa' is argued over by grammarians.

The opening words of a book are never arbitrary. In this passage, the commentator unpacks the phrase 'wa ba'd fa hadha', revealing layers of grammatical reasoning. You will learn how the 'fa' is justified by the imagined or estimated 'amma', and how the terms mustaqarr and laghw define the status of adverbial phrases. This prepares you to read any logical text with attention to its precise linguistic construction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three grammatical states of 'ba'd'
The commentator states that 'ba'd' is a temporal adverb with three states: its genitive is mentioned, forgotten, or intended. The state affects whether it is declinable or built on dammah. Note the distinction between 'forgotten' (meaning completely omitted with no intention) and 'intended' (omitted but still considered).
This classification is standard in Arabic grammar; similar rules apply to other adverbs.
2
Discover the two justifications for the 'fa'
The 'fa' in 'fa hadha' is justified either by imagining 'amma' (making a false judgment that it is present due to habit) or by estimating it (treating it as present in the arrangement of speech, a true judgment). The commentator notes that the more accurate view is the estimation, not mere imagination.
This distinction shows the care logicians take to defend their phrasing against objections.
3
Define mustaqarr and laghw
Mustaqarr is an adverbial phrase whose operative (the verb governing it) is always omitted, so the adverb takes the place of the operative. Laghw is its opposite: the operative is explicit, so the adverb does not occupy the operative's position and is considered null. The well-known view holds that the omitted operative in mustaqarr is general (like 'being'), while al-Sayyid al-Sharif allows it to be specific.
These terms are crucial for parsing logical propositions where adverbial phrases are used.
4
Understand how 'hadha' can refer to a mental entity
The commentator answers an objection: how can 'hadha' (this) refer to something mental? He explains that demonstratives can be used metaphorically to refer to mental objects by treating them as if they were sensible, emphasizing the clarity of the mental object and encouraging the learner.
This rhetorical device is common in prefaces to highlight the importance of the subject matter.

The phrase 'min dhawī l-afhām' is grammatically ambiguous: it can indicate the teacher as a possessor of knowledge or the student as a learner from them.

The Grammatical Device of Tadmin (Inclusion)
Commentator defines and applies the grammatical device of tadmin to the verb يتذكر.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of tadmin in a text and explain how it allows a verb to take on an additional meaning, as exemplified in the commentator's analysis of 'yatadhakkaru'.

Core Idea
Tadmin makes an intransitive verb transitive by implying the meaning of another verb.

In the commentary on the preface, the commentator employs the grammatical device of tadmin (inclusion) to resolve a syntactic problem: the verb 'يتذكر' (to recall) is intransitive but is followed by the preposition 'من'. By including the meaning of 'taking and learning', the verb becomes transitive. This lesson unpacks this key concept and its broader application in Islamic sciences.

Resolving Self-Containment in Logical Phrasing
Author resolves an objection that 'the first section is about logic' implies self-containment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definitional phrase implies self-containment and resolve it using tawjīh (interpretation) by distinguishing between words and meanings or implying an omitted word.

Core Idea
Self-containment is avoided by separating the container (words) from the contained (meanings).

In this passage, an objector points out that the author's phrase 'the first section is about logic' seems to make the section contain itself—a logical absurdity. The answerer shows that careful interpretation avoids this. You will learn a key skill: when a definition or description appears circular, look for an implicit distinction between the linguistic expression and its subject matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problematic phrase
Locate the phrase that the objector finds objectionable: 'القسم الأول في المنطق' (the first section is about logic). The objector claims that since the section is identical with the logical issues, it would mean 'the logical issues are in the logical issues'.
Self-containment (ظرفية الشيء لنفسه) occurs when something is said to be contained within itself.
2
Analyze the objector's reasoning
The objector equates 'the first section' with 'the logical issues' and 'logic' also with 'the logical issues'. Thus 'في المنطق' (in logic) becomes 'في المسائل المنطقية' (in the logical issues), making the section a container for itself. This reasoning assumes that 'في' always indicates literal containment.
The objection is valid only if the interpretation is literal and unqualified.
3
Examine the answerer's resolution
The answerer (the commentator) disagrees that the meaning of the book is limited to the issues. He proposes that 'القسم الأول' refers to the specific words (الألفاظ) that indicate specific meanings, and that 'في المنطق' means 'في بيان المنطق' (in explaining logic). Thus the section is words explaining logic, not logic itself. This breaks the identity and avoids self-containment.
The answerer introduces a distinction between words (اللفظ) and meanings (المعنى) and posits an omitted word (بيان) to clarify the relationship.
4
Apply the principle to other contexts
Whenever a definition or description appears to make something contain itself, check whether a distinction between the linguistic expression and its subject matter can resolve the issue. This is a common tool in Islamic logic and jurisprudence to harmonize apparent contradictions.
This method is called 'tawjih' (توجيه), which means to turn apparently invalid speech into correct speech.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The introduction is not a mere preface; it is a prerequisite that provides insight for the beginner.

When you open a classical Islamic text, you often find a 'mukaddimah' (introduction) that sets the stage. Is it just a polite opening? Not at all. In this lesson, we dive into the technical definition of the introduction as presented in a logic commentary. You will learn its linguistic roots, its two distinct types, and how scholars defend its definition against criticism. This will sharpen your ability to analyze the structure of any Islamic scholarly work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its linguistic root

The linguistic root gives insight into the logical function: the introduction leads into the main discussion.

Understand the two interpretations of 'book'
The commentator says the introduction can be considered either as a group of words (if the book is words) or as a group of meanings (if the book is meanings). This dual definition is the core of the discussion.

Distinguish the two types of introduction
The passage then separates introductions into two types: absolute (mulaq) – which includes the definition of the science and its goal – and qualified (muqayyad) – which includes clarifying the subject matter and other benefits that provide insight for the beginner.

List the first four heads: الغرض (purpose), المنفعة (benefit), السمة (title), المؤلّف (author). Notice that the commentator also distinguishes between المبادي (principles) and المقدمات (preliminaries) before discussing the heads.
2
Understand the purpose (gharad)
The purpose is the final cause that motivates the author to write. The commentator says: 'if the outcome of an action motivates the doer, it is called a purpose.' For the science of logic, the purpose is infallibility (عصمة) from error. Think of how an author like al-Ghazali states his purpose for writing Ihya' to revive religious sciences.
Purpose is distinct from benefit; it is what drives the author.
3
Understand the benefit (manfa'a)
The benefit is what the learner naturally desires, distinct from the author's purpose. It is what makes the learner active and willing to bear hardship. For example, the benefit of studying logic is gaining correct reasoning, which has value even if that was not the initial purpose of the logician who systematized it.
The commentator notes that God's actions have benefits but are not motivated by purposes.
4
Analyze the title (sima) and author (mu'allif)
The title (السمة) is the designation of the science, giving a summary of its content. The commentator derives 'mantiq' from 'nutq' (speech) to show it perfects internal and external speech. The author (المؤلف) is mentioned to calm the learner's heart by knowing his credibility. For instance, knowing that Ibn Sina wrote al-Isharat provides confidence in its logical content.
The marginal note by Abu al-Fath adds that the title defines the science by its description, and the author specifies who wrote it for trust.
5
Distinguish between purpose and benefit
Compare the two: the purpose (غرض) is a motivator for the doer (author), while the benefit (منفعة) is what results and is desired by the learner. The commentator illustrates this with the example of divine actions: they have benefits but are not caused by purposes. Apply this distinction when reading any preface: ask what motivated the author and what the reader gains.
This distinction is crucial for understanding why a text was written versus why it is useful.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the 'eight heads' that the ancients (the early Muslim scholars) would place at the beginning of their books. The commentator first clarifies the distinction between 'principles' (mabadi) and 'preliminaries' (muqaddimat): principles are what one starts with before the main subject, while preliminaries are what the commencement depends on with insight and desire, such as defining the science, its goal, and subject. He then states that the ancients used to mention the eight heads. The first four are explained: (1) the purpose (gharad) – to ensure that studying is not futile; (2) the benefit (manfa'a) – something naturally desired to motivate effort; (3) the title (sima) – the designation of the science, giving a summary of what will be detailed; and (4) the author (mu'allif) – to put the learner's heart at ease by knowing the author's standing. The commentator then delves into the theoretical distinction between purpose, benefit, and goal: if the outcome of an action motivates the doer, it is called a purpose; otherwise it is a benefit or goal. He notes that God's actions are not motivated by purposes, but they still have goals and benefits. The author of the commentary (likely the original text being commented on) intends that the ancients would mention the motivating cause for compiling the science, then follow it with the benefits that appeal to people. For the science of logic, the ultimate purpose is 'isma (infallibility from error). The commentator then explains the third head: 'sima' – it is like the name of the science, e.g., 'mantiq' is derived from 'nutq' (speech), referring to both external and internal speech, and the science perfects both. The name is either a verbal noun exaggerating its role or a place name indicating its function. Finally, the fourth head: 'mu'allif' – specifying the author to gain trust. The marginal notes by Abu al-Fath add that the third head defines the science by its description and the fourth specifies the author for reassurance. This passage is definitional, explaining the terminology and purpose of these introductory elements. In Islamic sciences, such introductions are common, e.g., in fiqh works where the author states the motivation for writing and the benefit of the knowledge contained therein.

Author explains the division of sciences and books into chapters.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A science and its book are divided into sections to facilitate learning.

This passage describes the seventh part of the sciences: division (qisma) and tabulation (tabwib). The commentator explains that this involves dividing both the science itself and the book that treats it into appropriate chapters.

3
Examine the division of a specific book
The commentator gives the example of 'our book' (probably Tahzeeb) divided into two main sections: logic and theology, each with its own subdivisions like introduction, aims, conclusion.
4
Apply to any Islamic science text
Recognize that most Islamic textbooks follow a similar pattern: a science is divided into topics, and the book is arranged accordingly. For instance, al-Shamsiyya has an introduction, three articles, and a conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the matn's enumeration of the sixth, seventh, and eighth parts of sciences. The commentator first explains what is meant by 'rank' (martaba) regarding logic: it should be studied after moral refinement and some geometry. He notes a contemporary opinion that it should be delayed until after literary sciences due to Arabic language prevalence. Then he explains 'division' (qisma) as the division of both the science and the book into chapters. He provides two examples: first, the general division of logic into nine or ten books (the Isagoge, definitions, etc.); second, the specific division of this book (Tahzeeb Sharh) into a logic section and a theology section, each with its own subdivisions, citing al-Shamsiyya. Finally, he defines 'teaching methods' (al-anha' al-ta'limiyya) as the path taught for general benefit, acknowledging confusion among commentators and affirming that his account is taken from Sharh al-Matali'.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (fihris) for the book 'Tahzeeb Sharh', listing the main topics and their corresponding page numbers. It serves as a structural overview, allowing the reader to locate specific discussions. The contents begin with the definition of logic and its subject, then proceed to concepts (tasawwurat) covering indication, simple and compound terms, universals, and the five predicables (genus, species, differentia, property, common accident). It concludes with the five arts: demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), rhetoric, poetics, and sophistry. The student should use this table as a reference for navigating the book's arguments.
