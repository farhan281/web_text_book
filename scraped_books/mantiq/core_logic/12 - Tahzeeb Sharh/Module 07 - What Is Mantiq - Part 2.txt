Distinguishing between Gharad (Purpose) and Manfa'a (Benefit)
Commentary clarifies the distinction between purpose and benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between gharad (purpose) and manfa'a (benefit) in the context of a scientific introduction, and apply this distinction to analyze the motivations of the founder and the learner.

Core Idea
Purpose drives the founder; benefit draws the learner.

A key insight in classical introductions is the difference between why a science was founded and why a student studies it. The passage carefully distinguishes between gharad, the purpose that motivates the founder, and manfa'a, the benefit that attracts the learner. This lesson explores that nuance and its theological implications regarding God's actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definitions
Find where the passage defines gharad and manfa'a. Gharad is what results from an act that motivates the agent to perform the act. Manfa'a (or fa'ida) is what results but does not motivate the agent.
The passage says: 'if it is a motivator for the agent... it is called a purpose (gharad); otherwise it is called a benefit (manfa'a).'
2
Apply to the Science of Logic
The passage states that the purpose (gharad) of logic is 'infallibility' (al-'isma), which was the goal of the founder. The benefit (manfa'a) for the student is to gain the ability to distinguish truth from falsehood, which naturally attracts learners.
The founder's purpose is specific and principled; the benefit is more general and appealing.
3
Consider the Theological Dimension
The commentator notes that God's actions are not motivated by purposes (aghrar), even though they contain countless benefits. This is because God is above being moved by external ends. This distinction preserves divine transcendence.
This shows that the gharad/manfa'a distinction is not just pedagogical but has theological weight.
4
Test Your Understanding
Think of another Islamic science, like kalam. The founder's purpose might be to defend the faith against heretics; the benefit for the student is certainty in belief. Identify which is which and explain why.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the parts of a scientific introduction. It defines the second, third, and fourth parts in detail, with explanation of key terms. The second part is al-manfa'a (benefit), which naturally motivates the student. The third part is al-sima (characteristic), which is the title of the science; the commentator explains the etymology of 'mantiq' (logic) from 'nutq' (speech), distinguishing external and internal speech. The fourth part is al-mu'allif (author), needed to give confidence to the student. The commentary also clarifies the distinction between gharad (purpose) and fa'ida (benefit): if the result motivates the agent, it is a purpose; otherwise it is a benefit. The commentator notes that God's actions are not motivated by purposes. The marginal notes by Abu al-Fath add that the characteristic defines the science by its specific property, and the author is specified for trust.
