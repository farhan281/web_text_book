Reconciling Hadiths on Beginning with Basmala and Hamd
Dialectical exchange resolving apparent contradiction between hadiths on beginning with basmala or hamd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of al-tawfiq to reconcile apparently contradictory hadiths by distinguishing between real, relative, and customary beginnings.

Core Idea
Reconcile contradictory hadiths by unpacking 'beginning' into real, relative, and customary.

When two hadiths command opposite actions, the scholar does not reject either but seeks harmony. In this passage, a commentator resolves the conflict between starting with 'bismillah' and starting with 'alhamdu lillah' by showing that 'beginning' has multiple senses. You will learn how to identify and apply these distinctions in juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting commands
Find the two hadiths: one commands beginning with tasmiya ('bismillah'), the other with tahmid ('alhamdu lillah'). Both seem to require placing that phrase first in a speech or action.
Recognize the apparent contradiction: both cannot be literally first.
2
Formulate the objection
The interlocutor argues that if 'beginning' means literal first, acting on one hadith violates the other. This is the standard dialectical move: highlight inconsistency.
The objection assumes 'beginning' is univocal (one meaning).
3
Analyze the primary resolution
The commentator introduces three types of beginning: real (precedes everything), relative (precedes some things but is preceded by others), and customary (precedes the intended main content). He assigns real beginning to tasmiya and relative/customary to tahmid.
This removes the contradiction because they are not competing for the same slot.
4
Examine the secondary objection
The interlocutor asks why not assign relative to tasmiya and real to tahmid. The commentator argues that tasmiya deals with God's Essence, which precedes attributes (tahmid), so the real beginning fits tasmiya better.
This shows how theological priorities inform the reconciliation.
5
Consider alternative definitions
Some scholars define relative beginning more broadly (absolute precedence, not necessarily preceded). This leads to partial overlap between real and relative. Understanding such nuances deepens one's ability to apply tawfiq.
Different schools of logic/terminology affect how reconciliation works.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange (question and answer) about reconciling two hadiths: one commands beginning with 'bismillah' and another with 'alhamdu lillah'. The interlocutor raises a problem: if we follow one hadith, we neglect the other, so opening with both seems impossible. The commentator resolves this by distinguishing three types of 'beginning': real (haqiqi), relative (idafi), and customary ('urfi). The hadith about tasmiya is considered as real beginning (before everything else), while the hadith about tahmid is taken as relative (precedes some things but not the tasmiya) or customary (precedes the main content). The interlocutor then asks why not reverse the assignments. The commentator answers that tasmiya deals with the Essence (dhat) of God, while tahmid deals with attributes (sifat); since the Essence precedes the attributes, the real beginning should be for tasmiya. Another objection asks why tasmiya cannot also be relative. The commentator explains that in the hadith of tasmiya, there is no idea of being preceded by something else, so relative beginning does not apply; but some scholars define relative differently, leading to a partial overlap. This shows careful legal and theological reasoning to harmonize textual evidences.

Key Terms
الابتداء الحقيقي (al-ibtida' al-haqiqi): Real beginning: starting with something that has nothing before it.
الابتداء الإضافي (al-ibtida' al-idafi): Relative beginning: starting with something that is preceded by something else but precedes other things.
الابتداء العرفي (al-ibtida' al-'urfi): Customary beginning: starting with something that precedes the intended main purpose.
التوفيق (al-tawfiq): Reconciliation between conflicting texts by interpreting them in compatible ways. [Note: In usul al-fiqh, this is a standard method; here applied to hadith.]

Two Reasons for Describing Instead of Naming
Author gives two reasons for using the description instead of naming.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the two rhetorical reasons given in the matn for using the description 'the one He sent' instead of explicitly naming the Prophet.

Core Idea
Describing instead of naming can magnify and uniquely identify — rhetorical tools in Islamic texts.

In this passage, the commentator explains why the author of the matn chose to describe the Prophet as 'the one He sent' rather than mentioning his name. Two reasons are given: one based on veneration, the other on uniqueness of identification. This lesson unpacks those reasons and shows how they function in Islamic discourse.

Guided Path
1
Find the two reasons
Locate in the Arabic where the commentator lists the two aspects (وجهين). The first begins with 'الأول: إن عدم التصريح يشعر بالتعظيم والإجلال'. The second begins with 'الثاني: إن هذا الوصف من حيث أنه لا يتبادر الذهن منه إلا إلى ذاته'.
The marker 'وجهين' signals two reasons.
2
Explain the first reason
The first reason is that not explicitly naming the Prophet indicates magnification (تعظيم) and veneration (إجلال). The commentator argues that avoiding the direct name elevates the subject.
3
Explain the second reason
The second reason is that the description is so specific that the mind immediately goes to the Prophet alone. It functions like a proper name but with added rhetorical force.
The phrase 'لا يتبادر الذهن منه إلا إليه' is key.
4
Reflect on description vs name
Consider why the author chose a description over a name. In Islamic tradition, descriptions can carry layers of meaning (ta'dhim, tawqir) that a simple name may not convey, especially when following Qur'anic style.
Wasf (description) can be more expressive than ism (name) in certain contexts.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical unit (naqd wa jawab) addressing why the author of the matn used the description 'on the one He sent' instead of explicitly naming the Prophet Muhammad. The commentator first gives two reasons: (1) not naming indicates magnification and veneration; (2) the description is so specific that the mind immediately goes to the Prophet. Then two objections are raised: (1) If not naming is a sign of veneration, then Allah should not name Himself even more so, but He does in the Qur'an (e.g., 'Allah' in Surah al-Fatiha). Also, both not naming and naming can be seen as veneration or blessing, so why choose one? (2) If the description uniquely identifies, then Allah has attributes like 'al-Rahman' that also uniquely identify, so why not use those? The answer is that this follows the textual precedent: in the Qur'anic verse about sending blessings (al-Ahzab 56), Allah did not explicitly name the Prophet, whereas in the verse of praise (al-Fatiha 2), He did explicitly name Himself. So the author merely follows the Qur'anic style. Another point: the absolute term 'man arsalahu' (the one He sent) goes to the perfect individual, which is Muhammad, so it effectively names him. This passage uses Islamic examples (Qur'anic verses) to argue the point.

Dialectical Defense of the Descriptive Epithet
Author responds to two dialectical objections about the descriptive epithet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange (objections and answer) regarding why the Prophet is described rather than named, and evaluate the strength of the defense.

Core Idea
A dialectical objection can be answered by appealing to textual precedent (ittiba' al-nass).

After giving the reasons for using a description, the commentator anticipates two objections and provides a response. This lesson walks through the dialectical structure: naqd (objection) and jawab (answer). Understanding this exchange is key to mastering the adab al-bahth tradition in Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection
The first objection argues: if not naming indicates veneration, then Allah should not name Himself a fortiori (first awla), but He does (e.g., in al-Fatiha 2). Also, both naming and not naming can be seen as veneration or blessing, so why choose one? The objection is about consistency.
The phrase 'بالطريق الأولى' indicates a fortiori reasoning.
2
Identify the second objection
The second objection argues: if the description uniquely identifies the Prophet, then Allah has attributes like al-Rahman that also uniquely identify Him, so why not use those? This challenges the uniqueness claim.
The objection points to Allah's attributes as similar descriptive identifiers.
3
Find the answer
The answer (جواب) is that the author is following the Qur'anic text (ittiba' al-nass). In the verse about sending blessings (al-Ahzab 56), Allah did not explicitly name the Prophet, but in the verse of praise (al-Fatiha 2), He did name Himself. So the author’s choice matches the Qur'an's practice.
The key phrase is 'اتباع النص'.
4
Analyze the strength of the answer
Consider whether the answer fully addresses the objections. It shows that the author is not making an independent judgment but following divine precedent. However, does it explain the reasoning behind the Qur'anic choice? The answer is procedural rather than substantive, but it suffices in a dialectical framework where adherence to text is authoritative.
In jadal, citing the text (nass) is a strong move.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical unit (naqd wa jawab) addressing why the author of the matn used the description 'on the one He sent' instead of explicitly naming the Prophet Muhammad. The commentator first gives two reasons: (1) not naming indicates magnification and veneration; (2) the description is so specific that the mind immediately goes to the Prophet. Then two objections are raised: (1) If not naming is a sign of veneration, then Allah should not name Himself even more so, but He does in the Qur'an (e.g., 'Allah' in Surah al-Fatiha). (2) If the description uniquely identifies, then Allah has attributes like 'al-Rahman' that also uniquely identify, so why not use those? The answer is that this follows the textual precedent: in the Qur'anic verse about sending blessings (al-Ahzab 56), Allah did not explicitly name the Prophet, whereas in the verse of praise (al-Fatiha 2), He did explicitly name Himself. So the author merely follows the Qur'anic style. Another point: the absolute term 'man arsalahu' (the one He sent) goes to the perfect individual, which is Muhammad, so it effectively names him. This passage uses Islamic examples (Qur'anic verses) to argue the point.

Distinguishing Need for Logic from Need to Learn It
Objection that human nature suffices for correct reasoning is answered, showing need for logic remains.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the objector's confusion in a dialectical exchange and apply the distinction between intrinsic logical need and external learning to analogous cases in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Logic is inherently needed; learning it is only a method, not the essence.

This passage presents a common objection: if human nature (fiṭra) is sufficient for correct reasoning, then why study logic? The answer is a masterclass in dialectical precision—it separates the inherent need for logical principles from the contingent need to study them formally. By mastering this distinction, you will learn how to defuse objections that confuse essential and accidental requirements.

The essential need for logical principles is independent of whether they are innate or acquired. The objection confuses the metaphysical need (logic as a normative system) with the contingent need (learning it from books).

The Five Premise Types in Non-Demonstrative Qiyas
Author defines the five types of premises: mashhurat, musallamat, maqbulat, maznunat, and mukhayyilat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five premise types (mashhurat, musallamat, maqbulat, maznunat, mukhayyilat) and explain their roles in dialectical, rhetorical, and poetic syllogisms.

Core Idea
Not all arguments aim at certainty; these five premise types are tools for persuasion with different epistemic foundations.

In the Islamic logical tradition, demonstration (burhan) yields certainty, but much of human discourse—dialectic, rhetoric, poetry—relies on premises that are not certain. The author defines these five types, each with a distinct basis: universal consensus, opponent concession, authority, probability, and emotional influence. Understanding them allows you to analyse arguments in kalam, fiqh, and other disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Locate the five definitions
In the Arabic passage, find where each of the five terms is introduced and note the defining characteristic given by the author.
The terms appear in order: mashhurat, musallamat, maqbulat, maznunat, mukhayyilat.
2
Identify the basis for each type
For each type, determine the epistemic or social basis: consensus (mashhurat), opponent concession or disciplinary convention (musallamat), authority of a trusted figure (maqbulat), preponderant but non-certain judgment (maznunat), emotional effect without assent (mukhayyilat).
Mashhurat can be universal or group-specific.
3
Analyse the relationship between maqbulat and maznunat
The commentary explains that maqbulat is more general than maznunat, because it includes both certain and probable statements from authorities. The term maznunat, when contrasted, refers to probables not from authority. This resolves the apparent contradiction in rhetorical syllogisms that use both.
The example of 'So-and-so walks at night, so he is a thief' illustrates maznunat without authority.
4
Relate each type to its qiyas
Mashhurat and musallamat are used in dialectical qiyas (jadal). Maqbulat and maznunat are used in rhetorical qiyas (khataba). Mukhayyilat are used in poetic qiyas (shi'r). Note that rhetorical qiyas can also include maqbulat that are certain.
Mukhayyilat gain power when combined with rhyme or meter.

Commentary
This passage defines the five types of premises used in the non-demonstrative arts of logic: jadali (dialectical), khatabi (rhetorical), and shi'ri (poetic) syllogisms. The author begins by defining 'common opinions' (mashhurat): propositions universally accepted or accepted by a specific group, like the goodness of beneficence or the badness of slaughtering animals among Indians. Next, 'conceded premises' (musallamat): propositions conceded by an opponent in debate or established in one science and accepted in another by convention, e.g., jurists accepting that 'the command indicates obligation' from usul al-fiqh. 'Accepted premises' (maqbulat) are taken from authorities believed to be reliable, such as saints (supported by miracles) and sages (distinguished by intellect). 'Probable premises' (maznunat) are those judged by the mind as preponderant but not certain, e.g., a conjecture based on behavior. 'Imaginative premises' (mukhayyilat) are propositions that do not command assent but influence the soul through emotional persuasion, especially when rhymed or metered. The passage also includes a discussion of the relationship between maqbulat and maznunat: a question arises because rhetorical syllogism yields probability, so its premises must be probable, yet it includes both maqbulat and maznunat. The answer is that maqbulat are more general: they include both certain and probable propositions, and when contrasted, 'maznunat' refers to probables outside the scope of maqbulat. Another repulsion is given to avoid the misconception that maznunat are more general; the example of inferring theft from night-walking shows probables not based on authority. The commentary uses examples from fiqh (command indicating obligation) and geometry to illustrate conceded premises, and from everyday reasoning for probables.
