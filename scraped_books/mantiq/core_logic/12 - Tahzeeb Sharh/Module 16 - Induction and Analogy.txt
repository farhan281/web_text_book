The Three Types of Proof in Islamic Logic
Author divides arguments into three types and defines each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between syllogism, induction, and analogy, and identify which type of reasoning is used in a given Islamic argument.

Core Idea
Every logical argument moves either from universal to particular, particular to universal, or particular to particular.

This lesson introduces the threefold classification of arguments that forms the backbone of Islamic logic. The author explains that reasoning can proceed from a universal rule to its particulars (syllogism), from particulars to a universal rule (induction), or from one particular to another under a common universal (analogy). Understanding this classification helps you recognize the structure of arguments in kalam, fiqh, and tafsir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of reasoning
Look at the starting point and endpoint of the argument. Does it begin with a universal claim or a particular observation?
The three types are defined by the direction: universal→particular (qiyas), particular→universal (istiqra'), particular→particular (tamthil).
2
Apply to the example: animal jaw movement
The text gives an example: observing that humans, horses, and cows move their lower jaws when chewing leads to the universal claim 'every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing.' This is induction.
3
Apply to the example: wine and bang
The analogy example: wine is prohibited because it intoxicates; bang is also an intoxicant, so bang is prohibited. This moves from one particular (wine) to another (bang) under the universal 'intoxicant'.
This is tamthil; in usul al-fiqh it becomes qiyas with strict conditions.
4
Check the classification against the objection
An objector says that reasoning from universal to universal exists. The author replies that the classification itself is based on induction (observation of common usage), not rational necessity, so it is not exhaustive in a rational sense.
This shows the pragmatic approach of Islamic logicians.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') and situates it among the three types of argument. The author begins by explaining that proof (hujja) is of three kinds: reasoning from a universal to its particulars (syllogism, qiyas), from particulars to a universal (induction, istiqra'), and from one particular to another under a common universal (analogy, tamthil). Induction is then defined as the proof in which one reasons from the judgment of particulars to the judgment of their universal. The commentary addresses a potential objection that this classification might exclude reasoning from one universal to another; the response is that the classification is itself inductive (based on observation) rather than rational (necessary). The passage then gives concrete examples: for induction, one observes that individual animals (human, horse, cow) move their lower jaw when chewing, and concludes that all animals do so. For analogy, an example is given: wine is prohibited because it intoxicates, and bang (an intoxicant) shares that property, so bang is also prohibited. The author then distinguishes between complete induction (istiqra' taam), which examines all particulars and yields certainty (and is essentially a syllogism called 'divisive syllogism'), and incomplete induction (istiqra' naaqis), which examines only most particulars and does not yield certainty. The term 'induction' is often used for the incomplete type, though it may also include the complete type if the definition focuses on the method of examination rather than the result.

Induction: Complete vs. Incomplete
Author distinguishes complete from incomplete induction and explains their certainty levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between complete and incomplete induction and explain why only complete induction yields certainty.

Core Idea
Complete induction is certain but is really a syllogism; incomplete induction is probable but practically useful.

This lesson dives deeper into induction, which the author defines as reasoning from particulars to a universal. The key distinction is between complete induction (istiqra' taam), which examines all particulars and yields certainty, and incomplete induction (istiqra' naaqis), which examines only most and yields probability. The commentator notes that complete induction is essentially a syllogism called 'divisive syllogism' (qiyas muqassam). Understanding this distinction prevents overconfidence in arguments from incomplete data.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
The author defines induction as reasoning from the judgment of particulars to the judgment of their universal. This is the core idea.
The term 'particulars' can mean all or most, leading to two types.
2
Examine the two types of induction
Complete induction (istiqra' taam) covers all particulars; incomplete (istiqra' naaqis) covers only most. The commentator says that when all are covered, the result is certain and the argument is actually a syllogism.
Complete induction is called 'divisive syllogism' because it divides the universal into its parts.
3
Apply to the animal jaw example
The example given is observing humans, horses, cows, etc. moving their lower jaws. If we observe all animal species, it is complete; if only many, it is incomplete. The text treats this as incomplete unless proven exhaustive.
In practice, most scientific inductions are incomplete.
4
Assess the certainty level
Complete induction yields certainty (yaqin); incomplete yields probability (zann). The commentator stresses that incomplete induction is what is usually meant by 'induction' in logic.
This is crucial for evaluating arguments in fiqh and kalam: an incomplete induction does not prove a universal rule.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') and situates it within a threefold classification of proofs: syllogism (qiyas), induction, and analogy (tamthil). Induction is reasoning from particulars to a universal, as opposed to syllogism which goes from universal to particular. The author also discusses the logical structure, noting that induction can be reduced to two conditional syllogisms via reductio ad absurdum. The key distinction is between complete induction (covering all particulars) and incomplete induction (covering most). Complete induction yields certainty and is actually a form of syllogism, while incomplete induction is probabilistic. The commentary uses an example from natural theology: observing that humans, horses, and cows move their lower jaw when chewing leads to the universal claim that every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing. Another example from jurisprudence: wine is intoxicating (haram), and betel nut is another intoxicant; by incomplete induction, one might conclude all intoxicants are haram. Then it notes that the definition derived by the author (Taftazani) from al-Farabi and al-Ghazali, i.e., 'the examination and tracing of particulars to establish a universal judgment,' has an apparent tolerance (tasamuh). It further explains that the term 'istiqra' can refer to the source meaning (the act of examining) or the argument resulting from that examination; here it is defined in the first sense. The passage then discusses the classification of induction into complete (tam) and incomplete (naqis). Ultimately, the technical meaning of induction (istiqra' istilahi) is limited to seeking a universal judgment from tracing most particulars; complete induction and arguments to particular judgments are considered syllogisms, not induction in the technical sense.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') and clarifies its types and ruling. It begins by giving the correct definition: 'To establish a universal judgment.' Then it notes that the definition derived by the author (Taftazani) from al-Farabi and al-Ghazali, i.e., 'the examination and tracing of particulars to establish a universal judgment,' has an apparent tolerance (tasamuh). The reason is that such tracing is not a known proposition (ma'lum tasdiqi) leading to an unknown proposition (majhul tasdiqi), so it does not qualify as a hujja (argument). The author tolerates this to indicate that the term 'istiqra' is used by transmption (naql) from its literal meaning, not by improvisation (irtijal). The commentary then distinguishes between irtijal (using a word without connection) and naql (transferring with a connection). It also explains that the definition specifies a universal judgment as the sought object, not a particular one; this is clarified by analyzing the phrase 'to establish a universal judgment' as either a description or a genitive construction. The text then quotes definitions from al-Farabi and al-Bazdawi, noting the tolerance in them. It further explains that the term 'istiqra' can refer to the source meaning (the act of examining) or the argument resulting from that examination; here it is defined in the first sense. The passage then discusses the classification of induction into complete (tam) and incomplete (naqis). Complete induction examines all particulars and reduces to a dividing syllogism (qiyas muqassam), an example of which is given: 'Every animal is either rational or non-rational; every rational is sentient; every non-rational animal is sentient; therefore, every animal is sentient.' However, the text notes that if the sought judgment is particular (e.g., 'Some animal is a horse'), then tracing some particulars yields certainty. Ultimately, the technical meaning of induction (istiqra' istilahi) is limited to seeking a universal judgment from tracing most particulars; complete induction and arguments to particular judgments are considered syllogisms, not induction in the technical sense.

The commentary by Abu al-Fath and Isma'il elaborates: complete induction is actually not induction but a divided syllogism; terminological induction is only the incomplete type that yields probability. If a complete survey is made, it falls under syllogism, not induction. Similarly, arguing from particulars to a particular judgment is also a syllogism, not induction. Thus, the true terminological induction is limited to incomplete induction that yields probability.

Key Terms
الاستقراء (al-istiqra'): Induction: a logical argument that infers a universal judgment from surveying most particulars (incomplete) or all particulars (complete); terminologically, only incomplete induction is called induction. [Note: In earlier logic, istiqra' was often a broader term; here it is specifically distinguished from complete enumeration which becomes divided syllogism.]
الاستقراء التام (al-istiqra' al-tam): Complete induction: examining all particulars to reach a universal judgment; reduces to divided syllogism and yields certainty.
الاستقراء الناقص (al-istiqra' al-naqis): Incomplete induction: surveying most particulars to infer a universal judgment; yields only probability.
القياس المقسم (al-qiyas al-muqassam): Divided syllogism: a compound syllogism of categorical and disjunctive premises, where the disjunctive is affirmative universal preventing emptiness. [Note: This term is used specifically in this commentary; in broader logic it may be called qiyas sharti.]

The Method of Tamthil: Co-occurrence and Elimination
Explanation of tamthil's method and premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the three premises of a valid tamthil (analogical argument) in Islamic legal reasoning and identify the role of al-dawran wa al-tardid in establishing the 'illa.

Core Idea
Tamthil is valid only when the 'illa (cause) is established via co-occurrence and elimination.

Tamthil (analogy) is a cornerstone of Islamic jurisprudence, allowing jurists to extend rulings from known cases to new ones. This lesson unpacks the method for establishing the critical second premise—the 'illa (effective cause). You will learn the three premises of a valid tamthil and how the method of al-dawran wa al-tardid (co-occurrence and elimination) secures the second premise, which is the most problematic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three premises of tamthil
From the passage, extract the three premises: (1) the ruling is established in the original case (al-asl), (2) the cause of the ruling is a specific attribute (al-'illa), and (3) that attribute exists in the branch case (al-far'). Write them in your own words.
These correspond exactly to the structure of qiyas in usul al-fiqh.
2
Understand the difficulty of the second premise
The commentator states that the first and third premises are obvious, but the second requires justification. Why? Because the existence of the 'illa in the original must be proven, not assumed. This is where al-dawran wa al-tardid comes in.
3
Learn al-dawran wa al-tardid
Al-dawran means the 'illa co-occurs with the ruling (when the 'illa is present, the ruling is present). Al-tardid means when the 'illa is absent, the ruling is absent. This method confirms that the attribute is indeed the cause.
This is a standard method from usul al-fiqh, often used to prove the 'illa for analogical extension.
4
Apply to a real fiqh example
Consider the prohibition of wine due to intoxication. The original (asl) is wine, the ruling is 'prohibited', and the 'illa is intoxication. The branch (far') is nabidh (date drink) which also intoxicates. The three premises apply, and al-dawran wa al-tardid confirms intoxication as the cause.
This example is classic in Islamic legal theory.

Dawaran: Co-variation as a Marker of Causality
Author explains dawaran and tardid as methods to prove the 'illa in analogical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of dawaran to test whether an attribute is the effective cause ('illa) in an analogical argument from Islamic jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Dawaran uses presence and absence of an attribute to indicate causation, but it can also indicate a condition.

In this lesson, we explore the first method for establishing the 'illa in analogical reasoning: dawaran (co-variation). The author explains how if a ruling exists when an attribute exists and disappears when the attribute disappears, that attribute is considered a candidate cause. We will examine the classic example of wine and intoxication to see how dawaran works, and note its limitations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and candidate attribute
In the example, the ruling is prohibition (hukm al-hurma) and the attribute is intoxication (isqār). Note that the text states the ruling follows the attribute in existence and non-existence.
2
Check for co-variation
The text says: 'ما دام مسكرا حرام، وإذا زال عنه الإسكار زالت حرمته' – as long as it is intoxicating, it is forbidden; when intoxication ceases, prohibition ceases.
3
Recognize the inference and its limitation
The method concludes that the attribute is the cause because of this correlation. But the author later notes that co-variation could also indicate a condition (shart) or a part of a general cause, so dawaran alone is not conclusive.
This critique will be explored in a later lesson.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the two methods used in analogical reasoning (tamthil) to establish the effective cause ('illa): dawaran (co-variation) and tardid (sifting/division). The author defines dawaran as tracing the ruling's occurrence with the presence of an attribute and its removal with the attribute's absence, using the example of wine: it is forbidden when intoxicating, and when intoxication ceases, so does the prohibition. Tardid (sabr wa taqsim) involves listing all possible attributes of the original and eliminating those that fail the test, again using wine: the possible causes are enumerated (grapes, liquefaction, specific state, taste, smell, intoxication) and all except intoxication are eliminated because they appear in non-prohibited substances like treacle. The passage then critiques both methods: dawaran fails because it could also indicate a condition or a part of a general cause, not necessarily the cause; tardid fails because the enumeration may be incomplete, and even if a common attribute is present in both original and branch, the original may have a special condition required for causality or the branch may have a preventer. The marginal notes (Isma'il) clarify the etymology of 'sabr' as examination or collection, both fitting the context of sifting through possibilities.

Key Terms
الدوران (al-dawarān): Co-variation: the correlation of a ruling's existence and non-existence with an attribute, taken as evidence of causation.
العلة (al-'illa): The effective cause that brings about a ruling in analogical reasoning.
الحكم (al-ḥukm): The ruling or legal judgment that is being explained.
الوصف (al-waṣf): The attribute or property being considered as a potential cause.

Tardid: Sifting Through Possibilities to Identify the Cause
Author explains dawaran and tardid as methods to prove the 'illa in analogical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of tardid (sabr wa taqsim) to enumerate and eliminate possible causes to identify the 'illa in an analogical argument.

Core Idea
Tardid narrows down causes by listing all candidate attributes and eliminating those that do not satisfy the condition of co-variation.

The second method for establishing the 'illa is tardid, also known as sabr wa taqsim (examination and division). The author explains that one first enumerates all possible attributes of the original case, then tests each by checking whether it exists without the ruling. The attribute that survives elimination is taken as the cause. We will walk through the wine example where several attributes are eliminated until intoxication remains.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate all candidate attributes
In the wine example, list: grapes (element), liquefaction (al-mi'ān), specific state (al-kawn al-makhsūs), specific taste (al-ta'm al-makhsūs), specific smell (al-rā'iha al-makhsūṣa), intoxication (al-isqār).
2
Eliminate attributes that exist without the ruling
Check each: grapes exist in treacle without prohibition, so not a cause. Similarly, the others (except intoxication) are eliminated by finding cases where they exist but the ruling is absent.
The text mentions treacle (dibs) as a counterexample for grapes.
3
Identify the surviving attribute
Only intoxication remains, so it is determined as the 'illa.
Note: This method assumes exhaustive enumeration, which may not be possible.

Critique of Dawaran and Tardid: Logical Weaknesses
Author explains dawaran and tardid as methods to prove the 'illa in analogical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical weaknesses in the methods of dawaran and tardid as critiqued in the text.

Core Idea
Both dawaran and tardid are insufficient to prove causality due to possibilities of conditions and incomplete enumeration.

After explaining both methods, the author declares them weak and provides reasons. Dawaran can be due to a condition rather than a cause, and tardid's enumeration may be incomplete, and even if the cause is common, special conditions in the original or branch may affect the ruling. This lesson unpacks these criticisms.

Guided Path
1
Understand the critique of dawaran
The author says: 'الجزء الأخير من العلة العامة والشرط المساوي يدار المعلول عليه مع أنه ليس بعلة' – the final part of a general cause and an equivalent condition co-vary with the effect but are not the cause. So dawaran cannot distinguish cause from condition.
2
Understand the critique of tardid (first issue)
First: 'حصر العلة في الأوصاف المذكورة ممنوع' – the enumeration of attributes may not be complete, so the real cause might be missing from the list.
This is a problem of induction.
3
Understand the critique of tardid (second issue)
Second: 'كون المشترك علة في الأصل لا يلزم منه كونه علة في الفرع' – even if the common attribute is the cause in the original, it may not be in the branch due to special conditions (shart) in the original or preventers (mani') in the branch.

الجزء الأخير (al-juz' al-akhīr): The final part of a general cause, which may co-vary but is not the complete cause.
السبر والتقسيم (al-sabr wa al-taqsīm): Examination and division: the method of tardid.

Key Takeaway
The weaknesses of dawaran and tardid demonstrate that analogical reasoning in Islam requires careful consideration of conditions and completeness, and these methods provide only probable, not certain, knowledge of causation.
