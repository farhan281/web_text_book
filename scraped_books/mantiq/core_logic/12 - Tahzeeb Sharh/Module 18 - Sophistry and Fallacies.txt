Verbal Fallacies in Sophistical Syllogisms
Defines sophistical syllogism and explains verbal fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal fallacies arising from homonymy and word-image confusion in Islamic logical arguments.

Core Idea
Sophistical syllogisms deceive by camouflaging false premises as true ones via verbal ambiguity.

The passage defines the sophistical syllogism and then immediately illustrates how verbal ambiguity—especially homonymy and treating words as literal images—can trick the mind. This lesson focuses on recognizing these verbal traps so you can dismantle deceptive arguments in Islamic scholarly discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the sophistical syllogism
Note that it is composed of 'estimative premises' (wahmiyyat) and 'deceptive premises' (mushabbahat). The deceptive premises are false but resemble true ones due to ambiguity.
Wahmiyyat are judgments of the estimative faculty about non-sensibles; mushabbahat are false propositions that seem true.
2
Examine the homonymy example
The passage presents the example of 'wajib' used in two senses: necessary existence vs. praiseworthy. Identify how the argument equivocates between these meanings.
The fallacy occurs because the same word is used with different definitions in different premises.
3
Analyze the word-as-image fallacy
Consider the example of a painted horse on a wall being called a 'horse', then concluding it neighs. This confuses a representation with the actual thing.
This is a verbal fallacy because the word 'horse' is used literally for a picture, not a real animal.
4
Summarize how verbal ambiguity creates false resemblance
Both homonymy and word-image confusion make false premises look like true ones. Recognizing these patterns helps you spot sophistical arguments.

Commentary
This passage defines the sophistical syllogism and discusses its fallacies. It begins by identifying the sophistical syllogism as composed of 'estimative premises' (al-wahmiyyat) and 'deceptive premises' (al-mushabbahat). The commentator explains the etymology of 'sophistry' from the Greek 'sophia' meaning disguised wisdom. 'Wahmiyyat' are judgments of the estimative faculty about non-sensibles analogized to sensibles, e.g., 'every existent is something.' 'Mushabbahat' are false propositions resembling true ones due to verbal or conceptual ambiguity. The text then criticizes later logicians for their brief treatment of the five arts, calling it a harmful abbreviation. It proceeds to detail verbal fallacies: homonymy, as in the example of 'obligatory' (wajib) having different meanings (necessary existence vs. praiseworthy), and treating a word as a literal image, as in claiming a painted horse is a horse and therefore neighs. Conceptual fallacies include ignoring the non-existence of the subject in universal affirmatives (e.g., 'every human-and-horse' has no real subject), confusing natural with universal propositions (e.g., 'man is an animal' doesn't make man a genus), and mixing mental with external attributes (e.g., concluding substance is an accident because it exists in the mind). The passage also notes that conceptual ambiguity can be material (begging the question) or formal (violating syllogistic conditions). Finally, it reiterates the criticism of later logicians for their neglect, stating they should have presented the complete syllogisms and their conclusions for each art.

Conceptual Fallacies and the Critique of Brevity
Explains conceptual fallacies and criticizes later logicians' brevity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify conceptual fallacies as material (begging the question) or formal (missing syllogistic conditions) and evaluate the critique of later logicians' brevity.

Core Idea
Conceptual fallacies hide errors in the substance or structure of reasoning, not just in words.

After discussing verbal fallacies, the passage turns to conceptual fallacies—errors in the meaning or logical form that make an argument unsound. It distinguishes material fallacies (like begging the question) from formal fallacies (like missing syllogistic conditions). The text also sharply criticizes later logicians for their harmful abbreviation (iqtisar mukhill) of the five arts. This lesson trains you to detect these deeper fallacies and understand the importance of thorough presentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify conceptual fallacies from the passage
The passage lists several examples: ignoring non-existence of subject (human-and-horse), confusing natural with universal (man as genus), mixing mental with external (substance is accident), material fallacy (begging the question), and formal fallacy (violating syllogistic conditions).
These are errors in the content or structure, not in word choice.
2
Distinguish material from formal conceptual fallacies
Material fallacies (like musadara 'ala al-matlub) involve the content of premises; formal fallacies occur when the syllogistic conditions (e.g., minor positive, major universal in first figure) are violated.
The passage explicitly divides conceptual ambiguity into material and formal sides.
3
Analyze the critique of later logicians
The author calls their treatment 'iqtisar mukhill' (harmful abbreviation), contrasting it with praiseworthy ikhtisar. They claim the later logicians summarized and neglected the five arts, failing to provide complete syllogisms and their conclusions.
This critique is a call for thoroughness in logical education.
4
Connect conceptual fallacies to real Islamic reasoning
For each example, consider how it could appear in fiqh or kalam: e.g., begging the question in proving God's existence, or confusing mental attributes with external ones in theology.

Definition and Premise Types of Sophistical Syllogism
Defines sophistical syllogism and its premise types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a sophistical syllogism and distinguish between its two premise types: wahmiyyat and mushabbihaat.

Core Idea
Sophistical syllogisms deceive through imaginative premises (wahmiyyat) or ambiguous premises (mushabbihaat).

A sophistical syllogism is not a valid proof but a deceptive argument that leads the mind to accept false conclusions. The passage defines it and explains the two types of premises that compose it. Understanding these helps you recognize when an argument is relying on imagination or ambiguity rather than sound reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of sophistical syllogism
Find the sentence that defines al-qiyas al-sufisti. Note that it is composed of two types of premises.
2
Distinguish wahmiyyat from mushabbihaat
Wahmiyyat are judgments of the imagination about non-sensible things based on analogy to sensible things. Mushabbihaat are false propositions that resemble true primary or well-known propositions due to ambiguity.
Example of wahmiyyat: 'Everything that exists is [something]' – a weak analogy.
3
Recognize the Greek origin of 'sophistical'
The term is derived from Greek 'Sopha Estha' meaning disguised wisdom. This shows the historical context of the concept.
4
Understand the critique of later scholars
The passage criticizes later logicians for giving a deficient abbreviation (iqtisar mukhill) of the five arts. This highlights the importance of thoroughness in logical exposition.

Verbal Ambiguity in Sophistical Syllogisms
Illustrates verbal ambiguity with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify verbal ambiguity (ishtibah lafzi) in logical arguments, specifically homonymy (ishtirak) and treating representations as real things.

Core Idea
Verbal ambiguity occurs when a word has multiple meanings (homonymy) or when a representation is mistaken for the thing itself.

The passage gives two striking examples of verbal ambiguity that lead to absurd conclusions. Understanding these helps you detect when an argument exploits the vagueness of language rather than sound reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of verbal ambiguity
The passage presents two types: (1) homonymy (ishtirak) – a word with multiple meanings, and (2) treating a representation as the actual thing (e.g., a picture of a horse as a real horse).
2
Analyze the homonymy example with 'wajib'
The argument asks: 'Is any evil thing wajib?' It then concludes absurdly. The fallacy is that 'wajib' has two meanings: necessary of existence (wajib al-wujud) and obligatory to act upon (wajib shar'i). The argument equivocates between them.
In Islamic theology, Allah is wajib al-wujud; in fiqh, actions are wajib.
3
Analyze the representation example with the horse picture
The argument says: 'This picture of a horse is a horse; every horse neighs; therefore this picture neighs.' The fallacy is that the word 'horse' in the premise refers to a representation, not a real horse.
This is similar to confusing a concept with its instance.
4
Generalize the principle
Verbal ambiguity arises when a term is used with different meanings in different premises, making the argument invalid.

Conceptual Ambiguity in Sophistical Syllogisms
Lists examples of conceptual ambiguity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conceptual fallacies (ishtibah ma'nawi) in logical arguments, including material defects (begging the question) and formal defects (invalid mood), as well as fallacies involving subject existence, natural vs universal propositions, and mental vs external existence.

Core Idea
Conceptual ambiguity arises from errors in matter (begging the question) or form (invalid syllogistic mood) or from subtle confusions in the interpretation of propositions.

Beyond verbal tricks, many fallacies arise from deeper conceptual mistakes. This passage catalogs several types: when the subject does not actually exist, when a natural proposition is mistaken for a universal one, when mental existence is confused with external existence, and when the syllogism violates formal conditions. These are common in theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy of non-existent subject
The example: 'Every human and horse is a human; every human and horse is a horse; therefore some human is a horse.' The error is that 'human and horse' does not refer to any existing thing; so the subject is empty.
In Islamic logic, this relates to the condition that the subject in affirmative propositions must have external existence.
2
Distinguish natural from universal propositions
The example: 'Man is an animal; animal is a genus; therefore man is a genus.' The fallacy is taking a natural proposition ('man is an animal' meaning by nature) as a universal proposition, which leads to an invalid inference.
The natural proposition states an essential property, not a universal quantifier.
3
Recognize confusion of mental and external existence
The example: 'Substance exists in the mind; everything in the mind is an accident; therefore substance is an accident.' This erroneously applies a property of mental existence to external existence.
In Islamic philosophy, substance (jawhar) exists externally, not merely in the mind.
4
Understand material and formal conceptual ambiguity
Material ambiguity (begging the question) occurs when the premises assume the conclusion. Formal ambiguity occurs when the syllogistic conditions are violated (e.g., minor negative and major particular in first figure).
The passage gives a vague example for begging the question; the formal example is clear.

(إسماعيل) اقتصار مخل: وهو تقليل اللفظ والمعنى والاختصار تقليل اللفظ وكثرة المعنى، وهذا محمود، وذلك مذموم. قد أجملوه وأهملوه وكان الواجب عليهم تصوير الصناعات الخمس كإتيان القياسات ونتائجها وبيان أحكامها.
The assents. The sophistical syllogism. And sometimes it is sophistical, composed of wahmiyyat and mushabbihaat. His statement: 'And sometimes sophistical' is attributed to sophistry, which is derived from Sufista, an Arabized form of 'Sopha Estha', a Greek language meaning disguised and deceptive wisdom. His statement: 'And from wahmiyyat' are the propositions that the imagination judges regarding non-sensible things by analogy to sensible things, such as saying 'Everything that exists is [something]'. His statement: 'And mushabbihaat' are false propositions resembling true primary or well-known propositions due to verbal or conceptual ambiguity. Know that what the later scholars mentioned in the five arts is a deficient abbreviation; they summarized it and neglected it despite it being important. For verbal ambiguity: like the error occurring due to the word being homonymous, as one says: 'Is any evil thing obligatory or not obligatory? If it is obligatory, then every obligatory thing is good, so some evil is good. And if it is not obligatory, then it does not exist at all; for what is not necessary of existence does not exist.' The fallacy is because 'necessary of existence' is different from 'obligatory to act upon'; they are only called 'wajib' by homonymy. The meaning of the first wajib is that its existence is necessary; the meaning of the second wajib is that its existence is praiseworthy. And due to treating the word like a sensible form, as we say of a picture of a horse painted on a wall: 'It is a horse', and 'Every horse neighs', so that picture neighs. Or conceptual: like the error being due to not observing the existence of the subject in the affirmative [proposition], as we say: 'Every human and horse is a human', and 'Every human and horse is a horse' resulting in 'Some human is a horse'; the error is that the subject of the two premises does not exist, or there is no existing thing that is both human and horse. And like taking a natural proposition in place of a universal one, as we say: 'Man is an animal', 'Animal is a genus', so 'Man is a genus'. And like taking mental [propositions] instead of external ones, such as 'Origination is originated', and 'Everything originated has an origination', so 'Origination has an origination'; and conversely, 'Substance exists in the mind', and 'Everything existing in the mind is subsisting by the mind', and 'Everything subsisting by the mind is an accident', so 'Substance is an accident'. (Tahrir al-Qawa'id al-Mantiqiyya) Or conceptual: Know that conceptual ambiguity is either from the aspect of matter: that the intended of some premises is one thing, that is begging the question, as in our saying 'Every human is a human', 'Every human is a human'? Actually the example: 'Every human is a human, and every human is a human'. Or from the aspect of form: that the condition for production is missing, as when the minor is negative and the major is particular in the first figure. (Isma'il) Deficient abbreviation: It is reducing the wording and meaning; conciseness is reducing the wording while the meaning is abundant; this is praiseworthy, that is blameworthy. They summarized it and neglected it, and it was incumbent upon them to depict the five arts, such as presenting the syllogisms and their conclusions and explaining their rulings.

Commentary
This passage defines the sophistical syllogism and discusses its components and examples of fallacies. It begins by stating that a sophistical syllogism is composed of wahmiyyat (imaginative premises) and mushabbihaat (ambiguous premises). The commentator explains the derivation of 'sophistical' from Greek meaning disguised wisdom. Wahmiyyat are judgments made by the imagination about non-sensible things by analogy to sensible things, illustrated by the example 'Everything that exists is [something]'. Mushabbihaat are false propositions that resemble true primary or well-known propositions due to verbal or conceptual ambiguity. The passage then criticizes later scholars for their deficient treatment of the five arts in logic, saying they omitted important details. It proceeds to give detailed examples of verbal ambiguity (ishtibah lafzi) and conceptual ambiguity (ishtibah ma'nawi). The verbal ambiguity example involves the homonymous term 'wajib' meaning both 'necessary of existence' and 'obligatory to act upon', leading to a fallacy. Another verbal ambiguity example treats a picture of a horse as a real horse, leading to the absurd conclusion that the picture neighs. Conceptual ambiguity includes fallacies such as failing to ensure the subject actually exists in affirmative propositions, taking a natural proposition as universal, and confusing mental existence with external existence. The passage also mentions two types of conceptual ambiguity: material (begging the question) and formal (invalid mood). Finally, it distinguishes between deficient abbreviation (economy of words and meaning) and praiseworthy conciseness (economy of words with rich meaning), noting that the later scholars fell into the former.

Key Takeaway
Conceptual ambiguity encompasses fallacies of empty subject, mistaken quantification, domain confusion, begging the question, and invalid syllogistic form – all of which undermine the soundness of an argument.
