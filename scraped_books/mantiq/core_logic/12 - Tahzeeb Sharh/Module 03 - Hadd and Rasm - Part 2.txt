The Method of Tahdid: Defining by Ascending to Broader Terms
Author defines tahdid as the method of taking essential definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tahdid method to construct a definition by listing broader terms and separating essential from accidental attributes in an Islamic science context.

Core Idea
Tahdid is the method, not the result: ascend to broader terms, then pick the essentials.

In this passage, the author clarifies what 'tahdid' (definition) actually means in logic. It is not just the final definition, but the process of extracting it. You will learn a practical procedure: when you want to define something, list all broader terms, predicate them, and separate what is essential from what is accidental. This method ensures your definitions are precise and correct, a skill vital for any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term to be defined
Start with the thing you want to define (the definiendum). The author says: 'if you wish to define a thing, you must posit that thing'. For example, in fiqh, you might want to define 'salah' (prayer).
2
Seek all terms more general than it
List all broader categories that include the thing. For 'salah', broader terms might include 'ibadah' (worship), 'action', 'obligation'. The text says 'seek all that is more general than it'.
These broader terms are the potential genera and differentiae.
3
Predicate those generals upon the thing
Apply each broader term to the thing, either directly or through a middle term (by reasoning). For example, say 'salah is an ibadah', 'salah is an action'.
This step checks whether the broader term truly applies.
4
Distinguish essential from accidental
Among the predicated attributes, separate those essential to the thing (without which it cannot be conceived) from those accidental. For 'salah', essential: it is an 'ibadah' containing specific acts; accidental: it is performed in a mosque. The author emphasizes distinguishing الذاتيات from العرضيات.
The essential ones will form the definition (genus + differentia).

The Method of Defining in the Sciences
Commentary explains the process of definition in the sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform the steps to define a term by seeking its genera and distinguishing essential from accidental attributes.

Core Idea
Definition is the act of explaining the limits of a thing by its essential properties.

In this passage, the commentator explains that definition (tahdid) is the act of setting the limits (hudud) of a thing. The method involves positing the thing, collecting its genera, and predicating them upon it, while carefully separating essential (dhati) from accidental ('aradi) properties. This process is foundational to all sciences, as it ensures that our definitions capture the true nature of things, not just superficial descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thing to be defined
Start by clearly stating the definiendum—the term you want to define. For example, if defining 'human', the term is 'insan'.
2
Seek all that is more general than it (genera)
Think of broader categories that include the thing, such as 'animal' for human. List these genera without yet worrying about which are essential.
Use the principle of wider extension.
3
Predicate the genera upon the thing
Assert that the thing belongs to each genus: 'Human is an animal.' This may be done directly or through a middle term (e.g., via intermediate genera).
The passage mentions 'with or without a middle term'—direct predication (al-haml bila wasita) is simpler.
4
Distinguish essential from accidental attributes
Among the predicated genera, identify which are essential (dhati) and which are accidental ('aradi). Essential attributes are those that are necessarily attached to the thing's essence, like 'rational' for human, while accidental ones like 'laughing' are not.
The author stresses distinguishing dhatiyat from 'aradiyat.
5
Determine if the definition is complete or incomplete
If the definition includes the immediate genus and a differentia that separates it from other species, it is a complete definition (hadd tam). If it only gives a broader genus or a property, it is a description (rasm).
The passage clarifies that 'definition' here includes both complete and incomplete, not just the technical hadd.

Constructing a Categorical Syllogism
Commentary explains the process of constructing a categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid categorical syllogism by identifying the minor, major, and middle terms from a desired conclusion.

Core Idea
The middle term is the key that connects the premises and yields the conclusion.

The commentator now turns from definition to syllogism, explaining how to construct a categorical (conjunctive) syllogism. Starting from the desired conclusion, we identify its two extremes (subject and predicate), then search for a shared term that connects them in two premises. This foundational procedure allows us to determine the figure and validity of the syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion (al-matlub) and its extremes
The conclusion has a subject (al-mahmul 'alayh) and a predicate (al-mahmul). For example, if the conclusion is 'The world is created', the subject is 'the world' and the predicate is 'created'.
These extremes are the minor and major terms of the syllogism.
2
Separate the minor and major premises
The premise that contains the subject (minor term) is the minor premise (sughra); the premise that contains the predicate (major term) is the major premise (kubra). The shared term between them is the middle term (hadd awsat).
The passage says: 'look at the two extremes of the desired conclusion so that the minor premise is distinguished from the major premise for you.'
3
Attach the other part of the conclusion to the other part of that premise
Once you have one premise, take the opposite extreme from the conclusion and try to match it with the remaining part of the first premise. The resulting pair will reveal the middle term if it is common.
This step ensures the premises share a term.
4
Check the composition of terms
See if the three terms (subject, predicate, and middle) arrange themselves into one of the four figures (ashkal). The figure is determined by the position of the middle term.
The passage says: 'if they compose according to one of the four compositions, then what is joined to the part of the conclusion is the middle term, and the productive figure is distinguished.'
5
Determine if the syllogism is simple or compound
If the terms do not compose directly into a single figure, the syllogism may be compound (murakkab). Then you apply the same procedure to each part until a common term is found.
The passage mentions: 'if they do not compose, then the syllogism is compound. Work with each one of them according to the mentioned procedure.'

Distinguishing Essential from Accidental Attributes
Criteria for distinguishing essential from accidental with defense of ease

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish essential (dhati) from accidental ('aradi) attributes using the criteria of necessary attribution and removal entailing removal of essence.

Core Idea
Essential attributes are those whose removal necessitates removal of the essence itself.

This lesson delves into the practical method for distinguishing between attributes that belong to the essence of a thing and those that are merely accidental. The author provides clear criteria and addresses a common confusion about necessary attributes (lazim). Mastering this distinction is crucial for constructing precise definitions in logic and Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attribute in question
Take any attribute predicated of a thing. For example, 'rationality' for humans or 'burning' for fire.
2
Apply the first criterion: necessary attribution without an agent
Check if the attribute is necessarily attributable to the thing without needing an external cause (jā‘il). If it is impossible for the thing to exist without this attribute, it is essential.
For example, 'rationality' is essential to human because a human cannot be conceived without it.
3
Apply the second criterion: removal entails removal of essence
Check if the removal of this attribute would require the removal of the essence itself (externally, mentally, and in consideration). If yes, it is essential.
For example, if 'humanity' is removed, 'rationality' is also removed, but the converse is the test for essentiality.
4
Handle necessary attributes (lazim) carefully
A necessary attribute like 'burning' for fire is not essential because its removal does not directly remove the essence of fire; it removes something from the essence first.
The passage clarifies that lazims that are not part of the essence do not meet the stricter criteria.
5
Distinguish genus from general accident, differentia from special property
Once essentials are identified, you can distinguish genus (jins) from general accident ('arad 'amm) and differentia (fasl) from special property (khassa).
This step completes the process of definition.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the method of distinguishing essential (dhati) from accidental ('aradi) attributes, which is crucial for definition. The author begins by stating that definition is the way to arrive at truth and act upon it. To define something, one must count what is clearly attributable to it or what, from its mere removal, the removal of the essence itself follows—these are essential; what does not meet this criterion is accidental. Then, by seeking all that is in its essence and all that is equivalent to it, one can distinguish genus from general accident and differentia from special property. The commentary then addresses a potential objection: if a necessary attribute (lazim) also entails the removal of the essence when it is removed, then it would be considered essential, but it is not. The response clarifies that the removal of a necessary attribute does not directly remove the essence; rather, it removes something from the essence, which then leads to the removal of the essence. This precision shows that the condition for essentiality is not that removal of the attribute entails removal of the essence in all cases, but that the attribute is necessarily attached such that its removal indicates a flaw in the essence. The author then argues against the famous view that distinguishing essential from accidental is difficult. He asserts it is easy, providing two clear criteria: (1) essential attributes are necessarily attributable without need for an external agent; (2) essential attributes are those whose removal entails removal of the essence externally, mentally, and in consideration. Accidental attributes are those that are contingently attached or whose removal does not require removal of the essence. Examples are given: burning is a necessary attribute of fire in external existence but not in the mind; universality is necessary for human in the mind but not externally. The commentary concludes by emphasizing that the distinction is straightforward, and that the compound of proof (i.e., syllogism) must rely on self-evident or theoretical truths.

Key Terms
ذاتي (dhati): Essential; an attribute that is necessarily attributable to a thing such that its removal necessarily entails the removal of the essence itself, and its attribution does not need an external agent. [Note: No significant shift; stable across the tradition.]
عرضي ('aradi): Accidental; an attribute that is contingently attributable to a thing and whose removal does not require the removal of the essence. [Note: No significant shift.]
جنس (jins): Genus; the universal that is predicated of many things differing in species in answer to 'what is it?'. [Note: In logic texts, genus is the broader category; in usul al-fiqh it may refer to a category of legal rulings.]
فصل (fasl): Differentia; the essential attribute that distinguishes a species from other species within the same genus. [Note: No significant shift.]
عرض عام ('arad 'amm): General accident; a universal accident that is predicated of many things of different species, like 'movement'. [Note: No significant shift.]
خاصة (khassa): Special property; an accidental attribute that is coextensive with the species, like 'laughing' for human. [Note: No significant shift.]
