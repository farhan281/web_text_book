The Nature of Tasdiq: Simplicity and the Object of Judgment
Author defines tasdiq and critiques al-Razi's view on object of judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the earlier philosophers' view of tasdiq as a simple judgment focused on the predicative ratio from al-Razi's composite view.

Core Idea
Tasdiq is a simple act of compliance, not a composite of judgment and conceptions.

In this passage, the author defines tasdiq (assent) and immediately enters a dispute with al-Imam al-Razi about whether tasdiq is simple or composite. He sides with the earlier philosophers who hold that the object of judgment is the predicative ratio itself, not its occurrence. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping the Islamic logical account of propositions and knowledge.

2
Locate al-Razi's claim that the judgment includes conception of the two extremes
Look for the phrase 'كما زعمه الإمام الرازي' where the author refutes the composite view.
3
Extract the author's alternative: the object of judgment is the predicative ratio
Note the phrase 'النسبة الخبرية الثبوتية أو السلبية' as the object of compliance.
4
Understand the dispute over the parts of the proposition
Earlier philosophers say three parts: subject, predicate, predicative ratio. Later philosophers add a fourth: occurrence/non-occurrence of restrictive ratio.
5
Evaluate why the author prefers the earlier view
The commentary argues that from 'standing' only one ratio is understood, so no need for a fourth part.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and critical commentary on the nature of tasdiq (assent) and its relationship to tasawwur (conception). The author begins by stating that if something is not a judgment (hukm), then it is conception. He then refutes al-Imam al-Razi's view that the judgment is a composite of the judgment itself and the conception of the two extremes. The author prefers the position of the earlier philosophers (al-mutaqaddimun) who maintain that the object of compliance (idh'aan) and judgment is the affirmative or negative predicative ratio (al-nisba al-khabariyya al-thubutiyya aw al-salbiyya), not the occurrence or non-occurrence of the restrictive ratio (al-nisba al-taqyidiyya). The author supports this by noting that the author of the matn will later indicate the tripartite division of the proposition's parts. A marginal note by Isma'il points out that al-Razi's use of 'za'ama' (claimed) indicates the weakness of his doctrine, because the division of knowledge requires a distinction between conception and assent that al-Razi's division fails to capture. Another note explains that the phrase 'predicative ratio' is broad enough to cover conditional propositions because conditionals are based on categoricals with added tools. The hashiah then expands on the dispute between the earlier and later philosophers: both agree that assent is simple, but they differ on its object. The earlier philosophers, whom the author supports, hold that the proposition has three parts (subject, predicate, and predicative ratio), and that assent is a distinct type of apprehension essentially different from conception. The later philosophers add a fourth part, the occurrence or non-occurrence of the restrictive ratio. The author asserts that the earlier view is correct because from a single predicate like 'standing' only one ratio is understood. The passage ends with a note by 'Abd referencing a future discussion on the parts of the proposition.

Key Terms
التصديق (al-tasdiq): Assent: an act of compliance (idh'aan) and judgment (hukm) that is simple and essentially distinct from conception. [Note: No significant shift—this term is stable across the tradition.]
التصور (al-tasawwur): Conception: apprehension of a single thing without judgment, e.g., conception of Zayd. [Note: No significant shift—this term is stable.]
النسبة الخبرية (al-nisba al-khabariyya): Predictative ratio: the affirmative or negative relation linking subject and predicate, which is the object of judgment according to the earlier philosophers. [Note: In later texts, this term may be specified further as 'nisba hukmiyya' to distinguish it from the restrictive ratio.]
النسبة التقييدية (al-nisba al-taqyidiyya): Restrictive ratio: the relation that qualifies the subject or predicate, considered by later philosophers as part of the proposition's structure. [Note: This term is central to the dispute between earlier and later philosophers regarding the parts of the proposition.]

Necessary and Acquired Knowledge in Conception and Assent
Division of conception and assent into necessary and acquired

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how conception and assent are each divided into necessary and acquired knowledge, and identify the rhetorical use of kinaya in the passage.

Core Idea
Knowledge is classified by its origin: without reflection (daruri) or with reflection (kasbi).

This lesson unpacks the author's division of tasawwur and tasdiq into necessary and acquired types. The passage uses an elegant rhetorical device — innuendo (kinaya) — to imply the division rather than stating it directly. Understanding this classification is foundational for later discussions of certainty and proof in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
The passage states that both tasawwur and tasdiq are divided by necessity (bi al-darura) into necessary (daruri) and acquired (kasbi) knowledge. Find the Arabic terms: الضروري and الكسبي.
The terms refer to knowledge obtained without or with reflection (nazar).
2
Determine what is being divided
The explicit wording divides the attributes 'necessity' and 'acquisition', not tasawwur and tasdiq themselves. The commentator explains that each of tasawwur and tasdiq takes a portion from each attribute, resulting in four subtypes.
This is the key insight: the division is understood implicitly.
3
Analyze the rhetorical device
The author uses kinaya (innuendo) to convey the division of tasawwur and tasdiq into necessary and acquired. The commentator praises this as more eloquent (أبلغ وأحسن) than explicit statement.
Kinaya here relies on the implication that if the attributes are divided, the entities possessing them must also be divided.
4
Evaluate why kinaya is preferred
The commentator notes that knowledge obtained through effort (here, understanding the implication) is more valuable. This reflects a pedagogical preference for engaging the reader's reasoning.
Compare with explicit statement: the implicit method requires thought, making the insight stick.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological classification of knowledge into necessary (daruri) and acquired (iktisabi) types, applied to both conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). The author states that they are divided by necessity into necessity and acquisition by consideration (bi al-nazar). The commentary then explains that this means each of conception and assent takes a portion from necessity and a portion from acquisition, resulting in necessary and acquired subtypes. The phrasing is indirect: the explicit division is of the attributes (necessity and acquisition), while the division of conception and assent themselves is understood implicitly (kinaya), which is more eloquent. The passage later clarifies that 'by necessity' (bi al-darura) indicates the division is self-evident (badihiyya), not needing proof. The commentator also defines three non-assenting apprehensions: takhyil (supposition where the mind holds a proposition without hesitation but with allowance of the opposite), shakk (doubt with equal hesitation), and wahm (estimation with preponderance of the opposite). A marginal note by Isma'il discusses the linguistic meaning of 'yaqtasimān' and explains why the annotation interprets it as taking a share each. Another note clarifies that the terms 'daruri' and 'kasbi' are used instead of 'darura' and 'iktisab' to properly attribute the division. The passage concludes with a note by Shaykh al-Islam that 'bi al-darura' can mean either 'by self-evidence' or 'by necessity'.

Non-Assenting Apprehensions: Takhyil, Shakk, Wahm
Definitions of takhyil, shakk, and wahm as non-assenting apprehensions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between takhyil, shakk, and wahm as categories of non-assenting apprehension based on the presence of hesitation and preponderance.

Core Idea
Not all mental grasp of a proposition involves assent; some are mere supposition, doubt, or estimation.

This lesson focuses on three specific types of non-assenting apprehension (idrak ghayr iqra'ani) mentioned in the passage: takhyil, shakk, and wahm. These are conceptions of a proposition that fall short of full belief. Distinguishing them is crucial for understanding epistemic certainty and the hierarchy of knowledge in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Extract the three terms
The passage lists three non-assenting apprehensions: التخييل (takhyil), الشك (shakk), and الوهم (wahm). Locate them in the Arabic text.
They are given as examples of 'non-assenting apprehension' (إدراك غير إذعاني).
2
Understand takhyil
Takhyil is defined as obtaining the form of a proposition without hesitation (bila taraddud) but with allowance (tajwiz) of the opposite. The mind accepts the proposition mentally but acknowledges the possibility of its falsehood.
Contrast with certainty: in takhyil, there is no doubt but also no firm belief.
3
Understand shakk
Shakk (doubt) is described as apprehension of the ratio with hesitation (taraddud) and allowing both sides equally. The mind is torn between two possibilities without preponderance.
This is the classic definition of doubt: equal probability.
4
Understand wahm
Wahm (estimation) is the conception of the ratio with preponderance (rujhan) of its opposite. The mind leans toward the falsehood of the proposition, but the inclination is not strong enough to become certainty.
Wahm is the weakest epistemic state, often associated with false assumptions.
5
Compare the three
Create a table: takhyil has no hesitation but allows opposite; shakk has hesitation with equal allowance; wahm has hesitation with preponderance of opposite. All are non-assenting because the mind does not affirm the proposition with conviction.
They lie on a spectrum between ignorance and certainty.

Understanding this division is key to evaluating whether knowledge claims are intuitive or inferential.

Proof of Error in Thought
Proof that error exists in thought

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to prove that error can occur in thought by using the impossibility of contradiction.

Core Idea
Error is inevitable because contradictory conclusions cannot both be true.

In this passage, the commentator demonstrates that human reasoning is fallible. He gives a concrete example: one thinker concludes the world is created, another that it is eternal. Since both cannot be true, at least one must be in error. This is a foundational argument for the possibility of error in logic and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the example
Find the two opposing conclusions mentioned: the world is temporally originated (ḥādith) vs. pre-eternal (qadīm).
2
Recognize the logical relation
These two propositions are direct contradictories (naqīḍ); they cannot both be true or both false.
In logic, naqīḍ means the negation of a proposition.
3
Understand the proof by contradiction
If both conclusions were correct, they would together form a contradiction (ijtimā' al-naqīḍayn), which is impossible. Therefore, at least one reasoning must be erroneous.
This method is foundational in Islamic logic and kalām.
4
Generalize the principle
Whenever thought can reach a proposition and its contradictory, error is present in at least one of the reasonings.
This establishes the need for rigorous logical analysis.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the phrase 'fīhi al-khaṭa' (there is error in it) from the original text. The commentator first proves that error can occur in thought by giving an example: one may reason to the conclusion that the world is temporally originated (ḥādith), and another may reason to the opposite, that it is pre-eternal (qadīm). Since both cannot be true, at least one is erroneous; otherwise a contradiction (ijtimā' al-naqīḍayn) would ensue. This shows that thought is fallible. Then the commentator explains why the term 'ma'qūlāt' (intelligibles) is defined as 'universal matters present in the mind'. The reason is that consideration (naẓar) does not apply to particulars, because particulars are perceived by sense (iḥsās), not intellect (ta'aqqul). The commentator argues that sense perception cannot lead to another sense perception or to universal concepts; e.g., seeing Zayd does not give you the perception of 'Amr nor the universal 'human'. Therefore, the term 'intelligibles' must refer to universals. The author begins by stating that a universal rule is needed to avoid error in thought, and that rule is logic. He then presents three premises: (1) knowledge is either concept or assent, (2) each is obtained either without reasoning or through reasoning, and (3) reasoning can contain error. These premises together show that people need a law (al-mantiq) to guard against error. The text then addresses a series of objections. First, an objection is raised that the premises only prove need for partial methods, not for universal rules. The answer is that partial methods are infinite and can only be known through universal rules, thus the need for universal rules follows indirectly. Second, some argue that a fourth premise about the insufficiency of human nature is needed. The answer is that this is already included in the third premise, because error can only occur if nature is insufficient. Third, it is objected that dividing knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq is unnecessary; dividing into necessary and theoretical would suffice. The answer explains that in order to prove the need for both parts of the science of logic (one leading to concepts and one to assents), we must first show that both concepts and assents can be non-necessary (i.e., theoretical), because if all knowledge were necessary, there would be no need for logic. Author defines the three types of linguistic indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given example of linguistic indication into one of the three types: mutabaqa, tazamun, or iltizam.

Core Idea
Words indicate meaning via whole, part, or necessary implication.

In this passage, the author defines the three fundamental modes of linguistic indication. Mastering these will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences, from Fiqh to Kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
The passage introduces three terms: mutabaqa, tazamun, and iltizam. They describe how a word relates to its meaning.
These are not properties of the word itself but of the relation between word and meaning.
2
Understand mutabaqa (conformity)
Mutabaqa is indication to the entire meaning the word was placed for. Example: the word 'house' indicates the whole building.
This is the default and most direct type of indication.
3
Understand tazamun (inclusion)
Tazamun is indication to a part of that meaning. Example: 'house' indicates a room, because a room is part of a house.
It relies on a part-whole relationship within the meaning.
4
Understand iltizam (implication)
Iltizam is indication to something external to the word's meaning that is necessarily implied. Example: 'roof' implies a house, because a roof cannot exist without a house.
This involves a necessary connection, not part-whole.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Take the word 'salah' (prayer). Its mutabaqa is the entire set of actions and recitations. Its tazamun might be 'ruku' (bowing). Its iltizam might be the validity conditions (e.g., facing Qibla) which are external but necessary.
A single word can have all three types simultaneously for different aspects.

Core Idea
A conceptual definition clarifies what something is; a proof establishes that something is true.

Necessary Relation in Implicative Signification
Explains condition of luzum for dalalat al-iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mental and customary necessary relations in implicative signification and apply them to Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Implicative signification requires a necessary bond — either reason or custom.

Implicative signification (dalalat al-iltizam) occurs when a word points to something beyond its core meaning, but only if there is a necessary relation (luzum) between them. This relation can be mental (rational) or customary (based on common usage). Understanding this condition is crucial for correctly identifying implications in Islamic texts, where terms often carry implied meanings by necessity or by established custom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of necessary relation
The author states that luzum is either mental ('aqli) or customary ('urfi). Mental necessity is based on rational impossibility of separation; customary necessity is based on habitual association.
The passage explicitly says 'min al-luzum aqlan aw urfan'.
2
Understand mental necessity through the blindness example
Blindness implies vision because one cannot conceive of blindness without the concept of vision. Reason judges that the conditioned (muqayyad) cannot be understood without the condition (qayd).
The objection that vision might be part of the meaning is resolved by noting that blindness is absence in relation to vision, with vision external.
3
Analyse the objection and response
The objection: if blindness is defined as absence of vision, then vision is part of the meaning (tadammun). The response: blindness is absence added to vision, so vision is external; the relation (idafa) is internal, the annexed term external. Thus it's iltizam.
The grammatical concept of idafa (construct phrase) is used to clarify.
4
Understand customary necessity through the generosity-Hatim example
Generosity implies Hatim by customary association, even though reason does not require it. This shows that luzum can be based on widespread usage.
Customary necessity is weaker than mental but sufficient for iltizam.
5
Apply to Islamic examples
For instance, the term 'khamr' (wine) implies intoxication by mental necessity (since the ruling is based on intoxication), while 'Makkah' implies the Sanctuary by custom (common usage). Practice distinguishing the two types in Islamic contexts.
Usul al-Fiqh relies on such distinctions for deriving rulings.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In earlier lessons, we learned that a word's meaning involves conformity (مطابقة) with its original sense, and that inclusion (تضمن) and implication (التزام) depend on it. But what if a word becomes so famous for a part or a concomitant that it is no longer used for its original meaning? Does that break the connection? The Subject of a Proposition: Simple vs Compound Based on Intention
Defines simple vs compound subject based on intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic word as simple or compound by determining whether its parts are intended to signify parts of its meaning.

Core Idea
A word is simple unless its parts are intended to signify parts of its meaning.

In this passage, the commentator defines the subject of a proposition. It may be simple (mufrad) or compound (murakkab). The key is intention: if a part of the word is intended to signify a part of the meaning, it is compound. This distinction is crucial for understanding how signification works in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being classified
Find the word in the proposition that serves as the subject. For example, 'زيد' or 'غلام زيد'.
2
Check if the word has meaningful parts
Break down the word into its components. Does the word consist of separate words or affixes that carry meaning?
Grammatical affixes like nunation or inflectional endings are not considered meaningful parts here.
3
Determine if the parts are intended to signify parts of the meaning
Ask: Are the parts intentionally used to convey separate aspects of the meaning? If yes, the word is compound. If the parts are only accidental (like letters) or not intended as separate signifiers, it is simple.
For example, in 'غلام زيد', both 'غلام' and 'زيد' are intended to signify [slave] and [Zayd] respectively, so it is compound.

Refuting the Claim that Every Meaning Has a Necessary Concomitant
Refutes the claim that every meaning has a necessary concomitant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the argument that every meaning must have a necessary concomitant using the distinction of 'evident concomitant in the specific sense' and the infinite regress objection.

Core Idea
Not every meaning has an evident concomitant; otherwise, understanding one thing would require knowing infinite things.

The commentator addresses an objection: Someone claims that every meaning must have a necessary concomitant because 'not being other than itself' is a necessary concomitant of everything. If so, then every meaning would have implicational signification, contradicting earlier statements. The commentator refutes this by showing that the relevant concomitant must be 'evident in the specific sense' (lazim bayyin bi al-ma'na al-akhass) and that the objection would lead to an infinite regress.

Define the required type of concomitant

If the objection were correct, then from perceiving one thing we would perceive an infinite number of things (since it is distinct from everything else). This is impossible, so the objection is invalid.
This is a reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage marks a new section on the division of the subject of the proposition (موضوع القضية). The subject is defined: if by a part of it the signification of a part of its meaning is intended, it is composite (مركب). Otherwise it is simple (مفرد). The commentator then addresses an objection: Someone might claim that every meaning must have a necessary concomitant, because the fact that a thing is not another thing is a necessary concomitant of everything. The commentator refutes this in two ways. The fact that a thing is not something else is not evident in this sense; we can conceive many essences without conceiving all the things they are not. The commentary continues with explanations of why the term 'اللفظ الموضوع' (the placed word) is used, emphasizing that only verbal conventional signification is considered, and that the four types of signifiers (verbal, written, gestural, etc.) are not described as simple or compound because they are not words. It also clarifies that composition arises from intention in usage, and that simplicity and composition are mutually exclusive based on the presence or absence of intention. Finally, the part intended in a composite word is the phonetically ordered part, not including grammatical morphemes that indicate additional meanings, otherwise many Arabic linguistic phenomena would wrongly be considered composites.

Four Conditions for Compound Expressions
Author enumerates the four conditions for compound expressions and four types of simple expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish compound from simple expressions by checking the four conditions (part, meaning part, indication, intention).

Core Idea
A compound expression requires intentional part-to-part indication; otherwise it is simple.

In logic, not every multi-word expression is a compound. To be compound, the parts of the expression must intentionally indicate parts of its meaning. This lesson unpacks the four conditions that must hold for an expression to be considered compound, and examines each type of simple expression that results when any condition is missing.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of compound
The author defines a compound expression (murakkab) as one where a part of its wording indicates a part of its meaning. If not, it is simple (mufrad). This sets up the four conditions.
2
List the four conditions
The four conditions are: (1) the expression has a part, (2) its meaning has a part, (3) a part of the wording indicates a part of the meaning, and (4) this indication is intended. Note that all four must be present for a compound.
The order is logical: existence of parts, then indication, then intention.

Apply the conditions to examples

Pay attention to the last example where indication exists but is not intended.

Identify the four types of simple expressions
Each condition's absence gives a distinct type of simple. Match each example to the corresponding missing condition. This reinforces the logical classification.
The types: (1) no part of word, (2) no part of meaning, (3) no indication, (4) indication not intended.

Two Senses of Self-Evident Concomitant
Author defines bayyin and ghayr bayyin lazim with examples, clarifies two meanings, and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the narrow and broad senses of 'self-evident' (bayyin) in necessary concomitants and correctly classify given concomitants.

Core Idea
Self-evident has two degrees: narrow (conception alone suffices) and broad (certainty may need a middle term).

In Islamic logic, necessary concomitants (lazim) are divided into self-evident (bayyin) and non-self-evident (ghayr bayyin). This passage from Tahzeeb al-Mantiq clarifies that 'bayyin' actually has two meanings: a narrower one where the mind immediately judges the concomitance upon mere conception, and a broader one where certainty is still achieved but may require a middle term. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating definitions and arguments in the Islamic sciences, particularly in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions
The passage states that the division of lazim into bayyin and ghayr bayyin is actually two divisions: one using the narrow sense and one using the broad sense. Identify each division: the first divides lazim into bayyin (al-akhass) and ghayr bayyin; the second divides into bayyin (al-a'amm) and ghayr bayyin.
2
Distinguish the two senses of bayyin
Bayyin bi-l-ma'na al-akhass: the judgment of concomitance arises immediately upon conceiving subject and predicate alone (e.g., evenness of four). Bayyin bi-l-ma'na al-a'amm: the judgment is certain but may depend on a middle term (e.g., the motion of the sphere). Compare their conditions.
The second sense is more general, covering cases that also fall under the first sense.
3
Apply to examples
Test each example: 'evenness of four' is bayyin even in the narrow sense. 'Temporality of the world' is ghayr bayyin because it requires a proof. 'Motion of the sphere' is bayyin in the broad sense if its cause is known, but not in the narrow sense.
The commentary mentions that perpetual motion is considered necessary due to its cause.
4
Understand the objection and resolution
An objection is raised that the author implies a single sense of bayyin. The resolution: the author merged the two divisions because the terms 'bayyin' and 'ghayr bayyin' apply to both senses. Recognize that the text's single division actually contains two distinct logical divisions.

Certainty is firm, real, and stable assent; all knowledge rests on self-evident truths.

This passage from the Tahzib al-Mantiq defines certainty (yaqin) with precision, listing conditions that exclude every form of non-knowledge. It then divides certain premises into primitive and theoretical, arguing that theoretical knowledge must be grounded in self-evident truths to avoid logical absurdities.

Core Idea
Certainty requires firm assent that matches reality and stays stable — anything less is not knowledge.

The passage defines certainty as firm assent corresponding to reality and stable. Each condition eliminates a common epistemic defect: doubt, illusion, conjecture, compound ignorance, and blind imitation. Understanding these conditions is crucial for evaluating the strength of any claim in Islamic sciences.

The Six Types of Self-Evident Propositions
Author enumerates and defines the six types of self-evident propositions with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the six types of self-evident propositions (badihiyyat) in Islamic logic and provide an Islamic example for each.

Core Idea
Self-evident propositions are not all the same: six distinct types ground certainty in different ways.

In Islamic logic, not all knowledge requires proof. Some propositions are self-evident (badihiyya) and form the foundation of all certainty. But what makes a proposition self-evident? The tradition identifies six distinct types, ranging from first principles (awwaliyyat) to reports so numerous they compel belief (mutawatirat). In this lesson, we will walk through the classification and learn how to recognize each type with its classic example from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification scheme
The author divides self-evident propositions by a dichotomous condition: either the mere conception of the terms and relation is enough (awwaliyyat) or it depends on an intermediary. Then by further conditions (whether the intermediary is sense, whether it persists, etc.), the other five types emerge. Follow the branching logic.
2
Identify awwaliyyat (first principles)
These require no intermediary. The example given is 'The whole is greater than the part.' The certainty comes directly from understanding the terms. Recognize them by their immediacy.
3
Distinguish mushahadat (observational)
These rely on an intermediary that is sense perception—either external (like 'fire is hot') or internal (like 'I am hungry'). The intermediary is not always present; you must actually sense it.
4
Recognize fitriyyat (innate propositions)
These need an intermediary, but that intermediary is always present in the mind when the terms are conceived. For example, 'Four is even' because the concept of equal division is instantly present. The proof is 'with them' (qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha).
5
Differentiate hadsiyyat, tajribiyyat, and mutawatirat
The remaining three depend on intermediaries that are not always present. Hadsiyyat involve a rapid intuitive inference (like moonlight from sun). Tajribiyyat come from repeated experience (like scammony purges bile). Mutawatirat are reports so numerous that collusion is impossible (like the existence of Mecca). Each has a distinct basis for certainty.

Commentary
This passage defines the six types of self-evident propositions (badihiyyat) that form the foundation of certainty. The author begins by stating that the foundations of assents (tasdiqat) are the certainties, which are either self-evident or theoretical. The self-evident are divided into six based on induction. The classification proceeds by a dichotomous division. The first division: either the mere conception of the two terms and the relation is sufficient for judgment (these are awwaliyyat, e.g., 'The whole is greater than the part'), or it is not sufficient, in which case it depends on an intermediary. If the intermediary is not sense (external or internal), it falls under the second category. The author then further subdivides: if the intermediary does not disappear from the mind when the terms are present, it is fitriyyat (innate), also called 'propositions whose syllogisms are with them', meaning the proof is immediately present, like 'Four is even' where the idea of evenness arises from division into equal halves. If the intermediary does not persist, it is either hadsiyyat (intuitive), involving instantaneous transition, or it depends on reports (mutawatirat) or repeated experience (tajribiyyat). The author also discusses mushahadat (observations), which are based on external or internal senses. A lengthy commentary explains the nature of intellection (hads) versus reflection (fikr), and addresses the conditions for mutawatirat, arguing that a fixed number is not required; rather, certainty is achieved when the conditions of integrity and prevention of collusion are met. Examples are provided for each type: awwaliyyat (whole greater than part), mushahadat (sun rising, fire burning, hunger/thirst), tajribiyyat (scammony purges bile), hadsiyyat (moonlight from sun), mutawatirat (Mecca exists), and fitriyyat (four is even). The passage ensures the student understands the basis of certainty in different kinds of self-evident propositions.
