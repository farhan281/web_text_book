Why Theoretical Premises Must End in Self-Evident Ones
Author explains impossibility of circularity and infinite regress in chains of theoretical premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two impossible scenarios (circularity and infinite regress) in a chain of epistemic justification and explain why theoretical premises must terminate in self-evident premises.

Core Idea
All theoretical knowledge rests on self-evident premises; otherwise, reasoning collapses into circularity or infinite regress.

This passage presents a foundational argument in Islamic epistemology. The author argues that if theoretical premises (nazariyyat) are not ultimately grounded in self-evident premises (badihiyyat), the chain of justification must either go on forever (tasalsul) or loop back on itself (dawr). Both are logically impossible, so the only coherent option is that theoretical knowledge terminates in self-evident truths. This argument is crucial for understanding how knowledge is built in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author claims that the chain of theoretical premises must end in self-evident premises. Find this claim in the Arabic passage.
2
Recognize the two alternatives
If the chain does not terminate, there are only two possibilities: it goes on without end (tasalsul) or it returns upon itself (dawr). Understand both possibilities.
3
Understand why both are impossible
Infinite regress is impossible because the mind cannot traverse an infinite number of steps to reach certainty. Circularity is impossible because it uses a premise to prove itself, which is a logical fallacy.
4
Conclude the necessity of self-evident premises
Since both alternatives are impossible, the only remaining option is that theoretical premises ultimately rest on self-evident premises. This establishes foundationalism in epistemology.

Therefore, the only coherent option is that theoretical knowledge terminates in self-evident premises. This is a classic argument in Islamic epistemology, paralleling arguments in Aristotelian logic.

The Middle Term as Cause in Demonstration
Author explains that the middle term in demonstration must be a cause for knowledge of the conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the role of the middle term as a cause in a demonstrative syllogism from Islamic kalam.

Core Idea
The middle term in demonstration is not just a connector but a real cause producing certainty.

In this passage, the commentator elaborates on the condition of the middle term in demonstrative syllogisms. Unlike mere logical linking, the middle term must serve as an epistemological cause that brings about knowledge of the conclusion. This idea is central to the theory of demonstration (burhan) and distinguishes it from other types of reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
Locate the statement that the middle term must be a cause for knowledge of the conclusion. This is the main point.
2
Understand 'cause' in this context
Realize that the cause here is epistemological: it produces certainty in the mind, not just a metaphysical cause.
The text says 'for attaining knowledge' (لحصول العلم) and clarifies it is in the mind.
3
Examine the example
The commentator gives the example of change (taghayyur). Change is the cause for the judgment that the world is created. See how change functions as the middle term linking 'world' and 'created'.
This example is from kalam arguments for the creation of the world.
4
See the middle term as proof
Recognize that the middle term is called 'wasitat al-ithbat' (means of proof). It is not just a formal element but the actual reason for the truth of the conclusion.
5
Distinguish from other syllogisms
Note that this condition applies especially to demonstration, but the author says 'in every syllogism' it must be a cause. Understand the special status of demonstration.
In dialectical or rhetorical syllogisms, the middle term may not be a real cause.

This passage explains the essential condition of the middle term in demonstrative syllogism (burhan). The author states that in demonstration, and indeed in every syllogism, the middle term must serve as a cause ('illa) for the attainment of knowledge of the conclusion. This cause is not merely logical but epistemological: it is what brings about certainty in the mind. The author illustrates with the example of change (taghayyur): when we observe change in the world, change becomes the cause for the judgment that the world is created (hadith al-‘alam). In the mind, change is the intermediary (wasita) that connects the subject (the world) to the predicate (created) and produces assent (tasdiq). Thus the middle term functions as a 'wasita ithbat' (means of proof) for the judgment. This distinguishes demonstrative syllogism from merely dialectical or rhetorical arguments: in demonstration, the middle term is a real cause, not just a logical connector.

The Six Types of Certain Premises for Demonstration
Author enumerates and defines the six types of certain premises used in demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the six types of certain premises used in demonstration, and classify a given proposition under its appropriate type.

Core Idea
Six categories ground demonstrative certainty: self-evident, observed, experimental, intuitive, recurrent, and innate.

Demonstration (burhan) requires starting points that are absolutely certain. The logicians enumerate six types of such premises, each with a distinct source of certainty. In this lesson, you will learn to recognise each type and its defining feature, so that you can evaluate the epistemic foundation of any argument in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching classification
The author lists six types: awwaliyat (primary axioms), mushahadat (observations), tajribiyat (experiments), hadsiyat (intuitions), mutawatirat (recurrent reports), and fitriyat (innate judgments). Notice that they are introduced as the basis for both burhan limmi and burhan inni.
2
Examine the definition of each type
For each type, note the condition that makes it certain. Awwaliyat need no proof; mushahadat rely on sense data; tajribiyat come from repeated experiments; hadsiyat from intuitive insight; mutawatirat from overwhelming transmission; fitriyat from an ever-present mediating concept.
3
Study the examples provided
The author gives concrete examples: 'the whole is greater than the part' (awwaliyat), 'the sun is shining' (mushahadat external), 'we have hunger' (mushahadat internal), 'scammony purges bile' (tajribiyat), 'the moon's light is from the sun' (hadsivat), 'Mecca exists' (mutawatirat), 'four is even' (fitriyat). Use these to cement the categories.
4
Distinguish between similar types
Compare mushahadat (direct sensory) with tajribiyat (repeated experiment). Note that hadsiyat often involve an inference from observed effects, whereas awwaliyat are purely a priori. Mutawatirat depend on social testimony, while fitriyat are innate judgments with a latent middle term.
5
Apply the classification to new examples
Practice by taking propositions from Islamic disciplines (e.g., 'the world is temporally originated', 'the Prophet ﷺ existed', 'nabidh is forbidden') and identifying which type they belong to, justifying your choice.

Commentary
This passage defines and exemplifies the six types of certain premises foundational for demonstrative reasoning (burhan). The author begins with the overarching classification: primary axioms (awwaliyat), observations (mushahadat), experiments (tajribiyat), intuitions (hadsiyat), recurrent reports (mutawatirat), and innate judgments (fitriyat). Each type is given a concise definition and an example. The primary axioms are self-evident propositions like 'the whole is greater than the part.' Observations are divided into external (e.g., 'the sun is shining') and internal (e.g., 'we have hunger'). Experiments rely on repeated experience, such as 'scammony is a purgative of bile.' Intuitions are immediate judgments derived from appropriate evidence, like the moon's light coming from the sun. Recurrent reports are those whose reporters cannot plausibly have conspired upon falsehood, e.g., the existence of Mecca. Innate judgments involve a mediating notion that is always present, as in recognizing that four is even because it is divisible into two equal parts. These premises constitute the starting points of certain knowledge.

Burhan Limmi vs Burhan Inni – Two Types of Demonstration
Author classifies demonstration into limmi and inni based on the middle term's causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan limmi and burhan inni by analyzing whether the middle term serves as a real cause in the external world or only as an epistemic cause in the mind.

Core Idea
The middle term's real-world causality determines if demonstration is limmi (reason why) or inni (fact).

This lesson introduces the foundational classification of demonstrative syllogisms into two types: burhan limmi and burhan inni. The key distinction lies in the role of the middle term: whether it is a cause of the conclusion both in the mind and in reality (limmi) or only in the mind (inni). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the explanatory power of proofs in Islamic sciences, especially in Kalam and medicine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term in the syllogism
Locate the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. This is the middle term that mediates the proof.
For example, in 'This person has putrefied humors; everyone with putrefied humors has fever; therefore, this person has fever,' the middle term is 'putrefied humors.'
2
Determine if the middle term is a cause in reality
Ask: Does this middle term actually cause the relation (affirmative or negative) in the external world, independently of our knowledge? Consult medical or physical science as needed.
In the example, putrefaction actually causes fever in reality, so it is a real cause.
3
Classify the demonstration as limmi or inni
If the middle term is a cause both in the mind and in reality, it is burhan limmi. If it is a cause only in the mind (i.e., it proves the conclusion but is not the real cause), it is burhan inni.
The text states: 'If it is also a mediator in subsistence... it is limmi; otherwise it is inni.'
4
Verify with a counterexample
Consider an inni example: 'Zayd is feverish; everyone feverish has putrefied humors; therefore, Zayd has putrefied humors.' Here, fever is the middle term, but fever is an effect of putrefaction, not its cause. So it is inni.
Thus, inni proves the fact (that Zayd has putrefied humors) but does not give the real cause.

He then explains the key distinction: if the middle term (al-wasita) is also a cause in reality (wasta fi al-thubut), then it is burhan limmi (demonstration of why), which shows both the truth of the judgment and its real cause. The example uses rotten humors causing fever: the middle term 'putrefied in humors' is the real cause of fever in reality, so the syllogism is limmi. If the middle term is not a cause in reality, then it is burhan inni (demonstration of that), which only establishes the thatness (inniyya) of the judgment in the mind without showing its real cause. The author then discusses two subcases of burhan inni: (1) the middle term is an effect of the judgment, as in 'Zayd is feverish, every feverish is putrefied in humors' — here fever is the effect, so the syllogism is called 'proof' (dalil); (2) the middle term is neither cause nor effect but both are effects of a third cause, exemplified by a fever that intensifies every other day and is burning, both caused by yellow bile. This subtype has no specific name. The commentary uses a medical example from the Islamic tradition, showing the integration of logic with the natural sciences.

Key Terms
برهان لمّي (burhān limmī): A demonstrative syllogism whose middle term is the real cause of the conclusion in reality, showing both the judgment and its cause. [Note: This term is stable in Aristotelian logic; no significant shift.]
برهان إنّي (burhān innī): A demonstrative syllogism whose middle term is not a real cause but merely establishes the existence of the judgment in the mind. [Note: Some later logicians use 'burhan inni' more broadly for any non-causal demonstration; here it is strictly contrasted with limmi.]
واسطة في الإثبات (wāsiṭa fī al-ithbāt): The middle term that serves as a means to prove the conclusion in the mind, i.e., epistemic mediation.
واسطة في الثبوت (wāsiṭa fī al-thubūt): The middle term that is a real cause for the relation in reality, i.e., ontological mediation.
