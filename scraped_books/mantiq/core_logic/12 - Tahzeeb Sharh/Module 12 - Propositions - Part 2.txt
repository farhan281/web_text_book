Classifying Conditional Propositions by Quantifier and Condition Scope
Classification of conditionals by quantifier and condition scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given conditional proposition as universal, particular, or singular by analyzing its quantifier and the scope of conditions it covers.

Core Idea
A conditional's universality depends on whether the judgment holds for all, some, or one specific condition of the antecedent.

In this lesson, we explore how classical logicians categorized conditional propositions (al-qadiyya al-shartiyya) according to the range of conditions under which the antecedent is considered. This classification is essential for evaluating the truth of compound statements and for correctly using quantifiers in logical arguments. The passage explains the three types—universal, particular, and singular—and the rationale for restricting conditions to those that are possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conditional
Determine whether the proposition is conjunctive (muttaṣila) or disjunctive (munfaṣila). This affects which quantifiers are appropriate.
Conjunctives use 'if/then' logic; disjunctives use 'either/or'.
2
Locate the quantifier (sawr)
Find the particle that indicates the scope: 'kullamā' (whenever), 'qad yakūn' (it may be), 'laysa al-batta' (not at all), etc. The quantifier reveals whether the proposition is universal, particular, or indefinite.
The passage lists quantifiers for each type and modality.
3
Classify the proposition
If the judgment applies to all conditions (e.g., 'kullamā'), it is universal. If to some unspecified condition (e.g., 'qad yakūn'), it is particular. If to a specific condition, it is singular.
Singular conditionals are rare; the passage focuses on universal and particular.
4
Check the possibility of conditions
Verify that the conditions considered are possible (mumkin al-ijtimāʿ). If impossible conditions were included, the conditional would not be universally true, because the implication or conflict would break down.
This ensures the quantifier accurately reflects logical relationships.

Commentary
This passage defines the types of conditional propositions based on the scope of the conditions (taqadir) of the antecedent. It is a detailed classification essential for evaluating the truth conditions of compound propositions. The author begins by stating that a conditional proposition is universal if the judgment holds for all conditions of the antecedent, particular if it holds for some unspecified conditions, and singular if it holds for a specific condition. Examples are given: 'whenever the sun is rising, daytime exists' is universal. Then the quantifiers (sawar) for each type are listed: 'whenever', 'whatever' for universal affirmative; 'not at all' for universal negative; 'it may be' for particular affirmative; 'it may not be' for particular negative. The text then provides a lengthy explanation of why only possible conditions are considered (taqadir al-muqaddam). The reason is that if impossible conditions were included, the conditional would not be universally true because the implication or conflict would break down. For conjunctive conditionals, some conditions would preclude the consequent; for disjunctive conditionals, some conditions would allow both sides to be true together, negating the incompatibility. This reasoning ensures that the quantifier accurately reflects the logical relationship. The passage then cites Tahrir al-Qawa'id al-Mantiqiyya to reiterate the quantifiers and introduces the idea of modalized conditionals (when the modality of luzum, 'inad, or ittifaq is mentioned). Finally, a marginal note from Shaykh al-Islam clarifies an example: the judgment that 'if a thing is an animal, it is a human' holds only for some conditions of animality.

The Original Nature of Conditional Parts
Author explains types of conditional parts and their potency status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the two parts of a conditional as originally categorical, conjunctive, disjunctive, or mixed, and explain why connecting words make them propositions in potency rather than full propositions.

Core Idea
The parts of a conditional are propositions in potency, not fully actual propositions, because the connecting word prevents independence.

In the previous lesson, we introduced conditional propositions. Now we examine their internal structure: what are the antecedent and consequent originally before the connecting word is added? This lesson clarifies that they are full propositions of various types, and the connecting word downgrades them to mere potency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts
Every conditional has an antecedent (muqaddam) and a consequent (tali). Find them in the example: 'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists.' The antecedent is 'the sun is rising' and the consequent is 'daytime exists'.
2
Determine their original status
The author says: 'originally (fi al-asl) they are two propositions.' Before the connecting word 'in' (if) was added, each part was a complete proposition capable of standing alone.
3
Classify the proposition types
The parts can be categorically, conjunctively, disjunctively, or mixed. For example, in 'if the sun is rising, daytime exists', both parts are categorical. In a more complex conditional like 'whenever it is that if A then B, then whenever not B then not A', the parts are conjunctive.
Mixed types combine different kinds: e.g., antecedent categorical, consequent conjunctive.
4
Understand propositions in potency
The connecting word (like 'in', 'law', 'idha') prevents the parts from being actual independent propositions. They become 'propositions in potency' (qadiyya bi al-quwwa) – close to actual but not fully actual because they are bound by the connector.
This is a key insight: a proposition cannot be both fully actual and connected to another; the connector sacrifices actuality.
5
Apply to the indefinite conditional
A conditional without a quantifier (e.g., 'if a thing is a human, it is an animal') is called muhmala (indefinite). Its parts are still originally propositions, but the conditional lacks specification of universality or particularity.

Commentary
This passage defines the nature and types of the parts (atraf) of a conditional proposition. The author begins by covering the case when a conditional is not universal or particular: it is indefinite (muhmala), meaning no quantifier is expressed. Example: 'if a thing is a human, it is an animal.' Another commentary from Nur Allah clarifies that the parts are propositions 'in potency close to actuality' (bi al-quwwa al-qariba min al-fi'l). Finally, an example of a conjunctive conditional with conjunctive parts is analyzed: 'whenever it is that if the sun is rising then daytime exists, then whenever daytime does not exist, the sun is not rising' – this whole is a single conjunctive conditional.

Conditions of Contradiction: Quality, Quantity, Modality
States and explains the three necessary conditions for two propositions to be contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two propositions satisfy the three conditions for contradiction (quality, quantity, modality) using examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
For propositions to contradict, they must differ in quality, quantity, and modality—not just one.

In Islamic logic, contradiction (tanāquḍ) is a strict relation: two propositions cannot both be true and cannot both be false. But not all opposing pairs meet this standard. The classical logicians laid down precise conditions: difference in quality, quantity (if quantified), and modality (if modalized). This passage from the Sharḥ of Tahdhīb al-Manṭiq explains these conditions, gives examples of failures, and clarifies the exclusion of mental propositions.

Guided Path
1
Check quality
Determine whether each proposition is affirmative or negative. For contradiction, one must be affirmative and the other negative.
If both are affirmative or both negative, they can both be true or both false, so not contradictory.
2
Check quantity (if quantified)
If the propositions are universal (every/none) or particular (some/some not), they must differ: one universal and one particular. Non-quantified propositions are excluded from this condition.
3
Check modality (if modalized)
If the propositions have a modality (necessary, possible, impossible), they must differ. For example, two necessary propositions can both be false, and two possible propositions can both be true.
The passage gives the example: 'Every human is a writer necessarily' and 'No human is a writer necessarily' can both be false.
4
Exclude mental propositions
The passage warns against mental or non-conventional propositions (e.g., 'some species is human'), which do not follow normal predication rules and are excluded from contradiction.
5
Test with examples
Apply the three conditions to a variety of pairs from Islamic sciences (e.g., fiqh or kalam) to determine if they truly contradict.
Example: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' and 'Some intoxicant is not forbidden' pass all conditions.

Author explains the contradictory of customary universal and why temporal propositions are omitted.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The contradictory of a customary universal is the absolute conditional; temporal and distributed propositions are omitted because they are not needed for conversion and syllogisms.

In this passage, the author explains how to find the contradictory of a customary universal modal proposition—a key skill for immediate inference. He then clarifies why he does not discuss the contradictories of temporal (al-waqtiyya) and distributed (al-muntashira) simple modals: they are not required for later topics like conversion (al-ʿaks) and syllogisms (al-aqīsa). Guided Path
1
Define the customary universal
The customary universal (al-ʿurfiyya al-ʿāmma) asserts the perpetuity of a relation as long as the subject is described by a certain attribute. For example, 'every writer moves his fingers as long as he is a writer' means that the movement holds at all times when the description 'writer' applies.
The condition 'as long as the description applies' is crucial; it modally restricts the proposition.

Find its contradictory
The explicit contradictory of a customary universal is the negation of that perpetuity. This negation entails that at some time when the description applies, the opposite (predicate not holding) actually occurs. This is the absolute conditional (al-ḥīniyya al-muṭlaqa).
The contradictory is not a simple 'some S is not P' but is modalized: 'actually at some time under the description'.

Apply the rule to an example
Take the proposition 'perpetually every writer moves his fingers as long as he is a writer'. Its contradictory is 'not some writer moves his fingers when he is a writer, actually'—meaning there is a time when a writer is not moving his fingers while being a writer.
Notice the change in quantity (from universal to particular) and quality (affirmative to negative), plus the modal shift.
4

The author did not discuss the contradictories of temporal (al-waqtiyya) and distributed (al-muntashira) absolute propositions. The reason is that these simple modals are not used in the subsequent discussions of conversion and syllogisms, so their contradictories are not needed.
This is a practical pedagogical choice: only necessary logical tools are introduced.

This disjunctive proposition is equivalent to the true contradictory.

Contradictories of Simple Modal Propositions
But regarding the particular [proposition], with respect to every single individual.
On the realities of compounds and the contradictories of simples you can derive details of contradictories of compounds. His saying: 'but regarding the particular, with respect to every single individual' means it is not sufficient in taking the contradictory of a compound particular proposition to disjunct between the contradictories of its two parts, which are the two universals, because the compound particular may be false, as in our saying 'Some animal is actually man, not always', and both of the contradictories of its two parts are also false, namely our saying 'No animal is man always' and our saying 'Every animal is man always'. Then the method of taking the contradictory of the compound particular is to posit all the individuals of the subject—since the contradictory of a particular is a universal—and then disjunct between the contradictories of the two parts with respect to each of those individuals. So in the mentioned example we say: 'Every animal is either always man or not always man', and then the contradictory is true, and it is a categorical proposition with a disjunctive predicate. So his saying 'with respect to every single individual' means the individuals of the subject.
But [the phrase] 'but regarding the particular' is a repulsion of the misunderstanding arising from his statement 'and regarding the compound etc.' that the compound particular proposition occurred as absolute, not restricted by universality, while the absolutes of the sciences are universals, so one might imagine that the disjunctive concept is also a contradictory of the compound particular.
The falsity of 'No animal is man always' and 'Every animal is man always' is apparent because the first entails negating the more specific from all individuals of the more general, and the second entails predicating the more specific of all individuals of the more general, and both are false.
If it is said: How can this categorical proposition with disjunctive predicate 'Every animal is either always man or not always man' be a contradictory of the mentioned non-permanent existential proposition, i.e., our saying 'Some animal is actually man, not always', since both propositions are affirmative, and one of the conditions of contradiction is difference in affirmation and negation as previously mentioned? The answer is that the application of 'contradictory' here is metaphorical; in reality it is equivalent to its contradictory. [Isma'il]

Commentary
This passage defines the correct method for deriving the contradictory of a compound particular proposition, and it is a commentary on an earlier matn. The author begins by noting a misunderstanding: one might think that simply disjoining the contradictories of the two parts (the two universals) suffices to form the contradictory of a compound particular. He demonstrates this failure with an example: 'Some animal is actually man, not always'. The two parts are 'is man' and 'not always', whose contradictories would be 'No animal is man always' and 'Every animal is man always', but both of these are false. The proper method requires taking the subject's individuals one by one. Since the contradictory of a particular is a universal, we must say: 'Every animal is either always man or not always man'. This is a categorical proposition with a disjunctive predicate. The author then clarifies that the phrase 'with respect to every single individual' refers to the individuals of the subject. He then addresses a potential objection: if the disjunctive proposition 'Every animal is either always man or not always man' and the original 'Some animal is actually man, not always' are both affirmative, how can they be contradictories? The answer is that 'contradictory' is used metaphorically; in fact, the disjunctive proposition is equivalent to the true contradictory.

Key Takeaway
The contradictory of a compound particular proposition is a universal categorical with a disjunctive predicate, formed by iterating over each individual of the subject.
