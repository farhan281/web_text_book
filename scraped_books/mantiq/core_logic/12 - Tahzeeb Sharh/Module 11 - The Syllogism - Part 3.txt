The Requirement of Certainty in Demonstrative Syllogisms
Author states the condition that all premises in burhan must be yaqini, distinguishing it from other syllogism types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a syllogism as burhani, safsata, jadali, or shi'ri based on the certainty of its premises.

Core Idea
A demonstrative proof requires every single premise to be certain; one weak premise ruins the whole argument.

Not all syllogisms are created equal. In Islamic logic, the highest form is the demonstrative syllogism (burhan), which yields certain knowledge. But what makes a syllogism demonstrative? The author of this passage gives a strict condition: every premise must be certain (yaqini). Any deviation, even a single conjectural premise, downgrades the argument to sophistry, contentiousness, dialectic, or even poetry. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for evaluating theological and legal proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The passage begins by naming 'the demonstrative syllogism and its parts'. Find the Arabic term for demonstration and note that it is defined by its composition.
2
Extract the condition for burhan
The author states that in a demonstration, all (bi-asriha) its premises must be certain. Contrast with other types: for example, a syllogism is considered fallacious if just one premise is conjectural (wahmi).
The word 'bi-asriha' emphasizes totality.
3
Map the hierarchy of premises
List the epistemic levels mentioned: yaqini (certain), mashhura (approved/commonly accepted), wahmi (conjectural), and mukhayyila (imaginative). Note that each corresponds to a type of syllogism: burhan, jadal, safsata, and shi'r.
The text also introduces mushaghaba as a subtype of safsata used against non-philosophers.
4
Apply the weaker-premise rule
Understand that the weakest premise determines the classification. A mix of mashhura and mukhayyila becomes poetic, not dialectical, because the imaginative premise is lower. This shows why burhan requires all premises at the highest level.

Commentary
This passage defines the foundational requirement for a demonstrative syllogism (burhan) in Islamic logic. The author begins by stating that a demonstrative syllogism is composed entirely of certain premises (yaqiniyyat). If a syllogism includes anything less than certainty, it falls into another category: if one premise is merely conjectural (wahmi), the whole is called sophistry (safsata); if used in argument against a non-philosopher, it becomes contentiousness (mushaghaba). The author then explains that in burhan, all premises must be certain, unlike other types of argument. For example, a syllogism can be considered fallacious even if only one of its two premises is wahmi, regardless of the other being certain. Furthermore, a syllogism must not contain premises of an even lower epistemic status, such as imaginative (khayali) premises used in poetry; if it does, it is considered poetic rather than dialectical. This highlights the strict hierarchy: certain (yaqini) premises are highest, followed by mashhura (approved, commonly accepted), then wahmi (conjectural), then khayali (imaginative). A dialectical syllogism (jadal) uses mashhura premises, but if it mixes in a khayali premise, it becomes poetic because the weaker drags down the stronger.

Rhetorical and Poetic Premise Types
Defines and expands on premise types for rhetorical and poetic syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify premises as rhetorical or poetic and identify the specific sub-types: musallamat, muqbalat, maznunat, and mukhayyilat, based on their definitions and the commentator's expansions.

Core Idea
Rhetoric and poetry differ not in syllogistic form but in the epistemic and psychological status of their premises.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the types of premises used in rhetorical and poetic syllogisms. While demonstrative syllogisms require certain premises, rhetoric uses accepted and conjectural premises, and poetry uses imaginative premises. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for analyzing arguments in various Islamic disciplines where certainty is not always sought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main syllogism types
The passage distinguishes rhetorical (khitabi) and poetic (shi'ri) syllogisms. They are defined by the types of premises they use.
2
List the premise types for each
Rhetorical: musallamat (accepted), muqbalat (from trusted), maznunat (conjectural). Poetic: mukhayyilat (imaginative). Also note 'opinions of a group' as rhetorical.
3
Understand the definition of musallamat
Propositions accepted from an opponent or from another science. The commentator clarifies they can be true or false, taken on trust.
Example: jurists accept premises from usul al-fiqh on taslim.
4
Distinguish muqbalat and maznunat
Muqbalat are more general, including certain and conjectural premises from trusted sources. Maznunat here are specifically conjectural premises not from trusted sources. The commentator resolves the apparent overlap.
The comparison is like 'animal' vs 'human'.
5
Understand mukhayyilat
Propositions that do not cause assent but affect the soul emotionally. Commentator notes rhyme/meter increases effect.
These are unique to poetic syllogisms.

Refining the Definition of Premises: Whole-Part vs. Condition Dependence
Author refines the definition of premises by distinguishing two types of dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between whole-part dependence and condition-conditioned dependence in the definition of premises, and explain why the broad definition is permissible.

Core Idea
Defining premises by the more general is permissible when the intended dependence is that of a whole on its parts.

In this passage, the commentator addresses an objection: the phrase 'upon which syllogisms are based' seems too broad because it could include external evidences. The response clarifies the two types of dependence – whole-part and condition-conditioned – and shows that premises are parts, not conditions. This teaches us how a definition can be broad yet precise by context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the implied question: the definition of premises as 'that upon which syllogisms are based' would also include the external evidences (dalā'il) that support the premises, making the definition too broad.
The objection assumes any dependence is included.
2
Understand the first part of the answer
The commentator says the definition can be considered a definition by the more general (al-a‘amm), which is permissible for some logicians, or a verbal definition (tafsīr), which is acceptable even if broad.
This shows that strict real definition is not always required.
3
Analyze the two types of dependence
Dependence (ibtinā') has two meanings: (1) dependence of a whole on its parts (parts are internal), and (2) dependence of a thing on an external condition (condition is outside). Distinguish them by inclusion vs. exclusion.
Parts are inside the whole; conditions are outside.
4
Apply to premises
Premises are parts of the syllogism, not external conditions. So the intended meaning is the whole-part dependence. Thus the definition, though general in wording, is restricted by context.
This resolves the objection.
5
Recognize the type of definition
The definition is by essential accident (‛araḍ dhātī) for the purpose of distinguishing premises from other things. Even if it is broader in itself, it serves the purpose.
Essential accident: a property that is necessary but not part of the essence.

Taksir and Tahlil: Constructing and Analyzing Syllogisms
Author explains taksir and tahlil methods for syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of taksir to derive a categorical syllogism in the correct figure and use tahlil to determine the figure of a given syllogism.

Core Idea
Taksir finds the middle term by expanding the conclusion; tahlil reveals the figure of any syllogism.

In the Islamic logical tradition, constructing a valid syllogism from a desired conclusion is a key skill. The method of taksir (multiplication) provides a systematic way to generate premises by exploring all possible subjects, predicates, and negations related to the conclusion's terms. Conversely, tahlil (analysis) is used to identify the figure and validity of syllogisms that may not be presented in standard form. This lesson unpacks these complementary methods as explained in the commentary on al-Tahzeeb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
Take the desired proposition (matalub) you want to prove. It has a subject (mawdu') and a predicate (mahmul). For example, 'The world is originated' has subject 'world' and predicate 'originated'.
Keep the subject and predicate as the two extremes (tarafan).
2
Generate lists of subjects, predicates, and negations
List all things that are subjects of each extreme (i.e., things that have that property) and all predicates of each extreme (i.e., properties that apply to them). Also list things that are negated from each extreme (i.e., properties that do not apply) and things from which each extreme is negated.
For example, for 'world', some predicates are 'changing', 'possible', 'existent'; for 'originated', some subjects are 'every changing thing' or 'some possible thing'. Also note negations like 'world is not eternal' or 'eternal is not originated'.
3
Examine relations to determine the figure

Takthir: Multiplying Premises from the Conclusion
Author explains takthir method for syllogism extraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the premises for a desired conclusion using the takthir method, identifying the correct figure from the relationships between subjects and predicates.

Core Idea
Takthir starts from the conclusion and works upward to discover the premises and syllogistic form.

In this passage, the author introduces the method of takthir, which is a systematic way to generate the premises needed to prove any desired proposition. By mastering this method, you can reverse-engineer syllogistic proofs found in Islamic sciences, ensuring your reasoning is structurally sound.

Guided Path
1
Posit the two extremes of the conclusion
Identify the subject (موضوع) and predicate (محمول) of the desired proposition. For example, in 'The world is temporally originated', the subject is 'the world' and the predicate is 'temporally originated'.
2
List all subjects and predicates related to each extreme
For both the subject and predicate of the conclusion, search for all possible subjects and predicates that can be truthfully attached, including those that are negated. This generates candidate middle terms.
Also consider terms that are predicated on the extremes or that the extremes are predicated on.
3
Compare relationships to identify the figure
Examine the discovered terms: if you find a term that is a predicate of the conclusion's subject and also a subject of the conclusion's predicate, you have a first-figure syllogism. Similarly for the other three figures based on the positions.
The four figures correspond to the four possible arrangements of the middle term relative to subject and predicate.
4
Verify quantity and quality conditions
Ensure that the resulting syllogism respects the standard conditions for its figure, such as universality, particularity, affirmation, or negation, to guarantee validity.
These conditions are detailed in earlier lessons on each figure.

Tahlil: Analyzing Syllogisms to Find Their Form
Author explains tahlil method for syllogism analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a given syllogism using tahlil to determine whether it is conjunctive or conditional, identify the minor and major premises, and find the middle term and figure.

Core Idea
Tahlil is the reverse of takthir: it breaks down a presented syllogism to reveal its underlying logical structure.

Often in Islamic texts, syllogisms are presented incompletely or non-standardly. Tahlil provides a step-by-step method to uncover the intended figure and mood, ensuring you understand the proof's validity. This passage explains how to distinguish conjunctive from conditional syllogisms and how to locate the middle term.

Guided Path
1
Check if a premise shares both parts of the conclusion
Look for a premise that contains both the subject and predicate of the conclusion. If found, the syllogism is conditional (istithna'i). Otherwise, if only one part is shared, it is conjunctive (iqtirani).
This initial classification guides the rest of the analysis.
2
Identify the shared part as minor or major
In a conjunctive syllogism, the premise that shares a part of the conclusion is either the minor premise (if it shares the subject) or the major premise (if it shares the predicate). The other premise introduces the middle term.
The passage says: 'that shared part is either the part that is the subject of judgment... so it is the minor, or the predicate... so it is the major.'
3
Join the other part of the conclusion with the other part of the shared premise
Take the part of the conclusion not shared (if the shared part is the subject, take the predicate; vice versa) and combine it with the other part of that premise. If they fit one of the four figures, the new term is the middle term and the figure is determined.
If they do not fit directly, the syllogism may be composite, requiring further analysis.
4
Repeat for composite syllogisms if necessary
If the combination fails to produce a figure, treat each part independently and repeat the process. Each part must relate to something in the syllogism; otherwise, it is not productive.
This step ensures that even complex arguments can be reduced to standard forms.

تحصيل مطلب من المطالب التصديقية فضع طرفي المطلوب واطلب جميع موضوعات كل واحد منهما وجميع محمولات كل واحد منهما سواء كان حمل الطرفين عليها أو حملها على الطرفين بواسطة أو بغير واسطة وكذا طلب جميع ما سلب عنه أحد الطرفين أو سلب هو عن أحدهما، ثم انظر إلى نسبة الطرفين إلى الموضوعات والمحمولات، فإن وجدت من محمولات موضوع المطلوب ما هو موضوع لمحموله فقد حصلت المطلوب من الشكل الأول أو ما هو محمول على محموله فمن الشكل الثاني أو من موضوعات موضوعه ما هو موضوع لمحموله فمن الشكل الثالث أو محمول لمحموله فمن الشكل الرابع كل ذلك بعد اعتبار الشرائط بحسب الكمية والكيفية. كذا في شرح المطالع. وقد عبر المصنف عن هذا المعنى بقوله : أعني التكثير إلى تكثير المقدمات آخذاً من فوق أي من النتيجة؛ لأنها المقصد الأقصى أي بالنسبة إلى الدليل قوله: والتحليل: في شرح المطالع كثيرا ما يورد في العلوم قياسات منتجة للمطالب لا على الهيئات المنطقية لتساهل المركب؛ اعتمادا على الفطن العالم بالقواعد، فإن أردت أن تعرف أنه على أي الشكل من الأشكال فعليك بالتحليل، وهو عكس الترتيب حتى حصل المطلوب، فانظر إلى القياس المنتج له، فإن كان فيه مقدمة تشارك المطلوب بكلا جزئيه فالقياس استثنائي، وإن كانت مشاركة للمطلوب بأحد جزئيه فالقياس اقتراني، ثم انظر إلى طرفي المطلوب؛ ليتميز عندك الصغرى عن الكبرى، فذلك المشارك إما الجزء الذي يكون محكوما عليه في المطلوب فهي الصغرى أو محكوما به فيه فهي الكبرى، ثم ضُمَّ الجزء الآخر من المطلوب على الجزء الآخر من تلك المقدمة، فإن تألّفا على أحد التأليفات الأربعة فما انضم إلى جزء المطلوب هو الحد الأوسط ويتميز الشكل المنتج، وإن لم يتألفا كان القياس مركبا، فاعمل بكل واحد منهما العمل المذكور أي ضع الجزء الآخر من المطلوب والجزء الآخر من المقدمة كما وضعت طرفي المطلوب في التقسيم، فلابد أن يكون لكل منهما نسبة إلى شيء ما في القياس، وإلا لم يكن القياس منتجا للمطلوب، فإن وجدت حدا مشتركا بينهما فقد تم القياس، وتبين لك المقدمات والأشكال والنتيجة. فقوله: وهو أي تكثير المقدمات إلى فوق وهو النتيجة كما مر وجهه
To obtain a desired proposition from among the assertoric propositions, posit the two extremes of the desired proposition and seek all the subjects of each one of them and all the predicates of each one of them, whether the two extremes are predicated upon them or they are predicated upon the two extremes, with a middle or without a middle, and likewise seek all that from which one of the two extremes is negated or what it is negated from, then look at the relation of the two extremes to the subjects and predicates. So if you find among the predicates of the subject of the desired proposition something that is a subject of its predicate, then you have obtained the desired proposition from the first figure, or if something predicated upon its predicate, then from the second figure, or from among the subjects of its subject something that is a subject for its predicate, then from the third figure, or a predicate for its predicate, then from the fourth figure, all after considering the conditions regarding quantity and quality. Thus in Sharh al-Matali'. And the author expressed this meaning by saying: I mean takthir – i.e., multiplying the premises – taking from above, i.e., from the conclusion, because it is the ultimate goal, i.e., relative to the proof. His saying: 'and al-tahlil' – in Sharh al-Matali' it is frequent that in the sciences syllogisms are given that produce the desired propositions not in the logical forms due to the incompetence of the composer, relying on the clever one knowledgeable of the rules. So if you wish to know on which of the forms it is, you must proceed by analysis, which is the reverse of arrangement until you obtain the desired proposition. So look at the syllogism that produced it. If there is a premise that shares both parts of the desired proposition, the syllogism is conditional (istithna'i), and if it shares one of the two parts, the syllogism is conjunctive (iqtirani). Then look at the two extremes of the desired proposition to distinguish for you the minor from the major; so that shared part is either the part that is the subject of judgment in the desired proposition, so it is the minor, or the predicate in it, so it is the major. Then join the other part of the desired proposition with the other part of that premise. If they combine in one of the four compositions, then what is joined to the part of the desired proposition is the middle term and the productive figure is distinguished. If they do not combine, then the syllogism is composite. So work on each one of them with the aforementioned procedure, i.e., posit the other part of the desired proposition and the other part of the premise just as you posited the two extremes of the desired proposition in the division. So each of them must have a relation to something in the syllogism; otherwise the syllogism would not produce the desired proposition. If you find a common term between them, then the syllogism is complete, and the premises, figures, and conclusion become clear to you. So his saying: 'and he is' – i.e., the takthir of premises upward, which is the conclusion, as its reason has passed.

Commentary
This passage explains the method of takthir (multiplication of premises) and tahlil (analysis) for extracting syllogisms from the sciences. The author first describes takthir: to prove a desired proposition, you start with its subject and predicate, then seek all subjects and predicates related to them, including those that are predicated or negated. This yields potential middle terms. Then you compare the subject and predicate of the desired proposition to these, and if you find a term that is a subject of the predicate's subject, you have a first-figure syllogism; similarly for the other three figures. All this must respect the conditions of quantity and quality. The commentator notes that the author expressed this as 'takthir from above,' meaning starting from the conclusion (the ultimate goal) and multiplying premises upward. Next, the passage discusses tahlil: often sciences present syllogisms not in standard logical form, so we must analyze them. To analyze, first determine if the syllogism is conditional (istithna'i) if a premise shares both parts of the conclusion, or conjunctive (iqtirani) if only one part is shared. Then identify the minor and major premises by seeing which part of the conclusion is shared. Then join the other part of the conclusion with the other part of that premise; if they form one of the four figures, the middle term is revealed. If not, the syllogism is composite, and you repeat the procedure. This method ensures you identify the correct form and validity. The commentator concludes by reaffirming that takthir proceeds upward from the conclusion.
