Sawa' al-Tariq as Metaphor: The Middle of the Path
Author defines 'sawa' al-tariq' as a metaphor for the straight path

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the metaphorical relationship between 'sawa' al-tariq' and 'al-tariq al-mustawi' and defend al-Dawani's interpretation against the criticism of linguistic forcedness.

Core Idea
The middle of the path is a metaphor for the straight path; a word can indicate its concomitant.

When the author uses the phrase 'sawa' al-tariq', he is not simply saying 'the straight path' in a literal way. Rather, he employs a rhetorical device called kinnaya (metaphorical indication), where the literal meaning (middle of the path) implies a necessary concomitant (straightness). In this lesson, we will unpack this relationship, see how it connects to the two meanings of 'hidayah', and examine how the commentator defends this interpretation against an objection that it involves unnecessary linguistic contortions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of 'hidayah'
The passage begins by distinguishing two usages of 'hidayah': the first is causing to reach (الإيصال), and the remaining usages are showing the path (إراءة الطريق). Note this distinction as it underlies the metaphorical reading.
This distinction is common in usul al-fiqh discussions of divine guidance.
2
Extract the literal and intended meanings of 'sawa' al-tariq'
The commentator explains 'sawa' al-tariq' as the middle (وسط) of the path that leads the walker to the desired end absolutely. Then he states that this is a metaphor (كناية) for the straight path (الطريق المستوي). The literal meaning is the middle; the intended meaning is straightness.
The relationship is based on necessary concomitance (تلازم): the middle of a path is a necessary concomitant of the path being straight.
3
Recognize the role of necessary concomitance in kinnaya
The marginal gloss (عبد) clarifies that the basis of kinnaya is accompaniment (لزوم); the speaker intends the necessary concomitant of the literal meaning, with the possibility of also intending the literal meaning. This is a standard definition from 'ilm al-bayan (rhetoric).
Understanding kinnaya requires knowing that the literal and intended meanings are linked by a necessary relationship.
4
Follow the objection and rebuttal regarding the Quranic verse
An objection is raised citing the verse 'Indeed We guided him to the way' (إنا هديناه السبيل). The objector argues that if 'hidayah' means showing the path, it cannot be transitive to a single object without a second object. The rebuttal states that the context is not clear, so the objection is not decisive.
This shows how the commentator actively defends his interpretation against scriptural counterexamples.
5
Analyze the defense of al-Muhaqqiq al-Dawani's interpretation
A critic claims that al-Dawani's interpretation of 'sawa' al-tariq' as the straight path involves three forced conceits: taking 'sawa' to mean straightness, using it as an adjective, and making the construction an idafa of attribute to described. The defense is that this is not a literal translation but a metaphorical indication (إشارة), just as 'long of the sword-belt' can metaphorically mean 'tall of stature'.
The defense emphasizes that kinnaya is a recognized rhetorical device, not a violation of linguistic norms.

The commentator begins by defining 'hidayah' (guidance) in two senses: causing arrival (isal) and showing the way (ira'at al-tariq). Then he explains 'sawa' al-tariq' as the middle of the path that necessarily leads to the goal, and clarifies that this is a metaphor (kinnaya) for the straight path. Finally, he links 'sawa' al-tariq' to the general meaning of true beliefs encompassing logical and theological principles. This passage demonstrates the commentator's method of combining lexical analysis, rhetorical theory (kinnaya), and dialectical defense.

Grammatical Analysis of 'Lana' – Attachment and Theological Implications
Grammatical analysis of 'lana' attachment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible attachments of the adverb 'lana' in 'wa ja'ala lana' and evaluate their theological implications.

Core Idea
Grammar shapes theology: one adverb, two attachments, different doctrines.

This passage from the logic text's preface dissects the phrase 'wa ja'ala lana' (and He made for us). The commentator debates whether the adverb 'lana' attaches to the verb 'ja'ala' (made) or to the noun 'rafiq' (companion). The choice is not merely grammatical—it forces a theological decision about whether God's actions have purposes and whether divine attributes are created. By working through this analysis, you will see how precise grammatical reasoning grounds theological claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key components
Locate the phrase 'wa ja'ala lana' in the Arabic. Recognize 'ja'ala' (He made), 'lana' (for us), and 'rafiq' (companion). The full context is about God making tawfiq (guidance) a best companion.
The phrase is part of the author's praise of God.
2
List the two possible attachments
Option A: 'lana' attaches to 'ja'ala', with 'lam' indicating benefit (intifa'), citing Quran 2:22. Option B: 'lana' attaches to 'rafiq', which requires fronting the object of the genitive (muqaddam ma'mul al-mudaf ilayh).
The first is deemed closer in wording (aqrab lafẓan), the second closer in meaning (aqrab maʿnan).
3
Examine implications of attachment to 'ja'ala'
If 'lana' attaches to 'ja'ala', then tawfiq is the object made and 'lana' indicates beneficiary. The 'lam' could be misinterpreted as purposive (taʿlili), implying God acts for a purpose—theologically problematic. The commentator refutes this by insisting the 'lam' is for benefit, not purpose. However, even with benefit, a problem remains: interposition (takhallul) of 'making' between tawfiq and its essential attribute (khayr rafiq) is prohibited.
Takhallul means that the act of making separates the thing from its essence or necessary concomitant, which is impossible.
4
Examine implications of attachment to 'rafiq'
If 'lana' attaches to 'rafiq', then tawfiq is not a separate created object; rather, its companionship is specified to us. This avoids the takhallul problem and aligns with the context of exclusive praise—since praise is stronger when the blessing reaches the praiser alone. This interpretation ensures tawfiq is 'for us' exclusively.
Grammatically, this attachment requires breaking the rule that the object of a genitive cannot precede the genitive, but adverbs (ẓuruf) allow such exceptions.
5
Assess which attachment is preferred
The commentator favors the second attachment (to 'rafiq') despite its grammatical irregularity, because it avoids the theological difficulties (purpose, takhallul) and best serves the author's intention of exclusive praise. The decision shows that meaning (maʿna) can override wording (lafẓ) when supported by contextual and theological reasoning.
The passage concludes with a reminder to ponder (taʾammul), indicating the debate is nuanced.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and theological commentary on the phrase 'wa ja'ala lana' (and He made for us) from the author's introduction. The commentator first identifies this section as discussing the parsing (i'rab) of the adverb 'lana' and the word 'tawfiq' (guidance). He clarifies that 'or specific' refers to the religion of Islam, and the first interpretation is preferred. The central grammatical problem is the attachment of the adverb 'lana': it either attaches to 'ja'ala' (He made) with the lam indicating benefit, analogous to the Quranic verse about the earth as a bed, or it attaches to 'rafiq' (companion), which would require unusual fronting. The commentator notes that the first is closer in wording, the second in meaning, because it avoids issues like making tawfiq a separate created thing. He then addresses theological objections: if 'lana' attaches to 'ja'ala', it might imply God's actions have purposes, which is refuted by interpreting lam as benefit not purpose. However, that benefit interpretation still leaves the problem of interposition (takhallul) between tawfiq and its essentials, which is prohibited. The commentator discusses various scholarly positions, including that absolute good is essential to tawfiq, and that the author intended to restrict tawfiq to us specifically for the sake of praise. Ultimately, he favors the attachment to 'rafiq' as more meaningful, despite grammatical irregularity. This passage demonstrates how grammatical analysis (i'rab) interacts with theological considerations in Islamic logic texts.

Definition of mustaqirr and laghw

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A 'mustaqirr' adverb stands in for its deleted operator, while 'laghw' is redundant because its operator is explicitly stated.

The passage provides a technical distinction between two types of adverbs in Arabic syntax: 'mustaqirr' and 'laghw'. This distinction is crucial for understanding how circumstantial expressions function in sentences. The author explains that 'mustaqirr' takes the place of its operator when the operator is omitted, whereas 'laghw' is attached to a mentioned operator and thus does not substitute for it.

Distinguishing Ṣidq for Concepts and Propositions
Author clarifies that the four relations apply to concepts via predication and to propositions via existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two technical uses of the term ṣidq in Islamic logic: predication for concepts and truth-as-actualization for propositions.

Core Idea
Ṣidq means predication for concepts, but truth-as-actualization for propositions.

In this short but crucial benefit, al-Taftazani clarifies a subtle terminological distinction that is easy to miss. The same Arabic word ṣidq is used in two different technical senses depending on whether you are talking about concepts (mufradāt) or propositions (qaḍāyā). Paying attention to the preposition—'ala for concepts and 'fi for propositions—is the key to understanding which sense is intended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts
Note that the author discusses ṣidq in relation to two distinct logical units: singular concepts (mufradāt) and propositions (qaḍāyā).
2
Observe the preposition used
With concepts, the preposition is 'ala (on/upon), as in ṣadaqa al-ḥayawān ‘alā al-insān. With propositions, the preposition is fī (in), as in hādhihi al-qaḍiyya ṣādiqa fī nafs al-amr.
The preposition is a direct indicator of which sense is meant.
3
Determine the meaning in each context
For concepts, ṣidq means predication (ḥaml) – the application of one term to another. For propositions, ṣidq means actualization (taḥaqquq) – the correspondence of the proposition with reality.
The author explicitly states that carrying (ḥaml) over propositions is inconceivable.
4
Apply the distinction to the example
The author gives an example from the conversion of conditionals. When he says 'whenever the content of the proposition 'every J is B' is true by necessity', the word 'true' here means actualized in reality, not predicated.
This shows how ṣidq functions in a propositional context: it refers to the actualization of the proposition's content.

The Two Ties of a Proposition: Position and Judgment
Explains the two ties of a proposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between ʿaqd al-waḍʿ and ʿaqd al-ḥaml in any proposition and explain their roles.

Core Idea
Every proposition has a positional tie and a judgmental tie — the logic behind its truth.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks the structure of a proposition in the sciences. He explains that every proposition reduces to two relations: the tie that links the subject's essence to its conceptual description (the 'title'), and the tie that links that essence to the predicate. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping the dispute between al-Farabi and Ibn Sina.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three elements of a proposition
The passage lists three: the essence of the subject, the truth of its description upon itself, and the truth of the predicate upon the subject.
These are the minimal components for a proposition to be realized.
2
Distinguish between the two ties
The tie of position (ʿaqd al-waḍʿ) is the relation of the subject's essence to its conceptual description (e.g., 'human' in 'Every human is rational'). The tie of judgment (ʿaqd al-ḥaml) is the relation of the subject's essence to the predicate.
The former is definitional, the latter is predicative.
3
Characterize each tie
The first is a restrictive composite with a universal positing (a definitional relation), the second is a predicative composite (an assertion).
This characterization comes directly from the commentary.
4
Understand why the mode matters
The relation in the tie of position must be qualified by a mode (possibility, actuality, necessity) for the proposition to be meaningful. This mode is what al-Farabi and Ibn Sina dispute.
This step sets up the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion on the truth conditions of propositions used in the sciences. The commentator first explains the structure of a proposition: it includes three elements—the essence of the subject, the truth of its conceptual description (e.g., 'human' in 'every human is rational') upon that essence, and the truth of the predicate description upon the essence. The relation between the subject's essence and its conceptual description must be qualified by some mode (possibility, actuality, necessity). Al-Farabi held that this quality in the tie of position is mere possibility: it is enough that the subject's description can apply to its essence, even if it never actually does. Ibn Sina disagreed, arguing that it must be actual in some sense (either in one of the three times: past, present, future, or in mental assumption). The commentator then explains the consequence for conversion of the two possibility propositions: if the tie is mere possibility (al-Farabi), then the two possibility propositions convert to a general possible (since possibility is symmetric). But if the tie includes actuality (Ibn Sina), they do not convert at all, because actuality is not symmetric. The commentator clarifies that this is a nuanced view, not the absolute claim that Ibn Sina denies conversion outright. He also clarifies that al-Farabi's 'possibility' is conceptual, not existential, so propositions like 'Every partner of God is impossible' are included (since 'partner of God' is impossible in reality, but the concept is possible logically). Ibn Sina's view is based on linguistic custom: when we say 'every black', we usually refer to things that are actually black at some time, not simply possibly black. For example, a white Roman slave who will never be black is not included in 'every black' according to Ibn Sina, but would be according to al-Farabi (since it is possible for him to become black, even if he never does). The passage ends noting that Ibn Sina's tie is possibility restricted to existence, covering also necessary attributes.

Al-Farabi vs. Ibn Sina: Possibility or Actuality in the Tie of Position?
Compares al-Farabi and Ibn Sina on the tie of position

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare al-Farabi and Ibn Sina's positions on the mode of the tie of position and explain the consequence for conversion of possibility propositions.

Core Idea
For al-Farabi, mere possibility suffices; for Ibn Sina, actuality is needed. This changes what counts as a valid scientific proposition.

The commentator now presents the core dispute. Al-Farabi held that the tie of position requires only that the subject's description possibly apply to its essence — even if it never actually occurs. Ibn Sina argued that linguistic and customary usage demands that it actually apply in some time (past, present, future) or at least in mental assumption. This disagreement has direct implications for which propositions are accepted in sciences and how they convert.

Guided Path
1
State each philosopher's position
Al-Farabi: the quality in the tie of position is mere possibility (imkan). Ibn Sina: it is possibility with actuality (imkan ma'a al-fi'l) either in existence or mental assumption.
The passage explicitly contrasts these views.
2
Understand the consequence for conversion
Under al-Farabi's view, two possibility propositions (e.g., 'Every J is possibly B') convert to a general possible. Under Ibn Sina's view (if actuality is in actual fact), they do not convert.
The commentator explains the logical outcome of each position.
3
Evaluate the example of 'Every partner of God is impossible'
Under al-Farabi, since the concept 'partner of God' is possibly applicable to some essence (though impossible in reality), it is included. Under Ibn Sina, if no actual partner exists, it would not be included because there is no actual application.
This shows how the choice affects the extension of the subject.
4
Test the example of 'every black' with a Roman slave
A white Roman slave who never becomes black is included in 'every black' for al-Farabi (since it is possible for him to be black), but excluded for Ibn Sina (since he never actually is black).
Ibn Sina's view aligns with linguistic custom: we don't call someone black if they never actually are black.

Critique of al-Farabi and al-Ghazali's Definitions of Induction
Critique of definitions of induction by al-Farabi and al-Ghazali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in the definitions of induction given by al-Farabi and al-Ghazali and explain why the commentator considers them lax (tasamuh).

Core Idea
A definition of induction must describe a logical proof, not just a process of tracking particulars.

In this passage, the commentator critiques the definitions of induction proposed by al-Farabi and al-Ghazali. He argues that defining induction as merely 'tracking particulars to establish a universal judgment' is too lax because the tracking itself is not a known assent (tasdiq) leading to an unknown assent. However, he excuses this laxity as a way to indicate that the term 'induction' is a transferred (naql) usage from its linguistic meaning. Understanding this critique helps you appreciate the precision required in logical definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions being critiqued
Find the definitions from al-Farabi and al-Ghazali in the translation. They define induction as 'perusal of particulars and tracking them to establish a universal judgment.' Also note al-Bazdawi's similar definition.
2
Understand the commentator's objection
The commentator says these definitions contain 'evident laxity' because the tracking process is not a known assent (tasdiq) that leads to an unknown assent. Therefore it does not qualify as a proof (hujja).
Refer to the concept of tasdiq from earlier lessons.
3
Recognize the excuse for the laxity
The commentator suggests the motive was to show that the term 'induction' is used by transfer (naql) from its linguistic meaning, not improvisation (irtijal).
This preserves the linguistic connection.
4
Distinguish between masdari and technical meanings
Induction (istiqra') can refer to the process (masdari) or the proof. The critic notes that the definition given applies to the process, but the technical term should be defined by analogy (muqayasa).
This clarifies the double usage.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the example of intoxicants: tracking most intoxicants (wine, etc.) to conclude that all intoxicants are forbidden is an incomplete induction. The process yields a particular judgment about each, not a universal, and thus falls under syllogism.
This shows why the definition is not precise.

As for what the author deduced from the statement of al-Farabi and Hujjat al-Islam (al-Ghazali) and chose, namely, the perusal of particulars and tracking them to establish a universal judgment, it contains evident laxity. Here is another aspect that will come, God willing, in the verification of analogy. His statement: 'to establish a universal judgment' either by way of description, so it is an indication that the sought thing in induction is not a particular judgment as we will verify, or by way of annexation and tanwin, so 'kulli' then is a substitute for the annexed. From the statement of al-Farabi: that induction is the judgment on a universal due to its existence in most particulars. And Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi said: it is the perusal of particular matters to judge by their judgment on a matter that encompasses those particulars. In it there is evident laxity. The meaning of laxity has passed in the explanation of the productivity of the fourth figure moods. It is as if it is for simile, and it is sometimes used in the place of doubt and weakness. So this is an excuse on behalf of the author for choosing this definition. ... There is another aspect: that 'induction' is applied to the masdari (verbal noun) meaning, which is perusal and tracking, and to the proof in which that tracking occurs, just as 'conversion' (al-'aks) is applied to the masdari meaning, i.e., replacement, and to the proposition resulting after the replacement. So what is intended here is the definition of induction according to the first meaning. As for its definition according to the second meaning, it is defined by analogy. It is not a particular judgment, for the tracking of most particulars undoubtedly yields certainty of the particular judgment as will appear, so it falls under the syllogism that yields the particular judgment, just as complete induction falls under it as you have known and will know.

Commentary
This passage critiques the definitions of induction provided by al-Farabi and al-Ghazali (Hujjat al-Islam) that define it as the mere perusal and tracking of particulars to establish a universal judgment. The commentator argues that this definition is too lax because the tracking itself is not a known assent (tasdiq) that leads to an unknown assent, and therefore it does not qualify as a proof. The laxity is excused, however, as a way to indicate that the term 'induction' for this type of proof is not an improvisation but a transfer from the linguistic meaning (perusal) to the technical term. The commentator then explains the distinction between the masdari (verbal noun) meaning of istiqra' (the process of tracking) and the technical meaning (the proof itself). He notes that incomplete induction can yield certainty about a particular judgment and thus falls under syllogism. This is illustrated with the idea that tracking most intoxicants (like wine) to conclude that all intoxicants are forbidden is a form of incomplete induction. The commentator defends the author's choice by showing that the definition aims to preserve the linguistic connection.

How to Grammatically Analyze 'Karr' in a Proverb
Grammatical analysis of 'karr' in the proverb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether 'karr' functions as a verb or a verbal noun in an istithna' construction and determine how its meaning changes with the prepositions 'ala' and 'an'.

Core Idea
A word's grammatical form can change the entire logical meaning of a proverb.

This passage from the commentary on the Tahdhib shows how a seemingly simple word like 'karr' can be parsed in multiple ways. The commentator, Abd, clarifies that the word can be either a past tense verb or a verbal noun, and that its meaning depends on the preposition. Understanding this is crucial for correctly interpreting the logical argument in the proverb.

Check the preposition

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and lexical clarification of the word 'karr' in the proverb 'hal huwa illa karrun 'ala ma farra 'anhu?' (Is it but a return to what one fled from?). The commentator, Abd, explains that 'karr' is on the pattern of 'farr' (both are past tense verbs meaning 'returned' and 'fled' respectively). He then discusses whether 'karr' here is used as a verb or a verbal noun. If it is a verb, it can appear after 'illa' with the verb deleted, as in 'ma anta illa sayran' (you are only walking). If it is a verbal noun, then the construction is simpler. He questions the need for a lengthy grammatical analysis, suggesting that the correct understanding is that 'karr' is a verbal noun. He further notes that the preposition changes the meaning: 'karr 'ala' means 'turning towards' (attack), while 'karr 'an' means 'returning from'. Therefore, if the proverb were 'karrun 'ala ma karrra 'anhu', it would mean 'a turning towards that from which one returned', which is more precise.
