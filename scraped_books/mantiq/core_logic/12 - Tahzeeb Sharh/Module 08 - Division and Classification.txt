Grammatical Options for 'Huda' in the Matn
Author examines grammatical options for 'huda' and their logical conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three grammatical interpretations of 'huda' (object of purpose, hal, or exaggerated adjective) and assess the logical-temporal conditions for each using evidence from the Qur'an and Arabic usage.

Core Idea
A single Arabic word can bear multiple grammatical readings, each with distinct temporal and logical implications.

The word 'huda' in the matn is not a simple gloss; it invites multiple grammatical analyses. The commentator presents three possibilities: as an object of purpose (maf'ul li-ajlih), a circumstantial accusative (hal), or an adjective of exaggeration. Each reading changes the meaning and requires specific conditions to be valid. Understanding these conditions is a core skill in parsing Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three grammatical candidates
Find the three options the commentator lists for 'huda': (1) object of purpose for 'arsalahu', (2) circumstantial accusative from the subject or object, (3) adjective with exaggeration like 'Zayd is justice'.
The Arabic uses technical terms: مَفْعُول لَهُ, حَال, and مبالغة.
2
Examine the temporal condition for object of purpose
Object of purpose usually requires that the purpose exists at the time of the action. Here, guidance occurs after sending, so there is a potential conflict. Note the objection raised: 'the time of guidance is after the time of sending'.
The objection is framed as a conditional: if lam is estimated, the times must align.
3
Analyze the refutation using Islamic examples
The commentator refutes the temporal condition by citing Qur'an 16:8 (horses for riding and adornment) and common speech ('I drank medicine for reform'). In both, the purpose exists after the action. Apply the same logic to 'huda' to show estimation of lam is valid.
These examples are from wahy (revelation) and 'urf (custom), both authoritative in usul.
4
Evaluate the hal interpretation
If 'huda' is a hal, it must be predicated of the 'owner of the state'. The infinitive cannot be predicated, so 'huda' must mean 'guide' (hadi). This is a linguistic metaphor. Note the resulting problem: it implies the Prophet was a guide at the moment of sending, but guidance occurs after.
The commentator flags this as a flaw: 'it would be understood that he is a guide at the time of sending, whereas he is a guide after it'.
5
Assess the exaggerated adjective option
The third option treats 'huda' as an adjective of exaggeration, like 'Zayd is justice'. This avoids the temporal mismatch and is deemed preferable by the commentator, especially when combined with mental metaphor.
The commentator says 'it is preferable, because the metaphor then is mental' (majaz 'aqli).

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical and rhetorical analysis of the word 'huda' (guidance) in the matn phrase 'هدى، هو بالاهتداء حقيق'. The commentator first offers several grammatical possibilities for 'huda': (1) it could be an object of purpose (maf'ul li-ajlih) meaning 'He sent him for guidance'; (2) it could be a circumstantial accusative (hal) from the subject (Allah) or object (the Prophet); (3) it could be a predicate adjective with exaggeration (as in 'Zayd is justice' meaning 'Zayd is just'). Then he discusses the phrase 'هو بالاهتداء حقيق': 'he is deserving of being guided' — this is a passive infinitive meaning 'that he be guided by him'. He then addresses a potential objection about the timing of the purpose: if 'huda' is an object of purpose, the guidance must exist after sending, but the object of purpose usually requires simultaneity. He refutes this with two examples from the Qur'an and common speech: (a) 'horses, mules, donkeys for you to ride and as adornment' — the adornment did not exist at creation but is an intended future purpose; (b) 'I drank medicine for the reform of the body' — reform occurs after drinking. Then he moves to the interpretation of 'huda' as a hal (circumstantial accusative). In that case, 'huda' must be understood as 'guide' (active participle) because a hal is a predicate of the owner of the state, and an infinitive cannot be predicated. This would be a linguistic metaphor (using the infinitive for the active participle). However, this leads to a problem: it would imply the Prophet was a guide at the time of sending, whereas his guidance occurred after he was sent. To solve this, the commentator introduces mental metaphor (majaz 'aqli), where the attribute is attributed to something indirectly. The preferred interpretation is that 'huda' is used as an exaggeration, and the attribution is mental, meaning the guidance is attributed to the Prophet as a guide, not as a guided one. He concludes that the expression 'اهتدى به' (he is guided by him) uses a passive construction that actually implies active guidance through a transitive preposition. This passage demonstrates the precision of Islamic grammatical and rhetorical analysis in interpreting sacred texts.

Metaphor Types in the Interpretation of 'Huda'
Author distinguishes metaphor types to resolve temporal issues.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between linguistic metaphor (majaz lughawi) and mental metaphor (majaz 'aqli) and explain why the commentator prefers the exaggerated reading with mental metaphor for 'huda'.

Core Idea
Mental metaphor (majaz 'aqli) resolves temporal discrepancies more elegantly than linguistic metaphor.

When 'huda' is taken as a circumstantial accusative, it must be metaphorically extended from an infinitive to an active participle (linguistic metaphor). However, this creates a problem: it implies the Prophet was a guide at the moment of sending, whereas his guidance occurred after. The commentator solves this by proposing an exaggerated reading with mental metaphor, which attributes the guidance directly to the Prophet as a guide. This lesson dissects the two types of metaphor and shows why mental metaphor is superior in this context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem with the hal interpretation
If 'huda' is a hal, it must mean 'guide' (hadi). But that would suggest the Prophet was guiding at the very moment of his sending, which is historically and logically problematic since his guidance unfolded after the revelation.
The commentator says: 'it would be understood that he is a guide at the time of sending, whereas he is a guide after it'.
2
Understand linguistic metaphor (majaz lughawi)
Linguistic metaphor occurs when a word is used for something other than its original meaning, like using 'huda' (guidance) to mean 'guide'. The commentator calls this 'مجاز في الطرف' (metaphor in the term).
This is a typical metaphor where the word shifts from an event to the doer.
3
Recognize the insufficiency of linguistic metaphor here
Even with linguistic metaphor, the temporal mismatch remains: the metaphor would imply the Prophet was already a guide. The context requires a future attribution.
Hence the commentator says 'there is another metaphor that does not suit the context'.
4
Introduce mental metaphor (majaz 'aqli)
Mental metaphor shifts the attribution of an action or quality from its true agent to a related entity. For example, 'فلان بنى القصر' (so-and-so built the palace) even if he only ordered it. Here, 'huda' is attributed to the Prophet as the source of guidance (by exaggeration), not to Allah the true guide.
The commentator prefers mental metaphor because 'المجاز في النسبة أبلغ عن المجاز في الطرف' (metaphor in attribution is more eloquent).
5
Apply the exaggeration (mubalaghah) principle
The reading 'Zayd is justice' is a model: 'huda' is used as an exaggerated adjective, meaning 'he is guidance personified'. With mental metaphor, the attribution of guidance to the Prophet is a figure of speech that does not conflict with the timing of actual guidance.
The commentator concludes: 'it is preferable, because the metaphor then is mental'.

The preferred interpretation is that 'huda' is used as an exaggeration, and the attribution is mental, meaning the guidance is attributed to the Prophet as a guide, not as a guided one. He concludes that the expression 'اهتدى به' (he is guided by him) uses a passive construction that actually implies active guidance through a transitive preposition. This passage demonstrates the precision of Islamic grammatical and rhetorical analysis in interpreting sacred texts.

Disparity and the Shared Term in Singular Terms
Author explains the second division of the singular term, covering disparity and the shared term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the ambiguous universal (المشكك) and the shared term (المشترك) based on the conditions of disparity and initial separate placement.

Core Idea
The ambiguous universal varies in application; the shared term is a word with multiple initial placements.

This passage from the Tahdhib al-Mantiq commentary explains two key concepts: the ambiguous universal (المشكك) where a term applies to individuals with disparity, and the shared term (المشترك) where a word is placed for multiple meanings from the beginning. Understanding the distinction is crucial for precise definition in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main concepts
The passage introduces التفاوت (disparity) and المشترك (shared term). Note that both are part of the second division of the singular term.

2

The commentator lists four ways a universal can be ambiguous: priority by causality, precedence by suitability, increase/decrease, and intensity/weakness. Example: 'existence' applies first to the Necessary Being, then to contingent.

A word is shared (مشترك) if it is placed for each meaning initially with a separate placement. Example: 'عين' for eye, gold, etc.
Transfer terms and general-placements like pronouns are excluded.

The passage excludes three cases: reality/metaphor, transferred terms (منقول), and general placements like 'هذا'.

Classifying Singular Terms: Transferred, Real, and Metaphorical
Author defines the three subdivisions of singular terms with multiple usages.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any singular term as transferred (منقول), real (حقيقة), or metaphorical (مجاز) based on its usage and original placement, and justify your classification using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Words shift meaning; the key is whether the original meaning is still readily understood without context.

This lesson unpacks a crucial classification in Islamic logic and language theory: how to handle singular terms that are used in multiple ways. The passage distinguishes between terms that have been so completely transferred to a new meaning that the original is forgotten (transferred), those that always retain their original meaning (real), and those that are used in a secondary sense without abandoning the first (metaphorical). Understanding this classification is essential for interpreting foundational texts in fiqh, usul, and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the word is placed for a meaning
The passage begins by restricting the discussion to words that are 'placed' (موضوع) for at least one meaning. If the word is not placed for any of its usages, it falls outside the division. So first, confirm that the word is a conventional term in the language.
This excludes words that are simply borrowed or occur without conventional assignment.
2
Check for fame in a second meaning
Examine whether the word is predominantly used in a secondary meaning such that the original meaning is no longer immediately understood without context. If so, it is 'transferred' (منقول). The original is 'abandoned' in the sense that context is needed to recall it.
Example given: 'salat' originally 'supplication', now predominantly 'ritual prayer'.
3
If not transferred, determine usage pattern
If the word is not famous in a second meaning and the original is still common, then look at the specific usage: when used in its original placed meaning, it is 'real' (حقيقة); when used in another meaning (not the original), it is 'metaphorical' (مجاز).
The word must be used sometimes in the original and sometimes in another meaning for this distinction to apply.
4
Confirm with the invented (مرتجل) subtype
The commentary adds that if the transfer was from a common noun to a proper noun without any suitability (e.g., 'Ja'far' originally a small river, then a person's name), it is 'invented' (مرتجل) but still falls under 'transferred' because it involves a shift from an original meaning.
This subtype confirms that transfer does not require similarity between meanings.

The Fourfold Classification of Single Words
Author classifies single word usages into mushtarak, manqul, haqiqa, and majaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given Islamic word as mushtarak, manqul, haqiqa, or majaz based on its usage history.

Core Idea
Word usage reveals whether it has one or multiple original meanings, or whether it was transferred.

In this lesson, we explore how Islamic scholars classify words based on their usage. The passage distinguishes between words that are shared (mushtarak), transferred (manqul), literal (haqiqa), and metaphorical (majaz). Understanding this classification is crucial for accurate interpretation of legal and theological texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word's original meaning
Determine what the word originally meant in the Arabic language. For example, 'salat' originally meant 'supplication'.
2
Check if it has multiple original meanings
If the word was assigned to more than one meaning from the beginning, it is mushtarak. Example: 'ayn' can mean eye, gold, essence, or knee.
3
Check if the original meaning is abandoned
If the word is now commonly used in a different meaning and the original is not instantly understood without context, it is manqul. Example: 'salat' is now primarily understood as ritual prayer, not supplication.
4
Distinguish haqiqa and majaz
If the original meaning is still used, when used in its original sense it is haqiqa (literal), and when used in a transferred sense it is majaz (metaphorical). Example: 'asad' used for the predatory animal is haqiqa; for a brave man it is majaz.

Commentary
This passage classifies single words (mufrad) based on their usage and meaning. The author begins by stating the second division of the single word. He identifies four categories: mushtarak (shared), manqul (transferred), haqiqa (literal), and majaz (metaphorical). Mushtarak refers to a word that has multiple original meanings from the beginning, like 'ayn' which can mean eye, gold, essence, or knee. Manqul is a word that originally had one meaning but was transferred to another, so much so that the original meaning is no longer immediately understood without context. If the original is still used alongside the new, then the word is haqiqa when used in its original sense and majaz when used in the transferred sense. The passage then addresses objections and explanations regarding the definition of manqul, particularly the necessity of a transmitter (naqil). The transmitter can be the religious law (shar'), common custom ('urf), or technical convention (istilah), giving examples for each: 'salat' for shar'i, 'dabba' for 'urfi, and 'kalima' for istilahi. The commentary also provides etymologies of 'haqiqa' and 'majaz' and discusses the concept of 'murtajal' (coined) as a subset of manqul.

Manqul and Its Subtypes: Shar'i, 'Urfi, Istilahi
Author explains types of manqul and their transmitters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given transferred word (manqul) is shar'i, 'urfi, or istilahi based on the transmitter and explain the condition of 'abandonment of original meaning'.

Core Idea
Every transferred word requires a transmitter: lawgiver, common custom, or technical custom.

In the previous lesson, we learned the overall classification. Here we focus on manqul (transferred words), which require a specific transmitter (naqil). The passage details three types: shar'i (religious law), 'urfi (common custom), and istilahi (technical convention). Understanding these subtypes is essential for interpreting terms in fiqh and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Confirm the word is manqul
Ensure the word originally had one meaning that is now abandoned in favor of a new meaning that comes to mind first. Example: 'salat' originally supplication, now ritual prayer.
The passage clarifies that abandonment is relative to context: without context, the new meaning is understood; with context, the original might still be used.
2
Identify the transmitter (naqil)
Determine who or what caused the transfer: the Lawgiver (shar'), common people ('urf), or specialists (istilah). The passage gives examples: salat (shar'i), dabba ('urfi), kalima (istilahi).
3
Classify the subtype
Based on the transmitter, label the word as manqul shar'i, manqul 'urfi, or istilahi. The passage also notes that murtajal (coined terms without appropriateness, like Ja'far) fall under manqul.

Haqiqa and Majaz: Literal and Metaphorical Usage
Author defines haqiqa and majaz and provides etymologies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqa (literal) and majaz (metaphorical) usage of a word and trace the etymological roots of these terms as given in the passage.

Core Idea
Haqiqa is a word used in its original assigned meaning; majaz is used in a transferred meaning without abandoning the original.

When a word retains its original meaning and is not abandoned, it can be used either in its literal sense (haqiqa) or in a metaphorical sense (majaz). The passage provides etymologies for these two terms and explains how they function in logical analysis. This lesson will help you identify haqiqa and majaz in Islamic texts and understand their linguistic foundations.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the word has a single original meaning
If the word was not originally shared (mushtarak) and has not been transferred (manqul), then it has one original meaning. Check if the original meaning is still in common use.
2
Identify the usage context
If the word is used in its original meaning, it is haqiqa. If it is used in a different meaning that is not the original, it is majaz. Example: 'asad' for the predatory animal is haqiqa; for a brave man it is majaz.
The passage uses the example of 'asad' to illustrate.
3
Analyze the etymology of 'haqiqa'
The passage derives haqiqa from either 'thabat' (establishment) or 'ta'yin' (determination), indicating that the word is established in its original place.
4
Analyze the etymology of 'majaz'
Majaz is derived from 'jawaz' (passing), indicating that the word has passed from its original meaning to another; it is a place of passing.

The Role of Farḍ in Defining the Particular

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the logician uses farḍ (mental assumption) rather than actual truth in the definition of the particular, and distinguish between farḍ and taqdīr.

Core Idea
Farḍ prevents God from being a particular and allows accidental universals.

The commentary explains a critical terminological choice: the matn uses farḍ (mental assumption) instead of actual truth. This subtle shift has major implications for which concepts count as universals. We will unpack the reasoning.

The First Two Relations Between Universals: Divergence and Equality
Commentary explains the first two relations between universals and why they are taught first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of universals as divergent or equal based on their extension, and explain why the author placed relations before the five universals.

Core Idea
Two universals are either divergent (no common individuals) or equal (same individuals) – and these relations are foundational for the five-universals classification.

The author of our matn placed the study of relations between universals before the study of the five universals themselves. Why? Because the five universals are each divergent from the others, and a key universal – species – has two meanings that overlap in a specific way. In this lesson, we dive into the first two relations: divergence and equality. Understanding these basic relations will equip you to see how the five universals are distinct and how they interrelate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two relations defined in the matn
Locate the Arabic terms for 'divergent' (متباينان) and 'equal' (متساويان). Note the condition: divergence occurs when ‘two universals wholly differ’ in their individuals; equality occurs when they ‘coincide completely from both sides’ (same set of individuals).
The matn only gives these two relations; the remaining two (generality/specificity one-way and mutual) are implied but not stated here.
2
Understand why relations are studied before the five universals
The commentary states: 'the author placed the explanation of relations before the explanation of the five universals because we claim in the division of the five universals that each of the five divisions is divergent from the other.' This means the five universals (genus, species, difference, property, accident) are each mutually exclusive classes – which is itself a relation of divergence.
Also, species (النوع) has two meanings with a 'generality in one respect' relation, which requires prior understanding of overlapping relations.
3
Apply the definitions to a concrete Islamic example
Consider the universals 'wajib' (obligatory) and 'mubah' (permissible) in Usul al-Fiqh. Are they divergent or equal? They are divergent: no action can be both wajib and mubah in the same situation. Contrast with 'human' and 'rational animal', which are equal (same individuals).
This shows how the tool works in real fiqh reasoning – avoiding contradictions.

Similarly, six examples for the disjunctive (munfaṣila) are given, mirroring the same combinations. The total images of difference (where the two parts are different in type) are said to be twelve (six for each kind). Then, inside each kind, there are three images of agreement (where both parts are of the same type: both categorical, both conjunctive, or both disjunctive). This gives 18 total images. Each image can be either affirmative (mūjaba) or negative (sāliba), yielding 36 propositional forms. The passage ends with an instruction to the reader to derive examples for all these forms, emphasizing careful reflection (tadabbur). This classification is foundational for understanding the structure of conditional syllogisms (qiyās istithnā'ī).

This passage defines the three essential parts of any science. The second, if the student acquiesces to it due to his good opinion of the teacher, is called postulated principles; if he takes it with disapproval, it is called begging the question. His statement: 'subject of the science' like their statement in natural science: 'Every body has a natural shape.' His statement: 'or an essential accident' like their statement: 'Every moving thing has an inclination.' His statement: 'or a composite of the subject with the essential accident' like the engineer's statement: 'Every mean proportional is the side of what is enclosed by the two extremes.' They are well-known sciences: as for them being sciences, because what is meant by premises are evident assents, and them being sciences is apparent because assent is a division of knowledge. As for the premises themselves, they are propositions, and it has been established in its place that the proposition is known, and acquiescence here is knowledge and assent, and the difference between assent and proposition is by knowledge and the known. As for them being well-known, due to the fame of their knowledge because of their self-evidence. It is called begging the question because by it the problems are produced that depend on verification by evidences first, then from them the syllogisms of the science are composed. 'In natural science' i.e. in the science investigating the natural body, and it has been made a subject in this problem, namely 'Every body has a natural shape.' So it has a natural shape, i.e. a shape required by the specific nature, and the natural body is a substance receptive to division in three dimensions, and its essential accident is motion and rest. 'Natural shape': i.e. a shape that accrues to it from its essence and nature, not by consideration of an external thing from its essence like the agent and others. (Isma'il) 'So it has an inclination': i.e. a natural inclination towards its center, and this problem is also from natural science, and its subject is motion which is essential to the subject, namely the natural body as has passed. Thus defined it by the Chief Master in his treatise on definitions. 'From the subject': meaning that his statement 'or a composite' is a universal under which are two individuals: one is the composite of the subject of the science and its essential accident, and the second is the composite of its type and its essential accident. As for the composite of the subject of the science and its type, it is the type itself included in his statement 'or a type of it', because its type is that subject with the specific differentia, and it is excluded from his statement 'or a composite' by the context of opposition. So understand. 'Every mean proportional etc.': Know that the subject of the science of geometry is magnitude, and its being mean in proportion is an essential accident, and magnitude is an accident receptive to division. The meaning of magnitude being mean in proportion according to the geometers is its being between two magnitudes such that the ratio of that mean magnitude to one of those two magnitudes is like the ratio of the other magnitude to that mean magnitude, like four between two and eight; for it is half of eight just as two is half of it, or it is said that eight is double of four just as four is double of two. And the meaning of the mean magnitude being a side of what the two extremes enclose is that the product of the magnitude with itself is like the product of one of the extremes with the other; for the product of four with itself is sixteen, just as the product of two with eight and vice versa is also sixteen. 'Either right angles' because the line standing on the transverse line is either straight or curved. If it is straight, it produces on its sides two right angles; if it is curved, it also produces on its sides two angles: one acute and the other obtuse, but they are equal to the two right angles, as is not hidden. 'And what is meant here etc.' is the answer to an implied question that the concomitant, i.e. the accident in the sense of the external predicate, like 'writer' for man, so the author's statement 'concomitant' alone suffices, and his statement 'external' is superfluous. The summary of the answer: what is meant here by 'concomitant' is the predicate only, by way of abstraction, and that is permissible. It is restricted by his statement 'external' to guard against the subsistence of the essential for the essence. (Burhan) 'For the accident etc.' is the cause for what is meant by 'concomitant' being only predicate; because the concomitant in the sense of the accident is the external predicate, meaning a combination of two things: one is external to the subject, and the second is predicated on the subject. So what is not predicated is not called an accident either, like a stone in relation to man: it is external but not predicated on him, so it is not said of the stone that it is an accident of man. Since the author said 'things external to them' before his statement 'concomitant', he has mentioned externality, so his statement 'concomitant' then remains in the sense of predicate. If it is said: it should be transitive with 'ala, not with 'lam', we say: the meaning is 'lāḥiqatun ʿalayhā' [concomitant upon them] for their essences, and the two pronouns refer to the subject matters. His statement 'for their essences' is an explanation of the cause of occurrence and predication, an explanation of what the predicates are predicated on, as is not hidden. 'And if he had sufficed etc.' is an objection, and its clarification is clear. 'According to appearance' means if after 'qābil' [receiving] it includes all primary accidents, and the accident being by the equal is among the essential accidents by consensus.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn discussing the parts of the sciences. The commentator begins by stating what is sought in a science: the subject matter of the science, or a type of it, or an essential accident of it, or a composite of these. He then clarifies that these are well-known sciences. The second category—premises accepted on trust—are called 'postulated principles' if the student accepts them due to good opinion of the teacher, and 'begging the question' if taken with disapproval. Notably, a single premise may be a postulate for one person and a begging for another, depending on the student's state of knowledge. The commentator then gives examples: in natural science, 'Every body has a natural shape' illustrates the subject of the science; 'Every moving thing has an inclination' illustrates an essential accident; and in geometry, 'Every mean proportional is the side of what is enclosed by the two extremes' illustrates a composite of subject and essential accident. He then explains each example in detail. For 'Every body has a natural shape', he explains that the natural body is a substance with essential accidents like motion and rest, and the shape is natural in that it follows from its essence. For 'inclination', he defines it as a quality causing resistance to motion. For the geometrical example, he elaborates on proportion and the meaning of 'mean proportional', using the numbers 2, 4, 8 to illustrate. The commentator then discusses the notion of 'concomitant' (lāḥiq) and 'accident' (ʿāriḍ). He clarifies that in this context, 'concomitant' means that which is predicated on the subject, but the qualification 'external' is added to distinguish it from essential attributes that are intrinsic to the essence. He argues that the author's use of 'concomitant' alone would suffice, but the addition of 'external' guards against mistaking essential attributes for accidents. Finally, he touches on the phrase 'for their essences' indicating that accidents accrue to the essence itself, not through an external cause.

Concomitant and Accident: External Predicates
Commentator explains the meaning of 'concomitant' and 'accident' and their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between a concomitant (لاحق) that is an external predicate and an essential attribute, and identify examples of each from the passage.

Core Idea
A concomitant is a predicate that is external to the subject but not essential.

The commentator discusses the terms 'concomitant' and 'accident', clarifying that they refer to attributes that are predicated on the subject but are not part of its essence. He distinguishes these from essential attributes, which are intrinsic. For example, 'writer' is an accident of man, but 'rationality' is essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the predicate being discussed
Find the attribute or term that is being called 'concomitant' or 'accident'. E.g., 'writer' for man.
2
Check if the predicate is essential or external
Determine if the attribute is part of the essence (quiddity) of the subject or external. Essential attributes are intrinsic; external attributes are not.
The commentator uses 'external predicate' (الخارج المحمول) as the definition.
3
Verify if the predicate is actually predicated on the subject
The attribute must be something that can be said of the subject. For example, 'stone' is not predicated of 'man', so it is not an accident of man.
This condition excludes purely external things that are not predicated.
4
Understand the commentator's refinement
The author's use of 'concomitant' alone suffices, but adding 'external' guards against mistaking essential attributes for accidents.
Essential attributes also accompany the subject, but they are not external.

The Three Branches of Theoretical Wisdom
Author classifies theoretical wisdom into three based on matter dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three subdivisions of theoretical wisdom based on their relation to matter: Ilahi (no matter), Riyadi (matter only in intellect), and Tabi'i (matter in both existence and intellect).

Core Idea
Theoretical wisdom is ordered by how much a thing depends on matter: from none (God) to fully (man).

The author presents the three branches of theoretical wisdom according to their dependence on matter. This is a fundamental classification in Islamic philosophy. The degree of material dependence determines the abstraction level of each science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of theoretical wisdom
The text lists: (1) Ilahi (divine science) – what does not depend on matter in existence or intellection, like God; (2) Riyadi (mathematics) – what does not depend on matter in existence but does in intellection, like the sphere (geometric shapes); (3) Tabi'i (natural science) – what depends on matter in both existence and intellection, like man.
These are sometimes called the 'three philosophies' in the Islamic tradition.
2
Understand the criterion: dependence on matter
The author uses two dimensions: existence (wujud) and intellection (ta'aqqul). Some things exist without matter (e.g., God), others exist in matter but we can conceive them abstractly (e.g., circle), and others are always material (e.g., a human body).
'Intellection' here means conceptualization – can we understand the thing without imagining it in matter?
3
Apply the criterion to examples
Angels: created from light, but do they have matter? Traditionally, angels are immaterial, so they fall under Ilahi. Numbers: they exist as concepts but can be abstracted, so Riyadi. Animals: they are material and understood as material, so Tabi'i.
This shows the hierarchy of abstraction: Ilahi is most abstract, Tabi'i is most concrete.

The Roots and Branches of the Divine Science (Ilahi)
Meibudi elaborates on Ilahi's roots and branches.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five usul (roots) and two furu' (branches) of Ilahi as presented by the commentator Meibudi, and relate them to the overall structure of metaphysics.

Core Idea
Ilahi is not a monolithic subject; it has foundational principles (usul) and derivative inquiries (furu').

After enumerating the three divisions of theoretical wisdom, the passage expands on Ilahi, the divine science. The commentator Meibudi provides a detailed structure: five roots that form the groundwork, and two branches that address the soul and the afterlife. This shows how Islamic philosophers organized metaphysical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
List the five usul (roots) of Ilahi
From the text: 1) General matters (al-umur al-'amma) – covering concepts like existence, unity, causality; 2) Proving the Necessary Being and His attributes; 3) Spiritual substances (al-jawahir al-ruhaniyya) – angels, intellects, souls; 4) Explaining the connections of the earthly with the vegetative power (probably the relation between celestial and terrestrial realms); 5) Explaining the system of possibles (system of contingent beings).
These five correspond to classical metaphysical topics in Ibn Sina's Ilahiyyat.
2
List the two furu' (branches) of Ilahi
The text states: 1) The investigation of the nature of the spirit (ruh), including the definition of the human spirit (al-ruh al-insani) and the trustworthy spirit (al-ruh al-amin, likely Gabriel); 2) The science of spiritual return (al-ma'ad al-ruhani), which is the afterlife.
The passage notes this follows what the Shaykh (Ibn Sina) mentioned in his treatise 'Division of Wisdom'.
3
Understand the relationship between usul and furu'
The usul establish the foundations (general metaphysics, God, spiritual substances, world connections), while the furu' derive specific consequences about the human soul and its destiny. This structure is logical: after proving God and spiritual substances, one can study the soul and its return.
This reflects the typical order of Ibn Sina's Ilahiyyat: first general metaphysics, then God, then spiritual beings, then the soul, then eschatology.

And each of them has three divisions. As for practical wisdom: it is either knowledge of the interests of a group sharing in civil society, called political science. As for theoretical wisdom: it is either knowledge of the states of what does not need matter in external existence and in intellection, like the Divine, and it is called the highest science, divine science, first philosophy, universal science, and metaphysics, and it is also rarely called pre-physics; or knowledge of the states of what does not need matter in external existence but does not need it in intellection, like the celestial sphere: it is the middle science, called mathematics and instructional; or knowledge of the states of what needs matter in external existence and intellection, like man: it is the first science, called natural science. (Maybudi) The branches of divine science: And divine science is knowledge of the states of what does not need matter in external existence and intellection, like the Divine. Its principles are five: first: general matters; second: establishing the Necessary and what befits Him; third: spiritual substances; fourth: explanation of the connections of earthly things with the vegetative force; fifth: explanation of the system of contingents. And its branches are two: the first of them is investigation of the nature of the spirit, including definition of the human spirit, and the trustworthy spirit. The second: the science of spiritual resurrection, as the Shaykh mentioned in his treatise 'Division of Wisdom'. (Tuhfa)

Commentary
This passage is from the epilogue of the book, discussing the classification of sciences and the place of logic. The author begins by clarifying that logic is not concerned with external existents as they are in themselves, but with mental concepts and propositions that lead to conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). Hence logic is part of wisdom (hikma) if wisdom is defined broadly as knowledge of existents. The author then distinguishes practical wisdom (concerned with human actions) from theoretical wisdom (concerned with what is beyond human volition). Theoretical wisdom is further divided into three: divine science (al-ilahi), mathematics (al-riyadi), and natural science (al-tabi'i). Each is defined by its object's dependence on matter: divine science deals with what is immaterial in existence and intellection (e.g., God), mathematics with what is immaterial but requires matter in existence (e.g., geometric shapes), and natural science with what requires matter in both (e.g., living beings). The passage then elaborates on divine science, listing its five principles and two branches (spirit and resurrection). This classification situates logic as a tool for the theoretical sciences, specifically dealing with mental existents. The commentary by Isma'il and Maybudi clarifies terminology. The key takeaway is that logic is not a science of external reality but of mental reasoning, yet it is essential for achieving true knowledge in the theoretical sciences.

Commentary
This passage continues the epilogue on the parts of sciences, focusing on three remaining points: the rank of a science, its division into chapters, and the educational methods for teaching. For the sixth point, the author explains that each science has a proper rank in the order of learning: logic should be studied after character refinement and some geometry, but in the current era (of the commentator), it is better to delay logic until after literary sciences because writings are in Arabic. The seventh point concerns dividing a science and a book into proper chapters. The author enumerates the nine (or ten) standard chapters of logic: Isagoge (five universals), definitions, propositions, syllogism, demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, sophistry, poetry, and optionally the study of expressions. He also describes the structure of the present book: first part on logic (introduction, two purposes, conclusion) and second on theology. The eighth point discusses educational methods, specifically the division (taqsim) which also includes composition of syllogism (tarkib al-qiyas). The author notes a historical practice of teaching geometry and ethics first, then logic, but recommends the order: Quran memorization, then Arabic morphology and grammar (the two fathers of sciences), then necessary fiqh, hadith, and finally logic (the art of the balance). This advice reflects a pedagogical sensitivity to the student's linguistic and religious foundation.
