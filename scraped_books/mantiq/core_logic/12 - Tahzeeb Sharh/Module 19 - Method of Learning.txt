The Three Parts of Every Science
States the three essential parts of any science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three structural parts (subject, problems, principles) of any Islamic science and identify examples from actual disciplines like kalam or usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Every science has a subject, problems, and principles — the skeleton of all Islamic disciplines.

This passage, labeled a conclusion, packs a lot: it names the three essential parts of any science and defines the subject and problems. The commentary expands on each term, clarifying that problems include both theoretical questions and hidden axioms. Even the critique of later logicians serves to remind us to focus on what is foundational.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the three-part structure
The passage states that every codified science must have three things: a subject (mawdu'), problems (masa'il), and principles (mabadi'). List them in order.
Principles are not elaborated in this excerpt but are understood as foundational premises.
2
Define the subject (al-mawdu')
The subject is the thing whose essential accidents are investigated; all research refers back to it. For example, in kalam the subject is the existence and attributes of Allah and related matters.
Essential accidents are properties that necessarily belong to the subject.
3
Define the problems (al-masa'il)
Problems are the propositions investigated in the science. The commentary says they are mostly theoretical but can be axioms needing a reminder (tanbih). For instance, in usul al-fiqh, 'Is qiyas a valid source?' is a problem.
Even hidden axioms count as problems because they are sought, albeit not by demonstration.
4
Understand the principles (al-mabadi')
Principles are foundational premises assumed or proven in another science. For example, 'Every temporally originated thing has a cause' is a principle used in kalam.
This part is not detailed in the passage but is essential.
5
Apply to an actual science
Take tafsir: its subject is the Quran, its problems are interpretations of specific verses, and its principles include rules of Arabic grammar and usul al-fiqh.
This exercise reinforces how the tripartite structure works in practice.

The source of the objection is the specification of seeking to demonstration, and the avoidance of the objection is based on its generality. 'Its interpretation': i.e., the interpretation of specifying seeking to demonstration as in some copies, on the basis that the specification is built on that since problems are theoretical and sought by demonstration, seeking is specified to demonstration as is predominant.

Commentary
This passage, labeled as a conclusion (khatima), discusses the essential parts of any science. It begins by stating that the parts of sciences are three: the subjects (mawdu'at), which are the things about which the science investigates essential accidents. The author then criticizes later scholars for spending too much time on conditional conjunctives (iqtiranat sharṭiyya) and their concomitants despite little benefit, recommending instead the books of the ancients, alluding to Ibn Sina's works 'Al-Shifa' and 'Al-Najah'. The commentary then expands on each part. The first part is the subject (mawdu'), which is the focal point of all investigations, and its essential accidents (a'rad dhatiyya). The second part is the problems (masa'il), which are the propositions investigated; they are usually theoretical but can be axioms that need a reminder (tanbih). The commentary discusses a variant reading where 'seeking' is specified to demonstration, but argues that 'seeking' covers both theoretical and axiomatic problems. The third part, though not explicitly mentioned in this excerpt, is presumably the principles (mabadi'), which are not detailed here. The commentary also defines terms like 'tadwin' (codification), 'khasa' (property), and 'bahth' (investigation). It clarifies that problems can be either theoretical (requiring demonstration) or hidden axioms (requiring a reminder), and that both are 'sought' in the science. The passage ends with a justification of the specification of seeking to demonstration as a predominant usage.

Truth is Known by Evidence, Not Authority
Author explains the principle that scholars evaluate statements by truth, not by status of speaker.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the verifiers' method of evaluating truth from the ignorant learners' method, and explain why truth is independent of the speaker's status.

Core Idea
Judge the statement, not the speaker – truth is known by evidence.

In Islamic scholarship, the highest rank belongs to those who evaluate statements based on their intrinsic truth, not the status of the speaker. This passage from the commentary on Tahdhib al-Mantiq contrasts two epistemic approaches: the verifiers (muhaqqiqun) who see truth clearly, and the ignorant learners who are swayed by reputation. Understanding this distinction protects against the fallacy of argument from authority and deepens your capacity for independent verification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups
The passage contrasts al-muhaqqiqun (the verifiers) with al-juhhal al-muta'allimun (the ignorant learners). Note the defining characteristic of each: verifiers know men by truth; the ignorant know truth by men.
This inversion of priorities is the core of the lesson.
2
Analyze the verifiers' method
For a verifier, if a statement is true and correct, they honor its speaker regardless of reputation; if it is ornamented falsehood, they dismiss the speaker even if he is highly esteemed. Truth is the sole criterion.
This reflects the principle of 'al-haqq ahaqq an yuttaba' (truth is more worthy of being followed).
3
Analyze the ignorant learners' method
The ignorant evaluate based on the speaker's fame: if famous for truthfulness, they accept even false statements; if famous as a liar, they reject even true statements. Their judgment is hostage to reputation.
This is the error of confusing the messenger with the message.
4
Understand al-Dawwani's refinement
Al-Dawwani explains that following the shaykh (the author of the matn) is a concession for the ignorant. Those who rise to perfection use the light of insight and ignore what is said or will be said – they see the truth directly.
This elevates independent verification (tahqiq) over blind taqlid.
5
Apply to real scholarship
Identify a situation in usul al-fiqh or kalam where a statement from a renowned scholar is evaluated. Would a muhaqqiq accept it without evidence? Would a jahil reject it from a less known scholar? Practise distinguishing the two approaches.
This step connects the lesson to actual Islamic sciences.

And as for the verifiers, they know men by the truth, not the truth by men. So if the statement is truthful and correct, they know that its speaker has a great rank in this science, and if the speech is ornamented falsehood, they know that its speaker is a worthless man, even if he is famous for high status and lofty position. As for the ignorant learners, they know the truth by men: if the speaker is famous for truthfulness and knowledge, they believe his statement is true even if it is actually false, and if he is a famous liar, they declare the statement false even if it is true in itself. The verifier al-Dawwani indicated this in his gloss on the text after quoting the Shaykh's words: 'We only followed the Shaykh's footsteps descending to the understanding of the ignorant who know truth by men. As for those who rise from the lowland of deficiency to the peak of perfection, they unveil the state of affairs with the light of insight and pay no attention to what is said or what will be said.'

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the principle that the verifiers (muhaqqiqun) judge statements based on truth, not the status of the speaker. The commentator distinguishes two types of people: the verifiers, who evaluate the statement itself—if it is true, they honor the speaker regardless of his reputation; if false, they dismiss him even if he is highly regarded. In contrast, the ignorant learners judge based on the speaker's reputation: if the speaker is known to be truthful, they accept even falsehoods; if known to be a liar, they reject even truths. The commentator then cites al-Dawwani, who explains that following the shaykh (the author of the matn) is a concession to the ignorant masses. The truly insightful, however, rely on their own vision and are not swayed by what is said or what will be said. This passage guards against the fallacy of argument from authority, emphasizing that truth is independent of personalities.
