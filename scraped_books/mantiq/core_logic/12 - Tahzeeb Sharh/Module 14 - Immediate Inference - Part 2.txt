Why Choose the Partial Negative in Contrapositive Conversion?
Justification for using partial negative in contrapositive conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to justify the use of the partial negative (juz'iyya) over the universal negative in the contrapositive conversion of temporal propositions using the principle of generality.

Core Idea
The partial is more general; if the more general is false, the more specific is false, ensuring necessary conversion.

In this lesson, we address a critical question: why did the commentator assume the partial negative and the general possible in the converse of temporal propositions? The answer reveals a deep logical principle about generality and necessary consequence. This lesson walks you through the proof (burhan) and its implications for modal logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
The questioner asks: why didn't the commentator use the universal negative as the converse of a universal negative temporal proposition?
The temporal proposition is like 'No moon is eclipsed at the time of quadrature'.
2
Identify the principle
The commentator responds: the partial (juz'iyya) is more general than the universal (kulliyya). If the more general is false, the more specific is false. But the converse must be true when the original is true. Using the universal could result in a false converse even when the original is true, violating necessary conversion.
The principle: عدم صدق الأعم يستلزم عدم صدق الأخص (lack of truth of the more general entails lack of truth of the more specific).
3
Apply the principle with an example
The author uses the animal/human example: negating 'animal' from something entails negating 'human', but not vice versa. Similarly, if the universal negative is false, the partial negative could still be true.
If 'Every animal is human' is false (more specific), 'Some animal is human' can still be true (more general).
4
Generalize to modal propositions
The same reasoning applies to choosing the general possible (mumkina 'amma) over the actual (fi'liyya): the general possible is more general and avoids counterexamples.
The commentator states: 'and we only chose the general possible in the converse so that there remains no place for the question'.

Commentary
This passage defines 'aks al-naqid (conversion by contrapositive) and explains why the partial negative and the general possible are chosen in the converse of temporal propositions. The author begins by giving two definitions: one according to the early scholars (qudama') and one according to the later scholars (muta'akhkhirun). The first definition states that contrapositive conversion involves exchanging the two contrapositives of the subject and predicate while preserving both truth and quality. The author then expands on this: the contrapositive of the first part (the subject) becomes the second part (the predicate) of the converse, and the contrapositive of the second part becomes the first part. Preserving truth means if the original is true, the converse is true. Preserving quality means the converse retains the affirmative or negative form of the original. An example is given: 'Every J is B' converts to 'Everything that is not B is not J'. 

The passage then addresses a potential objection: why did the commentator (the author of the sharh) assume a partial negative and a general possible in the converse of the temporal proposition (e.g., 'No moon is eclipsed at the time of quadrature')? The questioner argues that the converse of a universal negative should be a universal negative, not a partial. The author provides a proof (burhan) to justify this choice. The reasoning is that the partial (juz'iyya) is more general than the universal (kulliyya), and the general possible (mumkina 'amma) is more general than other modal propositions. If the more general (i.e., the partial) were false, then the more specific (the universal) would also be false. But the converse needs to be a necessary consequence of the original; if we choose the universal, it might be false while the partial could still be true, breaking the necessary conversion. The author uses an example from logic: negating 'animal' from something entails negating 'human', but the reverse is not true. Similarly, if the universal negative were chosen as the converse, it could be that the universal negative is false while the partial negative is true, so the conversion would not be absolute. Therefore, the partial is chosen to ensure that if the original is true, the converse is always true, avoiding counterexamples. The same reasoning applies to choosing the general possible over the actual. The passage concludes by noting that the definition of contrapositive includes preserving truth because it is a necessary consequent of the original.

Defining 'Aks al-Naqid: Ancient and Later Methods
Author defines 'aks al-naqid with ancient and later methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the ancient and later definitions of 'aks al-naqid and apply both methods to convert categorical propositions.

Core Idea
In 'aks al-naqid, ancients preserve quality; later logicians reverse it but both preserve truth.

This lesson introduces 'aks al-naqid (conversion by contraposition), an immediate inference that transforms a categorical proposition into its contrapositive. You will learn the definitions given by ancient and later logicians, with examples using the variables J and B. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying contraposition in Islamic legal analogy and theological argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Locate the definitions of 'aks al-naqid given for the ancients (qudama') and the later scholars (muta'akhkhirun). The ancient definition preserves truth and quality; the later definition changes quality.
2
Understand the key difference
Note that in the ancient method, the quality (affirmative/negative) of the original is preserved in the converse. In the later method, the quality is reversed: affirmative becomes negative and vice versa.
3
Apply the ancient method
Take the proposition 'Every J is B'. Following the ancient method, the contrapositive is 'Every non-B is non-J'. Verify that if the original is true, the contrapositive is also true.
4
Apply the later method
Take 'Every J is B'. The later method gives 'Nothing that is not B is J'. Notice the quality changes from affirmative to negative.
5
Check preservation of truth
Both methods require that if the original is true, the contrapositive is true. The passage notes that preservation of falsity is not required—a false original can have a true contrapositive.

Rulings of Contraposition: Affirmatives, Negatives, and Exceptions
Author states the parallel and details conversion rules with proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of contraposition to categorical and conditional propositions, identify exceptions for all-inclusive concepts, and verify the validity of a contraposition using the provided reductio proof.

Core Idea
Contraposition mirrors direct conversion: universal affirmatives convert to themselves; particular affirmatives rarely convert, with exceptions for all-inclusive concepts.

This lesson unpacks the rules of 'aks al-naqid (conversion by contraposition). We begin with the foundational parallel between affirmatives in contraposition and negatives in direct conversion, then explore detailed conversion patterns for various types of categorical and conditional propositions. We also examine an important exception raised by later logicians: all-inclusive concepts like 'thing' and 'general possible.' Finally, we walk through the logical proof that validates the contrapositive of a universal affirmative.

Guided Path
1
State the parallel rule
The passage opens with: 'The ruling of affirmatives here (in contraposition) is the ruling of negatives in direct conversion.' Understand that universal affirmatives become universal affirmatives in contraposition (preserving truth), while particular affirmatives do not convert (or rarely).
This parallels universal negatives converting to themselves in direct conversion, and particular negatives not converting.
2
Examine the exception for all-inclusive concepts
Later scholars noted that propositions with predicates like 'thing' (shay') cause the contrapositive to be false because the subject of the contrapositive may be empty. For example, 'Every man is a thing' has the contrapositive 'Every non-thing is non-man', which is false because 'non-thing' has no instances.
The author responds by restricting the rule to ordinary terms, not all-inclusive concepts.
3
Apply the conversion taxonomy to categorical propositions
The commentary lists how various types of categorical propositions convert by contraposition: perpetuals to perpetuals, commons to commons, specifics to specifics (with conditions), temporals/existentials to particular absolutes, possibles do not convert.
Focus on the overall logic: universal affirmatives convert to universal affirmatives, particular affirmatives do not convert, negatives convert to particular affirmatives.
4
Extend to conditional propositions
Universal conditional affirmatives also convert to themselves in contraposition; particular conditionals do not convert; conditional negatives convert to particular conditionals.
This shows the system extends beyond categoricals.
5
Verify the proof for universal affirmative contraposition
The passage provides a reductio: If the contrapositive of 'Every J is B' (i.e., 'Every non-B is non-J') were false, then 'Some non-B is not non-J' would be true, implying 'Some non-B is J'. Combining with the original yields 'Some non-B is B', a contradiction.
This indirect proof confirms the validity of contraposition for universal affirmatives.

Author states the basic rule of contraposition by analogy to simple conversion.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contraposition mirrors simple conversion but swaps negations.

Hence, contrapositive conversion fails for the possible.

Contraposition preserves truth for the particular negative under modal conditions.

Guided Path

Identify the original proposition

2
State the desired contrapositive
The contrapositive is: 'Some B is not J' under the same modal condition. Note that both subject and predicate are swapped and negated (simple conversion) but the proposition remains particular negative.

Assume an individual (e.g., Zayd) that is a member of 'some J'. Since the original is true, Zayd is not B in some time. The supposition method is central: pick a concrete instance.

Contrapositive of the Particular Affirmative to the Special Conditional
Author proves contrapositive of particular affirmative to special conditional

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to prove the contrapositive of a particular affirmative proposition to a special conditional using hypothetical assumption.

Core Idea
The contrapositive of a particular affirmative is not a simple universal but a non-permanent conditional.

This passage from the Tahdhib al-Sharh presents a rigorous proof of a subtle logical conversion: the contrapositive ('aks al-naqid) of a particular affirmative proposition yields a special conditional (al-'urfiyya al-khassa) that is not always true. The author uses a hypothetical assumption (ifttiraad) of an individual to derive the result. Understanding this proof deepens your grasp of immediate inference in the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its intended contrapositive
Find the original proposition: 'Some J is B as long as it is J, not always' (i.e., some J is actually not B). The contrapositive is: 'Some of what is not B is not J as long as it is not B, not always' (i.e., it is not the case that some not-B is not J actually). Note that the contrapositive is a special conditional.
2
Understand the method of hypothetical assumption (ifttiraad)
The author assumes an individual D that is J actually and not B actually. This is a standard technique in Islamic logic to instantiate existential claims with a concrete example.
3
Derive the second part of the contrapositive
From the assumption, we get that some not-B is J actually (the necessary condition). Since affirmation necessitates negation of negation, we deduce the truth of the contrapositive's second part.
4
Derive the first part using a reductio
Using the concrete example of Zayd (a moving finger who is not a writer), suppose the first part is false. Then Zayd would be a moving finger at some time when he is not a writer, leading to a contradiction with the coexistence rule. Therefore, the first part is true.
5
Address the gloss's objection
The objection is that the coexistence rule applies only to affirmative descriptions, but here we have a negation. The answer: the negation is privative (salb 'aduli), which counts as a description, so the rule holds.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof of the contrapositive of a particular affirmative proposition to a special conditional. The author begins by stating that the contrapositive is established in two parts. He then explains the conversion rule: if 'Some J is B, always but not always' (i.e., some J is actually not B) is true, then its contrapositive is 'Some of what is not B is not J, always but not always' (i.e., it is not the case that some not-B is not J actually). The proof uses a hypothetical assumption (ifttiraad): assume an individual, D, that is J actually (according to the verified doctrine of the Shaykh) and also not B actually (from the non-permanence of the original). From this, we derive that some not-B is J actually, which is the necessary condition of the non-permanent converse. Then the author establishes the second part of the contrapositive by showing that the necessary condition yields the conclusion. For the first part, he uses a concrete example: 'Some moving fingers are a writer, always but not always'. He assumes Zayd as a moving finger who is not a writer. By a reductio argument, he shows that Zayd cannot be a moving finger at the same time as being not a writer, thus establishing the first part: 'Some not-writer is not moving fingers as long as it is not a writer'. The proof relies on the rule that if two descriptions coexist, each is affirmed in the time of the other. The final gloss addresses a potential objection: the rule seems to apply only to affirmative descriptions, but here we have a negation. The answer is that the negation here is privative (salb 'aduli), which counts as a description, preserving the rule.
