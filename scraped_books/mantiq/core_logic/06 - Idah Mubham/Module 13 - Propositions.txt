The Five Relations of Words to Meanings
Author enumerates the five types of word-meaning relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five word-meaning relations (tawatu', tashakuk, takhaluf, ishtirak, taraduf) and provide a correct example from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The five relations of words to meanings are the foundation for avoiding ambiguity in Islamic reasoning.

Words are the tools of Islamic thinking, but they can be slippery. A single word might carry many meanings, or multiple words might share one meaning. This passage from Idah al-Mubham lists five fundamental ways words relate to meanings. Mastering these five categories will sharpen your ability to avoid confusion when reading fiqh, kalam, or tafsir.

Guided Path
1
Identify Tawatu' (Univocality)
Find where one word applies to many instances equally. Example: 'insan' (human) refers to every human in the same sense.
Contrast with tashakuk: here the meaning is identical across instances.
2
Identify Tashakuk (Amphiboly/Analogy)
Find where one word applies to many meanings that are related but not identical, with priority and posteriority. Example: 'wujud' (existence) said of God and creatures, but primarily of God.
The meanings share a relationship, unlike ishtirak.
3
Identify Takhaluf (Difference)
Find where a word has a single specific meaning, or where different words have different meanings. Example: 'hajar' (stone) refers only to a stone.
This is the default — one word, one meaning.
4
Identify Ishtirak (Homonymy)
Find where one word has multiple unrelated meanings. Example: 'ayn' can mean eye, spring, or coin.
The meanings are purely coincidental, no conceptual link.
5
Identify Taraduf (Synonymy)
Find where multiple words refer to the same meaning. Example: 'asad' and 'layth' both mean lion.
Opposite of ishtirak.

Commentary
This passage lists the five ways in which a word or words relate to their meanings. (2) Tashakuk (amphiboly/analogical): one word used for many meanings that are related but with priority and posteriority, e.g., 'existence' said of God and creatures analogically. (3) Takhaluf (difference): a word used for one specific meaning only (or words with different meanings). (4) Ishtirak (homonomy): one word used for multiple unrelated meanings, e.g., 'ayn can mean eye, spring, or coin. Its opposite is (5) Taraduf (synonymy): multiple words used for one meaning, e.g., 'asad' and 'layth' both meaning lion.

Classifying Words: Simple, Compound, Universal, Particular
Classifies terms by composition, universality, and essentiality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given term as simple or compound, universal or particular, and essential or accidental according to the classical logical framework.

Core Idea
Every term is simple or compound; simple terms are universal or particular; universals are essential or accidental.

Before we can build arguments, we must understand the building blocks: words. This passage lays out the fundamental classification of terms used in logic. You will learn to distinguish compound expressions like 'house door' from simple ones like 'lion', and then within simple terms, separate those that apply to many (universals) from those that apply to one (particulars). Finally, you will see that universals can be essential—part of the very nature of a thing—or accidental, like having a color.

Guided Path
1
Identify simple vs. compound
Look at the term. If part of it indicates part of the meaning of the whole, it is compound (murakkab). For example, 'house door' has 'door' indicating a part of the meaning. If not, it is simple (mufrad).
A single word like 'lion' is simple; a phrase like 'book of logic' is compound.
2
If simple, determine universal or particular
A simple term is universal (kulli) if it can be predicated of many individuals, like 'lion' can be said of Simba, Mufasa, etc. It is particular (juz'i) if it applies to only one, like 'Zayd' or 'this person'.
The passage gives 'asad' (lion) as an example of a universal.
3
If universal, classify as essential or accidental
A universal is essential (dhati) if it is part of the essence of its subject—what makes it what it is. It is accidental ('aradi) if it is outside the essence, like a property that can change. For example, 'animal' is essential for 'human'; 'white' is accidental.
The passage instructs: if it is inside the essence (indaraja), relate it to the essence; if it is an accident (kharaja), then it is accidental.

Third, universals are divided into dhati (essential) and 'aradi (accidental). An essential universal is one that is part of the essence of its subject; an accidental one is external to the essence.

Types of Statements: Request and Report
Classifies speech into request or report, and subdivides request.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any utterance as either a request (talab) or a report (khabar), and further identify the specific type of request (amr, du'a, iltimas) based on the speaker-addressee hierarchy.

Core Idea
Speech divides into request and report; requests are commands, supplications, or entreaties based on status.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational classification in Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh: every utterance is either a request (talab) or a report (khabar). Requests are further subdivided into three types depending on the hierarchical relationship between speaker and addressee. This schema is crucial for understanding the force of imperative statements in the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Look at the Arabic: 'وَاللَّفْظُ إِمَّا طَلَبٌ أَوْ خَبَرُ'. Utterances are either talab (request) or khabar (report). Understand the difference: talab requests an action; khabar states a fact that can be true or false.
This binary division is exhaustive – every utterance falls into one of these two.
2
Focus on the three types of talab
The text says 'وَأَوَّل ثَلاثَةٌ سَتُذكَـ ٣٦' – the first (talab) is three types: amr (command), du'a (supplication), and iltimas (entreaty). Memorize them based on the relative status: amr when speaker is superior, du'a when speaker is inferior, iltimas when equal.
The Arabic terms highlight the direction of authority.
3
Map conditions to each type
For each type, note the hierarchy: amr (استعلا – superiority), du'a (عكس – opposite, i.e., inferiority), iltimas (النَّساوِي – equality). Practice associating the term with the condition.
The text uses 'مع استعلا' for command, 'عكسه' for supplication, and 'في النساوي' for entreaty.
4
Apply with Islamic examples
Test with Quranic verses: 'أقيموا الصلاة' is amr from Allah (superior). 'ربنا اغفر لنا' is du'a from humans (inferior). A mutual request between two scholars is iltimas (equal).
This classification directly applies to usul al-fiqh when interpreting imperative verbs.
5
Understand its importance in usul al-fiqh
Jurists use this classification to determine whether a command implies obligation or just recommendation. A command from Allah (amr) creates a legal obligation; a supplication does not.
The classification is not merely grammatical but pragmatically decisive for rulings.

Distinguishing Whole, Universal, Part, and Particular
Defines the logical concepts of whole, universal, part, and particular

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between kull (whole) and kulliyya (universality), and between juz' (part) and juz'iyya (particularity) in Islamic logical propositions.

Core Idea
Judging a group as a whole is not the same as judging every individual separately.

In Islamic logic, understanding how a predicate relates to its subject is crucial. A judgment may apply to an aggregate collectively (kull) or to each individual distributively (kulliyya). Similarly, a judgment about some individuals is particular (juz'iyya), and a part (juz') is the individual component. This lesson clarifies these distinctions using examples from Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four key terms
Locate the Arabic terms: كُلّ (whole), كُلِّيَّة (universality), جُزْء (part), جُزْئِيَّة (particularity). Understand that each corresponds to a different logical relation.
The passage also notes that the definition of juz' is self-evident ('clear knowledge').
2
Differentiate kull from kulliyya
Kull is a judgment on the entire aggregate (e.g., 'The army is strong' means the group as a whole). Kulliyya is a judgment on each individual separately (e.g., 'Every soldier is strong'). The same proposition can be interpreted either way depending on logical analysis.
In later logic, kull may be used synonymously for universal, but here the distinction is foundational.
3
Understand juz' and juz'iyya
Juz' is a part or individual component (e.g., one soldier). Juz'iyya is a particular judgment applying to some individuals (e.g., 'Some soldiers are strong'). The passage states that the knowledge of a part is clear.
Particularity (juz'iyya) contrasts with universality: it does not cover every individual.
4
Analyze sample propositions
Given a proposition like 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (kulliyya) or 'The community must establish prayer' (kull), determine which logical category applies. Check whether the predicate applies to each individual or to the group collectively.
Use examples from Islamic sciences: Fiqh rulings often use universal quantifiers; Kalam arguments may refer to wholes.
5
Apply to a real argument
Take a Kalam proof: 'The world is temporally originated; everything originated has a cause.' Here 'the world' is a whole (kull) but the proof relies on the individual parts (atoms). Ensure you correctly identify whether the judgment is collective or distributive.
Misidentifying kull as kulliyya can lead to invalid inferences in theological debates.

Similarly, juz' (part) is an individual component, and juz'iyya (particularity) is a judgment about some individuals. This distinction is important for understanding propositions: a universal proposition (kulliyya) is about all individuals, while a particular proposition (juz'iyya) is about some.

Definition of Proposition (Qadiyya)
Defining the proposition as a truth-bearer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given statement qualifies as a proposition (qadiyya) in Islamic logic based on the criterion of admitting truth in itself.

Core Idea
A proposition is defined by its capacity for truth and falsity in itself.

The passage opens with the foundational definition of a proposition (qadiyya). In Islamic logic, a proposition is not just any sentence; it must admit truth in itself. This distinguishes it from commands, questions, and other non-assertoric expressions. Understanding this definition is crucial for all logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: 'qadiyya' (proposition) appears at the beginning.
2
Extract the definiens
The definiens is 'ما احْتَمَلَ الصِّدْقَ لِذاتِهِ' (what admits truth in itself).
3
Understand the condition 'in itself'
This excludes statements true/false by external context; e.g., imperatives and questions are not propositions.
4
Test the definition
Consider examples: 'The world is created' is a proposition; 'Do not lie!' is not, because it is a command.

ما احْتَمَلَ الصِّدْقَ لِذاتِهِ جَرَى بَيْنَهُمُ قَضِيَّةٌ وَخَبَرا. ثُمَّ القَضَايَا عِنْدَهُم قِسْمانِ: شَرْطِيَّةٌ وَحَمْلِيَّةٌ. وَالْأَوَّلُ كُلِّيَّةٌ وَشَخْصِيَّةٌ، وَالْأَوَّلُ إِمَّا مُسَوَّرٌ وَإِمَّا مُهْمَلُ. وَالسُّورُ كُلِّيًا وَجُزْئِيًا يُرَى وَأَرْبَعُ أَقْسَامُهُ حَيْثُ جَرَى. إِمَّا بِكُلِّ أَوْ بِبَعْضٍ أَوْ بِلا شَيْءٍ وَلَيْسَ بَعْضُ أَوْ شِبْهِ جَلا. وَكُلُّها مُوجَبَةٌ وَسالِبَهُ فَهيَ إِذَا إِلى الثَّمانِ آيِبَةٌ. وَالأَوَّلُ المَوْضُوعُ فِي الحَمْلِيَةِ وَالآخِرُ المَحْمُولُ بِالسَّوِيْ. وَإِنْ عَلَى التَّعْلِيقِ فيها قَدْ حُكِمَ فَإِنَّهَا شَرْطِيَّةٌ وَتَنْقَسِمْ أَيْضاً إِلى مُتصِلَةٍ وَمُنْفَصِلَةٍ. جُزَاهُما مُقَدَّمٌ وَتَالٍ. أَمَّا بَيَانُ ذاتِ الأَنْصَالِ: ما أَوْجَبَتْ تَلازُمَ الجُزْأَيْنِ وَذاتِ الانْفِصَالِ دُونَ مَيْنِ. مَا أَوْجَبَتْ تَنَافُرًا بَيْنَهُما أَقْسامُها ثَلاثَةٌ فَلْتَعْلَم: مانِعُ جَمْعٍ أَوْ خُلُوٍ أَوْ هُمَا، وَهُوَ الْحَقِيقِيُّ الأَخَضُّ فَاعْلَــمَا.
What admits truth in itself is called among them a proposition and a statement. Then the propositions according to them are two divisions: conditional and categorical. The first (categorical) is universal and particular, and the first (categorical) is either quantified or unquantified. The quantifier is seen as universal and particular, and its four parts where it occurs: either 'all' or 'some' or 'nothing' and 'not some' or its like is clear. And all of them are affirmative and negative, so they then go to eight. The first is the subject in the categorical, and the last is the predicate with equality. And if upon condition a judgment is made, then it is conditional, and it also divides into conjunctive and disjunctive. Their two parts are antecedent and consequent. As for the explanation of the nature of connections: what necessitates the co-occurrence of the two parts is the conjunctive; what necessitates repulsion between them, its kinds are three, so know: preventing combination or preventing disjunction or both, and it is the most specific real (haqiqi), so know.
Source pages p. 30

Commentary
This passage defines the proposition (qadiyya) as something that admits truth and falsity in itself. Propositions are divided into two main types: categorical (hamliyya) which predicates something of a subject, and conditional (shartiyya) which involves a conditional relation. The categorical is further divided into universal and particular, and can be quantified or unquantified. The quantifiers (sawr) are 'all' (kull), 'some' (ba'd), 'none' (la shay'), and 'not some' (laysa ba'd), yielding four types. Combined with affirmation and negation, we get eight basic proposition types. The subject and predicate are identified in the categorical. If the judgment is conditional, it is either conjunctive (muttasila) or disjunctive (munfasila). The conjunctive requires co-occurrence (talazum) of the two parts (antecedent and consequent). The disjunctive requires repulsion (tanafur) between them, and has three types: preventing combination only (mani'at al-jam'), preventing disjunction only (mani'at al-khuluw), or both (mani'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw), which is called the true exclusive disjunction (haqiqi). This classification is the standard one for propositions in Islamic logic.

Types of Propositions: Categorical and Conditional
Dividing propositions into two main types.

Learning Objective

Types of Conditionals: Conjunctive and Disjunctive
Classifying conditionals into conjunctive and disjunctive, and subdividing disjunctive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conjunctive (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila) conditionals and distinguish the three kinds of disjunction.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions are either conjunctive (if-then) or disjunctive (either-or), with three types of disjunction.

The passage moves to conditionals, dividing them into conjunctive (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila). Conjunctives require co-occurrence (talazum), disjunctives require repulsion (tanafur). Disjunctives are further of three types: preventing combination, preventing disjunction, or both.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts
Every conditional has an antecedent (مقدّم) and consequent (تالٍ).
2
Understand conjunctive
Conjunctive conditionals require co-occurrence (تلازم) of the two parts: if antecedent true, consequent true.
Example: 'If you travel, shorten the prayer.'
3
Understand disjunctive
Disjunctive conditionals require repulsion (تنافر) between the parts: they cannot both be true (or both false) in certain ways.
4
Learn the three types of disjunction
A) مانع جمع: cannot both be true. B) مانع خلأ: cannot both be false. C) مانع جمع وخلأ: both conditions hold, the true exclusive disjunction.
The third is called الحقيقي (haqiqi).

Count the total combinations

Types of Conditional Propositions: Conjunctive and Disjunctive
Author divides conditional propositions into conjunctive and disjunctive types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals and identify their parts (antecedent and consequent).

Core Idea
A conditional is either conjunctive (if...then, with coexistence) or disjunctive (either...or, with mutual exclusion).

Conditionals are propositions where a judgment depends on a connection (ta'liq) between two parts. This lesson defines the two main types: conjunctive (muttasila) and disjunctive (munfasila), and introduces their parts: antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali). Understanding these types is essential for analyzing conditional arguments in Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional
Look for a proposition where a judgment is made based on a condition, often signaled by 'if' or 'either...or'. For example, 'If the sun rises, then it is day' is conditional.
2
Label the parts
The first part is called the antecedent (muqaddam), the second part the consequent (tali). In the conjunctive, the consequent follows from the antecedent; in the disjunctive, the parts are alternatives.
3
Determine the relationship
If the condition implies coexistence (talazum), it is conjunctive (shartiyya muttasila). If the condition implies mutual exclusion (tanafur), it is disjunctive (shartiyya munfasila).
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Example: 'If a person breaks the fast intentionally, then he must perform kaffarah' is conjunctive. 'Either the marriage is valid or it is invalid' is disjunctive.

Three Types of Disjunctive Conditional
Author enumerates three types of disjunctive conditional

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three types of disjunctive conditional is being used and determine whether both parts can be true or false.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction has three forms: prevent conjunction, prevent emptiness, or both.

This lesson breaks down the three types of disjunctive conditional (al-shartiyya al-munfasila) as presented in the matn. Each type is defined by what it excludes: conjunction (being true together), emptiness (being false together), or both. Mastering these distinctions is crucial for correctly interpreting conditional statements in logical arguments and for building valid reasoning in fields like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three names
Memorize the Arabic terms: مانعة الجمع (mani'at al-jam'), مانعة الخلو (mani'at al-khulu), and الحقيقية (al-haqiqi). The first prevents conjunction, the second prevents emptiness, and the third prevents both.
2
Determine what each prevents
For each type, identify exactly what it forbids between the two disjuncts. Mani'at al-jam' forbids both being true. Mani'at al-khulu forbids both being false. Al-haqiqi forbids both being true and both being false.
3
Test truth possibilities
For mani'at al-jam', both can be false but not both true. For mani'at al-khulu, both can be true but not both false. For al-haqiqi, exactly one is true; both true and both false are impossible.
4
Identify the strictest type
Al-haqiqi is called 'the more specific' (الأخص) because it imposes the strongest restriction: precisely one disjunct is true. It is the true exclusive OR.

Relations Among the Three Types of Signification
Author explains interrelations of three signification types with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mental from external concomitants and determine which types of signification apply based on the meaning's structure and concomitant.

Core Idea
Only mental concomitants count for logical signification; external ones are irrelevant.

This passage clarifies how the three types of signification—agreement (muṭābaqah), implication (taḍammun), and concomitance (iltizām)—interrelate. It first establishes that only mental concomitants are valid for logical signification, unlike in uṣūl al-fiqh where external concomitants are considered. Then it explains the entailment relations and enumerates the three possible combinations: both taḍammun and iltizām when the meaning is compound with a mental concomitant; only taḍammun when compound without a mental concomitant; only iltizām when simple with a mental concomitant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of signification
Recall the definitions: muṭābaqah (the whole meaning), taḍammun (a part), iltizām (a necessary accompaniment). Note that taḍammun and iltizām are secondary to muṭābaqah.
2
Understand the condition for iltizām
The author stresses that only mental concomitants (lāzim dhihnī) count for logical iltizām. External concomitants, like the blackness of a crow, are not considered in logic, though jurists include them.
This is a key difference between logic and uṣūl al-fiqh.
3
Analyze the entailment relations
Both taḍammun and iltizām entail muṭābaqah, but muṭābaqah does not entail them. If a meaning is simple and has no mental concomitant, muṭābaqah stands alone (though the author notes exceptions).
Entailment means that whenever taḍammun or iltizām is present, muṭābaqah is also present.
4

Using the examples: if a meaning is compound (e.g., 'human' = animal + rational) and has a mental concomitant (e.g., 'capable of learning'), both taḍammun and iltizām apply. If compound but no mental concomitant (rare), only taḍammun. If simple (e.g., 'point') with a mental concomitant (e.g., 'extensionless'), only iltizām.
Simple meanings have no parts, so taḍammun is impossible.

Distinguishing Tawāṭu', Tashākuk, and the Other Three Relations
Author defines the five types of word-meaning relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish tawāṭu' from tashākuk and identify the correct relationship type (including ishtirāk, tarāduf, tabāyun) for given word-meaning pairs from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Tawāṭu' means equal application; tashākuk means graded; ishtirāk means multiple meanings; tarāduf means multiple words for one meaning; tabāyun means completely different.

The passage gives clear examples for each of the five relationships. In this lesson, we focus on the definitions and the key difference between tawāṭu' and tashākuk, which often confuses beginners. We also look at the remaining three: ishtirāk, tarāduf, and tabāyun, using examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Read the definitions and examples
The passage defines tawāṭu' as when the meaning applies equally to all individuals (e.g., 'insān' for all humans), and tashākuk as when it applies with varying intensity (e.g., 'nūr' stronger in sun than moon). Note that in tashākuk, the meaning is still one, but its instantiation differs in degree.
The commentary uses light in the sun vs. moon to illustrate tashākuk.
2
Contrast tashākuk with ishtirāk
In ishtirāk, the meanings are completely different (e.g., 'ayn' for eye and spring). In tashākuk, the meaning is one but varies in intensity. The passage warns that one might initially mistake tashākuk for ishtirāk due to the variation.
This is a common pitfall.
3
Identify tarāduf and tabāyun
Tarāduf (synonymy) occurs when multiple words share one meaning, like 'insān' and 'bashar'. Tabāyun (difference) occurs when two words have no common predication, like 'insān' and 'faras' (horse). Both are illustrated in the passage.
Tabāyun is about cross-word comparison, not within a single word.
4
Practice with Islamic examples
Take a word like 'ṣalāt' (prayer). Does it have one meaning applied to different types of prayer (tashākuk) or multiple meanings (ishtirāk)? Check the usage in fiqh. Similarly, consider 'al-'Ilm' (knowledge) as applied to God and humans – that is tashākuk.
These examples connect to tafsir and kalam.
5
Test yourself
Without looking back, classify the relationship for 'nūr' (light) between sun and moon. Then check your answer against the passage.
This reinforces the key distinction.

Distinguishing al-Kulliyya from al-Kull in the Hadith of Dhu al-Yadayn
Commentator clarifies the logical distinction between universality and totality using the hadith of Dhu al-Yadayn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the logical concepts of universality (al-kulliyya) and totality (al-kull) in a given proposition, using the hadith of Dhu al-Yadayn as a case study.

Core Idea
الكلية مفهوم عام، والكل مجموع الأفراد.

In this passage, the commentator explains a subtle logical point embedded in a well-known hadith. When the Prophet responded 'كل ذلك لم يكن' (all of that did not happen), the word 'كل' could be understood either as a universal quantifier (kulliyya) or as referring to the total sum of parts (kull). The commentator, relying on Dhu al-Yadayn's reply, shows that the correct interpretation is the universal one. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise logical analysis of propositions in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two competing interpretations
The word 'كل' can mean either the universal concept (kulliyya) or the totality of parts (kull). Understand the difference: kulliyya refers to a predicate that applies to the whole category; kull refers to the whole as a sum.
2
Analyze the first statement
The Prophet said 'كل ذلك لم يكن' (all of that did not occur). Consider: if 'كل' is taken as totality, it would negate every individual part. If taken as universality, it negates the entire category.
3
Examine the response
Dhu al-Yadayn said 'بل بعض ذلك قد كان' (rather, some of that did occur). This response only makes sense if the initial 'كل' was a universal concept, because affirming a particular of a universal does not contradict the universal negation if the universal is understood as a general property, not a set of individuals.
4
Derive the criterion
The commentator uses the response to prove that 'كل' here is from the category of kulliyya, not kull. So the criterion is: if affirming a particular is consistent with the original statement, then the term is universal; if inconsistent, it is totality.
5
Apply to similar cases
In logical analysis of Quranic or Hadith texts, always check whether 'كل' is being used as a universal concept or as a total sum, based on the context and subsequent qualifiers.

The Four Types of Categorical Propositions by Subject Quantity
Classification of categorical propositions by subject quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a categorical proposition is personal, absolute, universal, or particular based on the nature of its subject and the presence of a quantifier.

Core Idea
The quantity of a categorical proposition is determined solely by the scope of its subject: particular, universal unspecified, universally quantified, or particularly quantified.

After defining the proposition, the author turns to its primary division into conditional and categorical, then focuses on the categorical type. Categorical propositions (hamliyya) have a subject and predicate. Their quantity depends on the subject: is it a particular term (like 'Zaid') or a universal term? If universal, is there a quantifier? And if quantifier, is it universal or particular? This classification yields four distinct types, each essential for syllogistic logic. In this lesson, we will walk through these four types using examples from Islamic sources.

Guided Path
1
Examine the subject of the proposition
Look at the subject term. Is it a proper noun or a specific individual (like 'Zaid')? If so, the proposition is personal (shakhsiyya). If it is a general term (like 'insan' / human), then it is not personal.
2
Check for a quantifier (sawr)
If the subject is universal, see if there is an explicit quantifier (like 'kull' or 'ba'd'). If no quantifier, the proposition is absolute (muhmala). If a quantifier is present, proceed.
Absolute propositions are sometimes treated as universal by default in syllogisms, but here they are a separate type.
3
Identify the type of quantifier
If the quantifier is universal (e.g., 'kull', 'jami''), the proposition is universal (kulliyya). If it is particular (e.g., 'ba'd', 'wāḥid min'), the proposition is particular (juz'iyya).
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Take the statement 'The Prophet Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah'. Subject is a particular, so personal. 'Every Muslim is required to pray' has universal quantifier, so universal. 'Some scholars are righteous' is particular. 'Man is a creation of Allah' has no quantifier, so absolute (though often taken as universal in context).

The Eight Types: Combining Quantity and Quality in Categorical Propositions
Classification of eight types by combining quantity and quality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to generate all eight types of categorical propositions by applying the parameters of quantity (personal, absolute, universal, particular) and quality (affirmative, negative).

Core Idea
Each categorical proposition has both quantity (scope of subject) and quality (affirmation/negation), combining to produce eight distinct forms.

After establishing the four types based on quantity, the author adds that each type can be either affirmative (mujaba) or negative (saliba). This yields eight proposition types. For example, the personal affirmative 'Zaid is a writer' and personal negative 'Zaid is not a writer'; absolute affirmative 'Man is an animal' and absolute negative 'Man is not a stone'; universal affirmative 'Every man is an animal' and universal negative 'No man is a stone'; particular affirmative 'Some men are animals' and particular negative 'Some men are not stones'. Recognizing these eight forms is essential for constructing and evaluating categorical syllogisms, especially in determining mood and validity.

Guided Path
1

2

3

Author defines modified propositions and divides them into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish modified propositions from simple ones and identify the three types based on where the negation is embedded.

Core Idea
A modified proposition hides the negation inside its terms, not at the copula.

In the previous lesson, you learned about the eight categorical propositions. Now the author adds a twist: any of those eight can become 'modified' if the negation particle is absorbed into the subject or predicate. This transforms the logical landscape, doubling the number of propositions. Let's see how.

Guided Path
1
Understand the contrast between simple and modified
Recall that a simple proposition (muhassala) has the negation particle (if present) as the copula negation, e.g., 'No Zayd is a writer.' A modified proposition (ma'dula) embeds the negation into the subject or predicate, e.g., 'Every non-Zayd is a writer.' The key is whether the negation is part of the term itself.
The negation can be part of the subject (موضوع), predicate (محمول), or both.
2
Identify the three types of modified propositions
1. Subject-modified: negation in the subject only, e.g., 'Every non-human is a stone.' 2. Predicate-modified: negation in the predicate only, e.g., 'Every human is non-stone.' 3. Both-modified: negation in both, e.g., 'Every non-human is non-stone.' These correspond to embedding the negation particle into the respective term.
The proposition remains affirmative or negative in its outer form; the modification is about internal composition.
3
Count the resulting total propositions
Each of the eight simple propositions can be made modified, giving eight pairs. Thus 8 simple + 8 modified = 16 proposition types total. The author says 'by multiplying two in eight' – i.e., each of the eight has two possibilities: simple or modified.
This classification is important for syllogistic rules because term distribution can change in modified propositions.

The Four Parts of a Categorical Proposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject, predicate, relation, and copula in any categorical proposition from Arabic logical texts, and classify it as binary or ternary.

Core Idea
A proposition has hidden parts: the relation and copula, even when not spoken.

This lesson dissects the categorical proposition into its components. The text from Idah al-Mubham explains that a proposition consists of four parts: subject, predicate, relation, and copula. Recognize the implicit relation
The relation (nisba) is the connection of affirmation or negation between subject and predicate. It is not spoken but understood from context or case endings (i‘rab).

Spot the copula if present
The copula is a word like 'huwa' (is) or 'kana' (was) that explicitly indicates the relation. It is optional in Arabic and often omitted.
When present, it makes the proposition ternary; when absent, binary.

Classify as binary or ternary
Count the explicit parts: subject and predicate only = binary; if copula is mentioned, it becomes ternary (or quaternary if relation and copula are counted separately).
Some scholars count relation as a separate part, making it quaternary.

Three Types of Negated Propositions (al-Ma'dula)
Author enumerates three types of ma'dula and gives muhassala examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of al-ma'dula and distinguish them from al-muhassala in any categorical proposition.

Core Idea
The negation tool's location within the subject or predicate determines the type of ma'dula.

In categorical logic, propositions can be positive or negated, but not all negations are alike. The author here distinguishes three types of negated propositions based on where the negation tool is placed. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing the quality of propositions in Islamic logical arguments, especially in Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam.

Guided Path
1
Locate the negation tool
In any proposition, find the word 'لا' or 'ليس'. Determine whether it is attached to the subject term, the predicate term, or both, or appears separately outside the terms.
2
Classify by location
If the negation tool is part of the subject (e.g., 'لا شيء مما هو حيوان'), it is ma'dulat al-mawdu'. If part of the predicate (e.g., 'هو لا فرس'), it is ma'dulat al-mahmul. If in both, it is ma'dulat al-tarafayn.
3
Distinguish from muhassala
If the negation tool appears outside the subject and predicate (like 'لا شيء من الجماد بإنسان' where 'لا' is separate), the proposition is muhassala (simple negative), not ma'dula. Muhassala also includes affirmative propositions without negation.
4
Practice with examples

The Four Parts of a Categorical Proposition
Author defines subject and predicate, then introduces relation and copula, and distinguishes bipartite from tripartite propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject, predicate, relation, and copula in any Arabic categorical proposition.

Core Idea
A proposition has not only a subject and predicate but also a hidden relation and an optional copula.

In classical logic, every categorical proposition contains a subject (what is spoken about) and a predicate (what is said of it). But there is more: the relation between them and sometimes a word linking them. This lesson breaks down each component so you can dissect any proposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Find the part of the proposition that the judgment is about. It is called al-mawdu'. For example, in 'Zayd is standing', the subject is 'Zayd'.
The subject is like a base that carries the predicate.
2
Identify the predicate
Find the part that is affirmed or denied of the subject. It is called al-mahmul. In 'Zayd is standing', the predicate is 'standing'.
The predicate is like a ceiling carried by the wall.
3
Recognize the relation
Understand that there is always a relation (nisba) connecting subject and predicate, even if not spoken. It is the affirmation or negation itself.
The relation is implicit; you infer it from context.
4
Spot the copula if present
Look for a word like 'huwa' or 'kana' that explicitly indicates the relation. In 'Zayd huwa qa'im', 'huwa' is the copula. In Arabic, it is often omitted.
The copula is optional and indicates time (timeless or temporal).

Conditional Propositions: Types and Parts
Defines conditional proposition and its two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a conditional proposition, classify it as conjunctive or disjunctive, and label its antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions are either if-then (implication) or either-or (exclusion).

Having studied categorical propositions, we now turn to conditional propositions, which are fundamental in logical inference, especially in Islamic legal reasoning. This lesson defines the conditional proposition, distinguishes its two main types, and clarifies their parts. The passage from the matn and its commentary provides the core definitions and examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two-part structure
A conditional proposition always consists of two component categorical propositions. Look for a connector like 'if' or 'either...or' that links them.
The connector indicates whether the relation is conditional (talazum) or disjunctive (tanafur).
2
Determine the type of connector
If the connector is a conditional particle (e.g., 'in', 'idha'), the proposition is conjunctive (muttasila). If it is a disjunctive particle (e.g., 'imma'), it is disjunctive (munfasila).
Example: 'If the sun rises, then day exists' uses 'in'; 'The number is either even or odd' uses 'imma'.
3
Check the logical relation
For a conjunctive conditional, the parts must be inseparable (talazum) – one necessarily implies the other. For a disjunctive conditional, the parts must be mutually exclusive (tanafur) – they cannot both be true.
In Islamic law, a conditional ruling like 'If you enter ihram, then you must make intention' shows talazum; disjunctives appear in options like 'The ruling is either obligatory or forbidden.'
4
Label the parts
The first part in order is the antecedent (muqaddam), and the second part is the consequent (tali). This applies to both conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals.
In 'If the sun is rising, then the day exists', 'the sun is rising' is the antecedent; 'the day exists' is the consequent.

The Three Types of Exclusive Disjunction
Definitions and rules for three exclusive disjunctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the three types of exclusive disjunction (مانعة جمع, مانعة خلو, مانعة جمع وخلو) and apply the rules for constructing them.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction has three flavors: preventing conjunction, preventing disjunction, or both.

In Islamic logic, disjunctive conditional propositions (الشرطية المنفصلة) are not all alike. The author distinguishes three types based on what they exclude: conjunction (both true), disjunction (both false), or both. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for evaluating arguments that use 'or' (أو) in theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of exclusivity (انفصال)
The particle 'أو' combines two propositions into one. Exclusivity means that the two parts cannot be both true (جمع), or both false (خلو), or both. This sets the stage for the three types.
2
Examine the first type: مانعة جمع
This type prevents both parts from being true, but allows both to be false. Example: 'The body is either white or black' – it cannot be both, but it can be red (neither).
Think of opposites that have a middle ground.
3
Examine the second type: مانعة خلو
This type prevents both parts from being false, but allows both to be true. Example: 'Zayd is either in the sea or he does not drown' – if he is not in the sea, he doesn't drown (so they aren't both false), but he could be in a boat (both true).
Think of scenarios where at least one is true.
4
Examine the third type: مانعة جمع وخلو (الحقيقية)
This type prevents both conjunction and disjunction: the two parts cannot be both true nor both false. Example: 'The number is either even or odd' – a number cannot be both even and odd, nor can it be neither.
This is the strictest and most common form in logical arguments.
5
Apply the rules for constructing each type
For مانعة جمع: combine a thing with a specific subset of its negation. For مانعة خلو: combine a thing with a more general version of its negation. For الحقيقية: combine a thing with its direct negation or something equivalent.
These rules ensure the disjunction behaves as intended.

Understanding Contradiction and Its Conditions
Defines contradiction and gives rules for finding contradictories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two categorical propositions are genuine contradictories according to the classical Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires both opposition in quality and, for quantified propositions, opposition in quantity.

Contradiction (tanāquḍ) is a fundamental relation between propositions used to test truth and construct arguments. In Islamic logic, it is not simply any opposition but a precise logical relation with specific conditions. This lesson unpacks the definition given in the matn and the commentator's expansion, focusing on how to form correct contradictories for singular, indefinite, and quantified propositions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of contradiction
The passage defines contradiction as two propositions differing in affirmation/negation such that one is true and the other false, necessarily. Identify that this excludes differences between terms or quantity alone.
The original meaning is affirmation and negation of the same thing, e.g., 'Zayd' and 'not Zayd', but here it applies to propositions.
2
Distinguish contradiction from other oppositions
Not every pair of opposed propositions is contradictory. For example, 'Every human is an animal' and 'Some human is an animal' differ in quantity but both can be true—they are not contradictory. Similarly, 'Zayd is virtuous' and 'Zayd is not a sinner' may both be true because the predicate differs.
The key is that contradictories must be true/false exclusively, which requires opposition in quality and, for quantified propositions, also in quantity.
3
Apply the rules for forming contradictories
For singular or indefinite propositions, the contradictory is formed by simply changing the quality (affirmative to negative or vice versa). For quantified propositions, change both the quantifier (universal to particular or vice versa) and the quality.
Memorize the four cases: universal affirmative ↔ particular negative; universal negative ↔ particular affirmative; singular/indefinite: just flip quality.
4
Note the additional conditions of eight unities
The commentator mentions that genuine contradiction also requires agreement in eight aspects (e.g., subject, predicate, time, place, relation, etc.), all reducible to the unity of the judgmental relation. This ensures the two propositions are about exactly the same matter.
In practice, these conditions are usually assumed unless otherwise specified.

Reduction of the Eight Unities: From Enumeration to the Judgmental Relation
Author discusses the shift from eight unities to the judgmental relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical development of the condition for contradiction from eight unities to the judgmental relation, and evaluate why the verified scholars considered the latter more precise.

Core Idea
Later logicians reduced the eight unities to subject and predicate; the verified scholars refined this to a single condition: unity of the judgmental relation.

The passage not only lists the eight unities but also records a scholarly shift: later logicians (al-mutaʾakhkhirūn) found that the eight could be collapsed into unity of subject and predicate, while the verified scholars (al-muḥaqqiqūn) pointed out that even that is not fundamental—what matters is that the same judgmental relation (nisba ḥukmiyya) is either affirmed or denied. This lesson explores the intellectual progression and why the final view is considered superior.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three stages mentioned
The passage distinguishes: (1) earlier logicians who required eight specific unities, (2) later logicians who required only subject and predicate unity, and (3) verified scholars who required unity of the judgmental relation. Note the terminology: 'al-mutaqaddimūn', 'al-mutaʾakhkhirūn', 'al-muḥaqqiqūn'.
These terms correspond to historical phases in Islamic logic.
2
Understand the reduction process: how do the eight unities reduce to subject and predicate?
Later logicians argued that conditions like time, place, and addition are implicitly contained in the subject or predicate. For example, 'Zaid is sitting in the mosque' has 'sitting in the mosque' as the predicate; if the predicate changes to 'not sitting in the house', the predicate itself is different, so the contradiction fails due to lack of predicate unity. Thus, only subject and predicate unity need to be checked explicitly.
3
Grasp the verified scholars' critique of even that reduction
The verified scholars noted that the eight unities are not exhaustive—one could add unity of state, instrument, etc. Instead of listing conditions, they argued that the essential requirement is that the same relation (nisba) between subject and predicate is being affirmed and denied. This shifts the focus from enumerating conditions to the logical structure of the judgment itself.
The nisba ḥukmiyya is the 'glue' that connects subject and predicate; it must be identical in both propositions.
4
Evaluate which view is most defensible
Consider the author's endorsement: 'This is the correct view.' Why might the judgmental relation condition be superior? It avoids the problem of incomplete lists and captures the essence: contradiction occurs only when the exact same link between subject and predicate is both asserted and denied.

The Six Types of Certain Premises (al-Yaqiniyyat)
Author enumerates and defines the six types of certain premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the six types of certain premises (al-yaqiniyyat) in Islamic epistemology and explain their rational classification.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge in Islam comes from six sources: reason, inner sense, experience, recurrent reports, intuition, and external senses.

In this passage, the author classifies the types of premises that yield certainty (yaqin) in the Islamic sciences. Understanding these six categories is essential for evaluating the epistemic basis of arguments in kalam, fiqh, and usul. Each type corresponds to a distinct way of knowing, from pure reason to sense perception and testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first type (awwaliyat) in the classification
The passage starts with 'the second', so recall that the first type (al-awwaliyat) was covered earlier. These are premises known by reason alone, such as 'the whole is greater than the part'.
Awwaliyat are the most basic certainties, requiring no empirical input.
2
Contrast inner perception (mushahadat) with external sense-perception (mahsusat)
Mushahadat (also called wijdaniyyat) are perceived by inner senses like hunger and pain, without needing reason. Mahsusat are perceived by the five external senses. Note that some scholars include mahsusat under mushahadat, reducing the count to five.
Inner senses include hunger, thirst, pleasure, pain.
3
Differentiate mujarrabat (experiential) and hadsiyyat (intuitive) based on repetition
Both combine reason and sense, but mujarrabat require repetition (e.g., 'scammony is a purgative'), while hadsiyyat do not (e.g., 'the moon's light is from the sun'). Hadsiyyat yield only strong opinion (zann qawi).
Mujarrabat are known through trial and error; hadsiyyat are intuitive leaps.
4
Recognize the unique source of mutawatirat
Mutawatirat are known by reason combined with hearing (hearing testimony) under the condition of overwhelming frequency that precludes collusion on falsehood. Example: knowledge of distant cities like Ghazza.
This type underpins historical knowledge and tawatur in hadith.
5
Apply the author's rational division
The passage gives a tripartite division: (1) intellect alone (awwaliyat), (2) no intellect needed (wijdaniyyat and mahsusat), (3) intellect plus other factors (mujarrabat, mutawatirat, hadsiyyat). Understand that the last three types may not constitute proof for those lacking personal experience or report.
This division clarifies the epistemological hierarchy among the certainties.

Second: the observed (mushahadat), also called the found (wijdaniyyat), which are perceived by the inner senses without depending on reason; such as a person's hunger and thirst, their pleasure and pain. Third: the experimental (mujarrabat), which are judgments by reason and sense with repetition; like our saying: 'Scammony is a purgative' and 'wine is an intoxicant.' Fourth: the mutawatir (mutawatirat), which are judgments by reason with the sense of hearing; like our knowledge of Ghazza and al-Shafi'i, due to many reporters whose collusion on falsehood is impossible. Fifth: the intuitive (hadsiyyat), which are judgments by reason and sense without depending on repetition; like the knowledge that the light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun, i.e., strong opinion about that. Sixth: the sense-perceptions (mahsusat), which are perceived by one of the five external senses: hearing, sight, smell, taste, and touch. All of them are in the head specifically except touch, which extends to the rest of the body. Some have included sense-perceptions in the observed (mushahadat) by making them encompass what is perceived by the external senses, so they count the certainties as five. The reason for restricting the certainties to six is that the meaning either the intellect is independent of it, so it is the primary (awwaliyat); or it does not need it, so it is the found (wijdaniyyat) and sense-perceptions; or it needs it and something else, so it is the experimental, mutawatir, and intuitive. The knowledge obtained from the last three does not constitute proof for others, because he may not have experience, tawatur, or intuition, due to his lack of sharing that with the one who deduces. Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of the six types of certain premises (al-yaqiniyyat) in Islamic epistemology. The author begins by listing each type sequentially, starting from the second type (indicating that the first type, 'primary intuitions' or 'awwaliyat', was mentioned earlier). The second type is 'mushahadat' (observed), also called 'wijdaniyyat' (found), which are inner perceptions like hunger and pain that require no reasoning. The third is 'mujarrabat' (experimental), which are judgments from repeated experience combining reason and sense; the example given is that scammony is a purgative, and wine is an intoxicant—both known through repeated testing. The fourth is 'mutawatirat' (recurrent reports), known through overwhelming transmission that precludes collusion, such as knowledge of distant cities and historical figures. The fifth is 'hadsiyyat' (intuitive judgments), which are strong opinions arising from reason and sense without repetition, like knowing that moonlight is from the sun. The sixth is 'mahsusat' (sense-perceptions), obtained by the five external senses; note that touch is not confined to the head. Some scholars subsume sense-perceptions under mushahadat, reducing certainties to five. Then the author provides a rational division: if the intellect is sufficient alone, it is 'awwaliyat'; if no intellect is needed, it is 'wijdaniyyat' or 'mahsusat'; if both intellect and other factors are needed, it is 'mujarrabat', 'mutawatirat', or 'hadsiyyat'. Finally, it is noted that knowledge from the last three types cannot serve as evidence for others who lack the relevant experience, widespread report, or intuition. This passage clarifies the different sources of certainty and their epistemic status.
