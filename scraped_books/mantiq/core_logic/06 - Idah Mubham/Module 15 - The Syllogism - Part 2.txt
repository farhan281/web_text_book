Valid and Invalid Moods in the Connected Conditional Syllogism
Author explains the two productive and two unproductive moods of the connected conditional syllogism with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four moods of the connected conditional syllogism are valid (productive) and which are invalid (unproductive), and explain why using the relationship between ground (malzum) and necessary (lazim).

Core Idea
Only affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent yield valid conclusions in a connected conditional syllogism.

In Islamic logic, the connected conditional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i al-muttasil) is a powerful tool for deriving conclusions from conditional premises. However, not every combination of premise and exception is valid. This passage from the Sharh on al-Sullam explains exactly which two moods are productive and which two are unproductive, grounding the distinction in the logical relationship between the ground (malzum) and the necessary (lazim). By studying this passage, you will understand why affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent are fallacious moves that must be avoided in theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the syllogism
Find the conditional premise (e.g., 'If this is a human, it is an animal') and the exceptional premise that affirms or negates part of it. Note the antecedent (muqaddam) and consequent (tali).
The example in the passage uses 'human' and 'animal'.
2
List the four possible moods
The four combinations are: affirm antecedent, deny consequent, affirm consequent, deny antecedent. Write them down.
3
Apply the ground/necessary principle
For each mood, check if it follows the logical rule: affirming the ground (malzum) necessitates the necessary (lazim); denying the necessary necessitates denying the ground. The valid moods align with these rules.
The passage explicitly states: 'the existence of the necessary does not imply the existence of the ground, and negating the ground does not imply negating the necessary.'
4
Distinguish productive from unproductive
Moods that follow the rule are productive (affirming antecedent and denying consequent). The others are unproductive (affirming consequent and denying antecedent).
5

Understand that the matn's phrase 'wad' dha' (positing that) refers to affirming the antecedent, and 'raf'' (removing) refers to denying the consequent. The 'aks (converse) refers to the unproductive moods.
This clarifies the author's earlier statement in al-Sullam.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the productive and unproductive moods of the connected conditional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i al-muttasil). The author begins by stating that from such a syllogism, only two moods yield a valid conclusion: affirming the antecedent (istithna' 'ayn al-muqaddam) and denying the consequent (naqid al-tali). The other two moods—affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent—are invalid. The example given is the conditional: 'If this were a human, it would be an animal.' Affirming the antecedent ('it is human') validly yields the consequent ('it is animal'). Denying the consequent ('it is not animal') validly yields the negation of the antecedent ('it is not human'). However, affirming the consequent ('it is animal') proves nothing because the consequent is a necessary consequence; the existence of the necessary does not entail the existence of the ground (al-lazim la yalzam min thubutihi thubut al-malzum). Similarly, denying the antecedent ('it is not human') does not prove anything because it is the ground; negating the ground does not necessitate negating the necessary (nafy al-malzum la yaqtadi nafy al-lazim). The author then connects this explanation to the text of the matn (al-Sullam), clarifying that the matn's expression 'putting that' (wad' dha) refers to affirming the antecedent, and 'removing' (raf') refers to negation, and the converse (al-'aks) refers to the two unproductive moods. Thus, the four moods are summarized: two productive (affirming antecedent, denying consequent) and two unproductive (affirming consequent, denying antecedent). In the Islamic sciences, such reasoning is common: e.g., 'If this is khamr, it is haram. It is khamr, therefore it is haram' (affirming antecedent). Or, 'If it is wine, it is off limits. It is not off limits, therefore it is not wine' (denying consequent). Invalid would be: 'It is off limits, so it is wine' (affirming consequent), since many things are forbidden without being wine.

Disjunctive Syllogisms: Types and Productive Moods
Author classifies disjunctive syllogisms into three types and specifies the productive moods for each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of disjunctive conditionals and determine which moods are productive for each type.

Core Idea
The validity of a disjunctive syllogism depends on whether the disjunction is exclusive, inclusive, or weak.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the matn of al-Sullam to explain the three types of disjunctive syllogisms and the specific moods that yield valid conclusions. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly applying disjunctive reasoning in Islamic legal and theological arguments, where choices between mutually exclusive or non-exclusive alternatives often arise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of disjunction
Note that the author mentions three types: man'at al-jam' wal-khuluw (exclusive), man'at al-jam' fqat (inclusive), and man'at al-khuluw fqat (weak). Each has different truth conditions.
Exclusive: exactly one true. Inclusive: at most one true. Weak: at least one true.
2
Understand the truth conditions of exclusive disjunction
In exclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam' wal-khuluw), both disjuncts cannot be true together and both cannot be false; exactly one is true. Example from the text: 'The number is either even or odd.'
This yields four productive moods.
3
Understand the truth conditions of inclusive disjunction
Inclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam' fqat) prevents both being true but allows both to be false. So if one is true, the other must be false, but if one is false, the other may be true or false.
This yields only two productive moods.
4
Understand the truth conditions of weak disjunction
Weak disjunction (man'at al-khuluw fqat) prevents both being false but allows both to be true. So if one is false, the other must be true, but if one is true, the other may be true or false.
The text says its rule is the converse of inclusive: only denying one yields affirming the other.
5
Relate truth conditions to productive moods
For exclusive: affirming one disjunct entails negating the other, and negating one entails affirming the other (four moods). For inclusive: only affirming one entails negating the other (two moods). For weak: only negating one entails affirming the other (two moods).
Memorize which moods work for each type.

Commentary
This passage explains the disjunctive conditional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i al-munfasil). It begins by quoting three lines from al-Sullam's matn (verses 104-106), then provides a commentary. The matn states that in a disjunctive syllogism, positing one disjunct produces the negation of the other, and similarly the converse (i.e., negating one produces positing the other) — but only for the 'more specific' type, which is the exclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam' wal-khuluw). Then the matn distinguishes two other types: a preventer of conjunction only (man'at al-jam' fqat) and a preventer of disjunction only (man'at al-khuluw fqat). For the preventer of conjunction, positing one disjunct produces the negation of the other, but the converse does not hold (i.e., negating one does not produce the other). For the preventer of disjunction, the rule is the opposite: negating one produces the other, but positing one does not produce negation of the other. The commentator then elaborates: The disjunctive syllogism composed of exclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam' wal-khuluw) yields four productive moods: (1) affirming one disjunct entails negating the other; (2) affirming the other entails negating the first; (3) negating one entails affirming the other; (4) negating the other entails affirming the first. Example: 'The number is either even or odd.' Affirming 'even' yields 'not odd'; affirming 'odd' yields 'not even'; negating 'even' yields 'odd'; negating 'odd' yields 'even'. For the inclusive disjunction (man'at al-jam' fqat), where both cannot be true but both can be false, only two moods are productive: affirming one disjunct yields the negation of the other. But negating one does not yield the affirmation of the other, because both could be false. (The third type, man'at al-khuluw fqat, where both cannot be false but both can be true, is alluded to in the matn but the commentary only mentions the first two explicitly.) In Islamic law, an example of exclusive disjunction: 'This animal is either a cow or a horse. It is a cow, so it is not a horse.' An example of inclusive disjunction: 'Either he is sick or he is absent. He is sick, so he is not absent.' But if he is not sick, he might still be absent or not — no conclusion.

Productive Forms of Disjunctive Conditional Syllogisms
Explains the three types of disjunctive conditionals and their valid inference patterns.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of disjunctive conditionals (exclusive, inclusive, exhaustive) and determine which istithna' (exception) forms yield valid conclusions for each.

Core Idea
The validity of a disjunctive syllogism depends entirely on which type of 'or' is used.

In this passage, the author breaks down the disjunctive conditional syllogism into three types based on the nature of the disjunction. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying such syllogisms in Islamic legal and theological reasoning, where arguments often take the form 'either A or B'. This lesson will guide you through the rules for each type using clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of disjunction
Determine whether the disjunction is exclusive (mani'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw), inclusive (mani'at al-jam' only), or exhaustive (mani'at al-khuluw only). The difference lies in whether both disjuncts can be true/false together.
Ex: exclusive: 'the number is even or odd'; inclusive: 'the thing is white or black'; exhaustive: 'Zayd is in the sea or does not drown'.
2
Determine the istithna' (exception)
Check whether you are affirming (wad') or negating (raf') one of the disjuncts. This is the 'istithna'' in the syllogism.
3
Apply the rule for exclusive disjunction
For exclusive (man'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw), both affirming one disjunct and negating one disjunct produce valid conclusions. Affirming A yields negation of B; negating A yields affirmation of B.
Four productive modes: two from affirmation, two from negation.
4
Apply the rule for inclusive disjunction
For inclusive (man'at al-jam' only), only affirming a disjunct is productive. Affirming A yields negation of B. Negating A yields no conclusion because both could be false.
Example: 'the thing is white or black' – if it is white, it is not black; but if it is not white, it might be green, not necessarily black.
5
Apply the rule for exhaustive disjunction
For exhaustive (man'at al-khuluw only), only negating a disjunct is productive. Negating A yields affirmation of B. Affirming A yields no conclusion because both could be true.
Example: 'Zayd is in the sea or does not drown' – if he is not in the sea, he does not drown; but if he is in the sea, he might still not drown.

The author first recapitulates the rule for the conjunctive conditional (muttaṣil) to contrast with the disjunctive: in the conjunctive, negating the consequent entails negating the antecedent, and affirming the antecedent entails affirming the consequent. Then he explains that for the disjunctive, the productive forms depend on the type of disjunction. There are three types: exclusive (man'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw), where the two disjuncts cannot both be true nor both false; inclusive (man'at al-jam' only), where they cannot both be true but can both be false; and exhaustive (man'at al-khuluw only), where they cannot both be false but can both be true. For the exclusive, affirming one disjunct entails negating the other (two forms from positing), and negating one disjunct entails affirming the other (two forms from removal). Thus four productive modes. For the inclusive, only affirming a disjunct yields a conclusion (negating the other); negating does not because both could be false. For the exhaustive, only negating a disjunct yields a conclusion (affirming the other); affirming does not because both could be true. The author gives examples: for exclusive, the number is either even or odd (cannot be both or neither); for inclusive, white/black (a thing cannot be both but can be neither, e.g., green); for exhaustive, Zayd is either in the sea or does not drown (cannot be neither but can be both, i.e., he is in the sea and does not drown—but drowning is not implied by being in sea). In Islamic jurisprudence, similar reasoning applies: e.g., an act is either obligatory (wajib) or forbidden (haram) — if it is exclusive, then affirming wajib negates haram, and negating wajib would affirm haram only if the disjunction is exhaustive (which it isn't); typically such rulings have different logical forms.

Continuous Compound Syllogism (Muttasil al-Nata'ij)
Explains the continuous type of compound syllogism with the grave-robber example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a continuous compound syllogism by using each intermediate conclusion as the minor premise of the next syllogism in the chain.

Core Idea
In a continuous compound syllogism, each intermediate conclusion becomes the minor premise of the next step, forming an unbroken chain.

The author mentions two types of compound syllogism: continuous (muttasil) and separated (mafṣūl). The continuous type is fully explained and exemplified. Understanding how to chain conclusions is essential for multi-step arguments in Islamic sciences, such as establishing a chain of rulings or theological proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first simple syllogism
Take the first two premises and derive their conclusion. In the grave-robber example: premise 1 (minor) = 'the grave-robber takes wealth secretly', premise 2 (major) = 'every secret taker is a thief'. The conclusion is 'the grave-robber is a thief'.
This conclusion is then used as the minor premise in the next syllogism.

Use the conclusion as the minor premise
The conclusion from step 1 becomes the minor premise for the next syllogism. The major premise is the third original premise: 'every thief has his hand cut off'. So the second syllogism has minor = 'the grave-robber is a thief', major = 'every thief has his hand cut off'.
This is the essence of 'continuous': the conclusion is inserted as a premise.
3
Derive the final conclusion
From the second syllogism, we derive the final conclusion: 'the grave-robber has his hand cut off'. No further steps are needed here, but the process could continue indefinitely.
The verse says 'and so on consecutively' (إلى هلم جرًا), indicating possible extension.
4
Recognize the pattern
In a continuous compound syllogism, each step follows the same pattern: take the conclusion of the previous step, combine it with a new major premise, produce a new conclusion. This is like a logical chain.
This is analogous to a sorites in Western logic.

Compound Syllogism and Its Two Types
Author defines compound syllogism and divides it into connected and separated conclusions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a compound syllogism and distinguish between the connected-conclusions and separated-conclusions types.

Core Idea
Compound syllogisms chain multiple premises; the intermediate conclusions may be explicit or implicit.

So far we have studied simple syllogisms with two premises. However, real arguments often require more than two steps. The classical logicians recognized this and developed the concept of the compound syllogism (qiyās murakkab). This lesson explains its structure and two varieties: one where each intermediate conclusion is spelled out, and another where they are omitted for brevity. The fiqh example of the grave robber (al-nabbāsh) will make the chain clear.

Guided Path
1
Identify the compound structure
Look for an argument with more than two premises. In the example: 'The grave robber takes property secretly; everyone who takes property secretly is a thief; every thief has his hand cut off.' That's three premises leading to one final conclusion.
Count the premises: at least three.
2
Trace the intermediate conclusions
In the connected type, each intermediate conclusion is stated. From premises 1 and 2 we get 'the grave robber is a thief', which then becomes a premise with statement 3 to yield the final conclusion.
These intermediate conclusions serve as both conclusion and premise.
3
Recognize the separated type
In the separated type, the intermediate conclusions are omitted. The argument appears as a single step, but logically it consists of multiple simple syllogisms whose conclusions are left implicit.
This is a shorthand form; the logical chain is the same.
4
Compare the two types
The connected type explicitly shows the reasoning chain; the separated type is more concise. Both are valid compound syllogisms, differing only in presentation.
Which type is more persuasive? The connected type is clearer for teaching; the separated type is more common in ordinary debate.

Second, using a word as a synonym when it is not truly synonymous, like 'sword' and 'sharp,' where 'sharp' is not a perfect synonym because it adds a qualification. Under conceptual error, he lists four types. The first is confusing a false proposition with a true one, as in the example of the ship passenger: the motion of the ship is accidental motion, not essential motion, so the syllogism mistakenly treats accidental as essential. The second is begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlab), where the conclusion merely restates one of the premises. The third is applying a judgment of a species to the genus, e.g., concluding that a horse is rational because all animals are rational—but rationality is a property of the species human, not the genus animal. The fourth is treating a conjectural matter as if it were certain, e.g., assuming that speaking knowledgeably implies being a scholar, when it could be mere imitation. Each error invalidates the syllogism materially.
