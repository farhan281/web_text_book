Commentary
This passage is a biographical list of the author's own works, presented as an appendix or introduction to his book 'Idah Mubham'. The list is organized by scientific discipline, beginning with Fiqh (no. 17), then Balagha (rhetoric, nos. 18–22), Mantiq (logic, nos. 23–25), Hisab (arithmetic, nos. 26–29), Wad' (conventional language, no. 30), Mawa'iz (sermons, nos. 31–32), Tibb (medicine, nos. 33–37), then a miscellaneous section covering various sciences such as merits of imams, water discovery, letter mysteries, talismans, geomancy, and advice to rulers (nos. 38–48). The final entry (no. 49) is a 'thabat'—a catalogue of his teachers, their authorizations (ijazat), and his own works. The author concludes by stating that besides these he has other compositions, and that he is unparalleled in his time. This list gives the reader a sense of the author's scholarly breadth and the scope of his intellectual contributions.

Learning Objective

A bibliography maps the intellectual tradition behind a text.

Learning to parse them sharpens your scholarly instinct—you begin to see the tradition behind each claim.

Guided Path
1
Scan for author-title pairs

Here we have Mukhtar al-Sihah by al-Razi, al-Mustasfa by al-Ghazali, etc.
2
Classify the genre of each work
Decide whether it is a dictionary (like Mukhtar al-Sihah), a work of usul al-fiqh (like al-Mustasfa), a biographical dictionary (Mu'jam al-Mu'allifin), or a modern lexicon (al-Mu'jam al-Wasit).
This helps you guess why the author cited it—e.g., for lexical or methodological support.
3
Note edition details
Check publisher, year, and edition type (e.g., photographic reproduction). This shows the scholarly apparatus and helps verify citations.
The editions listed here are mostly modern reprints, common in contemporary tahqiq.
4
Connect to the main text's argument
Reflect on how each reference might be used in a logical work like Idah Mubham. Lexical works clarify terms; usul works provide principles; biographical works identify scholars cited.
Al-Mustasfa, for example, is a key source for definitions and rules in logic-legal reasoning.
