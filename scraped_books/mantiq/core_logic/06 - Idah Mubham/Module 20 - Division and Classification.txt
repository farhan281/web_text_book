The Four Core Topics of Logic
Enumerates the four main topics of logic from the two-fold division of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four core topics of logic and explain how they arise from the division of knowledge into tasawwurat and tasdiqat and from the distinction between principles and aims.

Core Idea
Logic's four topics derive from two cross-cuts: conception/assent and principles/aims.

This passage presents a foundational classification of logic's subject matter. By understanding this structure, you will see how the entire discipline is organized around two types of knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq) and two aspects (mabadi' and maqasid). This lesson unpacks that framework so you can map any logical discussion to its proper place.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Recognize that all acquired knowledge is either a simple conception (tasawwur) or a judgment (tasdiq). This is the primary division of the science.
Tasawwur is grasping a meaning without affirming or denying; tasdiq involves assent to a proposition.
2
Distinguish principles from aims
For each type of knowledge, there are foundational elements (mabadi') and goals (maqasid). Principles are the building blocks; aims are the products.
The passage states: 'li-kullin minha mabadi' wa maqasid'.
3
Map the four topics
For tasawwur, principles = five universals, aims = definition. For tasdiq, principles = propositions, aims = syllogism. These are the four core topics.
The five universals are genus, species, differentia, property, accident.
4
Confirm the confinement
The passage concludes that logic is confined to these four topics (فانحصر فن المنطق في هذه الأبواب الأربعة). Understand that additional topics are only auxiliary.
Later the passage acknowledges that some count differently, but the core is four.

Commentary
This passage classifies the subject matter of logic. It begins by noting that all sciences (i.e., objects of knowledge) are confined to tasawwurat (conceptions) and tasdiqat (assents). Each of these has two aspects: principles (mabadi') and aims (maqasid). The principles are the foundational elements, and the aims are what one seeks to produce. For tasawwurat, the principles are the five universals (al-kulliyat al-khams: genus, species, differentia, property, accident), and the aim is the explicatory statement (al-qawl al-sharih), i.e., definition or description. For tasdiqat, the principles are propositions (al-qadaya) and their rulings (like truth values, modality, etc.), and the aim is the syllogism (qiyas) in its various forms. Thus, logic is confined to these four topics: five universals, definition, propositions, and syllogism. The author notes that discussions of signification (dalalat) and words (alfaz) are only included in logic books because the discussion of the five universals depends on them—they are preparatory. Further, if one considers the five arts (the branches of demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, sophistry) as divisions of qiyas, then the topics become eight. Adding an independent treatment of words makes nine. This passage shows how logic was structured in the tradition, with a clear division between the theory of concepts and the theory of judgments.

Alternative Counts of Logic's Topics
Shows the derivation of alternative counts (8 and 9) from the four-topic structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why some scholars count logic's topics as eight or nine instead of four.

Core Idea
The number of logic's topics varies depending on whether preparatory topics and subdivisions of the syllogism are counted separately.

The passage notes that the four-topic structure is not the only way to organize logic. Some add the five arts of the syllogism (making 8), while others also separate the investigation of words (making 9). This lesson shows how these alternative counts arise and what they reveal about the scope of logic.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four core topics
Remember that the base count includes five universals, definition, propositions, and syllogism. This is the starting point for variations.
These four are derived from the two-fold division of knowledge.
2
Divide the syllogism into its five arts
The syllogism has five branches: demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), rhetoric (khataba), poetics (shi'r), and sophistry (mughalata). When each is treated as a separate topic, the single topic 'syllogism' expands into five.
This is mentioned in the passage as 'man nazara ila aqsam al-qiyas al-khamsa'.
3
Compute the eight-topic count
Replace the one syllogism topic with its five arts: 4 - 1 + 5 = 8. The eight topics are: five universals, definition, propositions, demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, sophistry.
Some authors, like Ibn Sina, structure their logic books this way.
4
Add the independent study of words
The investigation of words (mabahith al-alfaz) is originally auxiliary but can be counted as a separate topic, yielding 9 topics.
The passage says some treat it as an independent topic ('mabahith al-alfaz mustaqillan').

Division of Simple into Particular and Universal
Author divides simple expressions into particular and universal with definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given term as particular (juz'i) or universal (kulli) by applying the criterion of participation (shirkah).

Core Idea
A simple term is particular if it blocks sharing; universal if it allows it.

This passage introduces the fundamental division of simple expressions into particular and universal. This distinction is the bedrock of all subsequent logical operations: predication, definition, and syllogism depend on universals. The key criterion is whether the meaning can be shared among multiple individuals. Let's walk through the logic of this division.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the criterion of 'shirkah'
The author defines particular (juz'i) as that whose conception prevents participation (shirkah) among individuals. Understand that participation here means multiple individuals can equally be described by the term. This is a conceptual test, not an empirical one.
2
Apply the criterion to 'Zayd'
Conceive the meaning of 'Zayd'. Does it allow for more than one Zayd? No – Zayd is a specific person. So it prevents participation, thus it is particular.
Proper names are the paradigmatic particulars.
3
Apply the criterion to 'lion'
Conceive the meaning of 'lion'. Does it allow for more than one lion? Yes – many lions share the same nature. So it does not prevent participation, thus it is universal.
Common nouns like 'lion' are universals.
4
Distinguish from other senses of 'particular'
The logical sense of juz'i is not 'having parts' but 'not universal'. This is a shift from everyday usage.
Be aware of this shift to avoid confusion.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional passage that continues the discussion of simple expressions (mufrad) by dividing them into two types: particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli). The author first notes that the compound expression will be discussed later in the contexts of definitions, propositions, and syllogisms. Then he focuses on the simple expression. The division is based on whether the conception (tasawwur) of the meaning allows sharing (shirkah) among multiple individuals. A particular is that which, when you conceive its meaning, that conception prevents the possibility of more than one individual sharing in that meaning. For example, 'Zayd' is a proper name: when you conceive the meaning of 'Zayd', you conceive a specific individual, and it is impossible for another individual to be that same 'Zayd' (in the same reference). For example, 'lion' is a universal: when you conceive the meaning of 'lion' (a predatory animal), this conception does not prevent the possibility of many individual lions. Classifying Universals by External Existence
Author enumerates six types of universal based on existence of individuals in the external world

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given universal according to whether its individuals exist in the external world, and if not, whether their existence is possible or impossible.

Core Idea
Universals are evaluated by real-world instantiation: none, one, or many individuals, with possibility or impossibility.

In this passage, the author introduces a practical classification of universals (kulliyāt) based on the existence of their individuals outside the mind. This is not merely theoretical—it helps logicians and theologians determine whether a concept refers to something actual, possible, or impossible. The classification is hierarchical: first by number of individuals (zero, one, many), then by modal status (possible or impossible for zero individuals). Understanding this will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main types
Read the first part of the passage and list the three basic categories: universal with no individuals, with one individual, and with many individuals. These are based on actual existence in al-khārij (the external world).
The author uses 'wujida' (existed) to mean actual existence, not hypothetical.
2
Note the subdivision of the first type
Focus on the universal with no individuals. The author says it is either impossible to exist (like conjunction of opposites) or possible but not actualized (like a sea of mercury). This modal distinction is crucial.
The passage breaks off here, but the same subdivision likely applies to the other two types.
3
Relate to your own examples
Think of a universal from Islamic theology, e.g., 'prophet' (nabī). How many individuals does it have? Thousands, so many. 'Final prophet' (khātam al-anbiyā') has one individual. 'God' (Allāh) has one individual, but note it is unique in kind. 'Conjunction of two opposites' has none, and it's impossible.
This exercise trains you to connect abstract classification to concrete Islamic concepts.
4
Apply the classification to a new universal
Take the universal 'angel' (malak). According to Islamic belief, many angels exist, so it falls under type 3. If someone says 'winged horse' (like Burāq), it has one known individual, so type 2. This shows how the classification works in practice.
Always base your classification on actual knowledge from revelation and reality.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of universals (kulli) based on the existence of their individuals in the external world (al-kharij). The author enumerates three main categories: (1) a universal with no existing individuals; (2) a universal with one existing individual; (3) a universal with many existing individuals. The passage breaks off at this point, presumably the subsequent subcategories follow a similar pattern. This classification shows that universals are not only abstract concepts but are evaluated in terms of their real-world instantiation, which is important for logical reasoning about existence and predication.

A term's meaning determines if it is particular or universal; existence of individuals further classifies universals.

This passage lays the groundwork for logical analysis by defining how terms relate to individuals. The singular term is divided into particular (meaning prevents sharing) and universal (meaning allows sharing). Then, universals are sorted by whether they actually have existing individuals. Mastering this classification is essential for understanding definitions, propositions, and syllogisms.

Six Subdivisions of Universal Based on Existence
Details the six types of universal based on existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal into one of six types based on its existence and number of individuals, using examples from Islamic theology and science.

Core Idea
Universals divide by existence: impossible, possible but uninstantiated, unique, finite, or infinite.

Islamic logicians classify universals not only by their logical properties but also by their relation to concrete existence. This lesson unpacks the six-fold division based on whether a universal has zero, one, or many individuals, and within each, whether instantiation is possible, impossible, finite, or infinite. Understanding this taxonomy sharpens your conceptual analysis for fields like kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage elaborates on the six subdivisions of the universal. The first type (no individuals) has two: impossible existence, like 'conjunction of opposites', which cannot exist; and possible existence but currently non-existent, like 'sea of mercury' (mercury is fluid but not an ocean). The second type (one individual) also has two: impossibility of multiplicity, like 'the worshiped rightly' (referring to God, who is uniquely worshiped); and possible multiplicity but currently only one, like the sun (scientifically, there is only one sun). The third type (many individuals) has two: finite individuals, like humans (countable); and infinite individuals, like 'bliss of Paradise' (endless) or 'perfection of Allah' (attributes without limit). This classification shows the sophistication of Islamic logic in considering ontological categories.

Key Terms

جواز الوجود (jawāz al-wujūd): Possibility of existence; the universal can have individuals but currently has none. [Note: Standard usage.]
تناهي (tanāhī): Finitude; the individuals of the universal are limited in number. [Note: Used in mathematics and logic consistently.]
عدم التناهي (ʿadam al-tanāhī): Infinitude; the individuals are unlimited, e.g., bliss of Paradise. [Note: Often discussed in theology regarding divine attributes.]

Literal and Figurative Use of Description and the Ordering of Simple and Composite
Author presents a benefit on literal/figurative description and justifies the order of composite before simple.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between literal and figurative descriptions of terms and meanings regarding simplicity/composition and universality/particularity, and explain why the order composite before simple is justified.

Core Idea
Description applies literally to words for simplicity/composition and to meanings for universality/particularity; the composite precedes the simple because it is positive.

This lesson unpacks a subtle point about how we describe words and meanings. It also answers an important question about the order of topics in the text, revealing a principle of priority based on positivity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the main benefit
The author states a two-fold distinction: (1) The word is described as simple or composite literally, while describing the meaning as such is metaphorical. (2) The meaning is described as universal or particular literally, while describing the word as such is metaphorical.
Recognize the asymmetry: each property attaches primarily to one side.
2
Analyze the reasoning for asymmetry
Words are constructs that can be compounded; their simplicity or composition is inherent. Meanings are concepts that apply to many or one; their universality or particularity is intrinsic.
3
Address the ordering question
A critic objects: why does the author discuss composite before simple, when simple is a part of composite? The answer: composite has positive meaning (it signifies combination), while simple has privative meaning (absence of composition). Affirmation is nobler than negation.
Similarly, universal is before particular.
4
Apply to Islamic scholarly texts
In usul al-fiqh, the ordering often follows this principle: positive concepts precede negative ones, e.g., commands before prohibitions.

Distinguishing Whole (Kull) from Universal (Kulli) and Part (Juz') from Particular (Juz'i)
Author defines whole, universality, part, particularity, and their distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a whole (kull) and a universal (kulli), and between a part (juz') and a particular (juz'i), using definitions and examples from the Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A whole has parts; a universal has particulars. Never confuse the two.

In this passage, the author defines key terms: whole (kull), part (juz'), universality (kulliyya), and particularity (juz'iyya), and crucially distinguishes between a whole and a universal, and between a part and a particular. This distinction is foundational for precise judgments in logic and Islamic legal theory, where understanding the scope of a ruling depends on whether it applies to every part or every particular. The mat example clarifies: a mat is a whole composed of threads; each thread is a part, not a particular. But 'mat' is also a universal that applies to each individual mat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions from the matn verses

These are foundational definitions that the commentator expands.

The commentator gives the example: 'The people of al-Azhar are scholars' as a judgment on the whole, even though some individuals are not scholars. Notice that the judgment may not hold for every part.
Kull is a judgment on the aggregate, not necessarily on each component.
3
Contrast with kulliyya (universality)
Contrast with: 'Every human is capable of understanding' – this judgment applies to every individual (particular), not to parts. This is universality.
Kulliyya is a judgment that covers every particular.
4
Examine the distinction between kull and kulli, juz' and juz'i
The author explains: a universal (kulli) is a concept applying to individuals (juz'iyyat). A whole (kull) is an aggregate of parts (ajza'). Each part is not a particular. For example, Khalid is a particular of the universal 'human'. The mat's threads are parts of the whole mat, not particulars.
Parts compose the whole; particulars instantiate the universal.

Test the rule: judgment on universal vs. judgment on whole
The author shows: a judgment on a universal (e.g., 'Human is rational') applies to every particular (Zaid, Amr, Khalid). A judgment on a whole ('Make me a mat') does not apply to each part (thread alone is not enough). This distinction prevents logical errors in rulings.
This has direct application in fiqh when interpreting commands.
