Distinguishing Hamd and Shukr: Linguistic vs Technical Definitions
Author defines hamd and shukr linguistically and technically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the linguistic and technical definitions of hamd and shukr as presented in the commentary.

Core Idea
Praise (hamd) and thanks (shukr) are not synonyms — each has a precise technical meaning in Islamic discourse.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks the foundational terms of the basmala and hamdala. Before interpreting the first line of al-Sullam, he clarifies what hamd and shukr mean both in language and in technical usage. This terminological precision is essential for understanding how Muslim logicians and theologians build their arguments.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definitions
Find where the author states 'hamd lughatan' and 'hamd urfan'. Notice the shift from linguistic to technical.
The linguistic definition is 'thana'' (praise), while the technical adds 'fi'lun yunbi'u 'an ta'zim al-mun'im' (an action indicating magnification of the Benefactor).
2
Identify genus and differentia of hamd technically
The technical definition has a genus (fi'l, action) and a differentia (indicating magnification due to beneficence). Extract these two components.
3
Contrast hamd with shukr
Note that shukr linguistically is 'juhd' (effort) but technically is 'sarf al-'abd jami' ma an'am Allah bihi ila ma khalaqahu li-ajlihi' (directing blessings to their intended purpose). This shows hamd focuses on magnifying the Benefactor, while shukr focuses on using blessings correctly.
4
Understand why this matters for the commentary
The commentator uses these definitions to explain why the basmala and hamdala are structured as they are. For example, the nominal sentence indicates permanence, fitting the continuous nature of hamd.

The Three Types of Conventional Signification
Enumerates the three types of conventional signification of words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of conventional signification (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) and provide an Islamic science example of each.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: whole, part, or necessary attendant.

In Islamic logic, a word placed by convention (wad') can signify its meaning in three distinct ways. Understanding these types is essential for precise interpretation of texts in Islamic sciences, especially when deriving rulings or definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
From the passage, note the three terms: mutabaqa (correspondence), tadammun (inclusion), and iltizam (commitment). Each describes a different relationship between a word and what it signifies.
2
Understand mutabaqa (whole)
Mutabaqa is when a word signifies its entire, complete meaning. For example, the word 'house' signifies the whole building, not just a part.
3
Understand tadammun (part)
Tadammun is when a word signifies a part of its meaning. For example, 'house' can signify a room, which is part of the house.
4
Understand iltizam (necessary attendant)
Iltizam is when a word signifies something outside its literal meaning but necessarily entailed by it, provided the mind recognizes this entailment. For example, 'house' might signify a roof, if the mind accepts that a house must have a roof.
The condition: the entailment must be intellectually recognized.
5
Distinguish with examples from Islamic sciences
Practice distinguishing the three types using terms from fiqh, kalam, or tafsir. For instance, in kalam, the word 'body' (jism) by mutabaqa signifies the entire substance, by tadammun signifies a part (e.g., dimension), and by iltizam signifies being subject to accidents.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of conventional signification (dalala wad'iyya). Mutabaqa (correspondence) is when the word signifies its entire meaning, e.g., 'house' signifies the whole building. Tadammun (inclusion) is when it signifies a part of the meaning, e.g., 'house' signifies a room. Iltizam (commitment) is when it signifies something necessitated by the meaning, e.g., 'house' signifies the roof, if the mind commits to that necessity. The condition for iltizam is that the entailment be recognized by the intellect.

Defining Ḥamd and Shukr
Author defines ḥamd and shukr linguistically and technically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the linguistic and technical definitions of ḥamd and shukr and identify the key components of their technical definitions.

Core Idea
The technical definition of ḥamd transforms praise into an act indicating magnifying the Benefactor.

The author begins his commentary by clarifying the key term al-ḥamd, both linguistically and technically. This distinction is essential for understanding the verse and the logical structure of praise. He also defines shukr and notes its relationship to ḥamd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition of ḥamd
Find it in the Arabic: الثناء بالكلام على المحمود بجميل صفاته. This defines praise through speech for beautiful attributes.
2
Extract the technical definition of ḥamd
The author says: فعل يُنَبِّي عن تعظيم المنعم، بسبب إنعامه على الحامد أو غيره. This adds the element of indicating magnification.
3
Compare with the definition of shukr
Shukr linguistically is the same as ḥamd with substitution; technically it is صرف العبد جميع ما أنعم الله تعالى به عليه إلى ما خُلِقَ لأجله. Note the shift from praise to directing blessings.
4
Analyze the genus and differentia in the technical definition of ḥamd
The genus is 'فعل' (act) as implied; the differentia is 'ينبي عن تعظيم المنعم بسبب إنعامه' (indicates magnifying the Benefactor due to His beneficence). This is a complete description (rasm).

Author explains the verse and answers the first question about nominal sentence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the verse uses a nominal sentence for praise and identify the rhetorical device of barāʿat istihlāl.

Core Idea
The nominal sentence reflects the permanence of Allah's essence.

The author explains the meaning of the verse and then raises two questions. The first question asks why the praise is given in nominal form rather than verbal. The answer ties grammar to theology: because Allah's essence is permanent, the nominal sentence, which indicates stability, is appropriate. Core Idea
Tasawwur grasps single meanings; tasdiq affirms or denies relations.

In this passage, the author defines the two fundamental divisions of acquired knowledge: tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether we are dealing with simple concepts or propositions. The commentary expands with an example to solidify the concept.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cognitive act
Given a statement, ask: does it express a simple concept or a judgment? If it is a mere concept, it is tasawwur; if it involves affirmation or negation, it is tasdiq.
2
Check for judgment
If there is a claim that something is true or false, it is tasdiq. For example, 'Zayd is standing' contains a judgment; 'Zayd' alone does not.
The passage explicitly states tasawwur is perception of a single meaning.
3
Apply the definitions
Use the source: tasawwur = perception of a single meaning (e.g., 'Zayd'); tasdiq = perception of a relation (e.g., 'Zayd is standing').

Core Idea
Logic is built on four pillars: principles and purposes of concepts and assents.

This passage from Idah al-Mubham presents the foundational structure of Islamic logic. It divides all logical inquiry into two types of knowledge—conceptualization (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq)—and for each, specifies principles (mabadi') and purposes (maqasid). Understanding this division helps you see how every logical tool fits into a coherent system.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Notice the text explicitly divides logic into tasawwur (concept formation) and tasdiq (judgment/assent). Each has its own principles and purposes. Check the Arabic for 'مبادئ التصورات' and 'مبادئ التصديقات'.
2
Distinguish principles from purposes
The principles are the building blocks: for concepts, the five universals (الكليات الخمس); for assents, propositions (القضايا). The purposes are what we aim to produce: explanatory statement (القول الشارح) and syllogism (القياس).
3
Note the variations in counting topics
The author mentions that some scholars count eight or nine topics by including the five parts of syllogism or treating words as a separate topic. This shows flexibility in organizing the material.
4
Apply the classification
Given any logical element, test whether it belongs to tasawwur or tasdiq, and whether it is a principle or purpose. For example, 'the world is changing' is a proposition (principle of assent), while 'the world is originated' is a conclusion (purpose of assent).

Definitions of Explanatory Statement and Argument
Defines explanatory statement and argument as purposes of concepts and assents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qawl sharh (explanatory statement) and hujja (argument) by identifying their genus and differentia, and distinguish between them using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
An explanatory statement defines a concept; an argument produces assent.

After outlining the four topics, the passage defines two key terms: al-qawl al-sharih (the explanatory statement) and al-hujja (the argument). These are the purposes of conceptualization and assent respectively. Understanding their definitions helps you see how logicians use precise language to classify knowledge operations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The text defines two terms: 'القول الشارح' and 'حجة'. Locate each in the Arabic and note what is being defined.
2
Extract the genus and differentia for qawl sharh
For qawl sharh, the genus is 'word' (لفظ) and the differentia is 'conveys a single meaning' (مفاد به معنی مفرد). The example 'rational animal' for man illustrates it: it is a phrase that gives a single concept.
The definition type here is a hadd lafzi (verbal definition) or rasm, as it describes the function.
3
Extract the genus and differentia for hujja
For hujja (argument), the genus is 'word' (لفظ) and the differentia is 'benefits assent' (مفيد للتصديق). It is further specified as a syllogism (قياس). The example: 'the world is changing, everything changing is originated, therefore the world is originated.'
Hujja is defined both as a word producing assent and as a syllogism, indicating it is a type of argument.
4
Compare the two definitions
Both are 'words' but serve different purposes: qawl sharh leads to a single concept, while hujja leads to a judgment (assent). Note that qawl sharh may be a single word or phrase, while hujja is a combination of premises.

Classification of Indication: Types and Subtypes
Author defines dalala and divides it into six types, then focuses on verbal conventional and its three subtypes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any sign into six types based on its form (verbal/non-verbal) and its connection (conventional/natural/rational), and identify the three subtypes of verbal conventional indication: mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam.

Core Idea
Indication is understood through two axes: the sign's form and the mode of connection.

This passage lays the foundation for understanding how words convey meanings in logic. The author defines indication (dalala) as understanding one thing from another, then classifies all signs into six types using two criteria: whether the sign is verbal or non-verbal, and whether the connection is by convention (wad'), nature (tab''), or reason ('aql). The focus then narrows to verbal conventional indication, which is further divided into three subtypes: correspondence, inclusion, and commitment. Mastering this classification is essential for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Define indication
Extract the definition: understanding of one thing from another. Note the example of 'heaven' and the judgment.
The definition applies to all types of signs, not just words.
2
List the two criteria
Identify the two axes: type of sign (verbal vs non-verbal) and mode of connection (conventional, natural, rational). This yields 2x3=6 types.
Each combination is a distinct type of indication.
3
Construct the 2x3 matrix
Place the examples from the passage into each cell: e.g., non-verbal conventional: gesture; non-verbal natural: redness for shyness; non-verbal rational: world for Creator; verbal conventional: 'lion' for animal; verbal natural: groaning for illness; verbal rational: speech for life.
Memorizing examples helps internalize the categories.
4
Focus on verbal conventional: identify the three subtypes
From the passage, verbal conventional indication is divided into mutabaqa (whole meaning), tadammun (part of meaning), iltizam (necessary concomitant). Use examples: 'lion' indicates the animal (mutabaqa), the body of the lion (tadammun), the ferocity (iltizam).
These subtypes apply only to conventional verbal signs.

The Six Types of Signification According to the Sign
Author defines signification and enumerates six types based on sign (verbal/non-verbal) and mode (placement/nature/reason).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given sign into one of the six types of signification by identifying whether the sign is verbal or non-verbal and whether it indicates by convention, nature, or reason.

Core Idea
Signification is understanding one thing from another; every sign is either verbal or non-verbal and indicates by placement, nature, or reason.

Before we can reason with signs, we must understand what a sign is and how it functions. This lesson dissects the foundational concept of dalala (signification) and its six-fold classification based on the nature of the sign. Mastering this classification will sharpen your ability to analyze how evidence operates in Islamic sciences, from the explicit words of revelation to the natural signs in the universe that point to the Creator.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sign and signified
In any instance of signification, there is a sign (dal) and a signified (madlul). Look at the example 'the word 'sky' indicates the judgment.' Here the word is the sign, and the mental understanding is the signified. Practice isolating these two in the examples given.
2
Determine if the sign is verbal or non-verbal
The sign is either a word (lafz) or something else (ghayr lafz). In the passage, 'sky' is verbal; smoke is non-verbal. Decide which category each example belongs to.
Non-verbal signs include gestures, inscriptions, natural phenomena, etc.
3
Identify the mode of indication: convention, nature, or reason
For each sign, ask: does it indicate by human-made convention (wad'), by an inherent natural connection (tab'), or by rational inference of cause and effect ('aql)? For example, redness indicating shyness is natural; smoke indicating fire is rational.
The mode is independent of the sign being verbal or non-verbal.
4
Combine both criteria to classify the sign
Now you have a 2×3 grid: verbal/non-verbal crossed with wad'/tab'/'aql. This yields six types. Match each example from the passage to its cell. For instance, 'the word lion indicating the predatory animal' is verbal-conventional.
5
Apply the classification to a new example
Take an example from Islamic knowledge: the sound of the adhan indicates the time for prayer. The sign is verbal (lafz) and it is conventional (wad'). Therefore it is verbal-conventional signification. Practice with other examples like the crescent moon indicating start of Ramadan (non-verbal rational), or the Prophet's raised hand during the farewell sermon indicating a prohibition (non-verbal conventional).
This skill is essential for Usul al-Fiqh where the types of evidence (adilla) are categorized similarly.

The Five Word-Meaning Relationships
Enumerates five word-meaning relations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five word-meaning relationships (tawāṭuʾ, tashākuk, ishtirāk, tarāduf, takhāluf) and classify a given example into the correct type.

Core Idea
Word-meaning relations reduce to four logical combinations, plus intensity variation.

In this passage, the author presents the fundamental classification of how words relate to their meanings. Understanding these relationships is crucial for precise interpretation in Islamic sciences, especially in Usul al-Fiqh and Arabic grammar. We will break down the five types using a logical grid and examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical grid
Consider two variables: the word (single or multiple) and the meaning (single or multiple). This yields four possibilities. The author mentions five because tashākuk is a subtype of the single-word-single-meaning case.
Takhāluf (multiple words, multiple meanings) is the fifth but not elaborated here.
2
Identify single-word-single-meaning types
If the word is one and the meaning is one, check if the meaning applies equally (tawāṭuʾ) or with varying intensity (tashākuk). Example: 'insān' is tawāṭuʾ, 'nūr' is tashākuk.
Tashākuk causes doubt (shakk) because it appears univocal at first glance.
3
Identify single-word-multiple-meaning
When one word has multiple distinct meanings, it is ishtirāk (homonymy). Example: 'ʿayn' means eye, spring, etc.
The meanings are conceptually separate, not degrees of one meaning.
4
Identify multiple-word-single-meaning
When multiple words share the same meaning, it is tarāduf (synonymy). Examples: 'insān' and 'bashar' both mean human.
True perfect synonyms are rare in Arabic.
5
Apply to new examples
Given a word or phrase, determine the number of words and meanings, then apply the appropriate term. For instance, 'salah' (prayer) is mutawāṭiʾ; 'iman' (faith) is mushakkak (varies in strength).
This skill directly aids in interpreting legal and theological texts.

The Whole vs the Universal: Key Distinctions in Logic
Author defines whole, part, universality, and particularity with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the whole (al-kull) and the universal (al-kullī) and between the part (al-juz') and the particular (al-juz'ī), and correctly classify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The whole is a collection of parts; the universal is a concept that applies to individuals.

In this lesson, we unpack four fundamental terms: al-kull (the whole), al-juz' (part), al-kulliyya (universality), and al-juz'iyya (particularity). The author explains them with vivid examples from everyday life and Islamic scholarship. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for logical analysis, especially when dealing with categorical propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four terms in the Arabic passage
Locate the words الكل, الكلية, الجزء, الجزئية in the Arabic text. Note that the author also uses الجُزْء and الجُزْئي (particular) interchangeably. Understand that each term has a distinct definition and function.
2

The example for al-kull shows a judgment on a group as a unit, not applying to every member.
3
Compare the whole (kull) with the universal (kullī)
The author stresses that al-kull is composed of parts (ajza'), while al-kullī applies to individuals (juz'iyyat). For example, the mat (kull) has parts (warp, weft) but is not applied to them separately; 'human' (kullī) applies to individuals like Zayd.
This distinction is often misunderstood. A judgment on a whole (e.g., 'Make me a mat') does not cover its parts individually, whereas a judgment on a universal (e.g., 'Human is rational') covers every particular.

Avoiding Circularity in Definitions
Prohibition of including judgment in definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular definitions by checking whether they include a judgment about the definiendum.

Core Idea
A definition must presuppose conception; embedding a judgment creates a loop.

A definition should clarify what a thing is without requiring prior knowledge of it. If a definition includes a judgment (hukm) about the thing, such as 'salat is obligatory worship', the judgment presupposes conception of salat, leading to circular reasoning (al-dawr). This lesson shows you how to detect and avoid such circularity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined and list all parts of the definition.
2
Spot any judgment (hukm)
A judgment is any statement that predicates something of the thing, e.g., 'obligatory', 'permissible', 'exists'. Is there a part that is a ruling about the thing?

Using 'Or' in Definitions: Doubt vs Division
Rules for using 'or' in definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish 'or' of doubt from 'or' of division in definitions and determine whether each is permissible.

Core Idea
A thing's essence cannot be defined by alternatives; but if it splits into types, each type gets its own definition.

The use of the word 'or' (aw) in definitions can either indicate doubt about the essence or a division into distinct types. The logicians forbid 'or' of doubt because it leaves the quiddity undefined, but allow 'or' of division because it actually gives separate definitions for separate things. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the word 'or' in a definition
Read the definition carefully and identify where 'or' appears. Note the terms on both sides.
2
Determine if the 'or' indicates doubt (shakk)
If the 'or' suggests that the definiendum could be either of two possibilities, it is 'or' of doubt. Example from passage: 'the dullard is he who does not understand or does not seek understanding'—the defining property is uncertain.
Such 'or' is forbidden in hadd because it fails to capture a single determinate essence.
3
Determine if the 'or' indicates division (taqsim)
If the 'or' signals that the defined term has two distinct senses, each with its own definition, it is 'or' of division. Example: 'nazar is thought leading to knowledge or preponderant opinion'—this defines two different types of nazar.
This is permissible because each part is a separate definition for a separate type.
4
Apply the rule to rasm
In rasm (descriptive definition), 'or' of division is also allowed, as in 'man is a laughing animal, or an animal receptive to knowledge and writing.'
Properties can be listed alternatively because they are not essential.

Hadd vs Rasm: Essential and Descriptive Definitions
Distinction between hadd and rasm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a hadd (essential definition) from a rasm (descriptive definition) and explain why rasm can have alternative properties while hadd cannot.

Core Idea
Essential definition locks one quiddity; descriptive definition can list multiple signatures.

The passage concludes by drawing a key distinction: a quiddity (mahiyya) cannot have two different differentiae (fusul) because that would imply two essences, but it can have multiple distinctive properties (khasas) that distinguish it in different ways. This difference underlies the contrast between hadd (essential definition) and rasm (descriptive definition). Understanding this helps you evaluate definitions in Islamic sciences critically.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether the definition uses a differentia (fasl) or a property (khassa)
A differentia is a essential characteristic that enters into the quiddity; a property is a non‑essential but distinctive trait. In 'human is a rational animal', 'rational' is a differentia. In 'human is a laughing animal', 'laughing' is a property.
2
Check if the definition allows alternative differentiae
If the definition uses 'or' between two candidates for the differentia, it cannot be a hadd because the essence would have two forms. Example: 'human is a rational or laughing animal' is invalid as hadd.
The passage says: 'it is impossible for a quiddity to have two dividing differentiae alternately.'
3
Check if the definition allows alternative properties (khasas)
Properties can be listed alternatively in a rasm, as in 'human is a laughing animal, or an animal receptive to knowledge and writing.'
This is permissible because properties do not constitute the essence.
4
Classify the definition as hadd or rasm
If the definition gives the genus and the essential differentia, it is a hadd. If it gives the genus and one or more distinctive properties, it is a rasm.

Definition of the Proposition (Qadiyya) as Truth-Bearing Statement
Author defines qadiyya as a compound that admits truth and falsity essentially, excluding insha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given statement is a proposition (qadiyya) by applying the criteria of being a compound that essentially admits truth and falsity, and distinguish it from performative utterances (insha').

Core Idea
A proposition is defined by its capacity for truth and falsity, not by actual certainty.

In Islamic logic, the qadiyya (proposition) is the basic unit of assent (tasdiq). This passage gives its precise definition: a compound that essentially admits truth and falsity. The definition is carefully crafted to include even statements we know with certainty to be true or false, while excluding performative speech acts like commands and questions. Understanding this distinction is essential for constructing sound arguments in Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definiens
The definiendum is 'qadiyya' (proposition). The definiens is 'a compound that admits truth and falsity essentially'. Break this into parts: it must be a compound (not a single term), and it must have the intrinsic possibility of being true or false.
2
Analyze the condition 'lidhatihi' (essentially)
The word 'lidhatihi' clarifies that the capacity for truth and falsity is intrinsic to the proposition itself, not dependent on external factors like the speaker's credibility. This includes statements known to be certainly true (e.g., reports of Allah) or certainly false (e.g., 'one is half of eight'), because in themselves they could be otherwise.
This prevents confusion about why certain propositions are still considered propositions even when their truth value is beyond doubt.
3
Understand what the definition excludes (insha')
The definition excludes all performative utterances (insha') such as commands, prohibitions, interrogatives, and exclamations. These do not have a truth value in themselves. For example, 'Give me water!' is neither true nor false.
This boundary is crucial: only declarative statements (khabar) qualify as propositions in logic.
4
Apply the definition to sample statements
Take statements from Islamic sciences and test them: 'Allah is one' (qadiyya, because it admits truth/falsity essentially), 'The prayer is obligatory' (qadiyya), 'Do not steal' (insha', command), 'Is the world created?' (insha', question).

The Definition and Conditions of Qiyas (Syllogism)
Author defines qiyas linguistically and technically, explains its conditions with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions of a valid syllogism and test whether a given argument meets them.

Core Idea
A syllogism must entail its conclusion by itself, not by an external premise.

The classical logicians defined qiyas with precision. This lesson unpacks the technical definition and its three conditions, showing you how to distinguish a proper syllogism from a mere statement. By mastering these conditions, you will avoid common fallacies in theological and juridical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the definition
The definition states: 'an expression composed of two or more premises from which follows necessarily by themselves another statement.' Break down each part: composition, premises, entailment, conclusion.
The linguistic meaning is 'measuring something upon the example of something else', but the technical definition is more precise.
2
Extract the three conditions
Condition 1: composition from at least two premises. Condition 2: the conclusion is another statement, distinct from each premise. Condition 3: the entailment is by the premises themselves, not requiring an external premise.
These conditions exclude single propositions, self-conclusions, and arguments needing extra assumptions.
3
Apply the conditions to the examples
Check the given examples: 'The world is changing and everything changing is temporally originated' satisfies all three. 'Every human is rational and every rational is a human' fails condition 2 (conclusion equals premise). 'Zayd = Amr and Amr = Bakr' fails condition 3 (needs transitivity premise).
The grave-robber example is compound but still satisfies conditions.
4
Distinguish simple vs compound syllogism
A simple syllogism has exactly two premises. A compound has more than two. The passage notes that compound syllogisms reduce to simple ones.
This prepares for later lessons on syllogistic figures.

This passage is a definitional exposition of the syllogism (qiyas) in logic. The author begins with the matn (poem) stating the essence of qiyas as composed of premises that essentially entail a conclusion. Why the Causal View of Inference Is Rejected
Refutes the philosophers' causal view of inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the author's argument against the philosophers' view that reasoning is a cause (illa) of the conclusion.

Core Idea
A cause cannot be separated from its effect, but reasoning and its conclusion do not coexist.

The author only explicitly refutes the fourth view, that of the philosophers, who claim reasoning is the cause ('illa) and the conclusion is the effect. His argument is simple: a cause always accompanies its effect, but reasoning (the act of thinking) and the conclusion (knowledge) are opposites and cannot occur together. This lesson unpacks that logical critique.

Guided Path
1
State the philosophers' claim
The philosophers say: 'النتيجة معلول للنظر، وهو علة' – the conclusion is an effect of reasoning, and reasoning is its cause.
2
Identify the author's criterion for a cause-effect relationship
The author asserts that a cause does not separate from its effect ('العلة لا تفارق معلولها'). This means they must coexist.
This is a standard Ash'ari critique of necessary causal connection.
3
Check whether reasoning and conclusion coexist
The author states that reasoning does not accompany the conclusion ('النظر لا يجامع النتيجة'), because reasoning is the opposite of knowledge ('ضد العلم').
Reasoning is a process, knowledge is its product; they are temporally distinct.
4
Conclude the refutation
Since a cause and effect must coexist but reasoning and conclusion do not, the philosophers' view is false ('باطل').
Thus the causal model fails, and the author supports the intellectual necessity view.
