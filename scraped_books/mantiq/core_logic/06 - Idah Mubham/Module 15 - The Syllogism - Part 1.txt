Definition of Syllogism
Author defines syllogism as a discourse that necessarily implies another statement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two essential parts of a syllogism (premises and conclusion) and explain the meaning of 'necessitation by itself'.

Core Idea
A syllogism is a discourse that, by its structure, forces a conclusion.

This opening section of the chapter on syllogism gives a precise and powerful definition. The author wants us to understand that a syllogism is not just any collection of statements, but a carefully structured discourse that yields a necessary conclusion. This definition will be the foundation for all the rules that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined. The author defines 'al-qiyas' (syllogism).
2
Extract the genus
The syllogism is composed of propositions (qadaya). So its genus is a discourse (qawl) made up of statements.
3
Extract the differentia
The differentia is that this discourse has a form (sura) that necessitates (mustalzim) by itself (bi al-dhat) another statement (qawl an akhar). This means the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises due to the structure.
4
Understand 'by itself'
The phrase 'bi al-dhat' indicates that the necessitation is due to the argument's own form, not external factors like content or context.
This distinguishes syllogism from accidental implication.

Division of Syllogism: Conjunctive Type
Author divides syllogism into conjunctive and conditional, and describes conjunctive as specific to categorical propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the conjunctive syllogism from the conditional syllogism and state the type of propositions it uses.

Core Idea
Conjunctive syllogisms work only with categorical propositions, no conditions needed.

After defining syllogism in general, the author immediately divides it into two types. He then describes the first type, the conjunctive syllogism, with two key features: it produces its conclusion by force and it is specific to categorical propositions. This sets the stage for a deeper study of categorical syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the division
The author says syllogism is two types. One is called conjunctive (iqtirani). The other type is not named here but is conditional (istithna'i).
This division is fundamental in Aristotelian logic.
2
Identify the characteristic of conjunctive
The conjunctive syllogism indicates the conclusion by force (bi quwwa). This means it is deductively valid; the conclusion follows necessarily.
3
Identify the scope
It is specific to categorical propositions (hamliyya). Categorical propositions affirm or deny a predicate of a subject unconditionally.
Example: 'All humans are mortal' is categorical; 'If it rains, the ground is wet' is conditional.
4
Note the implication
The other type (conditional) uses conditional or disjunctive propositions. This classification helps in analyzing arguments.

The Definition and Structure of Qiyās
Author defines qiyās, divides it into iqtirānī and istithnā'ī, and explains its structure and composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a categorical syllogism (minor premise, major premise, middle term) and distinguish it from the conditional syllogism based on the author's definitions.

Core Idea
Qiyās is a machine: premises in, conclusion out – by logical necessity.

This passage lays the foundation for all of logic: what a syllogism is and how it works. We are told qiyās is not any random combination of statements, but a structured discourse that forces a conclusion. The author then splits qiyās into two species – categorical (iqtirānī) and conditional (istithnā'ī) – and gives us the anatomy of the categorical syllogism: two premises (minor and major) and the crucial middle term that links them but vanishes in the conclusion. Mastering this skeleton is the first step to building valid arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition of qiyās
Line 73 says qiyās is composed of propositions that necessarily entail another discourse by itself. This is the axiomatic core: a syllogism is a self-contained argument, not dependent on external proof.
The phrase 'bi-al-dhāt' (by itself) emphasizes internal necessity, not borrowed authority.
2
Note the two divisions of qiyās
Lines 74-75 tell us qiyās is either iqtirānī (categorical) or istithnā'ī (conditional). The categorical one uses only categorical (ḥamlī) propositions and yields the conclusion 'by force' of its form.
Focus on the categorical for now; the conditional is introduced but not detailed here.
3
Identify the parts of a categorical syllogism
Lines 79-81 give the terms: each premise is either minor (ṣughrā) or major (kubrā). The minor contains the smaller term (subject of conclusion), the major contains the larger term (predicate of conclusion). The middle term (wasaṭ) is common to both premises but absent from the conclusion.
The middle term is the link; it is 'omitted' (mulghā) in the conclusion.
4
Understand the condition for validity
Line 79 says the minor must be included in the major. This refers to the logical relationship: the subject of the conclusion must fall under the predicate of the conclusion via the middle term.
In standard categorical logic, this means the minor premise asserts something belongs to the middle term, and the major asserts the middle term belongs to the major term.

The Four Figures of the Categorical Syllogism
Author defines the four figures of the categorical syllogism and their validity conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four figures of a categorical syllogism based on the position of the middle term and state the validity conditions for each figure.

Core Idea
The middle term's position determines the figure; each figure has its own validity conditions.

In this lesson, we learn how to classify categorical syllogisms by their figure—the arrangement of the middle term without considering quantifiers. The author defines four figures and gives essential conditions for a valid syllogism in each. Understanding this classification is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic logic, especially in Kalam and Fiqh proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand what a figure is
The figure (shakl) is the arrangement of the two premises of a syllogism, determined solely by the position of the middle term (subject or predicate) in each premise. Quantifiers (universal/particular) are not considered; they define the mood (ḍarb).
The middle term is the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
2
Learn the four figures
There are exactly four figures: 1st: middle term is predicate in minor and subject in major. 2nd: middle term is predicate in both. 3rd: middle term is subject in both. 4th: middle term is subject in minor and predicate in major (reverse of 1st).
Memorize the patterns: 1st: حمل بصغرى وضعه بكبرى; 2nd: حمله في الكل; 3rd: وضعه في الكل; 4th: عكس الأول.
3
Memorize validity conditions for each figure
1st figure: minor must be affirmative, major must be universal. 2nd figure: premises must differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative), and major must be universal. 3rd figure: both premises must be affirmative, and at least one must be universal. Conditions for 4th figure are not given here but follow from conversion.
Deviating from these conditions makes the syllogism invalid, even if the premises are true.
4
Apply to a Kalam example
Consider the proof for the world's temporality: 'The world is originated' (minor, affirmative) and 'Everything originated has a cause' (major, universal). This is a valid first-figure syllogism. Check that minor is affirmative and major universal—conditions satisfied.
If the minor were negative, the syllogism would be invalid.

Constructing a Categorical Syllogism
Author explains syllogism construction via term inclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the minor, major, and middle terms in a categorical syllogism and verify that the minor premise is included under the major premise.

Core Idea
The minor premise must be included under the major for a necessary conclusion.

This passage from the matn gives the rule for constructing a valid categorical syllogism. You learn the roles of the three terms and how the premises must relate. The key is the 'inclusion' (indiraj) of the minor term under the major term via the middle term. Let's break down each part.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
In a categorical syllogism, there are three terms: the minor term (subject of conclusion), the major term (predicate of conclusion), and the middle term (the link that appears in both premises but not in conclusion). Locate them in the conclusion first.
For example, in 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; every nabidh is intoxicant; therefore every nabidh is forbidden', the minor term is 'nabidh', the major term is 'forbidden', the middle term is 'intoxicant'.
2
Determine which premise is minor and which is major
The minor premise contains the minor term and the middle term. The major premise contains the major term and the middle term. The text says: 'That which has the minor term is its minor, and that which has the major term is its major.'
In the example, 'every nabidh is intoxicant' is the minor premise (contains 'nabidh' and 'intoxicant'), and 'every intoxicant is forbidden' is the major premise (contains 'intoxicant' and 'forbidden').
3
Check the inclusion condition
The minor premise must be 'included under' (andraja fi) the major premise. This means the middle term's relation to the minor term must fall under the major term's scope. For a universal affirmative, this is satisfied if the minor term is a subset of the middle term, and the middle term is a subset of the major term.
In our example, nabidh is under intoxicant, and intoxicant is under forbidden, so the inclusion holds.
4
Verify the conclusion follows necessarily
If the premises are true and the inclusion is proper, the conclusion must follow. The text says: 'The necessary consequent of the premises comes according to the premises.' So test with an example: if the premises are true and the form is valid, the conclusion is certain.
This is the essence of qiyas burhani (demonstrative syllogism).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conclusion takes the weakest premise; figures only for categorical; demonstration ends in self-evident truths.

This lesson compresses several essential rules for constructing valid demonstrative syllogisms. These rules are foundational for Islamic logical analysis, especially in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the weaker premise
In a categorical syllogism, compare the two premises: negative is weaker than affirmative, particular is weaker than universal. The conclusion must match the weaker in both quality and quantity.
If one premise is negative and the other affirmative, the conclusion must be negative. If one is particular and the other universal, the conclusion must be particular.
2
Apply the figure restriction
The discussion of figures (first to fourth) and their conditions apply only to categorical (hamli) syllogisms, not to conditional (sharti) ones.
Conditional syllogisms have their own rules for validity.
3
Recognize permissible deletion
Sometimes a premise or the conclusion is omitted in discourse because it is already known to the audience. This is not a logical flaw but a rhetorical economy.
The missing part can be supplied when needed.
4
Ensure valid termination
A demonstrative syllogism must rest on necessary, self-evident premises. It cannot involve circular reasoning (dawr) or an infinite regress of premises (tasalsul). The chain must terminate in certainty.
Otherwise the proof is not demonstrative.

Man'at Jam' (Preventer of Conjunction)
Defines and exemplifies man'at jam'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a man'at jam' disjunctive conditional and distinguish it from other types.

Core Idea
Man'at jam' allows both parts to be false but not both true.

The first type of disjunctive conditional is man'at jam', which prevents the antecedent and consequent from being true together. It corresponds to the logical 'not both' operation. In Islamic sciences, this type appears when two options are mutually exclusive but not exhaustive, e.g., a thing can be neither.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunctive statement
Find the word 'إما' (either) and the two parts (muqaddam and tali).
2
Check truth conditions
Can both parts be true together? If no, it may be man'at jam'. Can both be false? If yes, then it is man'at jam'.
Man'at jam' permits both false, so if both false is impossible, it is a different type.
3
Apply to the example
Take 'The body is either white or black' – it cannot be both white and black (conjunction prevented), but it could be red (both false possible). This is man'at jam'.

And it has three divisions: 1. Commentary
This passage classifies the disjunctive conditional into three distinct types based on what they prevent. The disjunctive conditional (shartiyya munfasila) generally indicates opposition, but the degree of opposition varies. The first type, man'at jam' (preventer of conjunction), prevents the two parts from being true together but allows both to be false. The example given is 'The body is either white or black'—it cannot be both white and black, but it could be another color like red (both false). The second type, man'at khulu (preventer of absence), prevents both parts from being false together (i.e., at least one must be true) but allows both to be true. The example is 'Zayd is either in the sea or he does not drown'—both false is impossible (if he is not in the sea, he does not drown; if he is in the sea, he might drown or not, but 'he does not drown' is true if he is not in the sea), but both can be true if he is in a boat (in the sea and not drowning). The third type, man'at jam' wal khulu (preventer of both conjunction and absence), prevents both conjunction (both true) and absence (both false), meaning exactly one must be true. The example is 'The number is either even or odd'—this is an exclusive disjunction. The teacher should note that these types correspond to logical connectives: type 1 is like 'not both', type 2 is like 'at least one' (inclusive or), type 3 is exclusive or.

The example given is 'The body is either white or black'—it cannot be both white and black, but it could be another color like red (both false). The second type, man'at khulu (preventer of absence), prevents both parts from being false together (i.e., at least one must be true) but allows both to be true. The example is 'Zayd is either in the sea or he does not drown'—both false is impossible (if he is not in the sea, he does not drown; if he is in the sea, he might drown or not, but 'he does not drown' is true if he is not in the sea), but both can be true if he is in a boat (in the sea and not drowning). The third type, man'at jam' wal khulu (preventer of both conjunction and absence), prevents both conjunction (both true) and absence (both false), meaning exactly one must be true. The example is 'The number is either even or odd'—this is an exclusive disjunction. The teacher should note that these types correspond to logical connectives: type 1 is like 'not both', type 2 is like 'at least one' (inclusive or), type 3 is exclusive or.

Core Idea
A syllogism is premises that necessarily entail a conclusion; it is either simple or compound.

Syllogism (qiyas) is the foundational inference form in Islamic logic. This lesson unpacks its definition and division into simple and compound types, as presented in the classical text al-Sullam. Divisions of Qiyas: Iqtirani and Sharti
Author divides qiyas into conjunctive and conditional, and defines qiyas iqtirani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a conjunctive (iqtirani) and a conditional (sharti) syllogism, and explain why the conclusion is not part of the syllogism itself.

Core Idea
Qiyas splits into two: conjunctive (implicit conclusion) and conditional (explicit conclusion), but the conclusion is concomitant, not a part.

In this passage, the author divides qiyas into two fundamental types: conjunctive and conditional. The key distinction lies in how the conclusion relates to the premises. You will learn to identify each type and understand a subtle but important logical point: the conclusion is a necessary concomitant of the syllogism, not a component of it. This insight helps clarify the structure of arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of qiyas
Look at the beginning: the author states qiyas is divided into iqtirani (conjunctive) and sharti (conditional). Note that the conditional will be discussed later.
2
Understand the definition of iqtirani
The conjunctive syllogism indicates the conclusion potentially (bi-l-quwwa): the conclusion's matter (meaning) is implicit, but its form is not stated. Example: 'The world is originated' as a conclusion from unstated premises.
The conclusion is not explicitly written out in its full form.
3
Contrast with sharti
The conditional syllogism indicates the conclusion actually (bi-l-fi'l): the conclusion is stated with both its matter and form, as in the example 'If this is human, it is animal; but it is human; therefore it is animal.'
The conclusion appears explicitly in the syllogism.
4
Grasp the author's critique
The author argues that the conclusion, even when mentioned, is not part of the syllogism. It is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of the premises, and a concomitant cannot be part of its necessitating thing (malzum).
This is a nuance: the conclusion is not a component but a result.
5
Apply to a concrete Islamic example
Consider a categorical syllogism from fiqh: 'Every intoxicant is haram; khamr is an intoxicant; therefore khamr is haram.' This is iqtirani because 'khamr is haram' is implicit in the premises, though we state it as conclusion. In contrast, a conditional syllogism like 'If it is khamr, it is haram; but it is khamr; therefore it is haram' explicitly states the conclusion.
The same conclusion appears in both, but the form of the syllogism differs.

Commentary
This passage presents the division of qiyas into two types: iqtirani (conjunctive) and sharti (conditional). The author defines the conjunctive syllogism as that which implies the conclusion potentially (bi-l-quwwa), meaning the conclusion is contained in the premises in terms of its matter but not its form, as in the earlier example 'the world is originated'. The conditional syllogism implies the conclusion actually (bi-l-fi'l), meaning the conclusion is explicitly stated in its matter and form, as in the example 'If this were a human, it would be an animal; but it is a human; therefore it is an animal.' The author then critiques this characterization: although the conclusion is mentioned in the conditional syllogism, it is not truly part of the syllogism because the conclusion is a necessary concomitant, not a component. He advises understanding this nuance. The conjunctive syllogism can be composed of categorical (hamli) and conditional propositions, though the matn notes it is predominantly categorical. Finally, he gives instructions on constructing the conjunctive syllogism in the valid manner.

Key Terms
القياس الاقتراني (al-qiyas al-iqtirani): The conjunctive syllogism, in which the conclusion is implied potentially, not explicitly stated in its form. [Note: In later texts, iqtirani is synonymous with categorical syllogism.]
القياس الشرطي (al-qiyas al-sharti): The conditional syllogism, in which the conclusion is mentioned explicitly with its matter and form. [Note: Also called istithna'i (exceptive) when it uses an exceptive premise.]
بالقوة (bi-l-quwwa): Potentially, meaning the conclusion is implicit in the premises.
بالفعل (bi-l-fi'l): Actually, meaning the conclusion is explicit.

Definition and Construction of the Categorical Syllogism (Qiyas Iqtirani)
Author defines qiyas iqtirani and outlines its four construction rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the categorical syllogism and apply its four construction rules to verify the validity of a syllogism.

Core Idea
A categorical syllogism contains its conclusion potentially, not actually; its construction requires a middle term, proper ordering, validity check, and subsumption.

This passage introduces the foundational concept of the categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani). Before learning complex jadal or munazara, one must understand what makes a syllogism count as a proof. The author first distinguishes it from the conditional syllogism, then lays down four practical rules for constructing a valid categorical syllogism. Pay close attention to the distinction between potential and actual indication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its genus
The passage defines 'qiyas iqtirani' as a type of qiyas. What is the genus (jins)? The genus is 'qiyas' (syllogism). The differentia is 'indicating the conclusion potentially'.
2
Distinguish potential vs actual indication
Compare 'dalla bi al-quwwa' (potential) with 'dalla bi al-fi'l' (actual). In a categorical syllogism, the conclusion is present only materially (terms) not formally (as a proposition). In a conditional syllogism, the conclusion appears both materially and formally.
3
Learn the four construction rules
The author enumerates four rules: (1) find a common middle term, (2) order premises (minor before major), (3) ensure both premises are true (validity), (4) ensure minor premise subsumes under major via middle term.
4
Apply rules to the given example
Take the example 'the world is changing, every changing thing is originated, therefore the world is originated'. Check: middle term = 'changing'; minor premise first; both premises true? subsumption? This validates the syllogism.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe on the definition and construction of the categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani). The author begins by defining it as a syllogism that indicates its conclusion potentially (bi al-quwwa), meaning the matter (madda) of the conclusion is present in the premises but not its form (sura). For example, in the syllogism 'the world is changing, and every changing thing is originated', the conclusion 'the world is originated' is present in the premises materially (the terms 'world' and 'originated') but not formally as a proposition. This distinguishes it from the conditional syllogism where the conclusion appears both materially and formally. The author then notes a subtle point: the conclusion is a necessary concomitant (lazim) of the syllogism, not a part of it, so it cannot literally be 'in' the syllogism as a component. After clarifying that the categorical syllogism can include conditional premises, the author provides four rules for its construction: (1) Finding a common middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) that relates the subject of the conclusion (minor term) to its predicate (major term). In the example, 'changing' (taghayyur) serves as the middle term. (2) Ordering the premises: the minor premise (containing the subject) must be placed before the major premise. (3) Ensuring validity: the conclusion is valid only if both premises are true; falsity in either premise invalidates the conclusion. (4) The minor premise must be subsumed under the major premise – i.e., the minor term must be included in the major term via the middle term. The passage ends by defining the minor, major, and middle terms: the minor term is the subject of the conclusion, the major term is the predicate, and the middle term is the one repeated in both premises and omitted in the conclusion.

Four Conditions of a Valid Syllogism
Author enumerates four conditions for a valid syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain each of the four conditions for a valid syllogism and verify whether a given syllogism satisfies them.

Core Idea
Validity depends on structure and truth: both premises must be true and properly linked.

In this passage, the author spells out four essential conditions that a syllogism must meet to be considered valid. These conditions ensure that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises. Mastering them is key to constructing sound arguments in Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition
The middle term must be a comprehensive description connecting the two extremes of the conclusion. In the example, 'change' links 'world' and 'originated'.
2
Identify the second condition
Proper ordering: the minor premise must precede the major premise. The footnote adds that both premises cannot be negative or particular (no syllogism results).
3
Identify the third condition
Truth of the premises. The conclusion is a necessary consequent of the premises; it is correct only if both premises are correct. A false premise invalidates the syllogism even if the conclusion is accidentally true.
4
Identify the fourth condition
Inclusion of the minor premise in the major premise: the subject of the major premise must encompass the subject of the minor premise. This ensures the middle term properly links them.
5
Apply the conditions to a new example
Try 'Every act of worship requires intention; prayer is an act of worship; therefore prayer requires intention.' Check each condition: Is there a comprehensive middle term? Are premises ordered? Are both true? Is there inclusion?

Defining Premises and Terms in a Syllogism
Author defines minor, major, and middle terms with an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the minor premise, major premise, minor term, major term, and middle term in any categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Terms are named by their role in the conclusion: subject is minor, predicate is major, and the connecting term is middle.

This passage not only gives conditions but also precisely defines each component of a syllogism. Knowing which premise is minor, which is major, and what makes a term 'minor' or 'major' is essential for analyzing arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
In the example, the conclusion is 'The world is originated'. Its subject is 'world' and its predicate is 'originated'.
The conclusion is the claim being proven.
2
Find the minor premise
The minor premise contains the subject of the conclusion. Here, 'The world is changing' contains 'world'.
3
Find the major premise
The major premise contains the predicate of the conclusion. Here, 'Everything changing is originated' contains 'originated'.
4
Locate the middle term
The middle term is the term repeated in both premises. Here, 'changing' appears in both.
The middle term is omitted in the conclusion.
5
Understand the naming rationale
The subject term is called 'minor' because it usually has fewer individuals; the predicate term is called 'major' because it usually has more. Both are called 'terms' (حدود) as endpoints of propositions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Figure is the skeleton; mood adds the flesh of quantifiers.

In this lesson, we tackle two foundational terms in Islamic logic: shakl (figure) and darb (mood). Understanding their relationship is essential for analyzing any syllogism, whether in kalam proofs or fiqh analogies. The passage defines each and shows how they differ.

Guided Path

Remove words like 'every', 'all', 'no', 'some' to see the bare form. The figure depends only on the position of the middle term, not on quantifiers.
Quantifiers determine the mood (darb), not the figure.

Determine the figure
Look at the position of the middle term in each premise. If it is predicate in the minor and subject in the major, it is first figure, etc.

Now consider the quantifiers (universal/particular, affirmative/negative). The combination gives the mood, e.g., 'Barbara' for universal affirmative in both premises.
Mood is more specific; many moods can exist within one figure.

The Four Figures of Syllogism
Author defines the four figures based on middle term position and illustrates with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the figure of a categorical syllogism based on the position of the middle term.

Core Idea
The four figures are distinguished solely by the position of the middle term.

In categorical syllogisms, the arrangement of the middle term in the two premises determines the figure. Understanding this arrangement is essential for evaluating whether a syllogism is valid or needs conversion. The author of Idah Mubham defines each figure with clear examples from everyday reasoning and from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three terms
Identify the minor term (subject of conclusion), major term (predicate of conclusion), and middle term (appears in both premises but not conclusion).
2
Locate the middle term in the minor premise
Check whether the middle term is subject or predicate in the minor premise.
3
Locate the middle term in the major premise
Check its position in the major premise.
4
Apply the four figure definitions
1st: predicate in minor, subject in major. 2nd: predicate in both. 3rd: subject in both. 4th: subject in minor, predicate in major.
5
Verify with an Islamic example
Take the author's example: 'Every prayer is an act of worship' (minor), 'Every act of worship requires intention' (major). The middle term 'act of worship' is predicate in minor, subject in major → first figure.

Commentary
This passage from Idah Mubham begins by announcing that the types of the figure (shakl) are four. The author then defines each figure based on the position of the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat) relative to the subject and predicate in the two premises. The first figure occurs when the middle term is predicate in the minor premise (sughra) and subject in the major premise (kubra). He gives the example: 'The world is changing' (minor) and 'Everything changing is originated' (major), which yields 'The world is originated'. This is a standard categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara). The second figure has the middle term predicate in both premises; example: 'The world is changing' and 'Nothing ancient is changing', which leads to 'The world is not ancient'. The third figure has the middle term subject in both; example: 'The world is changing' and 'The world is originated', which yields 'Something changing is originated'. The fourth figure is the reverse of the first: middle term subject in minor, predicate in major; example: 'The changing is originated' and 'The world is changing', producing 'The world is originated'. The author then notes that many authors use variables like J, B, A for brevity (e.g., 'Every J is B' and 'Every B is A') but he prefers concrete examples for clarity. He even suggests a clearer Islamic example: 'Every prayer is an act of worship, and every act of worship requires intention'. He states the order of perfection: first figure best, then second. The passage concludes that any syllogism not in one of these four forms is invalid, giving a corrupt example: 'Every human is an animal' and 'Every horse is a neighing animal' (no middle term connection). Finally, he notes that a later statement about the second figure is repetition for pedagogical clarity.

Productive Moods of the First Figure
Author explains number of possible moods per figure and conditions for first figure productivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions for a valid first-figure syllogism and list the four productive moods with their conclusion types.

Core Idea
In the first figure, the minor must be affirmative and the major universal; this yields exactly four valid moods.

This passage from Idah Mubham explains how many possible moods exist for each syllogistic figure (sixteen) and then focuses on the conditions that make a mood productive in the first figure. The commentator quotes poetic lines from the matn and then provides his own concise conditions and examples. By understanding these conditions, you will be able to distinguish valid first-figure syllogisms from invalid ones in any Islamic science argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the combinatorial basis
The author states that each figure has 16 possible moods because each premise has 4 states (universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, particular negative). Not all combinations are productive.
2
Extract the two conditions for the first figure
From the poetic lines and commentary: the minor premise must be affirmative, and the major premise must be universal. The minor can be particular or universal, but must be affirmative. The major can be affirmative or negative, but must be universal.
3
Enumerate the four productive moods
Combine the allowed states: minor can be universal affirmative (U.A.) or particular affirmative (P.A.); major can be universal affirmative (U.A.) or universal negative (U.N.). This gives four combinations: (1) U.A. + U.A., (2) U.A. + U.N., (3) P.A. + U.A., (4) P.A. + U.N.
4
Match each mood to its conclusion
For the first figure, the conclusion's quantity follows the minor (universal if minor is universal, particular if minor is particular) and the quality follows the major (affirmative if major is affirmative, negative if major is negative). Thus: Mood 1: Both U.A. → universal affirmative. Mood 2: U.A. + U.N. → universal negative. Mood 3: P.A. + U.A. → particular affirmative. Mood 4: P.A. + U.N. → particular negative.
5
Check the Islamic examples
The author gives two examples: Mood 1 (Barbara): 'Every human is an animal; every animal is sensitive; therefore every human is sensitive.' Mood 2 (Celarent): 'Every human is an animal; no animal is a stone; therefore no human is a stone.' Both meet the conditions and yield valid conclusions.

The Conclusion Follows the Inferior Premise
Explanation of the rule that the conclusion follows the inferior premise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the quality and quantity of a categorical syllogism's conclusion by applying the rule of following the inferior premise (al-akhass).

Core Idea
The conclusion always takes the weaker quality and quantity from the premises.

In a categorical syllogism, the conclusion is not a free choice: it must be 'inferior' to the premises in two ways. Inferiority means the lower rank: negation (salb) over affirmation (ījāb), and particular (juz’ī) over universal (kullī). The author of this passage explains exactly how this rule applies to the moods of the first figure, showing that the conclusion always mirrors the weakest premise present. Understanding this rule is essential for constructing valid syllogisms and avoiding unwarranted conclusions in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of inferiority
Recall that 'akhass' (inferior) has two dimensions: quality (negation is inferior to affirmation) and quantity (particular is inferior to universal). Spot both in any premise.
Negation = salb, particularity = juz’iyya.
2
Survey the premises for inferiorities
For each premise, note its quality (affirmative or negative) and quantity (universal or particular). Determine which is inferior in each dimension.
A premise can be inferior in one or both dimensions.
3
Apply the rule to the first figure moods
In the second mood (كل، لا), the second premise is negative → conclusion is universal negative. In the third mood (بعض، كل), the first premise is particular → conclusion is particular affirmative. In the fourth mood (بعض، لا), both inferiorities combine → conclusion is particular negative.
The first mood (كل، كل) has no inferiority → conclusion is universal affirmative.
4
Generalize the rule for any figure
No matter which figure, the conclusion must always match the weakest (inferior) quality and quantity found among the premises. If both inferiorities appear, the conclusion combines them.
This prevents conclusions that are 'superior' to any premise.
5
Check for circularity or infinite regress
The author reminds us that the chain of reasoning must end in necessity without dawr (circular reasoning) or tasalsul (infinite regress). A valid syllogism avoids both.
Circularity would mean assuming the conclusion in the premises; regress would mean an endless chain of syllogisms.

Identifying Deleted Parts in Syllogisms
Author enumerates types of deletion in syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which part of a syllogism (first premise, second premise, or conclusion) is omitted and reconstruct the missing element.

Core Idea
Syllogisms often omit obvious premises – knowing how to spot deletions unlocks elliptical arguments.

In everyday reasoning, we rarely state every step of a syllogism. Logicians call this 'hadhf' (deletion). This passage from al-Idah al-Mubham shows the three types of deletion with examples from fiqh and kalam. Understanding deletion lets you parse arguments in Islamic texts where the author assumes you can fill in the gaps.

Guided Path
1
Find the conclusion
Look for the statement that follows from the premises. It may be explicit or implied. In elliptical forms, the conclusion is sometimes dropped entirely.
Often introduced by 'fa' or understood from context.
2
Identify explicit premises
Locate the given statements. They are the building blocks of the argument. Note which terms (subject/predicate) appear.
Use the key terms from the passage.
3
Check for a missing minor premise
If the subject of the conclusion does not appear in the major premise, a minor premise connecting that subject to the middle term is missing.
Example: 'Grave-robber takes property secretly' is not a direct minor; the missing minor is 'grave-robber is a thief'.
4
Check for a missing major premise
If the predicate of the conclusion does not appear in the minor premise, a major premise connecting that predicate to the middle term is missing.
Example: 'Man is rational, therefore he is animal' – missing major 'Every rational is animal'.
5
Check for a missing conclusion
If premises are given without a concluding statement, the conclusion is deleted. Reconstruct it by combining the terms.
Example: 'The world is changing, everything changing is originated' – implied conclusion 'Therefore the world is originated'.

Commentary
This passage explains a practical feature of syllogistic reasoning: often, one premise or the conclusion is omitted due to obviousness. The author lists three cases of deletion. First, deletion of the minor premise: the example given is a legal argument about the grave-robber (al-nabash). The argument is: the grave-robber takes property secretly; every secret taker is a thief; every thief is amputated; therefore the grave-robber is amputated. Here, the minor premise 'the grave-robber is a thief' is omitted because it is inferred from the first two statements. Second, deletion of the major premise: the example 'Man is rational, therefore he is animal' omits the major premise 'Every rational being is an animal.' Third, deletion of the conclusion: the argument 'The world is changing, every changing thing is originated' is given as an answer to the question 'What is the proof of the world's origination?' The conclusion 'Therefore the world is originated' is understood but not stated. Finally, deletion of both a premise and the conclusion together is illustrated by the Quranic verse: if there were multiple gods, the heavens and earth would be corrupted; but they are not corrupted, so there cannot be multiple gods. The implied premise (the corruption is absent) and the conclusion (no other gods) are both omitted from the explicit expression. This demonstrates how ordinary reasoning often relies on implicit steps.

Premises Must End in Necessary Knowledge
Author argues premises must be necessary or terminate in necessary to avoid circularity or regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why every valid proof must terminate in self-evident premises to avoid circularity and infinite regress, and reconstruct the conditional syllogism used to prove God's necessary existence.

Core Idea
Every proof ends in self-evident premises, otherwise reasoning collapses.

In this lesson, we learn a crucial epistemological principle: the premises of a syllogism must eventually rest on knowledge that requires no further proof. The author explains that if all premises were theoretical, we would fall into circular reasoning or an infinite chain of proofs. He then provides two examples: a simple categorical syllogism about even numbers, and a complex conditional syllogism proving God's necessary existence. This foundation is essential for any valid argument in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about premises
The author states that premises must end in necessity (self-evidence). If they were theoretical, they would require further proof, leading to circularity or infinite regress.
Necessary premises are known without reflection.
2
Understand the two logical problems
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is an endless chain of proofs, and circularity (dawr) is when a premise depends back on itself. Both are impossible in sound reasoning.
3
Examine the first example: categorical syllogism
The premise 'Everything divisible into two equal parts is even' is a necessary truth, requiring no proof. This illustrates a simple case of a necessary premise.
The syllogism: 'Four is divisible into two equal parts; everything so divisible is even; therefore four is even.'
4
Trace the second example: conditional chain for God's existence
The argument uses a series of conditionals: if not necessary, then contingent; if contingent, then originated; if originated, then needs an originator; if needs an originator, then multiple gods; if multiple gods, then corruption. The self-evident premise is 'If multiple gods, then corruption.'
This chain terminates in a necessary premise from the Quran.
5
Verify the argument's validity
Since the corruption of heavens and earth is negated (they are ordered), the consequent is false, so the antecedent (multiple gods) is negated. This proves that the original assumption (not necessary) is false, thus establishing God's necessary existence.
The logical form is modus tollens applied to the chain.

Then, premises must necessarily end in necessity (self-evidence), such that understanding their meaning requires no reflection, because if they were theoretical, knowledge of them would depend on something else, and that other thing would require reflection, and so depend on something else... leading to circularity or infinite regress, whether we return to the first thing depended on or go on without end. So it is determined that premises must be necessary, or end in necessary premises. Example of the first: 'Four is divisible into two equal parts, and everything divisible into two equal parts is even, so four is even.' Example of the second: when we want to prove the necessity of His existence, the Exalted, we say by means of a conditional syllogism: 'If He, glorified, were not necessary of existence, He would be contingent; and if He were contingent, He would be originated; and if originated, He would need an originator; and if needing an originator, there would be multiple gods; and if multiple gods, the heavens and earth would be corrupted; but their corruption is negated, so what leads to it is negated, namely contingency of existence and what follows from it, so the necessary existence of the Exalted is established. Thus we end at a necessary premise, namely: if there were multiple gods, the heavens would be corrupted.'

Commentary
This passage addresses the epistemological foundation of syllogistic reasoning: the premises that constitute the starting points of proofs must be self-evident (ḍarūrī). The author argues that if premises were theoretical (naẓarī), they would require further proof, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr). Therefore, some premises must be immediately known without reflection. He gives two examples. The first is a simple categorical syllogism: 'Four is divisible into two equal parts; everything so divisible is even; therefore four is even.' The premise 'Everything divisible into two equal parts is even' is a necessary truth. The second example is a longer conditional syllogism used to prove God's necessary existence. The chain of conditionals leads to the conditional premise 'If there were multiple gods, the heavens and earth would be corrupted,' which is a necessary premise known from the Quran (the verse quoted earlier). Since the consequent (corruption) is false, the antecedent (multiple gods) is negated, and thus the impossibility of multiple gods is proven, leading to the necessity of God's existence. This example also shows how a complex argument ultimately rests on a self-evident premise.

Valid and Invalid Moods of the Connected Conditional
Defines the exceptional syllogism and explains valid moods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two valid moods of a connected conditional syllogism and distinguish them from the two invalid moods.

Core Idea
In a connected conditional, only affirming the antecedent or denying the consequent yield valid conclusions.

The exceptional syllogism is a direct inference tool: the conclusion follows immediately from a conditional major premise and a minor premise that either affirms or denies part of it. This passage focuses on the connected conditional form and clarifies which operations are logically sound and which are fallacious.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional major premise
Find the 'if...then' structure. Locate the antecedent (al-muqaddam) and the consequent (al-tali). For example: 'If this is a human, then it is an animal.'
The antecedent is 'this is a human'; the consequent is 'it is an animal'.
2
Determine the operation in the minor premise
Look for the minor premise that either affirms or denies the antecedent or the consequent. In the passage, examples include: 'but it is a human' (affirming antecedent) and 'but it is not an animal' (denying consequent).
3
Apply the two valid rules
Rule 1: If the minor premise affirms the antecedent, conclude the consequent (modus ponens). Rule 2: If the minor premise denies the consequent, conclude the denial of the antecedent (modus tollens).
These are the only two valid moods for a connected conditional.
4
Recognize the invalid operations
Affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent are invalid. For example, from 'it is an animal' you cannot validly conclude 'it is a human', because the consequent could be true for other reasons. Likewise, from 'it is not a human' you cannot conclude 'it is not an animal', because the antecedent is only a sufficient condition, not necessary.
The passage explicitly warns: 'the reverse of them is not entailed' – i.e., affirming the consequent and denying the antecedent are not valid.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the exceptional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i), focusing on its connected conditional form. The author begins by quoting the matn lines and then expands on them. The core idea: the exceptional syllogism yields the conclusion directly (bi al-fi'l), unlike the conjunctive syllogism which yields it only potentially (bi al-quwwa). The matn states that if the conditional is connected (ittisali), then affirming the antecedent (wad' al-muqaddam) produces the consequent, and denying the consequent (raf' al-tali) produces the denial of the antecedent. The reverse—affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent—does not yield a valid conclusion. The commentary elaborates with examples. For instance, from the conditional 'If this is a human, then it is an animal', affirming the antecedent ('it is a human') gives 'it is an animal'; denying the consequent ('it is not an animal') gives 'it is not a human'. Affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent are invalid: affirming the consequent commits the fallacy of affirming the consequent, and denying the antecedent commits the fallacy of denying the antecedent. The commentator explains the reasoning: the consequent is a necessary outcome (lazim) of the antecedent (malzum), but affirming the consequent does not guarantee the antecedent (other causes could produce it), and denying the antecedent does not negate the consequent (the consequent could still be true for other reasons). This is a foundational lesson for understanding valid inference in conditional logic, crucial for legal and theological reasoning.
