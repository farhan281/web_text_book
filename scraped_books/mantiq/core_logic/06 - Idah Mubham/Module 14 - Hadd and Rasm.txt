Types of Definitions: Hadd, Rasm, and Lafzi
Enumerates the three types of definitions and their validity conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify definitions as hadd, rasm, or lafzi based on their components and evaluate their validity using the six conditions.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be coextensive, convertible, clear, non-circular, and free from homonymy and rulings.

This lesson introduces the three types of definitions recognized in Islamic logic: essential (hadd), descriptive (rasm), and verbal (lafzi). The passage also lays down the conditions that any valid definition must meet, such as being coextensive (muttarid) and convertible (mun'akis). These tools are essential for precise conceptual analysis in every Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three types
From the Arabic, identify that definitions are divided into three: hadd (using genus and differentia), rasm (using genus and property), and lafzi (replacing an unfamiliar word with a known synonym).
Memorize the Arabic terms: حَدّ, رَسْم, لَفْظِي
2
Differentiate complete and deficient forms
A complete hadd uses both a near genus and a differentia; a deficient hadd uses only a differentia or a distant genus. Similarly, a complete rasm uses a near genus and a property; a deficient rasm uses only a property or a more distant genus.
'Distant genus' means a broader category that is not the immediate genus.
3
List the six conditions for a valid definition
The passage states that every definition must be: (1) muttarid (coextensive – applies to all instances of the definiendum), (2) mun'akis (convertible – applies only to the definiendum), (3) zahir (clear, not obscure), (4) not circular (not defined by something known through the defined), (5) not a homonym without a contextual clue, and (6) not including rulings (ahkam).
Condition 6 means a definition should describe the essence, not prescribe legal judgments.
4
Apply the conditions to a sample definition
Take the definition 'Human is a rational animal'. Check: it applies to all humans (muttarid) and only to humans (mun'akis); it is clear; it does not use the defined term; it is not a homonym; it does not include rulings. Therefore it is a valid complete hadd.

Commentary
This passage classifies definitions (mu'arrafat) into three types: hadd (essential definition), rasm (descriptive definition), and lafzi (verbal definition). Tamm (complete) hadd is composed of genus and differentia, e.g., 'human is a rational animal'. Tamm rasm is composed of genus and property, e.g., 'human is a laughing animal'. Naqis (incomplete) hadd is with only differentia or with a distant genus (but not near). Naqis rasm is with only property or with a more distant genus. Lafzi definition is replacing an unfamiliar word with a more famous synonym, e.g., defining 'nadir' as 'rare'. Then it lists conditions for a valid definition: (1) muttarid (coextensive) — applies to all instances of the definiendum; (2) mun'akis (convertible) — applies only to it, not to others; (3) zahir (clear) — not obscure; (4) not using something known by the defined (avoid circularity); (5) not using a homonym without a contextual clue; (6) not including rulings (ahkam) in definitions, i.e., definitions should be descriptive, not prescriptive.

Types of Definitions: Hadd, Rasm, and Lafzi
Author classifies definitions into three types and explains their conditions and invalid forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given definition as hadd, rasm, or lafzi, and identify whether it satisfies the standard conditions.

Core Idea
Definitions are three types: essential by genus+differentia, descriptive by genus+property, and verbal synonym.

In Islamic logic, a definition (al-mu'arraf) is not just any explanation—it must be precise and rule-governed. This lesson introduces the three main types: complete essential definition (hadd tamm), descriptive definition (rasm), and simple verbal substitution (lafzi). You will also learn the conditions every definition must meet to be valid, such as being coextensive and convertible. Understanding these distinctions is essential for rigorous thinking in the Islamic sciences, where definitions form the foundation of arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Read the first two lines of the Arabic poem. They say the definiendum (mu'arraf) is divided into three: hadd, rasm, and lafzi. Memorise these categories.
Hadd is the highest form; lafzi is the simplest.
2
Extract the components of each type
For hadd, the text states it is 'by genus and differentia' (bil-jins wa fadl). For rasm, it is 'by genus and property' (bil-jins wa khassa). For lafzi, it is replacement by a more famous synonym.
Differentia (fasl) is essential; property (khassa) is accidental.
3
Recognise incomplete varieties
The poem mentions incomplete hadd (naqis al-hadd): either by differentia alone or with a remote genus. Incomplete rasm: by property alone or with a more remote genus. Learn these as common pitfalls.
A remote genus is not the immediate genus.
4
Apply the conditions for validity
The text lists conditions: every definition must be muttarid (coextensive), mun'akis (convertible), clearer than the definiendum, not circular, not including rulings (ahkam), and not ambiguous without a qarina. Check a definition against these.
These conditions ensure the definition is useful and non-deceptive.

The Rule of 'Or' in Definitions and Descriptions
Author states the rule that 'or' is impermissible in definitions but permissible in descriptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when the particle 'or' may be used in a definition (hadd) versus when it must be avoided, and apply this rule to Islamic definitions.

Core Idea
A definition must be exclusive; 'or' breaks exclusivity, but descriptions may list alternatives.

This short passage from the matn delivers a crisp rule: the particle 'or' (أو) has no place in definitions (hudud) but is acceptable in descriptions (rusum). Understanding this distinction is critical for constructing precise, unambiguous definitions in logic and Islamic sciences. The author urges you to memorize this transmitted rule and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical purpose of a definition (hadd)
A complete definition uniquely identifies a concept by its genus and differentia. It must be exclusive: every instance of the definiendum fits, and nothing else fits. Any ambiguity would defeat this purpose.
Recall that a hadd tamm requires a single differentia that distinguishes the species from all others.
2
Recognize the problem with 'or' in definitions
Using 'or' in a definition introduces ambiguity. For example, 'A human is a rational animal or a featherless biped' would allow two different kinds, breaking exclusivity. Thus 'or' is impermissible.
The passage explicitly forbids 'or' in hudud.
3
Contrast with descriptions (rasm)
A description may list multiple essential or accidental attributes. Since it does not claim to capture the essence uniquely, 'or' can be used to offer alternatives. For example, describing water as 'a liquid or a drink' is permissible.
The passage explicitly allows 'or' in rusum.
4
Apply the rule to an Islamic example
Consider a definition of 'kufr' (disbelief) in kalam: 'Kufr is denial of Allah or rejection of the Prophet.' Since it uses 'or', this would be a description (rasm), not a true definition (hadd). A proper definition would identify a single essential differentia.
This shows how the rule applies in theological discourse.

Terms for Concepts and Assents: Qawl Sharih and Hujja
Defines the technical terms 'qawl sharih' for conveying conception and 'hujja' for conveying assent, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qawl sharih and hujja as technical terms in Islamic logic, and identify examples of each from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Qawl sharih gives you a concept; hujja gives you a proof.

In logic, expressions serve different functions. Some convey a single concept, like a definition; others convey an assertion, like a syllogism. This lesson introduces the technical terms for these two functions: qawl sharih (explicatory statement) and hujja (proof). Understanding these terms is essential for grasping how logicians classify arguments and definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two functions of expressions
Read the passage and note that logicians distinguish between expressions that convey a single concept (tasawwur) and those that convey assent (tasdiq).
2
Define qawl sharih
According to the passage, qawl sharih is an expression that conveys a single concept. The example given is 'rational animal' in the definition of man, which as a whole conveys the single concept 'man'.
This includes both definitions (hadd) and descriptions (rasm).
3
Define hujja
Hujja is an expression that conveys assent. The passage equates it with a syllogism (qiyas) and gives the example: 'the world is changing, everything changing is originated, therefore the world is originated.'
In logic, hujja specifically means a syllogistic argument, not just any evidence.
4
Contrast the two using examples
Notice that the qawl sharih example ('rational animal') is a definition leading to a concept, while the hujja example (the syllogism) leads to a propositional conclusion. Practise by classifying other expressions from Islamic sciences.
A single proposition is not a hujja; a hujja must be an argument composed of premises and a conclusion.

Author defines the three word-to-meaning relations with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalalat al-mutabaqa, al-tadammun, and al-iltizam by identifying which aspect of meaning a word indicates in a given example from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
A word can indicate its full meaning, a part of it, or an external necessary consequence.

This passage from Idah al-Mubham introduces the foundational distinction between three ways a word conveys meaning. Understanding these types is essential for accurate interpretation of Qur'anic terms and juristic definitions. We will break down each type with the author's examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Read the Arabic passage and locate the definitions for المطابقة, التضمن, and الالتزام. Notice that each is defined by what the word indicates: the whole meaning, a part, or an external necessary.
The author also provides the etymology of the names.
2
Analyze the example for each type
For المطابقة: 'الإنسان' indicates the complete meaning 'حيوان ناطق'. For التضمن: the same word indicates only 'حيوان' or 'ناطق' within that whole. For الالتزام: 'الإنسان' indicates 'قبول العلم' and 'صنعة الكتابة', which are external but necessary.
Note that the necessary (لازم) must be in the mind, not necessarily in external existence.
3
Understand the naming rationale
المطابقة because understanding matches the coinage; التضمن because the part is included in the whole; الالتزام because the meaning is external but necessarily attached.
This helps remember which is which.
4
Apply the condition for iltizam
The passage states that اللازم must be necessary in the mind (لازم في الذهن). Distinguish between mental necessity only (e.g., sight is necessary for blindness, though externally impossible) and external necessity (e.g., evenness for four).
This condition prevents overextension of iltizam.

Entailment and Exclusivity Among the Three Denotations
Author explains how the three denotations entail each other and when they combine or occur alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to predict which types of denotation (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) apply alone or together given the simplicity/compositeness of the meaning and presence of a mental necessary.

Core Idea
Tadammun and iltizam always entail mutabaqa, but mutabaqa does not entail them.

We have learned the definitions of the three denotations. Now we explore how they relate. The author clarifies when each one occurs alone and when they combine. This understanding is crucial for accurately interpreting the meaning of words in Islamic texts, particularly in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether the meaning is simple or composite
A simple meaning (ma'na basit) has no parts, like 'point'. A composite meaning (ma'na murakkab) has parts, like 'human' composed of 'animal' and 'rational'. Tadammun requires a composite meaning because it indicates a part.
If the meaning is simple, tadammun cannot occur.
2
Identify whether the meaning has a mental necessary
A mental necessary (lazim dhihni) is a concept necessarily implied by the meaning in the mind. For example, 'human' necessarily implies 'capable of knowledge'. Iltizam requires such a mental necessary. Note that purely external necessities (e.g., blackness of crow) are not counted by logicians.
If there is no mental necessary, iltizam cannot occur.
3
Determine if mutabaqa is entailed
Mutabaqa (correspondence to the whole meaning) is always present when tadammun or iltizam is present. However, mutabaqa can occur without them. So mutabaqa is necessary but not sufficient for the other two.
Mutabaqa alone occurs when the meaning is simple and has no mental necessary.
4
Enumerate the four possible scenarios
The passage outlines four cases: (1) Composite with mental necessary → both tadammun and iltizam (and mutabaqa). (2) Composite without mental necessary → tadammun alone (plus mutabaqa). A term is compound only if its parts contribute to its meaning in an intended way.

In logic, not every multi-word expression is compound. Logicians impose four precise conditions to distinguish genuine compounds from mere aggregates. This lesson breaks down each condition with examples from Arabic and Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Condition 1: Presence of parts
Check if the term has parts. Single letters like hamzat al-istifhām have no parts, so they are singular.

2

Check if each part signifies something. Proper names like 'Zayd' have parts (letters) but those letters do not signify meanings, so 'Zayd' is singular.

Check if the part's meaning is part of the overall intended meaning of the term. For 'Abd Allāh' as a person's name, the parts mean 'servant' and 'God', but the intended meaning is the specific person, not a composite of those meanings. Hence it fails this condition.
The term's meaning must be composed of its parts' meanings.

Intentionality ensures that the composite meaning is actively used.

Commentary
This passage lays out four conditions that a linguistic expression must satisfy to be considered a compound (مركب) in logical analysis. The first condition is that the expression must have parts; a single letter like the hamza of interrogation is not compound. The second condition is that those parts must signify something; proper names like Zayd, even if they have parts (letters), do not have meaningful parts (the letters Z, a, y, d individually have no meaning), so they remain singular. Core Idea
A complete definition includes both genus and differentia/property; an incomplete one misses one crucial element.

After learning the three main types, we now explore their subcategories: each can be complete (tām) or incomplete (nāqiṣ). The passage details how an incomplete hadd lacks a near genus or uses only the differentia, while an incomplete rasm uses only the property. List the four subcategories

An incomplete rasm is only by property alone, without a genus. For example, 'the being that laughs' for man (laughter is a property, but no genus is given). This does not provide a complete picture.
A complete rasm requires both genus and property.

Complete and Incomplete Description (Rasm) and Verbal Definition
Author explains complete/incomplete description and verbal definition with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a description (rasm) and a definition (hadd), and identify the components of complete and incomplete rasm, as well as verbal definition.

Core Idea
Rasm replaces the essential differentia with a non-essential property; verbal definition merely substitutes a synonym.

This lesson covers the remaining three types: complete rasm (proximate genus + property), incomplete rasm (property alone or with distant genus), and verbal definition (synonym substitution). Examples include 'laughing animal' for human (complete rasm) and 'laughing' alone (incomplete rasm). Verbal definition is illustrated with 'ghadanfar' replaced by 'asad'.

Guided Path
1
Recognize that rasm uses a property
A property (khassa) is a non-essential but distinctive attribute, e.g., laughing for human. Understand that rasm does not capture the essence but still identifies the thing.
2
Identify complete rasm
Complete rasm requires both the proximate genus and a property. Example: 'laughing animal' for human – genus 'animal', property 'laughing'.
3
Identify incomplete rasm
Incomplete rasm uses only the property ('laughing') or with a distant genus ('laughing body'). It provides less information.
4
Understand verbal definition
This is simply replacing a less known word with a more famous synonym, e.g., 'ghadanfar' with 'asad' (lion). It does not analyze the thing itself.
It is used for clarifying vocabulary, not for conceptual analysis.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid definition must be coextensive, convertible, clear, not too distant, not too broad, and metaphor only with a clarifying context.

In Islamic logic, definitions must be precise to avoid ambiguity and error in reasoning. This passage presents six essential conditions that any correct definition must satisfy. Understanding these conditions equips you to critically evaluate definitions found in fiqh, usul, and kalam, ensuring they are both accurate and useful for drawing conclusions.

Locate the six conditions
Read the verse line (46-47) and identify each condition mentioned. The Arabic terms are: مطرد, منعكس, ظاهر, لا أبعدا, لا مساويا, ولا تجوزا بلا قرينة.
The verse is compact; the commentary expands each condition.
2
Understand 'mutarid' and 'mun'akis'
Mutarid means the definition applies to every instance of the defined (no omissions). Mun'akis means if the definition holds, the defined holds (no overreach). Together they ensure one-to-one correspondence.
Think of it as a mutual implication between definiendum and definiens.
3
Grasp 'zahir', 'la ab'ada', 'la musawiya'
Zahir: the definition must be clearer than the defined. La ab'ada: the genus should not be too remote (e.g., defining 'human' as 'substance' is too distant). La musawiya: the definition must not be broader than the defined (e.g., 'animal' for 'human' is too broad).
These ensure the definition actually clarifies rather than obscures.
4
Analyze the metaphor condition
Metaphorical definitions (majaz) are prohibited unless accompanied by a qarina that specifies the intended meaning. The passage gives examples: defining a scholar as 'a sea that enters the bathhouse' is invalid without qarina, but defining a dullard as 'a braying animal that enters the bathhouse' is valid because 'braying animal' functions as qarina.
The qarina must be explicit and unambiguous.

Consider the definition of 'ijma': 'agreement of the mujtahids of the ummah after the Prophet's death on a religious ruling'. Clarity and Non-Circularity in Definitions
Author explains conditions 2 and 4: definition must be apparent and not circular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify definitions that are invalid because they are more obscure than the defined or because they are circular.

Core Idea
A definition must be clearer than the defined and must not rely on the defined itself.

Two additional conditions ensure definitions actually clarify rather than confuse: the definition must be more apparent (azhar) than the defined, and it must not be circular. Let's examine these with examples from the text.

1
Identify the defined term and its proposed definition.

2

3

Avoiding Metaphor and Ambiguity: The Role of Qarina
Author explains conditions 3 and 5: no metaphor or ambiguity without qarina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition becomes invalid due to metaphor or ambiguity and determine whether a qarina can remedy it.

Core Idea
Metaphorical and ambiguous words are only allowed in definitions if accompanied by a clarifying qarina.

A definition must be precise in its wording. Using figurative language (majaz) or homonyms (mushtarak) introduces uncertainty unless a contextual indicator (qarina) pins down the intended meaning. The text explains these conditions with examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the definition uses metaphorical language.
Check if any word is used in a non-literal sense, e.g., 'donkey' for a dullard.
2
Look for a qarina.
Is there a contextual indicator that specifies the intended meaning? E.g., 'a donkey that writes' clarifies the metaphorical use.
3
Identify if the definition uses an ambiguous word (mushtarak).
Check for words with multiple meanings, e.g., 'ayn' can mean eye, spring, or the sun.
4
Check for a qarina with ambiguous words.
Without qarina, the definition is invalid. With a qarina like 'the luminous 'ayn'', it becomes valid.

Core Idea
In definitions, including a ruling causes circularity, and using 'or' of doubt introduces ambiguity – both are forbidden in essential definitions.

1
Identify the two prohibited elements

The term 'or' (أو) has different functions – doubt vs. division.
2

Consider defining 'al-fā'il' as 'the noun in the nominative case'. The nominative case is a ruling that depends on understanding what a subject is. If the definition includes this ruling, the definition depends on the ruling, which depends on the defined – creating a vicious circle (dawr).

In descriptive definitions (rusūm), 'or' is generally permissible, as in defining man as 'the laughing animal, or capable of knowledge and writing'.

The poet's statement about the hopeful one hoping for certain things from Allah. The commentator says these hopes are justified because of Allah's generosity.
2
Find the reason (ʿilla)
The phrase 'for He is the most generous' is explicitly given as the cause. Understand that this is a logical argument: because Allah is generous, He will bestow forgiveness and reward.
3
Analyze the superlative 'akram'
The commentator notes that the superlative is not used in its comparative sense because generosity is exclusive to Allah. This shows the commentator's attention to linguistic precision.
4
Observe the order: forgiveness then reward
The commentator notes that seeking forgiveness comes first, then seeking reward. This reflects the sequence of first removing bad qualities (takhliya) and then acquiring good qualities (tahliya). This is a common spiritual framework.
5
Apply the principle
Recognize that in Islamic spirituality, purification from sins precedes the acquisition of virtues.

And 'al-murtajī' means the hopeful one. And 'al-mannān' is an intensive form from al-mann, which is counting favors, and it is praiseworthy from Allah, blameworthy from creation. And 'al-maghfirah' means covering; the meaning of its encompassing sins is covering all of them. And the removal of the covering from hearts is an expression of the removal of the rust from them. And al-thawāb is the recompense of action, and action for the sake of reward is not blameworthy, although action for the sake of Allah Almighty as magnification of Him is more complete. And His saying: 'For He is the most generous...' etc., is the cause for His saying: 'al-murtajī' up to here, meaning: I only hoped from Him these things because He is the most generous of those who bestow them, and the superlative is not used in its proper sense, since generosity truly belongs only to Him, Exalted. And it is not hidden what is in seeking forgiveness first, and seeking reward second, of purification and adornment.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a line of the didactic poem explaining key terms. The author defines 'al-murtajī' as the hopeful one, 'al-mannān' as the one who bestows favors, noting that counting favors is praiseworthy when done by Allah but blameworthy from people. 'Al-maghfirah' is defined as covering, and its encompassing sins means covering all of them. The removal of the covering from hearts is the removal of rust (ran), a term from Quran (83:14). 'Al-thawāb' is defined as the reward of action; performing action for reward is not blameworthy, but doing it purely for Allah is more complete. The commentator then explains the logical connection in the poem: the phrase 'for He is the most generous' is given as a reason ('illa) for hoping for these things. He notes that the superlative 'akram' is not literal because generosity in reality belongs to Allah alone. Finally, he observes the order of seeking forgiveness first (removing the bad, takhliya) and then reward (adornment, tahliya) is not hidden in its wisdom.
