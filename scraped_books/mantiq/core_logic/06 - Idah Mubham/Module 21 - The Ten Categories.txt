Simple vs. Compound Expressions: The Core Definition
Author defines simple and compound expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given Arabic expression is mufrad or murakkab according to the logical criteria of intentional semantic composition.

Core Idea
In logic, simplicity is not about word count but about whether parts intentionally signify parts of the meaning.

The author begins by dividing all 'used' expressions into simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). This is a foundational classification in Islamic logic because it determines how we analyze propositions. Let's understand the precise criteria and apply them to familiar examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division
First note that the passage divides used expressions into two: mufrad and murakkab. The definition is given: mufrad is that whose part does not indicate a part of its meaning, while murakkab is that whose part does.
The author disregards insignificant expressions (muhmal).
2
Examine the example of mufrad
The example is 'Zayd'. Why is it simple? Because the parts 'Zay' and 'd' carry no independent meaning. The whole word points to one person without compositional structure.
3
Examine the example of murakkab
The example is 'Zayd is standing'. Here 'Zayd' indicates the subject part and 'standing' indicates the predicate part. Together they form a complete propositional meaning.
The indication of the part to the whole's meaning must be intentional.
4
Apply to other expressions
Consider 'Abdullah' as a proper name. Superficially it has two parts, but logically it is simple because the parts do not intentionally indicate parts of the meaning of the name. The name designates a specific person, not a combination of 'servant' and 'Allah'.
This is expanded in the footnotes.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional commentary that defines simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab) expressions, a foundational division in logic. The commentator begins by distinguishing between insignificant (muhmal) and used (musta'mal) expressions, noting that only the latter are considered. Then the used expression is divided into simple and compound. The simple is defined as that whose part does not indicate a part of its meaning, exemplified by 'Zayd' — a proper name whose components (Zay and d) carry no independent meaning. The compound, conversely, is that whose part does indicate a part of its meaning, like 'Zayd is standing' where 'Zayd' indicates the subject and 'standing' indicates the predicate. The commentator then cites two footnotes expanding this definition. The first footnote explains why logicians discuss expressions — not for their own sake, but because we need language for thought and communication. The second footnote (from al-Jundi) clarifies that the definition of 'simple' is not merely a matter of having no parts; it is more nuanced. It provides four types of expressions that are actually simple despite appearances: (1) expressions with no parts, like particles; (2) expressions with parts that carry no meaning; (3) expressions like 'Abdullah' where parts have meaning but that meaning is not part of the intended meaning; (4) expressions like 'Hayawān Nāṭiq' used as a proper name, where parts accidentally indicate meanings but not intentionally. The third footnote (also al-Jundi) provides four conditions for an expression to be truly compound: it must have a part, that part must indicate a meaning, that meaning must be part of the expression's intended meaning, and that indication must be intended by the speaker. These conditions rule out the four types mentioned. Thus the commentary ensures the student understands that logical composition is intentional semantic composition, not mere physical or conventional composition.

Key Terms
مفرد (mufrad): An expression whose part does not indicate a part of its meaning, including proper names and particles [Note: Stable across the tradition]
مركب (murakkab): An expression whose part intentionally indicates a part of its meaning, as in a proposition [Note: Stable across the tradition]
لفظ (lafẓ): Uttered expression; here divided into muhmal (insignificant) and musta'mal (used) [Note: No significant shift]

Four Conditions for Compound Expressions
Author defines simple and compound expressions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four conditions to determine whether an expression is truly compound (murakkab) and avoid common misclassifications.

Core Idea
A compound expression must fulfill four conditions: having a part that indicates a part of the meaning intentionally.

The commentator (al-Jundi) systematically extracts four conditions from the definition of compound expressions. These conditions serve as a checklist to exclude apparent compounds like proper names 'Abdullah' and 'Hayawān Nāṭiq'. Let's explore each condition with examples.

Guided Path
1
List the four conditions
The passage enumerates four conditions: (1) the expression has a part, (2) that part indicates a meaning, (3) that meaning is part of the intended meaning of the whole, (4) the indication is intended by speaker and listener.
These conditions are explicitly derived from the definition.
2
Apply condition 1: presence of a part
Expressions like hamzat al-istifham (the interrogative hamza) have no parts, so they are simple. Condition 1 excludes them from being compound.
3
Apply condition 2: part indicates a meaning
Names like 'Zayd' have parts ('Zay' and 'd') but they carry no independent meaning. Thus condition 2 fails, making them simple.
4
Apply condition 3: part indicates a part of the intended meaning
Consider 'Abdullah' as a proper name. Its parts 'Abd' and 'Allah' each have meaning, but that meaning is not part of the name's intended meaning (which designates a specific person). So condition 3 fails.
The name does not mean 'servant of Allah' in use; it is a label.
5
Apply condition 4: indication is intended
Names like 'Hayawān Nāṭiq' as a person's name have parts that accidentally indicate 'animal' and 'rational', but this indication is not intended by the speaker when referring to the person. Thus condition 4 fails, making it simple.
The parts are semantically irrelevant to the naming.
