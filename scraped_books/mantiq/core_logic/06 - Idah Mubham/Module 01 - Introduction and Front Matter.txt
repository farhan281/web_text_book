Interpreting a Title Page in Islamic Logic
Title page showing author, title, and publisher of a commentary on al-Sullam al-Munawraq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the matn, sharh, author, and commentator from a classical Islamic logic title page and explain the significance of the scholarly chain.

Core Idea
A title page is a scholarly passport: it reveals the text's lineage, genre, and academic context.

A title page may seem like mere bibliographic data, but in the Islamic tradition, it encodes the transmission of knowledge. The page you will study introduces Idah al-Mubham, a commentary on al-Sullam al-Munawraq. By learning to read such a page, you gain insight into how logical works were taught, commented upon, and passed down through generations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text (matn)
Look for the phrase 'شرح على متن' (commentary on the text). The work being commented on is 'السلم المنورق' (al-Sullam al-Munawraq) by al-Akhdari. This is the core primer in logic.
The matn is usually a concise, versified or prose summary of a discipline.
2
Identify the commentary (sharh)
The title 'إيضاح المبهم' (Clarification of the Obscure) is the name of the commentary. The author of this sharh is Ahmad al-Damanhuri, as stated. Note the birth and death dates (1101–1192 AH/1689–1778 CE).
Dates help place the commentator in historical context and may indicate which later commentaries he might have influenced.
3
Analyze the scholarly chain
The title page establishes a chain: al-Akhdari (matn author) → al-Damanhuri (commentator) → Mustafa Abu Zayd (editor of this edition). Each link represents a layer of transmission and interpretation.
In Islamic scholarship, such chains (isnad) are crucial for verifying authenticity and understanding school affiliation.
4
Note any textual anomalies
The first line 'الصاح المبهرة من معالي السالم' appears corrupt; it likely was 'إيضاح المبهم من معالي السلام' or similar. Be cautious when relying on OCR-read text; always cross-check with a physical copy.
Corruption in front matter can mislead. Compare multiple editions if possible.

Distinguishing Absolute and Restricted Praise
Analysis of the two forms of praise in the opening

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hamd mutlaq and hamd muqayyad based on sentence type, implication, and reward.

Core Idea
The two types of praise differ in form (nominal vs verbal) and entail different rewards (recommended vs obligatory).

The commentator begins by analyzing the author's opening praise, which is expressed in two forms: absolute praise using a nominal sentence and restricted praise using a verbal sentence. This distinction is not arbitrary; it carries theological significance regarding permanence, renewal, and the type of reward. Understanding this helps you appreciate the precision of the author and the commentator's method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of praise
Find where the commentator mentions 'حمدًا مطلقًا' and 'حمدًا مقيدًا'. Note that the first is a nominal sentence (indicating permanence) and the second is a verbal sentence (indicating renewal).
The first praise is for Allah Himself, the second is for the blessing of inspiration.
2
Determine the implications of each sentence type
A nominal sentence implies continuity and permanence, while a verbal sentence implies renewal and temporality. The commentator explains that the second praise uses a verbal sentence because the blessings (ni'am) are ever-renewing.
3
Identify the rewards associated with each
The commentator states that the first praise yields a recommended reward (mandub) and the second an obligatory reward (wajib). This is because the first praise is a recommended act of gratitude, while the second is obligatory as a response to the inspiration.
4
Understand the reason for the second praise
The second praise is performed to express gratitude for the inspiration to offer the first praise. The commentator notes that even the inspiration is a blessing requiring thankfulness.

Precise Definitions of Iman and Islam in Theology
Definitions of iman and islam with logical structure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the genus and differentia in the definitions of iman and islam as given in this commentary.

Core Idea
The definitions of iman and islam are logically structured with a genus and differentia, ensuring theological precision.

In the passage, the commentator provides clear definitions of iman and islam: iman is the heart's affirmation of what the Prophet brought, and islam is outward actions like prayer and fasting. These definitions follow the logical structure of hadd (definition) with a genus and differentia. Understanding this helps us see how theologians construct precise concepts and avoid confusion.

The genus is 'تصديق القلب' (affirmation of the heart). The differentia is 'بما جاء به النبي' (with what the Prophet brought). Together they form a complete definition (hadd haqiqi).
The genus 'affirmation' distinguishes iman from other acts, and the differentia specifies its content.

The genus is 'الأفعال الظاهرة' (outward actions). The differentia is implicit; the examples (prayer, fasting) indicate that these are specific prescribed actions. This is a description (rasm) rather than a complete definition.
The commentator adds that iman and islam are legally inseparable (متلازمان شرعًا), which is an additional theological point.
4
Reflect on the completeness of each definition
The definition of iman is complete: it includes all instances of iman and excludes non-iman. The definition of islam is incomplete as a hadd because it does not specify differentia that excludes other outward actions, but it suffices as a description.
The commentator does not evaluate the definitions, but we can assess them using logical criteria.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every Islamic scholarly work has a precise birth date, and its closing prayers carry profound meaning.

This final passage of Idah al-Mubham gives us the exact date the poem was composed and ends with a prayer for benefit. Knowing how to read such date formulas is essential for any student of Islamic texts, as it places the work in its historical context. Guided Path
1
Identify the date formula
The poet says the composition was in the early days of Muharram, from the year 'one and forty after nine hundred'. Note that the Arabic uses a reversed formulation: 'nine hundred' is the base, then 'one and forty' is added. This is a common way of expressing years in later Islamic texts.
Pay attention to word order: 'تسعة من المينة' = nine hundred, then 'إحدى وأربعين' = one and forty.
2
Calculate the year
Add the numbers: 900 (nine hundred) + 41 (one and forty) = 941 AH. So the work was completed in Muharram 941 AH, which corresponds to roughly July 1534 CE.
The commentator confirms this interpretation.
3
Understand the closing prayers
The author prays for perpetual blessings on the Prophet, his family, and companions, and asks Allah to make the work beneficial. This is a standard closing in Islamic scholarly works, reflecting the belief that all benefit comes from Allah.
The phrase 'as long as the sun cuts through the towers' is a poetic expression of perpetuity, showing the timelessness of the blessings.
4
Link to the broader context
Recognize that such colophons provide critical biographical data and connect the work to the author's life and times. This date allows us to situate the work historically and understand its place in the intellectual tradition.
