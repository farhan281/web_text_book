Classification of Utterances: Demand and Statement
Author defines lafz as either talab or khabar and subdivides talab into three.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any utterance as either demand (talab) or statement (khabar) and identify the subtypes of demand (amr, du'a, and the third ambiguous type).

Core Idea
Every utterance is either a demand or a statement; demands seek something, statements declare something.

This lesson introduces the fundamental classification of utterances in Islamic logic. The author divides all spoken expressions (lafz) into two main categories: demand (talab) and statement (khabar). Demand is further split into three types, one of which is unclear in the text. This classification is the bedrock for understanding how language functions in reasoning, particularly in distinguishing between informative statements and performative demands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Read the first part of the passage: وَاللَّفْظُ إِمَّا طَلَبٌ أَوْ خَبَرُ. Notice that every utterance (lafz) is either a demand (talab) or a statement (khabar). These are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
In logic, khabar can be true or false; talab cannot.
2
Recognize the three subtypes of demand
The text says: وَأَوَّلُ ثَلاثَةٌ سَتُذْكَرُ. The first category (demand) has three types. The next part lists: a command with superiority (amr), its opposite (supplication, du'a), and a third type involving confusion over the word النَّساوِي (possibly related to forgetfulness or something else).
The third type is textually problematic; later commentators clarify it as istifham (question) or tamanni (wish). For now, note the ambiguity.
3
Apply the classification to examples
Take sample utterances from Islamic sources: 'أقيموا الصلاة' is a command (amr), hence talab. 'الله غفور رحيم' is a statement (khabar). 'ربنا آتنا في الدنيا حسنة' is a supplication (du'a), hence talab. Practice classifying similar phrases.
Always check the intention: is the speaker seeking something or informing?

The Definition of Hamd: Linguistic and Customary
Author defines al-hamd linguistically and customarily.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the linguistic and customary definitions of 'al-hamd' and identify the key components of each definition.

Core Idea
Hamd is not just praise—it is magnification of the benefactor in action.

In this lesson, we examine the author's precise definitions of 'al-hamd' (praise, thanks). Understanding these definitions is crucial for interpreting foundational Islamic texts, especially the opening of the Qur'an. The author distinguishes between the linguistic meaning—verbal praise for beautiful attributes—and the customary meaning—an act that signifies magnifying the benefactor. This distinction reveals that hamd in the Shari'a is more than mere words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its two definition types
The term 'al-hamd' is defined both linguistically (lughatan) and customarily (urfaan). Note the two distinct definitions.
2
Extract the genus for the linguistic definition
Linguistically, hamd is 'praise by speech' (al-thana' bil-kalam). This is the broader category: it is a type of speech act.
The genus is praise expressed verbally.
3
Extract the differentia for the linguistic definition
The differentia is 'for beautiful attributes' (bi-jamil sifatihi). This distinguishes hamd from other forms of praise, such as flattery for non-beautiful attributes.
4
Extract the genus for the customary definition
Customarily, hamd is 'an act indicating magnification' (f'l yunabbi'u 'an ta'zim). The genus is an act, not just speech.
The shift from speech to action is significant.
5
Extract the differentia for the customary definition
The differentia is 'due to his bestowal on the praiser or others' (bi-sabab in'amihi 'ala al-hamid aw ghayrihi). The act of magnification is motivated by the benefactor's favor.

The Definition of Shukr: Linguistic and Customary
Author defines al-shukr linguistically and customarily.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and customary definitions of 'al-shukr' and explain how it differs from 'al-hamd'.

Core Idea
Shukr is using Allah's gifts as they were meant to be used.

This lesson focuses on the term 'al-shukr' (thankfulness). The author defines it similarly to hamd but with a crucial shift in emphasis. Linguistically, shukr is like hamd but with the subject being the thankful one. Customarily, shukr is the active use of Allah's blessings for their intended purpose. Understanding shukr helps us grasp the practical dimension of gratitude in Islam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its two definition types
The term 'al-shukr' is defined both linguistically (lughatan) and customarily (urfaan). Note the two distinct definitions.
2
Compare linguistic definition with hamd
Linguistically, shukr is the same as hamd in the terminological sense, but with the substitution of 'praiser' for 'thankful'. This means the focus shifts from the one praised to the one thankful.
The linguistic definition is not given anew; it mirrors hamd with a change of subject.
3
Extract the customary definition
Customarily, shukr is 'the servant's expenditure of all that Allah has bestowed upon him for that for which it was created' (sarf al-'abd jami' ma an'am Allahu bihi 'alayhi ila ma khuliqa li-ajlihi). This emphasizes active use of blessings.
The genus is 'expenditure' (sarf), and the differentia is 'for their created purpose'.
4
Notice the action-oriented nature of shukr
Unlike hamd which may be verbal, shukr customarily requires physical or financial expenditure. This distinction is key to understanding Islamic ethics.

The Rhetorical Choices in the First Line: Nominal vs. Verbal and Word Order
Author raises and answers two grammatical/rhetorical questions about the first line.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rhetorical reasons for using a nominal sentence and for word order in a given Arabic text.

Core Idea
The Basmala's structure reflects deep rhetorical awareness: permanence demands nominal, context decides word order.

The first line of the poem (likely 'al-hamdu lillahi') is not arbitrary. Classical scholars asked two precise questions: why a nominal sentence, and why 'al-hamd' before 'Allah'? The answers reveal the principles of balagha (eloquence) that govern every word of the Qur'an and prophetic speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two questions
Read the passage and locate the two questions: why nominal vs. verbal, and why word order. Note how each is introduced with 'لم' (why).
These are rhetorical objections, not grammatical errors.
2
Understand nominal vs. verbal semantics
In Arabic, nominal sentences (جمله اسمیه) imply permanence and stability; verbal sentences (جمله فعليه) imply renewal and temporality. This is a key principle of balagha.
Refer to the key terms.
3
Apply to the first question
The author says the praise is for Allah's essence, which is fixed and continuous. Hence the nominal sentence is suitable. Verify this reasoning.
Connect the attribute of permanence to the choice of sentence type.
4
Analyze the word order
The second question: why is 'al-hamd' placed before 'Allah'? The answer distinguishes between contextual importance (عارض) and intrinsic importance (ذاتي).
The context of praise makes 'al-hamd' incidentally more important, so it is fronted.
5
Grasp the principle of contextual appropriateness (مطابقة الكلام لمقتضى الحال)
This principle is the core of balagha: speech must match the situation. Here, the situation (praise) dictates the structure.
This principle applies to all eloquent Arabic.

Commentary
This passage presents two objections to the first line of the poem and their answers. First question: why did the poet use a nominal sentence (al-jumla al-ismiyya: 'al-hamdu lillahi') instead of a verbal sentence (e.g., 'hamidtu Allaha')? The answer: because the praise is for Allah's essence, which is eternal and continuous, so the nominal sentence, which implies permanence, is more appropriate. Second question: why is the word 'al-hamd' placed before 'Allah' when the name of Allah is more important? The answer: because the context (maqam) is praise, and although mentioning Allah is intrinsically (dhati) more important, the accidental (arid) importance of highlighting praise takes precedence in this context, in observance of balagha, which is matching speech to the situation. This shows the author's attention to rhetorical nuance.

Key Terms
الجملة الاسمية (al-jumla al-ismiyya): A sentence that begins with a noun, indicating fixity and permanence.
الجملة الفعلية (al-jumla al-fi'liyya): A sentence that begins with a verb, indicating renewal and temporality.

The Five Foundational Word-Meaning Relations
Enumeration of the five primary word-meaning relations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the five foundational word-meaning relations in Islamic logic: tawatu', tashakkuk, ishtirak, taraduf, and tabayun.

Core Idea
Five relations capture every way a word can relate to its meaning in logical analysis.

This lesson introduces the five primary relations between words and their meanings as established in classical Islamic logic. These relations form the foundation for understanding how terms interact in definitions, syllogisms, and debates. By mastering them, you will be able to analyze any Islamic text with precision.

Guided Path
1
Understand the logical derivation of the five categories
The passage explains that words are either universal or particular. For universal words, consider whether the word is single or multiple, and whether the meaning is single or multiple. This yields four combinations; the fifth case (multiple words and multiple meanings) leads to tabayun.
This derivation shows the systematic nature of Islamic logic.
2
Identify tawatu' (univocity)
When one word has one meaning that applies equally to all individuals, like 'human' applying equally to all humans.
The meaning does not vary in intensity.
3
Identify tashakkuk (gradation)
When one word has one meaning but applies with varying intensity, like 'light' being stronger in the sun than the moon.
Do not confuse with ishtirak; the meaning is single but graded.
4
Identify ishtirak (homonymy) and taraduf (synonymy)
Ishtirak: one word with multiple meanings (e.g., 'ayn' for eye or stream). Taraduf: multiple words with one meaning (e.g., 'insan' and 'bashar').
These are opposites of each other.
5
Identify tabayun (distinction)
When two words have different meanings and neither applies to any individual of the other, like 'human' and 'horse'.
This is the default for unrelated terms.

Distinguishing Report from Request in Utterances
Author classifies utterances into report and request.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether any given utterance is a report (khabar) or a request (talab) based on its truth-aptness.

Core Idea
The dividing line between report and request is truth value: reports can be true or false; requests cannot.

In logic, the fundamental division of utterances is between those that inform (khabar) and those that seek (talab). This distinction is critical because it determines whether an utterance can be evaluated as true or false. In this lesson, you will learn to apply this criterion using examples from Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance
Look at the linguistic expression in the Arabic passage. Determine what is being said.
2
Check truth-aptness
Does it make sense to say the utterance is true or false? If yes, it is a report; if not, it is a request.
For example, 'Zaid is standing' can be true or false, so it is a report. 'Know, O Zaid' cannot be true or false; it is a request.

Apply to examples from the passage

Note the implications

This distinction is foundational for evaluating the truth of statements and the binding nature of commands.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn verse that divides utterances. The commentator explains that a word (lafz) is either a report (khabar) or a request (talab). A report is an utterance that can be true or false, such as 'Zaid is standing'. A request is a construction (insha') that does not admit truth value, such as 'Know, O Zaid'. The first category (report) will be discussed later in the matn. The second category (request) has three subdivisions based on the status of the speaker relative to the addressee. If the speaker is superior (like a master to a servant), it is a command (amr). If the speaker is inferior (like a servant to a master), it is a supplication (du'a). If they are equal, it is solicitation (iltimas), as when one servant asks another for his turban. The commentator then references the original verse and notes that this topic is further elaborated in usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
خبر (khabar): An utterance that admits truth and falsehood, like a declarative statement that can be judged as true or false. [Note: In mantiq, khabar is a proposition; in later usul, it may have a broader meaning.]
طلب (talab): An utterance that seeks something and does not admit truth value; it is a construction (insha') rather than a report. [Note: Stable across mantiq and usul.]
أمر (amr): A request from a superior to an inferior, e.g., a master commanding a servant. [Note: No shift.]
دعاء (du'a): A request from an inferior to a superior, like a servant supplicating to his master. [Note: No shift.]
التماس (iltimas): A request between equals, e.g., one servant asking another for something. [Note: No shift.]

The Three Types of Request: Command, Supplication, and Solicitation
Author divides request into three types based on speaker status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any request utterance into command, supplication, or solicitation based on the relative status of speaker and addressee.

Core Idea
The type of request depends on social hierarchy: command from superior, supplication from inferior, solicitation from equal.

Once we identify an utterance as a request, Islamic logicians further subdivide it based on the status of the speaker relative to the listener. This classification is especially important in understanding the language of the Qur'an and Sunnah, where commands from Allah (Supreme) are distinct from supplications of the prophet (inferior to Allah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the request
First, ensure the utterance is a request and not a report. Requests include imperatives and supplications.
2
Determine speaker-addressee relationship
Is the speaker superior, inferior, or equal to the addressee in the context? Consider social roles.
3
Classify accordingly
If superior → command (amr). If inferior → supplication (du'a). If equal → solicitation (iltimas).
4
Apply to examples from the passage
Master commanding servant (amr), servant supplicating master (du'a), servant to servant (iltimas).
