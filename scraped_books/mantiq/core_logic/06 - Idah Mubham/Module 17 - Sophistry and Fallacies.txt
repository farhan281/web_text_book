Material and Formal Fallacies in Demonstration
Author categorizes errors in demonstration into material and formal types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify fallacies in demonstration as material or formal and identify specific sub-types such as ambiguity, accidental vs essential, and invalid figures.

Core Idea
Errors in demonstration arise from either false matter (premises) or invalid form (structure); both undermine certainty.

The author now turns from constructing valid demonstrations to diagnosing their failures. He divides errors into two fundamental categories: material (مادة) and formal (صورة). Understanding this division is essential for any scholar who must evaluate arguments in kalam or fiqh, where both the truth of premises and the validity of reasoning are under scrutiny.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The passage states that errors occur either in matter (مادة) or in form (صورة). Matter refers to the content and truth of premises; form refers to the logical structure, figure, and mood.
2
Distinguish material errors in wording vs meaning
Material errors can be in wording (لفظ) — like using an ambiguous term (اشتراك) or falsely treating synonyms as different — or in meaning (معنى) — like confusing a false premise with a true one.
Ambiguity can lead to equivocation fallacies in theological debates.
3
List specific material errors in meaning
The author lists: treating accidental properties as essential, mistaking a premise for the conclusion, applying a ruling of a species to its genus, and treating non-certain premises as certain.
Each of these undermines the epistemic value of the demonstration.
4
Identify formal errors
Formal errors include violating the valid figures (shakl) of syllogisms and failing to meet conditions for proper production, such as having an undistributed middle term or an invalid mood.
These errors make the argument invalid regardless of the truth of premises.
5
Apply the classification to Islamic examples
For each error type, think of a concrete example from kalam or usul al-fiqh. For instance, using a zanni (probable) premise as if it were qat'i (certain) is a material fallacy; using a syllogism with an undistributed middle is a formal fallacy.
This skill is crucial for evaluating proofs in Islamic scholarship.

Commentary
This passage classifies fallacies in demonstration (برهان) into two categories: material (مادة) and formal (صورة). Material errors concern the content of the premises, while formal errors concern the logical structure. The author first discusses material errors in wording (لفظ) and meaning (معنى). Wording errors include ambiguity (اشتراك) — using a word with multiple meanings inadvertently — and falsely treating synonyms as different (جعل ذا تباين مثل الرديف مأخذا). Meaning errors include confusing the false with the true, treating accidental properties as essential (جعل العرضي كالذاتي), or mistaking one of the premises for the conclusion (ناتج إحدى المقدمات). Another material error is applying a ruling of a species to its genus (الحكم للجنس بحكم النوع), which reverses the correct logical order. Also, treating non-certain premises as certain (جعل غير القطعي كالقطعي) is a material fallacy. Formal errors (الشأن) include violating the valid figures (الخروج عن أشكاله) and failing to meet the conditions for proper syllogistic production (ترك شرط الإنتاج من إكماله). These are purely structural mistakes, such as using invalid moods or missing conditions for a valid syllogism (e.g., undistributed middle term).

Begging the Question (Musadara)
First material fallacy: begging the question.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute instances of begging the question in syllogisms from Islamic theological and legal arguments.

Core Idea
Begging the question disguises a premise as a conclusion, yielding no new knowledge.

This lesson examines the first of three material fallacies: begging the question (musadara 'an al-matlub). In this fallacy, the conclusion is essentially identical to one of the premises after rewording, so the syllogism fails to produce any new knowledge. The author illustrates this with a simple example involving movement and change. Your task is to recognize this circular reasoning in Islamic discourse, such as in proofs where the conclusion merely restates an assumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion and premises
Locate the conclusion (natija) and the two premises in the given syllogism. In the example: 'This is a movement' (minor), 'Every movement is a change' (major), 'This is a change' (conclusion).
2
Check if the conclusion is a restated premise
Determine whether the conclusion is essentially identical to one of the premises after rewording. Here, 'This is a change' is just a restatement of the minor premise 'This is a movement' combined with the major – but the major already contains 'movement is change', so the conclusion adds nothing new.
Specifically, the conclusion's predicate 'change' is already implied by the major premise's predicate, and the subject 'this' is from the minor.
3
Evaluate epistemic gain
Ask: Does the conclusion provide information not already contained in the premises? If not, the syllogism is circular and commits the fallacy. The author notes that no 'additional knowledge' is obtained.
4
Construct an Islamic example
Create a similar circular argument from an Islamic science, e.g., 'This contract is invalid because it contains gharar; every contract with gharar is invalid; therefore this contract is invalid.' The conclusion merely restates the minor premise.
Ensure the conclusion is not genuinely inferred from both premises but is a disguised tautology.

Commentary
This passage identifies three types of material fallacies in syllogisms, where the error lies in the premises themselves rather than the form. The first is begging the question (musadara 'an al-matlub), where the conclusion is essentially a reworded premise, yielding no new knowledge—the example given is a syllogism that concludes 'this is a change' from premises that already imply it. The second is judging a genus by the judgment of a species, illustrated by inferring that a horse is rational because 'every animal is rational', confusing the genus 'animal' with the species 'man'. The third is treating a hypothetical or uncertain matter as certain, exemplified by assuming a confused speaker is learned simply because he uses scientific terms. Each fallacy is rejected because the premises do not provide genuine epistemic support for the conclusion.

Genus-Species Fallacy
Second material fallacy: judging genus by species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect when a property of a species is incorrectly applied to its genus in syllogisms, using examples from Islamic jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Attributing a species-specific trait to the whole genus leads to false conclusions.

The second material fallacy occurs when a property that belongs only to a particular species is mistakenly predicated of the entire genus. The author gives the example: 'The horse is an animal; every animal is rational; therefore the horse is rational.' Here, 'rational' is specific to humans, not all animals. This fallacy appears in Islamic legal reasoning when a ruling specific to one category is generalized to a broader category without proper evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus and species
In the syllogism, determine which term is the genus (jins) and which is the species (naw'). In the example: 'animal' is the genus, 'horse' is a species (but note: the fallacy involves judging the genus by the species, not the other way around).
Actually, the fallacy is 'judging the genus by the judgment of the species' meaning the predicate ('rational') belongs to the species 'human', but it is applied to the genus 'animal'.
2
Check if the predicate is exclusive to a species
Ask: Is the predicate (e.g., rational) true for all members of the genus? If it is only true for a subset (a species), then applying it to the whole genus is fallacious.
Rationality is a differentia of humans, not of all animals.
3
Test with an Islamic example
Consider the genus 'action' and the species 'prayer'. If one says 'Every action is obligatory; prayer is an action; therefore prayer is obligatory', the predicate 'obligatory' is not true for all actions – only some. This is the same fallacy.
The correct reasoning would be to specify which actions are obligatory and why.

Treating Uncertain as Certain
Third material fallacy: treating uncertain as certain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify arguments that mistakenly treat a hypothetical or uncertain matter as certain, particularly in Islamic theological and hadith contexts.

Core Idea
Assuming uncertain premises as certain leads to false conclusions, even in valid syllogisms.

The third material fallacy is treating a non-certain, hypothetical matter as if it were certain. The author gives the example of a person who fumbles in research but uses scientific terms: assuming that 'speaking the words of knowledge' implies being learned is an unwarranted certainty. This fallacy is common in Islamic sciences when weak evidence is treated as definitive, such as relying on isolated hadith for creedal matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the uncertain premise
In the syllogism, find the premise that is presented as certain but is actually uncertain or hypothetical. In the example: 'Everyone who speaks the words of knowledge is learned' is not necessarily true; one can repeat terms without understanding.
The minor premise 'This man speaks the words of knowledge' may be true, but the major is doubtful.
2
Assess the certainty level
Determine whether each premise is yaqini (certain) or zanni (probable). The fallacy arises when a zanni premise is treated as yaqini.
The author calls the uncertain matter 'al-amr al-wahmi ghayr al-qat'i' – a hypothetical, non-certain matter.
3
Check if the conclusion depends on that premise
If the conclusion relies on the uncertain premise, then the conclusion is also uncertain. The author notes the falsity is because of 'making the assumption of his being learned as if it were certain.'
4
Apply to Islamic sciences
Consider a hadith: 'This narration is in Sahih al-Bukhari; every hadith in Bukhari is authentic; therefore this hadith is authentic.' The major premise is generally accepted but not absolutely certain for every individual narration. Treating it as certain can lead to errors in hadith authentication.
This example highlights the need for rigorous isnad criticism.
