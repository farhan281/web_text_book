Why the Nominal Sentence in Praise?
Commentator answers why the nominal sentence is used.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the poet used a nominal sentence instead of a verbal sentence, grounding your explanation in the attribute of Allah's essence.

Core Idea
The nominal sentence fits Allah's eternal essence because it denotes stability.

The commentator raises a sharp question: why does the verse begin with 'al-hamdu lillah' (a nominal sentence) rather than a verbal one? His answer reveals a deep link between Arabic grammar and theological doctrine. Understanding this choice is key to appreciating how rhetorical form serves meaning in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question posed
The commentator presents the first objection: why use the nominal sentence (الجملة الاسمية) instead of the verbal one (الجملة الفعلية)? Locate this question in the Arabic passage.
2
Recall the property of each sentence type
A nominal sentence begins with a noun and indicates stability and permanence. A verbal sentence begins with a verb and indicates renewal and temporariness. This is a standard rule in Arabic grammar and rhetoric.
3
Connect sentence type to Allah's attribute
The commentator argues that Allah's essence (ذاته سبحانه) is eternal, fixed, and perpetual. Therefore, the nominal sentence, which signifies constancy, is the appropriate grammatical form for praising Him.
4
Assess the reasoning
Consider whether this argument is sound. Does the nominal sentence truly always indicate permanence? Could a verbal sentence also be used? Reflect on how the commentator uses the attribute to justify the grammatical choice.

Balagha: Prioritizing Context over Intrinsic Importance
Commentator answers why praise is placed before Allah's name.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of muqtada al-hal to justify word order choices in Arabic, using the example of 'al-hamdu' preceding 'lillah'.

Core Idea
In eloquence, what the situation demands can outweigh the intrinsic importance of the words involved.

The second objection in the commentary asks why 'al-hamdu' (praise) comes before 'lillah' (to Allah), even though mentioning Allah is inherently more important. The commentator's answer introduces a key principle of balagha: when the context strongly demands a certain element, it takes precedence. This is a powerful tool for understanding why many Quranic and poetic phrases are ordered as they are.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second objection
The objector notes that the noble name of Allah is intrinsically more important than the concept of praise, yet praise is mentioned first. Find this question in the Arabic passage.
2
Distinguish two types of importance
The commentator distinguishes between intrinsic importance (الأهمية الذاتية) and accidental/contextual importance (الأهمية العارضة). Intrinsic importance is inherent to the thing itself; contextual importance depends on the situation the speaker is in.
3
Analyze the context (muqtada al-hal)
The situation (المقام) is one of praising and thanking Allah. Because the purpose of the speech is to express praise, the word 'al-hamdu' gains contextual importance. The speaker's intent makes praise the focal point, so it is placed first to align with the listener's expectations.
4
Connect to the definition of balagha
The commentator explicitly states that this choice was made 'مراعاة للبلاغة التي هي مطابقة الكلام لمقتضى الحال' (out of consideration for eloquence, which is matching speech to the requirements of the situation). This defines the core of Arabic rhetorical theory.

The Five Arts of Proof
Author enumerates the five arts of proof and elevates demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the five arts of proof (rhetoric, poetry, demonstration, dialectic, sophistry) based on their premises and purpose.

Core Idea
Demonstration alone yields certainty; the other four arts serve persuasion, debate, emotion, or deception.

The author presents a complete classification of arguments in a concise verse. Knowing these five arts is essential because it tells you what kind of argument you are dealing with—whether it demands certainty, mere persuasion, or something else. Let us unpack each art and see why demonstration is the noblest.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the five arts
The verse lists: rhetoric (khataba), poetry (shi'r), demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), and sophistry (safsata). Note the order: rhetoric and poetry come first, then the three more technical ones. Try to recall them using the acronym RPSDS.
Rhetoric and poetry are often used for persuasion; the last three are more clearly logical.
2
Identify the defining feature of each art
Rhetoric uses persuasive but not certain premises; poetry uses imaginative premises to stir emotions; demonstration uses certain premises; dialectic uses commonly accepted premises or those conceded by an opponent; sophistry uses apparent but false premises.
Only demonstration leads to certainty (yaqin).
3
Rank them by epistemic strength
The author says demonstration (burhan) is the noblest (ajalluha). That is because it alone yields certain knowledge. Dialectic is next in strength, then rhetoric, then poetry; sophistry is the worst because it deceives.
This ranking is standard in Islamic logic.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive overview of the types of arguments in classical Islamic logic. It divides proofs into two broad categories: naqli (based on transmitted reports) and aqli (rational). From these, five arts are distinguished: rhetoric (khataba), poetry (shi'r), demonstration (burhan), dialectic (jadal), and sophistry (safasta). Demonstration is the highest because it uses premises that are certain (yaqini). The author then lists six categories of certain premises: awwaliyat (self-evident truths like 'the whole is greater than the part'), mushahadat (introspective observations such as 'I doubt'), mujarrabat (knowledge from repeated experiments, e.g., 'fire burns'), mutawatirat (knowledge from mutawatir reports, e.g., 'Mecca exists'), hadasiyyat (intuitive knowledge, e.g., 'the moon is eclipsed when earth interposes'), and mahsusat (sensory perceptions). These constitute the basis of demonstrative knowledge. Finally, the passage mentions a debate on how premises imply the conclusion: some say the implication is rational (necessary), some say customary (by habit), some say generative (tawallud, i.e., causation between knowledge of premises and conclusion), and some say obligatory (wajib). The preferred view is that the implication is rational (the first).

The Six Sources of Certain Premises
Author lists six types of certain premises that compose demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and define the six categories of certain premises that constitute demonstration (burhan).

Core Idea
Certain premises are the bedrock of demonstration; without them, no argument can be conclusive.

After naming demonstration as the highest art, the author details its building blocks: six types of premises that are absolutely certain. These are the raw materials of every demonstrative proof. Let us examine each one and see how they differ in origin.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the six categories
The verse lists: awwaliyat (primaries), mushahadat (observations), mujarrabat (experiments), mutawatirat (recurrent reports), hadasiyat (intuitive), and mahsusat (sensibles). Use the acronym AMMMHM to recall them.
These are all yaqiniyat (certainties).
2
Understand each category's basis
Awwaliyat are self-evident (e.g., the whole is greater than the part). Mushahadat are inner observations (e.g., 'I doubt'). Mujarrabat come from repeated experiments (e.g., 'fire burns'). Mutawatirat are from mass testimony (e.g., 'Mecca exists'). Hadasiyat are intuitive leaps (e.g., the moon is eclipsed because earth interposes). Mahsusat are external senses (e.g., 'the sun is bright').
Each has a distinct epistemic source.
3
Apply the classification to an argument
Take the premise 'Fire burns.' This comes from mujarrabat (experiment). The premise 'The whole is greater than the part' is awwaliyat. Practice assigning each certain premise you know to one of these six.
This skill is crucial for evaluating whether an argument truly counts as demonstration.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poetic line that divides logical arguments into two main categories: transmitted (based on revelation) and rational (based on intellectual premises). The rational arguments are further divided into five arts: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. The commentator explains each in turn.

First, he clarifies that by 'argument' (حجة) is meant the syllogism (قياس). The logician must consider both the material (content) and form of the syllogism to identify sources of error. The material is divided into transmitted (from Quran, Sunnah, consensus) and rational, which has five subdivisions.

1. **Demonstration (برهان)**: Mentioned but not detailed here; it will be explained later.
2. **Dialectic (جدل)**: Uses famous or conceded premises (e.g., 'Justice is good, injustice is bad') to overpower an opponent or convince someone who cannot handle demonstration. Its aim is victory in debate, not necessarily truth.
3. **Rhetoric (خطابة)**: Uses accepted or probable premises (e.g., from a trustworthy person, or a probable judgment) to motivate the listener toward beneficial actions in this life and the afterlife. An example is given: 'He does not mix with people, and whoever avoids mixing is arrogant; therefore he is arrogant.'
4. **Poetry (شعر)**: Uses imaginative premises that affect the soul, causing pleasure or aversion. Examples: 'Wine is a flowing ruby' (pleasure) and 'Honey is bitter vomit' (aversion). Its purpose is emotional influence.
5. **Sophistry (سفسطة)**: Uses false premises that resemble truth to create doubt and confusion, e.g., 'A picture of a horse on a wall: this is a horse, every horse neighs, so it neighs.' Its use is forbidden except in necessity, like repelling a stubborn disbeliever. The worst type is external fallacy, where a debater uses confusing and ugly speech to appear victorious despite ignorance.

The commentator notes that the poet did not order the arts systematically; the order given here is the commentator's own.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poetic line that divides logical arguments into two main categories: transmitted (based on revelation) and rational (based on intellectual premises). The rational arguments are further divided into five arts: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. The commentator explains each in turn.

First, he clarifies that by 'argument' (حجة) is meant the syllogism (قياس). The logician must consider both the material (content) and form of the syllogism to identify sources of error. The material is divided into transmitted (from Quran, Sunnah, consensus) and rational, which has five subdivisions.

1. **Demonstration (برهان)**: Mentioned but not detailed here; it will be explained later.
2. **Dialectic (جدل)**: Uses famous or conceded premises (e.g., 'Justice is good, injustice is bad') to overpower an opponent or convince someone who cannot handle demonstration. Its aim is victory in debate, not necessarily truth.
3. **Rhetoric (خطابة)**: Uses accepted or probable premises (e.g., from a trustworthy person, or a probable judgment) to motivate the listener toward beneficial actions in this life and the afterlife. An example is given: 'He does not mix with people, and whoever avoids mixing is arrogant; therefore he is arrogant.'
4. **Poetry (شعر)**: Uses imaginative premises that affect the soul, causing pleasure or aversion. Examples: 'Wine is a flowing ruby' (pleasure) and 'Honey is bitter vomit' (aversion). Its purpose is emotional influence.
5. **Sophistry (سفسطة)**: Uses false premises that resemble truth to create doubt and confusion, e.g., 'A picture of a horse on a wall: this is a horse, every horse neighs, so it neighs.' Its use is forbidden except in necessity, like repelling a stubborn disbeliever. The worst type is external fallacy, where a debater uses confusing and ugly speech to appear victorious despite ignorance.

The commentator notes that the poet did not order the arts systematically; the order given here is the commentator's own.

Rhetoric and Poetry as Rational Arts
Author defines rhetoric and poetry with examples and purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish rhetoric from poetry based on their logical definitions and purposes.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades with probable opinions; poetry moves the soul through emotional metaphors.

In this passage, the author defines two of the five rational arts: rhetoric (khataba) and poetry (shi'r). Both are types of qiyas (reasoning), but they differ in their material and purpose. Understanding these definitions helps you recognize how arguments can aim at persuasion or emotional influence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus of each art
Both rhetoric and poetry are placed under the broader category of qiyas (rational argument). Recognize that the author considers them as types of reasoning, not merely literary forms.
The genus for both is 'qiyas'.
2
Extract the differentia for rhetoric
Rhetoric uses premises based on opinion (zann) and allows the opposite to be possible. Its purpose is to persuade the listener toward beneficial action in this world and the next.
Example: 'This person does not mix with people; everyone who avoids mixing is arrogant; therefore this person is arrogant.'
3
Extract the differentia for poetry
Poetry is composed of propositions that cause the soul to expand (inbisat) or contract (inqibad), leading to desire or aversion. Its purpose is emotional movement.
Example: 'Wine is a flowing ruby' (expansion) or 'Honey is bitter vomit' (contraction).
4
Compare the definitions as descriptive (rasm)
Neither definition provides a complete essential definition (hadd haqiqi); they are descriptions (rasm) focusing on purpose and effect. Note that a rasm identifies the thing by its properties without revealing its essence.
In Islamic logic, a rasm is sufficient for identification but not for ultimate essential knowledge.

Sophistry: Definition, Fallacy, and Ruling
Author defines sophistry, gives a fallacious example, and declares its use haram with one exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sophistry, classify its fallacy as material, and state its ruling in Islamic law.

Core Idea
Sophistry uses false premises that look like truth; it is haram except to repel an obstinate disbeliever.

The author now defines sophistry (safsata) as the fifth rational art. It is a corrupt form of reasoning that employs false premises resembling truth to cause doubt. The passage provides a concrete example and discusses the ruling on its use, including the ugliest type: external fallacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sophistical example
The example: 'This is a horse (pointing to a picture on a wall); every horse neighs; therefore this neighs.' The first premise is false because the picture is not a real horse.
The argument is valid in form (first figure, Barbara) but unsound due to a false premise.
2
Classify the fallacy type
This is a material fallacy because the premise is false, not because the logical form is invalid. The premises resemble truth but are actually false.
This differs from formal fallacies where the conclusion does not follow from the premises.
3
Understand the ruling on sophistry
The author states: 'Using it is forbidden in all its types' (wa isti'maluha haram bi-jami' anwa'iha). However, an exception is made for necessity, like repelling an obstinate disbeliever, comparing it to using poison for malignant diseases.
The default ruling is absolute prohibition (haram).
4
Recognize external fallacy (al-mughalata al-kharijiyya)
This is described as a disputant who lacks understanding and submission to truth, using confusing speech to appear victorious and conceal ignorance. The author notes it is common in his time.
This is a procedural fallacy, not a logical one; it involves unethical debate tactics.

Definition of Demonstration. The greatest of them is demonstration, composed of premises that are certain. [It is composed] of primary [intuitions], observations, experiments, recurrent [reports], intuitive [propositions], and sense-perceptions. So those are the totality of certainties. I say: The greatest of these five is demonstration: it is composed of certain premises, such that belief in them is decisive, corresponding [to reality], stable, unchanging. The certainties according to the author are six: First: primaries (i.e., self-evidents), plural of 'first', which is what the mind judges without an intermediary that requires reflection; like 'the sky is above us' and 'the earth is below us.' (1) So it is dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, sophistry. (al-Malawi) (2) 'Ma' is a generic term in the definition that includes the five syllogisms, and 'composed of certain premises': certainty is a decisive belief corresponding to reality, impossible to change; certain premises are more general than being necessary or acquired; it is a condition that excludes rhetoric, dialectic, and others. Demonstration is of two types: (1) Lamī [a posteriori?]: where the middle term is a cause for the affirmation of the minor for the major in both mind and reality; like 'Zayd has putrid humors, and every putrid-humored person is feverish, so Zayd is feverish' – putrid humor is the cause for the affirmation of fever for Zayd; it is called lamī because it provides the 'why' (limiyya), i.e., the cause, for it answers the question 'why'. (2) Annī [a priori?]: where the middle term is a cause for that only in the mind; like 'Zayd is feverish, and every feverish person has putrid humors, so Zayd has putrid humors' – fever is a cause for the affirmation of putrid humor only in the mind, not externally; it is called annī because it confines itself to the 'thatch' (anniyya) of the judgment, i.e., its existence without its 'why', from their saying 'the matter is thus' – it is attributed to 'an' (that), while the first is attributed to 'lima' (why). (al-Malawi) (4) The certainties are of two types: (1) theoretical, which are known through reflection and reasoning; like knowledge of the world's origination after establishing evidence for it. (2) Necessary, which are the six mentioned by the commentator. (al-Jundi) (5) In (S): 'aql' and the affirmed from (H).

Commentary
This passage defines demonstration (burhan) as the highest of the five arts and lists the six types of certain premises that compose it. The matn states that demonstration is composed of certain premises, enumerating: primaries (awwaliyat), observations (mushahadat), experiments (mujarrabat), recurrent reports (mutawatirat), intuitives (hadasiyyat), and sense-perceptions (mahsusat). The commentator (al-Malawi) then expands: demonstration is a syllogism composed of certain premises, where certainty is defined as a decisive, stable belief corresponding to reality. He notes that the generic term in the definition covers all five syllogisms, and the condition of certainty excludes rhetoric and dialectic. The commentator then divides demonstration into two types: (1) lamī, where the middle term is a cause for the conclusion in both mind and reality, answering 'why' (lima); for example, putrid humors causing fever. (2) annī, where the middle term is a cause only in the mind (e.g., fever indicates putrid humors but does not cause them externally), answering 'that' (anna) only. A footnote further divides certainties into theoretical (nazari) and necessary (daruri), the latter being the six types mentioned by the commentator (al-Jundi). A textual variant notes that in one copy, the word 'aql' (reason) appears instead of something else.
