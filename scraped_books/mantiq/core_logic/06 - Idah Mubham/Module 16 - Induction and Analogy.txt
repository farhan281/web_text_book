Induction and Analogy: The Limits of Non-Demonstrative Arguments
Author defines induction and analogy as non-certain arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil) from the categorical syllogism and explain why they do not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Induction and analogy lack certainty; only the categorical syllogism proves conclusively.

In this passage, the author clarifies two types of reasoning that are often confused with the categorical syllogism: induction, which moves from particular to universal, and analogy, which moves from particular to particular. While both are useful in argumentation, they do not produce certain knowledge. Understanding their limitations is essential for rigorous reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the direction of reasoning in induction
Induction (istiqra') is reasoning from particular instances to a universal conclusion. For example, observing many cases of wine being intoxicating leads to the claim 'all wine is intoxicating'.
This is an incomplete (naqis) induction unless all instances are examined.
2
Identify the direction in analogy
Analogy (tamthil) is reasoning from one particular case to another particular case based on a shared attribute (jami'). It does not involve a universal premise.
In Islamic logic, this is distinct from the analogical reasoning (qiyas) in usul al-fiqh, which is based on a common cause ('illah).
3
Contrast with the logical syllogism
The logical syllogism (qiyas mantiqi) moves from universal premises to a particular conclusion. It is the only form that yields certain knowledge when properly structured.
The author refers to the categorical syllogism previously taught.
4
Recognize the epistemic status
The passage explicitly states that both induction and analogy 'do not yield certainty by proof'. They are plausible but not demonstrative.
In Islamic sciences, such arguments are considered zanni (probable) and require further verification.

Three Types of Argument: Induction, Syllogism, and Analogy
Author classifies arguments for assent into induction, syllogism, and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of arguments (induction, syllogism, analogy) based on their direction of inference and level of certainty.

Core Idea
Only syllogism yields certainty; induction and analogy are probabilistic.

In Islamic logic, arguments that produce assent (tasdiq) are classified into three categories: induction, syllogism, and analogy. The author enumerates these and explains that only the syllogism yields certainty. This lesson will help you understand each type and their logical strengths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Look at the beginning of the passage: 'المفيد للمطلوب التصديقي ثلاثة أقسام: (۱) استقراء. (۲) وقياس. (۳) وتمثيل.' These are the three types.
2
Determine the direction of inference
Induction goes from universal to particular, syllogism from particular to universal (inverse), analogy from particular to particular.
Syllogism here means categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani).
3
Locate the certainty claim
The passage states: 'والمفيد للقطع من هذه الثلاثة: القياس' – only syllogism yields certainty.
4
Understand why induction and analogy are uncertain
Induction may miss a counterexample (like the crocodile that moves its upper jaw). Analogy may have a different cause in the two particulars.
The example of nabidh and khamr shows analogy is probabilistic.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of arguments that produce tasdiq (assent), and it distinguishes three types: istiqra' (induction), qiyas (syllogism), and tamthil (analogy). The author first enumerates them, then notes that the original author (of al-Sullam) left out a fourth type: qiyas al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum). He explains qiyas al-khalf with an example from kalam: to prove that God is eternal, one supposes the opposite (that He is originated) and shows it leads to absurdity, thereby establishing the original claim. After this aside, the author defines each of the three types. Induction (istiqra') is reasoning from a universal judgment to particular instances, i.e., inferring a universal law from examining some particulars. The example given is that all animals move their lower jaw, based on observing the horse, human, and donkey; but this is incomplete because some animals (like crocodiles) move their upper jaw. Syllogism (qiyas) is reasoning from a universal to a particular, which is the inverse of induction. The example: the world is originated because it is an individual of the changing, and everything changing is originated. This is a valid syllogism. Analogy (tamthil) is reasoning from one particular to another particular based on a shared cause. The example is forbidding nabidh (date wine) by analogy with khamr (grape wine) because both share the intoxicating quality. The passage concludes that only the syllogism yields certainty; induction and analogy are uncertain because induction may miss a counterexample (like the crocodile) and analogy may have a different underlying cause in the two particulars. The commentary uses examples from fiqh (khamr and nabidh) and biology (jaw movement) which are consistent with the Islamic sciences tradition.

Key Terms
استقراء (istiqra'): Inference from a universal to particular instances; an inductive argument that examines some individuals to conclude a general rule. [Note: Later logicians distinguish complete (tam) and incomplete (naqis) induction; this text uses the common definition.]
قياس (qiyas): Inference from a universal to a particular, i.e., syllogism; the only one yielding certainty. [Note: In usul al-fiqh, qiyas refers to analogical reasoning; here it means categorical syllogism.]
تمثيل (tamthil): Inference from one particular to another particular based on a shared attribute; analogy. [Note: Often equated with fiqh qiyas; here distinguished from logical qiyas.]

Qiyas al-Khalf: Proving by Refuting the Opposite
Author explains the fourth type of argument: reductio ad absurdum (qiyas al-khalf).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct a qiyas al-khalf argument and explain its logical structure as a conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
Prove a claim by showing its negation leads to absurdity.

In addition to the three main argument types, the author discusses a fourth type omitted by the original text: qiyas al-khalf, or reductio ad absurdum. This argument proves a proposition by assuming its opposite and deriving an absurd conclusion. This lesson will walk through the example given: proving the eternity of God.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim to be proven
The claim is that God is eternal (qadim). The passage seeks to prove 'ثبوت قِدَمِه تعالى'.
2
Assume the negation
The argument hypothesizes: 'لو لم يكن قديما' – if He were not eternal, then He would be originated (حادث).
This step sets up the conditional.
3
Derive an absurdity
From the assumption that God is originated, an absurdity (المحال) follows. The passage says 'لو كان حادثا للزم المحال'.
The exact absurdity is not specified, but it is something impossible.
4
Conclude the original claim
Since the assumption leads to absurdity, the assumption is false. Therefore its opposite – that God is eternal – is true.
This is a classic modus tollens: if P then Q, if Q then R (absurd), not R, therefore not P, thus the original claim.

Complete and Incomplete Induction
Al-Sa'd defines induction and its two types based on al-Ghazali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete induction from incomplete induction and determine the certainty level of each.

Core Idea
Induction yields certainty only when all particulars are surveyed.

In this passage, al-Sa'd defines induction (istiqra') as the survey of particular instances to derive a judgment covering them. He then divides it into complete induction (covering all particulars, yielding certainty) and incomplete induction (covering most particulars, yielding probability). This distinction is essential for evaluating arguments from experience in Islamic sciences, especially in usul al-fiqh when establishing universal rulings from particular cases.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of istiqra'
Find the opening statement by al-Sa'd: '...وهو عبارة عن تصفح أمور جزئية ليحكم بحكمها على أمر يشمل تلك الجزئيات'. This is the definition of induction.
2
Distinguish the two types
Notice the condition: 'إما كلها' (all) for complete induction, and 'إما أكثرها' (most) for incomplete induction. This quantitative distinction determines the epistemic value.
Incomplete induction is the commonly known form.
3
Assess certainty
Complete induction, covering all individuals, yields yaqin (certainty). Incomplete induction, missing some, yields zann (probability). In practice, complete induction is rare in religious sciences.
Example: observing horses, humans, donkeys move jaw to conclude all animals do – but crocodiles move upper jaw, so the induction was incomplete.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed definitions from commentators. First, al-Sa'd defines induction (istiqra') as surveying particular instances to derive a judgment covering all of them. He distinguishes complete induction (all particulars) from incomplete induction (most particulars), noting that the latter is what is commonly meant. For example, observing that horses, humans, and donkeys move their lower jaw leads to the conclusion that all animals do, but it may be false if other animals (like crocodiles) are exceptions. Second, al-Sa'd defines analogy (tamthil) as likening a particular to another based on a shared attribute to transfer the judgment. He then decomposes analogy into four components: the universal major (the ruling, e.g., 'forbidden'), the universal middle (the shared attribute, e.g., 'intoxicant'), the minor (the new item, e.g., 'nabidh'), and the original (the item with the known ruling, e.g., 'wine'). This shows that tamthil can be expressed as a syllogism with a middle term, bridging analogy and deductive reasoning. The cause (illa) is the intoxicating quality, which is the basis for transferring the ruling from wine to nabidh.

Analogy (Tamthil) as a Syllogism
Al-Sa'd defines tamthil and shows its syllogistic structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decompose a tamthil (analogy) argument into its four limits (major, middle, minor, and original) and express it as a valid categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Analogy is not mere comparison; it is a hidden syllogism with a middle term.

Al-Sa'd defines tamthil as likening a particular to another based on a shared meaning to transfer a ruling. He then decomposes it into four components: the universal major (ruling), universal middle (shared cause), the minor (new item), and the original (precedent). This reveals that tamthil is essentially a categorical syllogism, bridging analogical reasoning and deductive logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four limits
From the text: (1) أكبر كلي = universal major (e.g., 'forbidden'), (2) أوسط كلي = universal middle (e.g., 'intoxicant'), (3) أصغر = minor (e.g., 'nabidh'), (4) أصل مشبه به = original precedent (e.g., 'wine').
The original precedent provides the link: wine is forbidden because it is intoxicating.
2
Extract the shared meaning (illa)
The shared meaning that justifies transferring the ruling is intoxication (الإسكار). This is the middle term that appears in the original judgment and in the new case.
The illa is the cause that connects the original and the new.
3
Rearrange as a categorical syllogism
Using the example: Major premise: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'. Minor premise: 'Nabidh is an intoxicant'. Conclusion: 'Therefore, nabidh is forbidden'. This is a first-figure Barbara syllogism.
The original precedent (wine) provides the connection but is not needed in the syllogism once the universal major is extracted.
