Defending the Simile of Ignorance as a Privation
Explains the simile of veils and clouds, defending its validity against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to defend the validity of a simile between a privation (like ignorance) and an existent (like clouds) using the definition of ignorance as a faulty perception, not pure non-existence.

Core Idea
Ignorance is not nothing—it is a faulty perception, so it can be compared to clouds as both are veils.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a classic objection: how can ignorance, which seems non-existent, be compared to clouds, which are existent? He clarifies that ignorance is not pure non-existence but a privation—a lack of knowledge that has a kind of existence. This lesson walks you through the definition of ignorance and the logic of valid comparison.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sides of the simile
Find what is being compared: 'the sky of intellect' and 'clouds of ignorance'. The common point is that both are obstructions (hijab). The simile is constructed by omitting the particle 'ka' and annexing the object of comparison.
The commentator gives an example: 'jubab al-jabal' (cloak of the mountain) originally 'like a mountain'.
2
Understand the objection
Why might someone say the comparison is invalid? Because ignorance is 'adami (non-existent) while clouds are wujudi (existent). Comparing non-existent to existent seems flawed.
This objection relies on the assumption that non-existents have no properties to share.
3
Analyze the answer
The commentator argues that ignorance is not pure non-existence (adam mahd). It is both lack of knowledge (adam al-'ilm) and perceiving a thing contrary to reality (idrak al-shay' 'ala khilaf ma huwa bihi). Thus it has a kind of existence as a psychological state.
This shows that conceptual privations can have effects and attributes.
4
Connect to the definition of jahl
The passage provides a nuanced definition: ignorance is not simply absence, but a positive faulty perception. This allows it to be treated as something with the property of obstruction, shared with clouds.
In Islamic epistemology, this is called 'jahl murakkab' (compound ignorance).
5
Apply to other similar comparisons
Think of other examples: comparing kufr to darkness (darkness is privation of light but has effects), or comparing falsehood to a veil. The same logic applies: as privations with positive aspects, they can be compared to existents.
This tool helps avoid invalid critiques of rhetorical figures in Islamic texts.

The Three Types of Linguistic Indication (Dalala)
Author defines the three types of conventional indication: mutabaqa, tazammun, and iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of conventional linguistic indication and apply them to Islamic textual analysis.

Core Idea
A word's indication is not monolithic; it can cover full meaning, part of it, or a necessary concomitant.

In Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh, understanding how words indicate meanings is foundational. This passage defines the three classical types of conventional indication (dalala wad'iyya): mutabaqa (conformity), tazammun (inclusion), and iltizam (concomitance). These distinctions help us interpret revealed texts with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definienda
Read the Arabic passage and note the three technical terms: دلالة المطابقة, تضمن, التزام. These are the three types being defined.
The terms are explicitly named in the text.
2
Understand mutabaqa
Mutabaqa means the word indicates its entire conventional meaning. For example, the word 'house' indicates the entire house, all its parts together.
This is the most straightforward type: the full definition of the word.
3
Understand tazammun
Tazammun means the word indicates a part of its meaning. For example, 'house' indicates the roof because the roof is a part of the house.
The part is inherent in the meaning.
4
Understand iltizam
Iltizam means the word indicates something external but necessarily associated in the mind, provided the mind makes the connection. Example: 'house' indicates the builder because the mind links a building to its constructor.
This requires an additional mental step; it is not part of the word's core meaning.
5
Apply the distinction
Given a specific word and what it indicates in a given context, classify it as mutabaqa, tazammun, or iltizam based on whether it indicates the whole meaning, a part, or a necessary concomitant.
Practice with Quranic and hadith examples to solidify.

Rules of Contradiction (Tanaqud) for Propositions
Author defines contradiction and provides rules for contradicting different proposition types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contradictory proposition for any given categorical proposition according to the rules of tanaqud.

Core Idea
Contradiction flips quality and, for quantified propositions, also flips the quantifier.

This passage establishes the fundamental logical relationship of contradiction (tanaqud). Understanding how to correctly form the contradictory of a proposition is essential for reasoning, debate, and verifying consistency in Islamic legal and theological arguments. The author provides clear rules for singular, unquantified, and quantified propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Determine if the proposition is singular (shakhsiyya), unquantified (muhmala), or quantified (mahsura). This determines which rule applies.
Singular propositions refer to an individual; unquantified have no quantifier; quantified have a universal or particular quantifier.
2
Apply the rule for singular or unquantified
Change only the quality: if affirmative, make it negative; if negative, make it affirmative. The quantity remains the same.
Example: 'Zayd is just' (affirmative singular) -> 'Zayd is not just' (negative singular).
3
Apply the rule for quantified propositions
Change both the quantifier and the quality. For universal affirmative, use particular negative. For universal negative, use particular affirmative.
Memorize: A (universal affirmative) -> O (particular negative); E (universal negative) -> I (particular affirmative).
4
Verify truth conditions
Ensure that the original and its contradictory cannot both be true nor both false. One must be true and the other false.
This is the essential definition of tanaqud.

Commentary
This section defines contradiction (tanaqud) as two propositions that differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative) such that one must be true and the other false. For singular propositions (shakhsiyya) and unquantified (muhmala) propositions, the contradictory is formed by simply changing the quality (from affirmative to negative or vice versa). Simple Conversion: Swapping Subject and Predicate
Author defines simple conversion and gives rules for different proposition types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply simple conversion to categorical propositions and correctly adjust the quantifier for universal affirmatives.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth – but watch out for the universal affirmative.

Simple conversion is a fundamental immediate inference used in Islamic logic. It allows you to derive a new proposition from a given one by swapping its subject and predicate. This skill is essential for checking consistency in arguments and for use in theological debates. In this lesson, we will learn the rule and its one key exception.

Guided Path
1
Define simple conversion
Conversion ('aks) means reversing the two parts (subject and predicate) of a proposition while keeping the truth value (sidq) and quality (kayfiyya, i.e., affirmative or negative). The passage states: 'Conversion is the reversal of the two parts of the proposition while preserving truth and quality.'
Quality and truth remain unchanged; quantity may change in one case.
2
Apply to a universal affirmative
For universal affirmative propositions (e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'), conversion changes the quantity from universal to particular. The passage says: 'except for the universal affirmative, so they substituted for it the particular affirmative.' So 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' converts to 'Some forbidden thing is intoxicant.'
This is the only case where quantity changes.
3
Apply to other categorical types
For universal negative, particular affirmative, and particular negative, both truth and quantity are preserved. Example: 'No riba is halal' (universal negative) converts to 'No halal is riba.' 'Some contract is invalid' (particular affirmative) converts to 'Some invalid thing is a contract.'
Check each type: universal negative (E) converts to itself, particular affirmative (I) converts to itself, particular negative (O) does not convert (invalid).
4
Handle unquantified propositions
The passage mentions that a negative unquantified proposition (important salbiyya) is treated like a particular negative, so conversion is not valid for it.
Unquantified negatives are equivalent to particular negatives in force.

Three Types of Verbal Conventional Signification
Author divides verbal conventional signification into three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of verbal conventional signification (mutabaqiyya, tadammuniyya, iltizamiyya) and correctly classify any given example according to its type.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: the whole meaning, its part, or its necessary attendant.

In this lesson, we examine how words convey meaning according to the logicians. The author classifies verbal conventional signification into three types based on what part of the meaning the word points to: the entire meaning, a part of it, or something necessarily attached. Mastering this classification is foundational for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its conventional meaning
Take a word like 'insan' (human). Its conventional meaning is 'rational animal' (hayawan natiq). Write down the full meaning it was placed for.
2
Determine if the signification is mutabaqiyya
If the word signifies its entire conventional meaning, it is mutabaqiyya (correspondence). Example: 'insan' for the whole of 'rational animal'.
Mutabaqiyya is the primary signification.
3
Determine if the signification is tadammuniyya
If the word signifies only a part of its conventional meaning, it is tadammuniyya (inclusion). Example: 'insan' for just 'animal' or just 'rational'.
The part is included in the whole meaning.
4
Determine if the signification is iltizamiyya
If the word signifies something external to the conventional meaning but necessarily connected to it in the mind, it is iltizamiyya (implication). Example: 'insan' for 'capacity for knowledge' – a necessary concomitant of being human.
The concomitant must be mentally necessary, not merely customary.
5
Practice with Islamic examples
Take the word 'salah' (prayer). Its full meaning: specific actions and conditions. Mutabaqiyya: 'salah' for the whole prayer. Tadammuniyya: 'salah' for ruku' (bowing) alone. Iltizamiyya: 'salah' for 'prevention from indecency' (since it necessarily follows from prayer).

Core Idea
Not every 'kull' is universal; context determines whether it is distributive (kulliyya) or collective (kull).

This lesson applies the technical distinction between al-kull (the whole) and al-kulliyya (universality) to a real prophetic hadith. Understanding Logical Contradiction (Tanāquḍ): Definition and Rules
Author defines contradiction and gives rules for finding contradictories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradictory propositions in Islamic logic and apply the rules to find the contradictory of any categorical proposition.

Core Idea
Contradiction is difference in quality (affirmation/negation) yielding one true and one false.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational concept of logical contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author defines it as two propositions that differ in quality (affirmation vs. negation) and are such that exactly one is true. We will learn the rules for constructing contradictories for singular, unrestricted, and quantified propositions, using examples from Islamic sciences. This tool is essential for precise argumentation in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of contradiction
Locate in the Arabic where the author defines tanāquḍ: 'ikhtilāf qaḍiyyatayn bi-al-ījāb wa-al-salb bi-ḥaythu taṣduqu iḥdāhumā wa-takdhibu al-ukhrā' (difference of two propositions in affirmation and negation such that one is true and the other false). Note that this excludes differences in terms or quantity alone.
The definition has two key parts: difference in quality (kayf) and the truth condition (one true, one false).
2
Distinguish contradictory from its equal
The author illustrates with the example of 'even' and 'odd': the contradictory of 'even' is 'not even', and 'not even' is equal to 'odd'. Understand that the 'equal of the contradictory' (musāwī lil-naqīḍ) is a term equivalent to the negation, not the negation itself.
This shows that a contradictory is always the direct negation, not a synonym.
3
Apply rules for singular/unrestricted propositions
For a singular proposition (shakhṣiyya) like 'Zayd is standing', or an unrestricted (muhmala) proposition like 'Man is animal', change only the quality: affirmative to negative, or vice versa. The contradictory of 'Zayd is standing' is 'Zayd is not standing'.
No change in quantity because singulars are treated as universals in some contexts; but the rule here is quality change only.
4
Apply rules for quantified propositions
For quantified propositions (muḥaṣṣara), change both quantifier and quality according to the formula: universal affirmative ↔ particular negative; universal negative ↔ affirmative particular. For example, the contradictory of 'Every human is mortal' is 'Some human is not mortal' (not 'No human is mortal').
This matches the standard Aristotelian square of opposition.

Simple Conversion and Its Three Types
Author defines 'aks mustawī and lists its three types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish among simple conversion, contrapositive consistent, and contrapositive inconsistent, and apply each to categorical propositions.

Core Idea
Conversion flips a proposition's parts; three types handle truth, quality, and quantity differently.

The logician al-Sabzawari defines 'conversion' ('aks) as the inversion of subject and predicate while preserving truth, with special rules for quantity and quality. This lesson unpacks his definition and the three types recognized in the tradition: simple conversion ('aks mustawī), contrapositive consistent ('aks al-naqīḍ al-muwāfiq), and contrapositive inconsistent ('aks al-naqīḍ al-mukhālif). You will learn how each type preserves or changes the proposition's truth conditions.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the core definition of 'aks
Read the matn: 'al-'aks is flipping the two parts (subject and predicate) of a proposition while preserving truth, quality, and quantity, except universal affirmative, which becomes particular affirmative.' Note that 'aks is a transformation of a single proposition, not a syllogism.
The Arabic 'قلب جزأي القضية' means reversing the order of subject and predicate.
2
Identify the exception and its rationale
The matn says universal affirmative propositions do not convert simply to a universal affirmative; they become particular affirmative. For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' converts to 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants', not 'Every forbidden thing is an intoxicant'. This is because the predicate may be broader.
This exception is a key difference from universal negatives, which convert universally.
3
Distinguish the two contrapositive types
The commentary lists two additional types: contrapositive consistent (muwāfiq) replaces each extreme by its contradictory, preserving both quality and quantity. Contrapositive inconsistent (mukhālif) replaces only the first extreme by the contradictory of the second, changing the quality. Work through the examples: 'Every human is an animal' yields 'Everything not an animal is not a human' (consistent) and 'Nothing not an animal is a human' (inconsistent).
'Consistent' means the converse agrees in quality with the original; 'inconsistent' means it differs.
4
Apply the rules to Islamic propositions
Take a fiqh proposition: 'Every wine is forbidden'. Write its simple conversion, contrapositive consistent, and contrapositive inconsistent. Check that each preserves truth according to the rules given.

Simple Conversion: Definition and Rules
Author defines simple conversion, its conditions, and exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct simple converse of any categorical proposition, applying the exception for universal affirmatives and the condition related to negation and particularity.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth, quality, and quantity, except universal affirmatives become particular.

This lesson introduces one of the most fundamental immediate inferences in Islamic logic: al-'aks al-mustawi (simple conversion). You will learn how to reverse the terms of a proposition while maintaining its truth conditions, and discover which types of propositions do not have a valid converse. The source passage defines the operation and its exceptions with clear examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operation
Simple conversion means taking a proposition and making its predicate the subject and its subject the predicate. For example, from 'Some human is animal' you get 'Some animal is human'.
2
Check preservation conditions
The converse must preserve truth (if original is true, converse must be true), quality (affirmative stays affirmative, negative stays negative), and quantity (universal stays universal, particular stays particular), with one exception.
3
Apply the exception for universal affirmatives
A universal affirmative (e.g., 'Every human is animal') does NOT convert to a universal affirmative. Instead, it converts to a particular affirmative ('Some animal is human'). This is because the predicate may not apply to all of the subject's instances.
This exception is unique to universal affirmatives.
4
Recognize excluded propositions
Propositions that combine negation (salb) and particularity (juz'iyya) – i.e., particular negatives – have no valid simple converse. Indefinite negatives are treated as particular negatives. Singular propositions, universals, and particular affirmatives all have valid converses.
Example: 'Some human is not animal' does not convert validly.
5
Test with examples
Practice converting different types: 'Zayd is a writer' (singular affirmative) converts to 'Some writer is Zayd'. 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' converts to 'Some forbidden is intoxicant'. Check each conversion for truth preservation.

Definition and Rules of Propositional Conversion (al-ʿAks)
Author defines al-ʿaks and enumerates its types and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct converse for any categorical proposition and explain which proposition types do not convert according to the rules of al-ʿaks.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate, but not all propositions convert equally—universal affirmatives become particular.

In the previous lessons, we studied the structure of categorical propositions. Now we learn how to derive new propositions from them by conversion—a key immediate inference. This lesson walks through the definition, the exceptions, and the conversion rules for each type, using examples from Islamic sciences to ground the operation in real scholarly practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of al-ʿaks
Identify that conversion means making the predicate the subject and the subject the predicate while preserving truth, quality, and quantity. The author stresses that when unqualified, it refers to simple conversion (al-mustawi).
The example given: 'Some human is animal' → 'Some animal is human'.
2
Recognize the universal affirmative exception
Note that a universal affirmative does NOT convert to a universal affirmative but to a particular affirmative. This breaks the pattern of preserving quantity.
Example: 'Every human is animal' → 'Some animal is human'.
3
Apply the condition: no combination of negativity and particularity
Conversion is valid for all propositions except those that combine both negation and particularity. Thus negative particulars and indefinite negatives (equivalent to particular) have no converse.
Check each proposition type against this condition.
4
Systematically convert each proposition type
Work through each type: singular affirmative/negative, universal affirmative/negative, particular affirmative, indefinite affirmative. For each, apply the rule and provide an Islamic science example.
For singular negative with universal predicate, the converse becomes universal negative.
5
Distinguish natural order from verbal order
Conversion applies only to propositions with natural order (categoricals and conjunctive conditionals) where meaning changes upon swapping. Disjunctive conditionals have only verbal order and do not convert.
Example: 'The number is either even or odd' → swapping does not change meaning.

Rules of Conversion for Categorical Propositions
Author explains conversion rules for universal negative, affirmative, and particular propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the correct conversion rule to any categorical proposition and determine whether the converse preserves truth.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate, but truth-preserving rules vary by quantity and quality.

Conversion is an immediate inference that allows you to swap the subject and predicate of a proposition without adding or removing content. However, not every proposition converts – the rules depend on whether the original is universal or particular, affirmative or negative. This lesson walks you through each case so you can confidently perform and verify conversions in your own reasoning.

Guided Path

Identify the proposition type
Determine the quantity (universal, particular, indefinite) and quality (affirmative, negative) of the proposition. This is the first step to knowing which rule applies.
Universal negatives start with 'No...' or 'Nothing of...'; universals with 'Every...'; particulars with 'Some...'.
2
Apply the conversion rule by type
Match the proposition type to the rule: universal negative converts to universal negative; universal affirmative to particular affirmative; particular affirmative to itself; indefinite affirmative to itself or particular affirmative; particular negative and indefinite negative do not convert.
For universal negative, also check if predicate is particular (proper name) – then it also converts to universal negative.
3
Verify the natural order condition
Conversion is only valid for propositions with natural order (categoricals and connected conditionals). Disjunctive conditionals do not convert. Check that the proposition is not a disjunctive conditional.
In categoricals, swapping changes meaning; in disjunctives, order is purely positional.
4
Write the converse and test with an Islamic example
After producing the converse, test it with a concrete example from Islamic sciences, such as a kalam or fiqh proposition, to confirm truth is preserved.
For instance, 'No human is a stone' becomes 'No stone is a human' – both are true.
