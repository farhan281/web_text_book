The Two Fundamental Types of Knowledge: Conception and Assent
Author defines concept and assent, then necessary and theoretical knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conception from assent and classify a given piece of knowledge as necessary or theoretical.

Core Idea
All knowledge is either conception (grasping a term) or assent (judging a relation).

This lesson introduces the foundational classification of all human knowledge according to the Islamic logical tradition. Understanding the difference between simply conceiving a concept and assenting to a proposition is the first step in learning how definitions and proofs work. The author also distinguishes knowledge that is self-evident (necessary) from that which requires reflection (theoretical).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Look at the Arabic text: 'إدراك مفرد تصورا' (perception of a single term is conception) and 'درك نسبة بتصديق' (perception of a relation is assent). Conception is grasping a single concept like 'human'; assent is judging a relation like 'human is rational.'
Conception does not involve truth or falsehood; assent does.
2
Determine the priority between them
The text says 'Give precedence to the first in order because it is prior by nature.' Conception is naturally prior because you must understand the terms before you can form a judgment about them.
This ordering is pedagogical: definitions are taught before proofs.
3
Distinguish necessary from theoretical knowledge
Theoretical (naẓarī) knowledge requires deliberate reflection (ta'ammul). Necessary (ḍarūrī) knowledge is obvious without reflection, like 'the whole is greater than the part.'
Both conception and assent can be necessary or theoretical.
4
Apply the classification to a new example
Take the statement 'Angels are created from light.' The concept 'angel' is a conception; the judgment that they are created from light is an assent. Is this assent necessary or theoretical? It requires reflection or revelation, so it is theoretical.
You can now analyse any piece of knowledge using this framework.

Commentary

The author then states that conception is naturally prior to assent, so it should be presented first in teaching. Then he divides knowledge into theoretical (naẓarī) which requires reflection (ta'ammul), and necessary (ḍarūrī) which is self-evident. This classification is foundational for the entire science of logic, as logic deals primarily with theoretical knowledge that requires reasoning.

The Two Types of Acquired Knowledge: Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author defines the two types of acquired knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate tasawwur from tasdiq and identify the necessary versus theoretical in each.

Core Idea
Every acquired knowledge is either a simple concept or an assent to a relation.

This lesson presents the foundational epistemological division in Islamic logic. Understanding the difference between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent) is the gateway to all further logical tools. The author also explains why tasawwur comes first in teaching and divides knowledge into necessary (self-evident) and theoretical (requiring reflection).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of acquired knowledge
Read the first sentence: 'The perception of a single thing is called tasawwur and the perception of a relation is called tasdiq.' Note that tasawwur is a simple mental form, while tasdiq involves a relation between two concepts.
Tasawwur is like understanding 'human' alone; tasdiq is like judging 'human is rational'.
2
Understand the natural priority of tasawwur
The author states that tasawwur is placed first in teaching because it is prior by nature. You must first grasp concepts before you can form judgments about them.
This order is not arbitrary but reflects the logical dependence of judgment on concepts.
3
Distinguish necessary from theoretical knowledge
The passage says theoretical knowledge needs reflection, while necessary knowledge is self-evident. Apply this distinction to both tasawwur and tasdiq.
For example, the concept of 'existence' is necessary; the concept of 'angel' requires reflection. Similarly, 'the whole is greater than its part' is a necessary tasdiq; 'the world is created' is theoretical.
4
Learn the tools that produce each type
The tool for tasawwur is 'qawl sharh' (definition/description), and the tool for tasdiq is 'hujja' (proof). Recognize that definitions lead to conception, arguments lead to assent.
In Islamic sciences, a definition of 'riba' leads to tasawwur, while a proof that riba is forbidden leads to tasdiq.

Defines the two kinds of knowledge and their means.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and identify their respective means (qawl sharh and hujja).

Core Idea
Knowledge is either conception or assent; each has its own tool.

This passage from Idah al-Mubham lays the foundation for all logical study. It declares that knowledge divides into two primary types: tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). Each is attained through a specific instrument: tasawwur through a qawl sharh (explicative statement) and tasdiq through a hujja (proof). Understanding this division is essential because every subsequent logical operation will be classified as either serving conception or assent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two kinds of knowledge
Read the Arabic and translation. Notice that the passage presents two parallel clauses: one about tasawwur and one about tasdiq. These are the two fundamental categories of knowledge in Islamic logic.
2
Note the instrument for each
Tasawwur is reached 'bi-qawl sharh' (by an explicative statement), while tasdiq is reached 'bi-hujja' (by a proof). Memorize these pairings: conception → definition, assent → proof.
3
Understand the foundational role
This classification is axiomatic. All later topics in logic (definitions, syllogisms, etc.) are tools either for tasawwur or for tasdiq. Recognize that mastering this distinction prevents confusion between defining a term and proving a proposition.

Types of Acquired Knowledge: Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Definition of acquired knowledge and its division into tasawwur and tasdiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and identify the four types of acquired knowledge (tasawwur daruri, tasawwur nazari, tasdiq daruri, tasdiq nazari).

Core Idea
Knowledge is cognition; it divides into concept and assent – the twin pillars of all understanding.

This passage opens the discussion of acquired knowledge by explaining its fundamental division into two categories: tasawwur (concept) and tasdiq (assent). The author, commenting on a verse matn, clarifies what each term means and further divides them into necessary and theoretical. This foundation is crucial for all subsequent logical and theological reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn and its commentary
The passage begins with verse lines (matn) followed by 'I say' (aqulu) – the commentary. Separate the matn lines from the commentary to see what the author is explaining.
The matn lines are: 'أنواع العلم الحادث ... فلتتبتهل' etc.
2
Understand the division of knowledge
The commentary states that knowledge is cognition of the known ('ilm: ma'rifat al-ma'lum). It then divides into tasawwur and tasdiq. Recognize that each division is further split into necessary (daruri) and theoretical (nazari), giving four types.
Necessary knowledge does not require reflection; theoretical knowledge requires deliberation.
3
Distinguish tasawwur from tasdiq using examples
Tasawwur is perception of a single meaning without judgment, e.g., the meaning of 'Zayd'. Tasdiq is perception of the occurrence of a relation, e.g., 'Zayd is standing' – you judge that standing actually occurs.
The key is the presence or absence of judgment (hukm).
4
Identify the tools corresponding to each type
The matn says: to reach a tasawwur, you use a 'qawl sharih' (explicative statement/definition); to reach a tasdiq, you use a 'hujja' (proof). This shows how the division directly applies to definition and argumentation.
This links the theoretical division to practical logical tools.

The Nature of Assent: Philosophers vs. Imam al-Razi
Contrast between philosophers and Imam al-Razi on the composition of assent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the philosophers' and Imam al-Razi's definitions of tasdiq and explain how the difference affects the analysis of propositions.

Core Idea
Is assent a single concept or a composite of four? This debate shapes how we understand every proposition.

In this passage, the author delves into a subtle but significant disagreement regarding the nature of assent (tasdiq). After explaining the components of the proposition 'Zayd is standing', he presents two positions: the philosophers consider assent to be only the fourth component (the perception of occurrence), while Imam al-Razi considers it to be the sum of all four components. This distinction has implications for how we analyze statements of faith and legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Find the proposition analyzed in the passage
The author uses 'Zayd is standing' (زيد قائم) as an example. Identify the four conceptual components he extracts from it: subject (Zayd), predicate (standing), relational judgment, and occurrence.
These are the building blocks of any proposition.
2
Understand the philosophers' view
The philosophers (al-Hukama') say that assent is only the fourth component: the perception of the occurrence of the relation. The first three are merely conditions (shurut) for assent, not part of it.
Thus assent is a simple concept in their view.
3
Understand Imam al-Razi's view
Imam al-Razi holds that assent is the entire set of four concepts together. In his view, assent is composite (murakkab).
This means that accepting a proposition involves simultaneously grasping all four components as a whole.
4
Note how the author resolves the difference
The author sides with the philosophers. He notes that the matn can be read with an omitted word: 'perception of the occurrence of a relation' rather than 'perception of a relation'. This subtle addition supports the philosophers' view.
This shows how interpretation of a text can align with a particular logical doctrine.

The first three are prerequisites, and the fourth is the actual assent. He presents two positions: the philosophers (al-Hukama') hold that assent is only the fourth, while Imam al-Razi holds that assent is the combination of all four. The author sides with the philosophers by assuming an omitted word ('occurrence') between 'perception' and 'relation' in the matn, meaning the verse says 'perception of the occurrence of a relation'.

Priority of Tasawwur and the Four Types of Knowledge
Defines tasawwur and tasdiq, orders them, and divides each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the natural order of tasawwur and tasdiq and classify a given piece of knowledge as daruri or nazari.

Core Idea
Tasawwur is the condition for tasdiq; knowledge is either immediate or requires reflection.

The logical journey begins with grasping concepts (tasawwur) before making judgments (tasdiq). The passage explains why tasawwur must come first, then divides each into necessary (daruri) knowledge that comes without effort and theoretical (nazari) knowledge that requires reflection. This gives us four fundamental categories of human knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main kinds of knowledge
From the passage, find the definitions of tasawwur (simple apprehension) and tasdiq (assent to a judgment). Notice that tasawwur is about grasping a meaning without judging, while tasdiq is about affirming or denying a relation.
2
Determine the natural priority
The author says tasawwur is 'prior by nature' (مُقَدَّم عليه طبعا). This means conceptually, before we can assent to a proposition, we must first understand its components (subject and predicate). Therefore, tasawwur must be studied first.
Analogy: You must know what 'wine' is before you can accept that it is forbidden.
3
Distinguish daruri and nazari
Daruri (necessary) knowledge does not require reflection; it is immediate. Nazari (theoretical) requires thought and reasoning. The passage gives examples: grasping 'one is half of two' is daruri, but 'one is half of a sixth of twelve' requires calculation.
Daruri does not mean it is infallible; it means it is self-evident or based on immediate sense perception.
4

Combine the two dimensions: tasawwur daruri, tasawwur nazari, tasdiq daruri, tasdiq nazari. For each, think of an Islamic example. E.g., tasawwur daruri: understanding the word 'Allah'; tasdiq daruri: affirming that 'the whole is greater than its part' is a necessary truth.

Two Doctrines on the Nature of Tasdiq: Simple vs Composite
Compares two doctrines on tasdiq: philosophers vs al-Razi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast the philosophers' and al-Razi's definitions of tasdiq and identify the four components in al-Razi's view.

Core Idea
For philosophers tasdiq is a single assent; for al-Razi it is four perceptions working together.

The passage presents two interpretations of tasdiq. The philosophers (al-hukama') view it as a simple act of grasping that a relation is actual. Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, however, analyzes it into four distinct perceptions: the subject, the predicate, the relational judgment, and the assent to that relation. This difference has implications for how we analyze knowledge and certainty.

Guided Path
1
Recall the simple definition of tasdiq
The philosophers define tasdiq as 'grasping that the relation is actual or not actual' – a single cognitive act of assent. It is not composed of smaller parts.
Example: assenting to 'Zayd is writing' is one mental event.
2
List al-Razi's four components
Al-Razi says tasdiq involves: (1) perception of the subject, (2) perception of the predicate, (3) perception of the relational judgment (affirmation/negation), and (4) perception that the relation holds or not. These are separable aspects.
3
Compare the two views
The key difference is simplicity vs. composition. For the philosophers, tasdiq is a single ‘yes’ or ‘no’. For al-Razi, it is a synthesis of multiple perceptions. The passage emphasizes that this difference is 'clear' (ظاهر).
Think of it like a smartphone: philosophers see it as one device; al-Razi sees it as screen, battery, processor, etc.
4
Apply to an example
Take the proposition 'The Quran is the word of Allah'. According to the philosophers, tasdiq is simply accepting that this relation holds. According to al-Razi, one must: grasp 'Quran', grasp 'word of Allah', grasp the affirmative relation, and then assent to that relation being true.
