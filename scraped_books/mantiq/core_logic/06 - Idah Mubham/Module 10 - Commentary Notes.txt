Decoding Islamic Colophons: Date and Imagery
Commentator reports the date of composition and explains the astronomical references in the colophon.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the date and astronomical imagery in an Islamic colophon.

Core Idea
Colophons encode dates and blessings in poetic astronomy.

This passage from the commentary on al-Sullam al-Munawraq reveals how the author concealed the composition date (Muharram 941 AH) and a perpetual blessing using celestial imagery: the sun cutting through zodiacal towers and the full moon rising in darkness. Understanding this unlocks a common feature of Islamic manuscripts where colophons embed chronological and devotional information in symbolic language.

Guided Path
1
Locate the date line
Find the phrase 'أوائل المحرم سنة احدى وأربعين وتسعمائة' in the Arabic. This gives the exact date: the beginning of Muharram 941 AH.
The year is given in words, not numerals.
2
Identify the key astronomical terms
The passage mentions 'أبرجا' (plural of 'برج'), 'بدر', and 'دجى'. Note their definitions: burj = one of 12 zodiacal signs (30 degrees each), badr = full moon on the 14th night, duja = darkness.
These terms are common in classical Arabic astronomical descriptions.

Understand the perpetual time expression
The phrase 'ما قطعت شمس النهار أبرجا' is not a conditional but a device to indicate 'as long as the sun continues its daily course through the zodiac', i.e., forever. The commentator clarifies this intention.
This is a standard rhetorical trope in early modern Islamic texts.
4
Relate the imagery to the solar year
The sun cuts one degree of a burj each day, completing the entire sphere (360°) in one solar year. This reinforces the idea of perpetual duration.
The lunar month reference (badr on 14th) complements the solar imagery.

Defending Metaphors: The Cloud of Ignorance Debate
Commentator explains the metaphor of removing ignorance as a cloud from the sky of intellect and answers objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two objections raised against a poetic metaphor in Islamic commentary and explain how each objection is resolved using linguistic and logical reasoning.

Core Idea
A metaphor's validity depends on shared attributes, not existential status.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks a dense poetic metaphor likening the intellect to a sky and ignorance to a cloud. He then addresses two objections: one about the grammatical conjunction, the other about comparing a non-existent (ignorance) to an existent (cloud). You will learn how the commentator uses Arabic grammar and a broader definition of ignorance to defend the metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Unpack the Metaphor
Identify the two sides of the comparison: intellect as sky, ignorance as cloud. Note the shared attribute: both are 'places' for stars (intellectual/spiritual vs sensory) and both are veils. Understand the grammatical process of removing the simile tool and converting to iḍāfah.
The original expression 'intellect like the sky' becomes 'sky of intellect' through deletion of the simile tool.
2
Identify the First Objection
Find the objection about the conjunction of 'remove' to 'bring out'. What type of conjunction is it? The commentator answers it is a cause-effect conjunction: removing the veil causes the manifestation of results.
In Arabic grammar, ʿaṭf al-sabab ʿalā al-musabbab is a recognized category of conjunction.
3
Identify the Second Objection
Find the objection that ignorance (non-existent) cannot be compared to a cloud (existent). How does the commentator respond? By offering an alternative definition of ignorance as mistaken perception, which is an existent quality.
This shows that definitions can be expanded to include both absence and misperception.
4
Evaluate the Responses
Assess whether the responses are logically sound. Both objections are resolved: the conjunction is explained by cause-effect, and the existential mismatch is resolved by redefining ignorance as an existent misperception, thus preserving the shared attribute (being a veil).
The responses rely on recognized linguistic and conceptual principles.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a poetic line that uses metaphor. The answer to the second is that ignorance can be defined not just as absence of knowledge but as a mistaken perception, which is an existent quality, so the comparison is valid.

The Three Types of Verbal Conventional Indication: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, and Iltizam
Defines three types of verbal conventional indication with commentary on the verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between the three types of verbal conventional indication (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) in any given example from an Islamic text.

Core Idea
Words indicate meanings in three ways: whole, part, or necessary concomitant.

In Islamic logic and legal theory, words are not random signs; they have conventional relationships with meanings. The author defines three specific types of indication: conformity (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadammun), and commitment (iltizam). Understanding these distinctions is essential for interpreting texts accurately, whether in Qur'anic exegesis, Hadith, or juristic reasoning. This lesson unpacks each type with examples.

Guided Path
1
Extract the three types
From the verse, identify the three named types: دلالة المطابقة (conformity), دلالة التضمن (inclusion), دلالة الالتزام (commitment). Notice the conditions: for iltizam, the connection must be by the mind ('aql).
The verse explicitly defines each: 'دلالة اللفظ على ما وافقه' (mutabaqa), 'جزئه' (tadammun), 'ما لزم' (iltizam).
2
Understand the scope of each type
Mutabaqa indicates the entire meaning assigned to the word. Tadammun indicates a part of that meaning. Iltizam indicates something external to the meaning but necessarily linked by the mind.
For example, 'man' indicates 'rational animal' (mutabaqa), 'animal' (tadammun), and 'capacity for speech' (iltizam).
3
Apply to Islamic examples
Take any Islamic term, such as 'salah' (prayer). Identify what each type indicates: the whole act (mutabaqa), its parts like ruku' (tadammun), and its obligation (iltizam).
This skill is crucial for interpreting legal and theological texts.
4
Analyze the commentator's clarification
The commentator points out that the verse uses 'دلالة اللفظ' and the title uses 'الوضعية', each omitting the other's word. This rhetorical device (ihtibak) ensures both together specify 'verbal conventional indication'.
This shows how careful reading of the text is needed to fully grasp the author's intended meaning.

This passage explains the three types of conventional (wad'iyya) indication of words. The author begins by quoting a verse that enumerates them: (1) dalalat al-mutabaqa (indication of conformity): the word indicates the entire meaning it was assigned to, e.g., the word 'man' indicates the whole animal rational. (2) dalalat al-tadammun (indication of inclusion): the word indicates a part of its meaning, e.g., 'man' indicates animal. (3) dalalat al-iltizam (indication of commitment): the word indicates something external to its meaning but necessarily connected to it by the mind, e.g., 'man' indicates the capacity for speech. The commentator then clarifies a subtlety: the verse uses 'dalalat al-lafz' (indication of the word), and the title says 'al-wad'iyya' (conventional). Together they show that the intended type is verbal conventional indication. He notes a rhetorical device called 'ihtibak': the title omits the word 'lafz' that is in the verse, and the verse omits the word 'wad'iyya' that is in the title, each supplying what the other lacks.

Literal Domains: Singular/Compound for Words, Universal/Particular for Meanings
Clarifies the literal domains of singular/compound and universal/particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the literal and metaphorical applications of singular/compound and universal/particular to words and meanings, and correctly classify any term from an Islamic science text.

Core Idea
Words are literally singular or compound; meanings are literally universal or particular.

In logic and usul al-fiqh, we often talk about 'singular terms' and 'universal meanings'. But are these descriptions literal or metaphorical? This short benefit from the text draws a crisp line: the properties of singularity and composition belong literally to words, while universality and particularity belong literally to meanings. Mixing them up leads to confusion. Let's unpack this distinction so you can apply it when analyzing definitions and propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dichotomies
The passage presents two distinctions: singular (مفرد) vs. compound (مركب), and universal (كلي) vs. particular (جزئي). Note that each distinction applies primarily to a different domain.
The first pair is about linguistic form; the second is about conceptual content.
2
Determine literal domain for each pair
The text states that singular/compound are literally (حقيقة) descriptions of words (لفظ). Universality/particularity are literally descriptions of meanings (معنى).
Memorize this: لفظ for مفرد/مركب, معنى for كلي/جزئي.
3
Understand the metaphorical cross-application
When we call a meaning 'compound' or a word 'universal', that usage is metaphorical (مجاز). The passage clarifies that such cross-description is not literal.
Example: 'Lion' is a singular word but its meaning is universal. Saying 'lion is universal' is metaphorical for the word, literal for the meaning.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
In usul al-fiqh, the term 'صلاة' (prayer) is a singular word. Its meaning is a particular act. But when we say 'الصلاة مأمور بها' (prayer is commanded), we treat the meaning as a universal command. The word 'صلاة' is not literally universal; the concept is.
This distinction avoids category errors when discussing general rules.

Commentary
This short note clarifies a terminological subtlety. The terms 'singular' (مفرد) and 'compound' (مركب) apply primarily and literally to linguistic expressions (لفظ). When we speak of a 'singular meaning' or 'compound meaning', it is a metaphorical extension. Conversely, 'universal' (كلي) and 'particular' (جزئي) apply literally to meanings (معنى); calling a word 'universal' or 'particular' is metaphorical. This distinction prevents confusion: a word like 'lion' is grammatically singular but logically universal in meaning. The classification focuses on the concept, not the word.
