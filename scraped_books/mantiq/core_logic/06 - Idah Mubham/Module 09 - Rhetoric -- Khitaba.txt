Distinguishing General and Specific Results: Avoiding Redundancy
Explains why the third verse is not redundant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general and specific results in a theological argument and identify when a later verse is not redundant.

Core Idea
Redundancy is avoided when a later verse specifies a subset of the earlier general claim.

An objection arises: the third verse seems redundant because the first verse already mentions results. The commentator clarifies that the first verse is general, while the third specifies results that were hidden due to subtlety. This lesson unpacks that logical distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the redundancy claim
Find in the passage where the objector says the first verse suffices for the third. Note the exact wording.
2
Extract the generality of the first verse
The commentator says the results in the first verse are general ('aamm), not restricted to hidden or non-hidden.
3
Note the specificity of the third verse
The third verse specifically addresses 'mukhaddarat'—results hidden due to subtlety. This is a subset of the general results.
4
Verify non-redundancy using the principle of umum wa khusus
General and specific are not redundant; the specific adds detail. Confirm that the third verse adds information about the nature of the results.

His statement: 'wabadat' i.e., appeared; it is an utmost excellence. His statement: 'shumus al-ma'rifa' i.e., knowledge like suns, so treatment similar to previous. 'Al-mukhaddarat' (hidden ones) from 'al-khidr' meaning concealment; 'munakashifa' (unveiled) meaning apparent. The intent of the verse is the completion of the removal of the veil from their intellects through the appearance of the suns of knowledge that were hidden due to their subtlety. (There are two questions about this verse:) First, the first verse suffices for it; so it would have been better to mention it alongside the first or after it, so that each is a result of the removal of veils. (The answer to the first:) The results in the first verse are general, whether distant and hidden due to subtlety or not; what is in the second verse is specific to the hidden subtle ones, so the first does not suffice for the second. (The answer to the second:) He placed the first verse first out of desire for 'bara'at al-istihlal', so it was not possible to place it alongside the third verse, and he was compelled to delay the third because it is the culmination of what preceded, so it was not possible to place it alongside the first.

Commentary
This passage explains the third verse about the 'suns of knowledge' (shumus al-ma'rifa) appearing. The commentator interprets 'wabadat' as 'appeared' and says it is a supreme excellence. 'Shumus al-ma'rifa' is an annexation: 'knowledge like suns', with the same method of simile as before (omitting the particle). 'Mukhaddarat' refers to hidden things, from 'khidr' meaning concealment, and 'munakashifa' means unveiled. The verse describes the complete removal of veils resulting in the appearance of knowledge that was previously hidden due to its subtlety. Two objections are raised: first, the first verse already covers results, so this verse seems redundant. The answer: the first verse is general (includes all results, whether hidden or not), while this verse is specific to results that were hidden due to subtlety, so no redundancy. Second, why not place this verse alongside the first? The answer: the first verse was placed first for rhetorical elegance (bara'at al-istihlal), and the third verse is the culmination (ghaya) of the removal process, so it is placed after the second verse, not alongside the first.

Rhetorical Order: The Role of Bara'at al-Istihlal
Explains the ordering of verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rhetorical device bara'at al-istihlal and explain why the third verse is placed after the second, not alongside the first.

Core Idea
Bara'at al-istihlal opens the composition with a hint of the theme, dictating order.

The second objection asks why the third verse is not placed next to the first. The answer uses the rhetorical principle of bara'at al-istihlal and the fact that the third verse is the culmination (ghaya) of the removal process. This lesson explores how rhetorical and logical considerations determine textual arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ordering objection
Find in the passage where the objector says the third verse should be alongside the first.
2
Define bara'at al-istihlal
This is a rhetorical device where the opening line hints at the overall theme. The first verse serves this purpose.
3
Explain why the first verse must come first
Bara'at al-istihlal requires the first verse to be at the very beginning, so it cannot be moved later.
4
Explain why the third verse is the culmination and must be last
The third verse describes the final result (appearance of hidden knowledge), so it must come at the end of the sequence.
