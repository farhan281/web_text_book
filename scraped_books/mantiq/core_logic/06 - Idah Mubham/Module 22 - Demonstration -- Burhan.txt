Learning Objective

Core Idea
A syllogism's chain of proof cannot go on forever; it must rest on bedrock certainties.

This lesson addresses a crucial epistemological requirement for any sound syllogistic demonstration. The author insists that the premises used in reasoning cannot all be speculative; they must eventually be grounded in self-evident truths. Without such foundations, any proof would either loop back on itself or continue indefinitely, both of which undermine certainty.

Author states the requirement and gives a simple categorical example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to verify whether a given syllogism's premises are either necessary themselves or trace back to necessary premises.

Core Idea
Demonstration requires premises that are either self-evident or traceable to them.

In demonstration (burhan), we cannot have an infinite regress of premises. The author explains that premises must either be self-evidently necessary or lead back to such premises. He gives a simple syllogism about the number four to illustrate a premise that is itself necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two premises
Find the two premises in the categorical syllogism: 'The four is divisible into two equals' and 'Everything divisible into two equals is even.'
2
Check each premise for necessity
Determine if each premise is self-evidently true. 'The four is divisible into two equals' is a necessary truth about numbers. 'Everything divisible into two equals is even' is also a necessary truth about evenness.
3
See if the conclusion follows
The conclusion 'The four is even' necessarily follows. Since both premises are necessary, the syllogism is built on necessary premises.
4
Understand the principle
The author's point: premises themselves can be necessary (like here) or can be traced back to necessary premises (like the next example).

A Compound Conditional Proof of God's Existence
Author gives a compound conditional argument for God's existence as an example of premises terminating at necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the chain of conditional syllogisms in a compound proof and identify the final necessary premise.

Core Idea
Complex proofs can be built from chains of conditionals, each step resting on a premise that ends in a necessary truth.

The author's second example is a profound theological argument using a series of conditional syllogisms. Starting from the assumption that God is not necessary, it leads through a chain to the absurd conclusion of cosmic corruption. By denying that consequence, we affirm God's necessary existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
This is a compound conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i muttasil) with a chain of conditionals. The overall form is modus tollens: If P then Q; if Q then R; ... if Z then W; not W, therefore not P.
2
Extract the conditionals
List each conditional: (1) If not necessary then possible. (2) If possible then originated. (3) If originated then needs originator. (4) If needs originator then multiple gods. (5) If multiple gods then corruption.
3
Identify the exception (istithna')
The exception is the negation of the final consequent: 'but corruption is negated' (the heavens and earth are not corrupted).
4
Apply modus tollens repeatedly
By denying the final consequent, we deny the antecedent of each conditional back to the original: therefore God's non-necessity is negated, so He is necessary.
5
Identify the necessary premise
The final conditional 'if multiple gods then corruption' is taken as a necessary premise, either from rational argument or revelation (Quran 21:22).

Refutation of Reasoning as Cause of Conclusion
Author refutes the fourth false philosophical position regarding causality of reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the author's argument against the philosophers' causal theory of reasoning.

Core Idea
A cause cannot separate from its effect; reasoning and knowledge are opposites, so reasoning cannot cause knowledge.

This short but powerful passage strikes at the heart of a key philosophical doctrine: that reasoning (nazar) generates the conclusion as a cause produces an effect. The author invokes a simple logical principle—cause and effect cannot be separated in time or existence—and then shows that reasoning and its conclusion (which is a form of knowledge) are mutually exclusive. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to dissect this refutation and appreciate its significance in the Islamic critique of Greek causal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
Locate the philosophers' claim: that the conclusion (natija) is an effect (ma'lul) of reasoning (nazar), and that reasoning is a cause ('illa). The author states this as the fourth false position.
The term 'عِلَّة' (cause) here is used in the efficient sense, not merely a reason or ground.
2
Extract the author's core argument
The author provides a two-step argument: (1) A cause does not separate from its effect (العلة لا تفارق معلولها). (2) Reasoning does not coexist with the conclusion (النظر لا يجامع النتيجة) because reasoning is opposite to knowledge (ضد العلم). Therefore, reasoning cannot be a cause of the conclusion.
The key premise: 'ضد العلم' (opposite of knowledge) – reasoning is a process that ceases once knowledge is attained.
3
Analyse the logical structure
The argument is a modus tollens: If reasoning were a cause, it would coexist with its effect. But it does not coexist (since it is opposite to knowledge). Therefore, reasoning is not a cause.
This is an immediate inference, not a full syllogism. The author relies on the principle of inseparability of cause and effect.
4
Assess the validity and force
The argument is valid if we accept the premise that cause and effect must coexist. The author's insight is that reasoning is a process that ends when its conclusion is known, so they cannot coexist. This exposes a fundamental mismatch between the causal model and the nature of reasoning.
Later logicians (like al-Taftazani) refine this: reasoning is a means to attain knowledge, but not an efficient cause.

Therefore, reasoning cannot be a cause of the conclusion in the technical sense used by these philosophers. This is an important critical point, common in later Islamic logic texts that distance themselves from the Faylasufs' causal theories.

Errors in Demonstration: Material vs. Formal Fallacies
Author lists types of errors in demonstration, dividing them into material and formal fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between material and formal errors in demonstration and identify the category of a given fallacy.

Core Idea
Every fallacy in demonstration is either in matter or form—knowing which tells you how to fix it.

In this crucial passage, the author classifies errors in demonstration (khata' al-burhan) into two fundamental types: errors in matter (al-madda) and errors in form (al-sura). Material fallacies involve false or ambiguous premises, while formal fallacies involve incorrect syllogistic structure. Understanding this division is the first step in diagnosing why an argument fails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The first line states that error occurs either in matter or form: 'فِي مَادَةٍ أَوْ صُورَةٍ'. These are the overarching types.
2
Locate examples of material fallacies
The passage lists material fallacies related to wording (اللفظ) and meaning (المعاني). Examples include ambiguity (اشتراك) and confusing accidental with essential.
3
Locate the description of formal fallacies
The second type is 'like going outside its figures and leaving the condition of the conclusion'. This refers to violating the rules of syllogistic figures (ashkal) or conditions for a valid conclusion.
4
Apply the distinction to a new example
Given a fallacy like 'using a false premise' vs. 'affirming the consequent', decide which is material and which is formal.
Affirming consequent is formal; false premise is material.

This classification is derived from Aristotle's Sophistici Elenchi and is standard in Islamic logic texts.

Material Fallacies in Wording: Ambiguity and Co-extensive Terms
Author lists types of errors in demonstration, dividing them into material and formal fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and avoid the fallacy of verbal ambiguity (ishtirak) and the misuse of co-extensive terms (taradif) in demonstration.

Core Idea
Ambiguity and mistaken identity of terms are material fallacies that undermine certainty even if the syllogistic form is valid.

The author warns against two specific errors in wording: using ambiguous terms (ishtirak) that shift meaning within an argument, and treating co-extensive terms (taradif) as if they have different meanings. These fallacies corrupt the premises because the same term or equivalent terms are not used consistently.

Guided Path
1
Understand ambiguity (اشتراك)
An ambiguous term has multiple meanings. In a syllogism, if the middle term is used in one sense in one premise and another sense in the other, the argument commits the fallacy of equivocation.
2
Understand co-extensive terms (الرديف)
Co-extensive terms are synonyms or terms with the same denotation. If a syllogism treats them as if they are different, it may lead to invalid reasoning, e.g., assuming 'man' and 'rational animal' are different when they are identical in reference.
3
Find the line in the passage
The author says: 'فَالْمُبْتَدَا فِي اللَّفْظِ كَاشْتِرَاكِ أَوْ كَجَعْلِ ذَا تَبَايُنِ مِثْلَ الرَّدِيْفِ مَأْخَذَا'. This means the first error in wording is like ambiguity, or like making something that has a true co-extensive term into something that has different meanings.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the argument: 'Every intoxicating drink is forbidden. Nabidh is an intoxicating drink. Therefore nabidh is forbidden.' But 'intoxicating' could mean chemically intoxicating or spiritually intoxicating (implied). If the meaning shifts, it's an ambiguity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A correct syllogism requires both true premises (no material fallacy) and valid form (no formal fallacy).

Types of Error in Demonstration: Material and Formal
Classifies errors in burhan into material and formal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify errors in demonstration into material (subdivided into wording and meaning) and formal categories, and identify specific fallacies such as ambiguity (ishtirak), false synonymy (tabāyun), and confusion of accidental with essential.

Core Idea
Any break in the consistency of the middle term — whether by wording or meaning — invalidates the demonstration.

This passage from Idah al-Mubham begins the conclusion (khatima) on errors in burhan. It establishes that sound demonstration requires avoiding errors in both matter (premises) and form (structure). Material errors are further divided into those related to wording — such as using an ambiguous term (ishtirak) or treating two different terms as synonyms (tabāyun) — and those related to meaning, like confusing an accidental attribute with an essential one. The examples clarify how each error breaks the repetition of the middle term, rendering the syllogism invalid. This taxonomy is foundational for critical thinking in Islamic sciences, where precision in terms and concepts is paramount.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish material vs. formal error
Determine whether the error lies in the content of the premises (e.g., false or ambiguous) or in the logical structure (figure/mood). The passage identifies both types.
Formal errors are briefly mentioned; the focus is on material errors.
2
For material errors, identify wording or meaning
If the error is in matter, check whether it originates from the wording (lafẓ) or the meaning (maʿnā). The passage gives two examples of wording errors and one of meaning error.
3
Analyze wording errors: ishtirak and tabāyun
In ishtirak, a single word with multiple meanings is used differently in the two premises, breaking the middle term. In tabāyun, two words thought to be synonyms actually have different meanings, also breaking the middle term.
Example: 'This is an eye (sun)' vs 'Every eye (spring) is flowing' — the term 'eye' shifts meaning.
4
Analyze meaning error: accidental vs. essential
Check if a property described in one premise is accidental (ʿaraḍī) while in the other it is essential (dhātī). The ship passenger example shows accidental motion ('moves' relative to the ship) confused with essential motion (self-motion), making the middle term inconsistent.
This error is subtle because the same word 'moves' is used, but the sense differs.
5
Verify the middle term's consistency
For any syllogism, confirm that the middle term (ḥadd al-wasaṭ) has exactly the same meaning in both premises. If it shifts, the syllogism is invalid regardless of truth of premises.
This rule is crucial in Islamic jurisprudence (usul al-fiqh) when deriving rulings.
