Three Opinions on Studying Logic
Author presents three scholarly opinions on the permissibility of studying logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scholarly opinions on the permissibility of studying logic and explain the condition attached to the permissible opinion.

Core Idea
Logic is permissible when grounded in revelation, but not without it.

This passage from the Idah al-Mubham presents three distinct positions on studying logic. Knowing these opinions helps you understand the traditional Islamic stance on logic as a tool, not an independent source of knowledge. The author supports permissibility but with a critical condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first opinion
The first verse states that Ibn al-Salah and al-Nawawi declared the study of logic prohibited (haram). Note the names of these two scholars.
This is the strictest position.
2
Identify the second opinion
A group (qawm) said that logic should be learned (yanbaghi an yu'lama). They are not named individually.
This position may imply it is recommended or obligatory, but the text does not specify.
3
Identify the third opinion
The famous correct opinion (al-qawlatu al-mashhuratu al-sahiha) is that logic is permissible (jawaz) for one who has a complete natural intelligence (kamil al-qariha).
This is the opinion the author endorses.
4
Examine the condition
The permissible opinion is conditional: the person must also practice (tumaris) the Sunna and the Quran so that he may be guided to the truth. This ensures logic serves revelation.
Without this condition, logic could lead to error.

Chapter on the permissibility of engaging in it. 15. The disagreement regarding the permissibility of engaging in it is upon three sayings: 16. Ibn al-Salah and al-Nawawi declared it prohibited, and some people said it should be learned. 17. Source pages p. 52

Commentary
This passage presents a critical classification of the rulings on studying logic. The author begins by announcing a chapter on the permissibility (جواز) of engaging in the science of logic. The first verse (15) states that the disagreement (الخلاف) on this issue is divided into three distinct sayings. The second verse (16) names the first two: Imam Ibn al-Salah and Imam al-Nawawi declared it prohibited (حرما), while another group (قوم) held that it ought to be learned (ينبغي أن يعلم). The third verse (17) gives the third, famous and correct opinion (القولة المشهورة الصحيحة): it is permissible (جوازه) for one who has a complete natural intelligence (كامل القريحة), provided that he also practices (تمارس) the Sunna and the Quran, so that through that practice he may be guided to the truth. The author thus endorses the permissibility position but attaches a condition of grounding oneself in the revealed sources, ensuring that logic serves rather than subverts the Islamic sciences.

Parsing the Structure of the Assents Section
Author enumerates the section's structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the organisational structure of the second section and identify the logical progression from propositions to syllogisms to a critique of demonstration.

Core Idea
The section is a journey: from propositions, through syllogisms, to a critique of certainty.

This passage is the structural blueprint for the entire second section on assents (tasdiqat). Before diving into detailed rules, it is crucial to understand how the author organises the material: from principles (propositions and their immediate inferences) to objectives (syllogism and its figures), then appendices, argument divisions, and finally a critical conclusion. This roadmap will help you see the logical flow and anticipate the key topics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main parts
Notice the division into 'mabadi'' (principles) and 'maqasid' (objectives). The principles cover qadaya (propositions) and their rulings; the objectives cover qiyas (syllogism) and its types.
This bipartite structure mirrors the distinction between material and formal logic.
2
Trace the sequence within each part
Under principles: first proposition types (categorical/conditional), then rulings (contradiction, conversion). Under objectives: syllogism, its figures, appendices, and divisions of argument.
The sequence shows logical dependency: you need to understand propositions before syllogisms.
3
Spot the conclusion's purpose
The section ends with 'clarifying the error of demonstration' (bayān khaṭa' al-burhān). This signals a critical turn: after learning how demonstrations work, the author points out their limits.
This is not a dismissal of logic but a refinement: knowing when a demonstration can fail.
4
Relate to your own learning path
Plan your study: first master propositions and their conversions, then practice constructing syllogisms in different figures, and finally evaluate critiques of demonstrative reasoning.
Using this roadmap will prevent confusion about where topics fit.

The second section: The Assents. Principles of the Assents (propositions and their rulings). The divisions of categorical and conditional propositions. Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for the second major section of the book, which deals with tasdiqat (assents), i.e., judgments that can be true or false. It begins with mabadi' (principles), covering qadaya (propositions) and their ahkam (rulings), including the division into categorical (hamliyya) and conditional (shartiyya), then the immediate inferences of tanāquḍ (contradiction) and 'aks (conversion). Next are maqasid (objectives), which refer to the qiyas (syllogism), its types, rulings, and figures (ashkal). Then lawāḥiq (appendices) expand on syllogistic matters. The section ends with a discussion of aqsam al-hujja (divisions of the argument) and a conclusion (khatima) clarifying the error of demonstration (burhan). This outline shows the progression from basic propositional logic to complex syllogistic reasoning, culminating in a critique of demonstration.
