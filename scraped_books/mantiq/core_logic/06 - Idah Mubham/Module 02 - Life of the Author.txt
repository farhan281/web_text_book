The Comprehensive Scholar: Lessons from Ahmad al-Damanhuri's Biography
Author provides a biography of the scholar Ahmad al-Damanhuri, his name, birth, education, and achievements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract key biographical details and explain the significance of the title 'al-Madhahibi' in the context of Islamic scholarly tradition.

Core Idea
Mastery of all four madhhabs made al-Damanhuri a symbol of comprehensive fiqh scholarship.

Biographies of great scholars are not mere historical records; they are models of how Islamic knowledge was pursued, transmitted, and embodied. The passage about Ahmad al-Damanhuri provides a window into the ideal of the 'comprehensive scholar' – one who does not restrict himself to a single school or discipline but seeks mastery across the spectrum of Islamic sciences. As you read this biography, pay attention to the qualities that made him an authority worthy of leading al-Azhar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chain of attributions
Notice that al-Damanhuri's name includes four madhhab attributions (al-Shafi'i, al-Hanafi, al-Maliki, al-Hanbali). This is unusual and foreshadows his title al-Madhahibi. List the four schools mentioned.
This shows he did not just study one madhhab but attained proficiency in all.
2
Examine his educational background
Read the description of his orphan arrival at al-Azhar and his diligent pursuit of knowledge. Note the phrase 'جد في تحصيله' (he was earnest in acquisition). Reflect on how hardship did not hinder his learning.
3
Catalog his fields of expertise
The passage lists several sciences: fiqh, chemistry, numerology, astronomy, philosophy, medicine. Write down each field. Notice that he authored works in each. This demonstrates the breadth expected of a leading scholar.
The diversity of fields challenges narrow specialization.
4
Analyze his professional trajectory
Trace his rise from an orphan student to Shaykh al-Azhar. Identify the factors mentioned: ijaza from scholars of all four schools, fatwa capability, prolific authorship, then the appointment after Muhammad al-Hifni.
His comprehensive knowledge was recognized by the scholarly community.
5
Evaluate the social dimension
His Hajj journey and the visit by Mecca's chief and scholars indicate his high status beyond Egypt. Consider how scholarly reputation transcended regional boundaries.
This illustrates the unity of the Islamic scholarly network (riḥla ṭalab al-'ilm).

Reading a Biographical Thabat: Al-Damanhuri's Teachers
Lists al-Damanhuri's teachers by madhhab followed by his works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract and classify information about a scholar's teachers from a thabat entry, identifying their madhhab affiliations and key biographical markers.

Core Idea
A thabat is a scholarly chain of transmission, not a simple list of names.

This passage from al-Damanhuri's biography lists his teachers organized by madhhab. Such lists are common in Islamic biographical works and serve to establish a scholar's intellectual pedigree. In this lesson, you will learn how to parse these entries and understand the information they convey.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
Notice that the passage first states where al-Damanhuri studied (al-Azhar), then lists teachers under each madhhab heading. Understand the hierarchy.
2
Extract names and epithets
Each teacher is introduced with 'Shaykh' followed by their full name and sometimes epithets like death dates or 'al-Basir' (the blind). Record these details.
3
Classify by madhhab
The teachers are grouped into Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali. Count how many per madhhab and note any patterns (e.g., many Shafi'i teachers).
4
Interpret the significance
Reflect on why listing teachers by madhhab matters: it shows breadth of study and intellectual lineage across schools.
5
Connect to the author's work
The passage ends with a note that al-Damanhuri compiled these in his own thabat (al-Lata'if al-Nuriyya). Understand that this list is a summary of that work.

أقول : أخبر المصنف أن تأليف هذا الرجز كان في أول شهر المحرم إحدى وأربعين وتسع مئة من الهجرة النبوية على صاحبها أفضل الصلاة والسلام، وتقدم معنى الصلاة والسلام: الأمان من النقائص، و«السرمد»: الدائم، وتقدم معنى الآل والصحب، وتقدم وجه تقديم الآل على الصحب. وقوله: «ما قطعت شمس النهار الخ، المقصود منه: التعميم في جميع الأوقات، كما في قوله فيما تقدم: «ما دام الحجا .... الخ. و الأبرج» جمع برج، وهو اسم لجزء من اثنا عشر جزءا، من الفلك الثامن، وهو مقسوم ثلاثين جزءًا، كل جزء يسمى درجة والشمس تقطع في كل يوم درجة، فتقطع الفلك في ثلاث مئة وخمسة وستين يوما، وهي عدة السنة الشمسية» و«البدر اسم للقمر ليلة أربعة عشر يوما من الشهر العربي، و «الدجى» جمع دُجْيَةٍ، وهي الظلمة. وَهَذَا أَخِرُ مَا أَرَدْنَا كِتَابَتَهُ ، وَنَسْأَلُ مَنْ وَفَقَّنَا لَهُ أَنْ يَنْفَعَ بِهِ ، إِنَّهُ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيءٍ قَدِيرٌ . وَصَلَّى اللهُ عَلَى سَيِّدِنَا مُحَمَّدٍ وَآلِهِ وَصَحْبِهِ وَسَلَّم .
I say: The author has informed that the composition of this rajaz was in the first of the month of Muharram, nine hundred forty-one, from the Prophetic Hijra—upon its owner be the best of prayers and peace. And the meaning of 'prayer' and 'peace' has preceded: safety from deficiencies; and 'al-sarmad': the perpetual. And the meaning of 'al' and 'sahb' has preceded, and the reason for giving precedence to 'al' over 'sahb' has preceded. And his saying: 'What the sun of the day has cut... etc.' The intended purpose is generalization in all times, as in his earlier saying: 'As long as the mind... etc.' And 'al-abruj' is the plural of 'burj', which is a name for a part of twelve parts from the eighth sphere, and it is divided into thirty parts, each part called a degree. And the sun cuts each day one degree, so it cuts the sphere in three hundred and sixty-five days, which is the number of the solar year. And 'al-badr' is a name for the moon on the night of the fourteenth day of the Arabic month. And 'al-duja' is the plural of 'dujyah', which is darkness. And this is the last of what we intended to write, and we ask the One who granted us success to make it beneficial. Indeed, He is over all things capable. And may Allah send prayers upon our master Muhammad, his family, and his companions, and grant them peace.

Commentary
This passage is the closing section of the commentary. The author begins by stating the completion date of the original poem (the 'rajaz') under commentary: the first of Muharram 941 AH. He then explains certain terms from the standard prayer formula: 'salat' (prayer) and 'salam' (peace), which he defines as 'safety from deficiencies', and 'sarmad' (perpetual). He notes that earlier commentary has covered the meanings of 'al' (family) and 'sahb' (companions), and why 'al' is mentioned before 'sahb'. Next, he explains a line from the poem that mentions the sun cutting through the day, stating that this is a generalization covering all times. He then elucidates celestial terms: 'abruj' (towers or zodiacal signs) are the twelve parts of the eighth sphere (the sphere of fixed stars), each divided into thirty degrees; the sun moves one degree per day, completing a full circuit in 365 days (the solar year). 'Badr' means the full moon on the fourteenth night, and 'duja' is darkness. Finally, he declares this is the end of the commentary he intended to write, asks Allah to make it beneficial, and concludes with the standard salutation upon the Prophet, his family, and companions.
