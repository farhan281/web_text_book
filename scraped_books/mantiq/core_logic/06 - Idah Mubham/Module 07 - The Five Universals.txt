Ignorance as Relative Non-being in al-Sullam
Author interprets the second line of al-Sullam and answers questions about its structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why ignorance is considered a relative non-being and how this resolves the objection against comparing it to clouds.

Core Idea
Ignorance is not pure nothingness; it is a privation with conceptual reality.

The second line of al-Sullam contains a beautiful analogy: Allah removes veils of ignorance from the intellect as clouds are removed from the sky. But a critic objects: how can you compare ignorance (non-being) to clouds (being)? In this lesson, we will unpack the commentator's answer, which hinges on a crucial distinction between absolute and relative non-being. You will learn to identify this logical move and apply it to similar challenges.

Guided Path
1
Locate the comparison in the verse
The poet compares the intellect to heaven and ignorance to clouds. Find the exact words in the Arabic. Note the implicit tool of comparison (kaf) is omitted. The commentator explains the structure: 'origin: from intellect like the heaven'.
The tool of comparison is dropped and the compared-to is added to the compared.
2
Identify the two questions raised by critics
The commentator mentions two questions. The first is about the grammatical category of the conjunction. The second is the ontological objection: ignorance is non-being, clouds are being – how can they be compared? Why is this a problem for the analogy?
3
Analyze the answer to the second question
The commentator says ignorance is not absolute non-being but a relative non-being. In what sense? Because it involves perceiving something contrary to reality. So it has a cognitive content. How does this justify the comparison? Because both ignorance and clouds are 'obstacles' – one obscures knowledge, the other obscures the sky. So the commonality is functional, not essential.
Ignorance is defined as 'perceiving the thing contrary to its reality', so it is not pure non-being.
4
Apply the distinction to a new case
Suppose someone says 'the absence of food is like a desert' – can that be valid? Using the same reasoning, absence of food is not pure non-being but a state of lack that has effects. Check if the commonality holds.
This shows how the concept of relative non-being allows analogies involving privations.

Parsing the Third Line of al-Sullam: Suns of Knowledge and Hidden Things
Commentator explains the third line and defends its distinctness and position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lexical meanings and logical scope of the key terms in the third line of al-Sullam and explain how this line relates to the previous lines.

Core Idea
The third line specifies the subtle hidden results that were only hinted at in the first line.

This passage from Idah al-Mubham guides you through the meaning of the third line of al-Sullam's logic poem. You will learn how the commentator unpacks the vocabulary and addresses two questions about the line's placement and scope, sharpening your ability to follow technical logical poetry and understand how definitions are built upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify key terms
Find the key words in the third line: 'badat', 'shumus al-ma'rifa', 'al-mukhadarat', 'munkashifa'. Recognize that 'al-mukhadarat' means hidden things, from 'khidr' meaning concealment.
2
Interpret the metaphor
Note that 'shumus al-ma'rifa' compares knowledges to suns, indicating clarity and illumination. The line describes these knowledges appearing and uncovering hidden things.
3
Analyze the first question
Understand why the first line does not suffice: the first line's 'results' (nata'ij) are general, while this line specifically refers to those that are subtle and hidden. The first is general, the third is specific.
4
Analyze the second question
Understand why the third line is not adjacent to the first: the first line was placed first for rhetorical effect (bara'at al-istihlal), and the third is the ultimate end (ghayah) of what preceded, so it must be delayed.

Classification of Words: Compound, Simple, Universal, Particular
Author divides used words into compound and simple, then simple into universal and particular, and explains essential vs. accidental predication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the logical classification of words into compound/simple and universal/particular, and distinguish essential from accidental predication.

Core Idea
Words are the building blocks of logic; their division into compound/simple and universal/particular determines how they function in definitions.

The author begins with a fundamental classification: all used words are either compound or simple. A compound word has parts that each contribute to the meaning; a simple word does not. The simple word is then further divided into universal (kulli), which can be predicated of many, and particular (juz'i), which cannot. Finally, the universal itself is predicated in two ways: essentially (dhati), when it enters the subject's definition, or accidentally ('aradi), when it does not. This parsing exercise is the first step in understanding how terms work in logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial division
Look at the first line: words are either compound (murakkab) or simple (mufrad). Understand the criterion for compound: its part indicates a part of its meaning. A compound like 'Abdullah' has 'abd' and 'Allah' each carrying part of the meaning.
The opposite (simple) is defined by negation: no part indicates a part of the meaning.
2
Distinguish universal from particular
Under simple words, note two categories: kulli (universal) and juz'i (particular). Universal applies to many (e.g., 'asad' applies to all lions), particular applies to only one (e.g., 'Zayd').
Universal does not mean 'all' in quantity; it means predicable of many.
3
Examine predication types for universals
The author explains that a universal can be predicated essentially (dhati) if it enters the essence of the subject, or accidentally ('aradi) if it does not. For example, 'animal' is essential to 'human' because it is part of its definition; 'laughing' is accidental.
This distinction is crucial in definitions: essential predicates are part of the hadd, accidental are not.

Commentary
This passage outlines the classification of words. Words are either compound (murakkab) – where a part of the word indicates a part of the meaning, like 'abdullah' (servant of Allah) – or simple (mufrad), where no part indicates a part of the meaning. The simple word is then divided into universal (kulli), which can be predicated of many individuals (e.g., 'lion' applies to many lions), and particular (juz'i), which cannot (e.g., a proper name like 'Zayd'). Finally, the universal itself is of two types regarding predication: it may be predicated essentially (dhati), meaning it enters into the essence of the subject (like 'animal' for 'human'), or accidentally ('aradi), when it does not (like 'laughing' for 'human').

Key Terms
مفرد (mufrad): A word whose part does not indicate a part of its meaning; simple expression. [Note: In grammar, mufrad means singular; here it is logical simple.]
كلي (kulli): A universal term that can be predicated of many, like 'animal'. [Note: Stable; but later logicians distinguish kulli by nature (bi al-tabi') and by convention.]
جزئي (juz'i): A particular term that cannot be predicated of more than one, like 'Zayd'. [Note: In some texts, juz'i may also refer to a part of a definition.]
ذاتي (dhati): Essential predication: the predicate enters into the subject's definition. [Note: In Aristotelian logic, 'essential' has precise technical sense; here it is used simply.]
عارض ('aradi): Accidental predication: the predicate does not constitute the subject's essence. [Note: Not to be confused with 'accident' in ontology; here it is a logical accident.]

The Five Universals in Islamic Logic
Author enumerates the five universals and distinguishes three types of genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the five universals (jins, fasl, naw', 'arad, khass) and categorize a genus as proximate, remote, or intermediate.

Core Idea
The five universals are the building blocks of every definition in Islamic logic.

This passage introduces the five universals (al-kulliyat al-khamsa), which are essential for constructing and analyzing definitions. Understanding these categories will help you dissect definitions in fiqh, kalam, and Arabic grammar.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the list of five
Commit to memory the five terms: jins (genus), fasl (difference), naw' (species), 'arad (common accident), and khass (property). Use the mnemonics provided by your teacher.
These are the five universals; do not confuse 'arad with 'aradi from earlier lessons.
2

For each universal, recall a clear example from an Islamic science. For instance: jins = 'animal' for humans; fasl = 'rational'; naw' = 'human'; 'arad = 'white' (can apply to many species); khass = 'ability to laugh' (unique to humans).
These examples come from the commentary; memorizing them will anchor the concepts.
3
Learn the three types of genus
Notice that the author divides genus into three: proximate (aqrab), remote (ab'ad), and intermediate (awsat). For humans, 'animal' is proximate, 'body' is remote, 'living being' is intermediate.
The genus type depends on how many levels away from the species.

Distinguishing Universal and Particular
Author defines kull and juz' and their derived forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between universal and particular judgments and correctly identify whether a judgment applies to all individuals or only a part.

Core Idea
Universality is a property of judgments, not a claim about the collection's existence.

This lesson defines the foundational terms 'universal' and 'particular' as they apply to logical judgments. Understanding this distinction is the first step in classifying propositions and building valid arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the definition
The author defines 'kull' (universal) as a judgment covering the entire collection (al-majmu'). Note that the judgment does not entail actual existence (laysa dha wuqu').
2
Distinguish universality from universality claim
When a judgment applies to every individual (kull fard), that property is called 'kulliyya' (universality). This is the attribute of the judgment, not a statement about the collection's ontological status.
3
Define particularity
If the judgment applies to only a part (al-ba'd), it is 'juz'iyya' (particularity). The particular (juz') is said to have a clear definition.
4
Apply to a real Islamic example
Consider the judgment: 'Every intoxicant is haram.' This judgment covers all individuals (kull fard) of the category 'intoxicant', so it exhibits universality (kulliyya). If we say 'Some intoxicants are haram', it would be particularity (juz'iyya).
The difference is crucial in Usul al-Fiqh for understanding the scope of legal rulings.

Five Universals and Three Types of Genus
Presents a poem defining the five universals and three types of genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five universals (genus, species, difference, property, accident) and classify genus into three types (near, far, intermediate).

Core Idea
The five universals are the building blocks of every definition in Islamic logic.

This poem from al-Sullam al-Munawwarq compresses the entire theory of definitions into two verses. By memorizing these lines, you gain the foundational taxonomy for analysing any concept: what it is, what distinguishes it, what accompanies it. In this lesson, we will unpack the poem line by line and see how the five universals and the three types of genus form the toolkit for constructing a complete definition (ḥadd) or description (rasm).

Guided Path
1
Identify the five universals listed in the poem
The poem explicitly names them in line 31: جنس (genus), فصل (difference), عرض (accident), نوع (species), خاص (property). Note the order: genus, difference, accident, species, property.
The term 'کل' (universal) is introduced earlier; here it specifies the five types.
2
Define each universal in logical terms
Genus answers 'what is it?' about the essence and applies to many species. Species applies to many individuals differing in number. Difference distinguishes one species from another within the same genus. Property is a non-essential attribute unique to a species. Common accident is a non-essential attribute that can change.
Property (خاص) is sometimes confused with accident; property is exclusive, accident is not.
3
Understand the three types of genus
Line 32 states genus can be near (قريب), far (بعيد), or intermediate (وسط). Near genus is the immediate broader category (e.g., animal for human). Far genus is more remote (e.g., substance). Intermediate is between them (e.g., living body).
The choice of genus depends on the purpose of the definition.
4
Connect the universals to the definition process
A complete definition (ḥadd) requires genus and difference. A description (rasm) uses property or accident. The three types of genus show the hierarchy from which we select the appropriate level for a definition.
This connection is crucial for applying the universals in fiqh, kalam, and other sciences.

Genus and Differentia
Author defines genus and differentia and splits differentia into near and far.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in any complete definition and classify differentia as near or far.

Core Idea
Genus tells you what kind, differentia tells you which one.

Genus and differentia are the two essential universals. Genus is the common part shared among species; differentia is the unique part that distinguishes. This lesson shows how to extract them from definitions and how differentia can vary by proximity.

Guided Path
1
Find the universal shared among many quiddities
Look for the term that answers 'what is it?' for multiple species. That is the genus (jins). Example: 'animal' for human, horse, dog.
2
Find the distinguishing universal
Look for the term that answers 'which thing is it?' and separates this species from others in the genus. That is the differentia (fasl). Example: 'rational' for human.
3
Verify they are parts of the quiddity
Both genus and differentia are essential universals; they constitute the species when combined.
4
Classify differentia as near or far
If the differentia distinguishes from species in the same immediate genus (e.g., 'rational' distinguishes human from animals), it is near. If it distinguishes from a broader genus (e.g., 'sensitive' distinguishes human from plants), it is far.

Genus is not a single rank but a ladder: you must know which rung you are on.

Parse the special property example
For man, 'potentially laughing' (الضاحك بالقوة) is necessary, as the capacity to laugh is inherent. 'Actually laughing' (الضاحك بالفعل) is separable, as a man may not be laughing at a given moment.
Laughing capacity defines the species, but actual laughing does not.

Apply the logic to other essences
Test other examples: for fire, 'heat' is a necessary common accident (fire always has the capacity to heat), but 'actually heating a specific object' is separable. For a rational animal, 'rationality' is necessary special property, while 'being a teacher' is separable special property.

Commentary
This passage classifies the last two of the five universals—special property (khassa) and common accident ('arad 'amm)—into two types each: necessary (lazim) and separable (mufariq). The necessary attribute never separates from the essence; for man, the common accident 'potentially moving' is always present (since man can move), and the special property 'potentially laughing' is always present (since man has the capacity to laugh). The separable attribute can separate from the essence; for man, 'actually moving' is not always present (e.g., when sleeping), and 'actually laughing' is also not constant. This division is crucial for understanding what defines a thing versus what may change without altering its identity.
