The Two Sides of Logic and Their Four Branches
Author divides logic into four branches

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two main epistemic categories in logic and explain how they generate the four branches of the discipline.

Core Idea
Logic's four branches arise from the intersection of tasawwur and tasdiq with mabadi and maqasid.

The passage opens by stating the purpose of logic: to distinguish sound from unsound thought. Thought aims at either conceptualizing an unknown or affirming a proposition. This gives logic two sides, each with foundational principles and final objectives. Understanding this architecture is essential for navigating any Islamic logic text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aims of thought
Find in the Arabic where the author says thought is for tasawwuri unknowns and tasdiqi unknowns. The phrase is 'تحصيل المجهولات التصورية أو التصديقية'.
These correspond to concept formation and judgment.
2
Locate the two sides of logic
The text says logic has 'طرفان: تصورات وتصديقات'. These are the two main divisions: conceptualizations and assents.
3
Note the two dimensions: mabadi and maqasid
For each side, there are principles (مبادئ) and objectives (مقاصد). The principles are the building blocks; the objectives are the end products.
The author uses the analogy of stone and cement for a wall.
4
List the four branches
From the passage: (1) principles of tasawwur = five universals, (2) objectives of tasawwur = explanatory statement. The same division applies to tasdiq, though not explicitly named.
The full four: principles of tasawwur, objectives of tasawwur, principles of tasdiq, objectives of tasdiq.
5
Connect to the purpose of logic
The overall purpose is to discern sound from unsound thought. The four branches provide the tools: through clear concepts (tasawwur) and sound arguments (tasdiq).

Commentary
This passage presents the overarching structure of logic. The author begins by reiterating the purpose of logic: to distinguish sound from unsound thought. Thought has two aims: conceptualization (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). Consequently, logic is divided into two main parts: tasawwurat and tasdiqat. Each part has its principles (mabadi) and its objectives (maqasid), yielding four sections. Their objective is the explanatory statement (al-qawl al-sharih), i.e., definition. The footnotes elaborate: thought is defined as movement from knowns to unknowns; principles are compared to raw materials for building; and the five universals are listed. In Islamic jurisprudence, for example, the five universals are used to define terms like 'prayer' by its genus (act of worship) and differentia (specific actions).

The Five Universals: The Principles of Conceptualization
Author lists the five universals as the principles of conceptualizations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five universals and explain their role as the building blocks of definitions.

Core Idea
The five universals are the raw materials from which every definition is constructed.

The passage tells us that the principles of conceptualizations are the five universals. These categories—genus, differentia, species, property, and common accident—are the components of every explanatory statement (definition). In this lesson, we explore each universal and see how they function in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Extract the list from the Arabic
Find in the footnote the phrase: 'وهي: الجنس والفصل والنوع والخاصة، والعرض العام'.
These are the five universals.
2
Define each universal
Genus (jins): the broad category a thing belongs to. Differentia (fasl): what distinguishes that thing from others. Species (naw'): the combination of genus and differentia. Property (khassa): a necessary but non-essential trait. Common accident ('arad 'amm): a non-necessary trait shared by many.
3
Analyze the example given in the passage
The footnote gives 'rational animal' for 'man'. Animal is the genus, rational is the differentia, and the species is man (rational animal). Property could be 'laughing', common accident could be 'walking'.
This example is classical from Porphyry.
4
Understand their role as 'mabadi'
Principles (mabadi) are like raw materials. The five universals are the fundamental components from which definitions (explanatory statements) are built. Without them, definitions would be arbitrary.
The passage uses the analogy of stone and cement for a wall.
5
Apply to an Islamic concept
Take 'wudu' (ablution). Genus: act of purification. Differentia: performed with specific actions and intentions. Species: the act itself. Property: e.g., it nullifies by certain events. Common accident: e.g., it is recommended before prayer.

Isaghuji and the Classification of Terms
Author explains Isaghuji and presents the term classification tree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the classification of terms (lafẓ) from singular/compound down to the five universals and distinguish essential from accidental universals.

Core Idea
Isaghuji is not just a book title — it is the name for the five universals that form logic's introduction.

This passage opens the commentary on Isaghuji, explaining the purpose and meaning of the term. The author unpacks 'Isaghuji' as the five universals and then lays out the entire classification of terms that leads to them. Understanding this tree is your first step into the logical framework that will govern definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify what Isaghuji refers to
The author equates Isaghuji with 'the five universals' and provides etymological explanations (Greek/Syriac, meaning 'introduction' or 'here we investigate'). Note that it is both a text title and a conceptual gateway.
2
Map the classification of lafẓ
Start from lafẓ (word), branch to mufrad (singular) and murakkab (compound). From mufrad, branch to kullī (universal) and juz'ī (particular). Then from kullī, branch to dhātī (essential) and 'araḍī (accidental). Visualize this tree.
This classification is hierarchical: each division exhausts the category.
3
Distinguish essential from accidental
Essential universals (jins, fasl, naw') constitute the essence of a thing. Accidental universals (khāṣṣah, 'araḍ 'āmm) do not constitute the essence but are still predicable.
Essential universals are used in real definitions (ḥadd); accidental ones are used in descriptions (rasm).
4
Locate the five universals in the tree
The five universals are the end nodes: jins (genus), fasl (differentia), naw' (species) under essential; khāṣṣah (property) and 'araḍ 'āmm (common accident) under accidental. Understand their roles as the building blocks of definitions.
Every definition in logic is composed from these five.

The Placement of Isaghuji and the Division of Lafz
Author justifies placement of Isaghuji and presents the tree of lafz divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the division of lafz (expression) from simple/compound down to the five universals and explain why the author discusses expression before the universals.

Core Idea
The five universals are found within the division of lafz, requiring us to first understand expression and indication.

This passage opens the commentary on Isaghuji by explaining why the author placed this chapter first. It then presents the famous tree of lafz divisions, showing how the five universals emerge from the classification of expression. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for grasping the structure of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim about placement
Find the sentence where the author justifies presenting Isaghuji before other chapters. What reason does he give?
He says it facilitates students who want to start the sciences.
2
Extract the tree of lafz divisions
Trace the hierarchy: lafz is divided into mufrad (simple) and murakkab (compound). Then mufrad is divided into juz'i (particular) and kulli (universal). Then kulli is divided into dhati (essential) and 'aradi (accidental). Finally, dhati includes jins (genus), fasl (differentia), naw' (species); 'aradi includes khassa (property) and 'arad 'amm (common accident).
This tree is the backbone of the five universals.
3
Locate the five universals in the tree
Notice that the five universals are exactly the items at the bottom: jins, fasl, naw', khassa, 'arad 'amm. They are subdivisions of the essential and accidental, which are subdivisions of the universal.
This shows why Isaghuji means 'the five universals'.
4
Understand the author's rationale for discussing expression first
The passage says: since the essential and accidental (the focus of Isaghuji) are divisions of the universal, and the universal is a division of the simple expression, we must discuss expression before the universals. Also, since meaning is understood through indication (dalala), we need to define indication first.
The author is showing why the topics of expression and indication are placed as an introduction.
5
Recognise the role of dalala
Find the sentence linking expression to indication: 'since understanding the meaning from the expression is by virtue of its indication upon it, it became necessary to first mention the definition of indication and its divisions.'
Dalala (indication) is the bridge between lafz and meaning.

The Three Types of Signification: Mutabaqa, Tadamun, Iltizam
Author defines the three types of signification with the example of 'insan'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of signification (dalalat al-mutabaqa, al-tadamun, al-iltizam) using the example of 'insan' and apply them to other Islamic terms.

Core Idea
A word's meaning is not one-dimensional: it can signify the whole, a part, or an inseparable mental concomitant.

Understanding how words convey meaning is foundational to Islamic logic. The author breaks down signification into three distinct types using the example of 'insan' (human). This lesson will help you identify each type and see how they operate in Islamic discourse.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of signification (dalala)
Signification is the relationship between a word and what it makes you understand. The author distinguishes three ways a word can signify its meaning.
2
Distinguish the three types
Correspondence (mutabaqa) is when the word signifies the entire meaning. Inclusion (tadamun) is when it signifies a part of that meaning. Implication (iltizam) is when it signifies something not part of the meaning but mentally inseparable from it.
3
Apply to the example of 'insan'

Author divides the essential predicate (dhati) into three: genus, species, and differentia.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The passage divides the essential predicate into three kinds based on the logical question they respond to. Core Idea
Rasm answers 'What thing is it?', not 'What is it?'.

In this lesson, we examine the precise logical definition of al-rasm (description). The author provides a concise formula that immediately differentiates rasm from genus, species, general accident, and specific property. Understanding this definition is crucial for accurately describing things in Islamic sciences, such as when a jurist gives a description of a contract without providing its genus and differentia.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key criteria
The definition of rasm has two parts: it is a universal (kulliy) and it is said about a thing in answer to 'What thing is it?' (ayyu shay'in huwa?). Also, the condition 'in its essence' (fi dhatihi) is added. Notice these two criteria: the type of question and the essential nature.
The question 'ayyu shay'in' contrasts with 'ma huwa' (what is it?) used for hadd.
2
See what is excluded by each criterion
By the criterion of answering 'ayyu shay'in', genus and species are excluded because they answer 'ma huwa' (what is it?). The general accident ('arad 'amm) is excluded because it does not answer any 'what' question at all—it is not a predicate in such a response. By the condition 'fi dhatihi' (in its essence), the specific property (khassa) is excluded because it is not an essential property.
The specific property is a necessary concomitant but not part of the essence (dhati).

Rasm uses essential non-defining properties (dhati) that are not the differentia. It provides a description that helps identify the thing without giving a complete definition. For example, 'man is a laughing animal' is a rasm because 'laughing' is a specific property, not an essential differentia.
In contrast, hadd uses genus and differentia, e.g., 'man is a rational animal'.

Rasm answers 'ayyu shay'in' (what thing is it?) while hadd answers 'ma huwa' (what is it?). Rasm uses non-defining essential properties or accidents; hadd uses defining essential properties (genus and differentia). Understanding this distinction is vital for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam.
In usul al-fiqh, a description of 'salah' as 'an act of worship with specific movements and recitations' is a rasm, not a hadd.

The author again divides it into inseparable and separable. The example for inseparable common accident is 'potential breather', which applies to man and many animals. The example for separable common accident is 'actual breathing', which also applies across species. The definition is given: a common accident is a universal predicated upon different essences. This definition excludes the genus (since genus is essential, not accidental) and the remote differentia (also essential). The footnotes elaborate that breathing is not part of man's essence but is common to many animals, thus it is a common accident. The preposition 'min' in 'from animals' is explanatory (bayaniyya), clarifying that breathing is shared. Again, the distinction between essential (dhati) and accidental (aradi) predicates is emphasized, as the genus and remote differentia are essential and thus excluded from this definition.

The General Accident and Its Two Types
Author defines the general accident and gives examples of its two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the general accident (al-ʿarḍ al-ʿāmm) in a classification and distinguish its inseparable (lāzim) and separable (mufāriq) types.

Core Idea
The general accident is a universal accidental predicate that spans multiple realities, unlike genus or differentia.

In this passage, the author defines the general accident, one of the five universals. It applies accidentally to diverse realities and is divided into inseparable (like breathing) and separable (like action). Understanding this distinction is key to precise definition in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The passage defines the general accident (العرض العام). Note that it is a type of universal accident.
2
Extract the definition (rasm)
The definition: 'a universal said over different realities under it, accidentally.' This excludes genus and differentia which are said essentially.
The condition 'accidental statement' is crucial.
3
Recognize the two types
Inseparable (lazim) – e.g., 'breathing potentially' for animals. Separable (mufariq) – e.g., 'action' for man.
Inseparability here means the attribute persists as long as the subject exists.
4
Apply the definition to a new example
Test a predicate: is it universal? Is it accidental? Does it apply to multiple realities? If yes, it is a general accident.
E.g., 'black' is a general accident for crows and other things, but not inseparable.
5
Avoid confusion with genus
Genus is essential; general accident is accidental. The definition excludes genus by the qualifier 'accidental'.
E.g., 'animal' is a genus, not a general accident.

Commentary
This passage defines the general accidental (al-ard al-amm). It begins by stating that the general accidental encompasses multiple realities (haqaiq) and is either inseparable (lazim) or separable (mufariq). The author provides two examples: 'breathing potentially' is an inseparable general accidental for man and other animals, while 'action' is a separable one. He then gives a formal definition (rasm) of the general accidental: a universal predicated over different realities under it. This definition excludes genus and remote differentia, because genus and differentia are said essentially (dhatiyyan), not accidentally. The condition 'accidental statement' ensures that only accidental universals are included. In Islamic logic, this classification helps distinguish accidents that may apply to multiple species from those that are specific to one. For instance, 'breathing potentially' applies to both humans and horses, but 'laughing' is a property specific to humans.

Key Terms
العرض العام (al-ʿarḍ al-ʿāmm): A universal accidental predicate that applies to multiple different realities, whether inseparable (lazim) or separable (mufariq). [Note: Stable across the tradition; some later logicians distinguish between awwali and shayyi.]
اللازم (al-lāzim): An accident that is inseparable from the subject, like breathing potentially for animals. [Note: In later logic, lazim can also refer to necessary concomitants in definition.]
المفارق (al-mufāriq): An accident that is separable from the subject, like action. [Note: No significant shift.]
