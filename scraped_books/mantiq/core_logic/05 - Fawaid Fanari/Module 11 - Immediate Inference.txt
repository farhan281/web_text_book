Derived Terms Indicate Causality of the Judgment
Author argues that the predicate of a derived term indicates the cause of the judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the attachment of a judgment to a derived term (mushtaq) indicates that the source of derivation is the cause of the judgment, using the Quranic thief verse as an example.

Core Idea
A derived term in a ruling reveals the cause behind the ruling.

In Islamic legal theory, identifying the cause (ʿilla) behind a ruling is crucial for analogical reasoning (qiyas). This passage from Fanari's logical commentary demonstrates how derived terms (al-mushtaqqāt) embedded in Qur'anic rulings point directly to the cause. By understanding this logical principle, you can see how jurists extract causal relationships from scriptural texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Find the author's statement that 'the application of the judgment upon the derived term indicates the causality of the source'. This is the central thesis.
2
Understand the three types of signification
The author mentions conformity (muṭābaqa), inclusion (taḍammun), and implication (iltizām). Notice how these are defined: each is a kind of signification by convention.
Conformity is for the whole meaning, inclusion for a part, implication for a necessary concomitant.
3
Link signification to causality
The author argues that the ordering of these three significations upon the signifier is because each signification is by convention for the whole. This underpins how derived terms carry causal indication.
4
Analyze the Qur'anic example
In verse 5:38, the judgment of cutting hands is attached to the derived term 'al-sāriq' (the thief). The source (ma'khadh) is 'sariqa' (theft). The attachment shows that theft is the cause of the cutting.
5
Generalize the principle
Any time a ruling (ḥukm) is attached to a derived term, the source of derivation is the cause. This is how jurists infer ʿilla from texts.

Third Objection and Answer: Two Types of Clear Implication
Author answers the third objection by distinguishing two types of clear implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-luzum al-bayyin bi al-ma'na al-akhass and al-a'amm, and apply this distinction to evaluate whether an implicative meaning is valid.

Core Idea
Not all mental connections are equal: one implication jumps automatically, another requires separate reflection.

The objector claims that 'the possessor of knowledge and writing' cannot illustrate implicative meaning because it does not follow immediately from 'man'. The author responds by revealing a crucial distinction within 'clear implication' – the most specific (akhass) and the general (a'amm). This distinction saves the example and clarifies a subtle but essential point in the theory of signification (dalalah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the objector's claim: why does he reject the example of 'man' and 'knowledge/writing'? He argues that from merely conceiving 'man', the mind does not immediately conceive those capacities, so the example fails.
2
Extract the author's response
The author classifies the implication between 'man' and those capacities as al-luzum al-bayyin bi al-ma'na al-a'amm (general clear implication). This means the mind first conceives each concept separately and then recognizes the necessary connection.
3
Define both types of clear implication
The most specific (akhass) is immediate: from 'two' to 'pairness'. The general (a'amm) requires separate conception: from 'man' to 'capacity for knowledge/writing'. Note that the definition given earlier was for the akhass, but the a'amm is still a legitimate implication.
4
Apply the distinction to the example
The example 'pairness of two' (zowjiyyat al-ithnayn) is given as an instance of the general sense, not the most specific. Since the a'amm is a condition for the akhass, the definition implicitly includes both. Thus the example is valid.
5
Recognize the broader debate
The author notes a dispute between Imam al-Razi (who accepts the general sense as sufficient) and the majority (who require the most specific). This shows the ongoing refinement of logical concepts.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange: an objection (naqd) and its answer (jawab) regarding the example used to illustrate implicative signification (dalalah iltizamiyyah). The objector argues that 'the possessor of knowledge and the craft of writing' cannot be an example of an implicative meaning because merely conceiving 'man' does not necessitate conceiving those two capacities. A better example, he says, is the 'pairness of two' (zowjiyyat al-ithnayn), since conceiving 'two' immediately brings to mind 'pairness'. The author responds by clarifying the type of mental implication (luzum dhihni) between 'man' and those capacities: it is clear implication in the general sense (luzum bayyin bi al-ma'na al-a'amm), meaning that after separately conceiving both terms, one recognizes the necessary connection. He then explains that the definition given earlier was for the clear implication in the most specific sense (luzum bayyin bi al-ma'na al-akhass), which involves immediate transition of the mind without prior separate conception. However, since the most specific implies the general, the definition also covers the general. The example (zowjiyyah) is for the general sense, not the specific, and thus the example is valid. Finally, the author notes a later dispute between Imam al-Razi (who accepts the general sense as sufficient) and the majority of logicians (who require the most specific), but this is discussed elsewhere.

Key Terms
اللزوم البيّن بالمعنى الأخص (al-luzum al-bayyin bi al-ma'na al-akhass): The most specific clear implication: the mind moves from the implied term (malzum) to the necessary concomitant (lazim) immediately without any need to first conceive each separately. Example: from 'two' to 'pairness'. [Note: This is the standard definition in the tradition; the author uses it as the definition given earlier.]
اللزوم البيّن بالمعنى الأعم (al-luzum al-bayyin bi al-ma'na al-a'amm): The general clear implication: the mind does not immediately connect the two concepts; rather, after conceiving each separately, it then recognizes the necessary connection. Example: from 'man' to 'capacity for knowledge and writing' (according to the author). [Note: This distinction is a later development; earlier logicians often considered only the akhass as proper luzum bayyin.]

Negative Quantifiers: Universal and Partial
Author defines universal and partial negative quantifiers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to express universal negative and partial negative propositions using the correct Arabic quantifiers and identify the role of negation placement.

Core Idea
Universal negative uses 'absolutely not'; partial negative adds a negation before the universal tool.

In logic, we need precise quantifiers to say 'none' vs 'not all'. This lesson introduces the Arabic expressions for universal and partial negative categorical propositions, showing how the position of negation changes the meaning. We also learn that the purpose of listing quantifiers is illustrative, not exhaustive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quantifier for universal negative
Find the Arabic phrase 'ليس البتة' (absolutely not) in the passage. This is the standard universal negative quantifier. Understand that it negates the predicate for every individual of the subject.
Other expressions might exist, but this is the core example.
2
Identify the quantifiers for partial negative
Find the phrases 'قد لا يكون', 'ليس دائما', 'ليس كلما', 'ليس مهما' (it may not be, not always, etc.). These indicate that the negation applies to some but not all individuals.
The footnote explains that the negative tool must precede the universal tool to produce a partial negative, e.g., 'ليس كل' vs 'كل ليس'.
3
Examine the formation of partial negative
Notice the rule: to form a partial negative, the negation word (ليس) comes before the universal quantifier (كلي). This yields 'not all are' instead of 'all are not'.
This is crucial for distinguishing between 'not all are' (partial negative) and 'all are not' (universal negative).
4
Understand the purpose of listing quantifiers
Read the statement: 'the purpose is to give examples based on common usage, not enumeration.' This means the author is not claiming these are the only possible quantifiers; they are just the most familiar.

Indefinite Propositions: Force and Logical Equivalent
Author defines indefinite propositions and their logical force.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an indefinite proposition (مهملة) and justify why it is logically equivalent to a particular proposition.

Core Idea
An indefinite proposition lacks a quantifier but acts as a particular proposition.

When no quantifier is present, a proposition is called indefinite or 'مهملة'. This lesson explains that such propositions are logically equivalent to particular propositions, not universal. We will see why the judgment on all individuals and on some individuals are mutually entailing, and how this applies in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Define indefinite proposition
Read the passage: 'وإما أن لا تكون كذلك: أي مخصوصة أو مسورة تسمى مهملة' – a proposition that is not quantified (neither universal nor particular) is called 'مهملة'. Examples: 'الإنسان ناطق' in categorical, 'إن جاء زيد فأكرمه' in conditional.
Note the distinction from particular: particular has a specific subject (e.g., 'some man'), while indefinite has an unspecified subject.
2
Understand the logical force of indefinite
The passage says: 'والمهملة في قوة الجزئية' – the indefinite is in the force of the particular. This is because the judgment on all individuals in general and the judgment on some individuals entail each other.
This entailment works both ways: if the judgment holds for all, it holds for some; and if it fails for all, it fails for some. So the indefinite cannot be stronger than particular.
3
Analyze the entailment argument
The footnote explains: 'كلما تحقق الحكم على الأفراد في الجملة تحقق الحكم على بعض الأفراد' – whenever the judgment on individuals in general is true, the judgment on some individuals is true. Similarly for falsity. This shows the logical equivalence.
This is a form of immediate inference: from universal to particular (subalternation). But the indefinite is not universal; it is treated as particular in force.
4
Apply to Islamic sciences
Consider a fiqh ruling: 'السارق يقطع' (The thief is amputated). Without a quantifier, it is indefinite. According to this lesson, it is logically equivalent to 'بعض السارقين يقطع' (Some thieves are amputated), but in practice jurists often interpret it as universal due to context. This shows the importance of quantifiers.
The indefinite's force being particular is a logical default, but context may override.

Negation Flips Disjunctive Types
Author explains negation's effect on disjunctive types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the result of negating a māni'at al-jam' and māni'at al-khuluww disjunction, and apply the rule that negation flips the type, given agreement in quality.

Core Idea
Negating a disjunctive proposition transforms its type: man' al-jam' becomes man' al-khuluww, and vice versa.

In classical logic, disjunctive propositions come in two main types: man' al-jam' (exclusive, cannot both be true) and man' al-khuluww (inclusive, cannot both be false). This passage reveals a powerful immediate inference: applying negation to the whole disjunction changes its type. By understanding this transformation, you can correctly derive the contradictory of a disjunctive statement, which is essential for argument analysis in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original disjunction type
Determine whether the original proposition is a man' al-jam' (prevention of conjunction) or a man' al-khuluww (prevention of void). Note the quality (affirmative or negative) of each component.
The two types are mutually exclusive in terms of the logical relation they assert.
2
Apply negation to the whole proposition
Assume the proposition is negated as a whole (e.g., 'Not: ...'). This external negation is the focus of the transformation.
3
Negate each component part
Propagate the negation into each disjunct, flipping their truth values. For example, 'in the sea' becomes 'not in the sea', and 'not drowning' becomes 'drowning' due to double negation.
This step mirrors the standard operation of De Morgan's laws in propositional logic.
4
Observe the resulting relational type
After negation, the relationship between the transformed components flips: if the original prevented conjunction (both true), the result prevents void (both false), and vice versa.
This is the core insight: negation exchanges the two types.
5
Verify the condition of quality agreement
The transformation rule holds only when both components agree in quality (both affirmative or both negative). If they differ, the outcome is the negative of the proposition that agrees in type.
The passage explicitly notes this exception: 'after agreement in quality'.

Contradiction of Universal Affirmative and Negative
Fanari states the rule of contradiction for quantified propositions with examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The contradictory of a universal is always a particular of opposite quality.

In logic, contradiction (tanāquḍ) requires two propositions that are always opposite in truth-value. For quantified propositions, this is only possible when one is universal and the other is particular, with opposite quality. Fanari clarifies this rule, showing that the subject term is the same in mention, so the contradiction holds.

Determine the contradictory's quantity and quality

This rule is derived from the square of opposition.

Why Quantified Contradiction Requires Different Quantity
Fanari explains why both universals can be false and both particulars can be true, and the treatment of muhmala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why both universal propositions can be false and both particular propositions can be true, and apply the rule that unquantified propositions follow the particular.

Core Idea
Two universals can both be false, and two particulars can both be true, so only a universal-particular pair guarantees contradiction.

Fanari explains a crucial subtlety: not just any pair of propositions of opposite quality are contradictory if they share the same quantity. Both universal affirmative and universal negative can be false (if not all individuals have the property), and both particular affirmative and particular negative can be true (if some do and some don't). Only by combining a universal with a particular of opposite quality do we get the logical guarantee of always opposite truth-values.

Guided Path
1
Recall the truth-conditions for universals
A universal affirmative is true only if every subject has the predicate; universal negative true only if no subject has it. They can both be false if some do and some don't.
Example: 'Every human is a writer' and 'No human is a writer' are both false if some humans are writers and some are not.
2
Consider the truth-conditions for particulars
A particular affirmative is true if at least one subject has the predicate; particular negative true if at least one does not. They can both be true if some have it and some do not.
Example: 'Some human is a writer' and 'Some human is not a writer' are both true if there is at least one writer and one non-writer.
3
See why a universal and particular differ
For a universal affirmative (A) and particular negative (O): if A is false, then at least one subject lacks the predicate → O true. If A is true, all have it → O false. So they are always opposite.
Similarly for universal negative (E) and particular affirmative (I).
4
Apply to unquantified propositions
An unquantified proposition like 'Human is an animal' is treated as particular in strength, so its contradictory follows the same rule: it is a particular of opposite quality.
Thus, 'Human is an animal' (treated as particular affirmative) contradicts 'Some human is not an animal' (particular negative).

Conditions for Contradiction in Quantified Propositions
Explaining that contradiction requires difference in quantity and that indefinite propositions act as particulars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two quantified propositions are genuine contradictories by checking their quantity and quality.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires opposite quantity and opposite quality—universal vs particular.

In this lesson, we examine the precise conditions under which two propositions form a genuine contradiction (tanāquḍ) in the logical square. The author explains that not all universal pairs are contradictory, and not all particular pairs are contradictory. He then extends the rule to indefinite propositions, which carry the force of particulars. Understanding this is essential for correct inference in Islamic sciences, especially in kalām and usūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of contradiction
Contradiction requires two propositions that cannot both be true and cannot both be false. Note that this is stricter than contrary (both false possible) and subcontrary (both true possible).
2
Identify the two types of quantified propositions
Universal (kulliyya) and particular (juz'iyya). In the square: A (all S are P), E (no S is P), I (some S is P), O (some S is not P).
3
Apply the condition: difference in quantity
For genuine contradiction, the two propositions must differ in quantity (one universal, one particular). The author gives examples: two universals can both be false (e.g., 'Every human is writer' and 'No human is writer'), so they are not contradictory. Two particulars can both be true (e.g., 'Some human is writer' and 'Some human is not writer'), so they are not contradictory.
The universal pair (A and E) are contraries; the particular pair (I and O) are subcontraries.
4
Check the status of indefinite propositions
Indefinite (muhmala) propositions lack a quantifier. They are treated as particular in force. Thus, an indefinite with a universal can be contradictory if the indefinite is interpreted as particular. Author gives example: 'Animal is moving' (indefinite positive) and 'No animal is moving' (universal negative) are contradictory, equivalent to 'Some animal is moving' and 'No animal is moving'.
5
Summarize the four contradictory pairs
The standard square of opposition: A-O and E-I are contradictories. All other pairs (A-E, I-O, A-I, etc.) are not.

One word, two meanings: the shadda tells you if 'aks refers to the process or the product.

The author clarifies that 'aks can refer either to the proposition that results from swapping subject and predicate, or to the very act of swapping itself. The Conversion Rule: From Universal Affirmative to Particular Affirmative
Example of conversion from universal affirmative to particular affirmative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform the conversion of a universal affirmative (A) proposition to a particular affirmative (I) and explain why the quantity changes.

Core Idea
A universal affirmative converts to a particular affirmative: the predicate becomes subject but with a 'some'.

The passage provides a classic example: 'Every human is an animal' converts to 'Some animal is a human'. This illustrates a fundamental rule of immediate inference: universal affirmative (A) propositions do not convert to a universal affirmative; they convert to a particular affirmative (I) because the predicate is not necessarily coextensive with the subject. Learning this rule is essential for working with syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition type
Determine quantity and quality: 'Every human is an animal' is universal affirmative (A).
2
Swap subject and predicate
The new subject is 'animal', the new predicate is 'human'.
3
Adjust the quantity
Since the universal affirmative conversion is valid only as particular, add 'some' to the new subject: 'Some animal'.
4
Check preservation of truth
If the original is true, the converted is necessarily true. Test with a counterexample: 'Every human is an animal' is true; 'Some animal is a human' is also true.

Definition and Conditions of Conversion ('Aks)
Definition of conversion and its two conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions of conversion and construct a valid converse from an original proposition.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate while keeping quality and truth intact.

This passage defines conversion ('aks) as a key immediate inference in logic. It distinguishes two meanings—the converted proposition and the act of conversion—and sets two essential conditions: preservation of quality (affirmation/negation) and preservation of truth value. We will unpack the definition and apply it to Islamic examples to see how conversion works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two meanings of 'aks
Note that 'aks can refer to either the converted proposition or the act of conversion. The author clarifies this to avoid confusion. In logic, we often focus on the operation itself.
The shadda on 'yuyayyaru' indicates the act; without it, a third meaning (autonomous conversion) arises.
2
Identify the swapping rule
The subject (or antecedent in conditionals) becomes the predicate (or consequent), and the predicate becomes the subject. This is the core structural change in conversion.
For categorical propositions, swap subject and predicate. For conditionals, swap antecedent and consequent.
3
Apply Condition 1: Preserve quality
The affirmative or negative nature must remain unchanged. If the original is affirmative, the converse is affirmative; if negative, the converse is negative. The author gives examples: 'Every human is rational' (affirmative) and 'No human is a stone' (negative).
Quality is preserved, but quantity may change (e.g., universal affirmative converts to particular affirmative).
4
Apply Condition 2: Preserve truth value
If the original is true, the converse must be true; if the converse is false, the original must be false. This ensures logical equivalence in truth under the conversion.
This is the nature of logical entailment: the converse follows from the original.
5
Construct a valid converse with an Islamic example
Take a true universal affirmative: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'. Swapping subject and predicate gives 'Every forbidden thing is an intoxicant', which is false. So the correct converse, preserving truth, is 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants' (particular affirmative).
Universal affirmatives (A) convert to particular affirmatives (I) by limitation. The passage does not emphasize limitation but truth preservation demands it.

Definition and Conditions of 'Aks (Conversion)
Author defines 'aks and explains its two conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two essential conditions of 'aks (conversion) and evaluate whether a given propositional conversion is valid according to Islamic logicians.

Core Idea
Conversion preserves quality and the truth relation, not individual truth values.

The logical operation of 'aks (conversion) is a fundamental tool for immediate inference in Islamic logic. In this passage, the author defines 'aks and carefully distinguishes its two conditions: preserving the quality (affirmation or negation) of the proposition, and preserving the relationship of truth and falsity between the original and its converse. Understanding these conditions is crucial for correctly applying conversion in kalam and usul al-fiqh arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's quality
Determine whether the original proposition is affirmative (mujiba) or negative (saliba). For example, 'Every human is rational' is affirmative; 'No human is stone' is negative. The converse must have the same quality; if the original is affirmative, the converse cannot be negative, and vice versa.
This is the first condition: بقاء السلب والإيجاب بحاله.
2
Determine the truth relation required
The second condition is that the truth relation between original and converse must be one of necessary entailment: if the original is true, the converse is true; and if the converse is false, the original is false. Note that the reverse (if original false then converse false) is not required.
This is the second condition: بقاء التصديق والتكذيب بحاله, interpreted as the collective relation.
3
Apply the conditions to example conversions
Take the original 'Every human is rational' (A). Its valid converse is 'Some rational is human' (I). Check: both are affirmative (condition one satisfied). For truth: if A is true, I is true; if I is false, A is false (condition two satisfied). Similarly, 'No human is stone' (E) converts to 'No stone is human' (E): both negative, and truth relation holds.
These examples are from the passage.
4
Beware of common misinterpretations
The author warns that the condition is not that the original and converse share the same truth value individually. For instance, it is not required that if the original is false, the converse is false. The only necessary relations are truth propagation forward and falsity backward. Understanding this nuance prevents errors such as claiming that 'Every human is rational' cannot convert to 'Some rational is human' because the falsity of the original would imply falsity of the converse – but that is not required.
The passage explicitly rejects the misunderstanding: ليس المراد إذا كذب الأصل كذب العكس.

The Role of the Meeting Condition in Affirmative Conversion

Conversion of Universal Negative Propositions
Author states and proves universal negative converts to universal negative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert any universal negative proposition using both the direct rule and the syllogistic proof.

Core Idea
Universal negative propositions convert to universal negatives, preserving truth and quality.

In this lesson, we examine the rule for converting universal negative propositions (e.g., 'No human is a stone'). The author states the rule and provides two proofs: one based on the concept of contact (mulāqāh) and another using a syllogistic arrangement. Understanding this conversion is essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
A universal negative proposition (e.g., 'No S is P') converts to 'No P is S'. The predicate becomes subject and vice versa while preserving truth.
The quality (negative) and quantity (universal) remain the same.
2
Understand the contact argument
If 'No S is P' is true, there is no individual where S and P meet. If the conversion were false (some P is S), there would be contact in that individual, making a particular affirmative true, contradicting the universal negative.
Contact (mulāqāh) means the subject and predicate coexist in an individual.
3
Follow the syllogistic proof
Assume 'No S is P' is true. If conversion were false, then 'Some P is S' is true. But 'Some P is S' (by conversion) implies 'Some S is P', contradicting the original. Alternatively, arrange 'No S is P' as major and 'Some P is S' as minor to deduce 'Some P is not P', an absurdity.
This shows the conversion must be true; otherwise, logic leads to a contradiction.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Use a real example: 'No intoxicant (khamr) is halal' converts to 'No halal is an intoxicant'. Verify using the contact argument: if some halal were intoxicant, then some intoxicant would be halal, contradicting the original.
This conversion is used in fiqh to draw implications from prohibitions.

Particular Negative Has No Necessary Converse
Author explains why particular negative propositions do not necessarily convert.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a particular negative proposition necessarily converts by testing it against the counterexample criterion.

Core Idea
Particular negatives never convert necessarily; truth of the converse is accidental, never guaranteed.

In this lesson, we examine one of the key rules of immediate inference: the conversion of particular negative propositions. The author shows that while some particular negatives may happen to have true converses, the form does not guarantee it. We will learn why this matters for valid reasoning in Islamic sciences, where a false inference can lead to erroneous legal or theological conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Given a proposition, determine whether it is a particular negative (sāliba juz'iyya): 'Some S is not P'. Ensure it uses 'some' and a negation.
Example: 'Some animals are not humans' is particular negative.
2
Construct the simple converse
Swap the subject and predicate without adding negation: 'Some P is not S'. For the example, the converse is 'Some humans are not animals'.
This is simple conversion (‘aks mustawī).
3
Test the converse for necessary truth
Ask: Is the converse true in every case where the original is true? If you find one case where the original is true and the converse is false, then conversion is not necessary.
The author uses 'some animal is not human' as a counterexample because the converse 'some human is not animal' is false.
4
Recognize accidental truth
Sometimes the converse may be true by coincidence (e.g., 'some stones are not humans' and 'some humans are not stones' are both true). But this does not make conversion necessary, because it fails for other pairs.
The qualifier 'necessarily' (لزوماً) in the rule allows for such accidental cases.

وإذا انعكس في بعض الأمثلة وصدق العكس كما صدق الأصل، فذاك لخصوص المادة لا للقاعدة كما مثل سابقاً بقوله: بعض الحجر ليس إنسانا - صدق بعض الإنسان ليس بحجر.
And here is a table showing the conversion of quantified propositions: direct conversion. The original proposition: example, the converse: example. Universal affirmative: Every human is an animal; particular affirmative: Some animal is human; like: some human is animal. Particular affirmative: some human is animal; particular affirmative: some animal is human. Universal negative: No human is a stone; universal negative: No stone is a human. Particular negative: Some animal is not a human; it has no converse; so it is not said: some human is not an animal. Necessarily. And if in some examples it is converted and the converse is true as the original was true, that is due to the specificity of the matter, not to the rule, as previously exemplified by his statement: some stone is not a human — it is true that some human is not a stone.

Commentary
This passage presents the conversion (عكس) rules for categorical propositions, which is a form of immediate inference. The author provides a table listing each original proposition type, an example, the converse type, and an example of the converse. For universal affirmative (e.g., 'Every human is animal'), the converse is particular affirmative ('Some animal is human'). For particular affirmative (e.g., 'Some human is animal'), the converse is also particular affirmative ('Some animal is human'). For universal negative (e.g., 'No human is a stone'), the converse is universal negative ('No stone is a human'). For particular negative (e.g., 'Some animal is not human'), there is no valid converse; one cannot infer 'Some human is not animal'. The author then adds a note: if in some material examples the converse happens to be true (like 'some stone is not human' converting to 'some human is not stone'), that is not due to the logical rule but to the specific content (material truth), not the form.

Key Takeaway
Conversion preserves truth only for universal negatives (unchanged quantity/quality) and for affirmatives (where universal becomes particular).
