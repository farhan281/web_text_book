The Divisions of Fallacy and the Supremacy of Demonstration
Author concludes the treatise by defining the two types of fallacy and asserting that only demonstration yields truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of fallacy and explain why only demonstration yields true beliefs according to this text.

Core Idea
Fallacy has two faces: sophistry and disputation, but only burhan delivers truth.

This passage closes the logic treatise with a clear classification of erroneous reasoning and a decisive statement about the only valid path to truth. As a student, you need to internalise these categories and understand why the author restricts certainty to demonstration. The passage also teaches you to recognize the ultimate purpose of logic: to secure true beliefs and eliminate false ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: al-mughalatah (fallacy). Note that the author immediately states it is confined to two divisions.
2
Extract the two divisions
List the two types: safsata (sophistry) and mushaghba (disputation). Sophistry is deceptive reasoning that appears true; disputation is contentious argument for its own sake.
The commentator adds that safsata comes from 'sufista' meaning embellished knowledge.
3
Note the exclusive claim about demonstration
The author declares that the only relied-upon method is burhan (demonstration). No other method yields true beliefs.
This is a strong epistemological claim: demonstration is both necessary and sufficient.
4
Reflect on the reason given
The reason: acquisition of true beliefs and removal of false beliefs is only through burhan. This ties the classification to the goal of logic.
5
Connect to the text's purpose
The passage ends the treatise with a prayer for true beliefs and protection from false ones, emphasising that logic's ultimate aim is not mere argumentation but truth and salvation.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding definition and assertion from the author of the logic treatise. The author first defines fallacy (al-mughalatah) as being of two types: sophistry (al-safsatah) and disputation (al-mushaghbah). Sophistry refers to deceptive reasoning that appears true, while disputation refers to contentious argument aimed at defeating an opponent regardless of truth. The author then declares that the only reliable method for attaining truth is demonstration (al-burhan). The reason given is that only through demonstration can one acquire true beliefs and eliminate false ones. This is a strong claim: demonstration is not merely superior but exclusive. The passage ends with a prayer for the reader and a benediction upon the Prophet and his family, marking the conclusion of the work. The logical structure is a simple classification followed by a normative statement.
