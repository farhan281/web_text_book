The Five Arts: An Overview
Author classifies syllogism by material into five arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five arts of syllogism and identify the type of premises each uses.

Core Idea
The five arts classify syllogisms by their material—the trustworthiness of their premises.

In this passage, the author gives a concise classification of syllogisms into five types, called 'industries' or 'arts'. This classification is based not on the form of the syllogism, but on the quality of its premises—whether they are certain, probable, accepted, imaginative, or deceptive. Understanding this classification is key to recognizing the purpose of each logical art.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the mnemonic
The author gives a simple formula: if from certainties -> burhan; from opinions -> khataba; from accepted -> jadal; from imaginatives -> shi'r; from deceptive resemblances -> mughalata. Repeat this mnemonic several times.
2
Identify the basis of classification
Notice that the classification is by the 'material' (madda) of the syllogism, i.e., the nature of the premises, not the form or figure.
3
Understand the five terms
Each art is named: burhan (demonstration), khataba (rhetoric), jadal (dialectic), shi'r (poetry), mughalata (sophistry). Learn the Arabic terms.

Demonstration (Burhan) and Certainty
Author defines demonstration as composed of certainties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define demonstration (burhan) and explain why it is the highest form of syllogism.

Core Idea
Demonstration uses certain premises (yaqiniyat) and is the only art that yields certain knowledge.

Among the five arts, demonstration (burhan) holds the highest rank because it is built upon certain and true premises (yaqiniyat). These premises include necessary truths, sensory data, and well-established empirical facts. In the Islamic tradition, burhan is the method of proof in kalam and falsafa when establishing the existence of God or the truth of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises of burhan
Find the term 'اليقينيات' (certainties) in the passage. The author states that if a syllogism is composed of such premises, it is called burhan.
Yaqiniyat are premises that are certain and cannot be doubted.
2
Contrast with other arts
Compare burhan with other arts: rhetoric uses opinions, dialectic uses accepted premises, etc. Burhan is unique in aiming at certainty.
3
Understand the purpose
The purpose of burhan is to arrive at truth and certainty. In Islamic sciences, it is used to prove foundational doctrines.

Dialectic (Jadal) and Rhetoric (Khataba)
Author distinguishes jadal and khataba by premise types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dialectic and rhetoric based on the type of premises they use.

Core Idea
Dialectic uses premises accepted by the opponent; rhetoric uses probable opinions for persuasion.

While demonstration aims at certainty, dialectic and rhetoric deal with less-than-certain premises. Dialectic (jadal) relies on musallamat – premises that the opponent concedes in debate, whether true or not. Rhetoric (khataba) relies on zanniyat – commonly held opinions that are probable but not certain. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating debates and persuasive speeches.

Guided Path
1
Locate the terms in the passage
Find 'المسلمات' (accepted premises) for jadal and 'الظنيَّاتِ' (opinions) for khataba.
2
Compare the epistemic status
Jadal's premises are accepted by the opponent, which may be false but are granted for the sake of argument. Khataba's premises are probable, meaning they are likely true but not certain.
3
Identify real-world examples
Think of a scholarly debate: the disputants agree on certain premises (musallamat) to avoid endless argument. A sermon often uses common beliefs (zanniyat) to persuade the audience.

Resolving Ambiguity in Definitions: The Case of the Particular
Author presents and refutes an objection to the definition of the particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve equivocation in a definitional argument by distinguishing the referent of a term.

Core Idea
The particular is universal only if you confuse the word with its referent.

In this passage, the author presents an objection that seems to show a contradiction in the definition of the particular. By carefully distinguishing two possible meanings of 'the particular', the author dissolves the objection. You will learn how to spot and resolve such equivocations in Islamic logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Extract the objection's syllogism
Identify the minor and major premises from the objector's statement: 'The particular is that which does not prevent partnership' (minor) and 'Everything that does not prevent partnership is universal' (major).
The major premise is implied but clear in context.
2
Recognize the contradiction
The conclusion 'The particular is universal' contradicts the original definition that the particular prevents partnership. This is the 'khalf' (contradiction) the objector claims.
The objector uses this contradiction to challenge the definition.
3
Distinguish two senses of 'particular'
The author suggests 'the particular' could mean either the individual referent (like Zayd) or the word 'particular' itself. Each leads to a different response.
This is the key move: the ambiguity breaks the syllogism.
4
Apply the refutation
If 'particular' means the individual, the minor premise is false because Zayd's concept includes individuation (prevents partnership). If it means the word, the conclusion is true but not contradictory, as the word is universal.
Thus, the objection fails because it equivocates.

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical exchange: an objection (irad) against the definition of the particular (juz'i) and the author's response. The objection constructs a categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani). The minor premise: 'The particular is that which does not prevent the very conception of its concept from the occurrence of partnership, like Zayd and 'Amr and others.' The major premise: 'Everything that is like that is universal.' Conclusion: 'Therefore the particular is universal.' This leads to a contradiction (khalf) with the original definition that the particular prevents partnership. The author (the responder) answers by distinguishing two meanings of 'the particular' in the objection. If by 'the particular' is meant the individual referent (like Zayd himself), then the minor premise is not conceded: Zayd, as an individual, does prevent partnership—his concept includes individuation, so he is not 'that which does not prevent.' If by 'the particular' is meant the word 'particular' (the term itself), then the contradiction is not conceded: the word 'particular' is a universal term that applies to many individuals (Zayd, 'Amr, etc.), so it is indeed universal, but that does not contradict the definition because the definition is about the concept of the particular, not the word. Thus the objection fails by equivocation.

Classifying Conditionals by Quantity
Author classifies conditionals by time quantifier

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any conditional proposition as makhsusa, mahsura (kulliya/juz'iyya), or muhmala based on its time quantifier.

Core Idea
Times in conditionals are like individuals in categoricals — they determine quantity.

Just as categorical propositions are quantified by the subject's individuals, conditional propositions are quantified by times or conditions. This lesson teaches you to identify whether a conditional is tied to a specific moment (makhsusa), universally quantified (kulliya), particularly quantified (juz'iyya), or left unrestricted (muhmala). Mastering this classification is essential for correctly analyzing conditional arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time specification
Examine the conditional for any explicit reference to time – 'now' (الآن), 'whenever' (كلما), 'sometimes' (قد), or no time word. This is the first clue.
In connected conditionals (شرطية متصلة), the time often appears in the antecedent or consequent.
2
Check if time is quantified
If there is a time word, see if it indicates a specific instant (مخصوصة) or a quantity (all or some). 'Now' means specific time; 'whenever' means all times; 'sometimes' means some times.
A universal quantifier like كلما (whenever) or دائما (always) indicates kulliya. The word قد (sometimes) indicates juz'iyya.
3
Determine the type
Based on the previous step: specific time → makhsusa; quantified (all times) → mahsura kulliya; quantified (some times) → mahsura juz'iyya; no time quantifier → muhmala.
The passage states that if the judgment is in a specific time (زمان معين), it is makhsusa. Otherwise, if the quantity of all or some time is specified, it is mahsura; else muhmala.
4
Apply to examples
Test your understanding on the examples provided: (1) 'If Zaid comes now...' → makhsusa; (2) 'Whenever the sun is shining...' → kulliya; (3) 'Sometimes if a thing is an animal...' → juz'iyya; (4) 'If the sun is shining...' (no time) → muhmala.
For disconnected conditionals, the same logic applies: 'Always, a number is either even or odd' → kulliya; 'Sometimes this thing is either an animal or a human' → juz'iyya.

Core Idea

In this passage, the author refines the definition of qiyas by adding a crucial condition: the entailment must be 'by themselves' (lidhatiha). The author warns against cases like qiyas al-musawah (equality syllogism) and al-zurufiyya (container relation), where the conclusion seems to follow but actually depends on a tacit external premise. Author defines the condition that the conclusion must be different from each premise.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The conclusion must be other than each premise, or the reasoning becomes circular.

In this passage, the author lays down a fundamental condition for any valid syllogism: the conclusion cannot simply be a restatement of one of the premises. Whether in categorical or conditional form, if the conclusion repeats a premise, the argument is either redundant or circular. Let's break down this rule and see why it matters.

The author treats both types separately, so be sure to apply the condition to the correct syllogism form.
2
Identify the prohibition: full identity, not partial overlap
The author explicitly states that the conclusion may share terms with the premises; only complete identity is forbidden. This nuance is crucial: a valid conclusion can reuse terms, but cannot be the same proposition.
Look for verbatim repetition of the entire sentence.
3
Understand the rationale behind the condition
Without this condition, the argument becomes either nonsensical (هذيانا) or circular (مصادرة). The example given shows that if the conclusion is identical to a premise, the proof is merely restating the claim, providing no new evidence.
This prevents begging the question.

Condition of Conclusion's Otherness from Premises
Author defines the condition of the conclusion being other than the premises and justifies it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a syllogism's conclusion is distinct from its premises and explain why this condition is necessary to avoid fallacies.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism's conclusion must be other than its premises, else it is circular or nonsense.

We have learned the basic structure of categorical and conditional syllogisms. Now we turn to a crucial condition: the conclusion cannot be identical to either premise. Al-Fanari explains why this condition is essential to prevent logical fallacies. This lesson will help you recognize when a syllogism violates this condition and understand the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
Locate the proposition that is derived from the premises. In a conjunctive syllogism, it is the statement that follows from the minor and major premises.
2
Check for identity with premises
Compare the conclusion with the minor premise and the major premise. If the conclusion is word-for-word identical to either premise, the condition of otherness is violated.
Also check in exceptional syllogisms: the conclusion must not be identical to the conditional premise.
3
Distinguish identity from part-whole overlap
The condition only prohibits the conclusion being the same as a premise, not that the conclusion shares a term or part with a premise. Al-Fanari explicitly says: 'As for it not being a part of one of the two premises, it is not required.'
A conclusion can legitimately contain terms from the premises.
4
Justify the condition
If the conclusion were identical to a premise, the argument would either be incoherent (هذيان) because it lacks a genuine inference, or it would commit begging the question (مصادرة) by assuming the conclusion as a premise, leading to circular reasoning.
This ensures that the premises serve as genuine evidence for the conclusion.

The second figure mirrors the first in its minor premise, making its conclusion naturally evident.

In the study of categorical syllogisms, the four figures are ranked by their naturalness and ease of inference. The first figure is the most natural, but the second is remarkably close. This lesson draws from a classical commentary to clarify why the second figure requires no reduction for a sound intellect, while the third and fourth do not share this privilege.

Mutual Convertibility of Conditional and Categorical Syllogisms
Author demonstrates how to convert between conditional (istithna'i) and categorical (iqtirani) syllogisms when disputing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert a conditional syllogism into a categorical syllogism and vice versa, preserving logical validity.

Core Idea
Conditional and categorical syllogisms are two faces of the same logical coin.

In dialectical practice, you may need to present your argument in a form your opponent finds more compelling. The author shows that conditional (istithna'i) and categorical (iqtirani) syllogisms are interconvertible, and gives precise examples of how to transform one into the other. This lesson will teach you this conversion technique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional syllogism's components
Find the antecedent (if-clause), consequent (then-clause), and the exception (istithna') which affirms the antecedent. In 'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists; the sun is rising; therefore daytime exists', the antecedent is 'the sun is rising', consequent is 'daytime exists', and exception is 'the sun is rising'.
2
Convert conditional to categorical
Replace the conditional premise with a universal categorical premise: 'Every time the sun rises, daytime exists'. Then use the exception as a minor premise: 'This time is a time the sun rises'. The conclusion follows: 'Therefore this time is daytime'. This yields a categorical syllogism in Barbara mood.
The universal premise must be derived from the conditional without changing meaning.
3
Convert categorical to conditional
Take a categorical syllogism like 'The world is changing; everything changing is originated; therefore the world is originated'. Conditions and Productive Moods of the First Figure
Enunciation of conditions and moods of the first figure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions of the first figure and list its four productive moods.

Core Idea
Affirmative minor and universal major are the keys to valid first figure syllogisms.

After establishing the need for conditions, the author explicitly states them: the minor premise must be affirmative and the major premise universal. These two conditions ensure the middle term is properly distributed, giving rise to exactly four valid moods out of the sixteen possible combinations. The first figure thus becomes the standard measure for all sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
The author writes: 'Its conditions for production are the affirmation of the minor and the universality of the major.' This means the minor premise (the one containing the subject of the conclusion) must be affirmative (A or I), and the major premise (the one containing the predicate of the conclusion) must be universal (A or E).
These conditions are necessary and sufficient for the first figure to guarantee a valid conclusion.
2
Understand why each condition is necessary
The minor must be affirmative to ensure that the subject of the conclusion is actually a member of the middle class. The major must be universal to ensure that the predicate applies to all members of the middle class. Together, they guarantee that the middle term is distributed at least once (in the major premise) and connects the subject to the predicate.
Distribution is key: the middle term must be universal in the major to cover all instances of the minor.
3
Recognize the four productive moods
Given these conditions, only four combinations of quantity and quality are valid: (1) minor A + major A → conclusion A (Barbara); (2) minor A + major E → conclusion E (Celarent); (3) minor I + major A → conclusion I (Darii); (4) minor I + major E → conclusion O (Ferio). All other combinations violate the conditions.
These four moods are traditionally named in the Latin tradition, but the author simply says 'four'.
4
See the first figure as the measure of sciences
The author emphasizes that the first figure is the 'miyar' (scale) for the sciences. It is the standard by which arguments are evaluated. Its validity is foundational, and it is used as a constitution (dustur) for resolving disputes and proving rulings in all disciplines.
This is why understanding its conditions is crucial for any Islamic scholar.

Deriving the Four Productive Moods of the First Figure
Conditions for productive moods in the first figure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the four productive moods of the first figure from the sixteen possible combinations by applying the conditions of affirmative minor and universal major.

Core Idea
Only four moods in the first figure are valid: eliminate negative minors and particular majors.

The first figure of the categorical syllogism is the most important and is called the 'standard of the sciences.' But not every combination of premises yields a valid conclusion. This passage explains the two key conditions that a first-figure syllogism must satisfy to be productive. By understanding these conditions, you will be able to quickly identify which moods are valid and why.

Guided Path
1
Start with the sixteen possible moods
There are four types of minor premises (A, E, I, O) and four types of major premises, giving 4×4 = 16 combinations. Write them down systematically.
A = universal affirmative, E = universal negative, I = particular affirmative, O = particular negative.
2
Apply the condition that the minor must be affirmative
Eliminate all moods where the minor is negative (E and O). Since there are 2 negative minors, each combined with 4 majors, that removes 2×4 = 8 moods.
Remaining minors: only A and I.
3
Apply the condition that the major must be universal
From the remaining moods (minor affirmative), eliminate those where the major is particular (I and O). The remaining minors are 2 (A and I), and each can combine with 2 universal majors (A and E), giving 2×2 = 4 moods. So remove the 4 combinations where the major is particular.
Remaining majors: only A and E.
4
List the four productive moods
The four valid moods are: AAA, EAE, AII, EIO. These satisfy both conditions. Verify that each has an affirmative minor and a universal major.
These are traditionally named Barbara, Celarent, Darii, and Ferio.
5
Confirm that no other moods remain
Check that all 16 possibilities have been accounted for: 8 removed by negative minor, 4 more by particular major, leaving exactly 4. If you try any other combination, either the minor is negative or the major is particular, violating the conditions.
This combinatorial method shows the necessity of the conditions.

The Conclusion Follows the Inferior Premise
Author states a key syllogistic principle and announces tables.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the quality and quantity of a categorical syllogism's conclusion by identifying the inferior premise.

Core Idea
The conclusion's quality and quantity are determined by the weaker premise.

In categorical syllogisms, not all premises are equal. Some are 'noble' (universal or affirmative) and others are 'lowly' (particular or negative). The conclusion always takes the rank of the inferior premise. This principle helps you predict the conclusion without memorizing every mood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quality and quantity of each premise
Look at the two premises of the syllogism. Determine whether each is universal or particular (quantity), and affirmative or negative (quality).
Universal and affirmative are 'noble'; particular and negative are 'lowly'.
2
Determine which premise is inferior
Among the two premises, the inferior one is the one that is lower in the hierarchy: particular is inferior to universal, negative is inferior to affirmative. If one premise is particular and the other universal, the particular is inferior. If one is negative and the other affirmative, the negative is inferior. If both inferiorities coincide, that premise is the most inferior.
Inferiority is determined by: particular < universal, negative < affirmative.
3
Apply the principle to the conclusion
The conclusion must have the same quality and quantity as the inferior premise. If the inferior premise is particular negative, the conclusion is particular negative. If it is universal affirmative, the conclusion is universal affirmative.
The conclusion cannot be better than the weakest premise.
