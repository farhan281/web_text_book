How to Read a Classical Islamic Logic Title Page
Title page of Fawaid al-Fanariyyah, a commentary on Isaghuji.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the book title, author, commentator, and publisher from a classical Islamic logic text's title page.

Core Idea
A title page is your first scholarly tool — it tells you who, what, and where.

When you pick up a classical Islamic text like Fawaid al-Fanariyyah, the title page is not just decoration. It contains essential metadata: the full book name, the chain of authorship (original author, commentator, and modern editor), and the publisher. Learning to parse this information ensures you know exactly which work you are studying and can trace its intellectual lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main title
Look for the largest or most prominent text on the page. Here it is 'الفوائد الفنارية' (The Fawaid al-Fanariyyah), which is the commentary name.
Often the title is repeated at the top and bottom.
2
Find the original author and commentator
The title page lists the commentator first (شمس الدين الفناري) and then the original author (أثير الدين الأبهري) who wrote the base text 'إيساغوجي' (Isaghuji).
The commentator is usually introduced with 'شرح العلامة' (commentary of the scholar).
3
Read the descriptive subtitle
The phrase 'شرح على متن إيساغوجي في المنطق' tells you this is a commentary on the logic text Isaghuji. This confirms the genre and subject.
Subtitles clarify the work's relationship to other texts.
4
Locate the editor and publisher
The modern editor (أ.د. عبدالملك عبد الرحمن أسعد السعدي العِراقي) and publisher (دار الفاروق) are listed for the critical edition.
Editors add scholarly value; the publisher indicates the edition's origin.
5
Synthesize the metadata
Combine all elements: you have a commentary (Fawaid al-Fanariyyah) by al-Fanari on al-Abhari's Isaghuji, edited by al-Sa'di, published by Dar al-Faruq in Amman.
This complete identification is essential for citation and further study.

Decoding a Title Page: Al-Fawaid al-Fanariyya
Presents title page and publication details of the commentary on Isaghuji.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a classical Islamic logic book's title page, including the work title, matn author, commentator, editor, and publisher.

Core Idea
A title page is a scholarly fingerprint: it tells you the work, its lineage, and its provenance.

When you open a classical Islamic logic text, the title page is your first encounter. It is not just decoration—it carries essential information about the work's identity and scholarly chain. In this lesson, we will dissect the title page of al-Fawaid al-Fanariyya, a famous commentary on Isaghuji, to learn how to read such pages with precision. This skill will serve you every time you pick up a new text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commentary title
Look for the main title of the work. Here it is 'الفوائد الفنارية' (al-Fawaid al-Fanariyya). This is the commentary itself, attributed to the commentator by the nisba 'al-Fanari'.
The word 'الفوائد' means 'benefits' or 'useful points', a common title element.
2
Identify the matn (base text) and its author
The phrase 'شرح ... على متن إيساغوجي' indicates that this is a commentary (sharh) on the matn 'إيساغوجي' (Isaghuji) by 'أثير الدين المفضل بن عمر بن المفضل الأبهري السمرقندي' (Athir al-Din al-Abhari). Note the full name and lineage.
The matn author is usually credited before or after the commentary author.
3
Identify the commentator
The commentator is named after 'شرح العلامة' (commentary of the scholar): 'شمس الدين الفناري' (Shams al-Din al-Fanari). This is the author of the commentary you are reading.
The word 'العلامة' (al-‘allama) is an honorific meaning 'the highly learned'.
4
Identify the editor and publisher
Modern critical editions include an editor. Here: 'تعليق وتوضيح أ.د. عبد الملك عبد الرحمن أسعد السعدي العراقي' with his academic affiliation. Also note the publisher: 'دار الفاروق للنشر والتوزيع' and place: 'عمان الأردن'.
Knowing the editor and publisher helps assess the edition's reliability.

Title page of Fawaid Fanari

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the matn and sharh of Fawaid Fanari and explain the role of Isaghuji in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
The title page reveals the living chain of transmission in Islamic logic.

This lesson examines the title page of Fawaid Fanari, a celebrated commentary on the foundational logic text Isaghuji. You will learn to distinguish the base text (matn) from its commentary (sharh) and appreciate how this structure preserves scholarly authority across generations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (base text)
Find the phrase 'متن إيساغوجي' on the title page. This is the original textbook authored by Athir al-Din al-Abhari. Note that a matn is a concise statement of core material.
Isaghuji is an introductory text on logic, covering the five universals, propositions, and syllogisms.
2
Identify the sharh (commentary)
Locate 'شرح العلامة شمس الدين الفناري'. This indicates that al-Fanari wrote an explanation (sharh) of the matn. A sharh unpacks, clarifies, and often expands upon the matn.
Al-Fanari's commentary is so well-known it has its own name: al-Fawaid al-Fanariyya.
3
Trace the chain of authorship
Note the sequence: matn author (al-Abhari, d. 1264) → sharh author (al-Fanari, d. 1430) → modern editor (Dr. Abdul Malik al-Sa'di). This chain shows how the text has been transmitted and enriched over centuries.
Understanding this chain is crucial for evaluating the authority and context of scholarly claims.
4
Recognize the publisher and modern contributions
The bottom line mentions 'دار الفاروق' (publisher) and 'تعليق وتوضيح' (notes and clarification) by the editor. Modern editions often include critical apparatus.
Always check the editor's role; some provide footnotes, while others correct the text based on manuscripts.

The Purpose and Method of the Editor's Commentary
Editor explains his motivation for adding clarifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the editor's motivation for writing ta'liqat and describe how the commentary layout aids learning.

Core Idea
A good commentary bridges the gap between classical scholarship and contemporary understanding.

The editor, Dr. Abdul Malik al-Sa'di, begins by praising Allah and the Prophet, then sets out his purpose: to make al-Fanari's concise commentary on Isaghuji accessible to today's students. He explains why earlier students did not struggle, and how his layout (text above, comments below) is designed to help. This lesson unpacks the rationale behind such scholarly additions and how they function as a learning tool.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem the editor identifies
Read the editor's statement that al-Fanari's commentary contains ambiguous expressions (عبارات غامضة) for modern students, though they were clear to earlier students. Why were earlier students able to understand? The editor says they had practiced solving expressions from classical books of various sciences.
This highlights how intellectual training changes across eras.
2
Analyze the editor's solution
The editor resolved to place the original text and commentary at the top of the page and add his own clarifications (تعليقات) at the bottom. Why this layout? It allows students to see the original source and the clarifying notes together, facilitating comparison and understanding.
This method is common in many Islamic scholarly works (e.g., hashiya).
3
Extract the editor's desired outcomes
The editor asks Allah for sincerity, a good end, and that his work benefits students of logic. This shows that his goal is both spiritual (ikhlas) and pedagogical (benefiting others). The addition of a date and his scholarly titles emphasizes the authority and intentionality behind the work.
4
Relate this to the broader tradition of commentary
Think about other commentaries in Islamic sciences (e.g., Tafsir, Sharh al-Muqni'). The editor's work is an expansion (ta'liqat) on a sharh. This is a common practice: a later scholar adds glosses to clarify or update an earlier commentary. Understanding this helps you navigate the layers of Islamic scholarly texts.
This is the 'expansion_probe' aspect of the passage.

Rhetorical Analysis of an Opening Praise
Author praises Allah for granting understanding and easing hardships.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rhetorical device of jinas (paronomasia) and metaphorical imagery in a classical Islamic scholarly introduction and explain their purpose in setting a tone of humility and gratitude.

Core Idea
The opening praise is a scholarly act of humility, gratitude, and acknowledgment of the difficulty of knowledge.

This lesson examines the opening lines of al-Fanari's commentary on the Fawa'id. These lines are not mere formality; they contain deliberate rhetorical choices that reflect the author's attitude toward his work. Understanding these choices helps us appreciate the broader intellectual culture of Islamic scholarship, where praise and humility are intertwined with the pursuit of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basmala and its role
The passage begins with 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim'. In Islamic scholarly works, the basmala opens every book, invoking Allah's name and seeking His blessing. Recognize this as a standard opening that aligns the work with Islamic tradition.
The basmala is not just a phrase; it situates the author's work within a sacred framework.
2
Find the verbal parallelism (jinas)
Look for words that sound similar but carry different meanings: 'lakhkhashta' (summarized) and 'khallastani' (delivered me) form a jinas between the letters 'kh' and 'l' vs 'kh' and 's'. Also, 'minah' (gifts) and 'mihan' (trials) are a perfect jinas. Identify these pairs and note the contrast in meaning.
Jinas is a rhetorical device that draws attention to the words and enhances the aesthetic quality.
3
Interpret the metaphor 'storms of virtues'
The phrase 'mihan awasif al-fada'il' literally means 'trials of the storms of virtues'. Understand that 'storms' symbolize overwhelming intellectual challenges, and 'virtues' refer to the lofty aims of knowledge. This metaphor expresses the difficulty of attaining true understanding.
The author thanks Allah for deliverance from such difficulties, implying that scholarship is arduous.
4
Connect to the purpose of the commentary
By opening with praise and acknowledgment of divine assistance, al-Fanari sets the tone for his commentary: he is a humble recipient of wisdom, not the originator. This sets the reader's expectation for a work that explains and builds on earlier scholars.
Such openings are conventional in Islamic scholarly literature to establish authority and sincerity.

Author states the principle that every science must be known by its unifying subject matter before study.

Learning Objective

He illustrates with oranges and camels to show how specification prevents confusion. This lesson unpacks that principle so you can apply it to your own study of logic and other Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological introduction from the author of Fawaid Fanari. It establishes a fundamental principle for studying any science: one must first know the unifying aspect (al-jihah al-wahdah) that binds together the multitude of issues under that science. The author begins by stating that every student of a 'multitude' (kathrah) — whether material or scientific — has the right (haqq) to know that multitude by its unifying aspect before delving into its parts. This ensures that the student does not miss what concerns him and does not waste effort on what does not. Additionally, knowing the purpose (ghayah) of the science increases diligence and prevents futile endeavor. The reason for this is that every science is a multitude of issues that are held together by a single essential aspect (jihah wahdah dhatiyyah), which makes them one science. That aspect is that the science investigates the essential accidents (al-a'rad al-dhatiyyah) of one subject matter (shay' wahid), whether that subject matter is a real unity (wahdah haqiqiyyah) or a conceptual unity (wahdah i'tibariyyah). The author then provides two concrete examples of material multitude to illustrate the principle: oranges and camels. In each case, the seeker must be given a set of distinguishing features (specifications) so that he can identify the desired items amidst a multitude of similar things. Without such specification, he might mistake one thing for another or fail to find what he seeks. The same principle applies to scientific multitude, which will be discussed later. This passage is foundational for understanding how the author approaches the definition and organization of the sciences.

Key Terms
كثرة (kathrah): A multitude of items or issues that are unified by a common aspect; can be material (like oranges or camels) or scientific (issues within a discipline). [Note: In later logical works, kathrah often refers to the multiplicity of individuals under a universal; here it is used more broadly.]
جهة وحدة (jihah wahdah): The single aspect that controls and unifies a multitude; the defining characteristic that allows recognition and classification.
أعراض ذاتية (a'rad dhatiyyah): Essential accidents; properties that necessarily belong to a subject matter and are studied by the science, as opposed to accidental attributes. [Note: This term is standard in logic for the subject matter of a science; no significant shift.]
وحدة حقيقية واعتبارية (wahdah haqiqiyyah wa i'tibariyyah): Real unity (e.g., a species) vs. conceptual unity (e.g., a genus or an accidental unity); the subject matter of a science can be either. [Note: This distinction is common in post-Avicennan logic; no significant shift.]

The unifying aspect and goal are the compass and purpose of any discipline.

Before diving into the details of any field of study, one must first grasp what unifies its subject matter and what ultimate purpose it serves. Core Idea

The Four Gates of Logic and Division of Qiyas
Table of contents entry on logic's structure and qiyas division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to outline the four gates of logic as listed in Fawaid al-Fanari's table of contents and explain the classification of qiyas by its matter.

Core Idea
Logic has a plan: four gates, and qiyas is classified by the certainty of its premises.

The table of contents mentions 'اعتبار الجهة الذاتية للمنطق أبواب المنطق وخطته أربعة' and 'أقسام القياس من حيث مادته لا هيكله'. This indicates that the work divides logic into four major sections and that syllogisms are categorized based on the epistemic quality of their premises, not their formal figure. Understanding this framework is crucial for navigating the entire commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four gates reference
Locate the line 'أبواب المنطق وخطته أربعة'. This tells you that logic is organized into four main 'gates' or chapters.
The four gates are typically: tasawwur (conception), tasdiq (assent), qiyas (syllogism), and shart (conditions). Verify in the full text.
2
Understand 'by matter not form'
The line 'أقسام القياس من حيث مادته لا هيكله' means that syllogisms are divided according to the substance (matter) of their premises (e.g., certain, probable, fallacious) rather than their formal figure (shakl).
This is a key shift from Aristotelian classification; see the explanation in the commentary.
3
Find the phrase 'اعتبار الجهة الذاتية'
This phrase means 'consideration of the essential aspect of logic'. It precedes the mention of the four gates, indicating the overarching perspective of the work.

The Isaghuji Roadmap: From Indication to Universals
Table of contents listing topics from Isaghuji.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to sequence the key topics of the Isaghuji (dalala, lafz, singular/compound, universals) as they appear in the table of contents of Fawaid al-Fanari.

Core Idea
The Isaghuji proceeds from indication to expression to universals — a logical progression.

After the introductory sections on logic's structure, the table of contents lists the specific topics from the matn (Isaghuji) that al-Fanari will comment on. These begin with al-dalala (indication), move through al-lafz (expression), its divisions, then to the universal and the five universals. This sequence is the standard order of Isaghuji, and understanding it helps you navigate the commentary and anticipate the logical development.

Guided Path
1
List the topics in order
From the table of contents, extract the following sequence: تعريف الدلالة, أقسام الدلالة, أنواع الدلالة اللفظية الوضعية, إيرادات على هذه التعريفات أقسام اللفظ, تعريف المفرد وأقسامه, المركب, أقسام اللفظ المفرد باعتبار معناه, إيراد على تعريف الجزئي, أقسام الكلي, الثاني من قسمي الكلي, الكليات الخمس, تعريف الجنس.
The list is found after 'الشروع في شرح إيساغوجي.'
2
Group into conceptual units
Group the topics: 1. Indication (dalala) and its types. 2. Expression (lafz) and its divisions (singular vs. compound). 3. The universal (kulli) and its divisions, including the five universals. 4. Definition of genus as a start.
This shows the progression from meaning (indication) to its verbal vehicle to the classification of concepts.
3
Relate to logic's purpose
Understand that these topics are foundational for tasawwur (conception). Dalala explains how words signify concepts, lafz deals with the linguistic units, and the universals are the building blocks of definitions and predication.

The Two Pillars of Logic: Tasawwurat and Tasdiqat
Division of the commentary into two chapters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two main parts of logic (tasawwurat and tasdiqat) and give examples of each from the commentary.

Core Idea
Logic is divided into concepts and assents – definitions vs. propositions.

The commentary explicitly divides into two chapters: 'مقاصد التصورات' (aims of concepts) and 'مبادئ التصديقات' (principles of assents). This division is fundamental in Islamic logic, corresponding to the two operations of the mind: forming concepts and making judgments.

Guided Path

The Logical Order of Topics – From Signification to Contradiction
Progression of topics in the commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the logical dependency of topics in a mantiq text and justify why one topic precedes another.

Core Idea
Each topic in logic builds on the previous; you cannot understand propositions without first understanding terms.

The table of contents shows a clear progression: first we study how words signify (dalalah), then the types of terms (mufrad, murakkab), then universals (kulliyat), then definitions (hadd/rasm), then propositions (qadiyya), and finally contradiction (tanāqud). This is not arbitrary but reflects the necessary order of learning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first major topic after introductions
Look for 'تعريف الدلالة' (definition of signification). This is the first substantive topic.
Signification is foundational because logic deals with concepts conveyed by words.
2
Trace the chain from signification to terms to universals
After signification comes 'أقسام اللفظ' (divisions of the term), then 'أقسام الكلي' (divisions of the universal). Universals cannot be understood without first knowing what a term is.
3
Follow from universals to definitions
After the five universals, the next topics define genus, species, etc., then move to definition (hadd) and description (rasm). Definitions rely on the concepts of genus and differentia (which are universals).
4
Continue to propositions and contradiction
Under tasdiqat, propositions are discussed first, then contradiction. Propositions are combinations of concepts; contradiction is a relation between propositions.
This shows that propositions must be understood before the rules of contradiction can be applied.
