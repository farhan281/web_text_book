Types of Unity and Definitions of Logic
Author classifies unity into real and conceptual, defines the essential and accidental unity of logic, and presents two definitions of logic from later and earlier scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real and conceptual unity and between essential and accidental unity of a science, and identify the two definitions of logic given by later and earlier scholars.

Core Idea
The unity of a science can be essential (based on its subject) or accidental (based on its utility).

This passage from al-Fanari's commentary explains how scholars define sciences by their essential or accidental aspects. It introduces two types of essential unity—real and conceptual—and applies them to logic, giving two definitions from later and earlier logicians. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for grasping how Islamic sciences are structured and defined.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of essential unity
Find the author's distinction between real unity (وحدة حقيقية) and conceptual unity (وحدة اعتبارية). Real unity is exemplified by the subject of arithmetic (number), where the multiplicity is genuinely unified. Conceptual unity is exemplified by the sources of usul al-fiqh (Book, Sunnah, consensus), which are not intrinsically one but are treated as a unified set.
Note that real unity corresponds to the subject matter having a natural unity, while conceptual unity is imposed by the mind.
2
Distinguish essential unity from accidental unity of a science
The author explains that a science has a self-essential unity (وحدة ذاتية) based on its subject, issues, and principles all relating to its essence. It also has an accidental unity (وحدة عرضية) based on external factors like being a tool for other sciences or having a goal. For logic, the essential unity is its subject (concepts and assents), while its accidental unity is its utility in preventing error.
Accidental unity follows the essential, as the utility depends on the nature of the subject.
3
Parse the later definition of logic
The later scholars (المتأخرون) define logic as: 'a science that investigates the essential accidents of concepts and assents (التصورات والتصديقات) from the aspect of their benefit in leading to unknowns.' Identify the genus (a science) and the differentia (investigation of essential accidents of concepts/assents as they lead to unknowns).
This definition focuses on the utility of concepts and assents in inference.
4
Parse the earlier definition of logic
The earlier scholars (المتقدمون) define logic as: 'a science that investigates the essential accidents of secondary intelligibles (المعقولات الثانية) which correspond to nothing external, from the aspect that they apply to primary intelligibles (المعقولات الأولى).' Notice the different subject: secondary intelligibles (like 'subject', 'predicate') rather than concepts/assents directly.
The earlier definition is more abstract, focusing on the formal relations of mental concepts.
5
Compare the two definitions
Both definitions agree that logic investigates something related to mental content (concepts/assents or secondary intelligibles) and aims at inference. However, the later definition emphasizes the utility in leading to unknowns, while the earlier emphasizes the correspondence between secondary and primary intelligibles. Both ultimately treat the same subject matter from different angles.
This shift reflects a development in the understanding of logic as a tool for knowledge.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of unity, now focusing on logic. The author first introduces the accidental unifying aspect (جهة وحدة عرضية) that follows the essential one: a science may be defined by its utility (كونها آلة) and its goal (غاية). He notes that scholars customarily define sciences by one of these two aspects, along with the goal and subject, before delving into the issues. He then explains the two types of essential unity: real unity (وحدة حقيقية) and conceptual unity (وحدة اعتبارية). Real unity is exemplified by the subject of arithmetic (number), where the multiplicity of numbers is genuinely unified as quantities. Conceptual unity is exemplified by the sources of usul al-fiqh (Book, Sunnah, consensus), which are not intrinsically one but are treated as a unified set of evidence. Applying this to logic, the author distinguishes its self-essential unity (الوحدة الذاتية) from its accidental unity (الوحدة العرضية). The essential unity consists in the fact that logic's subject, issues, and principles all relate to its essence. The accidental unity is its role as a tool (آلة) for other sciences and its goal of preventing error in definition and reasoning. The author then gives two definitions of logic: one by the later scholars (المتأخرين) and one by the earlier scholars (المتقدمين). The later definition: logic is the science that investigates the essential accidents of concepts and assents (التصورات والتصديقات) insofar as they lead to unknowns. The earlier definition: logic investigates the essential accidents of secondary intelligibles (المعقولات الثانية) by which they apply to primary intelligibles (المعقولات الأولى). Despite the difference, both agree that the subject of logic is concepts and assents from the aspect of leading to an unknown, whether conceptual or assertional.

Essential and Accidental Unity of a Science
Author explains essential and accidental unity with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the essential unity of a science from its accidental unity, using examples from arithmetic and usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A science's subject ensures its real unity; its instruments and goals give it merely conventional unity.

The passage begins by explaining that every science has two aspects of unity: one essential and inherent, another accidental and external. Understanding this distinction helps us grasp why logic is considered a single science despite its diverse problems.

Commentary
This passage is definitional, presenting the subject matter of logic and its definition according to two groups of scholars: the later (muta'akhkhirun) and the earlier (mutaqaddimun). The author begins by discussing the unity of a science, distinguishing between essential unity (al-wahdah al-dhatiyyah) and accidental unity (al-wahdah al-'aradiyyah). The essential unity is inherent in the science's subject, problems, and principles, as with arithmetic where the real unity is number. Accidental unity, such as logic's instrumentality or its purpose (avoiding error), is secondary. The author then states that logic is defined by its essential aspect, providing two definitions. The first, from the later scholars, defines logic as the science investigating the essential accidents of concepts (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat) insofar as they lead to unknowns. The second, from the earlier scholars, defines it as the science investigating the essential accidents of secondary intelligibles (al-ma'qulat al-thaniyah)—like genus, species, differentia, property—which do not correspond directly to external things but to primary intelligibles (like 'animal', 'human') that do correspond to external individuals. The author explains the process: from external individuals (e.g., Zayd) the mind abstracts primary intelligibles (animal, rational, etc.), then from these abstracts secondary intelligibles (genus, differentia, etc.). Both definitions converge on the subject of logic: concepts and assents as a means to reach the unknown.

Definition of Logic by the Later Scholars
Later scholars define logic by its subject and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the later scholars' definition of logic and identify its key components: essential accidents of concepts and assents as leading to unknowns.

Core Idea
Logic investigates the essential accidents of concepts and assents specifically as a path to the unknown.

The later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) defined logic in a way that highlights its practical purpose: it examines the internal properties of concepts and statements that help us reason correctly from known to unknown. This definition focuses on logic's function as a tool for knowledge acquisition.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
The definition is: 'A science in which is investigated the essential accidents of concepts and assents in terms of their benefit in leading to unknowns.' Identify the subject (tasawwurat and tasdiqat), the property studied (essential accidents), and the purpose (leading to unknowns).
2
Understand 'essential accidents'
These are the logical properties like definitions for concepts and syllogisms for assents; they are 'accidents' because they are not permanent, and 'essential' because they are internal to logic.
3
Appreciate the purpose
The definition specifies the perspective: 'min hayth naf'uha fi al-isal ila al-majhulat' (from the perspective of their benefit in leading to unknowns). Logic is not about concepts in themselves but as means to knowledge.

Necessity of Composite Definition and Hadd vs. Rasm
Argument for composite definitions, distinguishing hadd and rasm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given definition is a hadd (essential) or rasm (descriptive) and explain why all definitions must be composite.

Core Idea
Every definition is a composite of genus and differentia; a simple term cannot define an essence.

This passage from Fanari's commentary on al-Shamsiyya explains why definitions cannot be simple labels. To define something, we must attribute a differentia to a genus, making the definition inherently composite. The author then distinguishes between two types of definitions: hadd, which reveals the essence, and rasm, which merely distinguishes. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Read the opening sentence: 'the identifier necessarily requires conceptualizing the existence of something for something'. This is the claim that definitions are composite.
2
Understand the 'rational indicator'
The 'qarina 'aqliyya' is the mental link that allows us to move from the general (genus) to the specific (differentia). Without it, a simple genus or differentia alone cannot define.
For example, 'animal' alone is too broad; 'rational' alone is not a complete concept.
3
Analyze the examples
The passage breaks down 'natiq' (rational) as 'a thing that has rationality' and 'dahik' (laughing) as 'a thing that has laughter'. This shows that even seemingly simple terms involve composition.
Note that 'natiq' alone implies an underlying substance (thing).
4
Distinguish hadd from rasm
Hadd defines by the essence (genre + differentia that constitutes the quiddity). Rasm defines by a distinguishing property that does not reveal the essence. Example: 'rational animal' is hadd; 'laughing animal' is rasm.
The passage says hadd explains the core (kunh), rasm distinguishes (yumayyizu).

Definiens (المعرف) and Its Types: Hadd and Rasm
Author defines the definiens and distinguishes hadd from rasm with their signs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a hadd (definition by essence) and a rasm (definition by distinguishing feature) and apply this distinction to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The definiens must cause conception; hadd gives essence, rasm gives a distinguishing mark.

In classical logic, a definition (al-mu'arraf) is not just any description—it must be a cause for conceiving the thing. This passage from al-Fanari's commentary distinguishes two types: al-hadd (definition by essence) and al-rasm (definition by distinguishing feature). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating definitions in the Islamic sciences, where precise definitions ensure correct legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definiens (al-mu'arraf)
The definiens is that whose conception causes you to conceive the thing. This excludes assents (tasdiqat) and self-evident concomitants (lazim bayyin) because they require no acquisition effort.
Conception (tasawwur) is the target cognitive state.
2
Identify the two types
Hadd uses the essence (bi-kunhihi) – the thing's quiddity. Rasm uses a distinguishing feature (bi-wajh yumayyizuhu) – it does not reveal essence but sets the thing apart from others.
The phrase 'either... or' (imma... aw) includes both.
3
Examine the examples
Rational animal (al-hayawan al-natiq) is hadd for man – it gives the essence. Laughing animal (al-hayawan al-dahik) is rasm – it distinguishes man from other animals but does not reveal his quiddity.
The passage also notes that 'two necessitates evenness' is not a definition because it is self-evident.
4
Understand the division's sign
The division of the limited (mahdud) into hadd and rasm is indicated by a disjunction that prevents emptiness (infisal mani' min al-khuluww): the limited must be either hadd or rasm, no third option.
This sign is attributed to Shams al-A'imma al-Isfahani.

The Impossibility of Defining the Definiens and the Refutation of Identity

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Defining the definiens causes infinite regress; identity cannot stop it.

In this lesson, we examine a key objection in definition theory: the definiens itself cannot be defined, because that would set off an infinite chain of definitions. Some proposed that the definiens of the definiens is identical to the first, but this is rejected as impossible. Understand the infinite regress argument
The objection states that if you could define the definiens, then that definition would itself need defining, ad infinitum. This regress is impossible.
The philosophers (hukama) reject infinite regress.
2

Some tried to stop the regress by claiming that the definiens of the definiens is identical to the original definiens.
This is like saying 'existence exists'.

The Two Types of Conjunctive Conditional: Luzumiyya and Ittifaqiyya
Author classifies conjunctive conditional into implicative and accidental by presence of iqtida'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any conjunctive conditional proposition as either implicative (luzumiyya) or accidental (ittifaqiyya) by checking whether the conjunction is based on a real causal or correlational relation (iqtida') or merely coincidental.

Core Idea
Not every 'if...then' expresses a real connection; some are just coincidental.

In Mantiq, the conjunctive conditional (shartiyya muttasila) is not a monolithic category. The author divides it based on whether the connection between antecedent and consequent is grounded in a genuine implication (iqtida') or is merely coincidental. This lesson walks you through that classification, so you can recognize when an argument uses a real entailment versus a spurious one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
In any conjunctive conditional, locate the two parts: the 'if' clause (muqaddam) and the 'then' clause (tali). Write them down clearly.
Example: 'If the sun is rising, then the day exists.' Antecedent: sun rising; consequent: day exists.
2
Test for causal or correlational connection
Ask: Is there a known causal relationship (antecedent causes consequent, or vice versa), or do both share a common cause, or is there a mutual correlation (tadha'if)? If yes, it is luzumiyya.
3
Check for coincidence
If no such relation is known or intended, and the conjunction is merely accidental (like 'If man is rational, then the donkey is braying'), it is ittifaqiyya.
The author stresses that 'no implication' means the judge does not know of an implication, not that none exists in reality.
4
Classify the subtypes of luzumiyya
If luzumiyya, determine which subtype: (1) cause-effect (antecedent cause of consequent or vice versa), (2) common cause (both effects of a third), (3) mutual correlation (tadha'if).
Example of common cause: 'If the day exists, then the world is illuminated' (both are effects of sunrise).

When Does 'No Implication' Mean No Known Implication?
Author clarifies that 'absence of implication' refers to lack of knowledge, not ontological absence, resolving an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between subjective ignorance and objective absence of causal connection in logical implication, and why this distinction resolves the objection that perpetual propositions must be necessary.

Core Idea
Absence of implication in the mind is not absence in reality—this saves the accidental conditional from critique.

In the previous lesson, we learned that accidental conditionals (ittifaqiyya) have 'no implication' (iqtida'). But what does that mean? The author clarifies that it means the judge lacks knowledge of an implication, not that no real causal connection exists. This distinction is crucial because it prevents a serious objection: if two things always coexist, they must be necessarily connected—but accidental conditionals would then become impossible. The author shows that the classification depends on the epistemical stance of the reasoner, not on ontology.

Guided Path
1
Recall the objection
The objection states: If two things always coexist (e.g., rationality and braying are always together in their respective subjects), then their complete cause makes them inseparable, so there is a necessary connection. Therefore, accidental conditionals would always actually be implicative.
This objection would collapse the distinction between luzumiyya and ittifaqiyya.
2
Understand the author's clarification
The author says: 'The meaning of absence of implication is the absence of the judge's knowledge of implication, not its absence in itself.' So the classification is about what the one making the statement knows, not about the real causal structure of the world.
3
Apply the resolution
Even if two properties are always conjoined in reality (e.g., rationality and braying are permanent in their species), the person uttering the conditional may not know of any causal link. Hence, for them, the conditional is accidental; they are just stating a coincidence as known to them.
This respects the subjective perspective of the logician while leaving open the possibility of objective causal connections.
4
Connect to broader implications
This resolves the problem that 'the perpetual (da'ima) is more general than the necessary (daruriyya)'. A proposition can be perpetually true (always holds) without being necessary in the sense of having a known implication. The accidental conditional captures those perpetual truths that are not recognized as implicative.
This shows the importance of epistemological factors in logical classification.

Core Idea
A syllogism is not one statement — it is a logical machine built from two or more premises.

We have now entered the fourth chapter on the objectives of assents, which focuses on the syllogism (qiyas). The author begins by defining qiyas precisely. This lesson unpacks that definition so you can distinguish a true syllogism from a single proposition that merely implies something else. Objection to the definition of qiyas as circular begging the question

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The definition of qiyas is defended by distinguishing a compound proposition from two separate premises.

In this passage, the author preempts an objection that a compound proposition which implies its converse and contrapositive would satisfy the definition of qiyas, yet is not considered a syllogism. The response clarifies that a qiyas requires two distinct premises, not a single proposition with internal entailments. We will analyze this exchange using the tools of jadal (dialectical debate).

1
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
Find the objector's claim: that a compound proposition entailing its converse and contrapositive fits the definition but is not called qiyas. Note the grounds: the definition would include cases that are not syllogisms.
2
Identify the respondent's position (mudda'i)
The author is defending the definition. He rejects the objection by not conceding the premise. His initial response: the compound proposition is not 'statements' (aqwal) but a single statement composed of statements.

Analyze the respondent's jawab (answer)

4
Assess the outcome
By the rules of jadal, the respondent wins because he shows that the objector's example does not meet the necessary condition of having two premises. The definition remains valid.
The distinction between a single compound proposition and a composed syllogism is key.
