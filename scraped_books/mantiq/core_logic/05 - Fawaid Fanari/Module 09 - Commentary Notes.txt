Do the Three Significations Entail Each Other?
Author explains the logical relations between مطابقة, تضمن, and التزام.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which types of signification entail which, and evaluate al-Razi's claim that conformity entails implication.

Core Idea
Signification entailment is asymmetric: inclusion implies conformity, but not vice versa.

In this passage, the author clarifies the logical relations among the three types of signification: conformity (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadammun), and implication (iltizam). He uses counterexamples from kalam (Allah, the point) to show that conformity does not entail inclusion, and that inclusion does entail conformity. He also critically examines Imam al-Razi's view that conformity entails implication, offering a strong epistemological objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of signification
The passage deals with مطابقة (conformity), تضمن (inclusion), and التزام (implication). Make sure you understand each: conformity is the primary indication of a word for its whole meaning; inclusion is indication of a part; implication is indication of an external concomitant.
These are standard in Islamic logic.
2
Map the entailment claims
The author states: (1) conformity does NOT entail inclusion, (2) inclusion DOES entail conformity, (3) implication does NOT entail inclusion, (4) conformity's entailment of implication is debated (Imam al-Razi said yes, the author says not certain). Write these as if-then statements.
Note the asymmetry: inclusion → conformity, but not the reverse.
3
Analyze the counterexamples
For (1): The term 'Allah' has no parts, so conformity does not imply inclusion. For (3): The implied entity may be simple (have no parts), so implication does not entail inclusion. For (4): The word 'human' by conformity indicates 'rational animal'; does it entail the concomitant 'not being other than itself'? The author says no, because often we conceive essences without any concomitant thought.
These examples are from kalam (Allah) and common definitions (human).
4
Evaluate the critique of al-Razi
Imam al-Razi argued that conformity always entails implication, at least a negative concomitant (e.g., that the meaning is not other than itself). The author responds that we often conceive essences without any such thought—so the implication is not always realized. This is a key epistemological objection: mental presence of the concomitant is not guaranteed.
This critique targets the psychological necessity of the implication.

Commentary
This passage discusses the logical relations between the three types of signification. It states that conformity (mutabaqa) does not entail inclusion (tadammun), because there are simple entities like Allah and the point that have no parts, so conformity applies without inclusion. However, the opposite (inclusion entails conformity) holds because if a word indicates a part, it must also indicate the whole by way of the part. Next, implication (iltizam) does not entail inclusion, because the implied entity may be simple. Then the author turns to the claim of Imam al-Razi that conformity entails implication. The author rejects this as not always true, providing the example of the word 'human' which by conformity indicates 'rational animal' and might imply the concomitant 'not being other than itself', but often when we utter a word, we do not attend to such negative concomitants. The response to a counter-argument (that at least some implication occurs) is that we can conceive essences without any accompanying thought. The argument uses examples from kalam (Allah) and logic (definitions).

No Need to Restrict Implication to Mental Concomitance
Author critiques restriction of implication to mental concomitance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a specific restriction on the type of concomitance in implication is necessary, using the commentator's argument that any valid concomitance suffices.

Core Idea
The purpose of implication is transition; any valid concomitance achieves it.

In this passage, the commentator critiques the matn's condition that implication (iltizām) requires mental concomitance (luzūm dhihnī). He argues that this restriction is unnecessary because the purpose of stipulating any concomitance is to validate the mental transition from the signified to the necessary concomitant, and this is achieved regardless of whether the concomitance is mental or external. Understanding this refinement is crucial for applying the concept of dalālat al-iltizām correctly in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's restriction
The original text (matn) required that implication be based on mental concomitance alone. Locate this in the Arabic phrase 'تقييد دلالة الالتزام باللزوم الذهني'.
The matn's restriction is the target of the critique.
2
Understand the purpose of stipulating concomitance
The commentator states the purpose: to validate the mental transition (intiqāl) from the signified to the necessary concomitant and to regulate the signification. This is the core function of implication.
3
Apply the commentator's critique
The commentator argues that this purpose is achieved by any type of concomitance (mental or external), so the restriction is superfluous. If it were not achieved, then the relation would not be a necessary concomitance at all.
The phrase 'وإلا لم يكن اللزوم لزوما' emphasizes that a valid concomitance inherently enables transition.
4
Test with examples
Consider external concomitance (fire and heat) and mental concomitance (idea of a thing and its mental concomitant). Both allow the mind to move from one to the other, so both qualify for implication under the commentator's view.
Apply the rule: any necessary relation that permits transition suffices.

The second: that restricting the signification of implication to mental concomitance is unnecessary, because the purpose of stipulating concomitance is to validate the transition and regulate the signification, and these are achieved by any concomitance, otherwise it would not be a necessary concomitance. (That is: the matn restricted concomitance to mental concomitance, but there is no need for this restriction, because the purpose is that there be a transition from the signified to the necessary concomitant, and this is achieved by both mental and external concomitance. Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of a condition set by the original text (matn) regarding the definition of implication (التزام). The matn had restricted the necessary concomitance (اللزوم) in implication to mental concomitance (اللزوم الذهني). The commentator argues that this restriction is unnecessary because the purpose of requiring any concomitance at all is to ensure a valid mental transition from the signified (الملزوم) to the necessary concomitant (اللازم) and to regulate the signification. Both purposes are achieved by any type of concomitance, whether mental or external (خارجي). If one insists on mental only, then the essential benefit of the transition is lost, because the transition itself is the purpose. In other words, as long as there is a valid correlative relation (لزوم) that allows the mind to move from one concept to another, it suffices for implication; distinguishing between mental and external is irrelevant to the logical function.
