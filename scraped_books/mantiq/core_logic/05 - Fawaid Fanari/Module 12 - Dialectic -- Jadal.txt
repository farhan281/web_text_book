Why Answering with the Genus is Valid in Dialectical Questions
Author justifies answering with the genus when question encompasses two different realities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a genus answer is valid when a question encompasses two different realities, using the rules of jadal.

Core Idea
The genus answer is valid when the question itself includes two different realities.

In Islamic dialectic (jadal), objections are common when a respondent gives a general answer to a question about distinct things. This passage addresses such an objection: if someone asks 'What is Zayd and what is this horse?', answering 'animal' (the genus) might seem to ignore the species differences. The author justifies the genus answer by referring to the scope of the question. Let's see how this works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection being refuted
The passage begins by saying 'That his objection is also in the domain of rejection'. So someone objected that answering with the genus is invalid. Why might they object? Because a genus answer does not distinguish between species, e.g., human vs. horse.
The objector likely expects a species-specific answer.
2
Extract the justification for the genus answer
The author says the correctness of the genus answer 'looks to the inclusion of the question upon two different realities'. So the key is that the question itself mentions two different realities (e.g., Zayd and a horse). Therefore, a common genus (animal) is appropriate because it captures what they share.
The question is not 'What is the species?', but 'What are these?' covering multiple items.
3
Understand the second condition: treating the agreed-upon as one
The author adds 'making the agreed-upon in the ruling of one'. This means that the genus answer treats the shared aspect (the genus) as a single ruling. Since both realities share the genus, it is valid to give one answer for both.
This avoids the need to list separate species answers when the question is about multiple kinds.
4
Apply the rule to a new example
Suppose someone asks: 'Is wine and nabidh prohibited?' Both are drinks, but wine is explicitly prohibited, and nabidh may be debated. Answering with the genus 'intoxicants' and then ruling on intoxication as the cause is a valid genus-level answer, as it addresses the common evil.
This shows how genus answers work in fiqh debates.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical response to an objection. The author argues that when someone asks about two different realities (e.g., 'What is Zayd and what is this horse?'), answering with the genus (like 'animal') is valid because the question itself includes both different realities, and the genus answer treats the shared genus as a single judgment. The objection that the genus answer fails to distinguish between the species is rejected because the correctness of the genus answer depends on the question encompassing two different realities and on considering the agreed-upon genus as one. Thus, the genus answer is appropriate here.
