Definition of Burhan: Components and Distinction
Defining burhan and its components.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of burhan and identify its three essential components: genus (qiyas), composition (mu'allaf), and final cause (li-intaj al-yaqin).

Core Idea
Burhan is the syllogism that yields certainty, defined by genus, differentia, and final cause.

The first of the five arts is demonstration (burhan). The logician begins with a precise definition that distinguishes it from other forms of argument. This definition is not arbitrary—it is structured as a proper hadd (real definition) containing genus, differentia, and even the final cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the passage. It is 'al-burhan' (demonstration).
2
Extract the genus (jins)
The genus is 'qiyas' (syllogism). This is the broader category that includes all five arts.
The term 'qiyas' here refers to categorical syllogism, not analogical reasoning.
3
Identify the differentia (fasl)
The differentia is 'mu'allaf min muqaddimat yaqiniyya' (composed of certain premises). This distinguishes burhan from dialectic, rhetoric, etc.
The word 'mu'allaf' is included to attach the qualifying phrase 'of certain premises'.
4
Identify the final cause (ghaya)
The phrase 'li-intaj al-yaqin' (for producing certainty) specifies the purpose of burhan.
This is not part of a traditional hadd but is included to complete the four causes.
5
Understand the distinction from other arts
The definition excludes arts like dialectic (zanni premises) and rhetoric (persuasive without certainty).
The five arts differ by the matter of their premises and the strength of their conclusions.

So when the allusion to the investigations of form was completed, he pointed to the investigations of matter as well, saying: From the totality of the five arts: (Demonstration, which is a syllogism composed of certain premises for producing certainty) more general than being necessary or acquired from them. So the syllogism is a genus encompassing the five syllogisms. And 'composed' was mentioned so that his statement 'of certain premises' is attached to it, and it excludes rhetoric, dialectic, and others. (Footnote 1: 'Art' (ṣināʿa) is a psychic disposition from which voluntary actions issue without deliberation, and it is said: knowledge related to how an action is done.) (2) The form is the figure of the syllogism from a minor premise, major premise, and conclusion, regardless of the matter of the two premises placed in them. (3) The matter placed in the minor and major has an effect on the strength and weakness of the conclusion. These matters: some are certain, so the conclusion is certain – that is demonstration; some are probable, so the conclusion is probable, and they are four: dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, sophistry. And his statement: 'for producing certainty' is a final cause (ghāya) which he mentioned so that the definition contains the four causes. (Footnote 1 on page 169) The composed (muʾallaf) indicates the form by direct correspondence and indicates the efficient cause by implication, which is the rational faculty. The premises are the material cause, and 'for producing certainty' is the final cause. The four causes for every action: for example, you want to make a chair; its four causes: form is the shape of the chair; matter is the wood; efficient cause is the carpenter; final cause is sitting on it. Here, the syllogism needs the four for its existence: form – what preceded of the figures; matter – what is placed in the two premises from these arts; efficient cause – the one who constructs the syllogism; final cause – obtaining the result, either certainty or probability, when reasoning. Thus the form is like a mold and the matter is what is placed in it. And the definition of demonstration has encompassed the four causes.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of burhan (demonstration), the first of the five arts. The author begins by stating that the logician investigates both form and matter of arguments. He then provides the definition: 'Demonstration is a syllogism composed of certain premises for producing certainty.' The word 'syllogism' (qiyas) is the genus covering all five syllogistic arts. 'Composed' (mu'allaf) is included so that the following phrase 'of certain premises' can qualify it, thereby excluding dialectic, rhetoric, and other non-certain syllogisms. The footnote explains that 'art' is a psychic disposition for voluntary action. The commentary then analyzes the definition in terms of the four causes (al-`ilal al-arba`). The phrase 'for producing certainty' is the final cause (ghaya). Similarly, the syllogism needs these four: form (the figures of syllogism), matter (the premises from the various arts), efficient cause (the person constructing the syllogism), and final cause (obtaining a certain or probable conclusion). The author concludes that the definition of burhan encapsulates all four causes.

The Four Causes in the Definition of Burhan
Analyzing the definition via four causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four causes (formal, material, efficient, final) in the definition of burhan and apply this causal analysis to other logical definitions.

Core Idea
The definition of burhan contains all four causes: form, matter, agent, and purpose.

After defining burhan, the commentator shows how the definition itself embodies the Aristotelian four causes. This causal analysis is not mere philosophical ornament—it provides a systematic framework for evaluating the completeness of any definition or argument.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four causes
The four causes are: formal (sura – structure), material (madda – content), efficient (fa'il – agent), and final (ghaya – purpose).
The example of a chair is given in the text: form=shape, matter=wood, agent=carpenter, purpose=sitting.
2
Find the formal cause in the definition
The term 'mu'allaf' (composed) directly indicates the formal cause, i.e., the syllogistic structure composed of premises and conclusion.
3
Find the material cause
The phrase 'min muqaddimat yaqiniyya' (of certain premises) indicates the material cause – the content of the premises.
4
Find the efficient cause
The term 'mu'allaf' also implies the efficient cause by implication (bi-l-iltizam), which is the rational faculty that constructs the syllogism.
The text states: 'al-mu'allaf: ishara ila al-sura bil-mutabaqa, wa ila al-fa'il bil-iltizam'.
5
Find the final cause
The phrase 'li-intaj al-yaqin' (for producing certainty) is explicitly the final cause.

Self-Evident and Observational Premises
Author defines self-evident and observational premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify self-evident (awwaliyyat) and observational (mushahadat) premises and distinguish them based on their epistemic grounds.

Core Idea
Some truths require only reason; others need the senses.

In demonstration (burhan), not all certain premises are the same. The author begins with those requiring only conception of terms (awwaliyyat) and those requiring direct sense perception (mushahadat). Understanding this distinction is key to evaluating the certainty of any argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify awwaliyyat premises
Find statements that the mind accepts immediately upon understanding the terms, like 'the whole is greater than the part'. The author stresses that if someone imagines a part can be greater (like in elephant disease), they have not correctly conceived the terms.
2
Recognize the condition for awwaliyyat
No external evidence or inference is needed; only conception of the subject and predicate. The mind judges without assistance.
3
Identify mushahedat premises
These are based on direct observation through the external senses. Examples: 'the sun is bright' (sight), 'fire is hot' (touch). They are also called mahsusat.
4
Distinguish awwaliyyat from mushahedat
Awwaliyyat are known by reason alone; mushahedat require sensory experience. The former are necessary truths, the latter are contingent on observation.
5
Understand the classification's nature
The author notes this classification is 'a way of regulation, not a mental enumeration', meaning it organizes types but does not claim exclusivity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge can come from repeated experience, rapid inference, or overwhelming testimony.

Beyond self-evident and direct observations, certain premises are justified through repetition, intuition, or widespread reports. The author distinguishes these three types, each with its own epistemic basis, and emphasizes that this classification is for regulating demonstration, not a exhaustive list.

Guided Path

Understand mujarrabat (experiential premises)
These derive from repeated observations leading to a judgment of causation. Example: 'Drinking scammony facilitates the excretion of yellow bile' because it consistently follows. The author notes that if it did not happen regularly, the causal connection would not be asserted.
2
Understand hadsiyyat (intuitive premises)

Primary Propositions (al-Awwaliyat) in Demonstration
Defines primary propositions (awwaliyat) with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a primary proposition (awwaliyya) and distinguish it from propositions that require sense experience or proof, using the definitional criteria and examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Primary propositions are axiomatic once you understand their terms.

The foundation of demonstrative proof rests on certainties that the mind accepts without external aid. The first and most basic of these are primary propositions (awwaliyat). In this lesson, we will examine what makes a proposition primary, how it functions in demonstration, and why the examples given are paradigmatic. You will learn to recognize such propositions in Islamic theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of awwaliyat
Read the definition: a proposition is awwaliyya when the intellect judges it true merely by conceiving its two terms (subject and predicate). No sense experience or further reasoning is needed. The judgment is immediate and necessary.
This is the key characteristic: immediate assent upon understanding the terms.
2
Examine the first example
Consider: 'This is one; every one is half of two; therefore this is half of two.' The terms 'one', 'half', 'two' are understood. The conclusion follows necessarily from the premises, but the entire syllogism is based on awwaliyat: the minor premise ('this is one') and the major premise ('every one is half of two') are both primary. The intellect assents to them without needing to observe a particular one or half.
The example shows how awwaliyat can serve as premises in a demonstrative syllogism.
3
Examine the second example
Consider: 'The hand is a part of man; every part of man is less than the whole; therefore the hand is less than the whole man.' Here, the major premise ('every part is less than the whole') is a clear awwaliyya: once you understand 'part' and 'whole', you immediately know it is true. The minor ('the hand is a part of man') is also primary if you conceive 'hand' and 'man' correctly.
This example highlights that awwaliyat are often mathematical or mereological truths.
4
Distinguish from other certainties
Compare awwaliyat with propositions that require sense experience (e.g., 'fire burns') or testimony (e.g., 'Makkah is a city'). The latter are also certain but not primary because they depend on external input. The defining test: can you know it is true just by understanding what the words mean? If yes, it is awwaliyya.
This distinction is crucial for structuring demonstrative proofs.
