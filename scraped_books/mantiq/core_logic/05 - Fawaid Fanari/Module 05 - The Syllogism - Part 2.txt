Identifying Productive and Sterile Moods in the Fourth Figure
Author enumerates productive and sterile moods in fourth figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish productive from sterile moods in the fourth figure by applying the condition of 'two defects'.

Core Idea
In the fourth figure, a mood is sterile if it contains two defects (particulars or negatives) unless the minor is affirmative particular and major is negative universal.

The author provides numerous examples of premise combinations in the fourth figure, marking each as productive or sterile. By analyzing these examples, you will learn to quickly identify which mood combinations yield a valid conclusion. Understanding why a mood is sterile is just as important as knowing which are productive.

Guided Path
1
Review the condition for productivity
Two defects (particular premise or negative premise) are disallowed except one case. Defects: particularity (جزئية) and negativity (سلب).
A premise can have both defects (e.g., particular negative), which counts as two defects.
2
Examine the first productive example (AAI-4)
Both premises are universal affirmative—no defects. So productive. Conclusion is particular because of existential import in the fourth figure.
The conclusion has the minor term as predicate and major term as subject.
3
Examine a sterile example (AOO-4)
Premises: 'كل إنسان حيوان' (A) and 'بعض الحيوان ليس بإنسان' (Actually the example is 'كل إنسان حيوان' A and 'بعض الحيوان ليس انسان' O). This has one negative (O) and one particular (O) = two defects, so sterile.
Not the exception because minor is universal, not particular.
4
Examine the exception (IEO-4)
Premises: 'بعض الإنسان كاتب' (I) and 'لا شيء من الحجر بإنسان' (E). This has one particular (I) and one negative (E) = two defects, but it is the allowed exception. Conclusion: 'بعض الكاتب ليس بحجر' (O).
Memorize this specific combination: minor affirmative particular (I) and major negative universal (E).
5
Practice by identifying defect count
For each mood, count defects: each particular premise =1, each negative premise =1. If total >=2 and not the exception, it's sterile.
Example: two particular affirmatives (II) = two defects (two particulars) → sterile. Two universal negatives (EE) = two defects (two negatives) → sterile. Universal affirmative and particular negative (AO) = two defects (one particular, one negative) → sterile unless it is exactly IO? No, exception requires minor I and major E.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the fourth figure of the categorical syllogism. The author begins by defining the fourth figure: it is the figure in which the middle term is the subject of the minor premise and the predicate of the major premise. The condition for productivity in this figure is that two defects—that is, two particular premises or two negative premises—do not occur together, except in one specific case: when the minor is an affirmative particular and the major is a negative universal. This exception yields a valid conclusion. The author then states that there are six productive moods in this figure, and they can be reduced to the first figure by converting both premises (i.e., swapping the subject and predicate of each premise). The passage then enumerates examples of various premise combinations, marking which ones are productive and which are sterile (عقيمة). For instance, the combination of two universal affirmatives (AAA-4) produces a particular affirmative conclusion. The combination of a universal affirmative minor and a particular affirmative major (AII-4) produces a particular affirmative. The author provides examples using terms like 'human' (إنسان), 'animal' (حيوان), 'rational' (ناطق), 'moving' (متحرك), 'inanimate' (جماد), 'writer' (كاتب), and 'stone' (حجر), all rooted in Islamic sciences contexts. Finally, the passage shows how some of the conclusions can be converted: for example, the conclusion 'Some animal is human' (from a productive mood) converted yields 'Some human is animal', and another conversion yields a universal negative. This systematic treatment helps the student understand the valid moods of the fourth figure and the conditions for their productivity.

Three Material Types of Categorical Syllogisms
Classification of categorical syllogisms by material composition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given syllogism as categorical-categorical, conditional-conditional, or disjunctive-disjunctive based on the types of propositions composing it.

Core Idea
Categorical syllogisms can be built from categorical, conditional, or disjunctive propositions.

In this passage, the author expands the notion of categorical syllogisms beyond simple subject-predicate forms. He shows that the propositions composing a syllogism can be of three types: two categoricals, two conditionals, or two disjunctives. Understanding these types helps you recognize the logical structure and the appropriate inference principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of each premise
Look at the first premise and second premise. Determine if they are simple subject-predicate statements (categorical), if-then statements (conditional), or either-or statements (disjunctive).
2
Match with the three categories
If both are categorical, it is the first type. If both are conditional, it is the second type. If both are disjunctive, it is the third type. The passage also mentions a fifth type but does not elaborate.
3
Observe the inference principle
Each type has its own logical principle: for conditionals, transitivity of implication; for disjunctives, exhaustive division. We'll study these in subsequent lessons.

Conditional Syllogisms: The Principle of Transitivity
Example and principle of conditional syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid conditional syllogism using the transitivity of implication and identify the antecedent-consequent chain.

Core Idea
If A implies B and B implies C, then A implies C: the transitive property of implication.

The author illustrates a conditional syllogism with the example: 'If the sun is rising then daytime exists, and whenever daytime exists then the earth is illuminated; therefore if the sun is rising then the earth is illuminated.' The key principle is that the necessary condition of a necessary condition is itself a necessary condition. This is the transitivity of implication, foundational for reasoning with hypothetical statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditional premises
Each premise has the form 'If P then Q'. Note the antecedent and consequent of each.
In the example: Premise 1: If sun rising (P) then daytime (Q). Premise 2: If daytime (Q) then earth illuminated (R).
2
Find the common term connecting them
The consequent of the first premise is the antecedent of the second. That term 'Q' is the bridge.
Here 'daytime exists' is the common term.
3
Apply the transitivity principle
Since P implies Q and Q implies R, then P implies R. This is valid regardless of the truth of P, Q, or R.
The principle is called 'ملزوم الملزوم ملزوم' (the necessary condition of the necessary condition is necessary).
4
Verify the conclusion
The conclusion must have the form 'If P then R' or 'P implies R'. Check that it follows from the premises.

Disjunctive Syllogisms: Two-Level Division
Example and analysis of disjunctive syllogism with two-level division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to combine two disjunctive premises into a final exhaustive division and determine the conclusion.

Core Idea
When one disjunct is further divided, the final conclusion includes all original disjuncts plus the subdivisions.

The author provides a disjunctive syllogism example: 'Every number is either even or odd, and every even is either evenly-even or oddly-even; therefore every number is either even, odd, evenly-even, or oddly-even.' This shows how two disjunctive propositions can be combined to produce a more detailed classification. The logical process depends on the nature of the disjunctions involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first disjunction
The first premise divides the subject into mutually exclusive parts. In the example, 'Every number is either even or odd' is an exclusive disjunction.
Exclusive means it cannot be both.
2
Identify the second disjunction
The second premise further divides one of the disjuncts from the first. Here, 'every even is either evenly-even or oddly-even' divides 'even'.
The phrase 'كل زوج' indicates the second premise applies to all evens.
3
Combine the divisions
If the first disjunction is exhaustive and the second is also exhaustive for its subset, then the overall classification includes all original disjuncts and the subdivisions. The conclusion lists all possibilities: even, odd, evenly-even, oddly-even.
Note that 'odd' is not further divided, so it remains as a single option.
4
Check the role of the first disjunction's truth
The author notes: 'if the true disjunct from the first disjunction is oddness, then...' implying that the second premise only applies when the true disjunct is even. However, for the overall deduction, we consider all cases.

Core Idea
Mixed syllogisms combine categorical and conditional propositions using the broader-term principle to derive necessary conclusions.

Compound syllogisms that mix categorical (ḥamliyya) propositions with conditional (muttaṣila) or disjunctive (munfaṣila) ones are essential for constructing valid arguments in Islamic sciences. The author presents three distinct types, each justified by the relationship between the broader term (lāzim) and the narrower term (malzūm). Mastering these forms sharpens your ability to reason across different proposition types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first type: categorical-conditional
Look for a conditional premise (e.g., 'Whenever this is a human, it is an animal') and a categorical premise ('Every animal is a body'). The conclusion is a conditional with the same antecedent and a new consequent.
The validity relies on the principle that what holds for the broader term (animal) holds for the narrower (human).
2
Apply the lāzim-malzūm principle
The broader term (al-lāzim) is 'animal', the narrower (al-malzūm) is 'human'. Since 'body' is true of all animals, it must be true of all humans. Verify that the middle term is indeed broader.
This principle is foundational in usul al-fiqh when extending rulings from a general category to a specific one.

Analyze the second type: categorical-disjunctive
Find a disjunctive premise ('Every number is either even or odd') and a categorical premise ('Every even is divisible into two equal parts'). The conclusion replaces 'even' with its definition, producing a new disjunction.

This mirrors the logic of fiqh classifications.

One premise is a conditional ('Whenever this is human, it is an animal'), the other is a disjunctive ('Every animal is either white or black'). The conclusion is a conditional with a disjunctive consequent.
The division principle: if the broader term (animal) divides into parts, the narrower (human) also divides into those parts.

Valid Inferences in the Conjunctive Conditional Syllogism
Author defines the conjunctive conditional syllogism and its two valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two valid inference modes of a conjunctive conditional syllogism (modus ponens and modus tollens) and apply them to a theological argument.

Core Idea
Affirming the antecedent or denying the consequent yields certainty; the reverse does not.

The conjunctive conditional syllogism (al-qiyas al-istithna'i al-muttasil) is the foundation for many arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. When you have a conditional statement 'If A, then B,' knowing how to correctly infer from it is critical. This lesson teaches the only two valid modes: affirming A to conclude B, or denying B to conclude not-A. The other two possibilities (affirming B to conclude A, or denying A to conclude not-B) are fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the sentence of the form 'If...then...' in the argument. Example: 'If this intoxicant is khamr, then it is haram.' The antecedent is 'this intoxicant is khamr', the consequent is 'it is haram'.
In Arabic, often expressed with 'in' or 'idha'.
2
Check the minor premise
The minor premise will either affirm the antecedent or deny the consequent. Identify which one. If it affirms the antecedent, we have modus ponens. If it denies the consequent, we have modus tollens.
3
Draw the conclusion
If modus ponens, conclude the consequent. If modus tollens, conclude the denial of the antecedent. Example: 'It is khamr' (affirms antecedent) → 'Therefore it is haram'. 'It is not haram' (denies consequent) → 'Therefore it is not khamr'.
These are the only two valid modes.
4
Avoid invalid modes
Do not affirm the consequent to conclude the antecedent, nor deny the antecedent to conclude the denial of the consequent. Those are fallacies.
Common error in debates.

Valid Inferences in the Real Disjunctive Syllogism (Haqiqiyya)
Author defines the real disjunctive conditional syllogism and its four valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a real disjunctive premise and apply its four valid inference modes to a theological argument.

Core Idea
In real disjunction, affirming one part denies the other, and denying one part affirms the other.

A real disjunctive (haqiqiyya) is an 'either-or' that is exclusive and exhaustive: the two parts cannot both be true and cannot both be false. This type appears in many Islamic legal maxims, such as 'every action is either obligatory or not obligatory' (but careful: that is not exhaustive). A proper example: 'The reward for a person's deeds is either Paradise or Hell.' This lesson covers the four valid inferences: affirming A denies B, affirming B denies A, denying A affirms B, denying B affirms A.

Guided Path
1
Identify the real disjunctive premise
Look for a proposition that presents two options that are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. In the passage, the author refers to 'al-haqiqiyya' which represents opposition in both affirmation and denial.
Also called 'exclusive disjunction' in modern logic.
2
Check the minor premise
The minor premise will either affirm one part or deny one part. There are four possibilities: affirm A, affirm B, deny A, deny B.
3
Draw the corresponding conclusion
If you affirm A, conclude not-B. If you affirm B, conclude not-A. If you deny A, conclude B. If you deny B, conclude A. All four are valid for real disjunction.
This is exactly what the author says: 'The real produces by affirming each of the two parts the denial of the other, and by denying each of them the affirmation of the other: four [modes].'
4
Test with an example
Using the premise 'The reward is either Paradise or Hell.', evaluate each inference. For instance, 'The reward is Paradise' → 'It is not Hell' (valid). 'The reward is not Paradise' → 'It is Hell' (valid).
Ensure the premise is truly exhaustive; otherwise the inference may fail.

Author defines the exclusive of conjunction disjunctive and its two valid inferences.

Core Idea
Only affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent yield valid conclusions in implicative conditionals.

The exceptive syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) is central to Islamic logic. In this passage, the author systematically enumerates the productive and unproductive forms, focusing on the implicative conditional (muttasila luzumiyya). Understand the logical structure of an implicative conditional

This specific-generic relation is key to understanding which inferences are productive.

The minor premise can either affirm or deny the antecedent or the consequent. So we have four cases: affirm antecedent, deny antecedent, affirm consequent, deny consequent.

Core Idea
In muttasil lizumiyya, affirming antecedent and denying consequent are valid; the other two are invalid unless equivalent.

Inference Rules for Connected Conditional Syllogisms (Muttasil)
Author explains valid inferences for connected conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify valid and invalid inferences in a connected conditional syllogism, including cases of equal entailment.

Core Idea
In a connected conditional, only modus ponens and modus tollens are valid, unless entailment is equal.

Connected conditional syllogisms (qiyās istithnā'ī muttasil) are common in Islamic reasoning, especially in legal analogies. This lesson unpacks the precise rules: which exclusions yield valid conclusions and which do not. Understanding the distinction between general and equal entailment is crucial for sound argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the 'if...then...' statement. The part before 'if' is the antecedent (muqaddam), after 'then' is the consequent (tālī). In the source, 'If this is a human, then it is an animal' is an example.
2
Determine the exclusion (istithnā')
The syllogism excludes either the identical ('ayn) of the antecedent/consequent or its opposite (naqīḍ). In the example, the exclusion is 'it is not an animal' — the opposite of the consequent.
3
Apply the rule for general entailment
For general entailment, only excluding the opposite of the consequent (modus tollens) and excluding the identical of the antecedent (modus ponens) are valid. The source says excluding the identical of the consequent or the opposite of the antecedent produce nothing.
Modus ponens: if A then B, A, therefore B. Modus tollens: if A then B, not B, therefore not A.
4
Check if entailment is equal
If the antecedent and consequent mutually entail each other (equal entailment), all four forms are valid. The source explains that in equal entailment, the relation is between two necessary correlates (mutalāzimān).

The author first states the rule for muttasil: excluding the opposite of the consequent (tālī) yields the opposite of the antecedent (muqaddam). An example is given: 'If this is a human, then it is an animal; but it is not an animal, therefore it is not a human.' The reasoning is that the absence of the necessary (lāzim) entails the absence of the necessitated (malzūm). Conversely, excluding the identical of the consequent or the opposite of the antecedent produces no conclusion. The author then introduces a nuance: this is true for general entailment; if the entailment is equal (mutasāwiya), i.e., the antecedent and consequent are mutual necessary correlates, then all four possibilities (exclusion of identical or opposite of each) yield valid conclusions. The author clarifies that in equal entailment, each of the four forms is actually a relation between the two correlates (mutalāzimān). For a real disjunction (munfasila haqīqiyya), excluding the identical of one part yields the opposite of the other, and excluding the opposite of one part yields the identical of the other. This holds for real and exclusive disjunctions (man'at al-khuluw); for inclusive disjunctions (man'at al-jam'), the rule is reversed. The author notes that the Book (the primary text) does not detail this, but the principle is as stated.

True Disjunctive Conditional (Haqiqiyya)
Explains true disjunctive conditional and its four valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a true disjunctive conditional and apply both affirmation and negation inferences correctly.

Core Idea
In a true disjunction, affirming one part negates the other, and negating one affirms the other.

The true disjunctive conditional (haqiqiyya) is the strongest form of disjunction: its two parts are contradictory, so they cannot be together nor absent together. This gives four valid inferences: from affirmation of one to negation of the other, and from negation of one to affirmation of the other. The author illustrates with the even/odd example, but we will use an Islamic science example to ground the concept.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the disjunction
In a true disjunctive conditional, the two propositions are contradictory: one is the exact negation of the other. For example, 'This hadith is sahih' and 'This hadith is not sahih' are contradictory.
2
Check mutual exclusivity and exhaustiveness
True disjunction requires that the two parts cannot be true together (exclusive) and cannot be false together (exhaustive). In the hadith example, a narration cannot be both sahih and not sahih, and it must be one of them.
3
Apply the four inference rules
From affirming one part, conclude the negation of the other. From negating one part, conclude the affirmation of the other. These are all valid because the parts are contradictories.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Consider: 'This action is either wajib or ghayr wajib (not wajib).' Affirm wajib → not ghayr wajib. Affirm ghayr wajib → not wajib. Negate wajib → ghayr wajib. Negate ghayr wajib → wajib. All four inferences hold.

Exclusive Disjunction (Mani'at al-Jam')
Explains exclusive disjunction with its limited valid inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify exclusive disjunction and correctly apply only the affirmation-to-negation inference.

Core Idea
In exclusive disjunction, only affirming one part validly negates the other; negating gives no conclusion.

Exclusive disjunction (mani'at al-jam') means the two parts cannot both be true, but they may both be false. This restricts the valid inferences: you can conclude the negation of the other when you affirm one, but from negation you cannot conclude anything. The author's wood/stone example illustrates this, and we will apply it to an Islamic context.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the disjunction type
Exclusive disjunction is indicated when the two parts cannot be true together, but can be false together. For example, 'This animal is either a cow or a sheep' – it cannot be both, but it could be a camel (neither).
2
Test the inference from affirmation
If you affirm one part, you can validly conclude that the other is false. For example, from 'This animal is a cow' you conclude 'It is not a sheep'.
3
Test the inference from negation
If you negate one part, you cannot conclude the other because both could be false. From 'This animal is not a cow' you cannot conclude it is a sheep – it might be a camel.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider: 'This hadith is either hasan or da'if' (assuming hasan and da'if are exclusive but not exhaustive – a hadith can be sahih). If it is hasan, then it is not da'if (valid). If it is not hasan, it could be da'if or sahih – no conclusion.
This example assumes hasan and da'if are mutually exclusive but not covering all hadith grades.

Exhaustive Disjunction (Mani'at al-Khuluw)
Explains exhaustive disjunction with its inference rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify exhaustive disjunction and correctly apply only the negation-to-affirmation inference.

Core Idea
In exhaustive disjunction, only negating one part validly affirms the other; affirming gives no conclusion.

Exhaustive disjunction (mani'at al-khuluw) means the two parts cannot both be false, but they may both be true. This reverses the valid inferences from exclusive disjunction: negating one part yields affirmation of the other, but affirming one part does not negate the other. The author's Zayd example illustrates this, and we will replace it with an Islamic example for practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify exhaustive disjunction
Exhaustive disjunction means at least one part must be true, but both may be true. For example, 'This book is either a primary source or a secondary source' – it could be both (e.g., a commentary that is also a primary source), but it must be at least one.
2
Test the inference from negation
If you negate one part, you can validly conclude the other. For example, from 'This book is not a primary source' you conclude 'It is a secondary source'.
3
Test the inference from affirmation
If you affirm one part, you cannot conclude the negation of the other because both could be true. From 'This book is a primary source' you cannot conclude it is not a secondary source.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider: 'This scholar is either a faqih or a muhaddith.' If the scholar is not a faqih, then he is a muhaddith (valid). If he is a faqih, he might also be a muhaddith – no conclusion.
This example assumes the two categories are exhaustive (every scholar is at least one) but not exclusive (can be both).

Author gives examples of exclusive disjunctive syllogism with its rules.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In exclusive disjunction, affirming one disjunct negates the other; denying one does not affirm the other.

Check for fallacious reasoning
Be aware that the invalid inference 'deny one to affirm the other' is a common mistake. Always check the logical form against the rule.

Sophistry (Safsata): False Imaginations and Deceptive Resemblances
Definition of sophistry with three types of premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a sophistical argument according to its premise type: imaginary false, resembling truth, or resembling the widely accepted.

Core Idea
Sophistry uses premises that deceive the intellect, either by false imagination or by resemblance to truth or common opinion.

This passage defines sophistry (safsata) as a syllogism built from premises that are false in imagination (wahmiyya kādhiba), or that resemble truth but are not true, or that resemble widely accepted opinions but are not actually widely accepted. Three examples illustrate each type. The footnotes clarify the etymology and the distinction from strife. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to spot sophistical arguments in Islamic debates and explain why they fail.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition of safsata
Locate the sentence beginning with 'السَّفْسَطَة' and note the three types of premises: imaginary false, resembling truth, and resembling the widely accepted.
2
Analyse the first example (imaginary false)
The dead is inanimate; every inanimate is not feared; therefore the dead is not feared. Note that the conclusion is false though premises appear true.
The footnote explains that the intellect opposes the conclusion.
3
Analyse the second example (resembling truth)
This is a horse; every horse neighs; therefore this neighs. The major premise is not always true; some horses don't neigh.
It resembles truth because most horses neigh, but it's not universally true.
4
Analyse the third example (resembling widely accepted)
This person uses scholarly terms; everyone who does so is knowledgeable; therefore this person is knowledgeable. The major premise is not universally accepted; one can use terms without knowledge.
It resembles a widely accepted belief but is not actually widely accepted as a universal rule.
5
Check the footnote on etymology
The term derives from Greek meaning 'decorated wisdom' and indicates that sophistry adorns falsehood with the appearance of knowledge.

Strife (Mushāghaba): Contentious Disputation
Distinction between sophistry and strife and definition of strife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish strife (mushāghaba) from sophistry based on its purpose and the type of discourse it opposes.

Core Idea
Strife is contentious disputation aimed at refuting the opponent, regardless of truth; it opposes dialectic, not judgment.

The author introduces strife (mushāghaba) as a third type of argumentative discourse, distinct from both dialectic and sophistry. While sophistry opposes judgment (ḥukm) by using deceptive premises, strife opposes dialectic (jadal) itself, focusing on defeating the opponent rather than establishing truth. The footnote defines it as 'disputation to repel the opponent, right or wrong, not to manifest the truth.' This lesson helps you recognise when a debate has shifted from seeking truth to mere contention.

Guided Path
1
Find the mention of strife in the opening example
Look at the sentence about 'infinite void' and note that it is called sophistry if opposed to judgment, and strife if opposed to dialectic.
The same statement can be used in different ways.
2
Understand the distinction based on target
Strife is defined relative to dialectic: it is the use of such arguments to attack dialectical reasoning itself.
Sophistry targets judgment, strife targets the dialectical process.
3
Read the footnote definition
Footnote (2) explains that strife is 'disputation to repel the opponent, right or wrong, not to manifest the truth.'
This is the most explicit definition of strife in the passage.
4
Compare with sophistry
Sophistry uses false premises to oppose judgment; strife uses similar methods but targets the dialectical process, aiming to win the argument rather than reach truth.
Strife can involve any means, not just faulty syllogisms.
