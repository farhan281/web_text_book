Decoding Bibliographic References in Islamic Printed Works
List of four bibliographic references for the commentary edition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract and interpret the bibliographic data (publisher, date, edition type) from a classical Islamic printed work's reference list.

Core Idea
Bibliographic references in Islamic manuscripts and lithographs are the keys to tracing the transmission chain of a scholarly work.

This lesson introduces you to the bibliographic references found at the beginning or end of printed Islamic commentaries. These notes are not part of the original text but are crucial for establishing the provenance and accuracy of the edition. Understanding them helps you assess the reliability of the source you are using.

Guided Path
1
Identify the reference type
Determine if the reference is to a published commentary ('شرح'), a marginal note ('قول'), or a dictation ('إملاءات'). Look for keywords that indicate the nature of the source.
2
Locate the publisher and press
Find the name of the printing press (e.g., 'مطبعة عبدالله أفندي') and the city if mentioned. This tells you where and by whom the edition was produced.
3
Note the date
The hijri date at the end of the reference (e.g., 'آخر شوال ۱۳۰۸هـ') indicates when the print was completed. This is important for chronology and verifying contemporaneity.
4
Check for additional annotations
References like 'قول أحمد بآخر الشرح المذكور' show that the source is a statement appended to another work. Such annotations help locate the exact part of the text.

Source pages p. 180

Commentary
This passage is a list of bibliographic references appended to the edition of Fawaid Fanari. It identifies four sources used in the preparation of the commentary (ta'liq). The first reference is to a lithographic print of a commentary on Fatawa with annotations, published by Abdullah Efendi Press in 1308 AH. The second is a note attributed to Ahmad found at the end of that commentary. The third is Shawqi's commentary on al-Fatawa by Bostawi al-Jami' Muhammad Efendi, printed by Dar Sa'adat and Sinde Press in 1309 AH. The fourth is from the dictations and arrangements of the commentator (al-mu'alliq). Such references are typical in printed Islamic works to document the sources used by the editor or commentator.
