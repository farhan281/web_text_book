Why Incomplete Induction and Analogy Are Not Qiyas
Author explains exclusion of incomplete induction and analogy from qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qiyas (necessary entailment) and incomplete induction/analogy (probabilistic reasoning), and explain why the latter are excluded from the definition of qiyas.

Core Idea
Only arguments that produce certain, necessary conclusions qualify as qiyas.

In this passage, the author clarifies the boundaries of qiyas by showing what falls outside it: incomplete induction and analogy. These arguments yield only probability, not certainty. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the strength of arguments in Islamic sciences, especially usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scope of qiyas
The definition of qiyas includes even premises that are false, but it requires entailment (luzum). Incomplete induction and analogy do not meet this condition because they do not necessitate the conclusion.
2
Define complete and incomplete induction
Induction examines particulars to derive a universal rule. Complete induction covers all individuals and yields certainty; incomplete induction fails to cover all, leaving the possibility of counterexamples, and thus yields only probability.
Example given: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing' is incomplete because the crocodile moves its upper jaw.
3
Test the probabilistic outcome
If a premise is based on incomplete induction, the conclusion does not follow necessarily. The passage shows that from 'This is an animal' and 'Every animal moves its lower jaw' we cannot conclude 'This moves its lower jaw' – it might be a crocodile.
4
Examine analogy (tamthil)
Analogy, used in usul al-fiqh, reasons from one case to another based on a shared cause ('illa). Like incomplete induction, it yields probability, not certainty. Therefore, it is excluded from strict qiyas.
Example: analogizing whiskey to wine via the cause of intoxication.

The definition includes false premises as well. [The term 'alzam'] excludes incomplete induction and analogy, for even if they are admitted, they do not necessitate the intended conclusion because they are probabilistic. (1) If you say to the opponent: 'Every human is a stone,' and he concedes, then you say: 'Every stone is inanimate,' and he concedes, then he is compelled to admit the conclusion: 'Every human is a stone,' even though it is contrary to reality. (2) Induction is the examination of particulars to derive a universal rule, like: we examined the agent in 'Khalid stood,' 'Muhammad went,' and 'the teacher arrived' and found in every particular (its individual instances) that it is nominative, so the Arabs laid down the rule: 'Every agent is nominative.' It is divided into two types: Complete: when no individual deviates from the rule, like 'Every human is an animal' because we found no individual of its individuals that is not an animal. Incomplete: when an individual deviates from the rule, like 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing,' but we found the crocodile moves its upper jaw when chewing. (3) If we construct two premises, one of which is an incomplete induction, then there does not follow from them another statement which is the conclusion, e.g., 'This is an animal' and 'Every animal moves its lower jaw' does not necessitate 'This moves its lower jaw' – for it might be a crocodile. (4) It is the qiyas according to the usuliyyun and is called tashbih among the balagha scholars, like analogizing whiskey to wine due to the cause of intoxication. If we construct two premises from it, the conclusion does not follow. (5) That is: incomplete induction and analogy are probabilistic, not certain, so there is no entailment.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of qiyas, explaining what is included and excluded. The author first notes that the definition of qiyas even encompasses premises that are false, as shown by the example where the opponent concedes false premises and is forced to accept a false conclusion. Then it explains that incomplete induction (istiqra' naqis) and analogy (tamthil) are excluded from qiyas because they do not produce certainty. Induction is defined as examining particulars to extract a universal rule. Complete induction covers all individuals and yields certainty, but incomplete induction, where some individuals counterexample, yields only probability. The example of animal jaw movement illustrates that incomplete induction fails to guarantee the conclusion. Analogy (tamthil) is the qiyas of usul al-fiqh, like reasoning from one case to another due to a shared cause, but it also yields probability, not certainty. Therefore, neither incomplete induction nor analogy fit the strict definition of syllogism (qiyas) which must produce necessary entailment.

Key Terms
استقراء (istiqra'): Examining particulars to derive a universal rule; incomplete istiqra' yields probability. [Note: In later logic, istiqra' is often considered a type of argument with weaker force than qiyas.]
تمثيل (tamthil): Analogical reasoning based on a shared cause, called qiyas in usul al-fiqh and tashbih in balagha. [Note: In logic, tamthil is distinguished from qiyas iqtirani; in fiqh it is often synonymous with qiyas.]
