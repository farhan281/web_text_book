The Five Arts of Syllogism by Material
Author defines the five arts of syllogism based on their material

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any syllogism into one of the five arts based on the epistemic status of its premises.

Core Idea
A syllogism's art is determined by its premises: certain, probable, accepted, imaginative, or deceptive.

The author explains that syllogisms are divided into five categories based on their material (the premises), not their form. This classification tells us what type of knowledge each syllogism yields and in what context it is used. Mastering these five arts is essential for evaluating the strength of any argument you encounter in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of division
Recognize that the division is by material (mādda), i.e., the kind of premises, not by form (haykala). The text explicitly says 'من حيث مادته لا هيكلها'.
2
List the five arts with their defining premise types
For each art, note the Arabic term and the type of premises: Burhān (yaqīniyyāt), Khaṭābah (ẓanniyyāt), Jadal (musallamāt), Shi'r (mukhayyalāt), Mughālaṭah (shabīha bil-yaqīniyyāt aw al-ẓanniyyāt).
These premise types determine the epistemic value of the conclusion.
3
Understand the subdivision of sophistry
Note that mughālaṭah is further divided into safsaṭah (sophistical refutation) and mushāghabah (contentious argument).
This shows that deceptive arguments can take multiple forms.
4
Relate the five arts to the complete scope of logic
The five arts plus four other sections (concepts, propositions, syllogism, definition) constitute the nine topics of logic, with some later scholars adding a tenth (investigations of expressions).
This positions the classification within the broader curriculum of logic.

Core Idea
A true disjunctive requires mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive parts.

In this lesson, we explore numerical examples used by the author to illustrate the true disjunctive. By examining the classification of numbers into deficient, equal, and excessive, we see how the parts are both exhaustive and exclusive.

Guided Path

Identify the classification categories
From the passage, note the categories for numbers: deficient (sum of proper divisors  number).
The passage only mentions deficient and equal but the third is understood from the context of being a complete classification.
2
Check mutual exclusivity
Consider: can a number be both deficient and equal? No, because the sum of proper divisors cannot be both less than and equal to the number itself. Each category is exclusive.

Every integer greater than 1 falls into one of these three categories. The categories cover all possibilities without remainder.

The classification forms a true disjunctive proposition: 'The number is either deficient, equal, or excessive.' This is a true disjunctive because it is both exclusive and exhaustive.

Author defines jadal as a syllogism of widely accepted premises.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jadal uses socially accepted premises that shift across time and place.

In the five arts of logic, jadal (dialectic) is not just debate—it is a syllogism built on premises that a community accepts as true. This lesson unpacks its definition and its contextual nature.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of jadal
Find the Arabic sentence where jadal is defined. The definition begins with 'والجدل' and includes its genus and differentia.
The footnote (1) clarifies that this definition excludes the following three arts.
2
Identify the genus and differentia
The genus is 'qiyas' (syllogism). The differentia is 'mashhura' (widely accepted premises). This distinguishes jadal from other syllogisms.
3
Note the variation clause
The author adds that jadal varies with time, place, circumstances, etc. This means what is 'widely accepted' is relative.
This clause prevents the definition from being fixed.
4
Compare with certain knowledge
Jadal premises are not necessarily true; they are merely socially accepted. This is important for understanding jadal's role in dialectical debate.
Certain knowledge comes from premises that are true and necessary.

Poetry as a Syllogistic Art in Classical Logic
Author defines poetry as a syllogism composed of premises that expand or contract the soul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the definition of poetry (shi'r) as a syllogism composed of premises that affect the emotions, and distinguish it from demonstrative syllogisms.

Core Idea
Poetry is a syllogism aimed at emotions, not truth.

In the five-arts classification of classical logic, poetry (shi'r) is defined as a syllogistic composition that expands or contracts the soul. This may seem surprising, but it highlights that poetry has a logical structure even if its premises are imaginative. This lesson unpacks the definition and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate the definiendum in the Arabic: 'الشعر' (poetry). Note that it is the subject of the definition.
Definition begins with 'والشعر'.
2

The genus is 'قياس' (syllogism). This places poetry within the framework of logical composition, not mere ornamental speech.
In classical logic, 'qiyas' includes all structured arguments, not just deductive ones.

The differentia is that its premises are 'from which the soul expands or contracts' (مقدمات تنبسط منها النفس أو تنقبض). This distinguishes poetic syllogisms from demonstrative, dialectical, or sophistical ones.
The soul's expansion implies pleasure/desire; contraction implies aversion/repulsion.

The author gives two examples: one that expands the soul ('Wine is a ruby flowing') and one that contracts ('Honey is a vomit-inducing bitterness'). Determine how each example achieves its emotional effect through imaginative imagery.
The first example uses a metaphor (wine as ruby) to encourage consumption; the second uses a repulsive description to discourage.
5
Assess the definition's scope
Consider whether this definition covers all poetry. Does it include poetry that does not aim at emotional expansion/contraction? Reflect on the footnote's explanation of why such speech is called 'shi'r'.
The footnote states poetry may cause delight or repulsion, suggesting an emotional impact is essential.

Author defines sophistry as a syllogism of false premises resembling truth.

Core Idea
Sophistry is not just any false argument—it deliberately uses premises that look true.

This lesson unpacks the definition of sophistry as the fourth of the five arts of logic. The author gives a concise but precise characterization: it is a syllogism composed of false premises that nevertheless resemble the truth. This distinguishes it from demonstration (certain premises), dialectic (accepted premises), and rhetoric (persuasive premises). Understanding this definition is crucial for recognizing deception in theological and juristic debates.

1
Locate the genus in the definition
