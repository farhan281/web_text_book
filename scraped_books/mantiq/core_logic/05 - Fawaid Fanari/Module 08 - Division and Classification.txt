Verbal and Non-Verbal Indication
Author classifies indication into verbal/non-verbal, then conventional/natural/intellectual with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any indicator as verbal or non-verbal according to the passage.

Core Idea
The first cut: if the indicator is a word, it's verbal; otherwise, it's non-verbal.

Indication (dalala) is everywhere: a word, a gesture, a sign. But logic begins by dividing indication into two broad categories based on the nature of the indicator itself. This lesson unpacks the first division from the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the indicator
Look at the thing doing the indicating. Is it a spoken or written word?
2
Apply the verbal rule
If the indicator is a word (lafz), the indication is verbal (lafziyya). If not, it is non-verbal.
3
Check examples
Test with examples: the word 'Khalid' is verbal; a traffic light is non-verbal.
The passage also gives non-verbal conventional examples like lines and gestures.

Divisions of indication and its classification:
1. If the indicator is a word, then the indication is verbal. Otherwise, it is non-verbal.
2. It is conventional if a convention is involved, like lines, contracts, gestures, and markers. Otherwise, it is intellectual, like the indication of the world upon the Creator.
And verbal indication:
a. If it is mediated by convention, it is conventional.
b. Otherwise, if it is due to the requirement of the nature of the speaker to utter it upon the occurrence of the meaning, like the indication of 'ukh' upon coughing, it is natural.
c. Otherwise, it is intellectual, like the indication of a sound heard from behind a wall upon the existence of the speaker.
And the concern with respect to logic is conventional verbal indication, as is not hidden.
Let us give a summary of that:
Intellectual: verbal like knowing the life of one who speaks from behind a wall, non-verbal like knowing the maker of a carpet from seeing it.
Natural: verbal like the indication of groaning upon illness, non-verbal like the indication of paleness of the face upon fear.
Conventional: verbal like the indication of 'Khalid' upon his body and 'Jami' upon his building, non-verbal like traffic signals and line signs and the like.
(1) i.e., if the indicator is not a word.
(2) i.e., logic does not need all divisions, rather it needs conventional verbal indication.

Commentary
This passage classifies types of indication (dalala) in a systematic tree. It begins by dividing indication based on the nature of the indicator (dal): if it is a word (lafz), the indication is verbal; otherwise non-verbal. Non-verbal indication is then subdivided by whether a convention (wad') mediates: if yes, it is conventional (wad'iyya) like lines, contracts, gestures, and markers (such as water gauges); if no convention, it is intellectual ('aqliyya), like the world indicating its Creator. Verbal indication is similarly tripartite: if mediated by convention, it is conventional (e.g., the word 'Khalid' indicating a person); if not conventional but arising from the speaker's natural response to a meaning (e.g., the sound 'ukh' when coughing), it is natural (tabi'iyya); otherwise it is intellectual (e.g., a voice from behind a wall indicating a living speaker). The passage concludes by stating that logic is only concerned with conventional verbal indication, ignoring natural and intellectual types. A summary chart gives examples for each combination: verbal and non-verbal for each of intellectual, natural, and conventional. Footnotes clarify that non-verbal indicators are not words, and logic's domain excludes non-conventional types. The author's purpose is to delimit the subject matter of logic: it studies only those meanings conveyed by conventional words, not natural signs or intellectual inferences.

Key Terms
دلالة (dalāla): Indication or signification; the relationship by which one thing (the indicator) points to another (the indicated). [Note: No significant shift; stable across the tradition.]
دالّ (dāll): The indicator; that which points to something else, whether a word, sign, or object. [Note: No significant shift.]
لفظي (lafẓī): Verbal; indication where the indicator is a spoken or written word. [Note: No significant shift.]
وضعي (waḍʿī): Conventional; indication based on a prior agreement or convention (wadʿ) between people. [Note: In some later texts, waḍʿī may be contrasted with 'aqlī in Usul al-Fiqh contexts; here it is a subtype of indication.]
طبيعي (ṭabīʿī): Natural; indication arising from a natural connection, not from convention, such as a groan indicating pain. [Note: No significant shift; used in logic and linguisitics.]
عقلي (ʿaqlī): Intellectual; indication requiring a rational inference, not convention or natural connection, e.g., smoke indicating fire. [Note: No significant shift.]

Verbal Indication: Conventional, Natural, and Intellectual
Author classifies indication into verbal/non-verbal, then conventional/natural/intellectual with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conventional, natural, and intellectual verbal indication using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Verbal indication is not monolithic; it splits into conventional, natural, and intellectual based on the nature of the connection.

Just because the indicator is a word doesn't mean the indication works the same way. Sometimes the word's meaning depends on a convention, sometimes on a natural urge, sometimes on a rational inference. This lesson explores the three subtypes of verbal indication as discussed in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Check for convention
Is the meaning dependent on a prior agreement? If yes, it is conventional (waḍʿiyya).
2
Check for natural necessity
If not conventional, does the speaker naturally utter it when experiencing the meaning? E.g., 'ukh' when coughing. If yes, it is natural (ṭabīʿiyya).
3
Otherwise, it is intellectual
If not conventional and not natural, then it is intellectual (ʿaqliyya), requiring reasoning, like hearing a voice indicates a speaker.
The passage explicitly gives the example of 'Khalid' for conventional, 'ukh' for natural, and a voice behind a wall for intellectual.

Why Logic Studies Only Conventional Verbal Indication
Author classifies indication into verbal/non-verbal, then conventional/natural/intellectual with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to justify why logic (mantiq) restricts itself to conventional verbal indication and excludes natural and intellectual types.

Core Idea
Logic demands precise, agreed-upon meanings; only conventional verbal indications provide that stability.

After mapping out all types of indication, the author makes a crucial point: logic is not concerned with all of them. It focuses exclusively on conventional verbal indication. This lesson explains the rationale behind that limitation.

Guided Path
1
Recall the types of indication
Verbal conventional, natural, intellectual; non-verbal conventional, natural, intellectual.
2
Identify logic's purpose
Logic studies concepts and propositions that are precise and can be used in definitions and syllogisms.
3
Explain why natural/intellectual are excluded
Natural indications are involuntary and ambiguous; intellectual indications require context-dependent inference; only conventional indications have fixed meanings.
The passage states explicitly: 'والمقصود بالنظر للمنطق: الدلالة اللفظية الوضعية' (The concern with respect to logic is conventional verbal indication).

The Definition and Five Types of Simple Expressions
Author defines mufrad and classifies its five types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mufrad and classify any given expression into one of the five types based on divisibility of lafz and ma'na and whether parts indicate parts of meaning.

Core Idea
A simple expression can be divisible, as long as its parts don't point to parts of the meaning.

Welcome to the heart of logical terminology. In Islamic logic, the very first thing you must grasp is what counts as a 'simple expression' (mufrad). This passage shows you that simplicity is not about being indivisible; it's about whether the parts of the word point to parts of the meaning. The author then lays out a complete classification of five types based on this principle. Let's work through it step by step so you can master this foundational tool.

Guided Path

Grasp the definition of mufrad
Read the first sentence: 'Do you not see that if the proper name were other than 'the rational animal', the state of being a proper name would not change?' This shows that even a compound word like 'Hayawan Natiq' can be simple if its parts do not indicate parts of the meaning when used as a proper name. So note: simplicity depends on intended indication.
2
Identify the two criteria: divisibility and indication
The passage uses two criteria: whether the lafz (word) can be divided, whether the ma'na (meaning) can be divided, and crucially whether a part of the lafz indicates a part of the ma'na. The table (though garbled) lists combinations.

From the commentary, we have five cases: (1) indivisible in lafz and ma'na (e.g., hamzat al-istifham), (2) divisible in lafz but not ma'na (unclear), (3) divisible in ma'na but not lafz (like 'rajul'), (4) divisible in both but parts do not indicate (proper names like Zaid, Abdullah), (5) divisible in both and parts indicate (common noun like 'hayawan'). The Three Types of Disjunctive Propositions
Author classifies disjunctive propositions into three types based on truth conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of disjunctive propositions (ḥaqīqiyya, māni'at al-jam', māni'at al-khalū) and identify which conditions of truth and falsehood each imposes.

Core Idea
A disjunctive proposition's type depends on whether its parts can be true together, false together, or both.

In this passage, the author presents the three-fold classification of disjunctive propositions. Understanding these types is essential for correctly interpreting conditional arguments in Islamic logic, especially when dealing with exclusive or exhaustive alternatives. We will learn how each type is defined by its constraints on the truth and falsehood of its components.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types mentioned
The author names three types: ḥaqīqiyya (real), māni'at al-jam' fqṭ (exclusive of conjunction only), and māni'at al-khalū fqṭ (exclusive of disjunction only). Note that the real type is also called 'māni'at al-jam' wa al-khalū ma'an'.
2
Define the conditions for the real type
For the real type, the two parts cannot be true together (man' al-jam') and cannot be false together (man' al-khalū). The example given is 'the number is either even or odd'.
3
Define the conditions for exclusive of conjunction
This type only prevents the two parts from being true together; they may be false together. In other words, it excludes conjunction but allows disjunction (both false).
4
Define the conditions for exclusive of disjunction
This type only prevents the two parts from being false together; they may be true together. It excludes disjunction (both false) but allows conjunction (both true).
5
Test with provided examples
The passage gives an example for the negative of the real type: 'It is not at all that this human is a writer or Turk' – meaning both false and both true are possible, so that is not a real disjunction. Use this to verify understanding.

Commentary
This passage presents the classification of disjunctive propositions (al-qadiya al-munfaṣila) into three types. The author begins by stating that there are three divisions: real (ḥaqīqiyya), exclusive of conjunction only (māni'at al-jam' faqaṭ), and exclusive of disjunction only (māni'at al-khalū faqaṭ). The real disjunctive is one where the opposition is in both truth and falsehood; that is, the two parts cannot be simultaneously true nor simultaneously false. An example is: 'The number is either even or odd.' Here, a number cannot be both even and odd, nor can it be neither even nor odd. The real disjunctive is called 'preventive of both conjunction and disjunction' (māni'at al-jam' wa al-khalū ma'an). Its affirmative form is as given, while its negative form removes the opposition in truth and falsehood; e.g., 'It is not at all that this human is a writer or Turk' – meaning the two predicates (writer and Turk) can be both true (e.g., a Turk who is a writer) and both false (e.g., an Iraqi who is illiterate). The footnotes clarify key terms: 'opposition' (al-'inād) in the context of exclusive of conjunction means the non-assembly of the consequent with the antecedent; for exclusive of disjunction, it means the non-removal of both. Truth (ṣidq) here means affirmation (ithbāt), i.e., making one part the subject and the other the predicate in a composite sense. Falsehood (kidhb) means negation (nafy) of both parts together. The real type is called 'real' because it fully represents both opposing relations (affirmative and negative). The passage also gives a clarifying example: for the exclusive of conjunction only, one cannot say the number is both even and odd; for exclusive of disjunction, one cannot say it is neither. This classification is crucial for understanding conditional syllogisms and is used in fiqh when evaluating mutually exclusive or exhaustive alternatives. For instance, in legal reasoning, 'This action is either obligatory or forbidden' is a real disjunction if it cannot be both or neither.
