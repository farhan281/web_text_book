The Four Causes in the Definition of Demonstration
Author explains how the definition of demonstration includes all four causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four causes (form, matter, agent, final cause) within the definition of demonstration (burhan) and illustrate them with an example from logic.

Core Idea
The definition of demonstration implicitly includes all four causes: form, matter, agent, and final cause.

You have learned that burhan (demonstration) is a syllogism that produces certainty. But a syllogism is more than its structure; it is a complete act involving material, form, an agent, and a purpose. In this lesson, we will see how the classical definition of burhan encapsulates all four causes, making it a fully specified concept in Islamic logic. This analysis reveals the depth and completeness of the definition.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the four-cause framework
Note that the passage explicitly states every action has four causes: form (sura), matter (madda), agent (fa‘il), and final cause (ghaya). This framework is adopted from Aristotelian philosophy into Islamic logic to analyze definitions and arguments.
These causes are not always explicitly listed but are implicitly present in complete definitions.
2
Find the four causes in the definition of burhan
The definition of burhan includes 'muqaddimat' (premises) as matter, 'li-intaj al-yaqin' (for producing certainty) as the final cause, the author (mu’allif) as agent by implication (i.e., the rational faculty), and the form (sura) is the arrangement of the syllogism (the figures). The passage says the form is referred to by direct meaning (mutabaqa) and the agent by implication (iltizam).
The direct meaning of the word 'burhan' refers to its form; the agent is implied as the rational faculty that constructs it.
3
Apply the chair example to the syllogism
The footnote gives a chair example: form=shape, matter=wood, agent=carpenter, final cause=sitting. Map this to the syllogism: form=the syllogistic figure, matter=the premises (from the five arts), agent=the logician or rational faculty, final cause=obtaining certainty or opinion in inference. This clarifies abstract causes through a concrete analogy.
This analogy helps internalize the four-cause analysis as a universal tool.
4
Assess completeness of the definition
The passage concludes that the definition of burhan comprises all four causes. If any cause were missing, the definition would be incomplete. For instance, omitting the final cause would ignore the purpose of demonstration, reducing it to mere argumentation without a goal.
A complete definition must account for all causes to be exhaustive and useful.

And his statement: 'for producing certainty' is a final cause he mentioned so that the definition comprises the four causes. Commentary
This passage demonstrates how the definition of demonstration (burhan) implicitly includes the four causes (al-‘ilal al-arba‘). The author begins by noting that the phrase 'for producing certainty' (li-intaj al-yaqin) indicates the final cause (ghayah). Then he explains that the author (alm u'allif) refers to the form (surah) by the direct meaning of the word 'burhan' (mutabaqah) and to the agent (fa‘il) by implication (iltizam), identifying the agent as the rational faculty (al-quwwah al-‘aqilah). The premises (muqaddimat) serve as the material cause (madah). Thus all four causes are present in the definition. The footnote (1) provides an illustrative example from daily life: making a chair involves four causes: its form (shape), matter (wood), agent (carpenter), and final cause (sitting). Applying this to logic: the form of a syllogism is its figures (ashkal), the matter is the premises taken from the five arts, the agent is the one who constructs the syllogism, and the final cause is to obtain certainty or opinion through inference. The passage concludes that the definition of demonstration indeed comprises all four causes, showing that the burhan is not just a formal structure but a complete act with material, efficient, and final components.
