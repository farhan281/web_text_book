Defining Intuitive Propositions (Hadsiyyat)
Definition of hadsiyyat with contrast to discursive thought

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a hadsiyya proposition and distinguish it from discursive thought based on its instantaneous certainty.

Core Idea
Intuition gives certainty in a flash, not step by step.

In this passage, the author defines 'intuitive propositions' (hadsiyyat) as one of the foundational types of certain knowledge. Unlike gradual reasoning, hadsiyyat present the principles and conclusion to the mind all at once. Understanding this distinction helps us analyze how certainty is acquired in Islamic sciences, whether through immediate insight or discursive proof.

Guided Path
1
Extract the defining characteristics of hadsiyyat
Read the definition: 'مُقَدِّماتٌ يَحْصُلُ اليَقينُ فِيهَا بِسِنُوح المبادي والمطالِبِ للذِهْنِ دَفْعةً واحِدَةً' – it lists three key elements: (1) they are premises, (2) they yield certainty, (3) that certainty occurs by the simultaneous presentation of principles and conclusions to the mind. Identify these elements.
2
Contrast with discursive thought (fikr)
The author contrasts hadsiyyat with fikr: fikr is gradual (تدريجي) and varies in speed from person to person, while hadsiyyat are instantaneous (دفعي) and vary only in quantity (scarcity/abundance). Note the opposition between 'sudden' and 'gradual.'
3
Analyze the illustrative example
The example is: 'نُورُ القَمَرِ مستفادٌ مِنَ الشَّمْسِ' (the light of the moon is derived from the sun). The author explains that repeated observation of the moon's phases leads to an immediate certainty. This is not a step-by-step deduction but a flash of insight based on experience. Identify how this example fits the definition: the principles (observations) and conclusion (the moon borrows light) are grasped together.
The example also uses an analogy of a mirror and projector to clarify the intuitive grasp.
4
Identify the genus and differentia
The genus of hadsiyyat is 'premises yielding certainty' (مُقَدِّماتٌ يحصل فيها اليقين). The differentia is that this certainty occurs by sudden intuition (بِسِنُوح المبادي والمطالِب للذِهْنِ دَفْعَةً واحِدَةً). This distinguishes them from other certain premises like badīhiyyāt (self-evident) or qiyāsiyyāt (conclusional). Classify the definition as a hadd (complete definition).

This example shows that hadsiyyat often involve a flash of insight based on empirical observation, but the certainty is immediate once the mind sees the connection.

Mutawatirat: Certainty Through Mass Transmission
Definition of mutawatirat as certain premises via impossibility of collusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining conditions of mutawatirat and distinguish them from other types of certain premises.

Core Idea
Mutawatirat yield certainty because collusion on falsehood is impossible.

In the previous lessons, we learned about awwaliyyat (self-evident truths) and hadsiyyat (intuitive judgments). Now the author introduces mutawatirat, which are reports that yield certainty through the impossibility of widespread collusion on a lie. This concept is foundational in Islamic epistemology, particularly in the validation of hadith and historical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion for mutawatirat
Locate the phrase 'يستحيل العقل تواطئها على الكذب' (the mind deems their collusion on falsehood impossible). This is the core condition: the group of transmitters must be so large and reliable that it is inconceivable they could all agree on a falsehood.
2
Examine the example provided
The author gives: 'Muhammad (peace be upon him) claimed prophethood and showed the miracle at his hand.' Consider why this qualifies as mutawatir: it was transmitted by numerous companions and is widely known across generations.
3
Compare with other yaqiniyyat
Contrast mutawatirat with awwaliyyat (self-evident) and hadsiyyat (sensory-intuitive). Unlike awwaliyyat, mutawatirat depend on external transmission. Unlike hadsiyyat, they are not based on direct sensory experience but on testimony.
4
Apply the condition to a new example
Test whether knowledge of the existence of the city of Mecca is mutawatir. Since it is known through continuous mass transmission, it meets the criterion.

Qadaya Qiyasatuha Ma'aha: Propositions With Their Syllogisms
Definition of qadaya qiyasatuha ma'aha with the example of 'four is even'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify propositions where the middle term is immediately present in the mind and reconstruct the implicit syllogism.

Core Idea
Some propositions instantly bring their own syllogism to mind.

The author presents another special type of certain premise: propositions whose syllogisms are with them. These are not separate syllogistic steps; rather, the mind immediately supplies the middle term and arranges the syllogism without conscious effort. This concept explains immediate knowledge that is not self-evident but is derived from a latent syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Locate the phrase 'قضايا قياساتها معها' and the example: 'The four is even, due to a middle term present in the mind, which is division into two equal parts.' The key is that the middle term is 'حاضر في الذهن' (present in the mind).
2
Extract the implicit syllogism
The author reconstructs: 'Four is divisible into two equal parts; everything divisible into two equal parts is even; therefore four is even.' Identify the major premise (the universal), the minor premise (the instance), and the conclusion.
3
Explain why it is immediate
The mind does not need to search for the middle term; it is already there. The syllogism is formed 'في الحال' (instantly). This contrasts with explicit syllogisms where the middle term must be sought.
4
Apply to a new example
Consider: 'This act is forbidden because it causes harm.' The mind immediately supplies the syllogism: 'All harmful acts are forbidden; this act is harmful; therefore it is forbidden.' This is a qadiyya qiyasatuha ma'aha.

Understanding Awwaliyyat: Self-Evident Propositions
Author defines awwaliyyat as self-evident propositions requiring only conceptual grasp for certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish awwaliyyat (primary propositions) from other types of certain premises by applying the criterion of purely conceptual certainty.

Core Idea
Awwaliyyat are known by mere conception, needing no evidence from sense or reason.

Demonstration (burhān) rests on certain premises. The first and most foundational type are awwaliyyāt (primary propositions). These are so self-evident that once you understand the terms, you cannot doubt the truth. In this lesson, we will unpack the definition and see how these propositions anchor all certain knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its synonyms
Note that the author calls them 'awwaliyyāt' (primaries) and also 'ḍarūriyyāt' (necessities). They are also known as 'badīhiyyāt' (self-evidents). Recognise that these are the most basic certainties.
Different terms may appear in later texts; keep in mind they refer to the same category.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus: certain propositions (al-yaqīniyyāt). The differentia: the mind judges them true merely by conceptualizing the two extremes (subject and predicate), without needing sense or any other aid. This immediate judgment is the hallmark of awwaliyyat.
Contrast with propositions that require sense perception (maḥsūsāt) or induction (mujarrabāt).
3
Test the definition with examples
Examine the examples given: 'This is one' and 'Every one is half of two, so this is half of two' – once you conceive 'one' and 'half of two', the truth is evident. Similarly, 'The hand is a part of man' and 'Every part is less than the whole' – the conclusion is immediate. Check if any hidden assumptions are needed; there are none.
These examples are from arithmetic and common sense, but the same self-evidence applies to abstract axioms.
4
Apply the criterion to new candidates
Try to determine if a given proposition counts as awwaliyya. For instance, 'The whole is greater than the part' is a classic awwaliyya. 'Fire burns' is not awwaliyya because it requires observational experience. Use the differentia: does the truth become clear simply by conceiving the terms?
Be careful: Some propositions may appear self-evident but actually depend on subtle assumptions.

Definition of Mutawatirat (Recurrent Reports)
Author defines the fifth certainty, mutawatirat, with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mutawatir report and distinguish it from other types of certain propositions.

Core Idea
Mutawatirat are certain because collusion on falsehood is impossible.

The fifth type of certainty in Islamic epistemology is mutawatirat—propositions whose truth is guaranteed by overwhelming transmission. This lesson unpacks the definition and applies it to the prophetic example, showing how tawatur yields immediate certainty without syllogistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Find the definiendum
The passage begins with 'Mutawatirat' and then defines them. Identify the term being defined.
2
Extract the genus
The author says they are 'propositions about which the intellect judges' (qadaya yahkumu al-‘aqlu biha). That is the broader category.
3
Extract the differentia
The distinguishing feature: transmitted by a group whose collusion on falsehood is intellectually impossible. This is the core condition for tawatur.
Note the emphasis on intellectual impossibility, not mere improbability.
4
Examine the example
The author gives the report ‘Muhammad claimed prophethood and performed miracles’. Why is this mutawatir? Because so many people witnessed it that they could not have all agreed on a lie.
5
Test the definition
Check if any other type of certainty (sensory, syllogistic, etc.) fits this definition. It should not, ensuring the definition is exclusive to mutawatirat.

In this passage, the author classifies premises used in logical reasoning into five categories based on how the intellect acquires them. والتحقيق أن الحدسيات من الظنيات لا من اليقينيات؛ لأن الحدس المجرد نظرية قد تخطئ، وقد تصيب. وإن حصل اليقين في بعضها فإنما حصل بالتجربة والتكرار فهي من المجربات.
2 - Observed things: They are what the intellect perceives by means of the inner sense, and they are the found things, such as: The one who leaves food is hungry, and every hungry person is pained, so the one who leaves food is pained.
3 - Experienced things: They are what the intellect perceives by means of repetition, such as: This is castor oil, and every castor oil is a laxative, so this is a laxative.
4 - Widespread things: They are what the intellect perceives because they have been transmitted by a group whom the intellect prevents from conspiring upon lying, such as: Harun al-Rashid is a king, and every king must administer justice, so Harun al-Rashid must administer justice. And like: Respect for a Muslim is commanded by the Prophet (peace be upon him), and everything commanded by the Prophet is accepted, so respect for a Muslim is accepted.
5 - Sensible things: They are what the intellect perceives by means of the five external senses, such as: This is a lemon, and every lemon is sour, so this is sour. And like: This is honey, and every honey is sweet, so this is sweet.
6 - Intuitive things: They are what the intellect perceives by means of intuition, such as: The light of the moon is derived from the light of the sun, because we see it complete the farther it gets and decrease the closer it gets. And from its light being from the sun's light, it does not burn its light, resulting: The moon's light does not burn its light.
The verification is that intuitive things are from the probable things, not from the certain things, because bare intuition is theoretical and may err and may be correct. If certainty occurs in some of them, it occurs only through experience and repetition, so they are from the experienced things.

Commentary
This passage defines five types of premises (muqaddimat) used in logical reasoning, categorized by how the intellect perceives them. The author follows a pattern: naming the type, defining it, and giving an example in syllogistic form. 

1. **Mushahadat** (observed things): Perceived by inner sense (al-hiss al-batini), also called wijdaniyyat (found things). Example: 'The one who leaves food is hungry; every hungry person is pained; therefore the one who leaves food is pained.' This is an introspective premise.

2. **Mujarrabat** (experienced things): Perceived through repetition. Example: 'This is castor oil; every castor oil is a laxative; therefore this is a laxative.' Repetition establishes a general rule.

3. **Mutawatirat** (widespread things): Perceived because transmitted by a group whose collusion on falsehood is inconceivable. Two examples: one historical (Harun al-Rashid) and one religious (respect for Muslims commanded by the Prophet). Both yield certain premises.

4. **Mahsusat** (sensible things): Perceived by the five external senses. Examples: lemon (sour), honey (sweet). These are direct empirical premises.

5. **Hadsayat** (intuitive things): Perceived by intuition (hads). Example: deducing that moonlight is derived from sunlight based on its waxing and waning. The author then critiques: intuition alone is only probable (zanni), not certain (yaqini). If certainty arises, it is due to repetition and experience, so such premises revert to mujarrabat. This is a key epistemological point.

Key Takeaway
The epistemological source of a premise determines its certainty: inner sense, repetition, mass transmission, and external senses yield certainty; intuition alone yields only probability.
