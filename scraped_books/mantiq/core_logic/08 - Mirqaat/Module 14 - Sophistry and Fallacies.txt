Refining Comitant Signification: The Imam's Objection
Author refines definition of comitant signification to address Imam al-Razi's objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the refined definition of comitant signification and explain why 'not being other than itself' is excluded from it.

Core Idea
Comitant signification requires a concomitant that the mind naturally transfers to, not any trivial relation.

Imam al-Razi objected that if every concept has the concomitant 'not being other than itself', then comitance is universal and inseparable from literal signification. The commentator refines the definition to the 'specific clear concomitant'—one that the mind immediately associates with the term—thus excluding such trivial concomitants. This lesson walks through the objection and the refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim under attack
Find the claim that literal signification can exist without comitance. The Imam objects to this by pointing out that even the most basic meaning has a necessary concomitant.
2
Extract the Imam's reasoning
The Imam argues that every concept has the concomitant 'not being other than itself', which is a clear concomitant in the general sense. Therefore, comitance is always present.
3
Locate the commentator's refinement
The commentator responds by restricting comitant signification to the 'specific clear concomitant'—one that the mind naturally transfers to. The phrase 'not being other than itself' does not meet this criterion because we often think of something without thinking of its not-being-other.
4
Test the exclusion
Check: When you conceive 'human', do you necessarily conceive 'not being other than itself'? The text says no, so it is excluded from comitant signification.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed response to the objection raised by Imam al-Razi (may God sanctify his soul) against the earlier claim that literal signification can exist without comitant signification. The key point is the definition of 'clear concomitant' (al-lazim al-bayyin). The original claim rested on the possibility that a word might have a simple meaning with no necessary concomitant. The Imam objected that every meaning has at least the concomitant of 'not being other than itself,' which seems to make comitance universal. The commentator defends the definition by restricting comitant signification to the 'specific clear concomitant' (al-bayyin bi al-ma'na al-akhass), which requires that the mind naturally transfers from the necessitated to the concomitant. The phrase 'not other than itself' does not qualify because we often think of a thing without thinking of its not being other than itself. However, the commentator also expresses a critical remark—he does not see why the logicians felt compelled to add this restriction, given that their own examples (like human and capability of knowledge) involve a broader concomitant (da'wa al-ma'na al-a'mm). The Imam's argument is presented as an 'ilzam' (obliging argument): since the logicians accept that 'human' signifies 'capable of knowledge and writing' by comitance, which is a broader concomitant, they cannot deny that every word signifies that its concept is not other than itself, which is an even clearer concomitant. Thus, the Imam forces the conclusion that comitance is inseparable from literal signification. The commentator advises the reader to memorize this nuance.

The Author's Ilzam Critique of the Restriction
Author criticizes the logicians' restriction using their own example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's dialectical (ilzam) critique against restricting comitant signification to the specific clear concomitant.

Core Idea
The logicians' own example of 'human' signifying 'capable of knowledge' uses a general concomitant, undermining their restriction.

After presenting the refinement, the commentator critically observes that the logicians' own example—'human' signifying 'capable of knowledge and writing'—involves a general concomitant, not a specific one. He uses this as an ilzam (obliging argument) to expose an inconsistency: if they accept this general concomitant, they cannot deny that every term signifies its not-being-other. This lesson analyzes the critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' admitted example
The logicians accept that 'human' signifies 'capable of knowledge and writing' by comitance. Note that this concomitant is not one the mind immediately transfers to; it is a general concomitant.
2
Compare with the restricted definition
The restricted definition requires the specific clear concomitant, but the example only qualifies as general. Thus, the logicians are inconsistent.
3
Follow the ilzam argument
The author (via the Imam) argues: if you accept a general concomitant here, you must accept that every word signifies its concept's not-being-other, which is an even clearer concomitant. This forces the conclusion that comitance is universal.
4
Evaluate the critique's force
The author questions why the logicians introduced the restriction at all, since their own practice contradicts it. The critique suggests the restriction is unnecessary.

The Sophistical Syllogism: Definition and Deception
Author defines the sophistical syllogism and explains its reliance on false imaginal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a sophistical syllogism and explain how wahmi propositions deceive by mimicking self-evident truths.

Core Idea
Sophistry disguises false imaginative propositions as self-evident truths to mimic demonstration.

In this passage, the author reveals the inner workings of the sophistical syllogism – a deceptive argument that uses false imaginative propositions (wahmiyyat) to trick the mind. Understanding this device is crucial for distinguishing genuine demonstration from persuasive fallacy in Islamic theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition
Identify the components: the sophistical syllogism is composed of false imaginative propositions invented for the imagination (wahm). The example given is 'Everything that exists is pointable to.'
This definition is a hadd lafzi: it gives the genus (syllogism) and differentia (composed of false wahmi propositions).
2
Recognize the deceptive resemblance
Note the author's claim that wahmi propositions strongly resemble primary axioms (awwaliyat). This resemblance is what makes them deceptive – they appear certain but are false about non-sensible matters.
Primary axioms are self-evident truths like 'the whole is greater than the part'.
3
Analyze the faculty of wahm
The author describes wahm as a faculty in the brain that perceives particular meanings and has great authority. It often overpowers reason, leading to error by judging intellectual matters with sensible categories.
Wahm is truthful about sensibles (e.g., 'every body occupies a direction') but false about intelligibles (e.g., 'everything that exists is pointable to').
4
Identify the fallacy mechanism
The fallacy occurs when wahm applies sensible judgments to non-sensible realities, causing a categorical error. This is the basis of sophistical syllogisms.
This is a form of category mistake – treating something non-sensible as if it were sensible.

Commentary
This passage defines the sophistical syllogism (al-qiyas al-sufisti) as a compound of false imaginative propositions (wahmiyyat kadhiba) invented for the imagination (wahm), such as measuring non-sensible things by sensible ones, e.g., 'Everything that exists is pointable to.' The author notes that wahmi propositions closely resemble primary axioms, which makes them deceptive. The etymology of 'sufisti' is given: from Greek 'sophos' (wisdom) and 'istā' (deception), meaning 'camouflaged wisdom'. The wahm is described as a faculty in the brain that perceives particular meanings and has great power, often overpowering reason. It judges intelligibles with sensible categories, leading to error. Sophistry is composed of these false wahmi propositions.

The Accidental Benefit of Sophistry: Protection and Testing
Author explains the accidental benefit of sophistical art

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the accidental benefit of sophistry from its intrinsic lack of benefit, and identify the two types of sophistical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Sophistry is useless in essence but useful as a tool for protection and testing.

You might think sophistry has no value at all. But the author of this passage explains that while sophistical arguments are false and deceptive in themselves, they do have an accidental benefit: knowing them helps you avoid being deceived and allows you to test the arguments of others. This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about the benefit
Locate the sentence where the author says 'not beneficial in essence' (غير نافعة بالذات) and 'beneficial accidentally' (نافعة بالعرض). Understand the distinction between essential and accidental benefit.
Essential benefit would mean the art itself produces truth. Accidental benefit means the art is useful for something else.
2
Understand the two accidental benefits
The author mentions two: (1) the practitioner is not deceived and does not deceive, (2) the practitioner can deceive others and test or oppose them. Recognize that the first is about self-protection, the second about active use.
The text says 'صاحبها لا يغلط، ولا يغالط ويقدر على أن يغالط غيره، وأن يمتحن بها'.
3
Distinguish the two types of sophistical syllogisms
The author distinguishes between the sophist proper (sufsṭā'ī) who encounters a philosopher and uses premises resembling necessary truths, and the quarrelsome sophist (mushāghibī) who uses premises resembling well-known opinions.

Defines musadara, gives example, and discusses whether it is material or formal.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Begging the question is not about false premises or invalid form, but about proving nothing new.

In this lesson, we examine the fallacy of musadara, where the conclusion is smuggled into the premises. The classical logicians debated whether this flaw is material or formal, until al-Shirazi offered a refined analysis: the premises are true and the form is valid, yet the argument fails to produce new knowledge. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to detect circular reasoning in theological and juristic arguments.

Bad Consideration of Predication (Sū' I'tibār al-Ḥaml)
Explains fallacy where a restrictive phrase is misinterpreted as part of subject or predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a restrictive phrase is misassigned in a categorical syllogism and correct the syllogism to restore validity.

Core Idea
Misplacing a restrictor like 'alone' can turn a valid syllogism into a fallacy.

In syllogistic logic, the placement of restrictive terms such as 'only' or 'alone' can dramatically affect validity. This lesson examines the fallacy of bad consideration of predication, where a restrictor belonging to the predicate is mistakenly treated as part of the subject, leading to an erroneous conclusion. We will analyze the classical example and learn how to correctly parse such propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the restrictor
Locate the word indicating restriction (e.g., 'alone', 'only') in the proposition. In the example 'Man alone is laughing', the restrictor is 'alone'.
2
Determine intended scope
Decide whether the restrictor is attached to the subject or the predicate. Does 'Man alone is laughing' mean 'Man is the only one laughing' (subject restriction) or 'Man is laughing alone' (predicate restriction)?
The ambiguity leads to two possible logical forms.
3
Recognize dual interpretation
The phrase 'Man alone is laughing' can be split into two propositions: (i) Man is laughing, and (ii) No one else is laughing. This dual nature makes it unsuitable as a single premise in a syllogism.
The minor premise is actually two propositions combined.
4
See the fallacy
If the restrictor is taken as part of the subject in the conclusion, the syllogism becomes invalid. The argument 'Man alone is laughing; every laughing is an animal; therefore man alone is an animal' fails because the minor premise is not a single categorical proposition.
The error is due to misassigning the restrictor to the subject when it belongs to the predicate.
5
Correct the syllogism
Reassign the restrictor to the predicate: 'Man is alone laughing' (meaning Man is laughing and nothing else does). Then the syllogism becomes: 'Man is alone laughing; everything that is alone laughing is an animal; therefore man is an animal', which is valid and yields 'Man is an animal'.
The restrictor must be consistently applied to the predicate in both premises.

Fallacies from Order of Connectors and Negations
Author classifies fallacies from order of connectors and negations, and modal fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify fallacies caused by the misordering of connectors, negations, and modality in Arabic propositions, and distinguish between even and odd degrees of negation.

Core Idea
The order of negation and copula changes the logical force of a proposition — a simple shift can produce a fallacy.

In classical Islamic logic, the placement of the connector (rabita) and negation (salab) relative to each other and to modality (jiha) can turn a valid inference into a fallacy. This passage classifies such fallacies and introduces the concepts of even (shaf'iyya) and odd (witriyya) degrees of negation. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly interpreting propositions in kalam and fiqh arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition's components
Spot the subject, predicate, copula (rabita), negation (salab), and modality (jiha) in a given Arabic proposition. For example, in 'Zaydun laysa huwa biqa'im', the negation 'laysa' precedes the copula 'huwa'.
The copula is often explicit as 'huwa' or 'hiya'.
2
Determine the order
Check whether the negation precedes or follows the copula, and whether modality precedes or follows negation. The order determines the proposition type: maddula (modified) if negation precedes copula, saliba (simple negative) if copula precedes negation.
Example: 'Zaydun laysa huwa biqa'im' (negation first) is maddula; 'Zaydun huwa laysa biqa'im' (copula first) is saliba.
3
Classify the proposition
Decide if it is maddula or saliba, and note the truth conditions. For modal propositions, 'bid-darurati an la yakuna' (negation after modality) is true of the impossible; 'laysa bid-darurati an yakuna' (negation before modality) is true of the possible.
This classification affects syllogistic validity.
4
Apply even/odd negation rules
Count the number of negation particles. Even number of negations yields an affirmative proposition; odd number yields a negative proposition. This is the concept of shaf'iyya (even) and witriyya (odd) degrees.
Negation of a negation (even) is affirmation; three negations (odd) is negation.
5
Detect the fallacy
Identify when a proposition is misinterpreted due to misorder or mistaken parity. For example, taking an odd negation (negative) as if it were even (affirmative) leads to a formal fallacy.
The passage specifically warns: 'taking odd negations in place of even negations is an error'.

Mental Existence (al-Wujud al-Dhihni): Proof and Defense
Presents proofs for mental existence and responses to objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' proof for mental existence and the theologians' objection, and evaluate the response.

Core Idea
Mental existence is necessary to account for our true judgments about impossible things; the mind houses them without physical change.

The passage delves into a major philosophical debate: do concepts have a real existence in the mind? The philosophers argue they do, citing our ability to judge about impossible things. The theologians object that this would make the mind physically affected. The resolution hinges on distinguishing between shadowy mental existence and original external existence. This lesson unpacks the core arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the philosophers' proof
We can conceive impossible things and make true judgments about them (e.g., 'this is impossible'). Since they have no external existence, they must exist in the mind.
2
Examine the theologians' objection
If conceiving heat made heat exist in the mind, the mind would become hot. Similarly, conceiving a mountain would make the mind huge.
3
Analyse the philosophers' response
They distinguish between original existence (al-wujud al-asil) and shadowy existence (al-wujud al-zilli). The mental image is a shadow, not the full reality, so it does not cause physical effects.
4
Note the further nuance
What exists in the mind is the quiddity (mahiyya) of the thing, not the concrete identity (huwiyya). The concrete mountain cannot fit in the mind, but its concept can.
5
Recognize the range of views
Some theologians, like Imam al-Ghazali, accepted mental existence, showing the view is not universally rejected.

Fallacy of Taking Cause for the Cause
Gives an example of the fallacy of misidentifying causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of taking a collective cause for an individual cause and recognize it in Islamic reasoning.

Core Idea
A collective cause cannot be mechanically divided among its parts.

In logic, it is a mistake to assume that an effect produced jointly by a group can be equally distributed to each member individually. This fallacy appears in everyday reasoning and even in theological and legal arguments if one is not careful. The classical example of seventy men carrying a stone illustrates how easily such an error occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the effect and the claimed cause
In the example, the effect is moving the stone 70 farsakhs. The claimed cause is seventy men carrying it. But note: the cause is collective — all seventy men together perform the carrying.
2
Examine the relationship between cause and effect
The effect is a single event: the stone is moved 70 farsakhs. The cause is a joint action of seventy men. The question is: can this joint action be divided into seventy individual actions each responsible for 1/70 of the distance?
3
Check the hidden assumption
The fallacy assumes that the collective cause can be broken down proportionally. But in reality, the men act together; one man alone cannot carry the stone any distance. The effect depends on the cooperation, not on the sum of individual capacities.
This is similar to the fallacy of division but applied to causes.
4
Generalize the fallacy
This fallacy occurs whenever someone treats a joint cause as if it were simply the sum of individual causes. For any collective effect, one must verify whether the cause operates distributively or collectively.

Fallacy of Applying Priority with Difference
Gives an example of wrongly applying priority reasoning when there is difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of applying priority reasoning when the items differ in their specific attributes, and distinguish it from valid priority reasoning.

Core Idea
Sharing a genus does not entitle sharing a specific differentia.

This passage from the Mirqat warns against a common logical misstep: assuming that because two things belong to the same broad category, they are equally entitled to a property specific to only one of them. The example compares man and sparrow: both are animals, but that does not make man no more entitled to the rational soul than the sparrow. The fallacy lies in ignoring the difference in specific nature. Understanding this helps avoid errors in analogy and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two items and their shared attribute
The passage mentions man and sparrow. They share the attribute of 'animality' (al-ḥayawāniyya). This is the genus or broad category.
Sharing a genus is a necessary but not sufficient condition for equal entitlement to a specific property.
2
Identify the specific attribute in question
The attribute under dispute is 'rational soul' (al-nafs al-nāṭiqa). This is a differentia specific to humans.
The rational soul is not part of the definition of animality.
3

The fallacy assumes that because both belong to the same genus, they are equally entitled to a differentia that only one of them possesses. This is invalid because the differentia is not entailed by the genus.
Priority reasoning (ṭarīq al-awlawiyya) is only valid when the attribute in question is common to both items.
4
Identify the fallacy type
This is a fallacy of illicit priority: applying a rule of 'firstness' or entitlement when the basis for priority (shared property) does not actually support the conclusion.

The correct reasoning would be: since rational soul is specific to humans, man is indeed more entitled to it than the sparrow, even though both are animals. The genus does not negate the specific difference.
Valid priority reasoning requires that the relevant attribute is shared; otherwise it is a fallacy.

The Fallacy of Transitive Similarity
Presents the fallacy and its resolution by distinguishing respects of similarity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute the fallacy of transitive similarity ('mumāthil al-mumāthil mumāthil') by distinguishing the respect of similarity.

Core Idea
Similarity is not transitive unless the same respect applies throughout.

This lesson examines the fallacy that 'the similar of a similar is similar.' Using the example of man, palm tree, and stone, the text shows how ignoring the specific respect of similarity leads to absurd conclusions. You will learn to spot this fallacy and apply the correct refutation, which is essential for sound analogical reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacious principle
The principle is that similarity is transitive: if A is similar to B, and B is similar to C, then A is similar to C. This seems plausible but fails when the respects of similarity differ.
2
Examine the given example
The example: man is similar to palm tree (in height), palm tree is similar to stone (in being soulless). The conclusion drawn is that man is similar to stone (soulless), making Zayd an inanimate object. Note that the respects are different.
The example uses 'Zayd' as a representative man.
3
Apply the refutation
The refutation: the similarity between man and palm tree is in one respect (e.g., height), not in the respect that palm tree is like stone (soulless). Therefore the conclusion does not follow. The fallacy is resolved by clarifying the respect.
The text also offers an alternative answer using mental existence theory.
4
Understand the deeper response
The alternative answer: even if something exists mentally (like the quiddity of fire), it does not cause material effects because mental existence is shadowy. This reinforces that similarity in mental existence does not transfer material properties.
This ties the fallacy to the broader theory of two-tier existence (external and shadowy).

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on various causes of logical fallacies (المغالطات). The author begins by explaining a primary cause: confusing mental existence (الوجود الظلي الذهني) with external existence. For example, when one imagines fire, the mind does not actually burn because the fire exists only as a mental copy, not a real external object. The error arises from taking an accidental property (العرض) that belongs to real existence and applying it to mental existence. 

Next, the author discusses the fallacy of 'taking one cause in place of another' (أخذ العلة مكان العلة). An example: seventy men carry a heavy stone seventy parasangs. One might mistakenly think that each man carried it one parasang, assuming equal contribution, whereas the cause (carrying) is collective, not distributive.

Another cause is improperly applying the 'method of priority' (طريق الأولوية) when there is a difference in genus. For instance, saying man is not more entitled to the rational soul than a sparrow because both share animality. This overlooks the specific difference that distinguishes man.

A further cause is 'neglect of aspects' (قلة المبالاة بالحيثيات). An example: 'Every white has whiteness entering its reality; Zayd is white; therefore whiteness enters Zayd's reality.' The error is that 'white' implies whiteness only in the aspect of being white, not in the aspect of being a human or animal. The aspect (حيثية) matters.

The 'like-of-like' fallacy (مماثل المماثل مماثل) is also mentioned. For example: man is like a palm tree (in height), and a palm tree is like a stone (in being non-souled). The erroneous conclusion would be that man is like a stone, i.e., inanimate. The error lies in assuming similarity holds across different attributes.

Another cause is 'taking the absence of the counterpart of a faculty in place of the contrary or contradictory' (أخذ عدم المقابل للملكة مكان الضد والنقيض). Rest is not motion where motion is expected, but rest as a privation (عدم الملكة) is often confused with the contrary. Similarly, blindness is lack of sight in something that normally sees. One might wrongly attribute rest to incorporeal beings or blindness to a wall, but these are irrelevant because the subject lacks the capacity.

Finally, the famous fallacy about 'acquiring the unknown' (تحصيل المجهول) is addressed. The fallacy argues that you cannot acquire unknown knowledge because if you know it, it's already known; if you don't know it, you won't recognize it when found. The refutation is that the sought object is known in one aspect (e.g., its general description) and unknown in another (e.g., its specific location). The example of an escaped slave: known in essence but unknown in location. When found, you recognize him by his known features. The fallacy confuses absolute knowledge with partial knowledge. The author concludes that seeking knowledge is valid because it is partly known and partly unknown; through inquiry, the unknown becomes known.

The Fallacy of Mistaking Absence for Opposite
Author explains the fallacy of taking absence for opposite.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An absence is not a contrary unless the subject can possess the property.

In logical debates, one common error is to treat the mere absence of a property as if it were a genuine opposite. For example, calling a wall 'blind' implies blindness is a privation of sight, but walls are not capable of sight. This fallacy creeps into many Islamic theological discussions, especially when describing beings with different capacities.

The Fallacy of the Unknown Object and Its Resolution
Author explains the unknown object fallacy and its resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the paradox of acquiring unknown knowledge and apply the solution of partial knowledge to avoid the fallacy.

Core Idea
The unknown is known in one respect and unknown in another, making its acquisition possible.

A famous sophistical argument claims that seeking unknown knowledge is impossible: if you know it, you don't need to seek it; if you don't, you can't recognize it. This lesson unpacks the fallacy and shows how partial knowledge resolves it, using the example of an escaped slave and the process of acquiring knowledge of a thing's essence.

Guided Path
1
State the paradox
The opponent argues: if the sought is known, seeking it is redundant; if unknown, you cannot identify it when found. Therefore, acquisition is impossible.
2
Identify the hidden assumption
The argument assumes the sought is either completely known or completely unknown, with no middle ground.
3
Introduce the solution: partial knowledge
The author responds that the sought is known in one respect (e.g., essence) and unknown in another (e.g., location or specific attribute).
4
Apply to the slave example
You know the slave's essence and form (known aspect), but not his location (unknown). When you find him, you recognize him by the known aspect.
5
Generalize to knowledge acquisition
When seeking the essence of something known by a property (e.g., 'human' known as writer), you move from the known property to principles, then back to essence.
This is the method of definition in logic.

The Sophism of Conditional Denial: Anatomy of a Fallacy
Author presents a sophism using conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional syllogism of the sophism and identify the fallacy of equivocation on the supposition type.

Core Idea
A conditional syllogism is only valid when its premises share the same domain of suppositions.

The sophism presented here tricks the unwary by mixing actual and hypothetical suppositions. We will break down its structure to see why it seems compelling but is actually invalid. This lesson sharpens your ability to detect similar fallacies in theological and juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The sophism attempts to prove: 'Whenever my proposition is not true, "Zayd is not standing" is true.' This is the target conclusion.
2
Reconstruct the two premises
First premise: 'If a proposition is not true, then "Zayd is standing" is not true.' Second premise: 'Whenever "Zayd is standing" is not true, its contradictory "Zayd is not standing" is true.' Note that the first premise uses a hypothetical antecedent about an arbitrary proposition being false.
The second premise is a universal conditional about Zayd's standing.
3
Determine the logical form
This is a chain of conditionals: If P then Q, If Q then R, therefore If P then R. In itself, this is a valid hypothetical syllogism. The fallacy lies not in the form but in the material content.
4
Spot the equivocation on suppositions
The minor premise (first conditional) refers to cases where a proposition is false, but some propositions (like 'The Necessary Existent exists') are impossible to be false. The major premise applies only to actual suppositions, but the minor includes impossible ones. This mismatch invalidates the inference.
The author explains that on impossible suppositions, both a statement and its contradictory can be false together.

The General Fallacy That Proves Any Claim
Author critiques a fallacy that claims to prove any statement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the 'general fallacy' and explain why it fails for necessary truths and universal matters.

Core Idea
A fallacy that claims to prove anything is itself only valid in limited cases.

This passage introduces a sophism that seems to prove any statement, true or false. The author and later scholars reveal its hidden constraints, showing that it does not work for necessary truths or universal matters. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to detect overreaching arguments in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Understand the fallacy's structure
The fallacy argues: if the claim (P) is false, its contradictory (¬P) is true; and if ¬P is true, then something (Q) is true. So it concludes: if not P then Q. This is a conditional chain.
The structure resembles modus ponens but with a hidden flaw.
2
Identify the subsumption error
The major premise (if ¬P then Q) may be true when considering actual situations, but the minor premise assumes a counterfactual impossibility (that ¬P is false when it is actually true). Therefore the minor does not fall under the major; the argument is invalid.
The author points out: 'there is no subsumption' because the judgment in the major is on actual suppositions, while the minor is on impossible hypotheticals.
3
Apply the scholarly refinement
Later scholars note that this fallacy is not truly universal. It fails when the contradictory involves a necessary truth (like 'The Necessary Existent exists') or when trying to produce a conditional of two conditionals. The contraposition of universal affirmatives is restricted.
The fallacy only applies to specific logical rules, not to all claims.

Resolving the General Fallacy: Correct Contraposition and Reductio
Author discusses the solution to the general fallacy through correct contraposition and analysis of reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to correctly perform contraposition while preserving term specificity and explain why the reductio argument in the fallacy fails.

Core Idea
Correct contraposition requires identical specificity; a reductio fails when the antecedent is impossible.

The author presents the correct solution: the term in contraposition must be taken in the same manner. He also examines a reductio argument that attempts to prove the claim and shows why it fails.

Guided Path
1
Restate the correct contrapositive
The correct contrapositive of 'If the claim is not established, then its opposite is established' is 'If the opposite of the claim is not established, then the claim is established'. The term 'opposite' is specific and matches the original.
The author states: فمعناه كلما لم يكن نقيض المدعى ثابتا كان المدعى ثابتا وهذا صادق
2
Understand the reductio attempt
Some try to prove the claim by reductio: assume the contrapositive 'If nothing is established, then the claim is established' is valid, and note that its antecedent is false (since something is established), so the claim must be true. This is an attempt at modus tollens.
3
Identify the flaw in the reductio
The reductio assumes that the contrapositive is valid, but it is not due to the equivocation. Even if it were valid, the antecedent 'nothing is established' is impossible (since the Necessary Being exists). An impossible antecedent can entail any consequent without contradiction, so the reductio does not force the claim to be true.
The author notes: والمحال جاز أن يستلزم نقيضه، فلا خلف.
4
Apply the lesson
When using reductio ad absurdum, ensure the assumed antecedent is possible; otherwise, the argument is unsound. Also, always verify that contraposition preserves specificity.
