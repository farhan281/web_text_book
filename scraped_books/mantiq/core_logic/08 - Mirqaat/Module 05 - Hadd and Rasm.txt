Division of Knowledge into Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Defines tasawwur and explains judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tasawwur as 'apprehension devoid of judgment' and distinguish it from tasdiq using the Zayd example.

Core Idea
Knowledge splits into two: concept without judgment, and judgment itself.

In this lesson, we encounter the foundational division of knowledge in Islamic logic: tasawwur (simple concept) and tasdiq (assent). The author defines tasawwur precisely and clarifies what judgment (hukm) means, using the concrete example of conceiving Zayd and standing without linking them. This distinction is the starting point for all further logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts
The passage states knowledge divides into two: tasawwur and tasdiq. Note that tasdiq is not defined here but contrasted.
2
Extract the definition of tasawwur
Look for the phrase 'al-tasawwur huwa al-idrak al-khali 'an al-hukm'. This is the definition.
3
Understand what hukm means
The author explains hukm as relating one thing to another affirmatively or negatively, or as the occurrence/non-occurrence of a relation.
4
Apply to the example
The example: conceiving Zayd alone or 'standing' alone without affirming or negating standing of Zayd. This illustrates tasawwur without judgment.

And it divides into two parts: one of them is called tasawwur (concept), and the second is expressed as tasdiq (assent). As for tasawwur, it is apprehension devoid of judgment (hukm). By judgment is meant relating one thing to another affirmatively or negatively; if you wish, you say 'positing' (iqa') or 'abstracting' (intiza'). And judgment may be interpreted as the occurrence or non-occurrence of the relation, such as when you conceive of Zayd alone or 'standing' alone without affirming standing for Zayd or negating it from him.

Elaboration is not suitable for this abridgment, as stated in 'Hashiya al-Hasan' on 'al-Tahdhib'.
'Positing' (iqa'an): Know that the words affirmation (ijab), negation (salb), positing (iqa'), abstracting (intiza'), and attribution (isnad) may each be applied to the complete relation (al-nisba al-tamma), whether categorical or conditional, conjunctive or disjunctive, and may be applied to the apprehension of that relation in a persuasive manner. Because these words suggest, according to language, that the soul has an act issuing from it after conceiving the relation, most later scholars claimed that judgment is an act of the soul. But the truth is what the commentator of 'al-Mutala'' said: that judgment, positing the relation, and attribution are all expressions and words, and the reality is that there is no effective act of the soul here; rather it is acquiescence and acceptance of the relation, which is the apprehension that the relation either holds or does not hold, so it belongs to the category of quality (kayf).

Commentary
This passage is a definitional lesson where the author divides knowledge into concept (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq), and then defines tasawwur. The passage begins with the matn stating that knowledge is divided into two: tasawwur and tasdiq. Tasawwur is defined as 'apprehension devoid of judgment' — meaning an awareness that does not include affirming or denying something of something else. The term 'hukm' (judgment) is then explained: it is the relating of one thing to another, either positively or negatively, or as the occurrence or non-occurrence of a relation. An example is given: conceiving of Zayd alone or of 'standing' alone, without either affirming standing of Zayd or denying it. The commentary then clarifies that this division (into tasawwur and tasdiq) applies only to acquired temporal knowledge, not to presential knowledge or God's eternal knowledge, which are not subject to such division. It notes a scholarly debate: some like al-Dawwani generalize the division, but the author sides with those who specify it to acquired knowledge. The commentary further explains the terms used for judgment (ijab, salb, iqa', intiza', isnad), noting that they can refer either to the relation itself or to the apprehension of it. The author defends the view that judgment is not an act of the soul but rather an acquiescence or acceptance, belonging to the category of quality. This is a key metaphysical point distinguishing mental acts from mental states.

الحكم (al-hukm): Relating one thing to another affirmatively or negatively; interpreted as occurrence or non-occurrence of a relation. The author clarifies it is not a mental act but an acquiescence. Scope of the Division and the Nature of Judgment
Restricts scope of division and refines nature of judgment.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the division into tasawwur and tasdiq does not apply to presential or divine knowledge, and state the author's view on the nature of judgment.

The division into tasawwur and tasdiq is only for acquired temporal knowledge, and judgment is a passive acquiescence, not an act.

Judgment in Tasawwur and the Four Meanings of Hukm
Author argues that judgment can occur in tasawwur and enumerates four meanings of hukm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four meanings of hukm and explain how judgment can appear in tasawwur according to later logicians.

Core Idea
Judgment is not always a separate act; it can be embedded in concepts through restrictive relations.

In classical logic, tasawwur (concept) is defined as devoid of judgment. Yet the author notes that a certain sense of judgment can exist in tasawwur. To resolve this apparent contradiction, later scholars introduced the notion of the 'restrictive relation' (al-nisba al-taqyidiyya). Mastering this nuance is crucial for understanding the boundary between concept and assent in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Note that tasawwur is defined as devoid of judgment, but the author mentions takhyil and shakk as examples where judgment occurs in tasawwur. Locate these terms in the Arabic.
2
Examine the later scholars' solution
Look at the phrase 'al-nisba al-taqyidiyya' and its description as 'between-between' (bayn bayn). Understand that this restrictive relation allows a judgment-like element to be part of a concept without full assent.
This is a key innovation of later logicians.
3
Extract the four meanings of hukm
From the enumeration, list the four distinct senses: (1) the thing judged (mahkum bihi), (2) occurrence or non-occurrence of the relation, (3) the relation as connector, (4) apprehension of occurrence or non-occurrence.
4
Apply to an example
Consider the concept 'possibility of the world' (imkan al-‘alam). This concept contains a relation of contingency. Identify which meaning of hukm is involved and explain how it is a restrictive relation rather than a full judgment.
This shows how the restrictive relation works in practice.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on judgment (hukm) within the definition of tasawwur. The author notes that when judgment is understood as the occurrence or non-occurrence of a relation (the second interpretation mentioned earlier), it can also be found in tasawwur, specifically in imaginative representations (takhyil) and doubt (shakk), which are actually types of tasawwur, not tasdiq. This seems contradictory: tasawwur was defined as devoid of judgment, but here judgment appears in it. The author remarks that this interpretation is valid only according to later scholars, because they distinguish a special 'restrictive relation' (al-nisba al-taqyidiyya) that is neither simply occurring nor not occurring, called 'between-between' (bayn bayn). Then the author enumerates four distinct meanings of the term 'hukm' (judgment): (a) the thing judged (i.e., the predicate), (b) the occurrence or non-occurrence of the relation itself, (c) the relation considered as a connector, and (d) the apprehension that the relation occurs or does not occur. This enumeration clarifies the ambiguity of the term 'hukm' and helps avoid confusion in logic. The passage is a refinement by the commentator, explaining that the subtle distinction allows judgment to appear in conceptual form.

النسبة التقيدية (al-nisba al-taqyidiyya): A restrictive relation that is the subject of occurrence or non-occurrence, called 'between-between', used to explain how judgment can appear in tasawwur. [Note: This term is specific to later logical analyses of judgment in concepts; not found in earlier Aristotelian texts.]

Distinguishing Word and Compound in Logic
Author defines mufrad and murakkab with four conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any expression as mufrad or murakkab by applying the four conditions for compound expressions.

Core Idea
Whether an expression is a word or compound depends on intentional part signification, not length.

In this lesson, you will learn the foundational distinction between mufrad (word) and murakkab (compound). This is not merely a grammatical classification but a logical one, crucial for understanding how meanings are built. The author defines each term precisely and provides conditions to test any expression, ensuring clarity in definitions and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression
Take the word or phrase you want to classify. For example, 'عبد الله' (Abdullah) as a proper name or 'الكتاب' (the book). Write it down clearly.
2
Check for parts
Determine if the expression has separable parts. Does it consist of more than one letter or word that could stand alone? For 'عبد الله', the parts are 'عبد' and 'الله'. For 'الكتاب', the parts are 'ال' and 'كتاب'.
Some expressions like the interrogative hamzah (أ) have no parts.
3
Check part signification
Do those parts individually have meaning? In 'عبد الله', 'عبد' means 'slave' and 'الله' means 'Allah'. In 'الكتاب', 'ال' is a definite article and 'كتاب' means 'book'. If parts have no meaning, the expression cannot be compound.
4
Check part-of-meaning
Is the meaning of each part a component of the intended meaning of the whole? For 'عبد الله' as a proper name, the intended meaning is a specific person, not 'slave of Allah'. Core Idea

Distinguishing Haqiqa, Majaz, and Muradif
Author defines haqiqa, majaz, and muradif with examples and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish haqiqa from majaz based on usage prevalence and identify true muradif (synonyms) according to the conditions given.

Core Idea
Haqiqa and majaz depend on usage polarity; muradif requires one meaning across multiple words.

Words can have multiple meanings, but not all are equal. Some are used literally, some metaphorically, and sometimes different words share the same meaning. This lesson clarifies these three categories using examples like 'asad' (lion) for the animal and the brave man, and 'asad' and 'layth' as synonyms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two usage criteria
Notice that haqiqa and majaz are defined relative to a word's usage: if the word is used in its original meaning and also in a secondary meaning, but neither is predominant, the original usage is haqiqa and the secondary is majaz.
The word must not have abandoned its first meaning entirely.
2
Understand the ʿalāqa requirement for majaz
For a word to be used metaphorically, there must be a connection (ʿalāqa) between the original and the secondary meaning. Without this, the usage would be a mistake (ghalat), as in saying 'Take this horse' while pointing to a book.
Commentators enumerate specific types of ʿalāqa, like similarity or causality.
3
Apply the distinction with the 'asad' example
The word 'asad' is haqiqa when referring to the predatory animal and majaz when referring to a brave man. Test this: the meaning of 'predatory animal' is the original, and 'brave man' is secondary, with a connection of bravery being like the lion's trait.
This illustrates the relational nature of haqiqa and majaz.
4
Define muradif and test with examples
Muradif occurs when multiple words share exactly one meaning. The passage gives 'asad' and 'layth' (both meaning lion) and 'ghaym' and 'ghayth' (both meaning rain clouds). Check that the meanings are identical, not just similar.

The Four Types of Definition in Islamic Logic
Author defines the four types of definition with conditions and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given definition as one of the four types (hadd tamm, hadd naqis, rasm tamm, rasm naqis) based on its components and coextensiveness.

Core Idea
A complete definition requires proximate genus and differentia; anything else is incomplete or a description.

Definitions in Islamic logic are not all the same. Some capture the essence completely, others only partially, and some merely describe by properties. Understanding these four types helps you evaluate the precision of any definition you encounter in Islamic sciences, from fiqh to kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definiens
Find the term being defined (al-mu'arraf bi al-fath) and the expression used to define it (al-mu'arraf bi al-kasr). The definiens is what is predicated of the thing to give its conception.
2
Determine whether the definiens is inside the definiendum or outside
Check if the definiens includes the essence of the definiendum (like genus and differentia) or an external property. If it is inside, it is a hadd; if outside, a rasm.
3
Check if the definiens uses proximate or remote genus
See whether the genus mentioned is immediate (al-jins al-qarib) or distant (al-jins al-ba'id). A proximate genus together with a proximate differentia gives a complete hadd; a remote genus gives a deficient hadd.
If only the differentia is used, it may still be a deficient hadd if allowed.
4
Check if the definiens includes a property (khassa)
If the definiens includes a property (like 'rising by day' for the sun) and a proximate genus, it is a complete rasm. If it uses a remote genus with a property, or a property alone, it is a deficient rasm.
The property must be necessary (lazima) and evident (bayyina) for rasm.
5
Verify coextensiveness (ittiraad wa in'ikaas)
According to later logicians, a valid definition must apply to all and only the instances of the definiendum. Check if the definiens is equal in extension: not broader (a'am) and not narrower (akhass).
Earlier logicians allowed broader definitions for mere distinction.

The Definer Must Be Better Known
Explains necessary conditions for a valid definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify violations of the condition that the definer must be more known and clearer than the defined in any given definition.

Core Idea
A definition fails if the definer is as obscure as the defined.

In Islamic logic, a definition (al-qawl al-sharih) must clarify the concept of a thing. The first and most basic rule is that the definer must be more known and clearer than the defined, otherwise the definition is useless. This prevents circular definitions, definitions by correlatives or opposites, and self-referential definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defined and definer
In any definition, locate the term being defined (al-mu'arraf) and the term(s) used to define it (al-mu'arraf bihi).
2
Check if the definer is more known
Is the definer clearly understood without reference to the defined? If the definer depends on the defined for its own understanding, the condition is violated.
Examples: defining 'father' as 'male parent' is fine, but defining 'father' as 'one who has a child' is circular if 'child' is defined via 'father'.
3
Test specific forbidden forms
The passage lists several invalid forms: definition by correlatives (e.g., defining 'father' as 'the one who has a son'), definition by opposites (e.g., 'black is that which opposes white'), definition by itself ('man is a human animal'), and definition by something only known through the defined ('sun is a star that rises in daytime').
Each of these violates the condition because the definer is not more known.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
Examine a definition from fiqh or kalam. For instance, if someone defines 'wudu'' as 'the act that makes prayer valid', this is problematic if 'prayer' is defined using 'wudu''.
Use real definitions from usul al-fiqh or kalam books.

Classification of Definitions and Coextensiveness
Classifies definitions and discusses coextensiveness requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a definition as complete/incomplete hadd or rasm and determine whether it satisfies the coextensiveness requirement (ittirād wa-in‘ikās).

Core Idea
Later scholars require the definer to be exactly coextensive with the defined; broader or narrower terms are invalid.

Building on the basic condition, Islamic logicians classify definitions based on the structure of the definer. The earlier scholars allowed broader terms for mere distinction, but later scholars insisted on coextensiveness (musawi). This lesson teaches the classification into hadd tamm, hadd naqis, rasm tamm, and rasm naqis, and the requirement that the definer be coextensive and convertible.

Guided Path
1
Determine the relation of definer to defined
Is the definer included in the concept of the defined, excluded from it, or composed of both? Inclusion means the definer shares the essence; exclusion means it is a separate property.
2
Classify as hadd or rasm
If the definer is included and equals the defined in concept and general scope, it is a complete definition (hadd tamm). If included but not equal in concept (only equal in generality), it is incomplete hadd. If excluded, it is a description (rasm) – and must be a necessary property. If composed of included and excluded, it is a complete rasm if it uses proximate genus and a distinguishing property.
The passage provides the example: complete hadd = proximate genus + proximate differentia.
3
Check coextensiveness (ittirād wa-in‘ikās)
According to later scholars, the definer must be exactly coextensive: all instances of the definer are instances of the defined (ittirād) and vice versa (in‘ikās). Broader or narrower terms are not allowed.
This condition is stricter than the basic rule of being better known.

Definitions are of four types based on essential vs. non-essential components and completeness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a real definition (haqiqi) and a verbal definition (lafzi) and explain the etymological basis for the terms 'hadd' and 'rasm' in logic.

Core Idea
Definitions can be real (capturing essence) or verbal (explaining word meaning); 'hadd' means prevention, 'rasm' means trace.

After classifying definitions by content, the author introduces another important distinction: real versus verbal definition. A verbal definition does not aim to give the essence; it simply clarifies what a word means. This is common in lexicons. The author then explains why the terms 'hadd' (limit/prevention) and 'rasm' (trace) are used for these logical concepts, grounding them in their Arabic linguistic origins.

Predication is the union of two concepts in existence — its taxonomy governs how we analyze every Islamic science proposition.

In logic, predication (al-ḥaml) is the basic relation between subject and predicate. Core Idea
Disjunctions differ by whether both parts can be true or false together.

Before applying inference rules, we must understand the types of disjunctive propositions. The author distinguishes three types in the source passage, and the commentary clarifies their truth conditions. This lesson helps you identify each type correctly.

Guided Path

Ask: Is it possible for both A and B to be true at the same time? If impossible, they are mutually exclusive in truth.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a judgment via a common meaning, but its methods of establishing causation are inherently weak.

In this passage, the author defines tamthil (analogical reasoning) as transferring a judgment from one particular to another due to a shared common meaning. The Two Movements of Discursive Thought (Fikr)
Describes the two movements constituting discursive thought.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the two mental movements in any instance of discursive reasoning, using the example of defining 'man' as a model.

Core Idea
Fikr requires two movements; intuition skips both.

The author distinguishes fikr (discursive thought) from hads (intuition) by emphasizing that fikr involves a double movement: from the desired conclusion to the principles, and then back to the conclusion. This lesson guides you through that process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired conclusion (matlab) in the example
In the example, the desired conclusion is the definition of 'man'. The mind first has a vague concept (e.g., 'writer') and seeks its essence.
2
Trace the first movement: from matlab to mabadi'
The mind searches stored knowledge and finds 'animal' and 'rational' as suitable principles. This is the first movement: from the desired to the principles.
The end of the first movement is the discovery of relevant principles.
3

Distinguishing Limmi and Inni Demonstration
Defines limmi and inni demonstrations with examples and commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between limmi and inni demonstrations by analyzing the causal role of the middle term in categorical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Limmi demonstrates the cause; inni demonstrates the fact without the cause.

This passage from Mirqat introduces a crucial distinction in Islamic logic: not all demonstrative syllogisms provide causal knowledge. Understanding the difference between limmi (demonstration of the reasoned fact) and inni (demonstration of the fact) is essential for evaluating proofs in kalam, fiqh, and other Islamic sciences. The author uses medical examples to illustrate how the same formal structure can yield different epistemic levels.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Read the opening sentence: 'Demonstration is of two types: limmi and inni.' This is the division being explained.
2
Understand the role of the middle term
In both types, the middle term (awsat) mediates the judgment. In limmi, the middle term is the real cause (illa) of the conclusion in the external world. In inni, the middle term is only a cause in the mind.
The author explicitly states that in inni the middle term 'may be an effect in reality.'
3
Analyze the limmi example
The example: 'Zaid is feverish because he has putrid humours.' The middle term 'putrid humours' is the real cause of fever. Write out the syllogism: major premise 'Everyone with putrid humours is feverish', minor 'Zaid has putrid humours', conclusion 'Zaid is feverish'.
Notice that the middle term is the cause both in the mind and in reality.
4
Analyze the inni example
The example: 'Zaid has putrid humours because he is feverish.' The middle term 'feverish' is not the real cause; rather, fever is an effect of putrid humours. The syllogism is: major 'Everyone feverish has putrid humours', minor 'Zaid is feverish', conclusion 'Zaid has putrid humours'.
This syllogism is still valid but only gives knowledge of the fact (that Zaid has putrid humours), not the cause.
5
Apply to Islamic theological arguments
Consider a kalam proof: 'The world is contingent, and everything contingent requires a creator, so the world has a creator.' The middle term 'contingent' is a real cause of needing a creator, so this is a limmi demonstration. Conversely, 'The world exists and every existing thing has a creator, so the world has a creator' is inni, because existence is not the cause of having a creator but rather an effect.
This shows how the distinction affects the epistemic strength of theological proofs.

Subjects and Principles in the Sciences: A Foundational Epistemological Rule
Author explains the rule about subject existence and classifies principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a science does not investigate its own subject's existence and classify its principles into conceptual and propositional types.

Core Idea
Every science takes its subject for granted; its real work is proving essential accidents, not the subject's existence.

In this lesson, we explore a fundamental epistemological rule of the Islamic sciences: the subject of a science is never proved or investigated within that science. Instead, the subject is taken as given. We then see how the principles (mabādiʾ) of a science are divided into conceptual definitions and propositional premises. This structure ensures that sciences do not fall into circularity.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Rule
The text explicitly states: لا يبحث عن وجود الموضوع ولا يبحث عن ماهيته. Identify that the existence and quiddity of a science's subject are not investigated within that science.
Examples: physician does not prove existence of human body; grammarian does not investigate reality of word.
2
Understand the Justification
The commentary argues that if investigating the subject's existence were a problem, it would lead to circularity. Because problems are proofs of essential accidents, and proving accidents depends on the subject's existence, so if subject existence were a problem, the thing would depend on itself.
This is a conditional argument: if P then circularity; therefore not P.
3
Define Principles
Principles (mabādiʾ) are what problems are built upon. They are not parts of the science but means (wasāʾil). Two types: conceptual (taṣawwuriyya) – definitions of subject, its parts, and essential accidents; and propositional (taṣdīqiyya) – premises, either self-evident or derived.
The text gives examples: definition of 'word' as conceptual principle.
4
Apply the Distinction
Given a statement from an Islamic science, determine whether it is a conceptual principle (definition) or a propositional principle (premise). Also, check if it violates the rule about subject existence.
For instance, in grammar, 'The word is an utterance placed for a meaning' is conceptual; 'Every utterance is either noun, verb, or particle' is propositional.

The commentary further clarifies that principles are not parts of the science but merely means (wasāʾil) to reach the sought propositions and conceptions. The author of 'Sullam al-ʿUlūm' is quoted to support this view, correcting the misconception that sciences have three parts (subjects, principles, problems); rather, only problems are parts, while principles are means.
