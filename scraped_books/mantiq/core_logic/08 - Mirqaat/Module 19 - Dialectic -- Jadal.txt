Badihah and Nazari: Attributes of Knowledge or the Known?
Disagreement on badihah/nazari as attributes of knowledge or known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the majority view that badihah and nazari are attributes of the known object and the author's view that they are attributes of knowledge, and justify the author's position using his epistemological argument.

Core Idea
Badihah and nazari are attributes of the act of knowing, not the thing known.

This lesson tackles a fundamental epistemological question in Islamic logic: are terms like 'self-evident' (badihah) and 'theoretical' (nazari) describing the knowledge itself or the object known? The author refines the majority position, arguing that these are primarily qualities of the cognitive act. You will learn to see how this distinction impacts theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two competing views
Locate in the Arabic where the author states the disagreement: 'they disagreed whether self-evident and theoretical are attributes of the knowledge essentially or the known object essentially.' The majority holds they are attributes of the known object; the author argues they are attributes of knowledge.
2
Understand the majority's reasoning
The majority thinks that theoretical reasoning aims at the known object itself, not the knowledge. So if an object is reached by reasoning, it is 'theoretical'; if not, 'self-evident'. Thus the attribute belongs to the object.
The passage says: 'thinking that what results from theoretical reasoning is the intended object, and the intended is not the attainment of the reality of knowledge.'
3
Analyze the author's critique
The author objects: the true purpose of theoretical reasoning is knowledge or disclosure of things, not the existence of the objects themselves except accidentally. So the attribute must belong to the knowledge act.
He says: 'the intended by theoretical reasoning is the knowledge of things or their disclosure, not the existence of the information themselves except accidentally.'
4
Grasp the implication for variation
If badihah and nazari are attributes of knowledge, then one and the same known object can be known by self-evident knowledge in one person and by theoretical knowledge in another, because the knowledge acts are different. The object itself is neither self-evident nor theoretical per se.
The author notes: 'Yes, the essence of the known object may be both self-evident and theoretical in the sense that a knowledge that does not depend on theoretical reasoning may pertain to it, so it becomes self-evident, and a knowledge that depends on theoretical reasoning may pertain to it, so it becomes theoretical accidentally.'
5
Apply the distinction to an Islamic example
Consider the knowledge of Allah's existence. For a prophet, this knowledge is self-evident (badihah) without reasoning. For a common believer, it is theoretical (nazari) reached through proofs. According to the author, the attribute 'self-evident' describes the prophet's knowledge, not Allah's existence itself. The object (Allah) remains the same, but the knowledge differs.
This example illustrates why the author's view is more precise.

Commentary
This passage discusses the nature of badihah (self-evident) and nazari (theoretical) knowledge, whether they are attributes of the knowledge itself (al-'ilm) or of the known object (al-ma'lum). The author begins by noting that from the previous discussion it is known that badihah and nazari vary according to persons and times. He then explains that if they are attributes of the known object, it is clear how one object can be theoretical for one person and self-evident for another. On the other hand, if they are attributes of knowledge, then the same object can be known by different types of knowledge: one necessary and one theoretical, making the object accidentally self-evident via one knowledge and theoretical via another. The author then presents the majority view that badihah and nazari are attributes of the known object, arguing that the purpose of theoretical reasoning is to obtain the known object itself, not the knowledge. However, the author criticizes this view, stating that the truth is that they are attributes of the knowledge itself, because the purpose of theoretical reasoning is to attain knowledge or disclosure of things, not the existence of the known objects except accidentally. Therefore, a single act of knowledge cannot be both self-evident and theoretical; they are distinct. However, the same known object can be known by both types of knowledge accidentally. The commentary follows the author's order: first the clarification of variation, then the two positions, then the author's judgment. This is a critical refinement of the majority view, typical of a hashiya. The example relates to how a single fact, like the existence of God, can be self-evident to a prophet but theoretical for an ordinary person.

The Dialectical Syllogism: Definition and Types of Premises
Author defines dialectical syllogism and explains its two types of premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the dialectical syllogism and distinguish its two types of premises: mashhurat and musallamat.

Core Idea
Dialectical logic uses accepted opinions, not absolute truths.

In disputation, arguments often rest on premises that are well-known or conceded, not necessarily proven. The author defines the dialectical syllogism (qiyas jadali) as a composition of such premises. This lesson unpacks that definition and the types of premises it involves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: القياس الجدلي (the dialectical syllogism).
2
Extract the genus
The genus is قياس (syllogism), meaning it has the same structure as other syllogisms.
3
Extract the differentia
The differentia is that it is composed of premises that are مشهورة (well-known) or مسلّمة (conceded) by the opponent, regardless of truth.
This separates it from demonstrative syllogisms which require certain premises.
4
Identify the two types of premises
Mashhurat are common opinions that vary by community, discipline, or temperament. Musallamat are specifically conceded by the opponent for the sake of argument.
Examples: 'Justice is good' is a mashhura; a fuqaha' conceding usul al-fiqh issues is a musallama.
5
Test a definition
Take an example: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' Is this dialectical? It depends on whether the premises are mashhura or musallama.
If the opponent accepts both premises, it's dialectical; if they demand proof, it's not.

Commentary
This passage defines the dialectical syllogism (qiyas jadali) as a syllogism composed of premises that are well-known or conceded by the opponent, regardless of truth value. The author distinguishes two types of premises: mashhurat (common opinions) and musallamat (concessions). Mashhurat are widely accepted opinions that vary by culture, temperament, or discipline; examples include general moral principles, culturally specific views like the Indian aversion to animal slaughter, and discipline-specific maxims in grammar and usul al-fiqh. Musallamat are premises specifically conceded by the opponent for the sake of argument, often from scholarly authority. The commentary warns against confusing mashhurat with self-evident truths (awwaliyat), and notes that dialectical arguments can use invalid forms if the opponent accepts them.

Distinguishing Mashhurat from Self-Evident Truths
Commentary warns against confusing mashhurat with awwaliyat and elaborates on musallamat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between mashhurat (well-known opinions) and awwaliyat (self-evident truths) and explain why the latter are certain while the former are not.

Core Idea
Well-known opinions can be false; self-evident truths cannot.

The commentator warns that mashhurat are often confused with self-evident truths (awwaliyat), as happened with the Mu'tazila who considered religious truths as necessary. He explains how to distinguish them: self-evident truths are known by reason alone, while mashhurat depend on culture, custom, or revelation. This lesson clarifies that distinction and also discusses musallamat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the warning
The commentator warns that mashhurat may be confused with awwaliyat, citing the Mu'tazila as an example.
The Mu'tazila thought 'Truthfulness saves from the Fire' is self-evident, but it is known only by revelation.
2
Find the distinguishing criterion
The criterion is to isolate the mind as if created just now: if a proposition is known without external factors, it is awwali; otherwise it is mashhura.
This is the 'born today' thought experiment.
3
Apply the criterion
Take a proposition like 'Killing a thief is obligatory.' If you set aside cultural conditioning, is it self-evident? Probably not; it depends on custom or revelation. So it is mashhura.
4
Understand musallamat
Musallamat are premises conceded by the opponent, which may be specific to a debate or a scholarly community. They do not need to be true or widely accepted.
Example: conceding a principle of usul al-fiqh in a fiqh debate.

It is achieved by isolating the mind from everything else so that it imagines as if it were created just now; thus well-known truths require proof, just as a man who disregards revelation does not know the Fire, let alone that truthfulness saves from it and lying leads to it. And because well-known truths may be false, while primary truths are only true.
'Musallama etc.': Conceded propositions are those that are conceded by the opponent; then discourse is built upon them to compel the opponent, whether they are conceded between the two disputants specifically or between their scholars, like the concession of fuqaha' of issues of usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines the dialectical syllogism (qiyas jadali) as a syllogism composed of premises that are well-known or conceded by the opponent, regardless of truth value. The author distinguishes two types of premises: mashhurat (common opinions) and musallamat (concessions). Mashhurat are widely accepted opinions that vary by culture, temperament, or discipline; examples include general moral principles, culturally specific views like the Indian aversion to animal slaughter, and discipline-specific maxims in grammar and usul al-fiqh. Musallamat are premises specifically conceded by the opponent for the sake of argument, often from scholarly authority. The commentary warns against confusing mashhurat with self-evident truths (awwaliyat), and notes that dialectical arguments can use invalid forms if the opponent accepts them.
