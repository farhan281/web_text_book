The Six Types of Indication
Enumeration of the six types of indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any example of indication into one of the six types: linguistic vs non-linguistic and conventional vs natural vs rational.

Core Idea
Indication is not just words; natural signs and rational inferences are also forms of indication.

Indication (dalala) is the relation between a sign and its meaning. The author systematically divides it into two main branches – linguistic and non-linguistic – each further subdivided into conventional, natural, and rational. This taxonomy helps you recognize the diverse ways we derive knowledge from signs, whether in daily life or in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sign type
Determine if the sign is a spoken word or sound (linguistic) or something else like an action, object, or natural phenomenon (non-linguistic).
Linguistic signs are produced by the vocal apparatus.
2
Determine the connection type
Ask: is the link between sign and meaning set by human/divine convention (wad'iyya), by nature without intention (tabi'iyya), or by rational inference ('aqliyya)?
Conventional: arbitrary assignment (e.g., 'Zayd'). Natural: instinctive sound (e.g., 'uh uh' for pain). Rational: logical deduction (e.g., smoke -> fire).
3
Combine the two axes
Match the linguistic/non-linguistic with the connection type to get one of six: lafziyya wad'iyya, lafziyya tabi'iyya, lafziyya 'aqliyya, ghayr lafziyya wad'iyya, ghayr lafziyya tabi'iyya, ghayr lafziyya 'aqliyya.
Examples from the passage: 'Zayd' (lafziyya wad'iyya), 'uh uh' (lafziyya tabi'iyya), voice behind wall (lafziyya 'aqliyya), signposts (ghayr lafziyya wad'iyya), horse neighing (ghayr lafziyya tabi'iyya), smoke (ghayr lafziyya 'aqliyya).
4
Apply to new examples
Practice with examples from Islamic sciences. For instance, the word 'salat' linguistically conventionally indicates the ritual prayer; the sight of a crescent moon naturally indicates the start of a month; the rational inference from the complexity of the universe to a Creator is non-linguistic rational indication.
Check your classification against the definitions.

Enumeration of indication into six sections. Indication and its sections: what has the signifier being the utterance, and non-linguistic: what has the signifier not being the utterance. Each of them is of three types: first: linguistic conventional, like the indication of the word 'Zayd' of its referent. Second: linguistic natural, like the indication of the word 'uh uh' – with damm on the hamza and sukun on the ha, and it is said with fatha – of chest pain; for nature compels the production of this sound at the occurrence of pain in the chest. Third: linguistic rational, like the indication of the sound 'Dayz' heard from behind the wall of the existence of the speaker. Fourth: non-linguistic conventional, like the indication of the four markers of their referents. Fifth: non-linguistic natural, like the indication of the horse's neighing of the demand for water and fodder. Sixth: non-linguistic rational, like the indication of smoke of fire. 'al-wad'iyya' etc.: attributed to convention such that convention has an input: either setting the very word for its meaning, like setting 'Zayd' for his essence, or setting the parts of the word for their meanings, like setting 'Zayd' and 'qa'im' for their meanings; for although the setting of the composite for its meaning is not established from the setter, the setting of its parts for their meanings is established. al-wad' in language is 'nihadan' and in technical terminology: 'the specification of something for something such that whenever the first thing is uttered or perceived, the second thing is understood from it.' What is meant by linguistic conventional indication is the utterance such that whenever it is uttered its meaning is understood for knowledge of its convention. 'al-tabi'iyya' etc.: it is that which is produced by the production of nature of the signifier at the occurrence of the signified, whether the signifier is an utterance, like the indication of 'uh uh' of coughing, or non-linguistic, like the kicking of the animal for fodder. 'uh uh': with fatha on the hamza or damm with the ha. It is said: 'the man said uh' if he coughed. As for with damm on the hamza and sukun on the kha' with shadda, it indicates pain, and if the hamza is fatha it indicates regret. This is in al-Qamus and others; the author has been lenient in the interpretation of 'uh uh'. This is an example of linguistic natural indication. The kicking of the animal is an example of non-linguistic indication. In al-Sirah, 'rakada': movement of the leg, and 'rakada al-faras' if it ran, and from it is His word: 'And kick with your foot'. 'al-'aqliyya': it is attributed to reason because of the lack of intervention of convention and nature in it; the relation of influence is considered in it. So it includes the indication of the effect of the cause, like smoke of the cause like fire, and the indication of the cause of the effect, and the indication of one of two effects of the other, like the indication of smoke of the heat, both being effects of fire. 'al-nusub': i.e., what is set up on the road to perceive the distance, and the lines and signals and contracts i.e., the fingers' joints which indicate numbers.

Conventional, Natural, and Rational Indication
Detailed explanation of the three types of connection: conventional, natural, rational.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conventional (wad'iyya), natural (tabi'iyya), and rational ('aqliyya) indication based on the source of the link between sign and meaning.

Core Idea
The three types of connection differ in what establishes the link: deliberate assignment, natural causation, or rational inference.

The passage elaborates on the three types of connection that can exist between a sign and its meaning. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly interpreting signs in Islamic sciences, especially when dealing with linguistic texts versus natural phenomena and logical proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand conventional indication (wad'iyya)
The link is set by a deliberate act of assignment (wad'), either divine or human. The sign is assigned to the meaning arbitrarily. Example: the word 'Zayd' is conventionally set to indicate a specific person.
The passage defines wad' as 'the specification of something for something such that whenever the first thing is uttered or perceived, the second thing is understood.'
2
Understand natural indication (tabi'iyya)
The link arises from nature itself without any intentional assignment. The sign is naturally produced by the signified situation. Example: the sound 'uh uh' naturally occurs when someone has chest pain; the horse neighs when it desires water.
The passage notes that nature compels the production of the sign at the occurrence of the signified.
3
Understand rational indication ('aqliyya)
The link is established through a rational inference. The sign does not inherently refer to the meaning, but reason deduces the connection. Example: smoke indicates fire because reason infers that smoke is an effect of fire.
The passage says rational indication 'considers the relation of influence,' so it includes cause-effect and effect-cause inferences.
4
Compare and contrast
Test yourself: for each example, ask: did someone assign this sign to this meaning? If yes, it's conventional. Is the sign naturally produced by the situation (like a reflex)? If yes, it's natural. If neither, and you need reasoning to connect the two, it's rational.
Note that linguistic natural indication is still linguistic because the sign is a sound, but the connection is not conventional.

The Three Types of Linguistic Conventional Indication
Conclusion of six types and introduction of three subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three subtypes of linguistic conventional indication (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) and explain why the logician prioritizes this type of indication.

Core Idea
Linguistic conventional indication is the easiest for teaching and learning, and it comes in three exhaustive forms.

In this passage, the author completes the discussion of the six types of indication and explains that the logician (al-mantiqi) restricts his investigation to linguistic conventional indication. The reason is that it is the most effective for conveying knowledge. He then introduces the three subtypes—mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam—based on a rational division of the relationship between the word and its meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the focus of the logician
Locate the sentence where the author says 'المنطقي إنما يبحث عن الدلالة اللفظية الوضعية'. Why does the logician limit his investigation to this type alone?
2
Understand the reason given
The author states that conveying and acquiring knowledge (الإفادة والاستفادة) is facilitated easily by linguistic conventional indication, unlike other types which involve difficulty. Contrast this with natural, rational, or written indication.
The passage lists natural (طبعية), rational (عقلية), signals (إشارات), and writing (كتابة) as less convenient.
3
Recognize the three subtypes
Note the three terms: مطابقية (mutabaqa), تضمن (tadammun), التزام (iltizam). They correspond respectively to the word indicating the entirety of its meaning, a part of it, or something external that is necessarily connected.
These are the three divisions of linguistic conventional indication relied upon in discourses and sciences.
4
Grasp the rational division
The author explains that when a word indicates a meaning by convention, that meaning either is exactly the meaning set (mutabaqa), or is a part of it (tadammun), or is outside but connected (iltizam). This is an exhaustive division because no other relation is possible.
The passage states: 'هذا الحصر عقلي ليس فيها احتمال سوى الثلاثة' (this enumeration is rational; there is no possibility other than these three).

Classifying Singular Terms by Unity and Multiplicity of Meaning
Author presents the second classification of singular terms based on unity or multiplicity of meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any singular term into one of the six categories (juz'i, mutawati', mushakkik, mushtarak, manqul, haqiqa/majaz) and determine whether two categories can coexist in the same term.

Core Idea
A term's meaning is either one or many; categories within a division are exclusive but can combine across divisions.

The previous lesson classified singular terms by independence of meaning. This lesson introduces a second classification based on whether the term has one meaning or many. This distinction is crucial for understanding how terms are used in Islamic texts, especially when a term may have shifted meaning or multiple usages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion of the new division
The passage divides singular terms based on whether they have one meaning or many meanings.
This is the second division of al-mufrad.
2
List the three types for terms with one meaning
Find juz'i (particular), mutawati' (univocal), mushakkik (equivocal). Mutawati' applies equally to all instances, while mushakkik applies with priority to some.
Example: 'insan' is mutawati', 'wujud' is mushakkik.
3
List the three types for terms with many meanings
Find mushtarak (shared), manqul (transferred), haqiqa/majaz (literal/metaphorical). Manqul has a new conventional meaning; haqiqa/majaz has a literal and a figurative sense.
Example: 'ayn' is mushtarak, 'salat' is manqul, 'asad' is haqiqa/majaz.
4
Analyze the disagreement about the scope of the division
Some say the division applies to all singular terms; the well-known view restricts the first three to nouns only, while the others apply to all.
Al-Bihari adds that metaphor primarily occurs in nouns.
5
Apply the logical rule of mutual exclusivity across categorizations
Categories within the same division (e.g., juz'i and mutawati') cannot coexist in one term. But categories from different divisions can combine (e.g., juz'i and mushtarak).
Test with 'Zayd': it is juz'i and can be mushtarak if multiple persons.

Substance and Accident: The First Two Categories
Author lists the ten categories, defines substance and accident, then examines quantity and quality as accident categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define substance and accident using the criterion of existence in a subject, and correctly classify any given entity as substance or accident.

Core Idea
Everything that exists is either self-subsistent (substance) or dependent on a substrate (accident).

In this lesson, we will examine the foundational division of all existing things into substance and accident. This distinction is the basis for the ten categories. Understanding it is crucial for logical definition and for analyzing arguments about the nature of things, especially in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the high genera
Read the first lines of the passage: the author states there are ten high genera (al-ajnas al-ʿāliya). Notice that they encompass everything in the world. They are also called the ten categories (al-maqūlāt al-ʿashr).
The term 'high genera' means they are the most general classes under which everything falls.
2
Extract the definitions of substance and accident
Locate the sentences: 'al-jawhar: huwa al-mawjūd lā fī mawḍūʿ...' and 'al-ʿaraḍ: huwa al-mawjūd fī mawḍūʿ'. Substance exists not in a subject; it is self-subsistent. Accident exists in a subject. Note that the author gives bodies as an example of substance.
The phrase 'fī mawḍūʿ' means dependent on a substrate for existence.
3
Apply the definitions to examples
Consider common things: a body (like a stone) is a substance; its color is an accident because color exists in the body. Think of other examples from Islamic sciences: the substance of a human (body and soul) versus accidents like knowledge or life.
In kalam, substances are often atoms; accidents are temporary attributes.
4
Reflect on the role of categories in logic
The author notes that investigation of the categories is not from logic itself, but early logicians included them for benefit in definition and description. This shows that logic uses these categories as tools, not as its core subject.
This distinction helps you appreciate why definitions in fiqh or usul al-fiqh often reference substance/accident.

Author lists the ten categories, defines substance and accident, then examines quantity and quality as accident categories.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Quantity is divisible by essence; quality gives properties without necessitating division or relation.

After the general division of accidents, the author delves into two specific categories: quantity (al-kamm) and quality (al-kayf). Guided Path

The author says: 'الكم هو العرض الذي يقبل القسمة والتجزّي لذاته' (quantity is the accident that accepts division and partition by its essence). Note that it is the essence of quantity to be divisible, unlike quality.
This definition sets quantity apart from other accidents.
2
Distinguish continuous and discrete quantity
The criterion is whether the parts share a common boundary. If yes (e.g., the parts of a line), it is continuous (al-muttaṣil). If no (e.g., separate units of number), it is discrete (al-munfaṣil). The author gives magnitude and time as examples of continuous, number as discrete.
Continuous quantity includes both stable (magnitude) and non-stable (time).
3
Break down continuous quantity further
Continuous quantity is either non-stable (time, where parts do not coexist) or stable (magnitude). Magnitude divides by dimensions: body (3D), surface (2D), line (1D). Note the term 'teaching body' (jism taʿlīmī) refers to a geometric body.
This subdivision is purely conceptual, not physical bodies in the sense of substances.
4
Identify the four kinds of quality
Quality is an accident that does not require division or relation. The author lists four inductive categories: sensible (e.g., colors, sounds), psychic (e.g., knowledge, emotions), those specific to quantities (e.g., shape, evenness), and dispositional (e.g., health, strength).
The phrase 'استقرائي' indicates this enumeration is based on observation and not a priori deduction.
5
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider the quality of 'knowledge' (ʿilm): it is a psychic quality. The quantity of a prayer's time (four rakʿas for ẓuhr) is discrete quantity (number). The continuous quantity of distance for shortening prayer (travel) is magnitude. These examples show the practical relevance.

Form and Matter of Syllogism; Epistemic Classification of Premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the form and matter of a syllogism and classify premises according to their epistemic status, identifying which types are used in demonstration, dialectic, and other arts.

Core Idea
Syllogistic validity depends on both correct form and appropriate matter (premises of suitable certainty).

In this passage, the author shifts from discussing the form of syllogisms (the four figures and their conditions) to the matter – the propositions that constitute the premises. The matter is classified by its epistemic quality, determining which type of argument (burhan, jadal, khitaba, etc.) it belongs to. Understanding this classification is essential for evaluating the strength of arguments in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish form and matter
Form is the arrangement of premises (figure and mood); matter is the content of premises (propositions). The author states that every syllogism must have both.
Form has been covered earlier; now focus is on matter.
2
Identify the binary division of premises
Premises either produce assent (tasdiq) or another effect (like imagination). Those producing no effect (doubt) are disregarded.
3
Classify premises producing assent
Premises producing assent yield either opinion (maznunat) or certainty (yaqiniyyat). Opinionative premises give only probability.
4
Subdivide certain premises
Certain premises are subdivided into six types: necessarily accepted (wajib qabuluha), well-known (mashhurat), received (maqbulat), conceded (musallamat), similative (mushabbihaat), and estimative (wahmiyyat). Each has a distinct basis.
Necessarily accepted are decisive; well-known are based on public fame; received on authority; conceded on disputant agreement; similative on resemblance; estimative on judgment of the estimative faculty.
5
Connect to the five arts
The type of premises determines the art: necessarily accepted for demonstration (burhan); well-known and conceded for dialectic (jadal); similative for sophistry (safsatah); imaginative for poetics (shi'r); opinionative for rhetoric (khitaba).
This connection is derived from the broader Islamic logical tradition, not explicitly detailed in this passage.

The Classification Tree and the Remaining Three Certain Premises
Systematic classification of the six types of certain premises via a logical division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the logical division of certain premises into awwaliyyat, mushahadat, fitriyyat, hadsiyyat, mutawatirat, and tajribiyyat, and define each.

Core Idea
Certain premises are classified by a binary tree: first, whether conceptualization suffices; if not, whether an intermediary is needed; and so on.

The passage provides a systematic classification of all six certain premises. In this lesson, we will follow the logical division starting from whether conceptualization suffices, leading to awwaliyyat or further divisions that yield mushahadat, fitriyyat, hadsiyyat, mutawatirat, and tajribiyyat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first division
The passage states: either conceptualization of the two extremes with the relation suffices (awwaliyyat), or not.
2
Follow the second division

3

4
Finally, the last two
For those without intuition, the judgment comes from either widespread reports (mutawatirat) or repeated experiments (tajribiyyat).
