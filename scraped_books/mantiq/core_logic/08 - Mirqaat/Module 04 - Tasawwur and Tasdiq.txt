Why Logic begins with Defining 'Ilm
Author explains why logic begins with defining 'ilm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author of Mirqat al-Wusul begins with a definition of 'ilm and how this relates to the purpose of logic.

Core Idea
The goal of logic—safeguarding thought—cannot be understood without first defining knowledge itself.

This lesson examines why the logical text opens with the concept of knowledge. It connects the purpose of logic to the nature of 'ilm, showing that without a clear definition, the very need for a safeguard against error becomes unintelligible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's stated reason for beginning with 'ilm
Read the opening sentence: 'إنما ابتدأ بالعلم وتقسيمه؛ لأن غاية المنطق مستلزمة لرسمه'. The author says he began with knowledge and its division because the goal of logic necessitates its definition.
The goal of logic is to provide a safeguard (عصمة) against error in thought.
2
Trace the logical chain from logic's goal to the definition of 'ilm
The passage argues: the goal of logic is the safeguard → thought can err → theoretical knowledge requires thought → we must know what knowledge is to know that logic is a safeguard. This chain makes defining 'ilm foundational.
3
Recognise the problem of circularity avoided
If we don't define 'ilm first, we cannot establish that logic's product (the corrective method) qualifies as knowledge or safeguards it. The author preempts this by starting with 'ilm.
4
Understand the role of 'ilm's divisions
The author also divides 'ilm into intuitive (بديهي) and theoretical (نظري), which is necessary because theoretical knowledge is subject to error and thus requires the safeguard that logic provides.
This division is part of the definition of 'ilm itself.

The Five Meanings of 'Ilm and the Maturidi Position
Author lists five meanings of 'ilm and discusses Maturidi position

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the five meanings of 'ilm listed by the author and explain the Maturidi position on knowledge.

Core Idea
Scholars agree on 'ilm's function as disclosure but differ on its essence—five main positions, with Maturidis affirming it as a simple attribute with relation.

This lesson unpacks the various definitions of 'ilm presented in the text. You will learn the five meanings, the sixth addition, and how the Maturidi school diverges from the philosophers on whether knowledge is an eternal attribute or a temporal occurrence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five meanings of 'ilm from the text
The passage lists: (1) attainment of the form (حصول الصورة), (2) the form attained (الصورة الحاصلة), (3) presence in the perceiver (الحاضر عند المدرك), (4) the soul's acceptance (قبول النفس), (5) relation (الإضافة). Note that the sixth (perceptive state) is mentioned separately.
These correspond to the author's list: the first four are attributed to philosophers, the fifth to some theologians.
2
Note the sixth meaning and its context
The passage mentions a sixth view: 'the perceptive state' (الحالة الإدراكية). It is not part of the author's five but is a well-known school. Also, 'the present in the perceiver' is debated whether it is distinct from the attained form.
3
Analyze the Maturidi view in contrast to the philosophers
The Maturidis say knowledge is a simple attribute with a relation (صفة بسيطة ذات إضافة), called the revelatory state. They affirm it is inherent in the knower, not occurring only at the moment of apprehension, unlike the philosophers who say knowledge occurs temporally when apprehending the known.
This has theological implications for Allah's knowledge: it is eternal, not dependent on creation.
4
Recognize the real nature of the disagreement
The author stresses that this is not a verbal dispute but a real difference (اختلاف معنوي). Each position has distinct implications for how knowledge is understood, especially in theology.
Understanding this helps avoid superficial reconciliation.

Two Definitions of Tasdiq: Philosophers vs. Imam al-Razi
Author presents and contrasts definitions of tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the philosophers' and Imam al-Razi's definitions of tasdiq and identify the three differences between them.

Core Idea
Tasdiq: one simple judgment or a composite of judgment and conceptions?

In this passage, we encounter a foundational debate in Islamic logic: is tasdiq (assent) a simple judgment that accompanies conceptions, or is it itself a composite of judgment and the conceptions of subject, predicate, and relation? The philosophers argue for simplicity, while Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi argues for composition. Grasping this difference is essential for understanding how propositions are analyzed in the Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
Locate the definition of tasdiq according to the philosophers (al-hukama') and according to Imam al-Razi. The philosophers define it as 'al-hukm al-muqarin lil-tasawwurat' (the judgment accompanying the conceptions). Al-Razi defines it as 'majmu' al-hukm wa tasawwurat al-atraf' (the composite of the judgment and the conceptions of the terms).
Note that both agree tasdiq involves judgment and conceptions; they differ on whether the conceptions are part of tasdiq or external.
2
Extract the components of each definition
For the philosophers, tasdiq is just the judgment (hukm); the three conceptions (subject, predicate, relation) are conditions (shart) for its existence but external to it. For al-Razi, tasdiq includes both the judgment and those three conceptions as internal parts (ajza').
Al-Razi's example: assenting to 'Zayd is standing' produces three items of knowledge: Zayd, standing, and the relational meaning (copula).
3
Compare the three differences
The passage lists three specific differences: (1) Tasdiq is simple (basiṭ) on the philosophers' view, composite (murakkab) on al-Razi's. (2) The conceptions are external conditions for the philosophers, internal parts for al-Razi. (3) The judgment is identical to tasdiq for the philosophers, but only a part for al-Razi.
These differences have implications for the logical analysis of propositions in Islamic sciences.
4
Understand the commentator's position
The author of this text (the commentator) endorses the philosophers' view, arguing that tasdiq is a single real reality (haqiqa waqi'iyya muhassala), not a conventional composite. He calls al-Razi's composite view 'a conventional thing' (amr i'tibari).
This shows how logical definitions are debated and settled within the tradition.

Thus the three conceptions are a condition for the existence of tasdiq, and hence there is no tasdiq without conception. Second, the conception of the two terms and the relation is a condition for tasdiq, external to it on their view, but a part (i.e., internal component) of it according to him. Third, judgment itself is tasdiq according to them, but according to him it is an internal component. Thus said al-Allama al-Razi in Sharh al-Shamsiyya.

Commentary
This passage defines tasdiq (assent) and presents two contrasting views: that of the philosophers (al-hukama') and that of Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. The philosophers define tasdiq as simply the judgment (al-hukm) that accompanies three conceptions (the subject, predicate, and relation). The three conceptions are conditions for the existence of tasdiq, external to it. Imam al-Razi, on the other hand, defines tasdiq as a composite of both the judgment and the conceptions of the terms. He illustrates this with an example: when one says 'Zayd is standing' and assents to it, three items of knowledge arise: knowledge of Zayd, knowledge of 'standing', and knowledge of the relational meaning (the copula), which in Persian is 'hast' for affirmation and 'nist' for negation. The commentator then endorses the view of the philosophers, arguing that tasdiq is a single real entity, not a composite of multiple ideas (which would be conventional/mental constructs). He then lists three specific differences between the two views: (1) Tasdiq is simple (basiṭ) for the philosophers, composite (murakkab) for al-Razi. (2) The conceptions of terms and the relation are external conditions for tasdiq on the philosophers' view, but internal parts on al-Razi's view. (3) The judgment itself is identical to tasdiq according to the philosophers, but only an internal part according to al-Razi. The source is attributed to al-Allama al-Razi's Sharh al-Shamsiyya.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge cannot be entirely self-evident nor entirely theoretical; both are necessary to avoid circularity and infinite regress.

This passage from the Mirqat introduces a fundamental division in Islamic epistemology: some conceptions and assents are self-evident, requiring no thought, while others are theoretical, requiring reflection. The author also provides a powerful argument for why knowledge must include both types, and hints at a debate with Imam al-Razi over the scope of the self-evident.

Core Idea
A word is compound only if its parts intentionally signify parts of its meaning.

This passage precisely defines what makes a word compound (murakkab) by listing four necessary conditions. Understanding these conditions allows you to correctly classify words that may appear compound but are actually single (mufrad), such as proper names or particles. The Three Types of Single Word: Ism, Kalimah, Adah
Author divides the single word into three types based on independence and time relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any single Arabic word from the Qur'an or Hadith as ism, kalimah, or adah using the criteria of meaning independence and time association.

Core Idea
Every single word in Arabic is either an independent noun, an independent verb with time, or a dependent tool.

Before we can analyse propositions and syllogisms, we must master the building blocks: single words. The logicians divided single words into three precise types. This classification, deceptively simple, is the doorway to all logical analysis in Arabic. In this lesson you will learn to apply the two criteria that distinguish ism from kalimah from adah, using examples directly from the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1
Check for independence of meaning
Ask: Can this word be understood on its own without needing another word to complete its meaning? For example, 'Zaid' is understood alone. 'From' cannot be understood alone; it needs something like 'Zaid' ('from Zaid').
If the meaning is not independent, it is an adah (particle) and you are done.
2
If independent, check for time association
Ask: Does the word's meaning inherently indicate a specific time (past, present, future)? For example, 'daraba' (he struck) is past tense. 'Yadribu' (he strikes) is present/future. 'Qā'im' (standing) does not indicate time by itself.
If it does not indicate time, it is an ism (noun). If it indicates time, it is a kalimah (verb).
3
Apply to a concrete example
Take the word 'raḥīm' (merciful) from the Basmala. It is independent (you know what 'merciful' means) and does not carry a time (it is an attribute, not past/present/future). Hence it is ism.
4
Distinguish between logic and grammar terminology
Note that the logicians use 'kalimah' specifically for verbs, while grammarians use 'kalimah' for any word. Conversely, the logicians use 'adah' for particles, while grammarians call them 'ḥurūf'. Do not confuse the two terminologies.
This shift is crucial when reading logic texts.

The Four Variations in Skeptical Terms
Author explains the four types of variation in skeptical terms with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the four types of variation (awwaliyya, awlawiyya, shidda, ziyada) that characterize skeptical (mushakkik) terms.

Core Idea
Skeptical terms vary in four distinct ways: causal priority, essential preference, qualitative intensity, and quantitative increase.

The author elaborates on why some universal terms are called 'skeptical' (mushakkik): their applicability to individuals is not equal but varies in specific ways. He defines four types of variation: awwaliyya (priority in causality), awlawiyya (preference in essence), shidda (intensity in quality), and ziyada (increase in quantity). Each has a precise logical meaning and is illustrated with examples from metaphysics and physics.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reason for the name 'mushakkik'
The term is called 'skeptical' because the gradation in applicability causes doubt (shakk) in the observer. This is not arbitrary but follows defined logical patterns.
The four variations are the patterns.
2
Define awwaliyya (priority in causality)
The universal applies to one individual as the cause for its applicability to another. Example: 'existence' applies to Zayd (the cause) and then to his son Amr (the effect).
The priority is causal, not temporal.
3
Define awlawiyya (preference in essence)
The universal applies to one individual by virtue of itself and to another by virtue of an external relation. Example: 'light' applies to the sun intrinsically and to the earth by reflection.
One individual has the property essentially, the other derivatively.
4
Define shidda (intensity) and ziyada (increase)
Shidda: one individual has more instances of the quality, indistinguishable in position (e.g., whiteness in snow vs. ivory). Ziyada: similar but instances are distinguishable in position (e.g., quantity). Note: Peripatetics restrict shidda to quality and ziyada to quantity; Illuminationists do not distinguish.
Memorize the Peripatetic distinction.
5
Apply the variations to new examples
For each variation, think of an example from Islamic sciences, such as 'knowledge' for awlawiyya (God knows essentially, humans acquire knowledge), or 'life' for awwaliyya.

Mental attributes exist only in the mind; confusing them with external existence produces fallacious arguments.

In Islamic logic and philosophy, a common fallacy is to treat concepts that exist only in the mind as if they have external reality. For example, when we say 'man is a universal', the universality is a mental accident, not an external property. In this lesson, we will examine this fallacy and learn how to detect it, using the example of the impossible being considered existent.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the mental/external distinction
Understand that some attributes (like universality, origination) are mental accidents that require mental existence; they do not exist externally.
2
Identify the first example: 'Man is a universal'
Note that 'universal' is a mental attribute; it is false to think man is universal in external reality.
3
Analyze the second example: 'The impossible is existent'
The argument treats impossibility as an external attribute, leading to a paradox. Recognize that impossibility is a mental consideration.
4
Apply the resolution
Identify that the fallacy arises from mistaking a mental predicate for an external one. The correct response is to clarify the distinction.
5
Formulate the general rule
Whenever an argument uses a mental attribute as if it were external, the argument is suspect. Always check whether the predicate exists in the mind or in reality.

Fallacy of Confusing Example with the Thing Itself
Author identifies the fallacy of confusing an example with the thing itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of taking an example for the thing itself and explain why it invalidates the syllogism.

Core Idea
An example of fire is not fire; confusing them leads to a false syllogism.

The author begins with a fallacy that initially seems valid: saying of a picture of fire 'this is fire' and then concluding it burns because all fire burns. The error lies in the minor premise. This fallacy also underlies the denial of mental existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy structure
The argument: 'This (example of fire) is fire; every fire burns; so this burns.' The minor premise is false because the example is not real fire.
The fallacy is called 'taking the example in place of the thing'.
2
See how it appears in the mental existence debate
The deniers of mental existence argued: if the image of fire in the mind were fire, the mind would burn. They confused the mental image with the external reality.
The philosophers later clarify that the mental image is only shadowy existence.
3
Distinguish example from reality
Always check whether the term used in the argument refers to the actual object or a representation. If it's a representation, the minor premise 'this is X' is false.
This distinction is crucial in kalam and fiqh when discussing concepts vs. realities.

Philosophers' Proof for Mental Existence
Author presents the philosophers' proof for mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the philosophers' categorical syllogism for mental existence and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
We make true judgments about impossibilities; that requires their existence in the mind.

The philosophers begin with agreed facts: we conceive impossibilities and judge them with true statements such as 'the impossible is possible in general.' Since these objects have no external existence, they must exist in the mind as mental entities. This is a categorical syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The philosophers aim to prove that things have a mental existence (al-wujud al-dhihni) in addition to external existence.
The conclusion is: 'Therefore, the impossible exists in the mind.'
2
Extract the major premise
The major premise: 'Everything about which a true fixed judgment is made has existence in the affair itself (fi nafs al-amr).' In other words, for a judgment to be true, its subject must have some mode of existence.
This is the universal affirmative premise.
3
Extract the minor premise
The minor premise: 'The impossible and the conjunction of contradictories are subjects of true fixed judgments (e.g., 'the impossible is possible in general').'
These have no external existence, so the existence must be mental.
4
Assess the syllogism's validity
The syllogism follows the first figure (Barbara): all A are B; all C are A; therefore all C are B. Here A = 'objects of true judgment', B = 'exist in some mode', C = 'the impossible'. The conclusion 'the impossible exists in the mind' is valid.
The philosophers add a second proof about universals, but the core is this syllogism.

Theologians' Objection and Philosophers' Response on Mental Existence
Author presents the theologians' objection and the philosophers' response on mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the theologians' conditional objection to mental existence and explain the philosophers' distinction between mental image (sura) and essence (huwiyya).

Core Idea
Mental images are shadowy; they lack the real attributes of the things they represent.

The theologians objected: if conceiving heat made heat in the mind, the mind would become hot; since it does not, conceiving heat does not produce real heat. The philosophers answer by distinguishing between the mental image (with shadowy existence) and the real essence. This distinction resolves the objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the theologians' objection as a conditional syllogism
The objection: If conceiving heat caused real heat to exist in the mind, then the mind would be hot (condition: antecedent P = conceiving heat causes real heat in mind; consequent Q = mind is hot). But the mind is not hot (not Q). Therefore, conceiving heat does not cause real heat in the mind (not P). This is modus tollens.
The theologians used this to argue that mental existence is impossible, because if it were real, physical effects would follow.
2
Extract the philosophers' response
The philosophers reject the antecedent: what exists in the mind is not the real essence (huwiyya) of heat, but a mental image (sura dhihniyya) with a shadowy existence (wujud zilli). Such an image does not have the real property of heat, so the mind does not become hot.
The response introduces a distinction between two kinds of existence: mental (shadowy) and external (real).
3
Apply the distinction to the mountain example
Similarly, when we conceive a mountain, the vastness of the real mountain cannot fit in the mind, but the universal concept of a mountain (its quiddity) can be conceived without its external size.
The objection that the mountain is too large is answered by distinguishing the essence (huwiyya) from the concept (mafhūm).

ومنها: أخذ مثال الشيء مكانه كما تقول لمثال النار : إنه نار وكل نار محرق فهو محرق. وهذا الاشتباه هو الذي احتج به المنكرون للوجود الذهني حيث قالوا: لو حصلت الأشياء بأنفسها لزم. المنكرون للوجود الذهني إلخ: اعلم أن الحكماء وغيرهم متفقون على أن النار مثلا لها وجود به يترتب عليها أحكامها، ويصدر عنها آثارها من الإحراق والإضاءة وغيرهما، وهذا هو الوجود الخارجي العيني. واختلفوا في أن لها وجودا آخر غير الوجود الخارجي أم لا؟ فذهب الحكماء إلى أن لها وجودا ذهنيا أيضا يقال له: الوجود الظلي. وأثبتوا بأنا نتصور الممتنع واجتماع النقيضين وغيرهما لا وجود له في الخارج، ونحكم عليها بأحكام ثابتة صادقة كالإمكان العام وغيرها والحكم بالأحكام الثابتة عليها يقتضي ثبوتها في نفس الأمر، وليس لها ثبوت في الخارج؛ فلا بد أن يكون في الذهن وهو المطلوب. وبأن من المفهومات ما هو متصف بالكلية التي ثبوتية مقتضية لوجود الموصوف لها، وكل موجود في الخارج فهو جزئي؛ فيكون المتصف بالكلي موجودا في الذهن. واعترض المتكلمون النافون بالوجود الذهني بأنه لو اقتضى تصور الشيء حصوله في الذهن لزم كون الذهن حارًا أو باردا، فإذا تصورنا الحرارة والبرودة حصلنا في ذهننا ولا معنى للحار والبارد إلا ما فيه الحرارة والبرودة، وبأن حصول حقيقة الجبل والسماء مع عظمها في ذهننا غير معقول؟ وأجاب الحكماء منهما: بأن الحاصل في الذهن صورة ذهنية موجودة بوجود ظلي لا هويّة عينية موجودة بوجود أصيل. والحار ما يقوم به هوية الحرارة وماهيتها موجودة بوجودة عيني لا ما يقوم به ماهية الحرارة موجودة بوجود ذهني وبأن ما يمتنع حصوله في الذهن هو هوية الجبل والسماء؛ فإن هوياتهما موجودة بوجود خارجي يمتنع أن يحصل في أذهاننا. وأما مفهوماتها الكلية وماهياتها الموجودة بوجودات ظلية لا يمتنع حصولها في الذهن؛ إذ ليست موصوفة بصفات تلك الهويات. ثم لا يخفى عليك أن بعضا من المتكلمين كالإمام وتابعيه لم ينكروا بالوجود الذهني.
And among them: taking the example of a thing in place of it, as you say of the example of fire: 'It is fire, and every fire is burning, so it is burning.' And this confusion is what the deniers of mental existence used as evidence, for they said: 'If things were to occur by themselves, it would follow.' The deniers of mental existence etc.: Know that the philosophers and others agree that fire, for example, has an existence by which its judgments are arranged and its effects issue, such as burning and lighting, and this is the external concrete existence. But they differ as to whether it has another existence besides the external existence or not. The philosophers hold that it has a mental existence as well, called 'shadowy existence' (al-wujud al-zilli). They prove this by saying: We conceive the impossible, the conjunction of contradictories, and other things that have no existence in the external world, and we judge them with true fixed judgments such as general possibility and others; and judging them with fixed judgments requires their establishment in the affair itself, and they have no establishment in the external world; so they must be in the mind, which is what is sought. And because among concepts, some are described by universality, which is an affirmative property requiring the existence of its subject, and everything existing in the external world is particular; so the subject of universality exists in the mind. The theologians who deny mental existence objected: If conceiving a thing required its occurrence in the mind, it would follow that the mind would become hot or cold; for when we conceive heat and cold, they occur in our mind, and there is no meaning of hot and cold except that which contains heat and cold. And because the occurrence of the reality of the mountain and the sky with their vastness in our mind is unintelligible. The philosophers answered both objections: That which occurs in the mind is a mental image existing with a shadowy existence, not an external essence existing with a real existence. And the hot is that in which the essence of heat and its quiddity exist with a real existence, not that in which the quiddity of heat exists with a mental existence. And that which is impossible to occur in the mind is the essence (huwiyya) of the mountain and the sky; for their essences exist with an external existence, which is impossible to occur in our minds. As for their universal concepts and quiddities existing with shadowy existences, their occurrence in the mind is not impossible, since they are not described with the attributes of those essences. Then it should not be hidden from you that some of the theologians, like the Imam and his followers, did not deny mental existence.

Commentary
This passage presents the fallacy of confusing an example (mithal) of a thing with the thing itself. For instance, one might say of a picture or mental image of fire, 'This is fire,' and then conclude it burns, which is false. This fallacy is identified by the author as the same confusion that led the deniers of mental existence (al-munkirun lil-wujud al-dhihni) to argue: if the image of fire were truly fire in the mind, then the mind would burn, etc. The author then launches into a detailed discussion of the debate on mental existence. He states that philosophers (hukama') and others agree on external existence but differ on whether things have a separate mental existence. The philosophers affirm mental existence (al-wujud al-dhihni), also called shadowy existence (al-wujud al-zilli). They provide proofs: (1) We conceive impossibilities and other non-existents and make true judgments about them (e.g., 'the impossible is possible in general'), which requires that these objects have some sort of existence in the mind. (2) Some concepts are universal, and universality is a property that requires a subject; since external existents are particular, the universal must exist in the mind. The theologians (mutakallimun) who deny mental existence object: If conceiving heat made heat occur in the mind, the mind would become hot; similarly, the vast mountain cannot fit in the mind. The philosophers answer: What occurs in the mind is a mental image (sura dhihniyya) with a shadowy existence, not the actual external essence. The mind becomes hot only if the essence of heat subsists in it with real existence, not with mental existence. The mountain's essence (huwiyya) is too large for the mind, but its universal concept and quiddity can be conceived. Finally, the author notes that some theologians (like Imam al-Ghazali and his followers) did not deny mental existence, indicating a spectrum of views.

Key Takeaway
The theologians' conditional objection is refuted by the distinction between mental image (shadowy existence) and real essence; the mind contains only the form, not the reality.
