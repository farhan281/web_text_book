The Dependency Rule of Implicit and Comitant Signification
Author defines implicit and comitant signification and states dependency rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dependency relationship among the three types of verbal signification and resolve the objection about the universality of concomitants.

Core Idea
Implicit and comitant signification are contingent on literal, but literal stands independent.

In the Islamic logical tradition, words convey meaning in three ways: literally, implicitly, and comitantly. This lesson explores a foundational rule: implicit and comitant signification cannot exist without literal, but literal can exist without them. We'll also tackle a subtle objection that challenges this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its grounds
Locate the core statement: 'Implicit and comitant signification do not exist without literal.' Note the three analogies (part-whole, necessary-necessitated, follower-followed) that justify the dependency.
The rule is not just an assertion; it is reasoned.
2
Examine the objection and response
Find the objection: 'Every meaning has a necessary concomitant.' The author responds by restricting comitant to the 'clear' concomitant that the mind transfers to. Understand why this restriction is necessary to preserve the rule.
The objection threatens the possibility of literal existing alone.
3
Define the three types
From the passage, extract the definitions: literal (whole meaning), implicit (part of meaning), comitant (external necessary concomitant). Note that comitant requires clarity to be relevant.
Use the example of 'human' and 'capable of knowledge' to see the clear concomitant in action.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Think of a word like 'الصلاة' (prayer). What does it signify literally (the ritual act), implicitly (its components), and comitantly (conditions like purification)? Verify that the implicit and comitant depend on the literal.
This shows the rule's practical relevance in fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of verbal signification: literal (al-mutabaqah), implicit (al-tadammun), and comitant (al-iltizam). The author states a key rule: implicit and comitant signification cannot exist without literal signification. The reason given is that a part cannot be conceived without the whole, a necessary concomitant cannot be conceived without the necessitated, and a follower cannot exist without the followed. However, literal signification can exist without them because a word may be placed for a simple meaning that has no parts or necessary concomitants. The author then anticipates an objection: 'Every meaning has a necessary concomitant, the least being that it is not other than itself.' The response distinguishes the relevant concomitant as the 'clear' (bayyin) one that the mind readily transfers to, such as the concomitance between 'human' and 'capable of knowledge and writing.' The author also discusses an example concerning blindness and sight, clarifying that blindness does not include sight as a part. The commentary further explains that the logical relation is 'real' for logicians but 'estimated' for Arab grammarians. The two cases of literal without others are: (1) simple meanings like the Necessary Existent, and (2) meanings we conceive without thinking of their concomitants.

The Four Relations between Two Universals
Author explains the four types of relation between two universals: equality, absolute generality, mutual exclusivity, and partial overlap.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the four types of relation (equality, absolute generality, mutual exclusivity, partial overlap) holds between any two given universals and express that relation in categorical propositions.

Core Idea
Four relations cover every possible logical connection between two universal terms.

In Islamic logic (al-Manṭiq), understanding how two universals relate is crucial for building definitions and syllogisms. For example, is 'man' the same as 'rational'? Is 'animal' broader than 'man'? This lesson introduces the four relations and their propositional expressions, as explained by the author of Mirqāt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two universals
Ensure both terms are universals (kullī), not particulars. The author only considers universals because particulars cannot be used in syllogisms as middle terms.
2
Check coextensiveness
Does each term apply to every individual the other applies to? If yes, the relation is equality (tasāwī). For example, 'man' and 'rational' apply to exactly the same individuals.
Represented by two universal affirmatives: 'Every man is rational' and 'Every rational is man'.
3
Check for one-way inclusion
If one term applies to all individuals of the other but not vice versa, the relation is absolute generality and particularity (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ muṭlaq). The broader term is the general, the narrower is the specific. Example: 'animal' and 'man'.
Represented by a universal affirmative from the general side and a particular negative from the specific side: 'Every man is an animal' and 'Not every animal is a man'.
4
Check for no overlap
If neither term applies to any individual of the other, the relation is mutual exclusivity (tabāyun). Example: 'man' and 'horse' have no common individuals.
Represented by two universal negatives: 'No man is a horse' and 'No horse is a man'.
5
If none of the above, partial overlap
If the terms overlap partially (some individuals are common, some not), the relation is generality and particularity from one aspect (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh). This is the fourth relation, not detailed in this passage but mentioned for completeness.

Also, he did not consider it in two concepts, because concepts are either two universals, two particulars, or one universal and one particular; the four relations are not realized in the latter two cases. As for two particulars, they can only be mutually exclusive. As for a particular and a universal: if the particular is a particular of that universal, it is absolutely more specific; if it is not a particular of it, it is mutually exclusive with it. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory passage. It introduces the four possible relations between two universals: 1) Equality (al-tasāwī): both universals are coextensive, each applies to all individuals of the other, e.g., 'man' and 'rational'. 2) Absolute generality and particularity (al-ʿumūm wa al-khuṣūṣ al-muṭlaq): one is broader in extension, like 'animal' over 'man'. 3) Mutual exclusivity (al-tabāyun): no overlap, e.g., 'man' and 'horse'. 4) Partial overlap (mentioned later in text as 'generality and particularity from one aspect') is hinted at but not elaborated here. The passage then provides a meta-commentary on why the author only discusses relations between universals, not particulars or mixed cases: particulars are only considered incidentally and cannot be 'acquisitive' or 'acquired' (perhaps a reference to the role of universals in syllogisms). The commentary also details the logical expressions corresponding to each relation: equality is expressed by two universal affirmatives; absolute generality by a universal affirmative from the broader and a particular negative from the narrower; mutual exclusivity by two universal negatives. The text also analyses the contradictories of these relations, noting that contradictories of equals are also equal, etc.

The Four Logical Relations Between Universals
Author defines the four logical relations between universals with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four logical relations between two universals and apply them to Islamic science examples.

Core Idea
Two universals can relate in only four ways: equality, exclusion, absolute inclusion, or partial overlap.

In this passage, the author introduces the four fundamental logical relations that can hold between any two general concepts. Understanding these relations is essential for classifying things correctly in any Islamic science, from fiqh to kalam. We will see how 'white' and 'animal' overlap partially, and then generalize to all four possibilities.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two meanings of 'particular'
Note that 'particular' here refers to a concept that is not fully universal, but can still overlap with a universal. This is the second meaning.
In earlier logic, 'particular' was the individual; here it is used for both real and relative particular.
2
Identify the four relations from the text
From the example of white and animal, extract the four relations: equality (both apply to same individuals), mutual exclusion (no common individual), absolute generality (one always includes the other), and partial generality (each has exclusive instances).
The author lists them as numbers 1-4.
3
Map each relation to a logical diagram
For each relation, sketch a Venn diagram showing the overlap. This will help you visualise the classification.
Partial generality is the 'one aspect' overlap.
4
Apply the relations to an Islamic science example
Take two terms from usul al-fiqh, such as 'wajib' and 'salat'. Determine their relation: Are they equal? Exclusive? One includes the other? Do they partially overlap?
Use the four categories.

Negations of the Four Logical Relations
Commentary explains the negations of the four relations with examples of mutual exclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to characterize the logical relation between the negations of any two concepts given their original relation.

Core Idea
Negation transforms logical relations: mutual exclusion becomes partial exclusion, and 'general-specific from one aspect' remains the same.

In this passage, the commentator explains how the negation of two concepts changes their logical relation. You already know the four relations: equality, mutual exclusion, generality/specificity, and partial overlap. Now learn how those relations behave when you negate both terms. This is essential for evaluating arguments that use negated terms, such as in usul al-fiqh when comparing negative 'illas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original relation
Determine which of the four relations (equality, mutual exclusion, generality/specificity, partial overlap) holds between the two original concepts. For example, 'animal' and 'white' have partial overlap (general-specific from one aspect).
2
Form the negations
Take the contradictory negation (naqid) of each concept. For 'animal', negation is 'non-animal'; for 'white', 'non-white'.
Negation must be contradictory, not merely contrary.
3
Determine the relation between negations
Apply the patterns from the commentary: if originals are mutually exclusive, their negations have total mutual exclusion (e.g., existent/non-existent); if originals are partial overlap, negations also have partial overlap (e.g., animal/white). If originals are universal/particular (generality/specificity), negations have mutual exclusion (e.g., non-stone/non-human).
The passage gives four example pairs covering all cases.

Conditions for Contradiction in Quantified and Modal Propositions
Author establishes conditions for contradiction in quantified and modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions of difference in quantity and modality for contradiction and apply them to determine contradictory pairs in Islamic logical texts.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires both quantity and modality to differ; otherwise, two propositions can be false together or true together.

In Islamic logic, contradiction between propositions is not simply a matter of opposite truth values. For quantified propositions (المحصورات), the universal and particular must differ; for modal propositions (الموجهات), the modality must differ. This lesson parses the conditions and illustrates them with examples, including an objection and its response.

Guided Path
1
Parse the condition for quantified propositions
Note that two universal propositions can both be false (e.g., 'Every animal is human' and 'No animal is human' when the subject is more general than the predicate). Two particular propositions can both be true (e.g., 'Some animal is human' and 'Some animal is not human'). Thus, contradiction for quantified propositions requires one universal and one particular.
The key is that the quantifiers (كلي وجزئي) must differ.
2
Parse the condition for modal propositions
Two necessary propositions can both be false in a matter of possibility (e.g., 'Every human is a writer by necessity' and 'No human is a writer by necessity' are both false). Two possible propositions can both be true (e.g., 'Every human is a writer possibly' and 'Not every human is a writer possibly' are both false? Actually, they can be true or false depending; the point is that same modality does not guarantee contradiction). Therefore, contradiction requires difference in modality (e.g., necessary vs. possible).
The contradictory of a necessary proposition is the possible in the general sense (الممكنة العامة).
3
Analyze the objection and response
An objector argues that the truth of two particulars is not due to same quantity but due to the indefinite subject. The author responds that logical properties are based on the concept of the proposition, not external specifics. Since the particular proposition's subject is indefinite, the condition of quantity difference remains necessary.
Understand that the unity of subject in contradiction means unity of the conceptual description, not the specific essence.
4
Apply the conditions to a given pair
Given two propositions, check: (1) If they are quantified, do they have different quantities? (2) If they are modal, do they have different modalities? If both conditions are met (or only one applies), they are potentially contradictory.
Practice with the examples in the passage and create your own.

Simple Conversion of Universal Negative Propositions
Definition of simple conversion and proof for universal negative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform simple conversion on a universal negative proposition and prove its validity using the method of contradiction.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate; universal negatives convert to themselves.

In this lesson, we learn the basic operation of simple conversion – a key tool in immediate inference. The focus is on universal negative propositions, which convert without change. We will also study the rigorous proof by contradiction that establishes this rule, using a classic example from the tradition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of simple conversion
Conversion means swapping the subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality. The term 'simple' distinguishes it from other forms like conversion by contradiction.
2
Identify the rule for universal negative
A universal negative (E) proposition like 'No A is B' converts to 'No B is A'. This is known as 'simple conversion' because it remains the same type.
3
Walk through the proof by contradiction (khalaf)
Assume the conversion is false. Then its contradictory (some B is A) is true. Combine that with the original premise to produce a syllogism that leads to an absurdity (some B is not B). This proves the conversion must be true.
The proof uses a first-figure syllogism with a contradictory premise.

Objection and Answers on Temporal Conversion
Author presents an objection to conversion of temporal universal and provides two answers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze an objection to the conversion of temporal universal propositions and evaluate the adequacy of the responses.

Core Idea
Conversion of temporal propositions must adjust tense to preserve truth.

In previous lessons, you learned that a universal affirmative proposition converts to a particular affirmative. But what happens when the proposition involves time? The author presents a clever objection that seems to violate this rule, then offers two insightful answers. This lesson will sharpen your ability to handle temporal nuances in logical conversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector states that 'Every old person was young' is true, but its simple converse 'Some young person was old' is false. Recognize the problem: the original is about a past state, while the converse is about a past state of a current young person. Note the temporal mismatch.
2
Understand why the converse fails
The converse 'Some young person was old' would mean that some individual who is currently young was old in the past. This is false because not all young people become old; some die young. So the objector claims the conversion rule is violated.
3
Examine the first answer
The first answer rephrases the converse: instead of 'Some young person was old', it should be 'Some being that was young is old'. This changes the subject to something that was young (in the past) and is now old. This converse is true because those who are old now were indeed young before.
This preserves the past tense in the subject.
4
Examine the second answer
The second answer argues that preserving the exact tense (the relative relation) is not required for conversion. So the converse can be 'Some young person will be old', which is true because some currently young people will grow old. This uses a future tense.
This answer relaxes the condition of tense preservation.
5
Compare the two answers
Both answers show that the objection does not disprove the conversion rule; they simply adjust the tense. The first answer keeps the past in the subject, the second shifts the predicate to future. Both yield true converses, saving the rule.
The key insight: conversion of temporal propositions requires careful attention to the time of the relation.

Conversion by Contraposition: Early and Later Definitions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contraposition swaps and negates terms; later logicians refined it to avoid empty-term pitfalls.

This passage explains conversion by contraposition (عكس النقيض), a type of immediate inference. The early logicians defined it as swapping the contradictories of both terms while preserving truth and quality. Later logicians, however, found counterexamples with universal concepts like 'thing' and redefined it: negate the second term and put it first, keep the first term as second, and reverse the quality. Understanding this shift is crucial for correctly applying contraposition in Islamic logical works.

Identify the early definition
The early logicians defined contraposition as making the contradictory of the first part second, and the contradictory of the second part first, while preserving truth and quality. For example, 'Every human is an animal' becomes 'Every non-animal is non-human'.

Core Idea
'Aks al-naqid swaps term contradictories; it preserves truth but may change quantity.

You have already learned about standard conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi). Now we explore another immediate inference: 'aks al-naqid, the inverse of the contradictory. This operation takes a proposition and transforms it by replacing each term with its contradictory and swapping their positions, while maintaining truth and quality. However, as we shall see, this method only works reliably for certain proposition types.

Guided Path
1
Recall the early definition
The early logicians define 'aks al-naqid as: make the contradictory of the first part the second, and the contradictory of the second part the first, preserving truth and quality. This means we take the original subject and predicate, negate each, and swap their positions.
Contradictory of a term is its negation, e.g., 'non-human' is the contradictory of 'human'.
2
Apply to a universal affirmative (A)
For 'Every S is P', the inverse is 'Every non-P is non-S'. This preserves truth and quality. Example: 'Every human is an animal' becomes 'Every non-animal is non-human'.
The universal affirmative inverts to itself in form, just with negated terms.
3
Apply to a particular affirmative (I)
For 'Some S is P', the inverse would be 'Some non-P is non-S'. But this fails: 'Some animal is non-human' is true, but its inverse 'Some human is non-animal' is false. So the operation does not preserve truth for particular affirmatives.
The early logicians concluded that particular affirmatives do not invert via 'aks al-naqid.
4
Apply to a universal negative (E)
For 'No S is P', the inverse is 'Some non-P is not non-S'. Not a universal negative, because 'No non-P is non-S' would be false. Example: 'No human is a horse' inverts to 'Some non-horse is not non-human' (true, e.g., a wall).
The universal negative becomes a particular negative; the quantity changes.
5
Apply to a particular negative (O)
For 'Some S is not P', the inverse is 'Some non-P is not non-S'. This preserves truth. Example: 'Some animal is not human' inverts to 'Some non-human is not non-animal' (true, e.g., a horse).
Particular negatives invert to particular negatives, preserving both truth and quantity.

The Basic Rule: Conclusion Follows the Inferior Premise
Author states the basic rule of conclusion following the inferior premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that the conclusion follows the inferior premise in quality and quantity to determine the quality and quantity of a conclusion given two premises.

Core Idea
The conclusion's quality and quantity are determined by the weakest premise.

This lesson explains a foundational rule in categorical syllogisms: the conclusion takes on the inferior (weaker) quality and quantity of the two premises. Understanding this rule allows you to predict the conclusion's form without constructing the entire syllogism. It is the starting point before refinements by Ibn Sina.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quality of each premise
Determine whether each premise is affirmative or negative. The inferior quality is negative, so if at least one premise is negative, the conclusion must be negative.
If both premises are affirmative, the conclusion is affirmative.
2
Identify the quantity of each premise
Determine whether each premise is universal or particular. The inferior quantity is particular, so if at least one premise is particular, the conclusion must be particular.
If both premises are universal, the conclusion may be universal or particular (more on this later).
3
Apply the rule to determine conclusion
Combine the inferior quality and inferior quantity. The conclusion will have the weaker of each. For example, if one premise is negative and one is particular, the conclusion is negative particular.
This rule is a simplification; Ibn Sina's correction refines it.

Ibn Sina's Correction: Refined Rule for Conclusion
Author presents Ibn Sina's correction of the rule for conclusion dependency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn Sina's refined rule to determine the conclusion's quantity, quality, and modality based on the minor and major premises, including the two exceptions.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina refined the rule: conclusion follows the minor in quantity, major in quality/modality, with two exceptions.

The simple rule 'conclusion follows the inferior premise' is not absolute. The master logician Ibn Sina in al-Isharat showed that the conclusion's quantity follows the minor premise, while its quality and modality follow the major premise, except in two special cases. This correction is crucial for accurate syllogistic reasoning in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the minor and major premises
In a categorical syllogism, the minor premise contains the subject of the conclusion, the major premise contains the predicate. Identify which is which.
The minor is often labeled 'الصغرى' and the major 'الكبرى'.
2
Determine quantity from the minor premise
According to Ibn Sina, the conclusion's quantity (universal/particular) follows the minor premise. If the minor is particular, the conclusion is particular; if universal, the conclusion can be universal.
This differs from the simple rule where quantity followed the inferior.
3
Determine quality and modality from the major premise
The conclusion's quality (affirmative/negative) and modality (necessary/possible/absolute) follow the major premise. So if the major is negative, the conclusion is negative; if the major is necessary, the conclusion is necessary, etc.
Again, differing from the simple rule where quality followed the inferior.
4
Check the two exceptions
Exception 1: If the minor is possible (mumkina) and the major is not necessary, then the conclusion's modality follows the minor (possible), not the major. Exception 2: If the minor is affirmative necessary and the major is absolute (non-modal), then if the major is universal, the conclusion becomes affirmative necessary like the minor; if the major is particular, the premises contradict and no valid syllogism exists.
These exceptions show the nuance in Ibn Sina's system.

The Fallacy of Conversion by Contrapositive
Author presents a fallacy and two solutions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy in a contrapositive conversion and explain the two scholarly correctives offered.

Core Idea
Contrapositive conversion fails when the scope of terms shifts.

This lesson tackles a subtle logical puzzle: how a seemingly valid contrapositive conversion of a conditional can lead to an absurdity. The author presents a problematic case and then records two distinct responses from scholars that preserve logical consistency. By the end, you will not only spot the fallacy but also understand how Islamic logicians resolved it.

Guided Path
1
Examine the original conditional and its conversion
Read the Arabic: 'If not some things were established, the claim would be established, while it is some things.' This is the problematic conversion that seems to lead to a contradiction. Notice the terms 'thing' and 'claim'.
The conversion is meant to be a contrapositive, but it results in a reduction to absurdity.
2
Analyze the first response: difference in generality
Some scholars reject the conversion because the term 'thing' in the original is general (any thing), but in the conversion it becomes specific (some things). They provide the correct conversion: 'Whenever that thing is not established, the claim is established.' This preserves truth.
This response shows that contrapositive conversion must keep the same term scope.
3
Examine the second response: impossible antecedent
Others accept the conversion but argue that the antecedent becomes impossible. An impossible antecedent can entail any consequent, including its opposite, so no contradiction arises.
This response relies on the logical principle ex falso quodlibet.
4
Evaluate which response resolves the fallacy
Compare the two: the first directly invalidates the conversion due to term mismatch; the second accepts it but neutralizes the absurdity. Both show that the conversion is not sound as originally presented.
The first response is more common in classical logic texts.

The second response, via an alternative formulation, accepts the conversion but argues that the antecedent is impossible, and an impossible antecedent can entail its opposite, so no contradiction arises. The passage illustrates the importance of precision in logical operations and the handling of universality and particularity.

Key Takeaway
Contrapositive conversion is only valid when the terms retain the same scope; otherwise it leads to fallacy, which scholars resolve either by rejecting the conversion or by neutralizing the consequence.
