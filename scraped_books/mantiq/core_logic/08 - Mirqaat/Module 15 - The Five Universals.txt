Definition and Three Types of al-Mufrad
Author defines al-mufrad and its three types with commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic term as ism, kalima, or adaa based on independence of meaning and association with time.

Core Idea
The singular term is divided by meaning independence and time association into noun, verb, and particle.

This passage defines the singular term (al-mufrad) and establishes its three types: ism, kalima, and adaa. Understanding this classification is foundational for analyzing propositions in logic and for precise reasoning in Islamic sciences. The commentary refines the condition of time association to avoid confusions with names of actions and participles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic. Here, the author defines 'al-mufrad' (the singular term). Notice that the definition is given by example first, then a formal division.
The passage starts with examples: 'Zayd' and 'standing' indicate their meaning; 'archer' and 'the arrow' indicate theirs.
2
Extract the basis for division
The author divides mufrad based on two criteria: (1) whether the meaning is independent (mustaqill bil-mafhumiyya) or not, and (2) whether it is associated with a specific time (zaman) from the three (past, present, future).
Independence means the term is understood without needing another term to complete its meaning.
3
Classify the three types
If independent and not time-associated → ism. If independent and time-associated → kalima. If not independent → adaa. Work through the examples: 'Zayd' is ism; 'yaḍribu' (he strikes) is kalima; 'fī' (in) is adaa.
Note that in logic, kalima roughly corresponds to verb, but excludes some verbs like 'aḍribu' (I strike) because it is composite.
4
Apply the time-association refinement
The commentary specifies that the time association in kalima must be 'primary' (awwaliyyan) according to the word's placement (wad'). This excludes names of actions (asmā' al-af'āl) like 'ṣah' (be quiet) which indicate future but are not kalima, and also excludes active/passive participles which are not primarily time-associated.
Also excluded are words associated with a time outside the three, like 'ṣabūḥ' (morning drink) which is associated with morning (a specific time but not one of the three).
5
Test classifications with Islamic examples
Given terms from Islamic sciences, decide their type. For instance, 'ṣalāh' (prayer) is ism (independent, no time); 'yaṣlī' (he prays) is kalima (independent, time-associated); 'min' (from) is adaa (not independent). Verify using the criteria.
Be careful: 'ṣā'imin' (fasting) as active participle is not kalima because its time association is not primary.

Commentary
This passage defines the singular term (al-mufrad) and divides it into three types: ism (noun), kalima (word/verb), and adaa (tool/particle). The passage is a blend of matn and sharh. The matn states that the singular term is either independent in meaning (needs no annexation) and not associated with time (ism), independent and associated with time (kalima), or not independent (adaa). The commentary expands by clarifying the condition of time association: it must be a primary association with one of the three times (past, present, future) to exclude names of actions (e.g., صه for 'be quiet') and verbal nouns like صبوح (morning drink) which are associated with a time outside the three. The commentary also explains the rationale for naming: 'kalima' from 'wounding' because time changes, and 'ism' from 'suluww' (elevation). This distinction is crucial for understanding how logicians classify terms, differing slightly from grammarians.

The Six Types of Mufrad by Meaning
Author presents the division of mufrad into one-meaning and many-meaning types with scholarly disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any mufrad term into one of the six types based on its meaning, and explain why categories from different divisions can combine.

Core Idea
A word's meaning may be one or many, each giving three types, and categories from different divisions can intersect.

In this passage, the author moves from dividing mufrad by independence of meaning to dividing it by unity vs. multiplicity of meaning. Understanding this classification is crucial for analyzing definitions and avoiding ambiguity in Islamic sciences. We will walk through the six types and the scholarly disagreement about what is being divided.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two main branches
The passage divides mufrad into those with one meaning and those with many. Identify the three types under each branch: for one meaning: al-juz'i, al-mutawati', al-mushakkik; for many: al-mushtarak, al-manqul, al-haqiqa wa al-majaz.
These are presented as six categories total.
2
Understand each type's distinctive property
For one-meaning types: juz'i denotes a specific individual; mutawati' applies equally to all individuals; mushakkik applies with varying priority. For many-meaning: mushtarak has multiple unrelated meanings; manqul has a transferred meaning; haqiqa/majaz refer to literal vs. figurative meaning.
Note that haqiqa and majaz are paired as a single division.
3
Examine the disagreement about the basis
Some scholars say the division into universal, particular, etc. applies only to nouns, while the other divisions apply to all mufrad. The well-known view is that all divisions apply to mufrad in general (nouns, verbs, particles). Al-Bihari adds that metaphor by essence is only in nouns.
This nuance affects how you classify verbs and particles.
4
Apply the rule of combination
Categories from the same division cannot combine (e.g., a term cannot be both juz'i and mutawati'), but categories from different divisions can (e.g., a term can be juz'i and mushtarak). This is key for real analysis.
Example: 'Zayd' is juz'i and also could be mushtarak if it refers to multiple persons.

Commentary
This passage presents a second division of mufrad based on the number of meanings a word has. The author begins by noting that after dividing mufrad according to whether it independently conveys meaning (i.e., as a complete vs. incomplete expression), the focus shifts to unity vs. multiplicity of meaning. There is a scholarly disagreement about what exactly is being divided: is it mufrad in general, or only nouns for some categories? Some say the division into universal, particular, univocal, and equivocal applies specifically to nouns, while the division into homonym, transferred, and literal/metaphorical applies to all mufrad types (noun, verb, particle). The well-known view is the latter. Muhibb Allah al-Bihari's opinion is also mentioned, that metaphor by essence is only in nouns; in verbs and particles it is secondary. The author then clarifies that a word with one meaning (according to its original coinage and usage) has three types: the particular (juz'i, denotes a specific individual), the univocal (mutawati, applies equally to all its individuals), and the equivocal (mushakkik, applies with varying priority or intensity). A word with multiple meanings also has three types: homonym (mushtarak, has multiple meanings by different coinages), transferred (manqul, original meaning shifted to another), and literal vs. metaphorical (haqiqah and majaz). Finally, the author notes that categories from the same division cannot combine (e.g., a word cannot be both particular and univocal), but categories from different divisions can (e.g., a word can be both particular and homonym).

Mutawāṭi': The Univocal Universal Term
Defining mutawāṭi' and clarifying equality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define mutawāṭi' and distinguish it from proper names and mushakkik.

Core Idea
A univocal term applies equally to all its individuals without gradation.

In this passage, the author first divides terms into particular (proper names) and universal. Then among universals, he defines mutawāṭi' as one that applies equally to all its individuals. He clarifies that 'equality' here does not mean absolute identity of degree, but rather the absence of specific types of gradation found in mushakkik.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial division
Notice the author divides terms into 'determinate and specific' (proper names) and 'not specific' (universals). Proper names denote a single individual; universals apply to many.
Examples of proper names: Zayd, 'this', 'he'.
2
Define mutawāṭi'
Find where the author says 'the truth of that meaning upon all its individuals is in the way of equality'. This is the definition of mutawāṭi'. Note the example 'human' for Zayd, 'Amr, Bakr.
Mutawāṭi' means 'univocal' or 'agreed'.
3
Understand the equality condition
The author explains that equality does not mean no difference at all (since truth on four individuals is more than on one). Rather, it means absence of the four specific types of gradation: priority, preference, intensity, excess.
This prevents a false objection that mutawāṭi' cannot exist.
4
Test with examples
Check if a term like 'human' is mutawāṭi': Does it apply equally to all humans? Yes, because being human is not a matter of degree.
Compare with 'existence' which is mushakkik.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed explanation of the terms juz'i (particular), mutawāṭi' (univocal), and mushakkik (equivocal). The author begins by stating that a word with one meaning is either determinate and specific, i.e., it denotes a particular individual, which is called a proper name (ʻalam) like 'Zayd', 'this', or 'he'. It is better to call this section the genuine particular (juzʼī ḥaqīqī). The second type is when the meaning is not specific but has many individuals. This is of two kinds: first, if the term applies equally to all its individuals without any priority, preference, intensity, or excess—this is mutawāṭi', like 'human' applying to Zayd, 'Amr, and Bakr. Second, if it does not apply equally, it is mushakkik. The author then addresses potential objections: proper names like 'Allah' are universal in concept but in usage they are particular. Pronouns and demonstratives are also particular despite general coinage because each occasion is a separate individual. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mushtarak is a word with multiple independent meanings, not a single meaning with multiple instances.

In logic, after covering words with single meanings, the next category is words with multiple meanings. The author introduces the crucial distinction between verbal homonymy (mushtarak lafzi) and conceptual homonymy (mushtarak ma'nawi). This distinction is foundational for precise definition and argumentation in Islamic sciences.

Distinguishing Particular and Universal Concepts
Author divides concept into particular and universal, defining each and addressing nuances.

The key differentiator is whether the very concept prevents multiple instances, not external reality.

In Islamic logic, every concept in the mind is either particular or universal. The distinction hinges on a single criterion: does the mere mental representation of the concept allow it to apply to many individuals? The passage we study defines these two categories with careful qualifications, including a critical nuance that prevents misclassification of unique entities like Allah. Six Divisions of the Universal Based on Existence of Instances
Author enumerates the six divisions of the universal based on existence of instances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal concept into one of six divisions based on whether its instances exist externally, and if so, how many.

Core Idea
The universal's sixfold classification depends solely on existence and count of instances.

In Islamic logic, the universal (al-kulli) is not just a logical concept; its classification into six divisions helps us understand the ontological status of concepts. This passage presents the standard sixfold division, ranging from impossible universals like 'not-a-thing' to infinite universals like rational souls. Understanding this classification sharpens your ability to analyse theological and philosophical concepts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first branching: impossible vs. possible existence
The primary division is whether the instances of a universal are impossible in external existence (like 'not-a-thing') or possible (like 'phoenix'). If impossible, it falls in the first division.
Impossible universals are those whose concept entails non-existence, so no instance can exist.
2
For possible universals, check if any instance exists
If a universal is possible but has no actual instances (e.g., phoenix), it belongs to the second division. If it does have instances, proceed to the next step.
3
If one instance exists, determine whether more are possible
Some universals have exactly one actual individual, but additional instances may be impossible (e.g., Wajib al-Wujud, God) or possible (e.g., the sun, if another sun is conceivable). This separates the third and fourth divisions.
This distinction is crucial in theology: God's oneness is not just accidental but necessary.
4
If many instances exist, distinguish finite from infinite
Universals with multiple instances are further divided into those with finite individuals (e.g., the seven planets) and those with infinite individuals (e.g., rational souls). This gives the fifth and sixth divisions.
Infinite here means not limited in number, not necessarily actually infinite in a mathematical sense.
5
Apply the classification scheme to new examples
Practice by taking a universal like 'nabidh' (a drink) and determining its division. (Answer: possible, exists many, finite? Actually, many types of nabidh exist, but possibly finite? The key is to follow the logical tree.)
Always start from the top: impossible or possible, then existence, then count.

Commentary
This passage presents a classification of the universal (al-kulli) into six types based on the existence of its instances. The first division is those universals whose individuals are impossible in external existence, like 'not-a-thing' (la-shay'), 'not-possible' (la-mumkin), and 'not-existing' (la-mawjud). These are universals that cannot have any instance because their very concept negates existence. The second division is those whose individuals are possible but have never existed, like the 'phoenix' (al-'anqa') and 'a mountain of rubies' - these are conceivable but not realized. The third division is those that have only one individual and any other is impossible, like the Necessary Existent (Wajib al-Wujud) - only God can be Necessary Existent. The fourth is those that happen to have only one individual but could have others, like the sun according to those who consider multiple suns possible. The fifth are those with many finite individuals, like the seven planets (known at the time). The sixth are those with infinite individuals, like rational souls (al-nufus al-natiqa), or individuals of humans, horses, and goats.

The commentary expands on this by citing Tusi's Sharh al-Isharat, noting that the classification proceeds by first considering whether a universal has instances or not; then among those with instances, whether it has one or many; and among those with one, whether more are impossible or possible; among those with many, whether finite or infinite. The commentary also clarifies that 'not-a-thing' and similar universals must have no existence either externally or in the mind because anything assumed externally is a thing, and anything assumed mentally is a thing, so the concept 'not-a-thing' can never be true of anything actual - that would be a contradiction.

Defining Universal and Particular by Mental Permissibility
Author defines universal and particular with two interpretations and explains 'juz' al-'aql'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between universal and particular based on the criterion of mental permissibility (jaza al-'aql) and correctly classify examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A universal is what the mind permits to be many; a particular is what the mind restricts to one.

The foundational distinction in logic is between universal concepts, which can be mentally applied to many individuals, and particular concepts, which inherently refer to a single instance. This lesson unpacks the definitions as presented in the Mirqat, focusing on the phrase 'the mind permits' (jaza al-'aql). Understanding this distinction is crucial for all subsequent logical operations, from definition to syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion
The passage defines universal and particular by appealing to the mind's permission (jaza al-'aql). Determine what exactly is being permitted or restricted: multiplicity of instances.
The criterion is purely mental; it does not depend on actual existence.
2
Test the universal example
Consider the example 'human' (insan). Ask: from its mere conception, does the mind allow it to apply to more than one? Yes, because many individuals can be conceived as human. This confirms it as a universal.
The example is from the passage itself.
3
Test the particular example
Consider 'this man' (hadha al-rajul). From its mere conception, the mind is constrained to a specific individual. It does not permit multiplicity. This confirms it as a particular.
The passage uses 'this man' as the paradigm particular.
4
Apply the test to Islamic concepts
Take a concept like 'prayer' (al-salat). The mind can conceive many different prayers, so it is universal. Now take 'the noon prayer of today' – the mind restricts it to one specific instance, so it is particular.
This extends the definition to fiqh terminology.
5
Recognize the scope of the definition
The definition relies solely on mental conception, not on external existence. Even if a universal has only one existent instance, it remains universal if the mind permits many.
For example, 'the sun' is a universal concept because the mind can conceive multiple suns, even though only one exists.

Then the passage explains the phrase 'jaza al-'aql' (the mind permits) by contrasting it with a situation where the mind is constrained. The universal allows multiplicity, while the particular inherently restricts to one. This definition is foundational for understanding the classification of concepts in logic.

Sixfold Division of the Universal
Author enumerates the six divisions of the universal based on existence of individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal concept into one of the six divisions based on the existence of its individuals in the external world.

Core Idea
Universals are divided by whether their individuals exist or not, and if so, how many.

The classification of universals into six types is a fundamental tool in Islamic ontology. It distinguishes between concepts that can have real instances and those that cannot, and further refines this by number and possibility. This lesson will guide you through the sixfold division using examples from Islamic theology and natural philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion
Notice that the division is based solely on the existence or non-existence of individuals of the universal in the external world (ʿālam al-khārij). This is the primary axis.
2
Distinguish between possible and impossible existence
First, check whether the universal's individuals can possibly exist (mumkin al-wujūd) or are impossible (mumtaniʿ al-wujūd). Impossible examples: 'nothing', 'associate of Allah'.
3
For possible universals, check actual existence
If possible, ask: do any individuals exist? If none exist, it's the second division (e.g., phoenix). If some exist, proceed.
4
Count the existing individuals
If individuals exist, is it exactly one or many? If one, check if a second is impossible (third division, e.g., Allah) or possible (fourth, e.g., sun as per some).
5
If many, determine finitude
If many individuals exist, are they finite (fifth division, e.g., seven classical planets) or infinite (sixth, e.g., human souls)?

Distinguishing Universal from Particular: Simultaneous vs. Sequential Applicability
Author presents an objection and clarifies the type of multiplicity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a concept is universal or particular based on the criterion of simultaneous applicability (ala wajh al-ijtima') versus sequential applicability (badalan).

Core Idea
A universal applies to many simultaneously; a particular applies one at a time.

The definition of universal as 'applicable to many' seems to apply even to particular mental images like the image of a specific egg. But careful examination shows that the definition requires simultaneous applicability, not sequential. This distinction is crucial for understanding the five universals and avoiding errors in conceptual analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the examples given by the objector: the mental image of a specific egg, the phantom seen from afar, the infant's sensation. Why does the objector think these are universals?
These are all particular images that can be applied to multiple individuals in succession.
2
Understand the key distinction
The answer introduces a crucial distinction: applicability can be simultaneous (ala wajh al-ijtima') or sequential (badalan). Which one defines universality?
Simultaneous applicability means the concept applies to all its instances at once, not one after another.
3
Apply the distinction to the examples
Show that the mental image of a specific egg applies to many eggs only one at a time, not all at once. Why does the image carry unity from its origin?
The image is derived from a particular material (madda mu'ayyana juz'iyya), so it inherently has a unity that prevents simultaneous application.
4
Formulate the correct definition
State the corrected understanding: universal = concept applicable to many simultaneously. Particular = concept applicable to many only sequentially, or applicable to one only.
This resolves the objection without altering the nature of mental images.

Defending the Definition of the Universal: Collective vs. Successive Predication
Author raises and answers a dialectical objection against the standard definition of universal and particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between collective and successive predication, and use this distinction to defend the definition of the universal against the objection that particular mental images can apply to many.

Core Idea
A universal must apply to many together, not one after another.

The definition of the universal as 'that which can be predicated of many' seems vulnerable to a counterexample: mental images of particular eggs, a blurry distant shape, or an infant's first perception of a parent appear to apply to multiple things, yet they are considered particulars. The author anticipates this objection and refines the definition by specifying that the multiplicity must be simultaneous ('collective'), not successive ('alternate'). This distinction saves the definition and deepens your understanding of how concepts relate to particulars.

Guided Path
1
State the objection in your own words
The objector gives three examples: the mental image of a specific egg (when eggs are replaced without the viewer's knowledge), the blurry shape seen from afar (which cannot be distinguished), and the infant's image of a parent (which applies to any similar person). These images seem to apply to many individuals, yet they are considered particulars. Why does this challenge the definition?
The objection aims to show that the definition of the universal is too broad, because it would include these particulars.
2
Identify the key distinction in the answer
The author answers by clarifying that the definition of the universal requires predication 'on the basis of togetherness' (على وجه الاجتماع), not 'alternately' (بدلا). That is, the concept must be applicable to all individuals at once in a single assertion, not one at a time.
The Arabic terms are crucial: معا vs بدلا. Without this distinction, the objection would be valid.
3
Analyze each example to see why it is successive, not collective
For the egg image: The image is taken from one specific egg; when other eggs are substituted, the observer thinks each is the same egg, but the image cannot apply to all eggs simultaneously because it is tied to the memory of one egg. For the distant shape: it could be Zayd or 'Amr, but it is not predicated of both at once. For the infant: the image corresponds to each parent separately, not both together.
In each case, the concept applies to multiple instances but only one at a time, not all together.
4
Formulate the corrected definition
After incorporating the answer, the definition of the universal becomes: 'that which can be predicated of many collectively (معا) in one assertion.' The particular is 'that which can only be predicated of one, or of many only successively (بدلا).'
This refinement preserves the original definitions while nullifying the objection.

The Relative Particular: A Species Under a Genus
Author defines the relative particular as a species under a genus and contrasts it with the real particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the relative particular in a hierarchy of universals and distinguish it from the real particular.

Core Idea
A relative particular is a species under a genus — particular to that genus but universal to individuals.

In this passage, the author introduces a second meaning of 'particular': the relative particular. Unlike the real particular (e.g., Zayd), which cannot be predicated of many, the relative particular is a concept that is more specific than another. For example, 'human' is particular relative to 'animal', but universal relative to individual humans. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the hierarchy of the five universals.

Trace the hierarchy

Differentiate real vs relative
The author contrasts the real particular (al-juz'i al-haqiqi) with the relative. Zayd is a real particular because it cannot be predicated of many. The relation is 'umum wa khusus mutlaq: every real particular is a relative particular, but not vice versa.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory explanation of the five universals, focusing on the relationship between true species (al-nawʿ al-ḥaqīqī) and relative species (al-nawʿ al-iḍāfī), and the ordering of genera. The author begins by stating that between true species and relative species there is a 'generality and specificity in some respect' (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh), meaning they overlap partially. They coincide on individuals like 'man' (al-insān), which is both a true species (since it is predicated of individuals with the same essence) and a relative species (since it is predicated of individuals and also of its parts, e.g., Zayd and the Turk, as genus in answer to 'what is it?'). However, true species can apply without relative species in simple realities like 'intellect' (al-ʿaql) and 'point' (al-nuqṭa), which are true species but not relative species because they have no individuals below them. Conversely, relative species applies without true species in intermediate categories like 'animal' (al-ḥayawān), which is a relative species (as it is predicated of man and other species) but not a true species because it is a genus. The author notes that al-Shaykh (Ibn Sīnā) in al-Shifāʾ holds absolute generality and specificity between them. Then the author moves to the ordering of genera (tartīb al-ajnās). There are four ranks: high genus (al-jins al-ʿālī) like substance (al-jawhar); low genus (al-jins al-sāfil) like animal; middle genus (al-jins al-mutawassiṭ) like growing body; and isolated genus (al-jins al-mufrad) like intellect (if substance is not a genus for it). The low genus has no genus beneath it but has a genus above; the middle has genera both above and below; the high has no genus above; the isolated is completely separate. The author then raises a subtle issue about 'absolute body' (al-jism al-muṭlaq): because body is composed of matter and form, and the matters of elements and heavens differ, absolute body may not be a single essence. This is a note for the reader to ponder.

The Descending Order of Types and the Necessity of a Lowest Species
Author explains how species are arranged in descending order and the necessity of a lowest species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument for the necessity of a lowest species in the hierarchy of types.

Core Idea
Types must terminate at individuals, else infinite regress destroys their very existence.

In this passage, the author explains how species are arranged in a descending hierarchy, from the highest type down to the lowest. He then provides a logical argument that this hierarchy must have a lowest type—a species below which there are only individuals. Without it, types would never be instantiated in reality. Let's walk through the reasoning to see why a finite hierarchy is necessary for the existence of both types and individuals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions for a 'highest type'
The text defines the highest type (al-naw' al-'ali) as having a type under it but no type above it. Notice that this definition is relative: it depends on what is above and below in the hierarchy.
2
Recognize the relative nature of the arrangement
The commentary clarifies that the arrangement is 'idafiyya' (relative), not real. A real type cannot have another real type above it, because that would make it a genus, not a species. So the hierarchy is a conceptual tool for classification, not an ontological chain.
3
Extract the conditional argument for a lowest type
The author presents a conditional: if there were no lowest type (i.e., infinite descending chain), then each type would have a type under it, leading to no individual ever being realized. But types exist only through individuals. Therefore, the chain must terminate at a lowest type that yields individuals.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: infinite regress would make types impossible.
4
State the conclusion
The hierarchy must be finite, ending at a type below which there are only individuals. This ensures that types are instantiated in reality.
Without this lowest type, the whole classification system would collapse.

The definition of the differentia: it is the intermediate species. Sometimes no species is under it and a species is above it, and it is the lowest species, also called the species of species. Section three: the differentia, which is a universal predicated of a thing in answer to 'what thing is it in its essence?' as when a human is asked 'what thing is he in his essence?' and the answer is 'rational'. It has two divisions. The species of species: the lowest is only called the species of species because the species-ness of a thing is relative to what is above it, unlike the genus, for the genus-ness of a thing is with respect to what is under it. So the lowest of species, under which there is no other species – the true species – is called the species of species. The highest of genera, above which there is no other genus – the most general of genera – is called the genus of genera. - The differentia, etc.: Know that the part of essence is confined to genus and differentia; because it either is shared between the essence and some type of species different from it in reality, or is not shared. If not shared, it is a differentia because it distinguishes the essence from others in a general manner essentially. If shared, then either it is the complete commonality between it and some type of species different from it in reality, or not. If it is, then it is the genus, according to what Allama al-Razi said in his commentary on Matali' al-Anwar. 'Universal' etc.: His saying 'universal' is a genus encompassing all universals. By his saying 'predicated of a thing in answer to 'what thing?'' he excludes the genus, species, and general accident, because the genus and species are predicated in answer to 'what is it?', and the general accident is not predicated at all. By his saying 'in its essence' he excludes the property, because even though it distinguishes the thing, it is not in its essence and substance but in its accident. Know that the meaning of 'what' ('ayy) although in language it is a request for a distinguisher absolutely, they have conventionally established that it requests a distinguisher that is not predicated in answer to 'what is it?'; so no objection occurs here, so ponder. And it has two divisions: that is, the differentia has two divisions: near and far. Know that the genus also has two divisions: near and far. Since it was not mentioned before, we mention the genus first, then we mention the differentia. Genus is of two types: near if the answer to the question of essence – whichever essence is assumed – and about some of what shares with it in that genus, the answer is exactly the same as the answer about that essence and about everything that shares with it in that genus, like 'animal' with respect to human and horse. For if one asks 'what are human and horse?', the answer is 'animal', and similarly if one asks about human and all that shares with it in animality, the answer is also 'animal'. Every Muqawwim of the Higher is Muqawwim of the Lower
Rule: Every muqawwim of the higher is muqawwim of the lower, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that every constitutive universal of a higher genus is also constitutive of its lower genera, using examples from the classification of living beings.

Core Idea
Constitutive features flow downward through the hierarchy of genera.

The author begins by stating a foundational rule: any universal that is constitutive (muqawwim) of a higher genus must also be constitutive of all lower genera under it. This ensures that definitions remain consistent as we move from general to specific categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the higher genus and its constitutive universal
Look at the first example: 'acceptor of dimensions' is constitutive of 'body', which is a higher genus.
2
Trace the lower genera under that higher genus
The lower genera include 'growing body', 'animal', and 'man'. Check if 'acceptor of dimensions' is also constitutive of each of these.
3
Verify the rule for each lower genus
The text confirms that 'acceptor of dimensions' is indeed constitutive of all those lower genera, demonstrating the rule.
4
Generalize the pattern
Understand that this rule applies to any constitutive universal: if it constitutes the higher, it must constitute every lower genus under that hierarchy.

Commentary
This passage presents several rules about the hierarchical relations of muqawwim and muqassim. It first states that any constitutive of a higher genus is also constitutive of the lower genera beneath it, giving examples like 'acceptor of dimensions' (constitutive of body, then also of growing body, animal, man). Then it discusses the differentia types again and repeats the dual relation (muqawwim/muqassim) with more detail. Then it states the converse: not every constitutive of a lower is constitutive of a higher (e.g., 'rational' constitutes man but not animal). Then it turns to the dividing aspect: every dividing of a lower is dividing of a higher (since if the lower genus is divided, the higher genus is also divided into the same types). But not every dividing of a higher is dividing of a lower (e.g., 'sensitive' divides growing body into sensitive and non-sensitive, but animal is already all sensitive, so it does not divide animal). The commentary clarifies that dividing the lower means obtaining it in a type, and that if something obtains the lower in a type, it also obtains the higher. These rules are useful for understanding the relationships among the five universals.

Not Every Muqawwim of the Lower is Muqawwim of the Higher
Rule: Not every muqawwim of the lower is muqawwim of the higher, with the rational example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a counterexample demonstrating that not every constitutive of a lower genus is constitutive of a higher genus.

Core Idea
Constitutive features can be specific to lower levels and not apply upward.

After establishing the downward flow of constitutive features, the author warns that the reverse is not always true. The example of 'rational' shows that a universal can be constitutive of a species (man) without being constitutive of its higher genus (animal).

Guided Path
1
Recall the first rule
Remember that every muqawwim of the higher is muqawwim of the lower. Now we test the converse.
2
Identify a constitutive of a lower genus
Consider 'rational' (al-natiq). It is constitutive of man, which is a lower genus (species) under animal.
3
Check if it is constitutive of the higher genus
Look at the definition of animal. Is 'rational' part of the definition of animal? No, because animal is a broader category that includes both rational and non-rational beings.
4
Conclude the rule does not hold upward
Therefore, 'rational' is not muqawwim of animal. This shows the second rule: not every muqawwim of the lower is muqawwim of the higher.

Rules of Dividing: Muqassim for Higher and Lower
Rules for muqassim: every muqassim of the lower is muqassim of the higher, but not every muqassim of the higher is muqassim of the lower.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the rules for muqassim (dividing universal): every muqassim of the lower is muqassim of the higher, but not every muqassim of the higher is muqassim of the lower.

Core Idea
Division flows downward but not upward; higher genera are always divided when lower genera are divided, but not vice versa.

The author then turns from constitutive (muqawwim) to dividing (muqassim) universals. He presents two parallel rules: any universal that divides a lower genus also divides the higher genus, but a universal that divides a higher genus does not necessarily divide a lower genus. The example of 'rational' and 'sensitive' clarify this.

Guided Path
1
Understand the meaning of muqassim
A muqassim is a differentia that divides a genus into two types (e.g., 'rational' divides 'animal' into rational and non-rational animals).
2
Apply the first rule: every muqassim of the lower is muqassim of the higher
'Rational' divides 'animal' (a lower genus relative to 'body'). According to the rule, it also divides 'absolute body' (the higher genus) into rational and non-rational bodies. This holds because if the lower genus is split, the higher genus must also be split accordingly.
3
Apply the second rule: not every muqassim of the higher is muqassim of the lower
'Sensitive' divides 'growing body' (a higher genus) into sensitive and non-sensitive growing bodies. But it does not divide 'animal' (a lower genus) because every animal is already sensitive; there is no non-sensitive animal. Thus, the division does not apply to the lower.
4
Generalize the rules
The first rule: if a differentia divides a lower genus, it also divides all higher genera. The second rule: if a differentia divides a higher genus, it may not divide a lower genus if the lower genus already possesses that differentia universally.

Observational Premises: External and Internal Senses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish observational premises from other premise types and categorize an observational premise as either external sense (hissi) or internal sense (wijdani).

Core Idea
Observational premises come from both external and internal senses—not just the five senses.

In this passage, the author defines the fourth type of premise in demonstration: al-mushahadat (observational premises). These are propositions that are judged directly by observation and sensation. The author then divides them into two broad categories: those derived from the five external senses (al-hissiyat) and those derived from the five internal senses (al-wijdaniyyat). This classification is foundational for understanding how empirical knowledge is structured in Islamic epistemology.

This category is called introspective findings, and the perceptions of pure intellect—namely universals—are not included in this category.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth type of premise in a demonstrative syllogism: mushahadat (observational premises). It is definitional, presenting a classification into two primary categories. The first category, called hissiyat (sensations), comes from the five external senses: sight, hearing, smell, taste, and touch. The second category derives from the five internal senses: common sense (hiss mushtarak), imagination (khayal), estimation (wahm), memory (hafiza), and the reflective power (mutasarrifa). This second category is termed wijdaniyyat (introspective findings), and the author explicitly excludes pure intellectual universals from this division. The passage serves as a foundational taxonomy for epistemology in demonstration.

The Five External Senses: Anatomy and Perceptibles
Explains each external sense's physical basis and perceptible qualities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five external senses, describe their anatomical bases, and list the perceptible qualities for each.

Core Idea
Each external sense is a faculty grounded in a specific organ, perceiving distinct qualities.

This passage from Mirqat al-Mafatih expands on the five external senses mentioned in the matn, providing anatomical and functional descriptions that link each faculty to a physical location and the type of qualities it perceives. Understanding these details is foundational for later discussions in kalam and usul al-fiqh about the reliability of sensory knowledge and the limits of human perception. By examining each sense in turn, we see how the Islamic intellectual tradition grounds epistemology in concrete, observable reality.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the five senses
Start by listing the five senses mentioned: sight (al-bāṣirah), hearing (al-sāmi‘ah), smell (al-shāmmah), taste (al-dhā’iqah), and touch (al-lāmisah). Notice that the author uses the feminine form for each power (quwwah).
The order may vary in other texts; here it follows a typical sequence.

Identify the anatomical seat for each sense
For each sense, locate the specific nerve or organ mentioned: sight in the two hollow nerves of the eye, hearing in the nerve spread in the ear canal, smell in the two protrusions at the front of the brain, taste in the nerve spread on the tongue, and touch as a power flowing through nerves throughout the body.
Touch is unique in being distributed, not localized.

List the perceptible qualities for each sense
Sight perceives lights, colors, and shapes; hearing perceives sounds; smell perceives odors; taste perceives tastes; touch perceives heat, cold, moisture, dryness, roughness, smoothness, hardness, softness, and others.
Touch has the broadest range of perceptibles.

Note the mechanism of perception

Reflect on the epistemological significance
Consider why a logician or theologian would include such detail: to establish the physical basis of sensory knowledge, which is a starting point for arguments about the existence of God, the nature of miracles, and the reliability of transmitted reports.

Mapping the Five Internal Senses: Functions and Locations
Explains each internal sense's function, location in the brain, and role in perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the five internal senses by their function and brain location, and explain their roles in the perception process.

Core Idea
The five internal senses form a pipeline from perception to storage to meaning extraction.

In Islamic philosophy, the internal senses bridge the gap between external perception and abstract thought. This passage from Mirqat al-Mantiq outlines their specific functions and anatomical locations, showing how the brain processes sensory data into meaningful judgments. Understanding this framework is essential for grasping later discussions on epistemology and psychology in the Islamic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the receiver of forms
Find the sense that 'depicts forms of perceived particulars' and note its location in the front first brain cavity. This is common sense (al-ḥiss al-mushtarak).
It is the entry point for all sensory data.
2
Distinguish preservation from perception
Notice that imagination (al-khayāl) preserves forms after objects vanish, while common sense perceives them. The author calls it a 'treasury.'
Imagination stores; common sense holds actively.
3
Analyze estimation (al-wahm)
Estimation perceives particular meanings (like hostility) attached to sensed objects. See the example of the sheep perceiving the wolf's hostility.
This sense is crucial for survival and is a precursor to abstract thought.
4
Link memory to estimation
Memory (al-ḥāfiẓah) stores meanings just as imagination stores forms. The author explicitly compares memory:imagination as estimation:common sense.
Memory is located at the end of the second cavity.
5
Examine the reflective power (al-mutaṣarrifah)
This sense in the middle cavity composes and analyzes forms and meanings. Its name changes depending on whether it serves intellect (thinking) or estimation (imaginative).
This dual role highlights the interface between sensory and rational faculties.
