Why Logic is Necessary: Proof from Error in Human Reasoning
Author proves necessity of logic via demonstrated error in thought.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument that error occurs in human thought and explain why this establishes the need for logic.

Core Idea
If human reasoning were infallible, there would be no disagreement—yet disagreement proves error.

The author begins by warning that not every arrangement of ideas is correct. He presents two opposing arguments about the world's origin to show that at least one must be false, thus proving that human reasoning can err. This error necessitates a law to protect us from falsehood. The passage then concludes that this law is logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contradictory claims
The author presents one person claiming the world is originated and another claiming it is eternal. Extract these claims from the Arabic: the first uses the syllogism 'the world is changing, and everything changing is originated'; the second uses 'the world is independent of the effect, and everything independent is eternal'.
2
Recognize the logical implication of contradiction
If both were true, that would be a conjunction of contradictories (impossible). If both false, that would be an elevation of contradictories (also impossible). So, one must be true and the other false. This shows that at least one reasoning process is erroneous.
3
Conclude that error exists in human thought
Since these two thinkers are rational and yet reach contradictory conclusions, error is a real phenomenon in human thinking. The occurrence of error is undeniable.
4
Understand the need for a protective law
Because human nature is not sufficient to always distinguish truth from falsehood, a law is needed to protect against error. The author compares it to a scale that separates the husk from the kernel.
5
Identify the law as logic
The author explicitly states that this law is 'logic' (al-manṭiq) or 'the balance' (al-mīzān). Logic provides the ways to acquire unknowns from knowns correctly.

The Nature of Thought: Theoretical vs. Intuitive Knowledge
Author defines thought and distinguishes theoretical from intuitive knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the ancient and later views on the nature of thought (al-fikr), and explain how the author's definition of thought as movement in the intelligibles leads to the distinction between theoretical and intuitive knowledge.

Core Idea
Thought is movement from known to unknown; its absence yields intuition, its presence yields theoretical knowledge.

After establishing the need for logic, the author delves into the definition of thought itself. He describes two movements involved in thinking: from principles to goal and from goal to principles. He then contrasts the ancient view (thought is the sum of both movements) with the later view (thought is the arrangement of the second movement). The author offers his own synthesis: thought is any movement in the intelligibles to obtain the unknown, whether one or both movements occur. This clarifies when knowledge is theoretical (when movement occurs) and when it is intuitive (when movement is absent).

Guided Path
1
Understand the two movements of thought
The passage describes two movements: one from the principles (mabādī) to the goal (maṭlūb) and another from the goal back to the principles. These are the two directions of intellectual travel.
2
List the four possible combinations of these movements
The author enumerates four cases: 1) first instantaneous, second gradual; 2) both instantaneous; 3) first gradual, second instantaneous; 4) both gradual. Note that the numbering may have omitted case 3 due to ancient manuscript confusion.
The text says '-۱- قد يكون الانتقال الأول دفعياً والثاني تدريجياً. - وقد يكونان دفعتين. ۲ - وقد يكون بالعكس. ٤- وقد يكونان تدريجيين.' This suggests four cases.
3
Compare the ancient and later views
The ancients held that thought is the sum of both movements; if one is absent, only intuition (badāha) is present. The later scholars held that thought is only the arrangement necessary for the second movement. Both views are criticized as flawed.
4
Notice the author's preferred definition
The author says: 'Perhaps the truth is that thought is movement in the intelligibles to obtain the unknown, whether both movements are realized or only one.' This broadens the definition to include any movement.
5
Apply the distinction to theoretical vs. intuitive knowledge
Theoretical knowledge (naẓariyya) is now defined by the presence of any movement. Intuitive knowledge (ḍarūra) is when movement is completely absent. This resolves the earlier contradictions.

The Five Arts and the Definition of Demonstration
Defines demonstration and lists five arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define demonstration (burhan) by identifying its genus and differentia, and correct the misconception that it uses only intuitive premises.

Core Idea
Demonstration is a syllogism of certain premises, not just intuitive ones.

The author introduces the five arts—demonstrative, dialectical, rhetorical, poetic, and sophistical—then focuses on demonstration. He defines it precisely and corrects a mistaken view. Understanding this definition is crucial for evaluating any demonstrative argument in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five arts
List the five types of logical arts mentioned at the beginning. They set the context for demonstration.
2
Find the definition of burhan
Locate the sentence that defines burhan. Note that it is defined as a syllogism (qiyas) composed of certain premises (yaqiniyyat).
The definition includes both intuitive and theoretical premises that end in intuitive ones.
3
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'syllogism' and the differentia is 'composed of certain premises'. This is a complete definition (hadd haqiqi).
4
Correct the misconception
The author explicitly denies that demonstration uses only intuitive premises. Understand why theoretical premises can be part of demonstration as long as they trace back to intuitive ones.
This avoids the error of thinking that only self-evident truths can be used in demonstration.

Limmi and Inni Demonstration: Causal vs. Factual Proof
Author divides demonstration into two types: limmi (causal) and inni (existential) with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between limmi and inni demonstrations and identify whether the middle term is a real cause or merely a mental cause.

Core Idea
In limmi, the middle term is the real cause; in inni, it is only a mental cause.

The science of demonstration (burhan) distinguishes proofs that explain the reason why (limmi) from proofs that merely confirm the fact (inni). This division is essential for understanding the epistemic strength of arguments. In this lesson, we will unpack the definitions and examples given by the author to master this foundational distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions
Read the opening sentence: 'البرهان قسمان: لمي وإني'. Note that demonstration is split into two types. The author will define each.
2
Analyze the limmi definition
For limmi, the text says 'الأوسط فيه علة لثبوت الأكبر للأصغر في الواقع'. So the middle term is a cause for the major's attribution to the minor in reality. Also it is an intermediary in judgment. This means the middle term is the real cause.
3
Analyze the inni definition
For inni, the middle term is a cause only in the mind (في الذهن فقط), not in reality. It may even be an effect of the major term. So it does not provide real causation.
4
Examine the examples
The limmi example: 'Zayd is feverish because his humors are putrefied' – here putrefaction is the real cause of fever. The inni example: 'Zayd's humors are putrefied because he is feverish' – here fever is not the real cause; the causality is reversed in reality.
5
Determine the direction of causality
In limmi, the middle term (putrefied humors) is the cause in both mind and reality. In inni, the middle term (fever) is a cause only in the mind; in reality, it is the effect. This is the key distinction.

Commentary
This passage is definitional and syllogistic, as it divides demonstration (burhan) into two types: limmi and inni. The author begins by stating that demonstration is of two types, then defines each. A limmi demonstration is one in which the middle term is a cause for the major term's attribution to the minor term both in the mind and in reality. It is called 'limmi' because it provides the cause (limiyya) and the reason why (al-illiyya). An inni demonstration is one in which the middle term is a cause only in the mind, not in reality; it may even be an effect of the major term. The author then gives clear medical examples. For limmi: Zayd is feverish because his humors are putrefied; the putrefaction is the real cause of the fever. The syllogism proceeds: Zayd has putrefied humors (minor premise), everyone with putrefied humors is feverish (major premise), therefore Zayd is feverish. Here the middle term (putrefied humors) is the real cause of the fever. For inni: Zayd has putrefied humors because he is feverish; the syllogism goes: Zayd is feverish (minor), everyone feverish has putrefied humors (major), therefore Zayd has putrefied humors. Here the middle term (fever) is not the real cause; rather, the putrefaction is the real cause of the fever, so the direction of causality is reversed in reality. The passage also includes marginal commentary clarifying that in a limmi demonstration, the middle term is a cause both mentally and externally, while in inni it is only a mental cause. This distinction is crucial for assessing the demonstrative force of a syllogism: limmi provides the reason why, while inni only confirms the fact. The examples are rooted in Galenic medicine, which was standard in the Islamic tradition. All illustrations adhere to the requirement of using Islamic sciences: here medical diagnosis and humoral theory are used.

A) It is a cause only in the mind, not in reality
B) It is a cause in reality but not in the mind
C) It is a cause both in the mind and in reality

Key Takeaway
Limmi demonstration provides the real cause; inni demonstration only provides mental justification.
