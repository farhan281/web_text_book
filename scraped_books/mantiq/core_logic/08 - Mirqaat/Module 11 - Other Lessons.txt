Resolving Circularity in Definition and Types of Necessity
Raises and resolves a circularity objection, then explains types of necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve a circularity objection in a definition, and distinguish intellectual from conventional necessity using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Circular definitions are avoided by distinguishing the type of knowledge required at each step.

Definitions in logic and other Islamic sciences must avoid circularity (dawr). This passage presents a common objection against the definition of 'indication' (dalalah) and resolves it through a precise distinction. Then it expands on the term 'necessity' (luzum) used in definitions, explaining that it includes both intellectual and conventional necessity. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for evaluating definitions in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and other disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the circularity objection
The objector claims that the definition of indication is circular because indication depends on knowledge of the significate, and knowledge of the significate depends on indication. Locate this in the Arabic: 'فإن قلت: إن الدلالة تتوقف على علم المدلول وهو يتوقف على الدلالة'.
The objection is a standard logical charge; you must see why it appears plausible.
2
Analyse the resolution
The response distinguishes between 'knowledge of the significate from the indicator' (علم المدلول من الدال) and 'absolute knowledge of the significate' (علم المدلول مطلقا). The definition requires only absolute knowledge of the significate for indication, not the specific knowledge from that indicator. Thus no circle occurs. Trace the reasoning in the response: 'قلت: إن علم المدلول من الدال موقوف على الدلالة لا علم المدلول مطلقا...'.
This resolution is a classic logical move: breaking the circle by introducing a distinction.
3
Distinguish intellectual vs. conventional necessity
The term 'necessity' (luzum) in the definition is broader than intellectual necessity. Intellectual necessity (اللزوم العقلي) is when the mind cannot separate two concepts, e.g., four and evenness. Conventional necessity (اللزوم العرفي) is when custom makes a link appear inseparable, e.g., generosity and Hatim. Note the examples: 'كلزوم الزوجية للأربعة' and 'كلزوم الجود للحاتم'.
Conventional necessity is often used in definitions of adjectival or relational terms.
4
Apply the distinction to the definition of indication
Why does the definition of indication need to include conventional necessity? Because the relation between an indicator and its significate is often customary, not purely intellectual. For example, the word 'fire' conventionally indicates heat, even though intellect could conceive a fire without heat (though impossible). This shows how the definition avoids being too narrow.
The passage says 'المراد باللزوم ههنا أعم من أن يكون عقليا أو عرفيا' – necessity here is general.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange followed by a definitional clarification. First, the commentator raises an objection: the definition of indication contains a vicious circle because it says 'knowledge of the indicator leads to knowledge of the significate', but to know the indicator as an indicator requires prior knowledge of the significate, which itself would require indication. The response resolves the circle by distinguishing two types of knowledge: the specific knowledge of the significate from this particular indicator (which depends on the indication), and the absolute knowledge of the significate (which does not depend on this indication). The indication depends on absolute knowledge of the significate, not the particular knowledge, so no circle. Then the commentator clarifies the meaning of 'necessity' (luzum) in the definition. It is broader than just intellectual necessity; it includes both intellectual and conventional necessity. Intellectual necessity is when the intellect cannot separate the concepts, like the impossibility of conceiving 'four' without 'evenness'. Conventional necessity is when custom makes the link so strong that it seems necessary, like generosity being inseparable from the person of Hatim in common thought, even though it is intellectually possible to separate them. This shows that the relation in indication can be based on custom, not just logic.

The Definition of Indication and Its Sixfold Division
Defines indication and outlines its six types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define indication (dalala) in logical terms and classify indications into six types based on two criteria: verbal/non-verbal and conventional/natural/rational.

Core Idea
Indication is the bridge from known to unknown; its sixfold classification maps every sign.

This lesson introduces the foundational concept of indication (dalala), the key to understanding how signs convey meaning. The passage defines indication and then splits it into verbal and non-verbal, each with three subtypes.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of dalala
Locate the definition in the Arabic: 'كون الشيء بحيث يلزم من العلم به العلم بشيء آخر'. Note that indication is a relation between two things (indicator and significate) such that knowledge of the first leads to knowledge of the second.
The indicator is called 'dal', the significate 'madlul'.
2
Identify the two main divisions
The passage divides indication into verbal (lafziyya) and non-verbal (ghayr lafziyya). The criterion is whether the indicator is a word or not.
Verbal: the indicator is a word (لفظ). Non-verbal: everything else.
3
Enumerate the three subtypes for each division
Both verbal and non-verbal are further divided into conventional (wad'iyya), natural (tab'iyya), and rational ('aqliyya). This gives a total of six types.
Write down: 1. conventional verbal, 2. natural verbal, 3. rational verbal, 4. conventional non-verbal, 5. natural non-verbal, 6. rational non-verbal.
4
Match each subtype with its example
From the passage, note the example for each: e.g., 'Zayd' for conventional verbal, 'uḥ aḥ' for natural verbal, 'Dīz' for rational verbal; the four indicators (nusub, khutut, isharat, 'uqūd) for conventional non-verbal; horse whinnying for natural non-verbal; smoke for fire for rational non-verbal.
Think about why each example fits its category.

Section on indication: Indication linguistically is guidance, i.e., rāh namūdan [in Persian]. In terminology, it is the state of a thing such that knowledge of it necessitates knowledge of another thing. Indication is of two types: verbal and non-verbal. Verbal: Enumeration of indication into six types: indication and its divisions: what has the word as the indicator, and non-verbal: what does not have the word as the indicator. Each of them is of three kinds: First: conventional verbal indication, like the indication of the word 'Zayd' for its namesake; second: natural verbal indication, like the indication of the word 'uḥ aḥ' – with ḍamma on the hamza and sukūn on the ḥāʼ, or with fatḥa – for chest pain; for nature compels producing this word when chest pain occurs; third: rational verbal indication, like the indication of the word 'Dīz' heard from behind a wall for the existence of the speaker; fourth: conventional non-verbal indication, like the indication of the four indicators for their significates; fifth: natural non-verbal indication, like the indication of the whinnying of a horse for seeking water and fodder; sixth: rational non-verbal indication, like the indication of smoke for fire. 'Conventional' etc.: attributed to convention (waḍʻ), such that convention has an entry in it, whether the specific word itself is placed for its meaning, like placing 'Zayd' for his essence, or the parts of the word are placed for their meanings, like placing 'Zayd' and 'standing' for their meanings; even if the whole phrase is not established by a single act of placing, the parts are. Convention linguistically is 'nihādan' [in Persian], and in terminology: 'allocating one thing to another such that whenever the first is uttered or sensed, the second is understood from it.' What is meant by conventional verbal indication is a word such that when it is uttered, its meaning is understood due to knowledge of its convention. Natural: it is that which is caused by nature producing the indicator when the significate occurs, whether the indicator is verbal, like the indication of 'uḥ aḥ' for coughing, or non-verbal like the kicking of an animal for fodder. 'Uḥ aḥ': with fatḥa or ḍamma on the hamza and a ḥāʼ with sukūn; it is said 'aḥa al-rajul' if he coughed. And with ḍamma on the hamza and sukūn on the khāʼ with tashdid, it indicates pain; with fatḥa on the hamza it indicates regret. This is an example of natural verbal indication. The kicking of an animal is an example of non-verbal natural indication. In 'al-Ṣirāḥ', 'rakḍ' means moving the foot; 'rakḍ al-faras' means it runs; from it is the verse: 'And [We said], 'Strike with your foot'' [Qur'an 38:42]. Rational: it is attributed to reason because there is no intervention of convention or nature; in it a relation of effect is considered, so it includes indication of the effect for the cause, like smoke for the fire, indication of the cause for the effect, and indication of one of the two effects for the other, like indication of smoke for heat – both are effects of fire. 'An-nuṣub': that which is set up on the road to measure distance, and lines, and signals, and knots (ʻuqūd): the counters (dawāl), which are knots of the fingers that indicate numbers.

Commentary
This passage provides the definition of indication (dalala) and its detailed classification. First, the definition is given: indication is a relation between two things such that knowledge of one (the indicator) leads to knowledge of the other (the significate). Indication is then divided into two major types: verbal (the indicator is a word) and non-verbal (the indicator is not a word). Each of these is further divided into three subtypes: conventional (wad'iyya), natural (tab'iyya), and rational ('aqliyya). The author enumerates all six types with clear examples. For conventional verbal, the example is the word 'Zayd' which indicates the person Zayd by convention. For natural verbal, the example is the utterance 'uḥ aḥ' which naturally occurs when someone has chest pain. For rational verbal, the example is hearing the word 'Dīz' from behind a wall, which leads rationally to the conclusion that there is a speaker. For conventional non-verbal, the example is the four 'dawāl' (finger-counting signs) used to indicate numbers. For natural non-verbal, the example is a horse's whinny indicating its desire for water. For rational non-verbal, the example is smoke indicating fire. The commentary then elaborates each type: explaining that 'conventional' involves a deliberate assignment of a sign to a meaning; 'natural' arises from an involuntary connection; 'rational' depends on a causal inference. The commentary also clarifies issues like the fact that a compound expression can be conventional based on its parts, even if the whole is not explicitly placed. It also corrects a potential misreading of 'uḥ aḥ' by citing lexical sources.

The Three Types of Verbal Indication
Explains conventional, natural, and rational verbal indication with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conventional, natural, and rational verbal indication using examples from Arabic language and Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Words can indicate meanings by convention, nature, or reason — each has a different epistemic basis.

We focus here on verbal indication, where the indicator is a word. The passage explains three subtypes: conventional (like 'Zayd'), natural (like coughing sounds), and rational (like inferring a speaker from sound).

Guided Path
1
Locate the three verbal subtypes in the Arabic
Find the terms: اللفظية الوضعية, اللفظية الطبعية, اللفظية العقلية. Note that they are all under the heading 'اللفظية' (verbal).
The passage states 'كل منهما على ثلاثة أنحاء' and then lists them.
2
Understand the basis for each subtype
For conventional, the connection is established by deliberate assignment (waḍʻ). For natural, it arises from an involuntary bodily reaction (tabʻ). For rational, it depends on an inference of cause and effect ('aql).
Check the commentary: 'الوضعية... منسوب إلى الوضع', 'الطبعية... بإحداث الطبيعة', 'العقلية... لعدم مداخلة الوضع والطبع'.
3
Examine the examples provided
Write down the example for each: conventional: 'Zayd'; natural: 'uḥ aḥ'; rational: hearing 'Dīz' and inferring a speaker. Understand why each fits.
For natural, note that it is a word (لفظ) but produced involuntarily, so it is verbal natural.
4
Apply the taxonomy to new examples
Think of other words: e.g., 'آه' for sighing (natural), 'الم' in the Quran (conventional), the sound of a broken glass implying an accident (rational). Classify them.

The Three Types of Verbal Signification: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, and Iltizam
Commentator defines matching, implicative, and concomitant signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate between the three types of conventional verbal signification (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A word can signify its entire meaning, a part, or a necessary external meaning.

Words are not limited to a single mode of signification. In Islamic logic, a conventional word placed for a meaning can signify that meaning in three distinct ways. Understanding these three types — matching (mutabaqa), implicative (tadammun), and concomitant (iltizam) — is essential for precise interpretation of texts in fiqh, kalam, and tafsir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching category
The term 'الدلالة اللفظية الوضعية' (conventional verbal signification) is the general concept. All three types fall under it.
This is the capacity of a word to convey meaning based on convention (wad').
2
Distinguish matching (mutabaqa)
Matching signification occurs when a word signifies the entirety of the meaning it was placed for. Example: 'insan' signifies the complete meaning of 'animal + rational'.
It is the most direct form of signification.
3
Distinguish implicative (tadammun)
Implicative signification occurs when a word signifies only a part of its original meaning. Example: 'insan' signifies just 'animal'.
The part is inherent in the whole.
4
Distinguish concomitant (iltizam)
Concomitant signification occurs when a word signifies a meaning external to its original meaning but necessarily connected to it. Example: 'insan' signifies 'capacity for knowledge' — an external necessary concomitant.
The mind transfers from the original meaning to the concomitant (lazim).
5
Apply the distinction to a new example
Take the word 'salah' (prayer). Its matching signification is the entire ritual; its implicative is a part like ruku'; its concomitant could be 'abstaining from lewdness' (from Qur'an 29:45).

The issue of skeptical gradation (tashkik) is recorded in the books of the art at length and in detail. It is a difficult issue, a battlefield of opinions, a slippery place, and due to narrow space and fear of deviating from the scope of the purpose—though the two [space and purpose] do not permit its detail and the exposition of the proofs of the Peripatetics and Illuminationists and the determination of the truth in it as it deserves—we see no harm in mentioning it in an abridged and concise manner such that it may be a lesson for beginners and an aid to understanding it in its proper place. The Illuminationists permitted it, while the Peripatetics forbade it. They (Peripatetics) said: There is no skeptical gradation in substantial quiddity nor in accidental quiddity; rather, gradation is in the attribution of the quiddity's individuals with the accident. So there is no gradation in body or in blackness, but rather in the truth of the accidental concept, like 'black' derived from the generic meaning of blackness as applied to its individuals. The Illuminationists, who affirm gradation in quiddity, said: A substance like the substances of the intellectual world is stronger in substantiality than other substances, like the substances of the lower world, and an animal and a body are more in animality and body-ness than another animal and body, like the human and elephant compared to the mosquito, as is mentioned in the Wisdom of Illumination. This is the summary of their claims stripped of proofs and elaboration, with what supports and opposes them in the detailed works. Because it makes the observer doubt etc.: because its individuals share the root of its meaning but differ by one of the three ways, so the observer, if he looks at the aspect of sharing, imagines it is univocal due to the agreement of its individuals in it, and if he looks at the aspect of difference, he thinks it is equivocal (mushtarak) as if it were a word with different meanings. Common Terms (Mushtarak) and Their Meanings
Defines common terms (لفظ مشترك) with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify common terms (mushtarak) by analyzing their original and transferred meanings as demonstrated in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A mushtarak word carries multiple meanings, and recognizing it prevents misinterpretation in sacred texts.

In Islamic logic and jurisprudence, words often have more than one meaning. This lesson introduces 'common terms' (mushtarak) — words that are used in both their original and a transferred sense. Using examples from the passage, you will learn how to identify such terms and understand their dual usage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term in question
Locate the word being discussed. The passage gives three examples: 'dābba', 'ṣalāt', and 'ism'. Each has an original meaning and a transferred meaning assigned by different groups.
The term must be recognized as having at least two distinct meanings.
2
Determine the original (literal) meaning
For each term, find what it originally referred to. For 'dābba', it was any creature that walks on earth. For 'ṣalāt', it was supplication. For 'ism', it was elevation.
The original meaning is the first subject (al-mawḍūʿ al-awwal).
3
Identify the transferred (figurative) meaning
See how the term was transferred to a new meaning by a specific group. The common people transferred 'dābba' to horse or quadruped. The Lawgiver transferred 'ṣalāt' to specific acts. The grammarians transferred 'ism' to an independent word.
The transferred meaning becomes common if used frequently.
4
Classify as mushtarak
If the term is used in both meanings without abandoning the original, it is called mushtarak. For example, 'asad' is used literally for the animal and figuratively for a brave man, making it a common term.
Mushtarak words are those that have multiple established usages.

Haqiqa and Majaz: Literal vs Figurative Meaning
Explains literal (حقيقة) and figurative (مجاز) usage with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between literal (haqiqa) and figurative (majaz) usage and justify the necessity of a relational link (ʿalāqa) in figurative transfer.

Core Idea
Figurative meaning is no accident; it requires a valid relational link to the literal meaning to avoid error.

Words can be used in their original sense (haqiqa) or in a secondary sense (majaz). This lesson explains the definitions of haqiqa and majaz, their etymologies, and the critical condition for figurative usage: a relational link (ʿalāqa) between the two meanings. Using the example of 'asad' (lion), you will learn to classify usage and verify the presence of ʿalāqa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its usage
Select a term like 'asad' from the passage. It is used in two contexts: the predatory animal (literal) and the brave man (figurative).
Determine which usage is being considered.
2
Determine if the usage is haqiqa or majaz
If the word is used in its original subject, it is haqiqa. If used in a secondary meaning with a relational link, it is majaz. For 'asad' applied to a brave man, it is majaz because the original meaning is the animal.
The passage states: 'it is literal in relation to the first and figurative in relation to the second'.
3
Verify the existence of a relational link (ʿalāqa)
The passage stresses that majaz requires a ʿalāqa between the original and figurative meanings to prevent error. For 'asad', the link is courage (both the lion and the brave man share courage). Without such a link, the usage would be erroneous, like saying 'Take this horse' pointing to a book.
The passage gives an absurd example to show what happens without ʿalāqa.
4
Classify the type of ʿalāqa (optional)
The passage notes that scholars have enumerated up to 25 relations. Common ones include similarity (ishbāh), causality, and part-whole. For 'asad', it is similarity (tashbīh) in ferocity or bravery.
Detailed classification is left to books of Uṣūl and Bayān.

Core Idea
Insha are speech acts that cannot be true or false; they include command, prohibition, wish, hope, interrogation, and call.

Guided Path
1

4

Core Idea
Incomplete compounds are building blocks of meaning; knowing their types helps parse any Arabic text accurately.

Having discussed expressions (alfaz) in general, we now focus on a crucial subcategory: the incomplete compound (al-murakkab al-naqis). This lesson breaks down the three main types—additive, descriptive, and restrictive—that appear constantly in Islamic texts. By mastering these, you will be able to accurately analyze phrases in the Quran, Hadith, and classical works.

Guided Path
1
Recognize an incomplete compound
An incomplete compound is a phrase that does not convey a complete meaning by itself; it needs additional context. Examples include 'Zayd's servant' or 'in the house'—these are not full sentences.

2
Identify the additive compound (idafi)
Look for a possessive construction (idafa): two nouns where the first is possessed and the second is the possessor, e.g., 'ghulamu Zaydin' (Zayd's servant). The relationship is ownership or affiliation.

Identify the descriptive compound (tawsifi)

Both noun and adjective must have the same definiteness prefix (al-).
4
Identify the restrictive compound (taqyidi)
Look for a prepositional phrase: a preposition followed by its object, e.g., 'fi al-dari' (in the house). This construction restricts or specifies the meaning of another word.
In some texts, taqyidi may include other restrictive phrases, but here it is limited to prepositional phrases.

Or with regard to singular compound, like: 'The bitter orange is sweet sour' is true, but if it is said individually: 'This is sweet and sour' it is not true. Or with regard to the plural compound, like: 'Zayd is a skilled physician' is true, but if it is combined and said: 'a skilled physician' it is false. [Commentary:] 'If the pronoun refers' means: if the hidden nominative pronoun in 'what he knows' refers to the sage, it is true; otherwise, i.e., if it does not refer to the sage but to the relative pronoun in 'what he knows', it is false; because the meaning then is that the sage's action is because the action knows the sage, and this is not correct, because knowledge is an attribute of rational beings and action is not a rational being. Al-aghalit (fallacies) is the plural of ughlutah, meaning that by which one is caused to err, like a marvel (u'jubah) and a laughingstock (udhukah).

Plural Compound (Jamʿ al-Munfaṣil)
Author defines and exemplifies the third fallacy type: plural compound.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify plural compound (jamʿ al-munfaṣil) fallacies, where separate statements when combined into one compound change truth value.

Core Idea
Combining two true separate statements into one compound can make the statement false.

The third fallacy type is the reverse: taking two separate true statements and combining them into a single predicate. The example 'Zayd is a skilled physician' is true when said as separate predicates ('Zayd is skilled' and 'Zayd is a physician'), but if you combine them into 'Zayd is a skilled physician', it becomes false because it implies a specific profession that may not be customary. This fallacy highlights how syntax matters.

Guided Path
1
Examine the separate statements
'زيد طبيب' (Zayd is a physician) is true; 'زيد ماهر' (Zayd is skilled) is true.
2
Consider the combined form
'زيد طبيب ماهر' (Zayd is a skilled physician) is a single predicate combining both.
3
Determine truth of combined form
The passage says it is false because it implies a specific compound attribute that is not accurate.
4
Understand why
The combination may imply a new meaning that was not present in the separate statements, such as an established profession.
The fallacy lies in the shift from two independent predicates to one compound predicate.
