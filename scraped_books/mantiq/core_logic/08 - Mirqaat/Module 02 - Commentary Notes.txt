Learning Objective

Core Idea
'Allah' is a proper name for the necessary existent, though it likely originated as a description.

The word 'Allah' is central to Islamic theology. In logic and linguistics, it is important to know whether it is a proper name (no further definition needed) or originally a description (implying a meaning). This lesson unpacks the definitions given in the source and shows how the commentator uses scholarly opinion to clarify the issue.

Guided Path

Identify the definiendum
Find the word being defined: it is 'الله' (Allah). The passage opens with 'لله: الله علم...'.
The term 'الله' is the subject of analysis.
2
Extract the genus
The author says it is a 'علم' (proper name). This is its genus: it names a specific entity without needing a description.
In Arabic grammar, علم is a proper noun.

Note the differentia
The proper name 'Allah' is for 'الذات الواجب الوجود المستجمع الجميع صفاته' (the essence that is necessarily existent, encompassing all attributes). This specifies the referent.
This differentia distinguishes it from other proper names.

Analyze the alternative view
The author quotes al-Baydawi, who says the most apparent view is that originally it was a description (وصف) but became dominant as a proper name.
This is a linguistic point, not a theological one.

Core Idea
Ibda‘ is creation from nothing — the definition itself excludes pre-existing matter.

In this passage, the author defines the term ibda‘, which appears in the opening line of the Mirqat. The definition is twofold: linguistic (having no parallel) and technical (bringing into existence without matter). The author then cites a Quranic verse as evidence. Understanding this definition is crucial for theological discussions about creation and divine power.

Guided Path

Distinguish linguistic from technical definition
The author first gives the linguistic meaning of ibda‘: 'having no parallel.' Then he gives the technical meaning: 'bringing something from non-existence to existence without matter.' Notice the shift from lexical to terminological meaning.

The technical definition can be broken down: 'bringing something from non-existence to existence' is the genus (it is a type of causing existence), and 'without any pre-existing matter' is the differentia that distinguishes ibda‘ from other forms of creation like takwin (which may use matter).

The author cites Q. 65:12 about the creation of seven heavens and the earth. Why is this evidence for ibda‘? Because the heavens and earth are created without matter (they are the first material things). So the verse supports that these are brought into existence directly.

The author adds a small note on the word 'ard' (earth), stating its origin is 'ardah' as shown by the diminutive 'aridah'. This is a philological clarification but not essential to the definition.

Intuition and Discursive Thought
Distinguishes intuition from thought

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish intuition (hads) from discursive thought (fikr) based on the number of mental movements.

Core Idea
Intuition grasps conclusions in a single leap; thought moves step-by-step.

The Islamic logicians distinguished between two ways of reaching knowledge: one that is immediate (hads) and one that is methodical (fikr). This lesson explores the definitions and examples provided in the commentary, and shows how intuition is associated with a 'holy power' in prophets and saints.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of mental movement
Notice that the passage defines 'thought' (fikr) as a two-step movement: from the desired conclusion to the principles, then back to the conclusion. In contrast, 'intuition' (hads) is a single, instantaneous movement from the conclusion to principles and back in one stroke.
The author emphasises that in hads there is no stepwise reasoning; the mind leaps.
2
Examine the conditions for intuition
The passage states that intuition often occurs after yearning and exertion, but sometimes without them. This shows that hads can be cultivated or occur spontaneously.
The mention of 'holy power' (quwwa qudsiyya) links intuition to prophetic and saintly knowledge.
3
Analyse the variability of intuition among people
People differ in their intuitive capacity: some have strong intuition (prophets, saints, sages), some weak, some none at all (extreme dullness). This variability explains why what is self-evident for one may be theoretical for another.
This sets up the later discussion on relativity of badaha and nazariyya.
4
Apply the distinction to Islamic sciences
Intuition is not a separate source of knowledge but a rapid form of inference. In kalam, the proof of God's existence is often presented as a deductive argument (fikr), but some scholars claim it can be known intuitively (hads) through fitra.

Relativity of Self-Evident and Theoretical Knowledge
Explains relativity of self-evident and theoretical knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the same proposition can be self-evident (badihi) for one person and theoretical (nazari) for another.

Core Idea
Self-evidence depends on the capacity of the knower, not on the object known.

The passage argues that what is self-evident for a prophet may be theoretical for a layperson. This lesson walks through the author's reasoning, showing that badaha and nazariyya are relative to persons and times, and explores both interpretations of this relativity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim of relativity
The author states that from the variability in intuition, it is known that self-evident and theoretical differ according to persons and times. This means the same object of knowledge can be known effortlessly by one person and require reflection by another.
This is based on the earlier discussion of hads: strong intuitives can grasp directly what others must reason about.
2
Examine the two perspectives
The passage considers two interpretations: (1) badaha and nazariyya as attributes of the known (ma'lum), and (2) as attributes of knowledge ('ilm). Under the first, relativity is straightforward: the same known can be badihi for one person and nazari for another. Under the second, relativity means that a single known can be known by two different acts of knowledge—one necessary, one theoretical.
The author notes that if one defines badihi as that whose absolute acquisition depends on reflection (which is contradictory), then relativity collapses.
3
Identify the author's resolution
The author favours the view that badaha and nazariyya are essentially attributes of knowledge. He clarifies that the aim of reflection is knowledge, not the external existence of the known. Consequently, a single act of knowledge cannot be both self-evident and theoretical; however, the same object can be known in two different ways.
This preserves the relativity while maintaining the logical distinction.
4
Apply to Islamic sciences
In usul al-fiqh, the impermissibility of shirk is self-evident to a Muslim (by fitra) but theoretical for a polytheist who must be convinced by argument. This illustrates the relativity across persons.

Are Badaha and Nazariyya Attributes of Knowledge or of the Known?
Debates whether badaha and nazariyya are attributes of knowledge or the known

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the arguments for and against the view that badaha and nazariyya are essential attributes of knowledge.

Core Idea
The author argues they are attributes of knowledge, not of the known object.

A deeper debate: do 'self-evident' and 'theoretical' describe the act of knowing or the thing known? The passage presents the majority view and the author's counter-argument, culminating in a precise resolution.

Guided Path
1
State the core question
The passage asks: are badaha and nazariyya attributes of knowledge ('ilm) essentially, or of the known (ma'lum) essentially? This is a metaphysical question about where these properties inhere.
The majority thought they belong to the known because reflection aims at the object.
2
Understand the majority position
The majority held that badaha and nazariyya are attributes of the known. Their reasoning: reflection (nazar) is directed at the known object, not at knowledge itself. Therefore, what depends on reflection is a property of the known.
The passage notes this is based on a 'misconception' that the aim of reflection is the object's existence.
3
Analyze the author's objection
The author argues that the true aim of reflection is knowledge (or disclosure) of things, not the external existence of objects. Therefore, badaha and nazariyya are essentially accidents of knowledge. The known is only accidentally self-evident or theoretical, insofar as it is the object of such knowledge.
This is a subtle but important shift: it preserves the logical purity of the categories.
4
Draw the conclusion
Consequently, a single act of knowledge cannot be both self-evident and theoretical; these are distinct types of knowledge. However, one and the same object can be known in two ways—once through self-evident knowledge and once through theoretical knowledge. Thus, the object may be called self-evident or theoretical accidentally.
The passage ends with 'fa tammal' (so reflect), urging the reader to ponder this distinction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
One word for two meanings (homonymy) or one word used figuratively (metaphor) can make a valid argument fail.

Logic demands precision. When a single word carries multiple meanings (homonymy) or is used in a figurative sense (metaphor), the unsuspecting reasoner may inadvertently argue from different senses of the same term, producing a fallacy of equivocation. This lesson breaks down the first classification of verbal error given by the author and shows how to spot such errors in Islamic scholarly discourse.

Verbal error is then subdivided into error due to composition (التركيب) and error not due to composition. The current passage focuses on the non-compositional type, which involves the words themselves.

Core Idea
A single written word can mislead if its vowels, dots, or grammatical role are misread.

The author classifies non-compositional verbal errors into three types: those arising from morphological derivation (tasrif), from ambiguous dotting (i'jam), and from ambiguous inflection (i'rab). These subtle differences can completely change the meaning of a word or phrase, leading to logical fallacies or misinterpretations of sacred texts. Understanding these sources of error is essential for anyone engaging with Arabic Islamic sources.

Guided Path
1
Examine 'al-mukhtar': active vs. passive
The word 'al-mukhtar' can be read as active (mukhtīr, with kasra on the ya) meaning 'chooser', or passive (mukhtār, with fatha) meaning 'chosen'. This error comes from morphology (tasrif) where a single root takes different vowel patterns to indicate different meanings. Notice how the written form without diacritics is ambiguous.
In Arabic manuscripts, short vowels are often omitted, so the reader must infer from context.
2
Analyze undotted sequence 'h.r'
The author gives an example of four undotted words: 'h.r al-humur humr humr'. Without dots (i'jam), these letters could be read in multiple ways. The correct reading is determined by applying a specific dotting pattern: first letter of each word dotted above, second of second word dotted above, second of first two and fourth of four dotted below. This yields 'khabar al-khubz khayr khabar' (the report of the bread is the best report).
The same principle applies to 'qasr barr' (a measure of wheat) vs. 'faqir baz' (a poor clothless man).
3
Parse 'ghulam hasan' as compound or construct
The phrase 'ghulam hasan' without inflectional endings can be parsed either as an attributive compound (ghulam hasan = a beautiful boy, with hasan as adjective) or as a genitive construct (ghulam Hasani = the boy of Hasan, with hasan as a proper noun). This ambiguity arises from lack of i'rab (inflection).
The author shows how this leads to a syllogistic fallacy when 'hasan' is used inconsistently.
4
Trace the syllogistic error
Consider the syllogism: 'This boy is a ghulam hasan (beautiful boy). Every ghulam hasan (beautiful boy) is qabih (ugly). Therefore this boy is ugly.' But in the major premise, 'hasan' is an adjective, while in the minor premise, it could be a proper noun. If read as 'Ghulam Hasan' (the boy of Hasan), the middle term shifts meaning, causing equivocation. The conclusion only follows if both uses are identical.
This illustrates how a grammatical ambiguity can undermine a logical argument.

Commentary
This passage continues the classification of verbal error, focusing on the second subtype of non-compositional error: error due to morphology (tasrif) or orthography/inflection (i'jam/i'rab). It gives the example of 'al-mukhtar' which can be active or passive depending on vowel pattern, showing how the same root with different vowels yields different meanings. Then it explains error due to lack of dots (i'jam) with the example of the four ambiguous words that become clear only when properly dotted, and error due to inflection (i'rab) with the example of 'ghulam hasan' which can be parsed as an attributive compound (ghulam hasan = a good boy) or as a construct (ghulam Hasani = the boy of Hasan), leading to a logical fallacy in a syllogism. The commentary elaborates on each example, showing how misreading due to handwriting or lack of inflection leads to false conclusions. This teaches the student to be meticulous with written text and to consider grammar and orthography to avoid logical errors.

Key Takeaway
Verbal error in Arabic can stem from three distinct linguistic layers: morphological form (tasrif), orthographic dotting (i'jam), and inflectional endings (i'rab), each capable of generating ambiguity that can lead to logical fallacies if not carefully resolved.
