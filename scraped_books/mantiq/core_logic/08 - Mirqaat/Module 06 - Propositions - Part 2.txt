Understanding the Conditional Special (Mashrutah Khassah)
Definition and composition of the conditional special proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a conditional special proposition and distinguish between its positive and negative forms using the condition of non-permanence by essence.

Core Idea
The conditional special is a compound of general conditional and general absolute, tied by non-permanence by essence.

The conditional special (mashrutah khassah) is a compound modal proposition that combines a general conditional with a general absolute. This lesson unpacks its definition, explains why non-permanence must be by essence, and shows how to construct positive and negative forms. Understanding this compound is essential for correctly analyzing modal propositions in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components
The conditional special is composed of a general conditional (mashrutah 'ammah) and a general absolute (mutlaqah 'ammah). Determine which is the first part and which is the second.
2
Understand the condition of non-permanence by essence
The text explains that non-permanence cannot be by description because the general conditional already implies permanence by description. So non-permanence must be by essence, meaning the predicate holds only in times when the subject has the description, not in all times of the subject's existence.
3
Construct the positive form
For a positive conditional special, the first part is a positive general conditional (e.g., 'necessarily every writer is moving his fingers as long as he is a writer'), and the second part is a negative general absolute (e.g., 'nothing of the writer is actually moving his fingers'). The combination yields 'not always'.
4
Construct the negative form
For a negative conditional special, swap the signs: first part is a negative general conditional, second part is a positive general absolute.
5
Verify the composition
Use the example given: 'necessarily every writer is moving his fingers as long as he is a writer, not always' to confirm that the negation of permanence is captured by the general absolute negative.

Understanding the Conventional Special Proposition
Author defines conventional special compound as an extension of general conventional with non-permanence by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conventional special proposition in Arabic logical texts and distinguish it from the general conventional and conditional special by its defining features.

Core Idea
The conventional special adds 'non-permanence by essence' to the general conventional.

In Islamic logic, propositions are classified not only by quantity and quality but also by modality. The conventional special ('urfiyyah khassah) is a refinement of the general conventional ('urfiyyah 'ammah) that captures the idea that a proposition holds only as long as the subject fits a certain description. This lesson unpacks the definition and shows how examples from the tradition illustrate this concept. Mastering this distinction sharpens your ability to analyze arguments in theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate the Arabic term 'العرفية الخاصة' in the passage. This is the definiendum – the concept being defined.
2
Extract the genus
The passage states that the conventional special is built upon 'العرفية العامة' (general conventional). The genus is therefore the general conventional proposition.
General conventional propositions imply permanence according to description, e.g., 'Every writer is moving his fingers as long as he is writing'.
3
Find the differentia
The differentia is 'قيد اللادوام بحسب الذات' (the condition of non-permanence by essence). This means the proposition does not claim permanent essence-level truth; it only lasts as long as the subject meets the description.
Compare with plain general conventional which would imply permanence by essence (though usually not – this is why the condition is added).
4
Compare with the conditional special
The author's examples mirror those of the conditional special but use 'دائما' (always) instead of 'بالضرورة' (necessarily). Both share the non-permanence condition, but the choice of modal word distinguishes the two types.
So the conventional special is the 'always' counterpart of the 'necessarily' conditional special.

Defining the Existential Non-Necessary Proposition
Author defines existential non-necessary as general absolute with condition of non-necessity by essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the existential non-necessary proposition and explain why logicians restrict it to non-necessity by essence.

Core Idea
The existential non-necessary is the general absolute plus a specific condition of non-necessity by essence.

In this lesson, we examine a specific type of compound proposition called the existential non-necessary (wujudiyyah la-daruriyyah). The author defines it as the general absolute with the condition of non-necessity by essence. We'll see why they chose this condition rather than non-necessity by description, and how this fits into the classification of propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: 'existential non-necessary' (wujudiyyah la-daruriyyah). This is a compound proposition type.
2
Extract the genus
It belongs to the genus 'general absolute' (muṭlaqah 'āmmah). This is the broader category of propositions it falls under.
3
Identify the differentia
The distinguishing condition is 'non-necessity by essence' (lā-ḍarūrah biḥasb al-dhāt). This separates it from other general absolute propositions.
Non-necessity by essence means the predicate is not necessary of the subject due to the subject's own essence.
4
Understand the alternative rejected
Note that logicians could have conditioned the general absolute by 'non-necessity by description' but did not, because they did not study the logical rules (conversion, contradiction, syllogism) for that combination.
5
Apply to examples
Observe the positive example: 'Every human is actually a writer, not necessarily' and the negative: 'No human is actually a writer, not necessarily'. Recognize how the condition 'not necessarily' expresses non-necessity by essence.

Understanding the Existential Non-Permanent (Wujudiyyah La-Da'imah)
Definition of existential non-permanent and its alternative name.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the existential non-permanent compound proposition and differentiate it from the simple general absolute by its condition of non-permanence.

Core Idea
The existential non-permanent is a 'double' general absolute: it asserts existence but denies permanence.

In this lesson, we explore a compound proposition type derived from the general absolute: the existential non-permanent (al-wujūdiyyah al-lā-dā'imah). This proposition is not simply a general absolute; it adds the condition that the predication is not permanent by essence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping the full taxonomy of propositions in Islamic logic, especially as used in kalam and fiqh arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find the definiendum in the Arabic: الوجودية اللادائمة. This is a compound proposition type.
It is often contrasted with the permanent general absolute.
2
Extract the genus
The passage states it is the general absolute (المطلقة العامة) with a condition. The genus is the general absolute proposition.
3
Extract the differentia
The condition is non-permanence by essence (قيد اللادوام بحسب الذات). This distinguishes it from the simple general absolute, which has no such condition.
4
Understand the compound composition
The passage explains it is composed of two general absolutes: one positive and one negative. The positive asserts actual existence (e.g., 'Every human is actually laughing'), and the negative denies permanence ('not always').
5
Note the historical context
The alternative name 'Alexandrian absolute' arises because Alexander of Aphrodisias interpreted Aristotle's examples as implying non-permanence, though Aristotle used them to avoid implying permanence.

Temporal and Extended Compound Propositions
Author defines temporal and extended compounds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between temporal and extended compound propositions and identify their logical form with the condition of non-permanence by essence.

Core Idea
Temporal and extended compounds are absolute propositions restricted by non-permanence by essence.

This lesson introduces two compound propositions derived from absolute temporal and absolute extended propositions by adding the condition of non-permanence by essence. Understanding these helps in analyzing modal statements in Islamic logic, which are foundational for kalam arguments about temporality and attributes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base proposition
Both temporal and extended compounds are built upon absolute propositions (absolute temporal and absolute extended). The absolute form is unqualified by time or permanence.
The absolute temporal includes the idea of 'at some time' without restriction.
2
Recognize the added condition
The condition 'non-permanence by essence' (al-ladawam bi-hasb al-dhat) is added to the absolute proposition, transforming it into a compound. This means the predicate holds at some times but not at all times.
The condition is by essence, not by accident.
3
Examine the examples
The temporal example: 'Necessarily every moon is eclipsed at the time of earth's interposition, not always.' The extended example: 'Necessarily every human breathes at some time, not always.' Both use 'necessarily' and 'not always'.
The temporal refers to a specific time condition; the extended is more indefinite.
4
Contrast temporal and extended
Temporal compounds are tied to a specific temporal condition (like a lunar eclipse), while extended compounds are about an indefinite time period (like breathing). Both share the condition of non-permanence.
This distinction matters for analyzing permanence claims in Islamic theology.

Understanding the Possible Special (Mumkinah Khassah)
Author defines possible special as a proposition judging removal of absolute necessity from both sides.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a possible special proposition and explain why its positive and negative forms have the same meaning.

Core Idea
The possible special denies absolute necessity on both sides, making positive and negative expressions logically equivalent.

In this passage, we encounter the possible special (mumkinah khassah), a compound proposition from the modal logic of Islamic jurisprudence. While it may seem strange that a positive and a negative proposition can share the same meaning, this is exactly the case here. Understanding this concept is crucial for correctly analyzing modal arguments in kalam and usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core judgment
The possible special judges 'removal of absolute necessity from both sides.' This means it asserts that neither the affirmation nor the negation is necessary for the subject.
2
Break down the composition
The passage says it is composed of two general possibles (mumkinah 'ammah): one positive and one negative. Recognize that a general possible simply states possibility for one side, e.g., 'It is possible that S is P'.
3
Compare positive and negative forms
The text explicitly states there is no difference in meaning; the only difference is wording. So 'It is possible that Zayd is writing' and 'It is possible that Zayd is not writing' both mean that neither writing nor not-writing is necessary for Zayd.
4
Test with a concrete Islamic example
Apply this to a kalam example: 'It is possible that the world is temporally originated' and 'It is possible that the world is not temporally originated' both indicate the world's contingency—neither existence nor non-existence is necessary.

The Absolute General with Non-Necessity
Definition of the absolute general modal proposition with non-necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of the absolute general modal proposition qualified by non-necessity according to the essence and distinguish it from unqualified propositions.

Core Idea
Modal propositions qualify the mode of predication; 'non-necessity' adds a conditional dimension to an absolute general.

This lesson introduces the first type of modal proposition from the six listed by the author. Understanding how the absolute general is combined with a modal condition like 'non-necessity according to the essence' is foundational for grasping the entire system. Focus on the phrase structure: the categorical core remains, but now it carries a modal rider.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base proposition
The base is the absolute general (al-mutlaqa al-'amma), which is a categorical without any modal condition. In the example 'Every human is a writer', the predicate 'writer' is affirmed of 'every human' without temporal or necessity qualification.
The absolute general alone would be simply 'Every human is a writer', but here it is combined with a condition.
2
Extract the modal condition
The condition is 'non-necessity according to the essence' (qayd al-ladarura bihasb al-dhat). This means the predicate is not necessarily true of the subject in the sense of essential necessity; it is only actual in the present, not required by the essence.
The phrase 'bi-hasb al-dhat' indicates the condition attaches to the subject's essence, not to a description.
3
Analyze the expression in affirmation and negation
In the affirmative: 'Every human is a writer in actuality, not by necessity'. In the negative: 'No human is a writer in actuality, not by necessity'. Both include the explicit 'not by necessity' to remove any implication of necessity.
The negation also carries 'not by necessity' because it is denying existence, not necessity.
4
Compare with unqualified absolute general
The unqualified absolute general would leave open whether the predicate is necessary or contingent. Here, the condition of non-necessity narrows it to actuality without permanence, making it a specific modal type.
This distinction is crucial for later conversion and syllogistic rules.

The Logical Significance of 'Not Always' and 'Not Necessarily'
Author explains modal term indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the explicit meaning and the implicative indication of modal terms in Islamic logic, and correctly identify which proposition type 'ladawam' and 'ladurura' indicate.

Core Idea
'Not always' implies a general absolute; 'not necessarily' directly means the general possible.

In Islamic modal logic, the terms 'al-ladawam' (non-duration) and 'al-ladurura' (non-necessity) are used to qualify propositions. This passage clarifies an important subtlety: these terms do not directly define the proposition types they indicate. Rather, one of them indicates its proposition indirectly through necessary consequence, while the other directly equals the proposition. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion when interpreting modal statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two modal terms
Locate 'al-ladawam' (لا دوام) and 'al-ladurura' (لا ضرورة) in the Arabic passage. Note that they are negations of 'duration' and 'necessity' respectively, and they are used to qualify categorical propositions.
2
Examine the examples provided
The author gives two examples: 'Every human is laughing actually, not always' and 'Every animal is walking actually, not necessarily.' See how each is rephrased as a conjunction of an affirmative and a negative. This shows the logical content behind the modal qualification.
3
Distinguish explicit meaning from implicative meaning
For 'al-ladawam', its explicit (mutabaqi) meaning is simply the negation of perpetuity. But the general absolute proposition (a proposition true at some time without requiring duration) is not that explicit meaning; it is a necessary consequence (lazim) of it. For 'al-ladurura', its explicit meaning is the negation of necessity, which is exactly the general possible proposition.
4
Understand why the author uses 'indication' (إشارة)
Because the relationship is different for each: 'al-ladawam' points to the general absolute via implication, while 'al-ladurura' directly expresses the general possible. The term 'indication' covers both cases, allowing the author to speak uniformly.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the definitions of two modal terms: 'al-ladawam' (non-duration) and 'al-ladurura' (non-necessity). The author explains that these terms are not synonyms for the 'general absolute' and 'general possible' propositions, but rather they indicate them. The reasoning hinges on the distinction between the explicit (mutabaqi) meaning and the implicative (iltizami) meaning. For 'al-ladawam,' its literal meaning is the negation of duration, which is not directly the general absolute (which is a proposition that includes both possibility and actuality without duration). The general absolute is a necessary consequence (lazim) of 'al-ladawam,' not its direct meaning. In contrast, 'al-ladurura' literally means the negation of necessity, which is exactly the general possible (since possibility is defined as negation of necessity). Therefore, the author uses 'indication' (ishara) to cover both cases: for 'al-ladawam' it is an indication via implication, and for 'al-ladurura' it is a direct indication. The examples provided illustrate how statements with 'not always' or 'not necessarily' reduce to conjunctions of affirmative and negative propositions. This is a subtle point in modal logic that guards against confusing the term itself with the proposition it characterizes.

Definition and Twofold Division of the Connected Conditional
Definition and twofold division of the connected conditional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between luzumiyya and ittifaqiyya connected conditionals by identifying whether a relationship (‘illaqa) exists between the antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
A connected conditional without a relationship is merely accidental, not a genuine implication.

In this passage, the author defines the connected conditional as a judgment of a relation conditioned on another relation. He then divides it into two types: one where there is a genuine relationship (causality, common effect, or correlation) between the parts, and one where there is not. This division is crucial for evaluating whether a conditional statement expresses a necessary logical connection or a mere coincidence.

Commentary
This passage defines the connected conditional (al-shartiyya al-muttasila) and then expands on its types. The definition states that the connected conditional judges a relation (nisba) to be true on the condition of another relation being true (in affirmative) or false on the condition of another being false (in negative). Examples are given: 'If Zayd is human, he is an animal' (affirmative) and 'If Zayd is human, he is not a horse' (negative). The passage then divides the connected into two types based on whether there is a 'relationship' (al-'illaqa) between the antecedent and consequent: if there is, it is called luzumiyya (necessitating); if not, ittifaqiyya (accidental). However, the commentary notes that there is actually a third type: the absolute (mutlaqa) which is more general and does not specify whether the connection is necessary or accidental. The accidental itself is further subdivided into particular and general: the particular accidental can only have true components, while the general accidental allows a true consequent with an impossible antecedent. The relationship that justifies necessary implication is elaborated: either one is a cause of the other, or both are effects of a common cause, or they are correlatives (like fatherhood and sonship). The author favors including correlation as a valid relationship. This is essential for understanding how syllogisms function with conditional premises. The examples from fiqh and kalam: e.g., in usul al-fiqh, conditional rulings often involve causal connections ('illa). The division guards against conflating accidental co-occurrence with genuine logical implication.

The three disjunctive types are distinguished by whether they exclude truth, falsehood, or both.

Essential vs. Accidental Disjunctive Conditionals

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Essential opposition is conceptual; accidental opposition is circumstantial.

In the previous lesson, you learned the three kinds of disjunctive conditionals. Now, each kind is further divided into two: essential and accidental. The difference lies in the source of the mutual exclusion. If the two parts are inherently incompatible by their meanings, it is essential. If their incompatibility arises merely from the specific situation, it is accidental. Mastering this distinction will sharpen your analysis of 'either...or' statements in Islamic scholarly arguments.

Division of Disjunctive into Essential and Accidental
Author defines two types of disjunctive propositions with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'anadiyya and ittifaqiyya disjunctive propositions and provide correct Islamic examples for each.

Core Idea
The incompatibility in a disjunctive can be essential or merely coincidental.

The author of Mirqat al-Sullam explains that disjunctive propositions, though divided into three truth-table patterns (man'at al-jam', man'at al-khuluw, man'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw), can also be classified by the nature of the incompatibility between their two parts. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing compound arguments in Islamic logic, especially when the source of opposition is conceptual versus accidental.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of a disjunctive
In the Arabic, the author states: 'المنفصلة بأقسامها الثلاثة قسمان: عنادية واتفاقية'. Notice that the division applies to all three types of disjunctives based on the source of incompatibility.
The division is not a third type parallel to man'at al-jam' etc., but a cross-cutting classification.
2
Understand 'anadiyya (essential)
The incompatibility is due to the essences of the two parts (لذاتهما). Example: 'the number is even or odd' – the concepts of even and odd are inherently incompatible. They cannot both be true nor both false (if we consider man'at al-jam' wa al-khuluw).
The example 'Zayd is in the sea or he does not drown' is also 'anadiyya because being in the sea essentially precludes not drowning (under normal circumstances).
3
Understand ittifaqiyya (accidental)
The incompatibility is merely coincidental (بمجرد الاتفاق). Example: 'This is black or a writer' – there is no conceptual opposition between blackness and being a writer; they just happen to be incompatible in this instance.
The author explains that in this example, the two parts are not both true (because writing is absent) nor both false (because blackness is present), illustrating the accidental nature.
4
Test the examples provided
Why is 'Zayd in the sea or not drowning' 'anadiyya? Because the concept of being in the sea (without a miracle) is inherently incompatible with not drowning. Why is 'black or writer' ittifaqiyya? Because the concepts are compatible; the incompatibility arises only from the specific situation.
This step reinforces the distinction by analyzing the author's own examples.
5
Derive man'at al-jam' and man'at al-khuluw from the ittifaqiyya example
The author notes that from the example 'black or writer', we can see that it is man'at al-jam' (cannot both be true because writing is absent) and man'at al-khuluw (cannot both be false because blackness is present). Realize that truth-table patterns are separate from the essential/accidental classification.
This shows that 'anadiyya and ittifaqiyya are not tied to specific truth-table patterns; they are about the source of incompatibility.

The Need for Taqadir in Conditionals
Author explains the classification of conditionals and the necessity of taqadir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the types of conditionals (shakhsiyya, kulliyya, juz'iyya, muhmala) and explain why the natural proposition (tabi'iyya) is not applicable, using the concept of taqadir as analogous to individuals in categoricals.

Core Idea
Conditionals require suppositions (taqadir) because the entailment must hold across all possible states.

The passage draws a parallel between the classification of categorical propositions and conditionals, but then introduces a crucial difference: the natural proposition has no place in conditionals. Why? Because conditionals are inherently about the relationship between antecedent and consequent under varying circumstances. This leads to the concept of taqadir—the suppositions that define the scope of the conditional judgment.

Guided Path
1
Map categorical divisions to conditionals
Recall that categoricals are divided into shakhsiyya (personal), mahsura (quantified: kulliyya and juz'iyya), muhmala (indefinite), and tabi'iyya (natural). The author states that conditionals are divided the same way, except tabi'iyya.
Tabi'iyya is a proposition that is not quantified and the subject is taken as an unqualified nature.
2
Identify why tabi'iyya is excluded from conditionals
The author says that the conditional judgment is not conceivable without considering taqadir. Unlike categoricals, where you can take the subject 'human' as a nature, in conditionals the antecedent must be considered under various suppositions. Therefore, the unquantified natural form is impossible.
An unquantified categorical like 'Human is an animal' is meaningful, but an unquantified conditional like 'If Zayd is human, he is an animal' is actually universal because it implicitly covers all suppositions.
3
Define taqadir
From the passage: taqadir are 'the states that can co-occur with the antecedent, even if they are impossible in themselves.' They are the possible circumstances under which the antecedent is considered.
These suppositions can be necessary or accidental to the antecedent. The key is that the logical relation (luzum or 'anad) is evaluated across these states.
4
Analogize taqadir to individuals in categoricals
Just as categoricals are evaluated over individuals (afrad) that fall under the subject term, conditionals are evaluated over suppositions (taqadir) that accompany the antecedent. For example, in 'Whenever Zayd is human, he is animal,' the suppositions include Zayd being a writer, laughing, etc.
This analogy helps understand how quantification works in conditionals: universal means the consequent holds under all suppositions; particular means under some suppositions.
5
Apply the concept to an impossible antecedent
The author gives the example 'Whenever a human is a horse, he is an animal.' Even though the antecedent is impossible, the conditional is considered true because in any supposition where a human is a horse (itself impossible), the consequent follows. This shows taqadir can include impossible states.
This is important for logical analysis: the truth of conditionals depends on the connection, not the actuality of the antecedent.

Commentary

It first states that the antecedent (مقدم) and consequent (تالي) themselves carry no truth value while they are parts of the conditional; they only acquire judgment after analysis. Then it classifies conditionals based on the types of their parts: either the parts are similar (both categoricals, both conjunctive conditionals, or both disjunctive conditionals) or different (one categorical and one conjunctive, one categorical and one disjunctive, or one conjunctive and one disjunctive). Initially, this gives six types each for conjunctive and disjunctive conditionals. However, for conjunctive conditionals, mixed types (different parts) split into two due to the inherent distinction between antecedent and consequent: the antecedent is naturally prior and cannot be swapped without changing meaning. In disjunctive conditionals, the antecedent and consequent are distinguished only by order; swapping them does not change the logical content, so no split occurs. Consequently, conjunctive conditionals have 9 types (3 similar + 6 mixed from 3×2) and disjunctive conditionals have 6 types (3 similar + 3 mixed). The commentary then provides nine concrete examples of conjunctive conditionals covering all categories: from two categoricals, from two conjunctives, from two disjunctives, and various combinations of categorical with conjunctive/disjunctive in both orders, and conjunctive with disjunctive in both orders. These examples illustrate how the logical form changes based on the composition of parts. The key takeaway is the importance of recognizing the nature of the antecedent-consequent relationship in determining the type of conditional.

The Definition of Contradiction and Its Essential Condition
Author defines contradiction with essential condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify genuine logical contradictions in Islamic arguments and distinguish them from accidental material incompatibilities.

Core Idea
Genuine contradiction is intrinsic formal opposition, not just any affirmation-negation pair.

This lesson unpacks the precise definition of tanaquḍ — the engine of many proofs in kalam and usul. We will see why the condition 'by itself' (bi-dhātihi) is vital for distinguishing true logical opposition from merely accidental incompatibility, using examples like 'Zayd is standing' vs. 'Zayd is not standing'.

Guided Path

Identify the two propositions
Find an affirmative and a negative proposition in the definition. The example is 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not standing'. Note that they share the same subject and predicate but differ in quality.
2
Apply the 'by itself' test
Ask: Does the truth of one directly imply the falsity of the other without any additional premises? In the example, yes. In the material case ('Zayd is human' vs. 'Zayd is not rational'), it requires the premise that 'rational' necessarily follows from 'human'.
3
Check for material equivalence
If the opposition relies on a necessary concomitant (lāzim musāwī) like 'rational' for 'human', then it is not genuine contradiction. The incompatibility is due to material equivalence, not pure logical form.
The author calls this 'by itself' (li-dhātihi) to exclude such cases.
4
Recognize the scope
Understand why the definition is limited to propositions: only propositional contradiction is useful in syllogisms. Contradiction in simple terms is known by analogy and not needed for the logician's purpose.
The commentator anticipates the objection that rules should be general, and replies that generality is relative to the art's purpose.

Commentary
This passage defines formal contradiction (tanaquḍ) between propositions and clarifies its essential condition. The author first gives the definition: contradiction is the difference between two propositions by affirmation and negation such that the difference itself (by its very nature) necessitates that one is true and the other false. The example is 'Zayd is standing' and 'Zayd is not standing' — these are genuine contradictories because the truth of one directly implies the falsity of the other due to the logical opposition of the predicates, not due to any external equivalence. The commentator then explains the etymology: naqḍ originally means 'undoing' (ḥall) and then came to mean nullification in general; the verbal form tafāʿul indicates mutual action. Next, he notes that the definition is restricted to propositions because only propositional contradiction is useful in syllogisms; contradiction in simple terms (like 'human' vs. 'non-human') is known by analogy and not needed here. He anticipates an objection: shouldn't the rules of logic be general, covering all particulars? The answer is that generality is relative to the logician's purpose; since they have no need for simple-term contradiction in their arguments, they limit the discussion to propositions. Finally, the condition 'by itself' excludes cases where the opposition is not intrinsic but depends on material equivalence. For example, 'Zayd is a human' and 'Zayd is not rational' are not genuine contradictories because 'rational' is a necessary concomitant (lāzim musāwī) of 'human'. The falsity of one implies the truth of the other only because one statement is equivalent in meaning to the other's negation, not due to pure logical form. Thus, true contradiction requires purely formal opposition, not just contingent material incompatibility.

Conditions of Contradiction in Categorical and Modal Propositions
Defines conditions for contradiction in categorical and modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether two categorical or modal propositions are contradictory by checking the required differences in quantity and modality, using examples from kalam.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires formal conditions: quantity difference for categoricals, modality difference for modals.

In Islamic logic, precise conditions for contradiction are crucial for valid argumentation in kalam and usul al-fiqh. This passage from the Mirqat defines these conditions for categorical (quantified) and modal propositions, addressing a common objection to clarify why difference in quantity is essential.

Guided Path
1
Identify proposition types
Recognize that the passage discusses two types of propositions: categorical (maḥṣūra) with quantifiers, and modal (muwajjaha) with modalities like necessity or possibility.
2

The two contradictory categoricals must differ in quantity: one universal and one particular. The passage shows that two universals can both be false and two particulars can both be true.

The Four Basic General Modal Propositions
Defines general possible, general absolute, hylic possible, and hylic absolute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the four general modal propositions: general possible, general absolute, hylic possible, and hylic absolute.

Core Idea
Modal propositions qualify necessity or permanence; these four generals anchor modality.

In Islamic logic, propositions are not merely categorical: they can be modal, indicating necessity, possibility, or actuality under conditions. This lesson defines the four fundamental general modal propositions that form the basis for more complex modal distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Understand general possible (al-mumkina al-ʿāmma)
General possible means the negation of necessity from the side opposite the judgement. For example, if the judgement is affirmative, the opposite is negative; the proposition is possible if that negative is not necessary. Identify the judgement and its opposite side.
This is not mere possibility in a loose sense, but specifically the non-necessity of the opposite.
2
Understand general absolute (al-muṭlaqa al-ʿāmma)
General absolute means the proposition is not true at all times. It allows for truth at some times and falsehood at others. Note how it contradicts a universal affirmative: if one is true at all times, the other must be false at some times.
This is the basic absolute proposition without any temporal restriction.
3
Understand hylic possible (al-ḥīniyya al-mumkina)
Hylic possible judges possibility during a specific description (ḥīn). Example: 'Everyone with pleurisy can cough while having pleurisy.' The possibility is tied to the subject being under that description.
The modality is restricted to the time when the description applies.
4
Understand hylic absolute (al-ḥīniyya al-muṭlaqa)
Hylic absolute judges actual affirmation or negation at some times during the description. Example: 'Everyone with pleurisy actually coughs at some times while having pleurisy.' It asserts actuality, not just possibility.
Contrast with hylic possible: actual vs. possible.

Contradictory of Complex Propositions
Method for contradictory of complex propositions.

Learning Objective
Derive the contradictory of a complex proposition by decomposing it into its parts and forming a disjunction that prevents emptiness (māniʿat al-khuluw).

Core Idea
The contradictory of a complex proposition is a disjunction of the contradictories of its parts.

Complex propositions (murakkabāt) combine two simple propositions with different modalities. Finding their contradictory is not a simple negation; the passage teaches a systematic method: break the complex into its two parts, take the contradictory of each, and combine them in a disjunction that cannot be false. This ensures that the resulting disjunction is logically equivalent to the real contradictory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts of the complex
Determine the two simple propositions that compose the complex, noting their qualities (affirmation/negation) and modalities.
The example in the passage: 'Necessarily every writer moves his fingers as long as he is a writer, not always.' This has a conditioned part and an absolute part.
2
Find the contradictory of each part
Apply the rules for simple proposition contradiction. For the conditioned part 'Necessarily every writer...', its contradictory is 'Not some writer... with hylic possibility'. For the absolute part 'not always', its contradictory is 'some writer always'.
3
Form a disjunction that prevents emptiness
Combine the two contradictories with an exclusive 'or' that covers all possibilities. This disjunction (manʿat al-khuluw) cannot be false because if one contradictory is false, the other must be true.
The passage says: 'Either this contradictory or that.'
4
Verify the result
Check that the disjunction is a necessary equivalent of the real contradictory. The passage clarifies that the disjunction is not the contradictory itself but its equivalent.

Rules for Contradictories of Conditionals: Genus, Species, and Quality
Author explains rules for contradictories of conditionals and method for compounds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules for constructing the contradictory of any conditional proposition, including compound conditionals, using agreement in genus and species and opposition in quality, and explain why the contradictory of a compound is a disjunctive equivalent.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a conditional must match its genus and species but flip its quality.

In this lesson, we delve into the precise rules for constructing the contradictory (naqid) of conditional propositions (shartiyyat). The author lays down a clear principle: the contradictory must agree with the original in genus (conjunctive or disjunctive) and species (implicative or exclusionary), but oppose it in quality (affirmative or negative). For compound conditionals, the contradictory is not a simple proposition but a disjunctive concept that is a necessary equivalent of the true contradictory, which is the removal of the entire compound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus (jins) of the conditional
Determine whether the conditional is conjunctive (mutasila) or disjunctive (munfasila). This is the genus.
For example, 'if A then B' is conjunctive; 'either A or B' is disjunctive.
2
Identify the species (naw') of the conditional
Within the genus, identify the species: implicative (luzumiyya) or exclusionary (inadiyya). The passage mentions these two species.
Implicative conditionals assert a connection; exclusionary disjunctives assert mutual exclusion.
3
Note the quality (kayf) of the proposition
Is the conditional affirmative or negative? This is the quality.
Quality is one of the fundamental attributes of propositions.
4
Construct the contradictory by flipping the quality while keeping genus and species
The contradictory must have the same genus and species as the original, but the opposite quality. For example, an affirmative conjunctive implicative becomes a negative conjunctive implicative.
This rule applies to all simple (non-compound) conditionals.
5
For compound conditionals, use the disjunctive concept method
Analyze the compound into its two simple parts. Find the contradictory of each part (using steps 1-4). Then compose an exclusive disjunction (mani'at al-khuluw) of those two contradictories. This disjunction is not the true contradictory but a necessary equivalent.
The passage illustrates this with the special conditional (al-mashrutah al-khassah): the contradictory is a disjunction of the temporal possibility and the perpetual.

Infinite vs. Negative Propositions: The Role of the Copula
Author distinguishes infinite and negative propositions via order of negation and copula.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between infinite and negative propositions based on the order of negation and the copula in Arabic logical statements.

Core Idea
The position of negation relative to the copula determines whether a proposition is infinite or negative.

In Arabic logic, the order of 'laysa' (negation) and the copula (rābiṭ) is not a stylistic choice—it changes the logical type of the proposition. The passage illustrates this with the examples 'Zaid is not standing' and 'Zaid is not-standing'. Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding fallacies in theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the copula and negation

2

If negation precedes the copula, the proposition is infinite (معدولة): it negates the copula itself, making the predicate a non-real term. If the copula precedes negation, it is negative (سالبة): it simply denies the predicate of the subject.

Modality and Negation: Scope and Meaning
Author discusses fallacies arising from order of modality and negation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between propositions with modality before negation and those with modality after negation, and identify their truth conditions.

Core Idea
The position of the modal operator relative to negation changes the truth conditions of the proposition.

The passage shows how modality interacts with negation: 'necessarily he is not' and 'not necessarily he is' are both negative but have different meanings. The first applies to impossible things, the second to possible things. This distinction is vital in kalam for discussing necessity, possibility, and impossibility.

Even and Odd Negation Layers: Avoiding the Fallacy
Author explains even/odd negation layers and the fallacy of substituting odd for even.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a stack of negations results in affirmation or negation, and identify the fallacy of substituting odd for even layers.

Core Idea
An even number of negations cancels out to affirmation; an odd number yields negation.

The passage introduces the concept of 'even negations' (sulūb shaf‘iyya) and 'odd negations' (sulūb watariyya). A common formal fallacy is to mistake an odd stack for an even one, leading to an erroneous conclusion. Understanding these layers is essential for rigorous logical analysis in Islamic sciences, especially in usul al-fiqh where double negatives can flip a ruling.

Guided Path
1
Count the number of negations
Negation can be expressed by 'laysa', 'lā', or 'ghayr'. Count each instance in the sequence.
Example from passage: 'salb salb' (negation of negation) is 2 (even); 'salb salb salb' (negation of negation of negation) is 3 (odd).
2
Determine the parity
If the count is even, the net effect is affirmation; if odd, the net effect is negation.
The passage states: 'even orders... are affirmation' and 'odd orders... are negation'.
3
Identify the fallacy
The fallacy occurs when someone uses an odd number of negations where an even number is required, or vice versa, resulting in a reversed truth value.
The passage says: 'taking the odd negatives in place of the even negatives is an error'.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
In fiqh, one might say 'It is not the case that the prayer is invalid' (one negation = negative) vs. 'It is not that the prayer is invalid' (double negation = affirmative). Mistaking one for the other changes the ruling.
Use a simple example: 'Wine is not permitted' (negation) vs. 'Wine is not not permitted' (affirmation).
