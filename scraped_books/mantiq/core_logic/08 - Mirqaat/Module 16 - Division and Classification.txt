Definition of Mutawatii' and Mushakkik
Author defines mushakkik with examples and explains the reason for its name.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mushakkik term and explain why it is called mushakkik, using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Mushakkik terms share one meaning but apply with priority or intensity.

In Islamic logic, terms are classified by how they apply to individuals. Mutawatii' applies equally; mushakkik applies with gradation, causing doubt. This lesson focuses on the definition and reason for the name, with key examples like 'existence' (wujud) and 'whiteness' (bayad).

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristic of mushakkik
The text says it applies to some individuals by priority (الاولية), intensity (الاشدية), or precedence (الاولوية), and to others by their opposites. This gradation is the core feature.
The opposite could be posteriority or weakness.
2
Recognize the examples given
Existence (wujud) is applied to God (the Necessary) with priority and to contingents with posteriority. Whiteness (bayad) is applied to snow and ivory with different intensities.
These examples show how the same term covers multiple levels.
3
Understand why it is called mushakkik
It causes doubt (shakk) in the observer: the term shares a core meaning (like univocal) but differs in degree (like homonyms), so one hesitates between the two classifications.
The name reflects the psychological effect on the logician.
4
Distinguish from univocal and homonymous
A univocal term (متواطئ) applies equally (e.g., 'human' for all humans); a homonym (مشترك) has different meanings (e.g., 'ayn = eye, gold, knee). Mushakkik lies in between, with a single meaning but varying application.
This distinction is crucial for precise definition in Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of singular terms: mutawatii' (univocal) and mushakkik (equivocal by priority). It begins by stating that mushakkik applies to individuals with varying degrees of primacy, intensity, or priority, giving the example of 'existence' (wujud) when predicated of God versus contingent beings, and of 'whiteness' (bayad) when predicated of snow versus ivory. The author then explains that this type is called mushakkik because it causes doubt in the observer. The text then enters a lengthy discussion, starting with the example of existence, questioning whether the variation is really by intensity and weakness or by priority and posteriority. It presents the view of the Peripatetics that if existence varied by intensity, it would necessitate that the more intense and weaker types are distinct species, leading to either composition of genus and differentia or being accidents. The alternative proposed is that existence is a simple specific reality with a single meaning but varying in modes of occurrence—this is the Illuminationist position. The passage then summarizes the core disagreement: whether tashkik can occur in quiddities (essences) themselves or only in their accidental qualifications. The Peripatetics deny tashkik in substantial and accidental quiddities, asserting it only in the attribution of accidents, while the Illuminationists affirm it in quiddities, citing examples like substances of the intellectual world being stronger in substantiality. The passage concludes by returning to the original reason for the name mushakkik: because the common meaning causes doubt between univocity and homonymy.

The Tashkik Debate: Existence and the Peripatetic Objection
Author presents the Peripatetic objection to tashkik by intensity in existence and the Illuminationist alternative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Peripatetic argument against tashkik by intensity in existence and explain the Illuminationist alternative.

Core Idea
If existence varied by intensity, it would break down into distinct types, undermining its unity.

After defining mushakkik, the author delves into a debate about whether existence (wujud) is mushakkik by intensity. The Peripatetics argue that variance by intensity leads to logical problems, while the Illuminationists propose a graded unity. This lesson unpacks their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under scrutiny
The claim is that existence is mushakkik not only by priority but also by intensity (shidda) and weakness (du'f). An example: the existence of the Necessary (God) is more intense than that of contingent beings.
The author says this claim is 'محل تأمل' (open to reflection).
2
Extract the Peripatetic argument
If existence differed by intensity, then the intense and weak forms would be two distinct types (نوعان متبائنان). Then either absolute existence is a genus of these two types (meaning they are composed of genus and differentia) or it is an accident to them (meaning they have a reality beyond existence). Both options are problematic.
This is a conditional argument: if intensity, then undesirable consequences.
3
Understand the Illuminationist response
The Illuminationists say existence is a simple reality (بسيطة) without genus or differentia, having one meaning but varying in modes of occurrence (أنحاء التشكيك). Variance is not by type but by perfection and deficiency, which does not compromise unity.
This preserves the single meaning while allowing gradation.
4
Assess the status of 'intensity'
The author notes that intensity and weakness ultimately refer to perfection (كمال) and deficiency (نقصان), a key idea in Illuminationist metaphysics.
This frames tashkik as a matter of degree within a single reality.

Core Disagreement: Tashkik in Quiddities vs. Accidents
Author summarizes the Peripatetic and Illuminationist positions on tashkik in quiddities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Peripatetic and Illuminationist positions on tashkik in quiddities and evaluate their arguments.

Core Idea
Peripatetics limit tashkik to accidental attributes; Illuminationists extend it to quiddities themselves.

The passage concludes with a summary of the key disagreement: can tashkik occur in the essence (quiddity) of a thing, or only in its accidental properties? The Peripatetics deny the former, the Illuminationists affirm it with examples from substantiality and animality. This lesson breaks down the two positions.

Guided Path
1
State the core question
The question is whether individuals of a single quiddity (ماهية) can vary by priority, intensity, etc., without adding an external cause. In other words, can the quiddity itself be graded?
The author calls it a 'battlefield of opinions' (معركة الآراء).
2
Summarize the Peripatetic position
Peripatetics say: لا تشكيك في الماهية – no tashkik in quiddity, whether substantial or accidental. Tashkik only occurs in the accidental attribution (في صدق مفهوم العرضي). Example: 'black' (أسود) is derived from blackness but varies in its predication across individuals; blackness itself is not graded.
They distinguish between the quiddity (sawad) and the derived term (aswad).
3
Summarize the Illuminationist position
Illuminationists say tashkik occurs in quiddities themselves. For example, the substance of the intellectual world is stronger in substantiality than substances of the lower world; an elephant is more animal than a gnat. These differences are not accidental but essential.
They cite Hikmat al-Ishraq as source.
4
Evaluate the implications
If quiddities themselves are graded, then definitions (hadd) become relative; if tashkik is only accidental, definitions remain absolute. This affects how we classify beings in Islamic metaphysics.
This is a deep issue (مسألة عويصة).

Divisions of Terms with Multiple Meanings: Mushtarak, Manqul, Murtajal
Author defines mushtarak, manqul (with three subtypes), and murtajal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mushtarak, manqul (with its three subtypes), and murtajal, and identify each in a given Islamic text.

Core Idea
Not every word with multiple meanings is the same: how meanings are assigned determines the logical category.

In logic, understanding how words acquire multiple meanings is crucial for interpreting definitions correctly. This passage classifies multi-meaning terms into mushtarak (homonyms), manqul (transferred with suitability), and murtajal (transferred without suitability). You will learn to identify each type and see why the distinction matters for precision in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main categories
The passage divides multi-meaning terms into mushtarak, manqul, and murtajal. Note that manqul has three subtypes based on the transferrer.
All three are types of words with multiple meanings, but the mechanism of meaning assignment differs.
2
Analyze mushtarak: independent homonyms
A mushtarak word is placed independently for each meaning with no priority. Example: 'ayn for gold, eye, knee. No original meaning is abandoned.
Mushtarak does not require suitability between meanings.
3
Analyze manqul: transferred with suitability
A manqul word was originally for one meaning, then transferred to a second due to suitability (munasaba). The new meaning becomes prevalent and the original is abandoned. Subtypes depend on the transferrer: general custom (manqul 'urfi), the Lawgiver (manqul shar'i), or specialists (manqul istilahi).
Suitability is the key feature distinguishing manqul from murtajal.
4
Analyze murtajal: transferred without suitability
A murtajal word is transferred from an original meaning to a new one with no suitability, like the proper name Ja'far from 'small river'. The majority view separates it from manqul.
The lack of suitability makes it a distinct category.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional section that classifies singular terms that have multiple meanings. The author begins by stating that such terms are of two main types: mushtarak (homonym) and manqul (transferred). Mushtarak is defined as a word that was originally and independently assigned to each of its meanings, like 'ayn meaning gold, eye, and knee. The commentary then clarifies that the word is called mushtarak because it shares the same form across different meanings, not because it shares individuals. The author notes that there is disagreement on whether murtajal is considered mushtarak or manqul, but the majority view is that manqul and murtajal are separate categories. The name murtajal derives from the idea of composing a speech 'impromptu' without prior thought, because its assignment to the second meaning lacks a mediating suitability.
