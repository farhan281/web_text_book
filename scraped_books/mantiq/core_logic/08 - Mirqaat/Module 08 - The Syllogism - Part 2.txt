Fallacy of Composition Due to Ambiguous Qualifier
Author explains the fallacy caused by ambiguity of a qualifier belonging to subject or predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of ambiguous qualifier in a categorical syllogism and determine whether the qualifier belongs to the subject or predicate.

Core Idea
A single word like 'alone' can entirely change a syllogism's validity.

In this lesson, we examine a classic logical fallacy: when a qualifier is ambiguous as to whether it attaches to the subject or predicate. The logicians call this 'bad consideration of predication' (سوء اعتبار الحمل). By analyzing the example 'Man alone is laughing', you will see how a single word can produce a false conclusion. This skill is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous qualifier
Locate the word that causes the ambiguity. In the example 'Man alone is laughing', the qualifier 'alone' can be interpreted as modifying 'man' (subject) or 'laughing' (predicate).
2
Interpret the qualifier as part of the subject
Write the minor premise as 'Man alone [i.e., only man] is laughing'. Combine with the major 'Every laughing thing is an animal'. The apparent conclusion is 'Man alone is an animal' – which is false because it implies only man is an animal.
This interpretation leads to an invalid syllogism.
3
Interpret the qualifier as part of the predicate
Write the minor premise as 'Man is alone [i.e., only] laughing'. Then the major should be 'Everything that is alone laughing is an animal'. The conclusion becomes 'Man is an animal' – which is true.
This interpretation yields a valid and true syllogism.
4
Recognize the fallacy as bad consideration of predication
The error is that the minor premise is actually two propositions: an affirmative ('Man is laughing') and a negative ('Nothing else laughs'). Only the affirmative works with the major. Bundling them as one leads to a false conclusion.
This fallacy is called 'sū' i‘tibār al-ḥaml'.
5
Apply the lesson to Islamic reasoning
Consider a similar qualifier in an Islamic legal argument, like 'Only the prayer is obligatory'. The fallacy could lead to incorrect rulings if the qualifier is misassigned.

This fallacy is called by the logicians 'bad consideration of predication' with respect to the terms.

Fallacy of Major Not Being Predicated of All Middle
Author explains the fallacy when the major premise is not universal in the first figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a first-figure categorical syllogism fails because the major premise is not universal, and explain why the conclusion does not follow.

Core Idea
A true major premise is not enough; it must apply to every individual of the middle term.

In the first figure of the categorical syllogism, one crucial condition is that the major premise must be universal. A seemingly valid syllogism can lead to a false conclusion if this condition is violated. The present passage illustrates this with an example and explains the underlying cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism components
Extract the major, minor, and middle terms from the example: 'Every human is an animal; animal is a genus; therefore every human is a genus'. The major term is 'genus', the minor is 'human', and the middle is 'animal'.
2
Check the major premise for universality
The major premise is 'Animal is a genus'. Is this a universal proposition? It states a property of the essence of animal, not of every individual animal. This is what the author calls a 'natural' (tabī‘iyya) proposition, not a universal (kulliyya).
In the first figure, the major premise must be a universal affirmative or universal negative.
3
Recognize the fallacy source
Because the major premise is not universal, the predicate 'genus' does not apply to every individual of the middle term 'animal'. Hence the conclusion 'Every human is a genus' is false. The error is neglecting the condition of universality of the major premise.
The author states: 'The cause of the fallacy is only the neglect of the universality of the major premise.'
4
Formulate the condition
For a valid first-figure syllogism, the major premise must be universal. If it is only a natural proposition (about the essence), the syllogism is invalid. This condition is distinct from the truth of the premises.
Always verify the quantifier of the major premise in first-figure syllogisms.

Effect of Mixed Premises on Syllogism Classification
Author states that a syllogism's classification follows its weakest premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a syllogism with premises of different epistemic strengths into the appropriate logical art.

Core Idea
A syllogism takes the rank of its weakest premise.

In this final passage on the five arts, the author addresses a subtle challenge: what happens when a syllogism mixes premises of different types? The answer reveals a key principle: the whole follows the weakest part. This ensures that no syllogism falls outside the fivefold classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Find the author's rule: if one premise is not demonstrative, the whole syllogism is not demonstrative.
The rule applies symmetrically to all arts.
2
Notice the implicit objection
The commentator raises a concern: mixed-premise syllogisms might not fit any single art, breaking the fivefold classification.
Why is this a problem? The arts are supposed to be exhaustive.
3
Understand the solution
The composite follows the weaker premise. For example, a mix of certain (yaqini) and conjectural (maznuna) becomes rhetoric.
The weaker premise determines the art.
4
Apply the rule
Given a syllogism with one certain premise and one poetic premise, which art does it belong to? Poetics.
5
Reflect on exhaustiveness
Because every mixed syllogism falls under the art of its weaker premise, the five arts remain comprehensive.
No syllogism escapes classification.

Commentary
This passage is a logical principle followed by an objection-and-answer commentary. The author first states a general rule: if one premise of a syllogism is not demonstrative (burhani), the entire syllogism becomes non-demonstrative, taking on the category of the weaker premise (jadali, khitabi, etc.). He then concludes the discussion of the five arts (demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetics, sophistry), noting that the two goals of logic—conception and assent—are now complete. The commentary that follows addresses an implicit objection: since syllogisms can mix premises of different epistemic types, such mixed syllogisms might not fit into any single art, breaking the fivefold classification. The answer is that such composites are classified according to the weaker premise. For instance, a mix of certain (yaqini) and conjectural (maznuna) becomes rhetoric; a mix of conjectural and illusory (mawhuma) becomes sophistry. Thus, the five arts remain exhaustive.
