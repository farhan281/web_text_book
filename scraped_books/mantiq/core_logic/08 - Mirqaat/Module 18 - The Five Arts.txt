The Five Arts: A Material Classification and a Critique of Formalism
Critique and enumeration of five arts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the critique of overemphasis on syllogistic form and classify any syllogism into one of the five arts based on its premises.

Core Idea
Wisdom in logic lies in the material of premises, not just formal validity.

The author of the Mirqāt laments that later logicians have become obsessed with the formal structure of syllogisms, especially conditional ones, while neglecting the substance of premises. He argues that error more often arises from faulty matter than faulty form, and then lays out the classical division of syllogisms into five arts based on the epistemic status of their premises. This lesson helps you understand both the critique and the classification so you can evaluate arguments holistically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's grievance
Notice that the author faults later logicians for expanding on syllogistic form (especially conditionals) while 'rejecting the matter' (رفضوا أمر المادة). They confined themselves to mere definitions of the five arts.
The author uses strong rhetorical language to express disapproval, e.g., 'I do not know what matter invited them to that.'
2
Understand why matter is prioritized
The commentary explains that error can occur in form or matter, but error in matter is more frequent because one may think false premises are true or inappropriate premises are appropriate. Form laws are not enough to safeguard against this.
3
Learn the division by matter
The passage states: 'the syllogism, with respect to matter, divides into five divisions.' These are the five logical arts: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry.
The division is based on whether premises produce assent (تصديق) or another effect (imagination/takhyl).
4
Map each art to its material
Assent-producing premises yield either opinion (ظن) → rhetoric (خطابة) or certainty (يقين) → demonstration (برهان). If certainty is not attained but the premises are accepted by the public or opponent, it is dialectic (جدل); otherwise, sophistry (مغالطة). Imagination-based premises yield poetry (شعر).
5
Apply to a real example
Take a kalam argument: 'The world is temporally originated; everything temporally originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' The premises are certain for a mutakallim, so this is demonstration (برهان).
If the same premises were presented as merely acceptable to an opponent in a debate, it would be dialectic.

Commentary
This passage is a sharp critique of later logicians who overemphasized the formal aspects of syllogisms, especially conditional syllogisms, while neglecting the matter (premises). The author of the commentary (possibly the Mirqaat author) laments this trend and urges the student to study the works of the ancients. He then presents the division of syllogisms based on matter into five arts (صناعات خمس). The commentary explains that error mostly arises from inappropriate matter, so understanding the types of premises is crucial. The five arts are defined by their material: demonstration (برهان) uses certain premises; dialectic (جدل) uses premises accepted by the opponent or public; rhetoric (خطابة) uses premises producing opinion; poetry (شعر) uses imaginative premises; and sophistry (مغالطة) uses premises that deceive. The passage reinforces the importance of the material approach.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the later logicians' preoccupation with the form of syllogisms at the expense of their material. The author begins by praising the earlier scholars who gave detailed attention to the material (مادة) of syllogisms, i.e., the premises, because for students of the craft (صناعة) the benefit is greater. After this exhortation, he presents the division of syllogisms by material into five types: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry. The division is controlled by considering whether the premises yield assent (تصديق) or imaginative effect (تخييل). If assent, it is either opinion (ظن) or certainty (جزم). Opinion yields rhetoric; certain certainty yields demonstration; if certainty but with public acknowledgment or opponent acceptance, it is dialectic; otherwise, sophistry. If it yields imaginative effect, it is poetry. This classification is fundamental to the five arts (صناعات خمس).

The Five Arts of Syllogism Based on Material
Author defines five arts of syllogism based on material.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any syllogism into one of the five arts according to the material of its premises.

Core Idea
The five arts—demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, poetry, and sophistry—are defined by the kind of acceptance their premises produce.

The author presents a clear decision tree: premises either yield assent (tasdiq) or imaginative effect (takhyeel). If they yield assent, it is either opinion or certainty; from there, further conditions lead to the specific art. This lesson guides you through that classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary branch
Do the premises produce assent (tasdiq) or an imaginative effect (takhyeel)? The author states that the premises either yield assent or another effect (imaginative suggestion). The latter is poetry.
2
If assent, determine opinion or certainty
If the premises yield assent, decide whether that assent is opinion (zann) or certainty (jazzm). Opinion leads to rhetoric; certainty leads to further subdivision.
3
For certainty, check if it is certain certainty
If the premises yield certainty, ask: is it 'certain certainty' (yaqini)? The author says if it yields certain certainty, it is demonstration. This means the premises are certain and universally recognized.
4
If not certain certainty, check public or opponent acceptance
If the premises are not certain certainty, check if they are accepted by the general public (custom) or conceded by the opponent. If so, it is dialectic.
5
Else, it is sophistry
If the premises are not certain, not accepted generally, and not conceded by the opponent, they are likely false but made to appear true — that is sophistry.

The Five Logical Arts
Lists the five logical arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five logical arts and identify the primary purpose of each.

Core Idea
The five arts classify arguments by their purpose: proof, debate, persuasion, emotion, or deception.

The text introduces the five logical arts (al-sana'i' al-khamsa) that form the main branches of logic in the Islamic tradition. Each art corresponds to a type of argument or discourse used in different contexts, from rigorous proof to rhetorical persuasion. Understanding these arts helps you recognize the nature of arguments you encounter in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the list
The five arts are: burhani (demonstrative), jadali (dialectical), khatabi (rhetorical), shi'ri (poetic), and safsati (sophistical). Repeat them in order to fix the sequence in mind.
The Arabic terms are: برهاني, جدلي, خطابي, شعري, سفسطي.
2
Identify the purpose of each art
Burhani aims at certain truth (yaqin). Jadali aims at winning a debate using accepted premises. Khatabi aims at persuasion of the masses. Shi'ri aims at emotional effect. Safsati aims at deception using fallacious arguments.
These purposes are derived from the broader tradition; the passage only lists them.
3
Relate to Islamic sciences
Burhani is used in kalam for theological proofs and in usul al-fiqh for legal reasoning from certain premises. Jadali governs theological debates (munazara). Khatabi is employed in sermons (khutba) and da'wa. Shi'i appears in religious poetry. Safsati is identified to avoid it.
For example, a proof for God's existence is burhani; a debate about free will is jadali.
4
Test your recognition
Given a sample argument from Islamic texts, classify which art it belongs to. For instance, 'The world is created because it changes; everything that changes is created' is burhani. 'How can you believe in fate when you choose your actions?' is jadali.
Practice with multiple examples to internalize the distinctions.

This example shows that demonstration in Islamic disciplines often uses revealed premises as certainties.

Provides example of conjectural syllogism and conditions for persuasive premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a conjectural syllogism used in rhetorical argument, and identify the conditions for persuasive premises.

Core Idea
A conjectural syllogism can be formally valid but only probable.

The passage provides a concrete example of a maznuna: 'So-and-so wanders at night...' which is a categorical syllogism. However, because its premise is based on conjecture, the conclusion is only probable. The author also states conditions for premises used in this art: they must be persuasive, can be induction/analogy/flawed syllogisms if thought to produce the conclusion, and must be clearly expressed. Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
In the example 'So-and-so wanders at night...', the conclusion is 'So-and-so is a thief'. Find it in the Arabic: ففلان سارق.

The form is valid, but the premises are only probable.
4
Assess why it is conjectural
The major premise is not certainly true; it is a generalization that may have exceptions. Hence the conclusion is maznuna (preponderant but allowing the opposite).
Conjecture (zann) is the basis.
5
List conditions for persuasive premises
The text says premises must be 'persuasive to the listeners' (مقنعة للسامعين). They can be inductive, analogical, or even flawed syllogisms as long as they are thought to produce the conclusion. Also, the expression must be clear for quick understanding.

The Virtue of Falsehood in Poetry
Discusses the preference for false imaginative premises in poetry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why false imaginative premises are preferred in poetry and cite the evidence from the text.

Core Idea
Falsehood serves poetry's purpose because people respond more to imagination than to truth.

The text states that true primary premises are not permissible in poetry; false imaginative premises are considered good. This seems counterintuitive, but the author explains that the soul is more obedient to imagination than to assent, and most poetry relies on falsehoods.

Reflect on the purpose of poetry
Poetry aims to cause joy or sorrow, not convey truth. Hence, falsehood that achieves strong emotional effect is considered superior.

Analogical Reasoning in Poetic Syllogism
Provides an analogical example of poetic syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the analogical syllogism in the beloved-moon example and evaluate its validity as a poetic argument.

Core Idea
Poetic syllogisms use analogy (tamthil) to draw conclusions that affect the soul.

The author provides an example: the beloved is compared to the moon, leading to the conclusion that his garment tears. This is an analogical syllogism (qiyas tamthil). Let's analyze its logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The conclusion is: 'غلالة المحبوب تنشق' (the beloved's garment tears).
2
Find the analogy (tamthil)
The beloved is compared to a full moon: the poet says 'قد زرّ أزراره على القمر' (he buttoned his buttons on the moon), implying he is a moon.
3
Extract the implied major premise
The speaker says 'كل قمر كذلك فغلالته تنشق' – every moon's garment tears. This is a false but poetically plausible premise.
In reality, moons don't have garments, but in the imaginative world of poetry, this is accepted.
4
Recognize the form as analogical syllogism
The argument runs: Every moon tears its garment. This beloved is a moon. Therefore, his garment tears. This is a categorical syllogism in the first figure, but with an imaginary premise.
The source mentions it as an example of poetic syllogism.

Finally, the commentator dissects the wine poetry, explaining each comparison.
