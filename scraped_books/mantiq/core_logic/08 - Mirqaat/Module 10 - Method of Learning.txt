The Need for Logic – Why Error Occurs
Author establishes the possibility of error and the need for logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three prerequisites for the need for logic and identify the evidence for the possibility of error in reasoning.

Core Idea
If every arrangement were correct, there would be no disagreement – yet disagreement exists, proving error is possible.

The author begins with a stark warning: not every arrangement of premises leads to truth. He points to the existence of disagreement among thinkers as proof that error occurs. This establishes the fundamental need for logic as a tool to distinguish correct from incorrect reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's caution
The passage starts with 'Beware of thinking that every arrangement is correct.' Why might someone assume that? The author refutes it.
2
Find the evidence for error
The author says: if every arrangement were correct, there would be no disagreement among thinkers. But disagreement exists (e.g., about the world's origination). So error is possible.
3
Extract the three prerequisites
The need for logic depends on three things: (1) division of knowledge into tasawwur/tasdiq, (2) they are either badihi or nazari, (3) error occurs in nazar.
These are standard conditions in introductory logic works.
4
Explain the implication
The author only mentions these three after establishing the possibility of error. Logic is needed because error is possible and we need a method to avoid it.

Commentary
This passage is a cautionary chapter (fasl) on the necessity of logic. The author warns the reader not to assume that every arrangement of premises is correct and leads to sound knowledge. He argues by noting that if every arrangement were correct, there would be no disagreement among thinkers, but in fact disagreement and contradiction exist. He gives an example: someone might claim 'The world is originated' and argue using the premises 'The world is changing' and 'Everything changing is originated,' which is a valid syllogism; but others might disagree using different reasoning. The author then explains that the need for logic is established upon three prerequisites: (1) the division of knowledge into conceptualization and assent, (2) that each is either intuitive or theoretical, and (3) the occurrence of error in reflection. The commentator adds that al-Tusi in his Sharh al-Isharat elaborates on correctness in definitional and syllogistic arrangements. In definitions, correctness requires placing the genus first, then the differentia, and ensuring a unitary form matching the defined. In syllogisms, correctness involves proper order and connection of terms with respect to quantity (universal/particular), quality (affirmative/negative), and modality (necessity, possibility, etc.), and the syllogism must be in a productive mood. Corruption is the opposite. This underscores that logic is the tool to ensure correct reasoning.

Conditions for Correct Definitions and Syllogisms
Al-Tusi's criteria for correctness in definitions and syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list al-Tusi's criteria for correctness in definitions and syllogisms, and distinguish between correctness and corruption in both.

Core Idea
Correctness in definitions and syllogisms requires specific conditions: proper genus-differentia order, unitary form, and appropriate quantity, quality, modality, and productive mood.

The author, through al-Tusi's commentary, elaborates on what constitutes correct arrangement in definitions and syllogisms. In definitions, the genus must precede the differentia, and the parts must form a unitary whole. In syllogisms, the terms must be arranged correctly with respect to quantity, quality, and modality, and the syllogism must be in a productive mood. Corruption is simply the opposite.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two domains
The passage addresses correctness in two areas: definitions (qawl sharh) and syllogisms (qiyas).
2
For definitions: extract the two conditions
Al-Tusi says: (a) arrangement: genus first, then restricted by differentia; (b) form: the parts must have a unitary form matching the defined.
3
For syllogisms: conditions for premises and the whole
For premises: terms properly positioned and predicated; connection (quantity, quality, modality) correct. For the whole: arrangement of premises proper, and must be from a productive mood (ضرب منتج).
4
Contrast with corruption
Corruption in both cases is the opposite of these conditions.
5
Apply to an example
Take a syllogism: 'The world is changing; everything changing is originated; so the world is originated.' Check if it meets the conditions: terms in correct position, universal premises, affirmative, modality of necessity? (Assuming change is necessary? Probably yes, but the point is to check.)

Why the Logician Studies Words
Author explains why the logician discusses words: as signs and means of transition to concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the logician's rationale for studying words as signs and distinguish it from the grammarian's approach.

Core Idea
The logician studies words only as bridges to meanings, not for their own sake.

In this passage, the author clarifies why logic textbooks begin with a discussion of words. He explains that the logician's interest in words is purely instrumental: words are signs that lead to the concepts needed for definitions and proofs. This sets the methodological stage for the rest of the book.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two ways of reaching concepts
The passage mentions two means of transition: the explanatory statement (al-qawl al-shāriḥ) and the proof (al-ḥujjah). These are the tools by which we attain knowledge of unknown things.
These two correspond to definition and syllogism, the core of logic.
2
Recognize why words must be studied
Since both the explanatory statement and the proof are composed of words, the logician must first classify words into single and compound to understand how they combine to convey complete meanings.
The logician does not study words for eloquence, but as building blocks of rational discourse.
3
Contrast with the grammarian's purpose
A grammarian studies words to improve speech and avoid errors in iʿrāb (inflection) and syntax. The logician studies words only as signs that point to concepts.
This distinction is crucial to avoid confusing logic with linguistics.

The Four Conditions for a Compound Word
Author explains why the logician discusses words: as signs and means of transition to concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given expression qualifies as a compound (murakkab) according to the logicians' criteria.

Core Idea
A word is compound only if its parts each signify a part of its intended meaning intentionally.

After establishing why words matter, the author defines 'compound' with precise conditions. This is crucial because the two main tools of logic – the explanatory statement and the proof – are always compounds. Understanding what counts as a genuine compound prevents confusion.

Guided Path
1
List the four conditions for a compound word
The passage states: (1) the word has a part, (2) that part signifies a meaning, (3) that meaning is part of the intended meaning of the whole word, and (4) the signification of that part on that part of the meaning is intended.
These are often condensed into 'the parts must signify parts of the meaning intentionally'.
2
Apply to the example 'Zaid'
'Zaid' has parts (letters Z–A–I–D), but those letters do not signify any meaning. Therefore it fails condition (2) and is not a compound.
This shows that a proper name like Zaid is considered a single word (mufrad) in logic.
3
Apply to 'Abdullah' as a proper noun
'Abdullah' has two parts: 'abd' (slave) and 'Allah' (God), each signifying a meaning. However, when used as a name for a person, the intended meaning of the whole is not 'slave of God' but a specific individual. Thus conditions (3) and (4) are not met, so it is not a compound.
This illustrates that not every multi-part word is a logical compound; intention matters.
4
Apply to a genuine compound like 'kitāb al-ʿilm' (book of knowledge)
This expression has parts 'kitāb' and 'al-ʿilm', each signifying a meaning. The intended meaning of the whole is 'the book that belongs to knowledge', and the parts intentionally contribute that meaning. Thus it satisfies all four conditions and is a compound.
This is the kind of compound used in definitions and propositions.

The author notes that earlier logicians did not have this precise restriction; they gave examples like human signifying capacity for knowledge, which shows their intent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The foundation of any science rests on three pillars: its subject matter, its principles, and its problems. This lesson dissects each component and explains why the subject itself is never proven within the science. Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the concluding chapter of a logic work, discussing the fundamental components of any science. It begins by asserting a key principle: the existence and quiddity of the subject matter of a science are not investigated within that science itself. For example, a physician studies the human body but does not inquire into its existence or its nature as a body or rational animal; those are left to other sciences. The author then addresses an apparent inconsistency: natural science takes 'absolute body' as its subject, yet it discusses matter (hayula) and form (sura), which are parts of the body. The response is that such discussions are digressive (istitradiyya), meaning they are included only as asides.

The passage then divides the principles (mabadi') of a science into two types: conceptual (tasawwuri) and assertoric (tasdiqi). Conceptual principles are definitions provided for the subject, its parts, its particulars, and its essential attributes. Assertoric principles are premises from which the syllogisms of the science are composed. These premises may be either self-evident (badihiyya) or theoretical (nazariyya) but accepted on the basis of trust or stipulation.

The commentary elaborates that the subject and its essentials are taken as given (mafrugha 'anha) in the science. The reasoning is circular if one tried to prove them within the science: the problems consist of proving essential attributes, which presupposes the subject's existence. Therefore, proving the subject cannot be a problem. The author then notes a dispute: some hold that a science has three parts (subject, principles, problems), but the correct view is that principles are not parts but means (wasail). The statement 'three parts' is considered either an error or a lenient metaphor.

Finally, the commentary explains the subcategories of assertoric principles: common (mutawarifa) self-evident truths used in all sciences (e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'), and specific ones used only in certain sciences (e.g., geometric axioms). It distinguishes between posed axioms (usul mawdu'a), which are accepted without reluctance, and postulates (musadara), which are accepted despite some reluctance. A single premise can be considered a posed axiom for one and a postulate for another. The problems (masa'il) are the propositions that the science seeks to prove, such as those found in logic and grammar.

وينبغي أن يعلم إلخ: اعلم أنهم قالوا: إن الموضوع وذاتياته تكون مفروغة عنها في العلم. واستدل عليه بعضهم بأن إثبات موضوع العلم وأجزائه لا يكون مسألة في هذا العلم؛ لأن الموضوع ما يطلب له أعراض ذاتية. وما لم يعلم وجوده استحال أن يطلب له ثبوت شيء. ولأن مسائل العلم هي إثبات الأعراض الذاتية، وإثبات الأعراض يتوقف على ثبوت الموضوع وأجزائه، فلو كان ثبوت الموضوع وأجزائه مسألة من المسائل توقف الشيء على نفسه، فافهم. مباديه إلخ هي التي يتوقف عليها مسائل العلم اعلم أنها ليست من أجزاء العلوم، بل من الوسائل التي يتوسل بها للوصول إلى المطالب التصورية والتصديقية. قال صاحب "سلّم العلوم" في الحاشية: هذا هو الحق، وأما ما قيل أجزاء العلوم ثلاثة فخطأ أو مسامحة حاصله : أن القول بكون المسائل من أجزاء العلوم والمبادئ من وسائلها لا من أجزائها هو الحق. ومن قال: إن أجزاء العلوم ثلاثة: الموضوعات، والمبادئ، والمسائل. فهذا القول إما خطأ كما لا يخفى أو محمول على المسامحة بأن يقال: المبادئ لما كانت وسيلة إلى إدراك المسائل وموقوفة عليها، وشدة احتياج المسائل إليها صارت كالأجزاء؛ فعدها بالنظر إلى هذه الجهة، لكن عد الموضوعات من الأجزاء بالاستقلال فليس له وجه ظاهر لما قلنا حدود إلخ أي تعاريف تورد الموضوع الصناعة كتعريف الكلمة مثلا باللفظ الموضوع للمعنى المفرد وأجزائه إلخ :أي: حدود لأجزاء الموضوع كتعريف أجزاء الكلمة من اللفظ، والوضع، والمعنى المفرد مثلا.
ويسمى العلوم المتعارفة إلخ: سميت بذلك؛ لأن المقدمات هي التصديقات والتصديقات علوم ولبداهتها تسمى متعارفة، وهي إما عامة تستعمل في جميع العلوم كقولنا الكل أعظم من الجزء، والشيء الواحد إما أن يكون ثابتا أو منفيا، وإما خاصة ببعضها كقول أهل الهندسة: الأشياء المساوية لشيء واحد متساوية. أصولا موضوعة إلخ : كقول اقليدس في أول الهندسة: لنا أن نصل بين كل نقطتين بخط مستقيم، وأن نعمل بأي بعد شئنا خطا، وبكل نقطة شئنا دائرة. فإن كان التسليم إلخ : أي: فإذا أخذت تلك القضايا استنكار وتشكك من المتعلم سميت مصادرات؛ لأنه يصدر بها المسائل التي تتوقف عليها، كقول اقليدس: إذا وقع خط على خطين وكانت الزاويتان الداخلتان في جهة أقل من قائمتين، فإن الخطين إذا أخرجا في تلك الجهة يلتقيان. واعلم أن المقدّمة الواحدة قد تكون أصلا موضوعا عند شخص ومصادرة عند آخر. المسائل إلخ وهي القضايا المطلوبة المبرهن عليها في العلم كالمسائل الواقعة في المنطق والنحو وغيرهما العلوم.
The Conclusion: The principles and their divisions. It should be known that neither the existence of the subject nor its quiddity is investigated in the science for which it is a subject. Thus, the physician does not investigate the human body insofar as it exists, or as a body, or as a rational animal, nor does the grammarian investigate the reality of word and speech. And because the subject of natural science is absolute body, and the author of this art introduces discussions of matter and form in the natural sciences, it was problematic that matter and form are parts and constituents of the body. How then does he introduce these discussions in the natural sciences? An excuse was made on his behalf that these discussions are digressive. Second: its principles. Principles are that upon which the problems are based, and they are either conceptual, i.e., definitions introduced for the subject of the science and its parts, its particulars, and its essential attributes, or assertoric, which are the premises from which its syllogisms are composed, either intuitive or non-intuitive but accepted. 'It should be known, etc.': Know that they said that the subject and its essential attributes are taken for granted in the science. Some of them argued that proving the subject of the science and its parts cannot be a problem in that science, because the subject is that for which essential attributes are sought. As long as its existence is not known, it is impossible to seek the affirmation of something for it. And because the problems of the science are the affirmation of essential attributes, and the affirmation of attributes depends on the affirmation of the subject and its parts, if the affirmation of the subject and its parts were a problem, there would be a circularity. Understand. 'Principles, etc.': They are those upon which the problems of the science depend. Know that they are not parts of the sciences, but rather means by which one arrives at conceptual and assertoric demands. The author of 'Sullam al-'Ulum' said in the gloss: This is the truth. As for the statement that the parts of the sciences are three, it is an error or a lenience. The essence: the statement that the problems are from the parts of the sciences and that the principles are from their means, not from their parts, is the truth. Whoever said that the parts of the sciences are three: the subjects, the principles, and the problems, this statement is either an error, as is not hidden, or is interpreted as lenience, by saying that since principles are a means to understanding problems and are depended upon, and the dire need of the problems for them makes them like parts—so they were counted from this perspective. But counting the subjects as parts independently has no clear justification. 'Boundaries, etc.': i.e., definitions introduced for the subject of the science, like the definition of 'word' as 'an utterance put for a single meaning' and its parts, etc. 'And its parts, etc.': i.e., definitions of the parts of the subject, like the definitions of the parts of the word: utterance, conventional meaning, and single meaning, for example. 'It is called the common sciences, etc.': It is so named because the premises are the assertoric propositions, and assertoric propositions are knowledge, and due to their self-evidence they are called 'common'. They are either universal, used in all sciences, like our saying 'the whole is greater than the part', and 'a single thing is either existent or non-existent', or particular to some, like the geometers' saying 'things equal to the same thing are equal to each other'. 'Posed axioms, etc.': Like Euclid's statement at the beginning of geometry: 'It is possible for us to connect every two points with a straight line, and to draw a line with any distance we wish, and to draw a circle with any point we wish.' 'If acceptance is with reluctance, etc.': i.e., if those propositions are taken with reluctance and doubt by the student, they are called 'postulated', because the problems that depend on them are derived from them. Like Euclid's statement: 'If a line falls on two lines and the interior angles on one side are less than two right angles, then the lines, if extended on that side, will meet.' Know that a single premise may be a posed axiom for one person and a postulate for another. 'Problems, etc.': They are the propositions sought and proven in the science, like the problems occurring in logic, grammar, and other sciences.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the concluding chapter of a logic work, discussing the fundamental components of any science. It begins by asserting a key principle: the existence and quiddity of the subject matter of a science are not investigated within that science itself. For example, a physician studies the human body but does not inquire into its existence or its nature as a body or rational animal; those are left to other sciences. The author then addresses an apparent inconsistency: natural science takes 'absolute body' as its subject, yet it discusses matter (hayula) and form (sura), which are parts of the body. The response is that such discussions are digressive (istitradiyya), meaning they are included only as asides.

The passage then divides the principles (mabadi') of a science into two types: conceptual (tasawwuri) and assertoric (tasdiqi). Conceptual principles are definitions provided for the subject, its parts, its particulars, and its essential attributes. Assertoric principles are premises from which the syllogisms of the science are composed. These premises may be either self-evident (badihiyya) or theoretical (nazariyya) but accepted on the basis of trust or stipulation.

The commentary elaborates that the subject and its essentials are taken as given (mafrugha 'anha) in the science. The reasoning is circular if one tried to prove them within the science: the problems consist of proving essential attributes, which presupposes the subject's existence. Therefore, proving the subject cannot be a problem. The author then notes a dispute: some hold that a science has three parts (subject, principles, problems), but the correct view is that principles are not parts but means (wasail). The statement 'three parts' is considered either an error or a lenient metaphor.

Finally, the commentary explains the subcategories of assertoric principles: common (mutawarifa) self-evident truths used in all sciences (e.g., 'the whole is greater than the part'), and specific ones used only in certain sciences (e.g., geometric axioms). It distinguishes between posed axioms (usul mawdu'a), which are accepted without reluctance, and postulates (musadara), which are accepted despite some reluctance. A single premise can be considered a posed axiom for one and a postulate for another. The problems (masa'il) are the propositions that the science seeks to prove, such as those found in logic and grammar.

Key Terms
الموضوع (al-mawdu'): The subject matter of a science, which is taken as given and not proven within that science. [Note: Stable across the tradition; subject is always the thing whose essential attributes are sought.]
المبادئ (al-mabadi'): Principles upon which the problems of a science depend, divided into conceptual (definitions) and assertoric (premises). [Note: In earlier logic texts, mabadi' often included the eight heads; here it focuses on definitions and premises.]
المسائل (al-masa'il): The problems or propositions that the science aims to prove through evidence. [Note: No significant shift; standard term for derived claims in a science.]
التصورية (al-tasawwuriyya): Conceptual principles, i.e., definitions that clarify the meaning of terms. [Note: In later works, tasawwur can also refer to simple concepts; here it specifically means definitions.]
التصديقية (al-tasdiqiyya): Assertoric principles, i.e., premises that are accepted as true. [Note: Standard term for propositions that can be true or false.]
