Navigating an Islamic Library: Identifying Logic Texts in a Publisher's Catalog
Publisher catalog of Islamic books.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify key classical logic texts from a list of Islamic books and explain their place in the curriculum.

Core Idea
A madrasa library reveals the central texts of the Islamic logical tradition.

This page is a publisher's catalog listing books for sale. At first glance it seems like a simple inventory, but for a student of Islamic logic, it is a map of the curriculum. By learning to recognize the titles of standard logic texts such as Isaghuji and Sharh al-Tahdhib, you can begin to understand how logic was taught historically and which texts are essential. This lesson trains you to extract the logic component from any list of Islamic books.

Guided Path
1
Scan for logical keywords
Look for titles containing terms like 'mantiq' or names of logicians. Here, 'Isaghuji' is the Arabicized form of 'Isagoge', the standard introduction to logic by Porphyry, and 'Sharh al-Tahdhib' is a commentary on a logic text.
2
Observe catalog organization
The catalog groups books by subject (Hadith, Fiqh, Grammar, Logic). Logic titles appear alongside other sciences, showing integration into the curriculum.
3
Identify each book's function
Isaghuji is a beginner's matn; Sharh al-Tahdhib is an intermediate commentary. Note that al-Mirqat (the book itself) is also listed, indicating the publisher sells the text you are studying.
4
Reflect on curriculum progression
These books represent the core logic curriculum from introductory to advanced levels. Understand why they are listed together.

Commentary
This passage is a publisher's catalog page listing Islamic books for sale. It includes titles across various Islamic sciences: hadith (e.g., Sahih Muslim, Mishkat al-Masabih), fiqh (e.g., Al-Hidayah, Kanz al-Daqa'iq), logic (e.g., Isaghuji, Sharh al-Tahdhib), Arabic grammar (e.g., Al-Kafiyah, Hidayah al-Nahw), and poetry (e.g., Diwan al-Mutanabbi). The listing also mentions al-Mirqat, the very book from which this page is taken, indicating that the publisher offers multiple editions and related works. The presence of logic texts such as Isaghuji (a standard introductory matn on logic) and Sharh al-Tahdhib (a commentary on a logic text) shows that the publisher caters to students of the Islamic intellectual tradition, including those studying logic.
