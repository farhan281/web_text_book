Definition and Components of Tamthil
Defines tamthil and lists its four essential parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four essential components of tamthil and differentiate it from legal qiyas in Islamic jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Tamthil is analogical reasoning: a ruling proven in one thing through a shared cause with another.

The passage defines tamthil, the logical analogical reasoning distinct from but parallel to legal analogy. Understanding its four components—root, branch, common meaning, and ruling—is essential for evaluating arguments that infer from the known to the unknown, a core method in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Find where tamthil is defined in the Arabic. The definition starts with 'وهو إثبات حكم في جزئي...'.
The definition is the first sentence of the passage.
2
Extract the four components
Identify the root (asl), branch (far'), common meaning (ma'na jami'), and ruling (hukm) in the given example: 'العالم مؤلّف فهو حادث كالبيت'.
The example is: root = house, branch = world, common meaning = composition, ruling = origination.
3
Compare with legal qiyas
Note that theologians call it tamthil, while jurists call it qiyas. The components are the same, only terminology differs.
The passage mentions: 'التمثيل يسمّى في عرف الفقهاء قياسا'.
4
Verify completeness
Ensure the definition covers all necessary elements: a ruling transferred from one particular to another based on a shared meaning. Without any of these, it is not tamthil.
The author emphasizes that these four are indispensable.

It is that they enumerate the attributes of the original case, then they establish that what is beyond the common meaning is not fit to necessitate the ruling, due to the existence of those attributes in another locus with the ruling being absent from it. For example, in the mentioned example they say: The cause for the origination of the house is either possibility, or existence, or substantiality, or corporeality, or composition; and none of the mentioned other than composition is fit to be a cause for origination, for otherwise every possible, every substance, every existent, every body would be originated, while the Necessary Exalted, immaterial substances, and celestial bodies are not so. The theologians (mutakallimun) call it inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the absent (gha'ib); so the branch is absent and the root is witnessed. In their statement: 'The sky is originated because it is formed like the house' – the house is witnessed, the sky is absent, 'formed' is the common meaning, and 'originated' is the ruling. Tamthil must have these four. The jurists do not disagree with them except in terminology. 'Like the house' etc. means: the house is originated because it is composed, and this cause exists in the world, so it is originated like the house. 'The ruling co-varies' etc., as it is said: origination co-varies with composition existentially or non-existentially: existentially in the house, non-existentially in the Necessary. Co-variation is evidence that the orbit is a cause for the orbiting; so composition is a cause for origination. 'Sabr wa taqsim' etc., al-Qamus said: 'sabr' is probing the depth of wounds and other things; here it means examining the attributes of the root, i.e., which of them are fit for the causality of the ruling. Thus said al-Fadil al-Siyalkuti. And let it be known that these two methods – i.e., co-variation and probative division – are weak. As for co-variation: because the last part of the complete cause and the co-extensive condition both co-vary with the effect despite not being the cause. As for probative division: because the confinement of the cause to the mentioned attributes is objectionable; because the division is not forced between negation and affirmation, so it is possible that the cause is other than what you mentioned; then after conceding the validity of the confinement, we do not concede that the common [meaning] if it is a cause in the root then it necessarily follows that it is a cause in the branch; because it is possible that a particularity of the root is a condition for causality, or a particularity of the branch is a preventative thereof.

Commentary
This passage defines analogical reasoning (tamthil) as used in logic, distinguishing it from legal analogy (qiyas) but noting the parallel. The author first gives the definition: tamthil is establishing a ruling in one particular because that ruling exists in another particular due to a shared common meaning (ma'na jami' mushtarak). An example is provided: 'The world is composed, so it is originated like the house.' The world (al-'alam) is the branch (far'), the house (al-bayt) is the root (asl), composition (ta'lif) is the common meaning, and origination (huduth) is the ruling. The author then explains two methods logicians use to establish that the common meaning is indeed the cause ('illa) of the ruling. The first method is dawaran (co-variation), also called tard wa 'aks, where one observes that the ruling is present when the cause is present and absent when the cause is absent; this co-variation is taken as evidence that the meaning is the cause. The second method is sabr wa taqsim (probative division), where one enumerates all possible attributes of the root case and then eliminates those that do not qualify as cause by showing that they exist elsewhere without the ruling; the remaining attribute is taken as the cause. In the example, the possible causes for the origination of the house (imkan, wujud, jawhariyya, jismiyya, ta'lif) are eliminated except ta'lif, because the Necessary Existent, immaterial substances, and celestial bodies have the other attributes but are not originated. The commentary then explains terminology differences between jurists and theologians, emphasizing that the four elements (root, branch, common meaning, ruling) are essential. The passage ends with a critique of both methods: dawaran is weak because a co-extensive condition or final part of a complete cause also co-varies but is not the true cause; sabr wa taqsim is weak because the enumeration may be incomplete, and even if the cause is correctly identified in the root, it may not apply in the branch due to particular conditions or preventatives. This critique shows the epistemological caution logicians exercise regarding analogical reasoning.

Methods to Establish the Cause: Dawaran and Sabr wa Taqsim
Explains two methods to establish the 'illa: dawaran and sabr wa taqsim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the methods of dawaran and sabr wa taqsim to identify the likely cause in an analogical argument and assess their strength.

Core Idea
Dawaran uses co-variation; sabr wa taqsim uses elimination to pinpoint the cause.

Logicians developed two main methods to verify that a shared meaning is indeed the cause ('illa) of the ruling: co-variation (dawaran) and probative division (sabr wa taqsim). These methods are widely used in Islamic theology and jurisprudence to infer unseen properties from observed ones.

Guided Path
1
Understand the example
Review the analogy: the world is like the house; composition is claimed to be the cause of origination. The root is the house, the branch is the world, the common meaning is composition, the ruling is origination.
The example is 'العالم مؤلّف فهو حادث كالبيت'.
2
Apply dawaran: test co-variation
Check if origination coexists with composition in the observed case (house: composition exists, origination exists) and is absent when composition is absent (the Creator: no composition, no origination). This co-variation is taken as evidence that composition is the cause.
The text says: 'الحدوث دائر مع التأليف وجودا أو عدما'.
3
Apply sabr wa taqsim: eliminate alternatives
List all possible causes of origination in the house: possibility, existence, substantiality, corporeality, composition. Eliminate those that exist without origination. For example, possibility exists in the Creator but He is not originated, so possibility is not the cause. Only composition remains.
The enumeration in the text includes: الإمكان، الوجود، الجوهرية، الجسمية، التأليف.
4
Draw the conclusion
Composition emerges as the remaining cause. Note that both methods are probabilistic, not certain, as the later critique will show.
The text will later critique these methods.

Weaknesses of Dawaran and Sabr wa Taqsim
Critiques the two methods for establishing the cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain two key logical weaknesses of dawaran and sabr wa taqsim as argued by logicians.

Core Idea
These methods fail because co-variation can stem from non-causal conditions, and elimination may miss the true cause or ignore contextual particularities.

Despite their utility, the two methods for establishing the 'illa—dawaran and sabr wa taqsim—have inherent weaknesses. The logicians themselves point out that dawaran can be due to a concurrent condition or a part of the complete cause, not the cause itself. Sabr wa taqsim often relies on an incomplete enumeration of possibilities and assumes that a cause in the root applies uniformly to the branch, ignoring potential conditions or preventatives.

Guided Path
1
Understand the critique of dawaran
The author states that the last part of the complete cause and the co-extensive condition both co-vary with the effect but are not the cause. For example, consider the cause of a plant growing: soil moisture co-varies with growth, but the true cause might be nutrients, not moisture alone.
The text says: 'الجزء الأخير من العلة التامة والشرط المساوي مداران للمعلول مع أنه ليس بعلة'.
2
Understand the critique of sabr wa taqsim
First, the enumeration of attributes may be incomplete; the true cause might have been omitted. Second, even if the cause is correctly identified in the root, it may not apply in the branch due to a specific condition in the root or a preventative in the branch.
The text says: 'حصر العلة في الأوصاف المذكورة ممنوع... ثم بعد تسليم صحة الحصر لا نسلّم أن المشترك إذا كان علة في الأصل يلزم أن يكون علة في الفرع'.

They have for establishing that the common matter is a cause (illa) for the mentioned ruling numerous methods mentioned in the principles (usul). The most relied upon among them are two methods: First: co-variation (dawaran) – the later scholars and the ancients used to call it al-tard wa al-'aks – that the ruling co-varies with the common meaning, existence and non-existence, i.e., when the meaning exists the ruling exists, and when the meaning is absent the ruling is absent. So co-variation is evidence that the orbit (al-madar, i.e., the meaning) is a cause for the orbiting thing (al-da'ir, i.e., the ruling). The second method: probative division (sabr wa al-taqsim). It is that they enumerate the attributes of the original case, then they establish that what is beyond the common meaning is not fit to necessitate the ruling, due to the existence of those attributes in another locus with the ruling being absent from it. For example, in the mentioned example they say: The cause for the origination of the house is either possibility, or existence, or substantiality, or corporeality, or composition; and none of the mentioned other than composition is fit to be a cause for origination, for otherwise every possible, every substance, every existent, every body would be originated, while the Necessary Exalted, immaterial substances, and celestial bodies are not so. The tamthil etc.: Know that tamthil is called in the terminology of jurists qiyas (analogy); they call the original case (maqis 'alayhi) the root (asl), the derived case (maqis) the branch (far'), and the shared common meaning the cause (illa). The theologians (mutakallimun) call it inference from the witnessed (shahid) to the absent (gha'ib); so the branch is absent and the root is witnessed. In their statement: 'The sky is originated because it is formed like the house' – the house is witnessed, the sky is absent, 'formed' is the common meaning, and 'originated' is the ruling. Tamthil must have these four. The jurists do not disagree with them except in terminology. 'Like the house' etc. means: the house is originated because it is composed, and this cause exists in the world, so it is originated like the house. 'The ruling co-varies' etc., as it is said: origination co-varies with composition existentially or non-existentially: existentially in the house, non-existentially in the Necessary. Co-variation is evidence that the orbit is a cause for the orbiting; so composition is a cause for origination. 'Sabr wa taqsim' etc., al-Qamus said: 'sabr' is probing the depth of wounds and other things; here it means examining the attributes of the root, i.e., which of them are fit for the causality of the ruling. Thus said al-Fadil al-Siyalkuti. And let it be known that these two methods – i.e., co-variation and probative division – are weak. As for co-variation: because the last part of the complete cause and the co-extensive condition both co-vary with the effect despite not being the cause. Commentary
This passage defines analogical reasoning (tamthil) as used in logic, distinguishing it from legal analogy (qiyas) but noting the parallel. The author first gives the definition: tamthil is establishing a ruling in one particular because that ruling exists in another particular due to a shared common meaning (ma'na jami' mushtarak). An example is provided: 'The world is composed, so it is originated like the house.' The world (al-'alam) is the branch (far'), the house (al-bayt) is the root (asl), composition (ta'lif) is the common meaning, and origination (huduth) is the ruling. The author then explains two methods logicians use to establish that the common meaning is indeed the cause ('illa) of the ruling. The first method is dawaran (co-variation), also called tard wa 'aks, where one observes that the ruling is present when the cause is present and absent when the cause is absent; this co-variation is taken as evidence that the meaning is the cause. The second method is sabr wa taqsim (probative division), where one enumerates all possible attributes of the root case and then eliminates those that do not qualify as cause by showing that they exist elsewhere without the ruling; the remaining attribute is taken as the cause. In the example, the possible causes for the origination of the house (imkan, wujud, jawhariyya, jismiyya, ta'lif) are eliminated except ta'lif, because the Necessary Existent, immaterial substances, and celestial bodies have the other attributes but are not originated. The commentary then explains terminology differences between jurists and theologians, emphasizing that the four elements (root, branch, common meaning, ruling) are essential. The passage ends with a critique of both methods: dawaran is weak because a co-extensive condition or final part of a complete cause also co-varies but is not the true cause; sabr wa taqsim is weak because the enumeration may be incomplete, and even if the cause is correctly identified in the root, it may not apply in the branch due to particular conditions or preventatives. This critique shows the epistemological caution logicians exercise regarding analogical reasoning.
