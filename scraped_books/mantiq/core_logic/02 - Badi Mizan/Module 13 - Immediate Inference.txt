Simple Conversion: Swapping Subject and Predicate
Defines simple conversion and explains its rules for affirmative and negative propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform simple conversion on universal categorical propositions and identify the correct quantity of the converse.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate, preserving truth and quality, but quantity changes for affirmatives.

Here, al-Badī' al-Mīzān defines simple conversion, an immediate inference that derives a new proposition by swapping the subject and predicate. Understanding this operation is essential for later syllogistic reasoning, as valid conversions underpin certain syllogistic moods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition's type
Determine whether it is affirmative or negative, and universal or particular. The text focuses on universal affirmatives (every A is B) and universal negatives (no A is B).
Only universal examples are given, but the rules extend to particulars.
2
Swap the subject and predicate
Exchange the two terms: the subject becomes the predicate and the predicate becomes the subject.
The quality (affirmative/negative) remains identical.
3
Adjust the quantity if necessary
For universal affirmatives, the converse is particular affirmative (some B is A), because the predicate may be more general. For universal negatives, the converse remains universal negative (no B is A).
The text explicitly states that negatives convert universally, while affirmatives do not.
4
Check truth preservation
Simple conversion preserves truth only when the original proposition is true and the quantity rule is followed. Verify that the converse is indeed true given the original.
If the original is false, conversion is not guaranteed to preserve falsity.

Commentary
This passage explains simple conversion ('aks mustawī), a type of immediate inference. The rule: interchange subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality (affirmative/negative). However, quantity changes for affirmatives: a universal affirmative (like 'Every A is B') does not convert to a universal affirmative ('Every B is A') because the predicate B may be wider (e.g., 'Every man is an animal' does not imply 'Every animal is a man'). So universal affirmatives convert to particular affirmatives in classical logic (but the text here says quantity is not preserved, meaning the converse is not universal, implicitly it is particular). The text explicitly states that negatives do convert universally: from 'No man is a stone' we get 'No stone is a man', which is also true. This is a foundational rule for valid syllogisms.

Two Definitions of Contrapositive in Islamic Logic
Two definitions of contrapositive with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the early and later definitions of contrapositive and apply each to a categorical proposition.

Core Idea
The two definitions of contrapositive differ in whether they preserve or flip the quality of the original proposition.

This lesson presents two definitions of contrapositive ('aks al-naqīḍ) from earlier and later logicians. Understanding the difference is crucial for correctly performing immediate inferences in Islamic logic. The early method swaps both terms with their negations while preserving quality; the later method negates the predicate to become subject and flips the quality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general operation
Contrapositive is an immediate inference that transforms a proposition into another with the same truth value. Note the two competing definitions.
Both definitions agree on truth preservation.
2
Analyze the early definition
Early logicians swap each term with its negation while keeping quality the same. For 'Every human is an animal', they derive 'Every non-animal is non-human' (affirmative to affirmative).
This is similar to contrapositive in modern logic but with quality preserved.
3
Analyze the later definition
Later logicians take the negation of the predicate as the new subject, keep the original subject as the new predicate, and flip the quality. For 'Every human is an animal', they derive 'Nothing that is not an animal is a human' (affirmative to negative).
The quality changes from affirmative to negative.
4
Compare the results on the same example
Apply both definitions to 'Every human is an animal'. Early gives 'Every non-animal is non-human' (universal affirmative). Later gives 'No non-animal is human' (universal negative). They are logically equivalent but differ in form.
This shows the importance of knowing which definition is intended.
5
Practice with an Islamic example
Take a proposition from fiqh: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'. Apply both definitions and compare.
Early: 'Every non-forbidden is non-intoxicant'. Later: 'Nothing that is not forbidden is an intoxicant'.

From 'Every human is an animal' we get 'Nothing that is not an animal is a human' (a universal negative). The difference is that the earlier method keeps the quality the same (affirmative to affirmative) while the later method flips the quality.

Mental Entailment and Denotation: A Dialectical Exchange
Author presents an objection against the necessity of mental entailment and refutes it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical exchange about mental entailment and evaluate the objector's argument and the author's reply.

Core Idea
Not every logical consequence is a valid entailment; it must be mentally present after full understanding.

In this lesson, we examine a classic jadal (debate) about the role of mental entailment in denotation by entailment. The objector claims that mental entailment is not necessary, citing distant entailments and puzzles. The author responds by restricting what counts as a genuine entailment. Understanding this dialectic sharpens your ability to evaluate arguments about meaning and implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector says: 'We do not accept that mental entailment is a condition for denotation by entailment; otherwise, cases like distant entailments and puzzles would not have denotation by entailment, but they do.' Locate this in the Arabic beginning with 'إن قيل'.
The objector uses two counterexamples: distant entailments (al-lawāzim al-ba'īda) and puzzles (al-mu'ammayāt).
2
Extract the author's reply
The author responds by denying that distant entailments are among the denotations of words. For puzzles, he distinguishes: if after fully understanding the word's meaning, the puzzle's answer is still not grasped, then it is not a valid entailment; otherwise, mental entailment is present.
The key phrase is 'قلنا' introducing the reply.
3
Analyze the logical structure
This is a dialectical exchange: Objection (mu'tarid) → Response (jawāb). The objector's argument is: If mental entailment is necessary, then distant entailments would not be denotations, but they are; therefore mental entailment is not necessary. The author refutes by rejecting the minor premise: distant entailments are not denotations.
The author does not concede the counterexamples; he redefines the scope of denotation by entailment.
4
Evaluate who wins by jadal rules
The objector must provide valid counterexamples that are accepted as genuine entailments. The author successfully excludes distant entailments by definition. For puzzles, he conditionalizes: if they are not grasped after complete understanding, they are not denotations; if they are, then mental entailment exists, so the objection fails.
The author's reply is effective because it shifts the burden of proof back to the objector.

Relations Between Universals: Equality, Absolute Generality, and Generality from One Respect
Author defines three relations between universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three relations between universals (equality, absolute generality-specificity, and generality from one respect) using the logical conditions given in the passage.

Core Idea
The three relations between universals are defined by the direction and extent of their shared application.

This passage from al-Badi' al-Mizan introduces three fundamental ways two universals can relate: they may apply to exactly the same individuals, one may apply to all of the other but not vice versa, or they may partially overlap. Understanding these relations is essential for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two universals
Locate the two universals being compared. Note their individual application ranges—what individuals each applies to.
For example, 'human' and 'rational' are universals with overlapping application.
2
Test for equality
Check if each universal applies to every individual that the other applies to. If yes in both directions, the relation is equality.
This corresponds to two universal affirmatives.
3
Test for absolute generality-specificity
If one universal applies to all individuals of the other but not conversely, it is absolute generality-specificity. The more general is the one that applies to all of the other.
This corresponds to a universal affirmative and a particular negative.
4
Test for generality from one respect
If each universal applies to some individuals of the other but not to all, the relation is generality from one respect.
They intersect but neither contains the other completely.

Simple conversion preserves truth and quality, but not quantity in affirmative propositions.

Identify the definiendum

Simple Conversion (al-‘Aks al-Mustawī): Definition and Conditions
Author defines simple conversion and explains its conditions of truth, quality, and quantity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform simple conversion on categorical propositions and verify that the conditions of truth, quality, and quantity are satisfied.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality, but quantity changes for affirmatives.

Simple conversion is an immediate inference that transforms a proposition by swapping its subject and predicate. However, not every swap is valid: the converse must preserve truth (if the original is true) and quality (affirmation/negation), and the quantity rules differ for affirmatives and negatives. This lesson unpacks the precise conditions from the classical text so you can apply conversion correctly in Islamic logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and predicate
In the original proposition, locate the subject (mawḍūʿ) and predicate (maḥmūl). For example, in 'Every human is an animal', the subject is 'human' and the predicate is 'animal'.
2
Swap the terms
Simple conversion means making the subject become the predicate and the predicate become the subject. The operation is a straightforward replacement: the new proposition has the old predicate as subject and the old subject as predicate.
3
Check preservation of truth
The condition is not that the converse is actually true, but that if the original proposition is true, the converse must also be true. This is a conditional preservation. For example, 'Every man is a stone' is false, but its converse 'Some stone is a man' is also false — yet the conditional 'if the original is true then the converse is true' holds vacuously.
Do not confuse 'preservation of truth' with actual truth of the converse.
4
Check preservation of quality
The quality (kayfiyya) — affirmation or negation — must remain the same. If the original is affirmative, the converse must be affirmative; if negative, the converse must be negative. This ensures the converse is a true logical consequent.
5
Determine the quantity of the converse
For affirmative universals (A), the converse is a particular affirmative (I), because the predicate is often more general. For example, 'Every human is an animal' (A) converts to 'Some animal is a human' (I), not 'Every animal is a human' (A). For negative universals (E), the converse remains a universal negative (E). Example: 'No human is a stone' (E) converts to 'No stone is a human' (E). This rule is crucial to avoid invalid inferences.
Affirmative particulars (I) convert to particular affirmatives (I) — e.g., 'Some human is a writer' converts to 'Some writer is a human'. Negative particulars (O) do not convert validly.

Contrapositive: Two Definitions in Islamic Logic
Contrast of early and later definitions of contrapositive

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Contrapositive has two definitions: swap both negations (early) or swap only the predicate's negation with quality change (later).

This passage from al-Mīzān presents a key distinction in immediate inference. Early logicians defined contrapositive as exchanging both terms after negating them, preserving quality and truth. Later logicians defined it as taking the negation of the predicate as the new subject, keeping the original subject as the new predicate, but flipping the quality. Understanding this shift is essential for reading later commentaries and applying the operation correctly in substantive arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'aks al-naqīḍ (contrapositive). It is an immediate inference from a categorical proposition that preserves truth.

Extract the early definition
According to al-mutaqaddimūn: replace each term with its negation, then swap them. Quality remains unchanged. Example: 'Every human is animal' becomes 'Every non-animal is non-human'.
3
Extract the later definition
According to al-muta'akhkhirūn: keep the original subject as the new predicate, but make the negation of the original predicate the new subject, and change the quality (affirmative to negative). Example: 'Every human is animal' becomes 'Nothing that is not animal is human'.

Compare the two definitions
The early definition preserves the quality (both affirmative), the later flips it. Which one is logically equivalent to the original? The later definition is the classical contrapositive that is truth-preserving for A and O propositions, while the early one is not generally valid.
