The Two Categories of Knowledge: Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author defines knowledge as either tasawwur or tasdiq and explains their order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and explain the order of priority between them.

Core Idea
All knowledge is either concept or judgment; judgment depends on concept.

This passage establishes the foundational division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq. Understanding this division is essential because every logical operation, from definition to syllogism, builds upon these two categories. The author also clarifies that conception (tasawwur) has priority over assent (tasdiq), a principle that shapes how logical knowledge is arranged and taught.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Find the two types of knowledge mentioned in the Arabic: what are they called?
Look for the words 'تصور' and 'تصديق'.
2
Define tasawwur
Extract the definition: what is tasawwur? What is its key characteristic?
It is 'حصول صورة الشيء في العقل' – the mere obtaining of the form of a thing in the mind.
3
Define tasdiq
What components does tasdiq include? What is the role of judgment?
It is 'تصور معه حكم' – a conception along with a judgment, where judgment is 'إسناد أمر إلى أمر آخر إيجابا أو سلبا'.
4
Trace the dependency
Why must tasawwur come before tasdiq? What is the logical reason?
The text says 'لأن كل تصديق لا بد فيه من التصور' – every assent necessarily requires conception.
5
Apply the distinction
Classify a sample Islamic statement as either tasawwur or tasdiq.
Example: 'The world is temporally originated' is tasdiq; the concept of 'world' alone is tasawwur.

Commentary
This passage defines the two fundamental categories of knowledge in logic. The author begins by stating that knowledge (العلم) is divided into two kinds: conception (تصور) and assent (تصديق). Conception is defined as the mere obtaining of the form of a thing in the mind, without any judgment. For example, the concept of 'necessary existent' or 'human' is a conception. Assent, on the other hand, is a conception accompanied by a judgment (حكم). Judgment is defined as attributing one thing to another, either positively (إيجاب) or negatively (سلب). For instance, the proposition 'The world is temporally originated' involves the conception of 'world' and 'temporally originated', plus the affirmative judgment connecting them. The author then notes that conception must be given priority over assent in arrangement, because it is naturally prior: every assent necessarily depends on conception of its components. This is a foundational principle in Islamic logic: one must first acquire concepts before making judgments about them.

Defending the Division: Why 'Only' is Essential
Commentator explains the division of knowledge and defends adding 'only'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the word 'only' is necessary in the logical division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq, and refute the two main objections against it.

Core Idea
The word 'only' is the key to a proper logical division, not a redundant qualifier.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks a seemingly small word—'only'—in the division of knowledge. Without it, the division would be logically flawed. You will learn how the commentator defends the addition against objections about synonymy and necessary concomitance, and why the apparently redundant 'only' is actually essential for a clean, forced-interpretation-free division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division and the problem
The author divides knowledge into 'tasawwur only' and 'tasdiq'. Note that 'tasawwur' alone is often synonymous with 'knowledge'. Without 'only', the two divisions would not be exclusive, because 'tasawwur' already covers the entire concept of knowledge.
Synonymy creates a logical problem for division: the common term must apply to both divisions without overlap.
2
Understand the first objection: synonymy
Some object: if absolute tasawwur is synonymous with knowledge, then the division 'knowledge is either tasawwur only or tasdiq' would be impossible because the divider (knowledge) is already identical to one of the parts. The objection says 'only' is needed to break the synonymy.
This objection actually supports the use of 'only', but sees it as a forced fix.
3
Analyze the second objection: necessary concomitance
Others object: tasawwur and tasdiq are related by necessary concomitance (tasdiq cannot exist without tasawwur), so they cannot be mutually exclusive divisions. The commentator refutes this by distinguishing existential concomitance from logical contrariness—like even and odd numbers, which are mutually exclusive despite both requiring number.
Existential dependence does not prevent logical exclusion in definition.
4
Grasp the commentator's preferred solution
The commentator says the true reason for adding 'only' is to achieve a real division without forcing a 'condition of non-existence' interpretation. By using 'only', we get a natural division where 'tasawwur only' refers to bare conception, and 'tasdiq' refers to judgment, without requiring that the common term be reinterpreted.
This avoids affectation and deviation from apparent meaning.
5
Connect to the definition of tasawwur
The commentator then defines tasawwur as 'the obtaining of the form of a thing in the mind'. This definition serves to clarify what 'tasawwur only' means, and he even notes that saying 'the obtaining of the thing' would be better, but that is a minor refinement.
The definition anchors the meaning of the first division.

Why the Definition of Tasawwur is Placed Between the Divisions
Justifying the placement of the definition of tasawwur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to justify the placement of the definition of tasawwur between tasawwur and tasdiq based on the author's reasoning.

Core Idea
The definition is placed to highlight the division, not to define knowledge exclusively.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a structural question: why is the definition of tasawwur (conception) inserted between the two divisions (tasawwur and tasdiq)? He rejects two possible interpretations—that it defines only tasawwur or that it defines absolute knowledge—and offers his own: the placement signals that the division itself is the main purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rejected interpretations
The passage first rejects the possibility that the definition is exclusive to tasawwur (otherwise it would not be māniʿ, i.e., it would include other things). Then it rejects that it is a definition of absolute ʿilm (knowledge), since it would then be illogically placed between the two parts.
Note the term 'māniʿ' (exclusive) – a definition must exclude non-instances.
2
Understand the alternative: the definition serves the division
The commentator proposes that the definition is placed there to indicate that the main objective (al-maqṣūd al-aʿẓam) is the division (taqsīm) itself, not the definition. The definition helps one gain insight into the division by knowing the divided whole (al-muqsam).
This is a common pedagogical technique: placing a definition to clarify the following classification.
3
Apply this reasoning to another Islamic science example
In uṣūl al-fiqh, the definition of 'qiyās' (analogical reasoning) is often placed before its divisions (qiyās al-ʿilla, qiyās al-dalāla, etc.) to help the student understand the scope of the classification.
This shows that the logical principle is not unique to logic but used across disciplines.

The Meaning of 'Image of a Thing' and Its Comprehensive Scope
Analyzing and refining the definition of 'image of a thing'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'image of a thing' (sūrat al-shay') as the mental form after removing individual characteristics, and explain its scope regarding particulars and universals.

Core Idea
The image is a mental quality, not external reality, and it covers both false and true conceptions.

The definition of tasawwur mentions 'image of a thing' (sūrat al-shay'). The commentator clarifies this term: it is what remains in the mind after stripping away individual characteristics. However, he points out that this definition has difficulties with particulars and universals that do not exist. He then offers a better interpretation: the image is a quality (kayfiyya) that occurs in the intellect. This lesson dissects this definitional issue.

Guided Path
1
Extract the basic definition of 'image' from the text
The text states: 'صورة الشيء ما يؤخذ منه عند حذف المشخّصات' (the image of a thing is what is taken from it when individual characteristics are removed). This shows that the image is an abstract mental form, not the external object.
This is the standard understanding of sūra in logic: a mental representation devoid of particulars.
2
Identify the two shortcomings mentioned
The commentator notes that this apparent definition does not cover: (1) images of particulars insofar as they are particulars (since particulars have individual characteristics that are removed), and (2) images of non-existent universals (like 'nothingness' or 'impossible' because they have no external referent).
These are logical problems that the definition must address.
3
Understand the proposed solution: image as a quality
The commentator suggests a better interpretation: 'image' means a quality (kayfiyya) that occurs in the intellect. This quality can be of any object, whether particular or universal, existent or non-existent. It is simply the mental content.
This interpretation makes the definition more comprehensive and avoids the previous problems.
4
Apply the definition to a false conception
The commentator adds that the image is what is present in the knower, not necessarily matching external reality. So if someone conceives of a 'winged horse', that image is still a valid sūra for tasawwur because it is a mental quality.
This highlights that tasawwur does not require truth; it is mere apprehension.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary responding to several objections concerning the definition of tasdiq (assent). The author first clarifies a parenthetical phrase in the original definition: 'acquiring the form of a thing in the intellect' is a clause inserted between the two parts of the division (tasawwur and tasdiq). He then addresses an objection: if tasdiq is defined as 'a conception with a permanent accompanying judgment,' then each individual part — the conception of the subject, the predicate, the relation, and any combination of them — would itself count as tasdiq, which is absurd. However, the author points out that this objection is avoided because the intended 'permanent accompanying' is a considered attribute; the accompanying does not remove the judgment but rather includes it. Thus, tasdiq applies only to the combination of the three conceptions (subject, predicate, relation) plus the judgment. This is the position of the Imam (likely Fakhr al-Din al-Razi).

Next, a series of 'if it is said' objections are raised and refuted. The first objection concerns the domain of the division: if it is a single unified knowledge, then tasdiq — which is multiple perceptions — cannot be a part; if the domain is more general, then a composite of a full proposition plus something else would be excluded. The reply is that the domain is a single knowledge, and tasdiq is considered one by virtue of an additional 'social form' (hay'a ijtima'iyya) that unites its parts.

The second objection argues that this social form must be either knowledge or known, leading to absurdities. If it were knowledge, the parts of tasdiq would exceed four; if known, the composite of knowledge and known would be part of knowledge. The response: the social form is not part of tasdiq but a necessary concomitant, so no absurdity follows.

The third objection concerns the interpretation of 'single knowledge': if it means genuinely single, tasdiq is excluded; if conventionally single, conception is excluded; if the more general (which occurs only in one of them), both problems arise. The reply: the intended meaning is the more general single, but intending the general does not require attending to its particulars; one can intend 'knowledge' in general without specifying tasawwur or tasdiq. However, the author admits there is discussion (bahth) remaining.

Throughout, the author employs technical terms like 'hay'a ijtima'iyya' (social form) and 'al-wahid al-a'amm' (the more general single) to defend the definition. This passage demonstrates the dialectical nature of Islamic logic commentaries, where objections are systematically raised and resolved.

Natural Priority of Tasawwur over Tasdiq
Author explains the natural precedence of tasawwur over tasdiq and clarifies the necessary tasawwur need not be by essence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tasdiq depends on tasawwur, but not on essential definition—only on a conception tailored to the judgment.

In Islamic logic, every act of assent (tasdiq) presupposes some conception (tasawwur). But how much conception is needed? The author clarifies that the priority is natural, not causal, and that a complete definition is unnecessary. This lesson unpacks that relationship.

Guided Path
1
Understand natural priority (taqaddum bi al-tab')
Natural priority means the prior is needed by the posterior but is not its complete cause. The example of one being prior to two illustrates this: two depends on one, but one does not cause two.
2
Recognize that tasawwur is not a cause of tasdiq
Tasawwur is necessary for tasdiq (every tasdiq involves conception), but it is not its cause because tasdiq involves an additional act of judgment or assent.
3
Identify the minimal tasawwur required
Tasdiq requires conception of the subject, predicate, and relation, but not necessarily by essence (bi al-kunh). We can judge a body occupies space without knowing its essence.
4
Analyze the objection and response
An objection claims that if essential knowledge is not required, any conception would suffice. The author responds that each judgment demands a specific conception that the judgment implies (e.g., judging something laughs requires conceiving it as human).

This passage discusses the epistemological relationship between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). Learning Objective

Core Idea
Universality is a property of the concept itself, not of the word.

This lesson introduces a foundational division in logic: the split of all concepts into universal and particular. Understanding this division is essential for later topics like definition and syllogism. The passage emphasizes that universality and particularity are attributes of the concept, not of the linguistic expression.

Guided Path

The passage sets out to divide the concept into 'universal' (kullī) and 'particular' (juz'ī). These are the two types of concepts. Note that the author says 'universality and particularity are primarily and essentially attributes of the concept' — so they belong to the mental form, not to words.

The passage defines concept as 'what is acquired in the intellect, i.e., what is apt to be acquired in the intellect, whether actually or potentially, directly or indirectly.' This broad definition ensures that even potential objects of thought are concepts.

The standard definition of universal: a concept whose conception does not prevent it from being shared by many.

Definition of Universal and Particular with Conditions
Definition of kulli and juz'i with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between universal (kulli) and particular (juz'i) by applying the two conditions: conception (tasawwur) and its very nature (nafs).

Core Idea
The universal and particular are defined by mental conception, not external existence.

In logic, the foundational distinction between universal and particular governs how we classify concepts. This passage from al-Mizan defines them with precise conditions. By understanding these conditions, you will be able to test whether any given term is universal or particular.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Determine which term is being defined. The passage defines both 'particular' and 'universal' in sequence.
2
Extract the differentia
For the particular, the differentia is 'prevention of sharing by many by its very conception'. For the universal, it is 'not preventing'.
The condition 'by its very conception' is crucial.
3
Analyse the two restrictions
Understand why both 'conception' (tasawwur) and 'its very' (nafs) are necessary. 'Conception' excludes external factors; 'its very' excludes demonstration. Neither alone makes the definition coextensive.
The text says 'فيجب التقييد بهما؛ لئلا ينتقض التعريفان طردا وعكسا'.
4
Apply the definition
Test a term: Is it universal or particular? For example, 'human' is universal because its conception does not block multiple instances. 'Zayd' is particular because its conception designates a specific individual.

The Part-Whole Relation Between Universal and Particular
Analysis of universal as part of particular

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the universal is a conceptual part of the particular and resolve the apparent contradiction with predication.

Core Idea
The universal is a conceptual part, not a real part, of the particular; therefore it can be predicated of the whole.

After the definitions of universal and particular, the commentator addresses a puzzling question: how can the universal be a part of the particular when it is predicated of the particular? This reveals a key distinction between conceptual and real composition.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definitions
Remember that the particular prevents sharing by many (e.g., Zayd), while the universal does not (e.g., human). Usually, the universal is a part of the particular.
2
Identify the claim
The author states: 'الفرق بين الكلي والجزئي أن الكلي جزء للجزئي غالبا' meaning the universal is a part of the particular, making the particular a whole.
3
Recognize the problem
If the universal is a part, then it cannot be predicated of the whole, because a part (like a ceiling) is not predicated of the whole (house). But in logic, the universal is predicated of the particular (e.g., 'Zayd is human').
4
Apply the solution
The commentator distinguishes between real parts (الأجزاء الحقيقية) and conceptual parts (الأجزاء الاعتبارية). The universal is a conceptual part, not real; conceptual parts can be predicated. Predication is only impossible for real parts.
This resolution is found in the last part of the passage.
5
Test with an example
Consider 'human' as a conceptual part of 'Zayd'. We say 'Zayd is human', which is valid, confirming that the universal is a conceptual part.
