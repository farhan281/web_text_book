The Three Types of Indication: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, Iltizam
Author classifies the three types of indication by which words signify meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of word-meaning indication—mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam—using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A word's meaning has layers: full agreement, partial inclusion, and necessary association.

In this foundational passage, the author introduces the three classical types of linguistic indication (dalala) that every student of Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh must master. Understanding these types will allow you to analyze how words in the Qur'an and Sunnah convey both explicit and implicit meanings, a skill essential for sound legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basis of indication
Recognize that every word indicates its meaning by virtue of linguistic convention (wad'). The three types differ in how much of the meaning is conveyed.
2
Identify mutabaqa (agreement)
Mutabaqa is when the word indicates its entire conventional meaning. Example: 'human' indicates 'rational animal'—the complete definition.
This is the primary and most direct type of indication.
3
Identify tadammun (inclusion)
Tadammun is when the word indicates a part of its meaning. Example: 'human' indicates 'animal' or 'rational' alone, because these are components of the full meaning.
The part must be a necessary constituent of the whole meaning.
4
Identify iltizam (commitment)
Iltizam is when the word indicates something external to its meaning but necessarily associated with it. Example: 'human' indicates 'capable of knowledge and writing', which are not part of the definition but are essential qualities.
The association must be inseparable in the mind.
5
Apply to an Islamic term
Take the word 'salat'. Its mutabaqa is 'specific acts of worship including bowing and prostrating'. Its tadammun indicates 'bowing' or 'prostration'. Its iltizam indicates 'humility' or 'connection to Allah'.
Practice with other terms like 'zakat' or 'sawm'.

Commentary
This passage introduces the three classical types of indication (دلالة) by which words signify meanings. The author begins with the indication by agreement (مطابقة), which is the word indicating its full, conventionally assigned meaning. For example, the word 'human' indicates exactly 'rational animal' — that is its complete meaning. Next is indication by inclusion (تضمن), where the word indicates a part of its meaning. Finally, indication by commitment (التزام), where the word indicates something outside its explicit meaning but necessarily associated with it. For example, 'human' indicates 'capable of knowledge and writing', because these are essential characteristics of a human but are not part of the defining term 'rational animal'. These three types are foundational in logic and usul al-fiqh for understanding linguistic signification.

Classifying Words: Compound, Simple, Noun, Verb, Particle
Author classifies words into compound/simple and further into noun, verb, and particle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given word as compound, simple, particle, verb, or noun using the logical criteria from al-Mīzān.

Core Idea
Every word is either compound or simple; if simple, it is either a particle, verb, or noun.

In logic, words are not just grammatical categories; they have a precise logical function. This passage from al-Mīzān provides a clear decision tree for classifying any word you encounter in a proposition. Mastering this classification is essential for analysing definitions and arguments correctly.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish compound from simple
Determine whether the word can be broken into parts that each independently signify a part of the whole meaning. For example, 'rāmi al-sahm' (arrow-shooter) is compound because 'rāmi' indicates the shooter and 'al-sahm' indicates the arrow. If not, it is simple (mufrad).
Compound here means logically compound, not just a phrase.
2
Test for predicability
For simple words, check if they can be used as a predicate in a sentence (e.g., 'Zayd is ...'). If not, the word is a particle (adāt), such as 'lā' (no).
Particles cannot stand alone as predicates.
3
Check for time indication
If the simple word can be a predicate, examine whether its morphological form indicates a specific time (past, present, or future). If it does, it is a verb (kalima/fi'l). For example, 'ḍaraba' (he struck) indicates past time.
This is a distinctively logical criterion; grammar may also consider other features.
4
Identify the noun
If the simple word can be a predicate and does not indicate a specific time through its form, it is a noun (ism). Examples: 'Zayd', 'kitāb' (book), 'insān' (human).
The passage begins to subdivide nouns further, but this initial classification is complete.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification of words used in logical analysis. The author starts with words that indicate by congruence (المطابقة). If a word's parts are each intended to signify a part of the whole meaning, it is compound (مركب). For example, 'رامي السهم' (arrow-shooter) is compound because 'رامي' indicates the shooter and 'السهم' indicates the arrow. Otherwise, the word is simple (مفرد). A simple word that cannot be used as a predicate (e.g., alone in a sentence like 'Zayd is...') is a particle (أداة), such as the negative particle 'لا'. If it can be used as a predicate, then we check for time indication. If the word's morphological form indicates a specific time (past, present, future), it is a verb (كلمة/فعل). For example, 'ضرب' indicates a past action. If it does not indicate time, it is a noun (اسم), like 'زَيْد' or 'إنسان'. The author then begins to subdivide nouns: either their meaning is one or multiple; and if it is one, whether it is specific (e.g., proper noun) or not specific (e.g., pronoun or demonstrative). The passage cuts off before completing this subdivision.

Key Terms
مركب (murakkab): A word whose parts are intentionally signifying parts of its meaning. [Note: In grammar, murakkab can also refer to any phrase, but in logic it is specifically a compound expression with part-to-part meaning.]
مفرد (mufrad): A word that is not compound; its parts do not independently indicate parts of its meaning. [Note: Stable; contrasts with murakkab in logic and grammar.]
أداة (adāt): A type of simple word that cannot be used as a predicate, e.g., particles like 'لا'. [Note: In logic, adāt is a particle; in grammar it may include prepositions, conjunctions, etc.]
كلمة (kalima): In this context, a verb: a word that indicates a specific time through its morphological form. [Note: In Arabic grammar, kalima usually means any word; here the author specifies it as synonym for fi'l (verb).]
اسم (ism): A simple word fit for predication that does not indicate a specific time. [Note: Standard grammatical definition; stable across texts.]

Single Utterance: Proper Nouns, Univocal, and Equivocal Priority
Classification of single utterance into proper noun, univocal, and equivocal priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any single utterance as a proper noun (determinate) or common noun, and if common, as univocal (mutawāṭi') or equivocal in priority (mushakkak), using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Single words are either proper nouns or common nouns; common nouns are either univocal or equivocal in priority.

In Islamic logic, analyzing words begins with single utterances (mufrad). This lesson teaches you to distinguish between words that point to a specific individual (proper nouns) and those that apply to a class. Among class terms, you will learn to identify whether the meaning applies equally to all members (mutawāṭi') or more primarily to some (mushakkak). These distinctions are vital for interpreting legal and theological texts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify determinacy
Check if the word refers to a specific known individual. If it does, it is a proper noun (ʿalam), like 'Zayd' or 'this'. In such cases, no further classification of common nouns applies.
The term 'determinate' here means the word itself specifies a unique referent.
2
Check if the meaning is equal across individuals
If the word is not determinate, see if it applies equally to every individual of its meaning. If yes, it is univocal (mutawāṭi'), like 'human' applies equally to all humans. This is common for species terms.
Mutawāṭi' is a single concept with multiple instances of equal degree.
3
Identify priority in application
If the meaning applies more strongly or primarily to some individuals than others, it is equivocal in priority (mushakkak). Example: 'existence' applies primarily to Allah and secondarily to creation. The same word covers both but not equally.
Mushakkak is often used in theology for terms that apply to God and creatures analogically.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of utterances (alfāẓ) as they are used in logic. Finally, single words are related as synonyms (mutarādif) or different (mubāyin). The passage then moves to compound utterances (murakkab), dividing them into complete (tāmm) that give a complete meaning (like a sentence) and incomplete (ghayr tāmm). Complete compounds are either statements (khabar) that can be true or false, or non-statements like commands (amr), supplications (du'ā'), etc. Incomplete compounds are either restrictive (like 'the clever man') or non-restrictive (like 'Zayd's book'). Single Utterance: Homonyms, Transferred Terms, Literal and Metaphorical
Classification of multiple-meaning words: homonym, transferred, literal, and metaphorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify homonyms (mushtarak), transferred terms (manqūl), and distinguish between literal (ḥaqīqa) and metaphorical (majāz) usage in Arabic, with examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
When a single word covers multiple distinct meanings, it is either homonym, transferred, or used literally or metaphorically.

Many words in Arabic carry multiple meanings. This lesson teaches you to analyze whether a word was coined for several meanings equally (mushtarak), or transferred from an original to a new meaning (manqūl), and whether both meanings coexist (resulting in ḥaqīqa and majāz). These distinctions are essential for interpreting legal, theological, and literary texts accurately.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the word has multiple meanings
First, check if the word is used for more than one distinct meaning. For example, 'ayn can mean eye, spring, or self. If the word was coined for all those meanings equally without priority, it is mushtarak (homonym).
In mushtarak, no single meaning is more original; the word simply has multiple senses.
2
Check for priority and transfer
If the word was originally coined for one meaning and later used for another, it is manqūl (transferred). Identify who performed the transfer: general custom (like dābba originally any four-legged animal later specifically donkey), Islamic law (like ṣalāt originally supplication later ritual prayer), or technical convention (like grammarian's terms).
If the original meaning is abandoned, the word is purely manqūl; if both remain, see next step.
3
Determine literal vs metaphorical
If after transfer the original meaning is still in use, then the word is ḥaqīqa (literal) for the original meaning and majāz (metaphorical) for the new one. Example: 'asad (lion) for the animal is literal, and for a brave man is metaphorical.
The relationship between ḥaqīqa and majāz depends on context; a word can be literal in one context and metaphorical in another.

Compound Utterance: Complete and Incomplete, Khabar and Insha'
Classification of compound utterances into complete/incomplete and statement/insha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify compound utterances as complete or incomplete, and within complete compounds, distinguish between statements (khabar) that can be true or false and non-statements (inshā') like commands, supplications, and notifications.

Core Idea
A compound is either a complete sentence or a phrase; complete sentences are either truth-apt statements or non-truth-apt requests and notifications.

After analyzing single words, the logician turns to compounds (murakkab). This lesson teaches you to identify whether a compound gives a complete meaning (you can stop speaking) or not. Complete compounds are further divided into those that can be true or false (khabar) and those that indicate a request or reaction (inshā'). These categories are foundational for understanding the logical structure of arguments and legal speech acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify completeness
Check if the compound can stand alone and you can stop after it without requiring further speech. If yes, it is a complete compound (tāmm). Example: 'Zayd is standing'. If not, it is incomplete (ghayr tāmm), like 'the clever man' (a phrase).
Completeness in this sense is about grammatical and communicative independence.
2
Check for truth-aptness
For a complete compound, ask: can it be said to be true or false? If yes, it is a statement (khabar). Example: 'Zayd is standing' can be true or false. If not, it is a non-statement (inshā').
Inshā' covers commands, prohibitions, wishes, exclamations, etc. They do not have truth value but can be evaluated for correctness or appropriateness.
3
Further classify non-statements
If it is not a statement, determine if it indicates a request for an action by verbal form. With superiority it is command (amr), with submissiveness it is supplication (du'ā'), with equality it is entreaty (iltimās). If no request for action, it is notification (tanbīh), including wishes, calls, etc.
The categories of inshā' are based on the relationship between speaker and hearer and the type of speech act.
4
Identify incomplete compounds
If the compound is incomplete, it is either restrictive (taqyīdī) like 'the clever man' (adjective-noun), or non-restrictive like a noun with a particle.
Incomplete compounds often function as parts of a complete sentence.

The Four Conditions for a Compound Word
Author enumerates conditions for compound word

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given Arabic expression is compound (murakkab) by checking the four existential conditions.

Core Idea
A compound word is not just a word with parts; each part must indicate a part of the intended meaning, and that indication must be intended.

In this passage, the author sets out the precise defining conditions for a compound word. These conditions are existential—they must all be present for the word to be considered compound. By understanding them, you can classify expressions accurately, a crucial skill for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
List the four conditions
Identify the four conditions that must all be present for a word to be compound: the word has a part, that part indicates a meaning, that meaning is part of the overall intended meaning, and the indication of the part on that part of meaning is intended.
These conditions are derived from the definition given in the passage: 'if it is intended by its part... indication of a part of its meaning at the time it is intended'.
2
Apply to the example 'rāmī al-ḥijārah'
Check each condition for the expression 'thrower of stones'. The parts 'rāmī' and 'al-ḥijārah' each indicate a meaning (the thrower and stones), those meanings are parts of the overall intended meaning (someone who throws stones), and the indication is intended. Therefore it satisfies all conditions and is compound.
3
Examine counterexamples
For each of the four excluded types, identify which condition fails. The letter 'bā' as a proper name has no part. 'Zayd' has a part but it does not indicate any meaning. 'Abd Allah' as a proper name has parts that indicate meanings, but those meanings (servitude and divinity) are not part of the intended meaning (the individual person). 'Rational animal' as a proper name has parts that indicate parts of the intended meaning, but the indication is not intended (since it is a proper name).
4
Distinguish 'Abd Allah' and 'rational animal'
Both are proper names, but they fail different conditions. In 'Abd Allah', the composite meaning is external to the intended meaning, so condition 3 fails. In 'rational animal', the composite meaning is part of the intended meaning, but the indication is not intended, so condition 4 fails. This shows that each condition is independently necessary.

The Privative Nature of the Simple Word
Author defines simple word and justifies priority of compound

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the simple word is defined by the privation of the compound's conditions and why the compound is conceptually prior.

Core Idea
The simple word is defined by the absence of the totality of conditions, not by the negation of each individually.

After defining compound, the author addresses why he presented it first and clarifies the concept of simple. The simple is not simply 'not compound' in a piecemeal way; rather, it is defined by the failure to realize the entire set of conditions. This privative concept is conceptually dependent on the existential concept of compound, hence compound is presented first.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Recognize the potential objection: since the essence of the simple is prior (it has fewer conditions), it seems it should be defined first. The passage notes that one might argue the simple's essence is simpler, hence prior.
2
See the author's rebuttal
The author responds that the division is not by essence but by concept. The concept of compound is existential (its conditions are positive), while the concept of simple is privative (the absence of those conditions). The existential is conceptually prior to the privative, so compound is presented first.
3
Analyze the four types of simple
List the four categories of simple words given: (1) no part (e.g., interrogative hamzah), (2) has part but no indication (e.g., Zayd), (3) part indicates but not part of meaning (e.g., Abdullah as proper name), (4) part indicates part but indication not intended (e.g., rational animal as proper name). Each fails to meet the total set of conditions.
4
Contrast with a common misconception
Note that if we negated each condition individually, then Abdullah and rational animal would be compound because they have parts that indicate meanings. But the definition requires the totality of conditions to be absent; thus they are simple. This shows the privation is of the whole set, not each condition.

Classifying Nouns by Meaning: One or Many
Author begins classification of nouns by meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any noun as having a single meaning or multiple meanings based on the criteria given in the passage.

Core Idea
Nouns are divided first by whether their meaning is one or many; the one precedes the many.

In this passage, the author begins the classification of nouns (isma) by focusing on their meaning. The first division is between nouns whose meaning is one and those whose meaning is many. Within the 'one meaning' category, we further distinguish between specific (proper) and general (univocal/equivocal). This taxonomy is foundational for any logical analysis of terms in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the noun's meaning
Look at the word and determine the concept it intends to convey. Is it a single concept or multiple separate concepts?
2
Check if the meaning is one or many
If the word has only one conceptual meaning, it falls into the first branch. If it has multiple distinct meanings, it is a common term (mushtarak) or a word with a general convention like pronouns.
The author gives precedence to the one before the many.
3
If the meaning is one, determine if it is determined (muta'ayyin)
A determined meaning is specific to a single individual and cannot be shared (e.g., 'Zayd'). If not determined, the meaning is general and can apply to many.
If determined, it is a proper noun (alam) and a true particular.
4
If not determined, check if the meaning applies equally or with variance
If the meaning occurs in all individuals equally, it is univocal (mutawati'). If it occurs with difference in intensity or priority, it is equivocal (mushakkik).
Examples: 'insan' is univocal; 'nur' (light) is equivocal.
5
If the meaning is many, identify the type
If the multiple meanings are from different conventions, it is mushtarak. If they come from a single general convention applying to many instances (like pronouns), it is not mushtarak but a separate category.
The author discusses pronouns and demonstratives as having many meanings via a general convention.

Distinguishing Complete from Incomplete Compounds
Division of compound into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic compound expression as complete or incomplete using the criterion 'yasuhhu al-sukut 'alayh' and explain why a complete compound is considered 'existential'.

Core Idea
A complete compound stands alone; completeness is about syntactic independence, not information novelty.

This passage introduces a fundamental distinction in Islamic logic and linguistics: the difference between a complete compound (murakkab tamm) and an incomplete compound (murakkab ghayr tamm). The complete compound is one that can stand alone as an independent utterance; it does not require any additional words to complete its meaning. This criterion is not about whether the sentence provides new information — it may or may not — but about its structural self-sufficiency. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing statements in Quranic exegesis, legal rulings, and theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and note that the author divides the compound into two kinds: complete (tamm) and other than complete (ghayr tamm). This is the primary classification.
2
Extract the criterion for completeness
The author states that the complete compound is 'that upon which it is valid to remain silent' (yasuhhu al-sukut 'alayh). This means the expression does not need another word to convey its intended meaning. Paraphrase this criterion in your own words.
3
Understand the analogy to subject-predicate need
The author clarifies that the complete compound does not need another word 'as the subject needs the predicate and vice versa.' This shows that the need is mutual and structural: a subject alone is incomplete without a predicate, and vice versa. A complete compound has both or is self-sufficient in some other way.
In Arabic, even a one-word sentence like 'qum' (stand!) is considered a complete compound because it implicitly contains both subject and predicate.
4
Notice the scope of completeness
The author adds that it does not matter whether the expression conveys a new benefit (fa'idah jadidah) or not. So a tautology like 'al-insanu insanun' (man is man) is still a complete compound because it can stand alone, even though it adds no new information.
This prevents confusion between logical informativeness and syntactic completeness.
5
Apply the criterion to sample expressions
Take a few Arabic phrases and test them: 'Zaidun qa'imun' (Zaid is standing) — complete, because you can pause after it. 'In kana' (if it was) — incomplete, because it requires a following clause. Practice classifying.

Restrictive vs Non-Restrictive Incomplete Composites
Author defines incomplete composite and divides it into restrictive and non-restrictive types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between restrictive and non-restrictive incomplete composites and identify examples of each from Arabic phrases.

Core Idea
Incomplete composites mirror predicative judgments but lack full assertion.

In logic, not every combination of words forms a complete sentence. Some are phrases that serve to restrict meaning. This lesson unpacks the two types of incomplete composites — restrictive and non-restrictive — and shows how they relate to full predicative judgments. Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to parse definitions and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the incomplete composite
Look for a combination of words that does not form a complete declarative sentence. The passage defines this as 'murakkab ghayr tāmm' — a phrase, not a sentence.
2
Check if the second element qualifies the first
If the second word restricts or specifies the first (e.g., adjective modifying a noun, or possessive construction), it is restrictive. Example: 'al-rajul al-faṭin' (the intelligent man) — 'intelligent' qualifies 'man'.
The qualification can be in wording or meaning.
3
Classify as restrictive or non-restrictive
If it is not a qualification, it is non-restrictive. Examples include prepositional phrases like 'bi-Zayd' or particle + verb like 'qad qāma'.
Non-restrictive composites do not serve conceptual clarification.
4
Compare with predicative judgment
The passage notes that a restrictive composite mirrors a full judgment (ḥukm khabarī) but without assertion. So just as a judgment requires two nouns or a noun+verb, a restrictive composite does too.
This analogy helps you see the syntactic parallel.

Core Idea
The hierarchy mirrors itself, but the highest genus is 'genus of genera' and the lowest type is 'type of types'.

In Islamic logic, every term in a classification chain belongs to one of four levels for types and one of four levels for genera. Understanding these levels is essential for constructing correct definitions and avoiding confusion in debates. This lesson walks you through the author's systematic enumeration, with examples from classical ontology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term's kind

2
Locate its position in the chain
See if there is a term above it and a term below it in the same chain. If both exist, it is intermediate; if only one exists, it is extreme; if none, it is separate.

Apply the level label
For types: if it has no type above it, it is the highest (naw' 'ali); if it has no type below it, it is the lowest (naw' safil); if both, it is intermediate (mutawassit); if outside the chain, it is separate (mufrad). The same logic applies to genera.
The highest genus is called 'genus of genera'; the lowest type is called 'type of types' – do not confuse the two extremes.

Intermediate and Unique Genus in the Hierarchical Tree
Author provides examples of intermediate genus and unique genus using body and intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish an intermediate genus from a unique genus by analyzing their position in the genus-species hierarchy.

Core Idea
A genus can be intermediate between higher and lower genera, or unique with no genus above it.

The author uses concrete examples — growing body and intellect — to illustrate two positions a genus can occupy in the Porphyrian tree. Understanding where a genus sits in the hierarchy is crucial for defining terms accurately in Islamic logic. This lesson teaches you to identify each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchy for the intermediate genus
Look at the example: growing body (al-jism al-nami) and absolute body (al-jism al-mutlaq). Above them is substance (jawhar), which is a higher genus. Below them is animal (hayawan), a lower genus. Thus they occupy a middle position.
The same applies to absolute body; both are intermediate.
2
Extract the unique genus example
Intellect (al-'aql) is presented as a unique genus on the condition that substance is not its genus but a common accident. This means there is no genus more general than intellect.
Unique genus has no higher genus; it stands at the top of its branch.
3
Interpret the two scenarios for intellect
The author says intellect can be a unique genus if the ten intellects differ in species (i.e., they share intellect as a common genus), or a unique species if they agree in species (i.e., intellect is the specific essence of each). This shows flexibility in how 'unique' applies.
Unique genus vs. unique species depends on whether the individuals below are different species or the same species.
4
Relate to the genus-species tree
Visualize the hierarchy: at the top are highest genera (unique genera, no higher), then intermediate genera (with both higher and lower), then lowest species (no lower). The examples fit into this tree.
This helps in constructing and evaluating definitions.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional discussion providing examples of two types of genera: the intermediate genus (al-jins al-mutawassit) and the unique genus (al-jins al-mufrad).
