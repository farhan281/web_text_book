Beyond Three Types: Disjunctive Conditionals Are Not Exhausted by the Tripartite Schema
Author refutes claim that disjunctive conditionals are limited to three types using examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify disjunctive conditionals that do not fit the tripartite classification (mani'at al-jam', mani'at al-khuluw, haqiqiyya) and critique the claim that disjunctives are limited to these three types.

Core Idea
Disjunctive conditionals have more than three types; the tripartite classification is incomplete.

In classical logic, disjunctive conditionals are often divided into three types: exclusive (mani'at al-jam'), exhaustive (mani'at al-khuluw), and both (haqiqiyya). However, as the shaykh Ibn Sina points out in al-Isharat, this classification is not exhaustive. In this lesson, we will examine examples that fall outside these categories and learn to spot when a classification is based on incomplete induction.

Guided Path
1
Recall the tripartite classification
List the three standard types of disjunctive conditionals: mani'at al-jam' (both cannot be true), mani'at al-khuluw (both cannot be false), and haqiqiyya (both conditions combined).
These are the types that the author is refuting as exhaustive.
2
Examine the counterexamples from the passage
Look at the three examples: 'I saw either Zayd or Amr', 'Zayd is either not in the sea or he drowns', 'The scholar either worships God or benefits people'. For each, determine whether it fits any of the three standard types.
For instance, 'I saw either Zayd or Amr': both can be true (if I saw both) and both can be false (if I saw neither), so it is neither mani'at al-jam' nor mani'at al-khuluw.
3
Identify the logical flaw
The existence of these examples demonstrates that the tripartite classification is incomplete. The claim of exhaustiveness is false because genuine disjunctive conditionals exist outside the three types.
The author uses these examples to show that the logicians who claimed only three types committed the fallacy of incomplete enumeration.
4
Generalize the lesson
This case teaches the importance of complete induction (istiqra' tamm) before declaring a classification exhaustive. In Islamic sciences, premature closure can lead to missing valid categories.
For example, in Usul al-Fiqh, classifications of qiyas must be checked for completeness.

Definition and Division of Syllogism
Author defines syllogism and divides it into exceptive and conjunctive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between exceptive and conjunctive syllogisms and identify which type a given syllogism belongs to.

Core Idea
A syllogism necessitates a conclusion; its type depends on whether the conclusion is explicit in the premises.

This passage opens the chapter on syllogism (qiyās), the core of Islamic logic. The author first defines the syllogism as a compound discourse that compels a new proposition. He then divides it into two fundamental kinds: exceptive (istithnā'ī) and conjunctive (iqtirānī). Understanding this division correctly will allow you to classify any argument you encounter in Islamic scholarly texts.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition of syllogism
The syllogism is a discourse composed of premises that, when granted, necessitate another discourse by itself. Note the conditions: compound (multiple premises), the premises are granted, and the conclusion follows necessarily and essentially (li-dhātihi).
This definition is the foundation for all syllogistic reasoning.
2
Identify the exceptive (istithnā'ī) syllogism
In this type, the conclusion or its contradictory is explicitly stated in the premises. The example: 'Whenever the sun is rising, daytime exists; but it is rising; therefore daytime exists.' The conclusion 'daytime exists' appears as the consequent of the conditional premise.
The conclusion is mentioned 'bi al-fi'l' (actually) in the premises.
3
Identify the conjunctive (iqtirānī) syllogism
In this type, neither the conclusion nor its contradictory is explicitly stated. Example: 'Every human is an animal; every animal is sentient; therefore every human is sentient.' The conclusion is not in the premises; it is derived by the middle term.
The conclusion is implied, not stated.
4
Test your understanding
Given a syllogism, determine if it is exceptive or conjunctive by checking whether the conclusion or its contradictory appears among the premises. If yes, it is exceptive; if no, it is conjunctive.
This classification is the first step in analyzing any argument.

Terms and Figures of the Conjunctive Syllogism
Author explains terms and figures of the conjunctive syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the minor term, major term, middle term, and figure of any conjunctive syllogism.

Core Idea
The figure of a syllogism is determined by the position of the middle term in the two premises.

After distinguishing syllogism types, the author delves into the structure of the conjunctive (categorical) syllogism. He names the components: minor term (subject of conclusion), major term (predicate of conclusion), middle term (repeated between them). The arrangement of the middle term creates different figures, of which the author lists two: the first and the fourth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion and its subject and predicate
In a conjunctive syllogism, first find the conclusion (e.g., 'Every human is sentient'). The subject is the minor term (human), the predicate is the major term (sentient).
The minor term is always the subject of the conclusion.
2
Locate the premises containing the minor and major terms
The premise containing the minor term is the minor premise (sughrā); the premise containing the major term is the major premise (kubrā).
These are the two premises of the syllogism.
3
Find the repeated term (middle term)
The term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion is the middle term (ḥadd awsaṭ). In the example 'Every human is animal; every animal is sentient', the middle term is 'animal'.
The middle term connects the minor and major terms.
4
Determine the figure by the position of the middle term
If the middle term is predicate in the minor premise and subject in the major premise, it is the first figure. If it is subject in the minor and predicate in the major, it is the fourth figure.
Only first and fourth figures are mentioned here; second and third are omitted.
5
Practice on the given examples
Memorize the two figures and test on new examples. For instance, in 'Every body is composite; every composite is originated,' the middle term 'composite' is predicate in the minor and subject in the major, so first figure.

A syllogism is explicit in conclusion (istithna'i) or implicit (iqtirani).

The author introduces the foundational division of qiyas into two types based on whether the conclusion appears in the premises. 1
Understand the definition of qiyas
Read the definition: 'a statement composed of premises that, if accepted, necessarily entail another statement by itself.' Notice that the conclusion must follow from the premises without external addition.

Every categorical syllogism has five components: two premises, three terms.

Connected Conditionals: Necessary and Accidental
Classification of connected conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a necessary (luzumiyya) connected conditional from an accidental (ittifaqiyya) one by identifying whether a genuine relation exists between antecedent and consequent.

Core Idea
Not all if-then statements are based on a real connection; some are true by coincidence.

The author divides connected conditionals into two types based on the nature of the relationship between the parts. A necessary conditional has a genuine link (like cause and effect), while an accidental one is merely coincidental. This distinction is crucial when evaluating arguments that rely on conditionals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional as connected
Connected conditionals use 'if...then' structure. The antecedent and consequent are linked by a relation of connection.
2
Assess the relation between antecedent and consequent
Ask: Does the truth of the consequent depend on the truth of the antecedent due to a real connection (e.g., causality, definition)?
Example: 'If the sun rises, then day exists' has a causal relation.
3
Classify as necessary if there is a positive relation
If there is a necessary link (like cause and effect, correlation, or logical entailment), it is luzumiyya.
4
Classify as accidental if there is no relation
If both parts happen to be true but with no connection, it is ittifaqiyya.
Example from text: 'If man is rational, then the donkey is braying' – no relation, so accidental.

Disjunctive Conditionals: Real and Restrictive of Conjunction
Classification of disjunctive conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a real disjunctive (haqiqiyya) from a restrictive-of-conjunction (man'at al-jam') based on whether both disjuncts can be false simultaneously.

Core Idea
Disjunctive conditionals either forbid both disjuncts being true together or forbid both truth and falsity together.

Disjunctive propositions express alternatives, but they differ in strength. A real disjunction (haqiqiyya) allows only one of the two parts to be true; the other must be false, and they cannot both be false. A restrictive-of-conjunction (man'at al-jam') only prohibits both from being true; they can both be false. This distinction is important in legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunctive phrase
Look for 'either...or' (إما...أو) indicating a choice between two parts.
2
Check if both parts can be false
Ask: Is it possible that neither part is true? If yes, it is man'at al-jam'; if no, it is haqiqiyya.
Example: 'This number is either even or odd' – cannot be neither, so haqiqiyya.
3
Check if both parts can be true
In man'at al-jam', they cannot be both true, but may both be false. In haqiqiyya, they cannot be both true nor both false.
Example: 'This thing is either an animal or a stone' – can be neither (e.g., plant), so man'at al-jam'.
4
Classify the disjunctive
Based on the above, determine which type it is.

A syllogism is not just any argument; it must be composed of multiple propositions with formal entailment.

In this passage, the author defines the syllogism (qiyās) with precision, distinguishing the real intellectual syllogism from the metaphorical uttered one. He also clarifies what is excluded: single propositions, material entailments, and induction. Understanding these boundaries is crucial for correctly applying syllogistic reasoning in Islamic sciences.

The Two Conditions of a Syllogism: Granted Premises and Necessary Consequence
Author explains two conditions of the syllogism and excludes non-necessary inferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions that define a valid syllogism according to the logicians, and apply them to distinguish a purely formal syllogism from arguments based on material content, incomplete induction, or analogy.

Core Idea
A syllogism's validity depends on formal necessity, not on the truth of its premises.

When logicians define a syllogism as 'a composed statement from granted premises that necessarily yields another statement,' they include two subtle conditions. First, 'granted' means we assume the premises for the sake of argument, regardless of their real truth. Second, 'necessarily yields' means the conclusion follows from the form, not from the specific meanings of the terms. Understanding these conditions is crucial because many common arguments like incomplete induction (istiqra' naqis) and analogy (tamthil) fail to meet them, and are therefore not strictly syllogistic. In this lesson, we unpack these conditions with the commentator's clarifications.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two conditions in the Arabic passage
Find the phrases 'mattā sullimat' and 'lazima 'anhu'. These are the two conditions that the commentator explains.
The conditions are embedded in the definition of a syllogism.
2
Understand 'mattā sullimat': premises are assumed true
The commentator says premises do not have to be true in reality (sādiqa fī nafs al-amr). The condition is that they are granted (musallamat) for the sake of the inference. This covers both true and false premises.
This condition separates logical form from material truth.
3
Understand 'lazima 'anhu': necessary consequence due to form
The conclusion must follow necessarily from the premises as a whole, not because of the specific meanings of the terms. The example of man/stone/inanimate shows a conclusion that follows from material content (bi khuṣūṣ al-mādda), not from the syllogistic form.
This condition ensures that the inference is purely formal.
4
Identify what is excluded besides material implication: incomplete induction and analogy
Incomplete induction (istiqra' ghayr tamm) and analogy (tamthil) are excluded because they are conjectural (ẓanniyān) and their conclusions can fail to follow even if premises are granted. They do not produce necessary consequence.
These are not considered valid syllogisms in the strict sense.

The Rule for Conditional Syllogisms: Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens
Author divides qiyas into two types, defines each, and gives examples with rules for conditional syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of modus ponens and modus tollens to conditional syllogisms using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Affirming the antecedent yields the consequent; denying the consequent yields the denial of the antecedent.

The author provides a concise rule for the implicative conditional syllogism: if you affirm the antecedent, the consequent follows; if you deny the consequent, the denial of the antecedent follows. This is the foundation of conditional reasoning in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Look for a statement of the form 'if A then B' (al-muttasila al-luzumiyya).
2
Find the exception (istithna')
The exception will be either the affirmation of the antecedent or the denial of the consequent.
3
Apply the rule
If the antecedent is affirmed, conclude the consequent. If the consequent is denied, conclude the denial of the antecedent.
These are the only two valid forms; affirming the consequent or denying the antecedent are invalid.

Identify the exclusion (istithna')
In the minor premise, is an identical ('ayn) of a disjunct excluded (i.e., one disjunct is affirmed) or is a contradictory (naqiḍ) excluded (i.e., one disjunct is negated)?

3
Apply the rule
For exclusive: if you affirm one, the other is false; if you negate one, the other is true. For man'at al-jam' only: only the affirmation rule works. For man'at al-khuluw only: only the negation rule works.
Memorize: 'Istithna' al-'ayn yuntij al-naqiḍ' and 'Istithna' al-naqiḍ yuntij al-'ayn'.
4
Derive the conclusion
Based on the rule, write down the conclusion about the other disjunct.
The conclusion is either the contradictory or the identical of the other disjunct.

Why does the author say the fourth figure is not used?
A) It is invalid.
B) It is not obvious in productivity and is very unnatural.
C) Its premises are always particular.
D) It always produces negative conclusions.

Key Takeaway
The fourth figure is not invalid but is excluded from practical logic because its form is far from natural intuitive reasoning.
