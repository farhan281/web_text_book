The Four Types of Definition: Hadd and Rasm
Author defines definition (mu'arrif) and distinguishes hadd and rasm types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any definition as one of the four types: hadd tamm, hadd naqis, rasm tamm, or rasm naqis.

Core Idea
Definitions are either essential (hadd) or descriptive (rasm), and each can be perfect or imperfect.

The author presents a taxonomy of definitions that is crucial for precise conceptual analysis. By learning to identify whether a definition uses a genus, a differentia, or a property, you will be able to assess the type and completeness of any definition encountered in Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Look for the term that is being defined in the definition phrase. It is usually the subject of the sentence.
e.g., in 'human is rational animal', the definiendum is 'human'.
2
Find the genus if present
The genus is the broader category that includes the definiendum. It is usually a universal term like 'animal' or 'body'.
3
Find the differentia if present
The differentia is the distinguishing characteristic that separates the species from other members of the genus, e.g., 'rational'.
4
Check for a property (khassa)
A property is a non-essential characteristic that uniquely belongs to the species, e.g., 'able to laugh' for humans. If a property is used without a differentia, the definition is a rasm.
5
Classify the definition
Based on the presence/absence of genus, differentia, and property, determine if it is hadd tamm, hadd naqis, rasm tamm, or rasm naqis.
Use the following table: hadd tamm: genus + differentia; hadd naqis: differentia alone or remote genus + differentia; rasm tamm: genus + property; rasm naqis: property alone or remote genus + property.

Classifying Definitions: Hadd and Rasm, Complete and Incomplete
Author classifies definitions into four types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any definition into one of the four types: hadd tāmm, hadd nāqis, rasm tāmm, or rasm nāqis based on its components (genus, differentia, property).

Core Idea
A complete definition uses proximate genus and differentia; a complete description uses proximate genus and property.

After establishing the condition for a definition, the text presents a taxonomy. Definitions are divided into hadd (essential) and rasm (descriptive), each with complete and incomplete subtypes. Understanding this classification helps you assess how thoroughly a definition captures the essence or identifies the thing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components in the definition
Look for a genus (جنس) – a broader category, a differentia (فصل) – a distinguishing essential characteristic, and/or a property (خاصة) – a non-essential distinctive attribute. For example, in 'human is a rational animal', 'animal' is the genus, 'rational' is the differentia.
Differentia is essential, property is not.
2
Check the proximity of the genus
If the genus is the closest (proximate) genus, it yields a complete definition/description. If it is a remote genus, the definition becomes incomplete. For example, 'human is a rational substance' uses 'substance' (a remote genus) – that would be incomplete hadd (hadd nāqis).
Proximate genus is the immediate higher category.
3
Determine if it is hadd or rasm
If the distinguishing component is a differentia (essential), it is hadd. If it is a property (non-essential), it is rasm.
4
Assign the type
Combine: if hadd with proximate genus → hadd tāmm; hadd without proximate genus (only differentia, or differentia+remote genus) → hadd nāqis; rasm with proximate genus+property → rasm tāmm; rasm with only property or property+remote genus → rasm nāqis.

The Six Relations of Indication (al-Nisab al-Sitta)
Author enumerates six relations among mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three types of indication (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) entail which others, and justify each relation using the conditions of wad' and simple/composite meanings.

Core Idea
The three indications form a logical hierarchy: tadammun and iltizam always entail mutabaqa, but mutabaqa stands alone.

In the Mizan al-‘Adala, the author delves into the logical structure of indication (dalala). He clarifies that implicative indication (dalala iltizamiyya) does not require external existence—mental connection suffices. The heart of the passage is the systematic enumeration of six possible entailment relations among mutabaqa, tadammun, and iltizam. Understanding these relations is essential for analyzing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic, particularly in usul al-fiqh where the scope of a term's meaning determines legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of indication
Recall the definitions: mutabaqa (word signifies entire meaning), tadammun (word signifies a part of meaning), iltizam (word signifies something necessarily implied by the meaning).
2
Understand the condition for entailment
The author argues that both tadammun and iltizam involve conventional assignment (wad'), which itself entails mutabaqa. Therefore, tadammun and iltizam both entail mutabaqa.
This is the first relation.
3
Check mutabaqa's entailment of tadammun
Mutabaqa does not entail tadammun because simple meanings (like 'point') have no parts. Tadammun requires a composite meaning.
Second relation: mutabaqa does not entail tadammun.
4
Check mutabaqa's entailment of iltizam
Mutabaqa does not necessarily entail iltizam, unless the signified always has a necessary concomitant. The author discusses the Imam's view that every quiddity has a concomitant (e.g., 'not being other'), but the standard response restricts it to the more specific sense.
Third relation: mutabaqa does not entail iltizam in the strict sense.
5
Examine tadammun and iltizam
There is no mutual entailment between tadammun and iltizam. A composite meaning may lack a necessary concomitant, and a simple meaning may have one that is understood only after understanding the meaning.
Remaining three relations are negative: tadammun does not entail iltizam, iltizam does not entail tadammun.

Author defends the wording 'ala al-shay' against an objection.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The wording 'ala al-shay' broadens the definition to include both proximate and remote differentiae.

In defining 'fasl' (differentia), choosing the right wording is crucial. Al-Taftazani's use of 'ala al-shay' ('on the thing') was intentional to cover both al-fasl al-qarib (proximate) and al-fasl al-ba'id (remote). An unskilled critic objected that 'ala al-naw' would also suffice, but the author refutes this by clarifying that the original phrase from the five universals would have excluded the remote differentia. The critic mistakenly thought 'ala al-shay' was a 'qasr haqiqi' (real restriction). The author clarifies it is not; it is simply a more general phrase that covers both cases. Understand that precision in wording is about capturing all intended instances, not about artificial restriction.

Two Pitfalls in Definition: Circularity and Obscurity
Author states two pitfalls to avoid in definitions: circularity and obscure wording.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular definitions and obscure wording in Islamic logical texts, and distinguish between complete and incomplete definitions.

Core Idea
A definition must clarify the unknown, not mirror it or hide it in strange words.

We have seen what a good definition looks like. Now the author warns us of two fatal errors that make a definition useless: circularity (turning in a loop) and obscurity (using unfamiliar or ambiguous words). These are not just minor mistakes—they defeat the very purpose of definition, which is to produce knowledge. Let us examine each defect carefully and then see how they relate to the distinction between complete and incomplete definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two pitfalls
Read the Arabic and find the two things the author says must be avoided: (1) defining something by something equally known/unknown (circularity), and (2) using strange or barbarous words (obscurity).

Analyze circularity
The example is defining 'father' by 'son' and 'son' by 'father'. Notice why this fails: one term is known only through the other, so no new knowledge is gained. The listener is no better off than before.
This applies to any pair of correlatives (mutadayifan) in logic or usul al-fiqh.

Analyze obscure wording
The author says to avoid strange words (alfaz ghariba) that are not clearly indicative of the intended meaning. He adds that lack of clarity may come from strangeness, metaphor, or homonymy without context. The key is that the definition must be clear to the questioner, not to someone else.
The purpose of definition is to teach, so the terms must be more familiar than the definiendum.
4
Distinguish complete vs incomplete definitions
A complete essential definition (hadd tamm) does not admit increase or decrease in meaning, though it may be rephrased. Incomplete definitions (like rasm) can vary in meaning and may not be expressed by speech alone.
This helps you evaluate whether a given definition is a real hadd or merely a description.

A good definition must avoid circularity and obscurity, and a perfect definition remains invariant in meaning even if expressed differently.
