The Three Categories of Singular Words
Divides singular words into particle, verb, and noun based on predication and time indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given singular Arabic word as a particle, verb, or noun using the logical criteria of predication and time indication.

Core Idea
Every singular word is either fit for predication or not; if fit, it either indicates time by form (verb) or does not (noun).

Al-Mīzān divides the singular word (al-mufrad) into three classes: particle, verb, and noun. This division is foundational for understanding how words function in propositions. The key is the word's inherent fitness to be a predicate and whether it indicates a specific time through its morphology.

Guided Path
1
Apply the predication criterion
Ask: Is this word inherently fit to be a predicate (like 'Zayd' or 'kātib')? If no, it is a particle.
2
If fit, check time indication
Does the word indicate a specific time (past, present, future) by its morphological form (like 'kataba' indicates past, 'yaktubu' indicates present)? If yes, it is a verb.
3
If not, it is a noun
If the word is fit for predication but does not indicate a specific time through its form, it is a noun (e.g., 'rajulun', 'al-ʿilmu').

Commentary
This passage classifies the singular word (mufrad) into three categories: particle (adāh), verb (fi'l), and noun (ism). It begins by stating that after defining compound and singular, the author moves to subdivide the singular first because its essence is simpler. The first division is based on whether the word is inherently fit for predication (al-salāḥiyyah al-dhātiyyah li an yukhbar bihī 'an shay'). If it is not fit, it is a particle, like 'lā' (no/not). The verb inherently tells us when the action occurred. The imperative and prohibitive are special cases that are not excluded despite not having a specific time etymology? The Particle (Adāh): What Cannot Be Predicated
Divides singular words into particle, verb, and noun based on predication and time indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a particle by its lack of inherent fitness for predication and recognize the special case of the 'ma'dūlah' particle like 'lā'.

Core Idea
A particle cannot serve as a predicate because its meaning is non-independent.

The first category of singular words is the particle (adāh). The passage defines it negatively: it lacks the essential fitness to be predicated of something. Even 'lā', which can appear in a negative predicate ('Zayd lā ḥajar'), is fundamentally a particle because its original meaning is not self-standing.

Guided Path
1
Understand the criterion
A word that is not inherently fit to be a predicate (like an independent noun) is a particle.
2
Identify particles like 'lā'
The particle 'lā' is given as an example. Its meaning is non-independent; it only negates.
3
Examine the special case 'ma'dūlah'
'Lā' can be part of a predicate after 'turning away' from its non-independent meaning, as in 'Zayd lā ḥajar' (Zayd is not a stone). It is still a particle because its original meaning is not fit for predication.
4
Distinguish from nouns and verbs
Contrast particles with words like 'Zayd' or 'kātib' which can be predicates directly.

The Verb: Time Indication via Morphological Form
Divides singular words into particle, verb, and noun based on predication and time indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a verb by checking if it indicates one of the three times (past, present, future) through its morphological form (hay'ah taṣrifiyyah).

Core Idea
A verb is a word that inherently points to a specific time through its shape.

After excluding particles, the next division is between verbs and nouns. The verb is defined by its ability to indicate a specific time (past, present, or future) through its morphological pattern (hay'ah taṣrifiyyah). This indication must be via inclusion (bi al-tadammun) – the word's form itself carries the time.

Guided Path
1
Confirm predication fitness
Ensure the word can be a predicate (so it's not a particle).
2
Check for time indication by form
Does the word's pattern (like fa'ala, yaf'alu) imply a time? This is different from words that merely associate with time (like 'ams').
3
Verify the time is specific and from the three
The verb must indicate a definite time (past, present, future) – not absolute time (like 'madkhal') and not a time outside these three (like 'ṣabūḥ').
4
Test with examples
'Kataba' indicates past (specific), 'yaktubu' indicates present (specific). 'Ghadan' indicates future but not through morphological form, so it's a noun. 'Ams' (yesterday) is a noun.

Distinguishing Necessary and Separating Accidents
Author defines 'ard lazim and 'ard mufariq with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify accidents as necessary or separating and provide Islamic examples of each.

Core Idea
A necessary accident cannot part from its subject; a separating accident can.

In this passage, the author defines two key types of accident: the necessary (al-'ard al-lazim) and the separating (al-'ard al-mufariq). Understanding this distinction is fundamental to grasping how attributes relate to substances in Islamic logic, and it has direct applications in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh when analyzing properties of entities.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two definitions
In the Arabic text, identify where the definition of 'ard lazim begins (فهو أي الخارج الذي يمتنع انفكاكه) and where 'ard mufariq begins (وإلا أي وإن لم يمتنع انفكاكه).
2
Compare the condition
Note the condition for each: for necessary, it is impossibility of separation (امتناع الانفكاك); for separating, it is possibility of separation (جواز الانفكاك), regardless of actual permanence.
The key is logical possibility, not actual duration.
3
Examine the examples
Potential laughter (الضحك بالقوة) is necessary for humans because it is impossible for a human to lack the capacity to laugh. Actual laughter (الضحك بالفعل) is separating because it comes and goes. Illiteracy (الأمية) is separating because it can be removed by learning.
Think about why each example fits its category.
4
Understand the conventional usage
The author notes that he used the term 'accident' only with the separating type and omitted it with the necessary type, following convention. This is a terminological choice, not a logical inconsistency.
This step helps avoid confusion when reading other texts.

Core Idea
The difference between a simple negative and an affirmative modified is not merely verbal—it affects the logical interpretation.

In classical logic, confusion often arises between two types of propositions: the simple negative (like 'Zayd is not a writer') and the affirmative modified where the predicate itself is negative (like 'Zayd is non-writer'). Although both contain a negation word, their logical structure differs. This lesson unpacks the multiple criteria developed by logicians to resolve this ambiguity, including conceptual, material, and linguistic clues.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proposition types in the example
Consider 'Zayd is not a writer'. This could be a simple negative (negating the connection) or an affirmative modified (affirming 'non-writer' as predicate). Recognise the ambiguity.
The presence of the negation particle 'laysa' allows both interpretations.
2
Apply the conceptual criterion
Check whether the judgment is attribution (īqā') or removal (intizā'). In an affirmative modified, the judgment is still attribution (affirming the negated predicate), while in a simple negative, it is removal (negating the connection).
This criterion tells you the logical form but requires knowing the predicate's status.

Check the copula position in Arabic triliteral propositions
In Arabic, if a copula like 'huwa' is present, its position relative to the negation particle determines the type: copula before negation (e.g., 'Zayd huwa laysa bi-kātib') indicates affirmative modified; copula after negation (e.g., 'Zayd laysa huwa bi-kātib') indicates simple negative.
This criterion works only when the copula is explicitly mentioned.
4
Apply the material criterion from the first opinion
Determine if the subject is of a kind that would ordinarily have the predicate at the time of judgment. If yes, then the proposition is an affirmative modified (since the absence is notable); if no, it is a simple negative. For example, a man of beard age missing a beard is affirmative modified; a child missing a beard is simple negative.
This criterion considers the nature of the subject.

This passage is a detailed treatment of the distinction between simple (bāsiṭa) and modified (ma'dūla) propositions, specifically focusing on the confusion between the simple negative and the positive modified predicate. The author first notes that among the four proposition types (affirmative simple, negative simple, affirmative modified, negative modified), the only confusion is between the simple negative and the affirmative modified predicate because both contain a negation particle that could be interpreted as part of the predicate or as a sign of negation. The example 'Zayd is not a writer' illustrates this ambiguity. The author then presents multiple criteria for distinguishing them. First, in concept (mafhūm): the judgment in affirmative is attribution (īqā') and in negative is removal (intizā'). In matter (māddah): the simple negative is more general than the affirmative modified (i.e., it applies to more cases). In wording (lafẓ): in languages like Persian, the copula 'hast' (is) and negative 'nīst' (is not) make the distinction clear. In Arabic, when a copula (like 'huwa') is present, the position of the copula relative to the negation particle determines the type: if the copula precedes the negation, it is affirmative modified; if it follows, it is simple negative. In bipartite propositions (no copula), the distinction is by intention: whether one intends to affirm the negation or negate the affirmation. Finally, a conventional solution is to assign specific words: 'ghayr' and 'lā' for affirmative modified, 'laysa' for simple negative, or vice versa. Then three opinions (qīla) are given regarding the material distinction based on the suitability of the subject to have the attribute. For example, a man of beard age missing a beard is a case of affirmative modified (since he is supposed to have a beard), while a child or woman missing a beard is a simple negative (since they are not expected to have a beard). These opinions differ on the temporal scope and the consideration of species and genus, but all aim to capture the same underlying conceptual difference.

Key Terms
بسيطة (basiṭa): A simple proposition, either affirmative or negative, where the predicate is a positive term (e.g., 'Zayd is a writer' or 'Zayd is not a writer' without considering negation as part of the predicate). [Note: In later logic, 'basiṭa' can refer to the categorical proposition without modality; here it contrasts with 'ma'dūla'.]
موجبة معدولة المحمول (mawjūba ma'dūlat al-maḥmūl): An affirmative proposition whose predicate is a negated term (e.g., 'Zayd is not-writer'), i.e., the negation is part of the predicate, making it an affirmative in form but with a privative predicate. [Note: This usage is standard; some later texts distinguish between 'ma'dūlat al-maḥmūl' and 'ma'dūlat al-mawḍū'.]
سالبة محصلة (sāliba muḥaṣṣala): A simple negative proposition where the negation operates on the copula, e.g., 'Zayd is not a writer' (negation of the connection). [Note: Also called 'sāliba bāsiṭa'; no significant shift.]
إيقاع (īqā'): Attribution: the act of affirming a predicate to a subject in an affirmative proposition. [Note: In grammar, it can mean 'occurrence'; here it is a logical term for positive judgment.]
انتزاع (intizā'): Removal: the act of negating a predicate from a subject in a negative proposition. [Note: Less common; some authors use 'salab' or 'nafy' for negation.]
رابطة (rābiṭa): Copula: the word that links subject and predicate, e.g., 'huwa' in Arabic, 'hast' in Persian. [Note: In Arabic logic, the copula is often omitted; here it is explicitly considered for the distinction.]

Key Takeaway
The distinction between simple negative and affirmative modified propositions requires attention to conceptual, material, and linguistic criteria, and the material criterion often depends on whether the subject is expected to have the attribute.
