Reading the Title Page of Badi' al-Mizan
Title page identifying the book, author, and editors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a classical Islamic book's title page: title, author, genre (sharh), and death date.

Core Idea
A title page is not decoration—it encodes intellectual lineage and scholarly authority.

This lesson introduces you to the very first page of Badi' al-Mizan, a commentary on the logic text Mizan al-Mantiq. The title page tells you the book's name, its genre, the author, his geographical origins, his death date, and the modern editors. Learning to read such a page is a foundational skill for engaging with any classical Islamic work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the book title
The first words 'بديع الميزان' are the title, meaning 'The Novelty of the Scale'. It hints at the content: a commentary evaluating the 'scale' (mizan) of logic.
2
Recognize the genre
The word 'شرح' tells you this is a commentary (sharh) on an earlier work. The base text is 'ميزان المنطق' (The Scale of Logic). This is a typical format: a commentary explaining and sometimes expanding a shorter matn.
3
Extract the author's name and nisbahs
The author's full name is 'عبد الله بن ألَّه داد العثماني الطلبي المُلْتاني'. The nisbahs (al-Uthmani, al-Talabi, al-Multani) indicate his family or tribal affiliation and his origin from Multan (now Pakistan). These are crucial for placing the author geographically and historically.
4
Note the death date
The phrase 'المتوفى عام ٩٢٢هـ' gives the death year 922 AH. This dates the author and the work to the early 10th century AH (16th century CE).
5
Identify the editors
The phrase 'اعتنى به حمد العرفج خالد الحسيني' names the modern editors. This shows that the edition you are holding is a critical edition by contemporary scholars.

Understanding Matn and Sharh in Islamic Logic
Author praises God, explains significance of Badi Mizan and its sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the matn (base text) and sharh (commentary) in a passage from an Islamic logic work and explain their relationship.

Core Idea
Every commentary is a dialogue with its base text.

This passage from Badi Mizan opens with praise and introduces the book as a commentary on al-Shamsiyya. It reveals how later scholars built upon earlier works. Learning to distinguish between matn and sharh is essential for engaging with any advanced Islamic text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn (base text)
Look for the original work being commented on. In this passage, it is explicitly named: 'the text of al-Shamsiyya by al-Katibi'.
The matn is the core text that the commentator explains.
2
Identify the sharh (commentary)
Find the work that is doing the explaining. Here it is 'the commentary called Badi Mizan by Abdullah al-Talanbi'.
The sharh is the commentary that expands on the matn.
3
Analyze the relationship
The sharh is described as an abridgment (mukhtasar) of the matn, but also incorporating 'the pearls of al-Qutb and al-Sayyid and others who wrote on al-Shamsiyya'. This shows the layered nature of commentaries.
A sharh often references earlier commentaries, creating a hierarchy of explanation.
4
Recognize the sources used
The author mentions that the commentary includes insights from earlier scholars like al-Qutb, al-Sayyid, and others. This helps you understand the intellectual lineage.
Knowing the sources helps evaluate the depth and tradition of the commentary.

A critical edition's reliability depends on the quality and completeness of its manuscript sources.

This lesson explains how the editor of Badi' Mizan selected his sources from a handwritten copy and a printed copy with marginal notes, and for the matn, a copy accompanying the commentary and a standalone print.

Commentary
This passage is the editor's introduction to the edition, explaining the manuscript sources and the editorial methodology. It consists of two main sections: first, a description of the four copies used (two for the commentary and two for the matn), and second, eleven numbered points outlining the editorial decisions. The first manuscript (خ) is a complete handwritten copy from 1389 AH, chosen for its completeness and clarity despite later finding better ones; the second (ط) is a printed copy with marginal notes from three scholars, photographed by the Rashidiyya Library. For the matn, the first copy (ش) accompanies al-Talanabi's commentary, and the second (الأصل) is a standalone printed edition from 1284 AH with comments by al-Badi'. The methodology then details the 'eclectic method' (التلفيق), where the editor selects the most correct reading and notes variants in the margin. The annotation system uses (-) for omissions and (+) for additions in a given copy. Other points cover quotation formatting, marking the matn in bold within parentheses, punctuation, minimal formatting, book title symbols, presenting the matn before the commentary, grammatical corrections, editorial insertions in square brackets, and the decision to include a biography only for the commentator due to lack of information about the matn author.

The Talfiq Method and Annotation System
Editor explains the eclectic method and annotation conventions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the eclectic method (talfiq) and the symbols used in critical apparatus to track textual variants.

Core Idea
Talfiq combines the best readings from multiple copies without detailed error notes, using symbols for missing or added words.

Editors face variant readings. One approach is the eclectic method: choosing the most correct reading and noting alternatives in the margin. This lesson explains the talfiq method used in the Badi' Mizan edition and the annotation symbols (-) and (+).

Guided Path
1
Define talfiq in textual criticism
Read the first methodological point: 'اعتمدنا في الشرح منهج التلفيق'. Understand that the editor selects the best reading without listing every error, and notes the alternative only when equivalent readings exist.
This is distinct from 'talfiq' in fiqh.
2
Learn the annotation symbols
Examine point three: (-) before a symbol means the word is missing from that copy; (+) means it is additional. If no symbol, the copy has a different reading.
Symbols are consistently used for (ط), (خ), (الأصل), and (ش).
3
Apply to an example
Imagine an apparatus entry: 'كلمة (-) في ط' means the word is missing in ط. Practice interpreting a few hypothetical entries.
Check the margin when you see these symbols in the edition.
4
Understand the editor's principle
The editor chooses 'the most correct' (الأصح) and alerts only to 'the sound' (الصحيح). He does not flag every mistake. This speeds up reading but requires trust in the editor.
This is a choice; other editors might note all variants.

The Two Pillars of Knowledge: Concepts and Assent
Author begins with praise of Allah and states the book's subject as logic of concepts and assents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a concept (tasawwur) and an assent (tasdiq) and provide an Islamic example of each.

Core Idea
All knowledge is either a simple concept or an affirmed judgment.

The author mentions that Allah has adorned our breasts with comprehension of 'tasawwurāt' and 'tasdiq'. These two categories form the entire subject matter of logic: the first deals with definitions and concepts, the second with proofs and propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms in the passage
Find 'التصورات' (concepts) and 'التصديق' (assent) in the Arabic text. They are the objects of comprehension with which Allah adorned the breasts.
These terms are technical and will recur throughout the entire discipline.
2
Define tasawwur and tasdiq
A tasawwur is a mental representation without judgment, e.g., the concept of 'jinn'. A tasdiq is a judgment affirming or denying something, e.g., 'Jinn exist'. The former is the building block of the latter.
Tasdiq is the only type of knowledge that is true or false; tasawwur is neither true nor false.
3
Give an Islamic example for each
Example of tasawwur: the concept of 'ribā' (usury) as defined in fiqh. Example of tasdiq: asserting that 'Ribā is prohibited'. Without the concept, the judgment has no subject.
Usul al-fiqh relies heavily on this distinction: first define the term (tasawwur), then derive the ruling (tasdiq).

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The Logical Architecture: From Definitions to Demonstration
Author lists the chapter headings covering key logical topics from definitions to demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the logical progression of classical Islamic logic from definitions to demonstration.

Core Idea
The book's structure mirrors the mind's journey from simple concepts to certain proof.

Before diving into the details of logic, it helps to see the big picture. This table of contents outlines the sequence of topics in Badi Mizan, starting with definitions (ta'rifat) and ending with demonstration (burhan). This structure is not arbitrary—it reflects the logical order of learning: first define terms, then form propositions, then combine them into arguments, and finally achieve certain knowledge through demonstration. Understanding this roadmap will help you place each lesson in its proper context and see how the parts build toward the whole.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting point: definitions
The outline begins with 'Section on Definitions' (فصل في التعريفات). This is the foundation: all logical reasoning depends on clear terms.
2
Trace the move to propositions
Next comes 'Section on Defining Propositions and Their Parts' (فصل في تعريف القضايا وأقسامها). Propositions are built from defined terms and are the units of truth and falsity.
Note the subdivisions: categorical, modal, conditional.
3
Observe logical relations between propositions
The outline includes 'Section on Contradiction' (فصل في التناقض) and sections on conversion (العكس), which are relations that hold between propositions. These are essential for reasoning.
Conversion and contradiction are immediate inferences—they transform one proposition into another.
4
Move to syllogism and its structure
The next major section is 'Section: On Defining the Syllogism and Its Division' (فصل: في تعريف القياس وتقسيمه). The syllogism combines propositions into arguments.
The parts and figures of the syllogism are detailed here.
5
See the culmination in demonstration
The final sections cover induction (الاستقراء), analogy (التمثيل), and demonstration (البرهان). Demonstration is the goal: a syllogism that yields certainty.
This progression shows how logic moves from simple to compound, from terms to certain proof.
