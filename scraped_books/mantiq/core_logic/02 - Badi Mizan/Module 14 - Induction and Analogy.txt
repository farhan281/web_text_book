Induction: Complete and Incomplete
Author defines induction and divides it into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete and incomplete induction and explain why incomplete induction does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Induction's certainty depends on its completeness; partial observation can mislead.

Induction is a method of reasoning from particulars to a universal judgment. In this lesson, we examine its two types as presented by the author: complete and incomplete. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines the epistemic strength of inductive arguments used in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The passage defines 'istiqra'' (induction). Note that it is defined as inferring a universal judgment from particular instances.
2
Extract the division
The author divides induction into complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis) based on how many particulars are examined.
3
Understand complete induction
Complete induction examines all particulars. The author notes it is rarely used. Consider why: in many cases, it is impossible to check every individual.
4
Understand incomplete induction
Incomplete induction examines most particulars and generalizes. The author gives the example: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing' based on humans, beasts, and predators. But this is false because the crocodile is an exception.
5
Assess certainty
The author explicitly states that incomplete induction does not yield certainty due to the possibility of counterexamples. This is the key limitation.

Understanding Analogical Reasoning (Tamthil)
Author defines analogical reasoning and notes its equivalence to juristic qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of analogical reasoning (tamthil) and explain how it corresponds to juristic qiyas.

Core Idea
Analogical reasoning transfers a ruling via a shared effective cause.

In this passage, the author defines tamthil—analogical reasoning—and directly connects it to the juristic method of qiyas. This is a foundational concept in both logic and fiqh: by identifying a shared universal attribute that influences a judgment, you can reason from one particular case to another. The example of the world and the house illustrates how composition implies temporal origination. Understanding this definition will help you see the logical structure behind many legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of tamthil
Read the passage and identify the sentence that defines tamthil. Notice that it involves inferring from one particular (juz'i) to another particular based on a shared universal (kulli) that influences the judgment (mu'aththir fi al-hukm).
2
Extract the four components of the analogical argument
In analogical reasoning, there are four key parts: (1) the original case (asl), (2) the new case (far'), (3) the effective cause ('illa), and (4) the ruling (hukm). Find these in the example given: the world is far', the house is asl, composition is 'illa, and originated is hukm.
3
Understand the shared universal
The common attribute that justifies the inference is a 'universal that influences the judgment.' In the example, composition (ta'lif) is the universal shared by both the house and the world. This is what allows the conclusion that the world is originated, just like the house.
The 'illa must be relevant to the ruling.
4
Connect tamthil to juristic qiyas
The passage explicitly states that this form of reasoning is called qiyas in the terminology of jurists. In usul al-fiqh, qiyas is a primary source of law. Recognise that the logical structure of tamthil is exactly the same as the legal reasoning used to extend rulings from established cases to new situations.
This equivalence is important for understanding how logic and fiqh interact.

Induction: Complete vs Incomplete
Author defines induction, distinguishes complete from incomplete, and gives an example with crocodile.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete from incomplete induction and evaluate the certainty of an inductive argument.

Core Idea
Complete induction yields certainty; incomplete induction only probability, vulnerable to counterexamples like the crocodile.

Induction is the examination of particulars to reach a universal judgment. But not all induction is equal: some exhaust all cases and give certainty, while others rely on most cases and remain probabilistic. The author illustrates this with the crocodile — a classic Islamic warning against hasty generalization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inductive claim
Find the universal statement that the induction aims to prove. In the incomplete example: 'Every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing.'
2
List the examined particulars
Which specific cases did the induction examine? In the text: humans and beasts. Not all animals.
3
Check completeness
Did the induction cover all possible particulars? If not, it is incomplete. Here, many animals (like crocodile) were not examined.
4
Evaluate certainty
Complete induction yields certainty (yaqin); incomplete yields only probability (zann) because a counterexample may exist. The crocodile shows the risk.
5
Apply to Islamic sciences
Think of an induction used in fiqh or kalam. Is it complete or incomplete? What would a counterexample look like?

Understanding Analogy (Tamthil) and Its Use as Qiyas in Fiqh
Definition of analogy and its identification with fiqh qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of an analogical argument (tamthil) and distinguish it from a categorical syllogism, using examples from kalam and fiqh.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a judgment between two particulars via a shared meaning; it is the basis of fiqh qiyas.

This passage defines analogy (tamthīl) and explains its structure, noting that it is called qiyas in fiqh. You will learn how a particular is used as evidence for another particular through a common cause ('illa), and see an example from kalam: the world's composition implying its origination, like a house. This tool is essential for understanding legal reasoning and theological proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particulars (al-mushabbah and al-mushabbah bihī)
In the text, the 'mushabbah' (simile) is the case you want to prove, and the 'mushabbah bihī' (similitud) is the case with a known judgment. For example, the world is the simile and the house is the similitud.
In fiqh, these are called 'far'' (branch) and 'aṣl' (original).
2
Extract the common meaning ('illa jāmi'a)
The shared attribute that links the two cases. In the example, both the world and the house are 'composed' (mu'allaf), which is the cause for the judgment of origination.
In fiqh, the 'illa must be a common effective cause for the judgment.
3
Check how the judgment is transferred
The judgment that holds for the similitud (house) is transferred to the simile (world) because they share the cause. The text says 'to establish for the simile the judgment established for the similitud which is explained by that meaning'.
This transfer is valid only if the cause is truly common and effective.
4
Distinguish tamthil from categorical syllogism
Unlike a categorical syllogism that uses two universal premises to deduce a particular, tamthil moves from particular to particular via a shared universal. The author notes that in logic, tamthil is contrasted with qiyas (syllogism), but in fiqh, it is called qiyas.
This terminological shift is important to avoid confusion.

Commentary
This passage defines analogy (tamthīl) as the likening of one particular to another in a shared meaning, in order to prove for the compared object (mushabbah) the same judgment that exists in the object compared to (mushabbah bihī), which is explained by that shared meaning. The author then references a saying (probably from another scholar) that this is using a particular as evidence for another particular due to their sharing in a universal that is effective in the judgment. This type of reasoning is called qiyās in Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh). The author explains that this is because it involves attaching one particular to another and annexing it. The form that is agreed upon (the source case) is the original (aṣl), and the disputed form is the branch (far'). The common meaning between them is the comprehensive cause ('illa jāmi'a). An example is given: 'The world is composed, therefore it is originated (hadith), like the house.' This is a classic kalam argument for the createdness of the world, using the analogy of a house requiring a builder.

Analogy (Tamthīl): Definition and Components
Author defines analogy and relates it to jurists' qiyās.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four components of a logical analogy (mushabbah, mushabbah bihī, ma'nā sharīk, hukm) and relate it to the jurists' qiyās.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a ruling from a known case to a new one through a shared cause.

This passage defines tamthīl (analogy) as comparing one particular to another to extend a ruling. The author also explains that jurists call this qiyās. Understanding analogy is crucial for legal reasoning in fiqh, where rulings are extended from a known case (aṣl) to a new case (far‘) based on a shared effective cause (ʻilla).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particulars
Find the original case (mushabbah bihī/asl) and the new case (mushabbah/far‘) in the passage. For example, in 'the world is composed, so it is originated, like a house', the house is the original and the world is the new case.
2
Extract the shared meaning (ma'nā)
Determine the common attribute that justifies the ruling transfer. Here, both are 'composed' (mu'allaf). This is the 'illa (effective cause).
3
Verify the ruling transfer
Check that the ruling (ḥukm) from the original case is applied to the new case because of the shared meaning. The ruling is 'originated' (ḥādith). Since composition implies temporality, the universe is also temporally originated.
