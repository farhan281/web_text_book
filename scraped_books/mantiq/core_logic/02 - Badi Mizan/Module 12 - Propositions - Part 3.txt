Conditions for Contradiction: Historical Views and the Author's Position
Author discusses historical differences on conditions for contradiction and states his own view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the different historical views on the conditions required for contradiction and justify the author's minimal condition of unity of the judgmental relation.

Core Idea
The only essential condition for contradiction is unity of the judgmental relation—all other unities are derivative.

Scholars historically debated how many conditions are needed for two propositions to be contradictory. The author simplifies the debate to one essential condition: the unity of the judgmental relation. Let's trace the evolution from eight unities to one.

Guided Path
1
List the ancient eight unities
The ancients required eight unities: subject, predicate, time, place, condition, part, whole, relation, etc. (though eight are not all listed, they are implied). Recognize that these cover all possible aspects of the proposition.
2
Examine the three-unity view
Some scholars reduced to three: subject, predicate, time. They claimed condition, part, whole are under subject; place, relation, potentiality, actuality under predicate. Understand how they merged categories.
This shows an early attempt at simplification.
3
Understand the two-unity view
Later scholars reduced further to subject and predicate only. Time is considered part of predicate? Actually they argued that remaining unities fall under these two. But the author disagrees.
4
Grasp the author's one-unity view
The author (muṣannif) says only unity of the judgmental relation (النسبة الحكمية) is needed, meaning the negation must apply to exactly the same relation as the affirmation. All other unities are really aspects of this single condition.
This is the most minimalist and elegant condition.

Commentary
This passage defines contradiction (tanāquḍ) and discusses the number of conditions required for its occurrence. The commentator first clarifies the definition: contradiction is the difference of two propositions by negation and affirmation such that it necessitates by itself that one is true and the other false. Each part of the definition is explained: 'two propositions' excludes singular terms; 'by negation and affirmation' specifies the type of difference; 'such that it necessitates' excludes differences that do not necessitate truth values; 'by itself' excludes accidental necessity due to external factors or specific matter. A misinterpretation is rejected. Then the text reviews historical differences on the conditions for contradiction: the ancients required eight unities (subject, predicate, time, place, condition, part, whole, etc.), some reduced to three (subject, predicate, time), later scholars reduced to two (subject and predicate). The author (may Allah have mercy on him) simplifies to one essential condition: unity of the judgmental relation (al-nisba al-ḥukmiyya), meaning that the negation must apply to exactly the same relation as the affirmation. This view is presented as the correct one.
