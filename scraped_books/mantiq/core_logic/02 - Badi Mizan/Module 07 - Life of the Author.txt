The Man Behind the Commentary: Abdullah al-Talanbi
Biography of the commentator, establishing his scholarly credentials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the scholarly lineage and authority of the commentator using the biographical details from the passage.

Core Idea
A commentator's authority is built on his teachers, his mastery, and his scholarly reception.

Before diving into the logical content, we must know whose hand is guiding us. This lesson explores the biography of the commentator, Abdullah al-Talanbi, to understand why his commentary carries weight. We will examine his name, teachers, intellectual prowess, and how he was received by the ruler and scholars of his time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commentator's full name and nisbas
Read the opening lines of the Arabic. The commentator's name is given as Abdullah bin Ilah Dad (meaning 'Ibn Ata Allah'), with nisbas al-Uthmani, al-Talanbi (referring to a village near Multan), al-Multani, then al-Dihlawi. These indicate his lineage and geographic affiliations.
Nisbas often indicate place of origin or residence; al-Dihlawi shows he settled in Delhi.
2
Map his educational journey
He studied writing and arithmetic locally in Multan, then traveled to Iran to study logic (al-mantiq) and philosophy (al-hikmah) under the renowned scholar Abdullah al-Yazdi. This journey was crucial for accessing advanced rational sciences.
Iran was a major center for logic and philosophy in that period.
3
Note his teacher and what he studied
His teacher was al-Allama Abdullah al-Yazdi. Al-Talanbi studied logic and philosophy under him, mastering them in his youth. His contemporaries praised his intelligence, fluid mind, strong memory, and fast comprehension.
The teacher's name is explicitly mentioned; his reputation adds to the student's authority.
4
Observe how his contemporaries described his intelligence
The text uses phrases like 'bahr al-fudala' (amazed the scholars), 'fart dhaka' (excessive intelligence), 'saylan dhihn' (fluidity of mind), 'quwwat hafiza' (strength of memory), and 'sur'at idrak' (speed of comprehension). These are not generic praise but specific intellectual virtues valued in the tradition.
Such descriptions are part of the biographical tradition to establish credibility.
5
Connect these details to the authority of the commentary
Al-Talanbi's extensive training, his recognized mastery, and his appointment as 'Malik al-Ulama' (King of Scholars) by Sultan Sikandar Shah Lodi all combine to give his commentary significant authority. The reader can trust that he is a qualified exponent of logic.
In Islamic scholarship, the chain of learning and recognition by established authorities is key to a work's reliability.

From Iran to India: The Curriculum Revolution
Biography of the commentator, focusing on his curriculum reform.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the specific curriculum reform introduced by al-Talanbi and explain its significance for the study of logic in India.

Core Idea
One man expanded the Indian madrasa curriculum from two texts to a full rational sciences syllabus.

The passage reports that before al-Talanbi, Indian madrasas only taught Sharh al-Shamsiyya in logic and Sharh al-Sahifah in kalam. This lesson examines how he introduced advanced books ('kutub daqiqa') and what that meant for the tradition. We will also look at the Sultan's role in supporting this reform.

Guided Path
1
Identify the texts studied before al-Talanbi
The passage states that before his arrival, only two texts were studied in the region: 'Sharh al-Shamsiyya' in logic and 'Sharh al-Sahifah' in kalam (theology). This indicates a limited curriculum in the rational sciences.
Sharh al-Shamsiyya is a commentary on a logic primer; Sharh al-Sahifah is a commentary on a theological work.
2
Note the advanced texts he introduced
Al-Talanbi introduced 'al-kutub al-daqiqa fi al-ma'qul' (subtle books in the intellectual sciences). The passage does not name specific titles, but these are more advanced works in logic and philosophy, likely including works by al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and later commentators.
The phrase 'daqiqa' implies works that require greater intellectual rigor.
3
Recognize the role of Sultan Sikandar Shah Lodi
The Sultan appointed al-Talanbi as 'Malik al-Ulama' and honored him greatly. He even hid himself when al-Talanbi was teaching to avoid disrupting the lesson. This shows the Sultan's high regard and active support for educational reform.
Royal patronage was often crucial for educational changes in premodern India.
4
Reflect on the impact on logic studies in India
By expanding the curriculum, al-Talanbi raised the level of logical education in India. Students could now access more advanced materials, which deepened their understanding and prepared them for higher-level discourse in kalam and philosophy.
This reform is documented by the historian al-Bada'uni, lending it historical credibility.

Biography of the Commentator (1)
He is the virtuous, distinguished, and learned Shaykh Abdullah bin Ilah (meaning God, sometimes written Allah or Ilah) Dad (meaning gift), so the meaning is: son of the gift of God. The two words may be written together as in this commentary as 'al-Ilahdad', with a shaddah on the first dal, an error. He is al-Uthmani al-Tilanbi (with ta and ta) al-Multani then al-Dihlawi, one of the famous professors in India.
He was born in Tilanbah, with a damma on the ta, a village in the district of Multan. He learned writing and arithmetic, and studied Arabic for some days in his country. Then he traveled to Iran and studied logic and philosophy under the learned 'Abdullah al-Yazdi, and stayed with him for a long time until he excelled and mastered (the sciences) while in the flower of his youth and prime of his youth. He amazed the scholars with his extreme intelligence, fluency of mind, strength of memory, and quickness of understanding. He returned to his homeland as one of the greatest scholars, and took up teaching, teaching and benefiting for a time in his country. Then, due to seditions, he was compelled to leave those lands, and entered Delhi during the days of Sikandar Shah Lodi. The Sultan took advantage of his arrival and made him the 'king of scholars'.
He used to teach the subtle books on logic and philosophy with extreme verification and scrutiny. He was the one who introduced them into the curriculum and popularized them in those lands. Al-Badauni explicitly stated this in his history, saying: 'Before his arrival, they did not read in these regions anything except the Sharh al-Shamsiyya on logic and the Sharh al-Sahayif on theology. He expanded the curriculum and introduced the subtle books on the rational sciences.'
He said: 'Sikandar Shah honored him greatly, and would attend his sessions. If he found him occupied with teaching, he would hide himself in a corner of the assembly so that the lesson would not be disrupted by his arrival. When he finished, he would greet him and converse with him.'
He said: 'The Sultan gathered the scholars from the regions of India and divided them into two groups. He placed Shaykh Abdullah and his companion Azizullah on one side, and placed Shaykh Ilahdad al-Jaunpuri and his son Shaykh Bahkari on the other side. He commanded them to debate. They engaged in discussion and debate, and it became clear to him that the first group excelled the second in good conversation, and the second surpassed the first in skill of writing.' End quote.
