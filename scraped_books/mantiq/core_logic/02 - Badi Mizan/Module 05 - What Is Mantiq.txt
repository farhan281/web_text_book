The Division of Knowledge: Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Author defines 'ilm, tasawwur, tasdiq, and divides signification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq based on the presence of judgment in the definition.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either simple concept (tasawwur) or concept with judgment (tasdiq).

This lesson introduces the two fundamental divisions of knowledge in Islamic logic: mere conceptualization (tasawwur) and conceptualization accompanied by a judgment (tasdiq). Understanding this distinction is crucial because all logical reasoning builds upon propositions (tasdiqat) formed from concepts. We will parse the precise definitions given in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division of knowledge
The passage begins by dividing 'ilm into two types: tasawwur only, and tasawwur with hukm (judgment). Note that the first type lacks judgment, the second includes it.
2
Extract the definition of tasawwur
Tasawwur is defined as 'the attainment of the form of a thing in the intellect.' This is a simple apprehension without any affirmation or negation. Identify that it is purely cognitive without judgment.
3
Extract the definition of tasdiq
Tasdiq is defined as 'a concept with a judgment, which is attributing one thing to another affirmatively or negatively.' Notice the two components: a concept (tasawwur) plus a hukm (judgment). This judgment is the distinguishing feature.
4
Compare tasawwur and tasdiq
Contrast the two: tasawwur lacks judgment (no affirmation or negation), while tasdiq includes it. This difference is foundational: tasawwur provides the raw material, tasdiq produces a truth-apt proposition.

Signification (Dalalah) and Its Types
Author defines signification and its two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalalah and distinguish between verbal/conventional and intellectual/non-conventional signification.

Core Idea
Signification is the property of a signifier to lead to knowledge of the signified.

This lesson covers the definition of signification (dalalah) and its two divisions: verbal/conventional (lafẓī wadʿī) and intellectual/non-conventional (ʿaqlī ghayr wadʿī). Logic primarily deals with the first type, as it forms the basis of linguistic analysis. Understanding this division helps clarify how words convey meaning versus natural signs.

Guided Path
1
Define dalalah
Find the definition: 'the state of a thing such that knowledge of it necessitates knowledge of something else.' Identify the signifier (the thing known) and the signified (the something else).
2
Identify the two types of dalalah
The passage divides dalalah into two: lafẓī wadʿī (verbal conventional) and ʿaqlī ghayr wadʿī (intellectual non-conventional). Note that the first depends on human convention, the second on rational inference.
3
Distinguish the scope of logic
Logic, in this context, focuses on lafẓī wadʿī signification, i.e., how conventional words convey meaning. The other type (e.g., smoke signifying fire) is not the subject of logic.

Apply with an example
Consider the Arabic word 'kutub' (books). Its signification is lafẓī wadʿī: the word was conventionally assigned to mean books. In contrast, seeing a book on a desk signifies someone placed it there — this is ʿaqlī signification.

The Three Modes of Word Signification
Author classifies the three ways a word signifies its meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three modes of word signification (mutabaqa, tadamun, iltizam) and correctly classify examples from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
A word can signify its full meaning, a part, or an external necessary implication.

Words are not arbitrary; they are assigned to meanings by convention (wad'). Logicians classify how a word relates to its meaning into three types. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for precise interpretation of legal and theological texts, where a single word may carry multiple layers of signification.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of wad'
Recognize that words are assigned to meanings by convention. The signification types are based on that assignment.
2
Identify mutabaqa (correspondence)
When a word signifies its entire assigned meaning. Example: 'human' signifies 'rational animal' entirely.
3
Identify tadamun (inclusion)
When a word signifies a part of its meaning because that part is included in the whole. Example: 'human' signifies 'animal' (genus) or 'rational' (differentia).
The part must be a constituent of the full meaning.
4
Identify iltizam (implication)
When a word signifies something external to its meaning but necessarily implied. Example: 'human' implies 'capable of knowledge'.
The implication must be necessary, not accidental.
5
Practice with Islamic terms
Apply the classification to words like 'salat', 'zakat', 'iman'. Determine what each word signifies in each mode.

This passage explains the three modes of signification for a word that has been conventionally assigned to a meaning. (3) التزام (implication): the word signifies something external to the meaning but necessarily associated with it. The common example given is 'human' implying 'receptacle of knowledge' because humans are capable of learning. The text also mentions 'craft of writing' as another implication. Classification of Simple Words in Logic
Author divides words into simple and compound, then subdivides simple into noun, verb, and particle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given Arabic word is simple (mufrad) or compound (murakkab), and if simple, classify it as noun, verb, or particle based on the criteria of predicability and time indication.

Core Idea
Logical word classification depends on part-whole signification and predicability, not just grammar.

In logic, before we build propositions, we need to understand the building blocks: words. The author provides a crisp classification that cuts through grammatical complexity. By mastering this, you will be able to analyze any term in an Islamic argument—whether from the Qur'an, hadith, or fiqh—with precision.

Guided Path
1
Understand the primary division: mufrad vs murakkab
Ask: Is any part of the word intended to signify a part of its meaning? For example, 'thrower of the arrow' (ramī al-sahm) is compound because 'sahm' signifies an arrow. Otherwise it is simple.
The criterion is intention, not physical parts.
2
Apply the predicability test for simple words
Can this word be used as a predicate in a proposition (e.g., 'Zaid is [word]')? If no, it is a particle (adāt) like 'lā' (no) or 'hal' (whether).
Particles do not carry independent meaning for predication.
3
Distinguish verb from noun using time indication
If the word is suitable for predication and its morphological form indicates a specific tense (past, present, future), it is a verb (kalima/fi'l). If it does not indicate a specific time, it is a noun (ism).
The term 'kalima' here is specifically used for the verb, not any word.

Commentary
This passage defines the classification of words after discussing signification. Words that signify by correspondence are first divided into simple (mufrad) and compound (murakkab). A compound word is one in which a part of it is intended to signify a part of its overall meaning; for example, 'thrower of the arrow' (ramī al-sahm) is compound because 'sahm' (arrow) is a part signifying the object. (2) Verb (fi'l): suitable for predication and indicates a specific tense (past, present, future) through its morphological form. The term 'kalima' is used here synonymously with 'fi'l'. One-Meaning Nouns: Proper, Univocal, and Analogical
Classification of nouns with one meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any noun with a single meaning as a proper noun ('alam), univocal (mutawāti'), or analogical (mushakkik) based on determination and equality of application.

Core Idea
A noun with one meaning may apply to one (proper) or many – equally (univocal) or with priority (analogical).

In this passage, the author categorizes nouns starting from their meaning count. When a noun has a single meaning, we must check if it is determined to a specific individual (like 'Muhammad') – that makes it a proper noun. If not, does it apply equally to all members of its class, like 'human' (insān), or more strongly to some, like 'existence' (wujūd) for God? These distinctions are foundational for defining terms in logic and Islamic sciences, especially in Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the number of meanings
First, determine if the noun has one meaning or many. This lesson focuses on nouns with a single meaning.
2
Check if the meaning is determined to a specific individual
If the noun points to a particular entity (e.g., 'Makkah', 'Zayd') and is not a pronoun or demonstrative, it is a proper noun ('alam). The author specifically excludes pronouns, demonstratives, and customary references like 'anta'.
3
If not determined, test equal application
If the meaning applies to many individuals, ask: does it apply equally and uniformly to all? For example, 'insān' (human) applies equally to every human being. This is mutawāti' (univocal).
4
If not equal, classify as analogical
If the meaning applies to some individuals more primarily or more strongly, it is mushakkik (analogical). Example: 'wujūd' (existence) applies primarily to God, and secondarily to creatures.
Mushakkik terms are common in theology where one meaning has degrees.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed classification of nouns (asmā') based on their meaning and application. First, a noun may have one meaning or many. If one meaning, it may be a proper noun (علم) if it designates a specific individual (e.g., Zayd, Makka) and is not a pronoun, demonstrative, or customary reference. If the meaning is not fixed to one individual but applies to many, it is either متواطئ (univocal) if it applies equally to all individuals (e.g., 'human' applies equally to Zayd, Amr, etc.) or مشكك (analogical) if it applies more primarily to some (e.g., 'existence' applies to God primarily, to creatures secondarily). If it has many meanings, it is either مشترك (homonym) if coined for multiple meanings equally (e.g., 'ayn = eye, spring, spy), or منقول (transferred) if originally for one meaning and then moved to another. If the original meaning is retained alongside the new, the word becomes حقيقة (literal) for the original and مجاز (figurative) for the transferred sense, as in 'asad (lion) for the animal (literal) and the brave man (metaphorical).

Multiple-Meaning Nouns: Homonym, Transferred, and Literal/Metaphor
Classification of nouns with multiple meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between homonym (mushtarak) and transferred (manqūl) nouns, and identify the subtype of transferred terms (customary, religious, technical) and the literal/metaphor distinction when the original meaning is retained.

Core Idea
A noun with multiple meanings is either a homonym (coined for all equally) or transferred (moved from one meaning to another, possibly leaving the original).

When a noun has more than one meaning, the author asks whether it was originally coined for all those meanings equally (mushtarak) or if it was originally for one and later transferred to another (manqūl). The transferred category is further divided by the agent of transfer: general custom (urfī), religious law (shar'ī), or technical convention (istilāhī). If the original meaning is not abandoned, we have a pair: literal (haqīqa) for the original and metaphorical (majāz) for the transferred sense. This classification is vital for interpreting texts in Tafsir, Fiqh, and other disciplines.

Guided Path

Core Idea
A complete sentence is either true/false (khabar) or performative (inshā') – the first building block of logic.

In this passage, the author begins the logical analysis of language by dividing compound expressions. The first major division is between complete sentences that can stand alone and incomplete ones. Among complete sentences, the crucial logical distinction is whether they can be true or false. Objection on Judgment's Category and Resolutions
Objection about judgment's category and two resolutions, plus alternative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the objection that judgment cannot be an act of the soul while knowledge is a quality, and evaluate the two proposed resolutions.

Core Idea

A deeper philosophical objection arises: if judgment is an act (fi'l) of the soul, and knowledge is a quality (kayf), then how can tasdiq (which includes judgment) be a type of knowledge? Identify the objection's structure

2
Understand the first resolution
Redefine 'attribution' (isnad) as 'perception' (idrak) of the relation. Then judgment becomes a perception (a quality), solving the composition problem.

Understand the second resolution

Denotation by Entailment and Mental Necessity
Author defines denotation by entailment and stipulates mental entailment as a condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of denotation by entailment and verify whether the condition of mental entailment is met.

Core Idea
Denotation by entailment requires a mental necessity, not just any connection.

In the previous lessons, you learned about denotation by matching (mutabaqa) and by inclusion (tadammun). Now the author introduces the third type: denotation by entailment (iltizam). This type is crucial because it extends meaning beyond the literal placement. However, not every association counts—only those that are mentally necessary.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic passage: al-dalāla al-iltizāmiyyah (الدلالة الالتزامية). Notice that it is the third type of denotation.
2
Extract the core of the definition
The passage says it is 'denotation of a word to a meaning by the mediation of the placement' (دلالة اللفظ على المعنى بتوسط الوضع). This means the word is not placed directly for that meaning, but the connection comes through the placed meaning.
The 'mediation' is the placed meaning itself, which leads to the secondary meaning.
3
Identify the example and justify it
The example is 'human' (insān) denoting by entailment 'capable of knowledge' and 'craft of writing'. These are necessary consequences of being human, so they are entailed mentally.
4
Understand the condition of mental entailment
The author stipulates al-luzūm al-dhihnī: the secondary meaning must be automatically conceived whenever the primary meaning is in the mind. If not, it is not a valid entailment.
This condition prevents arbitrary or purely linguistic associations from being considered true logical entailments.

The Six Relations Among the Three Denotation Types
Author enumerates the six logical relations among mutabaqa, tadhammun, and iltizam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which of the three denotation types (mutabaqa, tadhammun, iltizam) entail each other and explain why, using examples from Arabic semantics.

Core Idea
Matching is foundational; inclusion and entailment both imply it, but they do not imply each other.

Words can denote meanings in three ways: by matching the whole meaning (mutabaqa), by including a part of it (tadhammun), or by entailing a necessary consequence (iltizam). Understanding how these three relate logically is crucial for precise interpretation of texts. This lesson maps out the six possible entailment relations among them.

Guided Path
1
Recall the three denotation types
Mutabaqa is denotation of the whole meaning (e.g., 'human' denotes the animal rationale). Tadhammun is denotation of a part (e.g., 'human' denotes 'animal'). Iltizam is denotation of something necessarily attached (e.g., 'human' denotes 'capable of knowledge').
2
Understand the entailment test
For each ordered pair (X, Y), ask: does X necessarily imply Y? For example, does tadhammun always imply mutabaqa?
Entailment here means logical necessity, not just probability.
3
Apply the test to all six pairs
Systematically check: (1) tadhammun → mutabaqa, (2) iltizam → mutabaqa, (3) mutabaqa → tadhammun, (4) mutabaqa → iltizam, (5) tadhammun → iltizam, (6) iltizam → tadhammun. The passage gives the results for each.
You may create a truth table.
4
Memorize the key conclusions
Inclusion and entailment both imply matching (1 and 2). Matching does not imply inclusion (3) because of simple terms like 'point'. Matching does not imply entailment (4) because some terms have no necessary consequence. Inclusion and entailment do not imply each other (5 and 6) because of composite/simple distinctions.
5
Note the nuance from al-Imam al-Razi
Al-Imam argued that every essence has at least the entailment of being not other than itself, which is a clear entailment in the general sense. The passage responds that denotation concerns the specific sense only, so matching does not entail entailment in that sense.
This shows how scholars may disagree on the scope of terms.

Commentary
This passage examines the logical relationships between the three types of denotation: mutabaqa (matching), tadhammun (inclusion), and iltizam (entailment). The Four Conditions of a Compound Word (Murakkab)
Defines compound via four conditions with example and objection response

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given expression is a logical compound by applying the four conditions and distinguishing it from grammatical compounds.

Core Idea
A compound word requires four conditions; if any is missing, it is singular.

In this passage, the author defines compound (murakkab) words that indicate by correspondence. The definition is precise: a word is compound only if it meets four specific conditions. Understanding these conditions helps avoid confusing logical compounds with grammatical ones—a common mistake. Let's walk through each condition and see how they apply to examples.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the four conditions
Look at the beginning of the passage: it states that a word is compound if it has (1) a part, (2) that part indicates a meaning, (3) that meaning is part of the overall intended meaning, and (4) the indication of that part is intended. List them clearly.
The passage lists these conditions in the objection paragraph and the reply.
2
Test the example 'rami al-sahm'
'Rami' is a part that indicates the archer; 'sahm' is a part that indicates the arrow. The archer and arrow are parts of the overall meaning of 'the archer of the arrow', and the indication is intended. Verify each condition.
This example shows all four conditions are met, so it is a compound.
3
Distinguish from grammatical compounds
Consider 'Abdullah' as a proper name. Grammatically it has two parts ('Abd' and 'Allah'), but logically it fails the third condition: the meanings (slave and Allah) are not intended as parts of the meaning of the person's name. Thus it is a logical singular.

A word's logical category is determined by its form's ability to predicate and indicate time.

The logician classifies single words not by grammar but by meaning: does the word intrinsically serve as a predicate? Does its morphological form mark a specific time? These two questions divide all mufradāt into three groups: adāt (particle), kalima (verb), and ism (noun). The grammarians may group words differently, but the logician's division cuts to the semantic essence.

Guided Path
1
Check predication suitability
Ask: can this word, by itself, function as a predicate in a proposition? If no, it is a particle (adāt). If yes, go to next step.
2
Check for time indication through morphological form
Does the word's pattern (like فَعَلَ or يَفْعَلُ) indicate a specific time (past, present, future)? If yes, it is a verb (kalima). If no, it is a noun (ism). Important: time indication must be through the morphological form, not through lexical meaning.
3
Verify with examples
Apply the criteria: لا (no) lacks predication suitability → adāt. ضَرَبَ (he struck) indicates past time via form → kalima. رَجُلٌ (man) does not indicate time via form → ism. غَداً (tomorrow) indicates time lexically, not morphologically → ism.
4
Remember the nuance
Not all Arabic verbs are kalimāt in logic. As Avicenna notes, first/second person present tense verbs are compound and admit truth/falsehood, so they are not kalima. This will be detailed in the next lesson.

Real and Existential Verbs and the Avicennian Refinement
Further division of kalima into real/existential and critique of compound verbs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real and existential verbs, and explain why first and second person present tense verbs are not considered kalimāt in logic.

Core Idea
Not every Arabic verb is a logical verb; composition determines logical status.

Within the category of kalima (logical verb), the author distinguishes two types: real verbs that denote both an event and a time, and existential verbs that denote only time. Moreover, Avicenna famously refines the definition: first and second person present tense verbs, though grammatically verbs, are compound in logic because they include an implied pronoun and can be true or false, thus they are not kalimāt. This lesson unpacks these distinctions and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of kalima
Real kalima (حقيقية) indicates both an event (ḥadath) and a time. Existential kalima (وجودية) indicates only time (e.g., kāna). Note that 'being' (kawn) is not an event; an event is a meaning attributed to the agent as subsisting in it.
2
Apply to examples
ḍaraba = real (event + past time). kāna = existential (past time only, no event). What about 'qāma' (he stood)? It indicates a standing event → real.
3
Examine the shaykh's critique
Avicenna points out that first and second person present tense (e.g., aḍribu, taḍribu) are Arabic verbs but not logical verbs because they contain a pronoun, making them compound propositions that can be true or false.
4
Contrast with third person present
Third person present (yaḍribu) is a kalima by agreement because it is simple (no pronoun) and does not admit truth/falsehood in itself. The explicit verbal noun (ṣūrat al-taṣrīḥ) is also simple but only predicates with its agent.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A division of a division is not a division of the original.

This passage presents a dialectical exchange: an objector argues that majaz (metaphorical speech) should be considered a type of signification by correspondence (dal bil mutabaqa) because it is a subdivision of a subdivision. The author refutes this by exposing a logical error. Understanding this refinement helps you avoid misclassifying terms in hierarchical divisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's chain of divisions

2

The author disagrees and provides a counterexample: 'Animal is either white or non-white; white is either animal or non-animal.' The non-animal is a division of white, but it is not a division of animal.

The principle 'a division of a division is a division' is not automatically transitive. The categories must be consistent. Here, the subdivision 'non-animal' under 'white' does not become a subdivision of 'animal' because 'white' is not a division of 'animal' in the same way.

Methodology for Comparing Modal Propositions: Materials vs. Concepts
Methodological note on how to compare modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between considering relations based on materials (mawadd) versus concepts (mafahim) when comparing modal propositions.

Core Idea
When comparing propositions, we consider their truth in themselves, not as predicates of each other.

The author concludes with a methodological clarification about how to properly consider relations between propositions. He warns against an objection that propositions cannot be true of each other, and explains that if relations are considered based on the materials (the actual truth values of the modal types), then such relations are not valid; but if considered based on concepts (the definitions), then compatibility can exist. This subtle point is crucial for avoiding confusion in logical classification.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The author anticipates an objection: how can we talk about relations (like one proposition being more general than another) when propositions themselves cannot be true or false of each other? For example, the statement 'the specific possible is more general than the temporal' is a metalinguistic relation, not a propositional one.
2
Distinguish two perspectives
The author distinguishes two ways to consider relations: by the materials (mawadd) of the modalities—i.e., the actual truth values of propositions in specific concrete cases—and by the concepts (mafahim) of the modalities themselves.
3
Apply the material perspective
When considering by materials, we compare whether one type of proposition is true whenever another is true. In that case, the relations (like generality) are not about the propositions themselves but about the sets of concrete judgments. The author notes that in this sense, propositions cannot be true of each other, so the objection arises.
4
Apply the conceptual perspective
When considering by concepts, we compare the definitions or natures of the modal types. For example, the 'general possible' concept is broader than the 'specific possible' concept because the former includes cases where possibility is not actual. In this conceptual sense, the relations are legitimate, and truth-value compatibility (tasaduq) can occur.
The author encourages deep reflection: 'تأمل، ثم تأمل'.

Refining the Definition of Qiyas: Conditions and Objections
Author clarifies conditions of qiyas, addresses objections about premise actuality, and explains why it is called qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three essential conditions of a valid qiyas (syllogism) and apply them to evaluate whether a given argument form qualifies as a qiyas.

Core Idea
A syllogism is more than premises: it requires a distinct conclusion, a specific form, and no extraneous support.

In this passage, the author refines the classical definition of qiyas by adding critical conditions. He addresses common objections, such as whether a single proposition like 'Everything changing is originated' can be a syllogism, and explains why the form of composition is indispensable. By the end, you will be able to apply these conditions to test whether any argument truly qualifies as a qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
From the passage, extract the conditions: (1) the conclusion must be distinct from both premises, (2) the conclusion must be necessitated by the premises themselves (li dhatihi, not via extraneous premise), and (3) the specific form (hay'a) of composition is essential for production.
These conditions exclude tautologies, analogies, and arguments with suppressed premises.
2
Examine the problematic categorical example
The author gives 'Every C is B and every B is B, so every C is B'. The conclusion is not truly distinct because it repeats one premise. This fails the first condition. Also, the form is defective.
Verify why it is not a valid qiyas according to the conditions.
3
Analyze the objection on actual vs potential premises
Someone objects: if 'propositions' means actual premises, then 'Everything changing is originated' would not be a qiyas (only one premise). But scholars accept it as a qiyas with a suppressed minor premise. The author concludes that the definition includes potential premises.
This shows the definition must be flexible enough to cover enthymemes.
4
Understand the etymology of qiyas
The author explains that qiyas is so called because it makes the unknown conclusion equal in cognizability to the premises. This ties back to the purpose of syllogism: to produce knowledge.

ولم يؤنث الضمير؛ ليعود إلى القضايا؛ لينبه بذلك على أن لهيئة التأليف دخلا في الإنتاج، وأن المطلوب لا يحصل من تلك القضايا إلا مع الهيئة المخصوصة.
وقوله: (لذاته) احتراز عما يلزمه قول آخر بواسطة مقدمة أجنبية، أو بواسطة مقدمة في قوة المذكورة.
وقوله: (قول آخر) أي مغاير لكل واحدة من المقدمتين إشارة إلى وجوب مغايرة النتيجة لكل من المقدمتين، وإلا لزم أن يكون كل قضيتين فرضتا قياسا، وليس بقياس، والمقدمة في قولنا: إن كان أ ب فج د، ولكن أب فج د، ليست ج د، بل لزومه لأب.
وفي قولنا: كل ج [؟] وكل ب ب فكل ج ب) ليست ج ب، بل ب هو موصوف بوصف خصوصية التأليف فلا يرد النقض مع أن الحق أن الأخير ليس بقياس.
ولقائل أن يقول: المراد من القضايا القضايا بالفعل أو الأعم، فإن أريد الأول لزم ألا يكون نحو قولنا: كل متغير حادث قياسا، وقد أجمعوا على أنه قياس محذوف الصغرى، وإن أريد الثاني لزم أن يكون القضية المركبة بالقياس إلى العكس قياسا؛ لأنها وإن كانت قضية واحدة بالفعل؛ لأنها اتحدت بالتركيب بحيث لا يطلق عليها بعد التركيب إلا قضية، لكنها قضايا بالقوة.
واعلم أنه إنما سمي القياس قياسا لأنه جعل فيه هيئة النتيجة المجهولة مساوية للمقدمتين في المعلومية.
(۱) -ط: (هيئة).
The incomplete and [the analogical], for their premises if accepted do not necessitate another thing; because they are conjectural and their implications can be contrary. And the pronoun was not made feminine to refer back to the propositions, to alert that the form of composition has an influence on production, and that the sought result is not obtained from those propositions except with the specific form. And His statement: (for itself) is a proviso against what necessitates another statement by means of an extraneous premise, or by means of a premise that is in the power of the mentioned one. And His statement: (another statement) means different from each one of the two premises, indicating the necessity of the conclusion being different from each of the two premises, otherwise every two premises assumed would be a syllogism, and it is not a syllogism, and the premise in our statement: 'If A B then C D, but A B, so C D', is not 'C D', but rather its implication from 'A B'. And in our statement: 'Every C is B, and every B is B, so every C is B' is not 'C B', but rather B is described with the description of the specificity of the composition, so the refutation does not apply, even though the truth is that the latter is not a syllogism. And it may be said: The intended from 'propositions' is actual propositions or the general? If the first is intended, then a statement like 'Everything changing is originated' would not be a syllogism, and they have agreed that it is a syllogism with the minor premise omitted. And if the second is intended, then the compound proposition with respect to conversion would be a syllogism; because even though it is one proposition actually, because it is unified by composition such that after composition nothing but a proposition is called it, yet it is propositions potentially. And know that it is only called 'qiyas' because the form of the unknown conclusion is made equal to the two premises in cognizability. (1) -T: (form).

Commentary
This passage is an expansion and clarification of the definition of qiyas (syllogism) given earlier. The author begins by distinguishing incomplete qiyas (al-ghayr al-tamm) and analogical reasoning (al-tamthil) from proper qiyas, noting that even if their premises are accepted, they do not necessitate a new conclusion because they are conjectural and their implications can fail. Then the author discusses the pronoun in the definition: it was not made feminine (despite referring to plural propositions) intentionally to emphasize that the form (hay'a) of composition has a role in production, and the desired result is only obtained with that specific form. Next, the author explains the phrase 'li dhatihi' (for itself) in the definition, stating it excludes conclusions that require an extraneous premise or a premise that is potentially present. The phrase 'qawl akhar' (another statement) is then clarified: it must be different from each premise; otherwise any two premises could be considered a syllogism. The author gives an example of a conditional syllogism ('If A B then C D, but A B, so C D') and shows that the premise 'C D' is not the conclusion, but rather the implication from 'A B' is the conclusion. A problematic syllogism 'Every C is B and every B is B, so every C is B' is discussed; the conclusion is not really 'C B' but the predicate B is described by the form, so it is not a valid syllogism. Then an objection is raised: does 'propositions' in the definition mean actual propositions or general (including potential)? If actual, then 'Everything changing is originated' would not be a qiyas, but scholars agree it is a qiyas with a suppressed minor premise. If general, then a compound proposition relative to its converse (like 'If A then B', when converted) would be a qiyas, even though it is actually one proposition but potentially multiple. Finally, the reason for the name 'qiyas' is given: because the form of the unknown conclusion is made equal to the two premises in terms of being known. This section refines the definition to avoid loopholes and clarify the conditions of validity.
