The Four Relations Between Universals
Author classifies four relations between two universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the four logical relations between two universals (mutasāwiyān, ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ muṭlaq, ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh, tabāyun) and give an example of each from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Four relations define how universals overlap — a key to precise definition and argument.

In this lesson, we explore the four possible logical relations between two universals: coextensive, absolute generality/specificity, partial overlap, and mutual exclusion. These relations are essential for understanding definitions and for constructing valid arguments in Islamic sciences, especially in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four relation types
Note the Arabic terms: mutasāwiyān (coextensive), ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ muṭlaq (absolute generality/specificity), ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh (generality/specificity in some respect), tabāyun (mutual exclusion). Each is defined by how the extensions of two universals relate.
2
Compare coextension and absolute generality
Coextension (mutasāwiyān) means each applies to exactly the same individuals, like 'man' and 'rational'. Absolute generality means one is broader and includes all of the other, but not vice versa, like 'animal' and 'man'.
In absolute generality, the broader universal is 'absolute' and the narrower is 'specific'.
3
Distinguish partial overlap from mutual exclusion
Partial overlap (min wajh) means each applies to some individuals of the other but neither covers all, e.g., 'animal' and 'white'. Mutual exclusion (tabāyun) means no common individuals, e.g., 'animal' and 'tree'.
4
Apply to Islamic examples
Consider 'muslim' and 'mu'min' – are they coextensive? In Islamic theology, all mu'min are muslim but not necessarily vice versa? Actually in Quranic usage, they are often coextensive but can be distinguished. Use examples from fiqh: 'salah' and 'sajdah' are not coextensive; 'salah' includes 'sajdah' but not all 'sajdah' are 'salah'.

Commentary
This passage defines the four possible relationships between two universals. The first is coextension (tasāwī): each applies to exactly the same extension, e.g., 'man' and 'rational' (all men are rational and all rational beings are men, in the medieval understanding). The second is absolute generality and specificity (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ muṭlaq): one universal is broader and includes the other, but not vice versa, e.g., 'animal' is predicated of all men, but 'man' is not predicated of all animals. The third is generality and specificity in some respect (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh): each applies to some of what the other applies to, but neither encompasses all, e.g., 'animal' applies to some white things (e.g., a white horse) and 'white' applies to some animals, but not all animals are white and not all white things are animals. The fourth is mutual exclusion (tabāyun): the two have no common members, e.g., 'animal' and 'tree' share no individuals. These relations are fundamental for determining logical subordination and for constructing definitions.

Two Senses of 'Particular': Real and Relative
Defines the particular in two senses: real (individual) and relative (the more specific relative to the more general).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a real particular (juzʾī ḥaqīqī) and a relative particular (juzʾī iḍāfī) and identify which sense applies in a given logical context.

Core Idea
A particular is either a unique individual or a universal that is more specific than another.

In this lesson, we clarify two meanings of 'particular' (juzʾī) used in Islamic logic. The first is the real particular, which cannot be predicated of more than one thing, like Zayd. The second is the relative particular, which is a universal considered more specific relative to another universal. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly analyzing definitions and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
From the passage, extract the definition of real particular (juzʾī ḥaqīqī) and relative particular (juzʾī iḍāfī). Real particular is an individual that is not predicated of many. Relative particular is a universal that is under a more general universal.
2
Understand the criterion
Real particularity is absolute – it cannot be shared. Relative particularity is relational – it depends on being more specific than something else.
3
Apply the concepts with 'man'
Take the example of 'man'. Is it a real particular? No, because it is predicated of many individuals (Zayd, Amr, etc.). Is it a relative particular? Yes, when compared to 'animal' because it is more specific.
4
Recognize relativity
The same universal can be particular in one relation and general in another. For example, 'man' is particular relative to 'animal' but general relative to 'Zayd'. This shows that particularity and generality are not fixed properties of a concept alone.
5
Test with other examples
Consider 'animal' relative to 'body' or 'living being'. Determine in each case whether it is a real or relative particular. This reinforces the relational nature of relative particularity.

This passage clarifies two uses of 'particular' (juzʾī). The first is the real particular (juzʾī ḥaqīqī): an individual that cannot be predicated of many, like a specific person. The second is the relative particular (juzʾī iḍāfī): a universal that is more specific than another universal, e.g., 'man' is a particular relative to 'animal' (since man is under animal). Thus particularity is relative: the same universal can be particular in one relation and general in another. For example, 'man' is particular relative to 'animal' but general relative to 'Zayd'.

Transferred Meaning (Manqul): Religious and Conventional
Defines types of transferred meanings and the distinction between literal and metaphorical usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given transferred term as either religious (manqul shar'i) or conventional (manqul istilahi) based on who performed the transfer.

Core Idea
Who transfers the meaning determines the type: the Lawgiver or the experts.

In this passage, the author defines 'transferred meaning' (manqul) and divides it into two types: religious and conventional. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting terms in Islamic law and grammar. The example of 'prayer' (salat) shows how a word can be moved from its original supplication meaning to a specific ritual. Let's explore the conditions and examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original meaning
Look for the original signification of the word before transfer. For example, 'salat' originally meant supplication.
2
Determine the transferring authority
Check if the transfer was made by the Lawgiver (shar') or by a specific custom (like grammarians).
3
Classify accordingly
If the Lawgiver, it is manqul shar'i; if a specific custom, it is manqul istilahi.

Literal and Metaphorical Meaning (Haqiqa and Majaz)
Defines types of transferred meanings and the distinction between literal and metaphorical usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a word is used literally (haqiqa) or metaphorically (majaz) based on its original coining and the presence of a relation.

Core Idea
Haqiqa is the original meaning; majaz is a transferred meaning with a relation.

After discussing transferred terms, the author turns to how a word can be used both in its original meaning (haqiqa) and in a secondary meaning (majaz). The example of 'lion' (asad) for a brave man illustrates the relation of courage. This distinction is essential for understanding language use in religious texts and everyday speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original meaning
Determine the meaning for which the word was first coined. For 'asad', it was the predatory animal.
2
Check current usage
Does the current usage match the original? If yes, it is haqiqa. If not, proceed.
3
Find the relation
If the current usage differs, identify the relation (e.g., resemblance, causality) between the original and current meanings. If there is a relation, it is majaz.

Synonymy (Mutaradif) and Opposition (Mubayin) in Words
Defines synonymy and opposition based on conceptual agreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between synonymous (mutaradif) and opposite (mubayin) word pairs based on agreement or disagreement in conceptual meaning (al-wasf al-'unwani), using examples from Arabic.

Core Idea
Two words are synonymous if they share a concept, opposite if they differ — regardless of whether their essences are the same.

The passage classifies relationships between two utterances. After discussing how a single word relates to its meaning, the author now examines how words relate to each other. This classification is foundational for understanding definition and dispute in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion for synonymy
Two words are synonymous (mutaradif) if they agree in the attributive description (al-wasf al-'unwani), i.e., the conceptual meaning. Note that identity of essence is not required.
Unity in essence (al-ittihad bi al-dhat) is explicitly excluded.
2
Identify the criterion for opposition
Two words are opposite (mubayin) if they disagree in the attributive description, regardless of whether their essences are identical or different.
3
Apply the criteria to examples
Recognize why 'ghayth' and 'matar' are synonymous (same concept 'rain') while 'insan' and 'natiq' are opposite (different concepts even though referring to the same essence).
The passage gives these examples explicitly.
4
Contrast with common misunderstanding
Synonymy does not require that the words refer to the same essence in every respect; only the conceptual meaning must match.
This avoids confusion with terms that are co-referential but conceptually distinct.
