The Five Universals: Essential and Accidental Categories
Defines the five universals and their subdivisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, general accident) in a given definition and classify it as essential or accidental.

Core Idea
The five universals are the building blocks of every definition in Islamic logic.

Every concept we reason with is either particular (juz'ī) or universal (kullī). The five universals classify all universal predicates into those that form the essence of a thing (genus, species, differentia) and those that are accidental (property, general accident). This lesson equips you to recognize each type and understand its role in logical definitions.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish Particular from Universal
A concept is particular if it can only refer to one individual (e.g., Zayd). A concept is universal if it can refer to many (e.g., human). Identify whether the concept you are given is particular or universal before proceeding.
This is the foundational divide.
2
Identify the Species (Naw')
The species is the universal that gives the complete essence of its particulars. It answers 'what is it?' and applies to many that share the same reality. For example, 'human' is the species for Zayd and 'Amr. Check if the concept describes the entire essence.
3
Identify the Genus (Jins)
The genus is part of the essence that is shared across different species. It also answers 'what is it?' but is broader. For example, 'animal' is the genus for human and horse. Determine if it is proximate (closest genus) or distal (more remote).
Proximate genus: 'animal' for human. Distal genus: 'body' for human.
4
Identify the Differentia (Fasl)
The differentia is the part of the essence that distinguishes the species from other species in the same genus. It answers 'what thing is it in its reality?' For human, 'rational' is the differentia. Check if it distinguishes within a proximate or distal genus.
5
Classify External Attributes as Property or General Accident
Attributes outside the essence are either necessary (inseparable) or separate. If exclusive to one species, it is property (khāṣṣa), e.g., 'capable of laughter' for humans. If not exclusive, it is general accident ('araḍ 'āmm), e.g., 'walking on two legs'. Determine exclusivity.
Property and general accident are accidental universals.

Commentary
This passage is the foundational lesson on the five universals (al-kulliyyāt al-khams), which are the essential tools for constructing definitions (ḥadd) and descriptions (rasm). It begins by distinguishing particular (juz'ī) and universal (kullī): a particular cannot be predicated of more than one (like Zayd), while a universal can (like 'human'). The universal that gives the complete essence of its particulars is species (naw'). For example, 'human' is the species of Zayd and 'Amr because it answers 'what is it?' and applies to all humans. The universal that enters into that essence but is not equal is genus (jins). It is predicated on many different species in answer to 'what is it?'. Genus is divided into proximate (qarīb) and distal (ba'īd). Proximate genus answers both the species and all coordinate species with the same word, e.g., 'animal' answers for human and horse. Distal genus is broader, e.g., 'growing body' includes plants as well, so the answer for human is not the same as for a plant. The universal that enters into the essence equally with the species is differentiate (faṣl). It distinguishes the species from others sharing the same genus. Proximate differentiate distinguishes from species under the proximate genus (e.g., 'rational' for human vs. horse under 'animal'), while distal differentiate distinguishes from species under a distal genus. Then the passage discusses accidents: necessary (lāzim) attributes that are inseparable from the thing, like blackness for an Abyssinian (for existence) or evenness for two (for essence). Necessary can be clear (bayyin) requiring no proof, like individuality for the one, or unclear (ghayr bayyin) requiring proof, like temporality for the world. Separate accidents ('araḍ mufāriq) are separable, either quickly (blush) or slowly (love). Finally, of all these attributes, if an attribute is exclusive to a single species, it is property (khāṣṣa), defined as a universal predicated accidentally only on that species, e.g., 'capable of laughter' for humans. If it is not exclusive, it is general accident ('araḍ 'āmm), like 'walking on two legs' which applies to humans and some birds. Every concept in logic—from terms to propositions to syllogisms—rests on this classification, so it must be memorized precisely.

Real and Relative Species: The Two Senses of al-Nawʿ
Defines species in two senses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real species (nawʿ ḥaqīqī) and relative species (nawʿ iḍāfī) and provide an example of each from the hierarchy of substances.

Core Idea
A species is not just a lowest rung; it is also any level viewed as a species relative to a higher genus.

The term 'species' (nawʿ) can be used in two distinct ways. The first, real species, is the lowest species that has no further species under it, like 'man'. The second, relative species, applies to any universal that can be considered a species in relation to a higher genus, even if it itself is a genus for something else. This distinction is crucial for understanding the hierarchical order of universals in logic and its application in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
In the passage, the author first defines real species (نوع حقيقي) as what is predicated of its instances and is the lowest level. Then he defines relative species (نوع إضافي) as any quiddity that is called, together with others, a genus in answer to 'what is it?' in a primary sense. Notice that both are called 'species' but in different senses.
2
Find the distinguishing feature
Real species is the final node in the hierarchy; it has no species below it. Relative species is any node that can be viewed from below as a species, even if it has species under it when viewed from above.
Example: 'Animal' is a relative species because it is under 'body', but is a genus for 'man'.
3
Apply to the examples
Classify 'man', 'animal', 'body', 'substance' into real or relative species. 'Man' is a real species. 'Animal' and 'body' are relative species. 'Substance' is not a relative species because it has no higher genus, but it fits the definition if considered from a perspective? The passage's definition requires a higher genus, so substance is not a relative species.
The passage later gives 'intellect' as a unique species if substance is its genus, showing how context affects classification.

The Four Ranks of Species and Genus: Building the Hierarchy
Enumerates four ranks of species and four ranks of genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the four ranks of species and the four corresponding ranks of genus, and correctly place any universal into its appropriate rank.

Core Idea
From highest to unique: the ranks of species and genus mirror each other in a complete hierarchy.

After distinguishing the two senses of species, the author presents a systematic enumeration of four ranks each for species and genus. These ranks form a complete hierarchical grid: from the highest to the lowest, plus a unique category that falls outside the linear order. Understanding this grid is essential for mastering the Porphyrian tree used in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four ranks of species
The passage says species ranks are four: the most general (aʿamm, e.g., body), the most specific (aʿkhaṣṣ, e.g., man, called species of species), the intermediate (e.g., animal), and the unique (e.g., intellect if substance is its genus). Note the criteria: generality and specificity.
2
Identify the four ranks of genus
Similarly, genus ranks are four: the highest (jins al-ajnās, e.g., substance), the lowest (e.g., animal), the intermediate (e.g., growing body), and the unique (e.g., intellect if substance is not its genus).
The unique type occurs when a universal does not fit into the linear hierarchy.
3
Map the correspondence
Notice that 'animal' appears as an intermediate species and also as a lowest genus. This shows that many universals have a dual role, being species relative to higher terms and genera relative to lower terms.
4
Apply to a new example
Given the hierarchy: substance → body → living body → animal → man, assign each to the appropriate rank of species and genus. For example, 'living body' is an intermediate genus and also a relative species.

The Four Relations Between Two Universal Terms
Author enumerates the four possible relations between two universal terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four relations between two universal terms (equality, absolute generality, generality from one aspect, disparity) and correctly classify a given pair.

Core Idea
Four relations define how universal terms relate: equality, absolute generality, partial overlap, and complete disparity.

In logic, universal terms (kullī) do not exist in isolation—they stand in relation to one another. The author of al-Mīzān classifies these relations into four types. Mastering this classification is essential for constructing correct definitions and analyzing syllogistic reasoning, because the validity of an argument often depends on how its terms are related.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four relations
Read through the passage and list the four types: equality (tasāwī), absolute generality (ʿumūm muṭlaq), generality from one aspect (ʿumūm min wajh), and disparity (tabāyun).
2
Understand the defining condition for each
For each relation, note the condition given: equality requires mutual inclusion; absolute generality requires one-way inclusion; generality from one aspect requires partial overlap; disparity requires no overlap.
3
Apply the definitions to the examples
Check how the examples—human/rational, animal/human, animal/white, animal/tree—satisfy the conditions. This will internalize the pattern.
4
Test with new pairs
Think of other pairs from Islamic sciences (e.g., 'wine' and 'intoxicant') and determine which relation they fall under.

Commentary
This passage classifies the four possible relations between two universal terms. The author begins with equality (tasāwī), where each term applies to exactly the same individuals, exemplified by 'human' and 'rational' — every human is rational and every rational is human. Next is absolute generality and specificity (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ muṭlaq), where one term (the more general) applies to everything the other applies to, but not vice versa, as 'animal' applies to everything 'human' applies to but not the reverse. Then is generality and specificity from one aspect (ʿumūm wa khuṣūṣ min wajh), where each term applies to some individuals the other does not, like 'animal' (applies to non-white animals) and 'white' (applies to white non-animals) overlapping in white animals. Finally, disparity (tabāyun), where no individual is common to both, like 'animal' and 'tree'. This classification is fundamental for understanding the relationships between concepts in definition and syllogistic reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define universal and particular concepts using the two conditions (tasawwur and nafs) and distinguish them in Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Universality is a property of conception, not external existence.

In this passage, the author lays the foundation for all further logical analysis by defining what it means for a concept to be universal or particular. The definitions are precise and include two key restrictions to avoid common misunderstandings. Understanding these definitions is essential for working with the five universals and for applying logic in Islamic jurisprudence and theology.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the notion of 'concept' (mafhūm)
The author equates 'meaning' (ma'na) and 'concept' (mafhūm) as the mental form obtained in the intellect. Every concept is a mental representation, regardless of whether it exists externally.
This is foundational for the definitions that follow.
2
Extract the definition of 'particular' (juz'ī)
A concept is particular if its very conception prevents the sharing of many in it. That is, simply by conceiving it, you understand it cannot apply to more than one individual, like the concept 'Zayd'.
The phrase 'very conception' (nafs tasawwuruhu) is crucial.
3
Extract the definition of 'universal' (kullī)
A concept is universal if its very conception does not prevent sharing among many. The intellect can suppose it true of multiple individuals, e.g., 'human' or 'animal'.
No external proof is needed; it's a matter of conception.
4
Analyse the need for both restrictions
The restriction 'conception' cuts off external existence; 'very' cuts off proof. If only one were used, definitions could be violated. For example, a concept that actually exists only once but could be conceived as shared would be universal by the definition.
Both ensure the definitions are coextensive.

Why Two Restrictions: Tasawwur and Nafs
Author explains why both tasawwur and nafs are necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinct roles of the 'conception' and 'very' restrictions in the definitions of universal and particular, and construct counterexamples that would arise if either were omitted.

Core Idea
Conception ignores reality; 'very' ignores proof — both are indispensable.

The author stresses that the definitions of universal and particular require two qualifications: 'conception' (tasawwur) and 'very' (nafs). Each plays a distinct role in avoiding loopholes. This lesson explores the precise function of each restriction and why neither alone is sufficient. Understanding this precision is critical for applying the definitions in rigorous scholarly work.

Guided Path
1
Identify what 'conception' (tasawwur) excludes
The restriction 'conception' means we disregard actual external existence. A concept may exist only once in reality but still be universal if the mind can conceive it applying to many. For example, 'the sun' is unique but its concept can be shared hypothetically.
This avoids confusing ontological uniqueness with logical universality.
2
Identify what 'very' (nafs) excludes
The restriction 'very' means we disregard the need for proof. Even if a concept's sharing requires a demonstration (like a geometrical property), as long as the concept itself does not bar sharing in conception, it is universal. Only if the very act of conceiving it prevents sharing is it particular.
This avoids making universality dependent on argumentation.
3
Construct a counterexample if only 'conception' is used
If only 'conception' were used, a concept that actually exists only once but can be conceived as multiple (e.g., 'Zayd' as a concept of a person) might mistakenly be called particular, but according to 'very', it is universal (the concept of Zayd can be shared in thought). Actually, Zayd is particular, so another example: 'the Prophet' concept is unique in reality but universal in conception? The text's point is that both are needed to avoid violations.
The text states: 'فالتقييد بالتصور يفيد قطع النظر عن الخارج، والتقييد بالنفس يفيد قطع النظر عن البرهان'.
4
Construct a counterexample if only 'very' is used
If only 'very' were used, a concept that requires a proof to be shared (like a complex theological concept) might be considered particular if no proof is given, but it is not particular by nature. The example is abstract; the key is that both conditions together fix the definition.
The author says neither suffices without the other.

The Universal as Part of the Particular
Author discusses the part-whole relationship between universal and particular and addresses the predication objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the claim that the universal is a part of the particular and address the objection that a part is not predicated of the whole.

Core Idea
The universal is a logical part of the particular, but predication is not whole-part inclusion.

The passage introduces an interesting ontological relationship: the universal is often a part of the particular, making the particular a whole. This raises an objection: if the universal is a part, how can it be predicated of the whole? For example, 'animal' is a part of 'human', yet we say 'human is an animal'. The author begins a response that is cut off, but we can reconstruct the standard logical answer. This lesson examines this puzzle and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claimed relationship
The author states that the universal is often a part of the particular. For instance, the universal 'animal' is a part of the particular 'human' (since 'human' is composed of 'animal' plus 'rational'). Thus the particular is a whole, and the universal is a part.
This is an ontological claim about inclusion.
2
Identify the objection
The objection: A part is not predicated of the whole. For example, the ceiling is a part of the house, but we do not say 'the house is a ceiling'. Similarly, if 'animal' is a part of 'human', how can we predicate 'animal' of 'human'?
This seems contradictory.
3
Formulate the response (implied in the tradition)
The people of this craft (logicians) respond by distinguishing between physical parts and logical parts. The universal is not a physical component; it is a logical constituent that is actually predicated of the particular. Predication is not based on part-whole inclusion in the physical sense but on essential attribution. The universal is the genus or differentia, which are said of the species or individual.

Commentary
This passage defines the foundational concepts of universal (kulli) and particular (juz'i). It begins by establishing that 'meaning' (ma'na) and 'concept' (mafhum) are identical in essence – both refer to the mental form (sura haasila fi al-'aql). The author then states that universality and particularity are primary attributes of the concept itself, as opposed to singularity (ifrad) and composition (tarkib) which are incidental to expressions. Thus, he divides the concept as such into universal and particular. The definition: a concept is particular if its very conception (nafs tasawwuruhu) prevents sharing among many. 'Sharing' means that the concept can be intellectually applied to multiple individuals; Zayd is the classic example of a particular. The author carefully specifies 'very conception' to exclude external considerations and proof. He then addresses an objection: if conception is defined as obtaining a form, then 'conception of the concept' would imply a second-order concept, leading to infinite regress. The reply is that the concept of the concept is identical to itself, like 'existence of existence'. For a universal, the definition is the negation: it does not prevent sharing. Both qualifications (conception and very) are necessary to avoid counterexamples. The author then explains 'sharing of many' as the intellect's ability to suppose the concept applies to many, thus including hypothetical universals (like a fictional species). An objection about hypothetical universals not being 'things' is answered by noting that 'thing' in the definition of conception is used in its broad linguistic sense, covering even non-existents (as per al-Qutbi's gloss). Finally, the passage discusses the ontological relationship: the universal is often a part of the particular, making the particular a whole. This leads to a paradox: if the universal is part of the particular, how can it be predicated of the whole (since a part is not predicated of the whole)? The author leaves the answer incomplete, but typically logicians respond that the predication is not one of whole-part but of an attribute inhering in the subject. The commentary emphasizes the precision of the definitions and the importance of both restrictions.

Proximate and Remote Genus Distinguished
Author defines proximate and remote genus using the answer to 'what is it?'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish proximate genus from remote genus by applying the criterion of whether the answer to 'what is it?' is the same or different for the species and its co-species.

Core Idea
Proximate genus gives one answer; remote genus needs intermediate steps.

When defining a species, the genus you place it in may be immediately above it or several levels higher. The passage introduces a clear test: ask 'what is it?' for the species and for another species sharing that genus. If the answer is identical, the genus is proximate; if different, it is remote. This distinction is crucial for constructing precise definitions (ḥadd).

Guided Path
1
Identify the species and potential genus
Take a species (e.g., human) and a candidate genus (e.g., animal). Also pick another species that shares that same genus (e.g., horse).
2
Apply the 'what is it?' question
Ask 'What is human?' and 'What is horse?' If the answer is the same (e.g., animal for both), the genus is proximate. If the answers differ (e.g., 'growing body' for human but 'animal' for horse), then the genus is remote.
3
Check consistency with all co-species
The criterion also applies when asking about the species and every other species sharing that genus. For proximate genus, the answer remains the same for all. For remote genus, at least one co-species gives a different answer.
The author illustrates with animal (proximate for human and horse) and growing body (remote for human).

Why Differentia Explicitly Requires 'Universal'
Author defines differentia as a universal internal and equal to the essence, and defends why 'kulli' is explicitly mentioned.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the differentia is explicitly described as a universal in its definition, contrasting with genus and species.

Core Idea
The differentia's definition explicitly includes 'universal' because 'predicated of something' alone does not guarantee universality.

In the previous lessons, we defined genus and species without explicitly mentioning 'kulli' (universal) because the phrase 'predicated of many' already implies universality. But when defining differentia, the author changes the wording to 'predicated of something', which is ambiguous. In this lesson, we will unpack why that change forces the explicit inclusion of 'kulli' and how the author defends this choice against a potential objection. Understanding this nuance sharpens our ability to evaluate definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of differentia
Locate the Arabic sentence where the author defines 'fasl' (differentia). Note its two key components: it is a universal that is internal to the essence and equal to the species. The example 'rational' for human is given.
The definition appears after the opening parentheses: 'al-kulli alladhi huwa (al-dakhil) fi mahiyyat ma tahtahu min al-juz'iyyat (al-musawi laha)...'
2
Compare the defining phrases for genus/species vs. differentia
Notice that in the definitions of genus and species, the author used 'al-sadiq 'ala kathirin' (true upon many). In the definition of differentia, he uses 'al-sadiq 'ala shay'' (true upon something). The first phrase already implies universality (many), while the second could also apply to a particular (one thing). This is why 'kulli' must be added explicitly to differentia.
3
Analyze the objection and the author's response
The objection argues: if 'al-sadiq 'ala shay'' is understood as 'predicate' (mahmul), then every predicate is actually universal, so the phrase would be equivalent to 'kulli' and the explicit mention would be unnecessary. The author responds by distinguishing between actual reality (al-waqi') and concept (al-mafhum). Even if in reality every predicate is universal, the concept of 'predicated of something' does not conceptually include universality, and definitions are based on concepts.
This is a key logical distinction: the same thing can be equivalent in reality but not in concept.
4
Apply the lesson to a real Islamic science example
Consider the definition of 'mujtahid' as 'one who exerts effort to derive rulings from the sources'. The differentia 'exerts effort' must be a universal property: it applies to any qualified jurist, not just one particular person. If the definition had said 'predicated of something', it might be mistaken for a label for a single individual (e.g., 'Imam Abu Hanifa'). Explicitly stating that it is a universal prevents that confusion.

Using 'Ayy' to Identify the Differentia
Author explains how the question 'ayy shay' huwa' isolates the differentia from other universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse a question using 'ayy shay' huwa' to determine whether the answer identifies a differentia or a mere property, based on the category requested.

Core Idea
The question 'ayy shay' huwa' isolates essential distinction, not accidental property.

When you ask 'What kind of [category] is this?' you are using 'ayy' to request something that distinguishes the thing from others sharing that category. The logician tells us that the answer must be a differentia or a distinctive property, but not a genus, species, or accident. Let's walk through how this question works and how to spot the differentia in the answer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category after 'ayy'
In the Arabic phrase 'ayy shay' huwa' or 'ayy [category] huwa', the word after 'ayy' specifies the category within which the distinction is sought. For example, in 'man, what animal is he?' the category is 'animal'.
The category determines what the differentia must distinguish from.
2
Determine if the answer gives essential or accidental distinction
If the answer gives a characteristic that defines the essence of the thing (e.g., 'rational' for man), it is a differentia (fasl). If it gives a distinctive but non-essential property (e.g., 'laughing' for man), it is a property (khassa) and provides accidental distinction only.
Essential distinction is tamyiz dhati; accidental is tamyiz 'aradi.
3
Exclude genus, species, and accident as answers
The passage says that 'ayy shay' huwa' excludes genus (jins), species (naw'), and accident ('arad) because they do not give a distinguishing attribute. For instance, answering 'animal' for 'man' does not distinguish man from other animals.
Genus as such is not distinguishing (lā yumayyizu aṣlan).
4
Apply the hierarchy of categories
The passage shows different levels: 'thing', 'body', 'growing body'. At each level, the differentia changes. For man as a body, the differentia is 'receptive of dimensions'; as a growing body, it adds 'growing'. This shows how narrowing the category refines the differentia.
The answer must be appropriate to the category level.

Implicational indication is not rigorous enough for logical definitions.

This shows the term 'sādiqa' is ambiguous.

The debate shows that to avoid reliance on implication, logicians explicitly include 'kullī' to ensure the definition is rigorous and unambiguous.
This principle applies to all definitions in Islamic logic.

Critique of the Relative Particular's Definition and Defense
Author critiques the definition of relative particular and defends it as a definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical problem of defining correlatives by each other and assess the validity of the defense.

Core Idea
Defining a correlative term using its correlate risks circularity, but some scholars treat it as a useful description.

In the previous lesson, you encountered the definition of the particular. Now the author turns to a specific critique: the definition of the 'relative particular' (al-juz'i al-idhafi) seems to rely on its correlate, the 'relative universal'. This raises a circularity problem. The author presents the objection and then a defense, and ultimately argues that the passage should be read as a definition. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse this debate and evaluate the strength of the arguments.

Guided Path
1
Locate the criticized definition
Find where the author mentions the definition of the relative particular. It involves the phrase 'something that is true of the mentioned meaning' and a comparison with the relative universal. Note that the definition appears to use the relative universal (the more general) to define the relative particular.
Look for the phrase 'فكذا' and the estimation given by the commentator.
2
Understand the objection
The objection: relative particular and relative universal are correlatives (mutadhaifan). One correlative cannot be part of the definition of the other because definition should be non-circular. Here, the relative universal is used, making it circular.
Correlatives are terms that imply each other, like father and son.
3
Examine the defense
The defense claims that the author's intention was not a strict definition but a ruling (hukm) from which the definition can be derived. So it is not a direct definition but a description that allows inference of the definition.
The defense argues: 'ليس مراده تعريف الجزئي الإضافي، بل ذكر حكم من أحكامه'.
4
Evaluate the author's counter-response
The author (the commentator) argues that other scholars (like the author of al-Qistas and the commentator of al-Isharat) treat it as a definition. Distorting it to something else is forced (ta'assuf). Thus, the author defends the view that it is indeed a definition despite the objection.
The author references 'صاحب القسطاس' and 'شارح الإشارات'.
5
Assess who has the stronger argument
Based on logical principles, is circularity always fatal? Consider that a definition can be non-circular if the correlate is used as a distinguishing feature rather than the genus. The debate hinges on whether the relative universal is being used as the genus or as part of the differentia.
Reflect on the distinction between hadd and rasm.

Real and Relative Species Defined
Author defines real species and relative species, clarifying their distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real species and relative species by analyzing their definitions and the exclusions in the passage.

Core Idea
The real species is defined by a single reality; the relative species is defined by having a genus.

In this passage, the author carefully defines two types of species—real and relative—and clarifies what each excludes. Understanding these definitions is crucial for correctly applying the five universals in any Islamic science that relies on precise definitions, such as fiqh or kalam. Let's unpack the logical distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of real species (al-naw' al-haqiqi)
Locate the statement: 'universal true of many agreeing in realities in answer to what is it'. This is the real species. Note the key element: the individuals share a single reality (e.g., all humans share the essence 'human').
The phrase 'in answer to what is it' means the species answers the question about the essence of the thing.
2
Identify the definition of relative species (al-naw' al-idhafi)
Find where it says: 'every essence upon which is predicated ... a genus absolutely in answer to what is it'. This is the relative species. It does not require a single reality; it merely requires that a higher genus can be predicated of it.
The relative species is relative because its 'specieshood' is only in relation to its genus.
3
Identify what is excluded from relative species
The passage excludes: (1) universals not subsumed under any genus (simple essences like the Necessary Being); (2) differentia, property, and common accident when considered relative to the genus; (3) the lower species (sinnf) because the genus is not predicated directly on it.
These exclusions prevent overextension of the term 'species'.
4
Analyze the relations between real and relative species
The passage gives three cases: both exist together (e.g., man), relative without real (e.g., body, an intermediate genus), and real without relative (e.g., simple essences like the point). Recognize each.
This shows the flexibility of the concepts across different levels of generality.

The Four Ranks of Relative Species
Author enumerates the four ranks of relative species: highest, lowest, middle, and singular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four ranks of relative species (highest, lowest, middle, singular) in a given chain of predication.

Core Idea
Relative species have four ranks based on their position in the predication chain: highest, lowest, middle, and singular.

In this lesson, we explore how the author of al-Mizan classifies relative species (النوع الإضافي) into four distinct ranks. Understanding these ranks is crucial for constructing correct definitions and grasping the hierarchical relationships between terms in logical analysis. The passage presents the four types: the highest species, the lowest species (also called species of species), the middle species, and the singular species which stands outside any chain.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the concept of relative species
Understand that a relative species can be a genus in relation to something lower and a species in relation to something higher.
This is in contrast to the true species (النوع الحقيقي) which has no such hierarchy.
2
Identify the chain of predication
Determine if the species belongs to a series where each term is predicated of the next in answer to 'what is it?'.
If there is a chain, proceed; otherwise the species may be singular.
3
Check if it is the most general
If above it there is only a genus and no species, it is the highest species (النوع العالي).
Example: absolute body (جسم مطلق) above which is only substance (جوهر), which is a genus.
4
Check if it is the most specific
If below it there are only individuals and no species, it is the lowest species (النوع السافل), also called species of species.
Example: man (إنسان) under which are only individual humans.
5
Check if it is between them
If it is more general than the lowest and more specific than the highest, it is a middle species (النوع المتوسط).
Example: animal (حيوان) which is above human and below body.

The Four Ranks of Genera and the Title 'جنس الأجناس'
Author lists the four ranks of genera and corrects a potential misunderstanding about naming.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four ranks of genera and correctly determine which genus merits the title 'جنس الأجناس'.

Core Idea
Genus-ness looks downward, so only the highest genus is called 'genus of genera'.

In the previous lesson, we learned about the ranks of species. Now the author applies the same classification to genera, listing four ranks. He then corrects a potential confusion: while the lowest species is called 'species of species', the lowest genus is not called 'genus of genera'. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly navigating hierarchies in definitions.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the four ranks
The passage lists four ranks of genera: the highest (al-ʿālī), the lowest (al-sāfil), the middle (al-mutawassiṭ), and the singular (al-mufrad). Identify these in the Arabic.
2
Spot the potential confusion
The author notes that one might mistakenly think the lowest genus, by analogy with the lowest species, is called 'genus of genera'. Recognize this error.
The confusion arises because the lowest species is called 'نوع الأنواع'.
3
Understand the correction
Genus-ness is relative to what is below it, so a genus is called 'genus of genera' only if it is above all other genera. The highest genus alone deserves this title.
Conversely, species-ness is relative to what is above, so the lowest species is 'نوع الأنواع'.
4
Apply the rule with examples
Using the example from the passage: 'al-Jawhar' (substance) is the highest genus, so it is 'جنس الأجناس'. 'al-Ḥayawān' (animal) is a lower genus and is not called that.
This rule prevents misnaming in hierarchical definitions.

And the ranks of genera are also four; because it is either the most general of the genera occurring in the chain, which is the highest, or the most specific of them, which is the lowest, or more general than the lowest and more specific than the highest, which is the middle, or distinct from all, which is the singular. Commentary
This passage parallels the previous discussion on species ranks by applying the same fourfold classification to genera (أجناس). The author states that genera also have four ranks: highest (العالي), lowest (السافل), middle (المتوسط), and singular (المفرد). The key nuance here is the naming convention: one might erroneously think that like the lowest species is called 'species of species' (نوع الأنواع), the lowest genus might be called 'genus of genera' (جنس الأجناس). The author corrects this by explaining that genus-ness is relative to what is below it, so the highest genus — which is above all other genera — is called 'genus of genera'. In contrast, species-ness is relative to what is above it, so the lowest species — which is under all other species — is called 'species of species'. For example, in the ranks of genera, 'substance' (الجوهر) is the highest genus and thus 'genus of genera', while 'animal' (الحيوان) is a lower genus, not the highest, so it does not get that title. This distinction prevents confusion between the hierarchy of genera and species.
