Theoretical Premises and the Example Syllogism
Author defines nazariyyat with a categorical syllogism example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the categorical syllogism used to acquire theoretical premises and identify its logical structure.

Core Idea
Theoretical premises are acquired by reasoning but become necessary through syllogistic proof.

The author defines theoretical premises (al-naẓariyyāt) as those acquired through reasoning, yet their proof is so evident that they resemble necessary premises. He illustrates this with a classic categorical syllogism from Islamic theology: the proof for the temporal origination of the world. In this lesson, we will break down that syllogism and understand how it transforms a theoretical claim into a necessary conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the theoretical premise being proved
The passage gives the conclusion: 'the world is temporally originated' (ḥādith). This is a theoretical claim that requires proof.
In Islamic theology, this premise is foundational for arguments for God's existence.
2
Extract the two premises of the syllogism
The minor premise: 'the world is changing' (mutaghayyir). The major premise: 'everything changing is temporally originated' (kull mutaghayyir ḥādith).
These are categorical propositions of the form 'All A are B' and 'All B are C'.
3
Identify the middle term
The middle term is 'changing' (mutaghayyir), which connects the subject 'world' to the predicate 'temporally originated'.
The middle term is the cause (ʿilla) in the syllogism – it explains why the conclusion follows.
4
Determine the figure and mood
This is a first-figure syllogism (Barbara): minor premise: all S is M, major premise: all M is P, conclusion: all S is P.
First figure yields certain conclusions directly without conversion.
5
Understand why this makes the premise 'necessary'
Al-Dasuqi explains that when the causal middle term is present in the mind, the judgment becomes as if necessary – like knowing 4 is even because it is divisible into equal parts.
This shows how a theoretical premise becomes certain through demonstration.

Division of Demonstrative Syllogisms: Limmi vs Inni
Author divides burhan into limmi and inni.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between limmi (causal) and inni (evidential) demonstrative syllogisms based on the role of the middle term.

Core Idea
In limmi, the middle term is the cause of the conclusion; in inni, it is merely a sign.

The author divides demonstrative syllogisms (burhan) into two fundamental types: limmi, where the middle term is the cause of the conclusion in reality and this is known in the mind, and inni, where the middle term is only evidence. This division is crucial for understanding the epistemological strength of different proofs. The subsequent commentary explores the precise meaning of 'with its causality' and how the middle term functions in each type.

Guided Path
1
Understand the division criterion
The division is based on whether the middle term is a cause (ʿilla) for the conclusion (the relation of the major to the minor) in reality, and whether this causality is known in the mind.
If both conditions hold, it is limmi; otherwise, it is inni.
2
Analyze the phrase 'with its causality'
The text says 'if the middle term is with its causality'. Commentators debate whether 'with' attaches to the middle term itself or to the causality. The correct reading is that the middle term is accompanied by its causality in the mind.
This ensures that the causal relationship is mentally apprehended, not just real.
3
Compare limmi and inni examples
A limmi example: 'Four is even because it is divisible into equal parts' – the middle term is the cause. An inni example: 'This is a house because there is smoke' – smoke is evidence, not the cause of the house.
In Islamic sciences, limmi is used for proofs from essence, inni for proofs from signs.
4
Examine the commentary chain
Al-Dasuqi adds that when the causality is present in the mind, the judgment becomes necessary. Al-‘Attar and al-Sharunubi analyze the syntax, showing how the phrase functions grammatically to clarify the logical point.
The grammatical analysis refines the definition, ensuring no ambiguity.

Division of Demonstrative Syllogism: Limmi and Inni
Author defines limmi and inni demonstrative syllogisms based on causality of middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between limmi and inni demonstrative syllogisms by analyzing the causal status of the middle term.

Core Idea
Limmi gives the 'why' in mind and reality; inni gives only the 'that'.

Demonstrative syllogisms are the gold standard of certainty in Islamic logic. But not all demonstrations are equal: some reveal the cause itself, others only a sign. In this passage, the author defines the two types—limmi (a priori) and inni (a posteriori)—based on whether the middle term is a real cause or merely an effect. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the strength of proofs in theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term in the example
In the example 'Zayd has putrefied humors; every putrefied-humor person is feverish,' the middle term is 'putrefied humors' because it connects Zayd to fever. Note that it appears in the minor premise as the predicate and in the major premise as the subject.
The middle term is the causal link.
2
Check causal status in the mind
The passage says the middle term is considered 'with its causality' for the relation in the mind. Here, 'putrefied humors' causes fever in our understanding: we know that putrefaction leads to fever. So it satisfies the mental causality condition.
All demonstrative syllogisms have mental causality.
3
Check causal status in reality
Now ask: does the middle term also cause the relation in external reality? In this example, putrefied humors are indeed a real cause of fever, not just a sign. So the middle term is a cause in both mind and reality.
If it were only a sign (e.g., fever indicates putrefaction but is not the cause), it would be inni.
4
Classify the syllogism
Since the middle term is a cause in both domains, the syllogism is limmi. It gives the 'why' (limmiyya) in both the intellect and external existence. If it were a cause only in the mind, it would be inni, giving only the 'that'.
This classification determines the epistemological value of the demonstration.

Distinguishing Burhan Inni from Burhan Limmi
Author introduces burhan inni with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish burhan inni from burhan limmi by analyzing the causal direction of the middle term in a categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
In burhan inni, the middle term is a sign, not a cause; in burhan limmi, it is the true cause.

This lesson introduces the distinction between two types of demonstration: inni (that) and limmi (why). Using the example of fever and putrid humors, the author shows how the same syllogism can be valid but the causal relationship differs in the mind versus external reality. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the strength of arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Extract the syllogism from the Arabic
Identify the three propositions: 'Zayd is feverish' (minor premise), 'Every feverish person has putrid humors' (major premise), and 'Zayd has putrid humors' (conclusion).
The Arabic reads: 'كَقولنا : زيد محموم»، وكلُّ محموم متعفّن الأخلاط»، «فزيدٌ متعفّنُ الأخلاط'.
2
Locate the middle term
The middle term is 'feverish' (محموم) because it appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. It connects Zayd to having putrid humors.
The author explicitly calls it 'الأوسط' (the middle).
3
Analyze the causal claim in the mind
The author states that the middle term is a cause for the conclusion in the mind—meaning it is used as a reason or sign to affirm that Zayd has putrid humors. In reasoning, we infer from the fever to the humors.
The text says: 'فإِنَّ الأوسط وهو «محموم»، وإنْ كانَ علَّةٌ لثبوتِ تعفن الأخلاط في الذهن'.
4
Contrast with external causality
The author then says that in external reality, the opposite is true: putrid humors are the cause of the fever. So the middle term (fever) is actually the effect, not the cause.
The text says: 'بل الأمر بالعكس' and Dassuqi clarifies: 'كَوْنُ تعفن الأخلاطِ علَّةٌ لِلحُمَّى'.
5
Classify the demonstration
Since the middle term is a sign (effect) rather than the real cause, this is a burhan inni (demonstration that something is the case). If the middle term were the real cause, it would be burhan limmi (demonstration why).
Burhan inni is also called 'دليل' (sign) in Arabic logic.

Accepted propositions are those taken from a person whose authority is trusted, such as a scholar or a saint. The author gives the example of 'a scholar or a saint', indicating that these figures are credible sources in rhetorical discourse. Probable propositions, on the other hand, are those believed with a preponderant probability but not certainty, like the example 'Every wall from which dust falls is collapsing.' This is a likely truth based on experience, but not absolutely certain. The purpose of such syllogisms is persuasion: to encourage people towards beneficial actions in ethics and worldly and religious affairs, as preachers and orators do. He states that for sound intellects, such propositions are innate and self-evident (fitriyyat), and even for weak intellects they can be demonstrated. Calling them probable is foolishness. This shows an important refinement: the classification of premises can vary based on the epistemic status of the source. The passage thus not only defines but also corrects a potential misunderstanding about the material of rhetorical syllogism.

The Non-Existent Subject Fallacy
Author explains a fallacy where the middle term's subject does not exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify fallacies in categorical syllogisms where the middle term or subject term lacks real existence, and contrast them with valid syllogisms using the same form.

Core Idea
A syllogism's validity depends on both form and material truth.

This passage from the Tadhheeb al-Mantiq commentary sharpens our understanding of syllogistic fallacy. The author demonstrates that a logically valid mood (third figure) can still produce a false conclusion if the subject term of its premises does not refer to anything real. Al-Dusūqī and al-Attār clarify the distinction between a non-existent composite subject and a genuine universal. By studying this fallacy, you will learn to check not only the form but also the material truth of premises—a skill essential in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacious syllogism
Locate the example: 'Every human-and-horse is a human; Every human-and-horse is a horse; therefore Some human is a horse.' Recognize that the conclusion is absurd.
2
Determine why it fails
The passage states the error: the subject ('human-and-horse') does not exist. Since no entity satisfies that description, both premises are false, even though the syllogistic form (third figure with valid mood Darapti) is correct.
Compare with the valid example of 'rational animal'.
3
Analyze the valid contrast
Al-Dusūqī contrasts with 'Every rational animal is an animal; Every rational animal is rational; therefore Some animal is rational.' Here the subject 'rational animal' does exist, so the premises are true and conclusion follows.
Both syllogisms share the same logical structure; only material truth differs.
4
Formulate the general lesson
A syllogism can be formally valid but materially unsound if its premises are false. This is a material fallacy (mughālaṭa māddiyya). Always check that the terms involved have real referents in Islamic sciences.
For instance, claiming 'every jinn-and-human is a prophet' would be absurd.

Premises of Sciences: Clear and Accepted
Author classifies premises of sciences into clear and accepted, with subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify premises of a science as clear or taken, and further subdivide taken premises into postulated principles and postulates based on the learner's acceptance.

Core Idea
Scientific syllogisms rest on premises that are either self-evident or accepted from authority.

Every science builds its conclusions on premises. Some premises are so obvious they need no proof; others are taken on trust from an expert. This lesson walks you through the author's classification, which is essential for understanding how knowledge is structured in any discipline, including the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Find where the author divides all premises into two types: clear (bayyinah) and taken (ma'khūdhah). The Arabic text says: 'إِمَّا مُقَدِّمَاتٌ بَيْنَةٌ ... أَوْ مُقَدِّمَاتٌ مَأْخُوذَةٌ'.
This is the fundamental taxonomy.
2
Define clear premises
Look for the description: 'واضحةٌ شديدة الوضوح بنفسها' – they are self-evident. Note they are also called 'common knowledge' (علوم متعارفة) as per al-'Attar.
They require no proof.
3
Define taken premises
Taken premises are 'مقبولةٌ ممَّن يُعتَقَدُ فيه، غيرُ بيِّنةٍ بنفسها'. The learner accepts them by good opinion (بحسن الظن). They come from a trusted authority.
They are not self-evident.
4
Subdivide taken premises
Al-Dasuqi explains: if accepted with good opinion without denial, they are 'أصول موضوعة' (postulated principles); if accepted with some denial or skepticism, they are 'مصادرات' (postulates).
The key difference is the learner's attitude.
5
Apply to an Islamic science example
Think of usul al-fiqh: the premise 'Certainty is not removed by doubt' is a clear premise. The acceptance of a solitary hadith (khabar al-wahid) as evidence is a taken premise; if accepted with full trust it is a postulated principle, if with slight doubt it is a postulate until corroborated.
This tool helps evaluate the foundation of any argument.
