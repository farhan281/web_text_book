The Four Compound Modal Propositions: Composition and Examples
Author defines four types of compound modal propositions, explaining their composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four compound modal propositions and describe its composition from a general conditional and a general absolute.

Core Idea
Compound modal propositions are built from a general conditional plus a general absolute—the negation of permanence.

In this passage, the author defines four key compound modal propositions used in Islamic logic: the special conditional, the temporal, the extended, and the common-conditional. Each is composed of two parts: a general conditional (or its temporal/extended/common equivalent) and a general absolute, which is simply the negation of permanence ('not always'). Understanding these compounds is essential for analyzing modal arguments in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four compound types
Look for the names: المشروطة الخاصة, الوقتية, المنتشرة, العرفية الخاصة. These are the four distinct compound modal propositions.
2
Extract the general conditional component
For each, the first component is a proposition that affirms necessity or permanence under a specific condition: e.g., 'as long as the subject's description holds' for the special conditional, 'at a specific time' for temporal, 'at some time' for extended, and 'as long as the description holds' for common-conditional (but with 'always' instead of 'necessarily').
Note the subtle difference: special conditional and common-conditional both use 'as long as description holds', but special conditional judges necessity, common-conditional judges permanence ('always').
3
Identify the general absolute component
The second component is always the phrase 'لا دائماً (not always)', which stands for a universal negative absolute proposition: 'Nothing is so at any time'.
This part negates permanence, so the whole compound means 'necessarily (or always) under condition, but not permanently'.
4
Check the composition
Verify that each compound is a conjunction of the two components. Note that the passage states which component agrees with the compound in quality and which disagrees.
For special conditional, the general conditional agrees in quality with the compound (both universal affirmative), while the general absolute disagrees (it is universal negative).
5
Apply to a new example
Create a new compound modal for a different subject and predicate, e.g., 'Every human is speaking as long as he is alive, not always'. Identify which type it is (mashruta khassa).

Commentary
This passage defines four types of compound modal propositions commonly discussed in Islamic logic. It begins with a 'Chapter on the disjunctive' and presents an initial example of a disjunctive proposition: 'Either some writer is not moving his fingers with possibility while he is a writer, or some writer is moving his fingers always.' This disjunctive arises from the negation of a compound conditional, as will be seen. The author then explains each compound type in turn. The first is the 'special conditional' (al-mashruta al-khassa), which judges a relation to hold with necessity as long as the subject possesses a certain description (e.g., 'as long as he is writing'), but not permanently; it is composed of a general conditional (affirming the necessity during the description) and a general absolute (negating permanence, expressed as 'not always'). The general absolute is a universal negative proposition: 'Nothing of the writer is moving fingers absolutely.' The second is the 'temporal' (al-waqtiyya), which judges necessity at a specific time rather than during a description; it also restricts with non-permanence, so it is composed of a general temporal (affirming necessity at that time) and a general absolute. The third is the 'extended' (al-muntashira), which judges necessity at some indefinite time, again with non-permanence, composed accordingly. The fourth is the 'common-conditional' (al-'urfiyya al-khassa), which judges permanence (not necessity) as long as the description holds, restricted by non-permanence. Each compound is thus a conjunction of two propositions: a general conditional and a general absolute. The general absolute is always 'not always', i.e., the negation of permanence. The passage ends with a note on the disjunctive form: if the subject is placed before the predicate, it ceases to be a true disjunctive and becomes a categorical with a disjunctive predicate, known as the 'categorical resembling the disjunctive'.

Differentiating Non-permanent and Non-necessary Existential Propositions
Author defines modal propositions with actuality and possibility conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the non-permanent existential and the non-necessary existential propositions and identify their compositional structure.

Core Idea
Actuality with a temporal condition vs. actuality with a possibility condition.

The author introduces two modal propositions that judge actuality but with different restrictions. Understanding their subtle difference is key to grasping contradiction and modal composition in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definienda and their conditions
Locate the definitions of al-wujudiyyah al-la-da'imah (non-permanent existential) and al-wujudiyyah al-la-daruriyyah (non-necessary existential). Note the condition: non-permanence (la dawam) for the first, non-necessity (la darura) for the second.
Both judge actuality (bil-fi'l) but differ in the essential modifier.
2
Extract the compositional structure
The non-permanent existential is composed of two general absolutes (mutlaqatan 'ammatan). The non-necessary existential is composed of a general absolute and a general possible (mumkinah 'ammah). Write down the two components for each.
3
Apply to the given example
Take 'Every human is standing actually, not always' (non-permanent). Break it into: (1) 'Every human is standing actually' (affirmative general absolute) and (2) 'No human is standing actually' (negative general absolute, understood from 'not always'). Check that they are opposite in quality and part of the composition.
The agreeing component is the affirmative, the opposite is the negative.
4
Contrast the two types
Compare the non-permanent and non-necessary examples. The second example uses 'sleeping' and 'not necessarily'. Observe that the non-necessary existential uses a general possible instead of a second general absolute. What changes in the meaning?

Commentary
This passage is from a section specifically on contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author begins by redefining the 'general absolute' (al-mutlaqah al-'ammah) as understood from 'not always' but now with the notion of actuality (bil-fi'l), giving the example 'no writer actually moves his fingers always' and notes it is opposite in quality. Next, the 'non-necessary existential' (al-wujudiyyah al-la-daruriyyah) judges actuality but restricted by essential non-necessity, composed of a general absolute and a general possible, e.g., 'every human is sleeping actually, not necessarily.' The general possible (al-mumkinah al-'ammah) is understood from 'not necessarily' and is opposite in quality. Finally, the 'particular possible' (al-mumkinah al-khāṣṣah) judges the negation of necessity from both sides (affirmative and negative), composed of two general possibles. The marginal note by al-Sharnubi provides the contradictory and disjunctive forms for each example, showing how these modal propositions relate to contradiction and disjunction.

The Particular Possible Proposition and Its Role in Contradiction
Author defines the particular possible proposition with its composition and relation to contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the particular possible proposition, explain its composition from two general possibles, and relate it to the disjunctive forms in contradiction.

Core Idea
The particular possible negates necessity on both sides, forming a disjunction of contradictory pairs.

The author defines a refined modal proposition that negates necessity from both the affirmative and negative sides. Together with al-Sharnubi's marginal note, we see how it generates a specific disjunctive proposition in the context of contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its unique condition
Find the definition of al-mumkinah al-khassah (particular possible). Note that it judges the negation of necessity from both sides—the agreeing side and the opposite side.
This is unlike the general possible which only negates necessity from one side.
2
Break down the composition
The particular possible is composed of two general possibles. What are these two? According to al-Sharnubi, the first is a universal affirmative, the second is a universal negative. Write out both components.
3
Relate to contradiction via the marginal note
Al-Sharnubi gives the contradictory of each component: the contradictory of the first (universal affirmative) is a particular negative necessary, and of the second (universal negative) is a particular affirmative necessary. The disjunction between these two contradictions forms a disjunctive proposition. How does the particular possible relate to this disjunction?
The particular possible itself is not a disjunction, but its components' contradictories form a disjunction.
4
Construct your own example
Create a particular possible proposition for 'Some humans are praying'. Write it as 'No human is praying necessarily, and not every human is not praying necessarily'? Actually, the form is: 'It is not necessary that every human is praying, and it is not necessary that no human is praying'? Follow the structure: negation of necessity from both sides. Then derive the disjunction of the contradictories as shown.

Commentary
This passage is from a section specifically on contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author begins by redefining the 'general absolute' (al-mutlaqah al-'ammah) as understood from 'not always' but now with the notion of actuality (bil-fi'l), giving the example 'no writer actually moves his fingers always' and notes it is opposite in quality. The Contradictories of Simple Modal Propositions
Author lists contradictories of simple modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory of each of the six simple modal propositions as listed in the text.

Core Idea
Each simple modal type has a unique contradictory that flips modality and temporality.

In this passage, the author provides a direct list of contradictories for six types of simple modal propositions. These relations are foundational for immediate inference in Islamic logic, allowing one to find the contradictory of any simple proposition quickly. Understanding this list is crucial for constructing valid arguments and refutations in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six simple modal types
The passage lists six simple propositions: necessary absolute, general conditional, absolute temporal, absolute extensive, absolute permanent, and general customary. Memorize these types.
These are the 'simple' (non-compound) propositions.
2
Match each with its contradictory
Follow the author: necessary absolute → general possible; general conditional → temporal possible; absolute temporal → temporal possible; absolute extensive → permanent possible; absolute permanent → absolute general; general customary → temporal absolute.
The contradictory is always a different modal type, often changing modality from necessary to possible or vice versa.
3
Recognize the pattern
Note that the contradictory generally inverts the modality (e.g., necessary to possible) and adjusts the temporal condition. For example, the general conditional (which includes a condition) is contradicted by a temporal possible (which does not require permanence).
This pattern will help when dealing with propositions not explicitly listed.

Why the Two-Part Concept Fails for Particular Compound Contradiction
Author explains why the 'concept of the two parts' is insufficient for contradicting particular compound propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the 'concept of the two parts' fails as a contradictory for a particular compound proposition and recognize the need for a more refined construction.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a particular compound cannot be a simple disjunction of universals.

In this passage, the author addresses a subtle point in the logic of contradiction: why a seemingly natural candidate for the contradictory of a particular compound proposition—the disjunction of the two universal contradictories of its parts—is insufficient. By examining a concrete example involving the predicate 'animal' and a non-permanent existential proposition, he shows that both the original proposition and this disjunction can be false together, violating the condition for true contradiction. This lesson will guide you through that reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Focus on the particular compound proposition under discussion: 'Some body is animal not always' (al-wujūdiyya al-lā-dā'ima). Note that it is a particular existential with a temporal modal 'not always'.
2
Understand the candidate contradictory
The 'concept of the two parts' (al-mafhūm al-mutaraddid) is the disjunction: 'Either every body is animal or every body is not animal'. This is formed from the universal contradictories of the parts of the original compound.
This candidate seems plausible because it appears to cover both possibilities.
3
Apply the joint falsehood test
Consider a scenario where the predicate 'animal' is constantly true for some bodies (e.g., horses) and constantly false for others (e.g., stones). Check whether both the particular and the disjunction can be false together in that scenario.
This is the key test for contradictory pairs: they must never be both false.
4
Observe joint falsehood
The particular 'some body is animal not always' is false because for those bodies that are animal, it is always true (so 'not always' is false). The disjunction 'either every body is animal or every body is not animal' is false because neither universal holds (animal is not true of all, nor false of all). Thus both are false.
Thus the disjunction fails as a contradictory.
5
Recognize the required correction
The proper contradictory must be constructed by taking the two parts (the two universal contradictories of the components) with their 'aspects' (bi-jahatay naqīḍay al-juz'ayn) to ensure that exactly one is true. This avoids joint falsehood.
The details of that construction are developed in subsequent passages.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The negation of a particular compound is a universal disjunction of two opposite modes.

In Islamic logic, propositions that combine quantity with temporal or conditional modifiers require careful negation. This lesson, drawn from Dassuqi's commentary, teaches the standard method: a particular affirmative with a 'not permanently' clause becomes a universal disjunction where each individual is either possibly not the predicate under the condition or always the predicate.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the proposition type
Identify which of the six types the proposition belongs to: conditional-specific, customary-specific, temporal, diffused, existential non-necessary, or specific possible. Each type has a specific modal structure.

Extract the components
Write the proposition as 'Some S is P under condition C, not permanently'. Note the subject (S), predicate (P), condition (C), and the modal adverb (e.g., 'necessarily').

Transform to 'Every S is either not P under C (with possibility) or P always'. The quantity changes from particular to universal, and the negation splits into two disjuncts: one negating the predicate under the condition with possibility, and the other affirming it permanently.

Ensure the modal terms match standard logical tables. For example, 'necessarily' in the original becomes 'possibly' in the first disjunct when negating, and 'necessarily' or 'absolutely' in the second disjunct for permanence.

Dassuqi then systematically provides examples for six types of partial propositions: (1) conditional-specific (mashruta khassa) – 'Necessarily, some writer moves his fingers as long as he is a writer, not permanently'; its negation is 'Every writer is either possibly not moving his fingers when he is a writer, or always moving his fingers'. (2) customary-specific ('urfiyya khassa) – 'Permanently, some writer moves his fingers as long as he is a writer, not permanently'; its negation is 'Every writer is either absolutely not moving his fingers when he is a writer, or always moving his fingers'. (3) temporal (waqtiyya) – 'Necessarily, some moon is eclipsed at the time of opposition, not permanently'; its negation is 'Every moon is either not eclipsed with general possibility at that time, or always eclipsed'. (4) diffused (muntashira) – 'Necessarily, some moon is eclipsed at some time, not permanently'; its negation is 'Every moon is either not eclipsed with possibility always, or always eclipsed'. (5) existential non-necessary (wujudiyya la-daruriyya) – 'Some human is actually sleeping necessarily'; its negation is 'Every human is either not sleeping always, or sleeping necessarily'. (6) specific possible (mumkina khassa) – 'Some human is sleeping with specific possibility'; its negation is 'Either every human is not sleeping necessarily or sleeping necessarily'. Each negation turns the original particular (some) into a universal (every) disjunction, and the temporal modality (e.g., 'not permanently') is transformed into a dual alternative: either the predicate is negated under the original condition but with possibility, or it is affirmed permanently. The exercise (tamrin) trains the student to apply the general rule that the negation of a particular affirmative with a temporal condition is a universal disjunctive negative proposition.

Experiential Propositions (Tajribiyyat) and the Hidden Syllogism
Author defines experiential propositions and explains the hidden syllogism that distinguishes them from induction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the hidden syllogism underlying experiential propositions and distinguish them from mere induction.

Core Idea
Experiential propositions gain certainty through a hidden syllogism inferring a cause from repeated observations.

The passage now defines experiential propositions (al-tajribiyyat) as those requiring repeated observation for the intellect to assent with certainty. This repetition implies a hidden syllogism (qiyas khafi): repeated uniform occurrences must have a cause. This causal inference distinguishes experiential propositions from induction (istiqra), which lacks such a reasoning. Thus, experiential propositions provide reliable premises for demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Find the definition of experiential propositions in the Arabic: 'والتجريبيات: وهي التي يحتاج العقل في الجزم بها إلى تكرير المشاهدة مرة بعد أخرى' (they are those for which the intellect needs repetition of observation to affirm them).
2
Understand the need for repetition
Why is repetition necessary? Because a single observation cannot yield certainty about a universal law. For example, one observation of fire burning does not prove that all fire burns.
3
Reconstruct the hidden syllogism
The hidden syllogism is: 'Repeated uniform occurrences must have a cause. (Major premise) This phenomenon has repeated uniform occurrences. (Minor premise) Therefore, this phenomenon has a cause.' Even if the cause is unknown, the inference is certain.
This is a causal inference, not merely enumerative induction.
4
Distinguish from induction
Induction (istiqra) simply collects instances without inferring a cause. Experiential propositions include the hidden syllogism, making them certain. For instance, repeated observation that alcohol causes intoxication leads to the experiential proposition 'Alcohol is intoxicating', whereas mere induction would only list cases.

That is, experiential knowledge is always supported by an underlying deductive reasoning, even if only implicit.

Definition and Conditions of Mutawatirat
Al-Dasuqi and al-Attar define mutawatirat and explain its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a report to be mutawatir and reconstruct the hidden syllogism that justifies its certain knowledge.

Core Idea
A report from a group impossible to collude yields certainty through a hidden syllogism known necessarily.

The mutawatirat are a key category of demonstrative premises in Islamic logic. This passage defines them and explains the conditions under which a report gives certain knowledge. We will unpack the definition, the hidden syllogism involved, and the two essential conditions: sensory basis and equality in the chain.

1

Find the term being defined: al-mutawatirat. Note that it is the fourth category of material premises for demonstration.

This passage defines the last category of material premises: nazariyyat (theoretical propositions). Despite this clarity, scholars differ on the number of such propositions—some say four, five, seven, or other numbers. The passage thus highlights a subtlety in classifying propositions that blur the line between necessary and acquired knowledge.

Commentary
This passage defines two key types of propositions used in dialectic (jadal): mashhoorat (renowned propositions) and musallamat (conceded propositions). Musallamat: Conceded Premises in Dialectic
Definition of musallamat and illustration via a syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid dialectical syllogism using a musallam proposition as a premise and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
Musallamat are premises conceded by the opponent, even if false, used to force a conclusion.

In dialectical debate, you can use any proposition the opponent concedes, regardless of its truth. This lesson explores musallamat (conceded propositions) and shows how they function in a syllogism, using the example from the passage.

Guided Path
1
Define musallam
Find the definition: 'propositions that are conceded by the opponent, upon which [the argument] is built.' The commentary adds: whether true or false.
2
Example from the text
The opponent concedes that Zayd is upright. This becomes a musallam premise.
3
Construct the syllogism
Combine the musallam with another premise: 'Every upright person is just.' This yields the conclusion 'Zayd is just'.
4
Analyze the syllogism
Identify figure and mood: First figure (Barbara), valid. But note that the major premise 'Every upright person is just' must also be conceded or universally accepted; otherwise it must be defended.
5
Distinguish from mashhoor
Musallam does not require wide acceptance; it only requires the opponent's concession. Mashhoor is widely accepted but not necessarily conceded by the opponent.
