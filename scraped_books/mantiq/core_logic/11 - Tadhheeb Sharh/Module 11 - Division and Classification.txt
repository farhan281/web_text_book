The Priority of Essential Over Accidental Division
Author defends priority of essential division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and accidental divisions of knowledge and explain why the essential division must precede the accidental in logical ordering.

Core Idea
Division by essence precedes division by attribute; what is intrinsic grounds what is contingent.

In Islamic logic, the first step is to classify knowledge itself. The commentator argues that knowledge must first be divided into tasawwur (concept) and tasdiq (assent) before dividing into necessary and theoretical. Why? Because the former is an essential distinction, the latter accidental. This order is not arbitrary but rooted in the nature of the divisions, as demonstrated by the irreversible relation between tasawwur and tasdiq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions of knowledge
Find the two pairs: tasawwur/tasdiq and daruri/nadhari. Note which pair is presented as foundational.
The passage argues the first pair is primary.
2
Understand the objection to the order
Someone might propose dividing first into necessary/theoretical, then into tasawwur/tasdiq. What is the commentator's response?
The objection is that the need for logic could be shown without such a complex order.
3
Grasp the rationale: essential vs. accidental
The division into tasawwur/tasdiq is by essence (dhati), while daruri/nadhari is by attribute (aradi). Al-Imad explains that the former is like genus into species, the latter is a division by accident.
Essential divisions are conceptual or intrinsic; accidental ones depend on external circumstances.
4
Apply the proof: irreversibility
The commentator gives a test: tasawwur can never become tasdiq, but theoretical can become necessary (if discovered to be self-evident). This shows which division is more fundamental.
If tasawwur could become tasdiq, the divisions would be interchangeable; but this is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn's statement that the division of knowledge is dependent on first dividing it into two parts. The commentator explains why the order of divisions is important. He argues that to show the need for both parts of logic (tasawwur and tasdiq), one must first divide knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq before dividing into necessary and theoretical. If one only divided into necessary and theoretical, one might think that all tasawwur is necessary, obviating the need for a discipline that leads to tasawwur. The objection that one could first divide by necessary/theoretical and then by tasawwur/tasdiq is refuted: such an inversion would be unreasonable because division by essence (tasawwur/tasdiq) is prior to division by attribute (necessary/theoretical). The commentator quotes al-Imad who states that the first division yields an essential distinction, while the second yields an accidental one. A proof is given: tasawwur can never become tasdiq, but theoretical can become necessary (by discovery of necessity), confirming the priority.

Division of Conception and Assent into Necessary and Acquired
Al-Attar explains the division of tasawwur and tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given piece of knowledge is a conception or an assent and classify it as necessary or acquired.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either conception or assent, and each is either necessary or acquired.

In this lesson, we explore the fundamental division of knowledge into tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent), and further into daruri (necessary) and iktisabi (acquired). This classification is the bedrock of Islamic logic, used to distinguish self-evident truths from those requiring evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of knowledge
The text states that conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) are the two primary categories. Conception is a mental representation without judgment; assent involves affirmation or denial.
2
Identify the two subcategories
Each of these is divided into necessary (daruri) and acquired (iktisabi). Necessary knowledge is self-evident or obtained without effort; acquired knowledge requires reasoning.
3
Understand the division relationship
The division is a partition: every instance of tasawwur or tasdiq is either daruri or iktisabi. The divided are the genera, and the divisions are the species.
This is analogous to dividing 'animal' into 'rational' and 'non-rational'.
4
Apply to examples
For instance, knowing that 'the whole is greater than the part' is a necessary tasdiq; knowing that 'the world is created' may be an acquired tasdiq after proof.

Commentary
This passage is a hashiya by al-Dasuqi and al-Attar on the introduction of the matn where the author states 'And they both divide' (wa yanqasiman). The 'they' refers to tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). The division is into necessary (daruri) and acquired (iktisabi). Al-Dasuqi briefly notes that this section is a prelude to the need for logic. Al-Attar provides a detailed parsing of the textual variants. He explains that there are two recensions of this phrase in manuscripts. The first recension reads 'yanqasiman' (they both divide) from the pattern infi'al, with the preposition 'ila' explicit, as in 'ila al-darura wa al-iktisab' (into necessity and acquisition). This reading requires interpreting 'darura' and 'iktisab' as meaning 'necessary' and 'acquired' because they are the divisions. Al-Attar explains that the divided term (tasawwur and tasdiq) is predicated of the divisions (necessary and acquired) in a manner akin to dividing a universal into its particulars. Without this interpretation, the predication is not straightforward, but it can be left as is if we understand 'division' as a predicate meaning 'knowledge possessing necessity/acquisition'. The second recension reads 'yaqtasimani' (they both divide) from the pattern ifti'al, with the preposition omitted. This can be read either in the active voice (as if the subject divides something) or the passive voice (as being divided). Al-Attar critiques the active reading because it would imply that the necessary and acquired are the divided, whereas the intended meaning is that tasawwur and tasdiq are divided into necessary and acquired. The passive reading avoids this but requires treating 'darura' and 'iktisab' as accusatives due to the removal of the preposition, which is a permissible linguistic leniency. Thus, the correct understanding is that tasawwur and tasdiq are divided into necessary and acquired, irrespective of the textual variant.

Textual Variants in the Division Phrase
Al-Attar discusses manuscript variants and grammatical interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the two manuscript readings of 'wa yanqasiman' and explain which one correctly preserves the logical division using grammatical principles.

Core Idea
Textual variants can change the logical meaning; careful grammar analysis ensures the correct doctrine is preserved.

The phrase 'wa yanqasiman' appears in different manuscript traditions with two spellings: 'yanqasiman' (with preposition 'ila') and 'yaqtasimani' (without). Al-Attar examines both, showing how grammar determines the intended meaning. This is a classic case of textual criticism in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two recensions
The first recension reads 'yanqasiman' (infi'al) with 'ila al-darura wa al-iktisab'. The second reads 'yaqtasimani' (ifti'al) without the preposition.
Both verbs mean 'they both divide', but the grammatical structure differs.
2
Analyze the first recension
With 'yanqasiman' and explicit 'ila', the meaning is straightforward: tasawwur and tasdiq divide into necessity and acquisition (interpreted as necessary and acquired). This is the correct logical division.
Al-Attar says this version is 'apparent' and needs only the interpretation that 'darura' means 'daruri'.
3
Analyze the second recension in active voice
Reading 'yaqtasimani' as active (subject doing the dividing) would invert the division: it would make tasawwur and tasdiq the dividers of necessity and acquisition, which is not intended. Al-Attar rejects this.
He says this interpretation would require dividing the necessary and acquired into conception and assent, contrary to the intended meaning.
4
Analyze the second recension in passive voice
Reading it as passive (they are divided) with 'darura' and 'iktisab' in the accusative due to removal of the preposition yields the correct meaning, but this is a rare grammatical construction. Al-Attar accepts it as permissible.
The passive reading is defended by noting that such constructions are common in Arabic as a linguistic license.

Commentary
This passage is a hashiya by al-Dasuqi and al-Attar on the introduction of the matn where the author states 'And they both divide' (wa yanqasiman). The 'they' refers to tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). The division is into necessary (daruri) and acquired (iktisabi). Al-Dasuqi briefly notes that this section is a prelude to the need for logic. Al-Attar provides a detailed parsing of the textual variants. He explains that there are two recensions of this phrase in manuscripts. The first recension reads 'yanqasiman' (they both divide) from the pattern infi'al, with the preposition 'ila' explicit, as in 'ila al-darura wa al-iktisab' (into necessity and acquisition). This reading requires interpreting 'darura' and 'iktisab' as meaning 'necessary' and 'acquired' because they are the divisions. Al-Attar explains that the divided term (tasawwur and tasdiq) is predicated of the divisions (necessary and acquired) in a manner akin to dividing a universal into its particulars. Without this interpretation, the predication is not straightforward, but it can be left as is if we understand 'division' as a predicate meaning 'knowledge possessing necessity/acquisition'. The second recension reads 'yaqtasimani' (they both divide) from the pattern ifti'al, with the preposition omitted. This can be read either in the active voice (as if the subject divides something) or the passive voice (as being divided). Al-Attar critiques the active reading because it would imply that the necessary and acquired are the divided, whereas the intended meaning is that tasawwur and tasdiq are divided into necessary and acquired. The passive reading avoids this but requires treating 'darura' and 'iktisab' as accusatives due to the removal of the preposition, which is a permissible linguistic leniency. Thus, the correct understanding is that tasawwur and tasdiq are divided into necessary and acquired, irrespective of the textual variant.

Sciences Are Distinguished by Their Subjects

Commentary
This passage explains the fundamental principle that sciences are distinguished from one another by their subject matters. The author begins by stating that the beginning of studying a science depends on knowing its subject, because sciences are not differentiated except through the differentiation of their subjects. He then provides a clear example from the Islamic sciences: the science of fiqh (jurisprudence) is distinct from the science of usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence) because their subjects are different. Fiqh studies the actions of the legally responsible persons (mukallafin) from the perspective of their legal rulings (halal, haram, sahih, fasid). Usul al-fiqh studies the revealed evidences (adilla sam'iyya) from the perspective of deriving legal rulings from them. The commentary by al-Dasuqi clarifies that the 'original distinction' (asl al-tamyiz) is obtained by conceptualizing the science via its definition, while the 'additional distinction' (ziyadat tamyiz) refers to the full discrimination achieved by distinguishing subjects. He then elaborates that subject distinction can be of two types: (1) by essence and consideration (dhatan wa i'tibaran), where the subjects are different entities, like fiqh and grammar; or (2) identical in essence but different in consideration, like grammar (nahw) and morphology (sarf), which both study Arabic words but from different aspects—grammar studies inflection (i'rab and bina'), while morphology studies weak letter changes (i'lal) and soundness (sihha). The commentary by al-Attar reinforces this by stating that the distinction of sciences follows the distinction of their subjects. If subjects are distinct by essence, the sciences are distinct by essence (e.g., usul al-fiqh vs. fiqh). If subjects are distinct by consideration, the sciences are distinct by consideration (e.g., literary sciences like grammar and morphology). He gives a further example from the physical sciences: the study of celestial bodies can be under astronomy (hay'a) when considered with respect to shape (e.g., spherical), or under natural science (tabi'i) when considered with respect to their nature (simple or composite bodies). Al-Attar quotes Abd al-Hakim to note that sometimes the same proposition (e.g., 'the earth is spherical') can appear in two different sciences but be proven by different proofs—an indication that the science is defined by its subject and the aspect of investigation, not just the proposition itself.

Two Modes of Distinction: By Essence and By Consideration
Commentators explain the two modes of subject distinction with examples from grammar, morphology, and natural science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two modes of subject distinction (by essence and by consideration) and apply them to examples from Arabic grammar/morphology and astronomy/natural science.

Core Idea
Subjects can be distinct either because they are different things or because they are the same thing considered from different angles.

Having established that sciences are distinguished by their subjects, the commentators elaborate on two ways this can happen: subjects can be different in essence (dhāt), like the subjects of fiqh and grammar, or they can be the same in essence but studied from different aspects (i‘tibār), like Arabic words in grammar and morphology. This lesson unpacks these modes with concrete examples from the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two modes
The text mentions distinction 'by essence and consideration' and 'identical in essence but different in consideration.' Locate these phrases.
2
Examine the first mode (by essence)
Subjects are different things, e.g., fiqh (actions of mukallafin) vs. nahw (Arabic words from inflection perspective). The sciences are distinct by essence.
3
Examine the second mode (by consideration)
Same subject but different aspects. Grammar studies Arabic words from the aspect of i‘rab and bina’; morphology studies the same words from aspect of i‘lal and sihha. Hence distinct sciences.
4
Apply to another example
The celestial bodies example: astronomy considers shape (spherical), natural science considers nature (simple/composite). This shows how the same object can be subject of two sciences depending on the aspect.
5
Note the implication
Sometimes the same proposition (e.g., 'earth is spherical') appears in two sciences but proven differently, confirming the aspect-based distinction.

Commentary
This passage explains the fundamental principle that sciences are distinguished from one another by their subject matters. The author begins by stating that the beginning of studying a science depends on knowing its subject, because sciences are not differentiated except through the differentiation of their subjects. He then provides a clear example from the Islamic sciences: the science of fiqh (jurisprudence) is distinct from the science of usul al-fiqh (principles of jurisprudence) because their subjects are different. Fiqh studies the actions of the legally responsible persons (mukallafin) from the perspective of their legal rulings (halal, haram, sahih, fasid). Usul al-fiqh studies the revealed evidences (adilla sam'iyya) from the perspective of deriving legal rulings from them. The commentary by al-Dasuqi clarifies that the 'original distinction' (asl al-tamyiz) is obtained by conceptualizing the science via its definition, while the 'additional distinction' (ziyadat tamyiz) refers to the full discrimination achieved by distinguishing subjects. He then elaborates that subject distinction can be of two types: (1) by essence and consideration (dhatan wa i'tibaran), where the subjects are different entities, like fiqh and grammar; or (2) identical in essence but different in consideration, like grammar (nahw) and morphology (sarf), which both study Arabic words but from different aspects—grammar studies inflection (i'rab and bina'), while morphology studies weak letter changes (i'lal) and soundness (sihha). The commentary by al-Attar reinforces this by stating that the distinction of sciences follows the distinction of their subjects. If subjects are distinct by essence, the sciences are distinct by essence (e.g., usul al-fiqh vs. fiqh). If subjects are distinct by consideration, the sciences are distinct by consideration (e.g., literary sciences like grammar and morphology). He gives a further example from the physical sciences: the study of celestial bodies can be under astronomy (hay'a) when considered with respect to shape (e.g., spherical), or under natural science (tabi'i) when considered with respect to their nature (simple or composite bodies). Al-Attar quotes Abd al-Hakim to note that sometimes the same proposition (e.g., 'the earth is spherical') can appear in two different sciences but be proven by different proofs—an indication that the science is defined by its subject and the aspect of investigation, not just the proposition itself.

Division of Speech into Complete and Incomplete
Al-Dasuqi and al-Attar explain the division of speech into complete and incomplete, and define the restrictive compound.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any linguistic expression as a complete or incomplete compound, and identify the restrictive compound subtype.

Core Idea
The complete-incomplete division depends on whether an utterance can be paused upon.

Logicians categorise utterances because reasoning is expressed in language. This lesson covers the foundational division into complete (tam) and incomplete (naqis) compounds, and within incomplete, the restrictive compound (murakkab taqyidi). Al-Dasuqi and al-Attar refine the definitions and terminology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance
Take a given expression, e.g., 'white donkey' (حمار أبيض) or 'Zayd is standing' (زيد قائم). Determine if it is a single word or a compound of two or more words.
Compounds are built from at least two words.
2
Check for 'sukut' (pausing)
Ask: Can you pause meaningfully after this expression? If yes, it is a complete sentence (tam) like 'Zayd is standing'. If not, it is incomplete (naqis) like 'white donkey'.
Sukut here means the speaker can stop and the listener finds the speech satisfying.
3
Classify incomplete as restrictive or non-restrictive
For incomplete compounds, check if one part restricts (qayd) the other. Example: 'hit in the house' (ضرب في الدار) – 'in the house' restricts 'hit'. If no restriction, e.g., 'Zayd and Amr', it is non-restrictive.
A restrictive compound is called murakkab taqyidi.
4
Apply the classification to examples
Practice with: 'the tall man' (الرجل الطويل) – incomplete restrictive; 'the sun rises' (تشرق الشمس) – complete; 'book of Zayd' (كتاب زيد) – incomplete restrictive.
Use the passage's criteria from al-Dasuqi and al-Attar.

Insha' and Khabar: The Logical Distinction
Al-Dasuqi and al-Attar explain the definition of insha' and its opposition to khabar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define insha' as an utterance whose primary meaning does not admit truth/falsity, and distinguish it from khabar, explaining why the opposition is real and not privative.

Core Idea
Insha' does not report truth; it creates reality through speech.

Besides the complete/incomplete division, utterances are also divided into khabar (informative) and insha' (performative). Insha' includes commands, prohibitions, and performatives like 'I sell'. Al-Dasuqi and al-Attar clarify that insha' does not admit truth/falsity in its primary sense, though it may imply a khabar. Al-Attar further argues that the opposition between khabar and insha' is real (taddad), not privation.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of insha'
From the passage: 'insha' is that whose meaning accompanies its utterance' (ما قارن معناه لفظه). Examples: 'I sold' (بعت), 'You are free' (أنت حر), 'Hit!' (اضرب).
This definition focuses on the immediate effect of the utterance.
2
Check admission of truth/falsity
Insha' does not admit truth/falsity in its primary meaning (باعتبار مدلوله المطابقي). But it may imply a khabar (e.g., 'Hit!' implies 'I am seeking hitting'), which can be true/false.
Al-Dasuqi emphasises that the primary meaning is the criterion.
3
Understand the khabar-insha' opposition
Al-Attar refutes the idea that the opposition is privation (where one is absence of the other). Instead, it is real opposition (تضاد حقيقي) because insha' includes diverse types (command, prohibition) with different implications.
This ensures both are independently defined categories.
4
Identify insha' in practice
Given utterances like 'Zayd is coming' (khabar) and 'Do not steal' (insha'), classify them based on the definition. Remember: if the utterance aims to report, it is khabar; if it aims to create a state (command, etc.), it is insha'.

The Particular and Universal in Concepts

Core Idea
The particular is defined by impossibility of predication due to mere conception, not by external proof.

How do logicians distinguish between a concept that applies to many and one that applies to only one? The answer lies in a subtle condition: 'by virtue of its mere conception in the mind.' This lesson unpacks that condition using the commentary of al-Dasuqi on the classical matn, showing how even the concept of God (wājib al-wujūd) can be universal in itself yet particular when proof is added. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise theological reasoning.

Identify the division of concepts
The matn divides concepts into particular (juzʾī) and universal (kullī). The particular is defined by the impossibility of being true of many 'by virtue of its mere conception in the mind.'
This division is the foundation of all logical discourse about definitions and propositions.
2
Analyze the condition 'bi-mujarrad ḥusūlihī'
The phrase means considering the concept stripped of any external proofs or additions. Only when this impossibility arises from the concept itself, not from an external factor, is the concept truly particular.

The Four Conceptual Relations
Author defines the four conceptual relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of relations between two concepts (mutabayinan, mutasawiyan, absolute general-specific, and general-specific in some respect) using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
The four conceptual relations are the map of how universals connect — every definition assumes one of these structures.

This lesson introduces the fundamental division of relations between any two concepts. This classification is essential for constructing definitions and arguments in Islamic logic. Understanding it prevents confusion when comparing terms, especially in legal or theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Know the exhaustive division
The author states that when two concepts are compared, they fall into exactly one of four categories: mutually exclusive, equal, absolutely general-specific, or general-specific in some respect. Memorize this fourfold division.
2
Understand mutual exclusivity (mutabayinan)
Mutual exclusivity requires complete separation: no individual falls under both concepts. The text gives the example 'human and horse' – nothing human is a horse, and nothing horse is a human. This is equivalent to two universal negative propositions.
The key phrase is 'complete separation' – both sides exclude each other entirely.
3
Distinguish the other three categories
Equality means identical extension (e.g., human and rational animal). Absolute general-specific means one includes all of the other and more (e.g., animal and human). General-specific in some respect means partial overlap (e.g., human and white). Each is defined by a different pattern of inclusion and exclusion.
Practice by testing whether the relation is mutual separation, one-sided inclusion, or partial overlap.
4
Apply the criterion of 'mutual separation'
The text emphasizes that mutual exclusivity requires separation from both sides. This excludes absolute general-specific (only one side) and equality (no separation). Use this criterion to classify any pair of concepts encountered in Islamic sciences.
This thoroughness prevents mistaking a hierarchical relation for mutual opposition.

Understanding Lizumiyya and Ittifaqiyya in Connected Conditionals
Author classifies muttasila into lizumiyya based on relationship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish lizumiyya from ittifaqiyya connected conditionals by examining whether the nexus is based on a genuine relationship (alaka) between antecedent and consequent, and evaluate examples from Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
A lizumiyya conditional reflects a real causal or necessary link; an ittifaqiyya is merely coincidental.

In this passage, the author divides connected conditionals (muttasila) into two types based on the presence of a real relationship (alaka) between the antecedent and consequent. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh, where only lizumiyya conditionals carry the weight of proof. The passage specifically states that the judgment of nexus (or its negation) must be due to a relationship for the conditional to be lizumiyya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent and consequent
Clearly isolate the 'if' part (muqaddam) and the 'then' part (tali) of the conditional. In Arabic conditionals, they are joined by 'in' and 'fa' or similar.
Example: In 'If the world is created, then it has a creator', the antecedent is 'the world is created' and consequent is 'it has a creator'.
2
Determine if there is a genuine relationship (alaka)
Ask: Is there a causal, necessary, or incompatibility relationship that connects the antecedent and consequent? The relationship must be intrinsic, not accidental.
Relationships include causality (creation implies creator), necessity (prophet implies infallibility), or incompatibility (sunrise implies absence of night).
3
Classify the conditional
If the nexus (or its negation in a negative conditional) is grounded in such a relationship, it is lizumiyya; otherwise, it is ittifaqiyya. The passage emphasizes that the judgment must be 'due to a relationship'.
The source gives examples: presumably the earlier examples of sun and night were lizumiyya because of the incompatibility relationship.
4

Take a conditional from Islamic theology, e.g., 'If a person is a prophet, then he is infallible'. Check if there is a relationship (divinely guaranteed infallibility) – it is lizumiyya.
Contrast with 'If it is Friday, then I go to the mosque' – if the relationship is merely habit, it is ittifaqiyya.

Divisions of the connected conditional. Then the connected conditional, whether affirmative or negative, is either implicative (lizumiyya), if that judgment of nexus or its negation is due to a relationship (alaka) between the antecedent and consequent, like the two given examples, for the judgment of nexus or its negation in them is due to a relationship.

Commentary
This passage divides the connected conditional (muttasila) into two types: implicative (lizumiyya) and, by implication, accidental (ittifaqiyya). The division is based on whether the nexus (or its negation) is grounded in a real, intrinsic relationship (alaka) between the antecedent and consequent. The author states that a conditional is lizumiyya if the judgment of nexus (for affirmative) or its negation (for negative) exists because of a relationship between the two parts. For example, in Islamic theology, the conditional 'If the world is created, then it has a creator' is lizumiyya because there is a causal relationship (creation necessitates a creator). Similarly, the text's own examples of sun and night are based on the real relationship that sunrise is incompatible with night existence. If the nexus were coincidental (e.g., 'If it rains, then the market is empty' without any causal link), it would be ittifaqiyya. This classification is important for determining the modality of the conditional – lizumiyya carries necessity. The commentator (al-Dasuqi) explains that the 'relationship' here means the existence of an appropriate connection; for the negation in a negative lizumiyya, the absence of relationship serves as the basis. This distinction helps in evaluating arguments: in jadal, a lizumiyya conditional can be used for proof, while an ittifaqiyya may be challenged.

The Four Types of Subject Matters in the Sciences
Author classifies subject matters into four types with nahw examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given question from an Islamic science into one of the four types of subject matters: subject of the science, a type of it, a self-accident, or a compound.

Core Idea
Every question in a science belongs to one of four categories of subject matter.

How do scholars determine what a science investigates? This passage from the Tadhheeb presents a key classification: the subject matters of a science's questions are either the science's overarching subject, a subtype of that subject, a self-accident of the subject, or a compound of the subject (or its type) with a self-accident. Using grammar (nahw) as an example, the author illustrates each category, then reminds us that examples are purely heuristic—don't get stuck on factual accuracy. This skill is essential for structuring any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the science's overarching subject
Determine what the science as a whole studies. For example, in nahw the subject is 'speech' (or 'word' according to some). Every question in that science will relate to this subject.
The subject of the science is its widest domain, e.g., 'speech' for grammar, 'acts of the morally responsible' for usul al-fiqh.
2
Classify the question's subject matter
Look at the question's topic and decide: is it exactly the subject of the science, a subtype of it, a self-accident of the subject, or a compound? Use the grammar examples as a template.
A question about 'noun' is about a type of 'word' (subtype). A question about 'declension' is about a self-accident of 'word'. A question about 'declinable word' is compound.
3
Check the example's role
The author warns that examples are only for illustration; they need not be factually correct. So classify based on the logical form, not the accuracy of the example itself.
If an example is historically inaccurate, ignore that—focus on the structural type it is meant to represent.

A Chapter: On the parts of the sciences.
[The subject matters of the questions]
As for (their subject matters), they are:
1. Either the subject of the science, like our saying in grammar for example: 'Every speech: either its predicate is mentioned or not.' For speech is the subject of grammar.
2. Or a type of it; i.e., a type of the subject of the science, like our saying: 'Every noun: either declinable or indeclinable.' For the noun is a type of 'word' which is the subject of the art.
3. Or a self-accident of it; i.e., a self-accident of the subject of the science, like our saying: 'The being built (binā') is either due to resemblance to the base of the original letter or due to lack of composition.' For binā' is a self-accident of the word.
4. Or compound; meaning that the subject matter of the question is composed of the subject of the science and its self-accident, like our saying: 'Every declinable word: either fully declinable or not declinable.' For 'word' is the subject of the science, and it has been taken in this question along with declension which is its self-accident.
Or compound from a type of the subject of the science and its self-accident, like our saying: 'Every declinable noun: either declined with letters or with vowels.' For the noun is a type of the subject of the science, and it has been taken in this question as declinable, and declension is its self-accident.
[Important note] And know that the purpose of presenting examples is to clarify the rules, whether they correspond to reality or not; for illustration is achieved by mere supposition. So if the examples we have presented are not corresponding to reality, you should draw the cloak of overlooking over the statement; for there is no discussion regarding the example.

Commentary
This passage defines the four types of subject matters that a science's questions can have, using examples from Arabic grammar (nahw). The author begins by stating that the subject matters (موضوعات المسائل) of a science's questions are of four kinds. The first is the subject of the science itself, e.g., 'speech' in grammar. The second is a type of that subject, e.g., 'noun' which is a type of 'word'. The third is a self-accident (عرض ذاتي) of the subject, e.g., 'binā'' (being indeclinable) which is an accident proper to words. The fourth is a compound: either compound of the subject and its self-accident (e.g., 'declinable word'), or compound of a type of the subject and its self-accident (e.g., 'declinable noun'). After the classification, an important note explains that examples are purely illustrative and need not match reality; one should not critique the example itself if the rule is clear. The note demonstrates the author's pedagogical concern: examples serve the rule, not the other way around.
