Navigating the Bibliography of a Classical Commentary
List of reference works used by the commentator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the reference works in a classical Islamic bibliography and infer the commentator's scholarly method from the selection.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the intellectual lineage and methodological choices of the commentator.

A classical commentary (sharh) is not an isolated text; it is built upon a network of earlier works. The bibliography at the beginning or end of a commentary lists the sources the commentator consulted. This lesson teaches you to read such a bibliography as a tool for understanding the commentator's scholarly approach, rather than as a mere list of titles.

Guided Path
1
Categorize each title by genre
Look at the titles: some are biographical dictionaries (e.g., 'al-A‘lām', 'al-Durar al-Kāmina'), others are historical works (e.g., 'Ta’rīkh ‘Ajā’ib al-Āthār'), and some are specialized biographies for exegetes or Hanafis. Write down the genre for each.
Biographical works often have words like 'tabaqāt' or 'a‘yān'.
2
Identify the time periods covered
Notice titles referring to specific centuries: 'al-Badr al-Ṭāli‘' covers after the 7th century, 'Ḥilyat al-Bashar' covers the 13th century. This shows the commentator's interest in later scholars.
3
Note the thematic specializations
Some titles focus on specific groups: 'al-Fawā’id al-Bahiyya' is about Hanafis; 'Ṭabaqāt al-Mufassirīn' is about exegetes. This indicates the commentator's areas of expertise.
4
Infer the commentator's scholarly network
The authors of these works (e.g., Khalil Mardam, al-Adnahi, al-Dawudi) represent the scholarly circles the commentator was connected to. A long list suggests wide reading and a desire for thoroughness.
Compare with the actual commentary to see which sources are cited most.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing the sources used by the commentator of the Tadhheeb Sharh. The list includes biographical dictionaries (e.g., al-A‘lām, al-Durar al-Kāmina), historical works (e.g., Ta’rīkh ‘Ajā’ib al-Āthār), and specialized biographical works for Hanafis, exegetes, and later centuries. The presence of works like Shadharāt al-Dhahab and al-Badr al-Ṭāli‘ indicates the commentator relied on well-known compilations of biographies from the Islamic tradition. Each title is a reference that the commentator would have used to cross-check information or to cite in the margins. The list is a testament to the scholarly method of grounding commentary in authoritative prior works.
