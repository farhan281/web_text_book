Demonstrative Syllogism and the Conditions of Certainty
Author defines demonstrative syllogism and certainty, listing six epistemic principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions of certainty (yaqīn) and list the six sources of certain premises in demonstrative syllogisms.

Core Idea
Certainty is unshakable, reality-corresponding belief from six specific sources.

This lesson unpacks the foundation of demonstrative reasoning: certain premises. You will learn how logicians define certainty, what it excludes, and the six types of propositions that yield certainty. Commentators al-Dasūqī and al-ʿAṭṭār clarify the definition.

Guided Path
1
Define demonstrative syllogism
Find the definition: a syllogism composed of certainties (yaqīniyyāt). Understand that material (premises) determines the kind of syllogism.
Burhānī refers to the highest form of proof.
2
Extract the three conditions of certainty
The definition of yaqīn includes: (1) belief (iʿtiqād), (2) belief that it cannot be otherwise, (3) correspondence to reality, and (4) impossibility of removal. Note the fourth is actually a condition or a result of the others.
Al-Dasūqī explains each exclusion.
3
Identify what each condition excludes
Condition (1) excludes doubt (shakk) and opinion (ẓann) because they lack firm belief. Condition (3) excludes ignorance (jahl). Condition (4) excludes blind imitation (taqlīd).
Taqlīd is excluded because the belief could be overturned.
4
List the six sources of certainty
Memorize the six: awwaliyyāt (primary intelligibles), mushāhadāt (sense data), ḥadsiyyāt (intuitive), tajribiyyāt (empirical), mutawātirāt (concurrent reports), fiṭriyyāt (innate).
These are the uṣūl of certain premises.
5
Apply the definition to an example
Check if a given belief meets the conditions. For instance, 'The world is created' can be certain if proven by a demonstrative syllogism from these sources.
Contrast with opinion or imitation.

The Three Conditions of Yaqeen and Their Exclusions
Author defines yaqeen with three conditions and the commentary explains each exclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions of yaqeen and determine which epistemic state each condition excludes.

Core Idea
Yaqeen requires correspondence, impossibility of removal, and a belief that it cannot be otherwise.

In this passage, the commentator unpacks the definition of yaqeen (certain knowledge). The definition consists of three conditions: a firm belief that the proposition cannot be otherwise, correspondence to reality, and immutability. Each condition excludes a different flawed epistemic state: zann (conjecture), jahl (ignorance), and taqlid (imitation). Mastering these exclusions helps you distinguish true certainty from mere opinion or blind acceptance.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition and its three conditions
Find the Arabic definition: 'مَعَ اعْتِقَادٍ بِأَنَّهُ لَا يَكُونُ إِلَّا أَنْ يَكُونَ كَذَا، اعْتِقَاداً مُطَابِقاً لِنَفْسِ الْأَمْرِ غَيْرَ مُمْكِنِ الزَّوَالِ'. Identify the three conditions: (1) belief that it cannot be otherwise, (2) correspondence to reality, (3) impossibility of removal.
The definition is stated first, then the commentary breaks it down.
2
Analyse the first condition and its exclusion
The phrase 'مَعَ اعْتِقَادٍ بِأَنَّهُ لَا يَكُونُ إِلَّا أَنْ يَكُونَ كَذَا' (with a belief that it cannot be except that it be such) excludes conjecture (zann) because conjecture allows for the possibility of the opposite. Doubt (shakk) is not even a belief, so it is automatically outside.
The commentator explicitly states: 'خَرَجَ بِهِ الظَّنُّ'.
3
Analyse the second condition and its exclusion
The phrase 'مُطَابِقاً لِنَفْسِ الْأَمْرِ' (corresponding to the reality itself) excludes ignorance (jahl), both simple ignorance and compound ignorance (jahl murakkab). Compound ignorance is believing something false while thinking it true.
The commentator says: 'خَرَجَ الْجَهْلُ' and later 'خَرَجَ الْجَهْلُ الْمُرَكَّبُ'.
4
Analyse the third condition and its exclusion
The phrase 'غَيْرَ مُمْكِنِ الزَّوَالِ' (impossible of removal) excludes imitation (taqlid). An imitator's belief can be shaken by counter-evidence, whereas yaqeen is immune to doubt.
The commentator repeats: 'خَرَجَ التَّقْلِيدُ' and 'خَرَجَ اعْتِقَادُ الْمُقَلِّدِ'.

Primary Propositions (Awwaliyyat) and Their Epistemic Status
Author defines awwaliyyat as immediate judgements and discusses obstacles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining features of awwaliyyat and distinguish between different reasons for failure to grasp them.

Core Idea
Awwaliyyat are self-evident, but not everyone can access them.

This lesson introduces the first category of syllogistic premises: primary propositions (awwaliyyat). These are judgements the mind makes immediately upon understanding the terms, without any proof. However, not everyone can grasp them equally. The commentator explains why some people fail, pointing to either intellectual deficiency or corrupted beliefs. Understanding awwaliyyat is crucial because they form the foundation of all certain knowledge in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of awwaliyyat
The author says awwaliyyat are propositions the mind judges solely by conceptualizing the two extremes (subject and predicate), without needing an intermediate. Note that this includes both necessary and theoretical terms.
Focus on the phrase 'بِمُجَرَّدِ تَصَوُّرِ الطَّرَفَيْنِ' – the immediacy of the judgement is key.
2
Examine the condition of conceptualization
The commentator clarifies that even if the terms are theoretical (nazari) in nature, the judgement can still be immediate if the terms are clearly understood. Example: 'one is half of two' – understanding 'one' and 'two' suffices for the judgement, even though the true reality of 'one' and 'two' may be difficult.
This shows awwaliyyat are not always simple; they can involve complex concepts as long as the mind makes the connection immediately.
3
Identify reasons for hesitation
The passage lists two obstacles that prevent the mind from accepting an awwaliy: (1) deficiency of instinct (as in children or the feeble-minded) and (2) defilement of the natural disposition with false beliefs (as in ignorant people).
The second obstacle is particularly important for theologians: false beliefs can blind a person to self-evident truths.
4
Contrast with other premise types
Although not detailed here, awwaliyyat are one of several categories of premises. They are foundational because they require no proof, unlike theoretical conclusions. This lesson sets the stage for understanding how all knowledge rests on such immediate judgements.
Later lessons will cover other premise types like mushahedat (observations) and mujarrabat (experiential).

Hads: Intuition as Instantaneous Mental Grasp
Author defines hads as quick mental transition and critiques its description as 'speed'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct description of hads as instantaneous mental transition and explain why 'speed' is a metaphorical expression.

Core Idea
Intuition is not fast reasoning – it is immediate grasp.

We often think of intuition as quick thinking. But according to the logicians, hads is not merely 'fast' – it is instantaneous. This lesson unpacks the definition and the subtle correction that distinguishes a valid logical concept from a misleading metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common definition
The well-known definition states: 'Hads is the speed of mental transition.' Locate this in the Arabic text.
2
Notice the critique
The author says that this definition contains looseness (tasamuh). Why? Because the transition in hads is 'instantaneous, not gradual'.
The key contrast is between daf‘i (instantaneous) and tadrījī (gradual).
3
Understand why speed implies gradual movement
Speed normally measures motion over time. If hads were truly a speed, it would involve a temporal process, but it does not.
Hence 'speed' can only be metaphorical here.
4
Recognize the correction
The author insists that in hads, the sought matters and principles are represented in the mind 'at once' (دفعة). This eliminates any notion of a step-by-step transition.

Definition of Hads and Its Distinction from Fikr
Author defines hads as the mind's quick transition and clarifies it is instantaneous, not gradual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish hads (intuition) from fikr (discursive thought) based on whether the transition is instantaneous or gradual.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The implicit syllogism behind tawatur is known by necessity, not reasoning.

This lesson uncovers the hidden logical structure that makes recurrent reports certain. The commentator al-Attar reveals an implicit syllogism that operates without conscious reasoning, which is why even a dullard can be certain of a mutawatir report.

2
Extract the two premises and conclusion

Determine the figure and mood

The first figure is the most perspicuous and yields certain conclusions.
5
Why is this syllogism 'khafi'?
Al-Attar says knowledge of this syllogism is 'hāsil bi al-ḍarūra' (obtained by necessity). It is not a conscious reasoning process; the mind grasps it instinctively.
This explains why even children and the dullard are certain of mutawatir reports.

Predicates are not parts of the subject; they are external accidents requiring proof.

The passage gives a logical argument that hinges on the very nature of demonstration in an Islamic science.

The Proof That Predicates Are External: A Conditional Syllogism
Reconstructs the proof as a compound conditional syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that predicates are external as a compound conditional syllogism (modus tollens) and verify its validity.

Core Idea
If predicates were parts, they wouldn't need proof; but they do; so they are not parts.

The sharh not only states that predicates are external but also provides a logical proof. Al-Attar's commentary analyzes this proof as a compound conditional syllogism, specifically modus tollens. By understanding this structure, you will see how logical reasoning is embedded in Islamic scientific methodology, and you will be able to construct similar proofs for other claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion the author aims to prove
The conclusion is that the predicates of problems are external to their subjects (محمولات المسائل خارجة عن الموضوعات). The whole proof is designed to establish this.
2
Find the conditional premise (al-muqaddam al-muttasil)
The conditional is: 'If the predicates of the problems were parts of their subjects, then they would not be demonstrated in the discipline.' (لو كانت محمولات المسائل أجزاء لموضوعاتها لم يبرهن عليها في الفنّ).
This is the antecedent-consequent relationship.
3
Identify the exception (al-istithnā')
The exception is the negation of the consequent: 'But the consequent is false' (لكن التالي باطل). The proof establishes that the consequent is false because the problems are indeed demonstrated.
The evidence for falsity: problems are declarative demands demonstrated in the discipline.
4
Apply modus tollens to derive the negation of the antecedent
From the conditional and the negation of the consequent, we infer the negation of the antecedent: 'So the antecedent is false' (فبطل المقدم). That is, it is false that the predicates are parts. Therefore, they are not parts.
This yields the desired conclusion: they are external.
5
Verify the validity of the argument
Modus tollens is a valid form. The truth of the premises ensures the truth of the conclusion. Al-Attar notes that the final phrase 'otherwise...' in the original is redundant because the conclusion is already reached.
No logical fallacy is present.

Understanding Accidents by Essence and by Part
Explains the two types of accidents and the universal-particular relation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an accident that attaches to a subject by its essence and by its part, using the examples and part-whole relation provided in the commentary.

Core Idea
Accidents attach either due to the thing's own essence or due to a part (universal) of it.

After establishing that predicates are external and accidental, the sharh specifies two ways an accident can attach to a subject: by the thing's own essence (li-dhātihi) or by a part of it (li-juz'ihi). The commentators, al-Dasooqi and al-Attar, elaborate on these types and explain the underlying part-whole logic between universals and particulars. This distinction is crucial for accurately attributing properties in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of accidents
From the sharh: an accident attaches either by its essence (لذاته) or by its part (لجزئه). Read the Arabic: 'وهو ما يلحق الشيء لذاته أو لجزئه'.
The conjunction 'or' shows a genuine division.
2
Examine the examples given
By essence: wonder (التعجب) attaches to human because he is human. By part: voluntary motion (الحركة بالإرادة) attaches to human because he is an animal. Note that animal is a part (universal) of human.
Al-Dasooqi clarifies that animal is a part of human.
3
Understand the part-whole relation between universal and particular
Al-Attar explains: 'animal' is a universal for 'human', and every universal is a part of its particulars. Conversely, 'human' is a particular for 'animal', and every particular is a whole for its universal.
This is a key logical relation: the universal is a part of the particular in the sense of comprising its essence.
4
Apply the classification to a new predicate
Consider the predicate 'rational' for human. Does it attach by essence or by part? Since rationality is constitutive of humanity, it attaches by essence. If it were by part, it would attach via a broader universal like 'animal', which is not the case.
This shows how to use the distinction.
