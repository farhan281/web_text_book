Establishing Scholarly Authority: The Biography of al-Taftazani
Author recounts the life, birth, traits, and teachers of al-Taftazani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the key markers of scholarly authority from a logician's biography in the Islamic tradition.

Core Idea
A scholar's biography is a credential that validates his logical commentary.

Why do logical works begin with the author's biography? In the Islamic tradition, the authority of a text rests on the author's scholarly credentials. This lesson examines the biography of Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani from the Sharh al-Tadhheeb to see how his mastery of multiple sciences, his teachers, and his intellectual lineage establish the weight of his logical writings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's full name and nisba
Extract his complete name and the nisba 'al-Taftazani'. The nisba shows his geographical origin in Khurasan, helping place him within the tradition of Khurasani scholarship.
2
List the sciences in which he excelled
The passage mentions many sciences. Count them and consider the breadth. His mastery of both transmitted and rational sciences means his logical commentary is informed by multiple disciplines.
3
Note his ability to give fatwa in two madhhabs
This shows his authority in fiqh and indicates his jurisprudence is not confined to one school, giving his logical work a universal perspective.
4
Trace his teachers and their fields
His teachers were leading scholars in rational sciences (al-Bakri), fiqh (al-Qazwini), and logic (al-Razi). This chain of learning (sanad) authenticates his knowledge.
5
Connect biography to the text
By establishing his credentials, the biography assures the reader that the commentary is written by a master who can clarify the complexities of logic.

Biography of the scholar Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani

First: His name and lineage: He is Mas'ud ibn 'Umar ibn Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr ibn Muhammad ibn Abi Sa'id al-Ghazi al-Taftazani, the jurist, theologian, dialectician, usuli, grammarian, rhetorician, logician.

Second: His birth: He was born in the village of Taftazan from the city of Nasa in Khurasan in Safar of the year 722 AH, in a family deeply rooted in knowledge, as his father was a scholar and judge, and likewise his grandfather and great-grandfather were among the virtuous scholars.

Third: His qualities: Al-Sa'd al-Taftazani was an imam of the imams of verification and scrutiny. The leadership of knowledge in the East ended with him in his time, and he surpassed his peers. He excelled in grammar, morphology, logic, semantics, rhetoric, usul, tafsir, kalam, and other sciences. He issued fatwas according to both the Shafi'i and Hanafi schools, and the leadership of the Hanafis ended with him in his time.

Fourth: His teachers: The scholar al-Taftazani received his knowledge from a group of the greatest professors of his time. Among them we mention: The scholar 'Adud al-Din 'Abd al-Rahman ibn Rukn al-Din 'Abd al-Ghaffar al-Bakri, who was an imam in the rational sciences, proficient in usul, semantics, and Arabic, and participated in the arts.

2 - The scholar Diya' al-Din 'Abd Allah ibn Sa'd Allah ibn Muhammad ibn 'Uthman al-Qazwini, known as Qadi al-Qarm al-'Afifi al-Shafi'i, one of the eminent scholars. He studied in his lands and took from Qadi 'Adud al-Din al-Iji and others. He studied under his father and Shaykh al-Khalkhali and advanced in knowledge. He had a large study circle around which the best students gathered. - Qutb al-Din Mahmud ibn Muhammad al-Razi, known as al-Qutb al-Tahtani, was the foremost of the theologians in logic and the ancient sciences, and one of the world's leading scholars in the rational sciences, deeply learned.

A great commentary harmonizes with predecessors while exercising independent critical judgment.

This passage introduces the methodology of al-Sharnubi in his commentary on al-Tadhheeb and the glosses of al-Dasuqi and al-‘Attar. While it may seem like a simple biographical note, it actually provides a template for evaluating any scholarly commentary: you must look for independence, mastery of sources, clarity, and the ability to resolve difficulties. Understanding these criteria will help you distinguish a truly valuable commentary from a mere compilation.

Identify the commentator's sources
Al-Sharnubi's commentary engages with three earlier works: the main text (al-Tadhheeb), the commentary by al-Khabisi (or perhaps a standard sharh), and the glosses (hāshiya) of al-Dasuqi and al-‘Attar. Note how he deals with multiple layers of commentary.

Combine the above points: a valuable commentary is independent, based on thorough study, clarifies difficult material, and respects earlier scholars while offering corrections. Use these criteria when you encounter any commentary in Islamic sciences.

The author's goal is to serve knowledge and religion by making logic easier to learn.

Connect logic to Islamic scholarly values
The taqriz links logic to 'hurriyyat al-ra'y' (freedom of opinion), 'istiqlal al-fikr' (independence of thought), and 'al-nazaha fi al-haqq' (integrity in truth). These values are upheld by mastering logic, which disciplines the mind to argue soundly without sophistry.
Logic is thus presented as a tool for intellectual integrity and service to deen.

The Definition of Taqdir and the Problem of Du'a
Al-Dasuqi defines taqdir and presents the problem of supplication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define taqdir as eternal determination and explain why it raises a problem for the efficacy of supplication.

Core Idea
Taqdir fixes the outcome; du'a seems redundant but is itself decreed.

In his commentary, al-Dasuqi unpacks the author's phrase 'qaddara Allahu' by defining taqdir as the eternal determination of events. This immediately raises a classic theological puzzle: if everything is already fixed, what purpose does supplication serve? He then notes that 'al-ghaniyy' (the Rich) is an attribute of Allah, 'Ubayd Allah' is the author's name, 'Faḍl Allah' his father's name, and 'al-Khabīṣī' is a nisba to the village Khabīṣa in Khurāsān (with a note by al-‘Aṭṭār that it might be from Kirmān). The main theological point arises from the author's phrase 'qaddara Allāhu' (may Allah decree). Al-Dasūqī raises an objection: if everything is decreed from eternity, then there is no point in supplicating for something that must happen or cannot be avoided. He gives the example of dying upon faith: if Allah has decreed that one will die a believer, then that is inevitable, so why ask for it? He then answers: the objection is not valid because the seeking (ṭalab) is itself part of the decree; the supplication is a means that Allah has decreed, so it is meaningful. The discussion is a classic one in kalām about the relationship between divine decree and human du‘ā’. Al-‘Aṭṭār then comments on the author's transition from first person (أقول) to third person (فيقول), calling it iltifāt according to al-Sakkākī's rhetorical theory, and notes that it avoids unnecessary length. Finally, he clarifies the nisba again, indicating a textual variant.

Core Idea
Supplication does not change decreed fate but is itself part of the suspended decree.

How can we pray for something that is already decreed? This question arises from a deep understanding of qadr. In this passage, the commentator al-Dasuqi (or al-Attar) addresses this puzzle: if Allah's determination in pre-eternity makes an event unavoidable, what meaning does prayer have? The answer lies in a subtle distinction within the Islamic doctrine of decree – a tool every theologian must handle with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paradox
The prayer 'qaddarallah' (may Allah decree) seems to ask for something that is already fixed. The commentator states: 'if it is a matter that has occurred, it is unavoidable, so there is no meaning to requesting it.' This is the core tension.
The objection assumes taqdir is unconditional and unchangeable.
2
Extract the objector's argument
The objector argues: if Allah's pre-eternal power attaches to an event (e.g., dying upon faith), then that event is unavoidable; therefore, requesting it is pointless. This is a conditional reasoning: if P then Q, and Q makes R meaningless.
3
Find the commentator's resolution
The commentator (via al-Attar) introduces the concept of qada' mu'allaq (suspended decree). Not all decrees are absolute; some are conditioned on actions, including supplication. Thus, the prayer is not redundant; it is the means by which the decree is fulfilled.
This distinction is known in kalam as al-qada' al-mu'allaq vs. al-qada' al-mubram.
4

Consider the Prophet's supplication for forgiveness: even though Allah's mercy is pre-eternal, the prayer is still meaningful because it is tied to the act of asking. Test this reasoning on a personal example like praying for health.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Scholarly humility is not weakness; it is a recognition of one's place in the tradition.

A textual colophon often carries more than a simple 'The End'—it reveals the scholar's social and intellectual world. The Etiquette of Correction in Islamic Scholarship
Author's concluding request for correction with etiquette.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the classical Islamic practice of taṣḥīḥ (marginal correction) and identify its underlying scholarly values in a given text.

Core Idea
Correct an error by noting it in the margin, not by erasing—preserve the original and honor the author.

In this final passage of his work, the author models the very humility and scholarly etiquette he hopes for from his readers. He asks not for blind praise but for constructive correction—and specifies the proper method. Understanding this protocol reveals much about the culture of Islamic learning, where respect for the text and its author goes hand in hand with the pursuit of truth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's three requests
Read the Arabic and find the three distinct appeals: (1) that the work be accepted by the ruler, (2) that peers forgive lapses, and (3) that explicit errors be corrected in a specific way.
The appeals form a crescendo from seeking acceptance to asking for active correction.
2
Analyze the metaphor of the winds
The author uses 'al-ṣabā' (east wind) and 'al-qabūl' (west wind) as metaphors for widespread fame. Understand that these winds carry things far—showing his hope for dissemination.
This is a common poetic device; it shows the author's ambition tempered by humility.
3
Clarify the meaning of 'tasih'
The phrase 'فليستروهُ بالتَّصحيح' literally means 'let them conceal it with correction.' In classical manuscript culture, taṣḥīḥ refers to writing the correct version in the margin, not erasing the original. Why? Because the original might actually be right, or the 'error' might be a different authentic reading.
This preserves the textual tradition and avoids losing potentially correct information.
4
Connect to scholarly practice
Reflect on how this etiquette applies to modern Islamic scholarship. For example, when a hadith scholar finds a mistake in a matn, they should note the correct version without deleting the transmitted text.
This principle is foundational in the sciences of tahqīq (editing) and hadith criticism.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A colophon is a scholar's final act of humility, gratitude, and scholarly attribution.

The colophon of a classical Islamic work is not merely a date stamp. It is a window into the author's piety, scholarly lineage, and the chain of transmission. In this passage, Hasan al-'Attar closes his commentary on the Tahdhib al-Mantiq with a prayer, a plea for leniency, and a careful attribution of his sources. Understanding these elements helps you appreciate the human and sacred dimensions of Islamic knowledge.

Locate the opening prayer
