Refuting Infinite Regress in Judgment Perception
Refutation of infinite regress in judgment perception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical problem of infinite regress in perception and explain how al-Attar resolves it by distinguishing the judgment as a unique perception.

Core Idea
Not every perception requires a meta-perception; the judgment-perception is self-sufficient, preventing infinite regress.

In this passage, al-Attar addresses an important objection: if the judgment is a perception, does it require another perception of it, leading to an infinite chain? He refutes this by showing that the judgment-perception is exempt from the necessity claim and that infinite regress does not apply here due to the nature of perception. Let's examine how he preserves the coherence of the system while maintaining the distinction between tasawwur and tasdiq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The passage begins by referencing an objection: if the judgment is a perception of tasawwur, then it would require a perception of that perception, leading to infinite regress. Locate where this worry is mentioned ('فما توهّم من عدم صحة ذلكَ لِلزومِ التّسلسل').
2
Understand al-Attar's classification
Al-Attar states that the judgment is a perception from the category of image (tasawwur) but is essentially different and exempt from the claim that all images are necessary. This sets the stage for the refutation.
The exemption implies the judgment is not a necessary image, avoiding the need for a further perception.
3
Analyze the refutation of infinite regress
The commentator argues that infinite regress does not apply because such cases (the judgment) are not subject to regress due to reasons like inadvertence (ذهول). The mind can have a perception without being aware of perceiving it.
This blocks the regress by denying that every perception requires a meta-perception.
4
Assess the resolution
Confirm that the refutation preserves the possibility of theoretical (kasbi) assent. The judgment-perception is self-sufficient, so no vicious regress follows.
This aligns with the broader epistemology that some perceptions are foundational.

Dawwani's Objection to Thought as Movement in Quality
Author presents Dawwani's objection and the response defending fikr as movement in quality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Dawwani's objection and evaluate whether thought satisfies the conditions for movement in the category of quality.

Core Idea
Thought cannot be a movement in quality because it lacks the infinite diversity of instants required.

This passage presents a philosophical puzzle: if thought involves a transition from one idea to another, is it a genuine movement? Al-Jalal al-Dawwani argues it is not, based on the nature of movement. The author then defends the classification by introducing the concept of attention and its varying intensities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under investigation
The authorities have stated that fikr (thought) is a movement (haraka) in the category of psychological qualities (kayfiyya nafsaniyya). Identify this claim in the passage.
The passage begins with the statement that this classification is what the authorities stated.
2
Extract Dawwani's definition of movement
Dawwani states that movement requires that in every instant a different individual from the category appears. This is his condition for movement. Find the Arabic phrase and paraphrase.
The key condition: 'it is necessary for the thing to be such that in every instant one can posit an individual from the category'.
3
Apply the definition to thought
Dawwani argues that in thought there are only limited elements (like genus, differentia, minor and major premises). Therefore it is impossible for the soul to be qualified by a new individual of knowledge in every instant. Explain why this fails the condition.
The limited number of sciences conflicts with the infinite instants required.
4
Examine the response: attention (iltifat)
The author anticipates an objection: the soul gradually shifts attention from one concept to another, with attention weakening and strengthening. However, the author counters that attention is an act of the soul and does not occur in the categories that admit movement. Trace this reasoning.
Attention is an act, not a quality in the relevant sense.
5
Consider the refinement: intensity of images
The final part of the passage suggests that if one considers different levels of attention as different images varying in intensity and weakness, then the soul does move among images. Assess whether this salvage the claim.
This is presented as 'not far-fetched' but not definitively accepted.

And the classification of the aforementioned movement as a movement in psychological qualities is what the authorities stated, and al-Jalal al-Dawwani investigated it: that in movement it is necessary for the thing to be such that in every instant one can posit an individual from the category in which the movement occurs, that individual not existing in the previous instant nor in the subsequent instant. The instants that can be posited in time are not stopping at a limit according to them, and likewise the posited individuals are not stopping. It is known that in the form of thought there are only limited sciences, especially in the return from premises to conclusions, for there is nothing there except knowledge of the genus and the differentia, for example, and the minor and major premises. Therefore it is not conceivable that the soul in every instant is qualified by an individual of knowledge that does not exist before or after. It cannot be said: the soul, when it observes the genus, for instance, and turns its attention to it, it moves from it to the differentia gradually, and its attention to the genus weakens gradually, and its attention to the differentia strengthens gradually, because we say: they have explicitly stated that attention is an act of the soul, and they have explicitly stated that there is no movement except in the categories of quantity, quality, location, and position, so there is no movement in attention. And even if that is granted, what they mentioned would be valid: that thought is a movement in quality. And this, even if it is said that the different levels of attention entail the different images in intensity and weakness, then the soul in every level of attention has an image at a level of intensity and weakness, differing in intensity and weakness from the previous and subsequent images, then it has a movement in images, not far-fetched.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange concerning the classification of fikr (thought) as a movement (haraka) in the category of psychological qualities. The author states that the scholars have explicitly classified the movement of thought as such. Al-Jalal al-Dawwani objects based on the definition of movement: movement requires that in each instant a different individual of the category appears, but in thought there are only a limited number of known elements (like genus and differentia, or minor and major premises). Therefore the soul cannot be qualified by a new individual of knowledge in every instant. The author then anticipates a counter-argument that the soul gradually shifts attention from genus to differentia, with attention weakening and strengthening. The response is that attention (iltifat) is an act of the soul, and acts of the soul are not in the categories that admit movement (quantity, quality, location, position). Thus attention itself does not involve movement. However, even if movement in attention were granted, the original claim would be valid. Moreover, if one considers that different levels of attention entail different mental images varying in intensity and weakness, then the soul moves among images, which is not implausible.

Essential Accidents: The Three Modes of Attachment
Definition of essential accidents with three subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an accident as essential based on the three criteria: attachment by essence, by part, or by coextensive external matter.

Core Idea
Essential accidents attach to a thing from within or from what is inseparable, not from outside.

The author opens by defining the subject of every science and immediately explains that sciences investigate essential accidents. He then gives three ways an accident can be essential: due to the thing's essence, due to a part of it, or due to an external but coextensive matter. Understanding these three modes is essential for recognizing what belongs to the core investigation of any science.

Guided Path
1
Find the subject and the accident
Identify the thing (subject) and the attribute (accident) in question. This is the first step for any analysis.
2
Check attachment by essence
Ask: Does the accident attach to the subject because of what it is in itself? If yes, it is essential by essence.
Example: wonder attaches to man by his essence.
3
Check attachment by a part
If not by essence, ask: Does it attach due to a part of the subject's nature? If yes, it is essential by part.
Example: voluntary movement attaches to man because he is animal, which is a part of his definition.
4
Check attachment by coextensive external matter
If not by part, ask: Does it attach due to something external that is exactly coextensive with the subject? If yes, it is essential by coextensive.
Example: laughter attaches to man because of wonder, which is coextensive with man.
5
Determine if not essential
If none of the above apply, the accident is not essential. It is accidental (gharib) and not the subject of scientific investigation.

And know that the subject of every science is what is investigated in that science regarding its essential accidents. Essential accidents are those that attach to a thing due to its essence — like wonder attaching to the essence of man — or due to a part of it — like voluntary movement attaching to man by virtue of his being an animal — or due to an external matter coextensive with it — like laughter occurring to man by virtue of wonder. They are called essential accidents because of their dependence on the essence of the subject. Sciences only investigate the essential accidents of their subjects — thus they said. Rather, the truth is that essential accidents are what attach to a thing due to its essence or due to what is coextensive with it, whether that be a part of it or external to it. End. Commentary
This passage defines the concept of essential and accidental accidents, which is crucial for understanding how sciences construct their problems. It begins by stating that the subject of every science is investigated regarding its essential accidents (a'rad dhatiyya). The passage then defines essential accidents as those that attach to a thing due to its essence, or due to a part of it (e.g., movement due to being animal), or due to an external matter coextensive with it (e.g., laughter due to wonder). These are called essential because they are grounded in the essence of the subject. In contrast, accidental accidents (a'rad ghariba) attach due to something external that is more general (e.g., movement due to body, which is more general than whiteness), or more specific, or different in genus. The passage emphasizes that sciences only investigate essential accidents. Then it quotes al-Sayyid al-Jurjani's hashiyah, who criticizes the view that the accident attaching by way of a more general part is essential; he insists that essential accidents are only those due to the essence or what is coextensive. Finally, it explains that 'investigation' in a science means predicating these accidents of the subject with a predication of identity (haml muwata'at), illustrated with examples from grammar: predicating inflection (i'rab) of a word, or the types of particles being uninflected, or the essential accidents of inflection (being verbal or estimated), etc.

Accidental Accidents: The Three Extraneous Causes
Definition of accidental accidents with three subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an accident is accidental (gharib) based on its cause being more general, more specific, or different in genus from the subject.

Core Idea
Accidental accidents arise from something unrelated to the subject's essence, introducing extraneousness.

After defining essential accidents, the author turns to those that are accidental (gharib). These attach due to external matters that are either more general than the subject, more specific, or different in genus. Recognizing these helps to exclude irrelevant attributes from scientific investigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the accident
Determine the thing that is the subject and the attribute that is the accident.
2
Find the cause of the accident
Determine why the accident is said to attach. Is it due to something external to the subject?
3
Compare the cause to the subject
Is the cause broader (more general) than the subject, narrower (more specific), or completely different in kind (different genus)?
4
Classify as accidental due to generality
If the cause is broader, the accident is accidental due to generality. Example: movement in white due to body (body is more general).
5
Classify as accidental due to specificity
If the cause is narrower, the accident is accidental due to specificity. Example: laughter in animal due to being man (man is more specific).

Sciences only predicate essential accidents using a predication of identity (haml muwata'at).

The author states that sciences only investigate essential accidents, and then explains the form of investigation: it is a predication called haml muwata'at. Core Idea
Three objections expose hidden assumptions in the virtual mutabaqa response.

The virtual mutabaqa response claimed that a verb without its subject still achieves actual correspondence because the subject is virtually present. However, this response is not without difficulty. The commentator raises three objections (wujuh) testing the logical limits of the response, each targeting a different aspect: the nature of supposition, the level of understanding required, and the underlying theory of verb coinage. This condition is crucial to avoid counterexamples like heat existing without fire. The follower may exist without the followed, but not when it is considered as a follower. Understand how the condition restricts the predicate.
Al-Dasuqi gives the example of heat: heat is a follower of fire, but it can exist in the sun; in that case it is not a follower of fire.
3
Examine the placement of the condition
Al-Attar explains that the condition is on the side of the predicate, not the subject. If it were on the subject, the middle term would not repeat. This ensures the syllogism's validity.
The subject is 'every follower', the predicate is 'does not exist without its followed, while being a follower.'
4
Consider al-Razi's objection and the Sayyid's resolution
Al-Razi objects that the same reasoning would imply that mutabaqa necessitates tadammun and iltizam symmetrically. The Sayyid proposes a different proof: tadammun and iltizam necessitate the positing (wad') which necessitates mutabaqa, thus avoiding the symmetry.
This shows that the dependency is not directly syllogistic but through the medium of conventional positing.
5
Summarize the logical dependency
The final conclusion is that tadammun and iltizam do not exist without mutabaqa always, as per the modal conclusion. The subtlety of the condition and the refinement by the Sayyid provide a robust proof.

The Reverse Does Not Hold: When Tadammun and Iltizam Do Not Entail Mutabaqa
Author explains that tadammun and iltizam do not always require mutabaqa, with examples of simple meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify cases where tadammun or iltizam exist without mutabaqa, using examples of simple meanings and necessary concomitants, and explain the epistemological nuance in the author's phrasing.

Core Idea
Mutabaqa is not reciprocally necessary; simple meanings prove tadammun and iltizam can stand alone.

The relationship between the three types of signification is not symmetrical. While mutabaqa often includes tadammun and iltizam, the reverse does not always hold. This passage explains two exceptions: simple meanings (no parts) and meanings without necessary concomitants. We will unpack the examples and the scholarly discussion, focusing on the conceptual distinctions and the debate about certainty versus possibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The matn states 'And no reverse', meaning that tadammun and iltizam do not necessarily require mutabaqa. Find the two cases provided as evidence.
The evidence involves simple meanings and meanings without necessary concomitants.
2
Understand 'simple meaning' (ma'na basit)
A simple meaning is one with no parts, e.g., 'point' or 'whiteness'. Such a word signifies by mutabaqa, but since it has no parts, it cannot signify by tadammun. However, it may still have iltizam if it has a necessary concomitant.
Examples from the passage: 'al-nuqta' (point), 'bayad' (whiteness).
3
See iltizam without tadammun
For a simple meaning like 'point', the concept implies indivisibility. This is iltizam (the word's meaning commits to this concomitant) but not tadammun (since 'point' does not contain 'indivisibility' as a part).
Al-Attar explicitly states: 'commitment does not necessitate inclusion.'
4
Note the epistemological nuance
The author uses 'realization' (tahaqquq) rather than 'possibility' (jawaz). Commentators like al-Attar critique this, arguing that we cannot be certain every essence has a necessary concomitant; it might be only possible. This highlights a tension between logical certainty and linguistic possibility.
Al-Attar quotes Abd al-Hakim on the sufficiency of possibility, but challenges the author's wording.

كَالنَّفْسِ النَّاطِقَةِ عِنْدَ مَنْ قَالَ بِقِدَمِ الْعَالَمِ، فَإِنَّ النَّفُوسَ الْمُجَرَّدَةَ عَنِ الأَبْدَانِ غَيْرُ مُتَنَاهِيَةِ الْعَدَدِ عِنْدَهُ. الدَّسُوقِيُّ (قَوْلُهُ : كَالنَّفْسِ النَّاطِقَةِ) أَيْ : الْمُفَكِّرَةِ بِالْقُوَّةِ، وَهِيَ عِنْدَهُمْ جَوْهَرٌ مُجَرَّدٌ عَنِ الْجِسْمِيَّةِ وَأَعْرَاضِهَا، وَهِيَ كُلِّيَّةٌ تَحْتَهَا جُزْئِيَّاتٌ لَا تَتَنَاهَى، وَهِيَ عِنْدَهُمْ قَدِيمَةٌ بِالنَّوْعِ، فَمَا مِنْ نَفْسٍ إِلَّا وَقَبْلَهَا نَفْسٌ وَهَكَذَا إِلَى مَا لَا نِهَايَةَ لَهُ، حَادِثَةٌ بِالشَّخْصِ؛ لِأَنَّ الْإِنْسَانَ عِنْدَهُمْ قَدِيمٌ بِالنَّوْعِ، وَحَادِثٌ بِالشَّخْصِ وَكُلُّ فَرْدٍ مِنْ أَفْرَادِ الْإِنْسَانِ لَهُ نَفْسٌ. (قَوْلُهُ : عِنْدَ مَنْ قَالَ بِقِدَمِ الْعَالَمِ أَيْ: وَهُمُ الْفَلَاسِفَةُ، فَلَا يَقُولُونَ بِحَشْرٍ وَلَا نَشْرٍ، وَلَا يَقُولُونَ: إِنَّ آدَمَ أَبُو الْبَشَرِ، فَهُمْ كَفَرَةٌ، وَمَثَّلَ بَعْضُ أَهْلِ السُّنَّةِ لِهَذَا الْقِسْمِ بِنَعِيمِ الْجَنَّةِ. وَفِيهِ: أَنَّ الْكَلَامَ فِي الْأَفْرَادِ الْمُوجَدَةِ بِالْفِعْلِ الْغَيْرِ الْمُتَنَاهِيَةِ، وَمَعْنَى عَدَمِ نِهَايَةِ الْجَنَّةِ : أَنَّهُ لَا يَقِفُ عَلَى حَدٍّ، بَلْ كُلَّمَا حَصَلَ شَيْءٌ خَلَفَهُ آخَرُ. وَأَمَّا مَا وُجِدَ مِنْهُ بِالْفِعْلِ؛ فَهُوَ مُتَنَاءٍ، فَالْأَحْسَنُ أَنْ يُمَثَّلَ لِهَذَا الْقِسْمِ بِالصِّفَةِ، جُمْلَةِ أَفْرَادِهَا صِفَاتِ الْمَوْلَى الْكَمَالِيَّةِ، فَإِنَّهَا مَوْجُودَةٌ بِالْفِعْلِ وَلَا تَتَنَاهَى مِنْ وَلَا يَرِدُّ قَوْلُهُمْ : كُلُّ مَا دَخَلَ فِي الْوُجُودِ فَهُوَ مُتَنَاءٍ؛ لِأَنَّ هَذَا بِالنَّسَبَةِ لِلْحَوَادِثِ؛ فَإِنَّ صِفَاتِ الْمَوْلَى الْكَمَالِيَّةِ قَدِيمَةٌ. (قَوْلُهُ : غَيْرُ مُتَنَاهِيَةِ الْعَدَدِ أَيْ : لِأَنَّهُ لَا أَوَّلَ لَهَا حَتَّى تُحْصَرَ فِي عَدَدٍ، وَعِنْدَنَا؛ أَنَّ كُلَّ مَا وُجِدَ فِي خَارِجِ الْأَعْيَانِ مِنَ الْحَوَادِثِ؛ فَهُوَ مُتَنَاءٍ، وَقَوْلُهُ: (الْمُجَرَّدَةُ مِنَ الْأَبْدَانِ) أَيْ: الْمُفَارَقَةُ لَهَا؛ لِأَنَّ النَّفْسَ عِنْدَهُمْ مُدَبِّرَةٌ لِلْجِسْمِ، وَغَيْرُ حَالَّةٍ فِيهِ؛ لِأَنَّ الْحَالَّ فِيهِ عَرَضٌ وَهِيَ عِنْدَهُمْ مُجَرَّدَةٌ عَنِ الْجِسْمِيَّةِ وَأَعْرَاضِهَا. (قَوْلُهُ : عِنْدَ مَنْ قَالَ بِقِدَمِ الْعَالَمِ) أَيْ : وَعَدَمِ التَّنَاسُخِ، أَمَّا مَنْ قَالَ بِالتَّنَاسُخِ؛ فَالنُّفُوسُ النَّاطِقَةُ عِنْدَهُ مُتَنَاهِيَةٌ، وَقَدْ مَثَّلَ الْجَلَالُ لِهَذَا الْقِسْمِ بِمَعْلُومِ اللهِ تَعَالَى وَمَقْدُورِهِ، قَالَ مِيرْ زَاهِدٍ: عَدَلَ عَنِ التَّمْثِيلِ بِالنُّفُوسِ النَّاطِقَةِ الْإِنْسَانِيَّةِ كَمَا هُوَ الْمَشْهُورُ لِيُوَافِقَ مَذْهَبَ الْفَلَاسِفَةِ وَالْمُتَكَلِّمِينَ، فَإِنَّ مَعْلُومَاتِ اللهِ تَعَالَى وَمَقْدُورَاتِهِ غَيْرُ مُتَنَاهِيَةٍ عِنْدَهَا بِخِلَافِ النُّفُوسِ؛ فَإِنَّ عَدَمَ تَنَاهِيهَا مُخْتَصٌّ بِمَذْهَبِ الْفَلَاسِفَةِ.
Like the rational soul according to those who say the world is eternal, for the souls disembodied from bodies are infinite in number according to him. Al-Dasuqi (his saying: like the rational soul) meaning: the potentially intellectual, and it is according to them a substance abstracted from corporeality and its accidents, and it is a universal under which are infinite particulars, and it is according to them eternal in species, so there is no soul except that before it is a soul, and so on ad infinitum, occurring in person because man according to them is eternal in species and occurring in person, and every individual human has a soul. (His saying: according to those who say the world is eternal) meaning: they are the philosophers, so they do not say there is a gathering or a spreading, and they do not say that Adam is the father of humanity, so they are disbelievers. Some Ahl al-Sunna gave an example of this type with the bliss of Paradise. But there is a point: the discussion is about individuals existing in actuality that are infinite, and the meaning of the non-finiteness of Paradise is that it does not stop at a limit, but whenever something occurs another follows it. As for what actually exists from it, it is finite. So it is better to give an example for this type with the attribute, the entirety of its individuals being the perfect attributes of the Lord, for they exist in actuality and are infinite, and their saying does not refute: 'Everything that enters into existence is finite' because this applies to temporal events; for the perfect attributes of the Lord are eternal. (His saying: infinite in number) meaning: because they have no beginning to be counted in number, and according to us, every temporal thing that exists in the external world is finite. And his saying: (disembodied from bodies) meaning: separate from them, because the soul according to them is the manager of the body and not subsisting in it, because what subsists in it is an accident, and it is according to them abstracted from corporeality and its accidents. (His saying: according to those who say the world is eternal) meaning: and the denial of metempsychosis; as for one who says metempsychosis, the rational souls are finite. And al-Jalal gave an example of this type with what Allah knows and what He is capable of. Mir Zahid said: he turned away from the example of the human rational souls as is well-known to agree with the school of the philosophers and the theologians, for what Allah knows and what He is capable of are infinite according to them, unlike the souls, for their infinity is specific to the philosophers' school.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a previous matn statement about 'infinite in number.' It begins by giving an example: the rational soul, according to philosophers who believe in the eternity of the world. Al-Dasuqi explains that the rational soul is a substance abstracted from body and its accidents, universal in species but infinite in individual souls (since each human has a soul and humans are eternal in species). He then notes that these philosophers deny resurrection and consider Adam not the first human, thus they are considered disbelievers. Some Ahl al-Sunna gave the example of the bliss of Paradise, but the author critiques this: the bliss of Paradise is finite in actual existence because although the future bliss is endless, what actually exists at any given moment is finite. Therefore, a better example is the perfect attributes of Allah (e.g., knowledge, power), which are actually infinite and eternal. Al-Dasuqi further clarifies that the term 'disembodied from bodies' means the soul is a separate substance managing the body. Finally, Mir Zahid mentions that al-Jalal used the example of Allah's knowledge and power to avoid the dispute between philosophers and theologians, since philosophers claim souls are infinite only in their own school. The commentary systematically evaluates and refines examples for the concept of actual infinite.

Key Takeaway
A valid example of actual infinite must exist in actuality and have no beginning; potential infinities like Paradise's bliss do not qualify.
