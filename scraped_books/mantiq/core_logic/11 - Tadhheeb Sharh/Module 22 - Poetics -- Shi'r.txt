Imaginative Premises in Poetic Syllogisms
Commentator defines poetic syllogism materials and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative premises in poetic syllogisms and analyze their logical structure regardless of truth value.

Core Idea
In poetry, logical validity yields to emotional effect: even false premises can move the soul.

This passage from the commentary on Tahdhīb al-Manṭiq defines the materials of syllogisms specifically for the art of poetry. Unlike demonstrative or dialectical reasoning, poetic syllogisms employ imaginative premises (mukhayyilāt) that stir the soul through metaphor and simile. The commentator explicitly forbids self-evident premises (awwaliyyāt) because they lack emotional impact. Instead, poets may use premises that are false or even impossible, as long as they follow the rules of language and produce a psychological effect. We will examine the requirements for poetic premises and see how they are structured into syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the requirements for poetic discourse
List the three conditions: speech must follow language rules, contain subtle metaphors or beautiful similes, and have propositions that affect the soul regardless of truth.
2
Distinguish between awwaliyyāt and mukhayyilāt
Note that awwaliyyāt (self-evident premises) are prohibited in poetry because they lack emotional effect, while mukhayyilāt (imaginative premises) are permitted even if false or impossible.
3
Analyze the first syllogism example
Extract the categorical syllogism: Major premise: Every moon has a shirt that splits. Minor premise: Zayd is a moon. Conclusion: Zayd's shirt splits. Identify the middle term (moon) and figure (first). Check formal validity.
The syllogism is formally valid (Barbara) but materially false; in poetry only formal structure matters for the imaginative effect.
4
Analyze the second syllogism example
The second example concludes a contradiction: 'I am both silent and speaking.' Identify the two premises: 'Every concealer of needs is silent' and 'Every revealer of needs is speaking'. Note that the major terms 'silent' and 'speaking' are opposites.
This syllogism is also formally valid but leads to a contradiction; poetry intentionally uses this for aesthetic impact.
5
Generalize the function of imaginative premises
Understand that in poetic discourse, the truth or falsehood of premises is irrelevant as long as they evoke a strong emotional or imaginative response in the listener.

Chapter on the materials of syllogisms. The commentator of Sullam al-`Ulum said: 'In poetry, it is necessary that the speech be in accordance with the rules of language, and that it have subtle metaphors or beautiful similes, and that its propositions be such that they affect the soul, whether true or false. So it is not permissible to use self-evident premises that do not have an effect, and it is permissible to use imaginative premises (mukhayyilat) even if they are false or impossible. For example: Zayd is a moon with a buttoned shirt, and every moon is like that, so his shirt splits, therefore Zayd's shirt splits. And sometimes the conjunction of two opposites is inferred, for example: I am a concealer of needs by tongue and a revealer of them by flowing tears, and every concealer of needs is silent, and every revealer of needs is speaking, so I am silent and speaking. End. And close to that is the saying of Bahaa' Zuhayr: I complain and thank his action, so marvel at a complainer who is from him complaining.'

Commentary
This passage is a commentary by the author (likely the sharh on Tahdhib al-Mantiq) defining the materials of syllogisms specifically for the art of poetry. It begins with the chapter heading 'On the materials of syllogisms.' Then it quotes the commentator of Sullam al-`Ulum who lays out the requirements of poetic discourse: it must follow grammatical rules, employ metaphors or similes, and its propositions (qadaya) must affect the soul regardless of truth value. The commentator then prohibits using self-evident premises (awwaliyat) which do not produce an emotional effect, and permits imaginative premises (mukhayyilat) even if they are false or impossible. Two examples follow. The first is a categorical syllogism: 'Zayd is a moon with a buttoned shirt; every moon has a splitting shirt; therefore Zayd's shirt splits.' This syllogism is invalid in reality because the major premise is false, but it serves poetic effect. The second example is a syllogism that concludes a contradiction: 'I am a concealer of needs by tongue and a revealer by tears; every concealer is silent; every revealer is speaking; therefore I am both silent and speaking.' This demonstrates that poetry can intentionally combine opposites for aesthetic impact. The passage ends with a line from Bahaa' Zuhayr illustrating the same idea. The overall point is that poetic syllogisms are judged by their rhetorical effect, not by logical truth.
