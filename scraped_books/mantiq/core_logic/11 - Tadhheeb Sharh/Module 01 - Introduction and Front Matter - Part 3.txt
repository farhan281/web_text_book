The Author's Humility and Apology
Author concludes with prayer, apology, and purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of scholarly humility in the author's closing prayer and explain why he seeks forgiveness and correction.

Core Idea
A true scholar admits his limitations and invites correction, seeing his work as a service, not a final authority.

The author of this logic textbook closes not with a boast, but with a prayer and apology. He admits his own forgetfulness, his limited access to sources, and his beginner's status in the field. This is not empty modesty; it is a deep scholarly practice rooted in the Islamic tradition of seeking forgiveness and welcoming correction. In this lesson, we will unpack his prayer and the metaphors he uses to understand the spirit of Islamic learning.

Guided Path
1
Locate the prayer
Find the phrase 'جزى الله خيراً من تأمل صنعتي' and the words that follow. This is the first part of the author's closing.
The author begins by praying for anyone who examines his work with kindness and correction.
2
Extract the metaphors
Identify the metaphors 'قلة البضاعة' (paucity of stock) and 'ورهلي في مضمار تلك الصناعة' (my walking in the racecourse of that craft). What do these two images suggest about the author's self-assessment?
These metaphors emphasize his lack of resources and his beginner's pace, not being an expert 'horseman'.
3
Understand the purpose of the work
The author says he wrote the book as 'تبصرة لمن تبصر وتذكرة لمن أراد أن يتذكر'. Explain the difference between a 'tabsira' and a 'tadhkira'.
Tabsira is a lesson for those who see and understand; tadhkira is a reminder for those who wish to remember.
4
Reflect on the scholarly practice
Consider why the author includes 'أستغفر الله من سهوي' (I seek forgiveness from Allah for my forgetfulness). How does this reflect the Islamic concept of accepting fallibility in intellectual work?
This practice is common among scholars, showing that even after effort, one should leave room for correction.

The Purpose of the Work: Lesson and Reminder
Author states the purpose of his book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dual purpose of the author's book as a lesson (tabsira) and a reminder (tadhkira), and connect it to the Islamic concept of beneficial knowledge.

Core Idea
The author writes not for self-glorification but as a humble offering for seekers of truth.

Near the end of his closing prayer, the author states the purpose of his book: 'تبصرة لمن تبصر، وتذكرة لمن أراد أن يتذكر' (a lesson for whoever sees, and a reminder for whoever wants to remember). These two terms—tabsira and tadhkira—are rich in meaning in the Islamic tradition. They show that his goal is not to impress, but to benefit. In this lesson, we will explore the significance of these concepts and how they align with the broader aims of Islamic education.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose statement
Find the phrase 'تبصرة لمن تبصر، وتذكرة لمن أراد أن يتذكر' in the Arabic. This is where the author declares the goal of his book.
The phrase comes after he explains his method: extracting issues from the commentary on al-Risala al-Shamsiyya.
2
Understand 'tabsira'
The word 'tabsira' comes from the root 'b-s-r', meaning sight or insight. What does it imply that the book is a 'lesson for whoever sees'?
It implies that the book provides clear insight for those who have the capacity to understand.
3
Understand 'tadhkira'
The word 'tadhkira' comes from 'dh-k-r', meaning remembrance. How is a reminder different from a lesson?
A reminder is for those who already know something but need to be reminded, perhaps after forgetting or becoming neglectful.
4
Connect to Islamic educational goals
Islamic scholars often emphasize that knowledge should be beneficial (nafi') and directed towards increasing taqwa. How do 'tabsira' and 'tadhkira' serve this purpose?
Both functions—teaching the unaware and reminding the forgetful—are central to the role of a scholar.

The Traditional Closing Formula
Author ends with standard closing formulas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the traditional closing formulas (istighfar, hasbala, tasliya) in the author's conclusion and explain their significance in grounding intellectual work in worship.

Core Idea
Every Islamic scholarly work concludes with praise to Allah, reliance on Him, and blessings on the Prophet, anchoring the pursuit of knowledge in faith.

The very end of the author's closing contains three classic formulas: 'والله المستعان، وعليه التكلان' (Allah is the One sought for help, and upon Him is reliance) and the tasliya 'وصلى الله على سيدنا محمد وعلى آله وصحبه وسلم'. These are not mere formalities; they express the Islamic worldview that all knowledge and effort depend on Allah's aid and that the Prophet Muhammad is the ultimate source of guidance. In this lesson, we will break down these formulas and see how they are used across Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Find the closing formulas
Locate the phrases after the main text: 'الخاتمة والله المستعان، وعليه التكلان' and 'وصلَّى الله على سيدنا محمد، وعلى آله وصحبه وسلم'.
These are often written at the very end of the book, after the author's closing remarks.
2
Understand 'hasbala'
The phrase 'والله المستعان، وعليه التكلان' is a form of hasbala (stating reliance on Allah). What does it mean to say 'Allah is the One sought for help'?
It acknowledges that the author's ability to complete the work came through Allah's aid.
3
Understand 'tasliya'
The tasliya is the blessing on the Prophet. Why do Islamic scholarly works end with prayers upon the Prophet?
It is a sign of gratitude for the Prophet's guidance and a request for his intercession and blessings on the work.
4
Connect to the broader tradition
Consider that nearly every classical Islamic book, from tafsir to fiqh to logic, ends with these or similar formulas. What does this uniformity tell us about the Islamic view of knowledge?
It shows that all knowledge is seen as part of worship and a gift from Allah, and that the Prophet is the ultimate teacher.

Reading the Colophon: What the End of a Book Tells Us
Colophon records completion of printing with editors and date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a printed Islamic scholarly colophon and explain their historical significance.

Core Idea
The colophon is a historical record of a book's journey from author to reader.

This lesson examines the colophon of the Tadhheeb Sharh edition, a concluding note that records the completion of printing along with editorial and publication details. Colophons like this one are valuable primary sources for understanding the transmission of Islamic texts and the scholarly networks involved in their production.

Guided Path
1
Locate the opening formula
Notice how the colophon begins with praise of Allah (بحمد الله تعالى). This traditional opening is common in Islamic colophons and signals the conclusion of the work.
2
Identify the main work and accompanying commentaries
The colophon lists the core text (Tadhheeb Sharh al-Khubaysi ‘ala Tahdhib al-Mantiq wa al-Kalam) and its glosses (by al-Dusuqi and al-‘Attar) and comments (by al-Sharnubi). This tells you exactly what is included in this edition.
Knowing the contents helps assess the edition's scope.
3
Find the names of editors and supervisors
Ahmad Sa‘d ‘Ali is mentioned as the supervisor (مصححًا بمعرفتي). He identifies himself as an Azhar scholar and head of the proofreading committee. This is a mark of authority and reliability.
The phrase 'bi ma‘rifati' indicates personal supervision.
4
Extract the date and place
The date is given in both Hijri and Gregorian: Thursday, 25 Safar 1356 AH (6 May 1937). The place is Cairo. This anchors the edition in time and space.
Dual dating is common in modern printed editions.
5
Note the publisher and press personnel
The press supervisor (Muhammad Amin ‘Imran) and director (Rustum Mustafa al-Halabi) are named. This bibliographic detail is crucial for identifying the printer and edition.
Press names often help distinguish editions.

Understanding the Ijazah: Certification in Islamic Logic
Author grants a teaching license after completion of the commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components and purpose of an ijazah and explain its role in authenticating the transmission of logic texts.

Core Idea
The ijazah certifies mastery and preserves the scholarly chain.

This passage is a traditional ijazah, a formal license granted by a scholar to a student who has mastered a text. Here, the author authorizes his student to teach a commentary on Tadhhib al-Mantiq. Understanding the ijazah system is crucial for appreciating how knowledge was transmitted and authenticated in the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opening praise and its significance
The author begins with a lengthy praise of Allah, highlighting intellectual blessings like knowledge of universals and particulars. This is not just rhetorical; it situates the study of logic within a grateful, God-centered epistemology.
Notice the language of 'kulliyat' and 'juz'iyat' which are key logical concepts.
2
Locate the certification statement
Find where the author states that his 'brother in Allah' has completed reading the book with reflection and mastery. This is the core of the ijazah: a personal attestation of the student's competence.
The phrase 'fa-ajaztuhu bi-tadrisihi' explicitly grants permission to teach.
3
Analyze the advice section
The author advises the student on piety, sincerity, following the salaf, and respect for scholars. This reflects the integration of moral and spiritual qualities with academic certification.
The advice also requests supplication, showing the reciprocal relationship between teacher and student.
4
Connect to the broader tradition
The ijazah is part of a system that ensures authentic transmission of texts and ideas. Without it, the chain of knowledge (sanad) would be broken, and the authority of a teacher would be questionable.
Compare this to modern academic degrees or certificates of completion.

This passage is the table of contents of Tadhheeb Sharh, a logical work. It lists the main sections and topics covered in the book, serving as a roadmap for the reader. The table begins with a discussion of connotation of implication (دلالة الالتزام) and the correlation between types of indication (التلازم بين أقسام الدلالة). It then moves to a section on the study of expressions (فصل في مباحث الألفاظ), covering definitions of compound and simple expressions, and types such as univocal (المتواطئ), equivocal (المشكك), polysemous (المشترك), transferred (المنقول), literal (الحقيقة), and figurative (المجاز). This table of contents shows the systematic structure of the book, typical of logical texts that progress from simple concepts to complex reasoning.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between categorical and conditional propositions based on the table of contents.

Core Idea
All logical propositions are either categorical or conditional.

This table of contents opens with the two fundamental types of propositions in Islamic logic: categorical (حملي) and conditional (شرطي). Understanding this division is the first step in analyzing any logical statement. The entire book builds on these two categories.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two main divisions
In the first line of the table of contents, you see 'أقسام القضيَّة' followed by two numbered items: 1- القضيَّة الحملية and 2- القضيَّة الشَّرطيَّة. These are the two broad classes of propositions.
2
Define categorical proposition
A categorical proposition predicates something of a subject without any condition, e.g., 'Every act is subject to a ruling.'
In Arabic logic, this is the basic assertive statement.
3
Define conditional proposition
A conditional proposition consists of two parts linked by a condition or disjunction, e.g., 'If the prayer is valid, then the ablution is valid.'
These are further divided into connected and disjunctive.
4
Identify the hierarchy
Notice that each main division then has its own subdivisions listed later. This shows the systematic structure of the book.
This hierarchy is typical in Mantiq works.

Subdivisions of Categorical Propositions
Details subdivisions of categorical propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize a categorical proposition by its quantity, quality, and modality based on the listed subdivisions.

Core Idea
Categorical propositions are classified by quantity, quality, and modality.

The table of contents immediately after listing the two main types, provides a detailed breakdown of categorical propositions. This includes divisions by quantity (particular, natural, universal, etc.), by mode of existence (external, true, mental), by quality (modified, unmodified, simple), and by modality (modal vs non-modal). Understanding these classifications is essential for evaluating categorical claims in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify quantity divisions
Find the line 'تقسيم القضيَّة الحمليَّة إلى: مخصوصةٍ، وطبيعيَّة، وكلية، وجزئية، ومهملة'. These are the five quantities: particular (makhṣūṣa), natural (ṭabīʿiyya), universal (kulliyya), partial (juzʾiyya), and indeterminate (muhmala).
Quantity tells us how many individuals are covered.
2
Identify quality and existence divisions
Next lines: 'تقسيم الحمليّة الموجبة إلى: خارجيَّة، وحقيقية، وذهنية' refers to affirmative categorical divided by existential mode: external (khārijiyya), true (ḥaqīqiyya), mental (dhihniyya). Then 'تقسيم الحمليَّةِ إلى: معدولةٍ، ومحصلة وبسيطة' divides categorical by quality into modified (maʿdūla), unmodified (muḥaṣṣala), and simple (basīṭa).
Quality distinguishes affirmative/negative; modification involves negating the subject or predicate.
3
Recognize modal division
The phrase 'الحمليَّة الموجهَةُ' introduces modal categoricals. Then 'تقسيم الموجهة إلى: بسيطة، ومركبة' divides them into simple and compound modals, which are listed in detail later.
Modality adds necessity, possibility, etc.
4
Apply to an example
Take the proposition 'Every fard (obligatory act) is commanded.' It is universal (kulliyya), unmodified (muḥaṣṣala), and non-modal (unless specified).
This exercise reinforces the categories.

Modal Propositions: Simple and Compound
Lists simple and compound modal propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the eight simple modals and seven compound modals listed in the table of contents.

Core Idea
Modal propositions specify the manner (necessity, possibility, etc.) of a statement.

The table of contents dedicates a significant section to modal propositions (الموَجَّهَة). They are first divided into simple and compound, and then each category lists specific modals. In Islamic logic, modals are essential for expressing necessity, permissibility, and other temporal conditions, especially in jurisprudential and theological contexts.

Guided Path
1
Locate the modal section
After the quality divisions, find 'الحمليَّة الموجهَةُ' then 'تقسيم الموجهة إلى: بسيطة، ومركبة'. This marks the start of modal propositions.
2
List the simple modals
Under 'أقسامُ المَوَجَّهَةِ البسيطة', the text lists eight items: absolute necessary, general conditional, absolute temporal, absolute widespread, absolute permanent, general customary, absolute general, general possible. Note that the text shows seven with a . but likely eight including the last two lines.
The eighth may be separate; traditionally there are eight simple modals.
3
List the compound modals
Under 'أقسام الموَجَّهَةِ المركَّبَة', the text lists: special conditional, special customary, temporal, widespread, non-necessary existential, non-permanent existential, special possible. That is seven compound modals.
The table of contents may repeat 'فهرس الموضوعات' in between, but the list continues.
4
Understand the role of modals
Modals express necessity (e.g., 'absolutely necessary'), temporality (e.g., 'absolute temporal'), or possibility (e.g., 'general possible'). In Islamic logic, these help classify propositions in arguments.
For example, 'The world is necessarily created' is a necessary modal.

Conditional Propositions: Connected and Disjunctive
Lists divisions of conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between connected and disjunctive conditionals and identify their subtypes.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions are either connected (if-then) or disjunctive (either-or).

The Five Syllogistic Arts in Islamic Logic
Lists the five syllogistic arts as the first chapter's topics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and distinguish the five types of syllogisms (qiyas) based on their epistemic value and purpose.

Core Idea
The five syllogistic arts are the tools for different knowledge claims: from certain demonstration to poetic imagery.

This passage from the table of contents introduces the five types of syllogisms recognized in Islamic logic. Understanding these categories is essential because every argument you encounter in Islamic sciences—whether in theology, jurisprudence, or rhetoric—fits into one of these frameworks. The demonstrative (burhani) gives certainty; the dialectical (jadali) structures debate; the rhetorical (khatabi) persuades; the poetic (shi'ri) evokes; and the sophistic (safsati) deceives. Knowing which type you are dealing with determines how you evaluate its validity and strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five types
From the list in the passage, find the five Arabic names: al-qiyas al-burhani, al-jadali, al-khatabi, al-shi'ri, al-safsati. Note their order—demonstrative is first, indicating its primacy.
These correspond to the five arts in Aristotle's Organon, but adapted into Islamic sciences.
2
Understand the epistemic ranking
Rank them by certainty: demonstrative (certain from true premises), dialectical (probable), rhetorical (persuasive but not rigorous), poetic (imaginative, no truth-claim), sophistic (fallacious, intended to deceive). This ranking reflects how each is used in Islamic scholarship.
The demonstrative is the gold standard for proofs in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.
3
Relate each to a real Islamic science use
For each type, think of a concrete example: burhani—the proof for the existence of Allah in al-Sanusi's creed; jadali—the debates between Ash'aris and Mu'tazilis; khatabi—a Friday sermon; shi'ri—al-Busiri's Qasidat al-Burda; safsati—fallacious arguments refuted in Ibn Taymiyya's works.

Introduction and Front Matter
beginner
8 min
The Parts of the Sciences (Ajza' al-Ulum)
Lists the parts of sciences as the second major chapter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the three components of any science: subject (mawdu'), principles (mabadi'), and problems (masa'il).

Core Idea
Every science is defined by its subject, principles, and problems; this structure organizes all Islamic disciplines.

The second chapter of the table of contents presents the parts of sciences (ajza' al-ulum). This framework is crucial for understanding how any Islamic science—whether fiqh, kalam, or usul al-fiqh—is organized. The subject is what the science studies, the principles are the foundational axioms and sources, and the problems are the specific questions or rulings derived. This structure allows a scholar to locate any issue within a discipline and see its relationship to the whole.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three parts
Find in the Arabic list: مَوْضُوعَاتُ العُلُوم (subjects), مَبَادِئُ العُلُوم (principles), مَسَائِلُ العُلُوم (problems). These are the three constitutive parts of any science.
The passage also breaks down problems further into subjects and predicates of problems.
2
Define each part
Subject: the entity or domain the science investigates (e.g., in grammar, it is Arabic words; in fiqh, it is actions of the mukallaf). Principles: the foundational premises that are accepted without proof within the science (e.g., in usul al-fiqh, the Quran and Sunnah as sources). Problems: the specific questions or conclusions that the science aims to determine (e.g., whether a specific act is wajib or haram).
Principles are often borrowed from higher sciences; for example, kalam provides principles to fiqh.
3
Apply to a specific Islamic science
Take the science of 'Ilm al-Hadith (hadith criticism). Its subject is the chains of transmission (sanad) and texts (matn). Its principles include rules like 'a chain must be continuous and narrators must be reliable.' Its problems include determining whether a specific hadith is sahih or da'if. This three-part division helps organize the entire field.
This framework is used by scholars like al-Tahanawi in his encyclopedia of sciences.
