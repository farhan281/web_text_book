Refutation of the Objection Against Induction and Analogy in Qiyas
Author refutes the claim that induction and analogy are not 'qawl' and thus not qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the objection against including induction and analogy in qiyas and identify how the author refutes it.

Core Idea
Induction and analogy are qiyas because they yield a composed judgment, not because they are actions.

In this passage, the author addresses a challenge: some argue that induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil) are not part of qiyas because they are not statements (qawl) but actions. The author refutes this, showing that they are indeed included in qiyas. Let's examine the objection and the refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
Find the part of the Arabic where the objector says 'we do not accept that induction and analogy are included in qawl'. The objector defines induction and analogy as actions, not statements.
2
Understand the objector's reasoning
The objector argues that because induction is the tracking of particulars and analogy is the attaching of a branch, they are processes, not composed propositions (qawl). Therefore, they cannot be part of qiyas.
3
Locate the author's refutation
The author says 'And with this is repelled what is said'. The word 'this' refers to the previous discussion (outside this passage) that defines qiyas broadly. The refutation implies that induction and analogy do produce a statement (qawl) because they lead to a judgment about the universal.
The full refutation is not given here, but the author asserts the objection is repelled.
4
Connect to the broader context
Note that the author goes on to discuss immediate inferences ('aks) to show what a non-compound statement looks like, further clarifying that qiyas requires a compound of statements, which induction and analogy can provide.

Immediate Inferences: Simple Converse and Converse of the Contradictory
Author explains the meaning of 'entails its converse' and 'converse of the contradictory'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the simple converse and the converse of the contradictory for any given categorical proposition.

Core Idea
A single proposition directly implies other propositions without forming a compound argument.

In this passage, the author clarifies the meaning of 'entails its converse' by giving two examples: the simple converse (al-'aks al-mustawi) and the converse of the contradictory ('aks al-naqid). These are immediate inferences, not compound syllogisms. Let's see how they work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition being used as an example
The author uses the proposition 'Every human is an animal' (كل إنسان حيوان). This is a universal affirmative (A) proposition.
2
Derive the simple converse
The simple converse of 'Every human is an animal' is 'Some animal is a human' (بعض الحيوان إنسان). This is a valid conversion (A to I). Note that the quantity changes from universal to particular.
3
Derive the converse of the contradictory
The example given is 'Everything that is not an animal is not a human' (كل ما ليس بحيوان ليس بإنسان). This is the contrapositive of the original, formed by taking the converse of the contradictory proposition. In this case, the contradictory of 'Every human is an animal' is 'Some human is not an animal'. The converse of that would be 'Some non-animal is a human'? But the author provides a universal negative, which is equivalent to 'No non-animal is a human' — this is the standard contrapositive. So the 'converse of the contradictory' here is actually the contrapositive.
The text's example is the contrapositive, not a simple conversion of the contradictory. This is a specific usage.
4
Distinguish between the two inferences
The simple converse preserves truth for A and E propositions (with conversion to I and E respectively). The converse of the contradictory (contrapositive) is valid for A and O propositions. Both are immediate inferences from a single proposition, not compound syllogisms.

Complete vs. Incomplete Induction in Islamic Logic
Chapter on induction and analogy; author states they yield only probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete induction (divided syllogism) from incomplete induction and classify an inductive argument accordingly.

Core Idea
Induction yields certainty only when its survey of particulars is exhaustive and every ruling is verified.

This lesson unpacks the definition of induction (istiqra') and its two types: complete and incomplete. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether an inductive argument can yield certainty (yaqin) or only probability (zann). The commentators al-Dasuqi and al-Attar clarify that when the term 'induction' is used absolutely in logic, it refers to the incomplete kind, while complete induction is actually a form of syllogism called 'divided syllogism' (qiyas muqassam).

Guided Path
1
Define induction
Identify the process described: surveying the particulars of a universal to extend a ruling to that universal.
The definition is from al-Sayyid's Hashiya: 'One must survey all particulars, then apply a single ruling on those particulars to extend it to the universal.'
2
Identify the two types
Complete (tamm) induction requires an exhaustive survey; incomplete (naqis) induction does not claim exhaustiveness. The conventional use of 'induction' refers to the incomplete type.
Al-Dasuqi deduces this from the context that the text says induction yields only probability.
3
Check the survey condition
Ask: Is the survey claimed to cover all particulars? If yes, it could be complete; if not, it is incomplete.
4
Determine epistemic outcome
If the survey is certain and the ruling for each particular is certain, the conclusion is certain. Otherwise, it is probable.
Al-Sayyid's analysis: 'If that survey is certain... and if the establishment of that ruling for those particulars is also certain, then it yields certainty.'

This passage opens a new chapter on induction (istiqrā') and analogy (tamthīl). The main text states that both methods yield only probability (ẓann), not certainty (yaqīn). Abd al-Hakim praises this as a refined analysis that clarifies the difference between a divided syllogism (complete induction) and ordinary (incomplete) induction. The Conditions for Certainty in Induction (al-Sayyid's Analysis)
Chapter on induction and analogy; al-Sayyid's analysis of epistemic conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Sayyid's three epistemic scenarios (certain survey + certain ruling, certain survey + probable ruling, claimed survey) to determine whether an inductive conclusion is certain or probable.

Core Idea
Certainty in induction requires both exhaustive survey and verified rulings for every particular.

This lesson focuses on al-Sayyid al-Sharif's refined analysis of induction as quoted in the passage. He breaks down the epistemic conditions: the survey (hasr) of particulars, the ruling (hukm) applied to each particular, and the universal conclusion. Depending on whether the survey is certain, probable, or merely claimed, and whether the rulings are certain or probable, the induction yields certainty or probability. This analysis is praised by Abd al-Hakim as a decisive clarification of the difference between a divided syllogism and ordinary induction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
Induction has three parts: the survey of particulars (hasr), the ruling on each particular (hukm), and the universal conclusion.
Al-Sayyid: 'One must survey all particulars, then apply a single ruling on those particulars to extend it to the universal.'
2
Scenario 1: Certain survey + certain ruling
If the survey is exhaustive (no particular missing) and the ruling on each particular is certain, then the universal conclusion is certain.
This is complete induction, equivalent to a divided syllogism.
3
Scenario 2: Certain survey + probable ruling
If the survey is exhaustive but the ruling on each particular is only probable, the conclusion is probable.
4
Scenario 3: Claimed survey
If the survey is not actually exhaustive but is claimed to be (some particulars ignored), the conclusion is probable, because one cannot rule out a contradictory particular.
Al-Sayyid: 'It yields probability... because the single individual follows the majority, but not certainty due to possible contravention.'

Induction: Definition and Certainty Status
Defines induction and states its probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define induction (al-istiqra') and explain why it yields only probability (zann) rather than certainty (yaqin).

Core Idea
Induction surveys particulars but never guarantees certainty about universals.

The logicians distinguish between qiyas (syllogism), which yields certainty, and induction (istiqra'), which yields only probability. Why? Because you can never examine every individual instance. In this lesson, you will learn the definition of induction and its place in Islamic logic.

Guided Path

The genus places induction under the category of examining particulars, and the differentia specifies the purpose.
3
Understand the epistemic claim
The passage explicitly says induction does not yield certainty (اليقين) but only probability (الظن). Reflect on why: because surveying all particulars is rarely possible, so the universal conclusion remains probable.
This is the key difference from qiyas, which can yield certainty when premises are certain.

Because induction does not produce certainty, logicians treat it as an appendix (لَوَاحِق) of qiyas, not part of qiyas itself. Qiyas is defined as the method that yields certainty; induction, lacking that, is adjunct.
This classification preserves the purity of qiyas as a certain method.

Induction (Istiqra'): Definition, Critique, and Probability
Author defines induction, critiques definitions, and explains its probabilistic nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct definition of induction as a composition of propositions and explain why incomplete induction yields only probability, not certainty.

Core Idea
Induction surveys particulars but never guarantees universality—only probability.

The classical logicians refined the definition of induction through rigorous critique. This passage shows how they evaluated multiple definitions before settling on one that captures both the process and its probabilistic nature. By mastering this, you will understand why inductive arguments in Islamic sciences must be handled with care.

Guided Path
1
Examine the Example
Look at the animal chewing example. The author describes examining particular animals to conclude that all animals move their lower jaw when chewing. This illustrates incomplete induction: a general rule derived from some, not all, particulars.
Notice that the example does not include 'most' – a deliberate omission to highlight the probabilistic nature.
2
Critique the Famous Definition
The author rejects defining induction as 'judgment on a universal due to its existence in most particulars' because that defines it by its result (the conclusion) rather than the process itself. A definition should capture the essence, not the outcome.
The qualifier 'most' is also problematic: conditions in definitions should realize the essence, not merely exclude.
3
Analyze the Preferred Definition
The author proposes: induction is a composition of propositions that include judgment on particulars to establish a judgment on the universal. This highlights the structure: premises about particulars leading to a universal conclusion.
Relative particulars (juz'iyyat idafiyya) are those subsumed under a universal, whether real or not.
4
Understand the Epistemological Limit
Incomplete induction does not yield certainty (yaqin) because there may exist an unexamined particular whose judgment contradicts what was examined. This is why inductive conclusions are probabilistic.
Certainty would require complete induction (istiqra' tamm) which examines every instance—rarely possible.

Analogy is not a metaphor—it is a structured argument transferring a judgment via a shared cause.

Analogy (tamthil) is often misunderstood as mere comparison, but the logicians define it as a rigorous argument: 'a statement of the participation of one particular with another.' Defending the Certainty of Analogical Arguments
Defense of analogy's certainty and rebuttal of critics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why analogy can yield certainty and respond to common criticisms using the passage's reasoning.

Core Idea
Analogy's certainty depends on the certainty of its premises, not on some inherent weakness in the form.

Many critics, including some logicians, have dismissed analogy as inherently weak. This passage vigorously defends analogy: if its premises (the existence of the 'illa and its presence in the new case) are certain, the conclusion is certain. The author goes so far as to say that anyone who doubts a certain analogy is not sane. We will unpack this defense and see how it relates to the standard logical framework.

The author states: 'فإنَّه راجع إلى القياس' – analogy goes back to syllogism. This means tamthil is not a separate form but can be expressed as a categorical syllogism. How does that redeem it?
If tamthil is a qiyas, it inherits the validity conditions of a syllogism.

The Structure of Tamthil (Analogy) and Its Syllogistic Form
Defines the components of analogy by mapping them to categorical syllogism parts with a fiqh example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four components of tamthil (al-asghar, al-shabih, al-akbar, al-awsat) and reconstruct the analogical argument as a categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
Tamthil is analogy reduced to syllogism: the common attribute becomes the middle term.

In this passage, the commentator explains how tamthil (analogical reasoning) can be translated into a categorical syllogism. The example of nabidh (date-wine) and khamr (grape-wine) is used to illustrate the components: the minor term (al-asghar), the analogue (al-shabih), the major term (al-akbar), and the middle term (al-awsat). Understanding this mapping is crucial for evaluating the logical force of analogical arguments in fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particulars
The analogy compares two particular things: the first particular (al-juz'i al-awwal) and the second particular (al-juz'i al-akhar). In the example, these are nabidh and khamr.
2
Find the common attribute ('illa)
What attribute is shared that grounds the analogy? Intoxication (al-iskar) is the 'illa (cause) that justifies transferring the ruling of prohibition from khamr to nabidh.
The 'illa must entail the ruling, not just any attribute.
3
Map to syllogistic terms
Recognize that the first particular becomes the minor term (asghar), the second becomes the analogue (shabih), the ruling becomes the major term (akbar), and the common attribute becomes the middle term (awsat). The syllogism is: Nabidh is intoxicating; every intoxicant is prohibited; therefore nabidh is prohibited.
The mutakallimun call these: al-gha'ib (absent), al-shahid (witness), and al-awsat.
4
Analyze the certainty condition
Note the commentator's warning: tamthil usually yields only probability unless the common attribute is certainly established and no particularities of the original or branch are conditions or hindrances. This is why tamthil is not divided into certain and probable like induction.
Attaining certainty about these conditions is very difficult, as al-Siyalkuti noted.

The Epistemological Limitation of Dawaran (Correlation)
Explains dawaran definition and its failure to yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why dawaran (correlation) does not yield certainty about the cause, using the concepts of juz' al-illa and shart musawi.

Core Idea
Correlation alone cannot distinguish cause from condition.

In qiyas (analogical reasoning), jurists often rely on correlation to identify the 'illa (cause) of a ruling. However, as al-Dasuqi explains in his commentary, dawaran is not sufficient for certainty because other factors—such as a part of the cause or an equal condition—also correlate perfectly with the ruling. This lesson unpacks that critique.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of dawaran
Dawaran is defined in the matn as the conjunction of a thing with another in existence and non-existence. That is, when the candidate cause is present, the ruling is present; when absent, the ruling is absent.
This is a basic correlation.
2
Identify the critique: confounding factors
Al-Dasuqi points out that the last part of the cause (juz' al-illa) and a condition equal to the cause (shart musawi) both correlate with the ruling in the same way as the cause itself.
Thus, correlation alone cannot single out the true cause.
3
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider the ruling that wine is prohibited. The intoxication property correlates with prohibition. But so does the property of being a liquid (a condition). So dawaran alone would not tell us which is the cause.
This shows the need for additional evidence.

The Epistemological Weakness of Tardid (Division)
Explains tardid and its failure to yield certainty due to non-exhaustive division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the method of tardid for identifying the cause and identify why incomplete enumeration undermines certainty.

Core Idea
Division can only yield certainty if the enumeration is exhaustive.

Tardid (also called sibr wa taqsim) is a method of identifying the cause by listing all possible candidates and eliminating those that do not apply. Al-Dasuqi explains that this method fails to produce certainty when the division is not exhaustive, leaving the possibility that the true cause is omitted. This lesson examines that limitation.

Guided Path
1
Define tardid
Tardid is the method of dividing possible causes and excluding those that do not fit, leaving one remaining as the cause.
Also known as sibr wa taqsim in usul al-fiqh.
2
Understand the critique
Al-Dasuqi states that tardid does not yield certainty because the division may not be exhaustive (ghayr hasir). It is always possible that the true cause is among the unlisted possibilities.
The strength of the argument depends on the completeness of the enumeration.
3
Apply to a fiqh example
Suppose one argues that the cause of prohibition of khamr is either intoxication or being a liquid. Eliminating liquid because non-intoxicating liquids are not prohibited leaves intoxication as the cause. But if there is a third unconsidered possibility (e.g., being a byproduct of grapes), the conclusion is not certain.
The example shows the need for a comprehensive survey.

الانتقاض بالواجب، فتعيَّنَ الأَوَّلُ، وَكِلا الوجهَيْن ضَعِيفٌ، أَمَّا الأَوَّلُ فَلِأَنَّ الاقتران وجوداً وعدماً في بعض الصُّورِ لا يفيد العليَّةَ، وَفِي جَميعِها إِنَّما يكونُ باستقراء تام، وهو مُتعذَّرُ أو متعسّر، وأمَّا الثَّاني؛ فَلِأَنَّ التَّقسيم غيرُ حاصرٍ، فيجوزُ أن تكونَ العِلَّةُ غيرَ مَا ذكرَ، وَلِلقومِ هَهُنا تقسيم يتضمن الفرق بين القياس والاستقراء والتمثيل بوجةٍ غيرِ مَا ذكر، وهو أنَّ الاستدلال إنْ كان بحال الكلّى على حال الجزئيّ ؛ فهو حال القياس، وإنْ كانَ بحال الجزئي على حالِ الكُلِّيَّ؛ فهو الاستقراءُ، وإنْ كانَ بحال الجزئي على حالِ الجزئيّ ؛ فهو التَّمثيل، وأمَّا الاستدلال بحالِ الكَليّ على حالِ الكليّ ؛ فهوَ احتمال عقلي لا يقدح في الحصرِ الاستقرائي.
Chapter: On Induction and Analogy. al-Dasuqi, al-Attar. 'Refutation by the necessary, so the first is determined. Both methods are weak: as for the first, because correlation in existence and non-existence in some cases does not indicate causality, and for all cases it would require a complete induction which is impossible or difficult; as for the second, because the division is not exhaustive, so it is possible that the cause is other than what was mentioned. And here the scholars have a division that contains the difference between qiyas, istiqra', and tamthil in a manner other than what was mentioned: that if the inference is from a universal state to a particular state, that is qiyas; if from a particular state to a universal state, that is istiqra'; if from a particular state to a particular state, that is tamthil; as for inference from a universal state to a universal state, that is an intellectual possibility that does not invalidate the inductive enumeration.'

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter on induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil). The author first presents a critique of the two previously discussed methods (coextension and elimination). He notes that refuting by the necessary (al-intiqad bi al-wajib) leads to determining the first (the cause?), but both methods are weak. Coextension (first method) only indicates causality if it is found in all cases, which requires complete induction, which is impossible or difficult. Elimination (second method) is weak because the division of possible causes may not be exhaustive, so the real cause could be something else. Then the author presents an alternative classification by 'the scholars' (al-qawm) that distinguishes qiyas, istiqra', and tamthil based on the logical direction: if reasoning goes from universal to particular, it is qiyas (syllogism); if from particular to universal, it is istiqra' (induction); if from particular to particular, it is tamthil (analogy). Inference from universal to universal is merely a rational possibility and does not affect the exhaustive induction. Thus the lesson provides both a critique of the earlier methods and a clear definition of the three types of reasoning.

Key Takeaway
The three fundamental types of inference are distinguished solely by whether the reasoning moves from universal to particular, particular to universal, or particular to particular.
