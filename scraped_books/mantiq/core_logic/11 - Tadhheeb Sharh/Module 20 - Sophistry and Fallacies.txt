The Liar Paradox and al-Dawwani's Solution
Author presents the liar paradox and gives al-Dawwani's solution based on the nature of khabar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Dawwani's solution to the liar paradox and identify why self-referential statements violate the truth conditions of a khabar.

Core Idea
A khabar cannot report about itself; truth requires an independent reality.

The liar paradox is one of the most famous logical puzzles, and Islamic logicians had their own version called 'the deaf root' (jadhr asamm). In this passage, the author presents the paradox and then offers a solution from the scholar al-Dawwani. The key to the solution lies in understanding what a khabar (declarative statement) really is: it is a report (hikaya) about a real state of affairs. When a statement refers to itself, it loses the external standard needed for truth or falsehood. Let's walk through the paradox and see how al-Dawwani resolves it.

Guided Path
1
Understand the paradox formulation
The paradox arises when someone says, 'All my statements today are false,' and says nothing else that day. If this statement is true, then its content ('all my statements are false') applies to itself, forcing it to be false. If it is false, then its negation holds, making it true. Thus, it seems both true and false at once.
This is a classic self-reference paradox.
2
Trace the two conditional branches
The author uses conditional reasoning: if true → false, if false → true. This shows that the same statement leads to contradiction under standard truth assignments. Identify how each branch works and why it fails to give a consistent truth value.
Each branch forces the opposite truth value due to self-application.
3
Understand al-Dawwani's definition of khabar
Al-Dawwani defines a khabar as a report (hikaya) about a real ratio (nisba waqi'iyya). A report is true if it matches that ratio, false if it does not. This definition presupposes that the ratio is distinct from the report itself.
The report and the reported-about must be different.
4
Apply the definition to resolve the paradox
In the liar statement, the report is about itself: the ratio it reports is its own content. But a report cannot be a report about its own ratio because there is no independent external reality to match. Therefore, the statement is not a genuine khabar—it is a meaningless self-reference. The paradox is dissolved, not solved within standard truth conditions.
This is why al-Dawwani says that 'a khabar cannot be a report about its own ratio.'

Commentary
This passage presents the famous liar paradox (jadhr asamm) and offers a solution from al-Dawwani. The author first describes the paradox: suppose someone says 'All my statements today are false' and says nothing else. If the statement is true, then its predicate (kadhib) applies to its subject (kalami), so it must be false. Thus, if true, it is false. If it is false, then the predicate does not apply, so it must be true. Therefore, it is both true and false. This is a classic antinomy. The author then notes that many responses exist. He selects al-Dawwani's solution: the essence of a khabar is a hikaya (report) about a real ratio (nisba waqi'iyya). This report either matches reality (true) or does not (false). A report cannot be a report about its own content. The reasoning is that the possibility of truth/falsity depends on the mental ratio's correspondence or non-correspondence with reality. If a statement reports about itself (e.g., 'This statement is false'), then the mental ratio (the content of the statement) is identical to the reality being reported about. There is no independent external reality to serve as the standard of correspondence. Consequently, the statement cannot be evaluated as true or false in the normal sense; the paradox arises from a confusion about the nature of hikaya. The text cuts off before completing the explanation, but the core point is that self-reference undermines the necessary condition of truth/falsity because the hikaya must be distinct from the mahki 'anhu.

The Liar Paradox and Its Resolution by al-Dawani
Presents the liar paradox and al-Dawani's solution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the liar paradox (al-jidhr al-asamm) and explain al-Dawani's solution that a khabar cannot be about itself.

Core Idea
Self-referential statements cannot be genuine khabar because they represent no external reality.

In Islamic logic, the liar paradox (mughalatat al-jidhr al-asamm) has been a classic challenge. If someone says 'All my speech today is false' and says nothing else, a contradiction arises. The great scholar al-Dawani resolved this by analyzing the nature of declarative statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paradox structure
The statement 'All my speech today is false' leads to a contradiction if it is the only speech. If true, then it must be false; if false, then it must be true. This is the liar paradox.
2
Understand the core issue
The paradox arises because the statement refers to itself. Al-Dawani points out that a genuine khabar must represent an external relation, not itself.
3
Apply al-Dawani's solution
Al-Dawani argues that a self-referential statement cannot be a khabar because it would require the mental relation to agree with its own non-existence, which is impossible. Instead, such a statement is either meaningless or a performative (insha').
4
Compare with alternative solutions
The passage mentions other responses, including splitting the statement into two (e.g., 'my speech is false, false'). Al-Dawani also offers that solution, which allows for one part true and the other false.

Commentary
This passage presents the famous liar paradox (mughalatat al-jidhr al-asamm) and its analysis by several scholars, focusing on the answer of al-Dawani. The paradox is: If someone says 'All my speech today is false' and says nothing else, then if the statement is true, what it says must be the case, so it is false; if it is false, then its negation holds, so it is true. Thus it is both true and false, a contradiction. Al-Dawani's solution: The essence of a declarative statement is to represent (hikaya) an actual relation. A statement cannot represent itself because that would require the mental relation to agree with its own non-existence, which is impossible. A self-referential statement is not a genuine khabar; it has no meaningful content because the intended representation loops back on itself. Al-Khalikhali adds that truth and falsehood are properties of assents meant to represent reality; if no representation is intended, they do not apply. Mir Zahid explains that the subject of a judgment must be independent and precede the judgment, but a self-referential statement lacks independence. If considered a complete utterance, it would be a performative (insha') disguised as a declarative. Finally, al-Dawani offers another answer: the statement is equivalent to 'my speech is false, false,' which splits into two statements, one part and the whole, allowing for one true and one false. This passage is critical for understanding the conditions of declarative statements and the limits of self-reference in Islamic logic. For example, the statement 'This very statement is false' cannot be a valid khabar because it cannot represent an external reality; it would be like 'Wine is prohibited' which is a valid khabar because it represents a real divine ruling.

Key Terms
مغالطة الجذر الأصم (mughalatat al-jidhr al-asamm): The liar paradox; a self-referential statement that leads to contradiction. [Note: Also known as the 'solid root' fallacy in the tradition; equivalent to modern liar paradox.]
خبر (khabar): A declarative statement that represents an actual relation and can be true or false. [Note: Standard; here it is contrasted with insha' (performative).]
تصديق (tasdiq): The mental form (sura dhihniyya) intended to represent reality; the basis of true/false judgments. [Note: Al-Dawani and others emphasize that tasdiq cannot be about itself.]
إنشاء (insha'): A performative utterance that does not aim to represent reality, e.g., command, prohibition, question. [Note: No shift.]

Conditions for a Valid Declarative Statement (Khabar)
Focuses on the definition and conditions of khabar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the necessary conditions for a statement to be a genuine khabar and distinguish it from performatives (insha').

Core Idea
A khabar must represent an actual relation external to itself; self-reference negates its declarative nature.

The liar paradox teaches us that not every sentence that looks like a statement of fact qualifies as a khabar. Al-Dawani and later logicians established that a khabar must be about something outside itself, and the subject of judgment must be independent.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish khabar from insha'
A khabar aims to represent reality and can be true/false; an insha' (command, question) does not.
2
Identify the condition of external reference
The predicate in a khabar must apply to a subject that exists independently. A self-referential statement fails because the subject is the statement itself.
3
Understand the role of tasdiq
Assent (tasdiq) is a mental form intended to represent reality; it cannot represent itself.
4
Apply Mir Zahid's condition
The subject must be independent and conceptually prior.

Also, they mention in this context a famous fallacy called the solid root (al-jidhr al-asamm). It is that if someone says: 'All my speech today is false,' and he said nothing else today, then it follows that that speech is both true and false simultaneously. Because if it is true in reality, then the predicate—which is 'false'—must be true of its subject, which is 'my speech,' so it follows that his speech is false, and his speech is only 'my speech is false,' so it follows that it is false, while it was assumed true. And if it is false in reality, then this predicate cannot be true of its subject, which is 'my speech,' so it follows that this speech is true, because a declarative speech must be characterized by truth or falsehood, and cannot be devoid of both, while it was assumed false. The people gave many answers to this, among them the answer of Allama al-Dawani in a treatise related to this fallacy. He said: The essence of a declarative statement (khabar) is the story (hikaya) about the actual relation; either in a conforming manner, then it is true, or in a non-conforming manner, then it is false. So it cannot be a story about the relation that is its own content. Its clarification is that the basis of the possibility of truth and falsehood lies in the possibility of the mental relation agreeing with its actual existence or not. There is no doubt that if it is a story about itself by virtue of its existence in the mind, as in your saying: 'This speech is true or false,' pointing to that same speech, and it itself is the actual reality told about, then it cannot agree with its own non-existence, because it is impossible for a thing to agree with its non-existence. Therefore, if someone says: 'This speech is true,' pointing to that same speech, it is not a declarative statement (khabar); rather, it has no content. For the relation which is its content does not terminate in representing something in reality, but rather revolves upon itself. Perhaps the secret of that is that assent (tasdiq) is a mental form intended to represent something in reality; so it cannot be a story about itself, for the representation of a thing about itself is inconceivable. Because of this, the possibility of conformity and non-conformity became properties of assents. For if the form is not intended to represent something real, then error and correction do not apply to it. This was stated by al-Khalikhali. Mir Zahid said: The thing told about is the referent of the proposition, and its referent must precede it, so it cannot be imagined to be itself. Also, one cannot judge in this statement about itself, because the subject of judgment must be independent in concept and exist before the judgment. This statement, because it includes the relation, is not independent in concept and has no existence except after the judgment. So this statement, in that consideration, has no meaningful content; it is neither declarative nor performative. If, by impossible assumption, it were a complete utterance, it would be a performative (insha') in the form of a declarative. The performative that is confined to command, prohibition, interrogation and other types is the performative that is not in the form of declarative. Mir Sadr 'Asri al-Jalal al-Dawani answered that this statement is equivalent to 'my speech is false, false,' so there are two statements: one part and the other the whole. There is no impossibility in one of the two statements being true and the other false. There occurred between al-Jalal al-Dawani and him debates regarding the soundness of their respective answers and disputes about them.

Commentary
This passage presents the famous liar paradox (mughalatat al-jidhr al-asamm) and its analysis by several scholars, focusing on the answer of al-Dawani. The paradox is: If someone says 'All my speech today is false' and says nothing else, then if the statement is true, what it says must be the case, so it is false; if it is false, then its negation holds, so it is true. Thus it is both true and false, a contradiction. Al-Dawani's solution: The essence of a declarative statement is to represent (hikaya) an actual relation. A statement cannot represent itself because that would require the mental relation to agree with its own non-existence, which is impossible. A self-referential statement is not a genuine khabar; it has no meaningful content because the intended representation loops back on itself. Al-Khalikhali adds that truth and falsehood are properties of assents meant to represent reality; if no representation is intended, they do not apply. Mir Zahid explains that the subject of a judgment must be independent and precede the judgment, but a self-referential statement lacks independence. If considered a complete utterance, it would be a performative (insha') disguised as a declarative. Finally, al-Dawani offers another answer: the statement is equivalent to 'my speech is false, false,' which splits into two statements, one part and the whole, allowing for one true and one false. This passage is critical for understanding the conditions of declarative statements and the limits of self-reference in Islamic logic. For example, the statement 'This very statement is false' cannot be a valid khabar because it cannot represent an external reality; it would be like 'Wine is prohibited' which is a valid khabar because it represents a real divine ruling.

Key Terms
مغالطة الجذر الأصم (mughalatat al-jidhr al-asamm): The liar paradox; a self-referential statement that leads to contradiction. [Note: Also known as the 'solid root' fallacy in the tradition; equivalent to modern liar paradox.]
خبر (khabar): A declarative statement that represents an actual relation and can be true or false. [Note: Standard; here it is contrasted with insha' (performative).]
تصديق (tasdiq): The mental form (sura dhihniyya) intended to represent reality; the basis of true/false judgments. [Note: Al-Dawani and others emphasize that tasdiq cannot be about itself.]
إنشاء (insha'): A performative utterance that does not aim to represent reality, e.g., command, prohibition, question. [Note: No shift.]

Definition of Estimative Propositions (Wahmiyyat)
Definition of wahmiyyat and explanation of their falsity in non-sensibles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a wahmiyya proposition and explain why it is false when applied beyond sensory experience.

Core Idea
Wahmiyyat are false only when the wahm exceeds its domain; in sensibles, the wahm is truthful.

In this passage, the author defines one of the two materials of sophistical syllogisms: estimative propositions (al-wahmiyyat). Understanding what makes a proposition wahmiyya is crucial because these propositions appear convincing but are logically invalid. The commentary clarifies an important nuance: the estimative faculty (wahm) is not always mistaken; it is only false when it extends its judgments beyond the domain of sensible objects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify wahmiyyat (imaginary propositions) and explain why they are false only in non-sensible matters, distinguishing them from valid sensory judgments.

Core Idea
The wahm is truthful in sensibles but misleads in intelligibles—so wahmiyyat are false only when they exceed their domain.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of the sophistical syllogism and one of its components: wahmiyyat. The text explains that the estimative faculty (wahm) produces false propositions when it judges in non-sensible matters, and it provides examples to clarify when its judgments are trustworthy and when they are not. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and genus
Find what is being defined (the sophistical syllogism and wahmiyyat) and the broad category they belong to (syllogism composed of deceptive premises, and false propositions respectively).

Analyze the domain condition
Understand why the author restricts wahmiyyat to non-sensibles: the wahm is a bodily faculty derived from sense perception, so it is truthful in sensory matters but false in purely intelligible matters.

Author explains the fallacy of the imagination regarding infinite space beyond the world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the imagination (al-wahm) is causing a false premise to appear necessary, and apply the method of pure reason to refute it.

Core Idea
The imagination can make falsehoods seem necessary; only pure reason can uncover the error.

In this passage, the author diagnoses a common epistemological fallacy: the imagination (al-wahm) presents a false proposition as if it were a necessary truth. The specific example is the belief that there is infinite space beyond the world. The author and commentators explain why this belief is false and how pure reason can correct it. Understanding this fallacy is crucial for any Muslim intellectual who wishes to distinguish between genuine necessary truths and persuasive falsehoods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the false proposition
Find the proposition that seems necessary but is actually false. In the passage, it is: 'وراء العالم فضاء لا يتناهى' (Beyond the world is an infinite space).
2
Check if reason disputes it
Examine the proposition using pure reason. Reason says space exists only between boundaries, so there is no space beyond the sphere. Therefore, the proposition is rationally false.
The imagination may still insist it is necessary.

Determine whether the judgment comes from pure reason or from the imagination. The imagination is immersed in material darkness and dominates reason, causing false premises to appear certain.

Gather necessary premises from pure reason that the imagination does not dispute, and deduce the opposite conclusion. For example: 'Everything that exists is finite; the space beyond the world would exist; therefore it is finite.' This shows the original proposition is an error of wahm.
This method is illustrated in the passage with the example of air being a body.

Identifying and Refuting Sophistical Arguments (al-Mushabbihāt)
Author defines sophistical arguments and illustrates equivocation and false tawatur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the cause of error in a sophistical syllogism (equivocation) and apply the method of refutation by distinguishing necessary truths from imaginative errors.

Core Idea
Sophistical arguments masquerade as truth; their flaw is ambiguity in terms or false claims of mass transmission.

Sophistical arguments (al-mushabbihāt) are false propositions that mimic truth, often by exploiting ambiguous terms or fabricating evidence. The key to refuting them is to strip away imagination and clarify meanings. In this lesson, we analyze the classic example of the picture of a horse and the false claim of tawatur about Caliph 'Ali's succession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent logical form
Look at the argument: 'This picture is a horse. Every horse neighs. Therefore, this picture neighs.' Recognize that it has the form of a valid categorical syllogism (Barbara).
The form is valid but the conclusion is false.
2
Spot the ambiguous term
The term 'horse' is used in two different senses: in the minor premise it means a metaphorical horse (a picture), in the major premise it means a real horse. This is equivocation (ishtibah).
The middle term 'horse' is not the same in both premises.
3
Recognize the cause: middle term not repeated
Al-Dasuqi explains that the cause of error is that the middle term is not truly repeated, i.e., it is not the same concept. The syllogism is invalid because the terms are not univocal.
A valid syllogism requires the middle term to be identical in meaning.
4
Apply the general method of refutation
The passage advises: strip reason from imagination (tajrid al-‘aql an al-wahm), reflect thoroughly, and use refutation (naqd). Distinguish necessary truths from errors of the imagination.
This is a spiritual-intellectual discipline, not just a mechanical check.
5
Test against false tawatur
Another form of sophistry is claiming mass transmission (tawatur) for a report that lacks it. The example: the Rafidah claim that 'Ali was appointed caliph, but only four or seven narrated it despite overwhelming motives. Such a claim is false by rational necessity.
Tawatur requires many narrators in every generation; a few cannot establish certainty.

2
Examine the subject of the premises.
In the invalid example, ask: Does the subject 'man-and-horse' actually exist? The passage says no. For the valid example, 'rational animal' does exist (e.g., humans).
3
Check the truth of the premises.
If the subject term is empty, any universal affirmative premise with that subject is false because there is no entity to satisfy the universal claim. The passage states the premises are false due to non-existence of the subject.
4
Contrast with the valid syllogism.
The valid syllogism uses a compound subject that actually exists, and its premises are self-evidently true (part-whole relationship). So the conclusion 'Some animal is rational' is true.
5
Generalize the lesson.
A syllogism's validity depends on form, but its soundness depends on existential import. In Islamic logic, the existence of the subject is a condition for categorical propositions.

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter on the materials of syllogisms and continues the discussion of sophistical arguments based on meaning. The sophistical example is: 'Every man-and-horse is a man' and 'Every man-and-horse is a horse' leading to 'Some man is a horse'. The error is that the subject 'man-and-horse' does not exist—there is no entity that is both a man and a horse. Hence both premises are false due to the non-existence of the subject. The commentator al-Dasuqi explains that in this type, the whole is a compound that does not apply to a single entity. He contrasts it with a valid third-figure syllogism: 'Every rational animal is an animal' and 'Every rational animal is rational', leading to 'Some animal is rational'. Here the subject 'rational animal' does exist, as it is a self-evident truth that the part is necessary for the whole. The difference is the existence of the subject. The commentator al-Attar notes that the invalid example is a third-figure syllogism where the middle term does not exist (i.e., the subject is empty).
