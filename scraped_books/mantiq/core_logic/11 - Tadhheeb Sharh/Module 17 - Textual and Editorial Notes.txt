Tanbih vs Istidlal: Distinguishing a Reminder from a Proof
Al-Attar refines al-Dasuqi's argument, stating the matn is a reminder, not a proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a statement functions as a tanbih (reminder) of a self-evident truth or an istidlal (logical proof), and apply the principle that corruption of a proof does not entail corruption of the proved.

Core Idea
A reminder points to the obvious; a proof demonstrates the obscure.

In this passage, al-Attar refines al-Dasuqi's argument by clarifying that the matn's statement about error in thought is not a proof (istidlal) but a reminder (tanbih) of a self-evident fact. He also notes a critical logical principle: the corruption of a proof does not necessitate the corruption of the proved. This lesson will help you distinguish between these two functions and apply this principle in evaluating arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refined
What is al-Dasuqi's argument that al-Attar is responding to? Al-Dasuqi used the fact of contradiction among rational thinkers as evidence that error occurs in thought. Al-Attar objects to this reasoning.
Al-Dasuqi's argument is that disagreement proves error; al-Attar rejects this.
2
Extract the logical principle
Al-Attar states: 'فساد الدليل لا يوجب فساد المدلول' (corruption of the proof does not necessitate corruption of the proved). Why is this principle important? It means even if an argument is flawed, the conclusion might still be true; disagreement does not automatically prove error.
This principle safeguards truth from weak arguments.
3
Distinguish tanbih from istidlal
Al-Attar says the commentator's statement is a 'تنبيه' (reminder) not 'استدلال' (proof). A reminder points to something self-evident (badihi), observable by all. A proof argues for something not obvious. Here, error in thought is self-evident, so it needs no proof.
Self-evident truths are known directly, not through inference.
4
Apply the distinction
Given the self-evident nature of the fact, the matn is drawing attention to it, not presenting a logical argument. Therefore, objections against the logical form of the argument miss the point.
Critiquing the form of a reminder is category error.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement by al-Attar of al-Dasuqi's argument. Al-Attar first objects to al-Dasuqi's reasoning that contradiction among rational people proves error in thought. He points out a logical flaw: 'corruption of the proof does not necessitate corruption of the proved' (فساد الدليل لا يوجب فساد المدلول). That is, even if someone's reasoning is flawed, the conclusion may still be true; conversely, disagreement does not prove that one party is necessarily wrong in thought — it could be that both are correct in different respects or that the disagreement is in matters of interpretation. Thus, al-Dasuqi's argument is not valid. Al-Attar then reinterprets the matn statement as a 'reminder' (tanbih) of an obvious fact, not a proof (istidlal). The occurrence of error in some instances of thought is self-evident (badihi) through observation, as al-Dawwani noted. The rhetorical question 'كيف' is an expression of amazement at anyone who would deny this obvious reality, not a logical demonstration. Therefore, the matn is not providing a philosophical proof but simply drawing attention to what is plainly observed: people make mistakes in reasoning.

Distinguishing Istidrāk from Istithnā' in Logical Definitions
Critical refinement of the grammatical reading of 'وهو وإن كان... إلا أنه'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between istidrāk (corrective) and istithnā' (exception) in Arabic grammatical constructions within logical definitions and evaluate their function using contextual evidence.

Core Idea
The particle 'إلا' can correct or except; knowing which changes the logical structure of a definition.

In logical definitions, precise wording is paramount. A single particle can shift the entire logical relationship. This lesson unpacks a critical debate over the phrase 'وهو وإن كان جزءاً... إلا أنه ليس تمام المشترك' in the definition of 'فصل' (differentia). By analyzing the conflicting interpretations of 'إلا' as either استدراك (corrective) or استثناء (exception), you will learn to navigate grammatical ambiguity in logical texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Construction
Locate the key phrase in the source: 'وهو وإن كان جزءاً... إلا أنه ليس تمام المشترك'. Notice the use of 'إلا' after a concessive clause.
The construction is common in logical definitions to qualify a previous statement.
2
Understand the Two Interpretations
The annotator (المحشي) claims 'إلا' is for istidrāk (corrective), not a real predicate. An objector argues it is istithnā' (exception) from an estimated negative like 'لا فرق بينهما إلا بهذا الاعتبار'.
The objector cites 'Abd al-Ḥakīm's gloss on al-Muṭawwal as support.
3
Examine the Evidence from al-Muṭawwal
The author (al-'Attar) points out that the objector misquoted al-Muṭawwal. The original phrase matches the sharh's construction, supporting the istidrāk reading.
Always verify quotations when resolving grammatical ambiguities.
4
Determine the Correct Reading by Context
The definition aims to show what differentia is despite being part of the essence. A corrective 'but it is not the complete commonality' fits the logical purpose better than an exception from an implicit negation.
Context and the author's intent guide the correct grammatical function.
5
Apply the Principle
When encountering 'إلا' in a logical definition, test whether it is a genuine exception by seeing if an explicit or implicit negation precedes it. If not, it is likely a corrective with an estimated predicate.
This principle helps avoid misinterpretation in other logical texts.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) by al-'Attar on the grammatical reading of the original definition of فصل. The author of the sharh had used the construction 'وهو وإن كان جزءاً... إلا أنه ليس تمام المشترك' which employs 'إلا' as a particle of استدراك (istidrāk, corrective). The commentator notes that this construction is common, but the words 'إلا' and 'لكن' are not actually predicates (خبر) in the sentence; rather they function as a corrective, although they occupy the position of a predicate, with the true predicate being estimated (مقدر) according to context. Al-'Attar then records that some scholar objected to this analysis using a similar phrase from 'Abd al-Ḥakīm's gloss on al-Muṭawwal, claiming that 'إلا' in such contexts is for exception (استثناء) from an estimated phrase ('there is no difference except...'), not istidrāk. However, al-'Attar points out that the objector misquoted al-Muṭawwal: the original phrase is exactly like the sharh's text, supporting the istidrāk interpretation. Thus al-'Attar defends the grammatical reading of the sharh and refines the understanding of the construction.

Correcting a Textual Distortion in the Fourth Figure Conditions
Commentators debate the correct reading of a phrase relating to the fourth figure's conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and correct a tashif (textual distortion) in a logical condition by analyzing pronoun reference and logical intent.

Core Idea
A single pronoun can change a logical condition; precision prevents invalid inferences.

In the Islamic logical tradition, even the most advanced texts underwent meticulous scrutiny. This lesson examines a debate among commentators about the correct wording of a condition for the fourth figure of the categorical syllogism. The original text read 'wa kulliyyatuhuma' (and their universality), but a closer look reveals that the dual pronoun is misleading. We will follow the reasoning that led to the correction to the singular 'wa kulliyyatiha'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problematic phrase
Locate the phrase 'wa kulliyyatuhuma' in the source. Note the dual pronoun '-huma' attached to 'kulliyya'.
The phrase appears in a condition for the fourth figure.
2
Understand the intended condition
The fourth figure requires that one premise is a universal negative and the other a particular affirmative. Specifically, the minor must be the universal negative and the major the particular affirmative.
Only one combination is productive: universal negative minor + particular affirmative major.
3
Analyze the pronoun reference
The dual pronoun in 'kulliyyatuhuma' would refer to two things. In context, it seems to refer to the two negative premises (universal and particular). But the particular negative is not intended here, so the dual is inaccurate.
The author's original phrase was meant to apply only to the minor negative premise.
4
Evaluate the proposed corrections
Al-'Attar explains that even interpreting the dual as referring to the two negatives is a distortion. The correct reading uses a singular pronoun 'wa kulliyyatiha', referring solely to the minor negative premise. This is supported by Rajab Afandi and found in some manuscripts.
The correction shows the importance of precise wording in logical conditions.
5
Apply the lesson
When reading logical texts, always check pronoun references against the logical conditions. If a pronoun seems ambiguous, consult the logical context and manuscript variants to determine the correct reading.
This skill is essential for studying classical Mantiq works.

This is supported by Rajab Afandi. The passage highlights the careful attention given to precise wording in logical texts, where a single pronoun can affect the meaning.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify textual notes in a logic commentary and evaluate the authenticity of an addition based on transmission criteria.

Core Idea
Not every addition in a manuscript is from the author; later scholars may append notes.

This passage is not about the logical structure of syllogism but about the textual reliability of the very book you are reading. Commentators often checked whether a passage belonged to the original author or was inserted later. Understanding this helps you trust the sources you study.

Detect the third possibility

This shows the commentator's thoroughness in considering all possibilities.
3
Trace the chain of transmission
The passage cites Mir Abu al-Fath and Rajab Afandi. Identify how the addition was transmitted and critiqued through these scholars.
The commentator ultimately dismisses the addition as having 'no benefit'.
4
Assess the commentator's verdict
The commentator criticizes Rajab Afandi's wording ('aspect') and says the proper method is to mention 'probability'. Evaluate the commentator's reasoning for rejecting the addition.
This reflects the scholarly standard of precision in textual criticism.
