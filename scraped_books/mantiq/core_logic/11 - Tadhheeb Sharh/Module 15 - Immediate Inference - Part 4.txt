Definition of Naqid and Its Effect on Conversion
Author defines naqid as obstruction and gives examples with logical variables.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical obstruction (naqd) in a conversion and distinguish between the two types of naqd that affect standard conversion and contraposition.

Core Idea
Naqd is the obstruction that blocks a valid conversion — without understanding it, you cannot know when a conversion fails.

This lesson defines a crucial concept in immediate inference: al-naqd (the obstruction). When converting categorical propositions, sometimes the conversion fails because of a logical obstruction. The author uses a universal affirmative example with variables to illustrate how naqd manifests and distinguishes two types. Understanding naqd is essential for correctly applying conversion rules in Islamic legal reasoning and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find 'al-naqd' in the Arabic. Note its definition here: the obstruction or contradictory that prevents a proposition from converting validly.
The term is used differently in other contexts (refutation), but here it is specifically about conversion.
2
Analyze the example with variables
The example is 'Every J is B' (universal affirmative). Trace: if true, then 'Every non-B is non-J' (contrapositive) is also true; otherwise 'Some non-B is J' would be true, which converts to 'Some J is non-B', creating a contradiction. This contradiction is the naqd.
The example shows how naqd arises when the contrapositive fails.
3
Distinguish the two types of naqd
The passage mentions two obstructions: one that prevents conversion in contraposition ('aks al-naqid), and another that prevents conversion in standard conversion ('aks al-mustawi). Understand the difference.
Standard conversion is simpler (e.g., from 'Every J is B' to 'Some B is J'), while contraposition involves negation.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Take the proposition 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'. Its contrapositive is 'Every non-forbidden is non-intoxicant'. If this contrapositive is false, then there is a naqd. Check the conversion.
This mirrors the logical variables example but with Islamic content.

The Universal Law of Contraposition
Author states the universal law for contraposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the universal law of contraposition to determine whether a given proposition converts by contraposition and to predict the result by analogy with standard conversion.

Core Idea
Contraposition uses the same proof as standard conversion, but the pattern of affirmative/negative is reversed.

In this section, the author presents a universal law that connects contraposition to standard conversion. This law simplifies the process of finding the contrapositive of any proposition: you can use the same reasoning that you use for standard conversion, but you must remember that the roles of affirmatives and negatives are swapped. This insight will save you from making mistakes when converting propositions in logical arguments, especially in Islamic legal reasoning where precise transformations are crucial.

Guided Path
1
Recall standard conversion results
Review the standard conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi) for the four proposition types: A (Every S is P) converts to I (Some P is S); E (No S is P) converts to E (No P is S); I (Some S is P) converts to I (Some P is S); O (Some S is not P) does not convert.
These results are foundational for understanding the universal law.
2
Understand the universal law
The author states: every proposition that converts in standard conversion by a proof also converts in contraposition by the same proof. Conversely, any proposition that does not convert in standard conversion due to an obstruction also does not convert in contraposition due to that same obstruction. This means the set of propositions that convert is identical for both operations.
The 'proof' refers to the logical justification for conversion, and 'obstruction' refers to cases where conversion fails.
3
Apply the law to determine convertibility
Given a proposition, first check if it converts by standard conversion. If it does, then by the universal law it will also convert by contraposition. If it does not, then contraposition is also impossible. For example, the universal affirmative 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' converts in standard conversion to 'Some forbidden is intoxicant', so it also converts in contraposition.
This step saves you from memorizing separate rules for contraposition.
4
Apply the reversal rule for the result
The author warns: 'the ruling of affirmatives here is the ruling of negatives in standard conversion, and vice versa.' So to find the contrapositive, treat affirmatives like negatives and negatives like affirmatives. For the universal affirmative, its standard conversion yields a particular affirmative; but in contraposition, it behaves like a universal negative in standard conversion, yielding a universal affirmative of negated terms: 'Every non-forbidden is non-intoxicant'. For the universal negative, standard conversion yields a universal negative; in contraposition, it behaves like a particular affirmative, etc.
This reversal is subtle; practice with examples is essential.
5
Test with Islamic examples
Practice by finding the contrapositive of 'No act of worship is invalid without intention' (i.e., every valid act requires intention). Standard conversion: 'No invalid act is an act without intention' (E to E). By the reversal rule, the contrapositive should behave like a particular affirmative in standard conversion, yielding 'Some act without intention is not invalid'? Actually, careful: The reversal rule means that the pattern of quantity and quality in the result follows the pattern for the opposite quality in standard conversion. Work through it systematically using the universal law.
Verification: The contrapositive of 'Every S is P' is 'Every non-P is non-S'; for 'No S is P', the contrapositive is 'Some non-P is not non-S' (if valid). The passage suggests that the same propositions convert, but the results differ. Use the reversal rule to derive.

Understanding Contraposition ('Aks al-Naqid) and Its Rule
Rule for contraposition and its relation to simple conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for contraposition to any categorical proposition and distinguish it from simple conversion.

Core Idea
Contraposition flips quality and swaps terms, while affirmatives follow the pattern of negatives in simple conversion.

In the previous lesson, you learned how to perform simple conversion by swapping subject and predicate. Now, the author introduces contraposition (عكس النقيض), a related but distinct immediate inference. The key insight is that contraposition does not simply mirror simple conversion: affirmatives in contraposition behave like negatives in simple conversion, and vice versa. This lesson will guide you through the universal rule and show you how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conversion types
The passage discusses 'aks mustawi (simple conversion) and 'aks al-naqid (contraposition). Note that they are distinct operations: simple conversion swaps subject and predicate; contraposition takes the contradictory of both subject and predicate and swaps them.
In simple conversion, the quality (affirmative/negative) remains the same; in contraposition, the quality flips.
2
Understand the universal rule
The author states: every proposition that converts by a certain proof in simple conversion also converts by the same proof in contraposition. However, a proposition that converts by contradiction (naqd) in simple conversion does NOT convert by that contradiction in contraposition. This means the proof method differs for contraposition.
The 'proof' here likely refers to a logical demonstration (burhan) that justifies the conversion.
3
Apply the quality swap rule
The author gives a practical rule: 'the rule of affirmatives here (contraposition) is the rule of negatives in simple conversion, and vice versa.' This means to find the contrapositive of a proposition, you must consider what happens to the opposite quality in simple conversion.
For a universal affirmative (A), in simple conversion it becomes a particular affirmative (I). The opposite quality (negative) in simple conversion: a universal negative (E) becomes universal negative (E). So by the rule, the contrapositive of A is a universal affirmative (A) with negated terms.
4
Test with an example
Take a universal affirmative from Islamic law: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (كل مسكر حرام). In simple conversion it becomes 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants' (بعض الحرام مسكر). In contraposition, according to the rule, it becomes 'Every non-forbidden thing is a non-intoxicant' (كل غير حرام غير مسكر), which is a universal affirmative.
Verify: the contrapositive has negated subject and predicate ('non-forbidden' and 'non-intoxicant'), and is affirmative (كل... هو...).
5
Check other proposition types
Apply the rule to universal negative (E), particular affirmative (I), and particular negative (O). For example, 'No riba transaction is valid' (E) in simple conversion becomes 'No valid transaction is riba' (E). In contraposition, according to the rule, negatives in contraposition follow affirmatives in simple conversion. Since a particular affirmative (I) in simple conversion remains particular affirmative (I), the contrapositive of E is a particular negative (O): 'Some non-valid transactions are not non-riba'? Actually standard result: 'Some non-valid transaction is not non-riba' which simplifies to 'Some non-valid transaction is riba'? Need precision. The point is to test the rule.
Classical mantiq teaches that the contrapositive of an E proposition is an O proposition, consistent with the rule.

Commentary
This passage defines the relationship between simple conversion (عكس مستوي) and contraposition (عكس نقيض). The author states a universal rule: any proposition that is convertible by simple conversion using a certain proof will also be convertible by contraposition using the same proof. Conversely, any proposition that converts in simple conversion due to contradiction (نقض) does not convert in contraposition due to that same contradiction. This means that contraposition requires a different proof (namely, the proof used for the other quality in simple conversion). The author then gives a practical warning: when testing propositions in contraposition, one must compare affirmatives in contraposition with negatives in simple conversion, and negatives in contraposition with affirmatives in simple conversion. This is because the conversion of a universal affirmative in simple conversion yields a particular affirmative, whereas in contraposition it yields a universal affirmative (like its original). Similarly, a universal negative in simple conversion yields a universal negative, but in contraposition it yields a particular negative. Thus the author emphasizes the inverse relationship between the two conversion types.

Proof by Assumption in Conversion of Special Conditionals in 'Aks al-Naqid
Commentators explain the use of assumption (iftirad) instead of reductio (khalaf) for conversion in 'aks al-naqid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the proof-by-assumption method (dalīl al-iftirāḍ) to convert a particular affirmative proposition in the special hypothetical (al-mashrūṭa al-khāṣṣa) to a special customary (al-‘urfiyya al-khāṣṣa) in ‘aks al-naqīḍ.

Core Idea
The proof by assumption replaces reductio for special conditionals in ‘aks al-naqid.

In the previous lesson, we saw how simple conversion uses reductio (khalaf). However, when converting special conditionals in ‘aks al-naqid, the commentators al-Dasuqi and al-‘Attar introduce a different method: proof by assumption (iftirad). Rather than assuming the opposite and deriving a contradiction, you assume an individual instance of the subject and derive the converted proposition directly. In this lesson, we will walk through their example and see how this method works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
The original is a particular affirmative in a special conditional: e.g., 'Some writer is necessarily not always immobile of fingers while writing.' Note the modality: special hypothetical (by necessity) or special customary (always).
The two special conditionals are al-mashrūṭa al-khāṣṣa and al-‘urfiyya al-khāṣṣa.
2
Understand the target conversion
In ‘aks al-naqid, the conversion is to the special customary. The passage states the result: 'Some immobile of fingers is a writer while immobile, not always.'
This is a contrapositive-like conversion.
3
Apply the assumption method
Assume an individual, say Zayd, such that Zayd is the 'some writer' in the original. Then Zayd is a writer and, due to the original's non-permanence, Zayd is immobile at some time. Then check if the converted proposition holds: is Zayd, being immobile, a writer? Yes, by the same assumption.
The assumption directly constructs the truth of the converted proposition.
4
Compare with reductio
Notice that this method directly verifies the conversion without assuming the opposite. This is why the commentators prefer it here.
Reductio would assume the conversion is false and derive a contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion by the commentators al-Dasuqi and al-‘Attar on the conversion of the particular affirmative proposition in the context of 'aks al-naqid (conversion of the contradictory). The new method is the assumption (iftirad), which involves taking an individual as an instance. The commentators clarify that the author's original explanation in the simple conversion used reductio, but here and in the simple conversion for the negative, they use assumption. They then give an example: if it is necessarily true that "some writer is not permanently immobile of fingers while writing" (in the special hypothetical), or always true (in the special customary), then it follows that "some immobile of fingers is a writer while immobile, not always". They demonstrate by assuming the subject is Zayd: Zayd is a writer by the assumption, and he is immobile by the non-permanence condition in the original proposition. This illustrates the conversion using the assumption method.

The rule for 'aks al-naqid (conversion of the contradictory) extends the range of valid immediate inferences. State the converted proposition

The conversion preserves the particular quantity and the contingent mode.

Given an instance D that is J, if D is not B (because 'some J is not B'), then D is a counterexample to 'every B is J', which is equivalent to 'not some B is J' being true. Since the condition 'B is not always' allows B to be absent in some individuals, the conversion is valid.
The rule works because the assumed individual D ensures the terms are not empty, avoiding empty-term fallacies.

Proof of Contraposition via Reductio
Proof of contraposition rule using reductio

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof of the contraposition rule using reductio ad absurdum and identify the point where the contradiction arises.

Core Idea
Contraposition is validated by assuming the opposite and deriving an impossibility.

This lesson examines a compact proof of the rule for conversion by contraposition (aks al-naqid). The author shows that if the original proposition with non-permanence (la dawam) is true, then its contrapositive must also hold. The proof uses a reductio ad absurdum: assuming the contrapositive is false leads to a contradiction with the original. By following the steps carefully, you will see how the logical chain forces the conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
The original is a proposition with non-permanence: 'Some J is B' (i.e., B holds for some instances of J, not always).
Non-permanence (la dawam) means the predicate does not hold for all subjects.
2
Understand the contrapositive target
The contrapositive of 'Some J is B' is 'Some not-B is not-J'? Actually, the rule is about swapping and negating both terms. The author is proving that if 'Some J is B' (with la dawam), then it is not the case that 'some not-B is J'? Wait, the passage states the contrapositive as the negation of the original? Let's parse: The proof shows that from 'some J is B' (non-permanently), it follows that 'it is not the case that D is J as long as D is B', which is equivalent to the contrapositive form. The exact formulation is complex; for the guided path, we simply follow the steps as given.
Focus on the logical steps, not the exact propositional form.
3
Extract the assumed instance D
Since some J is B, we pick one such instance and call it D. So D is B by the non-permanence condition.
D is an arbitrary individual that satisfies the original proposition.
4
Formulate the key conditional
The proof asserts: 'It is not the case that D is J as long as D is B.' To prove this, the author assumes the opposite: that D is J when it is B.
This assumption is what leads to contradiction.
5
Derive the contradiction
If D is J when it is B, then by simple conversion (since D is a term), B would be J when it is J. But the original proposition says that B is not J as long as J is (i.e., it is not the case that B is J whenever J is). This is a direct contradiction (khulf).
The contradiction shows the assumption was false, thus the key conditional holds.

Proof of the Contrapositive Under Mutual Exclusion
Author proves the first part of contrapositive under mutual exclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof of the first part of the contrapositive using reductio ad absurdum and identify the condition of mutual exclusion.

Core Idea
The contrapositive is not automatically valid; it requires temporal qualification and mutual exclusion.

In this passage, the author demonstrates how to derive the first part of the contrapositive from a particular affirmative proposition with a temporal qualification ('lā dawām'). The proof relies on the assumption that the two terms are mutually exclusive. We will step through the logical reasoning, highlighting the role of reductio ad absurdum and the concept of mutual exclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
The passage begins with a proposition: 'Some ج is ب' with the qualification of non-permanence (lā dawām). Understand that this means there is at least one individual that is ب while it is ج, but not always.
Non-permanence means the proposition is not eternally true; there are temporal exceptions.
2
Introduce a specific individual
Let that individual be denoted as د. So د is ب by virtue of the non-permanence.
This individual is the witness for the truth of the original proposition.
3
Assume the opposite of the desired conclusion
The desired conclusion is 'Some ب is not ج as long as it is ب'. To prove it, assume the contrary: that د is ج as long as it is ب.
This is the assumption we aim to disprove via reductio.
4
Derive a contradiction
From the assumption, it follows that ب is true when it is ج, but the original proposition implies that ب is not true when it is ج (due to mutual exclusion). This is a contradiction (khulf).
Mutual exclusion means that whenever one term applies, the other does not.
5
Conclude the contrapositive
Therefore, the assumption is false, so د is not ج as long as it is ب. Hence, 'Some ب is not ج as long as it is ب' is true, which is the first part of the contrapositive.
The result holds under the condition of mutual exclusion and temporal qualification.

Immediate Inference with Mutually Exclusive Terms
Author explains the inference from two mutually exclusive predicates to the inverse proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the inverse of a proposition with mutually exclusive predicates using the condition of non-permanence.

Core Idea
Mutual exclusivity and non-permanence together allow the inverse to hold in both parts.

This passage explains how to infer the inverse (al-aks) of a proposition when two terms are mutually exclusive over a subject, as demonstrated in the example of 'writer' and 'still-fingered'. The key is to assume a concrete individual and apply the non-permanence condition. We will walk through the steps to derive the inverse and understand why it is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its modal qualification
Find the original proposition in the Arabic. It is a negated universal with 'not permanently' qualification: 'Necessarily, not some B is C as long as it is B, not permanently.' Note the mutual exclusivity condition between B and C.
The original proposition has a 'lā dawām' (non-permanence) clause, meaning the predicate is not always true.
2
Recognize mutual exclusivity between the two descriptions
The passage states that B and C are mutually exclusive: whenever one holds, the other does not. This is crucial for the inverse to be valid. Verify that the descriptions cannot coexist in the same subject at the same time.
Mutual exclusivity is expressed by 'tanāfin'.
3
Assume a concrete subject that satisfies both descriptions at different times
Choose a specific individual (like Zayd) that is actually B (by the subject description) and also actually C at some time (by the non-permanence clause). This concrete instantiation grounds the logical deduction.
The assumption makes the inference tangible and preserves truth.
4
Apply the inverse by swapping the terms while preserving the mutual exclusivity and non-permanence
From the original 'Not some B is C as long as B' and the mutual exclusivity, deduce that 'Not some C is B as long as C' holds. Then, because the subject is actually C at some time, deduce that 'Some C is B actually' holds (the non-permanence part). The full inverse is the conjunction of these two parts.
The inverse mirrors the original's structure but with terms swapped, maintaining the same modal qualifications.

Proving Simple Conversion of Particular Negative with La Dawam Modality
Expansion of conversion rule via assumption and third figure syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof for the simple conversion of a particular negative proposition with la dawam modality using both the assumption method and the third figure categorical syllogism.

Core Idea
A particular negative with actuality (la dawam) converts simply, proven by assuming an instance and using third figure syllogisms.

In this passage, the commentator Dasuqi expands on a rule: if 'some B is not J' is true in actuality (i.e., not always), then its simple converse 'some J is not B' also holds. He provides a rigorous proof via the assumption method (dafil al-iftirad) and shows an alternative using two third figure syllogisms. We will walk through the logical steps to understand and verify this conversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Find the particular negative with la dawam modality in the Arabic passage. Note its form: subject = writer, predicate = possessor of still fingers, modality = 'as long as he is a writer, not always'.
The original is: 'necessarily, some writer is not a possessor of still fingers as long as he is a writer, not always'.
2
State the claimed simple converse
The conclusion claims: 'always, some possessor of still fingers is not a writer as long as he is a possessor of still fingers, not always'. Check that it swaps subject and predicate while preserving quality (negative) and modality (la dawam).
The converse has two parts: (1) some possessor of still fingers is not a writer... and (2) some possessor of still fingers is actually a writer (from the original's second part).

Understand the assumption method
The author assumes a specific individual, Zayd, who satisfies the original proposition: Zayd is actually a writer and (at some time) a possessor of still fingers. This 'hypothesis' allows reasoning from the particular to individual cases.

5

The author presents a streamlined proof: compose two separate third figure syllogisms. The first (as above) yields the first part; the second (Zayd is a possessor of still fingers, Zayd is a writer, therefore some possessor of still fingers is a writer) yields the second part.

Deriving the Contrapositive of a Special Conditional
Author derives the contrapositive of a particular affirmative special conditional using hypothetical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the step-by-step derivation of the contrapositive of a particular affirmative special conditional using hypothetical reasoning and compound syllogisms.

Core Idea
The contrapositive of a special conditional is proven by a proof by assumption using three premises and two first-figure syllogisms.

The special conditional (al-mashruta al-khassa) requires a more complex contrapositive derivation because of its temporal qualifier 'not always'. This passage walks through the proof using a hypothetical individual Zayd. Understanding this derivation deepens your grasp of how immediate inferences work in modal logic and prepares you for advanced syllogistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition and its contrapositive
Note the original: 'Some writer is finger-moving as long as he is a writer, not always'. The target is the contrapositive, which al-Dasuqi states as 'Some non-finger-moving is not a writer as long as he is not finger-moving permanently' and 'Some non-finger-moving is actually a writer'.
The contrapositive of a special conditional is itself a special conditional with a similar temporal condition.
2
Understand the meaning of 'not always' (la da'iman)
In the original, 'not always' means the predicate holds for some instances but not permanently. This is the key to the non-permanence (al-lā-dawām) that allows the derivation.
3
Set up the hypothetical assumption (fard)
Assume a specific individual Zayd as the 'some writer' in the original. Then Zayd is not actually finger-moving by the non-permanence condition.
This assumption is the first premise: Zayd is a writer.
4
Recognize the three premises used in the proof
The three premises are: (1) Zayd is actually a writer (from the assumption), (2) Zayd is not actually finger-moving (from non-permanence), (3) Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not finger-moving permanently (extraneous premise established by reductio).
Premise 3 is proven by rejecting its contradictory.
5
Combine premises in two first-figure syllogisms
First, combine premise 2 (after conversion) with premise 3 to get the first part of the contrapositive. Then combine premise 2 with premise 1 to get the second part. Both use the first figure (Barbara or Celarent style).
The conversion of premise 2: 'Zayd is not actually finger-moving' converts by accidental conversion to 'Some non-finger-moving is Zayd'.

Converting a modal particular negative requires a three-premise syllogism in the first figure.

Al-Dasuqi demonstrates how to convert a particular negative proposition under a special modal condition. The proof is not a simple immediate inference but a complex syllogistic construction involving hypothetical premises. Identify the original proposition

This rephrasing is key to understanding the conversion.

Assume a representative subject
Assume that the 'some writer' is Zayd. This allows us to treat the proposition as about an individual, making syllogistic reasoning possible.

Derive the three premises
From the assumption and the original, we get: (1) Zayd is a writer (by assumption); (2) Zayd is not moving (by the original's non-permanence); (3) Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving always (proved by contradiction).
Premise 3 is proven by showing that if it were false, it would contradict the original.

Construct the first syllogism for the first part
Combine converted premise 2 ('some not-moving is Zayd') with premise 3 to form a first-figure syllogism: Some not-moving is Zayd; Zayd is not a writer as long as not moving always; therefore some not-moving is not a writer as long as not moving always. This gives the first part.
The conversion of premise 2 yields a particular affirmative.

Construct the second syllogism for the second part
Combine converted premise 2 with premise 1: Some not-moving is Zayd; Zayd is a writer in actuality; therefore some not-moving is a writer in actuality. This gives the second part.
The two parts together constitute the full conversion.

مُتحرِّكَ الأصابع ليس كاتباً ما دام ليسَ مُتحرِّكَ الأصابع لا دائماً؛ أي: بعض ما ليس متحرّكاً ليسَ لا كاتباً بالفعل، وهو فى قوَّةِ: بعضُ ما ليس متحركاً كاتب؛ لأنا نفرضُ الموضوع وهو : بعض الكاتب زيد، وحينئذ؛ فزيد ليس متحرك الأصابع بالفعل بحكم لا دائماً في الأصل؛ لأنَّ مفهوم اللادوامِ في الأصل: أن بعض الكاتب ليسَ مُتحرِّكَ الأصابع بالفعل. وقد فرضنًا : أنَّ ذلك البعض زيد، وحينئذ ؛ فزيد ليس متحرك الأصابع بحكم اللادوامِ ، وَلَا شكَّ أنَّ زيداً ليس كاتباً ما دامَ ليس متحرك الأصابع دائماً، وإلا ؛ لكان زيدٌ كاتباً بالفعل حين هو ليسَ مُتحرّكاً .
(قَوْلُهُ: ولا شَكٍّ . إلخ) إشارة إلى المقدّمة الثَّالثة الأجنبية وهي سالبة عرفية عامة، وقد أثبتها بإبطال لازم نقيضها فيبطل نقيضها، ومتى بطل صحت هذه المقدمة وإلا ارتفع النقيضان وبذلك يكون معنا ثلاث مقدّمات : الأولى : زيد كاتب بالفعل وموجبها عقد الوضع بفرض بعض الكاتب زيداً والثَّانية : زيد ليس متحرك الأصابع بالفعل بحكم لا دوام الأصل والثَّالثة : الأجنبية وهي زيد ليس كاتباً ما دام ليس متحرّك الأصابع دائماً والطريق الأقرب أن تضم المقدِّمة الثَّانية بعد عكسها إلى المقدّمة الأجنبية وتركبهما على صورة الشكل الأوّل هكذا بعض ما ليس متحرّك الأصابع زيد وزيد ليس بكاتب ما دام ليس متحرك الأصابع دائماً ، ينتج : بعض ما ليس متحرك الأصابع ليس بكاتب ما دام ليس متحرك الأصابع دائماً ، وهو الصدر من العكس، ثم تضمّ الثَّانية المذكورة بعد عكسها إلى المقدّمة الأولى من مقدِّمتي الافتراض بجعل الأولى كبرى الشكل الأول: هكذا بعض ما ليس متحرّك الأصابع زيد وزيد كاتب بالفعل ينتج : بعض ما ليس متحرك الأصابع كاتب بالفعل وهو عجز العكس المشار إليه بلادائماً ، فالعكس بجزأيه ،صادق، ولم يسلك المحشّي هذه الطريقة مع أنها المتبعة تمشياً مع الشارح رغبة في الاختصار وطريقته في الحقيقة ترجع إليها بملاحظة الضَّمّ المذكور، والتركيب على هيئة الشَّكل الأوَّل إذا عكست المقدِّمة الثانية أو الشكل الثَّالث إن لم تعكسها، وسيشير إلى ذلك المحشّي أثناء تفسيره كلام الشارح . واعلم أن وجوب اطراد قواعد الفنّ تأبى عليهم صحة عكس الخاصَّتين من الموجبة الجزئية هنا والسالبة الجزئة ثمَّة إلى العرفية الخاصة وإقامة الدليل على صحة عكسهما لا يفيدهم؛ لأنه عرفية أو دائماً بعضُ (ج ب) ما دام (ج)
ويلزمُ ذلك أن يكونَ ليسَ مُتحرِّكاً ما دام كاتباً، وهذا اللازمُ مُنافٍ لصدرِ الأصل المفروض الصّحة، وهو أنَّ زيداً مُتحرِّك ما دامَ كاتباً، وما نافى الصَّادق كاذب، فيكون ذلكَ اللازم كاذباً، فيكون ملزومه كاذباً .
وحينئذ ؛ فيصدقُ قولنا : لا شك أنَّ زيداً ليس كاتباً ما دام ليسَ مُتحرِّك الأصابع دائماً ، ثمَّ إنَّ زيداً كاتبٌ من فرضنا أنَّ بعض الكاتب زيد، وحينئذ؛ فيصدق على زيد أنَّه كاتبٌ من الفرض المذكور، وأنَّه ليس بمتحرك بحكم لا دوام في الأصل، وأنَّه ليس بكاتبٍ مَا دامَ ليسَ بِمتحرِّك من قولنا : لا شكٍّ أنَّ زيداً ... إلخ . وإذا صدق على زيدٍ أَنَّهُ ليسَ بِمتحرّك، وأنَّهُ ليس كاتباً ما دام ليسَ مُتحرِّكاً ؛ صدق بعضُ مَا ليس بمتحرك ليس كاتباً ما دام ليسَ مُتحرِّكاً .
وهذا هو الجزء الأوَّلُ مِنَ العكس، وَلَمَّا صدق على زيد الموصوف بكونه ليس بمتحرك بحكم لا دوام في الأصل أنَّهُ كاتب بالفعل من الفرض السابق؛ صدق بعضُ مَا ليسَ مُتحرِّكاً كاتب بالفعل.
وهو مفهوم اللأدوام في العكس، وحينئذ ؛ فيصدقُ العكس بجزأيهِ، وَكَذَا يُقالُ العرفية الخاصة؛ إلا أَنَّكَ تبدلُ الضَّرورة بالدوام، فتأمل. (قَوْلُهُ : بالضَّرُورَة) أي: إن أردت المشروطة الخاصة . (قَوْلُهُ : أَوْ دَائِمَاً) أي: إن أردتَ العرفية الخاصة .
(قَوْلُهُ : بَعْضُ ج ب ظهرَ مِمَّا قرَّرْنَاهُ أَنَّ (ج) في جميع الدليل عبارة عن كاتب (ب) مثلاً، وأما (ب) عبارةٌ عن متحرّك .
معارض بصحة عكس السالبة الجزئية غير الموجهة بدليل العكس بأن يقال: إذا صدق بعض الإنسان ليس بحجر صدق بعض الحجر ليس بإنسان، وإلا صدق نقيضه وهو كلّ حجـ إنسان، ثم تعكسه إلى بعض الإنسان حجر ، وهو خلاف الأصل المفروض الصدق والفساد إنما جاء من نقيض العكس، فالعكس صحيح مع كون المعكوس سالبة جزئية، وقد سبق لنا تحقيق هذا المقام في العكس المستوي فراجعه لتعرف مقدار اضطرابهم وطعن بعضهم في بعض بدون طائل والله الهادي إلى سواء السبيل.
Section: On the conversion of the contradictory of the compatible and incompatible. 'Not moving fingers is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers, not always'; i.e., some of what is not moving is not not a writer in actuality, and it is equivalent to: some of what is not moving is a writer; because we assume the subject: he is some writer Zayd, and then Zayd is not moving fingers in actuality by virtue of non-permanence in the original; because the meaning of non-permanence in the original is that some writer is not moving fingers in actuality. And we have assumed that that 'some' is Zayd, and then Zayd is not moving fingers by virtue of non-permanence, and there is no doubt that Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always, otherwise Zayd would be a writer in actuality at the time he is not moving. (His statement: 'and there is no doubt etc.') is an indication of the third extrinsic premise, which is a universal customary negative, and he has proven it by invalidating the necessary of its contradictory, so its contradictory is invalidated, and when it is invalidated this premise is true, otherwise two contradictories would be raised, and by that we have three premises: first: Zayd is a writer in actuality and its positive is the act of postulation by assuming some writer is Zayd; second: Zayd is not moving fingers in actuality by virtue of non-permanence of the original; third: the extrinsic one, which is Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always. And the nearest way is to combine the second premise after its conversion with the extrinsic premise and compose them in the form of the first figure thus: some of what is not moving fingers is Zayd, and Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always, resulting: some of what is not moving fingers is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always, and that is the first part of the conversion. Then combine the mentioned second after its conversion with the first premise of the two hypothetical premises by making the first the major of the first figure thus: some of what is not moving fingers is Zayd, and Zayd is a writer in actuality, resulting: some of what is not moving fingers is a writer in actuality, and that is the second part of the conversion indicated by 'not always'. So the conversion in its two parts is true. And the commentator (al-muhashi) did not take this method although it is the followed one in accordance with the commentator (al-sharih) desiring brevity, and his method in reality returns to it by noting the mentioned combination and composition in the form of the first figure if you convert the second premise, or the third figure if you do not convert it, and the commentator (al-muhashi) will indicate that during his interpretation of the commentator's words. And know that the necessity of the regularity of the rules of the art prevents them from the validity of converting the two special propositions: the particular affirmative here and the particular negative there to the special customary (proposition), and presenting evidence for the validity of their conversion does not benefit them; because it is customary or always some of (j b) as long as (j). And that necessarily implies that he is not moving as long as he is a writer, and this necessary is contrary to the first part of the original assumed to be true, which is that Zayd is moving as long as he is a writer, and what contradicts the true is false, so that necessary is false, therefore its antecedent is false. Then at that time our statement is true: 'there is no doubt that Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always'. Then Zayd is a writer from our assumption that some writer is Zayd, and then it is true of Zayd that he is a writer from the aforementioned assumption, and that he is not moving by virtue of non-permanence in the original, and that he is not a writer as long as he is not moving from our statement 'there is no doubt that Zayd... etc.' And if it is true of Zayd that he is not moving, and that he is not a writer as long as he is not moving, then some of what is not moving is not a writer as long as he is not moving. This is the first part of the conversion. And when it is true of Zayd described as not moving by virtue of non-permanence in the original that he is a writer in actuality from the previous assumption, then some of what is not moving is a writer in actuality. This is the meaning of non-permanence in the conversion, and then the conversion in its two parts is true. And similarly it is said for the special customary, except that you replace necessity with permanence, so reflect. (His statement: 'by necessity') i.e., if you intend the special conditional. (His statement: 'or always') i.e., if you intend the special customary. Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof of a specific logical conversion: converting a particular negative proposition of the form 'Some A is not B' under a special modal condition (the 'special customary' or 'special conditional'). The author, presumably al-Dasuqi, begins by stating the proposition to be converted: 'Not moving fingers is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers, not always', which he clarifies means 'Some of what is not moving is a writer in actuality' (i.e., a particular affirmative, not a negative). He then sets up a hypothetical: assume the subject is Zayd, and that Zayd is a writer in actuality (from the assumption that some writer is Zayd). From the original proposition, it is true that some writer is not moving fingers in actuality, so Zayd is not moving fingers. The key step is establishing the premise 'Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always'. This is proven by contradiction: if Zayd were a writer when he is not moving, then (by the original proposition that says moving as long as writing) he would be moving when not moving, a contradiction. Thus the premise is true. Now we have three premises: (1) Zayd is a writer; (2) Zayd is not moving; (3) Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving always. Combining (2) and (3) in the first figure yields 'Some not-moving is not a writer as long as not moving always' (the first part of the conversion). Combining (2) converted with (1) yields 'Some not-moving is a writer' (the second part of the conversion). Thus the conversion (which is a conjunction of 'not always' and 'actual') is true. The author then notes that this method is the standard one, and that the commentator (al-muhashi) chose a different route for brevity. He also addresses an objection: the rules of logic would seem to forbid converting particular affirmatives and negatives to special customary propositions, but the proof shows it is possible. Finally, a separate objection about converting simple particular negatives is raised and rebutted: the conversion is valid because the falsity comes from assuming the contradictory of the conversion. This passage thus clarifies a nuanced point in the theory of immediate inference.

Objection to Simple Particular Negative Conversion and its Rebuttal
Al-Dasuqi addresses an objection to the conversion of simple particular negatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the objection against converting simple particular negatives and explain why the rebuttal is valid.

Core Idea
The conversion of simple particular negatives is valid; any apparent counterexample relies on assuming the contradictory of the conversion.

In the concluding part of the passage, al-Dasuqi addresses an objection: if converting particular negatives were always valid, then from 'Some human is not a stone' we could infer 'Some stone is not a human', but this seems to lead to a contradiction. Al-Dasuqi shows that the contradiction arises from the negation of the conversion, not the conversion itself. This clarifies an important principle in immediate inference.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector claims that converting a particular negative without modality (e.g., 'Some human is not a stone' to 'Some stone is not a human') can lead to a contradiction. He constructs a proof by assuming the conversion is false and deriving a contradiction.
The objector uses the same method as the earlier proof to argue against the conversion.
2
Identify the flawed assumption
Al-Dasuqi points out that the falsity in the objector's proof comes from assuming the contradictory of the conversion (i.e., 'Every stone is a human'), not from the conversion itself. If the conversion were false, its contradictory would be 'Every stone is a human', which after conversion would yield 'Some human is a stone', contradicting the original. The contradiction shows that 'Every stone is a human' is false, but that does not make the conversion false.
The objector mistakenly assumes that the falsity of the contradictory implies the falsity of the conversion.
3
Follow al-Dasuqi's rebuttal
Al-Dasuqi asserts that the conversion is actually valid. He refers to his earlier discussion in 'simple conversion' for a fuller treatment. The key is that the inference from 'Some A is not B' to 'Some B is not A' holds, and any attempted refutation uses a premise that is not actually derivable from the original.
This rebuttal preserves the standard rule of conversion for particular negatives (which is valid by contraposition).
4
Summarize the logical principle
The lesson is that when evaluating immediate inferences, one must distinguish between a valid inference and a spurious contradiction arising from misapplying the rules. The conversion of a particular negative is valid because it is a form of contraposition.
This principle is foundational for later work in syllogistic conversion.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed proof of a specific logical conversion: converting a particular negative proposition of the form 'Some A is not B' under a special modal condition (the 'special customary' or 'special conditional'). The author, presumably al-Dasuqi, begins by stating the proposition to be converted: 'Not moving fingers is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers, not always', which he clarifies means 'Some of what is not moving is a writer in actuality' (i.e., a particular affirmative, not a negative). He then sets up a hypothetical: assume the subject is Zayd, and that Zayd is a writer in actuality (from the assumption that some writer is Zayd). From the original proposition, it is true that some writer is not moving fingers in actuality, so Zayd is not moving fingers. The key step is establishing the premise 'Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving fingers always'. This is proven by contradiction: if Zayd were a writer when he is not moving, then (by the original proposition that says moving as long as writing) he would be moving when not moving, a contradiction. Thus the premise is true. Now we have three premises: (1) Zayd is a writer; (2) Zayd is not moving; (3) Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving always. Combining (2) and (3) in the first figure yields 'Some not-moving is not a writer as long as not moving always' (the first part of the conversion). Combining (2) converted with (1) yields 'Some not-moving is a writer' (the second part of the conversion). Thus the conversion (which is a conjunction of 'not always' and 'actual') is true. The author then notes that this method is the standard one, and that the commentator (al-muhashi) chose a different route for brevity. He also addresses an objection: the rules of logic would seem to forbid converting particular affirmatives and negatives to special customary propositions, but the proof shows it is possible. Finally, a separate objection about converting simple particular negatives is raised and rebutted: the conversion is valid because the falsity comes from assuming the contradictory of the conversion. This passage thus clarifies a nuanced point in the theory of immediate inference.

Proof of Conversion for Special Conditional
Proof of conversion for particular negative under special conditional

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that 'Some J is not B as long as J' converts to 'Some non-B is not J as long as non-B' under the special conditional modality.

Core Idea
Modal conversion works by deriving contradictions from the original assumption.

This lesson unpacks the proof for converting a particular negative proposition under the special conditional modality (المشروطة الخاصة). Using the concrete example of Zayd's movement and writing, the author demonstrates how logical deduction forces the converse to be true. Understanding this proof strengthens your grasp of immediate inference in modal logic.

Guided Path
1
State the original assumption
Assume the truth of 'Some J is not B as long as J' under the special conditional modality. For the proof, the author uses an instantiation: 'Zayd is moving as long as he is writing' (where J = writer, B = moving). This is the premise whose conversion we will prove.
The special conditional adds necessity: as long as the subject exists, the predicate holds necessarily.
2
Derive a necessary consequence
From the original, we deduce 'Zayd is not moving as long as he is not writing'. But this contradicts the original (which says he is moving as long as he is writing). By modus tollens, the original’s truth forces this consequence to be false.
The author uses reductio: the falsehood of the consequence implies the falsehood of its antecedent, leading to the 'non-permanence' conclusion.
3
Extract the first part of the converse
From the derived falsehood, we get 'Zayd is not writing as long as he is not moving'. Since Zayd is an instance of 'not moving', we abstract: some non-moving is not writing as long as non-moving. This gives 'some non-B is not J as long as non-B'.
4
Extract the second part of the converse
From the original assumption, Zayd is a writer (since some writer is Zayd), and from the non-permanence we have Zayd is not moving. So some non-moving is actually a writer, i.e., 'some non-B is J actually'. This covers the 'non-permanence' part of the converse.
The converse has two parts: (1) some non-B is not J as long as non-B, and (2) some non-B is actually J (non-permanence).
5
Combine to form the complete converse
The two parts together constitute the full converse: 'Some non-B is not J as long as non-B, with non-permanence regarding actuality'. This matches the form of the original particular negative under modal conditions.
The proof uses the special conditional; for special customary, replace necessity with permanence.

Extension to special customary and objection to unmodalized conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to adapt the proof for the special customary modality and evaluate al-Sharnubi's objection defending conversion of unmodalized particular negatives.

Core Idea
The same proof structure works for permanence, and even unmodalized conversion can be defended by contradiction.

Having seen the proof for the special conditional, this lesson shows how to extend it to the special customary by substituting 'permanence' for 'necessity'. Then we examine a critical note by al-Sharnubi that defends the unconditional conversion of particular negatives (e.g., 'Some human is not a stone') against skeptics, using a parallel proof-by-contradiction. Take the same proof as before, but replace the concept of 'necessity' (in 'as long as') with 'permanence'. The logical structure remains identical: derive a contradiction, extract the two parts. The converse will now have a 'non-permanence' part that relates to actuality rather than necessity.

Understand al-Sharnubi's objection
Al-Sharnubi argues that even an unmodalized particular negative (e.g., 'Some human is not a stone') converts to 'Some stone is not a human'. He proposes: if that converse were false, its contradictory 'every stone is a human' would be true, which then converts to 'some human is a stone', contradicting the original premise. Therefore the converse must be true.
This is a direct proof by contradiction (reductio) that does not rely on modalities.
3
Compare the modal and unmodalized cases
Both proofs use the same logical tool: assuming the converse false leads to a contradiction with the original assumption. However, the modal case requires stating the 'as long as' condition in both original and converse, while the unmodalized case works with simple categoricals.
Al-Sharnubi's proof shows that conversion holds even without modal restrictions, contrary to those who might deny it.
4
Assess the strength of the objection
The objection assumes the validity of converting a universal affirmative ('every stone is a human' to 'some human is a stone') which is a standard rule. The chain of reasoning is valid if we accept the basic conversion rules. This demonstrates that the conversion of particular negative is not controversial if we accept the standard logical framework.
Al-Sharnubi references 'the standard conversion' (العكس المستوي) showing the debate is about foundational assumptions.

Contraposition Under Non-Permanence: Construction and Correction
Author introduces contraposition and begins proof for its two types under the non-permanence modality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contrapositive of a particular affirmative proposition under the non-permanence modality and identify the compatible type.

Core Idea
Contraposition swaps negated terms; under non-permanence, 'Some C is B' yields 'Some non-B is non-C' conditionally.

This lesson unpacks the first proof of contraposition from al-Tahdhib. The author shows how a particular affirmative proposition 'Some C is B' under the modal condition 'not always' (non-permanence) gives rise to a contrapositive. We will follow the logical steps, note the subtle correction by al-Sharnubi, and see how this tool is used in Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
The original proposition is a particular affirmative with both terms restricted: 'Some (C) is (B)' under the modality of non-permanence (لا دائماً). This means there is at least one instance of C that is B, but not all C are B; some C are not B.
The modality is key: it is not a permanent universal.
2
Apply the non-permanence condition
Non-permanence implies that some C are actually not B. The author designates that 'some' as (D). So (D) is a specific individual that is C but not B (actually).
The variable (د) represents an instance of 'some C that is not B'.
3
Construct the contrapositive
Contraposition involves negating both subject and predicate and swapping them. From 'Some C is B', we get 'Some non-B is non-C'. But because of the modal condition, we must add 'as long as non-B is not always', meaning some non-B is non-C at least sometimes.
The Arabic: 'فبعضُ مَا ليسَ (ب) ليسَ (ج) ، ما دام ليس (ب) لا دائماً'.
4
Incorporate al-Sharnubi's correction
Al-Sharnubi notes that the text mistakenly says 'proposition of the original' when it should say 'proposition of the converse'. The original is indeed 'Some C is B' (particular affirmative), but the contrapositive is not the original; it's the converse (the resulting proposition). This corrects a potential confusion in reading.
This is a critical refinement: always distinguish the source proposition from the derived one.

Conversion by Contraposition for Particular Negative Propositions
Author defines and proves the rule for 'aks al-naqid al-muwafiq of a particular negative (not-always) proposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert a particular negative proposition with modality 'not always' into its valid contrapositive using the rule of 'aks al-naqid al-muwafiq.

Core Idea
The contrapositive of “Not always: some J is not B” is “Not always: some not-B is not J, as long as it is not B.”

This passage from the Tadhheeb teaches the rule for conversion by contraposition (al-'aks bi al-naqid) for a specific type of proposition: the particular negative with the modal condition 'not always' (la da'iman). While standard contraposition of a particular negative is invalid in categorical logic, the author shows how a qualified conditional converse is valid here. You will learn to apply this rule and see how it is proved by reductio.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Note the modality 'not always' and the form 'some J is not B'. This is a particular negative with a temporal condition.
The original is 'la da'iman: ba'd J laysa B'.
2
State the converse by contraposition
According to 'aks al-naqid al-muwafiq, the converse is 'la da'iman: ba'd laysa B laysa J, ma dam laysa B'.
The converse is also a particular negative with a temporal clause 'as long as it is not B'.
3
Understand the reductio proof structure
The author assumes a particular individual D that is a J actually not B (due to the non-permanence of the original).
This D is a witness that some J is not B in actuality.
4
Follow the contradiction
Assume that D is J while it is not B. Then derive that D would be both B and not B as long as it is J, contradicting the original proposition.
The original implies D is B as long as it is J, because if D is J and not B, then by the original D is B? Actually careful: original says some J is not B, but not necessarily all J. The proof uses a specific condition from the modality.
5
Conclude the validity of the converse
Since the assumption leads to contradiction, the converse must hold: D is not J as long as it is not B, and this holds for any such D, so the converse is true.
Thus the conversion by contraposition is valid for this type of proposition.

Proof of the First Part of Contrapositive Conversion Using Syllogisms
Al-Desouqi and al-Attar explain the proof of the first part of contrapositive conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the syllogistic proof that derives the first part of the contrapositive conversion from the original conditional proposition.

Core Idea
The contrapositive conversion is proven by assuming the opposite and deriving a contradiction using a hypothetical syllogism.

In this lesson, we examine how later logicians like al-Desouqi and al-Attar refined the proof for the first part of the contrapositive conversion. The proof cleverly uses an external conditional proposition and a categorical syllogism in the third figure to derive the contrapositive. We will walk through the logical steps, identifying the propositions and the syllogistic form.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original and the goal
The original is a conditional: 'Whenever Zayd is a writer, he is moving.' The goal is to prove the contrapositive: 'Some non-moving things are non-writers (as long as they are non-moving)' and 'Some non-moving things are actually writers.' We focus on the first part.
2
Introduce the external proposition
The external proposition is 'Zayd is not a writer as long as he is not moving.' This is an external conditional that will be shown true.
3
Derive the truth of the external proposition by contradiction
Assume the external proposition is false. Then its contradictory ('Zayd is actually a writer when he is not moving') is true. From that, we derive 'Zayd is not moving as long as he is a writer,' which contradicts the original. Hence the external proposition is true.
4
Combine the external proposition with a premise to get the first part via third-figure syllogism
The true external proposition and the fact that Zayd is not moving (taken from the original non-permanence) are combined in a third-figure syllogism: Minor: Zayd is not moving; Major: Zayd is not a writer as long as not moving; Conclusion: Some non-moving are not writers. This is the first part.
5
Reduce the syllogism to the first figure
The third-figure syllogism is reduced to the first figure by converting the minor premise. This shows the systematic nature of syllogistic deduction.

Two Parts of the Inverse of the Contradictory
Author defines the inverse of the contradictory with two parts and non-permanence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two parts of the inverse of the contradictory and explain the role of non-permanence (la dawam) in deriving them.

Core Idea
The inverse of the contradictory has two parts: one conditional, one unconditional.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of the inverse of the contradictory (ʿaks al-naqīḍ), a specialized form of immediate inference that involves conditional propositions. The author breaks down the inference into two parts, each derived from a different premise about the subject. Understanding these parts and the concept of non-permanence (al-lā dawām) is crucial for applying this logical tool correctly, especially in contexts where predicates are not always true of their subjects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premises
The passage states two premises about a subject (d): (i) it is not (b), and (ii) it is not (c) as long as it is not (b). Additionally, it is true that (d) is (c) in actuality. These are the raw materials for the inverse.
The first premise is conditional (as long as), the second is unconditional (in actuality).
2
Derive the first part of the inverse
From the conditional premise 'it is not (c) as long as it is not (b)', the first part of the inverse follows: 'some of what is not (b) is not (c) as long as it is not (b)'. This part preserves the conditional clause (as long as) and is the first component of the complete inverse.
The quantifier 'some' (baʿḍ) is used because the inference is from a particular subject (d) to a general statement about some non-b things.
3
Derive the second part and understand non-permanence
From the unconditional premise '(d) is (c) in actuality', the second part follows: 'some of what is not (b) is (c) in actuality'. This part introduces the concept of non-permanence (al-lā dawām), because the predicate (c) does not hold permanently for all non-b subjects—it holds actually for some, but not necessarily for all or always.
The second part is unconditional and reflects that the inverse has an aspect of actuality, not just conditionality.

The Concept of Non-Permanence in the Converse
Dasuqi and al-'Attar explain the concept of non-permanence in the converse and its syllogistic form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of non-permanence in the converse and reconstruct the third-figure syllogism used to derive it.

Core Idea
The converse is not always true; its non-permanence is derived from a third-figure syllogism about a specific subject.

This passage from Dasuqi and al-'Attar clarifies a subtle point about the converse: it is not always true. They show that the concept of non-permanence (al-lā dawām) arises from a specific syllogistic form that combines a particular affirmative with a negated actuality. Understanding this requires careful attention to the structure of the argument and the epistemological warning against blind imitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
The passage states that the converse is true in its two parts, but then introduces the concept of non-permanence. What does 'non-permanence' mean? It means the converse is not perpetually true; it holds only under certain conditions.
2
Extract the two components of non-permanence
Al-'Attar explains that the concept is obtained from two elements: (1) assuming a specific subject, e.g., 'Zayd is a writer', and (2) the negation of actual movement, e.g., 'Zayd is not actually moving', taken from the 'not always' qualification in the original proposition.
3
Reconstruct the syllogistic form
Dasuqi describes the syllogism: the description of the predicate forms the minor premise, and the description of the subject forms the major premise. This yields a third-figure syllogism (middle term is subject in both). The minor premise is 'Zayd is a writer' (I), the major premise is 'Zayd is not actually moving' (O), and the conclusion is the particular negative 'Some writer is not actually moving' (O). This syllogism can be reduced to the first figure by converting the minor premise.
The reduction shows the logical connection between the original 'not always' and the derived non-permanence.
4
Understand the warning against taqlid
Al-'Attar concludes by admonishing the reader to understand this point independently and not to be an imitator. This emphasizes that logical concepts must be grasped through analysis, not blind acceptance.
