The Three Types of Verbal Indication
Author defines the three types of indication and their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given verbal indication as mutabaqa, tadammun, or iltizam based on the relation between the word and the meaning.

Core Idea
A word indicates its meaning in three ways: fully, by part, or by a necessary mental link.

This passage from the Tadhheeb establishes the foundational tripartite division of verbal indication used throughout Islamic logic. Understanding these three types is key to analyzing definitions, interpreting legal texts, and avoiding fallacies in argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and the meaning in question
Start by isolating the word (lafz) and the meaning (ma'na) it is said to indicate. Ask: what is the word, and what exactly is the meaning it conveys?
The word may have multiple meanings; focus on the one under discussion.
2
Check if the meaning is exactly what the word was coined for
If the meaning is the same as the original lexical meaning (wad'), then it is mutabaqa (conformity). No further condition is needed; only knowledge of the convention (wad') is required.
Even for ambiguous words (mushtarak), all possible meanings are indicated by mutabaqa.
3
If not, check if the meaning is a part of the original meaning
If the meaning is a part or component of the whole original meaning (e.g., 'house' indicating a door), then it is tadammun (containment). This also requires no extra condition; understanding the part follows from understanding the whole.
The word must be placed for a composite whole for tadammun to apply.
4
If not, check if the meaning is external but necessarily linked in the mind
If the meaning is external to the literal meaning but is mentally necessary (luzum dhihni) such that understanding the literal meaning compels understanding the external meaning, then it is iltizam (implication). This type requires the condition of mental concomitance.
If no such mental link exists, the word does not indicate that external meaning by iltizam.

Commentary
This passage is a new chapter defining the three types of indication (dalala): mutabaqa (conformity), tadammun (containment/implication), and iltizam (concomitance). The matn begins by stating that for indication of an external meaning (i.e., something other than the literal meaning), a condition is required. The commentary immediately clarifies that this condition applies specifically to implicative indication (dalala iltizamiyya). The condition is a mental relation of necessary concomitance (luzum dhihni): the external matter must be mentally linked to the original meaning such that understanding the original meaning necessarily leads to understanding the external meaning. Without this mental connection, the word cannot indicate that external meaning. The commentator al-Attar quotes Abd al-Hakim, who uses a Qur'anic verse to illustrate that the condition is ontological (related to existence) rather than epistemic (related to knowledge). Some marginal notes warn that if the condition is not dependence, then merely being outside the literal meaning would suffice, reviving the earlier problem of infinite indications. The passage then contrasts the other two types: mutabaqa (literal indication) requires only knowledge of the original linguistic convention (wad'), even for ambiguous words (mushtarak) where all possible meanings are understood by the hearer, regardless of the speaker's intent. Tadammun (part-whole indication) also requires no extra condition because understanding the part is inherent in understanding the whole; a word placed for a whole necessarily indicates each part. The passage ends abruptly mid-sentence, apparently continuing on the next page.

The Condition for Implicative Indication: Mental Concomitance
Author specifies the condition required for implicative indication and explains its necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether an implicative indication is valid by checking the presence of a necessary mental relation between the literal and external meanings.

Core Idea
Without a necessary mental link, a word cannot imply an external meaning.

This lesson drills down into the condition required for implicative indication (dalala iltizamiyya). The passage argues that if there is no mental concomitance between the literal meaning and the external meaning, then the word cannot indicate that external meaning, because understanding it would be impossible. This condition prevents the proliferation of arbitrary implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning (al-ma'na al-mawdu' lahu)
Determine the meaning for which the word was originally coined (the wad'). This is the base from which any external implication will be drawn.
This is essential for any type of indication.
2
Identify the claimed external meaning (al-ma'na al-khariji)
What is the extra meaning that is said to be indicated by the word beyond its literal sense? Make sure it is distinct from the literal meaning.
If it is the same or a part, then it is mutabaqa or tadammun, not iltizam.
3
Test for necessary mental concomitance (luzum dhihni)
Ask: Whenever one understands the literal meaning, does one necessarily also understand the external meaning? Is the connection so strong that the mind cannot separate them? This is the key condition.
The relation must be mental (dhihni), not merely physical or customary, though custom may indicate such a relation.
4
Assess the consequence of lacking the condition
If there is no mental concomitance, then the word cannot indicate the external meaning by iltizam. To claim otherwise would violate the passage's argument: if it indicated, it would be understood, but it is not understood.
This prevents assigning unlimited implications to a word.

Primaries: The Self-Evident Foundation of Knowledge
Author defines primaries as self-evident propositions and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a primary (awwaliyya) proposition and explain why it does not require a middle term.

Core Idea
Primaries are judgments so basic that understanding the terms is enough to know they are true.

In this lesson, we examine the first category of the materials of syllogisms: al-awwaliyat. These are propositions that the intellect assents to immediately upon understanding their terms, without needing any proof. The author defines them and gives classic examples. Understanding primaries is essential because they serve as the foundational axioms upon which all demonstration is built.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the Arabic definition: 'الأوليات: وهي القضايا التي يحكم العقل بمجرد تصور الطرفين، ولا يتوقف على واسطة.' Note that the intellect judges merely by conceptualizing the two extremes (subject and predicate).
The definition emphasizes 'mere conceptualization' (مجرد تصور) as sufficient.
2
Identify the key condition
The condition is that no intermediate (wasita) is needed. This distinguishes primaries from demonstrated propositions that require a middle term in a syllogism.
3
Examine the examples
Consider 'One is half of two' and 'The whole is greater than the part'. Once you understand the terms (e.g., 'one', 'half', 'two'), you immediately see the truth without any reasoning.
These examples are canonical and have been used for centuries.
4
Check for common misunderstanding
Some think primaries must be trivial, but they can be about theoretical matters as long as the judgment follows directly from understanding the terms. For instance, 'The whole is greater than the part' is a general truth about quantity.
The author's commentary mentions that deficient intellect or false beliefs may hinder acceptance, but the proposition itself remains self-evident.
5
Distinguish from other categories
Primaries are one of the five arts (al-sana'i' al-khams) from which syllogisms are formed. They are the bedrock of demonstration; without them, no proof could begin.

Definition and Purpose of Dialectical Syllogism (Jadal)
Author defines dialectical syllogism and its aim of convincing those incapable of demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dialectical syllogism (jadal) and distinguish its purpose from demonstrative syllogism (burhan).

Core Idea
Jadal compels opponents using accepted premises, not certain demonstration.

The author opens the chapter on the materials of syllogisms by defining dialectical syllogism. Unlike demonstration, which yields certainty, jadal aims to convince those incapable of demonstration and to compel an opponent using premises accepted by both parties. The commentators clarify the two roles of the dialectician: preserving one's own opinion or demolishing the opponent's.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of jadal from the base text.
Identify the key components: it is a syllogism composed of famous or accepted premises (مشهورة أو مسلمة), used to compel the opponent (إلزام الخصم) or preserve an opinion (حفظ الرأي).
Note that the purpose includes both offense and defense.
2
Contrast jadal with burhan.
The commentators note that jadal can use induction or analogy, while burhan is only categorical syllogism. Jadal does not aim for certainty.

Jadal in Action: The Philosophers vs. Theologians Example
Author defines dialectical syllogism and its aim of convincing those incapable of demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical argument between philosophers and theologians regarding God's knowledge and identify how jadal works to compel an opponent.

Core Idea
The philosophers used the theologians' own premises to force a contradiction, demonstrating jadal in action.

Al-Dasuqi provides a vivid example of jadal: philosophers, who deny divine attributes, responded to theologians who affirm that God's knowledge pertains to particulars. The philosophers constructed a dialectical argument using the theologians' own acceptance of attributes to show that such knowledge would imply change in God, which is absurd. This is a classic case of jadal as a tool to compel the opponent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premises accepted by the theologians.
The theologians hold that God has attributes (like knowledge) and that God's knowledge pertains to particulars. These are accepted premises for the sake of argument.
2
See how the philosophers use those premises to derive a contradiction.
If God's knowledge pertains to particulars, then as particulars change, His knowledge would change—implying change in God. This leads to a false consequence, so the antecedent (that His knowledge pertains) must be false.
This is a conditional (istithna'i) argument: If P, then Q; Q is false, so not-P.
3
Understand the dialectical dynamics.
The philosophers are the 'questioners' trying to demolish the theologians' position. They are not asserting their own view but using the opponent's premises to force a conclusion.
This shows jadal as a tool for refutation, not for establishing one's own doctrine.

Author defines rhetorical syllogism as composed of accepted and probable premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of premises in a rhetorical syllogism and explain why its conclusion is probable, not certain.

Core Idea
Rhetorical syllogism uses accepted and probable premises to produce persuasion, not certainty.

He commends the commentator for including prophets among those from whom premises are taken, noting that other commentators like the author and Qutb al-Razi also mention prophets, saints, and sages as sources of accepted premises.
