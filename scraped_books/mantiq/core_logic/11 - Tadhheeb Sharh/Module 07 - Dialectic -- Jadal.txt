Refuting the Objection that Nuh's Mission Was Also Universal
Commentators answer the objection that Prophet Nuh's mission was also universal like Muhammad's.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the uniqueness of Prophet Muhammad's universal mission from that of Nuh by analyzing the dialectical arguments in the commentary.

Core Idea
Muhammad's mission is uniquely universal in intent, scope (jinn/angels), and continuity.

In this dialectical passage, commentators defend the particularity of the Prophet's universal message against an objection: Nuh's mission also became universal after the flood. The crucial logical difference is not mere universality of reach, but the modality of the intention (accidental vs. essential) and the inclusion of non-human beings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the claim that Nuh's mission was also universal after the flood, because all surviving humans were those in the ark. This challenges the uniqueness of Muhammad's universal message.
The objection is implicit in the phrase 'If you say: Indeed Nuh was sent to all creation also'.
2
Analyze al-Dasūqī's first reply
Notice the distinction between intentional universality from the beginning ('min awwal al-amr') and accidental universality resulting from events (the flood). Muhammad's mission was intended for all, while Nuh's became universal only after the flood.
This is a modal distinction: essential vs. accidental.
3
Understand the scope expansion
The term 'creatures' (al-khalāʾiq) includes jinn and angels. Muhammad was sent to them, but Nuh was not sent to jinn and angels, only to humans. This shows a difference in the reach of the message.
The commentator explicitly states that Nuh was not sent to jinn and angels.
4
Evaluate al-ʿAttār's additional arguments
Al-ʿAttār adds that Muhammad's message continues until the Day of Resurrection (unlike Nuh's, which ended) and that jinn were obligated to follow Muhammad, whereas their belief in previous scriptures was voluntary.
These arguments reinforce uniqueness through temporal continuity and obligation.

Explaining the Prophet's Mission: Legal Rulings and Noble Traits
Commentator explains the phrase and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection and response about the Prophet's mission encompassing ethics and evaluate the commentator's argument.

Core Idea
The Prophet's legal rulings produce noble traits; ethics are not separate.

This passage from al-Dasuqi's commentary defends the description of the Prophet as sent to complete noble traits against an objection: the Prophet was sent with legal rulings, not ethics. The commentator argues that legal rulings are a means to noble traits. Let's follow his reasoning and see how this dialectical exchange works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
The objector says: 'Indeed he was only sent with legal rulings, not with ethics and praiseworthy qualities.' Locate this in the Arabic and translation.
2
Identify the response (jawab)
The commentator replies: 'The purpose of expounding legal rulings is to act according to their requirements, and acting according to them results in noble traits.'
3
Analyze the premises
What is the purpose of legal rulings? The commentator implies they are means to an end. The end is noble traits.
4
Evaluate the logic
Does the response refute the objection? The objector claimed the Prophet was not sent with ethics; the response shows ethics are a consequence, not a direct content. Is that sufficient?
This is a typical debate move: showing that the objection misunderstands the relationship.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the description of the Prophet as 'the sent one to complete noble traits'. The commentator first explains the grammatical construction: 'Muhammad' is a substitute or clarifying conjunction for the preceding noun. Then he defines 'makārim' as praiseworthy qualities like good character, patience, and generosity. The 'lām' in 'li-ithmām' is interpreted as 'bā', i.e., he was sent with these traits. The genitive construction is an attribution of the adjective 'ithmām' to the noun 'makārim', meaning 'the one sent with complete noble traits'. An objection is raised: The Prophet was sent with legal rulings, not ethics. The answer is that the purpose of legal rulings is to act upon them, and acting upon them produces noble traits. So the mission includes ethics as a consequence. Finally, 'al-kirām' refers to those characterized by beautiful qualities, not just generosity.

The Necessity of Self-Evidence in the Knowledge Division
Author shows that proof for knowledge division requires self-evident premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that the division of knowledge into necessary and acquired ultimately relies on claiming self-evidence, and explain why self-evidence is necessary to avoid circularity and infinite regress.

Core Idea
The proof for knowledge's division into necessary and acquired itself depends on self-evident premises to avoid infinite regress.

The author defends the classic division of knowledge against a powerful objection: what if all knowledge is theoretical, acquired through reasoning? He shows that even the proof for that division must rest on self-evident foundations. This lesson unpacks that argument and its reliance on self-evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the assertion being defended
The author is defending the statement 'Knowledge divides into necessary and acquired.' This is a fundamental epistemological thesis in Islamic logic. Note that the proof given for this division is under attack.
2
Understand the objection
The opponent says: All concepts could be theoretical, and the chain ends in a self-evident affirmation (or vice versa), so no circularity or infinite regress occurs. This challenges the need for a division; if all knowledge can be theoretical, the proof collapses.
3
Analyze the response
The response depends on the impossibility of acquiring concepts from affirmations and vice versa. Al-Jalal notes a nuance: even if this impossibility is not proven, the argument works if we assume no such acquisition occurs. The key is that without this assumption, circularity or infinite regress would be inevitable.
4
Identify the final claim
The author concludes that the entire reasoning ultimately reduces to a claim of self-evidence in the desired conclusion. Without this claim, the argument would be impossible. This shows that the proof for the division itself must rest on self-evident premises to be valid.

The Distinction Between Dalil and Istidlal

Core Idea
Dalil is broader than istidlal: istidlal requires self-evident premises, while dalil only requires known premises, whether self-evident or not.

The Necessity of a Logical Canon: Dialectic on Fitra vs. Universal Rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection and answer in the debate over the need for a universal canon, and identify the conditional (modus tollens) form of the argument.

Core Idea
Error itself disproves the sufficiency of innate reasoning; a canon is needed.

In this passage, the commentator Attar examines a crucial objection to the introductory claim that a canon is needed because error can occur. The objection argues that our innate reasoning (fitra) might be enough, but Attar refutes this by pointing out that the very occurrence of error shows fitra is inadequate. Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim being derived

2
State the objection
The objection claims the derivation does not follow: error in particular reasoning does not necessitate a universal canon, because fitra might be sufficient (if activated) or one might know particular cases without a canon.
3
Analyse the objection's logical structure
The objection relies on a conditional: if fitra suffices when used, then error would not occur when fitra is used. But error could be due to not using fitra. Also, knowledge of particulars could suffice without a universal rule.
4
Present the answer and its logical form
The answer: the occurrence of error itself shows fitra is insufficient; otherwise error would not be conceivable. This is modus tollens: if fitra were sufficient, error would not occur; error occurs; therefore fitra is not sufficient.

Dialectical Syllogism: Materials and Epistemic Status
Definition of dialectical syllogism by its materials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the materials of a dialectical syllogism and distinguish them from demonstrative premises.

Core Idea
Dialectical syllogisms do not require truth, only acceptance—by fame or opponent concession.

In the previous lesson, we saw that a demonstrative syllogism yields certainty from certain premises. Now we turn to the dialectical syllogism, which is equally valid in form but uses materials that are only widely accepted or conceded. This lesson unpacks the categories of mashhoorat and musallamat, showing how they function in debate and why they need not be true.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining feature
Notice that the dialectical syllogism is defined not by its form but by its materials: mashhoorat (widely accepted) and musallamat (conceded by the opponent). These replace the certain premises required for demonstration.
The term 'jadali' here refers to the type of syllogism, not the debate itself.
2
Classify types of mashhoorat
Al-Dusuqi explains that fame can be universal (all people), majority (most), or specific to a group (e.g., theologians). Identify examples: 'justice is good' is universal; 'the oneness of God' is majority; 'impossible of infinite regress' is specific to theologians.
These categories determine how widely the premise must be accepted.
3
Understand the causes of fame
Fame arises from three sources: general benefit, innate compassion, or zeal/shame. For each example given, determine which cause applies. This explains why certain propositions become widely accepted even if not certain.
This shows the psychological and social basis of mashhoorat, not their logical necessity.
4
Recognize the flexibility of formal validity
Al-Attar notes that even an invalid mood (e.g., second figure with two affirmatives) may be used if the opponent accepts it as productive. Dialectical validity depends on the opponent's acceptance, not logical necessity.
This distinguishes dialectic from demonstration, where only valid forms are permitted.

Defining well-known premises and their contrast with self-evident propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish well-known premises (mashhurat) from self-evident propositions (awwaliyat) based on their dependence on custom and social consensus.

Core Idea
Well-known premises are accepted by tradition, not reason; they vary across communities and may be false.

When engaging in dialectical argument, one often relies on propositions that are widely accepted in a given community, even if they are not rationally certain. The text from Tadhheeb defines these as 'well-known premises' (mashhurat) and contrasts them with self-evident truths (awwaliyat). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in theology and jurisprudence, where premises are often taken from common knowledge rather than strict demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essential feature of mashhurat
Find in the passage that well-known premises do not require correspondence with reality (مطابقة الواقع) or universal agreement; they are accepted due to common opinion (تطابق آراء الكل).
The text says: 'المشهورة لا يعتبر فيها مطابقة الواقع وتطابق الآراء'.
2
Contrast with awwaliyat
Note that self-evident propositions are judged by reason alone without external input. The passage says a person in isolation would judge awwaliyat but hesitate on mashhurat.
Example from passage: 'العدل حسن' is mashhur, not awwali.
3
Recognize the cause of acceptance
The text states that the cause of judgment in well-known premises is practice of customs (ممارسة العادات). This explains why they vary over time and place.
Custom leads to variability like considering lying good if it brings benefit.
4
Evaluate truth value
Mashhurat can be false; the passage gives the example of Indians considering animal slaughter blameworthy, which is false but well-known.
Truth is not a condition; only acceptance matters.
5
Apply to Islamic science
In usul al-fiqh, premises like 'Consensus of the scholars is authoritative' are mashhur among jurists but not self-evident. Recognize that such premises rely on custom.
If mistaken for awwali, the argument may be overvalued.

Al-Dasuqi: And the intention is that well-known premises do not require correspondence with reality or universal agreement; whether they are certain or not. So some propositions are self-evident in one respect and well-known in another. Renown may reach a point where they are confused with self-evident propositions, and distinction is made between them: self-evident propositions are judged by reason even if one does not consider anything beyond the conception of the two extremes, without hesitation. Well-known propositions depend on something beyond the conception of the two extremes, such that if a person imagines himself not having observed anyone, nor engaged in any practice, then these propositions are presented to him; he would not judge them, but would hesitate. Because the cause of judgment is practice of customs. Hence, change may affect them, like considering lying good if it entails a great benefit, unlike self-evident propositions; for everyone considers it small compared to the part originally. Thus, the dialectical propositions are taken insofar as they are well-known or conceded, even if in reality they are certain, or even self-evident.
And the truth is that it is more general than demonstration in terms of form as well; because what is considered is inference according to concession, whether it is induction, analogy, or syllogism, unlike demonstration which is only syllogism.
Al-Attar: As among the people of India: slaughtering animals is blameworthy. These are called restricted well-known premises. They may be true, either theoretical like 'A triangle's angles equal two right angles', or necessary like 'Negation and affirmation do not coexist' — indeed most self-evident propositions — and they may be false as in the India example. End. Based on this, the 'al-' in 'al-nas' is valid for true generality. And Abd al-Hakim said: He did not intend by 'al-nas' true generality, since there is no proposition recognized by all individuals of people, but customary generality, i.e., from people of a century, region, town, craft, or other. And the consideration of aspect is necessary, i.e., reason judges them because of people's recognition, to exclude that.
Well-known propositions vary according to differences of times, places, and generations. So each people has well-known propositions according to their customs, like the ugliness of slaughtering animals among the people of India, unlike others.
As for conceded propositions: they are the propositions conceded by the opponent, upon which dialectic is built.

Al-Attar: (Discussion about) self-evident propositions, or restricting propositions to non-certain ones by the context of the division. For a proposition cannot be certain in one respect and not certain in another, since certainty does not coexist with its opposite. By this, the corruption is evident of what was said: dialectic is a syllogism composed of well-known or conceded propositions, even if in reality they are certain or self-evident, because it entails overlap of the five arts. End. This is supported by the author's saying: Well-known propositions are propositions whose opinions coincide upon them — like the goodness of doing good to parents, or the opinions of the majority — like the oneness of God, or the opinions of a specific group — like the impossibility of infinite regress.
His saying: 'And well-known propositions differ... etc.' — whether they are true or false. For this reason it was said: Temperaments and customs have influence on beliefs. For those with intense temperaments consider evil as good, and those with delicate temperaments consider gentleness and forgiveness as good. And people of customs consider what their custom has established as good, and what is otherwise as ugly. And each people has their own specific well-known propositions, like the notoriety in grammar: the subject is in the nominative case, and so forth.

Commentary
This passage defines and discusses the two main types of premises used in dialectical syllogisms: المشهورات (well-known premises) and المسلّمات (conceded premises). The author begins by stating the chapter's topic: the materials (مَوَاد) of syllogisms, offering examples like 'العدلُ حَسَنُ' (Justice is good) and 'الظُّلم قبيح' (Injustice is ugly). These are not certain propositions but are widely accepted. The commentary by الدسوقي clarifies that well-known premises do not require correspondence with reality or universal agreement; they may be certain in themselves but are used for their acceptance. A key distinction is drawn between self-evident (أوّلي) and well-known (مشهور) propositions: self-evident propositions are judged immediately by reason without any external influence, whereas well-known propositions depend on social practice and custom. For example, a person raised in isolation would not judge a well-known premise as true without prior exposure to societal norms. Moreover, well-known propositions can change over time and place, such as considering lying good if it serves a great benefit, which would never happen with self-evident truths. The discussion then addresses the relationship between dialectic and demonstration: dialectic uses not only categorical syllogisms but also induction and analogy, as long as the premises are conceded, while demonstration is restricted to certain syllogisms. The commentator العطار adds examples, such as the people of India considering animal slaughter blameworthy—a well-known proposition that is false in reality. He also discusses the scope of 'الناس' (people) in the definition, noting that it refers to customary generality rather than universal agreement. The topic of المسلّمات is introduced: they are propositions that the opponent concedes for the sake of argument, regardless of their truth. The passage concludes with a critique of the view that dialectical premises can be simultaneously certain and well-known, arguing that certainty and non-certainty cannot coexist in the same proposition with respect to the same respect. This highlights the methodological rigor in distinguishing the five arts (صناعات خمس) of logic.

Conceded Premises: The Tool of Dialectical Agreement
Defining conceded premises and their role in dialectic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conceded premises (musallamat) and explain their role in dialectical argumentation.

Core Idea
Conceded premises are granted by the opponent for the sake of argument, irrespective of truth.

In addition to well-known premises, dialectical arguments rely on premises that the opponent explicitly concedes. These are called 'musallamat' and are essential for building a case against an interlocutor. The passage defines them simply as 'propositions conceded by the opponent upon which dialectic is built.' This lesson examines their nature and use.

Guided Path
1
Define musallamat
Locate the definition in the passage: 'المسلّمات: هي القضايا التي تُسَلَّم من الخصم، فيُبنى عليها الجدل.'
They are propositions that the opponent concedes for the debate.
2
Note that truth is irrelevant
Al-Dasuqi comments that musallamat may be true or false; the key is acceptance by the opponent.
This is similar to mashhurat but the source of acceptance is different: specific agreement vs. custom.
3
Distinguish from mashhurat
Mashhurat are accepted by society; musallamat are accepted by the opponent in a specific debate. They may overlap but differ in basis.
A proposition can be both mashhur and musallam if the opponent also accepts it.
4
Use in practice
In munazara, one may concede a premise to move the argument forward, even if privately disbelieving it.
This allows the debate to proceed on common ground.
5
Apply to Islamic science
In a debate on whether the Quran is created, a Mu'tazili might concede 'The Quran is the speech of God' to a Hanbali, then argue that speech is created.
That premise becomes musallamat for that exchange.

Commentary
This passage defines and discusses the two main types of premises used in dialectical syllogisms: المشهورات (well-known premises) and المسلّمات (conceded premises). The author begins by stating the chapter's topic: the materials (مَوَاد) of syllogisms, offering examples like 'العدلُ حَسَنُ' (Justice is good) and 'الظُّلم قبيح' (Injustice is ugly). These are not certain propositions but are widely accepted. The commentary by الدسوقي clarifies that well-known premises do not require correspondence with reality or universal agreement; they may be certain in themselves but are used for their acceptance. A key distinction is drawn between self-evident (أوّلي) and well-known (مشهور) propositions: self-evident propositions are judged immediately by reason without any external influence, whereas well-known propositions depend on social practice and custom. For example, a person raised in isolation would not judge a well-known premise as true without prior exposure to societal norms. Moreover, well-known propositions can change over time and place, such as considering lying good if it serves a great benefit, which would never happen with self-evident truths. The discussion then addresses the relationship between dialectic and demonstration: dialectic uses not only categorical syllogisms but also induction and analogy, as long as the premises are conceded, while demonstration is restricted to certain syllogisms. The commentator العطار adds examples, such as the people of India considering animal slaughter blameworthy—a well-known proposition that is false in reality. He also discusses the scope of 'الناس' (people) in the definition, noting that it refers to customary generality rather than universal agreement. The topic of المسلّمات is introduced: they are propositions that the opponent concedes for the sake of argument, regardless of their truth. The passage concludes with a critique of the view that dialectical premises can be simultaneously certain and well-known, arguing that certainty and non-certainty cannot coexist in the same proposition with respect to the same respect. This highlights the methodological rigor in distinguishing the five arts (صناعات خمس) of logic.

Definition and Scope of Jadal (Dialectic)
Definition and scope of jadal (dialectic) with a historical example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of jadal (dialectic) and distinguish it from burhan (demonstration) in terms of premises and purpose.

Core Idea
Jadal uses conceded premises to force an opponent, while burhan uses certain premises to produce knowledge.

In this passage, the author defines jadal (dialectic) as a tool for argumentation using premises that the opponent concedes. Unlike demonstrative proof (burhan), jadal aims not at absolute certainty but at convincing the incapable or forcing the opponent. The passage also broadens jadal beyond syllogism to include induction and analogy. Let's dissect this definition step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of jadal
The passage states jadal is 'to convince the one incapable of demonstration' and 'to force the opponent'. Note the dual goal: it can be defensive (preserving one's view) or offensive (demolishing the opponent's).
2
Identify the types of premises
Jadal uses 'mashhoor' (renowned) or 'musallam' (conceded) premises. The example of jurists conceding usul al-fiqh issues illustrates the second type, where both disputants or their scholarly community accept the premises.
3
Distinguish jadal from burhan
The author notes that jadal is broader in form: it can be a syllogism, induction, or analogy, while burhan is only a syllogism. The key is that jadal's premises are conceded, not necessarily certain.
4
Analyse the historical example
The philosophers built a dialectical argument using the theologians' own concession (that God has attributes) to force a contradiction about God's knowledge of particulars. This shows how jadal exploits conceded premises.
5
Summarise the definition
Jadal is a logical argument (qiyas or non-qiyas) composed of mashhoor/musallam premises, aiming to convince the incapable or to force/refute an opponent.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of dialectic (jadal) as a distinct art. It opens with a chapter heading: 'On the materials of syllogisms', but then immediately discusses the discourse (al-kalam) which is for forcing the opponent, using premises that are conceded either between the two disputants or among their scholarly community, exemplified by jurists conceding usul al-fiqh issues. The main purpose stated is to convince the one incapable of demonstration. Al-Dasuqi expands: the purpose of jadal is dual: to convince the incapable and to force the opponent. He describes two roles: the dialectician as respondent (preserving his view, trying not to be forced) and as questioner (objecting, demolishing the opponent's view, trying to force). He gives a historical example: the philosophers refute the theologians' claim that God's knowledge extends to particulars by pointing out that this would require change in God's knowledge—an impossibility. This argument is built on the theologians' own concession that God has attributes, even though philosophers deny attributes. Thus it is a dialectical refutation. Al-Attar then clarifies that jadal is essentially a syllogism composed of renowned or conceded propositions to force the opponent. However, the author (likely al-Akhdari) notes that jadal is broader than demonstration in form, because it can use induction (istiqra') or analogy (tamthil) as long as the premises are conceded, whereas demonstration requires strict syllogism. Al-Attar also critiques the commentator for limiting jadal to forcing the opponent; in fact, jadal also aims at preserving one's own view or demolishing the opponent's. He references the commentator on Sullam al-'Ulum, who says the goal in jadal is called a 'position' (wad'), while in demonstration it is called a 'problem' (mas'alah). The final note clarifies that the example of jurists refers to the second type of conceded premises (among scholars).
