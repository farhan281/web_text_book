Logic's Subject: Essential Accidents of Concepts and Assents
Author defines logic's subject and why essential accidents of concepts and assents are studied.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why logic studies only essential accidents of conceptual and assent knowledge and identify the teleological role of logic in leading to the unknown.

Core Idea
Logic is a tool for moving from known to unknown; it only studies what serves that journey.

This passage clarifies the precise subject matter of logic. It is not every property of mental concepts and judgments, but only those 'essential accidents' that enable us to proceed from what is known to what is unknown. Understanding this teleological boundary is crucial: logic is not a random collection of mental observations but a purposeful instrument for knowledge acquisition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Locate in the Arabic the terms 'al-ma'lūm al-taṣawwurī' and 'al-ma'lūm al-taṣdīqī'. Understand that these are the two categories of knowledge that logic deals with: simple concepts (e.g., 'human') and judgments (e.g., 'human is rational').
These two categories are foundational in Islamic logic, mirroring the structure of definitions (tasawwur) and arguments (tasdiq).
2
Understand 'essential accidents'
The phrase 'al-a'rāḍ al-dhātiyya' refers to attributes that are necessary (but not part of the essence) to the thing studied. For logic, these are the attributes of concepts and assents that make them useful for inference—like generality, particularity, contradiction, etc.
Essential accidents are distinct from essential properties (those in the quiddity); they are what a thing necessarily possesses once it exists.
3
Grasp the teleological principle: al-īṣāl ilā majhūl
The passage states that the logician investigates concepts and assents 'min ḥaythu al-īṣāl ilā majhūl' – from the perspective of leading to the unknown. This is the purpose of logic: to provide the means to reach new knowledge from existing knowledge.
The term 'īṣāl' indicates a directedness; logic is a tool, not an end in itself.
4
Examine the commentator's expansion
The commentary quotes Sharḥ al-Maṭāli‘, distinguishing 'al-īṣāl al-qarīb' (proximate leading, without intermediary) from remote leading. This shows that logic treats even immediate inference (like simple conversion) as part of its domain, as long as it aids discovering the unknown.
This distinction helps us see that logic covers both simple and complex inferential movements.

How Does a General Term Signify a Particular?
Author raises and resolves an objection about the signification type of a general term for a particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct type of signification for a general term indicating a particular individual (e.g., 'my slaves' for Zayd) and explain why it is tazminiyya.

Core Idea
A general term signifies a particular by tazminiyya: the particular is a part of the collective meaning.

In logic, words signify meanings in three ways: mutabiyya, tazminiyya, and iltizamiyya. But what happens when a general term like 'my slaves' indicates a specific slave like Zayd? Does it fit any of these? An objection says no, but the correct answer is yes: it is tazminiyya. Let's see why.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three significations
Recall the definitions: mutabiyya (whole meaning), tazminiyya (part of meaning), iltizamiyya (external accompaniment).
2
Analyze the problematic case
Take the phrase 'جاء عبيدي' (my slaves came). When it indicates Zayd individually, why does it not fit mutabiyya? (Zayd is not the whole meaning.) Not tazminiyya? (Objector says Zayd is an individual, not a 'part'.) Not iltizamiyya? (Zayd is not external.)
The objector assumes 'part' must be a universal component, but the answer shows otherwise.
3
Consider the first answer
Some say it is mutabiyya because the phrase is equivalent to multiple propositions. Understand why this is not valid: equivalence does not mean the same signification.
The passage explicitly rejects this: 'لا يلزم من كون الشيء في قوة الشيء أنه يدل دلالته'.
4
Grasp the correct answer
The truth is tazminiyya: Zayd is part of the collective entity (الهيئة المجتمعة) of slaves, so he is a part of the meaning. Clarify that 'part' here includes individuals as components of a set.
The passage states: 'والحق : أنَّها تضمنيَّة ; لأن زيداً وإن كان جزئيا باعتبار ذاته؛ إلا أنه جزء من الهيئة المجتمعة'.
5
Distinguish from iltizamiyya
Note that the external meaning in iltizamiyya can be existential, non-existential, or relational, but Zayd is still inside the coined meaning as a part, not external.
This guards against the misconception that a general term's indication of a particular is iltizamiyya.

Three Views on What Words Signify
Section defining the three significations and debating the object of coining.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three positions on al-mawdu' lahu (mental forms, external things, meanings per se) and identify the arguments used for each.

Core Idea
Words can signify mental forms, external objects, or meanings in themselves—a key debate in logical semantics.

In this passage, al-Attar presents three competing views on what exactly a word is coined to signify: mental forms (as held by al-Farabi), external things (as held by al-Tusi), or meanings per se (the preferred view). Understanding these positions is crucial for grasping how definitions and propositions relate to reality. We'll work through each view and its supporting argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the First Position
Locate the view attributed to al-Farabi and Qutb al-Razi: that words are coined for mental forms (suwar dhihniyya). Note their argument: mental forms are known directly (ma'lum bi al-dhat), while external things are known indirectly.
Why does this matter? If words signified external objects directly, knowledge would depend on their existence.
2
Identify the Second Position
Find the view of al-Tusi, al-Taftazani, and others: words are coined for external things (umur kharijiyya). Their argument: we primarily attend to external objects, not mental images.
What's the counterargument? Mental forms are just mirrors; external things are the true objects of attention.
3
Identify the Third Position
The preferred view (al-haqq): words signify meanings as such (ma'ani min haythu hiya hiya), abstracted from mental or external particularities. The basis: language serves learning and teaching, which depends on meanings regardless of their mode of existence.
Both mental and external views have problems: some words have no external referent, others have no clear mental image.

Commentary
This passage is a section heading and commentary defining the three types of signification and then delving into a deep philosophical debate about what exactly words signify (al-mawdu' lahu). The author begins with the rule that a word does not indicate every external matter – referring to the condition in iltizamiyya. The commentary by al-Attar then expounds three positions among scholars. First, al-Farabi and Qutb al-Razi hold that words are coined for mental forms (suwar dhihniyya) because these are known directly (ma'lum bi al-dhat), while external things are known indirectly. Their argument is that if knowledge depended on external existence, then when the external object ceases to exist, knowledge would cease. However, a critique is presented: if by 'known by essence' they mean the mental image is imprinted in the mind, that is not necessary at all times; if they mean it is the primary object of attention, then external things could also be that. The author declares this third view to be the truth (al-haqq), arguing that the real subject matter (mawdu' lahu) is the thing itself, whether external or mental, not considered under any accidental characteristics. This resolves the problem that some words (like 'Allah') have no external or mental image in the ordinary sense, yet they have meaning. Then a marginal gloss from Mir Zahid critiques the externalist view, saying it is clearly false because many word meanings are not external, and the subject matter must be directly known, which external things are not (they are known indirectly). Therefore the externalist statement must be interpreted as meaning the thing itself regardless of its being in the mind. Finally, the author notes that 'the word' in the matn refers to the coined word. This passage is foundational for the epistemology of logic, as it clarifies that words signify the essence of the meaning, not its accidental attributes.

The concept that divides into universal/particular is the shadowy existence of the known, not the act of knowing.

When you conceive something, that thing obtains in your mind in two ways: as knowledge (original existence) and as the known (shadowy existence). The logicians' division into universal and particular applies only to the known, because universality and particularity are attributes of the known, not of the act of knowing. This distinction is crucial for understanding how definitions work and how the five universals are applied.

The same mental form is called 'meaning' (ma‘nā) when intended by the utterance, 'concept' (mafhūm) when understood from it, and 'denoted' (madlūl) when indicated. All are identical in essence, differing only in consideration.

The definite article in 'al-mafhūm' is for the genus (jins), not comprehensive (istighrāq), because division requires a genus term that applies to all species but not to individuals of the divided concept.
If 'al' were comprehensive, it would imply the division applies to each individual concept, which is not the case.

Chapter: On the Principles of Concepts. The concept—when he finished the preliminaries of commencing the science, he began the objectives, and he presented the investigations that lead to conception before the investigations that lead to assent, because every conception naturally precedes every assent without the reverse, and he presented the chapter on universals, which are for the most part parts of the definition, before its chapter for that reason, or because knowledge of them is beneficial in knowing it. It should be known first that the occurrence of a thing in the mind is of two modes: an original essential occurrence upon which effects follow, and a circumstantial shadowy occurrence upon which effects do not follow. For example, if you conceive the disbelief of the disbeliever, a form of his disbelief—which is knowledge—occurs in your mind, and by its standing in your mind you become knowledgeable of it, and the effects of knowledge of it follow. And since knowledge is identical to the known, his disbelief also occurs within that form as a circumstantial occurrence that does not necessitate being described by disbelief, and it is the shadowy existence of the known upon which the effects of that known do not follow. And analogous to this is the occurrence of the quiddity within the individual in the external world. And that the meaning (al-ma‘nā) is the mental form; it is applied to [both] knowledge and the known, because each of them occurs in the mind—the first with original existence, and the second with shadowy existence. And what is divided into universal and particular is the second meaning, based on the fact that they are two attributes of the known. Then that form is called 'meaning' (ma‘nā) from the perspective of its being intended by the utterance, and 'concept' (mafhūm) from the perspective of its being understood from it. So the commentator's statement 'the concept...etc.' intends by it the mental form in the second sense, and the secret of expressing with 'concept' instead of 'meaning' is that this is a division of it considering its occurrence in the mind even in some respect. And what is intended is the single concept, as he restricted it. And it is what is obtained in the mind, i.e., it is the meaning obtained in the mind from the utterance; i.e., what exists in the mind and is perceived, whether directly or indirectly. That is because the mind perceives universals without a medium, but particulars—if they are sensible, it perceives them through the common sense, and if they are non-sensible, it perceives them through the estimative faculty. And know that the thing obtained with the mind from the perspective of its occurrence in it is called 'obtained in the mind' and 'perceived'; from the perspective that it is perceived from the utterance it is called 'concept'; from the perspective that it is intended from the utterance and intended it is called 'meaning'; and from the perspective of the utterance's indication of it it is called 'denoted'. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and divisionary commentary from Tadhheeb Sharh on the chapter regarding the principles of concepts. It opens by explaining the logical arrangement of the text: after preliminaries, the author proceeds to the objectives, prioritizing investigations leading to conception (taṣawwur) over those leading to assent (taṣdīq), because conception naturally precedes assent. Similarly, the chapter on universals is placed before the chapter on definition because universals often serve as parts of definitions and knowing them aids in understanding definition. The commentator then introduces a crucial distinction: the occurrence of a thing in the mind is of two types—original essential existence (wujūd aṣlī) upon which effects follow, and shadowy circumstantial existence (wujūd ẓillī) upon which effects do not follow. He illustrates with the example of conceiving the disbelief of a disbeliever: the mental form of that disbelief is knowledge itself (original existence), leading to the knower being described as knowing, while the disbelief itself also occurs in that form as a shadowy existence without the mind itself becoming disbelief. This shadowy existence is what is meant by 'the known' in the mind. Analogously, the quiddity occurs within its external individual. The term 'meaning' (ma‘nā) is applied to the mental form that encompasses both the act of knowing (original existence) and the known (shadowy existence). The division into universal and particular applies to the second meaning (the shadowy existence), since universality and particularity are attributes of the known, not of the act of knowledge. The same mental form is called 'meaning' when considered as intended by the utterance, and 'concept' (mafhūm) when considered as understood from the utterance. The commentator's use of 'concept' instead of 'meaning' in the division is because the division considers occurrence in the mind even in some respect (i.e., shadowy existence). It is specified as the single concept (al-mafhūm al-mufrad). Next, the passage explains that the concept is 'what is obtained in the mind' from the utterance, perceived directly or indirectly: the mind perceives universals directly, but particulars require mediation (common sense for sensibles, estimative faculty for non-sensibles). It then clarifies that the same thing can be termed 'obtained in the mind', 'concept', 'meaning', or 'denoted' (madlūl) according to different aspects. Additionally, it distinguishes between the logical 'concept' (what is understood from the utterance in general) and the uṣūlī 'concept' (what is understood not in the place of articulation, i.e., implied meaning). The commentator also addresses the definite article 'al' in 'al-mafhūm', stating it is for the genus (jins), not for all individuals, because division requires a genus term. Finally, the discussion touches on the locus of mental forms: some hold that both universals and particulars are imprinted in the rational soul; the chosen view is that only universals are imprinted directly, while particulars are imprinted in the faculties and observed by the soul. On the first view, the 'circumstantiality' remains straightforward; on the second, 'in the mind' means 'present to the mind'. Abu al-Fatḥ concludes that the intended meaning is simply what is obtained in the mind, and this implies that universality, particularity, and their divisions are secondary intelligibles (‘āriḍāt al-māhiyyāt) that attach to quiddities.

The Many Names of Mental Content in Islamic Logic
Commentary explains that the same mental content is called concept, meaning, and denoted depending on consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish among the four terms (حاصل, مفهوم, معنى, مدلول) and explain why they refer to the same mental content under different considerations.

Core Idea
One mental essence, four names: perspective determines terminology.

In this passage, al-Dasuqi unpacks the foundational phrase 'what is obtained in the mind' and reveals a key insight: the same mental content can be named differently depending on how we look at it. Understanding this will protect you from confusing logical terms that are often treated as synonyms but have distinct relational nuances. We will trace the four names and see how they unify in essence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central concept
Locate the phrase 'الحاصل في العقل' in the Arabic. Al-Dasuqi defines it as the meaning that exists in the mind and is perceived by it, whether directly or indirectly. This is the umbrella term for all mental content.
2
Extract the four names and their perspectives
From the passage, list the four names: (1) حاصل/مدرك – from the perspective of its presence in the mind, (2) مفهوم – from being understood from the utterance, (3) معنى – from being intended by the utterance, (4) مدلول – from the utterance's indication. Notice that each name picks out a different relation.
3
Understand the principle of identity in essence and difference in consideration
Al-Dasuqi explicitly says 'الجميع متحدة بالذات مختلفة بالاعتبار'. This means the mental reality is one, but we call it different things depending on what aspect we focus on. This principle prevents terminological confusion in logic.
4
Apply the principle to an Islamic science example
Think of a ruling in usul al-fiqh: the same rule can be considered as explicit (منطوق) when directly stated, or implicit (مفهوم) when inferred. Just as the mental content is one with multiple names, the ruling is one but named differently based on how the text indicates it.

The commentator, al-Dasuqi, begins by glossing this phrase: it is the meaning obtained in the mind from the utterance. He then expands to explain that the mind perceives universals directly, but particulars through the senses or estimative faculty. Next, he makes a crucial terminological clarification: the same mental content can be called by different names depending on the aspect—'حاصل' and 'مدرك' from the perspective of its presence in the mind; 'مفهوم' from the perspective of being understood from the utterance; 'معنى' from the perspective of being intended; and 'مدلول' from the perspective of the utterance's indication. These are identical in essence but differ in consideration. This is a key point for understanding logical terminology. The commentary then returns to the original phrase, adding that it applies whether the indication is explicit (manṭūq) or implicit (mafhūm) in the terminology of uṣūliyyūn. Al-Attar then adds a note from al-Shamsiyya and Ibn Sīnā's al-Shifā', arguing that the definite article 'al' in 'al-mafhūm' is for the genus, not for all individuals, because dividing it would otherwise fail. The passage ends with a discussion about whether universals and particulars are imprinted in the rational soul or only universals, with the chosen view being that particulars are imprinted in the faculties. This leads to the conclusion that universality and particularity are secondary intelligibles (maʻqūlāt thāniyya) that attach to quiddities (al-māhiyāt). The passage systematically clarifies the nature of mental content and its terminology, preparing the ground for the division into particular and universal.

Distinguishing Concept from Instance in the Particular-Universal Debate
Author responds to the claim that the particular can be predicated of many, making it universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve apparent contradictions between particularity and universality by distinguishing between a concept (mafhum) and its instances (masadiq).

Core Idea
A concept can be universal even if its instances are particular — don't confuse the two.

The particular (juz'i) appears to be predicable of many individuals, which seems to make it universal. This apparent contradiction is a classic puzzle. The response hinges on a logical distinction between the concept of 'particularity' and the particular things that fall under it. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in definitions and classifications across Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims: The particular can be truly said of many individuals (Zaid, Bakr). Anything that applies to many is universal. Therefore the particular is universal, which is impossible.
The objection seems to show a contradiction.
2
Parse the ambiguous term
The term 'particular' can refer either to the instances (the actual individuals like Zaid) or to the meaning/concept of particularity.
This ambiguity is key to the refutation.
3
Apply the distinction
If 'particular' means the instances, the minor premise is false: instances are not predicated of many in the same way. If it means the concept, then the conclusion is not impossible: the concept 'particular' is indeed a universal that applies to each instance.
The concept is universal; the instances are particular.
4
Understand the deeper point
The concept (mafhum) of any term is universal; its instances (masadiq) are particular. This is true even for the term 'particular' itself.
This distinction is foundational in logic.

Predicating the Universal on Its Opposite: The Concept/Extension Distinction
Commentator defends the claim that the universal can be predicated of its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between predicating a universal on a concept and predicating it on individuals, and resolve the apparent contradiction in saying 'the particular is universal'.

Core Idea
A universal can be predicated on the concept of its opposite, but not on its individuals.

In this dialectical passage, the commentator defends the surprising claim that the term 'particular' is itself universal when considered conceptually. The objection that this leads to predicating a thing on its opposite is answered by distinguishing between concept and extension. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows why the predicate 'universal' applies to the concept of 'particular' but not to particular individuals like Zayd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that saying 'the particular is universal' is impossible because it predicates a thing on its opposite. Locate this objection in the text: 'if it is considered in terms of its concept and is universal, then it follows that a thing is predicated of its opposite... and that is invalid'.
The opposite here refers to particular vs. universal.
2
Extract the key distinction
The commentator introduces a distinction between concept (mafhum) and extension (misdaq). The concept of 'particular' is the meaning that applies to many; it is universal. The extension is the actual individuals like Zayd, which are particular. Identify this distinction in the commentary.
The same term 'particular' has two aspects: as a concept and as applied to individuals.
3
Apply the distinction to the objection
Using the distinction, the response is that predication 'the particular is universal' is about the concept, not the extension. Therefore, it does not predicate a thing on the individuals of its opposite (like Zayd). The prohibition is only on the latter.
So 'the particular is universal' is valid; 'Zayd is universal' is invalid.
4
Check the self-reference
Notice that the concept of 'particular' itself is a universal because it can be shared by many (all particular things). Al-‘Attar's verification states that the concept of what prevents common participation (i.e., the concept of 'particular') is itself universal. This shows self-referential consistency.
The concept of 'particular' is an instance of a universal.

Distinguishing Essential and Accidental Impossibility in Universals
Author defines two divisions of universal by external existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal as either essentially impossible or possible but non-existent, based on the external existence of its individuals.

Core Idea
A universal's individuals are either essentially impossible or possible—non-existence does not imply impossibility.

In Islamic logic, universals are not just mental categories; they have a relation to external existence. This passage by the author introduces a crucial distinction: some universals like a partner of Allah cannot exist by their very nature, while others like the 'Anqa' are possible but happen not to exist. Understanding this difference prevents confusion between essential impossibility and mere accidental non-existence, which is a common logical error.

Define essential impossibility
Essential impossibility (imtina' dhati) means the quiddity itself entails impossibility, such as a partner of Allah because it contradicts the essence of tawhid.
The commentator stresses that accidental impossibility (imtina' ghayri) is not intended.
3
Distinguish accidental impossibility
Something that is possible but fails to exist is impossible only by accident, not by essence. The 'Anqa' is possible in itself but non-existent due to external factors.
This distinction prevents confusing 'not existing' with 'cannot exist'.

Apply the distinction to new examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a concept is universal or particular based on the condition of mental consideration alone.

Universality and particularity depend purely on mental consideration, not on external proofs.

In this passage, the author discusses the concept of the Necessary Being (al-wajib) and how its classification as universal or particular depends on whether it is considered alone in the mind or together with the proof of tawhid. The commentator Al-Dasuqi clarifies that this classification is purely about mental consideration, not about external reality. Types of Coincidence Between Concepts
Author classifies concept coincidence types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the relation between two universal concepts as either partial coincidence (umum wa khusus min wajh) or complete coincidence (kulli), and within complete coincidence, distinguish co-extensives from absolute generality.

Core Idea
Concept relations are defined by how they share individuals — partially or entirely.

In the previous lesson, we learned about completely separate concepts (mutabayin). Here, the author turns to concepts that are not completely separate: they coincide in at least some individuals. This coincidence can be partial or complete. Complete coincidence has two subtypes: co-extensiveness (same extension) and absolute generality (one includes the other entirely). This taxonomy is essential for understanding how terms relate in Islamic sciences, especially in definition and proof.

Guided Path
1
Find the overall taxonomy
The passage divides 'not completely separate' into two main branches: coincidence in some (partial) and coincidence in all (complete). Identify these branches.
Look for the phrases 'fi ba'd al-suwar' and 'fi jami' al-suwar'.
2
Understand partial coincidence (umum wa khusus min wajh)
When two concepts share some individuals but each has exclusive ones, they have partial coincidence. Think of an Islamic example: 'mu'min' and 'muttaqi' — some muttaqi are mu'min, some mu'min are muttaqi, but not all are both.
The passage gives the example of 'sleeping' and 'awake' which partially overlap.
3
Distinguish complete coincidence subtypes
If they coincide in all individuals, either it is from both sides (co-extensive) or from one side (absolute generality). The note clarifies that 'from both sides' is explicitly added to allow the contrast.
Co-extensive: mutasawiyan; absolute generality: 'umum wa khusus mutlaq.
4
Apply the taxonomy
Given a pair like 'insan' (human) and 'natiq' (rational), test: do they coincide in all individuals? Yes. Is it from both sides? Yes, so they are mutasawiyan. For 'insan' and 'hayawan' (animal), it's absolute generality (umum wa khusus mutlaq).
For practice, try 'salat' (prayer) and 'du'a' (supplication) — every salat includes du'a but not every du'a is salat, so absolute generality.
5

The commentator Dusuqi explains that co-extensive concepts have identical extension but different intension, and he provides the logical representation as two universal affirmative propositions. Al-'Attar clarifies the condition of 'complete separation' and the addition of 'min al-janibayn'.
This shows how the base text is expanded by later scholars.

Distinguishing Co-Extensive Terms from Synonyms
Author defines co-extensive and synonymous terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutasawiyan (co-extensive) and mutaradifan (synonyms) by analyzing their intension and extension.

Core Idea
Co-extensive terms differ in meaning but share application; synonyms share both.

The passage introduces two important concepts: co-extensive terms (mutasawiyan) and synonyms (mutaradifan). Both agree in extension, but they differ in whether they also agree in intension. This distinction is crucial in definition (hadd) and in legal interpretation (tafsir), where two terms may refer to the same set of rulings but carry different nuances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage defines mutasawiyan: same extension, different intension. Mutaradifan: same extension and same intension. Mark these definitions.
Look for the phrases 'ittafaqa masadaqan wa ikhtalafa mafhuman' and 'ittahada mafhuman wa masadaqan'.
2
Extract the example
For mutasawiyan: 'insan' (human) and 'natiq' (rational). Their intensions: 'animal thinking potentially' vs 'essence with speech'. For mutaradifan: 'insan' and 'bashar' – both mean 'rational animal'.
These examples are from classical Islamic logic.
3
Understand the logical representation
Dusuqi says that mutasawiyan are equivalent to two universal affirmative propositions: 'every human is rational' and 'every rational is human'. For synonyms, the two terms are identical in meaning, so the propositions would be the same.
This representation helps test whether two terms are truly co-extensive.
4
Apply to new examples
Take the pair 'wajib' and 'fard' in Hanafi usul. If they are mutasawiyan, they have same extension but different intension: fard is proven by qat'i evidence, wajib by dhanni. If they are mutaradifan, they would be identical. Which is correct depends on the school.
Try also 'salat' and 'du'a' – are they mutasawiyan? No, because du'a has instances outside salat.
5
Check the commentary
Note that Dusuqi distinguishes mutasawiyan from mutaradifan explicitly. The commentary chain (base text, Dusuqi, 'Attar) adds that the reference of co-extensives is to two universal propositions.
This shows how later scholars reinforce the distinction.

The Asymmetry of Predication: Primary vs Secondary Existence
Abd al-Hakim refines the basis of predication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the direction of predication between particular and universal based on primary vs secondary existence.

Core Idea
Predication is one-way: the universal is predicated of the particular, not vice versa.

In logic, predication (al-haml) is the relation between subject and predicate. Early logicians thought it was symmetric: if 'Zaid is human' is true, then 'human is Zaid' should also be true. However, Abd al-Hakim shows that this is not the case. The asymmetry lies in the nature of existence: the particular exists primarily, and universals are abstracted from it. This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms
Recognize that in a predication like 'Zaid is human', 'Zaid' is a particular (juz'i) and 'human' is a universal (kulli). Particulars are individuals; universals are concepts applicable to many.
2
Understand the basis of predication
The basis (manat) is unity in existence (ittihad fi al-wujud). But this does not mean both share one existence; rather, one exists primarily (asala) and the other secondarily (tab'an).
The particular exists primarily; universals are abstracted from it.
3
Apply the criterion to examples
Test 'Zaid is human': Zaid exists primarily, human is abstracted from him, so predication is valid. Test 'human is Zaid': human does not exist primarily as a particular, so it is not valid without interpretation (e.g., taking 'human' to mean 'some human').
4
Consider the exception
Note that if one holds that natural universals exist externally (as al-Farabi and Ibn Sina held), then predication could be symmetric. But Abd al-Hakim follows the later view that rejects external existence of universals.

Commentary
This passage presents a refined investigation (tahqiq) by Abd al-Hakim into the basis (manat) of predication. He begins by stating that predication (haml) is founded on unity in existence (ittihad fi al-wujud), but clarifies that this does not mean two things share one existence; rather, one exists primarily (asala) and the other secondarily (tab'an), being abstracted (muntaza') from it. The particular exists primarily, while universals are abstracted from it. Therefore, the judgment of union between universals and the particular is correct only when the universal is predicated of the particular (e.g., 'Zaid is human'), not the reverse. If the opposite occurs (e.g., 'some human is Zaid'), it is either reversed in meaning or interpreted differently. This refutes the claim that because 'Zaid is human' is valid, 'human is Zaid' must also be valid, since union is not symmetrical. Then he analyzes predication of the particular on itself or on another particular. When a particular is predicated of itself (e.g., 'Zaid is Zaid'), there must be some diversity (even observation-based) for predication to be meaningful; otherwise it is impossible. When a particular is predicated of another particular (e.g., 'this laugher is this writer'), the predication is actually a judgment that two considerations (i'tibaran) coincide on one essence. Finally, he notes an exception: in the view of the ancients (like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina) who hold that the natural universal exists externally, then predication can be symmetric because both subject and predicate share existence equally.

This passage is a commentary on the division of necessary concomitants into those that follow from the quiddity (essence) and those that follow from existence. This passage introduces a key distinction in logic: the three considerations of the universal. Every universal term, such as 'animal', can be viewed from three angles. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in logical analysis and is foundational for the theory of predication.

Commentary
This passage defines the three aspects under which any universal can be considered, as explained by the commentator. The author begins by commanding the student to know that the universal has three considerations. The first is the logical universal (kulli mantiqi), which is the concept of universality itself, defined as that whose very conception does not prevent multiple individuals from sharing in it. It is a single compound concept that includes both the specific nature and the property of universality. This threefold distinction is crucial for understanding logical predication and prevents confusion between the concept itself, its instantiation, and their combination.

This passage from the Tadhheeb introduces a foundational division of the universal into three aspects. Understanding this distinction is essential for later discussions on definitions and the five universals. The logician specifically investigates the universal as a concept, not as a physical nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three universals
Locate in the Arabic the terms kulli mantiqi, kulli tabi'i, and kulli 'aqli. Notice how each is defined by its relationship to the concept (mafhum) and the subject (ma'rud).
2
Understand the logical universal
The concept of the universal itself, which the logician studies. It is the abstract notion without considering any specific application.
This is the focus of mantiq.
3
Understand the natural universal
The subject of that concept—the actual nature (tabi'a) that exists in external reality, such as 'human' as a species.
It is a 'nature among natures'.
4
Understand the mental universal
The composite of the concept and its subject, existing only in the mind. This combines the logical and natural aspects.
It is called 'aqli because it is a mental construct.

When this is established, we say: The concept of the universal is called logical universal because the logician investigates it; its subject is called natural universal because it is a nature among natures; and the composite of the two is called mental universal because it does not exist except in the mind. Al-Dasuqi: His statement: 'He only investigates it' means from the perspective of it being genus, species, difference, property, or general accident. His statement: 'and its subject' means from the perspective of it being his subject, not from its own essence as has passed. His statement: 'a nature among natures' means a reality among realities. His statement: 'except in the mind' means that the logical universal also has no existence except in the mind, and it is not said that it necessarily follows that the logical universal should be called mental, because the cause of the name does not require that the logical universal has a wisdom that necessitates calling it by another name, and this has no wisdom. Al-Attar: The logical universal is mental, said the commentator, and the question is apparent. The gist of the answer: We consider in the composite the essence of the natural, disregarding its being a subject of the logical, so it returns to abstraction, and this is widespread in their speech. And the statement of some: the composite of the concept of the accident and subject insofar as it is a subject, and his refutation of the commentator that what he said is a delusion, so reflect fairly. His statement: 'because the logician only investigates it' is the cause for the naming, and the meaning of the logician's investigation of it is that he takes the concept of the universal without attribution to a specific matter, and he brings forth rulings upon it so that those rulings are comprehensive for all that fall under the concept of the universal, not that he investigates the universal itself so that the proposition would be natural, as in al-Sayyid and Abd al-Hakim. His statement: 'because it is a nature of natures' means a reality of realities. His statement: 'because of its lack of actuality' means this concept only in the mind, because the composition of subject and accident is purely mental, whether we say with the existence of that which falls under it in the external. His statement: 'by another name' meaning other than mental, which is the logical, and the wisdom is the logician's investigation of it, because it is what leads to the unknown, unlike the particular. Commentary
This passage defines the threefold division of the universal (kulli) — a foundational concept in Islamic logic. The author explains that the universal can be considered in three ways: (1) its concept (mafhum) as studied by the logician, which is the logical universal; (2) the subject it applies to (ma'rud), which is the natural universal, because it is a real nature existing externally; and (3) the composite of both concept and subject, which is the mental universal, existing only in the mind. The commentary by al-Dasuqi clarifies that the logician only looks at the universal from the perspective of it being a genus, species, etc., while the natural universal is the nature itself, such as 'animal' being the subject of genera. Al-Attar addresses a potential confusion: why the logical universal is not also called 'mental', since it too exists only in the mind. The answer is that the cause of naming (the logician's investigation) does not necessitate calling it mental, and the composite has a distinct wisdom (leading to knowledge of the unknown). The discussion emphasizes the careful distinctions in terminology within the logical tradition.

The Distinction Between Logical and Mental Universal
Commentators address a potential confusion between logical and mental universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the logical universal is not called 'mental' and identify the cause of naming as the basis for this distinction.

Core Idea
The logical universal is not mental despite mental existence because its naming derives from the logician's investigation, not from its mode of existence.

In the commentary on the previous passage, al-Dasuqi and al-Attar address a potential confusion: since both the logical and mental universals exist only in the mind, why is the logical not also called mental? The answer hinges on the cause of naming (sabab al-tasmiya). This lesson unpacks that discussion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection is that since the logical universal also exists only in the mind, it should be called mental (aqli). Find where the commentator says 'no la yuqalu: yalzimu min dhalika an yusamma al-mantiqi 'aqliyyan'.
2
Understand the response
The response is that the cause of naming (the logician's investigation) does not necessitate calling it mental. The logical universal is named based on what the logician does, not where it exists.
The cause of naming is the logician's study, not its mental existence.
3
Examine the composite
The mental universal is a composite of concept and subject, bringing together both aspects. This composite has a distinct wisdom (hikma) in leading to the unknown.
This further justifies the separate name.
4
Appreciate the precision
This distinction shows the care logicians take to avoid conflating categories. The logical universal is the subject of mantiq; the mental universal is a construct of the mind combining logic and nature.

Why Self-Referential Statements Cannot Be Genuine Propositions
Author explains why self-referential statements cannot be genuine propositions and lack truth-value.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a self-referential statement like 'This statement is true' fails to be a genuine khabar and distinguish it from meaningful propositions.

Core Idea
A proposition must correspond to an external reality; self-reference creates a vicious circle that prevents correspondence.

In this passage, the author tackles the problem of self-referential statements that seem to refer to themselves. Using the definition of tasdiq (assent) as a mental form intended to correspond to reality, he shows that such statements cannot be true or false. We will unpack his argument step by step, examining the principle of non-contradiction, the nature of correspondence, and the contributions of later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
The author begins with the necessary impossibility of a thing coexisting with its absence. This is the principle of non-contradiction. Understand that this principle underpins the entire argument against self-reference.
This principle is invoked to show that a self-referential statement would require a contradiction.
2
Examine the definition of tasdiq
The author defines tasdiq as a mental form intended to correspond to reality (muhakah). This correspondence is the basis for truth and falsity. Recognize that if the form cannot correspond to an external object, it cannot be a genuine proposition.
Tasdiq here includes the property of being verifiable.
3
Apply the definition to a self-referential statement
Consider the statement 'This statement is true' pointing to itself. Its content does not terminate in an external reality but loops back on itself. Hence it cannot achieve muhakah, so it is not a khabar.
The statement lacks the necessary correspondence to be evaluated as true or false.
4
Analyze Mir Zahid's additional argument
Mir Zahid argues that the subject of a judgment must be conceptually independent and realized before the judgment. A self-referential statement fails because it includes a relation and is not realized prior to the judgment.
This reinforces the impossibility from a different angle.
5
Consider al-Dawani's alternative solution
Al-Dawani proposes that such a statement is equivalent to a two-part statement: 'my statement is false, false', where one part is true and the other false, thereby avoiding the paradox. Evaluate whether this solution preserves the possibility of meaningful self-reference.
This solution shifts the problem by reinterpreting the statement as composite.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of the possibility of self-referential propositions and an exploration of the properties of tasdiq (assent/judgment). The author argues that a proposition cannot refer to itself because the act of correspondence (muhakah) requires an independent object. The passage opens with a general principle: a thing cannot coexist with its absence. From this, it follows that a statement like 'This statement is true' referring to itself cannot be a genuine khabar (declarative statement) because its relational content cannot correspond to an external reality—it loops back on itself. The author explains that tasdiq is a mental form intended to represent reality; self-representation is impossible. Al-Jalal al-Dawani offered a different solution: such a statement is equivalent to 'my statement is false, false,' implying two statements (a part and a whole), so no logical impossibility arises. Absolute Generality Between Modes of Existence
Author explains the logical relation between external and nafs al-amr existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the relationship of absolute generality between existence in the external world and existence in nafs al-amr.

Core Idea
Everything external exists in divine knowledge, but not vice versa.

In Islamic logic, existence is not monolithic—it has distinct modes. This lesson unpacks the relationship between existence in the external world (al-wujud fi al-kharij) and existence in the absolute reality (al-wujud fi nafs al-amr), which includes divine knowledge. The author uses the logical concept of absolute generality (al-umum al-mutlaq) to describe their connection, clarifying a key distinction that underpins later discussions in theology and philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Modes of Existence
Locate the two terms: الموجود في الخارج (external existence) and الموجود في نفس الأمر (existence in nafs al-amr). Understand each as a distinct logical category.
2
Define the Relation: Absolute Generality
The author states the relation is العموم المطلق (absolute generality). This means every external existent is also in nafs al-amr, but not every existent in nafs al-amr is external. Confirm this by reading the sentence: 'لأنَّ كلَّ موجود في الخارج موجودٌ في نَفْسِ الأمرِ'.
This is a one-way inclusion, not equivalence.
3
Understand Why External Existence Does Not Follow
The author explains that existence in nafs al-amr is considered 'بقطع النظر عن اعتبار المعتبر' (regardless of any observer's consideration). Thus, something can be in nafs al-amr without having external instantiation.
This prevents the fallacy of assuming that every object of knowledge must exist externally.
4
Examine the Point of Convergence: Allah
The author gives Allah as an example of مادة الاجتماع (point of convergence): He exists both externally (we may see Him) and in nafs al-amr (His essence is known). This shows that the two categories can overlap without collapsing.
This example illustrates that absolute generality allows for intersection.

Two Meanings of Imkan: Modal vs. Potentiality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two meanings of imkan (possibility) as negation of necessity and as potentiality, and identify which sense is used in modal propositions vs. divisions of actuality.

Core Idea
Imkan is used in two distinct senses: modal possibility and potentiality — conflating them leads to confusion in logic and theology.

In this 'fā'ida', the author clarifies that the term 'imkan' is used homonymously (mushtarak) in logic. One sense is the modal notion (negation of necessity) studied in modal propositions. The other is potentiality (quwwa), which is a division of actuality. Understanding this distinction is crucial when reading texts on possibility and necessity, especially in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh.

The modal sense is the subject of the science of modal propositions; the potentiality sense is used in discussions of change and actualization.

The author defines actuality (al-fi‘l) as 'the thing being of the nature to be and it is actual'. Potentiality is its opposite. Notice that potentiality is never actual (first difference), unlike modal possibility which often is actual.

Core Idea
Potentiality and modal possibility differ in actuality, reversibility, and change; the second difference is proven by the impossibility of both existence and non-existence being in potentiality simultaneously.

The author lists three ways in which potentiality (quwwa) differs from modal possibility (imkān). Understanding these differences is essential for correct reasoning in modal logic and metaphysics. List the three differences

Then, what was mentioned is based on the view that concepts have contradictories, and it was said: they have no contradictories. The logicians' statement: 'the contradictories of two equivalent things are equivalent, and the converse of the contradictory is such... etc.' is taken metaphorically, as al-Khayyālī verified, considering that if one considers the relation between them, there would be opposition between them, either in truth and falsity, or in truth only, as will become clear. 'Abd al-Ḥakīm said in his marginalia on al-Khayyālī: The truth is that if two contradictories are interpreted as two things that mutually preclude each other by essence, i.e., two things that mutually preclude and oppose each other such that by essence the realization of one in actual existence entails the non-existence of the other, and vice versa, like affirmation and negation—when affirmation between two things is realized, negation is eliminated, and vice versa—then there is no contradictory for a concept (i.e., a form), because the realization of one form does not entail the non-existence of the other. For the forms of 'human' and 'non-human' both exist together without opposition. Except when their relation to something is considered; then two propositions arise that are mutually exclusive in truth if the negation particle is not made to revert to the relation of 'human' to something but is considered a part of it, and if the negation particle is made to revert to that relation, they are mutually exclusive in truth and falsity. The same applies to qualified and constructed concepts: there is no opposition between them except by noticing the occurrence of that relation and its removal according to the two considerations mentioned for single terms. And if two contradictories are interpreted as two mutually exclusive things, i.e., two things each of which is intrinsically opposed to the other, whether there is mutual exclusion in actualization and non-existence as in propositions, or merely a conceptual distance such that when one is compared to the other it is more distant than anything else, then a concept has a contradictory, like 'human' and 'non-human'. Al-Sayyid's Refinement: Concepts in Themselves vs. in Application
Presents al-Sayyid's refinement on the contradiction of concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Sayyid al-Jurjānī's refinement: a concept considered in itself has no contradictory, but when considered in relation to something, its contradictory is its negation.

Core Idea
Concepts have no contradictories in themselves — only when applied do they become contradictory.

After presenting two definitions, the passage includes al-Sayyid's gloss which offers a third perspective: a single concept in itself cannot have a contradictory, because adding negation produces a new concept. Only when we consider the concept's truth about something does its contradictory become its negation. This lesson traces the commentary chain and understands the final position.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Sayyid's starting point
He says 'a single concept, if considered in itself, has no conceivable contradictory.'
This directly addresses the question of whether concepts have contradictories.
2
Understand the mechanism for an intrinsic contradictory
The only way to get a contradictory for a concept in itself is to add the meaning of the negation particle, resulting in a new concept (e.g., 'human' + negation = 'non-human'). This new concept is extremely distant from the original.
This aligns with the broad definition of naqīḍ as maximal conceptual distance.
3
Shift to the relational consideration
If we consider the concept's truth about something (e.g., the concept 'human' applied to Zayd), then the contradictory of that application is the negation of the truth (Zayd is not human).
Now we have a genuine contradictory because it involves truth and falsity.
4
Link to previous definitions
This reconciles the two interpretations: concepts have no intrinsic contradictory but have relational contradictory. The strict definition applies to propositions, the broad to concepts when they are considered with negation.
