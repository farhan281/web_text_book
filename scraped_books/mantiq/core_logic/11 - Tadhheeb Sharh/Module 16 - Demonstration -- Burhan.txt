Refuting the Claim That Thought Is Always Correct: The Argument from Contradiction of Rational People
Commentator refutes the claim that fikr is always sawab using evidence of contradictory opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional (modus tollens) argument that the existence of contradictions among rational people refutes the claim that thought is always correct.

Core Idea
Contradiction among rational people proves thought is not always correct.

This passage presents a powerful logical refutation of the claim that thought (fikr) always leads to correct conclusions. The commentator uses the observed phenomenon of contradictions among rational people to construct a conditional argument. Let's analyze how this works step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
The claim is: 'Thought is always correct' (al-fikr sawāb dā’iman). The commentator expresses astonishment at this claim, implying it is false.
2
Extract the conditional premise implied by the claim
If thought were always correct, then rational people would never contradict each other. Because if every thought is correct, any two thoughts must be compatible (both true).
This is the hidden conditional that the claimant must accept.
3
Observe the factual evidence
The passage states: 'Rational people contradict each other.' This is an empirical observation from Islamic intellectual history, e.g., Sunni vs. philosopher on the creation of the world.
The evidence directly negates the consequent.
4
Apply modus tollens
From 'If P then Q' and 'Not Q', we conclude 'Not P'. Here, if thought is always correct (P), then no contradiction (Q). But there is contradiction (Not Q). Therefore, thought is not always correct (Not P).
This is a valid conditional inference.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the claim that thought (fikr) is always correct. The commentator begins by explaining the astonishment expressed by the phrase 'How?' This astonishment is directed at the claim that thought is always correct, since the observed fact that rational people contradict each other shows that thought is not always correct. The commentator clarifies that if thought were always correct, there would be no contradictions; but contradictions exist, so thought is not always correct. Then a marginal note from al-Attar is mentioned, discussing an objection about the corruption of evidence not necessitating corruption of the conclusion. The commentator dismisses this as fruitless and notes that the commentator's statement is a reminder, not a proof, because the matter is self-evident. The passage then gives a concrete example: the Sunni thinker concludes the world is originated, while the philosopher concludes it is eternal. These two conclusions cannot both be correct (that would be a conjunction of opposites) nor both wrong (that would be a negation of opposites), so one must be correct and the other wrong. This proves that thought is not always correct. The passage then moves to an even stronger evidence: a single person contradicting himself. This yields certainty (jazm) that thought can be wrong, whereas the contradiction between different people only yields opinion (zann). Al-Attar adds a note about the modality of the proposition. Finally, the commentator addresses a potential objection: perhaps the error is due to the people not seeking truth but simply sophistry. The commentator refutes this by saying that the term 'rational' (aql) implies seeking truth; but even if some act out of necessity, that does not undermine the argument. Thus, the overall argument is a reductio: if thought were always correct, there would be no contradictions; but there are contradictions, so thought is not always correct.

Self-Contradiction as Certain Proof of Error
Commentator refutes the claim that fikr is always sawab using evidence of contradictory opinions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why self-contradiction yields certainty that thought can err, while contradiction between different people yields only opinion.

Core Idea
Self-contradiction provides certain proof that thought is fallible.

The passage moves from the weaker evidence of contradiction between people to the stronger evidence of a single person contradicting himself. This escalation strengthens the refutation. Let's examine the epistemological difference.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of contradiction
The first type is between different rational people (e.g., Sunni vs. philosopher on the world's origin). The second type is a single rational person contradicting himself in his own thoughts.
Self-contradiction includes changing one's own view or holding inconsistent beliefs simultaneously.
2
Assess the epistemic certainty of each
Contradiction between people only yields opinion (zann) because we rely on external reports and may doubt their sincerity or accuracy. Self-contradiction yields certainty (jazm) because we have direct internal knowledge of our own thoughts.
The passage explicitly states this: 'مناقضة العاقل نفسه تفيد الجزم'.
3
Apply to the refutation
If a single rational person can contradict himself, then thought cannot always be correct. This is a stronger and more direct refutation of the claim.
This step confirms the logical conclusion with higher certainty.
4
Reflect on why self-contradiction is stronger
Knowledge of one's own mind is immediate, whereas knowledge of others' thoughts is indirect. Thus, self-contradiction provides an undeniable counterexample to the claim of infallible thought.
This aligns with the principle that self-evident (badīhi) truths are the foundation of knowledge.

Proof by Contradiction for Equivalents of Contradictories
Al-Dasuqi explains the proof by contradiction that the contradictories of equivalents are equivalent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a proof by contradiction for the equivalence of contradictories using a conditional syllogism and identify the existence condition that must hold for the proof to be valid.

Core Idea
To prove equivalence of contradictories, assume non-equivalence and derive an impossible consequence.

In this lesson, we explore a central logical tool: proof by contradiction (dafil al-khalf). We will see how al-Dasuqi and al-Attar use it to show that if two terms are equivalent (coextensive), then their contradictories are also equivalent. The proof proceeds by assuming the claim is false, deriving a contradiction, and concluding the claim must be true. However, as al-Sayyid points out, the proof requires careful attention to the existence of the subject. This lesson will equip you with the method and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim to be proven
The claim is: 'Each of the contradictories of two equivalent terms applies to exactly the same things as the contradictory of the other.' In formal terms, if A and B are equivalent (same extension), then non-A and non-B are also equivalent. The text uses the example 'non-human' and 'non-speaking' as equivalents.
The claim is a universal affirmative: 'Every non-human is non-speaking' and its converse.
2
Assume the negation of the claim
To prove the claim by contradiction, assume its contradictory: 'Some non-human is not non-speaking' (a particular negative). This is the negation of the universal affirmative. If this assumption leads to an impossibility, the original claim is true.
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is a particular negative.
3
Derive the impossible consequence
From the particular negative, infer the particular affirmative 'Some non-human is speaking' (by double negation: not non-speaking = speaking). This is impossible because it would mean that 'non-human' and 'speaking' overlap while 'non-human' and 'non-speaking' do not, contradicting the equivalence of 'non-human' and 'non-speaking'.
The impossibility is that one equivalent applies without the other.
4
Formalize the proof as a conditional syllogism
The proof has the structure: If the claim were false (P), then its contradictory (Q) would hold. But Q leads to an impossibility (R). Therefore, P cannot be false, so it is true. This is a conditional syllogism of the form modus tollens: If P then Q; Q false; therefore P false? Actually, here we assume ¬P, then derive contradiction, so ¬P is false, thus P true. The text explains it as 'exception of the contradictory of the consequent'.
This is a standard reductio ad absurdum, classified as conditional syllogism (istithna'i).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary by al-Dasuqi and al-Attar on the rule that the contradictories of two equivalent terms are also equivalent. The author begins by stating the rule: 'Thus each of the contradictories of the two equivalents applies to everything that the contradictory of the other applies to.' Then al-Dasuqi provides a clarification using an example: 'Every non-human is non-speaking' and its converse. He outlines the proof by contradiction (dafil al-khalf): assume the claim is false, then its contradictory (a particular negative) would be true, which entails a particular affirmative that is impossible because it would mean one equivalent applies without the other, leading to a contradiction. Al-Attar expands by explaining that the equivalence reduces to two universal affirmatives. He formalizes the proof: the claim is that everything to which the contradictory of one equivalent applies, the contradictory of the other applies. If this claim were false, its contradictory (a particular negative) would be true, which entails a particular affirmative that is impossible (since it would require the two equivalents to be true without being coextensive). Therefore, the original claim is true. He then applies this to the example. The commentator also notes that the phrase 'otherwise the very thing corresponding to one of the equivalents would be true' is not the direct contradictory but an explicit statement of an implication. An objection is then raised by al-Sayyid: the entailment from 'Some non-human is not non-speaking' to 'Some non-human is speaking' does not hold if the subject may be non-existent, because the negative with a modified predicate is more general and does not entail the specific affirmative. The secret is that affirmation requires the existence of the subject. The response is that in the example, 'non-human' applies to existing things, so the entailment holds. However, in general, if the contradictories do not apply to anything, the proof fails. Finally, al-Dasuqi clarifies that dafil al-khalf is a conditional syllogism (istithna'i) where assuming the denial leads to a contradiction. He further explains the structure: from the falsehood of one universal (e.g., 'Every non-human is non-speaking'), its contradictory (particular negative) is derived, which entails the impossible (particular affirmative). This establishes the original universal by eliminating the contradictory. The same reasoning applies to the other universal. The passage guards against the misunderstanding that the proof by contradiction requires the subject to exist in all cases, but it is shown that the proof depends on the applicability of the terms to actual existents.

Coextension (Dawaran): Inferring Cause from Presence and Absence
Coextension method with wine example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the coextension method to identify the cause of a legal ruling by checking its presence and absence.

Core Idea
Coextension reveals cause: if ruling and attribute are always together, the attribute is the cause.

This lesson introduces coextension (dawaran) as a key method for discovering the cause (ʿilla) of a ruling. The passage explains that coextension is an indicator: if the ruling is present whenever the attribute is present, and absent whenever the attribute is absent, then the attribute is likely the cause. Through the classic example of prohibition and intoxication, you will practice this logical inference.

Commentary
This passage defines two methods for identifying the cause ('illa) of a legal ruling: coextension (dawaran) and elimination (tardid). Burhan Limmi and Burhan Inni
Commentary defines burhan limmi and burhan inni with medical example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan limmi and burhan inni by identifying whether the middle term is a cause in both mind and reality or only in the mind.

Core Idea
The middle term's causation in reality decides if a demonstration is limmi (from cause) or inni (from effect).

In this passage, the commentator explains the two main types of demonstration (burhan) based on the role of the middle term. The key is not just that the middle term is a cause for the conclusion, but whether that causation exists in external reality or only in the mind. We will use the famous medical example to make this concrete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion (nata'ij) and the premises.
In the example 'Zayd has putrid humors; everything with putrid humors is feverish; therefore Zayd is feverish', the conclusion is 'Zayd is feverish'. The premises are about putrid humors and fever.
2
Locate the middle term (hadd al-awsat).
The middle term is 'putrid humors' (mut'affin al-akhlat), which appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
3
Determine if the middle term is a cause for the judgment in external reality.
In reality, putrid humors cause fever. So the middle term is a cause both in the mind (we use it to infer fever) and externally (it produces fever). This makes the demonstration limmi.
If the middle term were only a sign or effect, it would be inni.
4
Apply the definition: if cause in both, it is limmi; if only in mind, it is inni.
The commentator states: if the middle term is a cause for the relation in the mind and also in reality, it is limmi; otherwise, it is inni. This distinction is fundamental for evaluating demonstrations.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the materials of syllogisms, defining two types of demonstration: limmi (demonstration from cause) and inni (demonstration from effect). The author begins by explaining that the middle term must be a cause for the relation of the greater (predicate) to the lesser (subject) in the mind. If the middle term is also a cause for that relation in external reality, the demonstration is called limmi because it gives the cause both in the mind and externally. A medical example is provided: 'Zayd has putrid humors; everything with putrid humors is feverish; therefore Zayd is feverish.' Here, the middle term (putrid humors) is the cause of the fever in reality, so it is a limmi demonstration. Al-Dasuqi elaborates on the term 'inna-ness', noting it is derived from 'inna' (indeed) used in statements. Al-Attar then clarifies the meaning of 'humors' as the four bodily fluids and adds that the middle term being a cause for the relation means it is a cause for assenting to the predication. He also explains that 'limmi' is derived from 'lim' (why?), used to inquire about causes. The author further subcategorizes inni demonstrations: if the middle term is an effect of the judgment in reality, it is called a 'proof' (dalil), as in the example 'Zayd is feverish, everything feverish has putrid humors' (here the fever is an effect of the humors); otherwise it has no specific name, as in the example of fever that intensifies every other day and is inflammatory, where neither is a cause of the other but both are effects of the same cause (yellow bile).

Burhan Limmi vs Burhan Inni: Causality of the Middle Term
Author defines burhan limmi and inni based on middle term's causality in mind and externally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a demonstration as burhan limmi or burhan inni by analyzing whether the middle term is a cause for the conclusion in both mind and external reality, or only in the mind.

Core Idea
The middle term's causality determines whether a demonstration shows why or merely that.

In Islamic logic, demonstrations (barahin) are divided into two kinds based on what the middle term reveals. This distinction is crucial for evaluating the explanatory power of an argument. Let us learn how to identify each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term
Find the term in the syllogism that connects the subject and predicate. In the example, the middle term is 'putrid humors' connecting Zayd to fever.
Middle term is always the cause in burhan limmi.
2
Determine mental causality
Check if the middle term is a cause for the conclusion in the mind (i.e., it explains why the predicate belongs to the subject). In the example, putrid humors cause fever in the mind.
3
Determine external causality
Check if the middle term is also an external cause (real causal link). In burhan limmi it is; in burhan inni it is not—it is only a sign.
4
Classify the demonstration
If the middle term is a cause both in mind and externally, it is burhan limmi (demonstration of why). If only mentally, it is burhan inni (demonstration of that).

Commentary
This passage defines the two types of demonstration: burhan limmi and burhan inni. The author explains that the distinction hinges on whether the middle term is a cause for the relation in both the mind and external reality, or only in the mind. If the middle term is a cause both mentally and externally, the demonstration is limmi, which provides knowledge of the cause (the 'why'). If it is a cause only in the mind, the demonstration is inni, which only confirms that the relation holds in the mind, not its cause in reality. The Dassuqi commentary elaborates on the terminology: 'inniyya' is derived from the word 'inna' (verily), indicating affirmation, while 'limmiyya' is derived from the question 'lima' (why), indicating causality. Thus, burhan inni affirms the existence of a relation without providing its causal explanation.

Resolving the Doubt: Certainty of Burhan Inni
Author presents a doubt from Ibn Sina and al-Sayyid's answer regarding certainty of burhan inni.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between burhan limmi and burhan inni in terms of what each proves with certainty, and explain al-Sayyid's resolution of Ibn Sina's doubt.

Core Idea
Burhan inni is certain about some cause, not a specific cause—and that suffices for demonstration.

A doubt arises: Ibn Sina stated that certain knowledge of something with a cause requires knowledge of that cause. If so, how can inference from effect to cause (burhan inni) be a certain demonstration? Al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani clarifies that Ibn Sina meant knowledge of a specific unsensed thing; burhan inni only claims to know that some cause exists, not which one. This preserves its certainty as a demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Locate Ibn Sina's claim that certain knowledge of something with a cause comes only through its cause. Why does this threaten burhan inni?
The objector argues that the conclusion of burhan inni would not be certain, violating the definition of demonstration.
2
Find al-Sayyid's key distinction
Al-Sayyid reinterprets Ibn Sina: he meant knowledge of a specific unsensed thing requires its specific cause. But burhan inni does not aim for that.
The effect only indicates 'some cause', not a particular cause. This is the crucial distinction.
3
Apply the distinction to the doubt
Since burhan inni only claims certain knowledge that some cause exists, it does meet the condition for demonstration: it yields certainty about the existence of a cause.
The doubt is resolved because the required level of certainty is about existence, not specification.
4
Contrast with burhan limmi
Burhan limmi (cause to effect) gives certain knowledge of a specific effect. Burhan inni gives certain knowledge of some cause.
This asymmetry justifies classifying them as two distinct types of demonstration.
5
Verify the resolution
After al-Sayyid's clarification, does the original objection still hold?
No, because it confused the type of certainty required; al-Sayyid shows that burhan inni satisfies its own appropriate certainty.

And inference from the existence of an effect to the existence of some cause (not specifically), or from its non-existence to the non-existence of all its causes, or to the non-existence of a specific one of them, is a burhan inni.

The passage also explains the distinction: a specific cause yields a specific effect, but a specific effect only yields some cause. Essential Accidents: Real vs. Known and the Need for Proof
Author defends need for burhan for essential accidents by distinguishing real from known essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify essential accidents into three types based on their attachment to the subject, and explain why even essential accidents may require demonstration.

Core Idea
Essential accidents have no intermediary in reality, but knowing them often requires proof.

In this passage, the author defends the need for burhan to establish essential accidents. An objector claims that if essential accidents have no intermediary, they would be self-evident. The author replies by distinguishing the real essence from our knowledge: in reality there is no intermediary, but the knowledge may still require demonstration. Al-Dasuqi then expands the classification, dividing accidents into essential (dhati) and non-essential (gharib), with essential further subdivided into three types. This resolves the apparent contradiction and clarifies the role of demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's challenge
The objector argues: if essential accidents have no intermediary, then the propositions asserting them need no proof, contradicting the definition of masa'il as requiring demonstration.
2
Distinguish real vs. known essence
The author responds: in reality (nafs al-amr) there is no intermediary, but our knowledge of the attachment may require proof. Thus the need for demonstration is epistemological, not ontological.
This distinction is crucial for understanding why essential accidents are not automatically known.
3
Analyze al-Dasuqi's classification of essential accidents
Al-Dasuqi lists three types: (1) without any intermediary (e.g., amazement to man), (2) through a part (e.g., voluntary movement through being animal), (3) through a coextensive external intermediary (e.g., laughter through amazement).
Note that the coextensive intermediary is external but coextensive with the subject.
4
Distinguish essential from non-essential
Non-essential (gharib) accidents attach via an intermediary that is more specific (e.g., laughter to animal through being human), more general (e.g., movement to man through being animal), or disparate (e.g., heat to water through fire).
This classification shows that even essential accidents may require proof when the attachment is through an intermediary.
5
Assess al-'Attar's critique
Al-'Attar notes that the author's word 'rubba' (sometimes) contradicts earlier claims that masa'il are always theoretical. He suggests deleting it, indicating a scholarly dispute.
This highlights the sensitivity of wording in logical works.
