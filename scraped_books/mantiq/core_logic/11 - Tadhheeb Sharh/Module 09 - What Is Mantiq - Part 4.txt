The General Possible Proposition
Commentary defines the general possible proposition as the absence of necessity of its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the general possible proposition (al-mumkinah al-ammah) and explain why the absence of necessity of the opposite is a consequence of general possibility, not the propositional quality itself.

Core Idea
The general possible proposition is defined by the absence of necessity of its opposite, not by the quality of possibility itself.

In this passage, the commentator deepens the definition of the general possible proposition (al-mumkinah al-ammah). You already know that a modal proposition involves necessity, possibility, or actuality. Here, the focus is on how possibility is defined through negation: the absence of necessity of the opposite judgment. But careful—this absence is not the intrinsic quality of the proposition; it is a necessary consequence of the broader notion of general possibility (al-imkan al-amm). Let's unpack this subtle distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defined term
Locate the term 'al-mumkinah al-ammah' in the Arabic. Note that it is defined here as a proposition judged without necessity, perpetuity, or actuality, specifically by 'the absence of necessity of its opposite' (bi-'adam darurat khilafiha).
The opposite (khilaf) refers to the contradictory of the judgment.
2
Understand the two-part definition
The commentary first gives a negative condition: the judgment is not made with necessity, perpetuity, or actuality. Then it gives the positive core: 'or by the absence of necessity of its opposite'. This absence is the defining feature of the general possible.
The word 'or' (aw) indicates an alternative formulation, not a separate type.
3
Grasp the commentary's refinement
Al-Dusuqi clarifies that the absence of necessity of the opposite is not itself the quality (kayfiyya) of the proposition. Rather, it is a necessary consequence (lazim) of the quality of general possibility. General possibility is the deeper notion: it allows the judgment to be either necessary or merely permissible.
Think of it: if the opposite is not necessary, the judgment itself could be necessary or possible—both are covered.
4
Contrast the two interpretations of general possibility
The passage concludes by stating that general possibility is interpreted in two ways: (1) negation of essential necessity from the opposite side, and (2) negation of essential impossibility from the agreeing side. Both lead to the same class of propositions.
For an affirmative judgment, opposite = negative; negating necessity there means the affirmation can be necessary or contingent.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional commentary on the 'general possible proposition' (al-mumkinah al-ammah). It begins with a chapter heading indicating a section on definition and its divisions. The text defines the general possible proposition as one where the judgment is not made with necessity, perpetuity, or actuality of the relation, but rather with the absence of necessity of the opposite of the judgment. Al-Dusuqi expands on this definition, clarifying that the absence of necessity of the opposite is not itself the quality of the proposition, but a necessary consequence of the quality of general possibility (al-imkan al-amm). He explains that general possibility is the negation of necessity from the side opposite to the judgment. For example, if the judgment is affirmative, the opposite is negative; negating the necessity from the negative side allows the affirmation to be possibly necessary (obligatory) or merely permissible (contingent). Al-Attar then discusses the scope of the general possible, noting that it is more general than even the four simple propositions (presumably the unconditional, actual, perpetual, etc.). He cites al-Dawani's refutation of the claim that it is not more general than the general conditioned proposition. Finally, the author of the commentary suggests an alternative formulation for the definition of the general possible proposition, emphasizing that the judgment's opposite is not necessary. This aligns with the two interpretations of general possibility: either as negation of essential necessity from the opposite side, or as negation of essential impossibility from the agreeing side.

Definition of the Connected Conditional
Author defines the connected conditional and distinguishes positive and negative forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a connected conditional proposition and distinguish its affirmative and negative forms.

Core Idea
The connected conditional links two relations: one affirmed or denied upon the condition of another.

The connected conditional (al-shartiyya al-muttasila) is the foundation of conditional reasoning in Islamic logic. This lesson unpacks its definition from the matn, showing how a relation is judged to hold or not hold based on another relation. Understanding this definition is essential for evaluating arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh where conditional statements are common.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Look for the term being defined: 'al-shartiyya al-muttasila' (the connected conditional). Notice the author divides conditionals into connected and disjunctive first.
The definition applies only to the connected type.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'conditional proposition' (shartiyya). The differentia is that a judgment of one relation (nisba) is made upon the condition (taqdīr) of another relation.
The definition specifies 'affirmation of a relation' or 'negation of a relation'.
3
Analyze the affirmative example
The author gives: 'If the sun has risen, then daytime exists.' Here the relation 'daytime exists' is affirmed upon the condition 'sun has risen'. This is the affirmative connected conditional (muttasila mūjaba).
The example uses natural phenomena; in Islamic sciences, similar structures apply to theological or legal conditionals.
4
Analyze the negative example
The negative form negates a relation on a condition: 'It is not the case that if the sun has risen, then nighttime exists.' This judges that the relation (nighttime exists) does not hold under that condition.
Truth value depends on correspondence with reality, as the commentator notes.
5
Clarify the term 'nisba'
The commentator explains that 'nisba' here means the affair (amr) or content of the antecedent and consequent, not the syntactic relation. So 'existence of daytime' is the content, not the copula.
This prevents confusion with grammatical relations.

Commentary
This passage defines the connected conditional (al-shartiyya al-muttasila) as a type of conditional proposition. The author states that a conditional is either connected or disjunctive. The connected conditional is defined as a judgment of a relation (nisba) being affirmed (thubut) or negated (nafy) on the condition of another relation. The affirmative example is: 'If the sun has risen, then daytime exists.' Here, the relation affirmed is the existence of daytime, conditioned upon the relation of the sun's rising. The commentator (al-Dasuqi) clarifies that 'affirmation of a relation' means the occurrence of a relation, whether positive or negative. The condition 'upon the condition of another relation' includes both necessary (luzumiyya) and accidental (ittifaqiyya) connections, allowing for later divisions. The negative connected conditional is exemplified by 'It is not the case that if the sun has risen, then nighttime exists.' The commentator further explains that 'relation' in this context refers to the content of the consequent (tali) and antecedent (muqaddam), i.e., the 'amr (affair) itself, not the syntactic relation; thus 'existence of daytime' is something related (mansub), not a relation (nisba) per se. This precision ensures the definition distinguishes between the components and the connection between them.

The Connected Conditional (al-Muttasila): Definition and Types
Author defines the muttasila by affirming or negating a relation upon a condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between affirmative and negative connected conditional propositions in Arabic logical texts.

Core Idea
A connected conditional judges a relation between two propositions upon a condition.

In this lesson, we examine the definition of the connected conditional (al-muttasila) as presented in the commentary tradition. The author defines it as a proposition in which a relation is either affirmed or negated upon a condition. This distinction yields two types: affirmative and negative. Understanding this definition is crucial for analyzing conditional arguments in Islamic logic, particularly in juridical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
What is being defined? The connected conditional (al-muttasila). This is the term under analysis.
The Arabic term is المتصلة.
2
Extract genus and differentia
The genus is a conditional proposition (qadiyya shartiyya). The differentia is that it judges a relation (nisba) upon a condition (taqdir). Specifically, it either affirms or negates that relation.
The commentators note that 'nisba' is used metaphorically.
3
Distinguish affirmative from negative
Affirmative (mujiba) affirms the relation under the condition; negative (saliba) negates the relation under the condition. For example, 'If the sun is risen, the day is existent' is affirmative; 'If the sun is risen, the night is not existent' is negative.
The negation is of the relation, not of the consequent alone.
4
Examine the metaphorical use of 'nisba'
Al-Dasuqi explains that after the conditional particle enters, the relation is not within each clause but between them. 'Nisba' here metaphorically refers to the content of the consequent, not a predicative relation.
This avoids the mistake of thinking the conditional affirms the existence of the antecedent or consequent individually.
5
Enumerate the possible combinations
For both affirmative and negative, there are four cases based on whether the antecedent and consequent are existential or non-existential. E.g., affirmative: both existential, both non-existential, or one existential and the other non-existential. Negative follows the same pattern.
This exhaustive listing ensures the definition covers all possibilities.

Refining the Definition of Negative Conditionals
Commentators refine the definition of negative conditional to avoid false inclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a genuinely negative conditional from an affirmative conditional with a modified (negative) consequent, using the logicians' criterion.

Core Idea
Negativity in conditionals is about the connection, not the parts.

The original definition of a negative conditional might seem to include cases where the consequent contains a negation. However, this would conflate a negative judgement with a modified affirmative one. The commentators clarify that the negation must operate on the connection (ittisal) between antecedent and consequent, not merely appear in one of the parts. This refinement prevents false classification and aligns conditional logic with the established treatment of categorical propositions.

Guided Path
1
Pinpoint the original definition
The author defines a negative conditional as a proposition where the judgement is 'by its negation', i.e., the negation of the relation (the affirmation of the relation is denied). This is the starting point that needs refinement.
2
Examine the counterexample
Consider 'If the sun is rising, then night is not existent.' This proposition is about a connection: sunrise implies the non-existence of night. The consequent contains a negation, so the original definition might mistakenly count it as negative.
The example shows the definition's lack of exclusivity (man').
3
Locate where the negation operates
The negation in the example is inside the consequent, not on the connection itself. The judgement affirms the connection (sunrise → no night), so the proposition is affirmative. The negative particle modifies the consequent, making it a 'modified' proposition (muʿaddala).
The commentators stress: the negation must be of the connection (salb al-ittisal), not of a part.
4
Apply the categorical analogy
Just as in categoricals 'Zayd is not standing' is affirmative (subject-predicate) with a negative predicate, conditionals follow the same rule. The presence of a negative particle in a part does not make the whole proposition negative.
This analogy helps solidify the concept.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement by the commentators al-Dasuqi and al-Attar, clarifying a potential misunderstanding from the author's definition of negative conditional propositions. The issue arises from an example: 'If the sun is rising, then the night is not existent.' Some might think this is a negative conditional because the consequent contains a negation. The judgement is about the connection between the antecedent and the consequent as a whole; here the judgement affirms the connection (that the existence of sunlight implies the non-existence of night). Thus it is an affirmative conditional with a negative consequent—what is called 'modified affirmative' (muʿaddala). The commentators draw an analogy with categorical propositions: 'Zayd is not standing' is still an affirmative proposition (subject-predicate) but with a modified predicate. Similarly, in conditionals, the negation of the connection is what makes a proposition negative, not the presence of negation in the parts. Al-Attar quotes al-Sayyid (al-Sharif al-Jurjani) who emphasizes that the negation in conditionals is about the connection (ittisal) or disjunction (infisal) itself, not about the extremes (muqaddam and tali). Therefore, the four combinations of affirmative/negative extremes can appear in both affirmative and negative conditionals. This guards against the misunderstanding that the presence of a negative particle in the consequent automatically makes the whole proposition negative.

The Definition of Contradiction and Its Two Conditions
Author defines logical contradiction and explains why the condition 'and vice versa' is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the two core conditions that define logical contradiction (al-tanaqud) according to classical Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Contradiction is a two-way street: truth of each implies falsehood of the other, and vice versa.

In this lesson, we will study the precise definition of logical contradiction (tanaqud) as given by the author. The definition centres on two conditions: mutual implication of truth and falsehood, and the requirement of reversal. We'll see why both conditions are necessary with examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definiendum
Find the Arabic term being defined (التناقض). Notice that the definition is given right after 'فَصْلٌ: فِي التَّنَاقُضِ'.
2
Extract the first condition
The first part: 'مِنْ صِدْقِ كُلِّ مِنَ الْقَضِيَّتَيْنِ كَذِبُ الْأُخْرَى' — from the truth of each of the two propositions follows the falsehood of the other. This means if one is true, the other is false.
Note the word 'كُلِّ' (each) is important.
3
Identify the second condition (vice versa)
The phrase 'وَبِالْعَكْسِ' adds the reverse: from the falsehood of each follows the truth of the other. Without this, the relation would be one-sided.
This excludes cases where both can be false simultaneously.
4
Test the definition on an example
Consider the pair 'Every human is an animal' and 'No human is an animal'. The first is true, so the second is false, but if the first were false? The example shows that truth of one implies falsehood of the other, but falsehood of one does not guarantee truth of the other. So the second condition fails, meaning no genuine contradiction.
This is a key insight: without the reverse condition, we would consider such pairs as contradictory, but they are not.

Core Idea
For true contradiction, the implication must be intrinsic, not due to external factors or mediation.

The definition of contradiction includes the phrase 'li-dhatihi' (by itself). This condition is crucial to exclude cases where the implication is mediated by an external factor. The Intrinsic Condition (Li-Dhatihi) in Contradiction
Author excludes cases where implication is not intrinsic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given pair of propositions constitutes a true logical contradiction by checking if the mutual implication is intrinsic (li-dhat al-ikhtilaf) or mediated by material equivalence.

Core Idea
Not every pair that mutually implies falsehood is a contradiction — the implication must be by the logical form itself.

In defining contradiction, logicians require that the mutual exclusion be 'by itself' (li-dhatihi). This lesson examines two examples that appear contradictory but fail the intrinsic test. Understanding this condition prevents false positives in identifying contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Recall that true contradiction requires that from the truth of one, the falsehood of the other follows and vice versa (tanāquḍ). But the author adds a crucial restriction: this must be 'by the essence of the difference'.
The condition is li-dhat al-ikhtilaf.
2
Examine the first excluded example
The pair 'Zaid has motionless fingers' and 'Zaid is not moving' – both can be true if Zaid is motionless but not altogether immobile. So no mutual implication, hence no contradiction.
This fails the basic mutual implication condition.

Examine the second excluded example
The pair 'Zaid is a human' and 'Zaid is not a nāṭiq' – here truth of each does imply falsehood of the other, but only because 'human' and 'nāṭiq' are equivalent. This implication is not from the logical form but from material synonymy.
This fails the intrinsic condition.
4
Apply the intrinsic test
To check if a difference is intrinsic, ask: 'Does the mutual implication depend on the logical form (quality, quantity, and categorical structure) alone, or on extra-logical facts about the subject matter?' If the latter, it is not a true contradiction.

Section: On Contradiction. Unity is required in eight things: subject, predicate, time, place, relation, condition, potentiality, actuality, part, and whole. al-Dassuqi: His statement: 'potentiality and actuality' – they are one just as part and whole are one, and the waw in both means 'or', or they are interchangeable; i.e., either potentiality or actuality, and part or whole, so each one is one of the eight. al-Matar: His statement: 'subject' – he did not say 'that which is judged upon' because it includes conditionals, and he did not mention them because the consideration of the eight unities does not appear in conditionals as al-Isam noted. His statement: 'time' – an objection was raised that contradiction occurs in our saying: 'Zayd was father of Amr yesterday' and 'He is not a father to him today' despite the lack of unity of time. The answer: we do not concede that contradiction occurs in it, because the truth of one and falsity of the other is not due to the difference itself but to the particular matter, because fatherhood is a quality which, if it obtained yesterday, obtains today. As for what is said: 'Unity of time necessitates unity of place due to the impossibility of a thing being in two places at one time' – it is a mistake, for here there are two things: one is the affirmative relation and the other is the negative relation; it is permissible for both to exist in one time and each to be in a different place, as in our saying: 'Zayd is sitting now in the mosque, Zayd is not sitting now in the market.' The upshot: place is a container for the predicate, and time is a container for the relation. His statement: 'relation' – it is the repeated relation, like fatherhood and sonship. His statement: 'condition' – i.e., if a condition is considered in one of them, that condition must be considered in the other; so there is no contradiction when there is difference in it, by considering it in one but not the other, or by considering a condition in each that is contrary to the condition of the other. His statement: 'potentiality and actuality' – Abd al-Hakim said: what is meant by potentiality is absence of actuality at present with possibility for it, and by actuality is obtaining in the present; and they are not the same as possibility. Then there is no contradiction in: 1. 'Zayd is standing', 'Amr is not standing' – due to difference of subject. 2. Nor 'Zayd is standing', 'Zayd is not sitting' – due to difference of predicate. 3. Nor 'Zayd is standing by night', 'Zayd is not standing by day' – due to difference of time. 4. Nor 'Zayd is standing in the mosque', 'Zayd is not standing in the market' – due to difference of place. 5. Nor 'Zayd is father of Bakr', 'Zayd is not father of Amr' – due to difference of relation. 6. Nor 'The body is dispersive of sight, i.e., on condition of being white', 'The body is not dispersive of sight, i.e., on condition of being black' – due to difference of condition. al-Dassuqi: And it is apparent that they meant by actuality and potentiality here their two distinct meanings, not the two co-extensive ones of which one is part of the other. His statement: 'due to difference of subject' – i.e., if the subject, or predicate, or any of the mentioned matters differ, it is possible for both propositions to be true and both to be false, and then they are not contradictories, for contradictories cannot both be true nor both false; rather one must be true and the other false. His statement: 'dispersive' – i.e., weakening of sight, and because it was so, the ink with which paper is written was made black. And know that some of their examples for these unities are faulty, like their example for condition: 'Color is dispersive of sight, color is not dispersive of sight', and for part and whole: 'The Zinji is black', and al-Attar: 'and the two unqualified senses (mutlaq) among the modalities; do you not see that they can be qualified by possibility and absolute unqualified? So in reality they are two qualifications of the predicate, not a quality of the relation.'

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and commentary on the conditions for contradiction (tanāquḍ). The core matn states that for two propositions to be genuine contradictories, they must be unified in eight aspects: subject, predicate, time, place, relation, condition, potentiality/actuality, and part/whole. The commentator al-Dassuqi immediately clarifies that potentiality/actuality are considered as one condition (like part/whole), so the total is eight. al-Matar then explains each condition: 'subject' is used instead of 'that which is judged upon' to include conditional propositions, though the conditions of contradiction do not fully apply to conditionals. Regarding 'time', an objection is raised: if we say 'Zayd was father of Amr yesterday' and 'He is not a father to him today', the times differ yet there seems to be contradiction. The answer is that this is not genuine contradiction because the truth/falsity arises from the nature of fatherhood (which persists) not the logical form; unity of time is necessary for formal contradiction. A further mistake is refuted: the claim that unity of time necessarily implies unity of place because a thing cannot be in two places at once; the answer distinguishes the two relations (affirmative and negative) which can coexist in the same time but different places, as in 'Zayd is sitting in the mosque now' and 'Zayd is not sitting in the market now'. Thus place is a container for the predicate, time for the relation. 'Relation' means the relation that repeats, like fatherhood and sonship; 'condition' means if a condition is in one, the same condition must be in the other; otherwise difference of condition prevents contradiction. Then examples illustrate each condition: difference of subject (Zayd vs. Amr), predicate (standing vs. sitting), time (night vs. day), place (mosque vs. market), relation (father of Bakr vs. father of Amr), condition (being white vs. black). al-Dassuqi adds that the intended meanings of potentiality/actuality here are distinct, not overlapping. Finally, the commentator notes that some examples from earlier scholars are faulty, like the example for condition ('color is dispersive of sight') and for part/whole (the Zinji example). al-Attar further clarifies that the unqualified senses (mutlaq) are actually qualifications of the predicate, not the relation. The overall lesson is that formal contradiction requires strict parallelism in eight respects; otherwise the two statements may both be true or both false.

Simple Conversion: Definition and Conditions
Defines simple conversion and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the definition of simple conversion to categorical and conditional propositions and verify whether a given conversion is valid.

Core Idea
Simple conversion swaps terms while keeping truth and quality – but only for categoricals and conditionals.

In this lesson, we examine simple conversion (al-'aks al-mustawi), a key operation in immediate inference. The text defines it as exchanging the subject and predicate (or antecedent and consequent) while preserving truth and quality. Understanding its conditions is essential for valid reasoning in Islamic sciences, where such conversions are used to extend or test rulings and theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of simple conversion
Read the definition: it is the exchange of the two terms of a proposition while preserving truth (sidq) and quality (kayf). Note that 'exchange' means making the subject a predicate and vice versa (for categoricals), or making the antecedent a consequent and vice versa (for conditionals).
Quality refers to whether the proposition is affirmative or negative.
2
Determine which propositions admit conversion
The text states that conversion applies only to categorical and conditional propositions. Disjunctive propositions have no simple conversion. This is because the logical relationship between disjuncts is not preserved under simple term swap.
For disjunctive propositions, the incompatibility judgment remains unchanged regardless of order, so no conversion is needed.
3
Understand the two conditions for establishing a valid conversion
To prove that a given conversion is valid, two things are required: (1) the converted proposition must be a logical consequence (lāzima) of the original, proven by a universally applicable demonstration; (2) no more specific proposition than the converse is logically implied by the original.
These conditions ensure that the converse is the most specific necessary consequence.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the categorical proposition 'Every khamr (intoxicant) is haram (forbidden)'. Swapping terms gives 'Some haram things are khamr'. This preserves truth and quality (both affirmative) and is a logical consequence. Check that a more specific proposition like 'All haram things are khamr' is not implied (since not all haram things are intoxicants). Hence the conversion is valid.

Explanation of the Four Modalities in Conversion
Commentator clarifies the four modalities in conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four modalities in equipollent conversion and distinguish between absolute and conditional modalities.

Core Idea
The four modalities are not four simple terms but two basic modalities each qualified by a condition.

In conversion, the proposition is converted under one of four modal qualifiers. The commentator clarifies that the list 'by necessity, always, as long as J' is misleading: the last two are actually compound modalities. This parsing exercise will help you understand how modal logic applies to conversion in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four terms listed
The commentator lists three: بالضرورة, دائماً, ما دام ج. But he interprets them as four: الضرورة (absolute necessary), دائماً (absolute perpetual), بالضرورة ما دام ج (general conditional), دائماً ما دام ج (general customary).
2
Parse the commentator's explanation
He says 'أي: بالضرورة' for absolute necessary; 'أو دائماً' for absolute perpetual; 'أو ما دام ج' meaning either بالضرورة ما دام ج for المشروطة العامة or دائماً ما دام ج for العرفية العامة.
3
Understand the composition
The modality is not just 'ما دام ج' but a combination: necessity+conditional or perpetuity+conditional. Thus the four modalities are: absolute necessary, absolute perpetual, necessary as long as J, always as long as J.
4
Apply to examples from Kalam
For instance, 'The world is temporally originated necessarily' is absolute necessary; 'The world is temporally originated always' is absolute perpetual; 'As long as the world exists, it is temporally originated' is a conditional, and the modality is the whole compound.

Analogy (al-Tamthil) and Its Causal Methods
Definition of analogy and exposition of its two causal methods with critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of analogical reasoning (asl, far, illa) and explain why the two methods (dawaran and tardid) do not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers rulings through a shared cause, but its methods only yield probability, not certainty.

In this lesson, we study the definition of analogy (tamthil) as presented in the text. Analogy is a fundamental tool in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh) for extending rulings from known cases to new ones. The passage defines analogy and then introduces two methods for identifying the cause (illa): co-variation (dawaran) and elimination (tardid). The commentary by al-Dasuqi immediately warns that neither method produces certainty. This lesson will help you understand both the mechanics and the epistemic limitations of these methods.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of analogy
The text says 'جُزْئِيٌّ مُشَارِكٌ لِجُزْئِيٍّ آخَرَ' – a particular sharing with another particular. Identify the shared property as the cause (illa).
The definition emphasizes the sharing of an attribute, not just any resemblance.
2
Map the wine example
In the example: wine (asl) has intoxication (illa), which causes prohibition (hukm). The branch (far) is any other intoxicating substance.
This is the classic example in Islamic legal analogy.

Learn the first method: Dawaran
Dawaran is the constant conjunction of two things in existence and non-existence. The commentary explains that this fails because a co-extensive condition (shart musawi) may co-vary without being the true cause.

Tardid involves listing possible attributes of the root and eliminating those that are inconsistent. The commentary warns that if the division is not exhaustive, the true cause may be omitted.
Tardid requires a complete enumeration of possibilities, which is often impractical.

Both methods produce probabilistic knowledge (zann), not certainty (yaqin). Recognizing this is crucial for evaluating the strength of legal analogies.
In usul al-fiqh, qiyas is accepted as a source of law despite this probabilistic nature.

The Predicates of Sciences: External Essential Accidents
Author defines predicates of sciences as external essential accidents and answers an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of essential accidents (ʿāriḍ dhātī) and explain why predicates of scientific problems must be external to their subjects.

Core Idea
Predicates are not parts of subjects; they are external essential accidents that still require proof.

In this passage, the author defines the nature of predicates in the sciences. He argues that if predicates were parts of the subjects, they would be self-evident and need no proof. But since we do prove them, they must be external. He then classifies essential accidents into three categories based on how they attach to the subject, and addresses an objection regarding the need for proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Find the statement that predicates are external to subjects: 'محمولات المسائل فهي أمور خارجة عنها' – predicates are matters external to their subjects.
2
Understand the argument for externality
The author argues: if predicates were parts, they would not need proof because a part of a thing cannot be proven. Since we do need proof, they must not be parts.
This is a modus tollens conditional argument.
3
Distinguish the three types of essential accident
The author defines three ways an accident attaches: (1) by itself (e.g., wonder to man), (2) by its part (e.g., movement by will to man via animal), (3) by an external equivalent (e.g., laughter to man via wonder).
These correspond to classifications in Islamic logic.
4
Address the objection
The opponent says: essential accidents have no intermediary, so they should need no proof. The author replies: they have no intermediary in reality, but our knowledge of them may still require proof.

فَصْلٌ: في أجزاء العلوم. مَحْمُولَاتُ المُسَائِل فهي أُمُورُ خَارِجَةٌ عَنْهَا، أي: عن موضوعاتها؛ إذ لو كانت أجزاء للموضوعاتِ، لم يُحتَج في ثبوتها لها إلى برهان؛ لامتناع أن يكون جزء الشَّيء مطلوباً بالبرهان، لكنَّا نحتاج في ثبوتِ محمولات المسائل للموضوعاتِ إلى البرهان كما ذكرنا من أنَّ المسائل هي القضايا المطلوبة التي يبرهن عليها في العلوم، فالمحمولاتُ خارجة عن الموضوعات، وإلا لم يبرهن عليها في العلوم. لَاحِقَةٌ بالرَّفع؛ صفةٌ بعد صفةٍ لقوله «أمور» ؛ أي: محمولاتُ المسائل أمورٌ خارجة عن الموضوعاتِ، عارضةٌ لها لِذَوَاتِهَا. والعارضُ للشَّيء : ما يكون محمولاً عليه خارجاً عنه. وهو ما يلحق الشَّيء: لذاتِهِ : كَالتَّعجُبِ اللاحقِ للإنسان بواسطة أنَّه إنسان. أو لجزئه : كالحركة بالإرادةِ اللاحقةِ لِلإنسانِ بواسطة أَنَّه حيوانُ. أو لأمر خارج عنه مساو له : كالضَّحكِ العارض للإنسان بواسطة التَّعجُب. فإن قلت: العوارض الذاتيَّةُ ما لا يكون بينها وبين المعروضات واسطةٌ، فتكونُ المسائل غير محتاجةٍ إلى البرهان، وهذا خلافُ مَا ذكر من أنَّ المسائل هي القضايا المطلوبة التي يبرهن عليها في العلم. قلتُ: العوارض الذاتيَّةُ لا يكون بينها وبين المعروضات واسطةٌ بحسَبِ نفْسِ الأمر، وأمَّا العِلْمُ بثبوتها لها فربَّما يحتاج إلى البرهان.
Chapter: On the parts of the sciences. The predicates of the problems are matters external to them, i.e., to their subjects; for if they were parts of the subjects, their affirmation would not need a proof, because it is impossible for a part of a thing to be required by proof; but we need proof for the affirmation of predicates of problems to subjects, as we mentioned that problems are the sought propositions that are proven in the sciences. So predicates are external to subjects; otherwise they would not be proven in the sciences. 'Lahiqah' (accidental) in the nominative, an adjective after an adjective for the word 'umur' (matters): i.e., the predicates of problems are matters external to the subjects, accidental to them in themselves. The accidental to a thing is what is predicated on it externally. And it is what attaches to the thing: by itself: like the wonder attaching to man by virtue of being man. or by its part: like the movement by will attaching to man by virtue of being animal. or by a matter external to it and equal to it: like laughter attaching to man by virtue of wonder. If you say: Essential accidents have no intermediary between them and their objects; therefore the problems do not need proof, contrary to what was mentioned that problems are sought propositions proven in the science. I say: Essential accidents have no intermediary between them and their objects in reality; but as for knowledge of their affirmation to them, sometimes it needs proof.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of the predicates of scientific problems. The author begins by stating that the predicates of the problems are external to their subjects. He argues that if they were parts of the subjects, they would not require proof, since a part of a thing cannot be sought by proof. But since we do need proofs for these predicates, they must be external. He then clarifies that they are accidental (lahiqah) to their subjects. He defines the accidental as a predicate that is external to the thing. He then distinguishes three types of essential accidental (al-'arid al-dhati): (1) that which attaches to the thing by itself, e.g., wonder attaching to man by virtue of being man; (2) that which attaches by its part, e.g., movement by will attaching to man by virtue of being animal (animal is a part of man); (3) that which attaches through an external equivalent, e.g., laughter attaching to man by virtue of wonder (wonder is equal to man in relation to laughter). Then an objection is raised: Essential accidents have no intermediary, so they should be known without proof, which contradicts the need for proof. The answer distinguishes between the ontological fact (no intermediary in reality) and our knowledge of that fact, which may require proof. This resolves the tension.

Key Takeaway
Predicates of scientific problems are external to their subjects and are classified as essential accidents that attach by self, by part, or by equivalent; their ontological inseparability does not preclude the need for proof.
