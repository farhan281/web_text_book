The Three Meanings of 'Ilm (Knowledge)
Al-Dasuqi defines the three usages of 'ilm and identifies the intended meaning for the division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of 'ilm (knowledge) as used in logic and identify which meaning is intended in the division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq.

Core Idea
'Ilm in logic means perception (idrak), not rules or faculty.

Al-Dasuqi clarifies a critical terminological point before dividing knowledge into conception and assent. He explains that the word 'ilm is used in three different senses: the rules of logic, the habit of reasoning, and the act of perception. Only the third sense is relevant for the upcoming classification. This prevents a common confusion that could derail the entire study of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three usages
Extract the three meanings from the text: rules (qawa'id), faculty (malaka), and perception (idrak).
2
Determine the primary meaning
According to al-Dasuqi, the true (haqiqah) meaning is the third because 'ilm is a masdar (verbal noun). Applying it to the first two is metaphorical (majaz).
3
Apply the restriction
Understand why only the perceptual sense is intended for the division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq. The division concerns mental acts, not abstract rules.
This step is crucial for properly following the subsequent discussion.
4
Recognize the metaphorical shift
Note that applying 'ilm to rules or faculty is metaphorical. This awareness helps avoid confusion when encountering 'ilm in other logic contexts.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional clarification by al-Dasuqi regarding the term 'ilm (knowledge). He states that the word 'ilm is used in three senses: (1) the rules and principles themselves (qawa'id wa dawabit), (2) the acquired faculty (malaka) that results from practicing those rules, and (3) the act of perception (idrak). He then explains that the true (haqiqah) meaning is the third, because 'ilm is a masdar (verbal noun), and applying it to the first two is metaphorical (majaz). Finally, he asserts that in the context of the coming division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq, only the perceptual sense is intended. This guards against the mistake of thinking that logic deals with abstract rules or mental faculties rather than the actual acts of conception and assent.

Qiyas al-Khalf: Proving the Need to Know the Purpose
Al-Dasuqi explains qiyas al-khalf to prove knowledge of purpose is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct a qiyas al-khalf argument from a sharih passage and identify its conditional form (modus tollens).

Core Idea
Qiyas al-khalf establishes a truth by showing its opposite leads to absurdity.

In the opening of his commentary, al-Dasuqi explains that the first thing a student needs is to know the purpose of the science. He uses a logical argument called qiyas al-khalf. This is not just a rhetorical trick; it is a rigorous proof form that Islamic logicians use frequently. Let's walk through how it works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired proposition
What is the mahlub (desired truth) that the commentator wants to prove? It is: 'Every beginner must know the purpose of the science before starting.'
This is the claim we want to establish.
2
Identify its opposite (naqid)
The opposite is: 'The beginner does not know the purpose of the science.' This is what we assume for contradiction.
Also called the muqaddam in the conditional.
3
Derive the absurd consequence
If the beginner does not know the purpose, his pursuit of the science would be futile (عبث). The commentator lists three ways it could be futile: no benefit, harmful benefit, or benefit not worth the effort.
This is the tali (consequent).
4
Apply modus tollens
Futility (عبث) is impossible for a rational person. So the consequent is false. Therefore, the antecedent (not knowing the purpose) is also false. Hence, the beginner must know the purpose.
This is qiyas al-khalf: establishing the truth by refuting its opposite.

Al-Dasuqi: His saying 'if he does not know' means: if he does not truly believe. And this is an indication of qiyas al-khalf, which is proving the desired thing by refuting its opposite. So the desired thing is that every beginner knows the purpose of the science, and its opposite is not knowing it. But the commentator omitted the conditional premise, so the original is: 'it would be futile', meaning: and the consequent is false, so the antecedent is likewise false. His saying 'the purpose' means: the need. His saying 'futile' from the perspective that it is possible that that art has no benefit, or has a harmful benefit, or has a benefit that does not compensate for the effort in that science. And his saying 'his seeking it would be futile' means: the third is false, because futility is not befitting the rational person, so the antecedent is false, so it is established that the beginner does not begin the science unless he knows the purpose of the science. Thus, beginning in it depends on knowledge of the purpose. Al-'Attar: and its ends, because the student being insightful about something that has no achieved meaning necessitates restricting to what they intended. And according to this, the definition of the introduction is not valid by what beginning on the basis of insight depends on. And because distinguishing the science for the student does not depend on explaining the subject, but rather is attained by other means. Yes, the distinction of sciences in themselves is only by distinction of subjects, and the difference is apparent. His saying 'if he does not know the purpose of the science' — every benefit and wisdom that follows from an action is called a 'ghayah' (end), in that it is at the extremity of the action, and 'fa'idah' (benefit) in that it follows from it. So they differ in consideration and both include voluntary actions and others. As for 'gharad' (purpose), it is that for which the agent undertakes the action, and it is called the final cause. It does not exist in the actions of Allah Most High, even if their benefits are abundant. And the purpose may contradict the benefit of the action, as when one errs in his belief. Al-Sayyid said in his glosses on the Sharh al-'Adudi on al-Mukhtasar: 'So the commentator's saying "if he does not know the purpose..." means he believes, either with certainty or with presumption, the purpose of the science, i.e., the benefit that...' Al-Dasuqi: The summary is that beginning in a science is a voluntary action, and a voluntary action is not done by a free agent except after he is convinced that its benefit is such; the carpenter does not make a bed except after knowing that one sits on it. Therefore, the beginner in the science must believe before beginning that that science has a benefit, otherwise his beginning would be futile. And that benefit must be considered worthwhile by him in view of the difficulty incurred by the one who occupies himself with that science, whether it is actually worthwhile or not. Otherwise his beginning in it would be considered futile. His saying 'because if he did not conceive that' means: because the beginner, if he did not conceive that science by its definition — any definition — and his saying 'first' means: before beginning in it, and his saying 'he would not have insight' means: perception and knowledge in seeking it. Therefore, his beginning with insight depends on his conceiving it by its definition. As for the beginning itself, it does not depend on that but rather on his conceiving it in some way, such as being a science among sciences. Al-'Attar: And with additional specificity, such that its compilation is for the sake of that benefit, his seeking it would be futile. And this is an abbreviated statement, its detail is what al-Sayyid said: that beginning in a science is a voluntary action, so he must first know that that science has some benefit, otherwise beginning in it would be prevented as explained in its place. And that benefit must be considered worthwhile in view of the difficulty incurred in acquiring that science, otherwise his beginning in it and seeking it would be considered futile by custom, and his seriousness would certainly diminish. And the benefit must be the benefit that actually results from that science, for if it were not the same, perhaps...

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on the matn's statement about the need to know the purpose of a science. The commentators explain that a student must be convinced of the benefit of a science before studying it, otherwise it is futile. They employ qiyas al-khalf (proof by contradiction): if a student did not know the purpose, his pursuit would be futile, which is absurd for a rational person, so he must know the purpose. Al-Dasuqi clarifies that the 'purpose' (gharad) here means 'need' (hāja), and 'futility' can mean no benefit, harmful benefit, or insufficient benefit. Al-'Attar then distinguishes three related terms: ghāyah (end), which is the result; fā'idah (benefit), which is the outcome; and gharad (purpose), which is the agent's intention, noting that Allah's actions have no purpose but have benefits. The discussion continues with the need for the student to have a conception (tasawwur) of the science, and that beginning with insight depends on knowing its definition. Al-Dasuqi gives the example of a carpenter who only makes a bed after knowing its use. Finally, al-'Attar summarizes that the student must intend the actual benefit of the science, not something else. This entire passage emphasizes that the pursuit of knowledge must be goal-oriented and not random.

Distinguishing Gharad, Ghayah, and Fa'idah
Al-'Attar distinguishes gharad, ghayah, and fa'idah and relates them to the student's need.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between gharad, ghayah, and fa'idah and explain why Allah's actions have no gharad but have benefits.

Core Idea
Gharad is the agent's intention; ghayah is the end result; fa'idah is the benefit—crucial distinctions for understanding purpose in Islamic sciences.

Al-'Attar provides a precise taxonomy of terms related to purpose and benefit. This is not mere semantics; it has profound implications for how we talk about divine actions and human intentions. Without this distinction, one might wrongly ascribe a 'purpose' to Allah in the same sense as human purposes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
The passage discusses three terms: الغرض (gharad), الغاية (ghayah), and الفائدة (fa'idah). Notice how the author defines each.
These are all related to the 'why' of an action, but with different shades of meaning.
2
Define each from the passage
Ghayah: the end result that occurs after the action, at its extremity. Fa'idah: the benefit that follows from the action. Gharad: that for which the agent undertakes the action; the final cause.
Ghayah and fa'idah both refer to outcomes, but gharad is the motive.
3
Note the key difference: intention vs. outcome
Gharad is the agent's intention (niyyah), which may or may not be identical to the actual benefit. The passage gives an example of someone who errs in his belief: his gharad differs from the fa'idah.
This distinction is crucial for evaluating the correctness of intentions.
4
Apply to divine actions
The passage states that Allah's actions have no gharad (purpose/intention in the human sense), but they do have benefits. This prevents anthropomorphism in theology.
We cannot say Allah 'intends' something like a human does, but we can say His actions yield benefits.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on the matn's statement about the need to know the purpose of a science. The commentators explain that a student must be convinced of the benefit of a science before studying it, otherwise it is futile. They employ qiyas al-khalf (proof by contradiction): if a student did not know the purpose, his pursuit would be futile, which is absurd for a rational person, so he must know the purpose. Al-Dasuqi clarifies that the 'purpose' (gharad) here means 'need' (hāja), and 'futility' can mean no benefit, harmful benefit, or insufficient benefit. Al-'Attar then distinguishes three related terms: ghāyah (end), which is the result; fā'idah (benefit), which is the outcome; and gharad (purpose), which is the agent's intention, noting that Allah's actions have no purpose but have benefits. The discussion continues with the need for the student to have a conception (tasawwur) of the science, and that beginning with insight depends on knowing its definition. Al-Dasuqi gives the example of a carpenter who only makes a bed after knowing its use. Finally, al-'Attar summarizes that the student must intend the actual benefit of the science, not something else. This entire passage emphasizes that the pursuit of knowledge must be goal-oriented and not random.

Conditions for a Non-Futile Pursuit of Knowledge
Al-Dasuqi and al-'Attar list conditions for the student's benefit to avoid futility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions that a student's intended benefit must satisfy for the pursuit of knowledge to be non-futile, and apply them to a scenario.

Core Idea
A student must conceive the science, believe in its benefit, and ensure the benefit outweighs the effort—otherwise study is futile.

Al-Dasuqi and al-'Attar elaborate further that merely having a desire to learn is insufficient. The student must have a proper conception of the science, a genuine belief in its benefit, and that benefit must be significant enough given the difficulty. This is practical guidance for any aspiring scholar, rooted in logic and common sense.

Guided Path
1
Recognize that beginning a science is a voluntary action
Al-Dasuqi states: 'الشرع في العلم فعل اختياري'. This means the student chooses to start; it is not forced. Therefore, it must have a reasoned basis.
Voluntary actions require a motive (gharad).
2
Identify the first condition: belief in benefit
The student must be convinced (tasdiq) that the science has some benefit. Without this belief, the action is arbitrary.
This is derived from the carpenter example: he only works when he knows the bed will be used.
3
Identify the second condition: benefit is worthwhile
The benefit must be 'mu'taddan biha' (considered significant) given the effort. If the benefit is too small, the action is considered futile by custom.
This is a pragmatic condition: the effort must be proportional.
4
Identify the third condition: benefit must be actual
The benefit the student intends must be the actual benefit of that science, not something else. If he studies for a different reason (e.g., prestige only), his pursuit may be futile.
Al-'Attar emphasizes 'ولا بد أن تكون الفائدة هي الفائدة التي تترتب على ذلك العلم'.

The Two Types of Premises in a Science
Defines the two types of premises (evident and accepted) and states that syllogisms are built upon them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between evident and accepted premises and explain their role in constructing scientific syllogisms.

Core Idea
Every science rests upon premises: some self-evident, others accepted on trust.

In this lesson, we learn that the building blocks of any science are its premises. These premises are of two types: those so clear they need no proof, and those accepted from a reliable authority. Understanding this distinction is crucial because all scientific knowledge is derived through syllogisms that start from these premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Categories
Look at the passage: it says the parts of sciences are 'either evident premises or accepted premises.' Recognize that these are the two fundamental kinds of starting points.
The Arabic uses 'إِمَّا ... أَوْ' for the division.
2
Understand 'Evident Premises'
Evident premises (muqaddimat bayyina) are described as 'clear and extremely obvious in themselves.' They need no proof. Think of examples like 'the whole is greater than the part' in geometry or 'a command implies obligation' in usul al-fiqh.
These are often called 'badihiyyat' or 'awwaliyyat' in other texts.
3
Understand 'Accepted Premises'
Accepted premises (muqaddimat ma'khudha) are taken from someone believed in. They are not self-evident, but the learner accepts them due to good opinion (husn al-zann). Example: the premise that the Qur'an is the word of Allah.
These are also called 'musallamat' in later discussions.
4
Connect Premises to Syllogisms
The passage states that 'upon them are built the syllogisms of the science.' This means that all deductive arguments in a science ultimately rest on these two types of premises. The syllogisms derive new propositions (masail) from them.
In Islamic logic, a science is structured as: premises (evident/accepted) → syllogisms → conclusions.

Definition of Masail (Problems) in Sciences
Defines masail as propositions sought in a science and states their two components: subject and predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the masail of a given Islamic science and distinguish them from its premises (muqaddimat).

Core Idea
Masail are the conclusions of a science's syllogisms, not its starting points.

In this passage, the author defines the third component of a science after the premises: the masail (problems). These are the propositions that a science seeks to demonstrate, such as grammatical rules in nahw or logical rules in mantiq. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how each science is structured around its goals.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of masail in the Arabic.
Find the sentence that begins with 'المسائل، وهي قضایا تطلب في العلم'. This is the core definition.
The term 'قضایا' means propositions (plural of qadiyya).
2
Identify what masail are not.
Contrast with premises (muqaddimat). Masail are what we want to prove; premises are the starting points that are already accepted. Mixing them up would lead to circular reasoning.
3
Extract the two components of a mas'ala.
The passage says every mas'ala has a subject (mawdu') and a predicate (mahmul). For example, in logic: 'Every universal affirmative proposition converts to a particular affirmative' has subject 'universal affirmative proposition' and predicate 'converts to a particular affirmative'.
This is the standard structure of a proposition in logic.
4
Apply to a different science.
Take an example from fiqh: 'Wine is prohibited.' Here subject is 'wine', predicate is 'prohibited'. This is a mas'ala in fiqh because it is sought to be established by evidence.
The same analysis works for any Islamic science.

The Two Meanings of Principles and Premises in a Science
Author distinguishes usages of 'mabadi'' and 'muqaddimat' as what precedes the main subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two usages of 'mabadi'' and 'muqaddimat' in Islamic sciences and classify a given introductory text as either a book premise or a science premise.

Core Idea
Mabadi' and muqaddimat have two layers: what precedes a book and what enables genuine study.

In Islamic sciences, terms like 'principles' (mabadi') and 'premises' (muqaddimat) can be confusing because they refer both to the introductory material of a book and to the foundational knowledge required to study the science. This passage from al-Dasuqi's commentary clarifies these distinctions, helping you navigate any Islamic textbook with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms being defined
The passage begins by stating that 'mabadi'' (principles) are said of what is begun before the intended subject. Then it says 'muqaddimat' (premises) are said of that upon which the commencement of a science depends with expertise. Note the overlap: both refer to something that comes before the main content.
The starting point is the chapter heading: 'On the parts of the sciences'.
2
Notice the general-special relationship
Al-Dasuqi notes that 'mabadi'' is more general than 'muqaddimat' in this usage. That means all muqaddimat are mabadi', but not all mabadi' are muqaddimat. For example, an opening prayer (khatba) is a principle but not a premise in the sense of a prerequisite.
Internal principles (technical definitions, postulates) are still mabadi' but may not be muqaddimat.
3
Distinguish internal vs. external
The text explains that 'what is begun before the intended' can be internal (like definitions of terms used in the science) or external (like the need for studying the science). External ones are further tied to having insight (basira) and intense desire (fart al-raghba).
This links to the condition of studying with expertise (wajh al-khibra).
4
Separate book premises from science premises
Al-Dasuqi explicitly distinguishes two types: 'muqaddimat al-kitab' (premises of the book) – the introductory sections that precede the main text; and 'muqaddimat al-'ilm' (premises of the science) – the prerequisite knowledge needed to begin studying with insight.
The first is about the layout of the book; the second is about the reader's preparation.
5
Learn from the mathematicians' example
Al-'Attar quotes a commentator who praises mathematicians for clearly stating their conceptual and assertive principles at the start of their works, avoiding error. He criticizes philosophers and natural scientists for neglecting this, leading to confusion.
This practical lesson underscores why understanding these distinctions matters for precise scholarship.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of the terms 'principles' (mabadi') and 'premises' (muqaddimat) as used in the sciences. The chapter heading declares it is about the parts of the sciences. The main text states that 'mabadi'' is said of what is begun before the intended subject, and 'muqaddimat' is said of that upon which the commencement of a science depends with expertise (wajh al-khibra), meaning insight (basira) and intense desire (fart al-raghba). Examples include defining the science and stating its need, benefit, purpose, and subject. Al-Dasuqi comments that in this usage, 'mabadi'' is more general than 'muqaddimat'. He explains that 'what is begun before the intended' can be internal (technical principles) or external (prerequisites for insight, or even an introduction). He distinguishes two types of premises: those that are premises of a book (muqaddimat kitab) and those that are premises of a science (muqaddimat 'ilm). The former precede the main text; the latter enable proper study. Al-'Attar then quotes the commentator of Sullam al-'Ulum, who praises mathematicians for stating conceptual and assertive principles at the start to avoid error, and criticizes natural scientists and philosophers for neglecting this. The marginal note corrects the wording: the second usage should be described as depending on expertise, not just evident propositions.
