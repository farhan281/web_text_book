Definition and Division of Burhan
Author defines burhan etymologically and divides it into burhan limmi and burhan inni.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define burhan and distinguish between burhan limmi and burhan inni based on their epistemological status.

Core Idea
Burhan is a proof that cuts through all doubts—its two types explain either the cause or just the fact.

This lesson introduces demonstration (burhan) as the highest form of proof in Islamic logic. We will explore its etymological meaning and the critical division into two types, which forms the foundation for evaluating arguments in the Islamic sciences. Mastering this distinction is essential before studying the Five Arts.

Guided Path
1
Unpack the etymology
Identify the root 'baraha' meaning to cut. The author says 'barha min al-zaman' (a piece of time) to illustrate the sense of cutting. A burhan cuts through doubts.
This linguistic derivation is not merely decorative; it encodes the essential function of demonstration.
2
Extract the definition
From the etymology, the author defines burhan as a decisive proof that cuts through all doubts. Recognize that this implies certainty and finality.
3
Note the requirement of certainty
The author stresses that both premises must be certain (qat'iyya) for the conclusion to be certain. Without certain premises, the syllogism is not a burhan.
This is crucial: a burhan is defined by the quality of its premises, not just the form.
4
Identify the two divisions
Burhan is divided into burhan limmi (demonstration of why) and burhan inni (demonstration of that). Burhan limmi gives the cause (why the predicate belongs to the subject), while burhan inni gives only the fact that it belongs.
This distinction corresponds to the difference between explaining the reason and merely establishing the truth.
5
Relate to the Five Arts
Burhan is one of the Five Arts (sana'i' khams). Understanding its definition and division prepares you to identify burhan in arguments from kalam, fiqh, and other sciences.
In later lessons, you will see burhan applied to real theological and legal proofs.

The Necessity of Intermediate Genera in Predication
Author explains that higher genus cannot be predicated of species without intermediate lower genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a higher genus cannot be predicated of a species without its proximate genus, using the example of 'body' and 'man'.

Core Idea
A higher genus is predicated of a species only through the intermediate proximate genus.

In this passage, the author delves into the mechanics of predication when applying genera and differentiae to a species. He demonstrates that the highest genus does not directly attach to the species; rather, the proximate genus must come first. Understanding this ordering is crucial for constructing precise definitions in Islamic sciences, where the correct hierarchy of genera ensures that definitions are neither too broad nor too narrow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about predication order
Locate where the author states that the highest genus is not predicated first. The sentence 'فإنه لا يحمل جسم على الإنسان إلا الجسم الذي هو الحيوان' is key. Note the condition: body is only predicated if it is the body that is animal.
The author contrasts actual predication with abstract consideration of natures.
2
Trace the chain of genera
Follow the author's example: body, animal, man. He says 'فالحيوان أولاً ثم الجسم ثم الإنسان'. Observe the order: animal is predicated first, then body. This shows that animal is the proximate genus, and body is the distant genus.
The differentia (e.g., 'sensitive') also follows this order: it belongs first to animal, then to man.
3
Understand the condition for predication
The author emphasizes that 'the body that is not animal is negated of man'. This means that for a genus to be predicated of a species, it must be qualified by the intermediate genus. Without the intermediate, the predication fails.
Compare with the case of accidents, where existence in itself and existence in subject are identical; here, body's existence for man is not its own existence.
4
Apply to a new example from Islamic sciences
Choose a species from fiqh, such as 'salah' (prayer). Its proximate genus is 'ibadah' (worship), and a distant genus might be 'action' (fi'l). According to the rule, 'action' is predicated of 'salah' only through 'ibadah'. Test this: can we say 'salah is an action' without it being worship? No, because not all actions are worship.
This illustrates the practical benefit of the rule for definition in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed logical argument about the order of predication when applying genera and differentiae to a species. The author begins by contrasting the body that is a genus with the body that is matter. He argues that the genus (e.g., corporeality as a genus) does not have an independent existence prior to the species; rather, its existence in the species is identical to the existence of the species itself. Then, he sets up a thought experiment: consider a species and try to predicate its genus, the differentia of its genus, and the genus of its genus. He concludes that when we consider these things in actual predication (not just in their abstract natures), we find that the higher genus is not predicated first; instead, the lower (immediate) genus is predicated first. For example, 'body' cannot be predicated of 'man' unless it is the body that is 'animal'. The condition for a body to be predicated of man is that it be animal, because the body that is not animal is negated from man. The author then explains the nature of the predicated body: it is a substance with dimensions, and when predicated, it makes the substance necessary in terms of composition. He traces the chain: body as composed substance becomes animal, then animal becomes man. The order of predication is animal first, then body, then man. He addresses a potential objection: what if the effect (man) reaches the cause (body) before the cause in essence? He dismisses this by noting that for body and man, the existence of body is not the same as its existence for man, unlike the case of an accident whose existence in itself is the same as its existence in a subject. He emphasizes that we cannot connect body to man without animal, because what lacks animality is not man. The same logic applies to the differentia (e.g., 'sensitive') of the genus: it must be predicated first of the animal, then of man. The passage concludes by distinguishing between considering genera and differentiae in themselves (abstractly) versus in actual relation to a species. In actual predication, the higher genera are only present after the lower ones. He gives an analogy with simple genera like color and whiteness: we do not first take 'color' and then 'whiteness'; rather, whiteness exists first, and then color follows. This rigorous exposition is crucial for understanding how predication works in categorical logic, which is foundational for reasoning in Islamic sciences such as usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Distinguishing Placed Principles from Postulates
Author defines two types of non-evident premises and distinguishes them by learner's acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a placed principle (asl mawdu') and a postulate (musadara) based on the learner's acceptance.

Core Idea
A placed principle is accepted on trust; a postulate is demanded without conviction.

In logic, premises are not always self-evident. Some are accepted from other sciences, and some are demanded even if the learner is unconvinced. This passage clarifies the distinction between two types of non-evident premises: placed principles and postulates, and shows how the same premise can shift category depending on the science and the learner's attitude.

The Four Primary Parts of Dialectical Syllogisms
Listing the four primary parts of dialectical syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four primary parts (genus, definition, property, species) and explain their role as the material components of dialectical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Dialectical syllogisms rest on four predicables: genus, definition, property, and species.

Just as a building has bricks and beams, dialectical syllogisms have primary parts. The author lists four: genus (al-jins), definition (al-hadd), property (al-khassa), and species (al-naw'). These are the 'matter' (madda) from which dialectical premises are composed. Understanding these parts is essential to constructing and analyzing dialectical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four primary parts
From the passage, list the four parts: genus, definition, property, species. Note that these are the 'simplest parts' of dialectical syllogisms.
These are also known as the four predicables (al-alfaz al-arba'a) in logic.
2
Distinguish material from formal analysis
The author explicitly states that this analysis is 'from the aspect of matter, not form.' Understand that matter here refers to the content (terms), while form refers to the syllogistic structure.
3
Relate the parts to premises
The passage says the simplest parts are the things from which premises are composed. These parts are the 'limits' (hudud) of the premises, meaning the terms that define the subject and predicate.
4
Apply to a dialectical example
Consider a dialectical syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden; nabidh is an intoxicant; so nabidh is forbidden.' Identify the genus (intoxicant), definition (what intoxicant means), property (its effect), and species (types of intoxicants) if present.

Why Species is Not a Dialectical Predicate
Restriction that species is not a predicate in non-personal dialectical premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why species (naw') cannot serve as a predicate in non-personal dialectical premises, and identify the correct predicables for such premises.

Core Idea
Species is too specific for dialectical debate; it only predicates of individuals, not of universals in dialectical premises.

At the end of the passage, the author drops a key restriction: a non-personal dialectical premise cannot have a species as its predicate. Why? Because species is predicated only of individuals and sorts (ashkhas wa asnaf), and its relationship to a sort is not that of a genus but of a concomitant (lazim). Therefore, species does not appear among dialectical predicates. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its implications for constructing valid dialectical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Find the restriction in the passage
Locate the sentence: 'a dialectical premise, if it is not personal, the predicate is not a species.' This is the core claim.
Personal premises are about individuals (e.g., 'Zayd is a human'). Those can have species as predicate.
2
Understand why species is excluded
The author explains: species is predicated of individuals and sorts, but it is not a species for a sort. Its relation to a sort is like a concomitant (lazim), not like a genus. A concomitant is non-essential, whereas dialectical premises typically require essential or pervasive attributes.
Example: 'Human' is a species of the individual Zayd, but if we say 'Animal is a human', that is false. So species cannot predicate of a broader universal.
3
Distinguish between personal and non-personal premises
A personal premise has an individual subject (e.g., 'Zayd is a man'). There, species can be predicate. But in non-personal premises, the subject is a universal (e.g., 'Every animal'), and then species cannot be the predicate because the species is not a universal applicable to that universal.
Dialectical premises are often universal, so species is usually excluded.
4
Apply the rule to a dialectical argument
Consider the premise: 'Every human is rational.' Here 'rational' is a property (khassa), not species. If someone used 'Every human is a man', that would be redundant (species predicated of itself). The correct predicate for 'human' would be genus or property.
This shows why genus, definition, and property are used instead of species.

Ease of Refutation and Conditions for Definition in Dialectic and Demonstration
Author explains the ease of refutation versus proof and the conditions for defining in jadal and burhan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the conditions for proving a definition in dialectic from those in demonstration, and explain why refutation is easier than proof.

Core Idea
Refutation needs only one flaw; a demonstrative definition demands coextension.

This passage from the Mantiq Kabir introduces a key asymmetry between proof and refutation, and then applies it to definitions. Understanding this asymmetry equips you to evaluate arguments in Islamic sciences: a proof may require multiple conditions, but a refutation only needs to break one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general claim about proof and refutation
The author states that the harder something is to prove, the easier it is to refute. Why? Because proof requires multiple conditions, refutation only needs to falsify one.

2
Extract the three conditions for a dialectical definition
In jadal, to prove a definition one must show: (a) it is a predicate of the definiendum, (b) it is an essential constituent, (c) it is synonymous with the name (same meaning).
These are explicitly listed: 'أن يثبت أنه محمول وأن يثبت أنه مقوم ذاتي ويثبت أنه هو الاسم في المعنى'

Because a demonstrative definition requires more conditions, it is easier to refute (just show one condition missing) while harder to prove. In dialectic, since definitions rely on common repute, the conditions are fewer, making proof easier but refutation less definitive.

In demonstration, however, one cannot prove the existence of the definition in this way; rather, the definition is assumed, and the focus is on showing that it meets the additional condition of being coextensive in meaning and generality (musawin fi al-ma'na) to be a complete definition (hadd tamm). The author notes that in dialectic, definitions often rely on common usage (shuhra) rather than strict reality, so the strict conditions of demonstration are not needed. This distinction is a key methodological point in Islamic logic, differentiating the dialectical search for a plausible definition from the demonstrative proof of a true definition.

Core Idea
A proper definition needs a specific genus, not a universal predicate.

In dialectical debates (jadal), definitions are sometimes accepted based on fame rather than rigorous analysis. However, in true investigation (tahqiq), a definition must capture the essence of a thing through its genus and differentia. This passage warns against substituting the genus with a general predicate like 'existing' or 'thing', which fails to specify the unique quiddity of the defined term. Understanding this error is crucial for precise definitions in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam.

The definition fails to indicate the specific quiddity (mahiyya) of the thing being defined, because the general predicate does not delimit the essence.

The author begins by discussing the similarity (mushakala) between rhetoric and dialectic (jadal), noting that while both use commendable premises, dialectic's premises are certain (haqiqiyya) whereas rhetoric's are opinionative (zanniyya). Classification of Signifying Expressions
Author classifies signifying expressions into literal, transferred, dialectal, and ornamental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given signifying expression into one of the four categories (literal, transferred, dialectal, ornamental) and identify subcategories for transferred expressions.

Core Idea
Every word's meaning is determined by its type: literal, transferred, dialectal, or ornamental.

Signifying expressions are the raw material of logical reasoning. But not all words signify in the same way. In this passage, the author lays out four fundamental types that every logician must distinguish. Mastering this classification will help you avoid confusion when analyzing definitions and arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four main categories
Read the opening sentence: 'والاسم والكلمة وتصريفهما وكل لفظ دال فإنه حقيقي منقول إما لغةً وإما زينة.' Notice that every signifying expression falls into one of four types: literal (حقيقي), transferred (منقول), dialectal (لغة), or ornamental (زينة).
The context of 'اسم وكلمة' is from grammar, but the focus is on the classification of 'لفظ دال'.
2
Distinguish literal from dialectal
The literal (حقيقي) is the word used by common convention corresponding to meaning. The dialectal (لغة) is a word used by another tribe or nation, not the speaker's own. Consider: if a word is from a foreign language, it is dialectal; if it is standard in the speaker's language, it is literal.
'لغة' here refers to a specific dialect or foreign language, not the general concept of language.
3
Analyze transferred expressions
Transferred (منقول) expressions originally coined for one meaning and later moved to another. The author lists four subcategories: genus to species, species to genus, species to species, or by resemblance. Note the example 'خريف الحياة' (autumn of life) for old age – this is transfer by resemblance.
This type is common in fiqh when words like 'صلاة' are transferred from linguistic meaning to technical Islamic meaning.
4
Understand ornamental expressions
Ornamental (زينة) expressions depend on configuration or context, not just the letters. They are metaphorical and used in rhetoric and poetry. The author notes that such words are not purely 'Arab' (i.e., not standard literal Arabic).
Ornamental is less relevant for strict logical analysis; literal is preferred for clarity.
5
Connect to the principle of clarity
The passage concludes: 'وأوضح القول وأفضله ما يكون بالتصريح، والتصريح هو ما يكون بالألفاظ الحقيقية المستولية.' The clearest speech is explicit, using literal words. This shows that in logic and argumentation, literal expressions are prioritized.
This principle is used in usul al-fiqh when interpreting revealed texts.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of connected speech (muqassam, muqabil, muda'af, musari') based on their structural features.

Core Idea
Connected speech has four distinct patterns: divided, opposed, varied, and clashing.

In classical Arabic rhetoric, connected speech (al-kalam al-mawsul) is categorized into four types based on how its parts relate. Understanding these categories helps in analyzing the structure of eloquent texts, including the Qur'an and poetry.

Guided Path
1

2

Opposites enhance each other.
3
Examine variation in length
See if the parts differ in length but maintain a coherent system. This is muda'af (varied).
Systematic variation.
4
Check for rhyme or repeated elements
If the speech has similar endings or beginnings, or a repeated word, it is musari' (clashing) or rhymed speech.
