Error by Whole and Part in Definition (الكل والجزء)
Error concerning the whole and part, with justice example and three types of wholes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition of a whole merely lists its parts without indicating their mode of combination, and distinguish the three types of wholes.

Core Idea
A whole is not just the sum of its parts; the mode of combination must be part of the definition.

The author then addresses errors concerning the whole (kull). Defining a composite object by listing its parts with 'and' fails to capture how they combine to form a unity. For example, defining justice as 'modesty, courage, and wisdom' treats each as separate predicates. We will learn how definitions must reflect the type of combination: mere collection, addition with form, or mixture producing a new quality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the whole and its parts in the definition.
Find the composite being defined and the list of its components. E.g., justice: modesty, courage, wisdom.
2
Check if the definition only lists parts with 'and'.
If so, it likely commits the error. Each part is predicated separately, not as a single composite attribute.
3
Determine the type of whole.
Based on the three modes: mere collection (e.g., number), addition with form (e.g., house), or mixture with new quality (e.g., medicine).
4
Formulate a correct definition that includes the mode of combination.
For a mixture, include that the parts are combined to produce a new quality; for a house, include the arrangement.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of errors in definition, based on Aristotle's Topics. The author identifies several types: increase, decrease, whole/part, and conjunction with 'and'. Each is illustrated with examples from logic and natural philosophy. The discussion begins with errors by increase, where two correlative conditions are introduced, making the definition too narrow. For instance, defining beauty as both pleasing to hearing and sight excludes things pleasing only to one sense. The error by decrease occurs when the differentia is omitted, e.g., defining wind as 'movement of air' without specifying direction or magnitude. This shows that the essence of a thing is completed by its full set of attributes; genus alone is insufficient. The example of defining will as 'impulse towards good' is criticized for being too general: it should specify 'perceived good' because people may desire what is not truly good. This illustrates the need for proper generality and specificity. Then, errors concerning the whole are discussed: defining a composite by listing its parts with 'and' without indicating their combination, e.g., defining justice as 'modesty, courage, and wisdom' implies each is predicated separately, not that justice is a unity of these. The correct definition would be like 'rational animal' where 'rational' restricts 'animal'. Finally, the author explains how wholes arise from parts: (1) mere collection (like the number four), (2) addition to the collection (like a house, where arrangement matters), and (3) addition and form of collection (like a mixture, where a new quality emerges). For each, the definition must correspond to the mode of composition. The text emphasizes that definitions must capture the essence precisely, avoiding excess or deficiency. This has direct application in Islamic sciences: for instance, in fiqh, defining 'salah' must include its essential pillars and conditions without adding superfluous elements or omitting necessary ones.

جمع (jam'): Assembly or collection; the base mode of combining parts.

Three Types of Wholes and Their Definitions
Author enumerates three modes of composition of wholes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of wholes and specify what each requires in its definition.

Core Idea
A whole is not just a list of parts; the manner of combination defines it.

The author describes three ways a whole arises from parts: mere collection, collection with an added quality, and collection with a form. Understanding these is essential for constructing correct definitions in Islamic sciences, where defining composite concepts like ibada (worship) or aqd (contract) requires specifying both components and their arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Locate the three ways the author says a whole arises: 1) by mere collection (e.g., four from its parts), 2) with an addition beyond collection (e.g., a house), 3) with addition and a form of the collection (e.g., a mixture).
The Arabic terms are 'جمع فقط', 'زيادة على نفس الجمع', and 'زيادة على نفس الجمع وهيئة الجمع'.
2
Note what is added beyond mere collection
For type 2, the addition is a quality (كيفية) like the arrangement of a house. For type 3, it is a form (هيئة) and an additional quality that emerges after composition.
Type 3 is like a mixture (ممتزج) where parts lose their identities.
3
Determine what must be included in the definition
For type 1, defining it as 'collection of such-and-such' suffices. For types 2 and 3, the definition must indicate the quality or form of the collection.
The author explicitly says: 'أما ما يكون بالزيادة فلا يكفي هذا ما لم يدل على هيئة ذلك الجمع'.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion on the correct method of defining composite things. The author begins by contrasting two types of definitions: one that makes the thing more general and one that makes it more specific, using the example of 'good' in appearance versus reality. He then critiques definitions that list parts with the conjunction 'and', such as defining justice as temperance, courage, and practical wisdom, because this treats each part as an independent predicate without the required relation. The proper definition of a whole must account for how the parts combine. The author then enumerates three ways a whole arises from parts: 1) mere collection (like four), 2) collection with an added quality (like a house), and 3) collection with a form and quality (like a mixture). For the first kind, defining it as a sum suffices; for the others, one must indicate the form and arrangement. He further warns against using 'with' without specifying the relation, as in 'boldness with sound judgment' — this could imply different relations (e.g., cause, goal) that are not parts of the definition. Finally, he criticizes definitions that take the collective for the collected, like 'animal is a composition of soul and body', which fails to indicate the quality of the composition. Throughout, the author emphasizes that a definition must capture the essential structure, not just list components.

Why Listing Parts with 'And' Fails as a Definition
Author critiques definitions that list parts with 'wa'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error in defining a whole by listing its parts with 'and' and correct such definitions.

Core Idea
Listing parts with 'and' treats each part as an independent predicate, destroying the unity of the definition.

The author critiques the common practice of defining a whole by listing its components with the conjunction 'wa' (and), using the example of 'justice is temperance, courage, and practical wisdom.' We will see why this is logically flawed and how a proper definition must relate the parts.

Guided Path
1
Locate the faulty definition example
Find the sentence: 'كما يقال إن العدالة عفّة وشجاعة وحكمة علمية' (as when it is said that justice is temperance, courage, and practical wisdom).
This is a classical definition from Greek philosophy, but the author rejects it.
2
Understand why it fails
Each term after 'wa' becomes a separate predicate of the subject (justice). So it means 'justice is temperance' and 'justice is courage', etc. This does not show how they combine into one whole.
Compare with a proper definition like 'rational animal' where 'animal' is modified by 'rational'.
3
Identify the required relation
A correct definition should have one part restrict the other, as in 'حيوان ناطق' (animal that is rational). The parts should not be independent predicates.
The author says: 'فلا يكون كأنه قال عفة التي هي شجاعة' (so it is not as if he said 'a temperance that is courageous').

Author discusses the problematic use of 'with' in definitions and the need to distinguish parts from causes or goals.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The word 'with' can hide different relations; a proper definition must include only the essential parts, not causes or goals.

The author examines definitions that use the word 'with' to connect two terms, such as 'courage is boldness with sound judgment' and 'anger is grief with imagination of deserving.' He shows that 'with' can imply various relations – part, cause, goal – and a sound definition must clearly identify the relation.

The author's point is that the word 'with' does not guarantee that the following term is a part; one must check the actual relation.

Conditional Implication as a Test for Identity
Author presents conditional implication as a method to test identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use conditional implication to test whether two things are identical by checking if they have the same implications for positing and removal.

Core Idea
If two things are identical, they must have identical implications: positing one implies positing the other, and removing one implies removing the other.

Another powerful tool for testing identity is through conditional implications. If A and B are identical, then whatever follows from positing A also follows from positing B, and similarly for removal. The author illustrates with the example of air and void: they are not identical because removing air does not remove void. This lesson shows how to apply this test.

Guided Path
1
Understand the conditional test
The author says: 'if air and void are one, then what follows from removing air follows from removing void, and what follows from positing air follows from positing void.' So identity implies that all implications are shared.
2
Apply the test to the example
The author argues that air and void are not identical because if we remove air, void remains (according to proponents of void). So the implications differ.
3
Generalize the principle
The truth of a conditional is not in the truth of antecedent or consequent but in the implication (اللزوم). So when testing identity, we look at the relationship of implication, not the actual truth values.

Dialectic vs Rhetoric: Universal vs Particular Judgments
Author contrasts dialectic and rhetoric in terms of universal vs particular judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish dialectic from rhetoric based on whether their judgments are primarily universal or particular.

Core Idea
Dialectic seeks universal truths; rhetoric persuades in particular situations.

This lesson focuses on the key contrast between dialectic and rhetoric: the types of judgments they predominantly employ. While both are universal in scope, dialectic deals with universal propositions ('every X is Y'), and rhetoric deals with particular cases ('this specific thing at this specific time'). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying each art in Islamic argumentation.

Identify the basis of judgment

Judgments can be universal (kulliyya) like 'everything that is such-and-such is such-and-such' or particular (juzʾiyya) like judging a specific thing at a specific time.
This is the central contrast.
4
Identify which art uses which type
The passage explicitly says most judgments of dialectic are universal, while most of rhetoric are particular. Anchor this distinction.

Preferability by Entailment and Dependency
Author explains entailment and dependency as criteria for preferability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criteria of entailment and dependency to determine which good is preferable in Islamic ethical comparisons.

Core Idea
If one good contains another or is independent, it is better.

The author introduces two related criteria: entailment (istitbā') and dependency (iftiqār). If one good inevitably brings about another, but not vice versa, the entailing good is better. Also, if one good depends on another, the independent good is better. Classical Islamic examples like sultan and honor, or wealth and trade, illustrate these principles.

Find pairs of goods where one necessarily brings about the other. For example, having sultan (authority) entails honor, but honor does not necessarily bring authority.

This criterion assumes that the more inclusive good is superior.
3
Apply the dependency criterion
For dependency: if one good depends on another for its existence, the independent good is better. Example: wealth is independent of trade, but trade depends on wealth.
This is a different principle from entailment: here the focus is on existential priority.

Learning Objective

The Art of Takhyil and Weakness of Extraneous Actions
Author critiques use of invented fables and extraneous actions in poetry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between effective poetic takhyil that relies on imitation of actions and weak poetry that resorts to invented fables or extraneous actions.

Core Idea
True poetic takhyil comes from good imitation, not from simple inventions or stage actions.

The author emphasizes that a poet's excellence lies in good imitation through imaginative forms (mukhayyilat), especially actions. He criticizes the use of extraneous actions (af'al dakhilah) as a crutch for weak poetry. This lesson examines the criteria for effective takhyil and the signs of poetic weakness.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scope of poetic imagination
The author states that a poet can imagine what has been, what will be, and what is estimated to be, even if not real. This is the first condition for takhyil.
Poetry is not limited to literal truth.
2
Recognize the primary source of takhyil
The poet excels through imitation (muhakah) using imaginative forms (mukhayyilat), particularly actions. Good imitation is the key.
Imitation is the mechanism of takhyil.
3
Identify extraneous actions (أفعال دخيلة)
These are actions introduced by poets or reciters to make speech imaginative. They indicate that the poetry itself lacks imaginative power and needs external performance.
Such actions are a sign of weakness.
4
Assess the strength of a poet based on these criteria
The author says only the weakest poets are forced to use extraneous actions. A strong poet achieves takhyil through the words themselves.

The Three Aspects of Tonal Variation in Arabic Speech
Author defines 'waznat' and identifies three aspects: pitch, weight, and pause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three aspects of tonal variation (waznat) and explain how each affects meaning in Islamic rhetorical contexts.

Core Idea
Tonal inflections in Arabic speech carry meaning beyond words — through pitch, heaviness, and pause.

In this passage, the author introduces a non-phonemic feature of speech called 'waznat' — tonal inflections that shape meaning beyond literal words. He then breaks them down into three fundamental aspects that every speaker uses to convey emotion, emphasis, and even grammatical mood. Understanding this distinction helps you appreciate the subtle communicative tools in Quranic recitation and eloquent Arabic speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify what 'waznat' is
Locate the definition: 'waznat' are non-phonemic forms in tones used at various speech positions. They are not part of the phonemic system (like letters or vowels) but carry extra-linguistic meaning.
The term 'non-phonemic' means they do not change the lexical meaning of words but add pragmatic or emotional nuance.
2
List the three aspects
Find the three aspects mentioned at the end of the passage: pitch (hidda), heaviness (thiqal), and pauses (nathra). These three cover all tonal variation in imitation of sounds.
The author states explicitly that variation is 'only from three aspects'.
3
Examine the functions of each aspect
Note how each aspect affects meaning: pitch and heaviness convey emotions like confusion, anger, threat, or supplication; pause can turn a statement into a question or a question into exclamation. Pauses also indicate grammatical relations like condition, part, predicate, subject.
The passage gives examples: pause (nathra) changes sentence mood; pitch and heaviness indicate speaker's state.
4
Apply to Islamic texts
Consider how these aspects are used in Quranic recitation (tajweed) and oratory. For example, pauses (waqf) in the Qur'an can change meaning; pitch variation in prayer or sermon conveys earnestness.
This links to the science of tajweed and balaghah (rhetoric).
