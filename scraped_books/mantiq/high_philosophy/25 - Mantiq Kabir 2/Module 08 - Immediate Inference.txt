False Premises and Conditional Implications
Author discusses paradoxes of implication with numerical examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a true premise cannot imply a false conclusion, while a false premise may imply either a true or false conclusion, using examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A true premise must yield a true conclusion; a false premise can yield anything.

This lesson examines a foundational principle of conditional arguments: the relationship between the truth of premises and the truth of conclusions. The author illustrates this with the examples 'every number is even' and 'five is even' to show that from a false premise, the conclusion can be true or false, but from a true premise, the conclusion must be true. Understanding this is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional form
The author discusses two conditionals: 'If every number is even, then five is even' and 'If every number is even, then four is even'. Note the truth values of the antecedent and consequent.
2
Determine truth values
In the first conditional, the antecedent ('every number is even') is false, and the consequent ('five is even') is also false. In the second, the antecedent is false but the consequent ('four is even') is true.
3
Apply the rule
For the first case, a false antecedent leads to a false consequent; that is allowed. For the second, a false antecedent leads to a true consequent; that is also allowed. However, if the antecedent were true, the consequent must be true; a true antecedent cannot imply a false consequent.
4
Reflect on the implication for argument evaluation
When you encounter a conditional argument in kalam or usul, always check the truth of the premise. If the premise is true, the conclusion must be true; if the premise is false, the conclusion could be either, so the argument may be unsound.

Universal Knowledge and Particular Instances
Author distinguishes between universal knowledge and particular instances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why knowledge of a universal proposition does not entail knowledge of every particular instance, using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Knowing a universal does not require knowing all its particulars.

This lesson addresses a common confusion: if you know a general statement like 'every four is even', must you also know whether any given four is even? The author uses the 'sleeve test' to show that universal knowledge is conceptual and does not require exhaustive particular knowledge. This has important implications for how we understand general rulings in Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
A person claims to know that 'every four is even'. Then he is asked about a specific four (in his sleeve). He does not know if it is even. This seems to contradict his claim.
2
Identify the fallacious response
Some respond by limiting the universal to 'every four that I know', but the author rejects this as incorrect.
3
Grasp the author's answer
The author explains that the person knows the universal proposition 'every four is even' in itself, but this knowledge does not imply knowledge of every individual four. Universal knowledge is of the judgment, not of each instance.
4
Apply to other examples
The author gives analogies: knowing 'man' conceptually does not require knowing every man; knowing 'the world is originated' does not require knowing every world.
5
Connect to Islamic sciences
Consider the universal ruling 'every intoxicant is haram'. A jurist knows this as a general principle, but he may not know whether a specific substance is an intoxicant. That is fine; knowledge of the universal does not entail knowledge of all particulars.

The Nature of the Contradictory (Naqid)
Author redefines the contradictory and denies its knowability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the author's definition of 'contradictory' (naqid) as absolute non-existence from the common view of mere negation, and argue why knowledge of a thing does not entail knowledge of its contradictory.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a thing is not its negation but pure non-existence, which cannot be known.

This lesson tackles a subtle objection: if you know a thing, must you also know its contradictory? The author responds by redefining the contradictory of a thing as 'not-thing' (al-la shay'), which is pure non-existence. Since non-existence has no mental identity, it cannot be known. This clarifies that knowledge of a positive reality does not entail knowledge of its opposite.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that knowledge of a thing necessarily includes knowledge of its contradictory, because the contradictory is the removal (raf') of that thing, and removal is known when the thing is known.

The Reciprocal Relation Between Entailment and Contradiction
Author discusses entailment, contradiction, and the infinite chain of necessary conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the reciprocal relation between entailment and contradiction in logical analysis.

Core Idea
To contradict something, your position must entail its opposite.

This passage explores the foundational logical relation between entailment (luzum) and contradiction (tanaqud). The author shows that these are not separate concepts but internally related: a thing is contrary to another only if it entails what contradicts it, and it entails another only if it is contrary to what contradicts that other. This reciprocity ensures the impossibility of combining a thing and its opposite.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Familiarize yourself with the terms: mustalzim (that which entails), malzum (that which is entailed), lazim (necessary consequence), naqid (contradictory), munafi (contrary).
These terms are stable across the tradition.
2
Parse the definition of contrary
The author states: 'a thing is not contrary to another unless it entails what contradicts it.' This defines contrary (munafi) in terms of entailment (mustalzim).
3
Parse the reciprocal definition of entailment
The author also states: 'a thing does not entail another unless it is contrary to what contradicts that other.' This defines entailment in terms of contrariety.
This completes the reciprocal relation.
4
Understand the impossibility of contradiction
If these conditions were not met, then a thing and its opposite could coexist, which is impossible. Therefore, the relation is necessary.
5
Apply to a real Islamic example
Consider the ruling of an act being wajib (obligatory). Wajib is contrary to haram (forbidden). The entailment: wajib entails the negation of haram. This satisfies the definition.
This shows how the logical tool is used in usul al-fiqh.

The Infinite Chain of Necessary Conditions
Author discusses entailment, contradiction, and the infinite chain of necessary conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, according to the author, the chain of necessary conditions and necessary consequences is infinite.

Core Idea
Every existent can be a necessary condition for any other, leading to an infinite regress.

After establishing the reciprocity of entailment and contradiction, the author turns to the consequences: there is no end to the necessary conditionalities (malzumiyyat) and necessary consequences (lawazim) of a thing. Since every existent can serve as a necessary condition for any other, and the condition itself exists, it too has conditions, ad infinitum. This infinite multiplicity directly parallels the multiplicity of contrarieties and can be analyzed through exceptional syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of necessary condition
A thing is a necessary condition (malzum) for another if the non-existence of the thing entails the non-existence of the other. This is the inverse of entailment.
2
Recognize the infinite regress
For any existent X, there are infinite malzumiyyat and lawazim because 'every existent's being a necessary condition for the existence of this thing' means that for any existent Y, X's malzumiyya for Y exists, and that malzumiyya itself exists, so it too has malzumiyya, etc.
The author explicitly says 'the necessary-conditionalities are infinite, the necessary consequences are likewise.'
3
Connect to contrariety
The multiplicity of entailments leads to multiplicity of contrarieties, because each entailment corresponds to a contrariety relation (as defined earlier).
4
Correlate to exceptional syllogisms
Each entailment relation corresponds to a conditional syllogism (qiyas istithnai). These syllogisms are used by all people, but common usage often seems non-productive, e.g., 'If you were able, you would do it.'
This shows the practical relevance of the infinite regress.
5
Apply to a real Islamic example
Consider the existence of the world. In kalam, the world is contingent and requires a cause. The non-existence of the cause entails the non-existence of the effect. This is a conditional syllogism. The cause itself, if contingent, would require another cause. But in kalam, the chain terminates at the Necessary Being. The author's logical regress is distinct from causal regress.
This illustrates how logical analysis applies to theological arguments.

Are Conditional Syllogisms with Self-Evident Antecedents Useful?
Author analyzes conditional syllogisms with self-evident antecedents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a conditional syllogism with an explicit exception is beneficial or redundant based on the antecedent's prior assent.

Core Idea
If the antecedent is already known, a conditional syllogism is a burden, not a benefit.

The author extends his analysis of implicit premises to conditional syllogisms. He argues that when the antecedent of a conditional is self-evident or already assented to, stating the conditional premise in syllogistic form is redundant. The mind already grasps the implication, and the exception (e.g., modus ponens) adds no new information. Thus, such syllogisms are an unnecessary burden; one should instead use a simple 'whenever it is so, it is so' without syllogistic form.

Guided Path
1
Recall the structure of a conditional syllogism
A conditional syllogism has a major premise of the form 'if A then B', a minor premise affirming the antecedent (modus ponens) or denying the consequent (modus tollens), and a conclusion. The author focuses on modus ponens: if A is true, then B is true.
The passage uses 'if every B is A' as the antecedent and assumes the exception affirms it.
2
Recognize the timing of mental assent
The author claims that when the conditional premise is stated, the hypothesis and the assent to it occur to the mind before any exception is made. In fact, the assent precedes the time when the mind even considers the consequent.
Self-evident or already-known antecedents trigger immediate mental assent.
3
Evaluate the role of the exception
When the exception (affirming the antecedent) is uttered, it either brings nothing new (if the antecedent is already assented to) or repeats what is already known. In both cases, it does not add essential information.
The author says the exception does not 'bring about an essential matter in reaching the goal'.
4
Contrast with a useful conditional
A conditional syllogism is only beneficial if the antecedent is doubted (mashkuk). Then the exception actually informs the mind and leads to a new conclusion. Otherwise, it is a burden.
The passage implies that using self-evident antecedents in syllogistic form is 'takalluf' (an unnecessary burden).
5
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider: 'If a person prays with impurity, his prayer is invalid. Zaid prays with impurity. Therefore his prayer is invalid.' If the antecedent is not self-evident (e.g., the rule is known from revelation but not obvious), the syllogism is useful. But if it is self-evident that impurity invalidates prayer, then the conditional is redundant.

Essential vs. Verificational Negation and Universal Attachments
Author defends essential negation conversion and advises seeking universal attachments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential and verificational negation and identify universal attachments to construct valid syllogisms.

Core Idea
Negation is convertible in essence but not in verification; focus on universal attachments for productive syllogisms.

This passage tackles a subtle point in logical inference: the convertibility of negation. You will learn that a negative statement is necessarily convertible only when the negation concerns the essence of the terms, not when it concerns accidental, verificational attributes. The author then guides you to concentrate on universal attachments (lawāḥiq kulliyya) and to avoid unproductive searches that lead to invalid syllogistic figures. This prepares you to efficiently construct sound arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection against conversion of negation
The author presents an objection: separate properties (khuṣūṣ mufāriqa) can be negated of a thing, but the thing cannot be negated of those properties (e.g., 'a human is not laughing' is true, but 'laughing is not human' is false). Recognize that this challenges the universal convertibility of negation.
Separate properties are accidental, not essential.
2
Distinguish essential vs. verificational negation
The author argues that if negation is based on essence (i.e., 'this is not that' in terms of identity), then conversion holds necessarily: 'that is not this'. But if it is based on verification (actual existence or accident), conversion may fail. Identify which type of negation is at play in a given statement.
Essential negation is about the thing's quiddity; verificational negation concerns actual state.
3
Define universal attachments (lawāḥiq kulliyya)
A universal attachment is not what attaches to every individual of the subject, but what attaches to the universality of the subject (i.e., to the concept as a whole). Know that seeking such attachments is productive for syllogisms, unlike attachments to non-universal subjects.
Example: 'Human' is a universal attached thing; 'rationality' is a universal attachment that applies to the concept of human, not necessarily every individual.
4
Identify unproductive searches to avoid
The author specifies three cases: (1) attachments that attach to both extremes of a proposition or neither, (2) seeking whether the predicate of the conclusion is predicated of something that is negated of its subject (negative minor in second figure), (3) seeking whether the predicate is predicated of something of which the subject is not predicated (negative minor in third figure). Also avoid particular negative in fourth figure. Memorize these pitfalls to waste no effort.
These all involve invalid or non-productive syllogistic forms.

This passage presents a specific case of immediate inference: conversion. This means the universal affirmative propositions convert mutually. Understanding this helps manipulate categorical propositions in syllogistic reasoning, a key tool in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The middle term must be equal to be convertible; equality is the condition for immediate inference via conversion.

In this passage, the author concludes a discussion on the middle term's role in demonstration. He emphasizes that for a middle term to be convertible (mun'akis), it must be equal (musawi) to the terms. This is a key condition for valid immediate inference. We will dissect the logical conditions and apply them to Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1

2
Understand 'equal' in context
The author explains that the cause (middle term) is not specific to each subject but is 'equal to the effect' (مساوية للمعلول). This means the middle term applies to all and only those things that have the effect; it is coextensive.
Equality here means the middle term's extension is identical to the major term's extension, enabling conversion.
3
Relate to immediate inference
Conversion ('aks) is an immediate inference where the subject and predicate swap. For a universal affirmative proposition (e.g., 'Every A is B'), conversion is only valid if A and B are equal (coextensive). The middle term in a demonstration must satisfy this for the conclusion to be convertible.
In Aristotelian logic, 'Every A is B' converts to 'Some B is A' without equality; but here the author seems to require full equality for demonstration purposes.

Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the demonstration: 'Every intoxicating drink is forbidden; this drink is intoxicating; therefore it is forbidden.' The middle term 'intoxicating' is not equal to 'forbidden' (since not everything forbidden is intoxicating). Therefore, it is not convertible. But for a valid demonstration where the middle term is the cause, equality is needed.
In usul al-fiqh, the 'illa (cause) must be coextensive for qiyas to be certain.

Next, the more difficult (الأصعب) implies the easier (الأسهل): if it is possible to fast a month without break, it is possible to fast one day. The passage continues with a temporal rule: if the beginning is possible, the end is possible, and vice versa. For example, if it is possible to start a journey, it is possible to complete it. Similarly, if a later stage is possible (e.g., being a man), the earlier stage (being a youth) is possible. Things dependent on external help are possible; if every part is possible, the whole is possible (like building a house from possible bricks). Conversely, if the whole is possible, parts are possible. If a species is possible, the genus is possible (e.g., if 'fasting on Monday' is possible, 'fasting' as a genus is possible). If one correlative (e.g., father) is possible, the other (son) is possible. These rules form the basis for constructing rhetorical arguments from possibility.

Key Terms
نقيض الشيء (naqid al-shay'): The opposite or contradictory of a thing; used to infer possibility of the thing from its opposite.
الأصعب والأسهل (al-as'ab wa al-as-hal): The more difficult and the easier; logical relation: if the harder is possible, the easier is possible. [Note: Common in rhetorical topoi, not formal logic.]

Rules for Inferring Existence from Signs and Causes
Author gives rules for inferring existence from conditions and causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the rules of immediate inference from consequent to antecedent and from cause to effect, and distinguish necessary from probabilistic connections, using Islamic legal and theological examples.

Core Idea
The existence of a consequent or effect proves the antecedent or cause existed.

This passage from the Mantiq Kabir provides practical rules for inferring existence from signs and causal connections. These are not speculative but grounded in everyday reasoning and Islamic theology. For instance, the presence of forgetfulness proves prior knowledge, and the presence of capability and will proves the act occurred. The author also clarifies a key distinction between necessary and probabilistic connections, correcting a common misunderstanding about Aristotle's view on rhetorical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inference types
The passage presents two main types: consequent-antecedent (التابع والمتبوع) and cause-effect (السبب والمسبب). Understand that in both, the existence of the second item (consequent/effect) implies the existence of the first (antecedent/cause).
2
Test the consequent-antecedent rule
Apply the rule 'if the consequent exists, the antecedent existed' to the example: forgetting (nisyan) is the consequent of not-knowing? Actually, forgetting presupposes prior knowledge (the antecedent). So if a person forgot (nisyan), then he must have known ('ilm) before. This is a necessary connection in human psychology.
In Islamic legal reasoning, this rule is used to infer past events from present signs, e.g., the presence of wine indicates prior drinking.
3
Test the cause-effect rule
Apply 'if the cause exists, the effect exists' to the Ash'ari example: capability (qudra) and will (irada) together cause the act. So if both exist, the act must exist. This reflects the occasionalist view that God creates the act when power and will are present.
This is a strong causal inference used in kalam arguments for divine predetermination.
4
Understand necessary vs. probabilistic connections
The author notes that some of these connections are necessary (اضطرارية) and some are merely probabilistic (أكثرية). For example, the inference from forgetting to knowledge is necessary; but many causal links in everyday life are only probable (e.g., smoke indicates fire, but not always). This distinction affects the certainty of arguments in different sciences.
In rhetoric, premises are often probabilistic; in demonstration, they are necessary.
5
Apply the distinction to the arts of logic
The author corrects Aristotle's supposed view that all rhetorical premises are equally possible. Rather, they vary in modality. This is crucial for classifying arguments into the five arts: demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, sophistry, and poetics.
The rule helps decide whether an argument is suitable for burhan (certain) or khitaba (persuasive).

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the second common type: statements about the existent and non-existent. The author provides rules for inferring existence from signs and causes. First, if something is less prepared for existence but later exists with greater preparation, then it exists. For example, if a student was not prepared for a test but studied (greater preparation) and passed, the passing exists. Second, if the consequent exists, the antecedent exists. The example given: if he forgot (the effect of not knowing, but forgetting presupposes prior knowledge), then he had known (antecedent). For instance, in Islamic legal theory, if a person is found with wine (consequent of drinking), it indicates he drank (cause). Third, if the cause exists, the effect exists. The cause here is capability (qudra) and will (irada)—if both are present, the action exists. This echoes the Ash'ari occasionalist view: power and will create the act. Fourth, the necessary condition (ملزوم) and what revolves around it (مدار) imply each other. The author then summarizes: anything whose existence necessitates the existence of another, that other must exist. He cites Aristotle (The First Teacher) noting that some of these connections are necessary (اضطراري) and some are merely probabilistic (أكثرية). He corrects a misconception that Aristotle held that all rhetorical premises are equally possible; rather, they vary in certainty. This is important for distinguishing between demonstrative and rhetorical arguments. For example, in Islamic dialectic, a probable sign like 'this is wine because it ferments' is probabilistic, not necessary.
