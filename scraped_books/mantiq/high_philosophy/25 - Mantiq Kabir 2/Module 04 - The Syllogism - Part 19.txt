The Five Arts of Syllogism: Division by Material
Author enumerates the five arts of syllogism based on material.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any syllogism into one of the five arts based on the type of premises it uses.

Core Idea
The matter of premises determines the art: certainty, common opinion, probability, deception, or imagination.

This passage is a cornerstone of Islamic logic. It tells us that logical form is not enough; we must also consider what kind of premises are used. This division mirrors the Aristotelian Organon but is applied to Islamic discourse, from theological proofs to poetic imagery.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish matter from form
The author explicitly says the division is 'according to matter, not according to form'. Matter (madda) refers to the epistemic status of the premises (what they are made of), not the shape (form) of the syllogism.
Form concerns mood and figure; matter concerns content.
2
Identify the five types of matter
The five types are: certain (yaqiniyyat), common (mashhurat), probable (maznunat), deceptive (mushabbihaat), and imaginative (mukhayyalat). Each is introduced as a possibility for the composition.
The author lists them by negating the previous type: 'or not... but rather...'
3
Match each matter to an art
Certain premises yield demonstrative syllogism (burhani); common premises yield dialectical (jadali); probable premises yield rhetorical (khatabi); deceptive premises yield sophistical (mughaliti); imaginative premises yield poetic (shi'ri).
The Greek names are also given: Topica, Rhetorica, Sophistici, Poetica.
4
Apply the classification to an example
For instance, a syllogism using premises accepted by everyone (e.g., 'Justice is good') is dialectical. One using certain premises from revelation (e.g., 'Allah is one') is demonstrative.
Practice with Islamic examples from fiqh, kalam, or adab.

Commentary
This section is a clear enumeration of the five arts of syllogism (القياس) based on their material (المادة), not their form (الصورة). The author states that syllogism, as a composed statement, is divided into five types according to the nature of its premises. First, demonstrative syllogism (برهاني) uses certain premises (يقينيات). Second, dialectical syllogism (جدلي) uses commonplaces (مشهورات) and is called 'طوييقا' (Topica) in Greek. Third, rhetorical syllogism (خطابي) uses probable premises (مظنونات) and is 'ريطوريقا' (Rhetorica). Fourth, sophistical syllogism (مغالطي) uses deceptive premises (مشبهات) and is called 'سوفسطائي' (Sophistici). Fifth, poetic syllogism (شعري) uses imaginative premises (مخيلات) and is 'بوتطيقا' (Poetica). This classification is standard in Islamic logic, following Aristotle's Organon, and is crucial for understanding the scope of logic and its applications in different fields.

Five Material Types of Syllogism
Author classifies syllogisms into five arts based on material.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five types of syllogism based on their material (certainties, accepted opinions, plausible opinions, deceptive things, imaginative things) and match them to their Arabic names.

Core Idea
Syllogisms are classified by the material of their premises, not by their form.

The passage presents a widely accepted classification of syllogisms into five 'arts' based on the type of premises they employ. This classification is crucial in Islamic logic because it determines the epistemic value and purpose of the argument. You will learn to distinguish demonstrative, dialectical, rhetorical, sophistical, and poetic syllogisms by examining the nature of their premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of classification
Notice that the author explicitly states the division is 'according to matter, not form'. The form (mood and figure) is the same across syllogisms; what changes is the material, i.e., the type of proposition used as premises.
Matter here refers to the epistemological status of the premises (yaqiniyyāt, mashhūrāt, etc.).
2
List the five types in order
Read the passage and extract the five types: burhānī (demonstrative), jadalī (dialectical), khaṭābī (rhetorical), mughāliṭī (sophistical), shiʿrī (poetic). Note the sequence: it proceeds from the highest certainty to the lowest.
The author uses the enumeration letters [أ][ب][ث][ج][ح] to mark each type.
3
Define each material
For each type, identify the material that composes it: yaqīniyyāt (certainties) for demonstrative, mashhūrāt (accepted opinions) for dialectical, maznūnāt (plausible opinions) for rhetorical, mushabbihāt (deceptive things) for sophistical, and mukhayyalāt (imaginative things) for poetic.
These materials determine whether the conclusion is certain, probable, or merely persuasive.
4
Compare the Greek names given
The author provides Greek names: Topica, Rhetorica, Sophistic, Poetica (for the last four; the first may be implied). These are traditional labels but the author will later critique them.
These names are part of the heritage, but the author questions their accuracy.

Critique of Greek Names and the Division
Author critiques the Greek names and the division itself.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author's two critiques: (1) the Greek names for the syllogism types are not genuine Greek, and (2) the division into five is not exhaustive without defining the fifth by negation.

Core Idea
The author skeptically examines the received classification, questioning both its linguistic authenticity and its logical completeness.

After presenting the standard fivefold division, the author immediately raises two problems. First, the Greek names (Topica, Rhetorica, etc.) are not actually Greek because they contain the letter ṭā’ which does not exist in Greek. Second, the division itself is flawed because the fifth type (poetic) is only defined by lacking the materials of the first four, which makes the classification incomplete unless that negative definition is accepted. You will learn to read critically and evaluate the logical validity of a classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first critique: linguistic authenticity
Find the sentence where the author says the names 'are not from that language itself'. Then note the evidence: the letter ṭā’ (ط) is not found in Greek; instead, the equivalent is kāf (ك). For example, 'Topica' should be something like 'Kopica' if it were genuine.
This shows the author's awareness of historical linguistics and rejects blind acceptance of transmitted terms.
2
Identify the second critique: exhaustiveness of the division
The author says the division 'is not as it should be' and 'necessitates the limitation' (الحصر). Read the following: unless the fifth type is defined by the absence of the first four, the division does not cover all possibilities.
The poetic type lacks a positive material; it is defined negatively (not certain, not accepted, not plausible, not deceptive).
3
Analyze the conditional fix offered
The author presents a conditional: 'unless it is said that the fifth is characterized by absence of the first four'. This would make the division exhaustive (limited to five). But he adds that even this definition has problems ('there is discussion in it').
The author is pointing out that the classification is arbitrary unless we accept a negative definition, which is weaker than the positive definitions of the other four.
4
Assess the author's overall stance
The author is not simply presenting the classification; he is probing its foundations. This critical approach is typical of Islamic logicians who refined Greek logic.
The lesson is to question received taxonomies and demand proper definitions.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents for the third major section of the book, titled 'The third section on proofs and what pertains to them.' It systematically lays out the types and subtopics of syllogism (qiyas) that will be covered. The section is divided into four main types: first, on the form of syllogism, detailing the four figures (first, second, third, fourth); second, on mixed syllogisms (that combine different modal or qualitative premises), also across the four figures; third, on conditional syllogisms, including compositions from two conditionals, implications and coincidences, and common themes; fourth, on the remaining syllogisms, beginning with exceptional syllogisms (istithna'i), which are contextualized as conditional and completed by conjunctive (iqtirani) syllogisms, followed by reductio ad absurdum, direct and indirect forms, and necessary conditions like universality and affirmation. Further chapters cover compositions of many premises, division, analysis methods, conversion, circular reasoning, the necessary entailment of true premises, contradictory premises, begging the question, false cause, and advice for debate. This index shows the comprehensive and systematic approach of the book to logical reasoning, focusing on the technical aspects of syllogistic logic as applied in Islamic scholarly discourse.
