The Two Types of Division and Why They Cannot Define
Author critiques the use of division for definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of logical division and explain why division fails to produce a complete definition or prove equality between the defined and the definition.

Core Idea
Division reminds of differences but cannot prove equality—definitions require more.

In this passage, the author critically examines the role of division (qisma) in logic, particularly its use in establishing definitions (hadd). He distinguishes two types of division and argues that even the more precise type falls short of proving a definition because it cannot demonstrate equality (musāwāt) between the defined term and its putative definition. Understanding these limits is crucial for anyone who wants to construct sound definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of division
The author distinguishes: (1) division where the divided thing is not part of the divisor (e.g., 'every animal is either mortal or eternal'), and (2) division where the divided thing is part of the divisor (e.g., 'every animal is either a mortal animal or an eternal animal'). Notice that the second type repeats the divided term within each disjunct.
The second type is the one the author deems necessary for valid inference.
2
Test the necessity of the second type
The author claims that only the second type is needed; but even then it may be redundant. Examine the example: to conclude 'every human is an eternal animal', you need premises that already entail this. The division adds nothing new—it is 'wasted'.
This shows that valid syllogisms can stand without division.
3
Analyze the critique of division in definition
The author lists four failings: (1) division does not identify the most universal predicate to be divided (e.g., that 'animal' is the genus for 'man'); (2) even if you build a complex predicate by division, you cannot claim it is a definition because division cannot prove equality (musāwāt); (3) definition requires a genus and differences, but the genus is not acquired by division; (4) division does not distinguish essential (dhātī) from accidental (‘araḍī) attributes.
Each point independently shows why division is insufficient.
4
Assess the residual utility of division
Despite these criticisms, the author acknowledges that division is useful as a reminder (tanbīh) of the order of differences and what divides essentially vs. accidentally. For example, 'animal' divides into rational and non-rational essentially, but not into black and non-black.
Division has pedagogical value but not probative force.

Commentary
This passage is a critical examination of the role of division (qisma) in logic, particularly in proving definitions (hadd). The author begins by discussing two types of division: one where the divided thing is not taken as part of the divisor (e.g., 'every animal is either mortal or eternal'), and one where it is taken as part (e.g., 'every animal is either a mortal animal or an eternal animal'). The author then focuses on using division to establish a definition. He points out that division does not identify the most universal predicate to be divided; rather, one must already have that. He illustrates with an example: to define man, one must say 'man is a mortal animal' and then divide 'mortal animal' into rational and non-rational, leading to 'man is a rational mortal animal'. However, even this does not constitute a definition because division does not guarantee equality (musawāt) between the defined and the definition. Moreover, definition requires a genus and differences; the genus is not obtained by division, and while differences can be arranged by division, equality cannot be demonstrated. Essential (dhātī) and accidental ('araḍī) attributes are not distinguished by division. Thus, division is of little use in syllogism (qiyās) and especially in definition. Nonetheless, it has some utility as a reminder (tanbīh) of the order of differences and of what divides essentially versus accidentally. For example, animal is divided into rational and non-rational essentially, but not into black and non-black. The author concludes that division is useful only as a reminder, not as a positive proof.

The Three Types of Primary Exhaustive Divisions
Author categorizes primary exhaustive divisions into three types based on differentia or accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given primary exhaustive division into one of three types: by essential differentiae, by accidents primary to the genus, or by accidents not primary to the genus.

Core Idea
Primary divisions can be by essence or by accident, but must be immediate to the genus.

In logic, a primary exhaustive division (al-qisma al-mustawfa al-awwaliyya) is a complete sorting of a genus using either essential differentiae or certain accidents. But not every division that covers all members of a genus is primary. The author explains that primary exhaustive divisions come in three types, depending on whether the dividing properties are essential differentiae, accidents that are primary to the genus, or accidents that only attach to the genus after it becomes a species. Understanding these distinctions helps us evaluate definitions and classifications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus being divided
Look at the division statement and determine the overarching category (genus) that is being split into parts. For example, in 'number is divided into even and odd', the genus is 'number'.
2
Determine if the division is exhaustive
Check whether all members of the genus are covered by the division. A division is exhaustive if the parts together include every possible member. E.g., 'even and odd' covers all numbers.
3
Check if the division is primary
Ask: Does this division apply only to this genus, or does it also apply to other genera? If it applies to other genera, it is not primary. For instance, 'equal and unequal' applies to number, line, surface, etc., so it is not primary to number.
4
Classify the division into one of three types
If the division is primary, determine whether it is: (1) by essential differentiae, (2) by accidents that are primary to the genus, or (3) by accidents that become attached only when the genus becomes a specific species. Use the examples from the passage as a guide.
5
Test with an example from Islamic science
Apply the classification to a division from kalam or fiqh, such as 'existence is divided into necessary and possible'. Is it primary? Which type?

Primary vs Non-Primary Division of a Genus
Author introduces distinction between primary and non-primary divisions with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish primary from non-primary divisions of a genus using the test of applying the attribute to the genus without further specification.

Core Idea
A division is primary if the attribute applies to the genus's general nature itself.

The author uses number as a key example: number divides into equal/unequal and even/odd. The former is not primary because non-numbers also have equality/inequality; the latter is primary relative to non-numbers because the genus 'quantum' is not exhaustively divided by even/odd. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise logical classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus being divided
Find the genus in the example, e.g., 'number' or 'quantum'.
2
Identify the attributes used in the division
For number, attributes are equal/unequal and even/odd.
3
Test each attribute for primariness
Check if the attribute applies to the genus in its general nature without adding any specification. Use the author's test: 'take the nature of the genus as specified (e.g., some number, some body)'.
If the attribute can apply without further specification, it is primary.
4
Determine if the division is primary or not
Based on the test, label the division as primary or non-primary. For number, equal/unequal is not primary (since body also has it), even/odd is primary relative to non-numbers but not to the genus quantum.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An accident that is essential to the subject keeps the science under its natural genus; an extraneous accident moves it to a different science.

Sciences Equal in Rank vs Disparate Sciences
Author contrasts sciences with common genus vs none.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sciences that are equal in rank (sharing a genus) and disparate sciences (no common genus).

Core Idea
When subjects share a genus, sciences are equal; when they differ in quiddity, they are disparate.

The author extends the classification to sciences whose subjects are not nested. If they share a genus (e.g., quantity), they are called 'equal in rank'. If they have no common genus in their quiddities, they are 'disparate'. This distinction helps organize sciences by their subject relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify subjects of both sciences
For geometry, the subject is magnitude; for arithmetic, the subject is number. Write them down.
2
Check for a common genus
Determine if both subjects fall under a higher universal. Magnitude and number both fall under 'quantity' (al-kamm). They share a genus.
The passage says: 'they share in the genus, which is quantity'.
3
Classify as equal in rank
If they share a genus, the sciences are called 'sciences equal in rank' (al-ʿulūm al-mutasāwiyatu fī al-rutba). They are neither subordinate nor superior; they are peers under the same genus.
4
Contrast with disparate sciences
If subjects have no common genus (e.g., body and number), the sciences are 'disparate' (mutabāyina). They differ in complete quiddity.
Example: natural science and arithmetic. Body and number have no common genus; they are fundamentally different.

Same Subject, Different Aspects: Two Cases
Author explains differentiation by aspect on a common subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify pairs of sciences that share a subject but differ in aspect into two subcases: one absolute/one qualified, or both qualified.

Core Idea
The aspect of study differentiates sciences even when the subject is identical.

The author now considers sciences that share the same subject but differ in the aspect under which they study it. Two subcases arise: one science studies the subject absolutely, the other under a qualification; or both study it under different qualifications. This is crucial for understanding how medicine and natural science both study the human body, but from different perspectives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common subject
Find the entity that both sciences study. For example, the human body is studied by both natural science and medicine.
2
Determine the aspects
Natural science looks at the human body absolutely (min haythu huwa) i.e., as a body with essential accidents like motion and rest. Medicine looks at the human body under the aspect of health and disease (as qualified).
One aspect is absolute, the other is qualified. This is subcase 1.
3
Check for subcase 2
If both sciences use qualifications, identify each qualification. For celestial bodies: natural science looks at them as moving/resting; astronomy looks at them as having quantity.
Both are qualified, but different qualifications. This is subcase 2.
4
Apply the rule
If one is absolute and the other qualified, they are distinct but related; if both are qualified with different qualifications, they are also distinct. The aspect is the differentiating factor.
The author emphasizes that agreement in subject without aspect difference would make them one science.

As for principles, you find them, although they share in some way, they differ in another way: you find that the principles belong to geometry primarily and to optics secondarily; this is a matter whose proof we have completed. And difference of demonstrations does not cause difference in this topic, for there may be for one thing two different demonstrations, either from two middle terms one of which is predicated of the other only, like our statement: every human is an animal, and every animal is nourished; and our statement: every human is perfect, and every perfect is temperate. In the first, one of the two middle terms is under the other. As for the second, they differ, one not being under the other. Likewise our statement: every human is laughing, and every laughing is wondering, along with our statement: every human is bashful, and every bashful is wondering. For these, although they are among those in which one is convertible upon the other, one is not under the other. Thus it has become clear that difference of middle terms does not cause difference in the investigations from the perspective of their sciences being different, and the import of this consideration can be sought from the remaining figures.

Refining the Classification of the Poetic Art
Author critiques and refines the classification of the five arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the poetic art by its absence of the properties of the other four arts and evaluate the exhaustiveness of the classification.

Core Idea
The poetic art is defined not by what it has but by what it lacks.

The author critiques the common classification of the five arts (demonstration, dialectic, rhetoric, sophistry, poetics), pointing out that the division as recorded may not be accurate. He proposes a way to salvage it by defining the poetic art negatively, as lacking the features of the first four arts. This clarifies how exhaustive classification works in logic.

Categories of Improvements in Arguments
Author introduces the topic of improvements and lists their three categories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of improvements (taḥsīnāt) in arguments: related to utterance, arrangement, and speaker's states.

Core Idea
Arguments can be improved not only by their content but also by how they are worded, ordered, and delivered.

After discussing syllogisms and their premises, the author turns to the topic of improvements (taḥsīnāt), which are external factors that enhance arguments. He categorizes them into three kinds: those related to the wording (lafẓ), those related to arrangement (tartīb), and those related to the speaker's demeanor (hay'āt al-mutakallimīn). These improvements go beyond the logical content and are crucial for effective communication in debate and rhetoric.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the context
The author says that after covering types of syllogisms and premises, it is necessary to mention the 'followings, arrangements, and improvements.' This indicates that improvements are secondary but important topics.
2
Identify the three categories
The passage lists three categories: (1) related to the utterance (lafẓ), (2) related to arrangement (tartīb), (3) related to the states of the speakers. Note that the third category is external to both utterance and meaning.
3
Understand 'external to utterance and meaning'
The third category (speaker's states) includes factors like tone, confidence, appearance, etc. These are not part of the linguistic expression nor the logical content, yet they affect the argument's effectiveness.
4
Apply to Islamic debate (munāẓara)
In a theological debate, for example, the debater should use clear and eloquent language (utterance), present arguments in a logical order (arrangement), and maintain composure (speaker's states).
This ties to adab al-baḥth (etiquette of discussion).
