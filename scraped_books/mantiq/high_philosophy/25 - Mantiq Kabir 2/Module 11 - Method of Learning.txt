When to Distinguish General from Specific Agreement Conditionals
Author defends the general/specific distinction despite majority neglect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a logical distinction is useful or merely takalluf by applying the author's criteria from this passage.

Core Idea
Useful distinctions yield benefits the general case cannot provide.

This passage addresses a potential criticism: if the majority of philosophers, including Ibn Sina, did not bother distinguishing general from specific agreement conditionals, why should we? The author argues that while logic deals with universal laws, the distinction can still be beneficial in practice. Understanding this debate trains you to assess when a conceptual refinement is worthwhile and when it crosses into mere takalluf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mainstream position
State what most philosophers did regarding the general/specific distinction. They did not distinguish; they treated all agreement conditionals as one type.
2
Find the author's defense
What reasons does the author give for making the distinction? Look for phrases like 'additional benefits' and 'considering the general is not like considering the specific'.
3
Understand the scope of logic
How does the author define the subject matter of logical investigations? He says they deal with general universals that are laws for particulars, which might make the distinction seem irrelevant to pure logic.
4
Evaluate the utility
Explain why the author thinks the general and specific can be useful for different things. Give an example from Islamic sciences, such as the distinction in qiyas between general and specific illa.

However, the author defends the distinction made by others (including himself) as not a mere complication but yielding extra benefits. Logical investigations primarily deal with universal laws, but when addressing particular applications, the distinction can be useful. The general type may cover cases the specific cannot, and vice versa. Therefore, the author chooses to treat both to gather all possible benefits.

Necessity of Acquiring Premises for Valid Syllogisms
Author argues that knowing the definition of syllogism is insufficient without knowing how to acquire premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why knowledge of the definition of a syllogism is insufficient without knowledge of premise acquisition, using the author's analogical argument.

Core Idea
Knowing the definition of logic is useless without the skill to acquire premises.

In this passage, the author argues that logic requires practical skill in constructing syllogisms, not just theoretical definition. He compares it to medicine: knowing what a medicine is doesn't cure; you must know how to obtain it. This lesson will help you understand the epistemological priority of acquisition over definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's main claim
Find the sentence where the author states that knowing the definition (mahiyya) of a syllogism is insufficient. Look for keywords like 'لا يكفي' or 'لا يمكننا'.
The claim is that definition alone does not allow us to distinguish correct from incorrect syllogisms.
2
Extract the analogical argument
The author uses an analogy with medicine. Note the parallel: just as knowing a medicine's definition does not cure without knowing how to obtain and prepare it, knowing a syllogism's definition does not suffice without knowing how to acquire its premises.
The analogy is explicit in the text: 'كما أن علمنا بأن الدواء النافع ... لم يكفنا شفاء تلك العلة إلا وأن نعلم وجه طلبه وتحصيله وإيجاده'.
3
Analyze the parallel components
Identify the asl (original case: medicine) and the far' (new case: syllogism). The 'illa (effective cause) is the dependence on acquisition skills for benefit.
The 'illa is that in both domains, practical acquisition is necessary for the intended benefit (cure or valid inference).
4
Determine the conclusion
The author concludes that learning how to acquire premises and construct syllogisms (iktisab al-muqaddimat wa tahsil al-qiyasat) is necessary for anyone who wants to use syllogisms correctly.
This conclusion is stated as a necessity: 'كان الاشتغال بتعرف اكتسابه على وجه كلي ... من اللوازم'.

Six Modes of Teaching and Learning in Logic
Author enumerates six modes of teaching and learning, highlighting the mental type in syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the six modes of teaching and learning and identify the mental type as the logical foundation for syllogistic inference.

Core Idea
Teaching and learning are one in essence but two in consideration in syllogistic inference.

This passage presents a comprehensive classification of how knowledge is transmitted. For the logician, the mental type is paramount, as it explains how a single person can teach himself through the act of inference. Understanding this classification clarifies that learning in logic is an active, inferential process, not passive reception.

Guided Path
1
List the six modes
Read the Arabic and translation to identify each of the six types: industrial, dictational, disciplinary, imitative, prompting, and mental. Note the defining characteristic of each.
The author uses concrete examples for each mode (e.g., blacksmithing for industrial).
2
Focus on the mental type
The mental type is acquired through an audible or intelligible statement that produces belief, opinion, or conception. This is the mode relevant to logic and syllogism.
This contrasts with the other modes which involve physical practice, memorization, or trust in a teacher.
3
Understand the two aspects within one person
The author explains that mental teaching and learning can occur within a single individual. The person who infers the middle term (hads) is the teacher, and the person who derives the conclusion is the learner. These are two aspects of the same cognitive act.
The middle term is the key term that connects the premises in a categorical syllogism.
4
Grasp the unity of teaching and learning
The author concludes that teaching and learning are one in essence (bi al-dhat) but two in consideration (bi al-i'tibar). This means the same process of inference is simultaneously teaching and learning from different perspectives.
This insight prevents confusion between the act of inference and the roles we assign within it.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the modes of teaching and learning (ta'lim and ta'allum), relevant to the logical art because it establishes how knowledge is transmitted. The author states that all teaching and learning presupposes some prior knowledge. He then enumerates six types. First, industrial (sina'i): learning a craft like blacksmithing through practice. Second, dictational (talqini): memorizing poetry or texts by repetition. Third, disciplinary (ta'dibi): inculcating proper behavior through well-known methods. Fourth, imitative (taqlidi): accepting a belief based on trust in a teacher. Fifth, prompting (tanbihi): recalling something known but momentarily forgotten, like the example of the magnet. Sixth, mental (dhihni): acquiring new belief, opinion, or conception through a statement, and this is the type relevant to logic. The author elaborates that mental teaching and learning can occur between two people or within a single person from two aspects: one who discovers (by hads, intuition) the middle term of a syllogism is the teacher, and the one who derives the conclusion is the learner. Thus, teaching and learning are one in reality but two in consideration. This classification shows that logical education is not merely rote but involves active inference.

The Three Pillars of a Science: Principles, Subjects, and Problems
Author defines principles, subjects, and problems of sciences and divides premises into general and specific.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the principles, subjects, and problems of a science and classify a given premise as specific or general.

Core Idea
Every science rests on principles, investigates a subject, and seeks to prove its problems.

In this passage, the author lays out the foundational architecture of any theoretical science. He explains that every science has three essential components: principles (definitions and premises), subjects (the things investigated), and problems (the claims to be demonstrated). Understanding this framework is crucial for organizing knowledge and ensuring that demonstrations avoid circularity. We'll walk through each component and see how premises are further divided into specific and general types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
Find the three terms: principles (mabādi'), subjects (mawḍū'āt), and problems (masā'il). Note their roles: principles are used for demonstration, problems are demonstrated, subjects are what demonstration is about.
2
Examine the two types of principles
Principles are either definitions (of the subject and its parts/accidents) or premises (specific or general). Premises are the foundational propositions used in demonstrations.
3
Distinguish specific from general premises
Specific premises are confined to one science (e.g., 'motion exists' for natural science). General premises are shared: absolutely general (shared by all sciences, like the law of excluded middle) or general to several sciences (like 'things equal to the same thing are equal').
4
Analyze the examples given
Study the example 'every even number is divisible by two equal parts'—this premise is specific to arithmetic because its subject (even number) belongs to the subject of arithmetic (number). Contrast with 'everything either is or is not' which is absolutely general.

Constraints on the Predicate When Seeking Thatness
Author discusses the predicate in inniyya problems: cannot be genus or differentia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine why the predicate in a problem about 'thatness' (inniyya) cannot be a genus or differentia.

Core Idea
When asking whether something exists (inniyya), the predicate cannot be a definitional part (genus/differentia) because those are already inherent in the subject's nature.

The author discusses an important constraint on predicates in categorical problems. If the aim is to establish the existence or presence of a property (inniyya) rather than the cause (limmiyya), the predicate cannot be a genus or differentia. Why? Because if the subject's nature is already established, its definitional parts are self-evident and not subject to inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Understand inniyya vs limmiyya
Inniyya asks 'that it is' (existence or property presence), while limmiyya asks 'why it is' (cause). The passage focuses on inniyya.
إنية (inniyya) = thatness; لمية (limmiyya) = whiness.
2
Identify the condition
When seeking inniyya, and the subject's nature (طبيعة الموضوع) is already established (محصلة), the predicate cannot be a genus (جنس), differentia (فصل), or a combination of both.
The subject's nature being established means its definition is known.
3
Reason for the constraint
Essential predicates that appear in the definition (حد) of a thing must be evident in existence (بينة الوجود) when the thing is realized. So if the subject is established, these are already known, not sought.
This prevents circularity: you cannot prove existence by invoking definitional parts.
4
Note on demonstration via middle term
It is possible to demonstrate such predicates using a middle term, but that is not necessarily a syllogism (قياس). The middle term could be the definition or description of the predicate.
This distinguishes mere explanation from a formal syllogistic proof.

Distinguishing Error from Ignorance
Author distinguishes error from ignorance at the end of the passage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish error (khaṭa') from simple ignorance (jahl) and apply this distinction to examples from Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Error is a species of ignorance that involves false belief; simple ignorance is merely not knowing.

The passage ends with a crisp logical distinction: every error is ignorance, but not every ignorance is error. This differentiation is vital for properly characterizing knowledge states in Islamic sciences, especially in ijtihad and creedal matters.

Guided Path
1
Define Ignorance (jahl)
Ignorance is the absence of knowledge. It can be simple (not knowing) or compound (believing something false).
Simple ignorance is a lack; compound ignorance is a false belief.
2
Define Error (khaṭa')
Error is specifically compound ignorance: a false belief or judgment that contradicts reality.
The author equates error with false belief, not mere unawareness.
3
Understand the Relationship
The passage states: 'Every error is ignorance, but not every ignorance is error.' This means error is a proper subset of ignorance.
In logical terms: All errors are ignorance, but some ignorance is not error.
4
Apply to an Islamic Science Example
In fiqh, a mujtahid who makes a mistake in ijtihad commits an error (khaṭa'), because he holds a false belief about the ruling. A layperson who never heard of the ruling is simply ignorant (jahl basit).
The Prophet said: 'Whoever does ijtihad and errs has one reward.' This implies error is not blameworthy in the same way as willful ignorance.

Criteria for a Single Science: Subject and Principles
Author defines conditions for a single science: common first subject and first principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether two disciplines belong to the same science by checking their first subject and first principles.

Core Idea
A science is defined by its first subject and first principles, not by shared conclusions.

In the Islamic intellectual tradition, classifying sciences is not arbitrary. This passage from the logic tradition lays down precise conditions for when investigations form a single science. Understanding these criteria helps you see the structure of disciplines like kalām, fiqh, and uṣūl al-fiqh, and how they relate to each other.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first subject (al-mawḍūʿ al-awwal)
Look for the primary topic of inquiry—the genus under which all parts and species fall. For example, in geometry, the subject is magnitude; in optics, it is visible light.
The subject defines what the science is about at the most general level.
2
Identify the first principles (al-mabādiʾ al-ūlā)
Find the foundational premises, definitions, and axioms used to demonstrate essential accidents. The author stresses that these include definitions (ḥudūd), not just premises.
Principles are the starting points for proofs in the science.
3
Apply the test: do both fields share the same first subject?
If the subjects differ, the sciences are necessarily different. For instance, geometry studies magnitude; optics studies visible light—these subjects are distinct.
4
Apply the test: do they share the same first principles?
Even if they share some principles, if the principles belong primarily to one science and secondarily to the other, they are not the same science. The author gives the example of geometry and optics: principles belong to geometry primarily.
Shared principles do not guarantee unity; the origin matters.
5
Conclude whether they form a single science
Only if both the first subject and first principles are identical (or primary in the same way) are the investigations from one science. Otherwise, they are separate sciences.
This prevents false unification of disciplines that merely borrow from each other.

From Division to Anatomy: Two Methods for Premises
Author explains how division and anatomy yield premises for definitions and demonstrations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qisma (division of genus into species) and tashrih (anatomy of whole into parts) and use tashrih to deduce necessary properties for burhan limmi in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Division gives definitions; anatomy gives causes.

The author teaches that logicians must not rely solely on dividing a universal into its species. They must also decompose a whole into its parts—anatomy (tashrih)—to uncover necessary properties and causal connections. This lesson helps you master both methods, especially for constructing 'why' demonstrations (burhan limmi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods
Find the phrases 'qisma' (division) and 'tashrih' (anatomy) in the passage. Notice that the author says we should not restrict ourselves to division alone, but also use anatomy.
Qisma is the standard tool for genus and differentia; tashrih is the decomposition into physical or conceptual parts.
2
Analyze the biological example
Look at the example: 'every horned animal has no upper front teeth' and 'every horned animal has a rumen'. The author explains the cause: the material goes to the horn, and the animal does not macerate food. This is deduction from anatomy.
The cause ('illa) is derived from the composition: horn takes material, no teeth; no mastication requires rumen.
3
Link to burhan limmi
The passage says such deductions and their causes are useful for giving the 'why' demonstration (burhan limmi). Understand that tashrih provides the causal premises needed for a demonstration from cause to effect.
Burhan limmi answers 'why' by citing the cause—here, the anatomical structure.
4
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider the fiqh ruling on riba (usury). Decompose the transaction into its components (e.g., gold, silver, food, hand-to-hand exchange). The cause ('illa) of prohibition is sometimes derived from the nature of the items—this is tashrih at work.
In usul al-fiqh, identifying the 'illa often requires analyzing the parts of the subject.

We should not restrict ourselves to extracting things found in definitions and syllogisms only from the division of the universal into the particular, but also from the anatomy of the whole into parts, like the anatomy of animal and plant into its first parts such as instrumental organs, then second like simple organs, then third like humors, and so on to the last parts. And we should not restrict ourselves to that only, but we should reflect, when the parts and particulars are numerous, what attributes and accidents follow each one or each group, and also which parts follow which particulars. Know that when we deduced from division that animal falls under 'sensitive' and under 'having a soul', we deduced from anatomy that animal is composed of a solid substance and a fluid substance. Likewise we deduced the necessary concomitants of particulars from parts, such as: every horned animal has no upper front teeth — because the material goes to its horn; and every horned animal has a rumen — because it does not macerate [its food]; so its food must undergo some digestion before reaching the inner cavity. And every fish has no lung. Such deduced things and their causes are useful for giving the 'why' demonstration, even if not all of what we mentioned. For example, if it is said to us: 'Why does this animal have a rumen?' we say: 'Because it is horned.' And if it is said: 'Why does it not have a rumen?' we say: 'Because it is not horned.'

Commentary
This passage explains a methodological principle: the logician should not rely solely on the division of a genus into species (which provides the genus and differentia for definitions) but should also employ 'anatomy' (tashrih) — the decomposition of a whole into its parts. The author illustrates this with examples from biology, showing how knowing the structure of an animal (such as its composition from solid and fluid substances) yields necessary properties. The specific examples of horned animals lacking upper front teeth and having a rumen, and fish lacking lungs, are used to show how anatomical knowledge allows us to give causal explanations (the 'why') for observable traits. The passage emphasizes that such deductive inferences are useful for providing 'limmi' demonstrations (demonstrations of the reason why), and it concludes with a simple Q&A format showing how we answer 'why' questions using these deduced causes.

General Topics Benefit Particular Investigations
Author explains that general logical topics aid in particular inquiries and gives an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how general logical topics (al-mawadi' al-kulliyya) are applied to particular cases and identify the three types mentioned: opposites, analogues, and conjugations.

Core Idea
General logical topics are the most beneficial tools for particular inquiries in Islamic sciences.

This lesson introduces the concept of al-mawadi' al-kulliyya (general topics) and their utility in particular investigations. The author emphasises that mastering these general principles—such as opposites, analogues, and conjugations—sharpens one's ability to reason about specific cases. We will see how a general proposition like 'If every pleasure is good, then every pain is evil' can be instantiated to yield a particular conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of general topics
From the passage, note that the author lists 'opposites (al-mutaqabilat), analogues (al-naza'ir), and conjugations (al-tasarif)' as the most well-known and beneficial general topics.
These are broad categories of logical relations that recur across disciplines.
2
Understand the general-to-particular relationship
The author shows that a general statement like 'If every pleasure is good, then every pain is evil' is well-known. From this, a particular instance 'If some pleasure is good, then some pain is evil' follows and becomes known as well. This illustrates how general topics aid in particular inquiries.
The inference is a form of subalternation or specification of a universal to a particular.
3
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider a general principle in Usul al-Fiqh: 'If every intoxicant is haram, then every khamr (wine) is haram.' This general rule can be applied to a particular case: 'If a specific beverage is an intoxicant, then it is haram.' The general topic of 'analogues' helps in extending the ruling to new substances.
You can similarly use 'opposites' to derive rulings about halal from haram.
