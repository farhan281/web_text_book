Poetical Syllogisms: The Logician's Concern
Author defines poetry as imaginative speech and outlines the logician's interest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristic of poetry for the logician and explain why meter and rhyme are excluded from logical analysis.

Core Idea
Poetry's imaginative nature is the logician's only concern; meter belongs to music.

This lesson introduces the fourth type of syllogism: poetical syllogisms. The author defines poetry and clarifies what aspects the logician investigates. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion between logical analysis and other disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of poetry
Find the three formal characteristics given: meter (وزن), equality (تساو), and rhyme (تقفية). Note that the passage states poetry is 'كلام مخيل' (imaginative speech) first, then adds these three.
The essential element for logic is 'كلام مخيل'.
2
Distinguish the logician's domain
Locate the sentence: 'لا نظر للمنطقي في شيء من ذلك إلا في كونه كلامًا مخيلا'. The logician only considers the imaginative aspect.
Meter and rhyme are not the logician's concern.
3
Identify the other disciplines
The passage assigns meter to music ('صاحب علم الموسيقى') and the usage to custom ('بحسب الاستعمال').
This marks boundaries between logic and other sciences.

Poetry as Imaginative Speech: Definition and Takhyil
Defines poetry and the logician's interest in takhyil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define poetry as per the logicians, distinguish its imaginative aspect from meter/rhyme and from belief (tasdiq), and explain the concept of takhyil.

Core Idea
Poetry's power lies in imaginative representation (takhyil), not in truth or falsehood.

In this passage, the author introduces the fourth type of syllogism – poetic syllogisms – by first defining what poetry is for the logician. He carefully separates the formal properties of poetry (meter, rhyme) that belong to other disciplines from the core feature that interests him: imaginative speech (mukhayyil). He then unpacks the concept of takhyil, showing how it causes emotional expansion or contraction in the soul without intellectual deliberation, and explains that this effect is independent of whether the utterance is believed or true. Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating the role of poetry in Islamic sciences, particularly in rhetoric and literary theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the formal definition of poetry
Read the opening sentence: 'poetry is imaginative speech composed of metrical, equal-length, and rhymed utterances.' Note the three conditions. Understand that these are formal, external features.
The logician sets aside these conditions as not his primary concern.
2
Recognize the logician's unique focus
The author explicitly says: 'The logician has no concern with any of these except that it is imaginative speech.' Locate this statement and understand that meter and rhyme are studied by music and prosody experts.
This delimitation is key to understanding the logical perspective.
3
Distinguish takhyil from tasdiq
Read the definition of 'imaginative' as speech that makes the soul expand or contract without reasoning. Contrast this with belief (tasdiq), which is intellectual assent. The author emphasizes that one can believe a statement without being emotionally moved, and a false statement can be imaginative.
This separation is the core conceptual move.
4
Analyze the relationship between truth and imaginative power
The author notes that truthful descriptions should be more moving, yet people are more responsive to imaginative representation. He explains that imitation has wonder, while familiar truth is ignored. A true statement altered for effect can produce both belief and takhyil, but takhyil can also distract from belief.
Consider why novelty and imitation are effective.
5
Understand the two types of submission
The author distinguishes imaginative submission (taslim for wonder and delight in the utterance itself) from belief submission (acceptance that the thing is as stated). Note that poetry is for wonder or civil purposes, while oratory aims at belief.
This clarifies the different goals of poetry and oratory.

Commentary
This passage constitutes the beginning of a new section on poetic syllogisms. The author first defines poetry formally: it is imaginative speech composed of metrical, equal-length, and rhymed utterances. However, he strictly delimits the logician's interest: only the 'imaginative' aspect (مخيل) matters; meter and rhyme are studied by music and prosody specialists. He then defines 'imaginative' (مخيل) as speech that causes the soul to expand or contract without ratiocination, i.e., an emotional rather than intellectual response. A key point is that this effect is independent of belief (تصديق): one may believe a statement without emotional response, and a false statement can still be imaginative. The author analyses the relationship between truth and imaginative power: truthful descriptions should be even more moving, but people are more responsive to imaginative representation because they are jaded by familiar truths and curious about novelty. A true statement altered for effect can produce both belief and imaginative representation, but imaginative representation can also distract from belief. He distinguishes two types of submission: imaginative submission is for wonder and delight in the speech itself; belief submission is for accepting the proposition. Finally, the author notes that poetry serves either pure wonder or civil purposes (deliberative, judicial, exhortative), which it shares with oratory, but oratory aims at belief while poetry aims at imaginative representation. Probable beliefs are finite, but imaginative representations are not—though the text cuts off, implying they are unlimited.

Core Idea
Poetry serves civil functions beyond mere entertainment: advising, judging, and contesting.

In this passage, the author explains that poetry can be for pure wonder or for civil purposes. The civil purposes are categorized into three genera derived from Greek rhetoric: advisory (deliberative), judicial (forensic), and contestory (epideictic). Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing the intended function of any poem, especially in the Islamic poetic tradition where poetry often served political and moral ends.

The Elements of Poetic Artifice
Author classifies poetry's purposes into civil and wonder, and discusses poetic devices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components that make speech poetic: meter, sound, meaning, and the types of artificial devices (simple vs compound, similarity vs contrast).

Core Idea
Poetic effect arises from deliberate artifice in meter, sound, and meaning, through proportions of similarity and contrast.

The passage then enumerates the elements that give speech a poetic quality. These include meter, sound, meaning, and shared aspects. Wonder can occur naturally or through artifice—either simple or compound. Compound artifice in wording involves techniques like tasji' (hemistich matching) and metrical parallelism. Understanding these devices is key to analyzing the construction of Arabic poetry.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the four aspects that make speech poetic
The passage lists: what pertains to the time of speech (meter), what pertains to the heard sound, what pertains to meaning, and what is shared between heard and meaning. Write them down as: الوزن (meter), المسموع (sound), المفهوم (meaning), and المتردد (shared).
2
Distinguish natural wonder from artifice
Wonder can be without artifice (natural eloquence) or through artifice. Artifice can be simple or compound. The passage says: التعجب إما أن يكون من غير حيلة ... وإما أن يكون من حيلة.
Natural wonder is فصيح without كلفة (effort).
3
Identify compound artifice in wording
Compound artifice in wording includes: التسجيع (tasji'), مشاكلة الوزن (metrical parallelism), الترضيع (tarfi'), and الغلب (ghalb). The passage mentions them as examples: مثل التسجيع ومشاكلة الوزن والترضيع والغلب.
These are specific to Arabic prosody.
4
Understand the role of proportion (النسبة)
Artifice arises from proportion between parts. Proportion is either by similarity (مشاكلة) or contrast (مخالفة), and each can be complete or incomplete. This applies to wording or meaning.
This principle underlies many rhetorical figures in balagha.
