Distinguishing Genus from Matter
Author explains the genus-matter distinction with body as example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'body' as genus and 'body' as matter in a definition, and explain why the genus is predicated of the species while matter is not.

Core Idea
Genus is predicated of the species; matter is a non-predicated part.

The author raises a puzzle: how can 'animal' be a cause for 'human' being a body? The resolution lies in recognizing that 'body' has two distinct meanings: one as matter (a part) and one as genus (a predicate). This lesson unpacks that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of 'body'
In the passage, the author distinguishes 'body' taken as matter (madda) and 'body' taken as genus (jins). Notice the different conditions attached to each.
Body as matter: substance with three dimensions only. Body as genus: allows additional constitutive meanings.
2
Understand the condition for matter
When body is taken with the strict condition that no other meaning (like sense or nourishment) enters into it, it is matter. It is a part of the composite and not predicated of it.
Example: if you add 'sensitive' to body-as-matter, you get a composite, but 'body-as-matter' alone is not that composite.
3
Understand the condition for genus
When body is taken without that strict condition, i.e., allowing additional meanings to be part of its essence, it is genus. It is predicated of the composite (e.g., animal is body in this sense).
The genus is a universal predicable of many species.
4
Apply to the animal-human example
The author says: if animal were not body (as genus), it would not be animal. But body as matter is only a part, not the predicate. So the cause relationship works only when 'body' is taken as genus.
This resolves the puzzle: the specific (human) is a cause for the general (body-as-genus) because human includes body-as-genus in its definition.

Distinguishing Differentia from Form
Author explains the form-differentia distinction with sensitive as example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'sensitive' taken as form and 'sensitive' taken as differentia, and apply this distinction to definitional analysis of species and genera.

Core Idea
Form is a non-predicated part; differentia is predicated of the species.

Just as 'body' has two meanings, the author shows that 'sensitive' (hassas) can be understood either as a formal part of animal or as the differentia that distinguishes animal from other things. This lesson explores that parallel distinction.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the parallel between 'body' and 'sensitive'
The author treats 'sensitive' (hassas) the same way as 'body': it can be taken as form (sura) or as differentia (fasl). Identify this parallel in the passage.
The phrase 'وبإبانة الفرق أيضًا بين الحسّاس الذي هو الصورة وبين الحسّاس الذي هو الفصل' signals the analogy.
2
Understand sensitive as form
When 'sensitive' is taken as a part of animal's essence (like matter and form compose a substance), it is a formal part. As form, it is NOT predicated of the composite; rather, it is a constituent.
Analogous to body as matter: form is a part that combines with matter to make the composite.
3
Understand sensitive as differentia
When 'sensitive' is taken as the differentia of animal, it is predicated of the species (e.g., animal is sensitive). As differentia, it distinguishes animal from other genera like plant.
Differentia is a universal that can be said of the species, just like genus.
4
Apply to the human-animal example
The author says that human's form is the rational soul, and animal is taken as matter for human. But when animal is taken as genus, it includes sensitivity as a differentia. So human is animal (genus) and rational (differentia).
This shows how the specific (human) is a cause for the general (sensitive-as-differentia) because human includes sensitivity in its definition.

Existence of Cause in Itself vs For Something
Author distinguishes a cause's existence in itself from its existence for something else.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the existence of a cause in itself and its existence for something, using the examples of accident and substance.

Core Idea
A cause's existence for something may be identical to its own existence or distinct.

This lesson introduces a subtle but critical distinction in logic: the difference between a cause's existence in itself and its existence for something else. The author uses the example of an accident, where these two existences are identical, and contrasts it with a substance like 'body' and 'man', where they are not. Understanding this distinction sharpens our ability to analyze definitions and causal relationships in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of existence
Locate the two phrases: 'وجود العلة في نفسها' (existence of cause in itself) and 'وجودها لذلك الشيء' (existence for that thing). The author asks whether these are one and the same.
2
Examine the case of accident
The text states that for an accident (العرض), its existence in itself and its existence in its subject are identical ('واحد واحد'). Understand that the accident has no independent existence apart from its subject.
Example: whiteness exists only as a property of a body.
3
Contrast with body and man
For 'body' and 'man', the existence of body is not the existence for man. Body is a genus, man is a species. The existence of body does not automatically entail man; you need the differentia (rational animal).
4
Analyze the logical order of predication
The author explains that to connect body to man, you must go through animal. If you skip animal, the result is not man. This shows the necessity of proper genus-species ordering.
This is related to the rules of definition (hadd) and classification.
5
Reflect on the importance
The author emphasizes that these matters are subtle and useful in the sciences. Consider how this distinction applies to definitions and causal reasoning in Islamic disciplines.

Three Conditions of Universal Premises in Demonstration
Author defines universal in demonstration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a universal in demonstration and distinguish them from the ordinary universal in syllogism.

Core Idea
A demonstrative universal must apply to every instance, at all times, and primarily.

In the book of Syllogism, a universal premise is simply a predicate that applies to every member of the subject class. But in demonstration, Aristotle imposes stricter conditions: the universal must hold for every individual, at every time, and primarily. This lesson unpacks those conditions, which are essential for constructing certain proofs in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of 'predicated of all'
Compare the definition in the book of Syllogism (Kitab al-Qiyas) with that in the book of Demonstration (Kitab al-Burhan). Note that in syllogism it is merely 'predicated of every single one', but in demonstration additional conditions are added.
The shift in meaning is crucial: demonstration demands more than mere universal quantification.
2

The Arabic phrase 'في كل واحد وفي كل زمان وأولا' encapsulates these conditions.
3

This condition filters out transient universals.
4
Note the role of 'primarily' and its connection to immediate syllogisms
Without primacy, a syllogism might require an intermediate term, making it non-immediate. Demonstrative knowledge requires immediate premises that are self-evident or derived from primary ones.

Primary Predication and Its Types
Author explains degrees of 'first' predication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify primary predication and distinguish its types (by nature, causality, and by another).

Core Idea
A predicate is primary if it belongs to a subject directly, without an intermediate term.

Not all universal predications are primary. For a predicate to be primary (awwal), it must not be predicated of the subject through a more general term. For example, 'body' is predicated of 'man' via 'animal', so 'body is not primary for man. This lesson clarifies the concept of primary predication, its conditions, and the different types of primacy discussed by the author.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of primary (awwal)
Primary predication means the predicate belongs to the subject directly, not through an intermediate. The author states: 'a thing that belongs to something and does not belong to another except after it belongs to it, then it is for that thing primarily and before being for the other.' This is the core idea.
Primary predication is about the order of predication.
2
Analyze the example 'man is a body'
The author says 'Man is a body' is not primary because 'body' is first predicated of 'animal', then of 'man'. Trace the chain: body → animal → man. The predicate 'body' is more general than 'man' and requires an intermediate.
Contrast with 'man is an animal', where 'animal' is primary for man.
3
Recognize that essential predicates can be primary or non-primary
Genus, differentia, and necessary accident are essential (dhati) predicates. However, they are not always primary. For example, 'body' is a genus of man but is not primary because it is predicated via 'animal'. Only when the predicate is immediate and not through another is it primary.
The author explicitly states: 'وكل واحد من نوعي الذاتي قد يقال أولا وقد يقال غير أول'.
4
Distinguish the three types of primacy
The author mentions three types: by nature (bi al-tab'), by causality (bi al-'illah), and by another (bi al-ghayr). For example, the cause is primary to its effect in existence. Primacy by nature refers to what is prior in essence, like the genus to the species.
These categories are common in Aristotelian metaphysics and are adapted into Islamic philosophy.
5
Note that primary predicates can be more general or equal
A primary predicate may be more general than the subject (e.g., 'animal' for 'man') or coextensive (e.g., 'having angles equal to two right angles' for 'triangle'). Coextensive primary predicates often are essential accidents (al-'arad al-dhati).
The example of the triangle is a classic mathematical example adopted in Islamic philosophy.

Affirmation and Negation: Accident vs. Property and Definition
Rules for affirming/negating accident, property, definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between accident and property/definition in terms of what follows from their particular negation in Islamic logical arguments.

Core Idea
Accident survives partial negation; property and definition do not.

This passage from a classical Islamic logic work classifies the 'places of affirmation and negation' derived from the 'substance of position.' It explains how universal and particular demands apply differently to accident, property, and definition. Mastering this distinction is essential for correctly analyzing arguments about essential versus accidental predicates in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three predicate types
The passage mentions 'accident' (عرض), 'property' (خاصة), and 'definition' (حد). Recognize that these are categories of predicates in Islamic logic, each with different logical behavior.

Contrast the rule for accident
For accident, a particular negation does not negate the accident itself. E.g., 'Zayd is not writing' does not mean writing is not an accident. The author says: 'from its particular negation it does not follow that it is not an accident.'

For property and definition, a particular negation means the individual does not have that property or definition. E.g., 'Zayd is not capable of laughter' negates his humanity if laughter is the property of man. The author: 'from its particular negation it follows that it is not that.'

If an accident were coextensive (applies to all and only the subject), it would become a property. This helps in falsifying accidental claims: if someone claims an accident is coextensive, it is actually not an accident.

The Differentiae of Simple Relational Things
Author explains that the differentia of simple relational things is itself relational, not absolute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the differentia of a simple relational thing must itself be relational and distinguish the differentia from its part.

Core Idea
In the category of relation, the differentia is not a quality but a relation to a quality.

You have learned that definitions consist of genus and differentia. But what happens when the thing being defined is itself a relative? This passage from a classical logic work reveals a subtle point: the differentia of a relational thing cannot be an absolute category like quality or quantity—it must itself be relative. Let's see why this is necessary and how to avoid a common mistake.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objects of analysis
The author examines 'simple relational things' (المضاف البسيط) such as similarity, equality, and fatherhood. These are things whose entire quiddity is relational.

2

This rule prevents category mistakes in definitions.
