This passage defends the syllogism against objections that would undermine its structure. Condition for Circular Demonstration
Author defines circular demonstration and its necessary condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given demonstration is circular by applying the condition that the conclusion and the converse of only one premise are taken without further conversion.

Core Idea
A demonstration is circular only if it requires no further conversion beyond the first.

In Islamic logic, circular demonstration (al-bayan al-dawri) is a subtle concept. The commentator al-Shaykh clarifies a key condition: if the conclusion and the converse of one premise are taken with no need for further conversion, then the demonstration is not circular. This lesson unpacks that condition and shows how it distinguishes genuine circularity from mere appearance.

Guided Path
1
Locate the proposition
The author starts with the categorical proposition 'No B is A.' This is a universal negative statement (Salibah kulliyyah). Identify its subject (B) and predicate (A).
This proposition serves as an example for the discussion.
2
Understand al-Shaykh's condition
Al-Shaykh says: if the condition for circular demonstration were that the conclusion is taken together with the converse of only one premise, and no further conversion occurs, then the demonstration would not be circular. So circularity requires more than just one conversion step.
The converse of a premise is obtained by swapping subject and predicate (e.g., 'No B is A' becomes 'No A is B').
3
Apply the condition to the example
The demonstration in question likely uses the conclusion to prove a premise via conversion. But because only one conversion is needed (the converse of one premise), it does not meet the stricter condition for circularity—namely, that the proof goes around in a circle requiring no new premises.
The author affirms that it is not circular, as it negates the existence of a middle term between cause and effect.
4
Address the objection
A potential objection: if it is not circular, why mention it in the chapter on circles? The author replies that it does not mislead because al-Shaykh's clarification removes any false impression. This shows careful handling of definitions.
The objection is based on a misunderstanding of the scope of the chapter.

The Division of Utterances by Name and Concept
Author quotes Aristotle on the division of utterances by name and concept.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an utterance considered by name (sound) and by concept (meaning), and explain how this division helps identify sources of fallacy in Islamic logical argumentation.

Core Idea
Fallacy can arise from sound, meaning, or both — the division of utterances by name and concept exposes the source.

In this passage, the author quotes Aristotle on a fundamental division: utterances can be considered either as mere sounds (الاسم) or as bearers of meaning (المفهوم). This division is not merely theoretical — it is essential for diagnosing where fallacy occurs. Understanding this distinction will help you identify whether an opponent's error lies in the audible form of words or in the conceptual content, a crucial skill in theological debate (munāẓara).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects of utterances
The author introduces a division: utterances exist according to name (الاسم) and according to concept (المفهوم). The 'name' here refers to the mere sound, not the grammatical noun. The 'concept' is the meaning. These two aspects do not coincide — they are separate analytical perspectives.
2
Understand how deception relates to each aspect
The author says deception (تضليل) occurs according to the concept: error comes from the audible side, correctness from the conceptual. But fallacy is not entirely due to the utterance; the same utterance can be used either to mislead (by exploiting sound) or to convey truth.
3
Analyze the example: 'Being is one'
The example tests whether error is due to sound or meaning. If someone utters it without any specific meaning, only as a sound that could relate to many things, it's a fallacy of name. But if a concept is attached, and the word is ambiguous (mushtarak), the fallacy may be conceptual.
4
Apply the principle to a real Islamic science example
Consider the term 'wājib' in fiqh: it can mean obligatory act, necessary being, or even the attribute of obligation. If a disputant says 'Allah is wājib' without specifying, the fallacy could be in the name (mere sound with multiple possibilities) or in the concept (ambiguity of meaning). This distinction helps clarify the point of disagreement.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a division attributed to Aristotle (the First Teacher) regarding utterances: some are considered by their name (i.e., as mere sounds, al-ism) and some by their concept (al-mafhūm). The author explains that this division concerns how deception (taḍlīl) occurs. Error and mistake arise from the audible aspect, while correctness and perception come from the conceptual aspect. However, fallacy is not always due to the utterance (lafẓ). The same utterance can be directed toward either aspect. It can be used in a meaning other than what the respondent understands, thus causing fallacy, or it can be used in its true meaning without fallacy. Even when used in its true meaning, error can occur due to a mistake in the meaning itself. The author gives an example: the statement 'Being is one'—the error could be in the audible (i.e., misunderstanding the word) or in the concept (i.e., misinterpreting the philosophical claim). If someone utters it without thinking of any specific meaning, thinking only of a word that could relate to many things, that would be a fallacy of the name (sound). But he might also conceive a concept corresponding to the word, and the word may be ambiguous (mushtarak), leading to fallacy. The author then asserts that any utterance can be intended for either aspect, and error can involve both. However, an utterance was originally placed for the concept, not just for the sound; it is used for the sound only for the sake of conveying meaning. If the concept is invalid, then there is only misrepresentation, as with ambiguous terms. This analysis shows that fallacy is more fundamentally about meaning than about mere sound, though sound can be a vehicle for deception.
