Distinguishing Primary from Universal Judgments
Author explains the second cause of error where a judgment on all members is mistaken for universal when it is actually primary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a judgment about a class is universal or primary based on the condition of primariness.

Core Idea
A judgment about each individual is not automatically universal—it may be primary.

Sometimes we think that if a statement applies to every member of a group, it must be a universal judgment about the nature of that group. But the logicians warn that this may be a primary judgment, which applies to each individual separately without capturing a common universal. This distinction is crucial in avoiding errors in reasoning, especially in theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second cause of error
The second cause is the opposite of the first: a judgment is actually primary but is mistaken for universal. Look for the phrase 'الشبهة الثانية' and note that it is described as the reverse of the first cause.
2
Analyze the geometric example
The author gives an example about two lines that are concealed by a third line, making every interior angle on one side right. Notice that this judgment applies to every such pair of lines, but it is not about the universal nature of 'line pair'—it is about each individual pair's property.
The example is from geometry, but the logical structure is what matters.
3
Contrast with a true universal judgment
A true universal judgment would apply to the common nature itself, e.g., 'Every human is rational' – where 'rational' belongs to the nature of humanity. In the example, the property 'having right interior angles' is not part of the definition of a line pair; it is a primary attribute that happens to hold for every instance meeting a condition.
4
Apply the condition of primariness
The key is that the judgment is 'awwalī' (primary) because it attaches to each subject individually, not to a universal. Check if the predicate is part of the subject's essence or if it is an accident. If it is an accident applying to every individual, it may still be primary, not universal.

The Third Cause: Missing Common Name
Author explains how absence of a general term causes false primariness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a universal property is mistakenly thought to be primary to a specific species due to the lack of a common genus name in a particular science.

Core Idea
A missing shared name can hide a universal truth from view.

The third cause of error in logic arises not from a fallacy in reasoning, but from a failure of terminology. When a property truly belongs to a broader genus, but that genus lacks a common name in a particular discipline, the property appears to be specific to each species. This lesson explores that subtle but important epistemological pitfall.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal property and the species
Find the property that applies to multiple species. In the example, the property is 'proportional when transformed' applied to magnitudes and numbers.
2
Recognize the missing genus name
Determine the broader genus that encompasses both species. Here, it is 'quantity' (al-kamm), but this term is not used as a common genus in geometry or arithmetic.
3
Understand the mistake
Because the property is demonstrated separately in each science without a common name, it is thought to be primary to each subject individually, when in fact it is primary to the common genus.
4
Apply to other examples
The author mentions times, statements, and melodies as other phenomena that are essentially or accidentally quantities. Consider how the same pattern might occur there.

Classifying the Subject of Categorical Questions
Author classifies masaa'il into categorical/conditional and lists types of subjects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the subject of any categorical question from an Islamic science into one of the five types described by the author.

Core Idea
Categorical questions are defined by their subject, which can be the science's own subject, its species, or its essential accidents.

This passage from a classical logic text shows how to analyse the structure of questions. Every categorical question has a subject and predicate. The author provides a precise taxonomy for the subject, which is crucial for understanding how questions relate to their scientific context. We will learn to identify each type using examples from natural science.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish categorical from conditional questions
Note the author's initial division: questions are either categorical (hamliyya) or conditional (shartiyya). Focus on categorical ones, which have a subject and predicate without a condition.
2
Identify the first type: subject of the science itself
Look for questions whose subject is exactly the subject of the science they belong to. Example: 'Every body is divisible infinitely' is a question of natural science because body is the subject of natural science.
3
Identify the second type: a species of the subject
When the subject is a specific kind or species of the science's subject, like 'air trapped in water' or 'anger' — these are species of natural beings.
4
Identify the third and fourth types: essential accidents
The subject can be an essential accident ('arad dhati) either of the science's subject (e.g., motion — an accident of body) or of a species of that subject (e.g., time — an accident of motion).
5
Identify the fifth type: essential accident of a type of accident
The most specific case: the subject is an essential accident that belongs to only some instances of a broader accident. Example: slowness is an accident of only some motions (not uniform ones).

When Genus or Differentia Cannot Be Predicates
Author prohibits genus/differentia as predicates when nature is realized.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a predicate cannot be a genus or differentia based on the type of inquiry (inniyya vs limiyya) and the realization of the subject's nature.

Core Idea
If the subject's nature is fully known, seeking its genus or differentia as predicates is impermissible.

In this passage, the author begins by distinguishing between two types of inquiry: seeking the existence (inniyya) of a thing versus seeking its cause (limiyya). When the subject's nature is fully realized and we are only asking whether it exists, the predicate cannot be a genus, differentia, or their combination. This is because essential predicates that enter the definition are self-evident once the thing is realized. Let us unpack this condition and see how it applies in Islamic logic.

Commentary
This passage is a critical epistemological argument. The author is refuting the position that all tasdiq (assent) must be obtained through burhan (demonstration). He starts by showing the absurd consequences of that view: either circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul) would follow, or the knowledge would have to come from something other than demonstration, which contradicts the claim that there is no other way. He then offers the solution: first principles (awwaliyat) are not initially actual but potential, and they are acquired with the help of the senses (external and internal). Not all assents require demonstration; some, like the primary ones, are such that once their terms are conceptualized, the mind immediately affirms the relation without needing a middle term. The senses provide the initial conceptualizations (tasawwurat) that precede assent; without sensory input, the mind cannot begin its operation.
