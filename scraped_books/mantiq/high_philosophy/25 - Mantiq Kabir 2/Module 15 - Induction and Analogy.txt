Induction as a Syllogistic Structure: Term Role Reversal
Auth defines induction and illustrates with a three-term example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three terms in an inductive argument and explain how the minor term becomes the middle term.

Core Idea
Induction reinterprets particulars as the middle term, turning the original middle into the minor.

In this passage, the author presents induction (istiqra') as a valid form of reasoning with a syllogistic structure. He explains how the roles of terms shift: the particular subjects become the middle term, while the original middle term becomes the minor. This structural reversal is key to understanding how induction yields a universal conclusion from particular instances. Using concrete terms (human, horse, mule; little bile; long-lived), the author illustrates the mechanics of this transformation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms in the induction
Find the three Arabic variables: ج (minor), ب (middle), أ (major). In the example, ج = human, horse, mule; ب = little bile; أ = long-lived.
In the text, the author states: 'let the minor term be J (human, horse, mule), the middle term be B (little bile), and the major term be A (long-lived)'.
2
Understand the term role reversal
In an ordinary categorical syllogism, the middle term connects the minor and major. Here, the particulars (the usual minor) become the middle term, and the universal that was to be the middle becomes the minor. The author writes: 'what was a minor term becomes a middle, and what was a middle becomes a minor'.
This reversal is what makes induction distinct: we move from particulars (ج) to a universal conclusion about ب.
3
Formulate the inductive argument as a syllogism
Rewrite the induction as: 'Every ج is ب (humans, horses, mules have little bile) and Every ج is أ (they are long-lived), therefore Every ب is أ (little bile is long-lived).' This shows how the particulars (ج) serve as the middle term in the reasoning.
The original middle term ب (little bile) becomes the subject of the conclusion, and the major أ (long-lived) becomes the predicate.
4
Assess the completeness of the induction
The author's example assumes that all creatures with little bile are included in the enumeration (humans, horses, mules). This is a complete induction (istiqra' tamm). Check if the argument would remain valid if other animals with little bile but short lifespan were omitted.
Complete induction yields certainty; incomplete induction yields probability.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') as a method to establish a universal judgment by observing its particulars. The author explains the structural analogy to a categorical syllogism: the universal predicate or negated term corresponds to the major term, the subjects (particulars) correspond to the minor term, and the universal term being judged corresponds to the middle term. Through induction, the minor term (particulars) becomes the middle term (the universal), and the original middle term becomes the minor. The example given is: minor term (ج): human, horse, mule; middle term (ب): little bile; major term (أ): long-lived. The induction shows that 'little bile' is long-lived by observing that humans, horses, and mules (which have little bile) are long-lived. This is an inductive generalization.

Induction: Definition, Logical Structure, and Certainty
Definition of induction and its reduction to categorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct an inductive argument as a categorical syllogism with multiple middle terms and evaluate whether it yields certainty based on the finiteness of its particulars.

Core Idea
Induction is a categorical syllogism in disguise, swapping the roles of minor and middle terms.

Induction (الاستقراء) is a method of reasoning that moves from particular instances to a universal conclusion. The author here gives its precise definition, shows how it can be reduced to the familiar form of a categorical syllogism (القياس الاقتراني), and explains when it gives certain knowledge. This lesson will walk you through the logical anatomy of induction, so you can recognise it in Islamic scholarly arguments and assess its strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal judgment in the induction
Find the claim that the induction is trying to establish. In the example, it is 'Every long-lived animal has scanty bile.' This is the conclusion (النتيجة).
2
Map the logical terms to syllogistic roles
The author instructs: the predicate of the conclusion (scanty bile) is the major term (الحد الأكبر). The subject of the conclusion (long-lived animal) is the middle term (الحد الأوسط). The specific instances (human, horse, mule) are the minor term (الحد الأصغر). Note how the middle term here is the universal subject of the conclusion.
This is a reversal of the usual syllogism where the middle term is the one connecting the major and minor.
3
Reconstruct the induction as a categorical syllogism
The author gives an implicit syllogism: Major premise: 'Every long-lived animal is like human, horse, or mule' (or '...is one of these particulars'). Minor premise: 'Every human, horse, and mule has scanty bile.' Conclusion: 'Therefore, every long-lived animal has scanty bile.' This shows induction reducible to a categorical syllogism, but with multiple subjects (the particulars) acting as the minor term.
The multiple middle terms (the particulars) are the subjects of the major term, making the middle term effectively a conjunction of cases.
4
Assess certainty: finite vs. infinite particulars
The author states induction yields certainty only if the particulars are finite and fully known. If the instances are infinite or only partially sampled, the conclusion is not certain (ظني). This is a key point for using induction in Islamic proofs.
For example, if you examine every known intoxicant and find it forbidden, you can be certain that all intoxicants are forbidden only if the list is exhaustive.
5
Compare induction and syllogism in epistemic priority
The author notes that syllogism is prior and clearer by nature to the intellect, while induction is prior and clearer by sense. Know the distinction: syllogism is intellectual, induction is empirical.
This explains why induction is often used in sciences based on observation, like medicine or empirical fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed definition of induction (استقراء). The author explains that induction establishes a universal judgment (e.g., all long-lived animals have scanty bile) by observing that the predicate (scanty bile) is found in the particulars of the subject (animals like humans, horses, mules). He maps the logical terms: the predicate (major) is the affirmed universal, the particulars (subjects) are like the minor term, and the subject of the universal judgment is like the middle term. In a syllogism, the minor and middle have fixed roles, but in induction they swap: what would be the minor becomes the middle, and vice versa. The author illustrates with an example: let the minor (ج) be 'human, horse, mule', the middle (ب) be 'scanty bile', and the major (أ) be 'long-lived'. Through induction, we say 'Every animal with scanty bile is human, horse, or mule' and 'Every long-lived animal is like human, horse, mule', then conclude 'Every long-lived animal has scanty bile'. This reduces to a categorical syllogism except with multiple middle terms. The author then clarifies that induction is not really induction? Actually he says it is not induction because it is a judgment about a universal based on its particulars (that is its definition). He notes its canon is not based on truth but on the characteristic mentioned. Induction can be demonstrative (برهاني) if the particulars are finite and known with certainty, otherwise it is not. Induction provides popular compulsion (الإلزام المشهوري) not real compulsion. Finally, syllogism is prior and clearer by intellect, while induction is prior and clearer by sense.

Complete vs Incomplete Induction
Author refines induction definition and divides into complete/incomplete

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete and incomplete induction and explain why only complete induction yields certainty.

Core Idea
Only complete induction — where every particular is examined — yields certain knowledge.

Induction is a powerful reasoning tool, but not all induction is equally reliable. In this passage, the author clarifies the definition of induction and divides it into two types: complete and incomplete. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating the strength of inductive arguments in Islamic sciences, such as usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
Read the Arabic to find how the author defines induction. He gives two formulations: one from a previous discussion (establishing a judgment on a universal because it exists in its particulars, with the implication that all particulars have been covered), and another as a judgment on a universal due to sensing that judgment in its particulars.
Both definitions emphasize that induction moves from particulars to a universal.
2
Find the division into complete and incomplete
The author states that induction 'may be complete (tāmm) and may not be (ghayr tāmm).' Locate that sentence and note the key terms.
The division is based on whether all particulars have been examined.
3
Understand the condition for certainty
Complete induction requires that all particulars be fully covered and that no contrary instance exists. If these conditions are met, the induction yields certain (yaqīni) knowledge. Incomplete induction leaves room for doubt.
The author implies that only complete induction is certain; incomplete induction is merely probable.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider a legal example: 'In all known cases of riba in the Qur'an and Sunnah, the transaction involves excess. Therefore, any transaction with excess is riba.' Determine if this is complete or incomplete induction and whether the conclusion is certain.
This induction is incomplete unless all possible cases of riba have been examined, so the conclusion is probable rather than certain.

Understanding Induction: Definition, Types, and Use in Premises
Author defines induction, distinguishes complete from incomplete, and explains its use in establishing premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete from incomplete induction and explain how induction establishes a universal sought conclusion in an Islamic science context.

Core Idea
Induction builds universal judgments by surveying particulars, but only complete induction yields certainty.

Induction (istiqra') is a fundamental logical tool for establishing universal claims from particulars. In this passage, the author clarifies what induction truly is: not merely correcting premises, but reaching a universal sought conclusion. He then splits it into complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis) and outlines the epistemological conditions for using induction in syllogistic arguments. Let's parse these distinctions carefully.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
Find the two definitions given: first, affirming a judgment about a universal because it exists in its particulars with exhaustion; second, judging a universal by sensing that judgment in its particulars. Note the common element: induction moves from particulars to a universal.
The key is 'exhaustion' (isti'fa') for complete induction.
2
Distinguish complete from incomplete induction
The author says induction may be complete (tamm) or not. Complete induction means all particulars are examined, yielding certainty. Incomplete induction surveys only some particulars, yielding probability.
Complete induction is rare but certain; incomplete is more common but weaker.
3
Understand induction's true purpose
The author stresses induction is not defined by correcting the major or minor premise. Its essence is establishing a universal sought conclusion (matlab kulli). This refutes a common misunderstanding.
Induction can later become a premise, but that's secondary.
4
Apply the evidentness condition
What is clarified by induction must be more evident than, or at least not less evident than, the other sought. If the predicate's existence is known but its quantity is unknown, induction verifies the quantity.
This ensures induction serves as a proper proof, not an obscure one.
5
Recognize the role of induction in syllogistic premises
Induction can clarify the major or minor premise. When used, the middle term for the premise is less than for the conclusion, making the premise more evident. The author introduces 'two inductions' where a common middle term plus an additional middle for the conclusion are involved.
This prepares for using induction in actual syllogisms.

Conditions for Valid Induction in Establishing Premises
Author discusses the requirement that the premise established by induction be more apparent or at least not less apparent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether an induction satisfies the condition of being more apparent (or equally apparent) and whether the division used for complete induction is proper and non-circular.

Core Idea
Induction is valid only if its premise is at least as apparent as the conclusion and the division exhaustively covers the minor term.

Induction (istiqra') is a tool used to establish premises in syllogistic reasoning. But not every induction is valid. The author lays out two critical conditions: the premise must be sufficiently apparent, and if using complete induction via division, the division must be such that the minor term is fully covered without circularity. Understanding these conditions protects us from flawed arguments that seem inductive but are actually invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of induction
Determine whether induction is being used to establish the major or minor premise in a syllogism. The passage states it can be used for either.
The condition applies regardless of which premise is being established.
2
Check the apparentness condition
Compare the apparentness (zuhur) of the premise established by induction to the other sought conclusion. It must be more apparent (azhar) or at least not less apparent. Look for whether the other conclusion is unknown in predicate existence/negation while the premise is known absolutely except for quantity.
If both are equally apparent, they can stand as independent explanations.
3
Examine the division for complete induction
If using complete induction (istiqra' tamm) via division (qisma), ensure the division exhaustively covers the genus and that the minor term falls under one of the parts without overlapping divisions from other classifications. In the valid example, animal is divided into mortal and non-mortal, covering all animals.
The division must be such that the minor term (e.g., rational) does not exit from the division parts.
4
Test for circularity
Verify that the induction does not presuppose the conclusion. The invalid method uses a division that already includes the minor term as one part (e.g., animal into rational and non-rational), leading to a circular argument where the conclusion is assumed in the division.
The author compares this to mistakenly dividing color into body and whiteness, which is erroneous.

Commentary
This passage discusses the conditions under which induction (istiqra') is used to establish premises. He warns that mistakenly treating a classification as if it can be divided in two different ways without proper partitioning (like dividing color into types and then into body and whiteness) is an error. The passage is a technical explanation of how complete induction via proper division can be validly used in demonstration.

Incomplete induction, which does not survey all particulars, lacks the certainty required for demonstration and thus becomes a fallacy. Induction and Its Two Types: Complete and Incomplete
Author defines induction, distinguishes complete from incomplete, and compares it with syllogism.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Only complete induction yields certainty; incomplete induction gives only probability.

Induction is a form of reasoning from particular instances to a general conclusion. However, not all induction is equal. The author distinguishes two types: complete induction, which covers all particulars and reduces to a divided syllogism, and incomplete induction, which covers only some particulars and yields only probable opinion. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in the Islamic sciences, where induction is frequently used to establish universal premises.

Complete vs Incomplete Induction
Author classifies induction and evaluates its certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete from incomplete induction and identify which yields certainty and which yields probability according to classical Islamic logicians.

Core Idea
Complete induction is disguised deduction; incomplete induction is only probable.

In this passage, the author clarifies the epistemic status of induction. Not all inductions are equal—only when all particulars are examined does induction yield certainty, because it reduces to a disjunctive syllogism. Incomplete induction, based on a sample, remains probable. This distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic sciences, especially when universal premises are sought.

Analogy (Tamthil): Definition, Components, and Composite Nature
Author defines analogy as transfer of a ruling from original to branch via a common cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four terms of an analogical argument and explain why its conclusion is not definitive.

Core Idea
Analogy transfers a ruling via a shared cause, but yields only probability, not certainty.

Analogy (tamthil) is a cornerstone of Islamic jurisprudence, used to extend rulings from established cases to new situations. In this passage, the author defines it formally, breaks it into its four logical components, and cautions that its conclusions are probable, not certain. He also reveals that analogy is actually a compound of induction and syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four terms
The author lists four terms: the major (universal predicate), the middle (cause), the minor (original subject), and the similar (branch subject). Locate each in the example: 'this building is temporally originated because it is a body, and likewise the sky because it is a body'.
The major is 'temporally originated', the middle is 'body', the minor is 'this building', and the similar is 'the sky'.
2
Understand the role of the cause ('illa)
The cause is the common attribute that justifies transferring the ruling. It must be coextensive (mutarida) and may be reciprocating (mun'akisa). The author distinguishes two types of causes.
A coextensive and reciprocating cause ensures that the ruling follows the cause both in presence and absence.
3
Recognize why the conclusion is not definitive
The author gives two reasons: the ruling might be tied to something specific to the original, or there might be an impediment in the branch. Thus, the analogy does not produce certainty (qat').
This is a key limitation distinguishing tamthil from categorical syllogism.
4
See how analogy combines induction and syllogism
The first part (establishing the ruling in the original due to the common description) is reasoning from particular to general (induction). The second part (applying it to the branch) is reasoning from general to particular (syllogism).
Thus, analogy is a compound argument.

As for tamthil, it is a judgment on a particular due to its existence in another particular; it is agreed upon due to a similarity between them, as mentioned in the discussion of agreed-upon syllogisms. It is defined by different definitions among the jurists; the best is to say: transferring the uniform ruling from the original to the branch by a unified cause in both. (ت) The third method is the 'argument from prevention' (mu'araqa or dalil al-man'). It states that if the attribute were not the cause, the effect would not occur due to some preventer, but since the effect did occur, the attribute must be the cause. This is also used by dialecticians. 

Finally, the author makes a powerful critique: even if these methods prove that being a body is the cause of temporal origination in the building, they do not prove that corporeality as such (i.e., the essence of being a body) is the cause. In fact, he says it is impossible for corporeality to be a cause of origination because bodies are permanent, while origination is a contingent event—thus the cause cannot be the essence of corporeality itself. This nuance guards against overgeneralization from a single instance.

This is among what the Shaykh indicated in the Shifa'. And the truth about it is that experience insofar as it is experience does not produce certainty. How so? For experience often errs when the accidental is taken in place of the essential in it; so it produces opinion, not certainty, even if the opinion is predominant opinion.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion comparing induction (istiqra') and experience (tajriba) in terms of their ability to produce certainty. The author begins by clarifying that when a predicate's relation to its subject is not self-evident, it requires a proof. If the proof is a syllogism, it must involve a cause that applies to the universal, not just the particulars. Induction, whether complete or incomplete, does not yield true certainty—only the syllogism does. Then the author introduces experience as distinct from induction, using the medical example of scammony (saqamuniyya) being an evacuant of bile. Through repeated observation, the mind judges that this is not coincidental but a natural property, even without knowing the cause. An objector questions how certainty can arise without knowledge of the cause. The author replies that the frequency of the occurrence eliminates coincidence, as the coincidental is not constant or predominant. Another objection asks why experience yields knowledge while incomplete induction only yields opinion. The author responds that incomplete induction does not produce even predominant opinion reliably, while experience does under conditions. A further objection presents the scenario of observing only black people in Sudan—suggesting that repetition alone could lead to false universal beliefs. The author answers that experience works through a concealed syllogism: the observed repetition is tied to a conditional universal, provided no hindrance exists. This is what Ibn Sina indicated in the Shifa'. Ultimately, the author states that experience in itself does not yield absolute certainty; it often errs when accidental properties are mistaken for essential ones, and so it produces only predominant opinion, not certainty.

Three Ways the Senses Aid the Intellect
Author lists three additional ways sense aids the intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between induction, experience, and intuition as distinct aids to the intellect in acquiring knowledge beyond first principles.

Core Idea
Induction, experience, and intuition are distinct tools for accessing knowledge beyond first principles.

We have already seen how sense provides the intellect with first principles. Now the author explains three additional ways the senses assist: through induction (al-istiqra'), experience (al-tajriba), and intuition (al-hads). Each operates differently and applies to specific domains of knowledge, not to the primary intuitions themselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three aids
Find in the Arabic passage the three terms: الاستقراء (induction), التجربة (experience), الحدس (intuition). Note that they are introduced as ways the intellect seeks aid from sense.
2
Understand the scope of each aid
Induction moves from particular instances to general rules. Experience (tajriba) yields certainty about causal relationships through repeated observation, but does not apply to first principles. Intuition (hads) grasps the cause of something known directly, without syllogistic reasoning.

Compare with first principles
First principles (awwaliyat) are known directly through the senses and intellect without these methods. The three aids are for knowledge that goes beyond immediate self-evident truths.

Faculties of the Theoretical Intellect

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The theoretical intellect, when habituated and supported by auxiliary powers, becomes the instrument for all knowledge.
