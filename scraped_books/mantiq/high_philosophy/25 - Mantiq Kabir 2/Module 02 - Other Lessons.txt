The Definition and Classification of Arguments
Author defines argument and classifies it into syllogism, induction, and analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term 'argument' (al-hujja) and classify any given argument as qiyas, istiqra', or tamthil based on the generality relationship between evidence and conclusion.

Core Idea
Arguments are classified by the logical relationship of generality between evidence and conclusion.

In this passage, the author lays down the foundation of logic by defining the argument (al-hujja) and then sorting all arguments into three fundamental types: syllogism, induction, and analogy. This classification is not just a theoretical exercise; it is the key to understanding how proofs work in Islamic theology, jurisprudence, and grammar. By mastering these categories, you will be able to identify the logical structure of arguments you encounter in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its genus
Find the term being defined (الحجة) and its broader category (قول). Notice that the author defines argument as a type of speech/expression.
The genus is 'speech' (قول), not 'proposition' – it is broader.
2
Extract the differentia
What makes an argument different from other speech? It is 'composed of statements from which assent is produced' (مؤلف من أقوال يحصل منه التصديق). This differentia captures both certain (yaqini) and probable (zanni) arguments.
Assent (tasdiq) is the result; the argument itself is the instrument.
3
Understand the classification criterion
The author classifies arguments based on the generality of the evidence (what you infer from) relative to the conclusion (what you infer to). If evidence is more general, it's qiyas; if conclusion is more general, it's istiqra'; if neither, it's tamthil.
The criterion is relation of 'umum (generality) and khusus (specificity).
4
Apply the criterion to Islamic examples
Take a known kalam argument: 'Everything originated has a cause; the world is originated; therefore the world has a cause.' Here the major premise is more general than the conclusion, so it's qiyas. Contrast with induction: observing that many intoxicants are forbidden and concluding 'all intoxicants are forbidden' is istiqra'. Analogy: arguing that nabidh is forbidden because it shares the 'illa of intoxication with khamr is tamthil.
Be careful: in usul al-fiqh, qiyas often means analogical reasoning, but here it means deductive syllogism.
5
Resolve the universal-to-universal objection
The author anticipates an objection: what about inference from universal to universal? He replies that one universal is subsumed under the other, so it reduces to one of the three types. For example, 'All A are B' and 'All B are C' yields 'All A are C' – here evidence 'All A are B' is more general than conclusion? Actually need to check: the example is inference from 'All B are C' and 'All A are B' to 'All A are C'. The major premise 'All B are C' is universal, minor 'All A are B' is universal, conclusion 'All A are C' is universal. But one of the premises may be more general: 'All B are C' has subject B, which is a genus relative to A? The author's point is that it still fits because either the evidence is more general or the conclusion is more general or neither.
This shows the classification is exhaustive.

Distinguishing Part and Whole in Logic
Author argues that part and whole have distinct essential and accidental properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the logical properties of part (بعض) and whole (كل) and correctly apply the rules of implication between them in Islamic syllogistic and juristic contexts.

Core Idea
Part is universal, whole is particular — never conflate their properties.

In this passage, the author draws a sharp logical line between part (بعض) and whole (كل). Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding fallacies when moving from a part to the whole or vice versa. The key insight: a part, as a part, has universal reference (كلي), while the whole is a specific collection (جزئي). This lesson will guide you through the logical properties and their practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two logical subjects
The passage compares 'part' (بعض) and 'whole' (كل). Recognize that each has its own essence (ذات) and accident (عرض). The author asserts they are necessarily distinct.
2
Observe the universal/particular assignment
Note that the part is described as universal (كلي) because 'some of something' can refer to any instance. The whole is particular (جزئي) because it refers to a specific collection. This is a key logical difference.
The author adds that this holds for determinate things like 'every human', not for absolute things (otherwise the whole could also be universal).
3
Analyze the illustrative examples
The author gives the example of an Abyssinian: if some part is white, we cannot say the whole is white. Similarly for negation: 'some of the thing is not all of it' is valid, but 'all of the thing is not all of it' is nonsense. Understand how these examples demonstrate the distinct properties.
4
Apply the implication rules
The passage explains that if a part implies something, then what implies that part also implies that thing. Conversely, if a part is contrary to something, then what implies that part is contrary to that thing. This is a general rule for logical dependencies between part and whole.
These rules are foundational for syllogistic reasoning involving quantifiers.

Commentary
This passage is a logical analysis of the relationship between part (بعض) and whole (كل). The author argues that part and whole are distinct in their essence (ذات) and accident (عرض), and therefore they have different logical properties and implications. He begins by stating that the part is not thewhole, so it has attributes (لوازم) that cannot belong to the whole, and vice versa. This argument is foundational for understanding how quantifiers (some/all) affect logical inference in syllogistic logic.

The Objection to Deriving Categorical from Conditional
Author raises an objection: the categorical conclusion may not be actual in reality, so the derivation is doubtful.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the objection to deriving categorical conclusions from conditional premises and explain why the objector places the claim in the realm of denial.

Core Idea
A conditional form does not guarantee that its categorical conclusion actually holds in reality.

This lesson examines a critical objection raised against deriving a categorical conclusion from conditional premises. The objector argues that such a conclusion may not be actualized in the external world, placing the entire derivation in doubt. Understanding this objection is essential for distinguishing between logical validity and real-world truth in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Find the claim being objected to: that a categorical conclusion can be derived from conditional premises (e.g., from 'if A then B' to 'B is true').
The author says some predecessors held this view.
2
Extract the objector's stance
The objector says the claim is 'in the realm of denial' (ḥayyiz al-man‘), meaning it is not accepted as valid.
This is a strong rejection, not a minor correction.
3
Understand the reason: actualization depends on unknown factors
The categorical conclusion's truth in reality requires additional conditions (like the antecedent being true) that are not guaranteed by the conditional form alone. Since these conditions are unknown, one cannot be certain the conclusion holds.
The text says: 'إذ النتيجة الحملية لا تكون متحققة في نفس الأمر إلا وأن يتحقق من الأمور ما يحققه'.
4
Consider the preemptive counter-rebuttal
The objector anticipates the response that 'there are proofs that prove actuality' and dismisses it: even if actuality is established, it does not prove the result is a logical consequence of the syllogism.
This sets up a deeper discussion about when a result truly follows from its premises.

Aspects of Knowledge and Ignorance
Author discusses how the known is known from one aspect and unknown from another, distinguishing knowledge types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between different aspects of knowledge and ignorance (particular/universal, potential/actual) and explain how knowledge and opinion can coexist from different cognitive perspectives.

Core Idea
Knowledge and ignorance are not absolute; the same thing can be known from one aspect and unknown from another.

This passage from an advanced logic text explores the layered nature of human knowledge. It addresses a common misconception: that ignorance is simply the absence of knowledge. The author shows that ignorance can be contrary to knowledge, and that knowledge and opinion can coexist in the same mind from different aspects. Understanding this helps us avoid conflating different cognitive states in our reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The author states that the object of inquiry is known from one aspect and unknown from another. This establishes that knowledge and ignorance are not mutually exclusive; they can coexist in different respects.
2
Examine the runaway slave example
A runaway slave is known by a specific mark (the middle term) in terms of identity (who he is), but unknown in terms of location (where he is). This illustrates how the same object can be partially known and partially unknown.
The 'mark' is the middle term in a syllogism.
3
Distinguish types of knowledge
The author distinguishes knowledge as particular vs universal, and potential vs actual. This taxonomy explains why one can have universal knowledge but be ignorant of a particular instance, or have knowledge in potential but not in actuality.
Universal knowledge does not guarantee particular knowledge.
4
Analyze the courage example
One may know that something (like courage) is good in general, but mistakenly think it is evil in a specific instance. This shows how knowledge and opinion can coexist: universal knowledge (courage is good) and particular opinion (this act of courage is evil) are held simultaneously.
This is not a contradiction because they concern different aspects.
5
Analyze the void example
The mind may assent via syllogism that there is no void outside the world, yet the estimative faculty (wahm) may depict a void, leading to opinion that there is a void. When the syllogism is recollected, knowledge and opinion clash from one aspect but not from another, as they cannot both be consciously entertained at once.
This demonstrates the coexistence of knowledge and opinion from different cognitive faculties.

Why Experimentation Does Not Yield Certainty
Author distinguishes tajriba from istiqra' and argues tajriba does not yield certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between experimentation (tajriba) and induction (istiqra') and explain why tajriba alone produces only predominant opinion, not certainty.

Core Idea
Experimentation yields conditional knowledge, not certainty, because it depends on a suppressed syllogism and is vulnerable to mistaking accidents for essences.

We often think that repeated observation of a pattern gives us certain knowledge. But the author challenges this assumption. He distinguishes experimentation (tajriba) from induction (istiqra') and shows that even repeated observation cannot yield certainty unless it is backed by a syllogism linking the observed property to the essence of the thing. This lesson unpacks that argument and examines a striking hypothetical: if you only ever see black people, would that prove all humans are black?

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's core question
The author opens by asking why experimentation produces knowledge differently from induction. Note the key difference: induction (complete or incomplete) yields certainty only when complete, while experimentation is not simply a matter of completeness.
This sets up the epistemological puzzle.
2
Analyze the hypothetical scenario
Consider the imaginary case where only black people are observed in Sudan. If repeated observation alone necessitated belief, one would have to believe all humans are black, which is false. The author argues this shows that tajriba does not give certainty by frequency alone.
This reveals the problem: raw repetition can lead to error.
3
Extract the author's solution
The author says tajriba produces knowledge not by frequency but by an implicit syllogism (qiyas) that connects the observed property to the thing's nature. The syllogism might be: 'This thing has nature X; nature X necessitates property Y; therefore this thing has Y.'
This suppressed syllogism is what elevates tajriba above mere enumeration.
4
Understand conditional universality
The author says tajriba gives 'kulli bi-shart' — a universal conditional on the absence of hindrances (mani'). That is, the regularity holds always unless something prevents it. This is not absolute certainty but a conditional claim.
The condition 'unless there is a hindrance' acknowledges exceptions.
5
Recognize the error of mistaking accident for essence
The author warns that tajriba often errs when accidental properties ('arad) are mistaken for essential (dhati). In the Sudan example, blackness is accidental to humanity, so tajriba based on it gives only opinion, not certainty.
This is a crucial epistemic caution: correlation is not essence.

Testing the Primacy of a Judgment by Removing Meanings
Author describes the method for testing whether a judgment is primary by eliminating meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of removing meanings to determine whether a judgment is primary in an Islamic science context.

Core Idea
A judgment is primary to a subject only if it remains when all accidental attributes are removed.

In Islamic sciences, distinguishing primary (essential) judgments from accidental ones is crucial for sound reasoning. For example, in Usul al-Fiqh, identifying the effective cause ('illah) of a ruling requires knowing which attributes are essential. This lesson presents a logical method to test primacy by systematically removing attributes and observing whether the judgment persists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the judgment
Take the proposition in question. For example, from fiqh: 'Every intoxicating beverage is haram.' The subject is 'intoxicating beverage' and the judgment is 'haram'.
2
List all meanings (attributes) attached to the subject
Enumerate the attributes associated with the subject. For 'intoxicating beverage', attributes could be: 'liquid', 'made from grapes', 'fermented', 'causes intoxication'.
3
Remove all attributes except one and check the judgment
Eliminate all attributes except one, e.g., keep only 'causes intoxication'. If the judgment 'haram' still holds, then the judgment is tied to that attribute. For example, remove 'made from grapes' and 'fermented' but keep 'causes intoxication'; the ruling remains. But if you remove 'causes intoxication', the ruling disappears.
4
Vary the kept attribute while keeping others fixed
Replace the kept attribute with alternatives. For 'causes intoxication', vary the substance (grape wine, date wine, etc.). If the judgment remains constant, it strengthens the conclusion that the judgment is primary for that attribute (here, intoxication).
5
Conclude whether the judgment is primary for the attribute or the essence
If the judgment persists despite removal and variation of all attributes except one, and fails when that attribute is removed, then the judgment is primary for that attribute. In our example, the judgment 'haram' is primary for the attribute 'causes intoxication', not for being liquid or from grapes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish three ways one science can be subordinate to another and identify which science provides the 'why' (lim) and which provides the 'that' (inn).

Core Idea
The higher science gives the cause; the lower science gives the fact.

This passage from Aristotle's 'First Teaching' (Posterior Analytics) is expanded to classify the hierarchical relationships between sciences. The author explains that the higher science provides causal explanations (lim), while the lower science only knows the facts (inn). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for organizing knowledge in Islamic sciences and correctly attributing explanatory authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two levels of science
The passage distinguishes between the higher science (al-ilm al-a'la) and the lower science (al-ilm al-asfal). The higher science gives the cause (lim), the lower science gives the fact (inn).
2

One whole science is under another, like optics under astronomy, mechanics under geometry, and harmonics under arithmetic. They share a subject but differ in perspective.

A part of one science is under another, e.g., the halo and rainbow in natural science are studied under optics then geometry. The naturalist gives a vague cause, the optician gives the specified cause.

A specific question from one science has its cause in another, e.g., the slow healing of circular wounds is known by the physician (inn), but the cause is given by the geometer (lim) because the circle has the widest perimeter.

Definition of Courage (Shaja'a)
Author defines courage as a disposition enabling calm and readiness despite danger.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in a definition of a moral virtue from an Islamic text.

Core Idea
Courage is not just bravery but a disposition shaped by belief and positive self-estimation.

In this passage, the author defines courage (shaja'a) as a stable disposition (malaka) that enables a person to be at ease about safety and ready for adversity. This definition highlights the cognitive underpinnings of courage—belief in the nearness of salvation and confidence in one's ability to avert evil. Let's analyze the logical structure of this definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate the definiendum: 'shaja'a' (courage). Note that the author starts with 'wa amma al-shaja'a'.
2
Extract the genus (jins)
The genus is 'malaka' (disposition). Courage is placed in the category of a stable character trait, not a fleeting emotion.
In Islamic ethics, 'malaka' is a technical term for a firmly rooted habit.
3
Extract the differentia (fasl)
The differentia is twofold: (1) being at ease (husn al-rakha') regarding safety, and (2) being prepared (musta'iddan) for the occurrence of misfortune. Additionally, the author mentions cognitive causes: belief that safety is near and good opinion of one's ability to avert evil.
The differentia specifies what distinguishes courage from other dispositions like justice or generosity.
4
Assess definition completeness
Check if the definition includes all instances (every courageous person has these traits) and excludes non-instances (the reckless or cowardly do not). The cognitive elements ensure that courage is not merely physical bravery but a reasoned trait.
The author's definition appears comprehensive for a moral virtue.

Commentary
This passage defines courage (shaja'a) as a disposition (malaka) that allows a person to be at ease regarding safety and ready for adversity. The courageous person perceives harm as absent or distant, because he believes the means of salvation are near and he has a positive estimate of his ability to prevent anticipated evil. This definition emphasizes cognitive factors (belief and opinion) rather than just bravery.
