The Functional Role of Improvements in Logical Discourse
Author classifies improvements and explains their persuasive function.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of improvements (taḥsīnāt) and explain how each contributes to persuasion in rhetorical and logical contexts.

Core Idea
Improvements in logical speech are not decoration; they serve persuasion through imagination, melody, and speaker's demeanor.

After mastering syllogisms and their premises, the author turns to the finishing touches: improvements (taḥsīnāt) that enhance delivery and persuasion. This passage shows that these improvements are not mere ornament—they tap into human psychology through wording, arrangement, and the speaker's state. Understanding this helps us see why preachers, orators, and even jurists in debate pay attention to tone and expression.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of improvements
Read the passage and list the three areas that improvements pertain to: wording (including melody), arrangement (tartīb), and the speaker's states (hay'āt al-mutakallimīn).
The passage explicitly says: ba‘ḍuhā muta‘alliq bi-l-lafẓ, wa ba‘ḍuhā bi-l-tartīb, wa ba‘ḍuhā bi-hay’āt al-mutakallimīn.
2
Understand the role of imagination (takhayyul)
The passage states that imagination (takhayyul) aids persuasion and belief, and is used in both poetry and rhetoric. Note that this is not just artistic—it has a logical function: it helps the audience accept the conclusion.
Imagination is linked to 'al-akhdh bi-l-wujuh' (taking with expressions), suggesting a method of making abstract ideas relatable.
3
Analyze the correspondence between melody and emotion
The passage gives examples: anger emits one type of melody (heavy/loud), fear another (light/soft). The author hints that these correspondences are natural and can be used strategically to evoke the desired emotional state in the audience.
The word 'naghama' (melody) covers tone, pitch, and rhythm of speech.
4
Connect to Islamic oratory and debate
Consider how a khatīb (preacher) varies his tone to inspire hope or fear, or how a mutakallim (theologian) uses calm, measured speech in a debate. This shows that the science of logic includes not only formal validity but also effective delivery.

Author enumerates improvements related to utterance and tone

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tone and speaker's expression can evoke emotions and aid persuasion, beyond mere meaning.

In this passage, the author expands on the types of improvements that enhance speech. While logic focuses on valid inference, rhetoric and poetry use vocal qualities and speaker demeanor to influence the listener. This lesson will help you distinguish these rhetorical tools from the requirements of demonstration.

Recognize the role of imagination (تخييل) in rhetoric
The passage states that تخييل (imagination) can assist persuasion (الإقناع) and assent (التصديق), showing its function beyond poetry.
This is a shift from typical logical contexts where تخييل is only poetic.
4
Contrast with demonstrative sciences
Note the author's later point: in التعاليم, wording is trivial; only clarity matters. This highlights the unique role of these improvements in rhetoric and poetry.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the types of improvements, particularly those related to the form of the utterance and the speaker. It begins by listing: (1) improvements related to the form of the utterance and its tone (naghama), (2) improvements related to the form of the speaker that stir up imaginations, called 'taking by faces' (al-akhdh bi-l-wujūh), which is suitable for both poetry and rhetoric because imagination aids persuasion and assent. (3) Then it discusses tones specifically: heaviness (taṭḥīl), specification (taḥdīd), and mediation (tawsīṭ). It explains how tones correspond to passions like anger, fear, etc., and suggests that heaviness and loudness may follow a certain state, while seriousness and fear follow weakness of the soul. The purpose of using these tones is either to imagine oneself having that passion or to imitate a quality. Next, it discusses 'wazanāt' (meters), which are non-letteral tone states that can be used for various effects such as indication of accidents, providing respite, magnifying speech, or changing meanings (e.g., turning a declarative into an interrogative). The passage then notes that differences in tones during imitation come from three aspects: sharpness (ḥidda), heaviness (thiqal), and 'nathrāt' (perhaps meaning pitch changes or pauses). These are not exclusive to poetry but also found in rhetoric. The author stresses the great benefit of improving utterances in oratory and poetry. In contrast, the demonstrative sciences (ta'ālīm) require only clarity; wording is trivial, and assent does not differ with different expressions. However, in rhetoric and poetry, the same meaning changes according to wording, so one must strive to find expressions that make it probable (maẓnūn) in rhetoric and imagined (mutakhayyal) in poetry. The passage ends abruptly with 'fa-inna al-lafẓa al-jazl' (for the eloquent utterance...), indicating continuation.

The Homogeneity Condition for Simile and Metaphor
Rule for homogeneity in simile and metaphor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to assess whether a simile or metaphor uses homogeneous terms correctly by applying the condition of mutual similarity.

Core Idea
Similes and metaphors require homogeneity between the two compared objects to be effective.

When applying a simile or metaphor to two different things, the author insists that the two things must be homogeneous (mutajānisa). This ensures the figurative language fits both objects naturally. The example of the rose and Mars illustrates how to choose appropriate metaphorical expressions for each.

Guided Path
1
Extract the rule
The passage states: 'فيجب في التشبيه والاستعارة إذا استعملا في شيئين معا أن يكونا متجانسين' — when simile and metaphor are used for two things together, they must be homogeneous.
Homogeneous here means sharing a relevant characteristic or category.
2
Understand 'homogeneous'
The two things should belong to a similar class or have a property that allows the same metaphorical expression to apply appropriately to both. They need not be identical, but the metaphor must be fitting for each.
Example: rose and Mars are both celestial? No, rose is earthly, but both can be described metaphorically with expressions related to 'doubt' — 'doubt of caressing' for rose and 'doubt of experience' for Mars.
3
Analyze the rose and Mars example
The rose is associated with softness and beauty, so a metaphor about 'caressing' fits; Mars is a planet often linked to influence and experience, so 'doubt of experience' fits. Both share the concept of 'doubt' but with different attributes.
The homogeneity lies in the conceptual parallel, not literal similarity.
4
Apply the rule
If you were to compare a lion and a sheep metaphorically, you would need expressions that fit both. Since lion is predatory and sheep is prey, a common metaphor like 'king of the jungle' works for lion but not sheep. So they are not homogeneous in that regard.
One must choose metaphors that highlight a shared characteristic, e.g., both could be described as 'animals of the wild' but not with the same specific metaphor.

Eloquence and Correctness in Word Choice
Author provides guidelines for choosing correct words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given Arabic word is faṣīḥ (eloquent) and detect any laḥn (solecism) based on the author's criteria.

Core Idea
Eloquence demands correctness: a single solecism can corrupt the entire speech.

The author lays down a foundational principle: words must be eloquent, correct, and free from grammatical errors. This lesson unpacks what constitutes eloquence (faṣāḥa) and how to spot mistakes that violate it.

Guided Path
1
Define faṣāḥa
What does the author say about eloquence? According to the passage, words must be 'fasiyha sahiha' (eloquent and correct) with no laḥn.
2
Identify laḥn
Solecism (laḥn) is any grammatical error in the language. The author does not give examples here, but general Arabic grammar rules apply.
3
Check specificity
The author says words not used for simile or metaphor should be specific (khāṣṣa), not shared (mushtaraka).
4
Apply to an example
Given a sentence, test if its words are faṣīḥ. For instance, 'قال العالمُ العلمُ نافعٌ' has a grammatical error (العلمُ should be accusative). That constitutes laḥn.

ثم للغات في هذا أحكام فليس يمكن أن يقال فيها بقول كلي بل ينبغي أن تكون الألفاظ التي لا يراد فيها التشبيه والاستعارة ألفاظا خاصة غير مشتركة. ويجب أن يراعى فيها أمر التذكير والتأنيث والتثنية والجمع ؛ وبالجملة ما يلزم في حسن الدلالة. واعلم أن الكلام ربَّما ينفع إيجازه حين يراد به الإفهام فيجب أن ترد الحدود إلى الألفاظ المفردة. وربَّما ينفع بسطه للانتهاب حين يراد توكيد الإقناع والتهويل فيجب أن تبدل الألفاظ المفردة بالأقاويل. وقد يبدل الاسم بالقول إذا كان الصريح تشنيعا فيقال مثلا : عورة النساء ولمس النساء». وربَّما يبدل الاسم بالصفة المفردة فيقال: «بدل الاسم الصريح للجماع الوطء». وربَّما تركب الصفة في حال التشبيه والاستعارة. والشعراء يجتنبون استعمال اللفظ الموضوع ويحرصون على الاستعارة حرصا شديدًا حتى إذا وجدوا لفظا فيه يعتبر ما أخذوا ذلك اللفظ وتركوا غيره لكن يلزمهم أن يقولوا في كل شيء ما يناسبه، وأن لا يقصروا في الأمور العالية، وأن لا يتعرضوا للألفاظ السفسافة التي لا يستعملها إلا الغاغة.
Chapter: On the selection of what tests the word and the selection of what beautifies it. Words must be eloquent, correct, with no solecism according to the language. Then connectors must be fully observed. Connectors are the letters whose utterance requires them to return again and the connection of speech to them, such as when it is said: 'As for me, I have said such-and-such'; one should complete the speech by saying: 'And as for you, you did not do such-and-such', for stopping is a deficiency in the obligatory part of speech, even if there is no large gap between the two connectors with an intervening filler, and something of the link between them must be observed. And one must observe its right of precedence and delay, for we must say 'since it was such, it was such'; so 'since' has the right to be placed first, and we say 'it was such because it was such', so 'because' has the right to be delayed. For prioritizing the premise before the claim is permissible. A connector should not be inserted between a connector and its response except in certain places, like their saying: 'As for me, no; due to desire for your praise I left my people and sought you, but as for so-and-so, they follow them'; their saying 'due to' is inserted between 'as for' and 'as for', and it intervened and was not ugly. Sometimes it is not interposed but placed at the end, like their saying: 'As for me, I come to you, and as for so-and-so, he did not come to you.' Then the cause is introduced at the end, saying 'because of such-and-such'; and this is only good where the first connector has a strong relation to the second. Then languages have rulings on this; it is not possible to speak about them with a universal statement, but rather words that are not intended for simile and metaphor should be specific, not shared. One must observe in them the matter of masculine/feminine, dual, plural; in short, what is necessary for good indication. Know that speech sometimes benefits from conciseness when understanding is intended, so definitions must be reduced to single words. Sometimes its expansion benefits for plunder when strengthening persuasion and intimidation is intended, so single words must be replaced by phrases. The noun may be replaced by a phrase if the explicit is offensive, for example: 'the private parts of women' and 'touching women'. Sometimes the noun is replaced by a single adjective, like: 'instead of the explicit noun for intercourse, coitus.' Sometimes the adjective is compounded in the case of simile and metaphor. Poets avoid using the literal word and are extremely keen on metaphor, so that when they find a word in which there is something to consider, they take that word and leave others, but they must say in everything what is appropriate, not fall short in high matters, and not engage with sophistical words used only by the vulgar.

Commentary
This passage is a doctrinal exposition on the selection of proper words and connectors in rhetorical composition. It is a 'fann' (chapter) dedicated to the rules that test and beautify speech. The author begins by stating the fundamental requirement: words must be eloquent (faṣīḥa) and correct, free from grammatical errors (laḥn). Then he turns to connectors (ribāṭāt), defining them as particles that necessitate a return to something previously mentioned, like 'ammā' (as for) which requires a corresponding 'ammā' later. He gives examples and warns against stopping prematurely, which would be deficient. He emphasizes the order: when using 'li-mā' (since), it should come first, and 'li-annahu' (because) should come later. He allows insertion of a connector between a connector and its response only in specific cases, like the intervening 'li-ajli' (due to) between two 'ammā's. He then discusses the need for precise language: words not used for simile or metaphor should be specific (khāṣṣa) and not shared (mushtaraka). Gender, dual, and plural agreement must be observed for good indication (ḥusn al-dalāla). He contrasts conciseness (ījāz) and expansion (basṭ): conciseness aids understanding, so definitions should use single words; expansion aids persuasion and intimidation, so single words can be replaced by phrases. He gives examples of replacing explicit nouns with euphemistic phrases to avoid offensiveness (e.g., 'touching women' for intercourse). Finally, he describes poets' practice: they avoid literal words and prefer metaphor, but they must still choose suitable expressions for each context, avoid base words, and shun sophistical (safsāfa) words used only by the vulgar (ghāgha). The passage provides a comprehensive guide to rhetorical word choice and sentence structure.

Connectors (Ribāṭāt): Rules and Placement
Author explains connectors and their proper use.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules for using connectors like 'ammā' and 'li-mā' in Arabic discourse, ensuring proper order and completeness.

Core Idea
Connectors are the scaffolding of speech; they must be completed without gaps and placed in correct order.

The author defines connectors (ribāṭāt) as particles that require a return to them later. This lesson teaches the rules for their use: they must be completed, not interrupted, and their order (taqdīm/ta'khīr) must follow logical precedence.

Guided Path
1
Identify connectors in a sentence
Find particles like 'ammā' and 'li-mā' that demand a corresponding part later.
2
Check if the connector is completed
Ensure that a second part follows (e.g., 'ammā...fa-...'). Incomplete connectors are deficient.
3
Determine the correct order
For 'li-mā' (since), it must come before the consequence. For 'li-annahu' (because), it comes after.
4
Observe exceptions for insertion
A connector may be inserted between a connector and its response only in certain cases, like 'li-ajli' between two 'ammā's.

Conciseness vs Expansion and Euphemisms
Author contrasts conciseness and expansion, and discusses euphemisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to choose between conciseness (ījāz) and expansion (basṭ) in speech and replace explicit nouns with appropriate phrases for persuasion or politeness.

Core Idea
Speech wears two dresses: conciseness for clarity, expansion for persuasion; euphemism safeguards propriety.

The author discusses two modes of expression: ījāz (conciseness) when understanding is the goal, and basṭ (expansion) when persuasion and intimidation are needed. He also gives examples of replacing explicit nouns with euphemistic phrases to avoid offensiveness. This lesson teaches you how to apply these choices in Islamic discourse.

Guided Path
1
Determine the purpose of speech
Is the goal mere understanding (tafhīm) or persuasion (iqnā')? The mode depends on this.
2
Apply conciseness: reduce definitions to single words
If understanding is key, use single words for definitions (as in hadd).
3
Apply expansion: replace single words with phrases
If persuasion is needed, expand by using phrases instead of single terms.
4
Identify when to replace noun with phrase for politeness
When the explicit term is offensive (e.g., 'intercourse'), use a euphemistic phrase like 'touching women'.

Placement of Causal Connectors in Arabic Rhetoric

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Connectors have fixed positions: 'li-mā' precedes the claim, 'li-anna' follows it.

In Islamic rhetorical theory, the placement of causal connectors is not arbitrary—it follows logical and stylistic norms. This lesson unpacks the rules given by the author for using 'li-mā' and 'li-anna' in statements. Understanding these rules helps you compose and critique arguments in Islamic scholarly writing, where causal claims are central.

Guided Path
1

The passage introduces two causal connectors: 'li-mā' (since) and 'li-anna' (because). Note their distinct roles in linking cause and effect clauses.
2
Apply the precedence rule
The author states: 'li-mā' must be placed before the claim (e.g., 'Since it was such, it was such'), while 'li-anna' must be placed after the claim (e.g., 'It was such because it is such'). Internalise this as the default order.

Examine the exception for intervening connectors
Normally, no connector should come between a connector and its answer. However, the author allows an exception: when 'li-ajli' (because of) is inserted between two 'ammā' (as for) clauses, as in the example: 'As for me, no; because of desire for your praise, I left my people...'
This exception is accepted only when the first connector is strongly related to the second.
4
Recognise the alternative placement at the end
The cause can also be placed at the sentence's end, e.g., 'As for me, I come to you; as for so-and-so, he did not come to you. Then the cause is given: for such a reason.' This works when the first connector strongly links to the second.

Core Idea

After discussing connected speech, the author defines metaphor (isti'ara) precisely. Commentary
This passage defines the components of rhetorical speech and explains their functions. The author states that rhetorical speech involves two core elements: the subject matter and the proof that demonstrates it. Every rhetorical speech has three parts: the introduction (sadr), the narrative (iqtisas), and the conclusion (khatima). In consultation, there is no dispute; instead it indicates a slight benefit, and if it becomes like a dispute it turns into a complaint. The introduction is particularly useful in consultations to keep focus on the aim. The conclusion is exemplified by a phrase deferring to the audience's opinion. The author distinguishes between addressing listeners (who receive the introduction, narrative, and conclusion) and addressing opponents (who are met directly with proofs). Commentary
This passage is an expository treatment of the structural components of rhetorical speech and related considerations. The author begins by stating that the opening (al-sadr) and conclusion (al-khatima) are especially useful in consultations (mushawarat) so that the speaker keeps his goal in view. A virtuous one among the two virtuous ones. And the famous participates in praise as you know. And with the least verbal expression, praise becomes advice: as when you say, 'He is virtuous because he does such-and-such,' that is praise; if you say, 'Do such-and-such and you will be virtuous,' that is advice. When you know this, we say: Rhetoric is not built upon questioning about premises, although questioning has useful occasions in it, such as asking about a thing which if you answer with 'yes' entails for the opponent something in particular of what he says, and if you answer with 'no' that is or entails something bad, or the opposite. Rhetorical questions may also be from insignificant matters; the truth is to withhold judgment on them. Withholding judgment suggests possibility in order to escape obligation. Then speech in question and answer with the noble is not like that with the ignoble; what is suitable for the noble is allusion, which is enabling the meaning without explicit statement. As for speech about topics, kinds, moral virtues and vices, magnification and diminution, and other such things, it is part of what has been mentioned earlier in the book. And God knows best what is correct, and to Him is the return and the refuge. Commentary
This passage is a concluding section from a larger work on logic (Mantiq Kabir), specifically addressing rhetoric (khaṭāba). It begins by clarifying the relationship between praise (madḥ) and advice (mashūra), noting that with minimal verbal change, praise becomes advice. For example, saying 'He is virtuous because he does such-and-such' is praise, while 'Do such-and-such and you will be virtuous' is advice. The author then emphasizes that rhetoric is not fundamentally built on questioning about premises, distinguishing it from dialectic. However, questioning can be useful in rhetorical contexts, such as asking a question that, if answered affirmatively, forces the opponent into a specific position, or if answered negatively, leads to something objectionable. Some rhetorical questions are trivial, and the best course is to withhold judgment, which suggests possibility and helps avoid being obligated. The author then contrasts interactions with noble versus ignoble people: with the noble, allusion (ta'rīḍ) is appropriate—conveying meaning without explicit statement. Finally, the author notes that topics like moral virtues and vices, magnification and diminution, etc., have been covered earlier in the book. The passage ends with a colophon identifying the scribe and date.

The Role of Questioning and Etiquette in Rhetoric
Author discusses questioning in rhetoric and etiquette with nobles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of questioning in rhetoric and apply the etiquette of allusion with noble interlocutors.

Core Idea
Rhetoric is not built on questioning, but strategic questioning can obligate an opponent.

While rhetoric primarily persuades through advice and allusion, questioning can play a strategic role. The author notes that rhetoric is not fundamentally based on questioning premises, but questioning has useful occasions, such as forcing an opponent into a concesssion. The passage also teaches that with noble interlocutors, allusion is more appropriate than explicit speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim about rhetoric and questioning
Find the statement: 'إن الخطابة ليس بناؤها على السؤال عن المقدمات' – rhetoric is not built on questioning about premises.
This distinguishes rhetoric from dialectic (jadal).
2
Recognize the exceptions
Look for 'وإن كان للسؤال فيها مواضع نافعة' – questioning has useful occasions. Note the example: asking a yes/no question that forces a concession or reveals something unpleasant.
The question is designed to obligate (ilzām) the opponent.
3
Understand the tactic of withholding judgment
The author says some rhetorical questions are insignificant (مهملات) and the truth is to withhold judgment (التوقف). This suggests possibility and helps avoid being forced into a position.
Withholding judgment is a protective tactic.
4
Contrast etiquette with noble vs ignoble interlocutors
Find the phrase: 'الكلام في السؤال والجواب مع الكريم غير ما يكون مع اللئيم'. With the noble, use allusion (تعريض) – enabling the meaning without explicit statement.
Allusion respects the dignity of the noble interlocutor.
