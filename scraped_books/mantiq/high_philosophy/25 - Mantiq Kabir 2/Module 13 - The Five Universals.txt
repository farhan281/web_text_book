Universals as True Predicates and Their Finite Hierarchy
Author defines universals as true predicates and explains their finite hierarchy near, far, and intermediate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a universal as near, intermediate, or far relative to individuals based on the hierarchy described in the passage.

Core Idea
Universals form a finite chain from the most general down to individuals—there is no infinite regress.

In this passage, the author distinguishes true predication from accidental attribution and establishes that universals are the only true predicates. He then explains that these universals are arranged in a finite hierarchy: some are near to individuals, some far, and some intermediate. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for definition and demonstration in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish true predication from accidental attribution
The author says particulars like 'this whiteness' truly exist in individuals but are not true predicates when attributed. Identify the difference between a universal like 'human' (true predicate) and an accidental attribute like 'white' (not a true predicate).
2
Identify the three levels of universals
The passage states universals are near, far, or intermediate. For example, 'animal' is nearer to humans than 'substance'. Find the criteria for each level.
3
Trace the finite chain from individual to highest genus
The author asserts finiteness in both directions. Start from an individual like Zayd and ascend: human → animal → body → substance. Each step is a universal, and there is a highest universal beyond which nothing is predicable.
4
Explain why the chain must be finite
The passage argues that if it were infinite, it would be enclosed between two finites (the individual and the highest genus), which is impossible. Understand this reductio argument.

Commentary
This passage classifies the types of predication. The author distinguishes between particulars that are truly existent in individuals (like whiteness inhering in a body) and those that are not true predications (like 'white' attributed to a thing). Then he focuses on universals, which are the true predicates. Universals form a hierarchy: some are near to individuals (like 'animal' relative to 'human'), some far (like 'substance'), and some intermediate (like 'body'). He states that this hierarchy is finite in both directions: descending from the most general universal (e.g., 'substance') down to the individual (which cannot be predicated of anything else), and ascending from the individual up to the highest genus. Because it is finite, the intermediate universals are limited; otherwise, if infinite, they would be enclosed between two finites, which is impossible.

Key Terms
كليات (kulliyāt): Universals: terms that can be truly predicated of many things, forming a finite hierarchy from near to far to individuals. [Note: In earlier texts like Isaghuji, kulliyāt are the five universals (genus, species, etc.); here they are discussed as a continuum of predication.]
شخصيات (shakhṣiyāt): Particulars: individual entities that exist in reality but cannot be truly predicated of others except metaphorically.
حمل حقيقي (ḥaml ḥaqīqī): True predication: when the predicate inheres in the subject essentially, not accidentally.

How 'Intended Zayd' Can Be a Universal Term
Author explains how 'imagined Zayd' can be universal and the identity problem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a term that appears particular can function as a universal in a syllogism, and recognize the quantification condition for the major premise.

Core Idea
A term like 'intended Zayd' can be universal when considered by species, not numerical identity.

In this passage, the author tackles a subtle point: how a term that seems to refer to a specific individual (like 'intended Zayd') can actually be universal in logical analysis. He shows that the imagined or intended Zayd, when considered as a type (species), can apply to many instances over time. This has direct implications for the syllogism: if the middle term is universal, the major premise must be universal too, otherwise we get confusion. Let's break down the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Locate 'imagined Zayd' (mutawahham Zayd) and 'intended Zayd' (Zayd al-mu'anni) in the Arabic passage. Note how these are introduced as examples.
2
Understand the claim about universality
The author argues that 'intended Zayd' can be said of many individuals if considered by species (bi al-naw'), not by numerical identity. This makes it a universal term.
The distinction between numerical and specific unity is key.
3
Examine the candle analogy
Read the analogy of the bird formed from a candle, then melted and restored. Even though the matter is the same, the bird is not numerically identical; but the type remains. This illustrates how something can be universal by species.
The analogy helps to grasp the concept of identity over change.
4
Apply to syllogistic quantification
The author concludes that if the middle term is not particular (i.e., is universal by species), then the major premise must be universally quantified for it to be true. An indefinite major premise may seem true but fails under universal quantification.
This guards against the fallacy of assuming an indefinite premise supports a universal conclusion.

Distinguishing Matter from Genus in Definitions
Author explains the difference between matter and genus in definitional composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given universal term functions as matter or genus in a definition by applying the conditions of predication and part-whole analysis.

Core Idea
The same term can be either a part of a definition (matter) or a predicable genus, depending on whether it excludes or allows additional meanings.

In logic, the same word can play different roles in a definition. The author of this passage clarifies a crucial distinction: a universal like 'body' can be taken as 'matter' (a component of a composite, not predicated of it) or as 'genus' (a predicate that applies to many things). Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in constructing definitions of species like man. In this lesson, you will analyse the conditions that turn a universal into matter or genus, using the examples provided in the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two senses of 'body'
In the passage, the author first defines body as matter: a substance with three dimensions only, excluding any added meanings. Then he defines body as genus: the same three-dimensional substance but without excluding any added determinations. Notice the difference lies in the condition: one excludes, the other does not.
This distinction is not merely semantic; it affects predication.
2
Determine predication status
Body as matter is a part of the composite (e.g., the material component of man) and is not predicated of the whole. Body as genus is predicated of all composites that have those three dimensions, such as man, animal, etc. Check the author's statement: 'it is not predicated of that composite' versus 'it is predicated of every composite'.
3
Apply the same analysis to 'sensitive'
The author extends the analysis to 'sensitive' (ḥassās). Taken without additional conditions, it is a part (form/matter) and not predicated. Taken as allowing other conditions (like rationality), it becomes a differentia and is predicated. Identify which conditions turn it into a differentia.
Sensitive as differentia is predicated of the species (e.g., animal).
4
Use the criteria for matter, genus, or species
The author provides a test: if the universal can accommodate differentiae, it is a genus. If it is completed and sealed by some differentiae so that nothing else can enter, it is matter. If it includes all determinations, it is a species. Practise applying this test to the examples in the text.
This test works for composite essences; simple essences require mental consideration.
5
Verify with the priority argument
The author concludes by stating that corporeality is prior to animality in man only as matter, not as genus. This shows the practical import of the distinction for ordering definitional components. Check why this is the case based on the earlier criteria.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of essential accident (al-‘arad al-dhati) and distinguishes it from absolute or foreign accidents. Defending Definition: Resolving the Infinite Regress Objection
Author defends the definition of definition against infinite regress by invoking potentiality and geometric analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the potential/actual distinction and geometric analogy resolve the charge that defining 'definition' leads to infinite regress.

Core Idea
Defining definition is not a vicious infinite regress; it is a potential, not actual, series.

A common objection against defining 'definition' is that it leads to an infinite regress of definitions. The author responds by distinguishing potential from actual, and by analogy with geometric shapes. Let's analyze this defense.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector says: defining definition requires a definition of that definition, ad infinitum, which is absurd.
The passage records: 'you have defined by this definition. And if definition had a definition, then there would have to be a definition of the definition of definition ad infinitum; and that is absurd.'
2
Identify the author's distinction between potential and actual
The author responds that the definition of the absolute definition is not the definition of the definition of definition actually; it is only potentially a definition of that.
This is the key logical move: potential does not entail actual.
3
Understand the geometric analogy
Just as we can define an infinite number of shapes (triangle, square, etc.) by a single rule (counting sides) potentially, we can define definitions potentially without actual infinite regress.
The analogy shows that the potential to define does not require actual infinite steps.
4
Evaluate the success of the defense
The author denies the objection by showing that potentiality suffices and no actual infinite regress follows.
The passage concludes: 'in summary, their statement that it leads to infinity... is within the scope of denial'.

Core Idea
Property is convertible with the subject but does not reveal its essence.

Among the five universals, property (khassa) serves as a distinctive sign of the subject—it applies to all and only that subject, yet it never discloses the quiddity. This lesson unpacks the definition from the passage and teaches you how to recognize a property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate 'خاصة' (khassa) in the Arabic passage. It is defined right after genus. Read the definition carefully.
2
Extract the two conditions of property
The definition states two conditions: (1) convertibility (yan'akis) with the subject, meaning the predicate applies to all instances of the subject and only those instances; (2) it does not indicate the subject's quiddity (māhiyya).
3
Understand convertibility
Convertibility means that if the subject is present, the predicate is necessarily present, and if the predicate is present, the subject is necessarily present. For example, 'capable of laughter' is convertible with 'human' because every human is capable of laughter and only humans are capable of laughter (in reality).
This is different from a definition, which also indicates the essence.
4
Contrast property with definition
A definition (hadd) indicates the essence, while property only accompanies the essence without revealing it. If you mistakenly treat a property as a definition, you will miss the true nature of the thing.
For instance, 'capable of laughter' is not the definition of human; it is a property.

Core Idea
Every scientific question aims at one of four predicate types: genus, property, definition, or accident.

In Islamic logic, when we ask about a thing—like 'Is the soul a substance?' or 'Is the world created?'—we are seeking a specific kind of predicate. The author of this passage identifies four types of predicates that form the backbone of scientific inquiry. Understanding these helps you recognize the logical structure behind every question in the Islamic sciences.

Identify the question's subject and predicate

2

Predicative Identity and the Four Demands
Author explains the four types of predicative identity and the four corresponding types of inquiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given question about a thing into one of the four demands (genus, definition, property, necessary accident) and identify the corresponding type of predicative identity.

Core Idea
Every scientific inquiry reduces to one of four questions about a thing.

When we say two things are identical, it can be in four ways: as the same individual, as the same kind, by a unique property, or by an accidental attribute. The logician maps these onto four basic questions: what is its genus? what is its definition? what is its unique property? what necessary accidents does it have? In this passage, the author illustrates this using examples from philosophy, such as the soul and the world, showing how each type of identity corresponds to a distinct demand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of predicative identity
The passage lists four ways something can be said to be identical (huwa huwa): by number (individual), by definition (essential kind), by property (unique non-essential characteristic), and by accident (contingent attribute). The example of scattered waters shows identity by species but not by number.
These correspond to the five universals: genus (by definition), differentia (by definition), property, and accident.
2
Connect each identity type to a demand
The author says that every predicate sought belongs to one of these four. The first demand (the question 'Is it?') is the same for all; the second demand ('What is it?') differs. Asking 'Is the soul a substance?' is a demand for genus; 'Is the soul a self-moving number?' is a demand for definition; 'Is man receptive of knowledge?' is a demand for property; 'Is the world originated?' is a demand for necessary accident.
The necessary accident is a type of accident that always accompanies the thing, like 'being originated' for the world.
3
Recognize the universals in each category
The author states that in each of the ten categories, there is something that is a genus, something that is a differentia, something that is a property, and something that is an accident. This shows the pervasiveness of these four demands.
4
Distinguish absolute affirmation from specific inquiry
When asking about a thing 'Is it?' we seek absolute existence. When asking 'Is it a genus or a property?' we need to consult the specific place for that universal.
This helps refine the method of investigation.

Three Types of Predicates: Equivocal, Univocal, and Analogical
Classification of predicates into three types with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a predicate is used equivocally, univocally, or analogically in a given Islamic text.

Core Idea
How a word is predicated reveals whether its meaning is identical, different, or different with a core connection.

This lesson introduces the three fundamental ways a predicate can relate to a subject: equivocation (ishtirak), univocation (tawatu'), and analogy (tashkik). These distinctions are crucial for understanding definitions and arguments in Islamic logic, as they clarify whether a term is used in the same sense across different contexts. The passage from the matn gives examples to help you practice classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its multiple uses

2
Check if the meaning is identical across uses
Ask: Is the meaning exactly the same in every occurrence? If yes, it is univocal (tawatu'). If no, proceed.
3

If the meanings are completely different and have no common property, it is equivocal (ishtirak). If there is a common core but with priority and posteriority, it is analogical (tashkik).
For example, 'existent' has a core meaning (being) but applies primarily to substance and secondarily to accident.

Accident and Category Mismatch Tests for Genus
First two tests for invalid genus

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A true genus must not match the definition of an accident and must share the same category as its species.

This lesson introduces the first two criteria for rejecting an invalid genus. The author warns that mere commonality is insufficient; deeper logical checks are required. Avoiding Common Mistakes in Identifying Logical Properties
Author lists several common mistakes in using topoi to determine a property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and avoid five common errors in determining a logical property (خاصة) in Islamic scientific texts.

Core Idea
Not every attribute that accompanies a thing is a property—it must be coextensive and correctly conditioned.

In classical Islamic logic, the property (خاصة) is one of the five universals and plays a key role in definition and argumentation. However, many mistakes arise when applying the topoi for property. Guided Path

Recognize the importance of property among the five universals
Property (خاصة) is a universal predicate coextensive with its subject but not part of its essence. It must apply to every instance of the subject and only to that subject.

2
Understand the mistake of making the subject a property of its own property
The specific subject (مخصوص) cannot be a property of its properties, because one subject can have many distinct properties. If it were, it would have to be property for each, which is impossible.
The passage cites Aristotle's First Teaching on this.
3
Distinguish property from differentia
Differentia (فصل) is part of the essence and definition, while property is not. Mistaking differentia for property leads to circular definitions.
Differentia is narrower than property.
4
Check for concomitance and proper conditioning
A property must always accompany its subject, but if it exists before the subject or is not always present (like 'walking in the market' for Zaid), it cannot be a property. Also, some properties hold only 'by nature' (بالطبع), e.g., man is two-legged by nature, but not every actual man has two legs.

5

Conditions for a Genuine Property (Khāṣṣa)
Author defines necessary conditions for a property to be genuine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether an attribute qualifies as a genuine property (khāṣṣa) by checking the two conditions: essential attachment to the species and coextensiveness.

Core Idea
A genuine property must be both essential to the species and coextensive with it.

Among the five universals, the property (khāṣṣa) is a crucial tool for defining and distinguishing things. However, not every constant attribute that clings to a thing deserves the title of 'property'. In this passage, the author sets two strict conditions: the attribute must attach to the thing from the aspect of its species (i.e., it must be essential, not accidental), and it must be coextensive (applying to all and only that species). We will unpack these conditions and see them applied to concrete examples.

Guided Path
1

The passage defines what qualifies as a true 'property' (خاصة). Note that property is one of the five universals.
2
Extract the first condition
The property must be 'consequent to the thing from the aspect of its species' (لاحق للشيء من جهة نوعه). This means the attribute belongs to the essence of the species, not accidentally.
3
Extract the second condition
The property must be coextensive (مساوية): it applies to every member of the species and only to members of that species. The phrase 'from the aspect by which it becomes coextensive' indicates this.
4
Apply to the negative examples
The text gives two counterexamples: (1) Rest attaching to human from the aspect of body, not human—fails condition 1. (2) A consequent that attaches but is not coextensive—fails condition 2.
5
Summarize the criteria
A genuine property must satisfy both conditions. If either is missing, it is not a true property (خاصة حقيقية).

Core Idea
A property looks like a definition but does not capture the essence.

Is 'animal is composed of body and soul' a definition or just a property? At first glance it seems definitional, but the author argues it is only a property.
