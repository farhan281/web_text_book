Distinguishing Pure Homonymy from Analogical Terms
Author classifies terms and introduces analogical type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a term is purely homonymous (mushtarak), univocal (mutawatia'), or analogical (mushkik) using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Not all ambiguities are equal: some terms share a resemblance that deceives the mind.

The author stresses the importance of distinguishing mushtarak from mutawatia', then expands the classification by introducing a subtle third type: mushkik (analogical). These terms look univocal but are not, and they are a common source of fallacy in theological and legal debates. By learning their features, you will avoid being misled by apparent univocity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
From the passage, list the three categories: mushtarak (pure homonymy), mutawatia' (univocal), and mushkik (analogical, close to univocal). Understand their definitions.
Mushtarak indicates distinct meanings with no common core; mutawatia' applies with the same meaning; mushkik indicates with a resemblance but different essences.
2
Extract the defining features
For each category, extract the defining feature from the passage. Pure mushtarak has no shared meaning (e.g., opposites). Univocal has identical meaning. Analogical has a resemblance but essences differ fundamentally.
The author says mushkik is 'qarib al-shabah min al-mutawatia'' (close in resemblance to the univocal).
3
Apply classification to examples
Classify the given examples: 'jalal', 'nahil', 'jawn', 'qur'' are pure mushtarak (opposites). 'nur' and 'hayy' are mushkik. Explain why 'nur' is mushkik: both physical light and intellect share the notion of 'illumination' but essences differ. Compare with mutawatia' terms like 'insan' (human) applied to individuals.
The author compares the commonality in 'nur' to the commonality of 'body' for sky and human—corporeality is essential to both but the essences differ.
4
Recognize the warning
The author warns that such terms are 'yanabi' al-aghalit' (sources of fallacies). Be cautious when a term seems to apply uniformly across contexts (e.g., to God and creation) but may actually be analogical, leading to equivocation fallacies.
This is especially important in kalam and usul al-fiqh debates about attributes.

Distinguishing Synonyms from Heteronyms in Islamic Logic
Author warns against mistaking different terms for synonyms due to contextual variations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a set of terms that refer to the same object are true synonyms (mutaradifa) or different terms (mutabayina) by checking for added attributes or contextual variations.

Core Idea
Multiple names for one thing are not necessarily synonyms – each name carries a distinct aspect.

In Islamic sciences, precise terminology is crucial. A common logical error is assuming that different names for the same object are interchangeable. This lesson will teach you to detect when terms are truly synonymous and when they are not, using examples from sword names and technical terms in logic and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the set of terms referring to the same object
Find the common object that all terms point to. For example, 'sayf', 'muhannad', and 'sarim' all refer to a sword.
2
Check each term for an added attribute or relation
Determine if any term includes an extra quality beyond the core meaning. 'Muhannad' adds 'from India', 'sarim' adds 'sharp'. If such an addition exists, the term is not a pure synonym.
If no added attribute is found, the terms may be true synonyms (e.g., 'al-layth' and 'al-asad' if they have identical meanings).
3
Apply the principle to technical terminology in Islamic sciences
Consider terms like 'da'wa', 'qadiyya', 'matlub', 'natija', 'muqaddima'. All refer to a proposition, but each is used in a specific context (e.g., 'da'wa' when challenged, 'qadiyya' as a judgment). They are not synonyms because the aspect differs.
This principle is common in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and mantiq.
4
Test a new example from another Islamic science
Choose a set of terms from fiqh, such as 'fard' and 'wajib'. Research whether they are synonyms or different based on added conditions (e.g., in Hanafi law they are synonyms; in Shafi'i law they differ).
This step solidifies the skill.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An improvised homonym deceives you into thinking two meanings are linked when they are not.

This lesson examines a specific subtype of mushtarak: the murtajal (improvised). Although the original passage is incomplete, the core idea is that some homonyms arise spontaneously without one meaning being prior. Such terms can lead to fallacious reasoning if they are falsely assumed to share a common origin. We will explore how to detect this fallacy, especially in Islamic theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basic definition of mushtarak
A mushtarak is a name that signifies more than one meaning. In the passage, the author defines it as 'the name that expresses two designata'.
2
Distinguish murtajal from other types
The term 'murtajal' indicates that the homonym is improvised, not derived or borrowed from one meaning to another. The passage suggests there is 'no original coinage' (wa la al-wad).

Identify the potential fallacy
If someone argues that because one meaning of a word applies, another meaning also applies, they may commit an equivocation fallacy. Recognizing that the word is an improvised homonym reveals the lack of logical connection.
In Islamic sciences, this can appear when a term like 'wajib' is used in different contexts without clarifying the meaning.

Not all names for the same object are synonyms; each may carry a distinct connotation.

In logic, it is crucial to distinguish between terms that are truly interchangeable and those that only appear to be so. This lesson examines a common fallacy: confusing terms that differ in connotation (mutabāyina) with synonyms (mutarādifa). Understanding this distinction sharpens your analytical precision in Islamic sciences.

1
Identify the terms in question
Find the set of terms presented as potentially synonymous. For example, 'sword', 'Indian sword', 'sharp sword'.
2
Check for added connotations
For each term, isolate any additional meaning beyond the core referent. 'Indian sword' adds origin; 'sharp sword' adds attribute.
3

4

Confusing Essential Attribute with Necessary Concomitant
Author identifies confusion of essential attribute with necessary concomitant as a source of fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an essential attribute (dhātī) and a necessary concomitant (lāzim) and identify fallacies resulting from confusing them.

Core Idea
Not every inseparable property is essential.

This brief passage from the Mahakk al-Nazar alerts us to a frequent and subtle source of error in reasoning: mistaking a property that always accompanies a thing for its essence. The author notes that both essential attributes and necessary concomitants are inseparable from their subject, making them easy to confuse. However, only the essential attribute constitutes the quiddity of the thing; the necessary concomitant is merely a consequence of that essence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding fallacies in definition and argument.

Guided Path
1
Define dhātī and lāzim
Begin by clarifying the two terms: al-dhātī is the essential attribute that makes a thing what it is (part of its quiddity). Al-lāzim is a necessary concomitant that always accompanies the thing but is not part of its definition.
The passage emphasizes that both share the impossibility of separation (istihālat al-mufāraqa).
2
Find the common factor
Identify that both dhātī and lāzim are inseparable from the subject. For example, 'rationality' is inseparable from a human (essential), while 'being capable of laughter' is also inseparable but not essential.
This shared feature leads to confusion.
3
Spot the real difference
Understand the key difference: dhātī is part of the definition (taqwīm al-māhiyya), while lāzim is not. Lāzim follows from the essence but does not constitute it.
Example: 'having a shadow' is a necessary concomitant of a dense body, but it is not part of the definition of a body.
4
Recognize the fallacy
The fallacy occurs when someone argues that because a property always accompanies a thing, it must be essential. This is a non sequitur. For instance, concluding that 'being created' is essential to a temporally originated thing (it is a concomitant, not essential to its quiddity as 'possible existence').
The passage calls this confusion 'min muthārāt al-aghālīth al-kathīra' – a major source of fallacies.

The Fallacy of Time-Place Equivocation
Author defines the sixth fallacy of time-place equivocation with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of time-place equivocation in theological and everyday arguments and explain why apparently contradictory statements can both be true.

Core Idea
A statement can be true at one time and false at another — don't mistake temporal difference for contradiction.

In heated debates, we often hear two opposing claims and assume one must be false. But sometimes both are true—they just refer to different times or places. This lesson teaches you to spot this subtle fallacy, a tool essential for fair-minded evaluation of arguments in Islamic theology and law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Subject and Predicate
Find the common subject (e.g., 'the world', 'the boy') and the predicate that seems to be both affirmed and denied (e.g., 'originated', 'grows teeth'). Note that the subject is identical.
Only when the subject is the same can this fallacy arise.
2
Check for Temporal or Spatial Specification
Look for implicit or explicit time or place references. In the first example, 'at the beginning of its existence' vs. 'before or after'. In the second, 'the first year' vs. 'the year after'.
The fallacy often hides in unspecified times or places.
3
Determine Whether Both Statements Can Be True Together
If the predicate applies to the subject at one time/place but not at another, both statements can be true when each is correctly understood with its own time/place.
No logical contradiction exists if the time/place differs.
4
Apply the Rule in a New Example
Take an Islamic example: 'The Qur'an is created' and 'The Qur'an is not created'. These statements can both be true if one refers to the physical book (created) and the other to the divine attribute (uncreated). Time-place equivocation is a key tool in clarifying such debates.
Always specify the aspect (time/place/context) to avoid false contradiction.

The Fallacy of Generalization in Logical Inference
Example of the fallacy of generalization explained and critiqued

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of generalization (khaṭāʾ al-taʿmīm) in an argument by checking whether the cause (ʿilla) is more general than both the subject and the judgment.

Core Idea
A cause that is too broad cannot prove a specific conclusion.

This passage exposes a common logical mistake: assuming that a shared feature is sufficient to prove a specific claim. The author unpacks a simple example to show how a single missing premise and an overly general cause can lead to an invalid inference. We will learn to spot this error in arguments from fiqh and kalam, where such fallacies are unfortunately frequent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the reason given
The claim is 'This woman is pregnant.' The reason given is 'because she has a large belly.' Note that the reason is presented as a single statement, but it conceals a missing premise.
2
Reconstruct the missing premise
The author supplies the missing premise: 'Every pregnant woman has a large belly.' With this, the argument becomes a categorical syllogism: All pregnant women are large-bellied; this woman is large-bellied; therefore this woman is pregnant.
This syllogism is invalid in its form (second figure, double affirmative). The error is deeper than mere form.
3
Analyze the scope of the cause
The cause (ʿilla) is 'large belly'. Compare its scope to the subject ('this woman') and the judgment ('pregnant'). The cause is more general than the subject (other women can have large bellies) and more general than the judgment (large belly can result from dropsy, obesity, bloating, etc.).
A valid cause must not be broader than the subject or the judgment; it must uniquely or at least specifically connect them.
4
Conclude why the inference fails
Because the cause is too general, it does not necessitate the conclusion. Even with the added premise, the argument is invalid. The error is the fallacy of generalization.
This fallacy is prevalent in complex legal and theological debates.

Refuting the Fallacy of Deniers of Reasoning
Author refutes the mughalata by introducing a third epistemic state.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of deniers of reasoning and refute it by distinguishing between potential and actual knowledge and between conceptualization and assent.

Core Idea
Deniers of reasoning wrongly assume knowledge is either absent or present; the missing middle is potential knowledge through conceptualization.

A classic objection against the validity of reasoning (nazar) argues that seeking knowledge is futile: either you already know it or you don't, and if you don't, you cannot recognize it when found. This passage presents that objection and then refutes it by exposing an incomplete division. The resolution introduces a crucial third epistemic state—potential knowledge (bil-quwwa) and conceptual awareness (tasawwur)—which allows us to recognize the sought after reasoning. By understanding this, you can defend the rational pursuit of knowledge in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the fallacy
The denier presents a dilemma: the sought knowledge is either known or unknown. If known, seeking is redundant; if unknown, it is unrecognizable. This is a false dichotomy.
Note that the fallacy hinges on an incomplete division (qisma ghayr hasira).
2
Uncover the third epistemic state
The author points out a third possibility: knowledge 'in one respect and ignorance in another'. Specifically, potential knowledge (bil-quwwa) and conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) of the parts, without actual assent (tasdiq).
Potential knowledge is the capacity to accept the conclusion; conceptual knowledge is understanding the individual terms.
3
Analyze the runaway slave analogy
The analogy illustrates: you know the slave's essence (tasawwur) but not his location. You understand 'house' and 'being' conceptually, and have potential knowledge that he is in the house, but actual knowledge comes only through sight.
This shows how seeking is possible: you recognize the sought when found because of prior conceptual awareness.
4
Formulate the refutation
The refutation asserts that the objection's division is not exhaustive; there is a middle state. Therefore, reasoning can lead from conceptual potential to actual knowledge without circularity or infinite regress.
This preserves the validity of nazar in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Refuting a Fallacy about Universal Knowledge of Even Numbers
Author critiques a fallacy concerning universal knowledge of even numbers and refutes a flawed response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the confusion between epistemological and ontological conditions in a universal proposition and refute the fallacy that restricts universal truth to known instances.

Core Idea
A universal proposition is true in itself, not dependent on our knowledge of its instances.

This passage presents a classic sophistical dilemma: if you claim to know that every two is even, can you also know whether an unseen pair in a closed hand is even? The standard answer wrongly restricts the universal to known cases. The author shows why that answer is flawed and clarifies the proper understanding of universal propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fallacy's structure
The denier sets up a dilemma based on epistemic access. The respondent is forced into a contradiction: if he says 'yes' (the hand's content is even) without seeing, he is asked how he knows; if he says 'no', he contradicts his universal 'every two is even' because the hand supposedly contains two.
The fallacy plays on the ambiguity between the universal's truth and our knowledge of its application.
2
Recognize the flawed response
The respondent restricts the universal to 'every two that we know to be two'. This makes the universal contingent on knowledge, not on the nature of things.
This response is a common attempt to avoid the dilemma but is incorrect.
3
Apply the author's correction
The universal 'every two is even' means that any two, in themselves, are even. The truth does not depend on whether we know the specific instance is two.
The author emphasizes that the universal holds objectively, in accordance with divine knowledge.
4
Distinguish epistemology from ontology
The fallacy confuses the condition of knowing (epistemic) with the condition of being (ontological). The proper response is that the universal holds objectively, and ignorance of the particular does not undermine it.
This distinction is crucial in many Islamic sciences, such as usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage presents a fallacy and then criticizes the common answer. A denier of knowledge argues: 'Do you know that every two is even?' The respondent hedges. Then the denier asks about the contents of a closed hand: 'Is it even or not?' The dilemma: if you say yes, by what do you know since you cannot see; if you say no, you contradict your universal statement that every two is even, because you admitted that two items are in the hand. The standard reply is that the universal means 'every two that we know to be two is even' – and since we do not know the hand's contents to be two, we do not affirm it. The author declares this answer wrong despite its apparent clarity. The correct understanding is: when we say 'every two is even', we mean that any group of two in itself, regardless of our knowledge, is even. The truth of the universal does not depend on our epistemic access. The fallacy confuses the epistemological condition (our knowing it is two) with the ontological status (it being two). The proper response is that the universal is true objectively, but we may not know whether the particular instance falls under it due to ignorance of its quantity.

The Methodology of Gradual Reasoning from Evident Premises to Avoid Fallacies
Author explains how neglect of evident premises and lack of gradual reasoning cause fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of gradual reasoning from evident premises to avoid common fallacies in theoretical discussions.

Core Idea
Neglecting evident premises and skipping too many steps leads to confusion and error.

The author diagnoses the root cause of most fallacies: rushing into complex problems without building from clear, evident premises. He prescribes a method of gradual progression so that each new piece of knowledge is close to what came before. In this lesson, we will understand this method and practice it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evident premises (jaliyyat)
Recognize the self-evident or foundational premises in an argument. In the passage, the author uses 'Every two are a pair' as an evident premise.
2
Check for gradual progression
Examine whether the reasoning moves step by step from these premises without skipping intermediate degrees. The author insists on 'proceeding gradually until nothing remains hidden little by little'.
3
Detect skipped steps
When a conclusion is far from the initial premises with many degrees in between, it indicates a risk of fallacy. The mind cannot leap many degrees at once.
4
Predict fallacies from skipping
Common fallacies like equivocation, hasty generalization, or false analogy often arise when steps are skipped. For example, rushing from 'the world is temporally originated' to 'God exists' without intermediate steps can lead to invalid arguments.
5
Contrast with the bad habit
The author criticizes the habit of rushing into the depth of a problem (ghamrat al-ishkal) without securing near premises. The correct method is to proceed gradually.

The First Subtle Ambiguity: Shared Particles
First subtle ambiguity: shared particle causes equivocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a shared particle (like the pronoun 'huwa') causes equivocation in a logical argument and distinguish it from a valid use.

Core Idea
A single particle can cause a whole argument to collapse if its reference shifts.

Al-Razi's first subtle ambiguity shows how even a small word -- like the pronoun 'huwa' -- can derail an argument when it has multiple possible referents. In the example he gives, an argument about God's knowledge becomes invalid because 'huwa' could refer either to 'everything that God knows' or to 'God' Himself. This type of ambiguity is especially dangerous because it is often overlooked; once you know to check the reference of pronouns and connectors, you can spot the fallacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared particle or connector
Look for words like pronouns (huwa, hiya) or particles (waw, fā') that can refer to different parts of the sentence.
2
List all possible referents
Determine which nouns or phrases the particle could grammatically refer to.
3
Check dependence on a specific referent
If the argument's conclusion changes meaning depending on which referent is chosen, the argument may be equivocating.
4
Test each referent for consistency
Replace the particle with each possible referent and see if the argument remains valid. If one interpretation leads to a false or absurd conclusion, the fallacy is exposed.

The Second Subtle Ambiguity: Collective Truth Mistaken for Distributive
Second subtle ambiguity: collective truth mistaken for distributive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a statement that is true collectively (for the whole) but not separately (for each part), and avoid the fallacy of assuming distributive truth from collective truth.

Core Idea
What is true of a whole may not be true of its parts.

Al-Razi's second subtle ambiguity arises when a premise is true when taken as a whole (collectively) but is mistakenly thought to be true of each part individually. The coordinating particle 'waw' can signal either collective or distributive reading. For example, 'Five is even and odd' is true if taken as 'five is composed of an even part (two) and an odd part (three)', but it would be false if interpreted as 'five is both even and odd in the same respect'. This fallacy is common in dialectical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conjunctive statement
Look for statements with 'and' (waw) linking attributes or parts.
2
Determine collective vs distributive meaning
Ask: does the statement attribute the predicates to the whole at once, or to each part separately?
3
Check if collective truth implies distributive truth
If the statement is only true collectively, it is a fallacy to infer each part is true individually.
4
Analyze the function of 'waw'
Recognize that 'waw' can indicate all parts together, all attributes together, or only some parts. The context determines the function.

Identifying Tautology in Categorical Syllogisms
Author explains the fallacy where conclusion is same as premise, using synonymous terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a tautological premise in a categorical syllogism and explain why it invalidates the inference.

Core Idea
A premise that merely repeats itself cannot produce new knowledge—the conclusion must be genuinely distinct.

Sometimes a syllogism appears to make a logical deduction, but on closer inspection, one of its premises is tautological: it simply repeats a definition using synonymous terms. The author of this passage warns us about this deceptive tactic, using the example of 'Every human is a man' to show how the conclusion is already contained in the premise. In this lesson, you will learn to spot this fallacy and understand why it undermines the purpose of syllogistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Extract the three parts of the syllogism
Write down the minor premise, major premise, and conclusion from the Arabic example: 'Every human is a man', 'Every man is morally responsible', 'Therefore every human is morally responsible'.
The Arabic terms: 'bashar' (human) and 'insan' (man/insan).
2
Test the minor premise for synonymy
Check whether the subject and predicate of the minor premise are synonyms. According to the author, 'bashar' and 'insan' are synonymous—they refer to the same species. Replace one with the other: the premise becomes 'Every man is a man'.
In Arabic lexicography, synonymy is debated, but here it is accepted for the argument.
3
Identify the tautology
A statement like 'Every man is a man' is a tautology—it is always true but adds no information. Notice that this premise does not establish any connection between the subject and the major term that wasn't already there.
4
Determine the effect on the syllogism
Because the minor premise is tautological, the syllogism reduces to: 'Every man is morally responsible; therefore every man is morally responsible.' The conclusion is identical to the major premise. No new inference occurs.
The fallacy is called 'conclusion identical to premise' (al-natija 'ayn al-muqaddima).
5
Formulate a general rule
For a valid categorical syllogism, each premise must be logically independent of the other. A premise that is a tautology (e.g., using synonymous terms) fails this condition and makes the syllogism invalid.
This rule helps you avoid deceptive arguments in theological and juristic reasoning.
