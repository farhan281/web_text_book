The Two Types of Perception: Concept and Assent
Author defines the two types of perception: concepts and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between perception of a simple essence (tasawwur) and perception of a relation (tasdiq), and determine which type admits truth and falsehood.

Core Idea
All knowledge is either concept or assent; only relations can be true or false.

This passage from the Mahakk al-Nazar lays the foundation for all of logic by dividing perception into two irreducible types. Before you can reason correctly, you must understand that grasping a single concept is a different mental act than affirming or denying a connection between concepts. This distinction is the backbone of Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of perception
In the Arabic, find where the author states 'إدراك الأمور على ضربين' (perception of matters is of two types).
2
Define the first type: perception of simple essences
Note that this includes words like 'body', 'motion', 'world', etc. It is a concept without any judgment.
This is tasawwur.
3
Define the second type: perception of relations
This involves negation or affirmation between concepts, e.g., 'The world is not eternal', 'The world is originated'.
This is tasdiq.
4
Apply the key characteristic: truth and falsehood
Recognize that only the second type admits tasdiq (assent) and takdhib (denial). The first type does not.
Truth/falsehood depend on correspondence of the relation to reality.
5
Test your understanding with examples
Classify given examples as either tasawwur or tasdiq, and explain why.
Use the author's examples: 'world', 'originated', 'The world is originated'.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction to the book. The author first acknowledges the reader's aim to learn the conditions for correct syllogism (qiyas) and correct definition (hadd), and alerts to sources of error. He then declares that these two are the bonds of all sciences. After this, he sets out the foundational epistemological division: perception (idrak) is of two types. The first is perception of simple essences (idrak al-dhat al-mufrada), e.g., knowing the meaning of 'body', 'motion', 'world', 'originated', 'eternal'. These are concepts without judgment. The second is perception of the relation between these concepts (idrak al-nisba), either by negation or affirmation. He illustrates with the example: first you understand the individual concepts 'world', 'originated', 'eternal'; then you affirm or deny a relation, as in 'The world is not eternal' (negation) and 'The world is originated' (affirmation). The author emphasizes that perception only occurs in these two ways. Crucially, only the second type admits truth and falsehood (tasdiq wa takdhib); the first type does not. This is a clear statement of the distinction between tasawwur (concept formation) and tasdiq (assent), a foundational principle in Islamic logic and epistemology.

Simple and Compound: The Locus of Truth and Falsehood
Ghazali distinguishes simple expressions from statements, restricting truth/falsehood to the latter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between simple expressions (mufradat) and compound statements (khabar), and explain why truth and falsehood apply only to statements.

Core Idea
Truth and falsehood are properties of statements, not of isolated concepts.

Al-Ghazali opens his logical work by clarifying a fundamental point: truth and falsehood are not properties of single words or concepts, but of complete statements. Understanding this distinction is essential before we can analyse any theological or legal proposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of a statement
Find in the Arabic where Ghazali says a statement (khabar) consists of two simple terms: a description (sifa) and a described (mawsuf). These correspond to predicate and subject.
Examples from the text: 'the world' and 'originated' are the two simples that compose the statement 'the world is originated'.
2
Understand why truth/falsehood require composition
Notice that truth or falsehood arises only when the description is attributed to the described with affirmation or denial. Without this attribution, there is no judgment.
Contrast a simple term like 'world' (no truth value) with the statement 'the world is originated' (has truth value).
3
Apply the distinction to an Islamic example
Take the statement 'The Quran is uncreated.' Identify the two simple terms and explain why it can be true or false.
This example is from Kalam; the two terms are 'the Quran' (subject) and 'uncreated' (predicate).

Commentary
This passage is a foundational epistemological introduction. Al-Ghazali begins by distinguishing between simple terms (mufradat) and compound statements (khabar). Truth and falsehood apply only to statements, which consist of two terms (subject and predicate). He then proposes a technical convention: to call the apprehension of simple terms 'ma'rifa' (cognition) and the assent to a proposition 'ilm' (knowledge). This is justified linguistically: the verb 'arafa' takes one object, while 'alima' (like 'zanna') takes two objects. 

He then argues that all known perceptions reduce to these two types. Every proposition (tasdiq) requires prior knowledge of its two simple components. For example, to know that the world is originated, one must first have the concepts of 'world' and 'originated'.

Next, he divides both ma'rifa and 'ilm into a priori (badihi) and acquired (nazari). A priori ma'rifa is self-evident, like sensory perceptions. Acquired ma'rifa is a summary notion wanting detail. 

Finally, he states the two instruments of logic: acquired ma'rifa (tasawwur) is obtained by definition (hadd), and acquired 'ilm (tasdiq) is obtained by proof (hujja/burhan), which is the syllogism (qiyas). The book will be divided into two parts accordingly: one on qiyas and one on hadd.

Ghazali's Terminological Convention: Ma'rifa and 'Ilm
Ghazali introduces ma'rifa and 'ilm as technical terms for tasawwur and tasdiq, justified by Arabic grammar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why al-Ghazali renames tasawwur as 'ma'rifa' and tasdiq as 'ilm', using the grammatical behavior of verbs as justification.

Core Idea
Words should mirror meanings; grammar reveals the difference between mere cognition and propositional knowledge.

Al-Ghazali proposes a technical terminology: call simple apprehension (tasawwur) 'ma'rifa' and assent (tasdiq) 'ilm'. He supports this by appealing to Arabic grammar: 'arafa' governs one object, while 'alima' governs two. This linguistic grounding shows that the two cognitive acts are fundamentally different.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two types of cognition
Al-Ghazali identifies two types: the apprehension of a simple meaning (tasawwur) and the assent to a proposition (tasdiq). He wants to give them distinct names.
He proposes 'ma'rifa' for tasawwur and 'ilm' for tasdiq.
2
Analyze the grammatical rule
The verb 'arafa' (to know/cognize) takes only one object (e.g., 'I knew Zayd'), whereas 'alima' (to know propositionally) takes two objects (e.g., 'I knew Zayd is knowledgeable'), like 'zanna'.
This difference mirrors the logical difference: tasawwur grasps a single concept, tasdiq compounds two concepts into a judgment.
3
Apply the convention to Islamic statements
For 'I know that the world is originated', 'alima' is appropriate because it involves two objects. For 'I conceive the world', 'arafa' is used with one object.
This shows that linguistic usage supports the logical distinction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acquired conception requires definition, and acquired assent requires demonstration.

After establishing the two branches of knowledge, al-Ghazali introduces the two instruments by which we acquire new knowledge: definition (hadd) for obtaining the detail of a conception, and proof (hujja/burhan) for establishing assent. Guided Path
1
Review the division of ma'rifa and 'ilm

Identify the tool for acquired 'ilm

Demonstration moves from known premises to a new conclusion, yielding certainty.

Connect to the book's structure
Ghazali says the book will have two parts: one on qiyas (the touchstone of demonstration) and one on hadd (the touchstone of definition).
This shows the entire science of logic is organized around these two instruments.

The Three Types of Verbal Indication: Correspondence, Inclusion, Implication
Author classifies verbal indication into three types and exemplifies muṭābaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of verbal indication (muṭābaqah, taḍammun, iltizām) and identify them in examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
A word indicates meaning entirely, partially, or through necessary implication.

In this foundational lesson, the author introduces the three essential ways a word conveys meaning: correspondence, inclusion, and implication. This tripartite division is crucial for avoiding errors in reasoning because it clarifies how definitions and arguments are built from linguistic indications. We will unpack each type and see how they operate in Islamic scholarly discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Locate the three terms in the Arabic: المطابقة، التضمن، الالتزام. Notice that the author presents them as exhaustive categories of verbal indication.
These are foundational terms in Islamic logic.
2
Understand muṭābaqah
The word indicates its entire meaning exactly. The author uses 'al-bayt' (house) to illustrate: the word corresponds to the full concept of a house.
3
Understand taḍammun
The word indicates a part of its meaning because the whole includes the part. For example, 'house' implies the existence of a roof or walls as parts of its meaning.
This type is used when extracting partial implications.
4
Understand iltizām
The word indicates something not explicitly part of its meaning but necessarily connected to it. For 'house', this could be 'dwelling' or 'shelter' – something inseparable from the concept, though not part of its definition.
Later logicians further subdivide iltizām into 'natural' and 'rational'.
5
Apply the distinction
Practise with an example from Islamic law: the word 'ṣalāh' (prayer) indicates the entire ritual (muṭābaqah), its components like rukū' (taḍammun), and its spiritual benefits or preconditions (iltizām).
This shows how the three types operate in real Islamic sciences.

He imagined a flying human, since in the imagination is established the image of man alone and the image of flight alone, and this power combines them just as it separates the two halves of a man. And it is absolutely not within its capacity to invent an image that has no precedent in the imagination; rather, all its operations consist of separation and combination of forms already present in the imagination. And the point is: that the disparity between the intellect's perception of things and the imagination's perception is greater than the disparity between imagination and sight in the cognitive powers of man. For imagination cannot perceive abstract meanings stripped of extraneous accidents that are not included in their essence—I mean those that are not essential to them, as previously explained. For you cannot imagine blackness except in a specific amount of body, and with it a specific shape of body, and a specific position relative to you, near or far. And it is known that shape is other than color, and magnitude is other than shape; for a triangle has one shape whether small or large. The perception of these abstract simples is only by another power that we have agreed to call intellect. So it perceives blackness, and makes judgments, and perceives color-ness abstractly, and perceives animality and corporeality abstractly. And when it perceives animality, attention to rational and non-rational may not be present, even though animal does not lack both divisions. And when it continues in its reflection judging colors with a judgment, the meaning of blackness, whiteness, etc. may not be present to it. This is among the wondrous properties and exquisite actions of this power. So when it sees one horse, it perceives the absolute horse that is shared by small and large, grey and bay, distant and near in place; rather, it perceives horseness abstractly and absolutely, purified from every accident that is not essential to it. For specific magnitude and specific color are not essential to the horse, but rather accidental or concomitant in existence; since diverse magnitudes and colors share in the reality of horseness. These absolute abstractions that encompass different things are what the theologians call 'faces, states, and judgments', and the logicians call 'universal abstract propositions', and they claim that they exist in minds, not in external entities. Sometimes they express them as non-existent in the view of the author who affirms an external existence, but rather in the interior—meaning outside the mind and inside it. And the proponents of states say: they are established things; then sometimes they say they are existent and known, and other times they say they are neither existent nor non-existent, nor known nor unknown. Their heads have spun and their intellects have become lost concerning it.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exposition from Mahakk Nazar. The author begins by noting that the imagination can only combine or separate pre-existing images; it cannot invent something entirely new. The key point (المقصود) is that the intellect's mode of perception is fundamentally different from imagination's, more different than imagination is from sight. The author argues that imagination cannot grasp abstract meanings divorced from accidental features: for instance, you can only imagine blackness as tied to a specific size, shape, and position. He then shows that shape and color are distinct; a triangle has the same shape regardless of size. The only power that can apprehend such simple abstractions is the intellect (عقل). The intellect perceives blackness, makes judgments, and grasps universals like 'color-ness', 'animality', 'corporeality' without necessary reference to particular instances. For example, when perceiving a horse, the intellect directly abstracts 'horseness' that is common to all horses, stripped of accidental features like size or color. The author then identifies these abstract universals as the subject of debate among theologians and logicians: theologians call them 'وجوه وأحوال وأحكام' (faces, states, judgments), logicians call them 'قضايا كلية مجردة' (universal abstract propositions). They disagree over their ontological status: some say they exist only in minds, others claim they are 'non-existent' in the sense of not having extramental reality, and the proponents of 'الأحوال' (states) oscillate between affirming them as established things, then sometimes as existent and known, sometimes as neither existent nor non-existent nor known nor unknown, leading to confusion.

Author explains the intellect's ability to perceive abstract universals.

Learning Objective

The intellect can grasp the universal 'horseness' that is common to all horses, regardless of size or color. The Ontological Debate on Abstract Universals
Author reports and criticizes the debate on the reality of abstract universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three positions (theologians, logicians, proponents of states) on the ontological status of abstract universal propositions and describe the confusion of the proponents of states.

Core Idea
Abstract universals are said to exist in minds, but their reality is disputed among theologians and logicians.

After explaining the intellectual perception of abstractions, the author reports the different views on what these 'absolute abstractions' are. The theologians call them 'faces, states, and judgments', while logicians call them 'universal abstract propositions' and hold them to exist in minds. The proponents of 'states' are particularly confused, oscillating between existence and non-existence. This debate is crucial for understanding how Islamic scholars conceptualize universal meanings.

Identify the three groups mentioned

Note the confusion of the proponents of states

Real Conceptual Sharing vs. Mere Homonymy
Author distinguishes genuine conceptual sharing from verbal homonymy using the example of 'al-mushtari'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between genuine conceptual sharing (ishtirak ma'nawi) and mere verbal homonymy (ishtirak lafzi) using the author's criteria.

Core Idea
Not every shared name indicates a shared concept; universals must be real, not verbal.

The author warns against confusing genuine universal concepts with accidental homonyms. He gives the striking example of the word 'al-mushtari' meaning both 'buyer' and the planet Jupiter. This lesson will sharpen your ability to see when a shared term points to a real common essence and when it is just a linguistic coincidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of sharing
Find where the author distinguishes sharing in an 'intelligible matter' (amr ma'qul) from sharing in 'mere name' (mujarrad al-ism). The first is real conceptual sharing; the second is homonymy.
The example of black/white is real sharing; the example of 'al-mushtari' is mere name sharing.
2
Analyze the homonymy example: 'al-mushtari'
The word 'al-mushtari' refers to a buyer (a person who makes a contract) and also to the planet Jupiter. These two meanings share no conceptual content beyond the word itself.
In contrast, 'colorness' in black and white is a genuine universal shared by both.
3
Apply the distinction to an Islamic science context
Take a term like 'wudu' which in fiqh means a specific ritual, but in Arabic can also mean cleanliness. Is this a case of homonymy or conceptual sharing?
The context will often determine whether the term is used technically or loosely.
4
Understand the consequence of confusing them
Treating homonyms as if they share a universal leads to false syllogisms. For example, arguing 'Every al-mushtari is a buyer; Jupiter is al-mushtari; therefore Jupiter is a buyer' is invalid.
This is the fallacy of equivocation.

Commentary

Whether a Sahih hadith yields certainty or opinion depends on one's epistemological criteria.

First Principles vs. Mediated Propositions
Ghazali defines self-evident and mediated propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a first principle (al-awwali) from a proposition requiring a medium based on the criterion of immediate assent.

Core Idea
First principles need no proof; mediated propositions demand a middle term.

Ghazali opens this chapter by noting that when the mind considers a proposition, it either assents immediately or hesitates. This simple test separates self-evident truths from those that require demonstration. The example of 'Is nabidh forbidden?' shows that not all propositions are self-evident; some need a medium to connect subject and predicate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two mental states
When the reflecting mind combines two terms and relates them, it either immediately assents to the proposition or refrains. This is the fundamental classification.
2
Define a first principle
If the mind assents without hesitation, the proposition is a first principle (awwali). It is known without proof, cause, or reflection.
Examples in Kalam: 'The whole is greater than the part' or 'Contradictories cannot both be true.'
3
Define a mediated proposition
If the mind does not immediately assent, a medium (wasita) is required to produce knowledge. This medium becomes the 'face of the proof.'
4
Apply the test to the nabidh example
The mind does not know initially whether nabidh is forbidden. Therefore, it is not a first principle; a middle term (e.g., 'intoxicant') is needed to connect 'nabidh' to 'forbidden.'
This illustrates the need for syllogistic reasoning in Islamic legal epistemology.

The Face of the Proof: The Middle Term
Ghazali defines wajh al-dalil and its role.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the middle term in a categorical syllogism and explain how it functions as the 'face of the proof' that compels assent to the conclusion.

Core Idea
The middle term is the face of the proof; it connects subject and predicate to produce knowledge.

Ghazali clarifies a common confusion: the face of the proof (wajh al-dalil) is not the conclusion itself but the mediating term that forces the mind to assent. He illustrates with the question 'Is nabidh forbidden?' showing that a middle term like 'intoxicant' is needed. This lesson unpacks the logical structure of that argument.

Guided Path
1
Reproduce the initial question
The mind is asked: 'Is nabidh forbidden?' It does not know, so the two terms (nabidh and forbidden) are not yet connected.
2
Identify the missing middle term
To connect 'nabidh' to 'forbidden', we need a term that relates to both. The implicit middle term is 'intoxicant' (al-muskir), since nabidh is an intoxicant and all intoxicants are forbidden.
This is a standard example in Islamic legal reasoning.
3
Construct the categorical syllogism
Major premise: Every intoxicant is forbidden. Minor premise: Nabidh is an intoxicant. Conclusion: Therefore, nabidh is forbidden. The middle term 'intoxicant' appears in both premises.
This is a first-figure (shakl awwal) syllogism, mood AAA (Barbara).
4
Identify the face of the proof
The face of the proof (wajh al-dalil) is the middle term 'intoxicant' because it is the medium that relates to the subject (nabidh) and the predicate (forbidden), compelling assent to the conclusion.
Ghazali emphasizes that this is distinct from the conclusion itself.
5
Contrast with the confusion of the weak
The weak think that the face of the proof is the conclusion (the proposition itself). But it is actually the middle term that produces the conclusion.

Defining Immediate Knowledge (al-Awwali)
Author defines immediate propositions known without a mediator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposition is immediate (awwali) or requires proof based on the intellect's response.

Core Idea
Immediate knowledge is the intellect's spontaneous assent without any mediating proof.

This passage marks the foundational distinction in Islamic logic between propositions that are self-evident and those that require proof. The author defines immediate knowledge (al-awwali) as the intellect's immediate assent to a proposition when two terms are combined. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it separates axioms from conclusions, and helps avoid demanding proof for what is self-evident.

Identify the two terms combined by the intellect

2
Observe the intellect's reaction: immediate assent or hesitation
After presenting the combined terms to the intellect, note whether it assents immediately or refrains. Immediate assent indicates that the proposition is awwali (self-evident).
The author states: 'If it assents to it, then it is the immediate (al-awwali), known without a mediator'.
3
Recognize the synonyms for 'without proof'
The author lists several phrases: without a proof, without a cause, without a device, without reflection. None of these imply that the proposition is false or unprovable; they simply mean no external reasoning is needed.
These expressions all convey one meaning in this context.

How the Conclusion Is Latent in Premises and Needs Attention
Author explains how the conclusion is latent in premises and requires mental attention to become actual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four Islamic theological positions on how a syllogistic conclusion transitions from potential to actual through mental attention.

Core Idea
A conclusion is not automatically known from premises; mental attention (tafattun) is required to actualize it.

This passage tackles a key epistemological question in Islamic logic: how does the conclusion emerge from premises? The author uses the example of nabidh and a mule to show that even when you know the premises, the conclusion may not be active in your mind. You must be mindful of the implication. The passage then surveys four theological explanations of this process, reflecting the diversity of Islamic intellectual traditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The author states that the conclusion exists in the premises potentially (bi'l-quwwa), not actually (bi'l-fi'l). Locate this claim in the Arabic and note the examples given (intoxicant/nabidh, body/fox).
Potential existence means the conclusion is implicitly contained, like a universal includes its particulars.
2
Understand the necessity of tafattun
The author emphasizes that mere knowledge of premises does not actualize the conclusion. You must bring both premises to mind and reflect on how the conclusion is contained in them. This mental act is called al-tafattun.
The mule example illustrates a failure of tafattun: the person knew the syllogism but did not attend to it, leading to a false belief.
3
Analyze the mule syllogism
Extract the implicit syllogism: 'Every mule is barren; this is a mule; therefore it is barren.' The person's error shows that even valid reasoning can be ineffective without conscious application.
The syllogism is formally valid (Barbara), but the conclusion was not applied because the person did not reflect on it.
4
Distinguish the four theological positions
The author lists four views on how tafattun causes the conclusion: (1) Mu'tazila: generation (tawallud); (2) Philosophers: emanation from the Active Intellect; (3) Most Ash'aris: necessary implication (luzum); (4) Some Ash'aris: direct divine creation after reflection, with possible interruption of habit.
These positions reflect different metaphysical commitments about causality and divine action.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion of how a conclusion (natija) is obtained from premises (muqaddimat). The author begins by stating that the conclusion is already implicitly contained in the premises, but only potentially (bi'l-quwwa), not actually (bi'l-fi'l). He illustrates this with the example: your statement 'Every intoxicant is haram' includes all types of intoxicants, so the conclusion 'Nabidh is haram' is latent in the premises. However, the particular may not be present in the mind even when the universal is. Similarly, someone who says 'Body occupies space' may not be thinking of the fox specifically. So the conclusion is potentially present but may not be actualized. The author then clarifies that mere knowledge of the premises does not suffice; one must bring both premises to mind and reflect on how the conclusion is contained in them. Only then does the conclusion become actual. To illustrate, he gives the example of a person who sees a mule with a swollen belly and mistakenly thinks it is pregnant, even though he knows that all mules are barren and this is a mule. The person's own syllogism (every mule is barren, this is a mule, so it is barren) should prevent the mistake, but because he did not attend to the conclusion's implication, the wrong judgment occurred. The author then notes that the proximate cause of the conclusion's actualization is mental attention (tafattun) to the potential presence of the conclusion in the premises. The weak-minded fail to realize whether the proof is the same as the thing proved or not. The truth is that the thing proved (al-madlul) is distinct from the attention; it exists potentially in the premises. The attention is only the cause of its actualization. Finally, the author presents different theological positions on how the conclusion is obtained: the Mu'tazila hold it is generated (tawallud) by the attention; the philosophers say the Active Intellect (al-'aql al-fa''al) emanates the conclusion when the mind is prepared; most Ash'aris hold that the premises necessitate the conclusion by implication (luzum); some Ash'aris say God directly creates the conclusion after the premises and attention, as a matter of habit that could be broken (kharaq al-'ada). All these views agree that the conclusion is not automatically known; it requires active mental engagement.

Causal Theories of Inference: Tawallud, Luzum, and Fayd
Author enumerates three causal theories of the conclusion's generation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish three causal accounts of how the conclusion becomes actual: Mu'tazili generation, Ash'ari entailment, and philosophical emanation.

Core Idea
The same logical inference is explained by different causal theories: Mu'tazili generation, Ash'ari necessary implication, and philosophical emanation from the Active Intellect.

After establishing that tafattun is the proximate cause, the author presents three major theories of how that cause produces the effect (the actual conclusion). The Mu'tazila say it is generated (tawallud) necessarily. The philosophers say the mind, prepared by premises and attention, receives emanation from the Active Intellect. The Ash'aris say the premises entail the conclusion by luzum, a conceptual necessity. This lesson helps you compare these theories and understand their theological underpinnings.

Guided Path
1
Compare the three theories
Read the passage and list the three theories: Mu'tazili tawallud, philosophical fayd, Ash'ari luzum. Note that all three agree the conclusion is not directly known but must be drawn.
2
Identify the key difference
Tawallud is a causal generation (one thing necessarily produces another in the natural world). Fayd is epistemological emanation from a separate intellect. Luzum is a logical entailment inherent in the meaning of the premises.
The author aligns with luzum as the view of 'most of our companions'.
3
Understand why the author prefers luzum
The author is Ash'ari; luzum avoids both Mu'tazili natural causation (which limits divine power) and the philosophical emanation (which introduces a separate intellect). Luzum is a conceptual relation that does not involve physical causality.
This choice reflects a theological commitment to occasionalism.

Ash'ari Occasionalism and the Kasb of Knowledge
Author discusses Ash'ari occasionalist and kasb views on the conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Ash'ari occasionalist account of the conclusion's actuality and explain the debate on whether human power extends to it.

Core Idea
In Ash'arism, the conclusion is created by Allah after the premises according to His custom; human power only extends to bringing premises to mind.

The author presents two further refinements within the Ash'ari school after stating the luzum view. One view says the conclusion is created by Allah subsequent to the premises and attention, by His custom (ʿāda) which can be broken. Another debate concerns whether the servant has power over the actual conclusion. Some say no power extends to it; others say it does, making it an acquisition (kasb). This lesson explores how occasionalism and human acquisition apply to logical inference.

1

Ash'ari occasionalism holds that Allah creates every event directly. Here, the conclusion is created after the mental acts of bringing premises and attending to the entailment.

2
Distinguish potential vs. actual existence
The passage states the conclusion exists in the premises only potentially (bi-l-quwwa). Its actualization (bi-l-fiʿl) is a separate event created by Allah.
Human power extends only to the potential side – bringing premises to mind.
3
Analyze the kasb doctrine
Some Ash'aris say the servant can acquire the actual conclusion, meaning their power contributes to it, though Allah is the creator. Others deny any power over the actual conclusion, limiting human role to preparation.
This reflects broader kasb debates in kalām.

Commentary
This passage addresses the epistemological debate about the necessity of the conclusion in a syllogism. The author first gives a concrete example: every mule is sterile, this is a mule, therefore it is sterile. He explains that the cause of the conclusion's occurrence in the mind is the 'attention' (tafattun) to the fact that the conclusion is already implicit in the premises. Weak thinkers confuse the 'face of the proof' (wajh al-dalil) with the conclusion itself (madlul). The truth, according to the author, is that the conclusion is distinct from the attention, and the attention is merely the cause that brings it from potentiality to actuality. Then he enumerates five causal theories: (1) The Mu'tazila say the conclusion is 'generated' (tawallud) from the attention. (2) The philosophers say the mind's preparedness, aided by the Active Intellect, allows emanation (fayd) of the conclusion. (3) Most Ash'aris (the author's own school) say the premises entail the conclusion by necessary implication (luzum), not by generation. (4) Some Ash'aris say the conclusion is created by Allah after the premises are present, according to His custom, but the custom could be broken. (5) A further refinement: some say the servant has no power over the actual conclusion, only over bringing the premises to mind; others say the servant does have power over it, making it an 'acquisition' (kasb). This entire discussion illustrates the Islamic kalām engagement with logic, where the causal mechanism of inference is debated within a theological framework.

Solving the Paradox of Seeking Knowledge
Author resolves the paradox of seeking unknown knowledge by introducing partial conceptual knowledge and potential assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the author resolves the paradox of seeking unknown knowledge using the concepts of partial conceptual knowledge and potential assent.

Core Idea
We seek what we know potentially, not actually.

The paradox of seeking knowledge is a classic epistemological challenge: if you already know something, you don't need to seek it; if you are completely ignorant, you cannot recognize it when you find it. The author rejects the simple 'know or not know' dichotomy and introduces a third option: partial knowledge through conceptualization (tasawwur) and potentiality (al-quwwa). This lesson unpacks his solution using the example of seeking a lost slave and the theological proof for the temporal creation of the world.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paradox
The author begins with the question: How can one seek something unknown? The initial objection states that if you find it, you wouldn't know it's what you sought.
2
Reject the false dichotomy
The author points out that the division 'you know it or you do not know it' is not exhaustive. He adds a third: knowing it in one respect and being ignorant in another.
3
Define 'knowledge' here
He specifies that by 'knowledge' (ma‘rifa) he means conceptual understanding of individual parts (tasawwur), not full assent (tasdiq). This is key to the resolution.
Ma‘rifa here is 'other than science' (ghayr al-‘ilm).
4
Apply potentiality and actuality
The sought conclusion is known in potentiality (bi-l-quwwa) but not in actuality (bi-l-fi‘l). Potentiality means the capacity to accept assent; actuality means the assent is consciously present.
5
Analyze the slave example
The escaped slave is known by conceptualization (his essence), but his location is sought. We understand 'house', 'being', etc. conceptually, and we have potential assent that he is in the house. Actual assent comes from sensory sight. This illustrates how seeking works.

You cannot seek what you do not know at all; but you can seek what you know potentially.

Definitions and Proofs Terminate in Self-Evident Truths
Author explains that definitions and proofs must end in self-evident simples and primary propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a definition or proof has reached its foundation in self-evident concepts and explain why further regress is impossible.

Core Idea
Definitions and proofs cannot regress infinitely; they end in self-evident simples and primary propositions.

Have you ever tried to define a term and found yourself going in circles? The author of our text addresses this directly: every composite definition is built from simpler parts, and those parts themselves are composites until we reach simple concepts known by intuition. Similarly, every proof rests on premises that themselves require proof—yet this chain cannot continue forever. In this lesson, you will learn why definitions and proofs must terminate in self-evident truths, and how to recognize when a demand for further definition is misplaced.

Guided Path
1
Understand the regress problem in definitions
The author gives the example of defining 'body' (جسم) as 'composite of substances' (جواهر مؤتلفة). Then one can ask: 'What is the definition of substance?' This could continue indefinitely. Recognize that each composite term introduces two simpler terms, leading to an infinite regress if unchecked.
The regress here is not just theoretical; it mirrors a common challenge in Islamic sciences like Kalam when defining fundamental terms.
2
Identify the stopping point: self-evident simples
The author asserts that the regress ends with 'self-evident simples' (مفردات بديهية) that the intellect and sense know by primary knowledge. These are not defined essentially; their reality is directly grasped. For example, the concept of 'existence' (وجود) is innate.
These simples are the foundation of all definitions in a science, such as 'substance' (جوهر) or 'accident' (عرض) in Kalam.
3
Extend the same logic to proofs (tasdiq)
Every propositional knowledge (تصديق) that is not self-evident requires a demonstration (برهان), which consists of two premises. Each premise itself would need a demonstration, leading to a similar regress. This must end in self-evident primary propositions (أوليات), like 'two is greater than one'.
This mirrors the structure of arguments in Fiqh and Kalam, where foundational premises are taken as axiomatic.
4
Distinguish between verbal explanation and essential definition
The author warns that when someone asks for a definition of a self-evident term, they are only asking for a verbal explanation (شرح اللفظ), not the essence (حقيقة). The essence is already known innately. Demanding an essential definition of such terms shows obstinacy, like asking for proof that two is greater than one.
This distinction prevents endless debate over foundational concepts and moves the discussion to practical application in Kalam and Fiqh.

He then anticipates objections: the terms 'sukūn' (ambiguous), 'dhihn' (unfamiliar), and 'baṣīra' (suggesting sight) may be criticized. He defends that context clarifies ambiguity, 'dhihn' is used assuming common understanding, and the goal of real definition is conceiving the essence, not specific wording. In the Islamic tradition, 'ilm is central; e.g., the knowledge of the obligatory (wājib) or the forbidden (ḥarām) is this kind of certainty. The author's method is pragmatic: priority on conveying the meaning over technical jargon.

Author distinguishes conceptual knowledge from assent and identifies a fallacy of missing premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq, and identify the fallacy of assuming that conceptual knowledge alone yields certainty.

Concepts give understanding; assents require premises.
