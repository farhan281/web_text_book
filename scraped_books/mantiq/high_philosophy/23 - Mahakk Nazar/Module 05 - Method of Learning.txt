The Homonymy of 'Wajib' and Methodological Approach
Author explains that 'wajib' is a homonym and restricts its meaning to fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the homonymy of a term by distinguishing its usage across kalam, language, and fiqh, and apply the author's method of focusing on meaning before words.

Core Idea
A word's meaning changes with discipline; never assume a single definition.

This passage tackles a common pitfall: assuming a term has one fixed meaning. The author uses 'wajib' to show how the same word shifts between kalam (necessary existence), language (falling), and fiqh (obligatory action). He then gives a powerful methodological rule: set aside words and first grasp the intended meaning. This lesson will train you to spot homonymy and apply this rule in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Term
Locate 'al-wajib' in the passage. Note that it is called an 'ism mushtarak' (homonym).
Homonyms share the same spelling but differ in meaning.
2
List the Different Meanings
Extract the three meanings: for the mutakallim (necessary existence of God), for the linguist (falling), and for the fuqaha (obligatory action).
The Qur'anic verse and 'the sun set' are given as linguistic examples.
3
Understand the Methodological Rule
The author says: 'Set aside words and strip your consideration to the meaning first.' This means don't argue about definitions; first decide what the term refers to in context.
This rule prevents confusion from homonymy.
4
Apply the Scope Restriction
The author then limits 'wajib' (and similar categories) to actions of mukallafun only, excluding substances, other accidents, and animal actions.
This shows how meaning is narrowed within a discipline.

Commentary
This passage is methodological. The author first instructs the student to grasp the meanings before the words. He mentions that in this art (fiqh-related logic) there are five categories of words: wajib, mahzur, [da'ira?], mandub, makruh, and mubah. Then he emphasizes ignoring the words and focusing on the meanings first. He then illustrates that 'wajib' is a homonym (mushtarak): the mutakallim uses it for necessary existence (e.g., God is wajib al-wujud), the linguist uses it for falling (e.g., the sun set), and the fuqaha use it for a specific legal meaning. So the term has multiple definitions based on context. The current aim is the fiqh usage. Furthermore, the author explains that these categories are not applied to substances (jawahir) but to accidents (a'rad), specifically actions, and among actions, only those of the mukallafun (accountable persons), not animals. This restricts the scope of the discussion.
