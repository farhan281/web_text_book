Why the Third Figure Cannot Yield Universal Conclusions
Author demonstrates two examples of attempting a universal conclusion from the third figure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and critique arguments that attempt to derive universal conclusions from third-figure syllogisms in Islamic reasoning contexts.

Core Idea
The third figure only gives particular conclusions – universal claims from it are logical fallacies.

You may have encountered arguments that try to turn a few examples into a sweeping rule, like 'al-Hajjaj was brave and unjust, so every brave person is unjust.' This lesson unpacks why such reasoning fails: it uses the third figure of the categorical syllogism, which can never produce a universal conclusion. By understanding this rule, you will avoid a common sophism in theological and juristic argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the third figure structure
In the third figure, the middle term (al-'illa) is the subject of both premises. For example: 'al-Hajjaj is brave' and 'al-Hajjaj is unjust' – the middle term 'al-Hajjaj' is subject in each.
The middle term links the premises; here it is the specific individual.
2
Identify the intended conclusion
Check whether the conclusion is universal (every A is B or no A is B). In the example, the arguer claims 'Every brave person is unjust' – a universal affirmative.
Universal conclusions assert something about all members of a category.
3
Apply the rule of the third figure
Remember the logical rule: the third figure only yields particular conclusions (some A is B or some A is not B). The universal does not follow. The passage explicitly states this impossibility.
This is a fixed characteristic of the third figure's logical structure.
4
Diagnose the fallacy
The error is 'requesting a universal conclusion from the third figure' (talab natija 'amma min al-nazm al-thalith). The arguer confuses a valid particular inference with an invalid universal generalization.
The fallacy often appears in inductive leaps from specific cases to general rules.

Commentary
This passage critiques the fallacious attempt to derive a universal conclusion from a third-figure syllogism. The author first gave examples of sophistry in the first figure (omitting premises), and now shows that similar deception occurs in other figures, specifically the third. Two examples are given. The key lesson is that the third figure never yields a universal affirmative or universal negative conclusion; it only yields particular ones.

The Fallacy of Universalizing from Particular Instances in the Third Figure
Author criticizes invalid generalizations from particular instances using third figure examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error of seeking a universal conclusion from the third figure and reconstruct the valid particular conclusion.

Core Idea
The third figure only yields particular conclusions; universalizing from it is a fallacy.

Ibn Taymiyya exposes a common logical error: people often try to prove a universal rule using a single example, such as claiming 'Every brave person is unjust' because al-Hajjaj was both brave and unjust. This argument, when reconstructed, falls into the third figure of the categorical syllogism, which only supports a particular conclusion. By understanding the rules of the third figure, you will learn to spot this fallacy and apply correct reasoning in Islamic sciences like Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attempted conclusion
Read the argument and determine whether the conclusion is universal (every/no) or particular (some). In the al-Hajjaj example, the conclusion 'every brave person is unjust' is universal.
2
Find the middle term
Look for the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. Here, 'al-Hajjaj' is repeated in both premises, so it is the middle term.
A proper name like 'al-Hajjaj' is a particular term.
3
Determine the figure
Check if the middle term is the subject of both premises (third figure), predicate of both (second figure), or subject of one and predicate of the other (first figure). Here, 'al-Hajjaj' is subject in both 'al-Hajjaj is brave' and 'al-Hajjaj is unjust', so it is the third figure.
Ibn Taymiyya uses 'النظم الثالث' for the third figure.
4
Recall the rule of the third figure
The third figure only yields a particular conclusion (some S is P or some S is not P). A universal conclusion is impossible.
5
Correct the conclusion
Replace the universal conclusion with the valid particular one. For the al-Hajjaj example, the valid conclusion is 'Some brave people are unjust'.
The same applies to the commoner's claim: 'Some jurists are ill-mannered' and 'Some Sufis are immoral'.

Determining Syllogistic Figures by Middle Term Generality
Author explains classification of syllogistic figures based on middle term generality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the figure of a categorical syllogism by analyzing the generality relations of the middle term to the subject and predicate.

Core Idea
The figure depends solely on how general the middle term is relative to the subject and predicate.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya explains that the three figures of the categorical syllogism are determined by the relative generality of the middle term (called the 'cause') to the subject and the predicate. Knowing these conditions helps you distinguish valid argument forms from fallacious ones, especially in fiqh and kalam debates.

Guided Path
1
Locate the middle term (العِلَّة)
Find the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. It links the subject and predicate.
In the example, the middle term is 'عظيمة البطن' (large belly).
2
Identify subject and predicate of the conclusion
The subject (المحكوم عليه) is what is being judged; the predicate (الحكم) is the judgment applied to it.
In 'This woman is pregnant', subject = 'this woman', predicate = 'pregnant'.
3
Compare generality of middle term to subject and predicate
Determine if the middle term is more general, more specific, or equal to each. Generality means broader extension.
'Large belly' is more general than both 'this woman' (many have large bellies) and 'pregnant' (large belly can have other causes).
4
Apply the three conditions to determine the figure
1) Middle term more specific than both → third figure (only particular). 2) More general than subject, more specific than/equal to predicate → first figure (all four propositions). 3) More general than both → second figure (only negative conclusions).
The example fits condition 3, so it is second figure.
5
Check for the common fallacy
Second figure cannot yield affirmative conclusions. If you see an affirmative conclusion with a second figure middle term, the argument is invalid.
The pregnant woman inference is fallacious because it affirms the conclusion when it should be negative.

Analyzing Mixed Syllogisms: The Creator and the Throne Example
Author presents a mixed syllogism and warns of deceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decompose a mixed syllogism into its component conditional, categorical, and disjunctive parts and identify where a false premise may be hidden.

Core Idea
Mixed syllogisms can hide false premises; mastery of simple forms is the key to unmasking them.

This passage presents a complex mixed syllogism concerning the Creator and the Throne. The author shows how conditional, categorical, and disjunctive reasoning are combined, and warns that such complexity can conceal errors. Your task is to learn to dissect mixed arguments so that no deception escapes your scrutiny.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall conditional structure
Notice the argument begins with 'If the Creator is on the Throne, then...' This is the first type (conditional). Locate the antecedent and consequent.
2
Extract the disjunctive division
Within the consequent, three alternatives are presented: similar, larger, smaller. This is the third type (division and opposition). Recognize that these are mutually exclusive.
3
Follow the categorical chain
Each alternative leads to 'finite', then to 'body' via a categorical syllogism. Trace the chain: every equal/larger/smaller is finite; every finite is a body; so each alternative implies body. This uses the second type (correlation).
4
Identify the hidden premise
The argument assumes that finitude necessarily entails being a body (with three dimensions). This premise is questionable. Understand that this is where a false premise can be hidden in the multiplicity of steps.
5
Reduce to simple forms
Practice breaking the mixed syllogism into its simple components: a conditional, a disjunctive, and a categorical syllogism. This reduction is the tool to verify validity.

Analyzing a Mixed Syllogism: God on the Throne
Author illustrates a mixed syllogism about God on the Throne and warns of hidden errors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decompose a mixed syllogism (mukhtalitat) into its constituent categorical, conditional, and disjunctive patterns using the example of God on the Throne.

Core Idea
Mixed syllogisms combine three patterns; analyzing them reveals hidden structure and validity.

In this passage, the author presents a complex argument about the impossibility of God being on the Throne. This argument is not a simple syllogism but a compound one that weaves together categorical, conditional, and disjunctive reasoning. By learning to dissect such mixed syllogisms, you gain the ability to see through intricate proofs and verify their validity. Let's walk through the example step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall argument structure
The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: assume the Creator is on the Throne, derive a contradiction (that He would be a body), thus the assumption is false. Note the conclusion: 'so it is impossible that He be on the Throne.'
2
Extract the conditional pattern (talazum)
The passage begins with 'if He is on the Throne, then He is either equal, larger, or smaller.' This is a conditional linking the assumption to a disjunction.
This is the second pattern the author mentions.
3
Extract the disjunctive pattern (taqsim wa ta'and)
After stating that all such quantities are determined, the argument says: 'every determined is either a body or not a body; it is false that it is not a body, so it is established that it is a body.' This is a disjunctive syllogism: either body or not body; not not-body, therefore body.
This is the third pattern.
4
Identify the categorical pattern
The premise 'every determined is either a body or not a body' is a categorical proposition (A-type or E-type depending on interpretation). Also 'every equal, larger, and smaller is determined' is categorical.
This is the first pattern.
5
Combine and verify the conclusion
From the conditional and disjunctive steps, we get: if on Throne, then body. Since it is impossible for the Creator to be a body, the conditional is rejected, proving He is not on the Throne.
This uses modus tollens on the conditional derived from the compound.

The Mediator: How a Middle Term Makes Proof Possible
Author explains the need for a middle term and illustrates with the nabīdh example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the middle term in a categorical syllogism and explain how it connects the subject and predicate to produce necessary assent.

Core Idea
The middle term is the bridge that transforms ignorance into necessary assent.

When you encounter a claim like 'Nabīdh is harām' without immediate evidence, your intellect may not assent. The author of this passage explains that a mediator — a middle term — is required to link the subject and predicate. In this lesson, we will walk through the nabīdh syllogism to see how the middle term (muskir) functions as the logical bridge, and how two premises yield a conclusion that is both necessary and new.

Guided Path
1
Identify when direct assent fails
The author starts with the question 'Is nabīdh harām?' and the intellect says 'I do not know.' This indicates that the subject and predicate do not immediately connect in the mind.

Find the missing link
Since direct connection fails, we need a mediator. The mediator must be something that relates to both subject and predicate. In the example, the mediator is 'muskir' (intoxicant). Ask: What quality of nabīdh is also linked to the ruling 'harām'?

Construct the two premises
The author provides two premises: 'Every nabīdh is muskir' (known by experience) and 'Every muskir is harām' (known by revelation). Verify that each premise is true by the appropriate source.
4
Derive the conclusion
From these premises, the conclusion 'Nabīdh is harām' follows necessarily. The mediator (muskir) disappears in the conclusion but was essential.
5
Check necessity
The author says assent to the premises forces assent to the conclusion. This is the mark of a valid categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara). Confirm that if both premises are true, the conclusion must be true.

The Conclusion is Potential, Not Actual, in Premises
Author explains that the conclusion is potentially in premises, not actually

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between potential and actual existence of a conclusion in its premises and demonstrate why mere knowledge of premises does not automatically yield the conclusion without attentive reflection.

Core Idea
The conclusion is hidden in the premises, waiting for your active mind to bring it to light.

You may think that once you know the premises of a syllogism, the conclusion follows automatically. But the author of this passage warns that the conclusion exists only potentially in the premises; it becomes actual only when you consciously connect the universal and the particular. This is not a mere technicality—it has real consequences for how we reason in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Objection
Someone might say: 'The conclusion is not new; it is already contained in the premises.' The author admits this is partly true but shows why it is also false.
2
Separate the Two Aspects
First, the conclusion is linguistically and conceptually distinct (e.g., 'Nabidh is forbidden' is a separate statement from 'Nabidh is intoxicating' and 'The intoxicant is forbidden'). Second, the conclusion is implicitly contained in the universal premise (e.g., 'The intoxicant is forbidden' covers nabidh as a particular).
This is the key distinction: the conclusion is both other than the premises and present in them.
3
Analyze the Intoxicant Example
The universal premise 'The intoxicant is forbidden' includes all intoxicants. But when you have only that premise in mind, you may not think specifically of nabidh. The particular conclusion remains potential until you bring the two premises together.
4
Examine the Mule Analogy
A person sees a mule with a swollen belly and imagines it is pregnant, even though they know 'All mules are barren' and 'This is a mule'. Their knowledge of the premises does not prevent the false imagination because they did not actively connect them.
This shows that potential knowledge is not enough; you must reflect to actualize it.
5
Conclude the Epistemological Requirement
The conclusion emerges from potentiality to actuality only when you bring the premises to mind and consider the relationship between them. This is why syllogistic reasoning requires mental effort, not mere memorization.

Resolving the Fallacy of Universal Knowledge
Author presents a sophistical objection and its correct resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of confusing a universal proposition with the knower's knowledge of a particular instance, and correctly specify the logical contradictory of a universal affirmative statement.

Core Idea
A universal proposition is true of all instances, regardless of the knower's awareness.

In this lesson, we dissect a classic sophistical argument (maghalata) that tries to trap the respondent by mixing up a universal claim with the respondent's knowledge of a particular case. You'll learn to spot the confusion and to give the correct answer – one that preserves the universal's truth without falling into contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original universal claim
The claim is: 'Every two is even' (كل اثنين زوج). This is a universal affirmative proposition (A-type in Aristotelian logic).
2
Spot the objector's trap
The objector asks about a specific unknown pair: 'What is in my hand, is it even or not?' He forces a dilemma: if you say yes, he asks how you know without seeing; if no, he claims you have contradicted your universal statement because the hand contains two (two fingers).
The trap lies in assuming that knowing the universal means knowing every instance's truth immediately.
3
Analyze the incorrect answer
Some respond that they meant 'every two that we know to be two is even'. The author rejects this as wrong because the universal 'every two is even' is a statement about all twos in themselves, not dependent on our knowledge.
The incorrect answer changes the meaning of the universal from an objective truth to a conditional knowledge statement.
4
Understand the correct resolution
The correct answer is: 'I do not know that it is two' does not contradict 'every two is even'. The universal's truth is about the object, not my knowledge. The proper contradictory is 'some two is not even' (particular negative).
The universal remains true even if I do not know whether a particular case fits the antecedent.
5
Generalize the logical principle
This fallacy teaches us to distinguish between the truth conditions of a universal proposition (which are about the world) and the epistemic condition of the knower. A universal 'All A are B' is contradicted only by 'Some A are not B', not by 'I do not know that this A is B'.
This principle is crucial in Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam when dealing with general rulings.

Understanding Formal Defect in Syllogisms
Author identifies the second type of fallacy: defect in the form of the syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material defects in a syllogism and identify a formal defect in a given argument.

Core Idea
The form of a syllogism is as crucial as its content for certainty.

In logic, arguments can fail for two reasons: the premises may be false (material defect) or the structure may be invalid (formal defect). This lesson introduces the second type, which Ibn Taymiyya calls 'al-khalal fi al-surah' — a defect in the form. Even if your premises are true, if the syllogism is not correctly arranged, the conclusion does not follow.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two types of defects
The source distinguishes between material and formal defects. A formal defect concerns the syllogism's structure, not its content. Understand that Ibn Taymiyya is focusing on structural errors here.
The passage is an introduction; further text likely elaborates with examples.
2
Examine the definition of formal defect
The passage states: 'that its placement is not included in the corruption of the form of the arrangement of the proof'. This means the error lies in the order or pattern of the propositions. The syllogism's form itself is invalid.
The phrase 'نمطٍ من الجملة المذكورة' likely refers to a specific invalid pattern.
3
Apply to an example
Consider the syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. This drink is not an intoxicant. Therefore, this drink is not forbidden.' This is invalid (formal defect) because it commits the fallacy of denying the antecedent, even if premises are true.
The conclusion does not necessarily follow; the form is defective.
4
Contrast with material defect
If a premise is false, that is a material defect. For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. This drink is an intoxicant. Therefore, this drink is forbidden.' If the major premise were false, the argument is unsound but formally valid. Distinguish between unsoundness (material) and invalidity (formal).
A formally valid syllogism can have false premises; a formally invalid one cannot be saved by true premises.

Fifth Condition: Conclusion Must Not Be Identical to a Premise
Author defines the fifth condition that the conclusion must not be the same as the premise, illustrated by synonyms and a fiqh example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a conclusion is identical to a premise in a categorical syllogism and explain why such an argument is invalid.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism produces new knowledge; if the conclusion repeats a premise, the argument is circular.

Now that we have learned the first four conditions for a valid categorical syllogism, we come to a subtle but critical condition: the conclusion must not be identical to any premise. At first glance, this seems obvious, but logicians have identified cases where the identity is hidden—either through synonyms or by folding the conclusion into the premise. This passage from Mahakk al-Nazar explores both a verbal trick and a real fiqh argument where the middle term turns out to be identical to the conclusion. Understanding this condition protects your arguments from circularity and ensures that your syllogisms genuinely produce new knowledge.

Guided Path
1

The author gives two distinct examples: one using synonyms (bishr/insan) and one from Hanafi fiqh (specific fast). Locate each in the Arabic text.
The first example is a simple categorical syllogism; the second is more complex and involves a definition.
2

In the first syllogism, note that 'bishr' and 'insan' are synonyms. Therefore, the major premise 'every man (insan) is accountable' is essentially the same as the conclusion 'every human (bishr) is accountable'. Check if the syllogism actually proves anything new.
This is a clear violation of the condition: the conclusion is identical to a premise in meaning, even if different words are used.

The Hanafi argues that Ramadan fast does not require intention because it is a 'specific fast' (sawm 'ayn). The syllogism's middle term 'sawm 'ayn' is defined as a fast that does not require intention. Thus, the premise 'every specific fast does not require intention' already contains the conclusion. This is more subtle: the identity is not through synonyms but through definitional circularity. The middle term's meaning already includes the conclusion.

From these examples, formulate a general rule: A valid syllogism must have a middle term that is distinct from the major and minor terms, and its meaning must not already incorporate the conclusion. Otherwise, the syllogism is circular and does not produce new knowledge.
This condition is sometimes called the 'non-identity' condition or 'al-shart al-khamis'.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires the cause to be distinct from the conclusion.

The Third Rank of Overwhelming Probability in Qiyas
Definition of third rank with fiqh examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third rank of ghalabat al-zann in an analogical argument and determine whether the deletion of the distinguishing attribute is probable or certain.

Core Idea
In the third rank, deletion of the distinguishing attribute is probable, not certain.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya defines the third rank of overwhelming probability (ghalabat al-zann) in analogical reasoning (qiyas). Unlike the first two ranks where the deletion of the distinguishing attribute is certain or highly probable, here it is merely probable, requiring the mujtahid to weigh conflicting evidences. Two detailed fiqh examples illustrate this rank: the spread of manumission and the expiation for breaking the fast.

Guided Path
1

2
Locate the distinguishing attribute
Find the wasf fariq (distinguishing attribute) that differs between asl and far'. Here it is whether the added-to part is common or specific.
3
Assess probability of deletion

4
Conclude the rank
If the deletion is only probable, the argument belongs to the third rank of ghalabat al-zann. The mujtahid must follow his predominant opinion after weighing all evidences.

Dialectical Weighing of Probabilities in Juristic Qiyas
Dialectical exchange on expiation analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange of arguments in a third-rank qiyas and identify the role of counter-arguments (mu'arada) and responses (ajwiba).

Core Idea
In the third rank, the mujtahid must weigh counter-arguments to reach a probable conclusion.

The third rank of ghalabat al-zann is characterized by conflicting probabilities. Ibn Taymiyya presents a vivid dialectical exchange: arguments for analogizing intercourse to other means of breaking the fast are countered by considerations of deterrence and the unique treatment of intercourse in Islamic law. This lesson walks you through the structure of such a debate.

Guided Path
1
State the initial claim
The initial claim is that intercourse is analogous to eating bread for the purpose of expiation (kaffara) for breaking the fast. Since both involve breaking the fast, the same ruling should apply.
2
Identify the first counter-argument
The counter-argument: expiation is for deterrence (zajr), and intercourse is a stronger urge than eating bread, so it requires a stronger deterrent. Moreover, the Law treats intercourse differently by assigning a hadd punishment and invalidating Hajj.
3
Note the response
The response: bread is also desired while fasting, and degrees of desire cannot be precisely regulated because individuals vary. Therefore, the analogy might still hold.
4
Identify the final resolution
The passage concludes that the Law has regulated intercourse specifically with hadd and Hajj invalidation, making it a separate category. Thus, the analogy is not certain, and the mujtahid must follow his predominant opinion.
5
Assess the probability level
The conflicting evidences show that the deletion of the attribute of intercourse (i.e., treating it like other means) is only probable, placing the argument in the third rank.

The first type is the munasib 'illa, which is a suitable meaning that has a discernible appropriateness for the ruling. The example given is the description of intoxication (iskar) as the cause for the prohibition of khamr. Intoxication is a suitable attribute because it rationally leads to harm, which the prohibition aims to prevent.

Note that munasib does not require explicit textual proof; it is based on rational suitability. Compare this with mu'aththir, where the effect is proven by text or consensus.

Commentary
This passage from al-Ghazali's Mahakk al-Nazar classifies the second method of qiyas (analogical reasoning), where the jurist directly attends to the meaning considered as the 'illa (effective cause). The first method (discussed earlier) only involved deletion of differences. The second method is divided into three categories.

1. The meaning is munasib (suitable): The 'illa has a discernible appropriateness for the ruling. The example given is the description of intoxication (iskar) as the cause for the prohibition of khamr. Intoxication is a suitable attribute because it is rationally connected to the harm that prohibition prevents.

2. The meaning is mu'aththir (effective): The 'illa has a proven effect, either by explicit text (nass) or by consensus. Al-Ghazali cites Abu Hanifa's ruling that selling an item before taking possession is invalid due to gharar (uncertainty), and this cause does not apply to real estate. Abu Hanifa preferred the cause of gharar because its effect appears elsewhere by explicit text, such as the prohibition of selling a bird in the air (which is uncertain). Al-Ghazali refers to his own works for further details on the distinction between munasib and mu'aththir.

3. The third category is the most subtle: the common factor (jami') is neither suitable (munasib) nor effective (mu'aththir), but it suggests a suitable meaning without it being clearly identified. The example is the analogy between wudu and tayammum regarding the requirement of intention. Both are 'ritual purifications' (tahara hukmiyya), so they share a common meaning, even though there are many differences. This type of qiyas is called shabah (analogy based on resemblance). Al-Ghazali warns against being deceived by fanciful similarities, and notes that this type yields only preponderant assumption (ghalabat al-zann), not certainty. He concludes that this is the most hidden type of qiyas and requires careful verification. The passage emphasizes the importance of proper training to distinguish genuine suitability from mere illusion.

The Mu'aththir 'Illa: Effective Cause in Qiyas
Al-Ghazali defines the second type of 'illa, mu'aththir, with Abu Hanifa's example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mu'aththir 'illa (effective cause) in a qiyas argument and explain how its effect is proven by text or consensus.

Core Idea
A mu'aththir 'illa has its effect confirmed by explicit textual evidence or consensus, making it stronger than mere suitability.

In this passage, al-Ghazali introduces the second type of 'illa in the second method of qiyas: the mu'aththir 'illa. Unlike the munasib, which is merely suitable, the mu'aththir has its effect proven by explicit text or consensus. Al-Ghazali cites the example of Abu Hanifa's ruling that selling goods before possession is invalid due to gharar (uncertainty), and he prefers this cause because its effect appears elsewhere by text, namely the prohibition of selling a bird in the air.

Analyze the example of gharar

Unlike munasib, mu'aththir has its effect confirmed by textual evidence or consensus. This makes it a stronger type of 'illa.

The Shabah: Analogy of Resemblance in Qiyas

Second Objection: Motion Does Not Exist in an Instant

The Type of Entailment: Rules of Necessity Between Specific and General
Explains the rules of entailment for conditional syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of entailment (subalternation) to determine what follows necessarily from an affirmation or negation of a more specific or more general proposition in a conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
Affirmation of the specific entails the general; negation of the general entails the specific - but not vice versa.

This lesson unpacks the core rules of the entailment type (نمط التلازم), which governs conditional syllogisms. The passage gives a memorable framework for what follows necessarily when dealing with more specific and more general propositions. These rules are foundational for understanding how implications work in Islamic theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms: 'specific' and 'general'
In the rule, 'الأخص' (the more specific) and 'الأعم' (the more general) refer to related terms where the specific is a subset of the general, e.g., 'wine' (khamr) and 'intoxicant' (muskar).
2
Memorize the four implications
The passage lists four: (1) Affirming the specific entails the general. (2) Negating the general entails negating the specific. (3) Affirming the general does NOT entail the specific. (4) Negating the specific does NOT entail negating the general. These are the rules of subalternation.
3
Apply the rule to a fiqh example
Consider: 'If something is wine, it is intoxicating.' Affirming wine (specific) entails intoxicating (general). Negating intoxicating (general) entails not wine (specific). But affirming intoxicating does not entail wine (could be another intoxicant). Negating wine does not entail not intoxicating (other intoxicants exist).
4
Note the condition of equivalence
The passage also mentions 'مساواة اللازم للمقدم' (equivalence of necessary consequent to antecedent). When the consequent is equivalent to the antecedent, the entailment is bidirectional (affirmation and negation both work both ways).
This happens when the specific and general are actually coextensive.

The Type of Contradictoriness: The Principle of Non-Contradiction in Syllogisms
Introduces the type of contradictoriness and conditions for productive negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradictory propositions and determine whether a syllogistic conclusion follows from a negated disjunction, based on the principle of 'no meeting and no removal'.

Core Idea
Two contradictories cannot be true together nor false together; but without exhaustiveness, negation of one does not imply the other.

This lesson examines the third syllogistic type: نمط التعاند (the type of contradictoriness). The passage gives the defining property of contradictories: they cannot both be true nor both false. It also warns that if the contradictory pair is not exhaustive (i.e., not covering all alternatives), negating one does not necessarily produce a valid conclusion. This is crucial for using disjunctive syllogisms correctly in Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Define 'contradictories' (متناقضان)
Two propositions are contradictories if they are opposite in truth value such that they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. This is the principle of non-contradiction and excluded middle.
2
Understand the rule: 'لا يجتمعان ولا يرتفعان'
The passage says 'المتناقضان لا يجتمعان ولا يرتفعان' meaning the two contradictories do not come together (in truth) and do not go away together (i.e., they cannot both be false). This is the classic law of excluded middle.
3
Consider the note on exclusivity (الحصر)
The passage adds: 'عدم الحصر يلزم عنه عدم الإنتاج عند النفي' – if the contradictory pair is not exhaustive (i.e., there are other possibilities besides the two), then negating one does not produce a conclusion (the other is not necessarily true).
4
Apply to a fiqh example
Example of an exhaustive pair: 'This act is obligatory (wajib) or forbidden (haram)' – if it is not wajib, then it is haram, provided these are the only two options. Non-exhaustive: 'This act is halal or haram' (there is also makruh, etc.) – if it is not halal, it does not necessarily mean it is haram.

Use a fiqh example: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (general) and 'This beverage is an intoxicant' (specific). By rule 1, the beverage is forbidden. But rule 3 shows that 'forbidden' does not imply 'intoxicant'.
5
Distinguish from categorical syllogism
Note that unlike categorical syllogisms, the pattern of implication uses conditional premises and the entailment is based on the relationship of specificity, not on middle terms.

Commentary
This passage defines the second pattern of syllogism, called the pattern of implication. It begins by naming it and then provides a fiqh example of a conditional connected syllogism: 'Whatever leads to absurdity is absurd.' This is a principle in Islamic legal reasoning: any premise that results in a contradiction is itself false. The passage then distinguishes between conditional syllogism (qiyas sharti) and categorical syllogism (qiyas iqtirani), noting that the mode of indication (wajh al-dalala) in the pattern of implication is specific. The core logical content is a set of rules about the relationship between the specific (al-akhass) and the general (al-a'am): affirming the specific necessitates affirming the general (e.g., if Zaid is a man, then Zaid is an animal), and denying the general necessitates denying the specific (if nothing is an animal, then Zaid is not a man). However, affirming the general does not entail affirming the specific (not all animals are men), and denying the specific does not entail denying the general (Zaid not being a man does not mean he is not an animal). The passage concludes that the necessary consequent (al-lazim) is equivalent to the antecedent (al-muqaddam) in this pattern, and gives another example from the marginal notes.
