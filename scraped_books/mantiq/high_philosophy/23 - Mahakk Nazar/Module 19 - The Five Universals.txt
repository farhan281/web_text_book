Essential Predicates: Defining Genus of Genera and Species of Species
Author explains that jins al-ajnas and naw' al-anwa' are defined by essential predicates, not accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a predicate is essential (dhati) or accidental (aradi) and determine if it qualifies as a genus or species in a definition.

Core Idea
Only essential predicates define the hierarchy of genera and species.

In this passage, the author refines our understanding of the highest genus (jins al-ajnas) and lowest species (naw' al-anwa'). He addresses a common confusion: why 'existent' or 'substance' are not considered higher genera than 'body', and why terms like 'old man' are not lower species than 'man'. The key is that only essential predicates—those without which the concept of the thing vanishes—count. This lesson will guide you through the criterion of essentiality and how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchy of examples
The author gives a chain: body → growing → animal → man. Note that body is the most general (jins al-ajnas) and man is the most specific (naw' al-anwa'). The intermediate terms (growing, animal) are both species and genera relative to their neighbors.
This hierarchy is built on essential predicates only.
2
Extract the criterion for essentiality
The author says an essential predicate is one that is included in the answer to 'what is it?' such that if its existence is negated in the mind, the concept of the thing disappears. The triangle/heptagon example illustrates: 'shape' is essential, but 'existence' is not, because you can conceive a heptagon without knowing if it exists.
This test distinguishes dhati from lazim and 'arad.
3
Apply the criterion to counterexamples
Objection: 'existent' and 'substance' are more general than 'body'. Response: They are not essential to body because the concept of body does not require existence or substance (according to the philosophers' definition of substance as 'existent not in a subject', which is negative). Similarly, 'old man' is more specific than 'man' but is an accident (age), not an essential differentia.
Essentiality is about the definition, not mere generality or specificity.
4
Compare the two views on substance
The mutakallimun define substance as 'that which occupies space' (mutahayyiz) and consider it essential. The philosophers define it as 'existent not in a subject'—a negative definition that does not add an essential attribute. The author notes each convention is valid within its own framework.
This shows how the same term can be treated differently based on underlying beliefs.

So the invalidation of the knowledge of its existence does not invalidate from his mind the understanding of the reality of the heptagon. But if the 'shape' were invalidated from his mind, the heptagon would not remain a concept with him. So you have perceived a difference between the relation of existence to it and the relation of 'shape' to it; for the removal of one of them from the mind invalidates its reality, while the removal of the other does not invalidate it. This is what is intended by this convention. So their convention is valid according to their understanding and belief, not according to our belief and understanding.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the logical concepts of 'genus of genera' (jins al-ajnas) and 'species of species' (naw' al-anwa'), emphasizing that they are defined by essential (dhati) predicates, not accidental ones. The author begins by stating that when an essential predicate has nothing more specific below it, it is called 'species of species', and that this is the logicians' convention, which is acceptable and also used in Islamic sciences (like usul al-fiqh). He exemplifies with a classification: Body → Growing → Animal → Man. Here, body is the 'genus of genera' because there is no more general genus above it, and man is the 'species of species' because there is nothing more specific below it. The growing is a species relative to body but a genus relative to animal. The author then anticipates an objection: how can body be the most general genus when 'existent' and 'substance' are more general? And how can man be the most specific species when 'old man' and 'scribe' are more specific? The response is crucial: the logicians mean 'the more general that is essential' (al-a‘amm al-dhati), not any accidental or non-essential attribute. An essential predicate is one that enters into the definition of the thing, such that if the mind denied it, the very concept of the thing would disappear. Existence (wujud) is not essential because one can conceive of a triangle or a heptagon without knowing whether it exists; its non-existence does not destroy the concept. In contrast, 'shape' is essential to a triangle because without it, the triangle is inconceivable. The author then applies this to substance (jawhar): in the mutakallimun's view, substance is essential (as that which occupies space), so the most general genus is substance. But the logicians (philosophers) define substance as 'existent not in a subject', which is a negative definition and not purely essential. The author affirms that each group's convention is valid within their own framework, but they differ in belief and understanding. The passage is a definitional expansion, refining the earlier compressed definitions by highlighting the criterion of essentiality.

Substance as Genus: Two Perspectives
Author contrasts theologian and logician definitions of substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the theologian's definition of substance from the logician's definition and explain the implications for essence.

Core Idea
Substance means different things to theologians and logicians—both are valid in their own frameworks.

In this passage, the author addresses a key difference between kalam and mantiq: how they define 'jawhar' (substance). Understanding this difference is essential to avoid confusion when studying both sciences. The author clarifies that for theologians, substance is part of the essence (mahiyya), while for logicians it is a negative definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the theologian's definition
Find the phrase 'الْمُتَحَيِّزُ' (occupies space) in the Arabic. This is the kalam definition: substance is that which occupies space.
Note that this definition is positive.
2
Identify the logician's definition
Find the phrase 'الْمَوْجُودُ لَا فِي مَوْضُوعٍ' (exists not in a subject). The logicians define substance by absence of inherence.
The author remarks this is a pure negation (سُلْبٌ مَحْض).
3
Understand the author's position
The author says both definitions are valid according to their own systems ('بِحَسَبِ تَفَاهُمِهِمْ وَاعْتِقَادِهِمْ'), but not for us (theologians). Recognize that truth is not monolithic here.
This shows awareness of disciplinary perspectives.
4
Implications for essence
The theologian's substance is part of essence; the logician's negative definition does not make it essential. Understand why the author prefers the theologian's view for defining genus.
This connects to the later discussion of essential vs accidental.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of substance (jawhar) from the theologians' perspective (kalam) and contrasts it with the logicians' perspective. The theologians define substance as that which occupies space (mutahayyiz) and consider it part of the essence. The logicians define it as that which exists not in a subject, a negative definition. The author notes that their terminology is valid for them, but not for us. Then the author moves to accidental attributes that do not affect the essence, using examples from human development (sperm to embryo to human) and water to steam. The key point is that essential attributes (like shape for heptagon, or humanity for human) change the answer to 'what is it?' when they change, while accidental attributes (like tallness, whiteness) do not. This leads to understanding genus, species, and differentia: the genus is the common essential attribute, the species is the specific essence, and differentia is the distinguishing essential attribute.

Key Terms
الجوهر (al-jawhar): Substance; for theologians: that which occupies space; for logicians: that which exists not in a subject. [Note: The term shifts between kalam (material substance) and logic (subject not in subject).]
الماهية (al-mahiyya): Essence; the answer to 'what is it?'
الجنس (al-jins): Genus; the common essential attribute shared by multiple species.
النوع (al-naw'): Species; the specific essence that distinguishes one kind from others under the same genus.
الفصل (al-fasl): Differentia; the essential attribute that distinguishes one species from others within the same genus.

Essential vs Accidental Attributes
Author uses examples to distinguish essential from accidental attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the 'what is it?' test to determine whether an attribute is essential or accidental.

Core Idea
An attribute is essential if changing it changes the definition; accidental if it does not.

The passage uses vivid examples from human development and water to illustrate a fundamental principle: some attributes are part of the essence, others are not. Learning to distinguish them is crucial for correct definitions and sound reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recall the test question
The test is: if you change the attribute, does the answer to 'what is it?' (ما هو) change? If yes, the attribute is essential; if no, it is accidental.
The author emphasizes: 'ما هو' not 'من هو'.
2
Apply to human attributes
Consider tallness, writing, color. A human can be tall or short, white or black, and still be a human. The answer 'human' remains the same → these are accidental.
The passage gives examples: طويلاً وقصيراً، كاتباً، أبيض وأسود.
3
Apply to developmental stages
Sperm → embryo → newborn: each stage has a different answer to 'what is it?'. So being at a specific stage is essential to the identity.
The answer changes from 'سperm' to 'human'.
4
Apply to water
Water in liquid state answers 'water'. When changed to steam, the answer changes to 'steam' (or vapor). So liquid state is essential to water.
The passage: 'إذا استحال بخاراً تغير الجواب'.
5
Generalize the principle
Attributes are divided into those that change the answer (essential) and those that do not (accidental). This is the basis for identifying genus, species, and differentia.
The author concludes: 'انقسمت الصفات إلى ما يتبدل الجواب عن الماهية بتبدلها وإلى ما لا يتبدل'.

Learning Objective

What changes the 'what is it?' is essential; what does not is accidental.

Guided Path

5
Conclude the division
Attributes are divided into two categories: those that change the definition (essential) and those that do not (accidental). This is the key logical distinction.

As for what is more specific than man—such as being tall or short, writer or craftsman, white or black—this does not enter into the essence, because the answer to the request for the essence does not change with its change. For if we are asked: 'What is this?' we say: 'A man,' and he was small, then grew tall, or red or yellow, and is asked again: 'What is it?'—I do not say: 'Who is it?'—the answer would be the same. And if one points to what separates from the urethra during intercourse, and says: 'What is it?' we say: 'Semen,' then when it becomes an embryo then a newborn, and it is asked: 'What is it?' the answer is not fit to be said: 'Semen,' but rather 'Man' is said. Likewise water when heated: if asked: 'What is it?' we say: 'It is water' as in its cold state, but if it turns into steam by fire, the answer changes. So then, the attributes are divided into: those by whose change the answer about the essence changes, and those by whose change it does not change. So then, you have understood by this the meaning of genus, species, and differentia.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory explanation. The author, Ibn Sina or a commentator, aims to distinguish between essential (dhati) attributes that enter the definition (hadd) of a thing and accidental (aradi) attributes that do not. He begins by referring to those who consider substance (jawhar) a genus and hold that it enters the essence (mahiyya). Then he contrasts attributes that are more specific than 'man', such as tall/short, writer/craftsman, white/black—these do not enter the essence because when they change, the answer to 'what is it?' remains the same. For example, if a small person grows tall, the answer to 'what is it?' is still 'man'; the essence does not change. Similarly, the same semen (nutfa) becomes an embryo then a newborn, and the answer changes from 'semen' to 'man', indicating a change in essence. Water heated remains water, but when it becomes steam, the essence changes. Therefore, attributes are divided into those that alter the definition (essential) and those that do not (accidental). From this, the reader grasps the meaning of genus (jins), species (naw'), and differentia (fasl). The examples from human development and water illustrate how essential changes correspond to a change in the definition, while accidental changes do not. This is foundational for understanding the five universals in logic.

Genus is the broader category that enters the definition, species is the specific kind, and differentia is what distinguishes the species within the genus.
