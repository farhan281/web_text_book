Al-Ghazali's Coined Logical Terminology
Author explains his invented terminology for propositions, aiming for cross-school comprehension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify al-Ghazali's coined terms for negative and affirmative propositions and explain his rationale for creating them.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali invented his own logical terminology to unify the three schools of thought.

Imam al-Ghazali recognized that the terminology used in logic varied among theologians, jurists, and logicians. To prevent his readers from being limited to one school, he did not simply adopt one tradition. Instead, he selected common terms and coined new ones where necessary. This lesson will help you parse his terminological choices and understand their pedagogical purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard terms
In the source, two standard terms are given: القضية السالبة (negative proposition) and القضية الموجبة (affirmative proposition). These are the terms used in most logic books.
These are the conventional terms used by logicians.
2
Identify al-Ghazali's replacement terms
Al-Ghazali replaces القضية السالبة with القضية النافية, and القضية الموجبة with القضية المثبتة. These are his coined terms.
Notice the root change: from سلب (negation) to نفي (denial), and from إيجاب (affirmation) to إثبات (establishment).
3
Understand his motivation
Al-Ghazali states that he invented most of these terms himself because each group (theologians, jurists, logicians) had its own terminology. He did not want to follow one group exclusively, lest the reader become confined to that group's language.
His goal was to create a neutral terminology that would allow the student to understand any logical work regardless of its school origin.
4
Appreciate the benefit
By learning through al-Ghazali's terms, the student can later encounter other books and refer their terminology back to these meanings. This facilitates cross-school comprehension.
This is a pedagogical tactic to bridge gaps between scholarly traditions.

It lists several classic texts of hadith, history, and philosophy, along with their editors and publication details. The first entry is the edition itself, followed by Ibn 'Asakir's history of Damascus, al-Suyuti's Tadrib al-Rawi, and al-Ghazali's Tahafut al-Falasifa.
