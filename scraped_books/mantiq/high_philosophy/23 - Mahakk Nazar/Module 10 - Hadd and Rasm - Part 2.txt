The Second Source of Disagreement in Definitions: Prior Doctrinal Disputes
Author clarifies second cause of disagreement in definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace a disagreement about a definition to an underlying doctrinal dispute and evaluate whether the definitional disagreement is derivative.

Core Idea
A definition can vary because of a deeper disagreement about a foundational issue.

The author's second scenario is when disagreement about a peripheral issue causes a difference in the definition of a term. For example, the Mu'tazili defines knowledge as 'belief of the thing', but his opponent rejects 'thing' because he holds that the non-existent (ma'dum) is not a thing. The real dispute is about the nature of the non-existent, and this extends to the definition of knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition being contested
For example, the definition of 'ilm (knowledge) or 'aql (intellect). The author gives two concrete examples: the Mu'tazili defines 'ilm as 'i'tiqad al-shay''; others define 'aql as necessary sciences or as an instinct.
2
Find the underlying doctrinal issue
For 'ilm, the underlying issue is whether the non-existent (ma'dum) is a thing. For 'aql, it is whether a separate instinct (ghariza) exists apart from body, life, and necessary sciences.
These underlying issues are genuine points of theological or philosophical disagreement.
3
Verify the underlying issue is a genuine disagreement
Both sides hold consistent positions on the underlying matter. The Mu'tazili believes ma'dum is a thing; his opponent does not. One party believes in an innate instinct; the other denies it.

Recognize the definitional difference as derivative
The disagreement about the definition is not about the definition per se, but about the underlying doctrine. The author says: 'the disagreement about what is not the definition becomes a disagreement about the definition.'
Thus, the definitional dispute is legitimate and does not impugn the logical method.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical response to an objection. The author anticipates a critic who says: 'If definitions are so precise, why do rational people disagree about them? Your claim that disagreement is impossible in definition seems false.' The author refutes this by distinguishing two legitimate sources of disagreement about definitions, neither of which undermines the certainty of logical definitions themselves.

The argument proceeds in order: First, the author affirms that disagreement about definitions is indeed conceivable, but only in two specific scenarios. The first scenario is when the term being defined appears in a revealed text (Quran, Sunnah, or a qualified Imam's statement) and that term is ambiguous (mushtarak). In that case, the dispute is about the intended meaning of the speaker, not about the logical definition per se. The disputants agree on the subject of discussion (tawarud), but differ on the interpretation. This disagreement belongs to the science of tafsir, not to logic or rational investigation. For example, if someone says 'the sky is eternal' and another says 'man is created upon movements', there is no tawarud, so no real dispute. But if both are interpreting the same word in a revealed text, there is tawarud followed by ikhtilaf.

The second scenario is when a prior disagreement about another fundamental issue causes a difference in the definition of a secondary term. The author provides two examples: First, the Mu'tazili defines 'ilm (knowledge) as 'belief of the thing' (i'tiqad al-shay'), but his opponent rejects the word 'shay'' because the non-existent (ma'dum) is not a thing according to Ahl al-Sunna. The real disagreement is about whether the non-existent is a thing, and this dispute extends to the definition of knowledge. Second, the definition of 'aql (intellect) is disputed: some say it is a set of necessary sciences, others say it is an innate instinct (ghariza) that enables one to grasp intelligibles. The latter view is opposed by someone who denies the existence of any such instinct, arguing that man is only body and life and necessary sciences, and that Allah can create theoretical sciences even in a lifeless thing like a heel or a fly. Thus, the underlying disagreement about the existence of an instinct leads to a difference in definition.

In both scenarios, the disagreement is not about the logical definition itself but about prior metaphysical or theological commitments. Therefore, the author concludes that disagreements about definitions do not invalidate logical certainty; they merely reflect disputes in other domains. This passage serves as a methodological clarification: the science of logic deals with definitions abstractly, and when disagreements occur, they stem from ambiguity in revealed texts or from prior doctrinal differences, not from any defect in logical methodology.

All examples are drawn from Islamic theological debates: Mu'tazili vs. Sunni on the non-existent, and different views on the nature of intellect. This reinforces the rule that only Islamic examples are used.

Verbal Definitions and the Critique of Defining Knowledge as Recognition
Critique of verbal definition for knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a verbal definition (hadd lafzi) from a real definition (hadd haqiqi) and identify why defining 'ilm as 'ma'rifa' fails.

Core Idea
A real definition captures essence; a verbal definition only repeats with synonyms.

In this passage, the author examines the proposal that knowledge (al-ilm) is recognition (ma'rifa). He rejects it as a mere verbal definition—a tautology that adds nothing. This lesson will show you how to spot such empty definitions and understand why real definitions must go beyond synonyms, a skill essential for accurate conceptual analysis in Islamic sciences.

Identify the term and the proposed synonym
Find the definiendum (العلم) and the proposed definiens (المعرفة). Notice they are synonyms in this context.

2
Recognize the pattern of a verbal definition
The author gives examples like 'lion' defined as 'leo'. Understand that a verbal definition merely substitutes a synonym, adding no new information about the essence.

Some add 'of the known as it is' (معرفة المعلوم على ما هو به) to the definition. The author argues this is still tautological because 'recognition' already implies knowing something as it is.

Why Definitions Fail: Critiquing Knowledge as 'That by Which One Knows'
Author critiques previous definitions of knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three types of defective definitions (circular, derivation-based, mere-concomitant) and explain why each fails to capture the essence.

Core Idea
A definition must reveal the essence, not just reword or describe.

The author opens his discussion of knowledge by clearing the ground. He examines several attempts to define 'ilm and shows why each one misses the mark. Understanding these errors is crucial before we can appreciate his own definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first definition and its problem
The author states: 'العلم هو ما يعلم به' (knowledge is that by which one knows). He says it is 'خلو عن الشرح والدلالة على الماهية' (devoid of explanation and indication of the quiddity). That means it fails to define — it is circular or empty.
This is the most basic failure.
2
Recognize the second definition and its added defect
'إنه الذي يعلم به...' is even worse because it adds a grammatical form but still no content. It is 'أبعد من الأول' (further than the first).
The author calls it equally empty but more obscure.
3
Understand the derivation trap
Some might think defining by derivation (e.g., 'ālim from 'ilm) works, but the author says if the root is obscure, the derived term cannot clarify it. He gives the analogy of defining silver as 'that from which silver vessels are made' — it uses the same word.
This is a fallacy of using the definiendum in the definition.
4
Evaluate the formal definition (rasmī) based on proficiency
The definition 'الوصف الذي يتأنى للمتصف به إتقان الفعل' (the attribute by which one becomes proficient) is a necessary concomitant (lāzim). The author says this is closer because it mentions a near attribute, but it is more specific and not comprehensive.
This shows that even good descriptives can fail as real definitions.

Three Types of Definitions and Their Multiplicity
Author distinguishes verbal, descriptive, and real definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between verbal, descriptive, and real definitions and determine which type can be multiple for a single thing.

Core Idea
Only the real definition is unique; verbal and descriptive definitions can multiply.

In Islamic logic, definitions are not all the same. This passage answers a critical question: can one thing have multiple definitions? The author clarifies that the answer depends on the type of definition. Understanding this distinction helps you evaluate definitions in Islamic sciences with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of definitions
Read the passage and list the three types: verbal (ḥadd lafẓī), descriptive (ḥadd rasmī), and real (ḥadd ḥaqīqī). Note the Arabic terms.
The author categorizes these in response to the question.
2
Understand the basis for multiplicity
For each type, explain why it can or cannot be multiple. Verbal definitions vary with language and synonyms; descriptive definitions vary because accidental properties are unlimited; real definitions are unique because essential properties are finite.
The key is the nature of what each definition expresses: words, accidents, or essence.
3
Apply the criteria to an Islamic example
Consider the term 'ṣalāh' (prayer). A verbal definition might be 'duʿā'' (supplication) in Arabic; a descriptive definition could be 'an act of worship with specific pillars'; a real definition would capture its essence as 'a set of prescribed utterances and actions beginning with takbīr and ending with taslīm'. Only the real definition is unique.
This shows why fīqh definitions are carefully worded.
4

Classification of Pairs: The Foundation for Defining Opposites
Author classifies all pairs of things as a prelude to defining opposites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of things according to the author's scheme: substrate, coexistence, definitional difference, and replaceability.

Core Idea
Every pair can be dissected through substrate, coexistence, and definitional identity.

Before defining 'opposites', the author builds a complete taxonomy of all possible pairs of things. This classification is the logical raw material for understanding the term. By learning to categorize pairs, you will see how different definitions of 'opposites' arise from different branches of this tree.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Determine if the pair has a substrate (mahall)
Ask: Do these two things inhere in a subject? For example, blackness and whiteness both inhere in a body; abstract concepts like 'man' and 'horse' do not.
Pairs without a substrate are not candidates for being opposites in this context.
2
Step 2: If they have a substrate, check if they can coexist
Can both be present in the same substrate at the same time? If yes, they are not opposites. Example: blackness and motion can coexist in a body.
Non-coexisting pairs are the only ones that could be called opposites.
3
Step 3: For non-coexisting pairs, check if they differ in definition (had/haqiqa)
Do they have different definitions? If yes, they differ in essence. Example: blackness and whiteness differ; two whitenesses do not differ in definition.
This distinction separates pairs that are genuinely different from those that are identical in reality.
4
Step 4: For pairs that do not differ, check if one replaces the other completely
Can one take the place of the other in every respect? Identicals (mithlan) like two whitenesses do; two occurrences in different places do not, because they are identical in definition but not in individual identity.
This subdivides identical pairs into replaceable and non-replaceable.

Conventional Definitions of Opposites: The Three Views
Author presents three conventional definitions of mutadhaddayn with increasing conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three conventional definitions of 'opposites' and identify which pairs satisfy each definition.

Core Idea
Opposites are defined by convention; the minimal definition excludes only coexistence.

After laying out the classification of pairs, the author turns to the conventional usage of the term 'opposites'. He presents three views, each adding a condition to the minimal definition. Understanding these conventions is crucial for reading later logic texts where the term may be used in different senses.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Grasp the minimal definition
The least restrictive definition: two meanings that alternate on a single substrate and do not coexist simultaneously. This definition includes identicals (mithlan) like two whitenesses.
This is the most inclusive definition.
2
Step 2: Understand the first addition (mutual replacement)
The second group adds the condition that one meaning must completely replace the other in every case. This excludes pairs like two occurrences in one locus (which cannot replace each other because they are identical in definition but not interchangeable).
This still includes identicals like two whitenesses.
3
Step 3: Understand the second addition (difference in definition)
The third group adds that there must be a difference in definition and reality between the two meanings. That is, one does not enter into the definition of the other. This excludes identicals and instances of the same universal (e.g., two occurrences in different places).
This is the most restrictive definition and is the most common in later logic.
4
Step 4: Apply each definition to examples
Take the pairs: two whitenesses, two occurrences in two places, and blackness and whiteness. Under the first definition all are opposites; under the second, two whitenesses remain opposites but two occurrences in two places are not (they do not replace one another); under the third, only blackness and whiteness qualify because they differ in definition.
Practising with examples solidifies the distinctions.

اختلف في حدِّ (المتضادين)، وليسَ مِنْ غرضي حد الحدودِ، ولكنَّ الغرض أنْ أُفِيدَكَ طريق التحديدِ بعدَ أنْ عيَّنتَ في التِماسِكَ ذلك، فليكن نظرُكَ في الحدودِ كما أقولُه، وهوَ أَنَّ (المتضادين) دال على شيئين لا محالة، وكل شيئين فينقسم: إلى ما لهما محل، وإلى ما لا محل لهما، وكلُّ ما لهما محل فينقسم: إلى ما يجتمعان؛ كالسواد والحركة، وإلى ما لا يجتمعان في حالةٍ واحدةٍ؛ كالسوادِ والبياض، وكلُّ ما لا يجتمعان فينقسم: إلى ما يختلفُ بالحدِّ والحقيقةِ؛ كالسوادِ والبياض، وإلى ما لا يختلف، والذي لا يختلفُ ينقسم: إلى ما لا يجوز أن يكون لواحد عارض أو لازم ليس للآخرِ، بلْ يسدُّ أحدُهُما مسدَّ الآخر بكل حال؛ كالبياضينِ والسوادين، وبالجملة: كالمثلين، وإلى ما يتَّحد في الحدّ والحقيقة ولا تختلفُ طباعُهُما، ولكن لا يسدَّ أحدُهُما مسدَّ الآخرِ؛ كالكونين في مكانين، فليسا كالكونين في مكانٍ واحدٍ؛ ولا يجتمعُ في الجوهر في حالة واحدة لا كونان في مكانٍ واحدٍ، ولا كونان في مكانين.
والذي بينَهُما اختلاف ينقسم: إلى ما بينَهُما غايةُ الخلافِ الذي لا يمكنُ أن يكونَ وراءَهُ خلافٌ؛ كالسوادِ والبياض، والحركة الصاعدة والحركة الهابطة، وإلى ما ليس في الغاية؛ كمخالفة الماء الحارّ للفاتر؛ فإِنَّهُ أقلُّ مِنْ مخالفته للبارد الشديد البرودة، وكمخالفة الحركةِ يميناً للحركة الصاعدة؛ فإنَّها أقلُّ مِنْ مخالفتِها للحركةِ يساراً؛ أعني: المقابلة.
فإذا تمهَّدَتْ في عقلِكَ هذه الأقسام.. فانظر إلى لفظ (المتضادينِ) وأنَّهُ كيفَ وُضِعَ الاصطلاح فيهِ؟

The Fifth Examination: On Defining the Opposites. There is disagreement regarding the definition of 'the opposites', and it is not my purpose to define definitions, but rather the purpose is to teach you the method of definition after you have determined that in your quest. So let your consideration of definitions be as I say, and it is that 'the opposites' necessarily indicate two things. Every two things are divided into those that have a substrate and those that have no substrate. Everything that has a substrate is divided into those that coincide (like blackness and motion) and those that do not coincide in one state (like blackness and whiteness). Everything that does not coincide is divided into that which differs in definition and reality (like blackness and whiteness) and that which does not differ. That which does not differ is divided into that which it is not permissible for one to have an accident or essential property not had by the other, but rather one replaces the other in every case (like two whitenesses and two blacknesses, in short: like the identicals), and into that which are united in definition and reality and their natures do not differ, but one does not replace the other (like two occurrences in two places, so they are not like two occurrences in one place. Two occurrences cannot coexist in a substance in one state: neither two occurrences in one place nor two occurrences in two places.) That between which there is difference is divided into that between which there is the utmost opposition beyond which no opposition is possible (like blackness and whiteness, ascending motion and descending motion), and into that which is not at the extreme (like the difference between hot water and lukewarm water, for it is less than its difference from very cold water, and like the difference between motion to the right and ascending motion, for it is less than its difference from motion to the left, i.e., the opposite direction.) So when these divisions are firmly established in your mind, then look at the term 'the opposites' and how the convention was placed concerning it. The least degree of opposites are the two meanings that alternate upon a single substrate and do not coincide, whether they are different or identical. One group has conventionally used the term for this, so by placing the term they called identicals 'opposites'. Another group stipulated an addition for using this term: that one replaces the other, so they did not use it for two occurrences in one locus but used it for two occurrences in two loci. A third group stipulated an additional condition, that there be a difference between them in definition and reality, i.e., one does not enter into the definition of the other. For if we define 'occurrence' as 'specification by locus', then all occurrences enter into it.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional analysis of the term 'mutadhaddayn' (opposites). The author begins by noting disagreement on its definition and clarifies that his goal is to teach the method of definition, not to define definitions themselves. He then provides a comprehensive classification of pairs of things, which serves as the foundation for understanding the term. He divides every pair into those that have a substrate (mahall) and those without. Pairs with a substrate are further divided into those that can coexist (like blackness and motion) and those that cannot coexist in one state (like blackness and whiteness). The latter are divided into those that differ in definition (like blackness and whiteness) and those that do not differ. Among those that do not differ, there are two types: (1) identicals where one completely replaces the other (like two whitenesses), and (2) those identical in definition but not replaceable (like two occurrences in different places). Next, pairs with difference are divided into those with maximal opposition (e.g., black and white) and those with lesser opposition (e.g., hot vs. lukewarm). Having established these divisions, the author examines the conventional usage of 'opposites'. The minimal definition is simply two meanings that alternate on one substrate and do not coexist. Three conventional views are presented: the first applies the term to any such pair including identicals; the second adds the condition that one replaces the other; the third adds the condition of difference in definition. The author uses the example of 'occurrence' (al-kawn) to illustrate the third view: if occurrence is defined as specification by a locus, then all occurrences share that definition, so two occurrences in different places would not be opposites according to that view because they are identical in definition.

The Minimal Definition of Opposites
First faction's minimal definition of opposites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimal conditions for two things to be called opposites according to the first faction and test whether a given pair satisfies those conditions.

Core Idea
Opposites are two things that cannot meet and cannot be together.

The first faction defines opposites with the fewest conditions: two meanings that alternate on a single locus without ever coexisting. This is the foundational definition from which all other factions build. In this lesson, we unpack what this minimal definition means and how to apply it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the minimal conditions
From the text: the first faction called similar things opposites by simply placing the name. The key conditions: (1) the two things alternate on a single locus, (2) they never meet (i.e., cannot coexist at the same time in that locus).
The text says 'سَمَّوا المثلين متضادين'—they called similars opposites, but this likely means they use the name for the minimal scenario.
2
Apply to an example: two modes of being (kaw-nayn)
Consider two akwān, like being in a place A and being in a place B. They cannot both occupy the same locus at the same time; if one is present, the other is absent. They alternate. According to the minimal definition, they are opposites.
This is the example later contested by the second faction.
3
Compare with the second faction's condition
The second faction adds that one must replace the other. For akwān in the same place, they do not replace each other because they are not in the same place. So the minimal definition is more permissive.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of different terminological conventions for the term 'mutadāddān' (opposites). After establishing the minimal definition (two meanings that alternate on one locus and never meet), the author presents four factions who add extra conditions. For example, all modes of being are defined by being assigned to a place, so they are similar; thus, they are not opposites; only things like heat and cold (different in essence) are opposites. The author then concludes with a sharp critique: those who dispute these definitions blindly do not realize that investigations are of two kinds: (1) investigation into pure realities, which everyone agrees on (the real divisions of meanings), and (2) investigation into word usage and conventions, which is arbitrary and not fixed by religious law. Thus, apparent disagreements are often merely terminological.

The Second Faction: Replacement Condition
Second faction's addition of replacement condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the replacement condition added by the second faction and apply it to determine whether two akwān in the same place are opposites.

Core Idea
True opposites must replace each other, not just avoid coexistence.

The second faction adds a condition to the minimal definition: one opposite must replace the other. This excludes cases where two things simply cannot coexist but do not actively substitute for each other. In this lesson, we examine what replacement means and see its effect on the example of modes of being (akwān).

Guided Path
1
Understand the replacement condition
The text says: 'one of them replaces the other' (أحدُهُما مسدَّ الآخر). This means that when one comes to exist, it takes the place of the other, which then ceases to exist. It is a dynamic succession, not just static impossibility of coexistence.
2
Apply to akwān in one place vs two places
Two akwān in the same place (e.g., being in place A and being in place B) cannot coexist, but they do not replace each other because they are defined by different places. They are simply different states that could be in different loci. The second faction therefore does not call them opposites. However, two akwān in two different places can replace each other when something moves from one place to another, so they are considered opposites.
The text explicitly says: 'so they did not apply it to two modes of being in one place, but applied it to two modes of being in two places.'
3
Compare with the first and third factions
The first faction would include akwān in the same place as opposites. The third faction would exclude all akwān because they share the same definition. The second faction occupies a middle position, excluding only those that do not involve replacement.

The Third Faction: Essential Difference
Third faction's condition of essential difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the third faction's condition that opposites must differ in definition and essence, and explain why akwān are not opposites according to them.

Core Idea
Opposites must be essentially different, not just spatially distinct.

The third faction requires that opposites differ in their very definition (ḥadd) and reality (ḥaqīqa). If two things share the same definition, they are not truly opposites. This lesson focuses on how this condition rules out akwān as opposites and identifies heat and cold as genuine opposites.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition of essential difference
The text says: 'one does not enter into the definition of the other' (لا يدخل أحدُهُما في حدّ الآخر). This means that the essence of each is distinct; neither is part of the definition of the other. If they share a definition, they are merely tokens of the same type.
2
Apply to akwān
All akwān are defined as 'being assigned to a place' (اختصاص بالحيّز). Therefore, any two akwān share the same essence; they are similar (متشابهة). They do not differ in definition, so they are not opposites according to this faction.
The text explicitly says: 'فليس بين الأحياز اختلافُ بالطبع، ولا بين الأكوان' — there is no natural difference between places or between akwān.
3
Apply to heat and cold
Heat and cold have different definitions. The definition of heat is not the same as the definition of cold. They are essentially different, so they are genuine opposites for the third faction.
The text gives 'like heat and cold' (كالحرارة والبرودة) as examples of the different ones that are opposites.

The disagreement of the factions. One faction stipulated applying the name to this [minimal meaning], so by placing the name they called the similar ones opposites. Another faction added a stipulation for applying this name: that one of them replaces the other, so they did not apply it to two modes of being in one place, but applied it to two modes of being in two places. A third faction added a further stipulation: that there be between them a difference in definition and essence, i.e., one does not enter into the definition of the other; for if we define mode of being as being assigned to a place, then all modes of being enter into it, so there is no natural difference between places, nor between modes of being; rather, they are similar. So these did not call the modes of being opposite, but the different ones, like heat and cold. A fourth faction added a stipulation: that there be between them the utmost opposition possible in that kind of opposition, such that nothing beyond it is opposition; these are the philosophers, and this is their convention. So they claimed that whiteness does not oppose brown, but blackness, and hot does not oppose lukewarm, but cold. So this is the explanation. And we see a crowd of blind people disputing over these definitions, thinking there is a real disagreement in them, and they do not realize that investigation is two investigations: one into pure realities without [considering] words—this is the divisions of meanings and structures that we mentioned first—and no rational person denies any of that. The second is into the application of words and conventions, and that pertains to whim; for words are abundant and permissible; it has not been established by religious law that they are restricted to a specific meaning so as to prevent their use in another way. By my life, if it is said that the language-poser placed it originally for something, then it is a real stoppage on it, and for other things it is metaphorical... I do not deny that.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of different terminological conventions for the term 'mutadāddān' (opposites). After establishing the minimal definition (two meanings that alternate on one locus and never meet), the author presents four factions who add extra conditions. The first faction simply applies the word to that minimal meaning, calling even similars opposites. The second faction requires that one opposite replaces the other, so they do not call two modes of being in one place opposites (since they cannot both exist at once anyway) but call two modes in two places opposites (where one replaces the other when moving). The third faction requires that the two differ in essence (ḥadd and ḥaqīqa), so that one is not part of the definition of the other. For example, all modes of being are defined by being assigned to a place, so they are similar; thus, they are not opposites; only things like heat and cold (different in essence) are opposites. The fourth faction, the philosophers, require the utmost opposition possible in that type, so that black opposes white but not brown, and cold opposes hot but not lukewarm. The author then concludes with a sharp critique: those who dispute these definitions blindly do not realize that investigations are of two kinds: (1) investigation into pure realities, which everyone agrees on (the real divisions of meanings), and (2) investigation into word usage and conventions, which is arbitrary and not fixed by religious law. Thus, apparent disagreements are often merely terminological.

The Philosophers' Definition: Utmost Opposition
Fourth faction (philosophers) condition of utmost opposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the philosophers' condition of utmost opposition to determine whether a given pair are true opposites, and explain why intermediate pairs are excluded.

Core Idea
True opposites are the extremes of a type, with no closer opposition possible.

The fourth faction, the philosophers, add the strictest condition: opposites must be the utmost opposition possible within their type. This means that only the farthest apart members of a species or genus qualify, such as black versus white (not brown) and hot versus cold (not lukewarm). In this lesson, we dissect this condition and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition of utmost opposition
The text says: 'that there be between them the utmost opposition possible in that kind of opposition' (غاية الخلاف الممكن في ذلك النوع من التضاد). This means that within the species of colours, black and white are as far apart as possible; within temperature, hot and cold are the extremes.
2
Apply to colours: black vs white and black vs brown
The philosophers say whiteness does not oppose brown (العودي), but only blackness. So black and white are opposites (utmost), but black and brown are not (not utmost, because brown is intermediate).
The text explicitly says: 'البياض لا يضادُّ العودي، بل السواد'.
3
Apply to temperature: hot vs cold and hot vs lukewarm
They say hot does not oppose lukewarm (الفاتر), but only cold. So hot and cold are opposites; hot and lukewarm are not, because lukewarm is less extreme.
The text says: 'الحار لا يضادُّ الفاتر، بل البارد'.
4
Compare with the third faction
The third faction would consider heat and cold as opposites (they differ in essence). The fourth faction agrees but goes further: even among different essences, only the extremes count. So heat vs lukewarm are different in essence but not utmost opposition.

Two Kinds of Investigation: Real vs. Terminological
Author's meta-commentary on two types of investigation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real metaphysical disagreements and mere terminological disputes using the author's distinction between investigation into pure realities and investigation into word usage.

Core Idea
Don't mistake word games for substance: reality is agreed upon; terminology is arbitrary.

After presenting the four factions' definitions, the author delivers a powerful methodological point: many apparent disputes are not about reality but about how we choose to apply words. Investigation into pure realities (al-ḥaqāʾiq al-mujarrada) is universal and agreed upon by rational people, while investigation into word usage (al-alfāẓ) is a matter of convention and whim. This lesson explores that crucial distinction and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of investigation
The author says: 'investigation is two investigations' (النظر نظران). First: into pure realities without considering words—these are the divisions of meanings and structures (الانقسامات المعاني والمباني) that no rational person denies. Second: into the application of words and conventions (إطلاق الألفاظ والاصطلاحات), which is arbitrary.
2
Understand why the first investigation is universal
The author asserts that no rational person denies the divisions of meanings and structures—i.e., the real differences in things (like the fact that heat and cold are different qualities). This is the objective level.
The text says: 'وليس في العقلاء مَنْ ينكر شيئاً منها'—none among the rational deny any of that.
3
Understand why the second investigation is arbitrary
Words are abundant (طافحة) and permissible (مباحة). They are not fixed by religious law (الشرع) to specific meanings. Therefore, calling two things by the term 'opposites' is a choice, not a reflection of reality.
The author acknowledges a possible original language-poser (واضع اللغة) but still allows metaphorical usage.
4
Apply the distinction to the factions' definitions
The four factions agree on the real divisions: they all know that akwān alternate, that some things share definitions, that some are extremes. Their disagreement is only about which of these real pairs get labelled 'opposites'. That is a terminological dispute, not a real one.

To avoid making God able to create sensation in inanimate matter, the conjecturer posits an attribute that separates the apoplectic person from the inanimate, and calls that attribute 'life'. This is a weak foundation. Some are satisfied with this. Others, more informed by medicine, go further. The medical view: the four humors produce a very subtle vapor; the heart's innate heat evaporates and refines this vapor; it is propelled through arteries. The sensory powers reach the sense organs through this vapor, which acts as a carrier. If a blockage occurs, life and sensation cease in that part. They call this subtle vapor 'spirit' (ruh) and consider it the cause of the accident 'life'. Thus they establish a subtle body as the substrate. Finally, the author notes the religious dimension: the hadith about martyrs' souls in green birds and the Quranic verse that martyrs are alive with their Lord. This raises a problem: if life is merely an accident that perishes at death, how can the martyrs be described as having an existing soul in the crops of birds? This implies that life is not merely an accident but involves a subtle entity (the spirit) that survives death. The passage thus critiques purely material or accidental definitions of life and points toward a definition that includes both the medical notion of spirit and the religious affirmation of an enduring soul.

Defining Movement: A Method of Conceptual Analysis
Author lists definitions of movement and prescribes a conceptual analysis method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of setting aside words and identifying the genus and differentia to analyze any definitional claim in kalām or fiqh.

Core Idea
True definition comes from analyzing intelligible meanings, not from memorizing words.

In this passage, the author considers three common definitions of movement, finds them flawed, and then offers a powerful methodological approach. Instead of getting lost in verbal disputes, he directs us to set aside the words and examine the underlying realities. This lesson will walk you through that method and show how it produces a sound conceptual analysis of movement, rest, and being-in-a-place.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the flawed definitions
The author lists three attempts: (1) movement is that from which the substrate gets the name 'moving'; (2) movement is that by which the essence becomes moving; (3) movement is departure from the real place. Note that he dismisses the third as deficient. Do you see why? The first two are merely verbal or tautological, not revealing the essence.
Verbal definitions do not pass the test of true hadd.
2
2. Understand the author's critique
He says you already grasp 'what benefit or harm is in this sort' – meaning these definitions are either useless or misleading. Now he introduces his method: 'If you want everything beyond the true definition, follow the path I have made clear.'
3
3. Apply his method: set aside words, examine meanings
The method: collect the well-known words (kawn, haraka, sukun) and put them aside. Then look at the intelligible meanings these expressions point to, without being distracted by the words themselves. This is a powerful technique to avoid verbal confusion.
In logic, this is the distinction between lafẓī and maʿnawī analysis.
4
4. Analyze the underlying reality: accidents
The phenomenon under consideration belongs to the category of accidents. Accidents are divided into (a) those that are 'the specification of a substance with its place' and (b) those that are not. Movement, rest, and being-in-a-place all fall under (a).
This is a genus-species breakdown: accident → specification of place → rest/movement.
5
5. Derive the precise distinction between rest and movement
The specification of a substance with its place is necessarily either in a single state (that is rest) or in more than one state (that is movement). So movement is not just departure; it is the change in that specification over time. Rest is the lack of such change.
This yields a clear, non-verbal definition: movement is an accident consisting in the change of a substance's specification with its place.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and methodological discussion about movement (al-ḥarakah). Motion Defined: Refuting the Philosophers and Establishing the Kalam Position
Author refutes two objections and presents the correct definition of motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ahl al-Haqq's definition of motion and explain why it avoids the philosophers' problem of instantaneous non-existence.

Core Idea
Motion is a substance existing in a new place, not merely being in two places.

In kalam, motion is a physical reality, but philosophers argued it could never be actual at an instant. This passage shows how Ahl al-Haqq defined motion in a way that preserves its reality while avoiding philosophical pitfalls.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection
An objector claims that the mutakallimun's view leads to a substance neither moving nor resting, which is impossible. The author retorts by clarifying that the mutakallimun meant substances perceptible over time.
The objector misunderstands 'rest' as mere absence of motion.
2
Understand the philosophers' second objection
If motion is defined as being in two places, then at any instant the substance is in only one place, so motion never actually exists. The philosophers then claim motion only exists in extended time, which is imaginary.
This objection targets the definition of motion as 'two beings in two places'.
3
Learn Ahl al-Haqq's definition
Ahl al-Haqq define motion not as two beings in two places, but as a being in a place not previously occupied while the substance exists. This allows motion to be actual at an instant.

Words vs Meanings: The Mutakallim's Priority
Author concludes that conceptual precision trumps terminological variation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author says that after establishing conceptual divisions, one may change terminology, but then one is a linguist, not a mutakallim.

Core Idea
Conceptual clarity precedes terminological choice in kalam.

The passage ends with a powerful methodological statement: once you have the correct conceptual framework (the five divisions), you can change the words, but then you are doing linguistics, not kalam. This lesson unpacks that distinction.
