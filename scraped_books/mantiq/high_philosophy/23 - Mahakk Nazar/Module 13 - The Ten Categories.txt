Three Types of Attributes: Essential, Concomitant, Accidental
Author defines three types of predications: essential, necessary concomitant, and accidental.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given attribute as essential, concomitant, or accidental and explain the logical basis for your classification.

Core Idea
An essential attribute defines a thing; a concomitant always accompanies it but is not definitional; an accidental can separate.

In classical logic, correctly distinguishing how an attribute relates to a thing's essence is foundational for constructing accurate definitions and avoiding logical pitfalls. This lesson walks through the three categories—essential (dhātī), necessary concomitant (lāzim), and accidental (ʿāriḍ)—as presented by the author. Mastering these will sharpen your analytical skills for Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attribute in question
Start by isolating the attribute you want to classify. For example, 'body' with respect to 'tree', or 'shadow' with respect to 'horse'.
2
Test if the attribute is essential
Ask: Can the thing be understood without understanding this attribute? If the answer is no, the attribute is essential (dhātī). If yes, proceed.
Example: Without 'body', you cannot understand 'tree'. So 'body' is essential for 'tree'.
3
Test if the attribute is concomitant
If not essential, ask: Can the attribute ever separate from the thing in existence or imagination? If it cannot separate, it is a necessary concomitant (lāzim). If it can separate, it is accidental (ʿāriḍ).
Example: 'Shadow' cannot separate from a horse in sunlight (in customary reality), but you can understand horse without thinking of shadow. So it is concomitant.
4
Verify with the author's criteria
Check your classification against the author's definitions: essential is part of the definition; concomitant is inseparable but not part of the definition; accidental can be removed in imagination or existence.
The author warns that confusing concomitant (e.g., 'being created' for earth) with essential is a common fallacy.

And among the causes of many fallacies is the confusion of the concomitant with the essential; for they share the impossibility of separation, and its exhaustive treatment in this brief discussion is absolutely impossible.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains three types of attributes that can be predicated of a thing: essential (dhātī), necessary concomitant (lāzim), and accidental (ʿāriḍ). The author emphasizes mastering these relations because they are useful in both syllogism (qiyās) and definition (ḥadd).

First, he defines the essential (dhātī) as that which enters into the reality (ḥaqīqa) of a thing such that the thing cannot be understood without it. For example, blackness cannot be understood without the essence of color (lowniyya), and a horse or tree cannot be understood without body (jismiyya). If body were removed from the definition of tree, the very concept of tree would be nullified. Therefore, in definition, one must include such essential attributes—e.g., defining plant as a 'growing body'.

Second, he defines the necessary concomitant (lāzim) as that which never separates from the essence in existence, but understanding the essence does not require understanding that attribute. Example: the shadow of a horse or plant when the sun shines is always there, but one can understand horse or plant without thinking of shadow. Similarly, the earth being created (makhlūqa) is inseparable from earth, yet one can understand the earth before knowing it is created—we first know bodies, then prove their createdness. The concomitant is an appendage (tābiʿ) of the essence, not part of it.

Third, he defines the accidental (ʿāriḍ) as that which is not necessarily permanent; it can separate quickly (like the redness of shyness), slowly (like the yellowness of gold), or may not separate in external existence but can be removed in imagination (e.g., blueness of eyes, blackness of skin). Importantly, he clarifies that attributes like the earth being created or a body having a shadow are not accidental but concomitant, because their separation cannot be conceived.

Finally, he warns that confusing the concomitant (lāzim) with the essential (dhātī) is a major source of fallacies, because both are inseparable from the thing. The difference lies in whether understanding the essence depends on the attribute or not. This distinction is critical for accurate definition and valid reasoning.

Al-Muraddad: The Third Type of Words
Author defines a third category of words neither univocal nor equivocal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify a word as muraddad, distinguishing it from pure univocal (mutawāṭi'a) and pure equivocal (mushtaraka).

Core Idea
Al-muraddad bridges univocal and equivocal: shared essence, different necessary accidents.

In logic, words are typically classified as either univocal (mutawāṭi'a) – applying with the same definition to multiple things – or equivocal (mushtaraka) – sharing only the name. However, some words fall in between: they apply to things that share a core essence but differ in necessary accidents. This passage introduces a third category, al-muraddad, and illustrates it with a key theological term: 'maqdūr' (within power) as used for both the servant's action and Allah's action. Understanding this category sharpens your ability to analyze definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two criteria for al-muraddad
The passage states that al-muraddad words apply to multiple things that agree in essences (ḥaqā'iq) but differ in necessary accidents (ʿawāriḍ lāzima). Note that these accidents may be near or far.
The essence is the shared reality; the accidents are inseparable but not part of the definition.
2
Examine the example: 'maqdūr'
The author uses 'maqdūr' for both the servant's action (by acquisition, kasb) and Allah's action (by creation, ikhtirāʿ). Both share being 'within power', but the mode of power's relation differs.
This example is from Ashʿarī kalām; it shows how a single term can bridge two realities.
3
Compare with pure univocal (mutawāṭi'a)
Univocal words like 'body' apply to sky, man, and tree with exactly the same definition (corporeality). They differ only in external attributes. In muraddad, the difference is internal to the accident.
If you mistake muraddad for univocal, you ignore the intrinsic difference in the accident.
4
Compare with pure equivocal (mushtaraka)
Equivocal words like 'al-ʿayn' (eye/spring) share only the name; their definitions and essences are entirely different. In muraddad, there is a genuine commonality in essence.
If you mistake muraddad for equivocal, you overlook the shared essence.
5
Understand why a new term is needed
Since muraddad is neither purely univocal nor purely equivocal, the author coins a new term to fill the logical gap. This ensures that our classification of words accurately reflects their semantic behavior.
The term 'muraddad' means 'oscillating' – it swings between the two categories.

Commentary
This passage defines a third category of words in logic, situated between pure univocity (mutawāṭi'a) and pure equivocity (mushtaraka). The author calls it al-muraddad (the oscillating). The key characteristic is that the word applies to multiple things that share a common essence (ḥaqīqa) but differ in necessary accidents (ʿawāriḍ lāzima), which may be near or far. The author illustrates with the term 'maqdūr' ('within power') as applied to both the servant's action and Allah's action: each is called 'maqdūr', but the mode of power's relation differs (acquisition vs. creation). The author then clarifies why this category is neither pure equivocal nor pure univocal. If one mistakes it for pure equivocal, the error is that pure equivocal (e.g., 'al-mushtari' meaning both 'Jupiter' and 'buyer', or 'al-ʿayn' meaning 'eye' and 'spring') shares only the name, with no commonality in definition. But in the example, both instances share the definition of 'being within power' in some sense, so there is a commonality. If one mistakes it for pure univocal, the error is that pure univocal (e.g., 'body' applied to sky, man, and tree) shares an identical definition (corporeality) without any intrinsic difference; whereas in the example, the relation of power differs intrinsically. Hence, a distinct third term is needed: al-muraddad, indicating that the word oscillates between the two extremes, sharing some aspects of each. The author thus introduces a tripartite division of words: univocal, equivocal, and the intermediate muraddad.

The Hal and Ma Demands: Foundations of Inquiry
Author classifies the first two of four primary demands of inquiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the 'hal' and 'ma' demands and identify their subtypes based on Ibn Taymiyya's classification.

Core Idea
Every question has a specific demand; knowing it determines the form of definition.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya lays the foundation for understanding definitions by classifying the types of questions they answer. Not every question calls for a definition; only those demanding quiddity or explanation. He identifies four primary demands (matlab), and here he explains the first two: the 'hal' (whether) demand and the 'ma' (what) demand. Mastering these categories is essential for precise logical and theological inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching framework
The passage states that definitions answer only some questions. First, grasp that questions have a demand (matlab) and formula (sigha). The formulas are the 'mothers of demands.'
This is the key concept: every question type corresponds to a specific demand.
2
Recognize the four primary demands
The author says demands are many but mothers are four. Identify the first two: hal and ma. Note that the passage only describes these two in detail.
The other two (ayyu and lim) are not covered here.
3
Analyze the 'hal' demand
The 'hal' demand has two subtypes: asking about bare existence (e.g., 'Does God exist?') and asking about existence with an attribute (e.g., 'Is God alive?'). Distinguish these.
'Hal' always asks about existence, either simple or qualified.
4
Analyze the 'ma' demand
The 'ma' demand has three applications. The first is asking for explanation of a word (sharh al-lafz). Note the example: 'What is al-'uqar?' answered with 'wine'. This presupposes knowledge of the explaining term.
The other two applications (asking for quiddity and asking for description) are not detailed in this passage.
5
Apply the classification
Practice classifying sample questions into hal (existence or attribute) or ma (word explanation) categories based on the criteria given.
This skill is crucial for determining what kind of answer a question requires.

Commentary
This passage begins the first law (al-qanun al-awwal) of the first art (al-fann al-awwal) concerning the laws of logic. The author, Ibn Taymiyya, establishes that definitions are only given in response to questions, but only to certain types of questions, not all. A question is a request, and every request involves a demand (matlab) and a formula (sigha). The formulas are considered the 'mothers' of demands, meaning they are the primary categories. Although demands are numerous, their fundamental types are four. The first demand is the 'hal' (whether) demand, which asks about existence in two ways: first, asking about the bare existence (e.g., 'Does God exist?'), and second, asking about existence with a particular state or attribute (e.g., 'Is God the creator of humans?' or 'Is God alive?'). The second demand is the 'ma' (what) demand, which has three applications. The first application is when one asks for the explanation of a word, such as someone who does not know the word 'al-'uqar asking 'What is al-'uqar?' and being told 'wine' – this only works if the person already knows the word 'wine'. This classification of questions is foundational to understanding the purpose and scope of definition in logic.
