Understanding the Contradictory of a Proposition (Naqid al-Qadiyya)
Author explains the need for understanding contradictories and warns against fallacies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory of a given Islamic proposition and explain why indirect proof via contradiction is valid.

Core Idea
The contradictory is the key to indirect proof: when direct proof fails, disprove the opposite.

In Islamic logic, many arguments proceed indirectly: rather than proving a claim directly, one shows that its negation leads to absurdity. This requires a precise understanding of what constitutes a genuine contradictory. The author warns that contradictory relationships are not always obvious, and many fallacies arise from mistaking non-contradictory pairs for true contradictories. In this lesson, we will examine the definition and conditions of contradictories and see how they are used in kalam arguments.

Guided Path
1
Locate the author's definition
Find the Arabic phrase 'القضيتان المتنافيتان' which defines contradictory propositions. Note that the definition is given after the warning.
The definition is: 'كل قضيتين إذا صدقت إحداهما.. كذبت الأخرى بالضرورة' (every two propositions such that if one is true, the other is necessarily false).
2
Extract the core condition
The defining condition is mutual exclusivity and joint exhaustiveness: they cannot both be true and cannot both be false. This is a two-way relation.
This distinguishes contradictions from contraries (which cannot both be true but can both be false) and subcontraries (which cannot both be false but can both be true).
3
Identify the example
The author gives the example 'العالم حادث' (The world is originated). Determine its contradictory: 'العالم ليس بحادث' or 'العالم قديم' (The world is not originated).
This example is taken from kalam, where proving the world's origination is a central concern.
4
Understand the utility
The author states that when a claim cannot be proven directly, one can prove it by disproving its contradictory. This is the method of indirect proof (burhan al-‘aks or reductio ad absurdum).
This is widely used in Islamic theology, e.g., in arguments for the existence of God and the origination of the world.
5
Recognize the warning
Many mistakenly think contradictories are obvious, but without careful refinement, one may confuse non-contradictory pairs (e.g., contraries) with true contradictories, leading to countless sophistries.
Precise conditions include sameness of subject, predicate, time, place, etc.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of the contradictory of a proposition (naqid al-qadiyya) and explains its utility and pitfalls. The author states that sometimes a desired claim cannot be directly proven, but can be proven indirectly by disproving its contradictory. This makes understanding contradictories essential. He warns that many people think contradictories are obvious, but this leads to numerous fallacies. The reason is that at first glance, logical relationships seem clear, but without careful refinement, one easily mistakes non-contradictory pairs for true contradictories. The author then defines two contradictory propositions (al-qadiyatani al-mutanafiyatani) as any two propositions such that if one is true, the other is necessarily false, and vice versa. He gives the example: 'The world is originated'. The implied contradictory would be 'The world is not originated'. This is a classic example from kalam used in arguments for the temporal origination of the world.

Four Conditions for Genuine Contradiction
Author explains the first four conditions for true contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify violations of the four conditions that break a genuine contradiction between two propositions.

Core Idea
A true contradiction is a fragile thing: one ambiguous word, one relative term, or one shift from potential to actual and you've lost it.

The author presents the first four of six conditions that must hold for two propositions to be genuine contradictories. Understanding these conditions is crucial for avoiding fallacious arguments, especially in jurisprudence and theology where subtle ambiguities can lead to false dilemmas.

Guided Path
1
Read each condition as stated
The author lists four conditions: (1) unity of subject in essence, (2) unity of judgment, (3) unity of relation in relative matters, (4) equality in potentiality and actuality.
The conditions are sequential; each must be satisfied for a genuine contradiction.
2
Analyze the examples for each condition
For each condition, the author gives an example where the condition is violated, showing that the propositions are both true. Identify how the violation occurs in each case.
Examples: light (physical vs intellectual), coerced (different senses), father (different children), half (different wholes), water cold (potential vs actual).
3
Relate to Islamic sciences
Notice that the examples come from fiqh (coercion, guardianship) and kalam (God's creation in pre-eternity). Understand how overlooking these conditions leads to false contradictions in scholarly debates.
The author explicitly says there are many fallacies in legal and theoretical matters from this source.
4
Formulate a test for any pair of propositions
When presented with two propositions that seem contradictory, check: Are the subject and predicate identical in meaning? Is the relation the same? Are they about the same mode (potential/actual)? If any condition fails, the propositions are not genuine contradictories.
This test is a practical tool for evaluating arguments in Islamic texts.

Commentary
This passage enumerates and explains the first four of six conditions necessary for two propositions to be contradictories (naqīḍān). The author frames the discussion by stating that the truth of one necessarily follows from the falsity of the other only when these conditions are met. The first condition is unity of subject (ittiḥād al-maḥkūm ʿalayhi) in essence, not merely in wording. He illustrates with the example of 'light' (al-nūr): if one intends physical light and the other intellectual light, the propositions can both be true. He also gives a fiqh example: 'the coerced (al-muḍṭarr) is free' and 'the coerced is not free' are not contradictory if 'coerced' means different things (e.g., one trembling from fear, the other compelled by force). The second condition is unity of judgment (al-ḥukm). The author warns that the term 'ancient' (qadīm) can be ambiguous: in the Qur'an it means 'old' (a palm branch), but in kalam it means 'eternal'. If opponents intend different meanings, the propositions are not contradictory. He also revisits the coerced example to show that 'free' (mukhtār) has multiple senses. The third condition is unity of relation (ittiḥād al-iḍāfa) in relative matters. For instance, 'Zayd is a father' can be true relative to one child and false relative to another, so no contradiction. Similarly, numerical halfness depends on the whole. The author notes that many fallacies in jurisprudence and theology arise from overlooking this condition, citing the example of a woman being under guardianship in marriage but not in sale. The fourth condition is equality in potentiality (quwwa) and actuality (fiʿl). A proposition can be true in potentiality but false in actuality, like water being cold potentially but not actually. The author applies this to the theological debate about God's attribute of creation in pre-eternity (al-azal), noting that God is a creator in potentiality but not actually at that time. These conditions together ensure that a pair of propositions genuinely contradict each other, eliminating false contradictions due to ambiguity or context.

Unity of Relation in Contradiction
Third condition for contradiction: unity of relation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify cases where two statements appear contradictory but are actually compatible due to different relations, and apply the condition of unity of relation.

Core Idea
Two statements are truly contradictory only when the predicate is about the same relation.

Statements like 'Zayd is a father' and 'Zayd is not a father' can both be true if they refer to different children. This lesson explains the third condition for valid contradiction: unity of relation (ittihād al-iḍāfa). You will learn to detect fallacies in fiqh and rational sciences that arise from neglecting this condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relation in each statement
Look for implicit or explicit relations (e.g., father of whom, half of what). In the example 'Zayd is a father' vs 'Zayd is not a father', the relation is to different individuals.
The same predicate 'father' can apply to one person and not to another.
2
Check if the relation is unified
If the two statements involve different relations (e.g., father of Bakr vs. father of Khalid), they are not contradictory.
The condition requires the predicate to be affirmed/denied of the same subject under the same respect.
3
Apply to the fiqh example
Consider 'The woman is under guardianship' and 'The woman is not under guardianship'. These are both true if one refers to marriage (guardianship of agnate) and the other to sale (guardianship of judge).
Many juristic disputes stem from ignoring this condition.

Commentary
This passage defines the third through sixth conditions necessary for two statements to be truly contradictory (tanāquḍ). The author explains that for a genuine contradiction, the predicates must apply to the same subject under the same respect. The third condition is unity of relation (ittihād al-iḍāfa): statements like 'Zayd is a father' and 'Zayd is not a father' can both be true if they refer to different children—e.g., he is father of Bakr but not of Khalid. Similarly, 'ten is a half' and 'ten is not a half' are compatible if one considers half of twenty vs. half of thirty. The author explains that for a genuine contradiction, the predicates must apply to the same subject under the same respect. The third condition is unity of relation (ittihād al-iḍāfa): statements like 'Zayd is a father' and 'Zayd is not a father' can both be true if they refer to different children—e.g., he is father of Bakr but not of Khalid. Rule for Identifying Contradictory Propositions
Author states the precise conditions for genuine contradiction between propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether two propositions are genuine contradictories by applying the conditions of time, place, state, and quantity.

Core Idea
Genuine contradiction requires exact match in all conditions except truth value; for universals, one must be particular.

In logic and Islamic sciences, we often need to identify whether two statements truly contradict each other or only appear to. This lesson presents a precise rule from the text that spells out every condition required for genuine contradiction. We will learn to apply this rule to both singular and universal propositions, using examples from kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of proposition
First, determine whether the proposition is singular (about a specific individual/thing) or universal. The rule differs for each.
Singular: 'Zayd is in Baghdad'; Universal: 'Every man is a writer'.
2
For singular propositions, check the core condition
The contradictory must differ only in affirmation/negation, while agreeing exactly in time, place, state, relation, potentiality/actuality, and part/whole. If any condition differs, it is not a genuine contradiction.
Example: 'Zayd is in Baghdad at noon' vs 'Zayd is not in Baghdad at noon' – this is a contradiction.
3
For universal propositions, verify quantity difference
If the proposition is universal, one of the contradictories must be universal and the other particular. Two universal propositions about contingent matters can both be false; two particular can both be true.
Universal affirmative vs universal negative on a contingent matter are not contradictory; e.g., 'Every man is a writer' and 'No man is a writer' are both false.
4
Test for possible joint truth or falsehood
If it is possible for both propositions to be true together or both false, they are not genuine contradictories. The genuine contradictory pair must always have opposite truth values.
Use the provided examples: universal pair both false; particular pair both true; singular pair cannot both be true/false.
5
Apply the rule to new examples
Practice by constructing your own pairs and testing whether they satisfy all conditions. Extract examples from Islamic texts, such as statements about creation, obligation, or divine attributes.

The Four Types of Categorical Conversion
Enumerates four types of categorical propositions and their conversion rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the conversion rules for each of the four categorical propositions: universal negative, particular negative, universal affirmative, and particular affirmative.

Core Idea
Universal negatives and particular affirmatives convert to themselves; universal affirmatives become particular; particular negatives do not convert.

In this passage, the author introduces the concept of a convertible proposition (qadiyya mun'akisa) and systematically lists the conversion behavior of the four categorical proposition types. Understanding these rules is essential for immediate inference in logical reasoning, including in Islamic theological and legal arguments where propositions are reversed.

Guided Path
1
Recognize conversion
Understand that a proposition is convertible if, when it is true, its converse (subject and predicate swapped) is also true. The author sets aside singular propositions as not used in theoretical matters.
Conversion is an immediate inference; no additional premises are needed.
2
Identify the four types
The four categorical propositions are: universal negative (No S is P), particular negative (Some S is not P), universal affirmative (Every S is P), and particular affirmative (Some S is P).
These correspond to the standard A, E, I, O in Western logic.
3
Apply the rule for universal negative
A universal negative converts to itself: 'No S is P' becomes 'No P is S'. Example: 'No individual substance (mutahayyiz) is an accident' converts to 'No accident is an individual substance'.
4
Apply the rule for particular negative
A particular negative does not convert at all: from 'Some S is not P' you cannot infer any proposition with P as subject. Example: 'Some color is not blackness' does not yield 'Some blackness is not color' or 'All blackness is not color'.
5
Apply the rule for universal affirmative
A universal affirmative converts to a particular affirmative: 'Every S is P' becomes 'Some P is S'. Example: 'Every blackness is color' converts to 'Some colors are blackness', not 'All colors are blackness'.

This passage corrects a common logical mistake: assuming that if a specific colour like black is absent, then colour itself is absent. Conditions for Contradiction (Naqīḍ)
Conditions for contradictory propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six conditions for a true contradiction and apply them to evaluate whether two propositions are contradictory.

Core Idea
A true contradiction requires identity in subject, judgment, relation, potentiality, part, and time/place.

Contradiction is the strongest form of opposition between propositions. The outline provides precise conditions that must all be met for two propositions to be genuine contradictories. In Islamic theology, identifying a correct contradictory is crucial for arguments about God's attributes and the world's origin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key condition: union of subject
The first condition listed is 'اتحاد المحكوم عليه' (union of the subject). This means both propositions must be about exactly the same thing.
2
Identify the condition: unity of judgment
The judgment (الحكم) must be one and the same; you cannot compare a statement of existence with a statement of nonexistence regarding different aspects.
3
Identify the condition: union of relation in relationals
For relational propositions, the relation must be identical. For example, 'A is greater than B' and 'A is not greater than B' are contradictories only if the relation is the same.
4
Identify the conditions: equality in potentiality/actuality, part/whole, time/place
The propositions must be equally about potential or actual, about the same part or whole, and at the same time and place. Any change in these aspects can make the propositions compatible rather than contradictory.

Rules of Conversion ('Aks) of Propositions
Rules for converting propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the four rules of conversion to any categorical proposition and determine whether the converted proposition is valid.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate under strict rules: universal negative stays universal, universal affirmative becomes particular, etc.

Conversion (عكس) is an immediate inference that interchanges subject and predicate while preserving truth. The outline provides four rules covering each type of categorical proposition. Mastering conversion is essential for restructuring arguments in usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four types of categorical propositions
Propositions are universal affirmative (A), universal negative (E), particular affirmative (I), particular negative (O). The outline gives rules for each.
2
Rule for universal negative (E)
The outline says 'القضية السالبة الكلية عكسها مثلها' meaning the converse of a universal negative is a universal negative. Example: 'No man is stone' converts to 'No stone is man'.
3
Rule for universal affirmative (A)
It says 'القضية الموجبة الكلية تنعكس موجبة جزئية': the converse of a universal affirmative is a particular affirmative. Example: 'All men are mortal' becomes 'Some mortals are men'.
4
Rule for particular affirmative (I) and negative (O)
Particular affirmative (I) converts to itself (e.g., 'Some man is writer' becomes 'Some writer is man'). Particular negative (O) does not always preserve truth; conversion is not valid. Example: 'Some animal is not man' does not imply 'Some man is not animal' reliably.

The Three Figures of Categorical Syllogism
Three figures of categorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three figures of categorical syllogism and state the productivity conditions for the first figure.

Core Idea
The categorical syllogism has three figures, each defined by the middle term's position, with the first figure being the most productive.

The outline introduces the categorical syllogism (القياس الاقتراني) and its three figures (أشكال). The first figure requires an affirmative minor and a universal major to produce a conclusion. The second figure yields only negative conclusions, and the third figure allows proof of a specific ruling even when a general rule is absent. Understanding these figures is foundational for constructing valid theological proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the structure of a categorical syllogism
A categorical syllogism consists of two premises sharing a middle term, leading to a conclusion. The outline mentions 'لا بد من اشتراك بين المقدمتين لتحصل النتيجة' – the premises must share a term.
2
Identify the first figure and its conditions
The first figure has two conditions: 'إيجاب الصغرى' (the minor premise must be affirmative) and 'كلية الكبرى' (the major premise must be universal). The outline also notes that the middle term is subject in one premise and predicate in the other.
3
Recognize the second figure's character
The second figure is defined by the middle term being predicate in both premises. The outline states 'الشكل الثاني لا ينتج إلا القضايا السالبة' – it produces only negative conclusions. An exercise asks why it cannot produce affirmative conclusions.
4
Grasp the third figure's principle
The third figure has the middle term as subject in both premises. The outline gives the principle 'فوات الحكم العام لا يمنع من إثبات الحكم الخاص' – the absence of a general ruling does not prevent proving a specific ruling. This figure is useful when a universal premise is missing.

The Pattern of Contradiction
Defines the pattern of contradiction and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradictory propositions and determine valid inferences from their truth values.

Core Idea
Contradictories: one true, the other false – they cannot be both true or both false.

The pattern of contradiction is one of the fundamental tools in Islamic logic for immediate inference. It concerns the relation between two propositions that are exact opposites in truth value. This lesson unpacks the key rule: they cannot both hold (combine) and cannot both be absent (cease). We also examine a crucial condition: the propositions must be properly restricted (quantified) for the pattern to yield a productive conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Define contradictory propositions
Two propositions are contradictory if one is the negation of the other in both quantity and quality. For example, 'Every A is B' and 'Some A is not B' are contradictories.
The Arabic term is 'المتناقضان' (al-mutanaqidan).
2
Understand the two properties
The passage states they 'do not combine' (لا يجتمعان) meaning they cannot both be true, and 'do not both cease to exist' (لا يرتفعان) meaning they cannot both be false. Exactly one is true and one is false.
This is the law of non-contradiction plus excluded middle.
3
Apply the condition of restriction
The phrase 'عدم الحصر يلزم عنه عدم الإنتاج عند النفي' means if the propositions are not properly restricted (i.e., not subject to proper quantification like universal/particular), then negation alone yields no productive inference. The pattern requires that the contradictory relationship be between properly quantified propositions.
For example, if you only say 'Zayd is not standing', without a universal or particular quantifier, you cannot deduce anything productive about Zayd.
4
Test a pair of propositions
Given a pair, check if they are contradictories: they must have opposite truth values in every possible scenario. Use the square of opposition as a guide.
For Islamic examples, consider 'Every khamr is haram' and 'Some khamr is not haram' – they are contradictories.
