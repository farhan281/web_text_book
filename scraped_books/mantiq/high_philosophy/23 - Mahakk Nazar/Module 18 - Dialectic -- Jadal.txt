Truth's Use of Persuasion: The Proof Criterion
Author defends truth's use of persuasion for the incapable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between legitimate persuasion by truth-seekers and deceptive indoctrination by falsehood, using the author's criterion of verifiability.

Core Idea
Truth may persuade the incapable, but it can always produce proof.

A common objection holds that if falsehood uses indoctrination, truth should avoid it entirely. The author disagrees: the method itself is neutral. What matters is whether the content can be backed by demonstration when needed. This lesson unpacks that critical distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the hypothetical question posed at the beginning. It asks whether the people of truth may use the same path (method) as falsehood for common people, the weak, and children.
The objector assumes that using such methods taints truth.
2
Extract the answer and its basis
The author answers 'Yes' and gives a reason: not all souls are capable of grasping truth through demonstrative proofs (al-burhan) and discernment (al-furqan). This establishes a pedagogical justification.
The key term 'al-burhan' denotes certain proof.
3
Find the distinguishing criterion
The author contrasts the two groups: the people of truth would produce proof (burhan) if asked, whereas the people of falsehood only produce chatter. This is the critical difference—verifiability.
The phrase 'لو طلبوا بالبرهان لأظهروه' (if asked for proof, they would show it) is the linchpin.
4
Assess the refinement
The author refines the claim: the method is permissible, but it does not make truth and falsehood equal. Truth has an underlying rational foundation that can be revealed.
This is a 'critical refinement'—correcting a potential misunderstanding.

Verifying Definitions: Ittirād and In'ikās
Author explains definition verification via ittirād and in'ikās

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criteria of ittirād (consistency) and in'ikās (converseness) to test whether a definition in an Islamic legal or theological dispute is sound.

Core Idea
A definition is verified not by proof but by covering all and only its instances.

This passage from Mihakk al-Nazar presents a key method for defending definitions in Islamic dialectic. When an opponent disputes a definition, you cannot prove it by demonstration. Instead, you test it by two criteria: ittirād (every instance of the defined term satisfies the definition) and in'ikās (only those instances satisfy it). The example from fiqh—concerning the liability for a misappropriated item and its offspring—shows how this method works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under dispute
In the example, the definition of 'misappropriated' (maghsūb) is given as 'wrongful seizure of another's property'. Note the terms used: 'wrongful seizure' (al-yad al-'ādiyya) and 'property of another' (māl al-ghayr).
2
Check ittirād (consistency)
Does every case of misappropriated items satisfy this definition? The opponent concedes that the original item is misappropriated. For the offspring, we must check if it also involves wrongful seizure of another's property.
If any misappropriated item does not fit, the definition lacks ittirād.
3
Check in'ikās (converseness)
Does the definition apply only to misappropriated items? The opponent may deny that the offspring's possession is wrongful or that it is a seizure. If the definition also fits non-misappropriated items, it lacks in'ikās.
4
Compare definitions if the opponent offers one
If the opponent proposes an alternative definition, demand it and compare. Isolate the attribute where they differ, then examine that attribute for addition or deficiency.
This is the method of 'comparing two definitions' mentioned in the passage.
5
Conclude with ittirād and in'ikās
If the opponent still resists, assert that the definition is consistent and conversive. No further demonstration is possible; these two criteria are the final arbiters.
The author says: 'For this, it is not possible to establish a demonstration except that we say: It is consistent.'

Commentary
This passage presents the correct method for verifying definitions when faced with a dialectical opponent. The author states that the soundness of a definition is known by its ittirād (consistency: applying to all instances of the defined term) and in'ikās (converseness: applying only to those instances). If the opponent denies that the definition applies or that it is the correct definition, the only recourse is to assert the consistency and converseness of the definition. Thus, definition verification ultimately rests on ittirād and in'ikās, not on demonstration.

The Dialectical Method of Taird and In'ikās
Author explains how to use coextensiveness and compatibility to test definitions in dialectical disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the dialectical method of taird and in'ikās to test a contested definition in a dispute by comparing definitions and testing differences against conceivable cases.

Core Idea
The validity of a definition in a dispute is tested by its coextensiveness and compatibility, not by stubbornness.

In scholarly debates, definitions are often contested. This lesson presents a logical method to cut through stubborn disagreement: demand the opponent's definition, compare the differences, and test whether the added or omitted feature is necessary by conceiving of cases. The example of usurpation (ghasp) illustrates how taird and in'ikās resolve disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute over definition
The opponent denies that the offspring of a usurped item is itself usurped because he has a different definition of ghasp. Recognize that the dispute hinges on what constitutes a correct definition.
2
Demand the opponent's definition
When the opponent denies taird/in'ikās of your definition, ask him: 'What is your definition?' This forces him to commit to an alternative definition, which is necessary for comparison.
3
Compare the two definitions
List both definitions and find the exact point of difference: your definition (إثبات اليد العادية على مال الغير) lacks the condition of 'removal of the rightful hand' (izāla), while his includes it. The difference is a single added condition.
4
Test the difference by conceiving cases
Can you conceive of an instance that satisfies your definition but not the opponent's extra condition? Use the case of 'usurper from a usurper' (ghasib min ghasib): the second usurper has a wrongful hand but does not remove the rightful hand (already removed). Thus the opponent's condition is not necessary for ghasp.
This tests coextensiveness (taird) and compatibility (in'ikās): the definition must apply to all cases and only those cases.
5
Conclude the dispute
Since the opponent's definition is too narrow (it excludes valid cases like ghasib min ghasib), his added condition is invalid. Therefore your original definition is valid based on taird and in'ikās.

First Objection: Substance Neither Resting nor Moving
An objector argues that the team's definition leads to a substance being neither resting nor moving, which is absurd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute a dialectical objection that misapplies a general principle to an exceptional case in Kalam definitions.

Core Idea
The objector misapplies the motion/rest principle; the author refines it to persisting substances only.

In the previous lesson, a definition for the first specification of a substance was proposed. An objector now steps forward, arguing that this definition would force us to say a substance is neither resting nor moving – an impossibility in traditional Kalam. This objection exploits a well-known principle, but the author will show that it rests on a misunderstanding. Let's follow the dialectical exchange and see how the author refines the principle's domain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector says the definition leads to a substance being 'neither stationary nor moving'. Find this statement in the Arabic and note that he calls it 'absurd' (محال).
2
Locate the principle invoked
The objector appeals to the well-known Kalam principle: 'motion and rest are opposites, the substance is never free from them'. Identify this in the text and understand why the objector thinks the definition violates it.
The principle is accepted by the mutakallimun generally.
3
See how the author restricts the principle
The author points out that the principle applies only to substances that 'remain for a duration perceived by sense' (الباقي الذي يدركه الحس). The first instant of existence is not such a persisting substance, so the principle does not apply.
This is the key refinement: domain restriction.
4
Recognize the symmetrical absurdity argument
The author shows that if we define 'rest' merely as 'not moving', then by the same logic the first instant could be called 'moving' because it is 'not resting'. This symmetrical result undermines the objection.
The author dismisses the whole line of criticism as useless fuss after the meaning is clarified.

And just as the disease may become strong and repel the medicine's effect, so too there may be in the person's inner self and hidden qualities praiseworthy character and attributes pleasing to God Almighty that necessitate forgiveness for his crime, and do not necessitate the crime's departure from being a cause of punishment.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange where the author defends his definition of a crime being a 'cause' of punishment against an objection that would make punishment inevitable. The author first clarifies that 'cause' is understood through everyday analogies like eating causing fullness. The objector then argues that if it is a cause in this sense, forgiveness becomes impossible. The author responds by showing that physical causation often fails due to impediments (e.g., medicine not working, a distracted person not feeling pain). Similarly, a person's good qualities can lead to forgiveness without negating that the crime is the cause deserving punishment. Thus, the author preserves both the causal link and the possibility of mercy.

Understanding Dialectical Objections (Tahreeja) in Jadal
Series of dialectical objections without responses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of a tahreeja in a jadal text and reconstruct the implicit argument that makes it effective.

Core Idea
A tahreeja forces the opponent to revise by showing their definition leads to an undesirable consequence.

In Islamic dialectical debates (jadal), the tahreeja is a sharp objection that exposes a flaw in the opponent's definition or causal claim without needing a counter-assertion. The brief objections in this passage—about the meaning of 'punishment' and the nature of 'cause'—are classic examples. Although the responses are missing, we can analyse how each objection logically pressures the opponent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the targeted claim
Each tahreeja targets an unstated claim held by the opponent. For example, the objection 'So what is the meaning of punishment in it?' implies the opponent used a problematic definition of punishment. Locate the implicit position being challenged.
The first two objections question definitions, the third tests a causal relationship.
2
Extract the undesirable consequence
The third objection explicitly draws a consequence: 'If it were a cause in this sense, punishment would be obligatory.' This consequence is presumably unacceptable to the opponent or audience. Identify what makes it unacceptable.
The undesirability may be based on shared premises (e.g., punishment is not obligatory in this case).
3
Reconstruct the objection as a conditional argument
The third objection can be read as modus tollens: If (cause in this sense) then (punishment obligatory). But punishment is not obligatory (implicit). Therefore, it is not a cause in that sense. Reconstructing clarifies the logical force.
The missing premise is what makes the objection valid or strong.
4
Evaluate the objection's strength
Assess whether the consequence truly follows and whether it is genuinely problematic. A strong tahreeja forces the opponent to abandon or refine their claim. Consider if a counter-response could defuse it.
In jadal, the strength often depends on shared premises.
