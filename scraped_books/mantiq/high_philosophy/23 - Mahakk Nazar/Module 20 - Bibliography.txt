Reading a Bibliography in Islamic Logic Works
Author lists the sources and references used in the work.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a bibliography entry to trace the scholarly lineage and authority of a source in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the scholarly genealogy and authoritative tradition behind a text.

In Islamic scholarship, a bibliography is not just a list – it is a map of intellectual heritage. This passage from Mahakk al-Nazar lists key sources, including works by al-Ghazali and his commentators. By learning to read these entries, you can uncover the chains of authority and the foundational texts that shaped Islamic logical thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his credentials
Look at the author's full name, honorifics, death date, and madhhab affiliation. These indicators show the author's stature and school of thought.
For example, al-Ghazali is described as 'al-mujaddid, Hujjat al-Islam' – renewer and proof of Islam.
2
Determine the work's relationship to the main text
Is it a commentary (sharh), an original work, or a supercommentary? The relationship tells you how the source is used.
Al-Zabidi's Ittihaf al-Sadat al-Muttaqin is a commentary on al-Ghazali's Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din.
3
Examine publication details
Note the editor, edition number, publisher, and location. This helps assess the reliability and availability of the edition.
A critical edition by a scholar (e.g., Dr. Fahd al-Sarhan for Asas al-Qiyas) indicates scholarly verification.
4
Trace the intellectual lineage
Link the sources together: which sources comment on which, and how they connect to the subject of logic?
Al-Ghazali wrote Asas al-Qiyas on analogy; his Mahakk al-Nazar builds on that work.

The bibliography is partially cut off after 'الاقتصاد في الاعتقاد' by al-Ghazali, and includes a footnote explaining the indexing system. This demonstrates the scholarly rigor of the work, citing authoritative texts from the Islamic intellectual tradition, particularly from al-Ghazali and his commentators.

Bibliographic Sources in Mahakk Nazar
A list of referenced books including six major Hadith collections and other works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the major works cited in Mahakk Nazar into Hadith, biography, and legal theory categories and evaluate the importance of critical editions (tahqiq) in Islamic scholarship.

Core Idea
The bibliography is the foundation of scholarly authority – it shows the reliance on verified sources.

This passage presents a list of books referenced in Mahakk Nazar. The list includes the canonical Hadith collections, Siyar A‘lam al-Nubala' by al-Dhahabi, Sharh Madhahib Ahl al-Sunna by Ibn Shahin, and Shifa' al-Ghalil by al-Ghazali. Each entry provides full author details, editor, and publication data. This practice of citing critical editions reflects the modern academic standard of tahqiq.

Guided Path
1
Categorize each work
Scan the list and classify each entry as a Hadith collection (e.g., Sunan, Sahih), a biographical work (Siyar), a creedal work (Sharh), or a legal theory work (Shifa'). This shows the breadth of sources used.
2
Examine the editor information
Note the muhaqqiq (critical editor) for each work. For example, Muhammad Fuad Abd al-Baqi edited Ibn Majah and Tirmidhi. The presence of editors indicates reliance on verified texts (tahqiq).
3
Consider the publication dates
The editions are from the 20th century (e.g., 1954, 1977). Why are classical works being edited in modern times? Because of tahqiq – the effort to produce accurate, annotated versions.
4
Reflect on the significance of tahqiq
Critical editions correct errors, attribute texts properly, and provide commentary. This is essential for rigorous scholarship, especially in Hadith and legal theory.
