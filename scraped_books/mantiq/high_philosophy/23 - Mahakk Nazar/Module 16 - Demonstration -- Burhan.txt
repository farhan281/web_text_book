Conditions for a Valid Syllogism: Conceded Premises and Epistemic Levels
Author sets conditions for the conclusion to follow and explains how to prove each premise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions for a syllogistic conclusion to follow (premises must be conceded and match the epistemic standard of the field) and specify how to prove each premise using sense, experience, or transmission.

Core Idea
A syllogism is only as strong as its premises – they must be conceded and match the field's epistemic standard.

The author begins by stating a crucial point: the conclusion of a syllogism follows only if the premises are conceded (musallamat). But mere acceptance is not enough; the level of certainty required depends on the subject matter. Rational matters demand certainty (yaqin), while jurisprudential matters suffice with probability (zann). This bridges logic with epistemology, showing that logic alone does not guarantee truth – the premises themselves must be grounded in reliable sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition: premises must be conceded
The author says the conclusion only follows إذا كانت المقدمتان مسلمتين (if the two premises are conceded). This means the opponent in debate must accept them as true.
The term 'musallamat' implies agreement, not absolute truth.
2
Determine the required epistemic level
The level of certainty depends on the field: for rational (عقلي) matters, certainty (يقين) is needed; for jurisprudential (فقيه) matters, probability (ظن) suffices. Note that the same premise might be treated differently in different contexts.
In usul al-fiqh, zann is often sufficient for legal rulings.
3
Recognize the sources for proving premises
The author gives an example: the premise 'Every nabidh is intoxicating' is proven by sense (حس) and experience (تجربة). The premise 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' is proven by transmission (نقل) – specifically the hadith 'Every intoxicant is forbidden.'
This shows that logical syllogisms rely on empirical and revealed evidence.
4
Apply the condition to ensure the syllogism benefits
If you cannot prove either premise by its appropriate method, the syllogism will not benefit you (لم ينفعك القياس). This underscores that logic is a tool that depends on external knowledge.
The syllogism is only as useful as the evidence supporting its premises.

Commentary
This passage lays out the epistemological conditions for a syllogism to yield a conclusion. The premises must be accepted (musallamat) by the interlocutor. The premise 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' is proven through transmitted text (naql), specifically the hadith 'Every intoxicant is forbidden.' If one cannot prove either premise by its appropriate method, the syllogism is useless. This highlights the dependence of logic on external sources of knowledge: sense experience and revelation.

The Objection and the Seven Sources of Certainty
Objection and response on the attainability of certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Ghazali's response to the objection that certainty is unattainable and list the seven sources of certainty he identifies.

Core Idea
Certainty is attainable; al-Ghazali lists seven sources, beginning with self-evident truths.

Al-Ghazali has just laid out strict conditions for constructing certain syllogisms. Now he anticipates a reader's despair: if the conditions are so rigorous, is certainty even possible? In this lesson, we follow his reassuring answer: certainty has identifiable sources, and he enumerates seven categories, starting with the most fundamental – primary truths. Understanding this classification is essential for building reliable knowledge in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the hypothetical speaker's complaint about the difficulty of attaining certainty. Notice the tone of despair ('أين تحصل هذا عن نيله').
The objection is a rhetorical device to motivate the inquiry.
2
Trace al-Ghazali's response
Observe that he does not dismiss the objection but directly addresses it by distinguishing sources of certainty from sources of opinion.
He explicitly says he is not discussing opinion (al-zunun) because those are obvious to jurists.
3
Extract the number and naming principle
Al-Ghazali states there are seven sources (سبعة أقسام) and that each will be named after its cause. Note the word 'مجامعها' meaning their total.
The number seven is specific to al-Ghazali; later logicians often had different lists.
4
Connect to the next lesson
The first source is introduced with 'الأوّلُ:' – this signals the beginning of the explanation of primary truths, which will be the focus of the next lesson.
This lesson stops before the detailed definition of awwaliyat.

Commentary
This passage from al-Ghazali's Mahakk al-Nazar has an epistemological character. It begins by concluding the previous discussion: when you construct a syllogism from genuinely certain premises while observing all the required conditions, the conclusion you obtain will be necessarily certain, coextensive with the certainty of the premises. Then the author inserts a section heading ('Chapter Two: On the sources of certainty and belief') and immediately anticipates an objection. A hypothetical interlocutor says: 'You have made the conditions for certainty so stringent that you have rendered it unattainable and driven seekers to despair. So where can we find such certainty? What are its instruments and sources?' Al-Ghazali responds that he is not going to discuss the sources of mere opinion (ann al-zunun), since those are obvious to jurists and common people. Instead, he will enumerate the sources of genuine certainty (al-yaqin) and of beliefs that are mistaken for certainty. He recalls that these sources, as far as he knows, are confined to seven categories. For each he will assign a name derived from its cause. Then he begins with the first source: al-awwaliyat (primary truths). He defines them as pure intellectual propositions that the bare, disembodied intellect necessitates without any assistance from sense perception or imagination. Examples include one's knowledge of one's own existence; that a single thing cannot be both eternal and originated; that if one of two contradictories is true, the other must be false; and that two is more than one, along with similar cases. He emphasizes that these propositions are found imprinted in the intellect from the moment of its existence—so much so that the rational person imagines he has always known them and cannot recall acquiring them. This explains why they are called 'primary' or 'a priori': they are not derived from experience but are self-evident foundations for all other knowledge.

Primary Truths: The First Source of Certainty
Definition and examples of primary truths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define primary truths (al-awwaliyat) according to al-Ghazali and identify their key characteristic of being known purely by the intellect without sense or imagination.

Core Idea
Primary truths are imprinted in the intellect without sense or imagination – the bedrock of all certain knowledge.

Having announced the seven sources of certainty, al-Ghazali now explains the first and most foundational: primary truths (al-awwaliyat). These are propositions that the bare intellect necessitates without any help from the senses or imagination. They are self-evident and serve as the starting point for all demonstration. This lesson unpacks that definition and its implications for building certain knowledge in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the term being defined
Identify 'الأوليات' as the term al-Ghazali is defining. Note the phrase 'وأعني بها' (I mean by them) which introduces the definition.
2
Parse the definition
The definition has two parts: (1) these are 'عقليات محضة' (pure intellectual matters) – propositions known by reason alone; (2) they are acquired 'من غير استعانة بحس وتخيل' – without the aid of sense or imagination.
This distinguishes awwaliyat from empirical or imaginative knowledge.
3
Analyze the examples
Al-Ghazali gives four examples: knowledge of one's own existence, that one cannot be both eternal and originated, the law of non-contradiction, and that two is greater than one. For each, consider: could this be known through the senses?
The examples illustrate the self-evident nature of primary truths.
4
Understand the imprinting metaphor
Al-Ghazali says these propositions are 'found imprinted in the intellect from the beginning of its existence' (تُصادف مرتسمة في العقل مند وجوده). This means they are not learned but are immediately present when the intellect actualizes.
This is not innate ideas in the Platonic sense, but immediate recognitions upon first thought.

Calibrating the Imagination with Syllogisms
Author explains how to use syllogisms to overcome the imagination's limitations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how syllogisms can correct the imagination's limitations in abstract reasoning from the passage.

Core Idea
The imagination is reliable in sensory matters but fails in the abstract; syllogisms force acceptance of necessary conclusions.

This passage introduces the second path—a method for dealing with the imagination's resistance to non-sensory truths. The author explains that the imagination (wahm) is not entirely false; it agrees with reason on sensory and mathematical matters. The trick is to use syllogisms built from certain premises that the imagination accepts, and the syllogistic form generates a necessary conclusion. When the imagination recoils, reason recognizes it as a limitation, not a refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of the imagination's reliability
Find the domains where the author says the imagination agrees with reason: impossibility of a person in two places, geometry, arithmetic, and sensory perception. List these.
These are domains where the imagination functions reliably.
2
Locate where the imagination fails
The imagination only disputes regarding non-sensory matters because it represents them in sensory form. Identify the phrase 'فيما وراء المحسوسات'.
This is the key limitation: the imagination cannot grasp abstract concepts directly.
3
Extract the 'trick of reason' (حيلة العقل)
The author describes taking certain premises (يقينية) that the imagination accepts, organizing them into syllogisms, and using the necessary conclusion as a judge. Write the steps in your own words.
The premises must be ones the imagination already accepts (e.g., mathematical truths).
4
Understand the diagnostic process
When the imagination accepts premises, form, and the principle that such form produces a necessary conclusion, yet still rejects the conclusion, reason knows this is due to a deficiency in the imagination's nature. Paraphrase the author's reasoning.
This shows the syllogism serves as a corrective tool, not just a proof.
5
Reflect on epistemological significance
How does this method apply to Islamic theology? For example, arguments about God's existence or attributes often involve abstract concepts. Consider how a syllogism can overcome the imagination's resistance.
This connects the passage to actual Islamic sciences like Kalam.

Core Idea
Premises must be more evident than the conclusion, or the argument collapses into a vicious circle.

In demonstration, we use premises to prove a conclusion. But what if the premises are just as obscure as the conclusion — or even less known? The Lawgiver's Custom as a Criterion for Valid Qiyas
Critiquing a flawed analogy between female and male slaves, emphasizing need to know Lawgiver's custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an analogical extension (qiyas) is invalid due to ignorance of the Lawgiver's consistent custom, using the example of the male vs. female slave in compulsion to marriage.

Core Idea
The Lawgiver's custom determines which differences matter in qiyas, not absence of an obvious fariq.

This passage critiques a flawed analogy between a female slave (umm al-walad) and a male slave ('abd) regarding compulsion to marriage. The author argues that simply noting the absence of a distinguishing characteristic (al-wasf al-fariq) is insufficient; one must know the Lawgiver's consistent custom in similar rulings. This lesson will guide you through the logical steps of analyzing such critiques.

Guided Path
1
Identify the flawed analogy
The claimant says that the ruling of compulsion to marriage for a female slave applies to a male slave by analogy due to 'closeness' (qurb). Find the parts in the Arabic that describe this claim (e.g., 'إلحاق الأمة بالعبد للقرب').
2
Find the critique
The author rejects this, showing that femininity is a valid distinguishing characteristic in marriage matters because the Lawgiver's custom treats male and female slaves differently in marital compulsion. Note the phrase 'فلم يكن العبد في معنى الأمة في الإجبار'.
3
Examine the method
The author contrasts this with areas where the Lawgiver treats them alike (e.g., emancipation), showing that knowledge of the custom is essential. Look at 'يسلك بالذكر والأنثى فيه مسلكاً واحداً' and the example of black and white.
4
Apply the principle
Recognize that valid qiyas requires knowing the Lawgiver's 'ada (custom) across relevant rulings, not just superficial similarity. Consider the prostration example: time difference does not affect the ruling because the custom is consistent.
This step reinforces that custom is learned through repeated exposure to Sharia rulings.

The Third Rank of Preponderance of Opinion (Ghalabat al-Zann)
Defining the third rank of ghalabat al-zann with examples from emancipation and expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the third rank of ghalabat al-zann, distinguishing it from higher ranks by noting when the omission of the distinguishing characteristic is probable (maznun) rather than certain.

Core Idea
The third rank arises when the distinguishing characteristic's omission is probable, not certain, requiring careful weighing of countervailing evidence.

This passage defines the third rank of ghalabat al-zann, a key concept in usul al-fiqh for determining the effective cause in analogical reasoning (qiyas). Here, the omission of the distinguishing feature is merely probable, not certain, meaning the mujtahid must weigh conflicting indications. The author illustrates with examples from emancipation and expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rank being defined
The passage specifies the 'third rank' of ghalabat al-zann. Note that this rank deals with situations where the omission of the distinguishing characteristic is probable (maznun) rather than certain (maqtu' bihi).
2
Understand the condition: omission is probable
The key condition is that the omission of the description that differentiates the original case from the analogous case is only probable, not certain. This contrasts with higher ranks where the omission is certain.
The term 'inhidhaf al-wasf al-fariq' is used.
3
Examine the first example: emancipation of a specified half vs. common share
The author compares emancipating a specified half of a slave to emancipating a common share (al-juz' al-shayi'). The distinguishing characteristic is whether the part is specified or common. The omission of this characteristic is probable because in other transactions (sale, gift, pledge) the law distinguishes between specified and common, but in emancipation and divorce the common is treated like the specified.
This shows countervailing considerations (mu'aradat).
4
Examine the second example: omission of intercourse in expiation
Malik's example about expiation (kaffara) for breaking the fast: the distinguishing characteristic of intercourse is omitted analogically. Just as eating bread would lead to analogizing meat, fruit, water, the means of breaking the fast (alat al-iftaar) should not affect the obligation. The omission is probable, not certain.
5
Notice the role of countervailing considerations
Both examples involve countervailing indications from other areas of Sharia that weaken the probability, preventing it from reaching certainty. The mujtahid must weigh these and follow his preponderant opinion.
This distinguishes the third rank from higher ranks where countervailing are absent or weak.

Commentary
This passage defines the third rank of ghalabat al-zann (preponderance of opinion) in the methodology of determining the effective cause ('illa). Here, the omission of the distinguishing characteristic (inhidhaf al-wasf al-fariq) is probable (maznun) rather than certain (maqtu' bihi). It is probable that this characteristic has no effect on the ruling of emancipation, but it is not as certain as the omission of femininity in earlier examples. This is because the Law (Sharia) distinguishes between specified and common in transactions like sale, gift, and pledge, which countervails the assumption. However, in the specific context of emancipation and divorce, the common share is treated like the specified, so the matter becomes probable. The mujtahid must follow his preponderant opinion. A second example, from Malik's school, involves the omission of the attribute of intercourse (jama') in the expiation for breaking the fast. If the Law obligated expiation for eating bread, we would analogize to meat, fruit, water, because the means of breaking the fast (alat al-iftaar) should not affect the obligation—similar to how differences in means do not affect the cause. Thus, the omission of 'intercourse' is probable, leading to the third rank.
