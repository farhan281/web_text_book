Author enumerates and defines the three arts of logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three major divisions of logic as presented by the author.

Core Idea
Logic has three arts: building blocks, the syllogism itself, and appendices leading to definition.

The author organizes the entire science of logic into three distinct arts: preliminaries (sawābiq), the syllogism (qiyās), and appendices (lawāḥiq). Understanding this tripartite structure is essential for grasping how classical logic textbooks are organized. In this lesson, we will unpack each art and see how they relate to one another.

2

3
Identify the third art – appendices
These are topics that are revisited later, and after them the author will discuss definitions and their conditions.

Understand the relationship among the arts
The second art is the main purpose; the first provides the material, the third deals with ancillary topics. The author uses a simile: the sources (primary sciences) are like a garment, and the syllogism is like a shirt – the garment is the fabric from which the shirt is cut.

The Three Types of Verbal Signification: Mutabaqa, Tadammun, Iltizam
Author defines three types of verbal signification with examples and warns against using iltizam in rational argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any verbal signification as mutabaqa, tadammun, or iltizam and explain why iltizam is avoided in rational argument.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: whole, part, or necessary companion – but only the first two are safe for logical proof.

In this passage, the author introduces a foundational division in Islamic logic: how words convey meanings. Understanding the three types of verbal signification is crucial for avoiding errors in reasoning. The author not only defines each type but also issues a strong warning about the third type, iltizam, warning that it leads to infinite regress if used in rational inquiry. This lesson will help you distinguish these types and apply the author's caution in your own reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Read the first definition sentence. What are the three Arabic terms? (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam). Note that the author says they are the only types of verbal signification.
2
Understand mutabaqa with example
How does the word 'house' signify the whole house? This is direct correspondence – the word matches the full meaning. This is mutabaqa.
3
Understand tadammun with example
How does 'house' signify the roof? Because the roof is part of the house. Tadammun is a part-whole relation. The word signifies a part of its meaning.
The author also gives the example of 'horse' signifying the body, since horseness includes being a body.
4
Understand iltizam with example
How does 'roof' signify the wall? Not as part, but as a necessary external companion. The roof cannot exist without walls. This is iltizam – signification by necessary concomitance.
The wall is not part of the roof but is necessarily associated with it.
5
Apply the warning
The author forbids using iltizam in rational argument because it leads to an infinite chain (wall needs foundation, foundation needs earth, etc.). In contrast, mutabaqa and tadammun are bounded. So in logical analysis, always check whether your terms rely on iltizam and replace them with clear definitions using only mutabaqa or tadammun.
This ensures arguments are finite and sound.

The first chapter: On the signification of words for meanings. Know - may God grant you success - that the discussion of this is lengthy, but I do not address what I think you can grasp by yourself, and I confine myself to alerting you to divisions whose neglect gives rise to many errors. The Four Categories of Terms: Mutaradifa, Mutabayina, Mutawati'a, Mushtaraka
Author classifies terms into four categories based on word-to-meaning relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of terms into one of the four categories: mutaradifa (synonyms), mutabayina (diverse), mutawati'a (univocal), or mushtaraka (equivocal).

Core Idea
Words relate to meanings in four ways: many words one meaning, many words many meanings, one word many individuals of same kind, one word many distinct meanings.

In Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh, understanding how words relate to meanings is crucial for precise definitions. This lesson introduces the four-fold classification of terms based on the relationship between multiple words and multiple meanings. The author presents the categories and explicitly defines the first two: synonyms and diverse terms. The remaining two—univocal and equivocal—complete the framework essential for avoiding ambiguity in Islamic legal and theological discourse.

Guided Path
1
Understand the overall classification
The passage considers multiple words (alfaz muta'addida) in relation to multiple meanings (musammayat muta'addida). The author enumerates four categories and invents terms for them. Grasp that the classification is about the mapping between word forms and meanings.
The four terms coined by the author are: al-mutaradifa, al-mutabayina, al-mutawati'a, and al-mushtaraka.
2
Define mutaradifa (synonyms)
Synonyms are different word forms that refer to the same meaning without any difference in coverage. The author gives examples: al-khamr and al-'uqar (both meaning wine), al-layth and al-asad (both meaning lion). Verify that each pair covers the same meaning from the same angle.
In Islamic law, synonyms require careful handling; e.g., are 'khamr' and 'nabidh' synonyms? This affects rulings.
3
Define mutabayina (diverse terms)
Diverse terms are different words for different meanings. Examples: blackness (sawad), power (qudra), lion, key, sky, tree, etc. This is the majority of language. Note that the meanings are completely unrelated.
These are straightforward; no ambiguity between word and meaning.
4
Extend to the remaining two categories
Although the passage only explicitly defines the first two, the complete classification includes mutawati'a (univocal: one word applied to many individuals of the same nature, like 'insan' for all humans) and mushtaraka (equivocal: one word with multiple distinct meanings, like 'ayn' for eye, spring, gold). Understand how these differ from the first two.
Mutawati'a is like common nouns; mushtaraka is like homonyms. Both are critical for avoiding fallacies of ambiguity.

Three Relations of Meanings: Equal, General, Specific
Author classifies the ratio between two meanings into equality, generality, and specificity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the relation between two concepts (equal, more general, or more specific) using their extensions in Islamic kalām examples.

Core Idea
The relation between concepts is either equal, general, or specific – a tool for valid qiyas.

In this chapter, the author presents a fundamental classification of how two meanings can relate to each other. This classification is essential for constructing sound syllogisms (qiyas). You will learn to distinguish between coextensive concepts, broader concepts, and narrower concepts, using examples from kalām cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two concepts being compared
Look at the pair given in the text (e.g., 'body' and 'occupying space'). Determine what each concept denotes.
These are usually terms from Islamic theology or philosophy.
2
Check if the concepts are coextensive
Ask: Does every instance of the first fall under the second, and vice versa? If yes, they are equal (musawi). Example: every body occupies space, and everything occupying space is a body (on a certain view).
Equality does not mean identical definition, only same extension.
3
Check if one concept is broader
If every instance of the second falls under the first, but not vice versa, then the first is more general (a'am). Example: existence is more general than body, since not every existent is a body.
Generality means the concept applies to a larger set of individuals.
4
Check if one concept is narrower
If every instance of the first falls under the second, but not vice versa, then the first is more specific (akhass). Example: motion is more specific than body, because not every body is in motion.
Specificity means the concept applies to a subset.
5
Apply the classification to new pairs
Now practice with other pairs from Islamic sciences, such as 'necessary' and 'existent' in kalām, or 'lawful' and 'permissible' in fiqh.
Always consider the extension (individual instances) rather than the definition.

Defining Contraries Through Classification
Division of all pairs into types to isolate the definition of contraries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the full classification of pairs to identify which pairs qualify as contraries (al-mutaḍāddān).

Core Idea
Not everything that cannot coexist is a contrary; contraries must also differ in definition.

The author of this passage wants to teach you the method of definition, not just give you a ready-made definition. He takes the term 'contraries' and shows how to arrive at its precise meaning by systematically dividing all pairs of things. Follow his division carefully: it is a model for how logical definition works in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the starting point
Notice the author begins with the fact that 'contraries' refers to two things (شيئين). He then divides every pair into categories. Your task is to trace this division step by step.
The division is exhaustive: every pair falls into one branch.
2
First division: with or without a locus
Some pairs (like accidents) have a subject in which they inhere (محل), while others (like substances) do not. Contraries must have a locus, so pairs without a locus are excluded.
3
Second division: can they coexist?
Among pairs with a locus, some can be present together (e.g., blackness and motion in the same body) and some cannot (e.g., blackness and whiteness). Contraries cannot coexist, so pairs that can coexist are eliminated.
4
Third division: do they differ in definition?
Among pairs that cannot coexist, some differ in definition and reality (like blackness and whiteness) and some do not (like two individual whitenesses). Contraries must differ in definition. Thus, similars (مثلان) are excluded even though they cannot coexist.
This is the critical differentia: contraries are not simply non-coexisting pairs; they must have different essences.
5
Extract the definition
After the divisions, the only remaining category is: pairs that have a locus, cannot coexist in one state, and differ in definition and reality. That is the complete definition of contraries.
The definition is not stated explicitly but emerges from the classification.

Classifying Acts by Indication of Punishment: Wajib, Makruh, and Haram
Author classifies acts into wajib, makruh, and haram based on preponderance and indication of punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish wajib, makruh, and haram based on their indication of punishment and identify the epistemological basis (certain vs. conjectural) for each category.

Core Idea
The three legal categories are defined by what type of punishment they indicate, not by the act itself.

In this passage, the author presents a logical classification of acts according to the indication of punishment. This is a foundational tool in usul al-fiqh, as it shows how rulings are derived from evidence. We will parse the criteria for each category and see how they relate to certainty and conjecture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of division
The author divides acts based on ‘preponderance’ (tarjih) by punishment. Acts whose omission is preponderant are divided further; those whose commission is preponderant are wajib. Find the phrase ‘al-murajjah tarkuhu’ and note how it sets up the classification.
The division is not based on the act itself but on the consequence (punishment).
2
Distinguish certainty within wajib
The author notes that wajib can be known with certainty (like the five prayers) or by conjecture (mujtahad fihi). This shows that even within wajib, the epistemological strength varies.
The term ‘wajib’ is reserved for acts known with certainty, but the author acknowledges other acts with conjectural obligation.
3
Parse the two divisions of al-murajjah tarkuhu
Acts whose omission is preponderant split into: (1) those indicating no punishment for commission (makruh) and (2) those indicating punishment for commission (haram/mahdhar/ma'siya). Identify these in the passage and note their defining features.
The author also mentions a subcategory: acts with punishment in this world (e.g., from hadith).
4
Extract the definitions as conventional classifications
The author emphasizes that these are conventional terms (‘la haraj fi hadha al-istilah’). Recognize that the definitions are based on indication (ish'ara) from various sources (text, inference, etc.), not on intrinsic qualities.
This avoids rigid essentialism and accommodates ijtihad.
