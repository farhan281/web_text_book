The Logical Hierarchy: Syllogism, Premises, and Concepts
Author decomposes syllogism into premises, concepts, and terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decompose a syllogism into its constituent premises and concepts, and identify the minimal units required for logical reasoning.

Core Idea
A syllogism is two premises; each premise is two concepts; each concept is a simple meaning indicated by a term.

The author does not stop at the general principle; he provides a concrete hierarchy that every logic student must follow. He breaks down the syllogism (qiyas) into its minimal components: two premises, each composed of two concepts (ma'rifat). These concepts, in turn, are simple meanings (ma'ani) expressed by terms (alfaz). This lesson traces that decomposition and explains why this hierarchy is essential for correct reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of a syllogism
The author states that the least from which a syllogism is composed is two premises (muqaddimat). Each premise is an item of knowledge that can be true or false. Locate this in the Arabic.
These two premises, when combined, yield a conclusion. The author will discuss the conclusion later.
2
Break down a premise into its minimal parts
Each premise is formed from two concepts (ma'rifatan). One concept is the subject (mukhbar 'anh) and the other is the predicate (khabar) or description. Find where the author explains this.
This is the basic unit of a proposition: subject + predicate.
3
Trace further to simple terms and meanings
Every simple (mufrad) is a meaning (ma'na) and is indicated by a term (lafz). The author insists we must study simple meanings and terms before combining them into premises. Why is this important?
This ensures that the building blocks are sound before they are used in arguments.
4
Apply the hierarchy to a concrete syllogism
Take a simple syllogism from Islamic law: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. Nabidh is an intoxicant. Therefore, nabidh is forbidden.' Identify the two premises, the two concepts in each premise, and the simple terms and meanings involved.
This shows how the hierarchy works in practice.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological introduction that establishes the foundational principle for studying logic: one must begin with the simplest components before moving to composites. The author, likely Ibn Sina or a follower, argues that any composite (murakkab) can have defects in either its form (structure) or its matter (components). He illustrates this with concrete examples: a shirt's defect may be in the cloth or the stitching; a chair's defect in the wood or the joints; a wall's defect in the bricks or the mortar; a ceiling's defect in the beams or the plaster. This principle applies to syllogism (qiyas) and definition (hadd) as well. The author then draws an analogy to building a house: a builder first prepares simple tools (beams, bricks, clay), then if he needs bricks, he prepares their simple components (water, straw, soil, mold). He starts with simple parts, combines them into composites, and continues until the building is complete. Similarly, a student of logic must first examine the structure and form of the syllogism, and the material in which this structure is placed—the premises. The minimal syllogism consists of two premises (muqaddimat), which are two items of knowledge (ilman) that can be true or false. Each premise minimally consists of two concepts (ma'rifatan), one serving as subject (mukhbar 'anh) and the other as predicate (khabar). Thus the syllogism divides into two premises, and each premise divides into two concepts that are related. Every simple (mufrad) is a meaning (ma'na) and is necessarily indicated by a term (lafz). Therefore, we must first study simple meanings and their categories, and simple terms and their modes of indication. Only after understanding individual terms and meanings do we combine two meanings into a premise, examine the rules and conditions of premises, then combine two premises into a syllogism and study the correct method of formulation. The author strongly warns against skipping this order: anyone who tries to understand syllogism without this step-by-step path is like someone who wants to write joined letters without knowing how to write individual words, or wants to write words without knowing individual letters. This necessity is so absolute that even Allah's power is not described as creating composite things without the simple components (atoms), nor teaching joined writing without teaching letters and words. Thus, the author declares that the discussion of syllogism must cover three arts (funun), which will structure the remainder of the book.

The Three Arts of Logical Inquiry
Author outlines the three-part structure of the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to outline the three arts of logical inquiry and explain their logical order as presented in the source.

Core Idea
Syllogistic knowledge must be built from its simplest components, like writing from letters.

This passage presents the overall structure of the book by breaking down the syllogism into its basic parts. The author insists that you cannot understand the whole without first mastering the parts, just as you cannot write sentences without knowing letters. This principle guides the entire logical curriculum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three arts
Find where the author explicitly enumerates the three 'fann' (arts) in the Arabic. Note their names: al-sawabiq (preliminaries), ta'lif al-muqaddimat (composition of premises), and al-lawahiq (appendices).
The third art includes the study of definitions after the appendices.
2
Understand the order of the first art
See how the first art progresses: words → meanings → composing simple meanings into tasdiqi knowledge (a premise). Each step builds on the previous, similar to learning letters before words.
3
Recognize the writing analogy
The author compares someone who tries to learn syllogism without basics to a writer who cannot write letters. This analogy illustrates why the order is necessary.
Apply the same reasoning to any composite: parts precede the whole.
4
Relate the arts to the book's content
The first art prepares you; the second art is the main goal (correct syllogism); the third art is supplementary before discussing definitions.
The author says the second art includes 'primary certain sciences' that form the basis of composition.

Commentary
This passage is a methodological introduction outlining the structure of the book. The author begins by analyzing the syllogism into its components: a syllogism is composed of two premises, each premise of two cognitions (a subject and a predicate term). Since every simple term is a meaning indicated by a word, we must first study simple meanings and their divisions, and simple words and their modes of signification. Only after understanding simple words and meanings can we combine two meanings into a premise, examine its ruling and conditions, then combine two premises into a syllogism and study correct formulation. The author emphasizes this order as necessary: anyone who tries to understand syllogism without this foundational study is like a writer who cannot write letters or words—an impossible endeavor. This principle applies to all composites: parts necessarily precede the whole. The author then lays out the book's three-part plan. The first art is on preliminaries (al-sawabiq): studying words, then meanings, then composing simple meanings to form tasdiqi knowledge suitable as a premise. The second art is the main purpose: how to compose premises to yield a correctly structured syllogism; this art includes the primary certain sciences that form the basis of syllogistic composition, and their relation to the syllogism is like cloth to a shirt—the premises are the material from which the syllogism is shaped. The third art covers appendices, after which the author will turn to definitions and their conditions. This structure ensures a systematic progression from simple to composite, paralleling the natural order of learning.

Why Composites Require Simples First
Author establishes the principle that parts precede composites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that parts necessarily precede the whole to an example from an Islamic science.

Core Idea
Every composite presupposes its parts; you cannot master the whole without the simples.

The author extends the writing analogy to a universal principle: all composites require knowledge of their parts. This lesson focuses on that principle and how it applies not only to logic but to other fields like fiqh and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Extract the universal principle
Find the sentence where the author says 'the parts of the composite necessarily precede the composite.' This is the key principle.
2
Understand the theological application
The author uses God's power as an example: God is not described as creating the composite world without the simples. This reinforces the necessity of the order.
This is not a limitation on God, but an illustration of logical priority.
3
Apply the principle to another field
Think of an example from usul al-fiqh where a complex ruling (e.g., qiyas) requires prior knowledge of its simpler components (e.g., the 'illah, the asl).
Just as a syllogism requires premises, a qiyas requires its constituent parts.

A colophon (khatima) is a scribal note at the end of a manuscript, not part of the original author's text. It provides crucial historical data about the copying process. Understanding colophons helps you situate a text in its transmission history. This lesson uses the colophon of al-Ghazali's Mihak al-Nazar to practice extracting such information.

The Science of Syllogism: Structure and Topics
Author outlines the first major section on syllogism and its subtopics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the structure and key topics of the syllogism section as outlined by the author.

Core Idea
Every syllogism is composed of two assents (premises), each containing two concepts.

The author now introduces the first major section of the book: the science of syllogism (qiyas). He lists the topics to be covered: conditions of syllogism, sources of error, correction of forms, and the essential composition of a syllogism from assents and concepts. This lesson orients you to the core of logical argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Read the section heading
Find 'al-qism al-awwal: fi al-qiyas' – this is the first major section. It deals with categorical syllogism, not analogy.
2
List the subtopics
The outline mentions: conditions of syllogism (shurut), source of error (mansha' al-ghalat), correction of form (tashih surat al-mufrad wa surat tarkib al-qiyas), and the composition: every syllogism is composed of two assents (ilmayn) and each premise contains two concepts (ma'rifatayn).
This shows the author's systematic approach to teaching syllogism.
3
Understand the composition rule
The claim 'kull qiyas mu'allaf min ilmyn (tasdiqayn)' means every syllogism has two premises (both are assents). 'Kull muqaddima (ilm, tasdiq) mushtamila 'ala ma'rifatayn (tasawwuran)' means each premise contains two concepts (the subject and predicate). So a syllogism uses four concepts total.
This is a structural foundation for analyzing any argument.
4
Reflect on the goal
The author aims to teach how to deduce knowledge (istintaj al-ilm) from a syllogism. The topics listed (conditions, error, correction) are the building blocks for that skill.

The First Art: Preliminaries and Key Concepts
Author outlines the first art's three chapters covering words and meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the three chapters of the first art and identify the main topics: verbal indication, simple meanings, and the relations of generality and specificity.

Core Idea
The first art lays the groundwork: words indicate meanings, then meanings relate to each other.

The author now introduces the first art (al-fann al-awwal), which contains three chapters covering basic concepts needed for the rest of the book. This lesson gives you a roadmap of these foundational topics, from verbal indication to the relations among simple concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three chapters
The outline lists: al-fasl al-awwal: fi dalalat al-alfaz 'ala al-ma'ani (on verbal indication), al-fasl al-thani: fi al-nazar fi al-ma'ani al-mufrada (on simple meanings), and presumably a third chapter (though not fully listed here – it may continue). Note the structure.
2
Chapter 1 topics: words and fallacies
Chapter 1 covers types of verbal indication, the division of words into determinate (mu'ayyan) and absolute (mutlaq), and several fallacies: confusion of equivocal (mushtarak) with univocal (mutawati'), confusion of disparate (mutabayina) with synonymous (mutaradifa), and a fallacy about equivocal words being improvised.
These fallacies are meant to train the mind to avoid errors in reasoning about words.
3
Chapter 2 topics: relations among meanings
Chapter 2 discusses simple meanings and how they relate: more general (a'am), more specific (akhass), and equal (musawi). It also covers the relation in terms of essence and accidents: essential (dhati), inseparable (lazim), and accidental ('arid).
This is a precursor to the theory of definition and the five universals (but not fully named here).
4
Note the practical example
The outline includes 'mithal lil-ghalat fi al-lafz al-mushtarak min al-fiqhiyyat' – an example of error in equivocal words from fiqh. This shows the immediate application to Islamic jurisprudence.

Sources of Certainty in Islamic Logic
Author enumerates sources of certainty and distinguishes them from other premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three primary sources of certainty mentioned in the text: mutawatir from sense, experiential knowledge (mujarrabat), and primary axioms (awwaliyat).

Core Idea
Certainty arises from sense-reports, repeated experience, and innate axioms.

This lesson dives into the foundational sources of certain knowledge (yaqin) outlined in our text. The author identifies three primary sources: mutawatir reports grounded in sense perception, experiential knowledge (mujarrabat), and primary axioms (awwaliyat) inherent to the intellect. Understanding these is crucial for evaluating the strength of premises in any argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sources of certainty
Find the phrases 'التواتر عن المحس يقين', 'المجربات يقينية عند من جرّبها', and 'لا ينفك العقل عن الأوليات منذ وجد'. These are the three sources.
2
Analyze why mutawatir yields certainty
Mutawatir is based on repeated sense reports from multiple chains, eliminating doubt.
3
Analyze experiential knowledge
Mujarrabat gains certainty from repeated observation by the same person, relying on sense and reason.
4
Analyze primary axioms
Awwaliyat are self-evident truths inseparable from reason, e.g., the whole is greater than the part.
5
Differentiate from non-certain premises
Contrast these with mashhurat (widely accepted) and wahmiyyat (fanciful), which lack certainty.

Commentary
This passage is a structural outline of key topics in logic and epistemology, likely from a table of contents or summary. It opens by defining belief as certainty and distinguishing degrees of opinion. The sources of certainty are then listed: the mutawatir (widely transmitted reports) from sense perception yield certainty; secondary axioms are innate from the start; experiential knowledge (mujarribat) is certain for the one who experiences it; sense propositions are particulars. The outline then delves into the composition of experiential matters, combining sensory and rational elements, and notes that mutawatirat combine heard and rational elements. It criticizes limiting mutawatir to a specific number. The dialogue (tahrija) questions how to distinguish truth from falsehood if innate disposition is absent, attributing fallacious reasoning to sophists and skeptics. Two methods are given to uncover fanciful notions (wahmiyat): the estimative faculty (wahm) cannot perceive itself but admits that a correct syllogism yields truth. The outline then covers widely accepted premises (mashhurat), their acceptance due to mental readiness, incomplete induction, depth of research, and their prevalence in fiqh and kalam. Induction and Analogy Reduced to Syllogism
Index of topics covering sophistry, common premises, induction, and necessity of conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why induction (istiqrā') yields only probability and how analogy (tamthīl) is a form of syllogism.

Core Idea
Induction is a transition from particulars to a universal; incomplete induction yields probability; analogy reduces to categorical syllogism.

The second chapter of the third art claims that both induction and analogy are reducible to the syllogistic forms discussed earlier. This is a powerful claim: it means that the logical core of inductive and analogical reasoning is a deductive structure. This lesson dissects the definition of induction and the source of its probability.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of induction
Find the line 'الاستقراء: انتقال من جزئيات إلى حكم كلي'. Induction is defined as a transition from particulars to a universal judgment.
This definition is standard but highlights the direction of reasoning.
2
Identify the locus of fallacy
The text mentions 'موطن المغالطة في هذا المثال' (locus of fallacy in this example). This refers to a specific example (not given in the table) where induction goes wrong.
The fallacy arises from assuming that because some particulars share a property, all do.
3
Understand probability of incomplete induction
The line 'وجه ظنية الاستقراء الناقص' points to the probabilistic nature of incomplete induction. Incomplete induction (istiqrā' nāqiṣ) does not survey all particulars, so its conclusion is only probable (ẓannī).
Complete induction (istiqrā' tāmm) would yield certainty but is rarely achievable.
4
Relate to syllogism
The chapter title says induction and analogy 'necessarily return to what we mentioned', meaning they can be reformulated as categorical syllogisms with a major premise obtained by induction. Analogy (tamthīl) is a form of concluding from one case to another based on a shared attribute.
This reduction is a key methodological point in Islamic logic.

The Necessity of the Conclusion and the Act of Thinking
Index of topics covering sophistry, common premises, induction, and necessity of conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the claim that the conclusion is contained in the premises potentially and discuss the role of thought (fikr) in actualizing it.

Core Idea
The conclusion is implicit in the premises; thought (fikr) brings it from potentiality to actuality.

The third chapter addresses a fundamental question: how does the conclusion necessarily follow from the premises? The table lists several sub-topics: the two states of the perceived known, a query about the conclusion being contained in the premises, the distinction between the conclusion and its premises, and the idea that the conclusion is present potentially. It also raises the role of thought (fikr) and a theological question about human choice in deriving the result.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim about the conclusion
Find the lines 'النتيجة متضمنة بالمقدمتين' and 'النتيجة موجودة حقاً في المقدمتين ولكن بالقوة'. The author asserts that the conclusion is contained in the premises and exists in them potentially.
This is a classic logical doctrine: the conclusion is already implicit.
2
Understand the query and distinction
The query asks: if the conclusion is in the premises, is it additional or not? The table says it is not additional; rather, it is contained. Then 'وجه تفارق النتيجة عن مقدمتيها' discusses how the conclusion can be distinct from the premises while being contained.
The distinction is between explicit and implicit content.
3
Relate to thought and reflection
The line 'الفكر والنظر وسيلة العلم التصديقي' identifies thought and reflection as the means to acquire assentive knowledge (tasdiq). They bring the potential conclusion to actual awareness.
'Fikr' and 'nazar' are technical terms for inferential thinking.
4
Examine the theological dimension
The table ends with a question: 'هل للعبد اختيار في حصول النتيجة بعد استحضار مقدماتها؟' This asks whether humans have free choice in whether the conclusion follows after the premises are presented. This ties into debates about determinism and intellectual obligation.
Some deniers of nazar argue that if the goal is already known it is not sought, if unknown it cannot be verified—a fallacy the author points out.

The Fallacy of the Deniers of Reflection
Index of topics covering sophistry, common premises, induction, and necessity of conclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of the deniers of reflection and evaluate its logical flaw.

Core Idea
The fallacy falsely assumes knowledge is either fully actual or completely absent, ignoring potential knowledge.

At the end of the table of contents, the author lists a specific fallacy: 'من منكري النظر: في المطلوب إن عُرف لم يطلب، وإن جهل فلا سبيل إلى تحققه'. This is a classic skeptical argument against the need for reasoning. This lesson dissects this fallacy using the logical tools from the chapter on necessity of conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the argument structure
The fallacy presents a dilemma: either the sought conclusion (al-maṭlūb) is known or unknown. If known, there is no need to seek it; if unknown, there is no way to verify it. Therefore, seeking is futile.
This is a false dilemma because it ignores the third possibility: known potentially.
2
Isolate the false dichotomy
The claim assumes that knowledge is binary: either fully present (actual) or completely absent. However, the earlier chapter argued that the conclusion is present in the premises potentially (bil-quwwa). So it is neither fully known nor completely unknown.
The fallacy lies in the implicit assumption about the nature of knowledge.
3
Apply the distinction from chapter 3
Recall the query: 'النتيجة متضمَّنة بالمقدمتين' and 'النتيجة موجودة حقاً في المقدمتين ولكن بالقوة'. The conclusion is really present but in potentiality. So it is known in a sense (potential) but not actually known.
Thus reflection (nazar) is needed to transform potential into actual knowledge.
4
Formulate the correct response
The correct response is that the conclusion is known implicitly (bil-quwwa) in the premises, so it can be sought (since not actually known) and once derived it is recognized (as actualization of the potential).
This refutes the skeptical argument without denying the value of nazar.

Conclusion Is Potentially in the Premises
Explanation that conclusion is implicit in premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that the conclusion is contained in the premises potentially and not an additional statement.

Core Idea
The conclusion is already implicitly in the premises but emerges only when connected.

The table of contents states that the conclusion is contained in the two premises, not additional, and exists in them potentially. This lesson explores that key logical insight.

Guided Path
1
Recall the concept of potential existence
The text says 'النتيجة موجودة حقاً في المقدمتين ولكن بالقوة' (the conclusion truly exists in the two premises but in potentiality).
Potentiality means the conclusion is implicitly there, not explicitly stated.
2
Distinguish potentiality from actuality
The conclusion becomes actual when the syllogism is formed. The premises already contain the conclusion, but it needs the logical connection to be drawn out.
This is similar to a seed containing a tree potentially.
3
Contrast with the objection of deniers
The deniers of nazar argued: if the sought is already known, it is redundant; if unknown, it cannot be achieved. The text answers that conceptual knowledge does not imply assertive knowledge.
The premises give conceptual knowledge, but the conclusion is an assertive (tasdiqi) knowledge that emerges from them.

Syllogism of Cause vs. Syllogism of Indication
Division of syllogism into two types based on the middle term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qiyās al-'illa (syllogism of cause) and qiyās al-dalāla (syllogism of indication).

Core Idea
The middle term in a syllogism can be either the cause ('illa) of the conclusion or a mere sign (dalīl).

The text divides syllogism into two types based on the relationship of the middle term to the conclusion. In qiyās al-'illa, the middle term is the efficient cause; in qiyās al-dalāla, it is a sign that indicates the conclusion, possibly posterior in time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term's role
In qiyās al-'illa, the middle term is the cause that makes the conclusion true. In qiyās al-dalāla, it is a sign that points to the conclusion.
Example: smoke is a sign of fire; fire is a cause of heat.
2
Apply to an example
From the text: an example of qiyās al-'illa in theology and jurisprudence. The cause (like intoxication) is the middle that necessitates prohibition.
The 'illa in fiqh is the effective cause that triggers a ruling.
3
Note temporal relationship
Qiyās al-dalāla allows the sign to be later than the thing signified, e.g., a footprint indicates a passerby who came earlier.
This is important for affirming the existence of the signified despite temporal priority.

Distinguishing Essential from Accidental Attributes (Rule 2)
Rule on distinguishing essential from accidental attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential (dhati) and accidental ('aradi) attributes in a given quiddity (mahiyya).

Core Idea
Only essential attributes define what something is; accidental attributes merely describe it.

The second rule emphasizes the fundamental distinction between properties that constitute the essence of a thing and those that are external to it. This is critical for constructing correct definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quiddity (mahiyya)
Determine the thing whose attributes you are analyzing, e.g., 'insan' (human).
2
Separate essential attributes (dhatiyyat)
Attributes without which the thing cannot be conceived, like 'rational' for human.
3
Separate accidental attributes ('aradiyyat)
Attributes that can change without altering the essence, like 'tall' for human.
4
Apply the rule to an Islamic example
In defining 'salat', the essential attribute is 'specific actions with intention'; accidental attributes include location or time.

Constructing an Essential Definition (Rule 3)
Rule on constructing definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a complete essential definition (hadd haqiqi) by correctly identifying the genus and differentia.

Core Idea
A definition is complete only when it gives both the genus and the differentia.

The third rule provides the method for constructing definitions: after determining the genus, one seeks the differentia that distinguishes the species. If the differentia is not available, one may describe by necessary accompaniments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Choose a concept to define, e.g., 'nabidh' (date-wine).
2
Find its genus (jins)
Determine the broader category it belongs to, e.g., 'intoxicating beverage'.
3
Find its differentia (fasl)
Identify what distinguishes it from other members of the genus, e.g., 'made from dates'.
4
Combine them into a definition
Formulate: 'Nabidh is an intoxicating beverage made from dates.'
5
If differentia cannot be found
Use necessary accompaniments (lawazim) to form a descriptive definition (rasm).

Capturing the Definition (Rule 4)
Rule on capturing definitions through tard and 'aks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to verify that a definition is correct by testing for coextensiveness (tard) and mutual exclusion ('aks).

Core Idea
A definition is proven by being coextensive with the definiendum and excluding everything else.

The fourth rule explains how to capture a definition: not by demonstration (burhan) but by showing that the definition applies to all cases (tard) and only to those cases ('aks). This is the method of verification.

Guided Path
1
Formulate a candidate definition
Example: 'Salat is an act of worship with specific actions and intentions.'
2
Test for tard (coextensiveness)
Check if every instance of the definiendum satisfies the definition. Does every salat have those actions?
3
Test for 'aks (mutual exclusion)
Check if everything that satisfies the definition is an instance of the definiendum. Does every act with those actions count as salat?
4
Adjust the definition if needed
If tard fails, the definition is too narrow; if 'aks fails, it is too broad. Refine until both hold.

Sources of Defect in Definition (Rule 5)
List of sources of defect in definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of defects in definitions: errors from genus, from differentia, and from common rules.

Core Idea
Most errors in definition arise from choosing the wrong genus, wrong differentia, or violating the laws of definition.

The fifth rule enumerates the ways definitions go wrong. These include using a part instead of the genus, a non-essential attribute instead of the differentia, or defining by an opposite. Knowing these defects helps in critiquing and refining definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify defects from the genus
Look for errors such as substituting a part, a necessary accompaniment, or a species for the genus. Example: defining 'human' as 'substance' (too broad) or 'body' (part).
2
Identify defects from the differentia
Check if the differentia is not truly distinguishing, or if it is a non-essential attribute. Example: defining 'human' as 'laughing' (accidental).
3
Identify defects from common rules
Look for violations like defining by an opposite, by something more obscure, or by an equal. Example: defining 'night' as 'absence of day'.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Find a flawed definition of a fiqh term and diagnose the defect.

The Method of Definition Analysis in Mahakk Nazar
Index of topics on definition and terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to map the structure of definition examinations in Mahakk Nazar and distinguish between lexical, descriptive, and real definitions.

Core Idea
Definition analysis proceeds through examinations of specific terms, each revealing a logical principle.

This lesson orients you to the author's systematic approach to defining key logical terms. The index shows how each 'imtihan' (examination) tests a different aspect of definition, from the term 'hadd' itself to 'ilm', 'arad', 'hadith', 'mutadaddain', and 'hayah'. Understanding this structure will help you follow the rigorous method of logical inquiry that follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the index structure
Notice the recurring pattern: each major term is introduced with 'al-imtihan al-... fi ta'rif ...' (the ... examination on defining ...). This is the backbone of the book's pedagogy.
The index may not be continuous prose; it is a list of topic headings.
