The Hidden Syllogism in Experimental Knowledge
Author explains experimental knowledge as based on a hidden syllogism from repeated observations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how experimental knowledge (tajribah) depends on an implicit syllogism and identify the hidden reasoning in a concrete Islamic science example.

Core Idea
Experimental knowledge is not raw sensation—it is a judgment based on a hidden syllogism about causal regularity.

The passage reveals that when we learn from repeated experiences—like knowing bread satiates—we are actually using a syllogism without realizing it. This hidden syllogism (qiyas khafi) is the logical structure behind all empirical certainties. In this lesson, we dissect how the mind reasons from constant conjunction to causal judgment.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the phenomenon of constant conjunction
Identify from the example that bread is always accompanied by satiation, but not always by pain. This difference in constancy is the starting point.
The bread example shows satiation is constant, pain is not.
2
Distinguish between necessary and accidental association
Understand that the constancy of an effect with a cause (iṭirād al-'ādāt) is the basis for the hidden syllogism. Accidental associations (like pain with bread) do not produce reliable knowledge.
3
Formalize the hidden syllogism
Reconstruct the mind's implicit reasoning: 'If bread were not satiating, it would not always relieve hunger; but it does; therefore bread is satiating.' Similarly for pain: pain is not constant, so it is not attributed to bread. This is the hidden syllogism.
The mind does not articulate this verbally but reasons unconsciously.
4
Apply to a new Islamic science example
Consider the judgment that wine is intoxicating. Based on repeated observation, intoxication always follows wine consumption. Using the hidden syllogism, explain why this is considered certain knowledge in Islamic law.
This reasoning underpins qiyas in usul al-fiqh.

The Logical Structure and Limits of Induction in Islamic Jurisprudence
Definition and critique of induction with fiqh example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct an inductive argument as a categorical syllogism with a disjunctive premise and identify why induction yields only probability.

Core Idea
Induction reduces to syllogism, but its certainty depends on complete enumeration—which is rarely possible.

This passage defines induction and gives a classic fiqh example: the witr prayer is not obligatory because it is performed on a riding animal. The author shows that induction, when reconstructed as a syllogism, reveals its dependence on complete enumeration. Without completeness, the conclusion is only probable, not certain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inductive claim
The claim is that witr prayer is not obligatory because it is performed on a riding animal. The underlying universal principle is 'Every obligatory prayer is not performed on a riding animal.' This principle is derived from induction.
The claim is based on observed instances of obligatory prayers.
2
Extract the enumerated instances
The author lists qada' (make-up prayers), ada' (current performance of obligatory prayers), and nadhr (vowed prayers) as categories that have been observed not to be performed on riding animals.
These are the 'particulars' surveyed in the induction.
3
Reconstruct the argument as a syllogism
The author reduces the induction to a categorical syllogism with a disjunctive major premise: 'Every obligatory prayer is either qada', ada', or nadhr; each of these is not performed on a riding animal; therefore, every obligatory prayer is not performed on a riding animal.'
This reconstruction shows that induction's validity depends on the completeness of the disjunction.
4
Examine the opponent's objection
The opponent points out that witr itself is a type of ada' (performance), but the survey of ada' only included the five daily prayers, not witr. Thus the enumeration is incomplete: witr was not included in the instances surveyed.
The objection reveals that the universal conclusion cannot be certain because the enumeration is incomplete.
5
Assess the certainty level
The author concludes that this inductive argument is only imaginative (mukhayyal) and suitable for probabilistic matters (zanniyyat), not for certainties (qat'iyyat).
Complete induction (complete enumeration) would yield certainty, but it is rarely achievable.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and critique of induction (al-istiqra'). The author begins a new chapter stating that induction and analogy (tamthil) ultimately reduce to the syllogistic patterns already discussed. He defines induction as the process of examining particular instances in order to arrive at a universal judgment covering all those particulars. The example is a classic fiqh argument: witr prayer is not obligatory because it is performed on a riding animal, and the underlying principle ('all obligatory prayers cannot be performed on a riding animal') is derived from induction — we have observed that qada' (make-up prayers), ada' (current performance), and nadhr (vowed prayers) are all not performed on a riding animal. The author then reconstructs this inductive argument as a categorical syllogism with a disjunctive premise: every obligatory prayer is either qada', ada', or nadhr; none of these are performed on a riding animal; therefore, every obligatory prayer is not performed on a riding animal. This reconstruction shows that induction's validity depends on complete enumeration. However, the author immediately points out that this is only an imaginative (mukhayyal) argument, suitable for probabilistic matters (zanniyyat) and not for certainties (qat'iyyat). The reason is that the enumeration may be incomplete. The opponent can object that witr is also a type of ada', and the survey only included the five daily prayers, not witr. Thus the inductive generalization is flawed. The passage teaches that induction does not yield certainty because one can never be sure that all instances have been surveyed.

Defining Induction and Reducing It to a Syllogism
Author defines induction and shows its reduction to syllogistic form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reduce an inductive argument to its syllogistic form (al-nazm al-awwal) and identify the condition that the second premise must be universal.

Core Idea
Incomplete induction does not produce certainty because its reduction requires a universal premise that may not hold.

The author begins by defining induction (istiqra') as surveying particulars to reach a universal judgment. He then illustrates this with a fiqh example about the witr prayer and shows that to be rigorous, the induction must be reduced to a categorical syllogism. In this lesson, you will learn the mechanics of that reduction and the crucial condition that the second premise must be universal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the inductive claim
Read the fiqh example: 'Witr is not an obligation because it is performed on a riding animal while obligatory prayers are not.' The claim is that witr is not obligatory, based on the general rule that no obligatory prayer is performed on a riding animal.
2
Extract the inductive premise
The proponent says: 'We surveyed missed prayers, current prayers, vowed prayers, and all other obligatory prayers and found that none are performed on a riding animal.' Therefore they claim: 'Every obligatory prayer is not performed on a riding animal.'
This is the universal premise derived from induction.
3
Reduce to the first arrangement (al-nazm al-awwal)
The author reduces it to a categorical syllogism: Minor premise: 'Every obligatory prayer is either missed, current, or vowed.' Major premise: 'Every missed, current, or vowed prayer is not performed on a riding animal.' Conclusion: 'Every obligatory prayer is not performed on a riding animal.'
The syllogism is in the first figure (Barbara).
4
Check the universality condition
The second premise (major) must be universal: 'Every missed, current, or vowed prayer is not performed on a riding animal.' If this premise is not universal (e.g., only some), the syllogism fails.
This condition is crucial for validity.

Identify the three parts: major premise ('Every agent is a body'), minor premise ('The Creator is an agent'), and conclusion ('Therefore, the Creator is a body').
This is a categorical syllogism in the first mood (Barbara).
2
Examine the evidential basis of the major premise
The arguer claims the major premise is known by induction. Ask: Did the induction include the Creator in the survey? If yes, the premise already assumes the conclusion (begging the question). If no, the induction is incomplete.
3
Apply the author's test
The author tests by asking: Did you survey the Creator? If you say yes, you have included the point of dispute. If you say no, then your premise is based on only some agents, so it is particular, not universal.
A universal premise requires surveying all individuals of the class.
4
Recall the condition of the first mood
In the first mood (al-nazm al-awwal), the minor premise must be universal. If the major premise is not genuinely universal (because based on incomplete induction), the syllogism does not produce a valid conclusion.
Formally, the syllogism is valid, but it is unsound because the major premise is not justified.
5
Understand the epistemological limitation
Even complete induction only yields probability (zanniyyat), not certainty. Certain knowledge requires a different method, such as necessary knowledge or tawatur.
Al-Ghazali restricts demonstrative certainty to deductive syllogisms with certain premises.

Qiyas al-Dalaala from Customary Habit: The Physiognomy Example
Author gives an example of inference by indication from customary habit, specifically physiognomy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of qiyas al-dalaala from 'ada and distinguish it from causal inference using the physiognomy example.

Core Idea
In customary inference, the sign and signified are both effects of a single hidden cause.

This lesson introduces a subtle logical tool: inferring one thing from another not because they are causally linked, but because both are known by habit to accompany each other as results of a common cause. The example from physiognomy shows how experts read character from physical form. You will learn to recognize this non-causal, indication-based reasoning and understand its grounding in induction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two relata
In the physiognomy example, the two phenomena are: (a) broad chest and upper body bones (the physical sign), and (b) courage (the character trait). Note these are the things being related by inference.
2
Recognize the non-causal relationship
The text explicitly states that neither is the cause of the other. Instead, both are 'result of a single cause' by virtue of the running of habit (jaryan al-‘ada). The link is not direct causality but common causal origin.
This is the key distinction from qiyas al-‘illa.

The author says 'known by induction of animals' (عُرف باستقراء الحيوانات). The concomitance between broad chest and courage is not a priori but established through repeated observation across many animal species, especially the lion.
This induction is the epistemic foundation of the inference.

The inference is said to be 'by analogy to the lion' (قياساً على الأسد). The lion serves as a paradigm case where broad chest and courage are observed together. This analogy reinforces the general rule derived from induction.
Analogy here is not the core logical form but an illustrative reinforcement.
5
Note the conditions for valid inference
The text mentions 'under certain additional conditions' (عند شروط أخر مضمومة إليه). These conditions, which the author says are studied fully in the science, ensure that the indication is reliable and not accidental.
This acknowledges that not every physical sign is a valid indicator.

Learning Objective

Analogy in usul al-fiqh can be valid even without stating the common quality, as long as the mind senses a similarity and removes the difference.

Analogy and Two Types of Incomplete Cause Knowledge
Author presents a second example and defines two types of incomplete cause summary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two types of incomplete knowledge of the cause in analogical reasoning and apply them to a fiqh example.

Core Idea
Analogy requires full knowledge of the cause; partial knowledge still permits judgment but limits certainty.

In this passage, the author uses a surprising example—why is fornication not analogized to marriage regarding the prohibition of relatives?—to illustrate that the mind must perceive underlying meanings. He then formalizes two ways a mujtahid may lack full summary of the cause. We will break down his reasoning and learn to distinguish these types.

Guided Path
1
Identify the example and the excluded case
The Lawgiver prohibited marrying the mother or daughter of a woman one has had intercourse with, but only if that intercourse was in marriage (nikah), not fornication (zina). Locate this in the Arabic: 'لم يلحق به الزنا'.
The ruling is based on 'waṭ' al-ẓunūn' (doubtful intercourse) in marriage.
2
Analyze why fornication is excluded
The author explains that although prohibition is a restriction that could be a punishment, in marriage it is a sunnah, so punishment is inappropriate. Instead, the restriction serves as a blessing: it treats relatives like one's own mother/daughter, facilitating social ties and curbing lust. Thus, the underlying meaning (ma'na) differs from zina.
This shows that the mind must be aware of these meanings to 'delete' the difference (al-ḥadhf).
3
Extract the principle: awareness of meanings is necessary
The author states: 'فبهذا يتحقق أن الذهن لو لم يطلع على المعاني المعتبرة جملة لما تجرأ على الحذف'—the mind would not dare to delete differences without awareness of the considered meanings.
This principle underlies the two types of incomplete knowledge.
4
Understand the two types of lacking talkhīṣ
The one who aims to unify the cause needs to summarize it (talkhīṣ). Lack of talkhīṣ can occur in two ways: (1) knowing the principle of the cause but not its particular attributes (e.g., knowing that forgetfulness in prayer requires prostration but not the specific attribute of the Prophet’s forgetfulness); (2) this second type is implied by context—not knowing the principle at all (text cuts off).
Type 1 allows some judgment; type 2 blocks analogy entirely.
5
Apply to the example
In the example, the mujtahid knows that the cause is some blessing related to marriage, but may not know all its attributes. He can still judge that zina is not included because the blessing is absent. This illustrates type 1.

Two Types of Incomplete Knowledge of the Cause in Analogy
Ibn Taymiyya explains how analogical extension is possible even when the cause is not fully known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of incomplete cause knowledge in analogical reasoning and apply them to legal examples.

Core Idea
Legal analogy does not require full specification of the cause; partial or vague awareness suffices for valid extension.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya tackles a common epistemological obstacle in legal reasoning: what if the effective cause ('illa) behind a ruling is not fully known? He argues that analogical extension (al-ilḥāq) remains valid even when the cause is only partially understood. Through concrete examples from ḥadīth and fiqh, he identifies two distinct forms of incomplete knowledge: one where the essence of the cause is known but its specific attributes are not, and another where the cause is known only vaguely among several possibilities. This lesson will walk you through these two types and show how they operate in Islamic legal analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two initial examples
Ibn Taymiyya begins with two legal examples: (1) attaching the expiation (kaffārah) of fornication to that of zinā due to shared severity (taghlīẓ), and (2) not attaching the prohibition of a woman's mother after fornication to that after marriage because the cause there is grace, not punishment. Notice how in both cases, the mind grasps a common meaning without full specificity.
These examples show that some collective awareness of the meaning is necessary for analogy.
2
Understand the first type: essence known, attributes unknown
The first type is when you know the essence of the cause (aṣl al-ʿilla) but not its specific attributes (khuṣūṣ ṣifātihī). Ibn Taymiyya illustrates this with prostration for forgetfulness (sahw): we know the cause is compensation for deficiency, but we do not know if it is because of the deficiency itself or because it was forgetfulness. Even so, we can analogize other cases of forgetfulness to the prophetic example.
This means the general category (e.g., compensation) is known, but the precise subclass (e.g., deliberate vs. forgetful) remains unclear.
3
Understand the second type: neither essence nor attribute determined
The second type is when you have only a vague idea of the cause among several possibilities. The example is riba: raisins are analogized to dates before it is clear whether the cause is volume, taste, property, or staple food. Even without specifying, you know that raisins share something with dates that is not mere difference in name.
Here, the cause is known only as an imprecise but real commonality; the exact 'illa is suspended among options.
4
See the textual proof from ḥadīth
Ibn Taymiyya provides a third example from ḥadīth: the Lawgiver ruled that sprinkling water suffices for a boy's urine but explicitly made a different ruling for a girl's urine. Without that distinction, one would naturally analogize the girl to the boy, showing that some conception of the meaning (e.g., lightness of urine) is already present. This proves that a vague 'sensing of the meaning' is enough to begin analogical reasoning.
The explicit distinction blocks the analogy, proving that the default assumption is analogical extension based on perceived commonality.
5
Distill the epistemological principle
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that the mind must have a 'distant sensing' (istishʿār al-maʿnā ʿan buʿd) and a general idea (taṣawwur ijmalī) to allow attachment. The two types are two degrees of that sensing: one where the genus is clear, and one where only a vague resemblance is perceived. Both are sufficient for qiyas.
This principle frees legal reasoning from the requirement of perfect causal knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument about the nature of analogical reasoning (qiyas) in Islamic jurisprudence. Ibn Taymiyya defends the possibility of making analogical extensions even when the underlying cause ('illa) is not fully known in detail. He begins with two concrete examples: first, the ruling of expiation for fornication is attached to that of zina because both involve severity (taghlīẓ), not bounty; second, the prohibition of a woman's mother and daughter after intercourse is limited to marriage, not fornication, because the cause there is grace. These examples show that the mind must have a collective awareness of the relevant meanings. Then he introduces the core theoretical point: the cause may be known incompletely in two ways. The first is when the essence of the cause is known but its specific attributes are not, illustrated by the case of forgetfulness (sahw) in prayer: we know that the cause of prostration is compensation for deficiency or forgetfulness, but until we specify which, we can still analogize other cases of forgetfulness to the prophetic example. The second is when neither the essence nor the attribute is determined, but the cause is known vaguely among several possibilities, as in the case of riba where raisins may be analogized to dates based on a shared characteristic (volume, taste, property, or staple) even before the exact cause is identified. Finally, he provides a textual proof from a hadith: the Lawgiver's distinction between a boy's and a girl's urine in purification shows that without the explicit distinction, one would naturally analogize the girl to the boy, indicating that some vague conception of the meaning is indispensable for attaching rulings. The closing sentence about women urinating in still water seems to be an additional example but is cut off.
