The Two Parts of a Proposition and Their Discipline-Specific Names
Defines the proposition's composition and lists terminologies used by different disciplines.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two parts of a proposition according to the jurists' terminology and distinguish the different terms used for them by grammarians, theologians, and logicians.

Core Idea
The same logical proposition has different named parts depending on your discipline.

Every statement that can be true or false consists of two components: something is being talked about, and something is said about it. This lesson walks through the composition of a proposition (qadiyya) and shows how grammarians, theologians, jurists, and logicians each have their own terms for these two parts. Understanding these terms is essential for reading texts across the Islamic sciences without confusion.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the proposition's composition
A proposition is a compound of two cognitions related by affirmation or negation. For example, 'The world is originated' (al-ʿālam ḥādith) joins the cognition of 'world' and 'originated' with affirmation. If negated, the relation is negation.
The passage uses the theological example 'The world is originated' and 'The Creator is eternal'.
2
Identify the two parts: subject and predicate
The part that is being talked about is the subject, and what is said about it is the predicate. In 'The world is originated', 'the world' is the subject and 'originated' is the predicate.
3
Match discipline-specific terms to each part
Grammarians call the subject mubtadaʾ and the predicate khabar; theologians call them mawṣūf and ṣifa; jurists call them ḥukm and maḥkūm ʿalayh; logicians call them mawḍūʿ and maḥmūl. The author adopts the jurists' terminology.
Memorize these equivalences: they appear across logic, uṣūl al-fiqh, kalām, and Arabic grammar.
4
Understand the proposition's name changes by context
The same proposition is called a premise (muqaddima) when used in a syllogism, a conclusion (natīja) when derived from a syllogism, a claim (daʿwa) when asserted in debate with an opponent, and a demand (maṭlūb) when investigated alone.
This teaches that the logical function of a proposition determines its name.

The examples are rooted in Islamic theology: the createdness of the world and the eternity of the Creator are standard kalam arguments.

The Four Types of Propositions Based on the Subject
Enumerates the first division of propositions into four types based on the subject.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four types of propositions (personal, particular, universal, indefinite) based on the subject's quantification and determinacy.

Core Idea
Propositions are classified by their subject: specific individual, some, every, or none.

After dividing propositions into affirmative and negative, the next key division in Islamic logic concerns the subject. This lesson explains the four types: personal (fi 'ayn), absolute particular, absolute universal, and indefinite. Mastering these types is essential for analyzing syllogisms and arguments in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the four categories
The passage divides propositions based on the subject into: ta'yin (determinate), khusus (particular), umum (universal), and ihmal (indefinite). These correspond to the four types listed.
The term 'ta'yin' here refers to a specific individual, later often called shakhsiyya.
2
Identify the quantifier for each type
Personal propositions have no quantifier but a definite individual subject. Particular propositions use 'some' (ba'd). Universal propositions use 'every' (kull). Indefinite propositions lack a quantifier entirely.
3
Match each type to its example from the passage
Confirm each type with the given example: 'Zayd is a writer' (personal), 'Some people are writers' (particular), 'Every body is extended' (universal), 'Man is in loss' (indefinite).
The Quranic phrase 'Man is in loss' (Quran 103:2) is used as an indefinite proposition.
4
Understand the cause (illa) of the division
The subject is either a concrete individual (pointed to) or not. If not, it is either quantified (particular or universal) or not quantified (indefinite). This logical tree generates the four types.
This structural cause helps in constructing definitions and arguments.
5
Practice classifying new propositions
Take a proposition from Islamic sciences, e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' or 'The world is originated', and determine which type it belongs to.
Consider whether the subject is a specific individual, quantified, or indefinite.

Commentary
This passage provides the first detail (tafsil awwal) of the divisions of propositions. It begins by noting that propositions are already divided into negative and affirmative based on the predicate (mahkum fihi). But here the focus is on the subject (mahkum 'alayh). The examples are drawn from Islamic thought: 'Every body is extended' is a standard kalam/physics premise; 'Man is in loss' is a Quranic reference. The classification is foundational for logical analysis of propositions.

The Three Modes of Judgment: Imkan, Wujub, Istihala
Author classifies judgments into three modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given predicate-subject relation is one of possibility, necessity, or impossibility based on the passage's examples.

Core Idea
The mode of judgment is about the inherent relation, not the proposition's truth.

In this passage, the author introduces three fundamental modes of judgment: possibility, necessity, and impossibility. Understanding these modes is crucial for analyzing propositions in logic and Islamic sciences. Let's work through the definitions and examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three modes
The author lists al-imkan, al-wujub, and al-istihala as the three types of judgment. Note that these are modes of the relation between predicate and subject.
2
Examine the example for possibility
For 'Man is a writer' and 'Man is not a writer', note that writing is not essential to man, so both affirmation and negation are possible.
3
Examine the example for necessity
For 'Blackness is a color', the relation is necessary because blackness cannot exist without being a color.
4
Examine the example for impossibility
For 'Blackness is knowledge', the relation is impossible because blackness cannot be knowledge.
5
Test your understanding
Given a new example, determine the mode. For instance, is 'Man is an animal' necessary, possible, or impossible?

Commentary

Resolving the Objection: Mode vs. Propositional Truth
Author resolves an objection about modal classification of negative examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the mode of the predicate-subject relation and the truth value of the proposition, and resolve the apparent contradiction in the passage's examples.

Core Idea
The mode is about the relation alone, separate from affirmation or negation.

The author anticipates an objection: his examples of necessary and impossible judgments include negations that seem to contradict the mode. He clarifies that the mode applies to the relation itself, not the entire proposition. This is a crucial logical distinction for avoiding confusion in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector notes that 'Man is not a stone' seems necessary (since man is essentially not a stone), while 'Blackness is not a color' seems impossible (since blackness is essentially a color). So how can both be examples of different modes?
2
Identify the author's response
The author clarifies that he is considering the judgment (hukm) as a single relation, not the proposition containing affirmation or negation. The mode is determined by the relation itself, not the overall truth.
3
Apply the distinction
For 'Man is not a stone', the relation 'Man is a stone' is impossible, so its negation is necessary. For 'Blackness is not a color', the relation 'Blackness is a color' is necessary, so its negation is impossible.
4
Generalize the principle
The mode is determined by the original relation (without negation). The proposition's truth or falsity depends on affirmation/negation, but the mode remains tied to the inherent relation.

The Hidden Syllogism in Experiential Knowledge
Author defines experiential knowledge and the hidden syllogism underlying it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the hidden syllogism (qiyas khafi) that underlies experiential knowledge, formalize it as a conditional argument, and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
Experiential certainty arises from a hidden conditional syllogism the mind performs unconsciously.

You know that bread satiates, yet you never consciously syllogize it. The author reveals that this certainty springs from a hidden syllogism: if the cause were not real, the effect would not be regular; but it is regular, so the cause is real. This lesson makes the implicit explicit, turning a cognitive intuition into a logical form.

Guided Path
1
Recognize that repetition triggers a hidden inference
The author says the mind obtains knowledge 'by means of a hidden syllogism' (qiyas khafi). This syllogism is not consciously formulated in words.
2
Extract the two conditionals implicit in the argument
The author presents two conditional premises: (1) If this cause did not necessitate the effect, then the effect would not be consistent most of the time. (2) If the effect were due to chance, it would not be regular. Both lead to the same conclusion.
3
Observe the factual premise from repetition
Through experience, we observe that the effect is indeed consistent (e.g., bread always satiates, most of the time). This is the minor premise.
4
Conclude the causal relationship
From the conditional and the observation, the mind concludes that the cause is real (e.g., bread has the property of satiating). This is the conclusion.
5
Formalize the syllogism
Write it as: If the cause were not effective, then regularity would not occur. Regularity occurs. Therefore, the cause is effective. This is a valid modus ponens (on the contrapositive) or modus tollens depending on phrasing.
The author frames it as: 'If this cause did not necessitate it, it would not be consistent. It is consistent. Therefore, it necessitates.'

Commentary

As for the statement that lying is ugly, neither the nature of imagination nor the nature of the intellect judge it; rather, it is what a person has become accustomed to through customs, morals, and reforms. This too is a dark pitfall that must be guarded against. Few escape stumbling over these premises, and they are not confused with certainties (yaqīniyyāt), especially in the thick of syllogisms when the ranks and premises become numerous. And this much suffices concerning the premises that are the matter [of syllogisms] and ordering and composition. So what is derived from the five senses after guarding against the points of error in them is suitable for syllogism, and what is derived from the error of imagination is absolutely not suitable as matter. And the widely-accepted (mashhūrāt) are suitable for probabilistic juridical matters (al-fiqhiyyāt al-ẓanniyya), and are not suitable for anything else. And let us conclude with this the art of the purposes for demonstration (al-burhān).

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion about types of premises, specifically the widely-accepted (mashhūrāt) and their contrast with self-evident truths (awwaliyyāt) and imaginative falsehoods. The author, likely Ibn Taymiyya or a similar critic of Greek logic, presents a thought experiment: take a commonly accepted statement like 'Truthfulness is beautiful, lying is ugly' and examine it stripped of all cultural and educational influences. If you imagine yourself with no social contact, no religious upbringing, no moral training, can you still judge this statement as true? The author argues that you can doubt it, unlike self-evident truths such as 'two is greater than one' which cannot be doubted even in imagination. He then distinguishes between two innate faculties: the nature of the intellect (fiṭrat al-ʿaql) which compels self-evident truths, and the nature of imagination (fiṭrat al-wahm) which can lead to false but compelling beliefs, like the idea that the world ends in a void (an example of a false imaginative premise). The statement about truthfulness and lying is not necessitated by either; it is acquired through custom and moral training. The author warns that these acquired 'mashhūrāt' are pitfalls because they can be mistaken for certainties, especially in complex syllogisms. Finally, he categorizes premises: (1) those from the five senses after correcting for error—suitable for syllogism; (2) those from false imagination—utterly unsuitable; (3) the mashhūrāt—suitable only for probabilistic fiqh (juridical reasoning), not for demonstration (burhān). This reflects the typical Ashʿarī or Ḥanbalī view that only certain premises yield certainty; popular opinion has limited epistemic value.

The Four Types of Inquiry: Ma, Li-Ma, Ayyu, Hal
Author classifies logical questions: what, why, which, and whether.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any logical question into one of the four matalib (what, why, which, whether) and explain the purpose of each.

Core Idea
Every logical inquiry is one of four types: what, why, which, or whether.

In this passage, the author completes the classification of logical inquiries (matalib). After discussing the types of 'what' (ma) which ask for definitions, he now introduces three more: 'why' (li-ma) seeking causes, 'which' (ayyu) for specifying within a genus, and 'whether' (hal) for attributes. Understanding this framework is essential for recognizing what kind of answer a given question demands in Islamic scholarly discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four matalib mentioned
List the four types: ما (what), لم (why), أي (which), هل (whether). Note that the author had already discussed three types of 'ma' earlier; here he focuses on the other three.
2
Understand the purpose of 'why' (li-ma)
The passage says 'why' is a question about the cause ('illa). Its answer is a demonstration (burhan). In Islamic logic, this is used to ask for the reason or cause behind a phenomenon or ruling.
Example: 'Why is prayer obligatory?' seeks the cause ('illa) which might be a specific textual command.
3
Understand the purpose of 'which' (ayyu)
'Which' is used to distinguish something that is already known in its general category from other members. The author gives the example: 'What is a tree?' (answer: a body), then 'Which body?' (answer: a growing body). This follows a general definition.
So 'ayyu' refines a genus to a species.
4
Understand 'whether' (hal) and its scope
'Whether' asks about the attribute (sifa) of an existent. The author says other question forms like 'how', 'where', 'when' are included under 'hal'. Most inquiries, except the ones mentioned, revert to 'hal'.
Example: 'Is the world eternal?' is a 'whether' question.
5
Apply the classification to Islamic questions
Given a question from Islamic sciences, determine which matlab it belongs to. For instance, 'What is riba?' is 'ma' (seeking definition). 'Why is nabidh forbidden?' is 'li-ma' (seeking cause). 'Which type of water is pure?' is 'ayyu' (distinguishing within genus). 'Is fasting in Ramadan obligatory?' is 'hal' (asking about attribute/ruling).
Practice on more examples to internalize the categories.

Commentary
This passage completes the classification of types of inquiry (matalib). The author has already discussed the first two types (which are the three types of 'ma' – what? – i.e., definitions). Now he mentions the third: 'why' (li-ma), which asks for the cause, and its answer is the burhan (demonstration). Key Terms
مطلب لم (maṭlab li-ma): The question 'why', which seeks the cause of a thing; its answer is a demonstration (burhan). [Note: Stable; in later texts, li-ma is often associated with the four causes.]
مطلب أي (maṭlab ayyu): The question 'which', used to distinguish a specific member of a genus after the genus is known. [Note: Stable; sometimes called 'tasfih' or 'tamyiz'.]
مطلب هل (maṭlab hal): The question 'whether', asking about the existence or attribute of something. [Note: Stable; synonymous with 'hal basita' (simple existence) and 'hal murakkaba' (qualified existence).]
