The Simple Body and the Two Meanings of Receptivity
Author defines simple body and distinguishes two meanings of qabul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two meanings of qabul (al-isti'dad and mutlaq al-ittisaf) and explain why the author prefers the second in defining the simple body.

Core Idea
The simple body has real continuity; 'qabul' must mean actual attribution, not mere potentiality.

In this passage from the Hidayat al-Hikma, the author sets the stage for proving the existence of hyle (prime matter) by clarifying two key terms: 'the simple body' and 'receptivity'. Understanding the precise definition of the simple body and the intended meaning of qabul is essential to follow the subsequent proof. The author warns against confusing preparedness (potentiality) with absolute attribution (actual possession), as this would make the argument tautological.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'some bodies'
The passage states that 'some bodies' (ba'd al-ajsam) refers to the simple body, not the composite. Find this explicit clarification in the Arabic.
The author contrasts simple and composite to highlight the locus of real continuity.
2
Extract the definition of the simple body
The simple body is defined as not composed of other bodies and possessing real continuity (al-ittisal al-haqiqi). Note that composites lack real continuity because the specific forms of their parts persist.
Real continuity requires a single form, which is absent in composites.
3
Distinguish the two meanings of qabul
The author lists two meanings: (1) preparedness (al-isti'dad) – the non-existence of a thing in a substrate apt for it, opposite to actuality; (2) absolute attribution (mutlaq al-ittisaf) – not precluding actuality. Write down both definitions from the text.
The second meaning is broader and covers actual possession of a property.
4
Determine which meaning is intended in the phrase 'receptive to disjunction'
The author argues that the second meaning (absolute attribution) is intended. If the first meaning (preparedness) were used, the phrase 'receptive to disjunction' would simply mean 'continuous', making the statement redundant. Trace this reasoning in the passage.
The author also allows the first meaning if interpreted 'according to sense' (i.e., we observe the disjunction happening).
5
Test the conditional reasoning
Analyze the conditional: 'If qabul meant preparedness, then the judgment that the body must be continuous is null.' Explain why this would be a tautology: if disjunction is potential, it is currently non-existent, so the body is already continuous.
This shows the importance of choosing the correct meaning to avoid logical pitfalls.

Commentary
This passage is definitional and explicatory. The author begins by setting the context: the first art concerning what is common to bodies, and specifically the chapter on proving the hyle (prime matter). The author then clarifies the term 'some bodies' (ba'd al-ajsam): it means the simple body (al-jism al-mufrad), which is not composed of other bodies, as opposed to composite bodies. The reason is that unitary continuity (al-ittisal al-wahdani) is not found in composites because the specific forms of the simple components persist; real continuity requires a single form. The author then distinguishes two meanings of 'receptivity' (al-qabul). The first meaning is preparedness (al-isti'dad), which is the potentiality of a substrate to receive something, opposite to actuality. The second meaning is absolute attribution (mutlaq al-ittisaf), which does not preclude actuality. The author argues that in the statement 'some bodies receptive to disjunction', the intended meaning is the second, i.e., absolute attribution: when disjunction occurs to a simple body, it must have been continuous before disjunction, as perceived by the senses. If the first meaning were intended, the statement would be tautological because if the body has the potential for disjunction, then disjunction is currently non-existent, which already implies it is continuous; thus the statement 'receptive to disjunction' would just mean 'continuous', which adds nothing new. However, if we interpret 'receptive to disjunction' as meaning receptive according to sense (i.e., we observe it becoming disjoined), then it is valid to apply the first meaning (preparedness) because the disjunction was not apparent before. This sets up the stage for the proof of hyle.

The Corporeal Form and Its Dependence on Matter
Author argues that every body is composed of matter and form using a conditional syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional argument for the composition of every body from matter and form, and identify its logical form as modus tollens.

Core Idea
Every body must be composed of matter and form because form cannot be self-sufficient if it inheres.

This passage from al-Hidayat al-Hikmat presents a concise argument for the compositeness of bodies. The author uses a conditional syllogism to prove that corporeal form cannot be independent but must inhere in matter. Let's walk through the logical steps to see how he establishes the necessity of matter-form composition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
Find the claim the author wants to prove: the last sentence 'فكل جسم مركب من الهيولى والصورة' (So every body is composed of matter and form).
2
Extract the conditional premise
The text states: 'وإلا لاستحال حلولها في المحل المستلزم لافتقارها إليه' meaning 'otherwise its inherence in the subject would be impossible'. This sets up a conditional: if corporeal form were self-sufficient, then it could not inhere in a subject.
The phrase 'وإلا' implies 'if it were not dependent' i.e., if it were self-sufficient.
3
Identify the exception (istithna')
The argument relies on the fact that form does inhere in bodies. This is taken as given: we observe form in matter. So the consequent of the conditional is false: form does inhere, not impossible. This triggers modus tollens.
The author does not restate the observation explicitly; it is implied by the context of discussing bodies.
4
Formalize the argument
Write in modus tollens form: If P then Q. Not Q. Therefore not P. Here P = 'form is self-sufficient', Q = 'form cannot inhere'. Since form does inhere, P must be false: form is not self-sufficient.
5
Connect to the conclusion
Because form is not self-sufficient, it must depend on a subject (المحل), which is matter (الهيولى). Therefore form and matter together constitute every body. Hence every body is composite.

Conditional Arguments Against Hyle as Extension
Author argues that hyle cannot be line, surface, or body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate conditional arguments in philosophical reasoning, specifically regarding prime matter.

Core Idea
Prime matter cannot be extended because each possible extension leads to contradiction.

In this passage, the author uses conditional reasoning to eliminate the possibilities that prime matter (hayula) could be a line, surface, or body. By understanding these conditional arguments, you will sharpen your ability to assess claims about the nature of matter in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditional claims
The passage presents separate conditionals for hyle being a line, a surface, and a body. Locate each 'if… then…' structure in the Arabic.
The conditionals are introduced by phrases like 'لا يجوز أن تكون خطا' (it is not permissible that it be a line).
2
Extract the antecedent and consequent for each
For each claim, write the antecedent (the assumption that hyle is that extension) and the consequent (the impossibility that follows). For example, for the line: antecedent = 'hyle is a line'; consequent = 'either it prevents contact (line divides) or does not (overlap), both impossible'.
The consequent often contains a disjunction.
3
Analyze the logical form
Determine if the argument uses modus tollens (denying the consequent) or modus ponens. Here, the author denies the possibility of the consequent, so it is modus tollens: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
The conclusion 'hyle is not a line' follows from the impossibility of both disjuncts.
4
Evaluate the validity and premises
Check that the logical form is valid (modus tollens is valid) and that the premises (the impossibility of the consequents) are justified. The author appeals to geometric and metaphysical principles.
The premises are accepted within the Aristotelian framework; later critics might question them.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of conditional arguments proving that prime matter (hayula) cannot be a line, surface, or body. The author opens by stating the chapter's thesis, then addresses each possibility in turn. First, if hyle were a line, when two bodies approach it from opposite sides, either it would separate them (causing an impossible division of the line in two directions) or it would not separate them (leading to overlap and merger). Both outcomes are impossible, so hyle cannot be a line. Second, if hyle were a surface, the same reasoning applies: either it blocks the bodies' contact (but since it is dimensionless in depth, contact is still possible on one side) or it does not, leading to overlap. Both are invalid, as in the line case. Third, if hyle were a body, it would itself be composed of hyle and form, leading to an infinite regress, which is also impossible. Thus hyle must be a non-extended substance, setting the stage for the subsequent discussion on how it acquires position through form.

Defining Place and Refuting the Void
Author defines place as inner surface, rejects void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Peripatetic definition of place and explain why the void theory is rejected.

Core Idea
Place is the inner surface of the container, not an imaginary void or dimension.

In this passage, the author tackles the definition of place by first eliminating the possibility that it is a void. He argues that the void theory leads to contradictions whether void is pure nothing or an existing dimension. He then presents the three main schools on place and endorses the Peripatetic view that place is the inner surface of the containing body.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two candidates for place
The author states that place is either void (al-khala') or the inner surface of the containing body. Note the two options.
2
Analyze the refutation of void as pure nothing
If void were pure nothing, it could not have quantitative differences (larger or smaller voids). The author gives the example of void between two walls vs. two cities. This shows that void admits magnitude, so it cannot be nothing.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
3
Analyze the refutation of void as an existing dimension
If void were an existing dimension stripped of matter, it would be independent of a substrate, making it impossible for it to be associated with a body as its place. This leads to a contradiction.
The author says 'this is a contradiction' (هذا خلف).
4
Recognize the three schools on place
The author lists three views: Peripatetics (inner surface), Illuminationists (existing dimension), and Mutakallimun (imaginary dimension). Note the Arabic terms: مشائون, إشراقيون, متكلمون.
5
Determine the author's chosen definition
The author refutes the void and endorses the Peripatetic definition. He does not directly refute the other two schools here but states that he chooses the Peripatetic doctrine.
The phrase 'اختار المصنف مسلك المشائين' indicates his choice.

The Four Causes: Definitions and Distinctions
Author defines cause and enumerates four types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four causes (material, formal, efficient, final) and apply their definitions to examples from Islamic philosophy.

Core Idea
A cause is what something depends on for existence, and it comes in four essential types.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma introduces the fundamental concept of causality in Islamic philosophy. Understanding the four causes is essential for analyzing arguments about creation, change, and purpose. The example of a pot makes each cause concrete.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general definition of cause
Notice that the author first gives a broad definition: 'Cause is everything that has existence in itself, and from its existence the existence of another follows.' This sets the genus and differentia.
This definition is meant to cover all four types, though later objections will question its scope.
2
Identify the four divisions
The author lists four: material, formal, efficient, and final. Each is defined with the example of a pot.
Material and formal are parts of the effect; efficient and final are external.
3
Distinguish material from formal
Both are parts of the effect, but the material does not by itself make the effect actually exist (clay is not yet a pot), while the formal gives the effect its actuality (the pot's shape).
This distinction is crucial for understanding hylomorphism.
4
Distinguish efficient from final
The efficient cause brings the effect into existence (potter), while the final cause is the purpose for which it exists (the benefit).
Both are external, but one is the source, the other the goal.
5
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the creation of the human being: the material cause is the body, the formal cause is the rational soul, the efficient cause is God, and the final cause is the worship of God.
This shows how the four causes are used in Islamic thought beyond the pot example.

Objections and the Simple Cause Argument
Author discusses objections and the simplicity argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyse objections to the definition and enumeration of causes, and evaluate the conditional argument that a simple efficient cause can produce only one effect.

Core Idea
Logical objections refine causal theory; the simplicity argument uses conditional reasoning to defend a key metaphysical principle.

After presenting the four causes, the text addresses several objections and introduces a famous argument about the simple efficient cause. This part demonstrates how logical analysis is used to defend and refine philosophical doctrines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection: definition too narrow
The objection says the given definition of cause applies only to the efficient cause, not the others. The response broadens the definition to 'that which something needs in its existence'.
This shows the need for careful wording in definitions.
2
Identify the second objection: enumeration incomplete
Critics point out that condition, preparation, and absence of obstacle are also causes. The response restricts the division to direct causes without intermediary (علة بالذات).
This clarifies the scope of the causal analysis.
3
Analyze the simplicity argument
The text argues: if the efficient cause is simple (non-composite), it cannot produce two effects. The reasoning is conditional: if simple, then produces only one. Producing two would imply composition, which is impossible for a simple being.
This argument is used to support the principle 'from the One, only one proceeds'.
4
Evaluate the conditional structure
The argument can be formalized as: If simple, then not multiple. The proof is by contradiction: assume simple produces two, then it must be composite, contradicting simplicity.
This is a valid conditional inference within the Aristotelian framework.

Self-knowledge and the Illusion of Duality
Author argues that self-intellection does not necessitate a distinction between knower and known.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why self-intellection does not require a distinction between knower and known, using the argument from human self-awareness.

Core Idea
Self-knowledge is presential; it does not need a separate object to be known.

In this passage, the author tackles a classic objection: knowledge seems to require two distinct things—a knower and a known. But how can God know Himself without duality? The author defines knowledge as the presence of an abstracted essence, a broader concept that includes self-presence. He then offers a powerful proof from human experience: your own self-awareness does not require a second soul to know you. This lesson unpacks the logical structure of that argument and the definition of knowledge it relies on.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
The author asserts that self-intellection (ta'aqqul al-shay' li-dhatihi) does not necessitate taghayyur (distinction) between the intellect and the intelligible. This is the thesis to be defended.
2
Extract the definition of knowledge
Knowledge is defined as 'presence of the essence of a thing abstracted' (ḥuḍūr ḥaqīqat al-shay' mujarradan). Note that this is broader than knowledge of something distinct from oneself.
This definition is essential: it allows self-presence to count as knowledge without a separate object.
3
Analyze the human self-awareness argument
The author argues: every person is directly aware of their own self. If that required a separate intelligible object, then a person would need two souls—one to know and one to be known. This absurd consequence (khulf) proves that self-intellection does not require duality.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: assuming the opposite leads to an impossibility.
4
Understand the objection and response
The objection: knowledge is a relation (nisba) requiring two distinct relata, so how can God know Himself? The response: self-intellection is a different kind—presential knowledge (al-'ilm al-ḥuḍūrī) where the known is not distinct from the knower.
This distinguishes between acquired knowledge (knowledge by representation) and presential knowledge (direct presence).
