Classification of Perfect Composite Bodies by Growth and Sensation
Division of perfect composite bodies by growth and sensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any perfect composite body into mineral, plant, or animal using the criteria of growth (nama') and sensation (hiss).

Core Idea
All perfect composite bodies are either mineral, plant, or animal based on growth and sensation.

In this passage, the author presents a simple yet powerful classification of perfect composite bodies—those with a substantial form that preserves their composition. The division is made by two sequential criteria: first, whether the body has growth (inward, three-dimensional increase); second, whether it has sensation and voluntary motion. This yields three distinct categories: minerals (no growth), plants (growth but no sensation), and animals (both growth and sensation). Understanding this classification is essential for navigating discussions in Islamic natural philosophy, where these categories underpin arguments in Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject: perfect composite body
The passage begins with 'the perfect composite mineral [body]'—but the intended subject is any perfect composite body that has a specific form preserving its composition. Recognize that 'perfect composite' means a body with a substantial form.
The Arabic term 'المعادن' may be a textual corruption; the correct reading is likely 'الأجسام المركبة التامة'.
2
First division: by growth (nama')
Ask: Does the body have intrinsic three-dimensional growth? If no, it is a mineral (like stones, metals). If yes, proceed to the next step.
Growth here means a natural increase in size from within, not external addition.
3
Second division: by sensation (hiss) and voluntary motion
Among bodies with growth, ask: Does it have sensory perception and voluntary movement? If yes, it is an animal. If no, it is a plant.
Sensation includes the external senses; voluntary motion means movement by will.
4
Result: three categories
The division yields: minerals (no growth), plants (growth but no sensation), animals (growth and sensation). Memorize this tree: perfect composite body → growth? → no: mineral; yes: sensation? → no: plant; yes: animal.
This classification is exhaustive for composite bodies in natural philosophy.

Commentary
This passage provides a classification (taqsim) of perfect composite bodies, i.e., those that have a substantial form (sura naw'iyya) maintaining their structure. The author first divides by growth (nama'): bodies with growth (plants and animals) vs. without (minerals). Then among those with growth, he divides by sensation (hiss) and voluntary motion (haraka iradiyya): those with these faculties are animals, those without are plants. This is a classic Aristotelian division used in Islamic natural philosophy.
