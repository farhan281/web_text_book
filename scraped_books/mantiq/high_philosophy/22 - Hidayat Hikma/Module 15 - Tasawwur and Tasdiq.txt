The Four Stages of the Theoretical Intellect
Author enumerates the four stages of the theoretical intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four stages of the theoretical intellect and describe the key feature of each.

Core Idea
The intellect progresses from pure potentiality to active review of all knowledge.

In this passage, the author presents the four stages of the theoretical intellect, beginning with pure potential and culminating in full actual review. This hierarchical model underpins Islamic epistemology and psychology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four ranks
Find the four stages listed in the Arabic passage. They are introduced with 'المرتبة الأولى', 'الثانية', etc.
The stages are: hylic, habitual, actual, absolute.
2
Define each stage
Read the description for each stage. What characterizes each? The hylic is empty but prepared; the habitual has self-evidents and readiness; the actual has stored knowledge; the absolute reviews all.
3
Understand the order
Why is this order necessary? It moves from potential to actual, mirroring the soul's development.
4
Connect to the soul
The passage mentions the cognitive power (قوة عاقلة) as the rational soul. How do these stages relate to the soul's essence?

والنفس باعتبار القوة العاقلة لها مراتب أربع المرتبة الأولى: أن تكون خالية عن جميع المعقولات، بل هي مستعدة لها. وهي العقل الهيولاني، والمرتبة الثانية أن تحصل لها المعقولات البديهية وتستعد لأن تنتقل من البديهيات إلى النظريات، وهي العقل بالملكة والمرتبة الثالثة أن تحصل لها المعقولات لكن لا تطالعها بالفعل بل صارت مخزونة عندها وهي العقل بالفعل، والمرتبة الرابعة: أن تطالع معقولاتها المكتسبة وهي العقل المطلق، وتسمى معقولاتها عقلا مستفادا، ثم العقل بالملكة إن كان في الغاية تسمى قوة قدسية. العقل الهيولاني: سمي به تشبيها بالهيولى، كما أن الهيولى في حد ذاتها خالية عن جميع الصور ومستعد لها كذلك العقل في هذه المرتبة. المعقولات البديهية: السرفيه أن النفس إذا بلغت سن التمييز وأحست جزئيات كثيرة من أنواع مختلفة، تنبهت بملاحظة نسبة بعضها إلى بعض لجهة الاتحاد والاختلاف فاض إليها من المبدأ الفياض صورها الكلية حتى امتاز عندها نوع من نوع آخر، ثم تترقى حتى تستعد لأن تنتقل من الصور البديهية إلى النظريات. وهي العقل بالملكة: أي المرتبة الثانية من العقل تسمى العقل بالملكة؛ لما أن الكيفية الراسخة للانتقال إلى النظريات حصلت للنفس، والملكة هي الكيفية الراسخة. معقولاتها المكتسبة : المعقولات المكتسبة سواء كانت بديهيات أو نظريات، ومطالعة النفس بجميع معقولاتها إنما تكون في الدار الآخرة بعد المفارقة عن البدن، لا في الدنيا؛ لأن هناك موانع وصوارف من الأشغال الجسمانية، اللهم إلا في بعض النفوس الصافية. العقل المطلق لما كانت المعقولات تطالعها النفس صار العقل في هذه المرتبة کاملا، فإذا أطلق العقل يتبادر منه هذا العقل، فلذا سمي به القوة العاقلة : المراد بها النفس الناطقة، وهي جوهر مجرد في ذاته متعلق بالبدن تعلق التصرف والتدبير.
And the soul, with respect to the cognitive power, has four ranks. The first rank: that it be free from all intelligibles, rather is prepared for them; it is the hylic intellect. The second rank: that it acquires the self-evident intelligibles and prepares to transition from self-evidents to theoreticals; it is the habitual intellect. The third rank: that it acquires the intelligibles but does not review them actually, rather they become stored with it; it is the actual intellect. The fourth rank: that it reviews its acquired intelligibles; it is the absolute intellect, and its intelligibles are called acquired intellect. Then, if the habitual intellect is at its extremity, it is called sacred power. 'Hylic intellect': it is named by analogy with matter (hyle), for just as matter in itself is free from all forms and prepared for them, so too the intellect in this rank. 'Self-evident intelligibles': the meaning is that when the soul reaches the age of discernment and senses many particulars of different types, it is alerted by observing the relation of some to others regarding unity and difference, and there flows to it from the Gushing Principle their universal forms until a type is distinguished from another type for it, then it ascends until it is prepared to transition from self-evident forms to theoretical ones. 'It is the habitual intellect': meaning the second rank of intellect is called habitual because of the firmly rooted quality for transition to theoreticals having been acquired by the soul, and 'habit' is the firmly rooted quality. 'Its acquired intelligibles': the acquired intelligibles, whether self-evidents or theoreticals, and the soul's reviewing all its intelligibles occurs only in the hereafter after separation from the body, not in this world, because there are obstacles and distractions from bodily engagements, except in some pure souls. 'Absolute intellect': because the soul reviews the intelligibles, the intellect in this rank becomes complete, so when intellect is absolute, this intellect comes to mind, hence it is named so. 'Cognitive power': what is meant by it is the rational soul, which is a substance abstract in itself, attached to the body with the attachment of disposition and management.

Commentary
This passage enumerates and explains the four stages of the theoretical intellect (al-ʿaql al-naẓarī) within the cognitive power of the rational soul. The author lists them in ascending order: (1) the hylic intellect (al-ʿaql al-hayūlānī), which is pure potentiality, empty of all intelligibles but ready to receive them; (2) the habitual intellect (al-ʿaql bi'l-malaka), which has acquired the self-evident intelligibles (badīhiyyāt) and is prepared to move from them to theoretical ones (naẓariyyāt); (3) the actual intellect (al-ʿaql bi'l-fiʿl), where the intelligibles are present as stored knowledge but not currently being reviewed; and (4) the absolute intellect (al-ʿaql al-muṭlaq), also called the acquired intellect (al-ʿaql al-mustafād), where the soul actively reviews all its acquired intelligibles. The author adds that the habitual intellect, when at its utmost, is called the sacred power (al-quwwa al-qudsiyya). Then follows a detailed commentary on each stage: the hylic intellect is named by analogy with prime matter (hayūlā), which is formless but receptive. The self-evident intelligibles arise when the soul reaches the age of discernment (sinn al-tamyīz) and observes many particulars, leading to the emanation of universal forms from the Gushing Principle (al-mabdaʾ al-fayyāḍ). The habitual intellect is called such because it possesses the firmly rooted quality (al-kayfiyya al-rāsikha) for transitioning to theoretical knowledge. The soul's review of all its intelligibles (including both self-evidents and theoreticals) occurs only in the afterlife after separation from the body, except in some pure souls. The absolute intellect is complete because it reviews all intelligibles. Finally, the commentator clarifies that the cognitive power (al-quwwa al-ʿāqila) is the rational soul itself, a substance abstract in itself, attached to the body for disposition and management.

The Hylic Intellect and the Analogy with Matter
Author explains the naming and nature of the hylic intellect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the first stage is called 'hylic' and how the analogy with matter illustrates its nature.

Core Idea
The hylic intellect is like raw matter, formless but ready to receive all forms.

This passage delves into the first stage of the intellect, the hylic intellect, drawing a direct analogy with prime matter (hayūlā). Understanding this analogy is key to grasping the initial potentiality of the soul before knowledge acquisition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its etymology
The term 'hylic' comes from the Greek 'hyle' meaning matter. Find where the author explains this naming.
The author says: 'سمي به تشبيها بالهيولى'
2
Analyze the analogy
Compare the hylic intellect to prime matter. What do they share? Both are free from all forms but have the potential to receive them.
The passage says: 'كما أن الهيولى في حد ذاتها خالية عن جميع الصور ومستعد لها'
3
Apply the analogy to the soul
How does this relate to the soul? At the first stage, the soul has no actual intelligibles but is ready to receive them.

The Habitual Intellect and Sacred Power
Author explains the second stage and its extreme as sacred power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the habitual intellect from the hylic and actual intellects, and explain what makes it 'sacred power' at its zenith.

Core Idea
The habitual intellect possesses self-evident truths and the ability to move to theoreticals; at its peak, it becomes sacred power.

This passage describes the second stage of the intellect, the habitual intellect, which has internalized self-evident intelligibles and is ready to deduce theoretical knowledge. The author also highlights that at its extreme, this stage is called sacred power, a concept central to discussions on prophetic knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the habitual intellect
Find where the passage defines the second rank. What does it acquire? (self-evident intelligibles) and what is it prepared to do? (transition to theoreticals)

2
Understand 'malaka' (habit)
The term 'بالملكة' means 'with habit'. The passage explains that this is a firmly rooted quality (كيفية راسخة) that enables the transition to theoreticals.
3
Explore sacred power
The passage mentions that if the habitual intellect is at its extremity (في الغاية), it is called sacred power (قوة قدسية). What does this suggest about its capacity?
Sacred power is associated with direct intuition of theoreticals without need for discursive reasoning.
4
Compare with hylic intellect
How does the habitual differ from the hylic? The hylic is empty; the habitual has acquired some intelligibles and has a skill.

God's Universal Knowledge of Particulars
Proof that God knows particulars universally

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound argument that God's knowledge of particulars must be universal and not changing, and explain how the commentary clarifies the timeless aspect.

Core Idea
God's knowledge is universal and timeless because changing knowledge would contradict His necessary existence.

This passage tackles a major challenge in Islamic theology: how can an unchanging God know changing particulars? The author presents a logical proof that God's knowledge must be universal, not particular and changing. Then the commentary from al-Muhakamat refines this by explaining God's timeless perspective. In this lesson, we'll dissect the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Find where the author states the conclusion: God knows particulars in a universal way (على وجه كلي). This is the thesis being defended.
The claim is that God's knowledge is universal, not particular or changing.
2
Follow the reductio argument
The author argues that if God knew a particular as existent and later as non-existent, His knowledge would change, implying a change in His essence. This is unacceptable for the Necessary Existent.
The reductio is structured: assumption of particular knowledge leads to absurd consequence (change in essence), so the assumption must be false.
3
Examine the eclipse example
The author gives an example: you can know a particular eclipse universally by describing its characteristics, but that universal knowledge alone does not tell you if it is actually occurring now. This illustrates how universal knowledge of particulars works.
This shows that universal knowledge is abstract and does not entail temporal presence.
4
Connect the commentary's elaboration
The commentary from al-Muhakamat clarifies that God's knowledge is timeless: He knows all things equally, just as His non-spatiality makes all places equal. Thus, past, present, and future are all present to Him.
This expands the argument: God's knowledge is not subject to time, so it does not change.

Chapter on the Necessary Existent. [It] is not non-existent, and sometimes it is perceived as non-existent and not existing, so for each of them there is a separate mental image, and one of the two images does not remain with the second, so the Necessary Existent would have a changing essence, which is absurd. Rather, He perceives in a universal manner, just as you know a particular eclipse itself by saying about it: it is an eclipse that will occur after the movement of such a planet from such a direction northwards with such a quality, and so on to all accidentals, but you did not know it particularly; because what you knew does not prevent predication on many, and this universal knowledge is not sufficient for knowing the existence of that specific eclipse at that time unless observation is joined to it. Since what is obtained in the knowledge of Allah the Exalted is only what we mentioned, He does not know particulars except in a universal way. Renewal and succession refer to the occurrence of particulars, not to His knowledge as they claimed. Exalted is Allah above what the wrongdoers say, a great exaltation. "He does not know" etc.: The author of "al-Muhakamat" said: The meaning of their statement that He the Exalted knows particulars in a universal way is that He does not know them in terms of some occurring now, some in the past, and some in the future; rather, He knows them with a complete knowledge transcending entering under times, fixed for all eternity. This is just as He, the Exalted, since He is not spatial, His relation to all places is equal; in relation to Him, some are not near, some far, some intermediate. Similarly, since He is not temporal, His relation to all times is equal; in relation to Him, some are not past, some present, some future. And likewise for matters occurring in time. So existents from eternity to eternity are known to Him, each in its time, and in His knowledge there is no "was" and "is" and "will be"; they are always present to Him the Exalted at their times without any change. And their meaning is not what he imagined, but rather it is that His knowledge encompasses the natures of particulars, not their particularities and states.

Commentary
This passage is a philosophical proof that the Necessary Existent (God) knows particulars in a universal manner, not in a changing temporal manner. The author begins by stating the problem: if God knew a thing as existent and then as non-existent, His knowledge would change, implying a change in His essence, which is absurd because the Necessary Existent is immutable. Therefore, God's knowledge must be universal. An example is given: one can know a particular solar eclipse in a universal way by describing its characteristics, but this universal knowledge does not give certainty of its occurrence at a specific time without direct observation. Similarly, God's knowledge of particulars is universal, not dependent on temporal succession. The author then clarifies that the renewal and succession of events pertain to the particulars themselves, not to God's knowledge. The commentary from al-Muhakamat elaborates: God's knowledge is timeless; He knows all things equally, just as His non-spatiality makes all places equal to Him. Thus, God knows all existents from eternity to eternity, each in its proper time, without any change in His knowledge. The passage concludes that God's knowledge encompasses the natures (طبائع) of particulars, not their specific individual conditions.
