Classifying Bodily Motions: Voluntary, Natural, and Forced
Author classifies bodily motions by source and perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given bodily motion into voluntary, natural, or forced based on its source and the presence of perception.

Core Idea
Motion is defined by its origin and awareness: voluntary, natural, or forced.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma presents a clear tripartite classification of bodily motions. The author uses two criteria: whether the motion originates from within or without, and whether the moving entity has perception. Understanding this classification is foundational for subsequent discussions in physics and theology, particularly when analyzing causal relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first division: external vs. internal source
The passage begins by asking whether the motion is derived from an external source (مستفادة من خارج) or not. This is the primary fork in the classification. Mark each motion as either 'derived from outside' or 'not derived from outside'.
All motions fall into one of these two categories.
2
For internal motions, check perception
If the motion is not derived from outside, then examine whether the moving entity has perception (شعور). If it has perception, the motion is voluntary (إرادية). If it lacks perception, it is natural (طبعية).
Perception here means the ability to sense and will.
3
Recognize forced motion and its subtle cause
If the motion is derived from outside, it is forced (قسرية). However, the author warns that the coercer (القاسر) is not the true agent of the motion; he merely prepares the nature. The true agent is nature itself with the coercer's participation.
This corrects a common misunderstanding illustrated by the stone thrown upward.
4
Apply the criteria to a concrete Islamic example
Consider the motion of a thrown stone from a catapult during a battle. It is derived from outside (the catapult), so it is forced. The catapult operator is the preparer, not the true mover. Compare with a person walking voluntarily (internal with perception) and a falling rock (internal without perception).
This step solidifies the classification.

مستفادة من خارج أو لا تكون فإن لم تكن مستفادة من خارج، فإما أن يكون لها شعور أو لا يكون، فإن كان لها شعور فهي الحركة الإرادية، وإن لم يكن لها شعور فهي الحركة الطبعية، وإن كانت مستفادة من خارج فهي الحركة القسرية. مستفادة فيه إشارة إلى أن القاسر ليس فاعلا للحركة في الحركة القسرية، كما يتوهم في بادي الرأي في الحجر المرمي إلى فوق أن الرامي هو الفاعل للحركة القسرية، بل هو معد الطبعه والفاعل طبيعة بمشاركة القاسر.
Either it is derived from outside or not. If it is not derived from outside, then either it has perception or not. If it has perception, it is voluntary motion; if it does not have perception, it is natural motion. And if it is derived from outside, it is forced motion. In it is an indication that the coercer is not the agent of motion in forced motion, as might be imagined at first glance in the case of a stone thrown upward that the thrower is the agent of forced motion; rather, he is a preparer of the nature, and the agent is nature with the participation of the coercer.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of bodily motion based on the source and the presence of perception. The author begins by dividing motion into that which is derived from an external source and that which is not. For motions not derived from outside, a further division is made: if the moving body has perception (sha‘ur), the motion is voluntary (haraka iradiyya); if it lacks perception, it is natural (tabi‘iyya). Motions derived from an external source are forced (qasriyya). The author then clarifies a common misunderstanding about forced motion: in the example of a stone thrown upward, one might think the thrower is the direct agent of the forced motion, but in reality he is only a preparer (mu‘add) of the nature, while the true agent is the natural inclination (tab‘) acting with the participation of the coercer.

Key Terms
الحركة الإرادية (al-haraka al-iradiyya): Motion that originates from within a being that has perception and will. [Note: In later physics, voluntary motion is often tied to animal souls; here it is simply motion with perception.]
الحركة الطبعية (al-haraka al-tabi‘iyya): Motion that originates from within a being without perception, such as the falling of a stone. [Note: Stable term in Aristotelian physics.]
الحركة القسرية (al-haraka al-qasriyya): Motion imposed by an external agent, where the external agent is not the direct cause but a preparer. [Note: In some later interpretations, forced motion is denied; this text defends its reality.]
القاسر (al-qasir): The external agent that prepares the nature for motion, not the true efficient cause.
