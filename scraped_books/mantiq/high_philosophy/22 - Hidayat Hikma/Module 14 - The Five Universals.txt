The Five Internal Senses in Islamic Psychology
Author lists the five internal senses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and identify the five internal senses (al-hiss al-mushtarak, al-khayal, al-wahm, al-hafiza, al-mutasarrifa) and describe their basic functions.

Core Idea
Inside the brain, five faculties process sensations and meanings.

After covering the external senses, the author moves to the internal ones. These five faculties operate within the brain and are essential for perception, memory, and imagination. In this lesson, you will learn their names and roles.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic names
List the five internal senses in order: al-hiss al-mushtarak, al-khayal, al-wahm, al-hafiza, al-mutasarrifa. Note that al-mutasarrifa is sometimes called al-mutakhayyila.
The order is standard in Avicennan psychology.
2
Understand common sense
Al-hiss al-mushtarak receives all sensory forms. Without it, you would not perceive a single object as unified (e.g., the color and smell of an apple together).
It is the center where external senses converge.
3
Distinguish retentive from compositive
Al-khayal preserves images, al-hafiza preserves meanings, and al-mutasarrifa combines and separates images and meanings. For example, al-khayal keeps the image of a tree, while al-mutasarrifa can imagine a tree with golden leaves.
This distinction is crucial for understanding imagination versus memory.
4
Relate to real cognitive tasks
When you recognize a friend, al-wahm perceives the meaning 'friendly', al-hafiza recalls previous meetings, and al-hiss al-mushtarak combines the visual and auditory data.
These faculties work together in every act of perception.

Natural vs Logical Universal: Does the Universal Exist Outside the Mind?
Author defines universal and particular, distinguishes natural from logical universal, and refutes external existence of the natural universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the natural universal from the logical universal and reconstruct the proof that the natural universal does not exist externally.

Core Idea
The universal as a nature does not exist outside the mind; only individual things exist.

This passage from al-Abhari's Hidayat al-Hikma tackles a fundamental metaphysical question: does the universal (like 'humanity' or 'animality') exist in the external world? We must first separate two meanings of 'universal': the logical universal (a second-order concept) and the natural universal (the nature itself). The author argues that the natural universal cannot exist externally because that would lead to a contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of universal
Find where the author distinguishes 'al-kulli al-tabi'i' from 'al-kulli al-mantiqi'. What is the criterion? The logical universal is a secondary intelligible; the natural universal is the nature considered in itself.
The logical universal is not disputed; only the natural universal is debated.
2
State the author's thesis
What is the author's position? He denies the external existence of the natural universal. He says 'al-kulli laysa wahidan bil-'adad' (the universal is not one in number).
3
Extract the main premise
The author claims that external existence requires being 'one in number' (wahid bil-'adad). Why is that necessary? Because only definite individual things can exist in the external world; vague or common natures cannot.
This is a key metaphysical assumption.
4
Trace the reductio
Assume the natural universal exists externally. Then it must be a definite individual (one in number). But it is also predicated of many individuals. This leads to contradictory attributes: the same thing would be both here and there, etc. That is impossible, so the assumption is false.
This is a clear conditional argument: if P then Q, but Q leads to contradiction, so not P.
5
Consider the objection
The objector says the natural universal is 'one in species' or 'one in genus', not 'one in number'. This challenges the step that external existence requires being numerically one. The author's response is cut off, but you should reflect on whether 'one in species' would avoid the contradiction.
The objection attempts to show that the universal can be general while existing externally, but the author presumably has a reply.

Commentary
This long passage introduces the topic of universal and particular within the first art of the work, which deals with the divisions of existence. The author begins by stating that the universal is not one in number; otherwise, it would be identical to a numerically single thing. He then explains that in metaphysics (al-ilahiyat) we study existents that do not require matter. The 'divisions of existence' refer to general attributes (al-umur al-'amma) that apply to all quiddities, such as being one or many, universal or particular, etc. These general concepts are distinguished from specific categories like Necessary, substance, and accident. The key distinction is between the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) and the logical universal (al-kulli al-mantiqi). The logical universal is a secondary intelligible that attaches to concepts in the mind; it cannot exist externally, so it is not debated. The real debate is whether the natural universal exists externally. Some affirmed it, but the author (al-Abhari) denies it. He provides a reductio proof: if the natural universal existed externally, it would have to be one definite thing; but then it would be predicated of many, leading to contradictory attributes (e.g., being both here and there), which is impossible. Therefore, it does not exist externally. An objection is raised: the natural universal is not one in number but one in species or genus, so the contradiction does not follow. The passage cuts off at this objection, leaving the author's response unknown. The proof uses a conditional syllogism: if the universal exists externally, then it is one and many; but that is impossible; therefore, it does not exist.

Distinguishing the Natural Universal from the Logical Universal
The author defines the universal and distinguishes its two senses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) and the logical universal (al-kulli al-mantiqi) and explain why only the natural universal is the subject of the ontological dispute.

Core Idea
The logical universal is a mental tool; only the natural universal can be debated as existing externally.

Before diving into the refutation of universals, the author clarifies a crucial distinction: the term 'universal' here refers to the natural universal (the common nature), not the logical universal which is a secondary intelligible. If you miss this distinction, you will misunderstand the entire debate.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of the universal
Find the sentence where the author says 'الكلي: أي الطبعي لا المنطقي'. This directly distinguishes the two types.
2
Understand the logical universal
The logical universal is a secondary intelligible (المعقولات الثانية) that attaches to concepts in the mind. It cannot exist outside the mind because it is a mental construct.
Examples: the concepts of 'universality', 'particularity', 'genus', 'species'.
3
Understand the natural universal
The natural universal is the common nature shared by many individuals, like 'humanity' shared by Zayd and Amr. The question is whether this nature exists as a single real entity outside the mind.
This is the classic problem of universals in Islamic philosophy.
4
Apply the distinction
Explain why the author says the logical universal is not fit for dispute (فلا يصلح محلا للنزاع). Because its external existence is inconceivable, it cannot be the subject of a real dispute about existence.

Commentary
This passage is a combined definition and refutation from the section on Ilahiyat (metaphysics) concerning the universal and particular. The author first clarifies the context: the discussion of existence's divisions (taqasim al-wujud) refers to general matters (umur 'amma) that apply across all existents. He then specifies that the universal under dispute is the natural universal (kulli tabi'i), not the logical universal (kulli mantiqi), which is a secondary intelligible and cannot exist externally. The core argument against the external existence of the natural universal is presented: if it existed externally as a single entity, it would have to be one in number, but then it would be characterized by opposite attributes (e.g., black and white) because its particulars have opposite attributes. This is impossible. The author then addresses an objection: the universal might be one in species or genus, not in number, and such unity does not require opposite attributes. The reply is that species and genus are mental considerations; externally, the universal would be a single individual, and thus the objection fails. The correct view is that the natural universal in itself is neither one nor many; oneness and multiplicity are accidents (arad) that attach to nature depending on whether it is material or immaterial. For material natures, the nature is multiplied in individuals; for immaterial natures, it is confined to one. The existence of the natural universal externally means that the concept, when considered in itself without universality, is identical with the existence of its individuals, not that it exists separately. The author concludes by emphasizing that the universal is not a separate entity like a father distinct from his sons; rather it is a mental intelligible corresponding to the particulars.

The Argument Against the External Existence of the Natural Universal
The author refutes the external existence of the natural universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's reductio argument against the external existence of the natural universal and explain why the objection based on unity in species fails.

Core Idea
If the natural universal existed as one externally, it would have opposite attributes—an impossibility.

The author now presents a powerful argument against those who claim the natural universal exists as a single entity in the external world. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum: if the universal were one in number externally, it would have to possess all the attributes of its particulars, which is impossible. An objection based on unity in species or genus is then refuted by pointing out that such unity is mental, not external.

Guided Path
1
State the premise of the argument
The author says: if the natural universal existed externally, it would have to be a specific, determinate thing (شيء معين شخصي). But the universal is not determinate; it is ambiguous (مبهم). So its external existence is impossible.
The core is that external existence requires determination.
2
Identify the consequence of the opponent's view
Even if we grant that the universal is one in number externally, then it would have to be characterized by all the opposite attributes of its particulars (e.g., black and white, long and short). This is impossible because one thing cannot have opposite attributes at the same time.
3
Analyze the objection
The objector says: the universal is not one in number but one in species (e.g., human) or genus (e.g., animal). Being one in species or genus does not require having opposite attributes simultaneously.
4
Examine the author's reply
The author replies that being one in species or genus is a mental consideration (لحاظ العقل). In the external world, if the universal existed, it would have to be one in number and person (واحد بالعدد والشخص), so it would still suffer from the problem of opposite attributes.

فإن قيل: الكلي الطبعي على تقدير وجوده لا يكون واحدا بالعدد، حتى يلزم اتصافه بالضدين، بل هو واحد بالنوع كالإنسان أو بالجنس كحيوان واتصاف الواحد .
بالنوع أو بالجنس بالصفات المتضادة ليس بممتنع قلت: اتصاف الكلي الطبعي بالنوع والجنس إنما هو في لحاظ العقل دون الخارج؛ فإنهما من المعقولات الثانية، فعلى تقدير وجوده في الخارج لا يكون واحدا بالنوع والجنس، بل واحدا بالعدد والشخص، فيلزم اتصاف الشخص بالمتضادين. والحق أن الكلي الطبعي في حد ذاته ليس واحدا ولا كثيرا، بل هما من العوارض التي تلحق الطبيعة من خارج، فإن كانت الطبيعة مادية تكثر أفرادها بحسب اختلاف استعداد المادة، وإن كانت مجردة تنحصر في فرد واحد ؛ لعدم القابل للمتشخصات المتكثرة. ومعنى وجود الطبعي في الخارج أنه إذا لوحظ إلى مفهومه من حيث هو هو بدون لحاظ وصف الكلية ونسب إلى وجود زيد ،وعمرو، فهو موجود بعين وجودهما من دون تغاير وليس المراد أن الطبعي في نفسه موجود بدون الأفراد في الخارج، ثم يلحقه التشخصات فيتعدد؛ فإنه محال ضرورة أن كل موجود متعين بنفسه من دون العوارض، فلو كان موجودا بنفسه لكان متعينا بنفسه فكان شخصا، فلا يصلح الصدق على كثيرين وليست الشركة فيه كشركة أب واحد لبنين؛ فإنه أمر مباين، وليس الكلي مباين لأفراده.

Chapter on the Universal and the Particular. As for the universal, it is not one in number; otherwise the very same thing one in number would be [in different places]. In the divine things, i.e., in explaining the states of existents that do not need matter in their external and mental existence. By 'divisions of existence' he intends the general matters, and they are only called that because quiddity is divided by them in relation to existence, as you say: existing quiddity is one or many, universal or particular, originated or eternal. And 'general matters' are applied to concepts that are not specific to a division of existence, namely the Necessary, substance, and accident. 'The universal' means the natural one, not the logical one; for it is among the secondary intelligibles that attach to concepts in mental existence, and its existence in the external world is inconceivable, so it is not a valid subject of dispute. Rather the dispute is about the natural universal: does it exist externally or not? Some people affirmed its external existence, and some denied it, among them the author. He argued for it by his statement: 'As for the universal, it is not one in number.' The summary is that the universal is not a specific individual thing such that its existence could be conceived, and existence in the external world is only possible for a specific thing; necessarily the ambiguous cannot exist. And if the universal were one specific thing in the external world, it would be characterized by opposite attributes, and a specific thing being characterized by opposites is necessarily false, so its existence is false. If it is said: the natural universal, assuming its existence, is not one in number such that it would be characterized by opposites; rather it is one in species like 'human' or in genus like 'animal', and a thing being characterized by opposite attributes qua species or genus is not impossible. I say: the characterization of the natural universal by species and genus is only in the consideration of the intellect, not externally; for they are secondary intelligibles. So assuming its existence externally, it would not be one in species or genus, but one in number and person, so the person being characterized by opposites would follow. And the truth is that the natural universal in itself is neither one nor many; rather these are accidents that attach to nature from outside. If the nature is material, its individuals multiply according to the different preparedness of matter; if it is immaterial, it is confined to a single individual due to the lack of a receptacle for multiple individuations. And the meaning of the existence of the natural [universal] externally is that if one considers its concept as it is in itself without considering the attribute of universality and relates it to the existence of Zayd and Amr, it exists with the very same existence as them without difference. It is not meant that the natural [universal] in itself exists without the individuals externally, then individuations attach to it and it becomes multiple; for that is impossible necessarily, because every existent is determinate by itself without accidents. So if it existed by itself, it would be determinate by itself and thus be a person, and then it would not be fit for predication of many. And the sharing in it is not like the sharing of one father for sons; for that is a separate matter, and the universal is not separate from its individuals. 'As for the universal, etc.' Since it was well-known among the people that the universal is one thing shared among many things that are its particulars, he attempted to draw attention to the intended meaning of this statement. So he said: its meaning is not that the universal is one individuated thing in number existing in each of the particulars with one external existence; for if it were so, it would necessitate characterizing one thing in one state with opposite attributes, such as blackness and whiteness and others, necessarily because some particulars are characterized by blackness while others are characterized by whiteness, and some of them are characterized by length while others by shortness, and that is obviously impossible. Rather its meaning is that it is an intelligible concept obtained in the soul corresponding to each one of those particulars.

Commentary
This passage is a combined definition and refutation from the section on Ilahiyat (metaphysics) concerning the universal and particular. The author first clarifies the context: the discussion of existence's divisions (taqasim al-wujud) refers to general matters (umur 'amma) that apply across all existents. He then specifies that the universal under dispute is the natural universal (kulli tabi'i), not the logical universal (kulli mantiqi), which is a secondary intelligible and cannot exist externally. The core argument against the external existence of the natural universal is presented: if it existed externally as a single entity, it would have to be one in number, but then it would be characterized by opposite attributes (e.g., black and white) because its particulars have opposite attributes. This is impossible. The author then addresses an objection: the universal might be one in species or genus, not in number, and such unity does not require opposite attributes. The reply is that species and genus are mental considerations; externally, the universal would be a single individual, and thus the objection fails. The correct view is that the natural universal in itself is neither one nor many; oneness and multiplicity are accidents (arad) that attach to nature depending on whether it is material or immaterial. For material natures, the nature is multiplied in individuals; for immaterial natures, it is confined to one. The existence of the natural universal externally means that the concept, when considered in itself without universality, is identical with the existence of its individuals, not that it exists separately. The author concludes by emphasizing that the universal is not a separate entity like a father distinct from his sons; rather it is a mental intelligible corresponding to the particulars.

The Correct View: The Natural Universal's Mode of Existence
The author presents the correct ontological status of the natural universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the author's positive doctrine that the natural universal in itself is neither one nor many, and articulate how it exists externally as identical with the existence of its particulars.

Core Idea
The natural universal in itself is just a nature; oneness and multiplicity are accidental, not essential.

After refuting the external existence of the universal as a single entity, the author presents the correct understanding. The natural universal in itself (bi dhatihi) is neither one nor many; these are accidental attributes that attach to it depending on whether the nature is material or immaterial. The universal's external existence is nothing other than the existence of its particular individuals.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature's neutrality
The author states that the natural universal in itself is neither one nor many. These (one and many) are accidents ('arad) that attach to nature from outside. So the nature itself is indeterminate with respect to number.
This is a key ontological insight.
2
Distinguish material from immaterial natures
If the nature is material (like 'human'), it multiplies in individuals due to the different preparedness of matter. If it is immaterial (like 'intellect'), it is confined to a single individual because there is no material receptacle for multiple instances.
Examples: 'human' has many individuals; 'angel' might have only one per species in some theories.
3
Explain the meaning of external existence
The author says: when we consider the concept of the nature itself (without the attribute of universality) and relate it to Zayd and Amr, it exists with the very same existence as them (موجود بعين وجودهما). It does not exist separately and then become individuated.
This is a form of moderate realism: the universal is not a separate entity but is realized in the individuals.
4
Contrast with the father-son analogy
The sharing in the universal is not like one father having many sons (where father is separate from sons). The universal is not separate from its particulars; it is the very nature of each particular.

Section on the universal and the particular. I say: The attribution of the natural universal to species and genus is only in the consideration of the intellect, not externally; for they are from the secondary intelligibles. The meaning of the existence of the natural in the external is that when its concept is considered as it is without considering the description of universality and is attributed to the existence of Zayd and 'Amr, it exists by the very existence of both without distinction. It is not meant that the natural in itself exists without individuals externally, then individuators attach to it so it becomes multiple; for it is necessarily impossible because every existent is determined by itself without accidents. If it existed by itself, it would be determined by itself, so it would be an individual, and then it would not be applicable to many. And the commonality is not like the commonality of one father to sons, for that is a separate thing, and the universal is not separate from its individuals. As for 'the universal etc.', since what is well-known among the people is that the universal is one thing shared among multiple things that are its particulars, he attempted to point out the intended meaning of this statement. He said: It does not mean that the universal is one individualized thing by number existing in each of the particulars by one external existence; for if it were so, it would necessitate describing one thing in one state with opposite attributes, like blackness and whiteness and others, necessarily because some particulars are described with blackness while others are described with whiteness, and some with tallness while others with shortness, and that is self-evidently impossible. Rather, it means that it is an intelligible concept in the soul corresponding to each of those particulars. Meaning: one. Described with opposite accidents like being black and white – this is absurd. Rather, it is an intelligible meaning in the soul corresponding to each of its particulars externally, in the sense that what is in the soul, if it existed in any of the external persons, it would be that very person without any difference at all. As for the particular, it is determined only by its individuators that are additional to the universal nature; because 'each if it existed etc.' – in it there is an indication that that meaning which corresponds to each of its particulars does not exist externally; if it existed hypothetically and assumptively externally, it would be identical to each of its particulars with no distinction between them externally at all, just as Zayd externally is literally 'human' with no distinction between them externally – the difference is only in the mind's consideration. 'Additional etc.' – this judgment is sound for universal natures whose individuals multiply according to separate accidents that attach to them according to the difference in the receptivity of matter. As for the essence abstracted from matter, it is determined by itself and confined to one, because the basis of multiplicity according to them is the difference in the receptivity of the receptive subject, and the immaterial has no receptive subject to multiply. 'Because each etc.' – this is a minor premise of the second figure, and his statement 'and the individual' etc. is the major premise. The lām in his statement 'the individual' is for generality, and 'al-ghayr' in his statement 'non-preventive' means negation, conditioned by the difference of the two premises in quality and the universality of the major. The arrangement of the figure is thus: No universal is preventive of sharing, and every individuator insofar as it is an individual is preventive of sharing; therefore, no universal is an individual. This explanation indicates that the individual is not identical to the universal nor a part of it; because if it were identical to it or a part of it, its negation from it would not be sound, since the affirmation of the essential is necessary. Since the individual is not identical to the universal nor a part of it, it is an accident to it, thus it is additional to the universal. Syllogistic Proof: The Individual is Not the Universal
Author proves via syllogism that the individual is not the universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate a categorical syllogism in the second figure that proves the individual is an accident added to the universal nature.

Core Idea
A second-figure syllogism proves that no universal is an individual, showing the individual is extra to the nature.

The author provides a formal logical proof that the individual (shakhs) is not identical to the universal nor a part of it. This lesson guides you through that syllogism, revealing how the second figure works with an Islamic example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion to be proven
The conclusion is that no universal is an individual (a person/thing). The author states: 'therefore, no universal is an individual.'
This is the result of the syllogism.
2
Extract the two premises
The minor premise (sughra) is: 'No universal is preventive of sharing' (i.e., it can be predicated of many). The major premise (kubra) is: 'Every individuator as such is preventive of sharing' (i.e., it cannot be predicated of many).
In the text: 'because each etc.' is the minor, 'and the individual etc.' is the major.
3
Identify the middle term
The middle term is 'preventive of sharing' (mani' min al-sharika). It appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
4
Determine the figure and mood
This is a second-figure syllogism: the middle term is the predicate in both premises. The mood is Camestres (universal negative major, universal affirmative minor, universal negative conclusion). But the author specifically says the premises differ in quality: major is negative, minor is affirmative.
Second figure: P is M, S is M, therefore S is not P. Here: No universal is preventive (universal negative), every individuator is preventive (universal affirmative), therefore no universal is an individuator (or individual).
5
Verify the validity and implication
The syllogism is valid by second-figure rules (one premise negative, major universal). From this, the author concludes that the individual is not the universal nor a part of it; hence it is an accident added to the nature.

Section on the one and the many. As for the one, it is said of that which is not divisible in the aspect from which it is called one. And it may be [one] by genus, like human and horse, and it may be by species, like Zayd. [The commentator says:] 'The aspect etc.' — he only qualified it by his saying: 'from the aspect from which it is called one', because a single thing may be multiple from another aspect, like Zayd; for he is multiple with respect to his parts, so that aspect is not called one, but rather he is called 'one insofar as he is a single individual of human'; for he is one in terms of humanity, not admitting multiplicity.

Commentary
This passage defines 'the one' (al-wāḥid) and begins a classification of types of unity. The definition states that 'one' is said of something that is not divisible in the aspect from which it is considered one. The commentator explains the qualification: a thing might be one in one aspect but multiple in another. For example, Zayd is one as an individual human being (he is one in terms of his humanity), but he is multiple if we consider his parts (e.g., head, hands). The author then gives two types: unity by genus (like human and horse sharing the genus 'animal') and unity by species (like Zayd being one particular of the species 'human'). The commentator's expansion clarifies that the definition is aspect-relative, preventing confusion. This lesson uses the familiar example of Zayd, a specific person, to illustrate how something can be both one and many depending on the perspective.

Key Takeaway
The one is defined aspect-relatively; things can be one in genus (across species) or one in species (within individuals), and the same thing can be both one and many depending on the perspective.
