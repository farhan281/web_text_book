Critique of Refuting Hyle's Position
Commentator argues that refuting hyle having position is unnecessary for the proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the commentator's critique of the need to refute hyle having position and explain how the distinction between independent and accidental position resolves the objection from points.

Core Idea
Hyle is pure potentiality, not a substance with position — refuting its position is irrelevant.

In this passage, the commentator sharpens the discussion on hyle (prime matter) by arguing that the entire enterprise of refuting whether hyle has position is unnecessary. He shows that the hyle under consideration is not a substance with position in itself; such attributes accrue only through form. By clarifying that 'having position' means having it independently, he resolves the objection that a point has position but is indivisible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim being critiqued
The commentator is critiquing the need to refute that hyle has position, and to divide it into indivisible part, line, surface, and body. Find that claim in the Arabic.
The phrase 'لا حاجة إلى إبطال كون الهيولى ذات وضع' signals the critique.
2
Understand why the refutation is unnecessary
The commentator argues that the hyle discussed here is not in itself possessed of position (ليس في حد ذاتها ذات وضع). Attributes like continuity, discontinuity, being one or many, and location only attach to it through form. Therefore, the entire refutation is misdirected.
This is the core of the critique: hyle is pure potentiality, not a substance with position.
3
Analyze the objection and the response
An objector raises the point: a point (nuqta) has position but is indivisible. The commentator responds by clarifying that 'having position' means having it independently (بالاستقلال), not accidentally (بالتبع). A point has position only accidentally as part of a line, not independently.
This distinction saves the original principle that every substance with independent position is divisible.
4
Grasp the key distinction and its effect
The key is the shift from 'having position' to 'having position independently'. The earlier proof of the indivisible part's falsity only applies to substances with independent position. Since hyle does not have independent position, the objection from points does not affect the proof.
This refinement sharpens the definition and strengthens the overall argument.

Interdependence of Matter and Form
Author argues that matter and form are mutually dependent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why matter and form cannot be the cause of each other and describe their mutual dependence.

Core Idea
Matter and form are co-dependent: matter needs form for actual existence, and form needs matter for instantiation.

In Islamic philosophy, prime matter (hayula) and form (sura) are the fundamental constituents of physical bodies. This passage clarifies their relationship, arguing that neither can be the cause of the other's existence. Instead, they arise from a separate external cause and depend on each other in distinct ways. Let's unpack the logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the refuted claim
The author begins by considering the proposition that form is the cause of matter's existence. Note the conditional structure: 'If form were the cause, then it would be prior to matter.'
2
Extract the premise that contradicts the consequent
The author states that 'form does not exist before matter.' This negates the consequent of the conditional. What does this imply about the antecedent?
This is a classic modus tollens argument.
3
Derive the conclusion
From the conditional and the negation of the consequent, the author concludes that form is not the cause of matter. Each must originate from a separate cause.
4
Analyze the positive mutual dependence
The author then explains the two-way need: matter cannot exist in actuality without form, and form cannot exist without a material substrate. Identify the two reasons given.
Matter needs form for persistence; form needs matter for shaping.

Analyzing a Dialectical Exchange: Abu al-Barakat's Objection on Springs
Author presents Abu al-Barakat's objection and the answer regarding spring formation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deconstruct a dialectical objection and response in Islamic natural philosophy, identifying the logical structure of empirical arguments.

Core Idea
Objections in Islamic philosophy often test causal theories by deriving contradictory empirical consequences.

In this passage, the author presents a critique by Abu al-Barakat al-Baghdadi against the common vapor theory of spring formation. Abu al-Barakat offers an alternative cause and uses empirical evidence to undermine the vapor theory. The response does not reject his critique but expands the causal picture. Understanding this exchange sharpens your ability to analyze dialectical arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Target Claim
Determine which theory Abu al-Barakat is objecting to. He describes the transformation of vapors confined in the earth (استحالة الأهوية والأبخرة المنحصرة) as the claimed cause of springs. This is the target of his critique.
2
Extract Abu al-Barakat's Positive Thesis
He asserts that the true cause is the melting of snow and rainwater (ما يسيل من الثلوج ومياه الأمطار), supported by observation of correlation: springs increase when snow/rain increase.
3
Formalize His Argument Against the Vapor Theory
He provides a conditional argument: If the vapor theory were true, then springs would be more abundant in summer (because earth interior is colder in summer, which would hinder vapor transformation; but he argues the opposite: cold would reduce transformation, so more transformation would occur in winter? Actually his reasoning: interior is colder in summer, so vapor transformation would be less, so springs should be fewer in summer, but observation shows the opposite. This is modus tollens. Formalize: If P (vapor theory) then Q (springs more in summer). But not Q (springs less in summer). Therefore not P.
His premise about interior temperature may be counterintuitive; trust his claim for the sake of the argument.
4
Analyze the Response (Jawab)
The answer concedes that Abu al-Barakat's cause is valid (معتبر) but denies that it negates the vapor theory entirely. The response introduces the concept of multiple causality: both causes can operate together (لا ينافي أن يكون انقلاب البخار أيضا سببا لذلك في الجملة). The objection disproves the exclusivity of the vapor theory, not its existence as a contributing factor.
5
Evaluate the Dialectical Outcome
Determine whether the objection is fully refuted or merely circumscribed. Here, the objection's force is acknowledged, but its scope is limited. The dialectic ends in a synthesis: both causes are considered valid 'in general' (في الجملة). This is a common pattern in Islamic dialectics where a critique refines a theory rather than destroying it.

Distinguishing Logical Priority from Temporal Delay in Causality
Author explains that 'then' indicates logical priority, not temporal delay, and refutes a common error.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between logical priority (tarakhi) and temporal delay in causal statements, and identify the fallacy of assuming the agent's influence occurs before the effect's existence.

Core Idea
In causality, 'then' often means logical rank, not time passage.

This lesson tackles a subtle but crucial distinction in Islamic metaphysics: the word 'then' (ثم) in causal contexts does not always imply a temporal sequence. The author explains that for eternal effects, priority must be logical, not temporal. He then refutes a common error arising from observing human actions, where preparation precedes the effect in time. By grasping this, you avoid a false illusion about how the Maker gives existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of 'then' in the phrase
In the source, 'then' in 'then exists' is explained as indicating mere ordering (tarakhi), not temporal ordering. Note that the effect (the possible thing) is eternal, so it cannot have a temporal nonexistence. The ordering is in the mind, considering the essence before its emanation from the Maker.
Tarakhī sometimes means temporal delay; here it is explicitly non-temporal.
2
Understand the logical priority of nonexistence
The possible thing considered in itself is devoid of existence. This 'prior' nonexistence is not temporal but logical: it is the state of being considered before the attribution of existence from the Maker. The Maker gives existence at the state of existence, not at nonexistence or both, to avoid contradiction.
3
Examine the common error about agent influence
Many people think the agent's influence occurs when the effect is nonexistent, and the effect appears later. This illusion arises because human actions (like movements) seem to cause effects after a time delay. But movements are only preparations (mu'iddat), not true makers.
The passage calls this a 'false illusion' (tawahhum bāṭil).
4
Distinguish between true cause and preparation
A true cause (jāʿil) gives existence simultaneously; a preparation (muʿidd) necessitates existence after nonexistence. Human movements are preparations; the effect of power is not separate from the movement's existence. So the influence is simultaneous, not earlier.
Reflect deeply (tadabbur) to avoid the illusion.

Clarifying Definitions: Substance, Accident, and Intellectual Forms
Commentator explains technical terms and resolves the contradiction of intellectual forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the technical meaning of 'specification' and 'pervading' in the definitions of substance and accident, and resolve the apparent contradiction that intellectual forms seem to be both substance and accident.

Core Idea
The definitions are reconciled by distinguishing external existence from mental existence.

The commentary on Hidayat al-Hikma clarifies key terms in the definitions of substance and accident. It explains what 'specified with something' means, corrects a misunderstanding of 'pervading', and addresses a puzzle: if intellectual forms exist in the mind (a subject), they would be accidents, but they are substances. The resolution hinges on the distinction between external and mental existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms clarified
Locate the terms 'specification' (اختصاص), 'pervading' (سريان), and 'need' (حاجة) in the passage. Note how the commentator distinguishes the intended meaning from common usage.
2
Understand the correction on 'pervading'
The commentator insists that 'pervading' here means dependence, not physical pervasion like whiteness in a body. Why would the literal meaning cause problems? Because it would exclude parts inhering in their loci.
3
Extract the definition of substance
Substance is defined as a quiddity which, when existing externally, is not in a subject. The subject is independent of the state. The condition 'not in a subject' excludes forms that inhere in matter, like bodily and specific forms.
4
Apply to intellectual forms
Intellectual forms exist externally as substances (not in a subject) but in the mind they exist in a subject (the intellect). This would make them both substance and accident. The resolution: 'not in a subject' refers to external existence only, so no contradiction.
The commentator also suggests an alternative definition that would avoid the problem but rejects it.
5
Consider why the soul is not composite
The soul falls under substance but is not composed of genus and differentia; it is simple. If it were composite, the simple quiddity inhering in it would be divided, which is absurd.

Causal Priority Between Container and Contained Intellects
Glosses explaining causal priority between container and contained intellects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between being simultaneous with a cause and being a cause itself, using the example of container and contained intellects.

Core Idea
Simultaneity does not imply causality; only the cause has causal priority.

This lesson unpacks two crucial glosses that defend the main argument against a potential confusion about causal hierarchy. The author shows that although the container and the cause of the contained occur together, only the cause has true priority. Furthermore, assuming the container causes the contained leads to the impossible consequence of void, proving that both are effects of a higher cause, the first intellect.

Guided Path
1

2
Extract the causal claim
From the first gloss: the cause (second intellect) is prior to the contained, but the container is not prior because it coexists with that cause. Why is the container not the cause?

Analyse the reductio ad absurdum
The second gloss presents a hypothetical: if the container were the cause, then the contained's existence would be posterior. But then the non-existence of the contained with the existence of the container would be possible, which would entail the existence of void—an impossibility. Therefore, the container cannot be a cause.
Void (الخلاء) is deemed intrinsically impossible in this framework.
4
Conclude the true relationship
Since the container cannot be the cause, both container and contained must be effects of a single cause, the first intellect, existing simultaneously without causal priority between them.
This resolves the confusion about why the container is not prior.

هداية (٥) النفوس الناطقة الساذجة إذا ظهر لها أن من شأنها إدراك الحقائق بكسب المجهول من المعلوم، لزم لها من هذا الكسب شوق إلى التنزة بأن كانت موصوفة بالأخلاق الحميدة، ومجتنبة عن الذميمة. بكسب المجهول: اعلم أن الشارح الميبذي جعل قوله : "بكسب المجهول" متعلقا بقوله: "ظهر"، فيكون معنى الكلام أن النفوس الناطقة الخالية عن الكماليات إذا ظهر لها أن من شأنها إدراك الحقائق بسبب كسب المجهول من المعلوم. ويفهم منه أن علة الظهور كسب المجهول من المعلوم، ويرد عليه أنها إذا كسبت المجهول من المعلوم فلم تكن ساذجة أيضا، فما معنى قوله : "شوق إلى الكمال"؟ إلا أن يقال: إن المراد بكسب المجهول من المعلوم كسب بعض المجهول لا الكل ولا الأكثر، يعني أنها كسبت بعض المجهول وفرطت في تحصيل أكثره، فلزم لها بسبب كسب البعض شوق إلى الكمال، ولو جعل قوله: "بكسب "المجهول" متعلقا بإدراك، وقدر مضاف بعد "من" لصح المعنى، يعني إذا ظهر لها أن من شأنها إدراك الحقائق بكسب المجهول الكمال، فإذا فارقت البدن وليس معها سبب الكمال وآلته، يعرض لها الألم العظيم، وهو ألم النار الروحانية الموقدة التي تطلع على الأفئدة.
Hidayat (5): The simple rational souls, when it becomes apparent to them that it is their nature to perceive realities by acquiring the unknown from the known, there arises from this acquisition a longing for purification, provided they are described with praiseworthy morals and avoid blameworthy ones. Regarding 'by acquiring the unknown': Know that the commentator al-Mibdhi made his saying 'by acquiring the unknown' related to his saying 'became apparent', so the meaning of the speech is that the rational souls devoid of perfections, when it becomes apparent to them that it is their nature to perceive realities by means of acquiring the unknown from the known. It is understood from this that the cause of the becoming apparent is the acquisition of the unknown from the known, and it is objected to him that if they acquired the unknown from the known, then they would not be simple either, so what is the meaning of his saying 'longing for perfection'? Unless it is said: The intended meaning of acquiring the unknown from the known is acquiring some of the unknown, not all nor most, meaning they acquired some of the unknown and fell short in acquiring most of it, so due to acquiring some, longing for perfection arose for them. And if he made his saying 'by acquiring the unknown' related to 'perception', and estimated an addition after 'from', the meaning would be correct: meaning when it becomes apparent to them that it is their nature to perceive realities by acquiring the unknown to perfection, then when they leave the body and there is not with them the cause of perfection and its instrument, there occurs to them great pain, which is the pain of the spiritual fire that blazes up over the hearts.

Commentary
This passage discusses the state of 'simple rational souls' (al-nufūs al-nāṭiqa al-sādha) that have not yet attained full perfection but possess some knowledge. The main text states that when such a soul realizes that it is its nature to acquire the unknown from the known, it develops a longing for purification and virtuous morals. The commentary begins by clarifying the phrase 'by acquiring the unknown' (bi-kasb al-majhūl). The commentator al-Mibdhi relates this phrase to the word 'became apparent' (ẓahara), making the causal relationship: the realization occurs because of the acquisition. However, a problem arises: if the soul has already acquired some unknown, it is no longer truly simple. The resolution is that 'acquiring the unknown' here means acquiring only some of it, not all or most. The soul acquires a little and then falls short, which sparks longing for the rest. The commentary offers an alternative parsing: relating 'by acquiring the unknown' to 'perception' (idrāk) and adding an implied word to mean 'to perfection'. In that case, the soul perceives that it can reach perfection, but if it leaves the body without achieving it, it suffers the pain of a spiritual fire that burns over the hearts.
