Objection and Answer: The Stone and the Mountain
Refutes an objection against the necessity of rest between opposite motions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical structure of a dialectical objection and its refutation, and identify the fallacy of misapplying a rule to a different context.

Core Idea
The stone's rest does not transfer to the mountain; rules about motion apply to a single body.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma illustrates a classic dialectical move. The author defends the principle that rest must occur between opposite straight motions against a seemingly devastating counterexample. By understanding how he resolves the confusion, you learn to distinguish genuine necessary consequences from mere analogical fallacies.

Guided Path
1
State the rule under attack
The rule is: 'Rest is necessary between two opposite straight motions.' This means when a body moves in one direction and then reverses, there must be a moment of rest between the two motions.
2
Reconstruct the objection
The objector argues: If the rule holds, then a stone thrown upward, upon meeting a descending mountain, would come to rest before falling. This rest would cause the mountain to stop as well, which is absurd. Therefore, the rule must be false.
The objector uses modus tollens: if rule then absurd consequence, so rule false.
3
Identify the opponent's mistake
The opponent conflates two different reasons for rest: the stone's rest at the apex is due to lack of upward tendency (mayl al-ruju'), not due to an intervening rest between its own opposite motions. The mountain is a separate body with its own motion.
4
Follow the author's refutation
The author clarifies that the rule applies only to a single body reversing direction. The stone's rest is not a 'necessary rest' between opposite motions but a cessation of one motion before the other could begin. The mountain's motion is independent.
5
Generalise the logical lesson
A rule about a single body's motion cannot be directly applied to an interaction between two bodies. This is a common fallacy in dialectic: taking a principle out of its proper domain.
This skill is essential for evaluating objections in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical objection and answer. An opponent raises a problem: if rest must occur between opposite straight motions, then a stone thrown upward would come to rest at the point it meets a descending mountain, and that would cause the mountain to stop as well, which is absurd. The answer clarifies that the stone's rest at its apex is due to lack of upward tendency, not due to an intervening rest between opposite motions. The mountain, on the other hand, has its own downward motion and is not affected by the stone's rest. The necessity of rest applies only to the same body reversing direction, not to two different bodies colliding.

Key Terms
هداية (hidāya): Guidance; a section inserted to resolve a doubt or misunderstanding about the main argument.
اشتباه (ishtibāh): Confusion or doubt; a mistaken inference that appears plausible but is false.
ميل الرجوع (mayl al-rujūʿ): The tendency to return downward; the stone lacks this at its apex, so it is stationary for a different reason.

Parsing a Dialectical Objection on Vapor Transformation
Author presents and responds to an objection about vapor's role in water flow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditional syllogism structure in a dialectical objection and evaluate the author's response as a dialectical move.

Core Idea
A valid objection may be partially accepted; causal reasoning often involves multiple factors.

In this passage from Hidayat al-Hikma, the author engages in a philosophical debate about the cause of water flow. He presents an opponent's argument based on empirical observation, then gives a nuanced response. By parsing this exchange, you will learn how logical tools like conditional syllogisms and dialectical moves work together in Islamic natural philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim and objection
Read the passage: the opponent claims that vapor transformation has no role in water flow. He argues by presenting an empirical observation about temperature differences between summer and winter.
The word 'احتج' indicates this is a formal objection in jadal.
2
Extract the conditional syllogism
Formalize the argument: 'If vapor transformation were the cause, then water would be more abundant in summer. But water is not more abundant (by experience). Therefore, transformation is not the cause.' This is a modus tollens (denying the consequent).
Write it as: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
3
Evaluate the logical validity
Modus tollens is a valid form. However, validity does not guarantee truth of premises. The empirical premise (less water in summer) might be questioned, but the author does not contest it.
In dialectic, validity is separate from premise acceptance.
4
Analyze the author's response
The author says the opponent's cause is valid but does not preclude other causes, such as vapor transformation. This is a dialectical move: accepting the objection's force while preserving the original claim as at least partially true.
The word 'أنقص' (possibly meaning 'deficient') suggests the opponent's explanation is incomplete.
5
Assess the dialectical outcome
The author does not fully concede; instead, he reframes causality as multi-factorial. In jadal rules, this is an acceptable response if the objector cannot prove exclusivity.
The debate ends with a qualified position, not a knock-down refutation.

And that the transformation of the air and vapors confined in the earth has no role in that. He argued that the interior of the earth is colder in summer than in winter, so if their transformation were [the cause], then springs, canals, and well water would necessarily be more abundant in summer, but the matter is contrary to that, as experience has indicated. It was answered: the cause is deficient [?]; what he mentioned is valid, but it does not preclude that the conversion of vapor is also a cause for that in general.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange (jadal) typical of philosophical inquiry. The opponent presents a conditional argument: if vapor transformation caused water abundance, then summer would produce more water (since heat would increase vapor). But empirical observation shows less water in summer, so the antecedent is false. This is a modus tollens. The author's answer does not completely reject the opponent's reasoning but qualifies it: the opponent's factor is valid but does not exclude other causes. This shows that causal reasoning in natural philosophy often involves multiple interacting factors.

Key Terms
استحالة (istihala): Transformation or change of state, specifically of vapors into water or other substances. [Note: In physics contexts, it often refers to elemental change; stable across the tradition.]
احتج (ihtajja): To argue or present a proof; here it introduces an objection (naqd). [Note: In jadal, it can mean both proof and objection; context determines usage.]
تجربة (tajriba): Empirical experience or observation, used here as evidence. [Note: In falsafah, it is a source of knowledge; in later usul al-fiqh, it becomes systematic.]

Complete Cause and Contingency: A Dialectical Defense
Author defends against the implication that the argument entails eternal contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange about eternal causality and explain why contingent events are not necessitated by a complete cause.

Core Idea
Not all causal chains force eternity; only a complete cause necessitates its effect.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma presents a classic objection and response in Islamic philosophy. The objector tries to extend the argument for the eternity of the intellects to all contingent events. The author's reply hinges on the distinction between a complete cause and a partial one. Understanding this reply is essential for grasping how Muslim philosophers preserved the temporal origination of worldly events while maintaining the eternal causality of the intellects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection (Mu'tarid)
Read the objector's claim: 'This argument entails the eternity and perpetuity of contingent events, because they are also effects of Him by mediation.' What is the logical parallel the objector is drawing?
The objector is using an analogy: just as the intellects are eternal because they are effects of the First Cause, so too should all contingent events be eternal.
2
Identify the Reply (Jawab)
Find the author's response: 'Neither the Creator nor any of the intellects are a complete cause for any of the contingent events.' This directly denies the premise that the causal relation is the same.
3
Analyze the Key Distinction: Complete Cause vs. Partial Cause
The author introduces the concept of 'complete cause' (عِلَّة تَامَّة). A complete cause necessitates the effect; without it, the effect cannot exist. Contingent events do not have such a cause from the Creator or intellects; they arise through a chain of partial causes.
This distinction is crucial: if the cause is not complete, the effect is not necessitated to exist eternally.
4
Assess the Dialectical Outcome
Does the reply successfully block the objection? The objector assumed that any cause-effect relation leads to eternal co-existence. The reply shows that only a complete cause does, and contingent events lack that, so the objection fails.
The reply preserves the temporal origination of contingent events while maintaining the eternal causality of the intellects.

فإن قيل: يلزم من هذا البيان أزلية الحوادث وأبديتها؛ لأنها أيضا معلولات له تعالى بواسطة، قلنا: ليس البارئ تعالى ولا شيء من العقول علة تامة لشيء من الحوادث، بل في عدمها يدخل شيء حادث، وهكذا إلى غير النهاية
For if it is said: This argument entails the eternity and perpetuity of contingent events, because they are also effects of Him, exalted, by mediation; we say: Neither the Creator, exalted, nor any of the intellects are a complete cause for any of the contingent events. Rather, in their non-existence, something originated enters, and so on ad infinitum.
Source pages p. 114

Commentary
This is a dialectical exchange. An objector claims that if the chain of intellects is eternal and perpetual due to the concomitance of cause and effect, then the same reasoning would apply to all contingent events (hawādith) in the sublunary world, forcing them to be eternal as well. The author replies that the Creator and the intellects are not complete causes (علة تامة) for contingent events. Instead, contingent events come into existence through a chain of partial causes that ultimately go back to the intellects but not as complete causes. The phrase 'في عدمها يدخل شيء حادث' suggests that at each moment when a contingent event does not exist, some other originated thing comes into being, implying that the chain of causation for contingent events is infinite and not closed, hence they are not necessary. This preserves the contingency of worldly events.

Key Terms
حوادث (hawādith): Contingent events: originated things that come into being in time; the plural of حادثة. [Note: In kalām, حوادث can refer to all temporal things; here it specifically means events in the sublunary world.]
علة تامة (‘illa tāmma): Complete cause: the cause that necessitates the effect; without which the effect cannot exist. [Note: Used consistently.]
