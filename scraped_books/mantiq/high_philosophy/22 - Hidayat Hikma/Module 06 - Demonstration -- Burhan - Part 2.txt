Complete Cause and Eternality: The First Intellect Argument
Proving intellect's eternality via complete cause and answering event eternality objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition of the complete cause (ʿilla tāmma) to determine whether an effect is eternal, and distinguish between effects with complete vs. incomplete causes in a cosmological argument.

Core Idea
If the cause is complete, the effect is simultaneous; only effects of a complete cause share the cause's eternity.

In this passage, the author proves the first intellect's eternality using a key philosophical principle: a complete cause and its effect are concomitant (mutalāzimān). He then defends against an objection that would force the eternity of all events, a consequence that contradicts observable temporality. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and shows how the concept of 'complete cause' rescues the argument from the objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author asserts that the first intellect is eternal (azalī and abadī). This is the conclusion to be proved.
2
Find the logical principle
He states that the effect and its complete cause are concomitant: if the cause exists, the effect necessarily exists simultaneously, and if the cause ceases, the effect ceases. This is the major premise.
The complete cause (ʿilla tāmma) includes all conditions for the effect.
3
Apply the principle to the Creator and intellect
The Creator is the complete cause of the first intellect. Since the Creator is eternal, the first intellect must also be eternal, by the principle of concomitance.
4
Examine the objection
An objector argues: if every effect of an eternal cause is eternal, then events (which are also caused by God via mediation) would be eternal, contradicting their observed temporality.
5
Analyze the author's reply
The author denies that the Creator or any intellect is a complete cause of events. Events arise from a chain of non-complete causes, so they are not simultaneous with the Creator.
The non-existence of a prior cause allows a new event to occur ad infinitum.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical argument: the author first proves the eternality of the first intellect using the principle of concomitant causation, then answers an objection. The proof begins with a hypothetical: if an intellect were to perish, some constituent of existence would vanish, implying that the Creator or another intellect would become mutable—an impossibility. The author then reaffirms the philosophical axiom that a complete cause and its effect are necessarily simultaneous (mutalāzimān). Since the Creator is eternal (azalī) and perpetual (abadī), His immediate effect (the first intellect) is likewise. That effect, in turn, causes its own effect, and the chain continues. The core argument is a categorical syllogism: The complete cause is eternal; the first intellect is an effect of a complete cause; therefore, the first intellect is eternal. A reductio ad absurdum strengthens the point: if the effect ceased, the cause would be deficient, implying change in the Creator—which is impossible because it would make the immaterial material. The objection challenges this reasoning: if the intellects are eternal, then all events (e.g., the physical world) that are mediated effects must also be eternal, which contradicts observable temporality. The author's answer denies that the Creator or any intellect is a 'complete cause' (ʿilla tāmma) of events. Events arise not from a complete cause but from a chain of incomplete causes that include temporal intermediaries. Thus, events are not co-eternal with the intellects. The phrase 'in their non-existence something originated enters' means that the non-existence of a prior cause allows a new event to come into being, avoiding eternal simultaneity.

Every Event Preceded by Another: The Burhan al-Tatbiq
Proof that every event is preceded by another via correlation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound syllogism used in the Burhan al-Tatbiq, including the disjunctive premises and the correlation argument.

Core Idea
Every event is preceded by another because an infinite simultaneous series is impossible.

In this passage, the author presents a sophisticated proof that every temporal event must be preceded by another event. The proof does not rely on simple observation but on logical reasoning involving disjunctive syllogisms and a special argument known as the Burhan al-Tatbiq (proof of correlation). This tool is essential for understanding how philosophers and theologians demonstrate the temporal nature of the world and refute the possibility of an actual infinite. We will carefully unpack the logical steps and see how the correlation argument works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The passage aims to prove that for any temporal event (hadith), there is another event before it. Find where this claim is explicitly stated.
Look for 'كل حادث مسبوق بشرط سبق حادث' in the Arabic.
2
Extract the first disjunctive syllogism
The argument begins with a disjunction: events either exist always (eternally) or they occur after another event. The first option is rejected because a temporal event cannot be eternal. This establishes that every event is preceded by another event.
The disjunction is: إما أن توجد دائما أو بعد حدوث حادث آخر.
3
Extract the second disjunctive syllogism
Now consider the set of events that are each preceded by another. They must either exist simultaneously (all together) or successively (one after another). The author rejects simultaneous existence because that would require an actual infinite number of ordered events coexisting, which is impossible by the Burhan al-Tatbiq.
The disjunction: إما أن توجد على سبيل الاجتماع أو على التعاقب.
4
Understand the correlation argument
To prove the impossibility of an infinite simultaneous series, the author describes two series: one starting from a point A to infinity, another starting one step before A (say C). Correlating them term by term, either they are equal (impossible because the first is longer) or the second stops, forcing the first to stop as well. Thus both are finite.
This is burhan al-tatbiq: comparing two series to show finitude.
5
Consider the alternative formulation
The author also presents an alternative: the cause of an event cannot be entirely eternal, so it must contain an event, leading to an infinite regress of causes. This reinforces the conclusion without relying on correlation.
This is a different approach: regress in the chain of causes.

Commentary
This passage is a demonstration (burhan) that every temporal event must be preceded by another event, utilizing the proof of correlation (burhan al-tatbiq) to reject the possibility of an actual infinite simultaneous series. The author first describes the emanation scheme from the tenth intellect (active intellect) which gives elemental matter and forms via celestial motions, emphasizing that the preparedness of matter is not from the intellect alone but changes due to celestial motions. Then the core argument begins: every event is preceded by another event. The proof is a disjunctive syllogism: events either exist always or after another event; the first is impossible (a temporal event cannot be eternal), so the second holds. Furthermore, these preceded events either exist simultaneously or successively. Simultaneous existence of an infinite ordered series is impossible (by the proof of correlation), so they must be successive, implying an infinite regress of preceding events without a first. The author then presents an alternative formulation: the cause of an event cannot be entirely eternal, so it must contain an event, leading to an infinite chain. Finally, a question is raised about the impossibility of an infinite ordered series, and the answer explains the method of correlation: take two series, one starting from a point A to infinity, another from a point one step before A (say C). Correlate them term by term. Either they match (impossible because the first is longer by one) or the second stops, which forces the first to stop as well because exceeding a finite by a finite amount yields a finite. Thus no actual infinite simultaneous series exists. The commentator also notes a textual issue: the author's wording seems to use 'motions' where 'events' would be more appropriate for consistency, but the argument remains valid.

Initial Arguments Against Reincarnation
Author presents two quick arguments: souls originate with bodies, and two souls cannot direct one body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two initial arguments the author uses to refute reincarnation and assess their logical structure.

Core Idea
Reincarnation is impossible because souls are born with bodies and each body hosts only one soul.

The author begins his refutation of tanāsukh with two straightforward arguments. These arguments leverage the principle that souls originate with their bodies and that each person is aware of only one soul. Understanding these foundational points prepares you for the more complex proof that follows.

The Author's Original Compound Proof Against Reincarnation
Author presents a compound proof: attachment is compulsory; its basis is specific preparedness, which ends with the body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's compound syllogism (disjunctive + conditional) against reincarnation and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
The soul's attachment to body is compulsory and based on specific preparedness, which vanishes with the body.

The author presents his strongest proof against reincarnation, which he claims is original. It is a compound argument combining a disjunctive syllogism about the nature of attachment and a conditional syllogism about the basis of that attachment. This proof is rigorous and relies on essentialist ontology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first disjunction
The author says the soul's attachment (t‘alluq) to the body is either voluntary (ikhtiyārī) or compulsory (iḍṭirārī). Why is the voluntary option impossible? Find the reason: every rational person knows death is not chosen, and people love life but death overtakes them suddenly.
The disjunction is exhaustive; the two possibilities are presented as mutually exclusive.
2
Apply the disjunctive syllogism
Since voluntary is false, attachment must be compulsory. This is a disjunctive syllogism (eliminative). Confirm the logic: Either A or B. Not A. Therefore B.
This step is logically valid if the disjunction is exhaustive.
3
Identify the second disjunction
Given compulsory attachment, the basis (manāṭ) of attachment is either specific preparedness (isti‘dād makhṣūṣ) for a specific body or generic preparedness (isti‘dād naw‘ī) for a generic body. Define 'preparedness' as a state of the body that makes soul attachment valid.
Preparedness is an accident of the body.
4
Refute generic preparedness
Why is generic preparedness false? Because it would require one soul to be attached to all bodies of that type at once, which is impossible. Moreover, if a soul directed all bodies of a type, it would be an intellect (the Lord of the Species), not a soul.
The argument relies on the distinction between soul (attached to a single body) and intellect (universal director).
5
Confirm specific preparedness
Therefore the basis is specific preparedness. What follows? Specific preparedness is tied to the specific body; when the body corrupts, the preparedness ceases (like an attribute cannot exist without its locus). Hence the soul cannot attach to another body after death. Thus reincarnation is impossible.
This is the core of the proof: the impossibility of accident transfer.
