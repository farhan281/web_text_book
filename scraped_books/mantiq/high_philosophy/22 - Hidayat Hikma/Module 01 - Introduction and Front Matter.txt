Justification for Studying Ancient Philosophy
Author defends the continued study of ancient philosophy for students of Islamic sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three reasons the author gives for studying ancient philosophy within an Islamic context.

Core Idea
Ancient philosophy is a necessary tool for defending Islam from recurring false ideas.

In this preface, the author anticipates a common objection: why study outdated Greek philosophy when modern sciences dominate? He responds with three compelling justifications that every student of Islamic sciences must understand. This lesson walks you through those reasons, showing how they connect to the broader tradition of Kalam and the defense of the faith.

Guided Path

Identify the question the author addresses

The reasons are: 1. False ideas never disappear; 2. Historical enmity between Greek philosophy and Islam necessitates Kalam; 3. Philosophy contains many benefits.

Commentary
This passage is the preface (muqaddima) of the book Hidayat Hikma. The author opens with praise of Allah and blessings on the Prophet and his companions. He then anticipates an objection: why study ancient philosophy when it is outdated and modern sciences dominate? The author answers by giving three reasons. First, false ideas like the pre-eternity of the world and reincarnation never disappear; they reappear in new forms. Therefore, a student of Islamic sciences must be aware of them to defend the faith. Second, Greek philosophy has historically been hostile to Islamic teachings, and Muslim theologians (mutakallimun) have refuted many of its positions. To continue this refutation, students must learn Kalam, which uses logical and philosophical tools. Third, Greek philosophy contains many benefits, such as discussions of astronomy, human affairs, ethics, and even the afterlife. The author qualifies that not all of it is sound; some parts are objectionable, but the good should be taken, as the hadith says: 'The word of wisdom is the lost property of the believer.' However, he excepts theology (ilahiyyat), which is left entirely to divine revelation. Thus, the preface establishes the necessity and utility of studying philosophy, including logic, within an Islamic framework.

Key Terms
الفلسفة اليونانية (al-falsafa al-yunaniyya): Greek philosophy, the ancient philosophical tradition from Greece, which includes metaphysics, natural philosophy, and logic; studied and critiqued by Muslim scholars. [Note: In later Islamic contexts, 'falsafa' specifically refers to the Peripatetic tradition; here it encompasses Greek philosophy broadly.]
علم الكلام (ilm al-kalam): Islamic scholastic theology, which uses rational arguments to defend and explain Islamic beliefs; often engaged with philosophical concepts.

Three Reasons for Studying Ancient Philosophy: An Islamic Scholarly Justification
Publisher explains three reasons for studying ancient philosophy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three reasons given in this preface for studying ancient philosophy and explain the selective adoption principle based on the hadith.

Core Idea
Islamic scholars study non-Islamic ideas not to adopt them wholesale, but to discern truth from falsehood.

This preface addresses a modern objection: why study ancient Greek philosophy when we have modern sciences? The publisher provides three justifications that reveal a key Islamic intellectual principle: critical engagement with foreign knowledge. Understanding this principle is essential for any student of Islamic sciences when encountering non-Islamic ideas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Locate the question posed by critics of ancient philosophy in the passage. What is their concern? They argue that ancient philosophy is abandoned and people have turned to modern sciences.
The objection is in the Arabic after 'أما بعد'.
2
Extract the first reason
The need to know hostile ideas because they persist in new forms. Find the example given: eternity of the world (قدم العالم) and metempsychosis (التناسخ).
These false ideas reappear in different guises.
3
Extract the second reason
Historical antagonism between Greek philosophy and Islamic ideas. The mutakallimun rejected many philosophical views using legal proofs (الأدلة الشرعية), so learning kalam is necessary to distinguish correct from corrupt.
This reason is marked with '۲' in the Arabic.
4
Extract the third reason
Benefits in astronomy (فلكيات), human affairs (معاش ومعاد), and ethics (أخلاق فاضلة ورذيلة). The hadith 'الكلمة الحكمة ضالة المؤمن' is cited to support selective acceptance.
Except theology (إلهيات), which is based solely on divine law.
5
Synthesize the overarching principle
The believer takes wisdom wherever found, but theology is from divine law only. This is the selective adoption principle: accept truth, reject falsehood.
This principle is applied to all non-Islamic knowledge, not just ancient philosophy.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents from Hidayat Hikma, listing the chapter headings of the Second Art (Theology) and the Third Art (Angels). The Second Art focuses on establishing the existence and attributes of the Necessary Being (God), including chapters on proving His existence, the identity of His essence and existence, His unity, His necessity, His knowledge (of Himself, universals, and changing particulars), and His will and generosity. The Third Art deals with angels, specifically the intellects: proving the intellect, their multiplicity, their eternity, and how they mediate between God and creation. The page ends with a note ('yaddasht'). This is purely structural front matter and contains no argumentation or definitions.
