Rainbow Debate: Illusion vs Reality
Author defines rainbow and presents Peripatetic vs. others dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Peripatetic position on the rainbow as an illusion and the objection-response pattern used to defend it.

Core Idea
The rainbow: real object or optical illusion? The debate reveals how perception can deceive.

The rainbow appears real, yet philosophers debated whether it has external existence. In this passage, the author introduces the rainbow and the dispute between the Peripatetics, who call it an illusion, and others who assert its reality. You will learn to identify the dialectical structure of objection and response that underpins this debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing positions
The author presents two groups: the Peripatetics say the rainbow is an illusion (khayal), others say it exists externally. Mark these positions.
2
Extract the meaning of 'illusion'
The author defines 'illusion' here: seeing an image in a polished surface (mirror) and incorrectly believing the image is actually there. Note the key components: the object, the polished surface, and the mistaken belief.
3
Find the objection (mu'tarid)
An objector asks: if the image is not actually in the mirror, how can one see it there, since vision only pertains to what exists? Identify this objection.
4
Find the response (jawab)
The answer explains the optical mechanism: the visual ray reflects from the mirror to the object, then the object is seen. The mistaken belief arises because sight occurs after reflection. Identify this response.
5
Assess the dialectical outcome
The author notes that the response holds for extramission theory but not for intromission theory. Evaluate whether the objection is fully resolved or if the debate continues.

As for the rainbow, it occurs only from the impression of the light of the greater luminary (sun) in moist parts in a circular form. Rainbow: Know that the philosophers differed concerning the rainbow and similar phenomena like the halo: are they illusions (khayalat) or not? The Peripatetics held that they are illusions, while others held that they exist in external reality. The meaning of illusion here is that one sees the image of a thing together with the image of a polished object that manifests it, like a mirror, so one supposes that the image is actually present in it. It was said: If the image is not present in it, how can one conceive of seeing it in it? For vision only pertains to what is present. The answer was: Although the image is not present in a polished manifesting object, the rays emanating from the eye are reflected from it to the visible object, and then the visible object is seen. Since its sight occurs after the reflection of the visual ray from a polished object to it, one supposes that its image is imprinted in that polished object. This is correct according to the doctrine of those who posit the emission of rays from the eye to the visible object. As for the doctrine of those who posit the impression of the image in the eye, it is not. The explanation of this: If there exist, in the direction opposite to the greater luminary, moist parts in a circular shape such that the visual ray is reflected from those parts to that luminary, and behind them is a dense body, either a mountain or a dark cloud, and the luminary is near the horizon, and we look at those parts and the visual ray reflects from them to the luminary, we see the light of the luminary in those parts without its shape; because if the mirror is very small, the shapes of the parts do not appear completely. Moist circular parts, and the variation of their colors is due to the variation of the light of the luminary and the different colors of the clouds.

Commentary
This passage defines the rainbow and presents a philosophical dispute about its ontological status. The author begins by describing the rainbow as occurring from the impression of sunlight in moist circular particles. Then he notes that philosophers disagreed: the Peripatetics (followers of Aristotle) claim the rainbow is an illusion (khayal) — a mere appearance that does not correspond to an external object — while others argue it exists in external reality. The author explains the concept of illusion here: seeing the image of an object alongside the image of a polished surface (like a mirror) leads one to think the image is actually in that surface. An objection is raised: if the image is not actually in the surface, how can we see it there, since vision only apprehends what exists? The author provides the answer: the rays from the eye are reflected by the polished surface to the object, and then the object is seen. Because the sight occurs after reflection, one mistakenly believes the image is imprinted in the polished surface. The author notes that this explanation holds for those who believe rays exit the eye (extramission theory), but not for those who believe images enter the eye (intromission theory). He then gives further optical details: for the rainbow to appear, there must be moist circular particles opposite the sun, with a dense background (mountain or dark cloud), and the sun near the horizon. The light is seen in those particles without its shape due to the small size of the reflecting surfaces. The colors vary due to the sun's light and the cloud colors. This passage thus combines definition, dialectical dispute, and scientific explanation.
