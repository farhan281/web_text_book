Three Schools on Body Composition and the Place of Metaphysics in Natural Science
Author introduces the debate on body composition, classifies three schools, and states his plan to refute atomism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the three schools on body composition (mutakallimun, Ishraqiyyun, mashsha'un) and explain why the refutation of the indivisible part is placed in the natural sciences.

Core Idea
The composition of the body is contested between atomism, illuminism, and hylomorphism—and this metaphysical question grounds the study of nature.

This passage opens a discussion that might seem out of place: why does a natural science text begin by refuting a metaphysical doctrine? The author, al-Abhari, explains that the refutation of the indivisible part (atomism) is a necessary principle for understanding many natural phenomena. He then classifies the three major schools on the composition of bodies: the mutakallimun (theologians) who are atomists, the Ishraqiyyun (Illuminationists) who see body as a simple continuous substance, and the Peripatetics (mashsha'un) who posit a composition of hyle and corporeal form. Al-Abhari adopts the Peripatetic view, setting the stage for his refutation.

The Disjunctive Syllogism in Hyle's Place Specification
Disjunctive syllogism for hyle's place specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive syllogism in the argument about hyle's place specification and evaluate the need for a cause.

Core Idea
Without a cause, hyle's specification of a particular place is impossible preponderance without a preponderator.

In this passage, the author employs a disjunctive syllogism to analyze what happens when hyle receives body form. The argument eliminates absurd possibilities and then shows that the remaining possibility also requires a cause. Understanding this logical structure is essential for grasping the philosophical proof for a specifier (murajjih). This lesson walks you through the steps of the syllogism and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
When hyle (without position) acquires body form, there are three possibilities: it occupies no place, all places, or some places. This is the exhaustive disjunction.
The disjunction covers all logical possibilities.
2
Eliminate the first two options
The first option (no place) is impossible because body form gives extension, implying a place. The second option (all places) is impossible because a single body cannot be everywhere. The author claims these are self-evidently absurd.
These eliminations are based on the nature of bodyhood.
3
Examine the third option
The third option (some places) seems plausible but fails because hyle has equal relation to all places; selecting a particular one without a cause is preponderance without a preponderator, which is impossible.
This step introduces the principle of sufficient reason.
4
Consider the objection and response
An objector suggests that a specific form or state could determine the place. The response: a specific form only gives a universal place (e.g., air's form requires air's realm), not a specific spot; states have no role in place-requirement.
The objection is refuted by showing it fails to account for specification within the universal place.
5
Reconstruct the overall conclusion
Thus, hyle cannot acquire a specific place unless there is a cause (murajjih). The disjunctive syllogism shows that all options lead to impossibility without a cause, proving the necessity of a specifier.
This conclusion supports the broader philosophical position that contingent events require a cause.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of hyle, now focusing on what happens when body form (surat jismiyya) attaches to it. The author presents a disjunctive syllogism: when hyle becomes possessed of position (dhat wad'), it must either occupy no place, all places, or some places. The first two are absurd: nothing with position can be placeless, and a single entity cannot be in all places simultaneously. The third option is also impossible because hyle, being equal in relation to all places, would require a cause (murajjih) to specify a particular place. Without such a cause, the specification is arbitrary and impossible. An objector then suggests that hyle could acquire a specific form (like air) or a state that determines its place. The author responds that specific forms only determine a universal place (e.g., the element's natural position), not a particular location within that universal place; the same problem of specification recurs. States have no effect because place-requirement belongs to form alone. The author then notes a subtle point: if hyle belongs to a universal element, it already has a universal place, thus no specifier is needed. But he refutes this by arguing that if elemental hyle were formless, void would exist, which is impossible; celestial hyle cannot be formless either because that would entail change. The passage concludes with a contrast: the Islamic theologians (mutakallimun) reject hyle and form entirely, positing instead a body composed of indivisible parts (atoms) that can be cut and rejoined.

The Impossibility of Preponderance without a Cause and the Possibility of Hyle
Author argues that hyle can occupy any place and defends against the water-to-air objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the reductio proof that hyle cannot be restricted to a specific place and defend it against the water-to-air objection using the concept of previous position as a preponderator.

Core Idea
If hyle were restricted, that would require an impossible preponderance without a cause; thus it must be able to occupy any place.

The author presents a foundational argument in natural philosophy: prime matter (hyle) in itself has no inherent relation to any particular place. If it were constrained to some places, that would violate the rational principle that any preference among equals requires a cause (tarjih bi-la murajjih is impossible). But an objector raises a physical example—water turning into air—that seems to involve a change of place without a clear cause. The author responds by showing that the water's prior spatial position itself provides the required cause, thus preserving the principle.

Guided Path
1
Understand the reductio structure
The author argues: assume hyle were restricted to some places. Then there must be a cause (preponderator) selecting that place over others, because hyle's relation to all places is equal. But there is no such cause (since hyle in itself has no property favoring one place). Therefore the assumption leads to the impossible consequence of preponderance without a preponderator. Hence hyle cannot be restricted.
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum, showing that the negation of the claim (hyle is possible) is impossible.
2
Identify the objection
The objector says: when water turns into air, the resulting air ends up in a specific region (e.g., above the sea rather than above the land), even though all regions of air are equally available. This seems like a case of preponderance without a preponderator, which would refute the principle.
The objection uses a concrete physical example to challenge the abstract argument.
3
Analyze the response: previous position as preponderator
The author replies that the water has a previous position (wad' sabiq) that gives it a closer relation to the air directly above it. This proximity acts as a preponderator. So the transformation is not arbitrary; it is determined by prior location.
The key is that the water is not in a neutral state—it has a specific location that influences the outcome.
4
Apply the distinction: abstract hyle vs. concrete transformation
In the case of abstract hyle, there is no prior position, so the reductio holds. In the transformation, the water's previous position supplies a cause, so the principle is not violated. This shows that the principle of preponderance requiring a cause is universal and consistent.
This distinction prevents the objection from undermining the original argument.

Commentary
This passage is dialectical: it presents a proof concerning the nature of hyle (prime matter), then defends it against an objection using a detailed response. The author begins by stating the claim: hyle is possible (i.e., it can equally occupy any place). The proof is as follows: If hyle were to exist in some specific place rather than others, that would require a preponderator (a cause that gives preference) to choose that place over others, since hyle in itself has an equal relation to all places. But such preponderance without a preponderator is impossible. Therefore, hyle must be capable of occupying any place alternately when combined with bodily form. The author then addresses an objection: when water turns into air (or vice versa), the transformed substance ends up in a specific region of the air (e.g., above the sea rather than above land), seemingly without a preponderator. The response is that the transformed substance (water) has a previous position that provides a preponderator: proximity. Water is already in a specific location relative to the air above it, so there is a reason (proximity) for that air region to be preferred. Thus, the objection fails, and the original point about hyle's equal relation to all places is upheld for abstract hyle, which has no prior position. The Islamic example used is the transformation of water to air, which is a natural phenomenon discussed in physics.

Analyzing the Void Argument: Can Nothing Have Magnitude?
Author critiques the void argument against theologians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical argument against void as nothing, identify its hidden premise, and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
The void argument assumes essences are identical; if not, the conclusion doesn't follow.

In this passage, the author presents and critiques an argument used by the Illuminationists against the theologians who claim place is pure nothing (void). The argument hinges on the fact that void can be characterized as larger or smaller, which cannot be true of non-existence. We will formalize this argument and examine a crucial hidden assumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
The theologians claim that place (or void) is pure nothing ('la shay'a mahdan'). The Illuminationists argue against this. Locate this claim in the Arabic: 'المكان على تقدير كونه عبارة عن الخلاء لا يمكن أن يكون لا شيئا محضا كما هو مزعوم المتكلمين'.
2
Extract the main argument against the claim
The argument: Void has degrees (e.g., void between two walls is less than void between two cities). But if void were pure nothing, it could not have such properties. Therefore, void is not pure nothing. This is a modus tollens: If void is nothing, then it cannot have properties like increase/decrease. It does have these properties; therefore it is not nothing.
The author presents this as a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Formalize the argument
P: Void is pure nothing. Q: Void can have increase/decrease. The argument: If P then not Q. Q. Therefore not P. This is valid modus tollens.
4
Identify the hidden premise
The author points out that this argument depends on the assumption that material and immaterial dimension are similar in essence (تماثل البعد المادي والمجرد في الماهية). If the opponent denies this similarity, the argument fails because the property of having increase/decrease might only apply to material dimension, not to immaterial void.
This hidden premise is the key to the critique.
5
Evaluate the overall argument
The author does not fully endorse either side but shows that the Illuminationist argument is only convincing if the opponent accepts the similarity of essences. This reveals the dialectical nature of the debate.
Consider whether the argument is sound or merely valid.

Every Body Has a Natural Place: Proof by Elimination of Coercion
Author proves that every body has a natural place by eliminating coercion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive argument that proves every body has a natural place and explain why a body cannot have two natural places.

Core Idea
A body's place is either by nature or coercion; if coercion is absent, it must be by nature.

In this passage from Hidayat al-Hikma, the author establishes one of the foundational principles of Islamic physics: every simple body has a single natural place toward which it moves when unimpeded. Using a disarmingly simple disjunctive syllogism, he shows that a body must have a natural place if we imagine all external forces removed. He then proves that it cannot have two such places, because that would lead to contradiction. This argument was crucial for explaining why fire rises, earth falls, and water seeks its level.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial hypothesis
The author asks us to suppose there are no coercive forces (qawasir). Coercers are external forces that move a body against its nature, like throwing a stone upward.
This is a thought experiment to isolate the body's inherent tendency.
2
Identify the disjunctive argument
The body is in a place. That place must be either due to the body itself (bi-dhatihi) or due to a coercer (li-qasir). Since coercers are absent, it must be due to itself. This is a disjunctive syllogism: A or B, not B, therefore A.
3
Connect 'due to itself' with 'nature'
The author equates 'due to itself' with 'due to its nature' (tabi'a). The nature is the body's substantial form that necessitates a specific place. This establishes the existence of a natural place.
In Islamic philosophy, nature is an internal principle of motion.
4
Analyze the proof against two natural places
If a body had two natural places, and it occupies one, then it either seeks the other or not. Both cases lead to contradiction: seeking makes the first non-natural, not seeking makes the second non-natural. Hence a body has only one natural place.
This is a reductio ad absurdum by case analysis (taqsim).
5
Extract the logical structure
The first proof is a disjunctive syllogism; the second is a conditional proof with cases. Both are foundational tools in Islamic logic and natural philosophy.
Understanding these forms helps evaluate arguments in fields like kalam and usul al-fiqh.

The Natural Shape of Bodies: A Categorical Proof
Author proves every body has a natural shape, using finitude and the removal of coercers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the categorical syllogism proving every body has a natural shape and explain why simple bodies are spherical.

Core Idea
Every body has an intrinsic shape determined by its nature, not by external coercion.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma presents a rigorous proof that every body has a natural shape. The author uses a first-figure categorical syllogism and then distinguishes simple bodies (spherical) from composite bodies (shape determined by mixture). Understanding this argument sharpens your ability to analyze physical definitions using logical form.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim (natija)
The conclusion the author wants to prove is: 'Every body has a natural shape.' Locate this statement in the Arabic.
It appears at the beginning: 'كل جسم فله شكل طبعي'.
2
Break down the syllogistic premises
The proof uses three premises: 'Every body is finite' (كل جسم متناه), 'Everything finite is shaped' (وكل متناه فهو متشكل), and 'Everything shaped has a natural shape' (وكل متشكل فله شكل طبعي). Notice the chain: body → finite → shaped → natural shape.
This is a first-figure AAA mood (Barbara) if formalized as a chain of three propositions.
3
Validate the major premise via conditional argument
The author proves 'everything shaped has a natural shape' by a conditional: if we remove all coercers, the body takes a specific shape; that shape must be from its nature (since coercion is absent). So shape is natural.
This is a modus tollens reasoning: if shape were from coercion, then coercion would exist; coercion does not exist (by assumption), so shape is from nature.
4
Distinguish simple vs composite bodies
The author then classifies bodies into simple (basīṭ) and composite (murakkab). For simple bodies, natural shape is spherical; for composite, shape depends on mixture and species.
The spherical shape argument uses uniformity of power and matter.
5
Examine the spherical shape proof
A simple body has a single power and uniform matter. A single power in uniform matter produces uniform action. Only the sphere has uniform action in all directions; any other shape would require different actions in different directions, implying either different agents or different recipients, both absent in a simple body.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: non-spherical shape leads to a contradiction.

Author defines motion and rest, then proves a mover is necessary.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion is gradual actualization; rest is the absence of motion in a movable thing.

This lesson opens the section on motion and rest with precise definitions and a powerful logical proof. The author defines motion as a gradual exit from potentiality to actuality, and rest as the absence of motion in something capable of motion. Then he demonstrates that no body can move by virtue of its mere corporeality—there must be an external mover. Understanding these foundational definitions and this argument is essential for everything that follows in Islamic physics and theology.

The conclusion is that the antecedent is false—a body does not move by sheer corporeality. Therefore every moving body must have a mover distinct from its body.

Section on motion and rest. Motion is the gradual exit from potentiality to actuality. Rest is the absence of motion in that which is able to move. Every moving body has a mover other than its corporeality; because if a body moved by virtue of being a body, then every body would be moving, which is false. So the antecedent is false. Section on motion. That which exists in actuality is either fully actual with no potentiality, like the Creator and the intellects according to the philosophers, or it is actual in some respects and potential in others, like bodies. That which is potential can exit to actuality; otherwise it would not be potential but impossible, and it was assumed potential. So when it exits from potentiality to actuality, it is either instantaneous or gradual. The gradual exit of potentiality is called motion. Then motion is used in two senses: one is the body being between the beginning and end such that it is not at any point of the distance after reaching nor before – meaning a state of the body that necessitates its passage over the distance, and it does not settle as long as it is described by it at any of its points. This is called intermediate motion, and it undoubtedly exists externally. The second is the extended thing, beginning from the start of the distance continuing to the end, coinciding with the distance, divisible by its division – meaning a non-stationary extension with gradual parts that occurs by the body's gradual passage over the distance, and it is called sectional motion because it occurs by traversing the distance. Does it exist externally or not? Some say it does not exist externally but in the imagination. Others say it exists externally because it is the locus of time, and time subsists in it, and time exists externally, so its locus must exist externally. That which is fully actual cannot have motion because motion is for attaining a perfection that was absent, and since all perfection is already actual for it, motion is inconceivable for it. Similarly, rest is the absence of motion in that which can move.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion is not all alike; it differs by category: quantity, quality, place, and position.

In this passage, the author classifies motion into four fundamental types based on Aristotelian categories. Each type corresponds to a different accidental category: quantity, quality, place, and position. Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing change in the physical world, especially in Kalam discussions of temporality and accident theory.

Guided Path
1
Learn the accidental categories
The author lists four categories of accident that admit motion: al-kamm (quantity), al-kayf (quality), al-ayn (place), and al-wad' (position). Note that only four of the nine accidental categories allow motion essentially.
The other five categories do not admit motion; the passage says the reason is in longer works.
2
See the examples for each
For quantity: growth and decay (e.g., a plant increasing in size). For quality: water heating or cooling while remaining water (alteration). For place: translation of a body from one location to another (e.g., a bird flying). For position: circular motion of a sphere around its center.
The author stresses that motion requires the persistence of the specific form (e.g., water remains water).
3
Distinguish position from place
Motion in position changes the relation of parts to each other and to external things without necessarily changing the body's location. Circular motion is the prime example: the sphere stays in place but parts reorient.
This is crucial for understanding later objections in Kalam.
4
Apply the classification
Given a real phenomenon, determine which type(s) of motion are present. For example, a person standing up involves both place (change of location) and position (reorientation of parts).
Multiple types can coexist.

Distinguishing Positional Motion from Place Motion
Author discusses motion in position with an objection about rotating sphere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a rotating sphere is an example of motion in position without motion in place, and refute the objection that each part's movement implies whole movement.

Core Idea
A body can move in position without changing location because its parts reorient while the whole stays put.

The author presents a subtle objection: if every part of a rotating sphere changes place, does the whole sphere not also change place? This lesson unpacks the response implied by the passage, using the concept of motion in position as a change in the relation of parts without a change in the body's overall location.

Guided Path
1
Review the definition of motion in position
Motion in position is a change in the relation of a body's parts to each other and to external things, without necessarily changing the body's location. The example given is a sphere rotating about its center.
This is sometimes called 'circular motion'.
2
Analyze the objection
The objection: if every part of the sphere leaves its place (since each part moves to a new spot), then the whole sphere must also leave its place, because the whole is nothing but its parts.
The objection is based on the principle that if all parts change, the whole changes.
3
Identify the response
The passage cuts off, but the response is hinted: the whole maintains its place because the parts move within the same overall location. The center of the sphere does not move, and the aggregate of parts remains in the same region. So the whole's location is unchanged even though each part changes position relative to each other.
This shows that motion in position is a distinct type of change.
4
Apply to other cases
Consider a person turning around while standing in one spot. The parts (head, shoulders) change orientation, but the person's overall location stays the same. This is motion in position without motion in place.
Compare to walking, which involves both types.

Definition and Proof of Estimation (al-Wahm)
Author defines estimation and proves its existence using the sheep-wolf example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define estimation (al-wahm) by its genus and differentia, and reconstruct the conditional argument (modus tollens) that proves its existence.

Core Idea
Animals perceive meanings, not just forms – that is the work of estimation.

In this passage, the author defines estimation as a faculty in the brain that grasps particular meanings, like the sheep's perception of enmity in a wolf. He then offers a conditional proof: if estimation did not exist, the sheep could not distinguish friend from foe, but it does, so estimation must exist. This lesson will walk you through extracting the definition and the logical structure of the proof.

Guided Path
1
1. Read the Arabic definition of estimation
Find the clause starting with 'وأما الوهم فهو قوة...' Identify the genus ('قوة') and the differentia ('تدرك المعاني الجزئية الموجودة في المحسوسات'). Also note the additional locative description.
2
2. Formalize the proof as a conditional argument
The author states: 'فلو لم تكن تلك القوة... لما امتاز العدو والصديق' (If not that faculty, then no distinction). This is a conditional: If P (no estimation), then Q (no distinction). But we observe not-Q (distinction exists). Therefore, not-P (estimation exists). This is modus tollens.
Modus tollens: if P → Q, and ¬Q, then ¬P.
3
3. Verify the validity of the argument
Check that the observed behavior (the sheep flees from wolf and approaches lamb) indeed falsifies the consequent. Since the consequent is false, the antecedent must be false, so estimation exists. This is a valid deductive argument.

The Faculty of Memory: Definition and Proof
Definition and proof of memory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of al-hafiza and reconstruct the conditional proof for its existence.

Core Idea
Memory is the brain's faculty for preserving meanings; its existence is proven by recognition after forgetting.

In this passage, the author defines memory as a faculty located in the front of the third brain cavity that preserves particular meanings. He then provides a proof based on the phenomenon of recognition after a lapse of time. We will parse the definition and unpack the conditional argument that establishes the existence of this faculty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic passage. It is الحافظة (al-hafiza).
2
Extract the definition components
The definition gives a location (أول التجويف الآخر من الدماغ) and a function (تحفظ المعاني الجزئية). The genus is قوة (faculty).
3
Understand the proof structure
The proof is a conditional argument: if memory did not exist, then recognition after forgetting would be impossible. But we do recognize, so memory must exist. This is modus tollens.
4
Formalize the conditional
Write the argument in if-then form: If memory does not exist, then recognition is impossible. Recognition occurs, therefore memory exists.
The form is valid: denying the consequent leads to denial of the antecedent.

The argument is a conditional: if memory did not exist, then recognition would be impossible; but recognition occurs, so memory exists. This is a standard proof for a retentive faculty.

Understanding Al-Wahid: Types of Unity
Author defines al-wahid and its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify different ways in which a thing can be called 'one' according to the Islamic logical tradition.

Core Idea
Unity is relative to the aspect from which you consider the thing.

The concept of 'one' (al-wahid) is foundational in Islamic logic and metaphysics. It is not simply about numerical indivisibility but about the aspect from which something is considered undivided. The author provides a detailed classification that will help you distinguish between different types of unity, from generic unity to numerical unity, both genuine and non-genuine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of al-wahid
Find the phrase 'from the aspect from which it is called one'. This shows that unity is not absolute but relative to a particular consideration.
The same thing can be one from one aspect and many from another.
2
List the five types of unity
Extract from the text: unity by genus, by species, by accidental predicate, by subject, and by number. Note the examples given for each.
Man and horse are one in genus (animal); Zayd and Amr are one in species (human); cotton and snow are one in whiteness (predicate); writer and laugher are one in subject (human).
3
Distinguish numerical unity subtypes
Numerical unity is divided into genuine (indivisible) and non-genuine. Non-genuine further splits into continuity (potentially divisible into like parts, e.g., water) and composition (actually divisible into unlike parts, e.g., a house).
Genuine unity is like a point or the divine essence in kalam.
4
Apply the classification to new examples
Given a pair or object, determine which type of unity applies. For instance, all believers are one in faith (species? or accidental? depends on aspect).
Practice with Islamic examples to internalize the categories.

And it may be by genus, like man and horse; and may be by species, like Zayd and Amr; and may be by predicate (accident), like cotton and snow; and may be by subject, like the writer and the laugher. One (examples): man and horse are united in animality; and species, like Zayd and Amr united in humanity; and a descriptive predicate, like cotton and snow united in whiteness, which is predicated of them by nature since it is their description; and a subject, like the writer and the laugher united in man, which is their subject by nature. If not both existent but one existent and the other non-existent: if the locus of the non-existent is considered fit for the existent, they are privation and possession; if not considered fit, they are affirmation and negation. And opposition between two non-existents is not considered, because if both absolute, they are one and opposition is not conceivable as it requires difference; if relative, they co-exist.

This passage is a definitional classification dividing the concepts of 'one' (al-wahid) and 'many' (al-kathir) and then explaining the types of opposition (taqabul). Understanding Taqabul: Four Types of Opposition
Author defines taqabul and its four types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of opposition (taqabul) and apply them to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Opposition is not just contradiction; it requires a shared locus and aspect.

The author defines opposition (taqabul) as two things that cannot coexist in one locus from one aspect. This framework is essential for logical analysis, especially in defining terms and constructing arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. The four types are based on whether both are existent and how they relate to each other.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of taqabul
Opposition is defined as two things not coexisting in one locus from one aspect. The locus is the subject, and the aspect is the specific property considered.
Example: heat and cold cannot coexist in the same body at the same time.
2
Determine if both are existent
First check if both opposites are existent (positive). If yes, go to step 3. If one is existent and the other non-existent, go to step 4.
Existent means having real existence in the external world.
3
For two existents: distinguish relatives vs contraries
If each is conceived only in relation to the other, they are relatives (mutadayifan), e.g., father and son. If not relationally defined, they are contraries (mutadaddan), e.g., heat and cold.
Relatives depend on each other for definition; contraries do not.
4
For one existent and one non-existent: check the locus
If the locus (subject) is fit for the existent, it is privation/possession (adam wa-malaka), e.g., sight and blindness in the eye. If not fit, it is affirmation/negation (ijab wa-salb), e.g., existence and non-existence of a blind man's sight.
Privation implies the locus naturally receives the quality; negation does not.

The Five Types of Priority
Author enumerates and explains the five types of priority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five types of priority (al-mutaqaddim) as defined in classical Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Priority is not only temporal — causal priority coexists with its effect.

The concept of priority (al-taqaddum) permeates Islamic thought, from cosmology to jurisprudence. This passage from al-Hidaya al-Hikma enumerates five distinct meanings of 'prior', each applicable in different contexts. Understanding these distinctions prevents confusion, especially between temporal and causal priority, a point often misunderstood even by scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching claim
Note that the author begins by stating that priority is a mental consideration (dhihni), not an external entity. This sets the stage that the classification is conceptual.
Relations like priority are mental constructs.
2
List each type and its definition
Go through each of the five types: temporal, natural, honorary, rank-based, causal. For each, understand the defining condition (e.g., temporal: cannot coexist in origination; natural: the posterior cannot exist without the prior).
The passage provides clear Arabic definitions.
3
Analyze the examples
For each type, the author gives an example (e.g., one and two for natural; Abu Bakr and Umar for honorary; rows in mosque for rank; hand and pen for causal). Examine how the example fits the definition.
Examples are taken from everyday life and Islamic history.
4
Distinguish causal priority from temporal
Focus on the fifth type: causal priority. The author emphasizes that cause and effect are simultaneous in time, and priority is only in causality. Many people mistakenly think causal priority implies temporal delay.
This is a key subtlety: the complete cause cannot exist without the effect.

Chapter on the Prior and the Posterior. And that is in the mind, not in external existence. The prior is said of five things: first, the prior by time, which is obvious; second, the prior by nature, which is that the other cannot exist except while it exists with it, but it may exist without the other existing, like the priorness of one over two; third, the prior by honor, like the priorness of Abu Bakr over Umar; fourth, the prior by rank, which is that which is closer to a defined starting point, like the arrangement of rows in the mosque relative to the mihrab; fifth, the prior by causality, like the priorness of the movement of the hand over the movement of the pen. It is realized in the mind because affirmation and negation are relations, and relation is among mental considerations that have no realization except in the mind, and the author meant that by his saying: 'and that is in the mind.' The prior by time is that whose existence cannot coincide temporally with the other thing considered relative to it as prior, although they may coexist in duration in some cases, like the priorness of the father over the son. By nature, meaning the nature of the prior necessitates priority over the posterior, like the nature of the part necessitates priority over the whole. The one: for the one can exist without the two existing, but the two cannot exist except with it. The rows: for the rows are considered arranged from the mihrab of the mosque; what is closer to it is called the front row, and what is further is the back row. The prior by causality is the independent agent of efficacy, gathering the conditions of efficacy and removing its impediments, and the prior by cause coexists temporally with the effect because of the impossibility of the effect lagging behind its complete cause; priority is only in terms of causality. Many people, due to their habit of temporal priorities, do not fully acknowledge priority by causality; they think that the prior by cause exists in reality without the effect, then the effect comes into being through its influence. But this is nothing other than temporal priority, and demonstration has shown the impossibility of the effect lagging behind its complete cause in time. As for the posterior, it is said of what is opposite to the prior.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'prior' (al-mutaqaddim) and its five types, along with the corresponding 'posterior' (al-muta'akhkhir). The author begins by noting that the discussion of priority is a mental consideration, not an external existence, because relations like priority are mental constructs. He then enumerates the five types of priority: temporal, natural, honorary, rank-based, and causal. Each type is explained with examples. Temporal priority is straightforward. Natural priority is when one thing cannot exist without the other, but the other can exist without it, like the number one relative to two. Honorary priority is based on merit, such as the precedence of Abu Bakr over Umar. Rank-based priority is about spatial or conceptual proximity to a reference point, like rows in a mosque relative to the mihrab. Causal priority is the most subtle: the cause is prior to the effect in causality, but they coexist in time because the effect cannot lag behind the complete cause. Many people mistakenly think causal priority involves temporal delay, but demonstration refutes that. Finally, the posterior is simply the counterpart of each type of prior.

Four Senses of Eternal and Originated
Author defines four senses of eternal/originated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four senses of eternal and originated (by essence vs. by time) and classify any entity accordingly.

Core Idea
Eternal and originated each have two meanings: essential and temporal.

Before we can reason about the world's beginning, we must clarify what 'eternal' and 'originated' actually mean. The author gives four precise definitions that prevent confusion between essential dependence and temporal beginning. Understanding these categories is crucial for any theological or philosophical argument about creation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four terms
Look at the Arabic: there are four terms: القديم بالذات, القديم بالزمان, المحدث بالذات, المحدث بالزمان. Each combines a root (qadim/muhdath) with a qualification (bi al-dhat/bi al-zaman).
The root meanings: qadim = ancient, muhdath = newly occurring.
2
Extract the definition of each
Read each definition: 'that whose existence is not from another' (eternal by essence), 'that which has no beginning to its time' (eternal by time), 'that whose existence is from another' (originated by essence), 'that whose time has a beginning' (originated by time). Write each in your own words.
3
Apply distinctions to examples
Consider God: He is eternal by essence (self-existent) and eternal by time (no beginning). Consider the world (if created): it is originated by essence (depends on God) and originated by time (had a beginning). Consider a philosopher's possible eternal world: originated by essence but eternal by time.
This shows the four combinations are logically independent.
4
Check for shift alert
Note the shift alert: in kalam, 'qadim' often means uncreated, but here it distinguishes essence from time. This precision avoids conflating ontological dependence with temporal duration.
The same term can have different technical meanings across disciplines.

Chapter on the Eternal and the Originated. The eternal by essence is that whose existence is not from another; the eternal by time is that which has no beginning to its time; the originated by essence is that whose existence is from another; the originated by time is that whose time has a beginning, and there was a time when it did not exist, then that time passed and a time came when it became existent. And every temporal originated thing is preceded by matter and duration, because the possibility of its existence is prior to its existence. If it were not prior to its existence, then before its existence it would be either impossible or necessary, and both are false. As for the first, because the impossible cannot be conceived to exist at any time at all, so how could it be originated? As for the second, because the necessary is necessarily existent always; its non-existence cannot be conceived at any time, such that origination could be conceived. So when it is established that the originated thing before its existence is neither impossible nor necessary, it is determined that it is possible before its existence, because the concepts are limited to three. And that possibility is an existent thing, not non-existent; for if it were non-existent, the statement 'its possibility is negated' would be true, and from its truth the truth of 'there is no possibility for the originated thing' would follow, because a non-existent thing is not a thing such that its attribution to something could be valid. So if possibility were non-existent, its negation of the originated thing would be valid, because it is impossible for what is not a thing to be attributed to something. But the negation of possibility from the originated thing is false by the preceding demonstration. So its being non-existent is also false, due to the entailment between them. And when the possibility of the originated thing is existent before its existence, it is either subsistent by itself or subsistent by another. The first is false, because the possibility of existence is an expression of a quality of the relation of existence to essence, and quality is among accidents, not substances. So it is determined that it subsists by another.

Commentary
This passage defines four key terms: eternal by essence (God), eternal by time (what has no temporal beginning, like the world according to some), originated by essence (what depends on another), and originated by time (what has a beginning in time). The author then argues that every temporal originated thing must be preceded by both matter and duration. The proof proceeds as follows: every originated thing, before its existence, cannot be impossible (since impossible cannot exist at all) nor necessary (since necessary always exists and cannot have a beginning). Therefore, it must be possible. This possibility (imkan) is not a mere non-existence; it is an existent attribute. The author argues that if possibility were non-existent, then we could say 'its possibility is negated,' but that would imply that the originated thing has no possibility at all, which is false because we just established it is possible. Hence, possibility is an existent. Now, this existent possibility cannot subsist by itself because it is a quality (relation of existence to essence), and qualities are accidents that require a substrate. Therefore, it must subsist in another substance, which is the matter that precedes the originated thing. Thus, every temporal originated thing is preceded by matter (madda) and duration (mudda).

Proof of Matter and Duration for Temporal Origins
Author proves every originated thing is preceded by matter and duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound argument that every temporal originated thing is preceded by matter and duration, and identify each step of the reasoning.

Core Idea
Possibility must be an existent attribute requiring a substrate.

Having defined the terms, the author now proves a crucial metaphysical claim: every temporal originated thing (like the world) must be preceded by matter and duration. This is not obvious; it requires a chain of reasoning that moves through possibility, existence, and the nature of attributes. Let's walk through each logical step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The claim is: 'وكل حادث زماني فهو مسبوق بمادة ومدة' - every temporal originated thing is preceded by matter and duration. This is the conclusion to be proved.
2
First sub-argument: possibility precedes existence
The author argues that the possibility (imkan) of the originated thing is prior to its existence. He proves this by elimination: before existence, the thing is neither impossible nor necessary, so it must be possible. This is a disjunctive syllogism.
The three modalities come from Arabic logic: impossible, necessary, possible.
3
Second sub-argument: possibility is existent
Given that possibility exists prior to the thing, the author argues that this possibility is an existent attribute (amr wujudi), not a mere non-existence. The proof is by contradiction: if it were non-existent, then its negation would be true, leading to 'no possibility', which contradicts the first sub-argument.
This step is crucial: it establishes that possibility is real, not a mental fiction.
4
Third sub-argument: possibility requires a substrate
Now, this existent possibility cannot be a substance (because it is a quality, a relation of existence to essence). Therefore, it must inhere in a substance, which is the matter (madda) of the originated thing. And the priority in time is duration (mudda).
The conclusion: every temporal originated thing has pre-existing matter and duration.

Proof that Every Temporal Originated Thing Requires Matter and Duration
Proof that imkan is an existential attribute preceding origination.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that possibility (imkan) is an existential attribute that must precede the existence of a temporal thing and inhere in a matter.

Core Idea
Possibility is a real existential quality, not a mere negation, that requires a substratum.

The author of Hidayat al-Hikma presents a proof that every temporal originated thing (hadith zamani) is preceded by both a duration (mudda) and a matter (madda). While the duration requirement is obvious, the matter requirement is established through analyzing the ontological status of possibility (imkan). This lesson will guide you through the logical steps that show imkan must be an existential attribute inhering in a subject.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim
The author asserts that every temporal thing requires matter and duration. Focus on the matter part, which needs proof.
2
Identify the trilemma
Before existence, the thing must be either impossible, necessary, or possible. Show why impossible and necessary are impossible.
3
Conclude possibility
Hence, it must be possible before existence.
4
Prove imkan is existential
If imkan were non-existential, then 'its possibility is not' would be true, which entails 'there is no possibility for it'. But that is false because the thing is possible. Therefore, imkan is existential.
5
Conclude imkan inheres in a subject
Imkan is an accident (mode of relation), so it cannot be self-subsistent. It must inhere in something, which is the matter.

The author argues that the duration aspect is obvious: something that comes into existence after not existing requires a prior time. The matter aspect requires more proof. Objection and Response: The Distinction Between Affirming and Negating Non-Existential Attributes
Objection and response regarding the existential status of imkan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between affirming a non-existential attribute and negating an attribute, and evaluate the objection that the same argument would make non-existence and impossibility existential.

Core Idea
Affirming a negative attribute is not the same as negating it; this distinction saves imkan from being misconstrued.

The proof that imkan is existential faced an objection: if the argument is sound, then non-existence (adam) and impossibility (imtina') would also have to be existential, which is false. The commentator responds by clarifying the difference between asserting that 'its possibility is not' (which would be affirming a negation) and 'there is no possibility for it' (which is negating the attribute). This lesson unpacks this subtle logical distinction and its importance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The critic argues that the same reasoning used to show imkan is existential would also apply to non-existence and impossibility, leading to contradiction.
2
Analyze the critic's reasoning
The critic claims that if the proof works for imkan, it should work for adam and imtina' because they too are attributes that could be described as 'not' etc.
3
Understand the commentator's response
The commentator explains that there is a difference between saying 'its possibility is not' (affirming a negative attribute) and 'there is no possibility for it' (negating the attribute). The proof only works for the latter.
4
Clarify the distinction
Affirming a negative attribute means the attribute is non-existential, but it is still something attributed. Negating an attribute means simply denying its existence.
5
Apply the distinction
For adam and imtina', they are genuinely non-existent; affirming them as attributes is different from negating them. The proof only requires that we cannot have 'no possibility' while the thing is possible, but that does not apply to adam.

Classification of Existents: State and Place
Author classifies existents into substance and accident via state-place dependency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any existent into state or place based on the dependency relationship.

Core Idea
The direction of need determines whether we are dealing with hyle-form or subject-accident.

The passage begins with a fundamental division of existents into those that are 'specific to something and flowing in it' and those that are not. This leads to the concepts of state (hal) and place (mahal). The key is understanding the dependency relationship: does the place need the state, or does the state need the place? This classification is the foundation for defining substance and accident.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two broad categories
Recognize that the author divides existents into those that are 'specific to something and flowing in it' (involving a state and place) and those that are not. The latter are not subject to this classification.
2
Determine the direction of need
For existents involving a state and place, analyze whether the place needs the state or the state needs the place. This is determined by necessity: if one could exist without the other, the dependent one is the needing side.
If the place needs the state, it is hyle and the state is form. If the state needs the place, the place is subject and the state is accident.
3
Apply the correct terminology
Based on the direction of need, assign the terms: hyle (hayula) and form (sura) when the place needs the state; subject (mawdu') and accident (arad) when the state needs the place.

Substance and Accident: Definitions and Divisions
Definition and division of substance and accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the five types of substance from the nine categories of accident and classify any given existent accordingly.

Core Idea
Every existent is either a self-subsisting substance or an accident inhering in a subject.

This passage establishes the foundational metaphysical division of all existents into substance and accident. You will learn the precise definitions and the specific subtypes of each. This classification is indispensable for subsequent discussions in Islamic philosophy and theology, as it determines how we talk about God, the soul, and the physical world.

Guided Path

Substance can be: (a) matter (hayūlā) if it is a locus needing a state, (b) form (ṣūra) if it is a state needing a locus, (c) body (jism) if composite of matter and form, (d) soul (nafs) if it manages bodies, (e) intellect (ʿaql) if it is neither matter nor form nor body nor soul. Quantity: Definition, Circularity, and Divisions
Defines quantity and its divisions with a criticism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given quantity as discrete or continuous, and identify the circularity in defining quantity by equality.

Core Idea
Quantity is defined as that which accepts equality, but this is circular—better to say it accepts division.

This passage defines quantity and its divisions, but a critical note points out a circularity. Understanding how quantity is properly defined and categorized is essential for subsequent logical discussions, as quantity is one of Aristotle's categories. We will analyze the definition, its flaw, and the subsequent classification into discrete and continuous (constant and non-constant).

Identify the definition of quantity

2
Spot the circularity
The commentary says equality is unity in quantity, so defining quantity by equality is circular. The alternative is 'ما يقبل القسمة لذاته' (what accepts division in itself).
3
Distinguish discrete vs continuous
Discrete: number (no common boundary). Continuous: has common boundary. Continuous splits into constant (parts coexist, e.g., line, surface) and non-constant (time, parts do not coexist).

Quality: Definition, Types, and a Logical Nuance
Defines quality and its divisions with a note on definitional parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given quality as sensible, psychic, or dispositional, and explain why genus and differentia are not always external parts.

Core Idea
Quality is a disposition that does not essentially entail division, but some qualities may divide through their subject.

The passage now turns to quality, defining it negatively (not like quantity). It is divided into three main types: sensible, psychic, and dispositional. A critical note clarifies that genus and differentia are not necessarily separate parts in reality, addressing a potential objection about the simplicity of the soul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of quality
Find the Arabic: 'هيئة في شيء لا تقتضي لذاته قسمة ولا نسبة' (a disposition in a thing not entailing division or proportion in itself). Note the qualifier 'لذاته' – it does not entail division essentially.
2
Differentiate sensible qualities
Stable (sweetness, saltiness) vs unstable (blushing redness, fear-induced paleness). These are physical.
3
Identify psychic qualities
Divided into states (unstable, like initial writing skill) and faculties (stable, like mastery of writing). Example: writing at the beginning vs after mastery.
4
Examine dispositional qualities
Hardness (resistance to being affected) and softness (easy to be affected).
5
Analyze the critical note about genus and differentia
The author argues that genus and differentia are not external parts of the thing defined; they are unified in the subject. This is important for defining simple entities like the soul.

The Nine Accidental Categories in Hidayat al-Hikma
Author defines nine accidental categories with sub-divisions and critiques quantity definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the nine accidental categories and identify their sub-divisions as defined in Hidayat al-Hikma.

Core Idea
The nine accidental categories are the complete classification of all that inheres in substance.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma provides the classical definitions of the nine accidental categories (al-a'rad al-tis'a). You will learn the definition of quantity (al-kamm), its division into discrete and continuous, and a critical refinement of its definition. Then you will explore quality (al-kayf) and its three types, and the remaining six categories: where, when, relation, possession, position, and disposition (isti'dad). Understanding these categories is essential for analyzing metaphysical and physical attributes in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Define quantity and its types
Find the definition of quantity (al-kamm): 'that which accepts equality and inequality per se'. Note how it is divided into discrete (number) and continuous (magnitude and time). Continuous quantity is further split into constant (line, surface, body) and non-constant (time).
The definition uses equality, which the author later critiques as circular.
2
Recognize the critique of the quantity definition
The passage states that defining quantity via equality is circular because equality itself means 'unity in quantity'. A better definition is 'that which accepts division per se'. Understand why this avoids circularity.
Division is more fundamental than equality.
3
Extract the definition of quality and its three types
Quality (al-kayf) is defined as 'a state that does not per se necessitate division or proportion'. It divides into sensible (permanent and non-permanent), psychic (states and habits), and potential (hardness, softness). Also mention qualities specific to quantities (triangularity, evenness).
The author notes that genus and differentia are not separate external parts.
4
List the remaining six accidental categories
Read the definitions for: where (al-ayn: state due to place), when (al-mata: state due to time), relation (al-idafa: repeated relative state, divided into real and common), possession (al-milk: state due to surrounding, e.g., clothing), position (al-wad': form from relation of parts, e.g., sitting).
Possession and position are subtle; position includes celestial motion.
5
Understand the dual meaning of disposition (isti'dad)
The passage notes that 'isti'dad' has two meanings: (1) privative: absence of a thing in a capable substrate (not a quality because non-existential), (2) existential: accidents that make matter fit for something (a quality). Distinguish these.
This nuance is important in discussions of potentiality in Islamic philosophy.

Potential: i.e., qualities of the genus of potentiality, and they are of two types: one: non-passivity from others easily like hardness, and two: passivity from others easily like softness. Commentary
This passage from Hidayat al-Hikma defines the accidental categories (al-a'rad) after a preceding discussion of substance. It begins by defining quantity (al-kamm) as that which per se accepts equality and inequality, and divides it into discrete (like number) and continuous, the latter itself subdivided into constant (magnitude: line, surface, thickness) and non-constant (time). There follows a critical note on the definition of quantity using equality: it is circular because equality itself is defined in terms of quantity; a better definition would be 'that which accepts division per se'. The text then elaborates on discrete and continuous quantity, explaining that discrete has no common boundary between parts, while continuous does, and that the common boundary is not part of the two parts but a distinct type. Continuous quantity is further divided into constant (parts coexist) and non-constant (parts do not coexist). Next, quality (al-kayf) is defined as a state that does not entail division or proportion per se, and is classified into: sensible (permanent and non-permanent), psychic (states and habits), and potential (hardness and softness). The text then lists the remaining accidental categories briefly: 'where' (al-ayn: state due to location), 'when' (al-mata: state due to time), 'relation' (al-idafa: repeated relative state), 'possession' (al-milk: state due to something surrounding, like clothing), and 'position' (al-wad': form due to relation of parts). The section on relation distinguishes between real relation (in principles) and common relation (in derivatives). Finally, the explanation of 'position' notes that it can refer to the relation of parts within a body or to external things, as in the movement of the celestial sphere.

Al-Ayn: The Category of Place
Definition of the category of place (al-ayn)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of place (al-ayn) in any object and distinguish it from other categories like position or possession.

Core Idea
Place is a state arising from being in space, not the space itself.

In the Aristotelian categories adopted by Muslim philosophers, every accident falls under one of nine categories. The first we examine is al-ayn, the category of place. As the author defines it, al-ayn is not the location itself but the state a thing has because it is located in space. This distinction is crucial for understanding how categories classify entities.

Read the definition of al-ayn

The definiendum is 'al-ayn'. The definiens is 'a state that occurs for a thing due to its occurring in place.' The genus is 'state' and the differentia is 'due to occurring in place.'

Does it include all instances of being in place? Does it exclude things that are not in place? Consider an accident like 'whiteness' which inheres in a body; it is in place because the body is in place, so whiteness has a state of being in place indirectly.

Al-Mata: The Category of Time
Definition of the category of time (al-mata)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of time (al-mata) in any object and distinguish it from the category of place.

Core Idea
Time is a state arising from being in time, not the time itself.

Just as place is a category for spatial location, time (al-mata) is the category for temporal location. The author defines it analogously to al-ayn, emphasizing that it is the state of occurring in time, not the duration or moment itself. This symmetry helps in understanding how categories pair spatial and temporal aspects of existence.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of al-mata
The passage states: 'al-mata is a state that occurs for a thing due to its occurring in time.' Recognize the parallel structure with al-ayn.
2
Identify the genus and differentia
Genus: state (hala). Differentia: due to occurring in time (bi-sabab husulihi fi al-zaman).
3
Test the definition for completeness
Does it include all temporal occurrences? For example, an event like 'the creation of the world' occurs in time, so it falls under al-mata. What about timeless entities? They are excluded.
4
Contrast with al-ayn
Al-ayn is spatial, al-mata is temporal. A thing can have both a place and a time state simultaneously, but they are distinct categories.

Al-Idafa: The Category of Relation
Definition of the category of relation (al-idafa) with distinction between real and commonly known

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of relation (al-idafa) and distinguish between real and commonly known relation.

Core Idea
Relation is a relative state that recurs, considered either in principles (real) or in derivatives (commonly known).

Among the categories, al-idafa (relation) is unique because it involves a comparison between two things. The author defines it as a relative state that recurs, and then distinguishes two senses: real relation (like fatherhood as a universal) and commonly known relation (like 'father' as a concrete term). This distinction is important for applying the category correctly in logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of al-idafa
The passage says: 'al-idafa is a relative state that recurs.' Note that it is 'relative' (nisabiyya) and 'recurs' (mutakarrara).
2
Understand 'recurs'
A relation recurs when it is considered in comparison to another relation. For example, fatherhood is conceived relative to sonship; both are relations that recur between relata.
3
Distinguish real vs commonly known relation
Real relation (haqiqiyya) is considered in the principles (mabadi), i.e., the abstract universal. Commonly known (mashhura) is considered in derivatives (mushtaqqat), like 'father' and 'son' as concrete terms.
4
Test with examples
Fatherhood is a real relation; 'father' is a commonly known relation. Both fall under al-idafa but at different levels of abstraction.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the definition of two categories from Aristotle's ten: action (al-fi'l) and passion (al-infi'al). The author first gives the standard definitions: action is a state that occurs to a thing due to its influence on another, exemplified by a cutter while it cuts; passion is a configuration due to being affected by another, like something being heated while it heats. Then the commentator critiques the definition of action. He observes that the definition implies that action is something distinct from influence, being an effect of influence. But the obvious fact is that action is the very influence itself, not something else. Moreover, the example (cutter cutting) indicates that action is gradual, non-stationary in existence. This restricts the definition to physical bodies (like cutting) and excludes the act of God and the intellects, which are instantaneous. To avoid breaking the enumeration of exactly nine accidental categories (since action and passion are two of them), the definition should be more general. The commentator then clarifies that 'action' has two meanings: one is a verbal concept (masdari) abstracted from the action, which has no external reality; the other is a real quality existing in the agent externally, which has a relation to the object and causes the object. If the original author meant this second meaning by 'state', then his definition is acceptable, but then this quality is not an effect of the verbal (abstracted) influence; rather, it is the cause for abstracting that verbal concept. In other words, the real action (the quality) is prior, and the abstracted concept is derived from it. This refines the understanding of action and passion as categories.

Reconstructing the Conditional Reductio for the Necessary Being's Self-Sufficiency
Author argues that the necessary being's essence alone suffices for its attributes, using reductio.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional reductio argument for the self-sufficiency of the Necessary Being and identify its modus tollens structure.

Core Idea
The Necessary Being's self-sufficiency is proven by a conditional reductio: if dependent on another, it would not be necessary.

This passage from Hidayat al-Hikmah presents a foundational argument in Islamic philosophy. Using a conditional reductio, the author demonstrates that the Necessary Being's essence alone suffices for all its attributes. In this lesson, we will carefully parse the logical steps of this argument and see how conditional reasoning (istithna'i) is deployed in a metaphysical context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
The author argues that the essence of the Necessary Being is sufficient (kāfiya) for its attributes. This is the conclusion to be proved.
2
Find the assumption for reductio
The opposite assumption: if the essence were not sufficient, then some attribute would come from an external cause (ghayr).
This sets up a conditional: if not sufficient, then dependence on another.
3
Trace the major conditional
If dependence holds, then the presence/absence of that cause would determine the attribute's existence/non-existence. Consequently, the essence would not necessitate existence unconditionally.
The consequent of this conditional is a contradiction to the definition of 'necessary being'.
4
Examine the inner dilemma
The author considers two sub-possibilities: existence is necessitated with the attribute or without it. Both lead to a contradiction (khalaf) if an external cause is involved.
This tightens the reductio by showing no consistent scenario under the assumption.
5
Derive the conclusion
Since the assumption leads to an impossibility (the necessary being would not be necessary), the assumption is false. Therefore, the essence is sufficient—no external cause needed.
This is modus tollens: If P then Q; Q is false; so P false.

This argument is foundational in Islamic philosophy for establishing the self-sufficiency (ghina) of God.

The Disjunctive Syllogism Proving Existence is Not a Shared Genus
Author demonstrates that existence is not a common genus between the Necessary Being and contingents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three disjuncts in a theological trilemma and evaluate how each is eliminated to reach the conclusion.

Core Idea
Existence is not a genus: God's existence is identical to His essence, while contingent existence is accidental.

The passage presents a sophisticated disjunctive argument to prove that existence cannot be a shared genus between God and created things. By carefully parsing the three possibilities for absolute existence and showing each leads to absurdity, the author demonstrates the ontological uniqueness of the Necessary Being. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of that trilemma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis and conditional assumption
The author begins with a conditional: If the Necessary Being shared existence with contingents, then absolute existence would have a certain nature. This sets up the disjunction that follows.
Look for the phrase 'لو كان مشاركا للممكنات في وجوده' (if He shared existence with contingents).
2
Recognize the three disjuncts of the trilemma
The author presents three possibilities for absolute existence: it either necessitates disengagedness (tajarrud), necessitates non-disengagedness, or necessitates neither. These are the three horns of the trilemma.
The disjuncts are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
3
Examine the refutation of the first disjunct
If absolute existence necessitates tajarrud, then contingent existence would also be disengaged (not accidental to essence). But we know contingent existence is accidental (as shown by the heptagon example). So this disjunct is false.
The heptagon argument shows existence is not identical to essence in contingents.
4
Examine the refutation of the second disjunct
If absolute existence necessitates non-tajarrud, then God's existence would be accidental. But God's existence is identical to His essence, not accidental. So this disjunct is false.
God's existence is necessary and self-subsistent.
5
Examine the refutation of the third disjunct and conclude
If absolute existence necessitates neither, then tajarrud is merely possible for each individual. This would make God's tajarrud dependent on an external cause, contradicting His self-sufficiency. Since all three disjuncts are false, the original assumption (shared existence) is false.
The conclusion is that the Necessary Being does not share existence with contingents.
