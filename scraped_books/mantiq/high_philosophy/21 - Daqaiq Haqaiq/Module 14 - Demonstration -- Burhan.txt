Is a Negative Conclusion Self-Evident? The Objection and Response
Author responds to an objection that negative conclusions are self-evident without proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'evident' (bayyin) and 'close to evident' (qarib min al-bayan) in a logical argument and explain why a negative conclusion in certain syllogisms still requires proof.

Core Idea
Being close to evident is not the same as being evident; proof is still needed.

In this passage, the author anticipates an objection: if the middle term is affirmed of one extreme and denied of the other, isn't the negative conclusion obvious without further proof? The author responds with a crucial epistemological distinction that separates logical certainty from mere plausibility. Understanding this distinction is key to rigorous demonstration in Islamic sciences.

He distinguishes 'close to evident' (qarib min al-bayan) from 'evident' (bayyin).

Analyze the author's further argument
The author adds: 'وكل ما يتخيل في الاحتجاج على كونه بيناً فلا يزيد على نفس الدعوى'. What does this mean for the objection?
Any attempt to prove its self-evidence just repeats the claim, not a proof.
4

Without proof, the ruling rests on intuition, not certainty.

Distinguishing Qiyas al-Khalf from 'Aks al-Qiyas
Author defines qiyas al-khalf and distinguishes it from 'aks al-qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish qiyas al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum) from 'aks al-qiyas (conversion of the syllogism) based on the three criteria given in the passage: priority, type of negation, and dependency on a prior syllogism.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-khalf is a fresh reductio; 'aks al-qiyas depends on a prior syllogism.

In this passage, the author clarifies two related logical operations that often get confused: qiyas al-khalf (reductio ad absurdum) and 'aks al-qiyas (conversion of a syllogism). Both involve taking a contradictory proposition and combining it with an accepted premise, but they differ fundamentally. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly constructing proofs in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two operations
Locate the terms 'قياس الخلف' and 'عكس القياس' in the Arabic. Note that the author is comparing them.
2
Extract the first criterion: temporal priority
The author says: 'عكس القياس لا يكون إلا بعد قياس آخر، والخلف فلا يكون إلا مبتدأ'. This means 'aks always comes after another syllogism, while khalf begins anew. Underline this difference.
3
Extract the second criterion: type of negation
The author says that in 'aks the taken proposition may be a contrary (ضد) or a contradictory (نقيض), but in khalf it is only the contradictory. Check the Arabic for the words 'ضد' and 'نقيض'.
4
Extract the third criterion: dependency on a result
In 'aks, the taken proposition is always the contradictory of a conclusion that is the result of a previous syllogism (نقيض مطلوب هو نتيجة). In khalf, the desired conclusion is not necessarily a result of a prior syllogism. Note this difference.
5
Apply to an example
Consider a kalam proof: 'If the world were eternal, it would have no cause; but everything temporal has a cause; contradiction; therefore the world is temporal.' This is qiyas al-khalf because it starts afresh. Now imagine we already proved that 'every event has a cause', and then we take the contradictory of that conclusion to form a new syllogism; that would be 'aks. Identify which is which.
This example is from the tradition of proving the world's contingency.

Critique of Tard wa 'Aks as a Dialectical Method
Author critiques the method of tard wa 'aks used to validate analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the method of tard wa 'aks is insufficient for demonstrative certainty and identify its limitations when applied to juristic analogy.

Core Idea
Tard wa 'aks is dialectical: complete induction is impossible and cause may be compound.

This passage critiques the method of tard wa 'aks, which proponents of analogy use to justify causal links. The author argues that this method is dialectical, not demonstrative, because it requires impossible complete induction and ignores the possibility that the cause is compound. Understanding this critique helps us evaluate the strength of analogical arguments in Islamic law and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method being critiqued
What is tard wa 'aks? It is the method of checking whether the judgment is present when the proposed cause is present and absent when it is absent, used to validate the causal link in analogy.
The method relies on co-extensiveness (tard) and co-exclusiveness ('aks).
2
Understand the requirement of complete induction
The author states that tard wa 'aks requires induction of all particulars and all circumstances. Since the branch (far') is outside the original, complete induction is impossible, making the method incomplete.
Complete induction (istiqra' tamm) is rarely achievable in practice.
3
Analyze the compound cause objection
Even if complete induction were possible, the shared attribute may only be part of a compound cause. The judgment may depend on the whole set of attributes. Thus presence of the shared attribute in a new case does not guarantee the judgment.
This shows that tard wa 'aks cannot eliminate the possibility of a compound 'illa.
4
Apply the critique to a fiqh example
Consider the argument that 'intoxication' is the 'illa for prohibiting wine. Using tard wa 'aks, one checks: is every intoxicant prohibited? Is every non-intoxicant not prohibited? The critique shows that even if this holds, intoxication may be only part of the cause (e.g., combined with 'mind-altering'), so the analogy to other intoxicants is not certain.
This illustrates why usul scholars do not rely solely on tard wa 'aks for certainty.

Critique of Sabr wa Taksim Method for Causality
Author critiques the method of sabr wa taksim for establishing causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five key logical flaws in the sabr wa taksim method and explain why it does not yield certainty.

Core Idea
Sabr wa taksim fails because exhaustive enumeration is impossible and hidden causes may exist.

In usul al-fiqh, sabr wa taksim is a standard technique for identifying the 'illa (cause) of a ruling by enumerating all possible attributes and eliminating those that do not satisfy co-extensiveness. However, the author demonstrates that this method is far from rigorous. By examining his critique, you will learn to spot the hidden assumptions and weaknesses that undermine the certainty of such causal inferences.

Guided Path
1
State the claim being attacked
Identify the core claim of sabr wa taksim: that by listing all attributes of the original case and eliminating those that do not co-exist with the judgment, the remaining attribute is the cause.
2
Follow the deconstruction of exhaustiveness
The author argues that exhaustive enumeration is not guaranteed; there may be hidden attributes not captured. He compares it to a hidden object that requires great effort to see, unlike a camel in plain sight.
This challenges the assumption that all possible attributes are known.
3
Analyze the problem of compound and multiple causes
Even if exhaustive, the judgment may be caused by a combination of attributes or by a different cause in another case. The retained attribute might be common to multiple causes, so its presence does not prove it is the only cause.
4
Examine the non‑universality of the retained attribute
The retained attribute might be a genus that divides into species, and the cause may be specific to one species. For example, if the retained attribute is 'j', which splits into 'b' and 'd', the cause could be 'b' in one case and 'd' in another, defeating the inference.
5
Evaluate the critique’s internal validity
The author’s objections are logically sound: they identify genuine gaps in the method’s assumptions. The critique does not itself rely on an alternative method but shows why sabr wa taksim cannot yield certain knowledge.
This strengthens the argument that causal inference in fiqh requires additional controls.

Conflict between Intellect and Estimation in Epistemology
Author discusses the conflict between intellect and estimation in epistemological inquiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the judgments of intellect (ʿaql) and estimation (wahm) in theological arguments and explain why intellect takes precedence over estimation.

Core Idea
Intellect corrects estimation; do not mistake imaginative judgments for rational certainty.

In our study of reasoning, we often rely on different cognitive faculties. This passage highlights a crucial epistemological distinction: the faculty of estimation (wahm) can generate strong imaginative pictures that contradict rigorous intellectual proofs. The example of infinite dimensions beyond the world shows that estimation may lead us astray. Our goal is to understand how and why the intellect's verdict overrides estimation, especially in theological contexts where certainty is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two faculties involved
The passage contrasts estimation (wahm) and intellect (ʿaql). Estimation is an internal sense that judges particular meanings attached to sensory objects, often leading to false judgments. Intellect reasons using universal concepts and logical deduction. Note that both can produce judgments about the same matter, but they may conflict.
Estimation is not imagination (khayāl) but a higher internal sense that makes non-sensory judgments.
2
Examine the example: infinite dimensions beyond the world
The passage describes someone estimating infinite dimensions—either void-like or celestial—extending beyond the cosmos. This estimation suggests that space continues without end. However, the intellect, using a rational syllogism (qiyās ʿaqlī), judges that such infinity is impossible. Recognize that the estimated conclusion directly opposes the rational one.
The terms 'void-like' (khalāʾiyya) and 'celestial' (malāʾiyya) refer to different types of imagined extensions.

Distinguishing Burhan al-Inn from Burhan al-Lim
Author classifies demonstrations into burhan al-inn and burhan al-lim based on causal relationship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given demonstrative syllogism as either burhan al-inn or burhan al-lim by analyzing the causal relationship between its middle term and major term.

Core Idea
Two types of demonstration: one shows that something is, the other shows why it is.

In Islamic logic, demonstrations are not all the same. Some prove the existence of a property without revealing its cause, while others prove the property through its cause. The medieval scholars, especially in medicine and kalam, relied on this distinction to assess the certainty of arguments. This passage introduces the two types—burhan al-inn and burhan al-lim—using a vivid medical example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term and the major term
In the example, the middle term is 'shivering and burning urine', and the major term is 'tertian fever'. The minor term is the patient's body. The middle term links the subject to the predicate.
2
Determine the causal direction
Ask: Does the middle term cause the major, or is it caused by the major, or is there no direct causal link? Here, shivering and burning urine do not cause fever; they are symptoms (concomitants) of fever. So it's a sign, not a cause.
3
Apply the classification rules
If the middle term is neither cause nor effect of the major, but both are effects of a common cause or are concomitants, it is burhan al-inn. If the middle term is the cause of the major (or vice versa), it is burhan al-lim (here called dalil).
The passage notes that when the middle term is a cause, it must be a complete cause; when it is an effect, the cause must be unique.

Consider: 'This patient has a fever because his urine is burning.' Is the middle term (burning urine) a cause or a sign? It is a sign (symptom), so this is burhan al-inn. If instead you say: 'The fever causes the burning urine,' then the middle term (fever) is the cause, and the inference would be burhan al-lim.

Commentary

Fourth Chapter: On Demonstration. That which necessitates assent in demonstration is not the cause giving the relation, but rather its effect or concomitant. And every predicate attributed to a subject by affirmation or negation, perpetually or at a specific time, while the giver of that relation is a cause external to the essence of the subject and predicate, and the knower knows the existence of the predicate for the subject, or its existence, not from the cause giving it; therefore he does not know perpetual necessity nor temporal; for the relation in the known manner is not for the essence of the subject and predicate without possibility, but rather it is for them by necessity due to an external cause. So if it is known not from the aspect by which it was necessary in the known manner, then it has been known from an aspect other than the aspect by which it is impossible for that relation to be otherwise. Thus, even if it necessitates knowledge that such-and-such is such-and-such, it does not necessitate knowledge that it cannot be otherwise. That is like one who knows that the sky is spherical by observation and sensory perception and does not know the cause necessitating its sphericity perpetually; he does not know that it must be so, or not so, at a time other than the time at which his knowledge of it occurred. So unless the cause giving the relation is perpetually present from the demonstration of 'why', it will not be beneficial for perpetual certainty. And even if his perception of that is by sense or otherwise, there is no impossibility of its opposite. That is, for example, if it is said: 'Every human has an aptitude for knowledge' and 'Everyone who has an aptitude for knowledge is rational', then it does not follow the knowledge that every human is rational, to the extent that one does not believe that there is no human who is not rational at some time; for the power of aptitude for knowledge is perpetually for every human by means of rationality. So one who knows that not from the relation, he knows it either by sense or by another meaning, which does not necessitate perpetual certainty in all. And thus what is known for the human by its mediation is not perpetually certain. [Stray 'yujib'] Except that here the perpetuity of the power of aptitude for every human is due to its cause, namely the rational; thus the rational has become known, so no need to demonstrate it. If the demonstration of aptitude for every human perpetually depends on knowing that he is rational, and the rational depends on knowing the power of aptitude for every human perpetually, then it is a forbidden circle. But perhaps perplexity occurs from what might be doubted here by those not experienced in the sciences: that one who sees a perfected craft knows that it has a perfect maker, and likewise one who knows the existence of a brother as a middle term knows necessarily that he has a brother, and moreover one who knows that this number is odd knows by its means necessarily that it is not even, and one who knows that it is not odd knows by its means that it is not even, and likewise the reverse. And the knowledge in these forms does not vanish, nor at any time, and yet the inference was not by its cause. Rather, the inference in the first is by the effect, in the second by the concomitant correlative which is neither cause nor effect, and in the third by the opposite, and the opposite is not a cause for its opposite. And likewise the syllogism of the opposite (indirect proof): we know the truth by refuting its contradictory, a knowledge that does not vanish and is not from negation. The way of the solution is to say: As for the first, the subject (the minor term in the conclusion) is either particular or universal. If particular, as it is said: 'This house is a perfected craft, so it has a maker' — that is not perpetual, because of the possibility of its demolition and loss of belief. If universal, as it is said: 'Every house is manufactured' — then every house has a maker, so the relation of craftsmanship to the house and to the maker is like the relation of the power of aptitude for knowledge to the rational and to every human, in that it is known that that does not necessitate perpetual certainty, so similarly here. As for the second, each of the two correlatives: its knowledge is with knowledge of the other; if one is known, the other is known with it, not by it, and there is no need to seek it. But if the other is unknown, then what corresponds to it from the middle term is not known. Therefore, every syllogistic form in which the middle term is correlative to the major term, the judgment that it is demonstrative is impossible. As for the third, the exclusion of each one of even or odd, either affirmative or negative, the knowledge of it is either from its cause or not from its cause. If not from its cause, there is no way to its perpetual certainty, and thus there is no way to perpetual certainty of the necessary consequent from it. If the knowledge of it is from its cause, then it is not hidden that the cause of existence of each of them is only the existence of its boundary, and the cause of its non-existence is the non-existence of its boundary. And then it is apparent that the existence of the boundary of one is not known except after knowing the existence of the boundary of the other. And then such a syllogism has no meaning, for the conclusion in it is known before the exclusion. Commentary
This passage is an epistemological analysis of the conditions for perpetual certainty in demonstrative reasoning. The author begins by stating that in demonstration, the cause that gives the relation (nisba) between subject and predicate is not what directly necessitates assent; rather, it is the effect (ma'lul) or concomitant (mulazim) of that cause. The argument proceeds: when a predicate is attributed to a subject perpetually or at a specific time, the relation is due to an external cause. If the knower knows the relation without knowing that cause, he does not possess necessary knowledge because he does not grasp the aspect of necessity. For example, someone who observes the sky to be spherical but does not know the cause of its perpetual sphericity does not know that it must always be so. Similarly, the syllogism 'Every human has an aptitude for knowledge; everyone with such aptitude is rational' does not yield the perpetual certainty that every human is rational, because the aptitude is perpetually present only by means of rationality, and knowing this without the cause (rationality) gives only contingent knowledge. The author then identifies a potential circularity: if proving the perpetuity of aptitude requires knowing rationality, and proving rationality requires knowing aptitude, a forbidden circle ensues. Following this, the author examines examples that might seem to provide perpetual certainty: inferring a maker from a perfect craft, inferring a brother from the existence of a brother as a middle term, and inferring evenness or oddness from its opposite. Although such knowledge is stable, the inference is not through the cause. The first example (craft) uses the effect, the second (brother) uses a correlative concomitant, and the third (even/odd) uses an opposite. The author then presents a solution (al-hall) addressing three cases. For the first case (effect), the minor term is either particular (e.g., 'this house')—then certainty is not perpetual because the house may cease to exist—or universal (e.g., 'every house manufactured')—then the reasoning parallels the earlier example of aptitude and rationality, failing to yield perpetual certainty. For the second case (correlatives), if one correlative is known, the other is known concomitantly, not through a middle term; thus, a syllogism with a correlative middle term cannot be demonstrative because the conclusion is already implicit. For the third case (opposites), knowledge of evenness or oddness must come from its cause (the boundary/limit of the number). If not from its cause, certainty is not perpetual; if from its cause, then the cause of existence of one is the existence of its boundary, which requires prior knowledge of the other boundary, making the syllogism circular and thus useless. The passage concludes that all such examples in the sciences are presented merely as lenient approximations, not rigorous demonstrations.

When Stable Knowledge is Not from the Cause: Three Cases
Author resolves three cases where stable knowledge seems to come from non-causal middle terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze inferences from effects, correlates, and opposites and explain why they do not produce perpetual certainty.

Core Idea
Stable knowledge from effects, correlates, or opposites is not from the cause, so it lacks perpetual necessity.

The author now addresses objections. He presents three cases where knowledge seems perpetual even though the inference is not through the cause: the maker from the craft, the brother from the middle term, and even/odd from the opposite. He then provides solutions showing why these do not meet the criteria for demonstrative certainty.

Guided Path
1
List the Three Cases
Identify the three inference types: (1) effect to cause (craft-maker), (2) correlative to correlative (brother-brother), (3) opposite to opposite (even-odd). Each seems to give stable knowledge, but the author argues it is not perpetual.
The examples are: craft→maker, brother→brother (by middle term), odd→not even.
2
Analyze the Effect Case (Craft-Maker)
If the subject is particular ('this house'), the inference is not perpetual because the house may cease to exist. If universal ('every manufactured house'), the reasoning parallels the earlier aptitude example: the middle term (craftsmanship) is an effect, not the cause of having a maker, so perpetual certainty fails.
The author's solution: particularity or lack of causal connection prevents perpetuity.
3
Analyze the Correlative Case (Brother)
If one knows that someone has a brother (middle term), the existence of a brother is known concomitantly. But if the brother is unknown, the middle term is not known. Hence, the syllogism is either redundant (conclusion already known) or impossible. Demonstration requires that the middle term be unknown before the inference.
The author says: 'every syllogistic form in which the middle term is correlative to the major term, the judgment that it is demonstrative is impossible.'
4
Analyze the Opposite Case (Even-Odd)
Knowledge that a number is odd (or even) must come from its cause (the boundary/limit). If not from the cause, no perpetual certainty. If from the cause, then the cause depends on the opposite boundary, leading to circularity. Therefore, such syllogisms are useless for demonstration.
The author also mentions qiyas al-khalf (indirect proof) where truth is known by refuting the contradictory, but that still does not provide perpetual certainty from the cause.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological analysis of the conditions for perpetual certainty in demonstrative reasoning. The author begins by stating that in demonstration, the cause that gives the relation (nisba) between subject and predicate is not what directly necessitates assent; rather, it is the effect (ma'lul) or concomitant (mulazim) of that cause. The argument proceeds: when a predicate is attributed to a subject perpetually or at a specific time, the relation is due to an external cause. If the knower knows the relation without knowing that cause, he does not possess necessary knowledge because he does not grasp the aspect of necessity. For example, someone who observes the sky to be spherical but does not know the cause of its perpetual sphericity does not know that it must always be so. Similarly, the syllogism 'Every human has an aptitude for knowledge; everyone with such aptitude is rational' does not yield the perpetual certainty that every human is rational, because the aptitude is perpetually present only by means of rationality, and knowing this without the cause (rationality) gives only contingent knowledge. The author then identifies a potential circularity: if proving the perpetuity of aptitude requires knowing rationality, and proving rationality requires knowing aptitude, a forbidden circle ensues. Following this, the author examines examples that might seem to provide perpetual certainty: inferring a maker from a perfect craft, inferring a brother from the existence of a brother as a middle term, and inferring evenness or oddness from its opposite. Although such knowledge is stable, the inference is not through the cause. The first example (craft) uses the effect, the second (brother) uses a correlative concomitant, and the third (even/odd) uses an opposite. The author then presents a solution (al-hall) addressing three cases. For the first case (effect), the minor term is either particular (e.g., 'this house')—then certainty is not perpetual because the house may cease to exist—or universal (e.g., 'every house manufactured')—then the reasoning parallels the earlier example of aptitude and rationality, failing to yield perpetual certainty. For the second case (correlatives), if one correlative is known, the other is known concomitantly, not through a middle term; thus, a syllogism with a correlative middle term cannot be demonstrative because the conclusion is already implicit. For the third case (opposites), knowledge of evenness or oddness must come from its cause (the boundary/limit of the number). If not from its cause, certainty is not perpetual; if from its cause, then the cause of existence of one is the existence of its boundary, which requires prior knowledge of the other boundary, making the syllogism circular and thus useless. The passage concludes that all such examples in the sciences are presented merely as lenient approximations, not rigorous demonstrations.

Non-Causal Middle Terms and Perpetual Certainty
Analysis of middle term types and conditions for perpetual certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between causal and non-causal middle terms and explain why only causal middle terms yield perpetual certainty in demonstration.

Core Idea
Certainty requires the middle term to be the cause, not effect, correlative, or opposite.

In this passage, the al-Daqa'iq author analyzes three types of inferences that produce knowledge but not through the cause: inference from effect to cause, from a correlative concomitant, and from an opposite. He also includes argument by contradiction (qiyas al-khalf). He argues that these do not yield perpetual certainty (al-yaqin al-da'im) and then presents the solution: the middle term in a certain demonstration must give the judgment about the minor term perpetually, whether the cause is known in essence or only in existence. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the strength of arguments in Islamic sciences, particularly in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three non-causal middle terms
Find in the passage the three types of middle terms that are not causes: the effect (al-ma'lul), the correlative concomitant (al-mulazim al-muda'if), and the opposite (al-muqabil). Also note the inclusion of qiyas al-khalf.
Each of these middle terms produces knowledge, but not through the cause of the conclusion.
2
Analyze why each fails to give perpetual certainty
For effect-to-cause: if the subject is particular, knowledge is temporary (e.g., 'this house is well-built so it has a builder' – the house may be destroyed). If universal, the relation is not causal, so certainty is not perpetual. For correlative: each is known with the other; if one is unknown, the other cannot be inferred through it. For opposite: knowledge of each depends on its definition, leading to circularity. For qiyas al-khalf: it proves existence (aniyya) but not through the cause, so certainty is not perpetual.
The author uses the example of even and odd to illustrate opposites.
3
Understand the author's solution for perpetual certainty
The middle term must give the judgment about the minor term on a permanent basis, regardless of whether the cause is known in its type and manner or only in its existence. This is the condition for true demonstration (burhan).
Induction and experience yield perpetual universal opinion (zann), not certainty, because the cause is not known.

Qiyas al-Khalf and the Conditions for Perpetual Certainty
Author discusses qiyas al-khalf and conditions for the middle term to yield perpetual certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish qiyas al-khalf from certain demonstration and identify the condition that the middle term must satisfy to yield perpetual certainty (yaqin da'im).

Core Idea
Qiyas al-Khalf proves existence, not cause; perpetual certainty requires a middle term that is a constant cause or attribute.

In this passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq, the author clarifies that not all demonstrative arguments yield the same kind of certainty. Qiyas al-khalf (argument by contradiction) validly proves that something must be true because its opposite leads to absurdity, but it only gives knowledge of existence (anniyah), not of the cause. For perpetual certainty, the middle term must be a permanent giver of the judgment. This distinction is critical for evaluating theological and legal proofs.

Perpetual certainty requires a middle term that is a constant giver of the judgment, either as cause or as known attribute; qiyas al-khalf and induction give only existence or general opinion.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
?
Construct a qiyas al-khalf argument to prove that the Qur'an is not created. Explain why this argument only proves existence (anniyah) and not the cause of uncreatedness.
?
Identify the middle term in the following usuli syllogism: 'Every act that causes harm is prohibited; smoking causes harm; therefore smoking is prohibited.' Determine whether this middle term gives perpetual certainty or only general opinion, and justify your answer using the conditions from the passage.
