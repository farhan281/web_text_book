The Four Levels of Existence
Author lists and ranks the four existences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four existences (al-wujud al-arba'a) and explain which are considered real (haqiqi) and which conventional.

Core Idea
A thing exists in the world, in the mind, in speech, and in writing — only the first two are real.

In this passage, the author introduces a foundational epistemological framework: the four levels of existence. Understanding this framework is crucial for grasping how Islamic logicians and philosophers analyze the relationship between reality, mental concepts, and the signs we use to communicate. This lesson will help you map the chain from external objects to mental images to words on a page.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four existences
Read the end of the Arabic passage where the author lists: existence in external objects (al-a'yan), in the mind (al-'aql), in speech (al-lafz), and in writing (al-kitaba). Write them down in order.
2
Distinguish real from conventional
The author says the first two are 'real' (haqiqiyan) and the last two are 'not real' (ghayr haqiqiyan). Reflect on what 'real' means here: independent of human convention?
Real existences are objective; conventional existences vary with language and culture.
3
Understand why speech and writing are conventional
The author explains that words and writing differ across nations and eras. If another community had agreed on different signs, they would be equally valid for understanding.
This shows the conventional nature of linguistic signs.
4
Recognize the ultimate goal
The author states that the highest goal among existences is mental existence (al-wujud al-'aqli), which is the perfection of the soul. This ties the four-existence doctrine to Islamic teleology.
Mental existence is the goal of knowledge; it leads to eternal happiness.

The second introduction: Explaining the need for logic. That [need] is [fulfilled] by one or a group, or by their inspiration and sending their motivations, or the motivation of some of them to establish himself and generalize that to others over time through indication and repetition, like the action of parents with a nursing infant, and like the deaf-mute's definition of what is in his mind through indication, but these are only beneficial for those who are present and listening, not for the absent and the non-existent. And it was also a requirement of providence to establish that by which the benefit of sharing is achieved for the non-existent and the absent as well. That is only completed by that which can have stability and permanence for itself, and the lightest of that is what is related to voluntary action. Therefore, for each segment of sound, another form of writing was established that indicates it, either by way of convention (tawqif) or inspiration, as previously mentioned for speech. And from that [writing] is composed what is composed from segments, so writing indicates speech, and speech indicates what is in the soul of forms and meanings, although it cannot be achieved except with the passage of time, abundance of offspring, and repetition, like a non-Arab learning Arabic and an Arab learning a non-Arabic language. It may not require, for the scripts of writing to indicate that which is composed of words, more than knowing the correspondence of the simple [letters] of writing to the simple [parts] of vocal segments, and that is something easy. For this reason, speech and writing were not both indicating what is in the soul with a primary indication without the mediation of one of them. The most excellent of the later scholars said: Therefore, writing indicates what is in the soul through the mediation of speech, and speech does not indicate what is in the soul through the mediation of writing. Otherwise, the difficulty that the learner would face in memorizing the indications of the forms of writing for what is in the soul of forms would be equal to the difficulty he faces in memorizing the indications of words for the forms of writing in their diversity, since it is not possible to apply the letters of writing to the segments of speech as we explained, just as it is not possible to apply speech to writing. But I have a consideration regarding this: It is not impossible that writing was established before speech in the same way that it was established after speech. So the form that is made of writing indicating the word 'human' would itself indicate what is in the soul of the meaning of 'human', and similarly for each one according to its case. And if that is the case, there is no obstacle to the correspondence of these simple [letters] of writing to the simple [parts] of vocal segments, in the manner of correspondence of the segments to them as previously, and that is also something easy. The closer [view] is that this is only because the predominant usage is what is most needed, and that is the indicator of the inner [thought] and its dispensation for everyone, and perhaps one is restricted to it for the majority. So it was necessary that it be the lightest and easiest of the two in terms of bringing it about and learning it, and that is speech. So writing indicates the verbal utterance (nutq lisani) which indicates the psychological effect (athar nafsani) that corresponds to external existence (al-wujud al-khariji). For that reason it was said that a thing has an existence in the external world (fi al-a'yan) – i.e., outside the minds – and an existence in the mind (fi al-'aql), and an existence in speech (fi al-lafz), and an existence in writing (fi al-kitaba). But the first two existences are real (haqiqiyyan) as previously [mentioned], while the other two are not real, because they vary with different eras and nations. For what has been established of words and writing as indicators among a people by convention (tawqif) or agreement (istilah) is not something that others must follow and adhere to. Rather, if they had agreed on something else, that would be in terms of understanding according to the convention of others, as has occurred in observable reality. And the highest goal among these existences is none other than mental existence (al-wujud al-'aqli), which is the psychological effect, for it is the perfection of the human soul, and through it eternal happiness is attained, as previously. So on this basis, whenever external existence has an existence in the mind that corresponds to it, it is then called 'known' (ma'lum), otherwise it is 'unknown' (majhul).

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion from the second introduction of Daqaiq Haqaiq, where the author explains the need for logic by examining how knowledge is communicated. The text begins by describing various ways of conveying knowledge to others: through direct inspiration, repetition, and pointing, as seen in parents teaching infants or deaf-mutes using gestures. However, these methods only benefit those present. To include absent and future generations, stable signs are needed, leading to the invention of writing as a system of symbols that correspond to speech segments. The author then quotes 'the most excellent of the later scholars' (possibly Fakhr al-Din al-Razi or Nasir al-Din al-Tusi) who argues that writing is derivative of speech, mediating the inner thought through the spoken word. The author critiques this view, suggesting that writing could have been primary if established before speech. Nevertheless, he concludes that speech is easier and more basic for everyday use, so writing conventionally indicates speech which in turn indicates the psychological effect (the mental concept) that corresponds to external reality. This leads to the famous doctrine of the four existences: existence in the external world (al-a'yan), in the mind (al-'aql), in speech (al-lafz), and in writing (al-kitaba). The first two are real, while the latter two are conventional and vary across cultures. The ultimate goal is mental existence, which constitutes the perfection of the soul and the attainment of eternal happiness. The passage concludes by stating that when external existence finds a corresponding mental existence, it is called 'known' (ma'lum), otherwise it is 'unknown'. This sets the stage for defining knowledge in the next section.

The Chain of Indication and the Primacy of Speech
Author examines the relationship between speech and writing as indicators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the chain of indication from external reality to writing, explain why speech is normally primary, and summarize the author's critique of the view that writing must always mediate speech.

Core Idea
Writing indicates speech, which indicates the mental concept, which corresponds to external reality — but speech might not always be the necessary intermediary.

The author builds a chain of indication: writing → speech → mental concept → external reality. He then quotes a prominent later scholar who argues that writing always depends on speech. However, the author offers a critical consideration: it is not impossible for writing to be established before speech. This lesson unpacks that chain and the debate about the order of priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chain of indication
Find in the Arabic where the author says: 'writing indicates speech, and speech indicates what is in the soul.' Write down the chain from the most derivative to the most fundamental.
The chain: outer world → mental concept → spoken word → written symbol.
2
Understand the conventional view
The author attributes to 'the most excellent of the later scholars' the claim that writing indicates what is in the soul only through the mediation of speech, not vice versa.
This view makes speech the direct indicator of the mental concept.
3
Follow the author's objection
The author says: 'I have a consideration: it is not impossible that writing was established before speech.' He argues that if writing were primary, the same form of a written word could directly indicate the mental concept without speech.
This challenges the necessary priority of speech.
4
See the author's conclusion
Despite the theoretical possibility, the author concludes that in practice speech is the 'lightest and easiest' and therefore is primary. Speech is what is most needed for everyday communication.
Practice, not logical necessity, makes speech primary.

The Necessity of Logic for Transmitting Knowledge
Author introduces the need for logic by discussing communication across time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why stable signs like speech and writing are necessary for sharing knowledge across time and space, and how this justifies the need for logic.

Core Idea
Logic is needed because knowledge must be communicated via signs that work for absent and future generations.

The passage begins by asking why we need logic at all. The author's answer is that humans must share knowledge with those who are not physically present — the absent and the non-existent. Simple gestures and pointing only work face-to-face. To overcome this, we need fixed, repeatable signs: speech and writing. Logic then ensures these signs are used correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem: sharing knowledge with the absent
In the beginning of the passage, the author describes methods of conveying knowledge: inspiration, repetition, pointing. He notes that these only benefit those present. Find the sentence where he says 'these are only beneficial for those present.'
The phrase: 'هذه إنما ينتفع بها من كان من الحاضرين المستمعين دون المعدومين والغائبين'
2
Recognize the solution: stable signs
The author says that providence requires something that can have 'stability and permanence' (قرار ودوام). This leads to the invention of voluntary signs — i.e., speech and writing.
Voluntary action (fi'l ikhtiyari) allows for the creation of signs.
3
Understand the role of speech and writing
Speech and writing allow knowledge to be transmitted across time (طول الزمان) and space. The author uses the example of a non-Arab learning Arabic, which takes time and repetition.
The stability of writing allows knowledge to be recorded and later retrieved.
4
Connect to the need for logic
Logic (mantiq) is the tool that regulates how these signs (especially definitions and arguments) are used to ensure correct knowledge. Without logic, signs can be misinterpreted, leading to error.
This is why the entire discussion occurs in the 'second introduction' of a logic text.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion from the second introduction of Daqaiq Haqaiq, where the author explains the need for logic by examining how knowledge is communicated. The text begins by describing various ways of conveying knowledge to others: through direct inspiration, repetition, and pointing, as seen in parents teaching infants or deaf-mutes using gestures. However, these methods only benefit those present. To include absent and future generations, stable signs are needed, leading to the invention of writing as a system of symbols that correspond to speech segments. The author then quotes 'the most excellent of the later scholars' (possibly Fakhr al-Din al-Razi or Nasir al-Din al-Tusi) who argues that writing is derivative of speech, mediating the inner thought through the spoken word. The author critiques this view, suggesting that writing could have been primary if established before speech. Nevertheless, he concludes that speech is easier and more basic for everyday use, so writing conventionally indicates speech which in turn indicates the psychological effect (the mental concept) that corresponds to external reality. This leads to the famous doctrine of the four existences: existence in the external world (al-a'yan), in the mind (al-'aql), in speech (al-lafz), and in writing (al-kitaba). The first two are real, while the latter two are conventional and vary across cultures. The ultimate goal is mental existence, which constitutes the perfection of the soul and the attainment of eternal happiness. The passage concludes by stating that when external existence finds a corresponding mental existence, it is called 'known' (ma'lum), otherwise it is 'unknown'. This sets the stage for defining knowledge in the next section.

Prior Knowledge and the Matter-Form Analogy
Author argues that all knowledge requires prior suitable knowns arranged in a specific composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why prior suitable knowledge and specific composition are both required for any valid definition or proof, using the bed analogy.

Core Idea
No knowledge without prior suitable meanings arranged in a specific form – matter and form are inseparable.

The author begins by asserting that the unknown cannot be known directly; it must be reached through prior knowns that are suitable and specially arranged. He uses the analogy of a bed: meanings are like wood (material), and their arrangement is like the bed's form (shape). Just as a bed requires both proper material and proper form to be a bed, the path to knowledge requires both correct meanings and correct composition. This lesson unpacks this foundational idea and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The unknown cannot be known without prior knowns. Find the three possibilities (prior, posterior, simultaneous) and why only prior is acceptable. The author argues that posterior knowledge would require the path to be known while the target is unknown, and simultaneous knowledge would make definition arbitrary.
2
Understand suitability
The prior known must be connected to the sought. The author gives examples: for conceptualizing 'human', we need 'animal' and 'rational' (not 'intellect' and 'matter'); for assenting that the world is originated, we need 'the world is composed' and 'every composed thing is originated'.
Suitability is about relevance and connection.
3
Recognize specific composition
Not any arrangement works; a specific composition is required. The author says understanding 'animal' and 'rational' separately does not lead to knowing 'human' – they must be composed in a specific way. Similarly, random ordering of premises does not yield valid assent.
This sets up the need for a law governing composition.
4
Apply the bed analogy
The bed analogy: meanings are like wood (material), composition is like the bed's form. A bed requires both sound material and sound form. Likewise, the path to knowledge (definition or proof) is sound only if both meanings and composition are sound.
Corruption of either material or form corrupts the whole.

The unknown, insofar as it is unknown, is impossible [to know directly], and there must be a prior [known], otherwise it is either posterior or simultaneous. It cannot be posterior in knowledge, otherwise what leads to it would be known while it is unknown; and it cannot be simultaneous with it, since defining one of two things by the other is not more appropriate than the reverse, and definition must be by that by which the thing is known, not by that which is known along with it. And with its priority, it must be suitable – i.e., there is a connection between it and the sought – so that one can reach the sought through it. As for conceptualization, it is like 'animal' and 'rational' for example relative to what is unknown of the meaning of 'human', not relative to what is unknown of the meaning of 'intellect' and 'matter'. As for assent, it is for knowing that the world is composed and that every composed thing is originated relative to what is unknown of the meaning of the world being originated. If not for that, every prior known would be suitable to define whatever we suppose unknown, but it is not so. Nor are these items used in definition without composition and arrangement; otherwise understanding the meaning of 'animal' and 'rational' each individually would lead to knowledge of 'human', and similarly knowledge of 'world', 'composed', and 'originated' without arrangement would lead to knowledge that the world is originated, which is impossible. Classification of Expressions into Single and Compound
Author defines mufrad and murakkab, subdivides murakkab into complete and incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic expression as mufrad or murakkab tamm/naqis based on the indication of its parts.

Core Idea
An expression is single if no part indicates; compound if a part indicates a part of the meaning.

Logic begins with expressions (alfaz). Before building propositions or syllogisms, we must understand how words carry meaning. This lesson unpacks the foundational distinction between single and compound expressions, teaching you to analyze any Arabic phrase and determine its logical status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression to classify
Take an expression like 'Zayd', 'Zayd qa'im', or 'fi al-bayt'. Ask: Does the expression have parts that individually indicate something?
Parts refer to meaningful components, not just letters.
2
Apply the definition of mufrad
If no part of the expression indicates any part of the meaning (or anything else), it is mufrad. For example, the name 'Zayd' is mufrad because its letters do not individually indicate anything about Zayd.
The same applies to compound meanings like 'hayawan' – still mufrad because its letters are not independently meaningful.
3
Apply the definition of murakkab
If the expression has a part that indicates a part of the intended meaning, it is murakkab. For example, 'Zayd qa'im' has 'Zayd' indicating the subject and 'qa'im' indicating the predicate.
The entire meaning is built from parts.
4
Subdivide murakkab into tamm and naqis
If every part is independently indicative (like 'Zayd qa'im'), it is murakkab tamm. If a part requires something else to complete its indication (like 'fi' in 'Zayd fi al-bayt'), it is murakkab naqis.
Murakkab tamm is further divided into khabari (declarative) and non-khabari.
5
Test edge cases like 'Abdullah'
Consider 'Abdullah': if used as a proper name without intending the meanings of 'abd' and 'Allah', it is mufrad. If the parts are meant to indicate, it becomes murakkab.
This shows the role of convention (wad') in classification.

Commentary
This passage from Daqaiq Haqaiq classifies lafz (expression) into two primary categories: mufrad (single) and murakkab (compound). The author begins by defining mufrad: it is an expression that, by convention (wad'), indicates a meaning, and no part of it indicates anything at all—neither a part of that meaning nor something external. This condition holds whether the indicated meaning is simple (like 'nafs' or 'aql') or composite (like 'Zayd' or 'hayawan'). To illustrate, the letters 'ya' and 'dal' in 'Zayd' do not indicate any part of Zayd's meaning; similarly, 'ha' and 'ya' in 'hayawan' do not indicate anything. If those letters were to indicate something, they would not be considered parts of the word in that context, because the part-whole relationship depends on the intention of the one who coins the word (al-wadi'). The author refutes a potential objection: if someone thinks that a part of a word indicates something external when considered separately, then according to that opinion the word would not be single; however, the author argues that the convention determines whether a part indicates or not. Examples like 'Abdullah' are given: if it is used as a proper name and its parts (Abd, Allah) are not intended to indicate their individual meanings in the compound state, it is considered mufrad. If they are intended to indicate, it would be murakkab. The passage then defines murakkab: it is an expression that has a part which indicates a part of the meaning intended by the whole sentence. It is subdivided based on whether each part is independently indicative. If every part is independently indicative, it is a complete compound (murakkab tamm). This type is further divided into declarative (khabari) and non-declarative. For a declarative compound (like 'Zayd is standing'), it can only be composed of at least two parts: either two nouns (Zayd and qa'im), or a noun and a verb (Zayd qama), but cannot be composed of only verbs, only particles, or only a noun/verb with a particle alone (without a second noun/verb). If the compound is not declarative, it includes other types mentioned later. The second subtype of murakkab is the incomplete compound (murakkab naqis), where parts are not independently indicative because it contains a particle that requires an addition to complete its meaning, such as 'Zayd in the house' or 'on Zayd a dirham'. The particles 'fi' and 'ala' need their complements to convey a full meaning.

Identifying Singular Verbs (Kalima) in Logic
Author defines 'kalima' (verb) and explains its singularity conditions in logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a given Arabic verb form qualifies as a singular kalima in logical terminology by applying the criterion of independent signification of its parts.

Core Idea
A verb is singular only if no part independently signifies a meaning beyond its root.

In logic, the term 'kalima' corresponds roughly to the grammatical verb, but not every verb form counts as a singular 'kalima'. The key is whether any part of the word independently indicates a subject, tense, or other component of the proposition. This distinction is essential for constructing valid propositions in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verb form
Look at the word and determine its root letters and any prefixes or suffixes.
Focus on verbs used in Arabic.
2
Check for independent signification of parts
Determine if any prefix, suffix, or particle in the word has an independent meaning that contributes to the overall meaning, such as indicating the subject (e.g., 'tā' for second person) or tense (e.g., 'sīn' for future).
Independence means the part conveys a meaning even when considered separately from the verb, like 'sawfa' meaning future.
3
Evaluate based on the definition
If no part independently signifies, then the verb is a singular kalima. If any part does, it is compound.
The author also considers that even non-independent parts can produce composition if they have different significations.
4
Distinguish logical vs. grammatical perspective
Remember that the logician's analysis transcends any particular language; the same criteria apply to any language. The grammatical classification (e.g., 'fi'l') is not identical to the logical 'kalima'.

Why Incomplete Nouns, Pronouns, and Existential Verbs Are Not Particles
Author refutes objections that incomplete nouns, pronouns, and existential verbs might be particles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criteria of independence and propositional composition to distinguish incomplete nouns, pronouns, and existential verbs from particles.

Core Idea
Incomplete nouns, pronouns, and existential verbs can form complete propositions when specified, unlike particles.

This passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq addresses a common confusion: if some nouns and verbs seem to lack independence like particles, what makes them different? The author systematically refutes three objections, showing that each type retains its fundamental nature as a noun or verb. By following the arguments, you will learn the precise boundary between particles and other parts of speech.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector claims that incomplete nouns (e.g., 'alladhi'), pronouns (e.g., 'huwa'), and existential verbs (e.g., 'kana') lack independence, similar to particles. Identify what independence means: they cannot stand alone as subject or predicate in a simple two-part proposition.
2
Analyze the response for incomplete nouns
The author shows that 'alladhi' becomes independent after specification by a relative clause (sila) and referent (a'id). For example, 'alladhi huwa abyadu qa'im' is a complete proposition with 'alladhi' as subject. So it is not a particle.
3
Analyze the response for pronouns
The author argues that pronouns like 'huwa' in apposition (e.g., 'Zayd huwa qa'im') are semantically identical to their antecedent, unlike tools. They can also function as subjects in certain contexts. Therefore, they are nouns, not particles.
4
Analyze the response for existential verbs
When existential verbs like 'wajada' or 'kana' are used to predicate existence (e.g., 'Zayd wajada' meaning 'Zayd exists'), they form complete predicates. When used as copulas, they connect subject and predicate. In both cases, they are verbs, not particles.
5
Synthesize the distinction
The key criterion: nouns (including the three types) can form a declarative proposition from two parts (e.g., 'Zayd qa'im'), while particles cannot. Verbs are distinguished by their necessity of attribution. This clarifies the boundaries of the particle category.

But if used to predicate existence, like 'Zayd wajada' (Zayd exists), it forms a complete proposition with 'Zayd' as subject and 'wajada' as predicate. This is not possible for particles. The passage concludes by summarizing the distinction among the three parts of speech: nouns can form a declarative proposition from two parts (e.g., 'Zayd qa'im'); verbs and particles cannot. Verbs are distinguished by their necessity of attribution (i.e., they always require a subject). This clarifies the boundaries of the particle category.

This lesson examines the first type of faulty definition according to the author. A definition fails if the supposed genus is not truly predicable on all its species when asked 'what is it?'. 1
Identify the definition being tested

The genus must be a category of being, not an accidental attribute.

The author states: the genus must be predicated on its species in answer to 'what is it?' (jawab ma). This applied to all species under that genus. If a term fails this test, it cannot serve as the genus in a definition.

What is a Conditional Proposition?
Author defines conditional proposition and divides it into conjunctive and disjunctive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a conditional proposition (shartiyya) from a categorical proposition (hamliyya) and identify whether it is conjunctive or disjunctive.

Core Idea
The conditional relates two propositions, while the categorical predicates one term of another.

This passage lays the foundation for understanding conditional propositions in Islamic logic. The author contrasts them with categoricals and divides them into two types: conjunctive (implication) and disjunctive (opposition). Mastering this distinction is crucial for analyzing theological and legal arguments, where conditionals often carry the logical force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common genus
Both categorical and conditional propositions are 'decisive informative statements' (قول جازم خبر). Recognize that they share this core nature.
Do not confuse 'decisive' with 'true' – here it means a statement that is either true or false, not a question or command.
2
Pinpoint the differentia
Unlike a categorical, a conditional does not predicate one part directly of another (no 'is' or 'is not' between its two parts). Instead, it asserts a relation — implication or opposition — between two complete propositions.
For example, 'If it is wine, then it is forbidden' is a relation between two propositions, not a predication.
3
Recognize the two types of relation
If the relation is implication (luzūm), the conditional is conjunctive (muttaṣila). If the relation is opposition ('inād), the conditional is disjunctive (munfaṣila).
Implication means that if the first holds, the second necessarily holds; opposition means they cannot both be true or both false.
4
Understand the naming convention
Both types are called 'conditional', but the disjunctive is called so only metaphorically because it lacks the conditional particle (e.g., 'in' in Arabic). The author notes that this naming is derived from the nobler quality.
The conjunctive is the primary type; the disjunctive is secondary and will be examined later.

Defining Qiyas Correctly: Avoiding Circularity
Definition of qiyas and critique of circularity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qiyas using the author's formulation and explain why defining it as 'composed of premises' is circular.

Core Idea
A proper definition must not use the thing itself; 'premises' are parts of qiyas, so that definition is circular.

The author provides a precise definition of qiyas and then critiques a common but flawed definition. This lesson focuses on parsing that definition and understanding the logical error of circularity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's definition of qiyas
Find the sentence: 'al-qiyas ibaratun amma ullifa min ashya' qabilatin an takuna sadiqatan aw kadhibatan, idha wudi'at lazima 'anha li-dhatiha al-tasdiq bi-amrin akhar daruratan'. Note the three key qualifiers.
2
Understand each qualifier
First, 'min ashya' qabilatin li al-sidq wal-kidhb' excludes single propositions that entail their own converse. Second, 'idha wudi'at' means when posited/admitted, regardless of truth. Third, 'lazima 'anha li-dhatiha' ensures the entailment is intrinsic and necessary.
3
Analyze the critique of circularity
The author rejects defining qiyas as 'mu'allaf min muqaddimat' (composed of premises) because 'muqaddimat' are defined as parts of qiyas. Thus it becomes 'composed of parts of itself', which is circular and provides no new information.

The Parts of a Categorical Syllogism: Terms and Premises
Definition of hudud and muqaddimat as parts of categorical syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the terms (hudud) and premises (muqaddimat) in any categorical syllogism and distinguish them from propositions and simple terms.

Core Idea
The syllogism's skeleton: premises are its ribs, terms are the joints that connect them.

Before you can analyse or construct a syllogism, you must know its building blocks. This passage from 'Daqaiq al-Haqaiq' gives you the precise definitions: a syllogism is made of propositions, and propositions are made of simple terms. But not every simple term is a 'hadd' (term), and not every proposition is a 'muqaddima' (premise). Understanding these distinctions is your first step toward mastering Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two levels of parts
The passage tells you that propositions (qadaya) are the parts of a syllogism, just as simple terms (mufradat) are the parts of propositions. So there are two levels: mufradat are parts of propositions, and propositions are parts of syllogisms.
This is a hierarchical structure: syllogism → propositions → simple terms.
2
Identify when a simple term becomes a 'hadd'
A simple term is called a 'hadd' only when it is considered as a part of a proposition and serves as an extreme (like subject or predicate). The author likens it to the boundaries (hudud) used by mathematicians to mark the ends of lines.
Not every mufrad is a hadd; only those functioning as extremes in a proposition.
3
Recognize that a 'hadd' can be simple or composite
The passage explicitly says that a hadd may be composite in power of a simple term (e.g., 'liquid that intoxicates') or simple with no composition (e.g., 'khamr'). Both are treated as single logical units for the purpose of forming propositions.
This is important because in actual arguments, terms are often multi-word expressions.
4
Identify when a proposition becomes a 'muqaddima'
A proposition (qadiyya) is called a 'muqaddima' precisely when it functions as a part of a syllogism. Not every qadiyya is a muqaddima; only those that serve as premises in a syllogistic argument are called muqaddimat.
The passage states: 'al-qadaya allati hiya ajza' al-qiyas min haythu hiya ajza'uhu musammaat muqaddimat'.
5
Practice on a real syllogism
Take the syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is haram; every nabidh is an intoxicant; therefore every nabidh is haram.' Identify the hudud: 'intoxicant', 'haram', 'nabidh' (three terms). Identify the muqaddimat: the first two propositions are premises; the third is the conclusion, not a premise.
The conclusion is also a qadiyya but not a muqaddima in this syllogism.

Types of Knowledge Acquisition: Sense and Teaching
Author classifies how knowledge (tasawwur/tasdiq) is acquired: by sense or teaching, with subtypes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sense and teaching as modes of knowledge acquisition and identify the various subtypes of teaching described by the author.

Core Idea
Knowledge is acquired either by sense or by teaching—and teaching can happen even within one's own mind.

In this passage, the author begins his discussion of the materials of syllogism by categorizing how we come to know anything new. He distinguishes two fundamental paths: direct sense perception and teaching, which includes inner mental processes as well as interpersonal instruction. Understanding this classification is crucial because it determines the source and reliability of premises used in syllogistic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary categories of acquisition
The author states that every unknown sought matter (matlub) is acquired either by sense (hiss) or by teaching (ta'lim). Note that sense is not elaborated; teaching is the focus.
2
Recognize the broad definition of teaching
Teaching is defined as knowledge passing from a knower to a non-knower, and notably, it can occur between two persons or within a single person (intra-personal teaching through syllogism).
3
Name the subtypes of teaching
The author lists mental (dhihni), reflective (fikri), intuitive (hadsī), understanding (fahmī), artisanal, dictation, imitation, disciplinary. Focus on the first four: how do they differ?
4
Extract the tripartite structure of teaching
Teaching necessarily involves three elements: from which (prior knowledge), for which (the sought matter), and by which (the forms/compositions that connect them). This structure is foundational for understanding syllogistic materials.

Commentary
This passage begins the classification of the materials of syllogism by describing how any unknown matter (matlub) is acquired. The author states that acquisition is either through sense (hiss) or through teaching/learning (ta'lim/ta'allum). Teaching is defined as the transmission of knowledge from a knower to a non-knower. Interestingly, the author notes that teaching can occur within a single person: one who uses a middle term in a syllogism is simultaneously teacher (by intuition) and learner (by acquisition). Then the author enumerates various modes of teaching/learning: mental (dhihni), where the sought knowledge comes from rational statements; reflective (fikri), where the soul actively moves to find what leads to the goal; intuitive (hadsī), where the conclusion follows without active search; understanding (fahmī), where the information is obtained passively from a teacher; and others like artisanal, dictation, imitation, and disciplinary. Finally, the author introduces a tripartite structure: teaching/learning involves three elements: 'from which' (ma minhu) — the previously known appropriate information; 'for which' (ma lahu) — the sought matters; and 'by which' (ma bihi) — the forms and compositions that connect them. This passage sets the foundation for understanding how premises are acquired in syllogistic reasoning.

Key Terms
مطلوب (matlub): The sought matter, what one aims to know, either a concept (tasawwur) or an assent (tasdiq). [Note: In later logic, matlub is often specifically the conclusion of a syllogism; here it is broader.]
تعليم (ta'lim): Teaching — transmission of knowledge from a knower to a non-knower, including within one person via intuition and learning. [Note: No significant shift; standard usage.]
ذهني (dhihni): Mental mode of teaching/learning where knowledge is acquired through rational statements that yield a new sought matter. [Note: In other contexts, dhihni may refer to mental existence; here it is a type of learning.]
