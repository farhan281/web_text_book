The Life of Seif al-Din al-Amidi: A Scholar Caught Between Reason and Tradition
Biographical account of the author's life and controversies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key factors in al-Amidi's biography that illustrate the tension between rational sciences (mantiq, kalam) and traditional religious sciences in the medieval Islamic world.

Core Idea
Al-Amidi's life shows that logic was often controversial, not because it was foreign, but because it challenged entrenched scholarly authority.

Seif al-Din al-Amidi was not only a logician but also a jurist and theologian. His biography reveals how his expertise in rational sciences like logic and kalam repeatedly brought him into conflict with traditionalist jurists and rulers. This lesson will walk you through the key events of his life to understand the socio-intellectual context in which his logical works, such as Daqaiq al-Haqaiq, were produced.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Amidi's scholarly identity
The text describes him as usuli, mutakallim, faqih, and logician. Note the combination of traditional (usul al-fiqh, fiqh) and rational (kalam, mantiq) sciences.
This dual identity was a source of controversy.
2
Trace his geographical movements
He moved from Amid to Baghdad, then to Egypt, then to Hama and Damascus. Each move was partly due to accusations from jurists or rulers.
Follow the sequence: Baghdad (accusations) → Egypt (more accusations) → Hama (sponsored by Ayyubids) → Damascus (dismissed).
3
Analyze the accusations he faced
In Baghdad, jurists accused him due to his inclination toward rational sciences. In Egypt, he was accused of corruption of belief for extremism (likely in rational theology).
The text includes a poem about envy, suggesting the accusations were politically motivated.
4
Understand the political context under al-Ashraf
When al-Ashraf took Damascus, he banned the study of ancient sciences (philosophy, logic) in schools, forcing al-Amidi into house arrest until his death.
This illustrates the direct political repression of rational sciences.

سيف الدين الآمدي 551 - 631هـ/ 1156 - 1233م
–
علي بن أبي علي بن محمد بن سالم التغلبي، الأصولي المتكلم الفقيه، سيف الدين أبو الحسن الآمدي الحنبلي ثم الشافعي ولد بآمد (في ديار بكر سنة إحدى وخمسين وخمسمائة، وقرأ بها القرآن وتعلّم، وكان حنبلي المذهب فصار شافعياً أصولياً منطقياً جدلياً خلافياً وكان حسن الأخلاق، سليم الصدر كثير البكاء، رقيق القلب.
ارتحل إلى بغداد فتفقه على أبي الفتح بن المنّي الحنبلي، وسمع من ابن الفلسفة شاتيل، وصحب أبا القاسم بن فضلان الشافعي، وأخذ عنه الخلاف. ثم تعرض لاتهام الفقهاء له بسبب ميله إلى العلوم العقلية، فانتقل سنة 592هـ إلى مصر وبقي فيها مدرساً بالجامع الظافري بالقاهرة، وصنف فيها كتباً، وقد تعرض فيها أيضاً إلى محنة أخرى، حيث نُسب إليه فساد العقيدة لغلوه في وكتبوا فيه محضراً يتضمن ذلك، ووضعوا فيه خطوطهم بما يستباح به الدم فطلبوا من بعضهم أن يوافقهم فكتب: حسدوا الفتى إذ لم ينالوا سعيه فالقوم أعداء له وخصوم كضرائر الحسناء قلـ الحسناء قلن لوجههـا حــــداً وبغضاً إنّه لدميم ثم انتقل إلى حماة، وكانت ملوك بني أيوب كالمعظم والكامل يكرمونه وإن كانوا لا يحبونه كثيراً، وقد فوض إليه المعظم تدريس العزيزية، وانتقل إلى الشام، فاشتغل بالفلسفة والمنطق والكلام ومهر في هذه العلوم، فلما ولي الأشرف دمشق عزله عنها ونادى بالمدارس ألا يشتغل أحد بغير التفسير والحديث والفقه ومن اشتغل بعلوم الأوائل نفيته، فأقام الشيخ سيف الدين بمنزله إلى أن توفي بدمشق في صفر، ودفن بتربته في سفح قاسيون وله ثمانون عاماً.
A glimpse of the author's life: Seif al-Din al-Amidi 551-631 AH / 1156-1233 CE – Ali ibn Abi Ali ibn Muhammad ibn Salim al-Taghlabi, the usuli, theologian, jurist, Seif al-Din Abu al-Hasan al-Amidi, Hanbali then Shafi'i. He was born in Amid (in Diyar Bakr) in the year 551, and there he read the Qur'an and learned. He was of the Hanbali school then became Shafi'i, an usuli, logician, dialectician, disputant. He was of good character, pure-hearted, prone to weeping, tender-hearted. He traveled to Baghdad and studied jurisprudence with Abu al-Fath ibn al-Munna al-Hanbali, and heard from Ibn [al-Falsafa?] Shatil, and accompanied Abu al-Qasim ibn Fadlan al-Shafi'i, from whom he took disputation. Then he was subjected to accusations by jurists due to his inclination toward the rational sciences, so he moved in 592 AH to Egypt and remained there as a teacher at the al-Zafiri Mosque in Cairo, and authored books there. He also faced another ordeal there, where he was accused of corruption of belief due to his extremism in [something], and they wrote a memorandum to that effect, and put their signatures to it indicating that his blood was permissible. They asked one of them to agree with them, and he wrote: 'They envied the youth when they could not attain his effort, so the people are his enemies and adversaries, like the co-wives of a beautiful woman – they said to her face: enmity and hatred, for he is ugly.' Then he moved to Hama, and the Ayyubid kings like al-Mu'azzam and al-Kamil honored him, though they did not like him much. Al-Mu'azzam entrusted him with teaching at al-Aziziyya. He moved to Syria and occupied himself with philosophy, logic, and kalam, and excelled in these sciences. When al-Ashraf took charge of Damascus, he dismissed him and proclaimed in the schools that no one should occupy himself with anything other than tafsir, hadith, and fiqh, and whoever occupied himself with the sciences of the ancients would be exiled. So Shaykh Seif al-Din stayed in his home until he died in Damascus in Safar, and was buried in his tomb at the foot of Mount Qasiyun at the age of eighty.

Commentary
This passage is a biography of the author, Seif al-Din al-Amidi, a prominent scholar of the 6th-7th hijri centuries. The biography begins with his full name and major scholarly roles: usuli (specialist in usul al-fiqh), mutakallim (theologian), faqih (jurist). It notes his change of madhhab from Hanbali to Shafi'i, and his expertise in logic (mantiq), dialectic (jadal), and disputation (khilaf). The passage then traces his intellectual journey: he studied in Baghdad with Hanbali and Shafi'i teachers, but faced opposition from jurists who were suspicious of his interest in rational sciences. This forced him to move to Egypt, where he taught and wrote. Even there, he was accused of corrupt beliefs and a legal document sought to permit his bloodshed. The author's poetic response is quoted. He then moved to Hama, sponsored by Ayyubid rulers, and later to Syria where he delved deeper into philosophy, logic, and kalam. However, under the rule of al-Ashraf, a policy was enforced restricting study to religious sciences (tafsir, hadith, fiqh) and banning 'sciences of the ancients' (philosophy and logic). Al-Amidi was dismissed and confined to his home until his death. The biography ends with his age and burial place. This account is important for understanding the context of al-Amidi's logical works, which were written amid theological and political tensions.

Who Was Sayf al-Din al-Amidi?
Biographical account of the author's life, scholarly praise, and list of works.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to summarize the life, scholarly stature, and major works of Sayf al-Din al-Amidi based on his biography.

Core Idea
Al-Amidi's mastery in debate and usul al-fiqh earned him unique praise from contemporaries.

Before diving into the logical and theological content of Daqaiq al-Haqaiq, it is essential to know its author. This lesson introduces you to Sayf al-Din al-Amidi, a towering Shafi'i scholar of the 7th/13th century. The passage you will study contains firsthand accounts of his teaching ability, his role in shaping the rules of research, and the breadth of his writings. Understanding al-Amidi's background will help you appreciate the authority behind the arguments in this book.

Guided Path
1
Locate the chronological context
Note his birth and death dates (551-631 AH / 1156-1233 CE). This places him in the late Abbasid period, after al-Ghazali and before the Mongol sack of Baghdad.
2
Extract the praise from Ibn Abd al-Salam
Identify the three key statements: (1) his teaching was like conversation, (2) he taught the rules of research, (3) he alone could debate a heretic. Reflect on what these reveal about his reputation.
Ibn Abd al-Salam was a renowned Shafi'i scholar himself, so his praise carries weight.
3
Analyze the dream story
Al-Amidi dreamed of al-Ghazali, kissed him, and then memorized al-Mustasfa quickly. This story links him to al-Ghazali's legacy and suggests a spiritual transmission of knowledge.
Al-Mustasfa is a major work on usul al-fiqh by al-Ghazali.
4
List the major works
Scan the list of about twenty books. Focus on those mentioned: al-Ihkam, Muntaha al-Sul, Abkar al-Afkar, Ghayat al-Maram, and especially Daqaiq al-Haqaiq (the present book). Note their fields (usul, kalam, logic).
Daqaiq al-Haqaiq is described as explaining the meanings of philosophers and theologians.
5
Identify the biographical sources
The passage ends with references: al-Bidaya wa al-Nihaya, Mawsua Tabaqat al-Fuqaha, Tabaqat al-Shafiyya al-Kubra. These are standard works for further research.
These sources confirm the reliability of the information.

نبذة عن حياة المؤلف سيف الدين الآمدي 551 - 631هـ / 1156 - 1233
ويحكى أن الشيخ عز الدين بن عبد السلام قال: ما سمعت أحداً يُلقي الدرس أحسن منه، كأَنَّه يُخاطب، وإذا غيّر لفظاً من الوسيط كان لفظه أمس بالمعنى من لفظ صاحبه، وأنه قال ما علمنا قواعد البحث إلا من سيف الدين الآمدي، وأنه قال: لو ورد على الإسلام متزندق يشكك ما تعين لمناظرته غير الآمدي؛ لاجتماع أهلية ذلك فيه
ويحكى أن الآمدي رأى في منامه حجة الإسلام الغزالي في تابوت وكشف عن وجهه وقبله، فلما انتبه أراد أن يحفظ شيئاً من كلامه فحفظ "المستصفى" في أيام يسيرة، وكان يعقد مجلساً للمناظرة. ألف الآمدي نحو عشرين كتاباً، منها: الإحكام في أصول الأحكام (مطبوع في أربعة أجزاء، واختصره في كتاب سماه منتهى السول في علم الأصول (مطبوع)، أبكار الأفكار في علم الكلام، غاية المرام في علم الكلام (مطبوع، لب الألباب، دقائق الحقائق وهو هذا الكتاب المبين في شرح معاني الحكماء والمتكلمين، وله طريقة في الخلاف، ومختصر في الخلاف أيضاً، والمنتهى، ومنائح القرائح، وشرح جدل الشريف، وتعليقة حسنة.
ولمن أراد الاطلاع أكثر على حياة الشيخ الآمدي ينظر في ذلك، البداية والنهاية، لابن كثير، ج 13، ص 164. موسوعة طبقات الفقهاء اللجنة العلمية في مؤسسة الإمام الصادق (عليه السلام)، ج 7، 158. طبقات الشافعية الكبرى، تاج الدين السبكي، ج8، ص306.
An excerpt about the life of the author Sayf al-Din al-Amidi (551-631 AH / 1156-1233 CE). It is narrated that Shaykh Izz al-Din ibn Abd al-Salam said: I have not heard anyone deliver a lesson better than him, as if he was conversing, and if he changed a phrase from al-Wasit, his wording was more fitting to the meaning than the wording of its author. And he said: We did not know the rules of research except from Sayf al-Din al-Amidi. And he said: If a heretic came to Islam causing doubt, no one would be appropriate to debate him other than al-Amidi, because of the competence for that gathered in him. It is also narrated that al-Amidi saw in his dream Hujjat al-Islam al-Ghazali in a coffin, uncovered his face and kissed him. When he woke up, he wanted to memorize something of his speech, so he memorized al-Mustasfa in a few days. He used to hold a session for debate. Al-Amidi authored about twenty books, among them: al-Ihkam fi Usul al-Ahkam (printed in four volumes, and he abridged it in a book named Muntaha al-Sul fi Ilm al-Usul, printed), Abkar al-Afkar fi Ilm al-Kalam, Ghayat al-Maram fi Ilm al-Kalam (printed), Labb al-Albab, Daqaiq al-Haqaiq which is this book explaining the meanings of the philosophers and theologians, and he has a method in disagreement, and a summary on disagreement as well, and al-Muntaha, and Manaih al-Qaraih, and Sharh Jadal al-Sharif, and a good commentary. For whoever wants to learn more about the life of Shaykh al-Amidi, refer to: al-Bidaya wa al-Nihaya by Ibn Kathir, vol. 13, p. 164; Mawsua Tabaqat al-Fuqaha, the scientific committee of the Imam al-Sadiq Foundation, vol. 7, p. 158; Tabaqat al-Shafiyya al-Kubra by Taj al-Din al-Subki, vol. 8, p. 306.
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is a biographical sketch of the author, Sayf al-Din al-Amidi, taken from the front matter of the book. It begins with his birth and death dates in both Hijri and Gregorian calendars. Then it relates three anecdotes: first, that the renowned scholar Izz al-Din ibn Abd al-Salam praised al-Amidi's teaching style, noting that he spoke as if in conversation and that his modifications to the text of al-Wasit improved its meaning. Second, ibn Abd al-Salam credited al-Amidi with teaching him the rules of research, implying al-Amidi's foundational role in developing dialectical method. Third, ibn Abd al-Salam asserted that if a heretic (mutazandiq) were to challenge Islam, only al-Amidi had the qualifications to respond. The passage then recounts a dream of al-Amidi in which he saw al-Ghazali in a coffin, kissed him, and subsequently memorized al-Ghazali's work al-Mustasfa in a few days. This story underscores al-Amidi's connection to the legacy of al-Ghazali. The biography also lists al-Amidi's major works, including his famous usul al-fiqh text al-Ihkam and its abridgment Muntaha al-Sul, his kalam works Abkar al-Afkar and Ghayat al-Maram, and the present book Daqaiq al-Haqaiq, which he describes as an explanation of the meanings of philosophers and theologians. The passage ends with references for further reading, pointing to standard biographical sources like Ibn Kathir's al-Bidaya wa al-Nihaya and al-Subki's Tabaqat al-Shafiyya.
