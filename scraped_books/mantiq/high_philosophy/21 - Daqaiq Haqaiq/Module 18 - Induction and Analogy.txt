Complete and Incomplete Induction
Author defines induction and distinguishes complete from incomplete with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete induction from incomplete induction and determine which type yields certainty in demonstrative proof.

Core Idea
Complete induction covers all particulars; incomplete induction only some — only complete yields certainty.

Induction is a powerful tool for establishing universal truths from particulars. But not all induction is equal. In this passage, the author clarifies the crucial distinction between complete induction, which exhausts all cases, and incomplete induction, which covers only most. Understanding this distinction is essential for knowing when a claim is certain and when it remains probable — a key skill in Islamic theological and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Recognise that the passage defines 'induction' (istiqrā') as a relation between two universals based on particulars.
2
Extract the defining condition for completeness
Notice the author's condition: if the induction covers all particulars under the universal, it is complete (tāmm). See the example of 'moving thing' and 'body' covering animal, plant, mineral.
3
Contrast with incomplete induction
The author gives the example of 'animal moves lower jaw when chewing' based on most animals, but the crocodile is an exception. This shows incomplete (nāqiṣ) induction.
4
Understand the epistemological payoff
Complete induction yields certainty because the mind abstracts to the common nature; incomplete induction is only probable.
5
Recognise the contexts of usage
The author notes that incomplete induction is a fallacy in demonstrative proof (burhān) but not in definition (ḥadd); it can be used in dialectical contexts.

Induction is an expression that necessitates the attribution of a universal to another universal by affirmation or negation in order to verify its relation in that manner to what is under the attributed universal of the subjects. But if it covers all the particulars and subjects of the universal thing judged such that none is omitted, then the induction is complete and real, like the judgment that the moving thing is a body, by means of the affirmation of body for each part of what pertains to the moving thing from animal, inanimate, and plant — [complete]. Otherwise it is incomplete, like the judgment that the animal moves its lower jaw when chewing, based on induction of most animals. So what is like the first is true, sound, and beneficial in certainties; because if the mind abstracts from each of the specific properties of the particulars, it knows that it must be for the common nature among all or for its necessary concomitant, and it is precisely one in reality in the universal and the particular. For that reason, the judgment made on the particulars is applied to their universal. And what is like the second is not certain; for perhaps the judgment in them settled on the opposite of what was induced, as in the crocodile. So although it is a fallacy in demonstration, it is not a fallacy in definition; therefore its use in it is not for real necessitation, but for necessitation [in dialectical contexts].

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqrā') and distinguishes its two types. Induction is a process of establishing a universal relation (e.g., all A are B) by verifying that relation holds for the particulars under A. If the survey covers all particulars, it is complete induction (istiqrā' tāmm) and yields certainty. The author gives the example of inferring that every moving thing is a body because each subclass (animal, plant, mineral) is a body. If the survey covers only most particulars, it is incomplete induction (istiqrā' nāqiṣ) and yields only probability, as illustrated by the claim that all animals move their lower jaw when chewing—which is false for crocodiles. Incomplete induction can be a fallacy in demonstrative proof (burhān) but is acceptable in definition (ḥadd) or dialectical contexts. The commentary highlights the epistemological distinction: complete induction gives certainty because the mind abstracts to the common nature; incomplete induction is uncertain due to possible exceptions.

The Definition of Induction (Istiqra')
Author defines induction as reasoning from particulars to a universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two universals and the role of particulars in the definition of induction.

Core Idea
Induction bridges two universals by examining the particulars under one of them.

Induction (istiqra') is a key tool for arriving at universal judgments from particular observations. In this lesson, we unpack the precise definition given by the author, focusing on its logical structure. Understanding this definition will help you distinguish induction from other reasoning forms like syllogism and analogy.

As for induction, it is an expression for necessitating the attribution of a universal to another universal by affirmation or negation in order to verify that attribution in the manner to the subjects under the attributed universal. But if it has covered all the particulars and subjects of the universal that is judged, such that nothing of them is missing, then the induction is perfect and true, like the judgment that the moving thing is a body, by virtue of the body's being established for each part of what pertains to the moving thing—animal, inanimate, and plant—as a whole. Otherwise it is imperfect, like the judgment that every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing, based on induction of most animals. So the first type is true, certain, and useful for certain knowledge; because when the mind strips it of being for each of the properties of the particulars, it knows that it must be for the common nature of all or its necessary attendant, and it is the same in reality in the universal and the particular, and for that reason the judgment made on the particulars is passed on their universal. As for the second type, it is not certain; it may be that the judgment is not established contrary to what was induced, as in the crocodile. So it is a fallacy in demonstration but not a fallacy in definition; therefore its use in it is not for real obligation but for popular obligation. So induction is more general in that it is perfect or imperfect. And although it resembles categorical syllogism in that the subjects in it are like the minor term, the universal judged upon by the attributed universal is like the middle term, and the universal judged by is like the major term, yet it differs in that what ought to be the minor term in syllogism becomes the middle in induction, and what ought to be the middle becomes the minor. And induction is prior to syllogism with respect to sense; for often the first premises are acquired by sensory induction, while syllogism is prior by nature to induction in the intellect. In summary, induction is only induction for demonstrating the attribution of a universal to a universal by means of attribution to the subjects under the attributed universal, as defined.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (al-istiqra'), explains its two types—perfect and imperfect—and compares it to categorical syllogism. The author begins by giving a technical definition: induction is a reasoning that establishes a relation between two universals (e.g., 'every moving thing is a body') by examining the particulars under the subject universal. If all particulars are examined, it is perfect induction and yields certainty; if only most, it is imperfect and yields only probability. The example of perfect induction: judging that every moving thing is a body because each particular (animal, plant, mineral) is a body. The example of imperfect: judging that every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing, based on most animals, but the crocodile is a counterexample. The author then explains the epistemological value: perfect induction is useful for certain knowledge because the common nature is identical in the universal and particulars. Imperfect induction is not certain and can be fallacious in demonstration, but it is not fallacious in definition; it can be used for dialectical (popular) obligation. Next, the author compares induction to categorical syllogism: they share a structure (subjects as minor term, the universal under which they fall as middle, the predicate as major), but they differ in the roles of terms. In induction, what should be the minor term becomes the middle, and vice versa. Induction is prior to syllogism in terms of sense perception because many first premises are gained by sensory induction, but syllogism is prior by nature in the intellect. The passage concludes by reiterating the definition.

Perfect vs Imperfect Induction
Author classifies induction into perfect and imperfect with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify an inductive argument as perfect or imperfect and assess its certainty.

Core Idea

Not all inductions are equal. The author distinguishes between perfect induction that leaves no exception and imperfect induction that may have counterexamples. This distinction is crucial for knowing when we can claim certainty and when we must be cautious. In this lesson, we analyze the examples and the epistemological implications.

Induction vs Categorical Syllogism
Author compares induction to categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the term roles in induction and syllogism and explain their epistemic priority.

Core Idea
Induction inverts the roles of minor and middle terms compared to syllogism, and is prior in sense experience but posterior in intellect.

The author compares induction to categorical syllogism to highlight their structural differences. Understanding this comparison deepens your grasp of how each reasoning method works and when each is appropriate. In this lesson, we map the terms and discuss the epistemic relationship between the two.

Identify the comparison

2
Map the terms

3
Spot the inversion
What takes the role of minor term in syllogism becomes the middle term in induction, and vice versa. Explain why this inversion occurs.
4
Discuss epistemic priority
Induction is prior to syllogism in sense perception because we acquire first premises through sensory induction. Syllogism is prior by nature in the intellect because it provides the logical structure for deduction.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (al-istiqra'), explains its two types—perfect and imperfect—and compares it to categorical syllogism. The author begins by giving a technical definition: induction is a reasoning that establishes a relation between two universals (e.g., 'every moving thing is a body') by examining the particulars under the subject universal. If all particulars are examined, it is perfect induction and yields certainty; if only most, it is imperfect and yields only probability. The example of perfect induction: judging that every moving thing is a body because each particular (animal, plant, mineral) is a body. The example of imperfect: judging that every animal moves its lower jaw when chewing, based on most animals, but the crocodile is a counterexample. The author then explains the epistemological value: perfect induction is useful for certain knowledge because the common nature is identical in the universal and particulars. Imperfect induction is not certain and can be fallacious in demonstration, but it is not fallacious in definition; it can be used for dialectical (popular) obligation. Next, the author compares induction to categorical syllogism: they share a structure (subjects as minor term, the universal under which they fall as middle, the predicate as major), but they differ in the roles of terms. In induction, what should be the minor term becomes the middle, and vice versa. Induction is prior to syllogism in terms of sense perception because many first premises are gained by sensory induction, but syllogism is prior by nature in the intellect. The passage concludes by reiterating the definition.

Induction (Istiqra') and Its Relation to Syllogism
Author examines induction, its types, and its relation to categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish induction from categorical syllogism in terms of term roles and explain the conditions for using induction to establish a syllogistic premise without circularity.

Core Idea
Induction swaps the minor and middle terms compared to syllogism and must be more evident than the conclusion it supports.

Induction (istiqra') is a fundamental tool for establishing universal premises through enumeration of particulars. This passage from *Daqa'iq al-Haqa'iq* carefully distinguishes induction from categorical syllogism, highlighting their structural differences and the conditions under which induction can validly serve as a premise in a syllogistic proof. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh, where inductive reasoning often grounds first principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
Find how the author defines induction: it clarifies the relation of a universal to another universal by means of a relation to the subjects under the first universal.
See the phrase 'توسط نسبة إلى ما تحت المنسوب إليه من الموضوعات'.
2
Compare term roles in induction vs syllogism
In categorical syllogism, the minor term is the subject of the conclusion, the middle term connects the premises, and the major term is the predicate. In induction, the subjects (particulars) function like the minor term, but the author says the roles are swapped: what ought to be the minor term becomes the middle term, and vice versa.
The structural difference is key: induction enumerates particulars to establish a universal, so the middle term is the universal under which particulars fall.
3
Understand the priority of induction in sense and syllogism in intellect
Induction is prior to syllogism with respect to sensory experience because primary premises are often acquired by sensory induction. However, syllogism is naturally prior to induction in the intellect, as the mind understands logical relations.
This hierarchy indicates different epistemological roles.
4
Analyze the condition for using induction to establish a major premise
When induction is used to clarify the major premise of a syllogism, the division of particulars must be such that the minor term (subject of the conclusion) is not explicitly included in the division; otherwise it would be a petition of principle (musadara).
The example: to prove 'Every rational being is A', divide animals into rational and non-rational, ensuring that the minor term (rational) is not already assumed.
5
Evaluate the requirement that the inductive premise be more evident
The inductive premise must be more evident than the desired conclusion, either by being more famous or by the conclusion being unknown while the premise is known. If it is equal or less evident, the proof fails.
This ensures the argument does not rely on something equally obscure.

Analogy and Its Dialectical Methods
Defines analogy and critiques its two dialectical methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of analogical reasoning and critique its two methods (ṭard wa 'aks and sabr wa taqsīm) as non-certain.

Core Idea
Analogy does not yield certainty; its methods are dialectical, not demonstrative.

This passage defines analogy (tamthīl) as the reduction of one known case to another based on a common attribute. It then introduces the two methods proponents use to justify analogy: ṭard wa 'aks (co-presence and co-absence) and sabr wa taqsīm (probing and division). The author argues that both methods fall short of producing certainty, making analogy a dialectical tool rather than a demonstrative proof. Understanding this critique is essential for anyone who wishes to use analogy rigorously in Islamic sciences like uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of analogy
Locate the author's definition: analogy is reducing a known to another known in a ruling based on a common link (jāmi'). Note the example: 'The world is existent, therefore it is eternal like the Creator.' Identify the four components: branch (far'), root (aṣl), judgment (ḥukm), and cause ('illa).
The author uses the term 'mīthāl' (example) for analogy.
2
Extract the two dialectical methods
Find the description of ṭard wa 'aks: it checks whether the judgment always accompanies the cause and is absent when the cause is absent. Then find sabr wa taqsīm: it enumerates all possible attributes of the root, eliminates those that are not the cause, and retains one.
Both methods are introduced as the only ways proponents have to justify analogy.
3
Understand the critique of ṭard wa 'aks
Note the author's objections: (1) It requires complete induction of all particulars and circumstances, which is impossible because the branch is excluded. (2) Even if complete, it does not prove that the shared attribute is the cause, because the cause may be composite and the observed attribute only a part.
The author gives a hypothetical: the cause might be a combination of attributes, and the shared one is just a component.
4
Understand the critique of sabr wa taqsīm
Note the author's objections: (1) It assumes the judgment in the branch is not self-evident but depends on a cause, which is not necessary. (2) It requires that the attributes be limited to those enumerated, which is not certain. (3) The argument that thorough search would reveal any hidden attribute is flawed because many meanings are discovered only with great effort.
The author compares it to a person who cannot see a camel or mountain in front of them, but here it is not that obvious.
5
Apply the critique to a fiqh example
Consider the qiyas of nabidh to wine: wine is prohibited because of intoxication ('illa). To extend the prohibition to nabidh, one must establish intoxication as the cause. Test whether ṭard wa 'aks or sabr wa taqsīm would yield certainty. Conclude that neither method is fully certain, so the analogy is dialectical.
This example shows why uṣūl al-fiqh scholars often treat qiyas as a probable (ẓannī) proof.

The theologians (mutakallimun) customarily call the reduced—like 'world' here—a branch (far'), the reduced-to—like 'Creator'—a root (aṣl), the ruling—like 'eternal'—a judgment (ḥukm), and the common link—like 'existent'—a cause ('illa). It differs from induction (istiqrā') in that in induction, the judgment is upon a universal due to it being judged about its particulars, whereas in analogy, the judgment is upon one particular of a universal because of the other particular's sharing that universal. In summary, it does not produce certainty because it is not necessary that two things sharing a universal also share the judgment made about one of them, unless the shared attribute is the cause ('illa) of the sought judgment. There is nothing that indicates it is a proof for its proponents except two methods: one called al-ṭard wa al-'aks (coincidence and reversal) and the other al-sabr wa al-taqsīm (probing and division), both of which are dialectical. As for al-ṭard wa al-'aks, it means nothing other than the concomitance of the existence of the judgment with the existence of the cause, or its absence when it is absent. It requires induction of all particulars and all circumstances, which is impossible because the branch is excluded from it, so it is incomplete. Even if it were complete, it does not necessitate that the shared attribute is the cause in the root, because the cause might be composed of attributes and this is only part of it. When the judgment exists at its existence, it is because the remaining attributes that are parts are present, and the judgment depends on their completion. Thus, when it exists, the judgment is established due to the completion and perfection of the cause. Similarly, the judgment is absent when it is absent due to the absence of part of the cause. Hence, its existence in the disputed case does not entail the existence of the judgment, because the remaining attributes may be absent. As for al-sabr wa al-taqsīm, it is to infer that the common attribute in the analogy is the cause after listing all attributes of the root and eliminating that the cause is any of them except the one retained. This is only useful if the judgment in the branch is not established by its essence but by another meaning, which is not necessary, otherwise there would be infinite regress. If it is for another, the attributes must be limited to those enumerated, with no basis other than probing (sabr) and lack of awareness of anything else, which is also not certain. The claim that if there were another attribute, one would have discovered it after rigorous search—just as it is impossible for a healthy-sighted person to have a camel or a mountain in front of him and not see it—is not the case here, because many meanings are only perceived after great effort and sometimes not at all, unlike the mentioned example. And that the elimination is not for each one individually.

The example given is: 'The world is existent, therefore it is eternal like the Creator.' More importantly, certainty is a subjective state: if the soul still entertains the possibility of the contrary, the knowledge is not certain. This criterion applies to all certain knowledge, not just empirical.

The author lists three conditions: stripping the judgment from accidental features (tajrid al-hukm 'an al-'awarid), considering all relevant states (i'tibar al-halat), and seeking the common cause (talab al-sabab al-mushtarak).

Understand the ultimate criterion for certainty
The author shifts to a general criterion: the soul (nafs) must not find any possibility of the opposite when the proposition is presented. If it does, the knowledge is not certain.

4
Recognize the universal scope
The author explicitly states that this criterion is not limited to empirical knowledge; it applies to all certain knowledge (kull ma hasala 'inda al-nafs min al-'ilm al-yaqini).

وعلى الجملة، فالاستقراء والتجربة فإنهما يفيدان اليقين أن لو وقع الأمر من الغلط، في تجريد الحكم عن العوارض والمخصصات واعتبار الحالات، وطلب السبب المشترك كما بيناه. والمعتمد في ذلك كله ليس على التعلم والمعلم، بل على ذوق النفس والفطرة السليمة، فإن وجد من نفسه احتمال النقيض عند العرض عليها فلم يكن ما حصل له بيقين، وليس هذا مختصاً بما يحصل من التجربة والاستقراء فقط، بل مطلق في كل ما حصل عند النفس من العلم اليقيني.
And in summary, induction and experiment both produce certainty if the matter is free from error, in stripping the judgment from accidents and particularizing circumstances, considering the states, and seeking the common cause as we explained. And the reliance in all that is not on learning and the teacher, but on the soul's taste and sound innate disposition. If one finds in himself the possibility of the opposite when it is presented to him, then what he obtained is not certain. And this is not specific to what is obtained from experiment and induction alone, but is general in all that is obtained in the soul of certain knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological clarification about how empirical knowledge (tajriba and istiqra) yields certainty. The author begins by stating that induction and experiment produce certainty if one avoids error by correctly abstracting the judgment from accidental features and considering all relevant states, seeking the common cause. He then shifts to a general criterion for certainty: the soul must not find any possibility of contradiction when the proposition is presented. This is not unique to empirical knowledge but applies to all certain knowledge. The emphasis is on the subjective feeling of certainty rather than external methods.
