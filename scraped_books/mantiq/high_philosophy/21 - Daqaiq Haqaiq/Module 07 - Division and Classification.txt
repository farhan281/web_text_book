Two Primary Types of Signification: Mutabaqa and Tadammun
Author defines the two primary types of signification: مطابقة and تضمن.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalala al-mutabaqa and dalala al-tadammun using the house example and explain why mutabaqa is stronger.

Core Idea
Mutabaqa signifies the whole; Tadammun signifies a part within the whole.

Words do not always signify their meanings in the same way. Sometimes a word refers to its entire conventional meaning; other times it refers to only a part of that meaning. This distinction is crucial for understanding definitions and interpretations in Islamic sciences. Let us explore the two primary types of signification as defined by the author.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of signified
The author divides the signified (what a word signifies) into two: either the entire identity of the subject (as per original or customary usage) or a part of that subject. Read the first sentence carefully.
The signified is a concept (مفهوم) that the word conveys.
2
Analyze the example of 'house'
The word 'house' (البيت) signifies the whole structure (ceiling and walls together) in dalala al-mutabaqa. In dalala al-tadammun, it signifies only the ceiling or only the wall, but only because these are parts of the whole.
Tadammun is not direct homonymy or metaphor; it relies on the part-whole relationship.
3
Understand the condition for tadammun
Tadammun requires that the part be understood as included in the whole. If the word were originally placed for the part alone, it would be mutabaqa (via homonymy) or metaphorical usage.
This distinction prevents confusion with other types of signification.
4
Compare the strength of the two types
The author declares mutabaqa stronger because understanding it does not require any external mediation beyond knowing the word's conventional meaning. Tadammun, in contrast, requires the listener to already grasp the whole and then infer the part.
This strength is important for constructing valid definitions and arguments.

We say: That which occurs as a signified of a word—i.e., a concept—is either the very identity of its subject according to original or customary usage without change, or it is a part of its subject. What is like the first signification is called 'signification of correspondence' (dalala al-mutabaqa) because the word is placed upon its meaning without increase or decrease, like the signification of 'house' for the totality of the ceiling and that which supports it. And what is like the second signification is called 'signification of inclusion' (dalala al-tadammun), like the signification of 'house' for that which is inside its meaning, such as the ceiling alone or the supporter alone, insofar as it is included in the signified, not in the manner that the word is placed for it alone—for then it would be correspondence, and its signification upon it is either by way of homonymy or metaphor. And the stronger of the two significations is correspondence, because understanding its signified does not require mediation of anything external besides knowledge of the usage and convention of the word indicated upon its meaning. That is not the case with inclusion, for what occurs as a concept from it is dependent on understanding that it is part of the subject.

Commentary
This passage defines two fundamental types of signification (dalala). The author begins by stating that whatever a word signifies (its concept) is either the entire essence of its subject (as per original or conventional usage) or a part of that subject. The first type is called dalala al-mutabaqa (signification of correspondence), where the word is placed for the whole meaning without any addition or omission. The example given is 'house' signifying the complete entity comprising both the ceiling and the supporting structure (walls). The second type is dalala al-tadammun (signification of inclusion), where the word signifies a part of its meaning that is contained within the whole, like 'house' signifying only the ceiling or only the wall. The author emphasizes that this inclusion is not because the word is originally placed for that part alone—if it were, it would be mutabaqa, either through linguistic homonymy (ishtirak) or metaphorical usage (tajawwuz). Importantly, the author declares mutabaqa to be the stronger of the two, because understanding it requires only knowledge of the word's conventional meaning, without needing to infer a part-whole relationship. In contrast, tadammun depends on first grasping that the part is included in the whole. This classification is foundational in Islamic logic, as it underpins how definitions and descriptions convey meaning. For instance, the term 'human' has mutabaqa for the animal-rational composite, and tadammun for either 'animal' or 'rational' separately.

Key Terms
دلالة المطابقة (dalala al-mutabaqa): Signification of correspondence: the word signifies its entire conventional meaning without addition or omission. [Note: Stable across the tradition.]
دلالة التضمن (dalala al-tadammun): Signification of inclusion: the word signifies a part of its conventional meaning that is internally contained in the whole. [Note: Stable across the tradition.]
الوضع الأصلي (al-wad' al-asli): Original linguistic coinage; the primary assignment of a word to a meaning. [Note: Distinguished from 'customary usage' (al-'urf al-isti'mali) in later works.]

Univocal and Graded Terms: Classifying Simple Terms
Author divides simple terms and defines univocal and graded terms with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a simple term applied to multiple referents is univocal or graded, and provide an example from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Univocal terms apply equally; graded terms assign priority or intensity.

In this lesson, we examine how logicians classify simple terms when they refer to multiple things. The key distinction is whether the term applies equally or with variation in its meaning. This classification underpins how definitions work in all Islamic sciences, from fiqh to kalam.

Guided Path
1
Find the threefold division of simple terms
The passage begins by dividing simple terms into three cases: term single/referent multiple, term multiple/referent single, both multiple. Identify which case the discussion focuses on.
Focus is on the first case where one term refers to multiple referents.
2
Narrow to the first division: literal and single meaning
Within the first case, the term may be literal or not, and if literal, it may signify via a single meaning or multiple. The univocal/graded distinction applies only to literal terms with a single meaning for multiple referents.
3
Distinguish between equality and differentiation
If the referents are equal in that single meaning, the term is univocal (mutawāṭiʾ). If they differ in priority, intensity, or weakness, the term is graded (mushakkik).
Examples: 'animal' (univocal), 'existence' (graded).
4
Test with given examples
Apply the criteria: 'animal' for species (equal), 'existence' for substance and accident (priority to substance), 'white' for colors (intensity/weakness).
Ensure the differentiation is in the meaning itself, not externally.
5
Avoid the common mistake
The author warns that some terms like 'substance' applied to simple and composite substances appear graded due to priority, but the priority is external to the meaning. Hence they are truly univocal.
The gradedness must be in the intended meaning, not in external aspects.

Chapter Four: On the relation of simple terms to their meanings, whether they are nouns, verbs, or particles. We say: A simple term is either that [the term] is single and its referent is multiple, or [the term] is multiple and its referent is single, or both [term and referent] are multiple. If it is the first division, then it is either a literal term in its referents, meaning that it is used in them in the original convention without being metaphorical in some of them, or it is not original with respect to some of them. If it is literal in them, then it either signifies them with regard to a single meaning or not. If it signifies them with regard to a single meaning, then they are either equal in it such that none is more entitled to it nor is it stronger in some of them, and in sum no difference occurs among them in it at all, or they are differential in it. If they are equal without differentiation, the term signifying them is called 'univocal' (mutawāṭiʾ), like the term 'animal' applying to its different species: human, horse, and others, with regard to their sharing in what it is placed for, namely the growing, nourishing, reproducing, breathing, sensing, voluntarily moving body. But this is not exclusive to essential predicates and their divisions, as will come, such that it is said that univocity only applies to what is a genus, species, or differentia, as some people thought. Indeed, univocity for essential predicates is only from the aspect that the multiple referents of the essential term are equal in that with regard to which the term signifies them, not in respect of the shared meaning being essential. And something like this is not impossible in what is accidental, like 'walking', 'laughing' and the like, as will be verified later. And if the aspects of the term, i.e., its multiple referents, are differential in the primacy, priority, intensity, and weakness with which they are signified by the term, then the name signifying them is particular to the 'graded' (mushakkik). The first type is like the application of 'existence' to substance and accident, since it is more entitled to substance than to accident, and more prior to it, because the subsistence of accident is only by it, and as long as the existence of the subject is not prior—at least in essence—it is impossible for that which has no subsistence except by it and no existence except in it to exist. As for the second type, it is like the application of 'white' to different colored things in intensity and weakness of whiteness, and like the application of 'hot' and 'cold' to different things in intensity and weakness of heat and cold. One might doubt that what is not graded is graded, as in applying the name 'existence' to simple substance and its composite, and applying the name 'substance' to its subordinate types, and likewise every genus to its species due to the priority and posteriority among them. But that is only negligence of that due to which the differentiation occurs. One who investigates carefully knows that the differentiation between simple substances and composites is only in something external to the meaning of the name 'existence' and 'substance', and that no substance is more intense in its substantiality than another substance nor more entitled to it, and what the composite has of existence or substantiality is not additional to the existence of its parts and their substantiality; rather, the existence of the parts and their substantiality is the very existence of the composite and its substance. So there is no primacy, priority, intensity, or gradation. And what is found of substances prior to another substance, whether in time, place, honor, nature, or causality, is only a priority in something external to its priority in deserving existence and substantiality. The graded (mushakkik) must be due to differentiation in that which is signified by the word, not in other than it.

Homonymous Terms and Their Causes
Author defines homonymous terms and discusses causes of homonymy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a homonymous term (mushtarak) and identify the three causes of homonymy recognized by classical logicians.

Core Idea
Homonymy arises from coiner differences, similarity, or blessing, not from necessity.

Homonymous terms are a reality in any language, and Islamic sciences are no exception. When a single word carries multiple unrelated meanings, confusion can arise unless the cause of that ambiguity is understood. In this lesson, we explore how classical logicians defined homonymy and why they rejected a popular argument that it is a necessary outcome of having more things than words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of mushtarak
Find where the author defines a homonymous term. Note that it applies 'not with regard to a single meaning among them'. The example 'ayn (عين) is given: it can mean an eye, a water spring, gold, etc. These meanings have no common core.
Contrast with mutawāṭi' (univocal) terms that share a single meaning.
2
Recognize the three causes of homonymy
The author lists three valid causes after noting that logicians need not investigate them. First: differences among coiners (واضعون) – different groups independently assigned the same word to different meanings. Second: remote similarity (تشابه بعيد) – e.g., gold and the eye share luster. Third: seeking blessing (تبرك) – naming later persons after prophets.
These causes are not exhaustive but are the ones classical logicians typically discussed.
3
Understand why the author rejects the infinite-finite argument
Some argued homonymy is necessary because there are infinite things to name but finite syllables. The author refutes this: it is not self-evident that types of things are infinite (individuals may be infinite, but types are finite), and the argument requires proof he does not accept.
This shows that logical necessity is not assumed without demonstration.

The passage ends with the conclusion that the placing of names is as described.

Universal vs Whole: A Critical Distinction
Distinguishing universal from whole

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the universal (kulli) and the whole (kull) in Islamic logical texts and identify why confusing them leads to error.

Core Idea
The whole can be particular; the universal cannot. They are not synonymous.

In Islamic logic, it is easy to confuse the universal (kulli) with the whole (kull) because both imply a collection. However, the author warns that they are fundamentally different. Understanding this distinction is crucial for accurate definitions in sciences like usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the terms
Locate 'kull' and 'kulli' in the passage. Note that 'kull' refers to a whole composed of parts, while 'kulli' refers to a concept applicable to many.
2
Check particularity
The passage says the whole can be particular (e.g., this ten), meaning it is not shareable. Can a universal be particular? No, because universals are shareable by definition.
3
Examine correlation
The whole is correlative to its parts—you cannot know the whole without knowing its parts. The universal is not correlative to its instances; instances do not depend on the universal to be known.

Chapter Seven: On explaining the division of the essential into genus, species, and differentia. It is impossible for one differentia to be both constitutive and divisive with respect to one thing, because insofar as it is a divider it requires being more specific than what it divides, and insofar as it is constitutive it requires being equal to it, and that is impossible. Commentary
This passage is a dialectical defense of the dual role of the differentia (al-fasl) as both constitutive (muqawwim) of its species and divisive (muqassim) of its genus. The author begins by stating the impossibility of a single differentia being both constitutive and divisive with respect to the same thing, because a divider must be more specific than what it divides, while a constituent must be equal – a contradiction. However, this is possible relative to two things: a differentia constitutive of its species is a divider of what is above it (the genus), and what is a divider of a genus is constitutive of what is below it (the species). An example is 'sentient' as a differentia of animal and a genus for 'rational', the differentia of man. The author then presents three objections: (1) The differentia is an attribute of the specific genus, so if it were constitutive, circularity would arise because the attribute depends on the described. (2) The animal body remains after the removal of the differentia, contradicting its being a cause. (3) A single simple entity (the differentia) cannot produce two effects (constitution and division). The answer to the first objection is that the predication is symmetrical: 'some animal is rational' and 'some rational is animal' are both true, but this does not mean one is an attribute of the other; it means the same thing is described by both, so no circularity. The second objection is refuted by distinguishing the genus from matter; the body that remains is matter, not the genus. The third objection is answered by noting that a single simple can have different relations (respects) without multiplicity in itself, or one effect is essential and the other follows from a necessary concomitant. Finally, the author addresses the question why every species must have a constitutive differentia: either the species is distinguished from the genus or not. If not, there is no genus-species relation. If distinguished, the distinguisher is either constitutive or accidental. An accidental distinguisher does not create a genuine genus-species hierarchy (like individuals under a species), so it must be constitutive. Thus the necessity of a constitutive differentia is proven.

Commentary
This passage is a systematic classification of the types of questions (matālib) used in logical inquiry, following the Aristotelian categories of questions as adapted in Islamic logic. The author first enumerates nine demands: ما, هل, لم, أي, من, كم, كيف, أين, متى. Each is explained with its subdivisions.

For 'what' (ما), it has two uses: (1) requesting the lexical meaning when the term is unknown, e.g., 'What is punishment?' (عقاب), (2) requesting the essential definition when the word is known but the reality is unknown, e.g., 'What is man?'.

For 'whether' (هل), it also has two uses: (1) simple هل asking about existence, e.g., 'Is Zayd existent?' (هل البسيطة), (2) compound هل asking about a property beyond existence, e.g., 'Is Zayd knowing?' (هل المركبة). The answer to both is one of the two contradictories (affirmation or negation).

For 'why' (لم), it asks either for the cause (علية) or for mere assent (تصديق), i.e., justification.

For 'which' (أي), it seeks to distinguish a thing from others sharing a common property, e.g., 'Which animal?' — answered by a differentia, whether essential or accidental.

For 'who' (من), it identifies a rational individual.

For 'how many' (كم), it asks about quantity.

For 'how' (كيف), it asks about quality.

For 'where' (أين), it asks about place.

For 'when' (متى), it asks about time.

The author then notes that all these demands can be reduced to the هل format, e.g., 'Is he existent?' for ما, 'Is he knowing?' for كيف, etc., but this is only possible when the possible answers are finite and enumerable; otherwise, the question must be directed to a specific demand. This classification is foundational for understanding the structure of scientific inquiry in logic.

Distinguishing Simple and Compound هل
Author enumerates nine types of requests used in logical inquiry and explains each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the simple هل (hal basīṭa) and the compound هل (hal murakkaba) and correctly classify example questions.

Core Idea
Simple هل asks if something exists; compound هل asks if something that exists has a certain property.

The demand هل is one of the most versatile in Islamic logic. It appears in two forms: simple, seeking to establish existence itself, and compound, seeking to ascertain a property of an existing thing. This distinction is crucial for precise inquiry in theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of hal basīṭa
Find where the author defines simple هل: it asks about 'the essence of existence' (أصل الوجود), e.g., 'هل زيد موجود؟'
This is the most basic type of هل.
2
Locate the definition of hal murakkaba
Find where the author defines compound هل: it asks about 'a state additional to existence' (حالاً زائدة على الوجود), e.g., 'هل زيد الموجود عالماً؟'
The subject is already assumed to exist.
3
Contrast the two with examples
Compare the simple 'Is Zayd existent?' with the compound 'Is Zayd, who exists, knowing?'.
The compound adds a property (knowing) to an existing entity.
4
Apply the distinction to kalam questions
Classify theological questions: 'هل الله موجود؟' is simple; 'هل الله عالم؟' is compound because it assumes God's existence and asks about His attribute.
This helps avoid ambiguity in theological arguments.

Reducing All Demands to هل
Author enumerates nine types of requests used in logical inquiry and explains each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the condition under which all nine demands can be reduced to the demand هل and apply this reduction to simple cases.

Core Idea
All demands collapse into هل when the possible answers are finite.

The author concludes the chapter by noting that every demand can be reformulated as a هل question, provided the categories of possible answers are finite. This insight reveals the foundational role of the هل demand and the importance of finitude in logical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of reducibility
Find where the author says 'all of them may be subsumed under the demand هل' (لكنها بأجمعها قد تدخل في مطلب هل).
This is a key claim about the unity of inquiry.
2
Understand the examples given
The author lists examples: 'Is he existent?' (for ما), 'Is he knowing?' (for كيف), 'Is he in the house?' (for أين), etc.
Each original demand is turned into a هل question.
3
Recognize the condition of finitude
The author states that this reduction works only 'if the categories to which the inquiry is directed are finite and easy to enumerate'.
If infinite, the question must be specific.
4
Apply the reduction to a specific demand
Take the demand 'من' (who) and convert it: instead of 'من زيد؟' (Who is Zayd?), ask 'هل زيد هو هذا الشخص؟' (Is Zayd this person?). This works if the set of possible persons is finite.

5
Reflect on infinite cases
If the categories are infinite (e.g., 'ما هو الموجود؟' What exists?), reduction is not possible. The question must be asked using the specific demand.
This shows the limits of reducibility.

For 'where' (أين), it asks about place.

For 'when' (متى), it asks about time.

The author then notes that all these demands can be reduced to the هل format, e.g., 'Is he existent?' for ما, 'Is he knowing?' for كيف, etc., but this is only possible when the possible answers are finite and enumerable; otherwise, the question must be directed to a specific demand. This classification is foundational for understanding the structure of scientific inquiry in logic.

Key Terms
المطالب (al-maṭālib): The types of questions or demands used in logical inquiry to obtain unknown knowledge. [Note: This term is stable across the Islamic logical tradition, corresponding to the Aristotelian categories of questions.]
هل (hal): The demand asking about existence or a property; the most fundamental demand.
