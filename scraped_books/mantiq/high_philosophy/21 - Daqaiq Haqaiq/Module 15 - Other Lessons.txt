Caution on Necessary Predication with Possibility
Author warns against assuming necessity transfers from predicate to subject across different subjects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a necessary predication for one subject can coexist with a possible predication for another subject, and avoid the fallacy of illicit modal transfer.

Core Idea
Necessity for one does not force necessity for another when modality is directional.

This passage warns against a subtle misinterpretation in modal logic. When we say something is necessary for one subject and possible for another, we might think a contradiction arises, but the author shows that as long as we respect the direction of predication, both can hold. Let's walk through the argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modal claims
Extract the two modal statements: 'necessarily every b is j' and 'possibly every a is j'. Note that they seem to conflict if we consider transitivity, but the author argues they do not.
2
Consider the negation case
Also consider the case where 'j' is negated from 'a' by possibility: 'no a is j' possibly. How does this affect the necessity for 'b'?
3
Examine the author's condition
The author introduces a condition: if 'a' is necessary for 'j' (i.e., 'j' is necessary for 'a' in some sense), then because 'j' is necessary for 'b', we might think 'a' is necessary for 'b', but that would conflict with 'j' being only possible for 'a'.
4
Recognize the resolution
The key is that 'a' being necessary for 'j' and 'j' being necessary for 'b' does not entail that 'j' is necessary for 'a'—modality is not symmetric. So no contradiction arises.

Formal Validity vs Factual Truth in Syllogisms
Author explains that the conclusion follows from the premises even if they are false, but only hypothetically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish formal logical validity from factual truth in categorical syllogisms and identify the conditions under which a syllogism is productive (muntij).

Core Idea
A syllogism can be valid even if its premises are false; validity depends on form, not truth.

In this passage, the author clarifies a foundational point in logic: the conclusion of a syllogism follows necessarily if the premises are granted, even if those premises are false or unreal. This distinction between formal necessity and factual truth is crucial for evaluating arguments in the Islamic sciences. We will walk through the author's reasoning and apply it to a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism example
Locate the example in the Arabic: 'كل ما كان الفرد منقسماً بمتساويين فهو زوج' and 'كل ما كان زوجاً فهو عدد'. This is a first-figure, first-mood categorical syllogism. Note that the term 'fard' (individual) refers to a number divisible into two equal parts.
The example is from arithmetic, which was part of the Islamic sciences curriculum.
2
Extract the form and check validity
The form is: All A are B, All B are C, therefore All A are C. This is Barbara (AAA-1). Regardless of whether any A actually exists, the form is valid. Check that the middle term (even) is distributed correctly.
Validity is about structure, not content.
3
Understand the author's point about truth
The author says the conclusion may be thought unproductive if the premises are true but have false parts (i.e., the parts are not true in reality). However, productivity (intāj) does not depend on the actual truth of the conclusion; it depends on the formal necessity: if the premises are granted, the conclusion must be granted.
The key term here is luzūm (necessity) and taslīm (affirmation).
4
Apply the distinction to an Islamic science example
Consider the syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden. Every nabidh is an intoxicant. Therefore, every nabidh is forbidden.' This is valid in form even if someone denies that nabidh is intoxicating. The conclusion follows necessarily if the premises are accepted.
In usul al-fiqh, this is a valid qiyas even if the minor premise is disputed.

Why Co-occurrence Does Not Prove Causation
Author explains insufficiency of co-occurrence test for causality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why observing a ruling's presence with a retained attribute and its absence without it is insufficient to establish that attribute as the cause, given the possibility of multiple causes.

Core Idea
Co-occurrence alone does not prove causation when a single ruling can have multiple causes sharing an attribute.

In determining the cause (ʿilla) of a ruling, a common method is to test whether the ruling is present when the attribute is present and absent when the attribute is absent. However, this test can be misleading. The author of this passage points out that the ruling might be caused by two different causes in two different cases, and the retained attribute might be shared between them. This lesson unpacks that logical insight and shows why a deeper analysis is needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic causal test
The text describes a simple test: if a retained attribute (mustabqā) is present whenever the ruling is present, and absent whenever the ruling is absent, one might think it is the cause. But this is insufficient.
This test is often used in usul al-fiqh to identify the ʿilla.
2
Understand the problem of multiple causes
The author warns that the same ruling could have two different causes applying to two different loci (cases). The retained attribute might be common to both cases, but the real cause in each case might be a specific subtype of that attribute.
For example, two different types of transactions might share the attribute 'exchange', but the cause of permissibility could be specific to each type.
3
Apply the variable notation
The author uses variables: if the retained attribute is (ج) which divides into (ب) and (د), it is possible that in one case the cause is (ب) and in another it is (د). Thus (ج) being present does not guarantee it is the cause.
This abstracts the logical structure: the shared attribute is a genus, and the specific cause is a differentia.
4
Recognize the consequence for causal analysis
Simply observing co-occurrence of the ruling with the retained attribute can lead to a mistaken attribution of causation. One must further examine whether the retained attribute is divisible into subtypes that could independently be causes.
This is why the method of elimination (إبطال المحذوف) requires more than just a positive correlation.

The Variable Notation and the Requirement of Indivisibility
Author uses variable notation to illustrate problematic divisibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the variable notation (ج, ب, د) to analyze why a retained attribute that is divisible into subtypes cannot be considered a true cause unless indivisibility is established.

Core Idea
A retained attribute that divides into subtypes may mask multiple distinct causes; therefore, it must be shown to be indivisible or that the ruling accompanies it in essence.

The author introduces a formal variable notation to illustrate the logical problem: a retained attribute (ج) that is shared by two cases might actually be composed of two distinct subtypes (ب and د), each of which could independently be the cause. This lesson focuses on that notation and the necessary condition of indivisibility. You will learn to apply this abstract structure to concrete examples from Islamic legal theory.

Coexistence of Knowledge and Conjecture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the coexistence of knowledge and conjecture in two persons versus in one person, and explain the role of potentiality/actuality and different faculties.

Core Idea
Knowledge and conjecture can coexist in one person when one is potential and the other actual, or when they stem from different faculties.

This passage explores an intriguing epistemological question: how can one person simultaneously hold certain knowledge and mere conjecture about the same thing? The author clarifies that while such coexistence is impossible from the same aspect, it can occur when knowledge is potential and universal while conjecture is actual and particular, or when they issue from different cognitive faculties like intellect and estimative faculty.

Identify the two cases
Read the passage and locate where the author distinguishes between coexistence in two persons and in one person. Note the key phrases 'بالنسبة إلى شخصين' and 'بالنسبة إلى شخص واحد'.
This sets up the entire discussion.
2
Analyze the two-person case
Understand why coexistence in two persons is unproblematic. The author explains that Zayd's knowledge does not necessitate 'Amr's knowledge; each can have opposite states (actual/potential) based on different syllogisms or one syllogistic and one non-syllogistic reasoning.
3
Examine the one-person case
The author states that from the same aspect, actual knowledge and actual conjecture about the same thing cannot coexist in one person. But they can coexist if the knowledge is potential and the conjecture actual, or if one is based on intellect and the other on estimative faculty.
Look for the condition 'بجهة تخالف جهة الظن'.
4
Work through the examples
Study the mule and star examples. In both, we have potential universal knowledge (every mule is barren; every star is not fire) but actual particular conjecture (this mule is pregnant; this star is fire). Notice how the conjecture arises from sensory similarity.
These examples concretize the abstract distinction.
5
Apply to the even-number example
The final example: you know potentially that a number in Zayd's hand is even (universal principle) but are actually ignorant of its particular evenness. This shows how knowledge and ignorance can coexist similarly.
This extends the pattern to knowledge and ignorance.

The eighth chapter on how knowledge and conjecture of a thing, or ignorance of its opposite, can coexist in one thing. We say: Knowledge of a thing and conjecture of its opposite may coexist with respect to two persons and with respect to one person. If the first, then it is not impossible that they coexist from one aspect of the aspects of potentiality or actuality. For it is not necessary that what Zayd knows, 'Amr knows it. Rather, it is possible that conjecture occurs to Zayd actually of what is opposite to 'Amr's actual knowledge, and similarly by potentiality. Their support in that is from two different syllogisms, or one from a syllogism and the other not from a syllogism. If the second [i.e., in one person], then their coexistence from one aspect is inconceivable — i.e., that Zayd actually knows that every B is C and at the same time actually conjectures that not every B is C. But if that occurs to him together in one time regarding one thing, then necessarily the knowledge is from a direction other than the direction of the conjecture, such that one is actual and the other potential, or one is based on intellect and the other on estimative faculty. An example of the first: we potentially know that this mule is barren and that this star is not such as to be fire in its nature, because they are potentially included in what we know that every mule is barren and every celestial body is unlike fire in its nature. Yet we actually conjecture when seeing the mule with a swollen belly that it is pregnant, and that this star is fire because of its resemblance to fire in being luminous. The knowledge of the mule being barren and this star not resembling fire is potential in a universal manner, because they are potentially included under what we knew... then upon this, knowledge and ignorance of a thing can be with respect to two persons and with respect to one person. For example, you know that what is in Zayd's hand is an even number when it is divided into two equal parts, because it is potentially included in our universal knowledge, yet we are actually ignorant of its being even in a particular manner specific to it.

Commentary
This passage is epistemological, addressing how knowledge (علم) and conjecture (ظن) about opposite states of affairs can coexist. The author distinguishes two cases: between two persons, and within a single person. For two persons, coexistence is straightforward: Zayd knows something while 'Amr conjectures its opposite, either actually or potentially. Their grounds may differ. For one person, actual coexistence of opposite judgments about the same thing from the same aspect is impossible. However, they can coexist if one judgment is actual and the other potential, or if they issue from different faculties (intellect vs. estimative faculty). The author gives examples: we know universally and potentially that every mule is barren, but when seeing a mule with a swollen belly, we actually conjecture it is pregnant. Similarly, we know potentially that a star is not fire, but due to its brightness we conjecture it is fire. Thus, knowledge and ignorance can coexist in one person when the knowledge is potential and universal, and the ignorance is actual and particular. The author concludes with the example of knowing potentially that an object in Zayd's hand is even, yet being ignorant of its actual evenness in that particular instance.

Core Idea
Every question in Islamic inquiry can be reduced to one of nine types.

In Islamic logic and debate, asking the right question is half the answer. The author enumerates nine interrogative particles (مطالب) that cover all possible demands a seeker can make. This lesson will help you identify each particle and its function, from asking for essence ('ما') to asking about time ('متى'). Understanding this classification is essential for correctly framing questions in any Islamic science.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of questions (مطالب) used in inquiry. The author enumerates nine interrogative particles: ما, هل, لِمَ, أي, مَن, كم, كيف, أين, متى. Each particle has a specific function. For example, 'ما' can ask either for the meaning of a word (شرح معنى الاسم) for someone who does not know the conventional meaning, like asking 'ما العقاب؟' (What is 'uqāb? meaning a punishment or a bird?), or for the essence (الحقيقة) for someone who knows the word but not the essence, like 'ما الإنسان؟' (What is man?). 'هل' also has two uses: simple 'هل' asking about bare existence (هل البسيطة) like 'هل زيد موجود؟' (Is Zayd existent?), and compound 'هل' asking about a mode of existence (هل المركبة) like 'هل زيد الموجود عالماً؟' (Is the existent Zayd knowledgeable?), answered by affirmation or negation. 'لم' asks either for the cause (علية) or for assent (التصديق). 'أي' asks for a distinguisher (تمييز) that separates the thing from others sharing a common attribute, e.g., 'أي حيوان؟' (Which animal?) answered by a specific differentia. 'مَن' asks for a particular rational person. 'كم' for quantity, 'كيف' for quality, 'أين' for place, 'متى' for time. The author notes that all these questions can be subsumed under 'هل' if the categories are finite, but if infinite, the question targets one specific one. This classification is foundational for understanding the method of inquiry in the Islamic sciences, as each question type leads to a specific kind of answer.

The Four Scientific Demands and Their Order
Author reduces scientific demands to four and explains their necessary order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four scientific demands (mā, ayy, hal, lima) and explain the necessary order in which they must be used in inquiry.

Core Idea
Scientific inquiry follows a tight logical order: name first, then existence, then essence, then cause.

In this passage, the author reduces the many possible questions we can ask to just four core demands—What, Which, Whether, Why—and shows that they must be asked in a specific sequence. This ordering is not arbitrary; it reflects the logical dependency of each question on the answers to previous ones. Without it, inquiry becomes aimless.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scientific demands
From the text, find the four Arabic terms (مَا, أَيّ, هَل, لِمَ) and note which two are conceptual and which two are composite.
The author adds 'Which' to the traditional three.
2
Trace the order of the simple 'What' and 'Whether'
The 'What' that explains the meaning of the name comes before the simple 'Whether' because you cannot ask about existence without knowing what the term means.
The text says: 'ما الطالبة لشرح معنى الاسم متقدمة على هل البسيطة'.
3
Order the remaining demands
After existence is confirmed, the 'What' for essence and 'Which' for distinction become possible. Finally, 'Why' asks for the cause, which presupposes knowledge of essence and existence.
The author states: 'وهل البسيطة فمتقدمة على ما الطالبة للحقيقة وعلى أي' and 'لم فمتأخرة عن الجميع'.
4
Examine exceptions to the order
The author notes that the 'Why' for assent might precede the 'Why' for cause if the assent is based on sensory evidence. Find the example of the magnet.
The text says: 'كما في جذب المغناطيس للحديد' – we see attraction but don't know the cause.
5
Place the compound 'Whether'
The compound 'Whether' (هل المركبة) comes after the 'Why' of assent, because one must first assent to existence before asking about additional states.
The text says: 'هل المركبة فمتأخرة عن طالبة لمّية التصديق'.

Commentary
This passage refines the classification of demands to those specifically used in scientific inquiry. The author explains that the scientific demands are first divided into three: 'What', 'Whether', and 'Why'. According to a second division they become six, but ultimately, the remaining demands (like 'Who', 'How many', etc.) can be subsumed under the compound 'Whether' or 'Which'. Thus the scientific demands are four: 'What', 'Which', 'Whether', and 'Why'. Among these, 'What' and 'Which' seek conceptual knowledge (taṣawwur), while 'Whether' and 'Why' seek compound knowledge (taṣdīq). The author then establishes a necessary order for inquiry: First comes the 'What' that asks for the meaning of a name (e.g., 'What is wudu'?'), because without understanding the term, one cannot ask about existence. Next is the simple 'Whether' that confirms existence (e.g., 'Is wudu' obligatory?'), because one must know that the thing exists before seeking its essence or distinction. Then comes the 'What' that asks for the essence (e.g., 'What is wudu' in reality?') or 'Which' for distinction (e.g., 'Which act of worship is wudu'?'). Finally, 'Why' asks for the cause (e.g., 'Why is wudu' required?'). The author notes that the 'Why' for assent (e.g., 'Why do we believe wudu' is valid?') might precede the 'Why' for cause, if the assent is based on sensory evidence like the attraction of iron by a magnet—we see the attraction but do not know its cause. However, the compound 'Whether' (e.g., 'Is wudu' purifying?') comes after the 'Why' for assent, because one must first believe in the existence of the thing before asking about its attributes. This ordering reflects the logical progression of knowledge acquisition.
