The Necessity of Signs for Human Cooperation
Opening argument for signs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of signs (action, speech, writing) and explain why speech is the most natural temporary sign for conveying mental contents.

Core Idea
Humans need signs to share knowledge; speech is primary because it is action without crowding.

The author argues that human cooperation in knowledge requires communication of mental contents. The most immediate sign is action, and within action, vocalization (speech) is ideal because it is temporary and doesn't cause physical crowding. This lesson traces that argument and introduces writing as a permanent solution for absent audiences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The author states that there must be things to indicate what is in the heart. Note the phrase 'فلم يكن بُد من أشياء يُدَلّ بها على ما في الضمير'.
This sets up the need for signs (dalala).
2
Find the preferred sign
The author says the most deserving sign is action, and specifically vocalization. Identify the reasons given: 'لا استقرار له بعدما استغنى عنه' (no stability after conveying meaning) and 'لا فيه ضرر الازدحام' (no harm of crowding).
Vocalization is ideal because it disappears when done and doesn't occupy space.
3
Recognize divine endowment
Humans are peculiarly endowed with segmented letters and combinations. This is attributed to divine care ('العناية الإلهية').
Segmented speech is a uniquely human capacity.

Commentary
This passage continues the epistemological foundation by addressing the need for communication. The author argues that since humans need to cooperate in acquiring knowledge, there must be signs to convey what is in the mind. The most natural sign is action, specifically vocalization (speech), because it is temporary and does not cause crowding. Humans are uniquely endowed with segmented letters and varied combinations due to divine care. These segmented sounds—spoken words—serve as signs for meanings in the heart. The origin of this sign-system can be divine revelation, inspiration, or human convention developed over time through repetition, like a parent teaching a child or a mute person using gestures. However, such signs only benefit those present. To benefit the absent and future generations, a lasting and stable medium is needed: writing. Therefore, for each sound segment, a written shape was devised, either by revelation or inspiration as with speech. Then writing indicates spoken words, and spoken words indicate mental images and meanings. Learning this system may take time, but because writing is composed of simple elements corresponding to simple sound segments, it is relatively easy. Consequently, speech and writing do not both directly indicate mental contents; one mediates the other. The author then reports the view of 'the best of the later scholars' (أفضل المتأخرين) that writing indicates mental contents through the mediation of speech, not vice versa. The reasoning is that if speech required writing to indicate mental content, then learning would be more difficult because the correspondence between speech and writing is not symmetrical: writing can be systematically mapped to speech (since letters correspond to sounds), but speech cannot be systematically mapped to writing (since writing has many forms). The author then expresses his own doubt (وعندي فيه نظر): it is not impossible that writing was originally placed before speech, so that a written form could directly indicate the mental meaning without speech mediation. If so, the correspondence between simple written elements and simple mental elements would be straightforward. This is a subtle critique: there is no logical necessity that speech always precedes writing in indicating meaning.

The Origin of Language: Divine or Human?
Enumeration of language origins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish three origins of language (tawqif, ilham, convention) as presented by the author and evaluate which is most plausible for Arabic.

Core Idea
Language originates from divine revelation, inspiration, or gradual human convention.

The author enumerates how the segmented sounds became signs: either by direct revelation from God to a prophet or community, by inspiration and innate promptings, or by a human initiator who sets a term and then generalizes it through repetition and teaching, like a parent with a child or a deaf-mute using gestures. This lesson analyses these three modes of linguistic origin, which parallel debates in usul al-fiqh about the status of Arabic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three modes
Look for 'إما' (either) clauses: 'بطريق التوقيف والوحي' (by revelation), 'بإلهامهم وبعث دواعيهم' (by inspiration), 'أو داعية بعضهم إلى وضع نفسه' (by individual initiative and generalization).
These are the three possible origins for any sign-system.
2
Understand the examples
The author gives examples for the conventional mode: parents teaching a child and a deaf-mute using gestures. These show how signs are learned through repetition and indication.
The examples illustrate the third mode, which relies on social transmission.
3
Compare with divine modes
Note that divine modes (tawqif, ilham) are not elaborated with examples; the author only mentions them briefly. This suggests a preference for the conventional explanation or a recognition of theological ambiguity.
In usul al-fiqh, the dominant view is that Arabic is tawqifi, but some argue for convention.

Does Writing Always Mediate Speech? The Author's Doubt
Critique of speech mediation claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the later scholar's argument that writing indicates mental contents only via speech, and explain the author's doubt that writing could indicate directly without speech mediation.

Core Idea
Writing may not need speech as a middle step; direct signification is possible.

The author reports a view from 'the best of the later scholars' that writing indicates mental contents only through the mediation of speech. He then expresses his own doubt (وعندي فيه نظر): it is not impossible that writing was placed before speech, so that a written form directly indicates the mental meaning. This lesson unpacks that critique and its implications for the priority of speech over writing.

Guided Path
1
State the later scholar's claim
The later scholar says: 'الكتابة دالة على ما في النفس بتوسط اللفظ' (writing indicates what is in the soul through the mediation of speech). Note the reason: if writing indicated directly, learning would be harder because speech cannot be systematically mapped to writing.
The claim prioritizes speech as the primary mediator.
2
Extract the reasoning
The later scholar uses a difficulty argument: learning to associate writing with mental images would be as hard as learning speech if writing were direct. But because writing letters correspond neatly to speech sounds, it's easier.
The argument assumes that speech is primary and writing is secondary.
3
Identify the author's doubt
The author says: 'وعندي فيه نظر' – he has a doubt. He proposes that writing could have been originally set before speech, so that a written shape directly indicates the mental meaning of 'insan' (human) without needing to go through the spoken word.
This is a counterexample: no logical necessity forces speech priority.
4
Evaluate the force of the doubt
The author's doubt is a logical possibility. He doesn't claim it is actual, only that it is not impossible (ليس بممتنع). The corresponding of simple written elements to simple meanings is plausible.
The doubt undermines the later scholar's claim of necessity, leaving open that writing could be an independent sign.

Commentary
This passage continues the epistemological foundation by addressing the need for communication. The author argues that since humans need to cooperate in acquiring knowledge, there must be signs to convey what is in the mind. The most natural sign is action, specifically vocalization (speech), because it is temporary and does not cause crowding. Humans are uniquely endowed with segmented letters and varied combinations due to divine care. These segmented sounds—spoken words—serve as signs for meanings in the heart. The origin of this sign-system can be divine revelation, inspiration, or human convention developed over time through repetition, like a parent teaching a child or a mute person using gestures. However, such signs only benefit those present. To benefit the absent and future generations, a lasting and stable medium is needed: writing. Therefore, for each sound segment, a written shape was devised, either by revelation or inspiration as with speech. Then writing indicates spoken words, and spoken words indicate mental images and meanings. Learning this system may take time, but because writing is composed of simple elements corresponding to simple sound segments, it is relatively easy. Consequently, speech and writing do not both directly indicate mental contents; one mediates the other. The author then reports the view of 'the best of the later scholars' (أفضل المتأخرين) that writing indicates mental contents through the mediation of speech, not vice versa. The reasoning is that if speech required writing to indicate mental content, then learning would be more difficult because the correspondence between speech and writing is not symmetrical: writing can be systematically mapped to speech (since letters correspond to sounds), but speech cannot be systematically mapped to writing (since writing has many forms). The author then expresses his own doubt (وعندي فيه نظر): it is not impossible that writing was originally placed before speech, so that a written form could directly indicate the mental meaning without speech mediation. If so, the correspondence between simple written elements and simple mental elements would be straightforward. This is a subtle critique: there is no logical necessity that speech always precedes writing in indicating meaning.

Distinguishing Verbal Implications: Mutabaqa, Tadhamun, and Iltizam
Author explains two differences between mutabaqa and tadhammun and argues iltizam is not verbal implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mutabaqa from tadhamun and argue why iltizam is not considered a verbal implication based on the author's reasoning.

Core Idea
Iltizam is a mental transition, not a verbal implication.

In Islamic logic, words indicate meanings in three ways: conformity (mutabaqa), inclusion (tadhamun), and necessity (iltizam). However, not all logicians agree that iltizam is a genuine verbal implication. This passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq explains two key differences between mutabaqa and tadhamun, then delivers a strong argument against including iltizam among verbal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of implication
Locate the three terms: mutabaqa (conformity), tadhamun (inclusion), and iltizam (necessity). Note that mutabaqa refers to the word's entire meaning, tadhamun to a part of it, and iltizam to a necessary concomitant.
The author focuses on the distinction between mutabaqa and tadhamun in two respects.
2
Extract the two differences between mutabaqa and tadhamun
First difference: mutabaqa is more general than tadhamun because tadhamun requires the word to have parts, while mutabaqa can apply even to words without parts. Second difference: mutabaqa indicates the whole meaning, tadhamun indicates only a part.
The example 'al-āla' (tool) is used to illustrate a word with no parts, where tadhamun fails but mutabaqa applies.
3
Understand the rejection of iltizam as a verbal implication
The author argues that iltizam is not verbal because the concomitant is external to the word's meaning and is understood only through a mental transition. If that transition were removed, the concomitant would not be understood, which would not be the case if it were a genuine part of the word's denotation.
The example of 'human' implying 'ability to laugh' shows that laughter is not part of the meaning of 'human', yet it is a necessary concomitant.
4
Apply the distinction to Islamic examples
Think of a word like 'salat' (prayer). Its mutabaqa is the entire act of prayer; tadhamun includes specific parts like bowing or prostration. Iltizam might include the necessity of intention, but according to the author, that is not a verbal implication; it requires mental reasoning.
This distinction affects how scholars derive rulings from texts in Usul al-Fiqh.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A statement can be true as a whole but false in parts when the predicate's terms shift meaning.

In logic, we often encounter propositions that seem contradictory, like 'the eunuch is a man not a man'. This passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq teaches us that such statements are not necessarily false; they are true collectively but false individually because the same word carries different meanings in different contexts. Why a Conditional Proposition is Not Categorical: Equivalence vs. Identity
Author refutes the claim that a conditional proposition with equivalent meaning is itself categorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a conditional proposition from a categorical proposition based on the linguistic tools used, and refute the claim that logical equivalence implies identity of form.

Core Idea
Equivalence in meaning does not erase the difference in logical form.

In this lesson, we tackle a common confusion: thinking that a conditional proposition like 'Everything that is a human is an animal' is actually categorical because it is equivalent to 'Every human is an animal'. The author of our source text corrects this mistake by showing that two propositions can be equivalent in truth value yet fundamentally differ in their logical essence, as determined by the tools of predication and conditioning.

The key is that the person confuses equivalence with identity.
2
Understand the author's counterexample
The author gives the conditional 'Everything that is a human is an animal' and the categorical 'Every human is an animal'. They are equivalent (both true in the same cases), but the author argues they are not the same type. Note that the first uses 'ما كان...فإنه' which is a conditional structure, while the second uses 'كل إنسان' directly.

Grasp the reasoning: sharing a property does not imply identity of essence

The author emphasizes that the essence of categorical and conditional differs because of the tools used: 'الاشتراط' (conditioning) vs 'الحمل' (predication). A categorical proposition predicates a property of a subject directly; a conditional links two sentences with a conditional tool. Even if they are materially equivalent, they are structurally different.
