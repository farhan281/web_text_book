The Fallacy of Opposite Premises: When a Thing is Not Itself
Author explains how opposite premises lead to impossible identity conclusions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify opposite premises and explain why they lead to the absurd conclusion that a thing is not itself (khalf).

Core Idea
Opposite premises force the absurd conclusion that a thing is not itself.

In this passage, the author of Daqaiq al-Haqaiq warns against a subtle fallacy: using two premises with the same subject and predicate but opposite quality. Such a pair inevitably yields the identity paradox 'a thing is not itself' – an absurdity that signals a flaw in the reasoning. Understanding this fallacy is crucial for maintaining logical consistency in theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pattern of opposite premises
Locate the two premises in the passage that share exactly the same subject and predicate but differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative). For example: 'Every medicine is knowledge' (affirmative) and 'No medicine is knowledge' (negative).
The subject and predicate must be identical; any other difference would change the nature of the fallacy.
2
Trace the absurd conclusion
From these opposite premises, the author argues that the necessary conclusion is that 'a thing is not itself' – for instance, from the two statements above we would get 'Not every knowledge is knowledge' or, more directly, 'Medicine is not medicine.' Recognize that this is a logical impossibility (khalf).
This conclusion is not merely false; it contradicts self-identity.
3
Examine a concrete syllogism that produces the fallacy
Consider the example of Zayd: 'Zayd is a rational animal' (true) and 'Nothing that is a rational animal is a speaker' (false). This valid syllogism (first figure) yields 'Zayd is not a speaker', which contradicts the truth that Zayd is indeed a speaker. Now combine the false conclusion with the true original statement to get a contradictory pair leading to 'Zayd is not Zayd'.
The error here is using a false premise about the middle term (rational animal) as if it were true.
4
Understand the source of the error
The author notes that such errors arise from faulty conceptualization or imagination before reasoning. The thinker mistakenly treats a composite as separate from its parts or fails to notice the contradiction. In the Zayd case, the thinker incorrectly assumes that 'rational animal' excludes 'speaker', perhaps because of an imaginative confusion.
This step helps you guard against similar pitfalls in your own reasoning.

The sixth art: syllogisms composed of opposite premises. The subject in both premises is one, and the predicate is one, and there is no difference except in quality, as we say: 'Every medicine is knowledge' and 'No medicine is knowledge.' For although it follows from this that 'Not every knowledge is knowledge,' the error is not hidden in it. And even if we allow that a true conclusion may follow from false premises, we are not obliged to allow the true from opposite premises, because what follows from them is that 'a thing is not itself,' which is absurd. As for how it happens that a thinker falls into syllogisms from opposite premises such that it produces that a thing is not itself without awareness, that is because it may happen that he has a true original proposition and fallacious syllogisms that produce false conclusions from which, together with the original proposition, it follows that it is not itself. Its example: let the original proposition be 'Some numbers are odd,' and the fallacious syllogism: 'Every number is divisible into two equals,' and 'Nothing divisible into two equals is odd,' so 'No number is odd.' Then if you say 'Some numbers are odd' and 'No number is odd,' with respect to the second figure it produces 'Some numbers are not numbers,' and with respect to the third figure 'Some odd is not odd.' And sometimes the original proposition is false and the syllogism is sound producing its opposite. And sometimes he has two syllogisms, either both false or one of them, whose conclusions are opposite as before. And sometimes error occurs by suggesting that the composite is not one of its two parts — i.e., it is not itself by itself — then making the composite a middle term between the part negated and that to which the composite is affirmed, as in saying: 'Zayd is a rational animal' and 'Nothing that is a rational animal is a speaker.' That occurs in what is between his own self, as if it is not itself, so it produces that Zayd is not a speaker, while he was precisely a speaker. And not all of that is [as described]. So some conceptualization occurs initially before falling into some of these slips, or imagination by some of these imaginings.

Commentary

The author explains that when two premises have the same subject and predicate but differ in quality (one affirmative, one negative), they force a conclusion that contradicts self-identity—something is not itself. This is an absurdity (خلف). The author provides three scenarios: (1) when a true original premise is combined with a false syllogism, yielding a false conclusion that contradicts the original; (2) when two syllogisms produce opposite conclusions; and (3) when a composite term is mistakenly separated from its part, leading to the false inference that the composite lacks a property it possesses. The example of Zayd being 'a rational animal' but not 'a speaker' illustrates the last case. The author concludes that such errors arise from faulty conceptualization or imagination preceding the reasoning.

Recognizing Begging the Question (Musadara) through a Geometric Example
Author illustrates musadara with a geometric scenario.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify instances of the fallacy of begging the question where the conclusion is assumed in a premise.

Core Idea
Begging the question is circular reasoning: using the proposition to be proved as a premise.

This lesson introduces the fallacy of musadara 'ala al-matlub, or begging the question. The author provides a striking geometric example to illustrate how a proof can inadvertently assume what it tries to prove. Understanding this fallacy is crucial for evaluating the validity of arguments in Islamic sciences, especially when constructing proofs for theological or juristic principles.

Guided Path
1
Read the geometric example carefully.
The author describes a situation where from two lines and a base, a triangle with angles greater than two right angles would result, but this depends on an unproven assumption.
2
Identify the premise that assumes the conclusion.
The author says: 'this is only proven after the validity of that position' – so the proof assumes the position it aims to prove.
3
Generalize the structure of the fallacy.
The fallacy occurs when the argument's premises rely on the truth of the conclusion, making it circular.
4
Apply this to an Islamic science argument.
Consider a kalam argument that uses 'the world is created' to prove 'the world has a cause' when 'created' already includes having a cause, leading to circularity.
This is a typical example of musadara in kalam.

The eighth art: regarding considering useful matters in the forms of arguments. They meet, otherwise if they met in a direction then from them with the object upon which they both fall would result a triangle whose angles are greater than two right angles; because the two angles at its base are equal to two right angles and the angle formed by the two lines is in excess of them, which is a contradiction; for the angles of a triangle are equal to two right angles; this is only proven after the validity of that position, so he has explained the state of the angles in the two lines by something whose explanation is only completed by it—and that is begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlub). As for if it is disjunctive, it is either simple or composite. If it is simple, then begging the question with respect to the desired conclusion cannot be conceived; otherwise, the begging would be either in the disjunction or in the exception from it. It is not possible that it be in the disjunction, because granting it is not granting the desired conclusion, nor in the exception, because it is always in the disjunction opposite to the desired conclusion—how can the opposite of the desired conclusion be begged with respect to the very desired conclusion? As for if it is composite, it is on this same pattern as well. And sometimes two contradictory things are taken in the syllogism, like 'human' and 'rational', or a particular and a universal, like 'human' and 'animal', so it is thought that their ruling is one, then upon that is built the supposition of begging the question with respect to the first desired conclusion. In reality, that is not begging the question except according to supposition. It has divisions that we will mention in the Topics. And upon investigation of what we have indicated regarding real and apparent begging of the question, that in which the desired conclusion is begged resembles the syllogism composed of contradictory premises in taking the middle term in the conclusion, although they differ in the necessity of the falsity of one of the two contradictory premises in the syllogism composed of them, and the absence of this necessity in begging the question; for it is not impossible that the desired conclusion, along with what accompanies it, be true. And in that begging the question is used to prove a non-evident desired conclusion, while in the syllogism with contradictory premises it is the contradictory of a conceded desired conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the fallacy of begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlub). The author begins by illustrating a geometric example where the proof of the angles in two lines assumes the very point that needs to be proven, which is a clear case of circular reasoning. Then he examines whether such a fallacy occurs in conditional syllogisms. For disjunctive syllogisms (qiyas munfasil), he argues that simple disjunctive syllogisms cannot beg the question because granting the disjunction does not grant the desired conclusion, and the exception in the disjunctive is always opposite to the desired conclusion. Composite disjunctive syllogisms follow the same logic. He then notes that sometimes in categorical syllogisms, two terms like 'human' and 'rational' are taken as if they have the same extension, creating a false impression of begging the question, but this is only apparent, not real. The passage compares this fallacy to syllogisms with contradictory premises, highlighting that in begging the question the premises do not necessarily entail the conclusion's falsity, unlike contradictory premises which force a false conclusion. The author also distinguishes between real and apparent begging of the question, noting that real begging of the question still produces truth, while the syllogism with contradictory premises yields a falsehood. The discussion is rooted in the Aristotelian Topics (Topika).

Why Simple Disjunctive Syllogisms Cannot Beg the Question
Author analyzes musadara in disjunctive and composite syllogisms, and distinguishes real from apparent musadara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why simple disjunctive syllogisms are immune to the fallacy of begging the question and distinguish real from apparent musadara.

Core Idea
In a simple disjunctive syllogism, granting the disjunction does not grant the conclusion, so no circularity.

The author next examines whether musadara can occur in conditional syllogisms, focusing on the disjunctive type (qiyas munfasil). He argues that simple disjunctive syllogisms cannot involve the fallacy because the disjunction itself does not entail the desired conclusion, and the exception is opposite to it. This lesson clarifies the reasoning and also discusses apparent musadara when terms are taken as synonyms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about simple disjunctive syllogisms.
The author says it is impossible for musadara to occur in a simple disjunctive syllogism.
2
Understand the two components: the disjunctive premise and the exception.
The disjunctive premise (e.g., either A or B) and the exception (e.g., not A) are the two parts of the syllogism.
3
Consider why the disjunction cannot be a source of musadara.
Granting the disjunction does not grant the desired conclusion because it only presents possibilities; it does not assert which one is true.
4
Consider why the exception cannot be a source of musadara.
The author argues that the exception is always opposite to the desired conclusion, so it cannot be circular—how can the opposite of the conclusion be taken as a premise that assumes the conclusion?
This point requires careful analysis: in a typical disjunctive syllogism, the exception negates the other disjunct, not the conclusion itself. The author's statement may be interpreted as the exception being opposite to the desired conclusion in the sense that it negates the disjunct that is not the conclusion.
5
Distinguish real from apparent musadara with synonyms.
When two terms like 'human' and 'rational' are taken as having the same extension, it may seem that the conclusion is assumed, but this is only a supposition, not true circularity.
The author clarifies that this is apparent musadara, not real.

Identifying False Cause in Reductio Arguments
Author explains the fallacy of false cause in reductio arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a reductio argument commits the fallacy of attributing the absurdity to the wrong premise, using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
A reductio is valid only if the absurdity follows solely from the contradictory assumption.

This passage addresses a subtle but critical fallacy in reductio arguments (qiyas al-khalf). In Islamic logic, reductio is used to prove a conclusion by assuming its contradictory and deriving an impossibility. However, the author warns that sometimes the impossibility actually arises from other premises, not from the contradictory assumption itself. Misattributing the source of the absurdity leads to a sophistical argument. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to evaluate theological and juristic proofs that rely on reductio.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the structure of a reductio
Identify the desired conclusion (al-matlub) and its contradictory (naqid al-matlub) assumed for the sake of argument. The reductio aims to derive an impossibility (muhal) from the conjunction of this contradictory assumption with other premises.
In the passage, the author gives examples with variable letters to abstract the structure.
2
Locate the assumed contradictory
Find the proposition that is the opposite of what the arguer wants to prove. In the first example, the desired conclusion is 'not every animal is human', so the contradictory is 'every animal is human'.
The contradictory is the premise that the arguer intends to show leads to absurdity.
3
Check if the absurdity follows without the contradictory
Test whether the same impossibility would still be derivable if the contradictory assumption were removed. If the conclusion still follows from the other premises alone, then the reductio is fallacious.
The author shows this in the second example: the impossibility (كل ج أ) follows from (كل ج د) and (كل د أ) even if (كل ب ج) is removed.
4
Distinguish correct from fallacious reductio
A correct reductio requires that the impossibility only follows when the contradictory is present. If the impossibility persists after removing the contradictory, the cause is misattributed.
The passage emphasizes that the syllogism must be 'free from redundancy' (خليّاً عن الحشو) and that removing the contradictory must eliminate the absurdity.

We say: A syllogism may be set up to produce an impossibility, and its attribution is thought to be to a specific premise from it, while it is actually attributed to something else, such that the impossibility remains even if that specific premise is removed as a cause. Rather, that is only for a reductio syllogism (qiyas al-khalf), where the contradictory of the desired conclusion is present, and one seeks to conjoin it with another premise to produce an impossibility, which is thought to be attributed to the contradictory, but the cause is something else. Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a specific fallacy in reductio arguments (qiyas al-khalf). The author explains that sometimes a syllogism is constructed to produce an absurdity (muhal), and it is mistakenly thought that a particular premise is the cause of that absurdity, when in fact the absurdity follows from other premises. This fallacy does not occur in direct syllogisms, which aim for truth, but in reductio arguments where the contradictory of the desired conclusion is assumed. The author distinguishes two cases: either the terms of the absurdity are completely separate from the contradictory, or they share some terms but the absurdity still follows from other premises. Two examples are given. In the first, the desired conclusion is 'not every animal is human'; the contradictory is 'every animal is human', and from this conjoined with other premises, the absurdity 'every substance is an accident' is produced—but the absurdity actually follows from those other premises alone. In the second example, using variable letters (B, C, D, A), a reductio is set up: assuming 'not every B is C' leads to a contradiction via premises about C, D, and A, but the contradiction actually follows from the premises about C, D, and A alone, independent of 'every B is C'. The author stresses that for a correct reductio, the absurdity must truly follow only from the contradictory assumption and not from other premises. The passage underscores the need for scrutiny in reductio arguments to avoid false attribution of the cause of the absurdity.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new section (the 'Eighth Art') on useful considerations for analyzing syllogistic forms. The author states that analysis can be difficult due to several factors. He lists six sources of difficulty: 1) the conclusion itself, 2) the conditions of the syllogism, 3) the limits (hudud, i.e., definitions or terms), 4) the figure of the syllogism, and 5) the premise. (Two are mentioned in the text: 'from the figure and the premise' may be read as two separate items, or 'figure' and 'premise' as one; but the enumeration seems to have at least four, possibly five including 'conditions' as separate.) The author then elaborates on the first source: the conclusion. The problem occurs when the conclusion appears necessary and obvious to the mind, so that the imagination dismisses any possible defect, even though the syllogism is not actually productive of the desired conclusion. If it does produce, it is only through forced manipulation. He gives an example: statements about 'parts of substance' and 'substance' that make one think that the parts of substance are substance itself, which is a fallacy. The example illustrates how a flawed argument can seem convincing due to the immediate appeal of the conclusion.
