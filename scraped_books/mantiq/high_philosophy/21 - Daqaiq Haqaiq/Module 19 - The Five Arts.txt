Types of Non-Demonstrative Premises in Dialectic and Rhetoric
Author enumerates and defines types of premises used in dialectic and rhetoric.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five types of non-demonstrative premises based on their basis and purpose, and explain why they are suited for dialectic and rhetoric rather than demonstration.

Core Idea
Non-demonstrative premises compel assent without yielding certainty.

In Islamic logic, not all arguments aim at demonstrative certainty. Dialectic and rhetoric rely on premises that are accepted or plausible but not apodictic. This passage classifies such premises into five categories: juridical, deliberative, political, and two mixed types. Understanding these helps us see how various Islamic sciences (fiqh, kalam, siyasa) construct persuasive arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching purpose
Read the final sentence: the author states these premises are 'not beneficial for certainty' and their purpose is 'compulsion in the well-known based on what is mostly conjectured'. This frames all the categories below.
2
List the five categories
Scan the passage for the named types: juridical (الفقهيات), deliberative (التعقلية), political (السياسة), a mixed sensory/juridical? (الحسية فقهية), and intermediate (الوساطية). Note that the fourth is labeled 'fighiyya' but derives from mixing juridical and political.
The fourth category is confusingly called 'fighiyya' but the description says 'mixed from juridical and political'.
3
Analyze each category's basis
For each type, extract what makes it authoritative. Juridical rests on statements of trustworthy figures (Prophet, imams). Deliberative is individual reasoning for practical action. Political concerns governance. Mixed types combine juridical with political or praiseworthy opinions.
Deliberative premises are internal; the author contrasts them with oratory and dialectic.
4
Distinguish by scope and certainty
Notice the author says political premises are 'more specific' than deliberative. The mixed types are combinations. All are said to lack certainty and be prone to error in demonstration, but appropriate for dialectic and rhetoric.
This aligns with the five arts: demonstration yields certainty; dialectic and rhetoric work with probable premises.
5
Apply to an Islamic science example
Think of a fatwa based on Quran and Sunnah (juridical), a personal ijtihad about daily actions (deliberative), or a policy for public welfare (political). Place each in its category.

Commentary
This passage defines several types of non-demonstrative premises used in argumentation. The author begins by stating that these premises can be affirmative or negative, then enumerates categories. First are juridical premises (fiqhiyyat), whose basis is authoritative statements from the Lawgiver or qualified scholars. Second are deliberative premises (ta'aqquliyyat), which are internal reasoning for practical action, aiming at truth or probability but primarily to compel the self. They differ from oratory and dialectic because they require acceptance by the listener. Third are political premises (siyasat), applied to governance, more specific than deliberative. Then two mixed categories: one combining juridical and political (hissiyyat? the text says 'fighiyya' but seems mislabeled), and intermediate (wasatiyyat) mixing juridical and praiseworthy opinions. The passage concludes that all these types do not yield certainty and are prone to error in demonstration, but are appropriate for dialectic and rhetoric, whose aim is to compel assent based on generally accepted opinions.

The Five Arts of Logic: Classification and Purpose
Author classifies the five arts of logic and defines their purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the five types of syllogism according to their epistemic value and identify which art corresponds to each type of premise.

Core Idea
Demonstration yields certainty; all other arts produce only opinion, imagination, or nothing.

In this passage, the author introduces the five arts of logic by comparing them to the ranks of definitions. Just as definitions vary in completeness, syllogisms vary in the certainty they produce. Understanding this classification is essential for choosing the right method in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parallel between definitions and syllogisms
Notice how the author uses the same structure of ranks for both definitions (expository statements) and syllogisms. This shows that logic treats both conceptual and assertoric knowledge in parallel.
The author first lists ranks of definitions (complete real, incomplete, etc.), then states 'likewise the ranks of syllogism are different.'
2
List the five arts in order of epistemic value
From highest to lowest: demonstration (برهان) gives certainty; dialectic (جدلي) gives opinion resembling certainty; rhetoric (خطابي) gives dominant but weaker opinion; poetics (شعري) gives imaginative impression without assent; sophistry (مغالطي) gives nothing.
Memorize the Arabic terms: برهان, جدلي, خطابي, شعري, مغالطي.
3
Match each art to its purpose
Demonstration aims at certainty leading to virtue and happiness; dialectic at persuasion; rhetoric at influence; poetics at emotional effect; sophistry at deception or refutation.
The author explicitly states the purpose of demonstration: 'معرفة الطرق الموصلة إلى اليقين' (knowledge of ways leading to certainty).
4
Apply the classification to a real example
Consider a kalam argument for God's existence using the huduth (temporal origination) proof. This is demonstrative if its premises are certain. A fiqh debate about qiyas (analogical reasoning) often uses dialectical premises (accepted principles). Poetics is used in Islamic poetry for inspiration, not for proof.
This step connects the classification to actual Islamic sciences.

We say: Since acquired knowledges are divided into conceptual and assertoric, and conceptual is acquired by the expository statement, and assertoric by syllogism, just as the ranks of the expository statement are different — among them is the complete real definition that expresses the quiddity of a thing by all its essential properties, and the incomplete definition that distinguishes the thing from others by an essential property, and the complete and incomplete description that distinguishes it from others by an accidental property, and the verbal exposition that explains the meaning of the word, and the corrupt one that does not benefit anything at all, as was previously defined — likewise the ranks of the syllogism that benefits assertoric knowledge are also different: among them is that which benefits certainty, which is demonstration; that which benefits opinion resembling certainty, which is dialectic; that which benefits a predominant opinion but less than the first, which is rhetoric; that which induces an imaginative impression causing contraction and expansion of the soul without assent, which is poetics; and that which is corrupt and induces nothing at all, which is sophistry. For the purpose of other than it among the five divisions is nothing but producing opinion, imagination, refutation and obstinacy, or guarding against it, and the purpose in all that is inferior to the purpose of demonstration. And although dialectical matter is more general than demonstrative matter, in that the matter of dialectic is generally accepted propositions and postulates, as will come, while the matter of demonstrative syllogism is certainties such as (primary principles, observations, experiential data, intuitions, recurrent reports, innate premises of syllogism, and estimative [premises] in sensibles — and each of them is generally accepted and not convertible), it is not that what must be presented is like presenting consideration of absolute syllogism before the specified one, due to the misconception of equality between the two things. For the presentation of absolute syllogism before the specified one was not due to its generality, but because its relation to it is the relation of a constitutive element, and knowledge of a thing cannot be conceived before the conception of its constitutive elements. And it is not hidden that whoever wants to know a particular syllogism without knowing the reality of absolute syllogism has burdened himself with difficulty. And although dialectical matter is more general than demonstrative matter, its relation to it is not the relation of an accidental non-constitutive attribute. Therefore, whoever considers true matter insofar as it is true, even if he disregards its generality and its not being followed by the public, his assent is not disrupted, and what is reached by it is from demonstration.

Commentary
This passage introduces the first chapter of the work on demonstration. It begins by classifying acquired knowledge into conceptual and assertoric, and then draws a parallel between the ranks of definitions (expository statements) and the ranks of syllogisms that produce assertoric knowledge. The author lists five types of syllogistic argument: demonstration (برهان) yielding certainty, dialectic (جدلي) yielding opinion resembling certainty, rhetoric (خطابي) yielding predominant opinion, poetics (شعري) yielding imagination without assent, and sophistry (مغالطي) yielding nothing. The main argument is that demonstration should be given priority because it aims at certainty, which is the noblest goal, leading to the perfection of the soul and eternal happiness. The author then addresses a potential objection: dialectical matter (premises that are generally accepted or postulated) is more general than demonstrative matter (premises that are certainties). But he argues that generality does not necessitate priority; rather, the relationship is constitutive—absolute syllogism is like a constitutive element of particular syllogisms. Similarly, dialectical matter is not merely an accidental attribute of demonstrative matter; true matter, when considered as true, leads to demonstration regardless of its acceptance. Thus, demonstration remains supreme. The passage sets the stage for a detailed treatment of demonstration, emphasizing its epistemological superiority over the other arts. In the Islamic intellectual tradition, this classification is foundational for understanding how different sciences (kalam, fiqh, philosophy) use different argumentative methods.

Why Demonstration Is Supreme: The Priority of Certainty
Author argues for the priority of demonstration over other logical arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why demonstration is given priority over the other arts and refute the objection that dialectical material is more general.

Core Idea
Demonstration is supreme because it yields the highest goal: certainty, and its matter is constitutive, not just more specific.

After classifying the five arts, the author argues that demonstration should be studied first. He anticipates an objection: dialectical premises are more general (common to many fields), so shouldn't dialectic come first? He explains that priority is based on constitutive relation, not generality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about the purpose of demonstration
The author states that the purpose of demonstration is 'knowledge of the ways that lead to certainty' (معرفة الطرق الموصلة إلى اليقين). This is the noblest aim because certainty leads to virtue and eternal happiness.
2
Understand the objection from generality
The objection: dialectical matter (generally accepted propositions and postulates) is more general (أعم) than demonstrative matter (certainties). So one might think dialectic should be studied before demonstration.
Generality here means applicability across many disciplines, not logical generality.
3
Analyze the author's response: constitutive vs. accidental
The author uses the analogy of absolute syllogism vs. particular syllogism. Absolute syllogism is studied first not because it is more general, but because it is constitutive (مقوم) of every particular syllogism. Similarly, true matter (المادة الصادقة) is constitutive of demonstration; dialectical matter is only an accidental attribute (عارض غير مقوم).
Thus, priority is based on constitutive relation, not generality.
4
Apply this reasoning to a real case
Consider the kalam argument for creation: 'The world is originated; everything originated has a cause; therefore the world has a cause.' The premises are certain (from observation and reason). This is demonstration. Dialectical versions might use 'most people accept that the world is originated' but that is not certain. The truth of the premises is constitutive of the demonstration.
If someone objects that dialectical premises are more widely accepted, the author's response shows that truth (sidq) is what matters, not acceptance.

Chapter One: On its subject, purpose, and establishing its rank. We say: Since acquired knowledges are divided into conceptual and assertoric, and conceptual is acquired by the expository statement, and assertoric by syllogism, just as the ranks of the expository statement are different — among them is the complete real definition that expresses the quiddity of a thing by all its essential properties, and the incomplete definition that distinguishes the thing from others by an essential property, and the complete and incomplete description that distinguishes it from others by an accidental property, and the verbal exposition that explains the meaning of the word, and the corrupt one that does not benefit anything at all, as was previously defined — likewise the ranks of the syllogism that benefits assertoric knowledge are also different: among them is that which benefits certainty, which is demonstration; that which benefits opinion resembling certainty, which is dialectic; that which benefits a predominant opinion but less than the first, which is rhetoric; that which induces an imaginative impression causing contraction and expansion of the soul without assent, which is poetics; and that which is corrupt and induces nothing at all, which is sophistry. Therefore, the purpose of demonstration is only knowledge of the ways that lead to certainty. And just as it was necessary in view of the natural order, as previously explained, to place simple things before composites, and to place decisive statements before the forms of arguments and syllogisms composed from them, and to place consideration of absolute syllogism before that which is specified by matters and modalities — it is even more fitting that we consider demonstration among these five divisions, because it yields the noblest aims and the most complete goals, which is certainty that leads to attaining the virtue of the rational soul in this world and achieving eternal happiness in the hereafter. For the purpose of other than it among the five divisions is nothing but producing opinion, imagination, refutation and obstinacy, or guarding against it, and the purpose in all that is inferior to the purpose of demonstration. And although dialectical matter is more general than demonstrative matter, in that the matter of dialectic is generally accepted propositions and postulates, as will come, while the matter of demonstrative syllogism is certainties such as (primary principles, observations, experiential data, intuitions, recurrent reports, innate premises of syllogism, and estimative [premises] in sensibles — and each of them is generally accepted and not convertible), it is not that what must be presented is like presenting consideration of absolute syllogism before the specified one, due to the misconception of equality between the two things. For the presentation of absolute syllogism before the specified one was not due to its generality, but because its relation to it is the relation of a constitutive element, and knowledge of a thing cannot be conceived before the conception of its constitutive elements. And it is not hidden that whoever wants to know a particular syllogism without knowing the reality of absolute syllogism has burdened himself with difficulty. And although dialectical matter is more general than demonstrative matter, its relation to it is not the relation of an accidental non-constitutive attribute. Therefore, whoever considers true matter insofar as it is true, even if he disregards its generality and its not being followed by the public, his assent is not disrupted, and what is reached by it is from demonstration.

Commentary
This passage introduces the first chapter of the work on demonstration. It begins by classifying acquired knowledge into conceptual and assertoric, and then draws a parallel between the ranks of definitions (expository statements) and the ranks of syllogisms that produce assertoric knowledge. The author lists five types of syllogistic argument: demonstration (برهان) yielding certainty, dialectic (جدلي) yielding opinion resembling certainty, rhetoric (خطابي) yielding predominant opinion, poetics (شعري) yielding imagination without assent, and sophistry (مغالطي) yielding nothing. The main argument is that demonstration should be given priority because it aims at certainty, which is the noblest goal, leading to the perfection of the soul and eternal happiness. The author then addresses a potential objection: dialectical matter (premises that are generally accepted or postulated) is more general than demonstrative matter (premises that are certainties). But he argues that generality does not necessitate priority; rather, the relationship is constitutive—absolute syllogism is like a constitutive element of particular syllogisms. Similarly, dialectical matter is not merely an accidental attribute of demonstrative matter; true matter, when considered as true, leads to demonstration regardless of its acceptance. Thus, demonstration remains supreme. The passage sets the stage for a detailed treatment of demonstration, emphasizing its epistemological superiority over the other arts. In the Islamic intellectual tradition, this classification is foundational for understanding how different sciences (kalam, fiqh, philosophy) use different argumentative methods.
