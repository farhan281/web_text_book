Introduction to Conjunctive Syllogisms: The Two Bases
Author introduces the first art on conjunctive syllogisms with two bases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two bases of conjunctive syllogisms: categorical and conditional.

Core Idea
Conjunctive syllogisms split into two bases: categorical and conditional.

In this lesson, we begin the study of conjunctive syllogisms (القياسات الاقترانية). The author presents a foundational division: one base deals with syllogisms whose premises are categorical propositions, and the other with premises that are conditional propositions. Understanding this division is the first step in mastering the structure of logical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall structure
The passage introduces 'The First Art' which covers forms of conjunctive syllogisms. Note that it says 'contains two bases'. That is the key organizational claim.
Memorize: تشتمل على قاعدتين (contains two bases).
2
Distinguish the two bases
The first base is on conjunctives from categorical propositions (الحمليات). The second base is on conjunctives from conditional propositions (الشرطيات). Why is this division important? Because categorical and conditional propositions have different logical properties.
Categorical: 'Every A is B'. Conditional: 'If A then B' or 'Either A or B'.
3
Recall definitions of proposition types
Categorical propositions assert a predicate of a subject (e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'). Conditional propositions connect two parts (e.g., 'If it is wine, then it is prohibited'). The two bases correspond to these types.
These definitions are essential for applying the correct rules later.
4
Apply to real arguments
For example, a categorical syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is haram; nabidh is intoxicating; therefore nabidh is haram' belongs to the first base. A conditional syllogism: 'If the evidence is definitive, the ruling is decisive; this evidence is definitive; so the ruling is decisive' belongs to the second base.
Practice classifying arguments you encounter in Islamic texts.

Self-evident and Theoretical Propositions
Author distinguishes types of propositions and introduces the need for a method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any proposition as self-evident (badihiyya) or theoretical (nazariyya) based on the author's criteria.

Core Idea
Premises for syllogisms come in two kinds: those already known and those needing discovery.

In this chapter, the author explains how to acquire premises for syllogisms. He begins by distinguishing between self-evident propositions, which need no proof, and theoretical propositions, which require a method. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it tells you when you need to work and when you can rely on immediate knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Look at the proposition you want to use as a premise. Is it immediately obvious to any rational person? If yes, it is self-evident.
2
Apply the author's criterion
The author says: if a proposition is self-evident, there is no need to seek it because nothing clearer can be used to prove it. If it requires some reminder, it may still be self-evident with minimal clarification.
This matches the idea of 'badihiyya' in Islamic epistemology.
3
Classify as theoretical
If the proposition is not self-evident, it is theoretical and requires a syllogism to be proven. Then you must follow the method described later.

Chapter Five: On Acquiring Premises and How to Obtain Syllogisms of Desired Conclusions. Since we have finished explaining syllogisms and defining their types, it is fitting for us to complete the benefit by defining the ways of acquiring them in a universal manner, not in terms of whether it is demonstrative, rhetorical, dialectical, or otherwise as will come, for rarely does one benefit from knowing the reality of the syllogism when wishing to use it if one does not know the way of obtaining it, since he finds it already obtained. So we say: Assertoric meanings are either self-evident or theoretical. If they are self-evident, there is no need to seek them, for that by which they are made evident is not clearer than them, and the need of some of them, if any, is only for minimal recollection and reminder as previously. And if they are theoretical, then the manner of the way of acquiring every desired one of them is that you first posit one of the desired [conclusions], then you examine the definition of each one of the two terms, and what attaches to each one of them of essentials and accidents, and the essentials of the essentials and the accidents, and the accidents of each one of them in an exhaustive manner. Then you examine what is predicated of each one, and what each one predicates of something else, negatively or affirmatively. And do not mind whether the relation is natural, like the relation of accident to its subject or vice versa, or like the relation of accident to accident. And according to the increase of investigation into these matters, the benefit increases. And beware of investigating here about the consequent of a consequent: is it a consequent? Or about the antecedent of an antecedent: is it an antecedent? For that is employing what is already known. Or that you occupy yourself with investigating what attaches to the two terms, unless its relation to them differs in necessity or possibility or other than that which we have verified in the second figure. Or that you occupy yourself with what does not attach to them, due to its uselessness as has passed. Or that you seek opposition between the accidences of the two terms, or difference between them, in order to base upon it the negation of one term from the other, as some may imagine. And it is as it is said: 'Fire is hot and water is cold', or 'This is an animal and this is inanimate', for the middle term must be one, and were it not that what is hot is not cold and what is cold is not hot, and that what is animal is not inanimate.

Commentary
This chapter outlines a method for discovering the premises needed for a syllogism that yields a desired conclusion. The author begins by stating that after explaining the types of syllogisms, it is now time to show how to obtain their premises in a universal way, applicable to demonstrative, rhetorical, dialectical, and other types. This is important because knowing the theory of syllogism is useless without knowing how to construct one. The author then divides assertoric concepts (meanings that can be affirmed or denied) into two categories: self-evident (badihiyya) and theoretical (nazariyya). Self-evident propositions need no acquisition; they are immediately known. Theoretical propositions require a method. The method is as follows: first, take the desired conclusion (e.g., 'A is B'). Then examine the two terms (A and B) and their definitions (hadd). Next, exhaustively list all attributes that attach to each term, including essential attributes (dhatiyyat) and accidental attributes (aradiyyat), and also the attributes of those attributes. Then consider what is predicated of each term and what each term predicates of something else, whether negatively or affirmatively. The author advises not to be concerned whether the relation is natural (e.g., accident to subject) or accidental (accident to accident). The more exhaustive the search, the greater the benefit. He warns against certain unproductive lines of inquiry: investigating whether the consequent of a consequent is a consequent (since that is already known), investigating attributes that attach to the terms unless their relation differs in modality, investigating useless attributes, or seeking opposition or difference simply to negate one term from another. The latter is illustrated with an example: 'fire is hot and water is cold' or 'this is an animal and this is inanimate' – such oppositions cannot serve as middle terms because a middle term must be one and the same for both terms; the fact that hot is not cold does not provide a middle term linking fire and water. Thus, the method emphasizes finding a common attribute that relates to both terms in a productive way.

The Method of Exhaustive Search for Middle Terms
Author describes the method for acquiring premises for syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the author's method for acquiring theoretical premises: posit the conclusion, exhaustively examine the attributes of its terms, and select a valid middle term.

Core Idea
To find a middle term, you must know everything about your terms.

Once you identify a proposition as theoretical, the author provides a detailed procedure. This lesson walks you through that procedure step by step, showing how to inspect the terms of the desired conclusion, list all their essential and accidental attributes, and then find a common term that can serve as the middle link in a syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Posit the desired conclusion
Start with what you want to prove, e.g., 'A is B'. This gives you the two terms A and B.
The author says: 'first you posit one of the desired [conclusions]'.
2
Examine the definition of each term
For each term, look at its hadd (definition). This gives you the essential attributes (dhatiyyat). Also consider accidents (aradiyyat) and attributes of attributes.
3
List what is predicated of each term and what each term predicates
Consider all predicates that can be affirmed or denied of A and B. Do this exhaustively, whether the relation is natural (accident to subject) or accidental (accident to accident).
The author advises not to mind the type of relation.
4
Search for a common middle term
Look for an attribute that relates to both A and B in a way that allows a syllogism. The middle term must be one and the same. Avoid pitfalls: do not chase consequents of consequents, do not seek mere opposition or difference.
