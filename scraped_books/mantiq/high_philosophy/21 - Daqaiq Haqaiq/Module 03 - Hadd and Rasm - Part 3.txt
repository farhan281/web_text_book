Four Common Errors: Part, Differentia, Subject, Matter as Genus
Author enumerates six types of defective definitions related to genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish four erroneous definitions where a part, differentia, subject, or matter is mistakenly taken as the genus.

Core Idea
A true genus is essential and constitutive, not a mere part, accident, or material substrate.

The author of Daqā’iq al-Ḥaqā’iq enumerates six defective definitions; the first four involve substituting something other than the true genus for the genus. Mastering these errors equips you to critique definitions in Islamic sciences, such as Fiqh and Kalām, where precision is paramount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and proposed genus
Read the definition and locate the term being defined (definiendum) and the term being offered as its genus.
The genus is usually a broader category predicated on the thing.
2
Check essential predication
Is the proposed genus said of the definiendum in its very essence? Or is it only a part, a differentia, a subject, or matter?
A true genus is ‘predicated on the essence absolutely’, as the text states.
3
Classify the error
Determine whether the mistaken element is a part (juz’), differentia (faṣl), subject (mawḍū‘), or matter (māddah) using the author’s examples.
For instance, ‘body’ is a part of man; ‘power’ is a differentia of assent; ‘burnt wood’ is a subject; ‘wood’ is matter.
4
Explain why it fails
Articulate why the proposed element does not meet the criteria for a genus: it is not universal, not essential, or not constitutive in reality.
This step builds your ability to evaluate real definitions.

Commentary
This passage enumerates six types of defective definitions that involve mistakenly treating something other than the true genus as the genus. The author begins by noting that a genus must be predicated on the thing's essence absolutely, not on a part or an accident. The first error is taking a part as the genus: like 'body' is a part of man, but not his genus, because body is predicated on man only in a certain respect, not essentially. The second error is taking the differentia as the genus: e.g., defining 'assent' as 'power of opinion' – that is confusing the differentia (power) with the genus. The third error is taking the subject (whether generated or corrupting) as the genus for an accident: the genus must be constitutive in reality and existence, but a subject is only constitutive in existence, not in reality. For example, eternal life is a necessary accident of immortality, not its genus. The fourth error is taking matter as the genus: e.g., 'bed is wood' – wood is the matter, not the genus; the distinction was explained earlier. The fifth error is using a genus whose species are distinguished by accidents, not by essential differentiae, like dividing man into young/old – those are accidents, not species. The sixth error is using a genus that admits intensity/weakness while the species does not, or vice versa, like making quality the genus of quantity. All these show violations of the rules of proper definition, where the genus must be essential and constitutive.

Errors in Genus-Species Relations: Accidents and Intensity
Author enumerates six types of defective definitions related to genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to detect definitions where the genus-species relationship is flawed due to accidental distinctions or variations in intensity.

Core Idea
True species differ by essential differentiae, not by accidents or degrees.

The last two errors in the author's list concern the breakdown of the genus-species relation itself. A genus must differentiate its species by essential differentiae; if the supposed species differ only by accidents (e.g., young vs. old) or by intensity/weakness (e.g., quality and quantity), the definition fails. These errors remind us that logical classification mirrors real essences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the supposed genus and its species
Find the term claimed as genus and the items presented as its species.
E.g., 'man' supposedly divided into 'young', 'old', etc.
2
Check the differentia
Are the species distinguished by an essential differentia (faṣl) or only by accidents (ʿawāriḍ)?
Accidents like age, color, or shape do not constitute species differences.
3
Check for intensity/weakness
Does the genus admit variation in degree while the species does not, or vice versa? This signals a category mistake.
E.g., 'knowledge' as a genus for 'certain' and 'uncertain' knowledge: certainty is a degree, not a species.
4
Explain the error
State why the supposed genus-species relationship violates the rules of definition.
The author stresses that a true genus is constitutive and its species are essentially distinct.

Defects from Contrariety in Definitions
Author lists contrariety-based defects in definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify definitions that are defective due to contrariety between genus and species.

Core Idea
Contrariety defects occur when genus and species misalign in their contraries.

The author opens the chapter on defective definitions with errors from contrariety. He specifies two scenarios: either the genus has a contrary but the species does not, or both have contraries but the genus has an intermediate while the species lacks one. Understanding these conditions sharpens your ability to spot logical flaws in definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus and species
In the definition, locate the broader category (genus) and the narrower (species).
2
Check if the genus has a contrary
Determine if the genus has a logical opposite (e.g., substance/accident).
contrary = something that cannot coexist in the same subject.
3
Check if the species has a contrary
Does the species itself have a contrary? If not, the definition is defective when the genus does.
4
Examine the presence of a middle term
If both have contraries, see if there is an intermediate between the genus and its contrary, but not between the species and its contrary.
The author says the genus may have a middle while the species does not.

A defective differentia can be omitted, accidental, or cross-categorial.

The author now turns to errors specific to the differentia (faṣl). He distinguishes unique errors from those shared with property (khāṣṣa). Key errors include omitting a necessary differentia, using an accidental trait, and mixing categories. He then addresses a subtle objection about quantity/quality as differentiae for relatives.

Identify the differentia in the definition

E.g., laughter for humans is accidental.

Detecting Contradiction Errors in Definitions
Opens chapter on invalid definitions, first error type: contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify errors in definitions caused by contradiction between genus and species regarding contraries and intermediaries.

Core Idea
A definition's validity requires that species mirror the genus in contrary relationships.

This passage opens the second chapter on invalid definitions by focusing on the first error category: contradiction. It teaches us that a definition fails when the logical relationship between a genus and its species is inconsistent with respect to having contraries and intermediaries. By mastering these patterns, you can evaluate whether a definition is sound by checking if the species respects the same contrary structure as its genus.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish genus and species
In any definition, identify the broader category (genus) and the narrower category (species). For example, in defining 'wine' as 'intoxicant drink', the genus is 'drink' and the species is 'wine'.
2
Check if the genus has a contrary
Determine whether the genus has a known opposite (contrary). For 'drink', the contrary might be 'food' or 'non-drink'. If the genus has no contrary, this pattern does not apply.
A contrary is a term that is opposite in meaning, such as 'hot' and 'cold'.
3
Check if the species has a contrary
Now check the species. If the genus has a contrary but the species does not, the definition is flawed because the species should inherit the property of having a contrary from the genus.
For example, 'intoxicant' has a contrary 'non-intoxicant', but 'wine' itself may not have a direct contrary.
4
Check for intermediaries between contrary pairs
If both genus and species have contraries, examine whether there is an intermediate term between the genus and its contrary. If there is an intermediary for the genus but none for the species, the definition is inconsistent.
An intermediary is a term that falls between two contraries, e.g., 'tepid' between 'hot' and 'cold'.
5
Identify the contradiction pattern
Synthesize your findings to determine which of the two patterns described in the passage applies: (1) genus has contrary but species does not, or (2) both have contraries but intermediary exists only for the genus.

False Predication as a Condition for Invalid Differentia and Property
Explains first condition invalidating differentia/property: false predication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposed differentia or property is truly predicated of the entire species by applying the first condition of validity.

Core Idea
A differentia or property must be true of every individual of the species.

In Islamic logic, definitions are built from differentia (fasl) and property (khassa). But not every attribute that seems fitting actually qualifies. This lesson explores the first common factor that invalidates both: when the term used in the definition is not true of the species or part of it. We will walk through the types of false predication and learn to spot them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being proposed as differentia or property
Look at the definition and find the word that is supposed to be the differentia or property. For example, in 'man is the neighing animal', the term is 'neighing'.
The term is often an adjective or a relational noun.
2
Check if the term applies to every individual of the species
Ask: Is this term true of all members of the species? If any individual lacks it, the term fails. 'Neighing' is not true of any man, so it fails completely.
3
Distinguish between absolute falsehood and partial falsehood
The term may be completely false of the species (like 'neighing' for man) or only true of some but not all (like 'never erring' for scholars). Both are invalid.
Partial falsehood makes the term more specific than the species.
4
Consider if the term is a differentia or property of a coordinate or subordinate species
Even if true of all individuals, the term might belong to a different species (e.g., 'rational' is true of man but is the differentia of his species, not of the genus animal).
Coextensiveness alone is not enough; the term must be proper to the species being defined.

Commentary
This passage begins a discussion of the factors that jointly invalidate the two main components of definitions: the differentia (fasl) and the property (khassa). The author first identifies a common factor: the term used in the definition fails to be true of the species or part of it. This falsehood can be of several types. First, the term is not true of the defined item at all, either because it is a differentia or property of a coordinate species (e.g., defining man as a 'neighing' animal, which is proper to the horse), or because it is conceptually impossible while the species is actual (e.g., defining 'place' as 'dimensions in no matter,' which contradicts the existence of place). Second, the term is presented as existing in something but does not actually inhere in it, as in defining 'sleep' as 'weakness of sense'; if sleep were weakness of sense, then the sense itself would be the subject of sleep, which is absurd. Third, the term is true of some individuals of the species but not all, making it more specific than the species. For example, defining 'scholars' as 'those who never err' fails because some scholars do err; unless the definition is restricted to their knowledge, in which case it becomes a property of some scholars, not the entire species. Fourth, the term might be a species of the defined thing (e.g., defining 'animal' as 'man'), or a property or differentia of a species subordinate to the defined thing (e.g., defining 'animal' as 'rational' or 'laughing'), even if true of the species—these are not genuine differentiae or properties of the species itself. The passage thus establishes that truthfulness alone does not suffice; the term must also be coextensive and systematically appropriate.

Invalid Differentia and Property: Generality and Predication on Genus
Author lists conditions of being more general or being predicated of the genus, with detailed analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the two conditions that invalidate a proposed differentia or property: being more general (essentially or accidentally) and being predicated on the genus (or vice versa), and distinguish between predication of the simple nature and the compound description.

Core Idea
A valid differentia must be more specific than the species and not predicable of the genus in its simple nature.

In the previous lesson, we learned how to test a definition by checking if the differentia or property is too broad or too narrow. Now, the author presents two additional criteria: generality and predication relationship with the genus. These criteria help us identify false differentiae and properties that might seem plausible but fail upon closer logical inspection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main criteria
Look for the two conditions listed in the passage: (a) the term being more general than the definiendum, and (b) the term being predicated of the genus or genus predicated of it.
2
Distinguish essential vs accidental generality
Note the examples: essential generality (malaka for virtue) vs accidental generality (power of walking for man).
3
Understand the predication condition
Analyze how predication on the genus or of the genus on the simple nature invalidates the differentia or property.
4
Apply the distinction between simple and compound
Grasp why 'animal' is not predicated of 'rational' but is predicated of 'the rational'; this is the key subtlety.
5
Test your understanding
Use the conditions to evaluate sample definitions.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of how to identify invalid differentiae and properties by listing additional criteria. The author presents two main ways. First, the term used may be more general than the species. This generality can be essential (dhāti), as when 'disposition' (malaka) is used to define virtue—'disposition' is a genus of virtue, not a differentia or property. Or it can be accidental ('araḍī), as when 'power of walking and acceptance of knowledge' is used to define man; these attributes are accidental to man and also apply to horse and angel respectively, showing they are not unique to man. Second, the term may be predicated of the genus, or the genus may be predicated of it. The first sub-case—the term being predicated of the genus—is relatively obvious: if the term is a predicate of the genus and is more general or equal to the genus, it cannot be a differentia or property of the species under that genus, because a differentia must be more specific than the species. The second sub-case—the genus being predicated of the term—is more subtle. The author explains that the genus cannot be predicated of the simple nature of the differentia or property (e.g., 'animal' cannot be said of 'rational') but can be predicated of the compound term that describes the subject (e.g., 'animal' can be said of 'the rational' meaning a rational being). This distinction is crucial: 'animal' is not predicated of the nature of rationality itself, but of the being who is rational. The passage delves into a hypothetical: if the genus were predicated of the nature of the differentia (e.g., if 'animal' applied to the nature of 'rational'), then 'rational' would become a species of animal, which would require its own differentia, leading to an infinite regress. Hence, to avoid such confusion, the genus must not be directly predicated of the simple differentia or property. This careful analysis refines the conditions for a valid definition.

Three Invalidating Conditions for Differentiae and Properties
Author presents three more invalidating factors: temporal priority, subject-predicate reversal, and name-specificity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposed differentia or property is invalid due to temporal priority, subject-predicate reversal, or confinement to a name without its synonym.

Core Idea
A valid differentia or property must be timeless, correctly placed, and apply to all synonyms.

We have already seen several conditions that invalidate a proposed differentia or property. The author now adds three more subtle pitfalls: temporal priority (the definiens exists before the species), logical role reversal (the definiendum becomes the definiens), and the mistake of attaching the definition to a mere name while ignoring its synonym. Mastering these will sharpen your ability to critique and construct sound definitions.

Guided Path
1
Check for temporal priority
Examine whether the proposed differentia or property exists before the species or its genus. For example, 'growing' (al-nāmī) predates humans, so it cannot be a differentia of 'man'. If the definiens applies to things that are temporally prior, it is invalid.
The differentia should be coextensive and simultaneous with the species, not prior.
2
Check for subject-predicate reversal
Verify that the term meant to distinguish is not actually the subject (what is being defined) rather than the predicate. In 'fire is the property of the subtlest bodies', the relationship is reversed: 'the subtlest bodies' should be the differentia or property of fire, not the other way around. The differentia must logically describe the species, not be described by it.
The differentia should be a predicate (maḥmūl) of the species, not its subject.
3
Check for name-specificity ignoring synonyms
If a differentia or property is assigned to a name like 'insān' (man) but not to its synonym 'bashar', then it is not a true differentia of the species but only attached to a verbal label. True definitions target the meaning (ma'nā), not the word. Ensure that all synonyms of the species receive the same definition.
Synonyms must be treated identically; otherwise the definition is accidental and invalid.

Commentary
This passage concludes the enumeration of invalidating conditions for differentiae and properties by listing three more distinct factors. First, the term may be prior in existence to the species or its genus. The example given is 'the growing thing' (al-nāmī) in relation to man. The idea is that the differentia or property should not be something temporally antecedent to the species; the species (man) exists only after growth, so 'growing' cannot be a differentia or property of man because it applies to things before man exists. Second, the term intended to distinguish may actually be the specific thing itself, and the logical requirement is that it be a subject, not a predicate. The author illustrates this with a misdefinition: if one makes 'fire' the property or differentia of 'the subtlest bodies', the relationship is reversed; rather, 'the subtlest bodies' should be the differentia or property of fire, as fire is the subject. This condition ensures that the defining term plays the correct logical role. Third, the term may be a differentia or property for a specific name but not for its synonym. For example, one might define 'insān' (man) with 'nāṭiq' (rational) as differentia and 'ḍāḥik' (laughing) as property, but refuse to apply these same definitions to 'bashar' (another word for man). Since 'insān' and 'bashar' are synonymous, the definition should cover both names; if it does not, the term is not a genuine differentia or property of the species but merely attached to a particular verbal label. This highlights that definitions must target the conceptual species, not the arbitrary name.

Genus Cannot Predicate on Differentia's Nature
Author explains the impossibility of predicating genus on the nature of differentia/property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the error of predicating genus on the simple nature of a differentia or property and distinguish it from valid predication on the composite subject.

Core Idea
Genus cannot be predicated on the differentia itself, only on the subject described by it.

The author clarifies a subtle logical point: saying 'speaking is an animal' is false, but 'the speaking being is an animal' is true. This distinction is crucial for constructing valid definitions. Understanding this prevents a common error in logical definitions.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish simple from composite terms
Notice that 'speech' (natiq) as a simple differentia is not a species of animal, but 'speaker' (natiiq) as a composite (the thing that speaks) is. Identify which terms are simple (the attribute itself) and which are composite (the thing described).
The simple differentia is the nature of the attribute, while the composite refers to the subject bearing that attribute.
2
Apply the predication test
Try predicating the genus 'animal' on the simple differentia 'speech': 'Speech is an animal' is false. Then try on the composite 'speaker': 'The speaker is an animal' is true. The difference lies in what is being predicated on: the nature of the differentia vs. the subject described by it.
3
Generalize to property (khassa)
Apply the same test to property: 'Laughter is an animal' is false, but 'the laugher is an animal' is true. The error occurs only when predication targets the simple nature of the differentia or property, not the composite.
This principle holds for any differentia or property.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of invalid definitions, specifically focusing on errors in the application of genus, differentia, and property. The author begins by explaining a key logical principle: genus cannot be predicated on the nature of a differentia or property. To illustrate, he contrasts the simple terms (like 'speech' or 'laughter') with the composite terms (like 'speaker' or 'laugher'). Saying 'speech is an animal' is false, but 'the speaker is an animal' is true because the predicate 'animal' applies to the person described by 'speaker', not to the attribute of speaking itself. The author then lists several types of invalid definitions: (1) using an attribute that is logically prior in existence to the species (e.g., 'growing' as a differentia for human, since growth is common to plants); (2) confusing the subject and predicate in a definition (e.g., making 'fire' a property of 'the most subtle bodies' when it should be the opposite); (3) defining a name without its synonym (e.g., using 'speaker' for human but not for the synonym 'humanity'); (4) using an attribute that is not proper (e.g., 'medicine' or 'astronomy' as differentia for human, since these are acquired, not essential); (5) using the defined term itself (e.g., defining human by 'humanity'); (6) using an attribute that would belong to another if the original subject were absent (e.g., calling fire 'most subtle' because if fire were absent, air would be most subtle); (7) using an attribute whose opposite belongs to the opposite species (e.g., if 'opposite' is a property of the species, then its contradictory cannot be a property of the opposite). A valid definition must use attributes that are neither prior, reversed, nor synonym-based.

Invalid Definitions: Non-proper Attributes, Circularity, Conditionality, Contradiction
Author lists four more invalid definition types: non-proper, circular, conditional, and contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify four more types of invalid definitions: using non-proper attributes, circular definitions, conditional properties, and contradictory opposites.

Core Idea
A definition must use proper, non-circular, unconditional, and non-contradictory attributes.

The author concludes his list of invalid definitions with four more errors: (1) using an attribute that is not a true differentia or property (like 'medicine' for humans), (2) circularity where the definiendum itself is used, (3) using an attribute that would belong to another if the original subject were absent (conditional property), and (4) using an attribute whose opposite is a property of the opposite species. These errors undermine the logical integrity of the definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify non-proper attributes
The author says using 'the science of medicine' or 'astronomy' as a differentia or property for human is invalid because these are acquired, not essential or exclusive. They are not proper attributes.
A proper attribute (khassa) must be coextensive and essential in the sense of being inseparable from the species.
2
Detect circularity (definiendum in definiens)
If someone defines 'human' using 'humanity' or its definition, it is circular. The definiens should not contain the definiendum or its synonym. Example: 'Human is the humanity-possessing being' is invalid.
Circular definitions provide no new information.
3
Analyze conditional properties
The author warns against using an attribute that would belong to another if the original subject were absent. Example: calling fire 'most subtle bodies' because if fire were absent, air would be most subtle. This shows the attribute is not truly exclusive to fire.
The attribute must be permanently and exclusively attached to the species.
4
Spot contradictory opposites
If the opposite (naqid) of an attribute is a property of the opposite species, then using that attribute as a property is invalid because it would lead to a contradiction. For example, if 'opposite' is a property of 'black', then 'white' cannot be a property of 'black'.
The definition must avoid logical incompatibility.

Author explains errors in properties regarding temporal and potential modality.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A property must be qualified as primary, natural, or potential to avoid false universality.

In this passage, the author explains that a valid property must be either primary (first) by nature or potential. If a property is taken absolutely (without such qualification), it may be false because exceptions exist. For example, 'bipedal' as a property of humans must be understood as natural, not always actual, since some humans lack legs. Similarly, 'writer' is a potential property. This lesson teaches you to check the modality of a claimed property before accepting it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimed property
Look at the definiendum (the species) and the attribute claimed as its property (khassa). For example, 'bodies' and 'occurrence in the extension of pointing'.
2

3
Check if it is by nature or potential
If the property is not always actual, it must be understood as natural (bi al-tab‘) or potential (bi al-quwwa). For humans, 'bipedal' is natural; 'writer' is potential.
If taken absolutely, exceptions (e.g., amputees, non-writers) would falsify it.
4

Ensure that the property is presented with the correct modality. If it is primary, natural, or potential, it can be taken as a property; otherwise, it is an error.

Core Idea

Exclusivity and Primacy of Property
Author explains conditions of exclusivity and primary nature/potentiality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a proposed property is truly exclusive to a species and whether it is primary by nature or potentiality.

Core Idea
A property must be attached to the species in all modes (actual, natural, potential) and not transferable to another species.

The author now addresses deeper conditions: the property must be unique to the species even when the species is absent, and it must be primary—either by nature (bi-l-ṭabʿ) or by potentiality (bi-l-quwwa)—not merely accidental. The examples of fire, bodies, and man illustrate this.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposed property and its subject
E.g., 'most subtle body' is proposed as a property of 'fire'. Note the subject (fire) and the property.
2
Test exclusivity by counterfactual
Imagine the subject (fire) is absent. Would the property then apply to something else? If yes, it fails. Example: if fire vanished, 'most subtle' would shift to air, so it's not exclusive.
3
Check if the property is primary or accidental
A primary property belongs to the species by its nature or potentiality. 'Two-legged' is by nature (but not always actual), 'writer' is by potentiality. They are considered absolute if taken in that sense.
4
Determine the mode of attribution
If the property is attributed 'firstly' (awwal) or 'by nature', it can be taken absolutely. If accidental, it would not be a proper khāṣṣa.

Correlative and Composite Conditions
Author explains conditions for correlative and composite properties.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to verify that the correlative of a property matches the correlative of the species and that composite properties align with their simple parts.

Core Idea
Properties that involve relations or composition must mirror the relational structure of the species.

The final set of conditions deals with correlative terms (like 'double' and 'half') and composite properties (like 'laughing'). The author shows that the opposite or correlative of the property must correspond to the opposite or correlative of the species, and composite properties must be decomposed correctly. These are subtle but crucial for precise definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the property involves a correlative (muḍāyif)
E.g., 'more' is correlative to 'double'. Check the correlative of the species: 'half' for 'double'. The correlative of the property ('less') must map correctly.
2
Test the opposite condition
If the property has an opposite, say 'best' for 'justice', then the opposite of the property ('worst') should be a property of the opposite of the species ('injustice'). Otherwise, the property is not properly aligned.
3
For composite properties, separate simple parts
Take a composite property like 'laughing' (ḍāḥik). Its simple part is 'laughter' (ḍaḥk). Check: is laughter a property of man? No, because we say 'man is laughing' not 'man is laughter'. So the simple part must be a property of the species' corresponding part.
4
Verify that the composite property is not a property of the simple part
If 'laughing' were a property of 'laughter', then the property would be circular. Ensure the attribution is correct: the composite property belongs to the species, not to its simple component.

Testing Special Properties via Absence
Condition for detecting defective definitions via absence of property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a proposed specific property is valid by checking the condition of absence: if the absence of the property does not imply the absence of the species, the property is invalid.

Core Idea
A valid specific property requires that its absence entails the absence of the species, not the reverse.

Definitions can be defective when they rely on properties that are not truly specific to the species. The author gives a logical test: the absence of the property must be unique to the absence of the species. He illustrates this with the example of 'sensation' as a property of sight—its absence in stones does not make them blind, so it fails. This lesson will equip you to identify such defective definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposed property and species
Locate the candidate specific property (خاصة) and the species (نوع) it supposedly defines. In the passage, 'sensation' is proposed for sight, and 'blindness' is the absence of sight.
2
State the condition of absence
The key condition: if the property is truly specific, then whenever it is absent, the species must be absent. Formally: absence of property → absence of species.
That is, 'no sensation' should imply 'no sight' (blindness).
3
Find a counterexample
Look for a case where the property is absent but the species is present. The author uses stones: they lack sensation but are not blind. So the condition fails.
This disproves the claim that sensation is a specific property of sight.
4
Conclude on validity
If such a counterexample exists, the property cannot be a true specific property. The proposed definition is defective.
The author also discusses two interpretations of 'absence' and why both are impossible.

Condition for a Composite Property to Be Specific
Explains a condition for composite property specificity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a composite property (khassa murakkaba) is specific to a species and apply them to examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
A composite property's simple part must be specific to the simple substrate, not directly predicated.

In Islamic logic, a property (khassa) is a non-essential attribute coextensive with a species. When the property is composite, like 'laughing' (al-dahik) which combines laughter and a relation of possession, special care is needed to ensure it truly specifies the species. This passage outlines a precise condition for such composite properties to guard against faulty definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the composite property and its simple part
Look for a property that consists of a simple attribute plus a relation. For example, 'laughing' contains the simple part 'laughter' and a relation of being a possessor.
In Arabic, composite properties are often verbal forms like 'ḍāḥik' (laughing).
2
Check if the simple part is a property of the species' simple substrate
Determine whether the simple attribute (e.g., laughter) is a property of the species' essence (e.g., human qua human). It must be coextensive with the species but not part of its definition.
Laughter is specific to humans among animals, so it qualifies.
3
Verify that the simple part is not directly predicated on the species
We should not say 'the human is laughter' but rather 'the human is a laugher'. The simple part is possessed, not identical to the species.
Direct predication would imply the attribute is essential, which it is not.
4
Ensure the composite property is not specific to the simple part
The composite 'laughing' must not be a property of 'laughter' itself. Otherwise laughter would be the species and human would be merely a possessor of laughter.
This prevents circularity in defining the species.

The Condition of Absence Specificity for Properties
States condition on absence of property being specific to absence of species

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether a candidate property has a truly specific absence by distinguishing between absolute negation and privation.

Core Idea
A property's absence must be privative, not merely negative, to be specific to the species.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a property (khassa) must be coextensive with its species. Here, the author adds a further condition: the absence of the property must also be specific to the absence of the species. This prevents false properties like 'sensation' from being considered a property of sight, because its absence is not unique to blindness. To understand why, we need to distinguish two types of absence: simple negation and privation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the candidate property and its species
Sensation is proposed as a property of sight. The species is 'sight' and its absence is 'blindness'. Note the author's analysis.
2
Distinguish two interpretations of 'absence of property'
The absence could be absolute negation (salb) or privation ('adam al-malaka). Absolute negation applies to anything lacking the attribute, while privation only applies to a subject that could naturally possess it.
3
Apply the condition to absolute negation
If absence means absolute negation, then sensation is absent from stones, but stones are not blind. So the absence is not specific to blindness, failing the condition.
4
Apply the condition to privation
If absence means privation, then it requires a subject capable of sensation. Blindness is not capable of sensation, so the absence of sensation cannot be conceived for blindness. Thus the condition fails again.
5
Conclude why sensation is not a true property of sight
The condition of specificity is impossible to satisfy for this example, showing that sensation is not a property of sight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an indefinite proposition (muhmala) and explain why its truth value is logically ambiguous between universal and particular.

Core Idea
A proposition without a quantifier is logically ambiguous—it could be universal or particular.

In Islamic logic, propositions are classified by quantity. The indefinite proposition (muhmala) has a universal subject but lacks a quantifier, making its logical force unclear. Classical logicians insisted that such a proposition must be understood as potentially universal, not truncated to particular. This lesson clarifies that distinction and its implications for reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the subject type
Check whether the subject term is universal (kulli) in meaning. In a muhmala, the subject is a general noun not limited by a quantifier.
Example: 'Man is mortal' – the subject 'man' is universal.
2
Identify the absence of quantifier
Look for any quantifier like 'every', 'some', 'all', 'no'. If none, the proposition is indefinite.
Quantifiers make the quantity explicit; their absence leaves it ambiguous.
3
Consider possible interpretations
The same sentence can be read as universal ('Every man is mortal') or particular ('Some men are mortal'). Both are valid interpretations.
The logical context often determines which reading is intended.
4
Understand the logical consequence
If the indefinite were taken as only particular, then universal propositions could never be true because a universal implies the particular. Since universals are true, the indefinite must at least be compatible with a universal reading.
Thus, the indefinite is not equivalent to the particular; it is broader.

Definition of Contradiction and Conditions for Genuine Contradiction
Author defines contradiction and lists conditions for genuine contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a pair of propositions is a genuine contradiction by checking the conditions of identity of subject/predicate, quality difference, and essential entailment.

Core Idea
A genuine contradiction requires the truth of one to entail the falsehood of the other by essence, not accidentally.

This passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq defines the fundamental logical relation of contradiction (tanāquḍ). The author distinguishes genuine contradiction from pairs that merely appear contradictory due to accidental factors or ambiguous terms. Understanding this is critical for avoiding fallacies in argumentation, especially in theological and juristic debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Find the pair of propositions under consideration. Write them out clearly, noting their quality (affirmative/negative) and quantity (universal/particular).
In the text, the author uses examples: 'No human is a stone' and 'Some stone is a human'.
2
Check identity of subject and predicate
Ensure that the subject term refers to the same thing in both propositions and the predicate is the same concept. If the subject is ambiguous (homonymous), the pair may not be contradictory.
Example: 'eye' can mean the organ or a spring; the propositions can both be true.
3
Check quality difference
One proposition must be affirmative, the other negative. If both are affirmative or both negative, they cannot be contradictories.
The author states contradiction is 'difference in quality'.
4
Check essential entailment
Determine if the truth of one proposition necessarily implies the falsehood of the other by its own nature (li-dhātihā), not by accident or through conversion.
The author rejects pairs like 'No human is a stone' and 'Some stone is a human' because the entailment goes through conversion of the actual contradictory.
5
Beware of homonymy
If the subject term is ambiguous (ism mushtarak), the pair may not be genuine contradictories because the subjects differ in meaning, allowing both true or both false.
The author uses the example of 'al-‘ayn' to illustrate this pitfall.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not every definition is a complete real definition; some are descriptions or verbal clarifications.

This passage also provides a detailed classification of explanatory statements (qawl sharh), which are used to acquire concepts. The author lists five types, from the complete real definition down to the corrupt definition that gives no benefit. Understanding this classification helps you evaluate the quality of definitions used in Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Recall the role of qawl sharh

For instance, defining 'human' as 'rational animal' is a hadd haqiqi tamm (genus: animal, differentia: rational). Defining it as 'laughing animal' would be a rasm (laughter is accidental). A verbal definition explains the word, e.g., 'human means insan'.
