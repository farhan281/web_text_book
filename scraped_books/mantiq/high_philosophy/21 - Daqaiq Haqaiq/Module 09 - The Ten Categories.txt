Graded Equivocation (Tashkik) and Its Misidentification
Author defines tashkik and distinguishes it from false tashkik.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between graded (mushakkik) and univocal (mutawāṭi') terms, and correctly diagnose whether variation in application is internal or external to the meaning.

Core Idea
Graded terms vary in intensity; univocal terms do not, even if their referents differ in priority.

In this passage, the author defines the second type of term relation: tashkik (graded equivocation), exemplified by terms like 'white' applied to different shades. He then warns against mistakenly classifying terms like 'existent' or 'substance' as graded when in fact they are univocal. We will extract the diagnostic criteria for identifying true gradation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tashkik
Find the Arabic text where the author says 'وكتناول الأبيض للمتلونات المختلفة في شدة البياض وضعفه' – the term 'white' applies to different things but with varying intensity.
2
Contrast with mutawāṭi' (univocal)
The author implies that univocal terms have no variation in the meaning; they apply equally to all instances.
Univocal terms are like 'animal' applied to different species – no difference in animality.
3
Analyze the 'existent' case
The author argues that 'existent' applied to simple and composite substances appears graded due to priority, but the variation is external (e.g., temporal, causal) and not within the meaning of existence itself.
The substantiality of the composite is not greater than that of its parts; thus no intensity.
4
Formulate the rule
For a term to be graded, the variation must be in the very meaning signified by the word, not in accompanying attributes.
Compare with 'hot' – the intensity is part of the meaning of hotness.

As for the second [type], it is like the term 'white' covering different colored things varying in intensity and weakness of whiteness, and like the term 'hot' and 'cold' covering things varying in intensity and weakness of heat and cold. Sometimes doubt occurs that something is graded (mushakkak) when it is not, as in applying the term 'existent' to simple substance and its composite, and applying the term 'substance' to its subordinate types, and likewise for every genus upon its types due to priority and posteriority among them. That is nothing but heedlessness of the basis of the variation. He who investigates thoroughly knows that the variation between simple and composite substances is in something external to the meaning of 'existent' and 'substance', and that no substance is more intense in substantiality than another nor more worthy of it, and what the composite has of existence or substantiality is not additional to the existence and substantiality of its parts; rather the existence and substantiality of the parts is the very existence and substantiality of the composite. So there is no priority, no superiority, no intensity, and no gradation. What is found of substances preceding another substance—whether in time, place, honor, nature, or causality—that is precedence in something external to precedence in deserving existence and substantiality. Gradation must be due to variation in what is signified by the word, not in something else. If a word's application to things is not based on a single meaning shared between them, it is called 'equivocal' (mushtarak), like the word 'ayn' applying to a water spring, the seeing organ, and gold and the like. Examining the causes that necessitate it is not incumbent upon the logician qua logician. If we delve into that following the practice of the ancients, the most likely is that it is only due to different positors—that what one group placed for a meaning, another group placed for that same meaning—or due to remote resemblance, as in applying the word 'ayn' to gold and the seeing organ, either due to their resemblance in luster or otherwise, or the intention of seeking blessing by naming one thing with the name of another, as in naming some later persons after some earlier prophets and messengers; not because of what is said: that since named things are infinite while the syllables composing words are finite, it necessitates including many named things under one word. For one may say: I do not concede that the types of named things are infinite, even if individuals are infinite—that is not self-evident, and the theoretical requires a proof. And even if they were infinite, the placing of names for their named things is only by the positor's placement and intention of the name toward the named, and what he intended to name is finite. Hence if we wanted to place for the meanings of equivocal names a separate name for each, that would not be impossible for us due to their being necessarily finite. Moreover, what is composed from simple syllables, both long and short, is also infinite, just as named things are infinite even if the simples are finite. And the consideration of the increased difficulty in positing with increased memorization of names is a harm less than the harm of missing their purpose of understanding through placing equivocal expressions. After this exposition and verification of what is most plausible as the cause of placing equivocal terms, sometimes names are thought to be equivocal when they are univocal, and sometimes names are thought to be univocal when they are equivocal. As for the first: as in our saying 'principle' (mabda') for the point relative to the line, and 'principle' for the instant relative to time. The suspicion of equivocation arises here due to the difference in the subjects of the relation—line and time—but that has no effect, for applying the name 'principle' to the point is only because it is the first thing, likewise for the instant without regard to what that thing is, and likewise for everything called 'principle'. It is clear that there is no variation or difference, so it is univocal. As for the second: as in applying the name 'khamri' (wine-related) to grapes, the color, and the medicine. Although one might think it univocal due to the unity of that to which they are related—wine—it is only due to multiple different aspects. For the meaning of 'khamri' is not wine itself, otherwise the relative would be the same as the relatand, which is impossible. Nor is it the very essence of the subject that is related, otherwise that would hold for it without regard to the relation to wine. So it is just the thing that is described by what has come to it from its relation to wine. Necessarily, what has come to each of these named things from its relation to wine—so that it is called 'khamri' accordingly—is different from what has come to the other. For the relation of grapes to it is that it will become it and will come from it; the relation of the color to it is that its color is like its color; the relation of the medicine to it is that it may intoxicate like wine, or because wine is an ingredient in it, or for another reason. When what is signified by the word differs, there is no univocity—unless one makes the basis of applying 'khamri' to it simply its relation to wine without regard [to the specific ground].

Commentary
This passage from Daqaiq Haqaiq provides a detailed classification of how simple terms relate to their meanings. The author begins by defining the second type of term relation—tashkik (graded equivocation)—using examples like 'white' applied to varying shades and 'hot/cold' applied to differing intensities. This is contrasted with pure equivocation (ishtirak) where a term applies to completely different meanings with no shared core, like 'ayn' (eye/spring/gold). The author then addresses a common mistake: thinking that terms like 'existent' applied to simple and composite substances are graded, when in fact the variation in those cases is external to the meaning itself—for example, the substantiality of a composite is not greater than that of its parts, so there is no real gradation. Next, the author discusses the causes of equivocation, noting that the logician is not required to investigate them, but following the ancients, the main causes are: different positors coincidentally assigning the same word to different meanings, remote resemblance, and seeking blessing through naming later things after earlier ones. The author refutes the argument that infinite meanings necessitate equivocation by pointing out that types are finite and the intention of the positor is limited. Finally, the author illustrates two types of misidentification: (1) thinking a univocal term is equivocal, as in 'mabda'' (principle) applied to a point and an instant—the difference in subjects does not affect the single meaning of being 'first'; (2) thinking an equivocal term is univocal, as in 'khamri' (wine-related) applied to grapes, color, and medicine—the relation to wine differs in each case (causation, resemblance, intoxication), so the meanings are distinct. This careful analysis helps the student avoid superficial judgments about term meaning based on irrelevant factors.

Pure Equivocation (Ishtirak) and Misidentification Examples
Author defines ishtirak, explains its causes, and warns against misidentification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify pure equivocation (ishtirak), classify its causes according to the author, and detect false identifications of univocity and equivocity.

Core Idea
A term is equivocal (mushtarak) if it applies to things with no shared core meaning, even if they relate to the same thing in different ways.

This part of the passage defines pure equivocation (mushtarak) and discusses its causes. It then illustrates two errors: thinking a univocal term is equivocal (like 'mabda'), and thinking an equivocal term is univocal (like 'khamri'). We will learn to apply the author's criteria to avoid such mistakes.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of mushtarak
The author says: 'وأما إن كان تناوله لها لا باعتبار معنى متحد بينها فيسمى مشتركا' – a term applied not by virtue of a single shared meaning between the things it names.
This is the classic definition of pure equivocation.
2
Identify the causes of equivocation
The author mentions three causes: different positors coincidentally assigning the same word, remote resemblance (e.g., 'ayn' for eye and gold due to luster), and a later group naming something after an earlier respected being (e.g., prophets).
The logician is not required to investigate these, but the author presents them following the ancients.
3
Refute the necessity argument
The argument that infinite types force equivocation is false: types are finite, the positor's intention is finite, and syllables can also be infinite.
The author gives a dialectical defense against a common skeptical claim.
4
Analyze the 'mabda'' example
Though 'mabda'' applies to a point relative to a line and to an instant relative to time, the meaning is the same: 'first thing'. The difference in subjects does not affect the core meaning, so it is univocal.
This shows how one might mistakenly think a term is equivocal when it is not.
5
Analyze the 'khamri' example
Though all applications relate to wine, the ground of relation differs: grapes (will become wine), color (resembles wine), medicine (causes intoxication). The meaning of 'wine-related' is thus different for each, making it equivocal.
This shows how one might mistakenly think a term is univocal when it is equivocal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the diagonal truth relations (prevention of joint truth or joint falsehood) between any two categorical propositions in the extended square of opposition, including modified and privative forms.

Core Idea
Diagonal relations in the square are not always contradictory; they can prevent joint truth or joint falsehood depending on proposition type.

The square of opposition is a powerful tool for understanding logical relationships between propositions. The classical square includes four corners, but Islamic logicians extended it to include modified and privative propositions. This lesson unpacks the diagonal relations, which often behave differently from the standard contradictories, contraries, and subcontraries. The pattern holds for the diagonal between modified negative and privative affirmative (prevents joint truth), and between modified affirmative and privative negative (prevents joint falsehood). The intermediate case (wasiṭa) allows both to be true or false depending on the relation.

Key Takeaway
The diagonal relations in the extended square of opposition are not uniform: some prevent joint truth, others prevent joint falsehood, depending on the generality of the propositions involved.
