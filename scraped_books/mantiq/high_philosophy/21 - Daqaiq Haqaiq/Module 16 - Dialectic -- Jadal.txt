Real and Apparent Opposition in Dialectical Syllogisms
Author explains types of opposition in dialectical syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real opposition (contrariety/contradiction) and apparent opposition (based on necessary consequent or particular) in premises used for dialectical syllogisms.

Core Idea
Opposing premises can be really contradictory or only apparently so, and only real opposition yields a valid counter-syllogism.

In dialectic (jadal), a disputant aims to refute an opponent by deriving the opposite of what the opponent conceded. This is done by constructing a syllogism from premises that are opposite to the original ones. But not all opposite premises are equally effective. The author distinguishes between real opposition (by contrariety or contradiction) and apparent opposition (based on necessary consequent or particular). Understanding this distinction is crucial for constructing valid refutations.

3
Classify the type of opposition needed
Real opposition is by contrariety (e.g., 'Every A is B' vs 'No A is B') or contradiction ('Every A is B' vs 'Some A is not B'). Apparent opposition uses a necessary consequent or a particular. For instance, 'Every human is an animal' vs 'No rational is an animal' – here 'rational' is a necessary consequent of 'human', so it's not real opposition.

Determine if the opposition is real or apparent
Real opposition directly contradicts the original premise. Apparent opposition shifts the term to a consequent or particular. Only real opposition guarantees a valid counter-syllogism that produces the opposite conclusion.
The author notes that apparent opposition is 'by zann' not haqiqa.

Constructing a Dialectical Syllogism from Opposing Premises
Author explains construction and conditions of dialectical counter-syllogisms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a valid dialectical syllogism that produces the opposite of a conceded conclusion, using either real or apparent opposition, while respecting the restrictions on negative premises and particular terms.

Core Idea
To refute an opponent, take the opposite of one premise, but avoid two negatives and a particular in the first figure.

The author explains that when a disputant concedes a conclusion, the opponent can form a syllogism from opposite premises to derive the opposite conclusion. However, there are restrictions: if the original premises are all affirmative, their opposites are all negative, and a syllogism cannot have two negative premises. Therefore, one can only take the opposite of one premise. This lesson walks through the steps of constructing such a counter-syllogism, considering the figure and the type of opposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original syllogism that led to the conceded conclusion
Suppose an opponent concedes 'Some animals are rational' based on premises: 'Every human is rational' and 'Some animal is human'.
Use Islamic example: e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' and 'Khamr is an intoxicant' yields 'Khamr is forbidden'. The opponent concedes 'Khamr is forbidden'.
2
Determine the type of opposition needed
If you want to derive the opposite conclusion ('Khamr is not forbidden'), you need premises opposite to the original ones. You can take the opposite of one premise (e.g., 'No intoxicant is forbidden') or both.
The author notes that if original premises are both affirmative, their opposites are negative, and you cannot have two negatives.
3
Check the figure of the counter-syllogism
The counter-syllogism can be in any figure, but if it is in the first figure, the opposite taken cannot be a particular premise (because a particular premise cannot serve as a minor premise in the first figure according to the author).
The passage says: 'If it is from the first, then the opposite taken therein is not particular, for it would not be a minor premise; because it ...' (cut off).
4
Construct the counter-syllogism and verify validity
For example, original: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' and 'Khamr is an intoxicant' -> 'Khamr is forbidden'. Opposite: 'No intoxicant is forbidden' and 'Khamr is an intoxicant' -> 'Khamr is not forbidden'. This is a valid first-figure (Barbara) with one negative premise (Celarent).

Distinguishing Demonstrative from Dialectical Syllogism
Distinguishes demonstrative from dialectical syllogisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the goals and permissible strategies of demonstrative (burhani) and dialectical (jadali) syllogisms.

Core Idea
Dialectical argument aims at refutation, not objective truth.

In Islamic logic, not all arguments aim at discovering truth. The author of this passage draws a crucial line between demonstrative syllogisms used in the sciences, which must reflect reality, and dialectical syllogisms used in debate, where the goal is to refute the opponent and force them into contradiction. Understanding this distinction is essential before learning any dialectical strategies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts for syllogism use
The passage contrasts 'the demonstrative sciences' (al-ʿulūm al-burhāniyya) with 'dialectic or other than it' (al-jadal aw ghayrihī). Note that for demonstrative sciences, the syllogism must be 'according to how the matter is in itself' (ʿalā mā al-amr ʿalayhi fī nafsihi). For dialectic, the objective is 'refutation and forcing acceptance of the opposite of the opponent's aim' (al-tabkīt wa-l-ilzām ʿalā naqīḍ marām al-khaṣm).
The phrase 'other than it' includes other non-demonstrative arts like rhetoric or sophistry, but the focus is jadal.
2
Understand the shift in permissible tactics
The passage states that in dialectic, 'it is no problem for each of them to imagine ways to further his own purpose and to adopt what will protect him from refutation' (lā baʾs an yatakhayyala kull wāḥid minhumā fī taqrīb gharḍihi wa-sulūk mā yaḥtarizu bihī ʿan al-tabkīt wa-l-ilzām). This means strategic concealment and ordering of premises are acceptable, unlike in demonstration where one must follow the natural logical order.
3
Apply the distinction to a real example
Consider the proof for the world's temporal origination (ḥudūth al-ʿālam). In a demonstrative context, the argument must be presented with all premises clearly and in natural order. In a dialectical context, the questioner may reverse the order of premises to make it harder for the opponent to see the trap, as the passage later explains.

Chapter Three: On investigating what makes it easy for each of the two disputants to promote their argument in what they use of syllogism or what is used against them. We say: If the syllogism is used in the demonstrative sciences, it must be according to how the matter is in itself. If it is used in dialectic or other than it, the objective is nothing but truth; indeed, it is refutation and forcing acceptance of the opposite of the opponent's aim. And then, it is no problem for each of them to imagine ways to further his own purpose and to adopt what will protect him from refutation and coercion. Most necessary to observe that is when the question is about granting a premise, so that from them a syllogism results ultimately that produces the desired conclusion. At that point, one must indicate what promotes the purpose of the responder and the questioner. As for the responder: the first thing incumbent on him is caution, when granting each premise individually, against granting what is not granted, and to strive not to grant a repeated term that would serve as the middle term, for the syllogism is not completed without it if it is conjunctive. And he should reflect on the form of the middle term to know which figure it is in, and the conditions of its inference, and then on the connection of the desired conclusion to it, for the necessary conclusion from it may differ from the intended conclusion in quantity or quality or both. And if it is exceptive, he looks at what produces a conclusion and what does not, for premises may be granted and the desired conclusion does not follow, as when the exception in a conditional is the denial of the antecedent or the affirmation of the consequent. As for the questioner: what he uses of syllogism is either simple or compound. If it is simple, it is obligatory that he change the order from the syllogistic arrangement, by beginning with what comes later and postponing what comes first, for that is farther from alerting to the basis of the argument. For example, if the desired conclusion is the temporal origination of the world and the approach to it through composition, he should ask about the major premise by saying: 'Is not every composite originated?' For granting that before mentioning the minor premise, which is 'the world is composite', may be easier, due to the thought that it has no connection to the minor, and that there is no harm in granting it. It is not so when beginning with the minor, for then one must follow it with the major premise.

Commentary
This passage is a practical guide for dialectical debate (jadal). It begins by distinguishing between demonstrative and dialectical syllogisms: demonstrative aims at objective truth, while dialectical aims at refuting the opponent (tabkit) and forcing acceptance of the opposite of his view. In dialectical debate, each side can use strategies to further their argument and avoid being refuted. The passage focuses on two roles: the responder (mujib) and the questioner (sa'il). For the responder, the key is caution when granting premises: not to grant what is not granted, to avoid granting a repeated term that would act as the middle term (important for conjunctive syllogisms), and to analyze the figure and conditions of the middle term. The responder must also check that the conclusion that follows is indeed the intended one (in quantity/quality). For exceptive (conditional) syllogisms, the responder must watch for invalid forms like denying the antecedent or affirming the consequent. For the questioner, who constructs the syllogism, the advice is to present premises in reverse order (start with the major premise) to conceal the argument's structure. This is illustrated with the classic example of proving the world's temporal origination via composition: present the major premise 'Every composite is originated' first, before the minor 'the world is composite', because the opponent may more readily grant the major without suspecting the link to the conclusion. The passage thus gives concrete, practical advice for dialectical engagement.

Defensive Strategies for the Responder in Jadal
Advice for the responder on cautious premise granting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the defensive strategies prescribed for the responder (mujīb) to avoid granting harmful premises in a dialectical debate.

Core Idea
The responder must guard against granting premises that lead to refutation.

In a dialectical debate, the responder (mujīb) is the one being asked to grant premises. The passage gives crucial advice for him: he must be cautious not to grant premises that are not truly granted, avoid granting a repeated term that would serve as the middle term, analyze the figure and conditions of the middle term, and check that the conclusion matches the intended one. For conditional syllogisms, he must beware of invalid forms. This lesson breaks down these defensive tactics.

Guided Path
1
Avoid granting a repeated middle term
The passage says: 'strive not to grant a repeated term that would serve as the middle term, for the syllogism is not completed without it if it is conjunctive.' If the questioner asks for a premise that contains a term that could be the middle term in a categorical syllogism, granting it would allow the syllogism to be formed. The responder should either deny or qualify that premise.
The middle term (ḥadd al-awsaṭ) is the term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
2
Analyze the figure and conditions of the middle term
The responder should reflect on the form (hayʾa) of the middle term to know which figure (shakl) the syllogism belongs to, and the conditions for its inference (shurūṭ intājihī). Each figure has specific rules for a valid conclusion. If the questioner's syllogism is invalid, the responder can point that out.
For example, in the first figure, the middle term must be subject in the major premise and predicate in the minor.
3
Check the conclusion for match in quantity and quality
The necessary conclusion from the syllogism may differ from the intended conclusion (al-maṭlūb al-maqṣūd) in quantity (kamm) or quality (kayf) or both. For instance, the premises might yield a particular conclusion when a universal was intended, or an affirmative when a negative was intended.
4
Watch for invalid conditional syllogisms
For exceptive (conditional) syllogisms, premises may be granted but the conclusion does not follow. The passage gives the examples of 'denying the antecedent' (naqīḍ al-muqaddam) and 'affirming the consequent' (ʿayn al-tālī). These are classic fallacies in conditional reasoning.
Example: If P then Q; Not P; Therefore not Q (invalid). Or: If P then Q; Q; Therefore P (invalid).

Offensive Strategies for the Questioner in Jadal
Advice for the questioner on ordering premises

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct a dialectical syllogism using the questioner's strategy of reversing premise order to conceal the argument's structure.

Core Idea
Present the major premise first to hide the syllogism's trap.

The questioner (sāʾil) aims to construct a syllogism that forces the responder to accept the desired conclusion. The passage advises the questioner to reverse the natural order of premises: start with the major premise, then ask for the minor. This makes it harder for the responder to see how the premises connect to the conclusion. The classic example given is the argument for the world's temporal origination (ḥudūth al-ʿālam) via composition (tarkīb). This lesson shows you how to apply this strategy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the desired conclusion and the middle term
Decide what conclusion you want to force (the maṭlūb). Then identify the middle term (al-ḥadd al-awsaṭ) that will connect the two premises. In the example, the desired conclusion is 'The world is originated' (al-ʿālam ḥādith). The middle term is 'composite' (muʾallaf).
2
Determine which premise is major and which is minor
The major premise contains the predicate of the conclusion and the middle term. The minor premise contains the subject of the conclusion and the middle term. For the example: major premise = 'Everything composite is originated' (predicate: originated, middle: composite); minor premise = 'The world is composite' (subject: world, middle: composite).
3
Reverse the order: present the major premise first
The passage says: 'begin with what comes later and postpone what comes first' (yabdaʾ bi-l-muʾakhkhar wa-yuʾakhkhir al-muqaddam). So ask the respondent: 'Do you grant that every composite is originated?' before mentioning that the world is composite. This way, the respondent may grant the major premise without realizing its use in the upcoming syllogism.
The major premise seems less threatening because it is general and the specific subject (the world) is not yet introduced.
4
After the major is granted, introduce the minor premise
Once the major premise is granted, then ask: 'Do you also grant that the world is composite?' If the responder grants that, the conclusion follows necessarily. The responder may be caught off guard because the major premise was already accepted.
The passage notes that beginning with the minor premise would make the syllogism obvious, so the opponent would be more cautious.

Respondent's Duties in Dialectical Syllogism
Details the respondent's obligations to avoid conceding harmful premises.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key duties of a respondent in a dialectical syllogism and apply them to a sample exchange in Islamic theology.

Core Idea
In dialectic, the respondent must guard premises and the middle term.

Dialectical reasoning (jadal) is not about establishing truth alone, but about refuting the opponent. This passage details the crucial role of the respondent, who must carefully manage which premises are conceded. Understanding these duties is essential for anyone engaged in theological or juristic debate.

Examine the middle term carefully

Questioner's Premise-Ordering Tactics in Dialectical Syllogisms
Explains how the questioner orders premises to conceal his argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the questioner's premise-ordering tactics (simple and composite) to conceal the logical structure of a syllogism in a dialectical exchange.

Core Idea
Mix the order to hide the argument; ask the major first.

In dialectical exchanges, the questioner often wants to obtain premises without revealing the conclusion. This passage from Daqā'iq al-Ḥaqā'iq explains how to order questions strategically for both simple and composite syllogisms, using the example of the world's origination. Mastering these tactics sharpens your ability to construct effective arguments and defend against tricks.

Guided Path
1
Identify syllogism type
Determine if your argument is simple (two premises) or composite (chain of syllogisms). This affects the ordering strategy.
2
For simple syllogisms, reorder premises
Begin with the major premise (containing the predicate of the conclusion) and delay the minor. This makes it harder for the opponent to anticipate the conclusion.
Example: ask 'Is every composite originated?' before 'Is the world composite?'
3
For composite syllogisms, mix the order
Do not ask premises in order of proximity to the conclusion. Start with the middle term (a premise in the chain) then extremes, or vice versa, to confuse.
E.g., begin with 'Is every R H?' then 'Is every B D?' then 'Is every H J?'
4
Handle full presentation of the syllogism
If the opponent presents the whole syllogism at once, your only recourse as a respondent is to deny a premise or challenge the logical validity (e.g., missing common term, broken condition).

فيكون قد أتى بالقياس على ما يليق به من الترتيب، وهو إذ ذاك أشد فطنة لمأخذ الاحتجاج، ولما يوجب الحدوث للأصغر فمع قربه للتسليم أكثر. وحيث أوجب المعلم الأول البدأة بالحد الأوسط في الاقتران فإنما أراد به الابتداء بالمقدمة الكبرى لا الصغرى كما ظن قوم؛ لمخالفته ما أشرنا إليه من التحقيق، وأكثر ما ينفع ذلك في ما كان من القياسات على نمط الشكل الأول. وعلى هذا إن كان القياس مركباً فالواجب أن لا يكون السؤال عن المقدمات على الترتيب - أي عن المقدمة التي تلي المطلوب - ثم التي تليها، ثم التي تليها، إذ هو أقرب إلى التفطن بموضع الحيلة، بل يسأل على وجه التخليط بأن يبتدأ بالواسطة، ثم بالطرفين أو بالعكس. وبالجملة فلا يسأل عن مقدمة وما يليها، وذلك في الاقتران، كما إذا أراد بيان أن كل "ب" "ج" وكان قياسه (كل "ب" "د") و(كل "د" "ر") و(كل "ر" "هـ") و(كل "هـ" "ج") فإن ابتدأ بالطرفين فالواجب أن يبدأ بالكبرى؛ إذ هو أبعد عن الترتيب، وعن التفطن، فنقول أليس كل "هـ" "ج" ثم بما هو أبعد منه، وهو القريب إلى المطلوب، فنقول أليس كل "ب" "د" ثم بما بينهما وهو (كل "ر" "هـ") و(كل "د" "ر")، وإن ابتدأ بالواسطة بأن قال: (أليس كل "ر" "هـ" فالأول أن يتلوه بما هو الأقرب إلى المطلوب، وهو أن يقول: (أليس كل "ب" "د") إذ هو أبعد عن الترتيب، ثم بما هو الأبعد عن المطلوب، وهو (كل "هـ" "ج") ثم بما بقي، وعلى هذا يكون التخيل في الاستثنائيات أيضاً. وبالجملة فقلما يقع الغلط في هذه الأمور، إلا لغير المتدربين، وأما المتدرب من إذا كان السؤال عن مقدمات القياس مع نتيجته جملة كقول القائل أليس إذا كان "ب" "ج" وكل "ج" "أ" فـ (كل "ب" "أ") فلا حيلة غير الإنكار للمقدمات أو بعضها، وأن ينكر كون التأليف منتجاً، إما بأنه ليس له حد مشترك، أو أنه فات شرط من شروط الإنتاج، أو أنه لا مناسبة بين اللازم عنه وما وقع النزاع فيه.
So he has brought the syllogism in the order appropriate to it, and he is then more perceptive of the argumentative method, and regarding what necessitates the occurrence of the minor term, it is closer to acceptance. And when the First Teacher required starting with the middle term in conjunctive syllogisms, he thereby intended starting with the major premise, not the minor as some thought, because it contradicts what we indicated regarding verification. And most benefit from that occurs in syllogisms of the first figure. And upon this, if the syllogism is compound, it is necessary that the questioning about the premises not be in order — i.e., about the premise that follows the conclusion, then what follows it, then what follows it — since that is closer to perceiving the place of trickery. Rather, one should ask in a confusing manner by starting with the middle term, then the extremes, or vice versa. In summary, do not ask about a premise and what follows it, and that is in conjunctive syllogisms. For example, if he wants to show that every 'B' is 'J' and his syllogism is (every 'B' is 'D'), (every 'D' is 'R'), (every 'R' is 'H'), and (every 'H' is 'J'), then if he starts with the extremes, he must start with the major premise, for it is farther from order and from perception. So we say: 'Is not every 'H' 'J'?' then with what is farther than it, which is closer to the conclusion, so we say: 'Is not every 'B' 'D'?' then with what is between them, which is (every 'R' is 'H') and (every 'D' is 'R'). If he starts with the middle by saying: 'Is not every 'R' 'H'?', it is best to follow it with what is closest to the conclusion, i.e., 'Is not every 'B' 'D'?' because it is farther from order, then with what is farthest from the conclusion, i.e., (every 'H' is 'J'), then with what remains. And likewise the deception in conditional syllogisms. In summary, error rarely occurs in these matters except for the untrained. As for the trained, if the questioning is about the premises of a syllogism together with its conclusion, such as someone saying: 'If 'B' is 'J' and every 'J' is 'A', then every 'B' is 'A', then there is no trick other than denial of the premises or some of them, and denial that the combination is productive, either because it has no common term, or because some condition of productivity is missing, or because there is no relation between what is entailed and what is disputed.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical instruction on how to properly question a compound syllogism in a debate (jadal). The author begins by affirming that presenting a syllogism in its logical order enhances perceptiveness of the argument. However, he notes that the First Teacher (Aristotle) required starting with the middle term in conjunctive syllogisms, but this was misunderstood: the First Teacher meant starting with the major premise, not the minor. This applies especially to first figure syllogisms. Then the author gives practical advice: in compound syllogisms, do not ask about premises in order (i.e., starting from the premise nearest to the conclusion and then proceeding). That would allow the opponent to detect the trick. Instead, ask in a confusing order (takhliṭ), starting with the middle term, then the extremes, or vice versa. He illustrates with a four-term syllogism: every B is D, every D is R, every R is H, every H is J, aiming to prove every B is J. If you start with the extremes, begin with the major premise (every H is J) because it is farthest from the logical order, then ask about the premise closest to the conclusion (every B is D), then the middle ones. If you start with the middle term (every R is H), then follow with the premise closest to the conclusion, then the farthest, then the remaining. This confusing method also applies to conditional syllogisms. The author concludes that such errors rarely occur except for the untrained. The trained opponent, when confronted with a complete syllogism (e.g., 'If B is J and every J is A, then every B is A'), has no trick except to deny some premises or to deny that the combination is productive, either due to lack of a common term, missing a condition of productivity, or lack of relation between the entailment and the disputed matter. The commentary should emphasize that this is about the art of debate, not formal logic per se.
