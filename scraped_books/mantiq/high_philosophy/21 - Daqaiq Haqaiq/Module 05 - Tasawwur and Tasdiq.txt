External vs. Mental Existence: The Foundation of Logic
Author introduces the need for logic by distinguishing external and mental existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish external existence (wujud 'ayni) from mental existence (wujud 'aqli) and explain why both are real and universal.

Core Idea
Logic's subject matter is universal mental concepts that correspond to real external things.

In this lesson, we explore the author's foundational distinction between two modes of existence: external and mental. Understanding this distinction is crucial because logic deals with mental concepts that, while in the mind, correspond to external reality. The author insists that mental existence is not merely conventional but is real and universal, providing the stable foundation for logical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of existence
In the Arabic, locate where the author introduces 'wujud 'ayni' and 'wujud 'aqli'. Note the context: everything has an essence and existence, either sensible or non-sensible. The author distinguishes based on whether existence is considered externally or in the mind.
Pay attention to the terms 'al-wujud al-'ayni' and 'al-wujud al-'aqli'.
2
Understand the reality claim
The author says both existences are 'real' and do not differ with eras or nations. Reflect on why this is important for logic: logic must be based on universal truths, not changing conventions.
The claim that mental existence is real and universal is key to the objectivity of logic.
3
Trace the signifier-signified relationship
The mental existence is called a 'signifier' (dal) and external existence 'signified' (madlul). How does this correspondence enable knowledge? The soul becomes aware of external existence only through the mental form.
This relationship is foundational for understanding how concepts represent reality.
4
Connect to the need for logic
The author argues we need to acquire things in the mind (through mental existence) and then define them for others. Why does this necessitate logic? Because defining and communicating requires tools to ensure accuracy and avoid error.
This sets up logic as a tool for acquiring and conveying knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the second introduction of the book, where the author explains why logic is necessary and sets the epistemological foundation. He starts by stating that everything has an essence and existence, whether sensible (like physical objects) or non-sensible (like abstract concepts). The goal is to acquire these things in the mind as they truly are, because this acquisition perfects the human soul and leads to eternal happiness. The author then distinguishes two modes of existence: external existence (وجود عيني) and mental existence (وجود عقلي) also called psychic impression (أثر نفساني). External existence is for things existing outside the mind; for sensible things it is clear, but for non-sensible things, the term 'external existence' can still be used broadly to mean they have an essence. Mental existence is the form of the thing in the mind. Crucially, the author asserts that both existences are real and universal – they do not change with time, place, or convention. This is an important point: logic deals with universal truths, not cultural variations. The mental existence is what the soul uses to become aware of external things, and since it corresponds to external reality, it can be called a 'signifier' (دال) and the external thing a 'signified' (مدلول). The author then extends this to the social need for knowledge: just as an individual needs to acquire knowledge, he also needs to communicate it to others, because humans rely on each other for learning. This sets up the next discussion about language and signs.

Key Terms
الوجود العيني (al-wujud al-'ayni): External existence, the existence of a thing in itself outside the mind. For sensible things, it is obvious; for non-sensible things, it means the thing has an essence. [Note: In later Islamic philosophy, 'wujud 'ayni' often contrasts with 'wujud dhihni' (mental existence) but is used consistently.]
الوجود العقلي (al-wujud al-'aqli): Mental existence, the form or concept of a thing in the mind, also called 'athar nafsani' (psychic impression). It is universal and corresponds to external existence. [Note: This term is standard in logic and philosophy; no significant shift.]
الأثر النفساني (al-athar al-nafsani): Psychic impression, synonymous with mental existence; the trace left in the soul by the external thing. [Note: Used interchangeably with 'wujud 'aqli' in this text; later texts may distinguish them more precisely.]
دال (dal): Signifier, specifically the mental existence that indicates the external existence due to their correspondence. [Note: In logic, 'dal' generally means any sign, but here it is specifically the mental form.]
مدلول (madlul): Signified, the external existence that is indicated by the mental form. [Note: Standard usage.]

Defining Tasawwur and Distinguishing from Tasdiq
Author provides the rasm of tasawwur and distinguishes it from tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the rasm of tasawwur to classify any piece of knowledge as either tasawwur or tasdiq.

Core Idea
Tasawwur is a mental image without judgment; tasdiq adds a truth claim.

This passage establishes the foundational distinction between tasawwur (conceptual knowledge) and tasdiq (assent). The author provides a precise rasm (description) for tasawwur that rests on the absence of truth-value judgment. Understanding this distinction is essential for all subsequent logical operations, because definitions and premises depend on concepts, while syllogisms and proofs rely on assertions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Find in the Arabic where the author divides 'the known' into two categories: considered in itself or in relation to something external. Name them as tasawwur and tasdiq.
The division is in the first sentence: 'المعلوم إما أن ينظر إليه في نفسه... أو في نسبته...'
2
Extract the rasm of tasawwur
Locate the sentence that gives the description (rasm) of tasawwur. It is: 'حصول صورة مفرد ما في العقل، على وجه لا يتبعه حكم صدق أو كذب'.
The rasm is explicitly introduced with 'أما رسمه فهو عبارة عن...'
3
Break down the components of the rasm
Identify two parts: (a) 'حصول صورة مفرد ما في العقل' – acquiring a single mental image; (b) 'على وجه لا يتبعه حكم صدق أو كذب' – in a way that no judgment of truth or falsity follows it.
The 'image' means distinguishing the thing in the mind, by essential or accidental attributes.
4
Understand the exclusion clause
The phrase 'على وجه لا يتبعه حكم صدق أو كذب' is meant to exclude predicative relations (النسب الخبرية), because they involve a judgment of truth or falsity and thus belong to tasdiq.
The author explicitly says this clause is for exclusion (احترازاً عن النسب الخبرية).
5
Apply the rasm to examples
Test whether a given mental content qualifies as tasawwur: does it involve a truth-value judgment? If yes, it is tasdiq; if no, it is tasawwur.
For instance, the concept 'Zaid' is tasawwur; the proposition 'Zaid is standing' is tasdiq.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional explanation of conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) and its distinction from assent (tasdiq). He then explains that the term 'tasawwur' is derived from 'sura' (image) in the soul, even though a predicative relation also yields an image, but tasdiq is named by its specific attribute of judgment. This sets the foundation for the division of tasawwur into innate and acquired, which follows.

Division of Tasawwur: Fitri and Nazari
Author argues that conceptual knowledge divides into innate and acquired, rejecting extremes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three possibilities for the division of tasawwur and apply the disjunctive syllogism to reject the impossible ones, leaving the necessary division.

Core Idea
Not every concept is innate nor every concept learned; the mind starts with self-evident concepts and builds from there.

How do we acquire concepts? Some seem to come naturally, like the concept of existence, while others require reasoning, like the concept of a journey. The author argues that conceptual knowledge must be divided into innate (fitri) and acquired (nazari) by eliminating the other possibilities through a precise disjunctive argument. In this lesson, you will walk through that argument and see why it is logically necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial division
The author begins by dividing concepts based on how they are attained: either they are acquired without needing a prior principle (fitri), or they require such a principle (nazari). This is the primary distinction.
2
List the three possibilities
When considering the entire set of concepts, only three exhaustive possibilities exist: all are fitri, all are nazari, or some are fitri and some nazari. This is a classic disjunction.
3
Examine the rejection of 'all fitri'
The first possibility is rejected because it contradicts our common experience: we find many concepts that are not innate and are only acquired through learning. Additionally, innate knowledge without awareness is impossible, as knowledge requires consciousness.
4
Examine the rejection of 'all nazari'
The second possibility is rejected because it leads to infinite regress (tasalsul) or circularity (dawr). If every concept required a prior concept, we would never reach a starting point, or we would go in circles. Both are impossible in sound reasoning.
5
Conclude the remaining option
With two possibilities eliminated, the only viable option is that some concepts are fitri and some are nazari. This conclusion follows necessarily from the disjunctive syllogism, establishing a mixed division of conceptual knowledge.

As for its divisions: either its acquisition in the mind at the perfection of the faculty of perception does not need a principle that makes it known, or it does need. The first is called 'innate' and the second 'acquired'. And it divides into innate and acquired because either every concept is innate, or every concept is acquired, or some are innate and some acquired. The first is impossible because it contradicts what we find in ourselves regarding most concepts, and the acquisition of innate knowledge in a person without him being aware of it is impossible. Commentary
This passage classifies conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) into two types based on how it is attained. The author first presents the division: either a concept is acquired innately at the perfection of the faculty, without needing a prior principle (fitri), or it requires such a principle (nazari). He then argues why this division is necessary by considering all possibilities: either all concepts are fitri, all are nazari, or a mix. He rejects the first because we commonly experience concepts that are not innate, and innate knowledge without awareness is absurd. He rejects the second because if all concepts required prior concepts, we would have infinite regress or circularity, which is impossible. Thus, the only viable option is the mixed division. This argument is a classic disjunctive syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) that eliminates impossible alternatives, leaving the necessary one. The classification is foundational for epistemology and logic, as it allows for a hierarchy of knowledge from self-evident primary concepts to derived ones.

Key Terms
فطري (fitri): A concept that is immediately grasped by the mind without needing to be learned or deduced.
نظري (nazari): A concept that requires reasoning or reflection to be attained.
تسلسل (tasalsul): Infinite regress, considered impossible. [Note: Standard in Islamic logic.]
دور (dawr): Circular reasoning, considered impossible.

Defining Tasawwur and Its Distinction from Tasdiq
Author defines conceptual knowledge and distinguishes it from assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent) based on the presence or absence of truth-value judgment.

Core Idea
Tasawwur is the bare form of a thing; tasdiq adds a truth-value judgment.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational division of knowledge into tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). The author clarifies that tasawwur is knowledge of a thing's form without any claim of truth or falsity, while tasdiq involves a predicative relation that can be true or false. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping all subsequent logical operations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tasawwur
Find the phrase 'ما ليس بموجب صدق أو كذب' (not entailing truth or falsity) in the Arabic. This is the core of the definition.
Tasawwur is knowledge that does not involve a truth-value judgment.
2
Contrast with tasdiq
The author mentions 'النسب الخبرية' (predicative relations) which carry truth or falsity. Tasdiq is knowledge of such relations.
Tasdiq is always about a proposition, not a simple concept.
3
Understand the precaution
The phrase 'على وجه لا يتبعه حكم صدق أو كذب' excludes predicative relations. These relations are simple, not composite, and working with them is called tasdiq, not tasawwur.
This precaution prevents confusion between concept and proposition.
4
Note the derivation
Both tasawwur and tasdiq derive from 'صورة' (form). The author explains that the names are given based on the most specific attributes of each type of knowledge.
Derivation does not determine meaning; context does.

The Division of Tasawwur into Innate and Acquired
Author divides conceptual knowledge into innate and acquired and proves both exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument that proves the existence of both innate (fitri) and acquired (nazari) conceptual knowledge.

Core Idea
Not all concepts are innate nor all acquired; a mixture is necessary to avoid contradiction and infinite regress.

After defining tasawwur, the author turns to its division into innate (fitri) and theoretical (nazari). He argues that both must exist: if all concepts were innate, it would contradict our experience of learning; if all were acquired, it would lead to infinite regress or circularity. This argument establishes the need for logic to derive new concepts from innate ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three possible divisions
The author lists three possibilities: all tasawwur is innate, all is theoretical, or some are innate and some theoretical.
These are mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
2
Understand why all innate is impossible
The author says it contradicts what we find in ourselves ('خلاف ما نجده في أنفسنا'): we experience learning new concepts, so not all are innate.
This is an empirical argument from common experience.
3
Understand why all theoretical is impossible
If all concepts required prior concepts, we would have an infinite regress (تسلسل) or circularity (دور), which is impossible.
This is a logical argument against infinite regress.
4
Conclude the remaining possibility
Since the first two are impossible, the only option is that some concepts are innate and some are theoretical.
This is a valid disjunctive syllogism.
5
Identify the logical form
The argument is: either all fitri, or all nazari, or mixed. Not all fitri (empirical counterexample). Not all nazari (leads to regress). Therefore, mixed.
This is a classic disjunctive syllogism (modus tollendo ponens).

Defending Complete Real Definition against the Objection of Circularity
Author refutes the objection that defining by internal parts is circular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between holistic and individual enumeration of essential parts, and defend the validity of the complete real definition (hadd haqiqi tamm).

Core Idea
Defining by the whole of parts is circular; defining by each part individually is the complete real definition.

The logician faces a powerful objection: if you define an essence by its internal parts, you are defining the thing by itself, since the sum of parts is the essence itself. In this passage, the author dissects this objection and shows that it rests on a confusion between considering the parts as a collective whole versus considering them one by one. This distinction is crucial for understanding how definitions work in the Islamic logical tradition, especially in Usul al-Fiqh where essential definitions of key terms like 'wine' (khamr) determine legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector argues that defining an essence by its internal parts (dhatiyyat) is impossible because the sum of parts is the essence itself, making it a circular definition. Locate this claim in the Arabic: 'إنه لا يمكن أن تُعرَّف الماهية بمجموع الأمور الداخلة فيها فإن ذلك المجموع هو نفسها الماهية، وفيه تعريف الشيء من نفسه'.
The objector assumes that 'defining by parts' always means defining by the whole collective.
2
Distinguish two modes of considering parts
The author distinguishes: (a) considering the parts as a collective whole (the aggregate), and (b) considering each part individually (one by one). The Arabic says: 'الذاتيات الداخلة في الماهية إما ينظر إليها من حيث هي ذلك المجموع، أو ينظر إلى كل واحد واحد من آحادها على سبيل الاستقصاء'.
This is the key logical move.
3
Apply the distinction to the objection
The whole (aggregate) is indeed the essence, so defining by the whole is circular. But each individual part is not the essence. Therefore, defining by enumerating each essential part individually is not circular; it is a valid complete real definition (hadd haqiqi tamm). The author concludes: 'وعلى هذا فالتعريف بهذا الطريق هو التعريف بالحد الحقيقي التام'.
Thus the objection only refutes holistic definition, not enumerative definition.
4
Connect to the earlier objection on tasawwur
Recall that the same objector had argued that a simple term cannot be acquired through parts. The author's distinction here reinforces that acquisition of knowledge of an essence can occur by learning each essential part separately, without circularity. This resolves the broader dialectic about the possibility of acquiring tasawwur.
This ties the definition discussion to the earlier part of the passage.

What is Tasdiq? The Mind's Judgment Between Concepts
Author defines tasdiq and argues it requires prior conceptualization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tasdiq as the mind's judgment of a relation between two concepts and explain why it requires prior tasawwur.

Core Idea
Tasdiq is not just knowing two concepts; it is judging their relation.

In this passage, the author provides a precise definition of tasdiq (assent) and clarifies its relationship to tasawwur (conceptualization). He argues that merely knowing individual concepts—like 'body' and 'black'—does not yield truth or falsity until the mind relates them in a judgment. This insight is foundational for understanding how knowledge is built in the Islamic intellectual tradition, from theological proofs to juristic reasoning.

The author defines tasdiq using a rasm (descriptive definition). Find the phrase 'هو عبارة عن' which introduces the definition. The definiendum is 'التصديق' (tasdiq).

The author emphasizes that this judgment is what makes the proposition truth-apt (yielding truth or falsity).
3
Distinguish tasdiq from mere conceptual knowledge
The author explicitly states that tasdiq is not simply knowing the two concepts (المفردين). He gives an example: understanding 'body' and 'black' does not produce truth or falsity until they are related. This distinguishes the two cognitive acts.
4
Analyze the relationship of priority between tasdiq and tasawwur
The author argues that every tasdiq necessarily requires prior tasawwur of its terms, but the converse does not hold. He then addresses a potential counterexample: a proposition about 'the unknown' still involves conceptualizing 'the unknown' as a concept.
This step clarifies the logical dependency: tasawwur is independent, while tasdiq depends on it.

Refuting the Impossibility of Definition for Conception
Author refutes the argument that definition is impossible due to infinite parts of quiddity and applies the same to tasdiq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in the claim that definition is impossible due to infinite parts of quiddity, and explain why this claim fails for conception (tasawwur).

Core Idea
Definition is needed because we find ourselves ignorant of realities in themselves, not just their relations.

The opponent argues that every known quiddity has infinite divisions, making definition impossible. The author replies that this claim is not self-evident and must be proven. Moreover, our own experience shows we seek knowledge of things in themselves—this is ignorance of conception, not assent. Thus definition is not only possible but necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent says every known quiddity has infinite parts, so definition is impossible.
2
Note the author's first objection
The author points out that the claim is not self-evident (badihi); it is theoretical (nazari) and requires proof. Since no proof is given, the argument is unsupported.
3
Recognize the experiential argument
The author appeals to common experience: every rational person finds themselves ignorant of many individual realities and seeks to know them in themselves, not just in relation to others. This shows that we have a need for definition that gives conception (tasawwur).
4
Distinguish ignorance of conception from ignorance of assent
The ignorance experienced is clearly about the thing itself (tasawwur), not about a judgment (tasdiq). Therefore, definition, which provides tasawwur, must be possible.

Using a general logical principle—that what depends on something is more general and what is included in the meaning is more specific—he concludes that the existence of the specific (the terms in the meaning) does not follow from the existence of the general (the prerequisites). List the four main inquiries
From the passage, the author reduces scientific inquiries to four: ma (what), ayy (which), hal (whether), and lima (why). Note that ma and ayy are for conceptual knowledge (tasawwur), while hal and lima are for assent (tasdiq).
2
Distinguish between simple and compound forms
Each inquiry originally divides into two, making six, but the author focuses on four. For hal, there is simple (هل البسيطة) asking about existence, and compound (هل المركبة) asking about a state beyond existence. Similarly, lima asks either for the cause of existence or the reason for assent.
3
Determine the order of priority
The passage establishes: First comes ma (explaining the term's meaning). Then simple hal (existence). Then ma for essence or ayy for distinction. Then lima for cause/reason. Compound hal follows after lima for assent. The author explains exceptions: sometimes assent can precede conceptualization if obtained via senses.
4
Apply to a theological example
Consider the inquiry about God's existence. First we must understand the term 'God' (ma), then ask whether God exists (hal), then distinguish God's attributes (ayy), then ask why the world needs a creator (lima). This order mirrors the logical sequence in kalam proofs.

The third chapter: On the inquiries, the number of their divisions, and the manner of their order. And what was said in the first teachings is that the scientific inquiries are among these. They are, by the first division, three: the inquiry of 'what' (ma), 'whether' (hal), and 'why' (lima). And by the second division, as verified, they become six, since each of them divides into two. As for the remaining inquiries, they are included in the compound 'whether' in the manner known, except that the simplest of them is the inquiry of 'which' (ayy), for it is for distinction by what is particular to the thing. And that (compound 'whether') has a broader scope. And these may all enter into 'which', as one says: 'Which number is it?' and 'Which color is it?' and 'In which place?' and 'In which time is it?' And inevitably, it is feasible for whoever wishes to make it a scientific inquiry. Then at that point, the scientific inquiries become four: 'what', 'which', 'whether', and 'why', except that 'what' from among them is specific to conceptual demands (tasawwuri), and 'which' is also for conceptual demands, while 'whether' and 'why' are specific to compound demands (tasdiqi). So 'what' which seeks explanation of the meaning of the term is prior to the simple 'whether'. For one who does not know the referent of the term does not ask about its existence. And knowledge, even if it may be by definition (hadd) and limit (hadd), is only with respect to existence. So it is not known at the outset whether the said statement is a definition according to the term or according to the definition (hadd) except after knowledge of existence, as has preceded. And the simple 'whether' is prior to 'what' which seeks the essence, and to 'which' which seeks distinction. For the seeker of definition or distinction is only a seeker of conceptualization of an existing thing, either in the external world or in the mind. And as for 'why', it is posterior to all. For as long as one does not know the meaning of the explanation of its term, nor its existence, nor the conceptualization of its meaning, then seeking its 'why' is impossible, whether the 'why-ness' is for assent (tasdiq) or for cause. And the seeker of assent may sometimes be prior to the seeker of conceptualization when the assent is obtained by a syllogism whose middle term is not a cause, so it is not necessary that it be prior, because it is possible that the assent to a thing is sensory even if its cause is hidden, as in the attraction of iron by a magnet. And it is not possible that it be posterior to the seeker of the cause, for whoever obtains the cause obtains the assent. And as for the compound 'whether', it is posterior to the seeker of the 'why' of assent. For whoever does not assent to existence does not ask about a state additional to existence. And as for the seeker of causality, it may be prior to it sometimes and posterior at other times, for it is not necessary that seeking the additional state beyond existence requires knowledge of the cause of existence, nor is it necessary that knowledge of the additional state beyond existence requires knowledge of the cause of existence. And as for 'which', its relation to the seeker of the essence is like the relation of the seeker of assent to the seeker of the cause.

Commentary
This passage classifies scientific inquiries (al-matalib al-'ilmiyya) and establishes their logical order. The author begins by stating that the first division yields three inquiries: 'what' (ma), 'whether' (hal), and 'why' (lima). Then a second division doubles each, making six. The remaining inquiries, such as 'which' (ayy), are subsumed under the compound 'whether', but 'which' is noted as the simplest because it distinguishes by particular characteristics. The author then consolidates the inquiries into four: ma, ayy, hal, and lima. Of these, ma and ayy are for conceptual demands (tasawwuri), while hal and lima are for compound demands (tasdiqi). The passage then explains the priority order: the 'what' that seeks explanation of the term's meaning precedes the simple 'whether', because one must understand the term before asking about existence. The simple 'whether' precedes the 'what' seeking the essence and the 'which' seeking distinction, because conceptualization requires existence in mind or reality. The 'why' is posterior to all, since one cannot ask for a cause or reason without prior knowledge of existence and conceptualization. However, the 'why' for assent may sometimes precede the 'what' for conceptualization if the assent is obtained through a syllogism whose middle term is not a cause, as in sensory knowledge. The compound 'whether' is posterior to the 'why' of assent. The seeker of causality may be prior or posterior to the compound 'whether'. Finally, 'which' relates to the seeker of essence as the seeker of assent relates to the seeker of causality. The overall logical progression is from the basic conceptual grasp (ma/ayy) to existence (hal) to cause or reason (lima).
