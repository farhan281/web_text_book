Classification of Genus and Species in the Five Universals
Author defines jins, naw' bi al-ma'na al-awwal, and naw' bi al-ma'na al-mudaf, then notes their exclusion from division.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify essential universals into genus, species in the first sense, and relative species based on the criteria given in the text.

Core Idea
Essential universals are divided by whether they are predicated of individuals differing in number or reality.

In the previous lesson, we learned the five universals. Now we dive deeper into the classification of genus and species. The author presents a clear decision tree: if a universal is essential (dhati), look at what it is predicated of. This lesson will walk you through that tree so you can identify each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify if the universal is essential (dhati)
Only essential universals are subject to this division. The passage begins with 'if it is essential', so first confirm that the universal is dhati.
2
Check what the universal is predicated of: differing in number or in reality?
If predicated of subjects that differ only in number (e.g., Zaid and Amr are both 'man'), it is species in the first sense (lowest species). If predicated of subjects differing in reality (e.g., 'animal' said of man and horse), proceed to the next step.
Differing in number means individuals of the same species; differing in reality means different species.
3
Determine if it is also predicated of the subjects' predicates
If it is predicated of the subjects but not of their predicates (i.e., the species are not predicated of the genus), it is genus (jins). If it is predicated of both the subjects and their predicates (i.e., the relative species can be predicated of individuals and also of lower species), it is relative species (naw' bi al-ma'na al-mudaf).
For example, 'animal' is predicated of 'man' but 'man' is not predicated of 'animal', so 'animal' is a genus.
4
Understand the exclusion of highest genus and lowest species
The passage notes that the highest genus (summum genus) is not included in the division of genera because it has no genus above it. Similarly, the lowest species (infima species) is not included in the division of relative species because it has no lower species under it. The division only applies to intermediate terms.
For example, 'substance' is a highest genus; 'man' is a lowest species (species in the first sense).

According to what is known, if a [universal] is essential (dhati), then either it is said of things differing in number or in reality. If the first, then it is species in the first sense (naw' bi al-ma'na al-awwal). If the second, then either it is predicated [of the subjects] and not predicated of [their predicates] or it is predicated [of them]. If the first, then it is genus (jins); if the second, then it is species in the relative sense (naw' bi al-ma'na al-mudaf). It is also not hidden that the nature of genus qua genus is not included in the division, for it only applies to genera that are higher, not the intermediate and lower. Similarly, the nature of the relative species qua relative is also outside it, and it does not include anything except what is higher or intermediate, not lower.

Commentary
This passage provides a definitional classification of the five universals as they are understood in logic. It begins by dividing essential (dhati) universals: if an essential universal is predicated of subjects that differ only in number (i.e., individuals of the same reality), it is called 'species in the first sense,' which is the lowest species (infima species). If it is predicated of subjects that differ in reality (i.e., different species), then it is either not predicated of those subjects in a higher sense (the genus is predicated of species but not vice versa) or it is predicated of them (the relative species is predicated of individuals). The first is genus (jins), the second is relative species (naw' bi al-ma'na al-mudaf), which is the immediate genus. The author then adds an important note: the highest genus (summum genus) is not included in the division of genera into higher, intermediate, and lower because it has no higher. Similarly, the lowest species (naw' bi al-ma'na al-awwal) is not included in the division of relative species because it has no lower. This clarifies that the division only applies to intermediate terms. For example, 'animal' as a genus is intermediate between 'body' and 'man', but 'substance' as the highest genus has no genus above it.

Key Terms
ذاتي (dhati): Essential predicate that constitutes the essence of the subject; also called 'essential universal'. [Note: In later texts, dhati is often divided into 'all dhati' and 'part dhati'; here it is the generic essential universal.]
نوع بالمعنى الأول (naw' bi al-ma'na al-awwal): The species that is predicated of individuals differing only in number (the lowest species). [Note: This is the standard definition used in the Isaghuji tradition.]
جنس (jins): An essential universal that is predicated of subjects differing in reality (species) and is not predicated of those subjects when they are considered as subjects of a higher genus. [Note: Jins is stable; but here it specifically excludes the highest genus from the division.]
نوع بالمعنى المضاف (naw' bi al-ma'na al-mudaf): The relative species; a universal that is a species relative to a higher genus (its immediate genus). [Note: This term is used to distinguish from the absolute species (naw' bi al-ma'na al-awwal).]

Critique of a Faulty Division of the Five Universals
Author critiques a previous division of universals and identifies its logical flaws.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two logical flaws in a given division of the five universals: lack of exhaustiveness and hidden redundancy.

Core Idea
A division must be exhaustive; if it leaves out a possible category, it is invalid.

The author presents a division of universals by a leading scholar ('afdal al-muta'akhkhirin') and then exposes its logical weaknesses. Understanding these flaws will sharpen your ability to evaluate classifications in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the faulty division's structure
Locate the primary split: essential vs accidental. Then note how essential is further divided into what answers 'what is it?' (more general and more specific) and what does not (differential).
2
Locate the first flaw: non-exhaustiveness
The essential category is divided into more general and more specific, but the author points out that something could be co-extensive (neither more general nor more specific). This means the division is not exhaustive.
The author mentions that if 'more specific' were intended to mean 'co-extensive', it would be a stretch of language.
3
Locate the second flaw: hidden redundancy
The relative species is divided into what can be a genus and what cannot. But this introduces a third category (the non-relative species) that invalidates the claimed exclusivity of the two divisions.
The author argues that this extra category shows the division is not mutually exclusive.
4
Recognize the basis of the critique
The critique is based on the logical requirements for a division: it must be exhaustive (covering all possibilities) and have no overlapping categories.

Classifying the Five Universals by Essentiality and Scope
Defines genus, species, property, and common accident.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal into one of the five categories by checking whether it is essential and how broad its predication is.

Core Idea
The five universals are sorted by whether they are essential and how broad they are.

This passage from Daqaiq al-Haqaiq lays out the precise criteria for distinguishing the five universals: genus, species (two types), property, and common accident. Understanding this classification is the key to constructing valid definitions and avoiding confusion in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two criteria
The passage uses two main criteria: essentiality (dhati vs. non-dhati) and the scope of predication (more general, more specific, neither, particular to one kind, or common to many).
Essential means the universal is part of the quiddity of what it is predicated of.
2
Check essentiality first
If the universal is essential, it falls into one of three categories: genus, relative species, or absolute species. If it is not essential, it is either property or common accident.
3
For essential universals, determine scope
If it is considered from the aspect of being more general, it is genus. If more specific relative to a genus, it is species in the relative sense. If neither more general nor more specific, it is species in the absolute sense (lowest species).
For example, 'animal' is more general than 'human', so it is genus. 'Human' is more specific than 'animal', so it is relative species. But 'human' itself is also an absolute species when not compared to anything else.
4
For non-essential universals, check exclusivity
If the universal is particular to a single universal kind (e.g., laughter for humans), it is property. If it is not particular but shared across multiple kinds (e.g., whiteness), it is common accident.

Commentary
This passage, the tenth chapter, addresses the points of agreement and difference among the five universals. This whole discussion is specific to the logical aspect of the universals; otherwise, all five universals share in giving the name of the nature to their inferiors when considered without accidental attributes.
