The Universe and Motion
Al-Ghazali defines the universe and motion, including the motion of the outermost sphere.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the universe (al-‘alam) and motion (al-haraka) according to Al-Ghazali, distinguishing the two definitions of motion.

Core Idea
The universe is the totality of simple bodies, and motion is the actualization of potentiality through change.

After defining the elements, Al-Ghazali defines the universe as the sum total of all simple natural bodies. He then provides two definitions of motion: one from Aristotle (first entelechy) and a simpler one (emergence from potentiality to actuality). He also notes that every change is considered motion, and describes the motion of the outermost sphere.

Guided Path
1
Define the universe
Find the definition: the totality of all simple natural bodies. Also note the broader usage for any homogeneous set.
2
Identify the two definitions of motion
The first is 'first entelechy of the potential qua potential'; the second is 'emergence from potentiality to actuality not in an instant'.
3
Understand the scope of motion
Al-Ghazali states that every change (change in quality, quantity, place) is called motion.
4
Examine the motion of the whole
The outermost sphere moves circularly, encompassing all other motions and being fastest.

Commentary
This passage is a series of definitions of key concepts in Aristotelian physics. Al-Ghazali begins with the four elements: fire (hot-dry, moves away from center, located under lunar sphere), air (hot-moist, transparent, subtle, located between fire and earth), water (cold-moist, transparent, located between air and earth), earth (cold-dry, moves to center). Each is a 'simple body' with a natural place and motion. He then defines the universe (al-‘alam) as the totality of all simple natural bodies, but also notes a broader usage: any collection of homogeneous existents (e.g., world of nature, soul, intellect). Motion (al-haraka) is defined in two ways: as first entelechy of the potential qua potential (Aristotelian definition), or as emergence from potentiality to actuality not in an instant. Every change is called motion. The motion of the whole (the outermost sphere) encompasses all other circular motions and is fastest. Eternity (al-dahr) is a concept of permanence in the soul over all time. Time (al-zaman) is the measure of motion with respect to before and after. The instant (al-an) is a point shared by past and future. Place (al-makan) is defined as the inner boundary of the containing body, but also as the surface on which something rests, and a third hypothetical sense (void). Void (al-khala’) is defined as a dimension that can be imagined without matter, but the definition is cut off.

Time and the Instant
Al-Ghazali defines eternity, time, and the instant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define time (al-zaman) as the measure of motion and the instant (al-an) as the container shared by past and future.

Core Idea
Time measures motion, and the instant is the indivisible present that connects past and future.

Al-Ghazali defines eternity (dahr), time (zaman), and the instant (an). Eternity is the concept of permanence beyond time. Time is the measure of motion with respect to before and after. The instant is the point of time that is the common boundary of past and future.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish dahr from zaman
Eternity (dahr) is an intelligible concept of permanence; time (zaman) is a measure.
2
Define time
Time is the measure (miqdar) of motion, characterized by before and after (taqaddum and ta'akhkhur).
3
Define the instant
The instant (an) is a temporal container (zarf) that the past and future share; it is the present moment.
4
Note the alternative description
Time is also said to be a small amount in estimation connected to the real instant.

Place and Void
Al-Ghazali defines place and void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define place (al-makan) as the inner surface of the containing body, and define void (al-khala') as a dimension without matter.

Core Idea
Place is the boundary of the container, not the volume; void is a dimension imagined without matter.

Al-Ghazali defines place in two senses: as the inner surface of the containing body, and as the surface on which something rests. He also mentions a third sense (void) that does not exist. Void is defined as a three-dimensional extension that can be assumed without matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary definition of place
Place is the inner surface of the containing body that touches the contained body.
2
Identify the secondary definition
Place is also said of the lower surface on which something rests.
3
Understand the rejected third sense
A third sense would be finite dimensions of the occupant, but this would imply void, which is not existent.
4
Define void
Void is a dimension in which three perpendicular dimensions can be assumed without matter.

Extracting Genus and Differentia from Definitions
Author enumerates definitions of sun, moon, fire, air, water, earth, and world.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the genus and differentia from any definition in natural philosophy and evaluate whether it is a complete hadd or a rasm.

Core Idea
Every definition in natural philosophy has a genus and differentia that must be extracted to evaluate its completeness.

This passage from al-Ghazali's Mizan al-‘Amal presents definitions of celestial bodies and elements. Each definition follows a precise structure: a broader category (genus) and a specific feature that distinguishes it (differentia). By learning to identify these components, you will gain a tool for analyzing definitions in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Look for the Arabic term at the beginning of each definition. For example, 'الشمس' (the sun) is the term being defined.
2
Extract the genus (jins)
The genus is the broader category the term belongs to. For the sun, the genus is 'كوكب' (planet). For fire, it is 'جسم بسيط' (simple body).
The genus often comes right after the term.
3
Extract the differentia (fasl)
The differentia is what distinguishes the term within its genus. For fire, the differentia includes being hot and dry, and moving away from the center. For the sun, it is being the greatest and brightest and residing in the fourth sphere.
Differentiae are often multiple attributes.
4
Check the definition's completeness
A complete definition (hadd) should include all instances and exclude all non-instances. For example, does 'hot and dry simple body' apply to all fire and only fire? Consider if any other element shares these traits.
Al-Ghazali's definitions are based on Aristotelian physics; later scholars refined them.
5
Identify multiple senses of a term
The definition of 'العالم' (the world) shows how a term can have multiple definitions: one as the totality of simple bodies, another as a collection of homogeneous existents (natural, psychic, intellectual worlds).
Be alert for similar polysemies in Islamic texts.

Commentary

Definitions of Motion and Time
Author defines motion, dahr, time, and the now.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion is exit from potentiality to actuality, and time is its measure.

In this lesson, we examine al-Razi's definitions of motion and time. These are not everyday meanings but precise philosophical terms used in demonstrations about the natural world. Understanding them is essential for following arguments about creation, change, and the nature of existence.

Definitions of Rest, Speed, and Inclination
Author defines rest, speed, slowness, inclination, lightness, heaviness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Razi's definitions of rest, speed, slowness, and inclination to analyze simple physical scenarios and identify their role in kalam arguments about motion.

Core Idea
Rest is absence of motion in a movable thing; speed relates distance and time; inclination resists prevention.

After defining motion and time, al-Razi defines other motion-related concepts: rest, speed, slowness, and inclination. These definitions complete the vocabulary needed for physical demonstrations. We will see how rest is defined as a privation, and how speed and inclination relate to force and resistance.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of rest (السكون)
Rest is defined as 'absence of motion in what is disposed to motion, such that it is in one state for a time, found in two nows'. Identify the genus (absence) and differentia (in what is disposed to motion, over time). Note that rest is a privation, not a positive quality.
2
Analyze speed and slowness
Speed: motion covering a long distance in a short time. Slowness: short distance in a long time. These are relational definitions. Compare them with the modern concept of velocity.
3
Define inclination (الاعتماد والميل)
Inclination is 'a quality by which the body pushes against what prevents it from moving in its direction.' This is a power that resists opposition. Understand it as the cause of motion or tendency.
4
Connect inclination to heaviness and lightness
Heaviness is a natural power to move to the center; lightness to move away. These are specific types of inclination. See how they form a system: natural motion vs. forced motion.

Definitions of Heat, Cold, Moisture, and Related Qualities
Author defines heat, cold, moisture, dryness, rough, smooth, hard, soft, pliable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the physical qualities defined by al-Razi into active and passive categories and explain their effects as described in the passage.

Core Idea
Heat and cold are active qualities that affect composition; moisture and dryness are passive qualities affecting shape and confinement.

In this lesson, we cover al-Razi's definitions of heat, cold, moisture, dryness, and related terms like rough/smooth, hard/soft, pliable. These qualities are fundamental in Aristotelian physics and are used in medicine and natural philosophy. We will analyze each definition to understand its genus and differential.

Guided Path
1
Identify the active qualities: heat and cold
Heat is an active quality that moves upward, gathers similars, separates dissimilars, and causes rarefaction/condensation. Cold is an active quality that gathers things and contracts bodies. Note that both are 'فعلية' (active).
2
Identify the passive qualities: moisture and dryness
Moisture is a passive quality that easily accepts shaping but does not retain it. Dryness is passive and difficult to shape. Note the contrast: moisture yields, dryness resists.
3
Analyze definitions of surface and resistance qualities
Rough: surface with parts of different positions. Smooth: parts of equal position. Hard: does not accept pushing inward easily. Soft: accepts. Pliable: not quick to separate. These are further specifications of physical interaction.
4
Connect to Islamic medicine (Tibb)
These qualities (hot, cold, moist, dry) are the four primary qualities in humoral medicine. For example, phlegm is cold and moist, bile is hot and dry. Al-Razi's definitions provide the theoretical foundation for medical theory.

Foundational Definitions: Al-Mala' and Al-'Adam
Author defines fullness and non-existence as foundational physical concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mala' and al-'adam as presented in the Mi'yar al-'Ilm and distinguish their precise meanings.

Core Idea
Al-mala' is impenetrability; al-'adam is the potential absence that drives change.

In this passage, al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani provides precise definitions for two foundational concepts: fullness (al-mala') and non-existence (al-'adam). These definitions are not merely lexical; they are shaped by the metaphysical concerns of Ibn Sina, particularly causality and motion. Understanding these definitions is essential for grasping later discussions on the principles of temporal events.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms being defined
Locate al-mala' and al-'adam in the Arabic passage. Note that each is defined separately with its own distinct structure.
Al-mala' is defined first, followed by al-'adam.
2
Analyze the definition of al-mala'
Al-mala' is defined as a body (jism) by virtue of its dimensions preventing another body from entering it. The genus is 'body', and the differentia is the dimension-based prevention. This captures the idea of impenetrability.
The phrase 'min haythu' indicates a specific perspective.
3
Analyze the definition of al-'adam
Al-'adam is defined as a principle of temporal events (hawadith). It is a privative definition: the absence of an essence in a subject that is capable of receiving it. The key phrase 'min sha'nihi' indicates potentiality.
This is not a simple absence but a relational non-existence tied to receptivity.
4
Memorize the exact wording
Pay attention to the Arabic phrasing: 'min haythu' for al-mala' and 'min sha'nihi' for al-'adam. These nuances are critical for accuracy.
Memorization anchors are essential for building on these concepts later.
5

The statement on the division into cause and effect, and the description of the existent as being a principle and cause. The principle is a name for that which has completed its existence in itself either from itself or from another, then from it comes the existence of another thing that subsists by it, and this is called cause in relation to that for which it is a principle. Then it cannot be other than either being like a part of the effect, such as wood and the form of the bed for the bed, or not being like a part. That which is like a part may not necessitate by its existence the existence of the effect in act, and it is called matter, like wood for the bed; and it may necessarily necessitate the existence of the effect in act, and it is the form of the bed, and it is called the material cause, and the form is the formal cause. That which is not like a part is divided into what is separate from the effect and what is adjacent. The adjacent is divided into that which acquires a quality from the effect and is described by it, like the subject of an accident—one says 'the subject is hot, cold, black, white'—and into the opposite, that the effect acquires the quality from the cause, so the effect is described by the cause, like the form of wateriness for the common matter between water and air during transformation. And that common thing may be called hyle, and there is no objection to using this name and replacing it. As for the separate, it is divided into that from which the existence comes and the existence is not for its sake, which is the efficient cause, like the carpenter for the bed; and into that for the sake of which the existence of the effect is, which is the final cause, like the suitability for sitting for the vine and the bed. The first cause is the end (ghaya), for without it the carpenter would not be a carpenter, and its being a cause precedes all other causes because by it the causes become causes, and its existence is posterior to the existence of the whole; only its causality is prior. And the cause is always nobler than the receiver because the agent is a bestower and the receiver is a beneficiary. Then the cause may be essential or accidental, potential or actual, proximate or remote, and their examples have preceded.

Author classifies non-part causes into adjacent and separate, and within separate identifies efficient and final

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between adjacent and separate non-part causes, and within separate causes, identify the efficient and final causes.

Having covered part-like causes, the author now turns to causes that are not like a part of the effect. He divides them into adjacent (ملاق) and separate (مباين). Adjacent causes come in two varieties, and separate causes are either efficient or final.

Distinguishing Quwwa as Potentiality from Quwwa as Active Power
Distinguishing potentiality and active power with three differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between quwwa as potentiality (quwwa bi-iza' al-fi'l) and quwwa as active power (quwwa fa'ila) using the three criteria presented by the author.

Core Idea
Quwwa has two senses: one fades upon actualization, the other powers through it.

The term quwwa can be ambiguous: does it mean a passive potential or an active power? The author provides three clear differences to separate these meanings. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in later discussions of causality and change in Islamic philosophy and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings
Locate the two senses of quwwa: 'quwwa bi-iza' al-fi'l' (potentiality in contrast to actuality) and 'quwwa fa'ila' (active power). Notice that the author warns against confusing them.
The first is passive, the second is active.
2
First difference: persistence
The potentiality ceases when the thing becomes actual (e.g., the potential to become a tree ends when the tree exists). The active power remains while acting (e.g., fire's power to burn persists while burning).
Confusing these leads to thinking active power disappears during action.
3
Second difference: attribution
Active power is attributed to the moving principle (mabda' muharrik), while potentiality is attributed to the passive thing (amr munfa'il).
4
Third difference: nature of the corresponding act
The act opposite active power is a change (istihāla, kawn, or haraka) coming from a principle not affected by it. The act opposite potentiality is any actual existential occurrence (mawjūd hāsil), whether action, passion, or state.
5
Apply to the Ash'ari objection
The objector denies active power in fire. The author responds that he is only explaining the meaning of the term, not proving the reality. This shows the importance of keeping the two senses conceptually distinct.
The author refers to Tahafut al-Falasifah for the ontological discussion.

Complete and Incomplete: Definitions
Author defines 'complete' and 'incomplete' and distinguishes them from universal and particular.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of 'tamm' (complete) and its four modes, and distinguish it from 'kulli' (universal).

Core Idea
Complete means possessing all that belongs to it, not being universal.

The author defines two key terms: complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis). He explicitly warns us not to confuse them with universal (kulli) and particular (juz'i). This distinction is crucial because in logic, completeness is a metaphysical notion, while universality is a logical one. Let's unpack the definition carefully.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the distinction from universal/particular
The author begins by stating that ‘complete’ and ‘incomplete’ are NOT the same as ‘universal’ and ‘particular’. This is a common confusion. Understand that a thing can be complete (having all its parts) without being universal (predicated of many).
2
Extract the core definition of complete
‘Complete’ is defined as that which possesses everything that should belong to it, and nothing can belong to it that does not. This includes both actual and potential aspects.
The definition is negative as well: ‘there is nothing that can exist for it except that it exists for it.’
3
Identify the four modes of completeness
The author specifies four modes: (1) perfection of existence, (2) active power, (3) passive power, (4) quantity. Memorize these as the ways something can be complete.
These are not mutually exclusive; something can be complete in more than one mode.
4
Define the incomplete as opposite
The incomplete is simply the opposite: lacking what should belong to it. No extra complexity.
5
Apply the distinction with Islamic examples
Consider a human being: they are complete in existence (they exist), but not necessarily in active power (a child is not fully developed). Conversely, the universal ‘human’ is universal but not necessarily complete (it is an abstract concept, not a concrete thing).

Types of 'One' Based on Genus, Species, and Accident
Author enumerates the first five types of 'one' with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the types of 'one' based on genus, species, common accident, analogy, and subject using examples from the text.

Core Idea
The term 'one' has multiple meanings in logic, each revealing a different mode of unity.

The author begins by listing several ways in which things can be said to be one. These include being one in genus (like horse and human sharing animality), one in species (like the ignorant and knowledgeable sharing humanity), one by common accident (like crow and mouse both black), one by analogy (like the relation of king to city and intellect to soul), and one in subject (like the smell, taste, and color of an apple coexisting in the same apple). Commentary
This passage is a classification of the different ways in which the term 'one' (al-wahid) can be predicated. The author lists several types, each corresponding to a mode of unity or indivisibility. The passage begins by discussing things that are 'one in genus' (al-wahid bi al-jins), such as horse and human sharing animality; they differ only in number, species, and accidents but not in the genus itself. 'One in subject' (al-wahid fi al-mawdu') refers to attributes like smell, taste, and color of an apple coexisting in the same apple, but not in every respect. 'One by meaning in number' (al-wahid bi al-ma'na fi al-adad) refers to something like the head, which is one for a person but can divide into parts each having the meaning of head. 'One by definition' (al-wahid bi al-hadd) is something whose reality is unique, like the sun. Finally, it links the different types of unity to relations of similarity, equality, conformity, homogeneity, and likeness. The whole analysis clarifies the various meanings of 'one' and their opposites.

'One in subject' (al-wahid fi al-mawdu') refers to attributes like smell, taste, and color of an apple coexisting in the same apple, but not in every respect. 'One by meaning in number' (al-wahid bi al-ma'na fi al-adad) refers to something like the head, which is one for a person but can divide into parts each having the meaning of head. 'One by definition' (al-wahid bi al-hadd) is something whose reality is unique, like the sun. The author then subdivides based on multiplicity: things with actual multiplicity but one by composition (e.g., a house), things with potential multiplicity (e.g., body with continuous form), and things with no multiplicity at all (e.g., a simple substance according to philosophers, and the First Truth). For theologians, the indivisible atom (jawhar fard) is also one by number. The passage concludes by relating unity (al-wahda) to multiplicity (al-kathra) and defining the much (al-kathir) as that which contains a one but is not itself a one. Finally, it links the different types of unity to relations of similarity, equality, conformity, homogeneity, and likeness. The whole analysis clarifies the various meanings of 'one' and their opposites.

The Fourfold Division: Necessity in Different States
Third division: fourfold classification by state-relative necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories that arise when considering whether a thing has necessity in any state, using examples like writing, movement, and eclipse.

Core Idea
Some things are necessary under certain conditions but not in all states, yielding four modal categories.

The third division adds nuance by considering whether a possible thing has necessity in any state at all. This yields four categories: necessary (always necessary), possible (no necessity in any state), existent necessarily (necessary in some state), and existent without any necessity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the new criterion
The third division asks: does the thing have necessity in existence in any state? If yes, it is not a pure possible; if no, it is a pure possible.
This is finer than the second division.
2
Analyze the examples
The text gives three examples: writing for a human (no necessity in any state), movement for a moving thing (necessary in the state of being moving), and eclipse for the moon (necessary when earth interposes).
Writing is a pure possible; movement and eclipse are conditional necessities.
3
Derive the four categories
From these, we get: (1) necessary (wajib), (2) pure possible (mumkin), (3) existent necessarily in some state (mawjud daruratan), and (4) existent with no necessity at all (mawjud la darurata lahu al-battata).
Categories 3 and 4 are subdivisions of contingent things.

Everything Necessary by Another is Possible in Itself
Author proves modal relativity of necessity by another

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that a thing necessary by another is possible in itself using the tripartite modal division, and distinguish between intrinsic and extrinsic impossibility.

Core Idea
Modality is relative: a thing can be possible in itself, necessary by its cause, and impossible without it.

This passage is a cornerstone of Islamic modal logic. The author proves that anything that has necessity from an external cause must be possible in its own essence. This insight prevents confusion between intrinsic and extrinsic modal properties and lays the groundwork for understanding causation in kalam and falsafa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim and tripartite framework
Find the claim that 'everything necessary by another is possible in itself'. Note the three options for a thing's essence: possible, necessary, impossible.
The author uses a disjunctive argument: the thing must be one of these three.
2
Follow the disjunctive elimination proof
The author eliminates the two false options: if it were impossible by itself, existence by another would be inconceivable; if it were necessary by itself, it would not need another. Therefore, it must be possible by itself.
This is a valid disjunctive syllogism.
3
Understand the relative nature of modality
The author shows that a thing can be possible in itself, but when its cause is present it becomes necessary, and when its cause is absent it becomes impossible. These are not contradictory because they refer to different conditions.
Possibility, necessity, and impossibility can coexist under different considerations.
4
Distinguish impossibility by itself and by another
The author extends the analysis: some impossibilities are intrinsic (e.g., combining black and white), others are extrinsic (e.g., resurrection today due to God's knowledge). Identify the difference.
Extrinsic impossibility depends on an external preventing factor.

الثاني : أن كلّ ما هو واجب الوجود بغيره فهو ممكن الوجود بذاته، لأنه إما أن يكون باعتبار ذاته ممكن الوجود أو واجب الوجود أو ممتنع الوجود، والقسمان الأخيران باطلان إذ لو كان ممتنع الوجود بذاته لما تصور له وجود بغيره، ولو كان واجب الوجود بذاته لما كان واجب الوجود بغيره لما سبق، فثبت أنه ممكن الوجود بذاته. والحاصل أن كلّ ممكن بذاته فهو واجب بغيره، فالممكن إن اعتبرت علّته وقدر وجودها كان واجب الوجود، وإن قدر عدم ممتنع الوجود، وإن لم يلتفت إلى علته لا باعتبار العدم ولا باعتبار الوجود كان له في ذاته المعنى الثالث، وهو الإمكان، فإذن كلّ ممكن فهو ممتنع وواجب، أي ممتنع عند تقدير عدم العلّة فيكون ممتنعاً بغيره لا لذاته أو ممكناً من حيث ذاته إذا لم تعتبر معه علّته نفياً وإثباتاً وليس الجمع بين هذه الأمور متناقضاً، بل نزيد عليه فنقول : الممتنع أيضاً منقسم إلى ممتنع لذاته وإلى ممتنع لغيره، فاجتماع السواد والبياض ممتنع لذاته، وكون السلب والإثبات في شيء واحد صادقاً ممتنعاً لذاته، وفرض القيامة اليوم، وقد علم الله تعالى أنه لا يقيمها اليوم مستحيل، ولكن لا لذاته كاستحالة الجمع بين البياض والسواد، ولكن لسبق علم الله بأنه لا يكون واستحالة كون العلم جهلاً ، فكان امتناعه لغيره لا لذاته
Second: Everything that is necessary existence by another is possible existence by itself, because either it is, with respect to its essence, possible existence, or necessary existence, or impossible existence. The last two are false: if it were impossible existence by itself, existence by another would be inconceivable; if it were necessary existence by itself, it would not be necessary existence by another, as established before. So it is established that it is possible existence by itself. The conclusion is that everything possible by itself is necessary by another. The possible, if its cause is considered and its existence is supposed, is necessary existence; if nonexistence is supposed, it is impossible existence; if its cause is not considered, neither as nonexistent nor existent, it has in itself the third meaning, which is possibility. Therefore, every possible is impossible and necessary, i.e., impossible when nonexistence of the cause is supposed, so it is impossible by another not by itself, or possible from the aspect of its essence if its cause is not considered positively or negatively. The combination of these is not contradictory. Rather, we add: The impossible is also divided into impossible by itself and impossible by another. The combination of black and white is impossible by itself; the truth of negation and affirmation in one thing is impossible by itself; supposing the resurrection today while God knows that He will not bring it today is impossible, but not by itself like the impossibility of combining black and white, but because of God's prior knowledge that it will not happen and the impossibility of knowledge becoming ignorance. So its impossibility is by another, not by itself.

Commentary
This passage provides a tripartite division of essence (dhat) into possible, necessary, and impossible. The author argues that anything necessary by another must be possible in itself. The proof uses modal categories: if it were impossible by itself, it could never exist; if necessary by itself, it would not need another. Therefore it must be possible by itself. Then the author clarifies that possibility, necessity, and impossibility are not mutually exclusive when considering different relations. A possible thing, when its cause is present, becomes necessary (wajib bi-ghayrihi), and when its cause is absent, becomes impossible (mumtani' bi-ghayrihi). This is not a contradiction because the modal status is relative to the cause. The author extends this to the impossible: some impossibilities are intrinsic (e.g., combining black and white), while others are extrinsic (e.g., the resurrection today due to God's knowledge). This distinction is crucial for understanding causation and modality in Islamic philosophy.
