Evaluating Truth in Poetic Form
Author warns against dismissing all poetry as false and cites a truthful poetic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish truth-apt content from poetic form and identify truthful statements within poetry.

Core Idea
Poetry can carry truth; don't confuse form with content.

This passage cautions against dismissing all poetry as false. The author acknowledges that some poetry contains wisdom and truth, and urges the student to look at the meaning rather than the form. We will learn how to extract truth from poetic expression and apply this to Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the propositional content
Strip away meter and rhyme to find the core claim. In the example, the core claim is: 'Whoever spends hours gathering wealth for fear of poverty effectively causes poverty.'
2
Assess the claim's correspondence to reality
Determine if the proposition is true (haqq) or false. The claim accurately reflects a real consequence of miserly hoarding, so it is truthful.
3
Recognize the role of poetic form
Note how the meter and rhythm enhance impact but do not alter the truth value. The form makes the truth more effective in the soul, but the truth stands on its own.
4
Apply the principle to other examples
Practice by finding another poetic statement in Islamic literature, isolating its proposition, and evaluating its truth independently of its poetic form.
For instance, a line from a poem praising generosity: 'The generous hand is a cloud of rain.' The proposition is that generosity is beneficial like rain, which is true.

And when we have disparaged the state of poetry, it should not be thought that all poetry is false, for indeed from poetry is wisdom, and from eloquence is sorcery. So do not look at the form of poetry, but observe the meanings in all matters to be upon the straight path.

Commentary
This passage cautions against the wholesale rejection of poetry as false. The author acknowledges that some poetry contains wise truths, citing a line condemning miserliness. The key lesson is to evaluate the meaning, not the form. The example shows that a true proposition about human behavior can be expressed poetically without losing its truth. This is a methodological point for the student: when analyzing arguments, one must look beyond outward form to the content.

Imaginative Premises: Definition and Distinction from Conjecture
Author defines imaginative premises and explains their nature and inferiority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify imaginative premises (al-mukhayyilat) and distinguish them from verified conjecture (al-maznun al-muhaqqaq) using the author's criteria.

Core Idea
Imaginative premises are emotional inclinations, not knowledge; they move people but cannot justify fiqh rulings.

In classical Islamic logic, premises are classified by their epistemic value. Among the lowest are imaginative premises (al-mukhayyilat), which influence our feelings but provide no proof. This lesson explores the author's definition and examples, showing why such premises have no place in jurisprudential or theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Locate the formal definition
Find the sentence starting with 'وهي تشبيه الشيء' and identify the key elements: likening, shared description not the cause of praise/blame, and resulting inclination.
The definition is: 'likening a thing to something reprehensible or commendable because it shares with it a description that is not the cause of the reprehensibility or commendability, so the soul inclines due to it.'
2
Identify the cause of inclination
Note that the inclination is due to a shared description that is not the actual cause of praise or blame; differentiate this from verified conjecture which is based on real evidence.
3
Examine the examples
Analyze the examples: the blue-eyed blond (hearsay creates bias), the woman's name (aversion due to association), logic/arithmetic as atheist sciences (rejection due to label). For each, identify what is being likened to what, and why the inclination arises.
The examples show that the cause is not the intrinsic quality but an irrelevant shared description.
4
Apply the author's verdict
Check the conclusion: these imaginative effects are neither knowledge nor valid conjecture, so they cannot serve as premises in certainties, conjectural matters, or jurisprudential matters.
The author explicitly excludes them from demonstrative, probable, and legal reasoning.
5
Compare with verified conjecture
List characteristics of imaginative premises vs. verified conjecture based on the passage: imaginative premises are based on emotional association, while verified conjecture is based on real evidence.
Use the contrast between 'سبب محقق' (verified cause) and 'خيال محض' (pure imagination).

ولكنه ميل بسبب كإعتقادك أن من يخرج بالليل فيخرج لريبة، فإن ميل النفس إلى هذه التهمة لسبب. ولو كرر على سمع جماعة أن الأزرق الأشقر مثلا لا يكون إلا خائنا خبيثا فإذا رأوه كان ميل نفسهم إلى اعتقاد الخيانة أكثر من الميل إلى اعتقاد الصيانة، وهذا من غير سبب محقق بل خيال محض بسبب السماع. ولذا قيل: من يسمع يخل. فبين هذا وبين المظنون المحقق فرق ويقرب من هذا المخيلات وهي تشبيه الشيء بشيء مستقبح أو مستحسن لمشاركته إياه في وصف ليس هو سبب القبح والحسن، فتميل النفس بسببه ميلا وليس ذلك من الظن في شيء، وهذا مع أخس الرتب يحرك الناس إلى أكثر الأفعال، وعنه تصدر أكثر التصرفات من الخلق إقداما وإحجاما، وهي المقدمات الشعرية التي ذكرناها فلا ترى عاقلا ينفك عن التأثر به حتى أن المرأة التي يخطبها الرجل إذا ذكر أن اسمها اسم بعض الهنود أو السودان المستقبحين نفر الطبع عنها لقبح الاسم، فيقاوم هذا الخيال الجمال ويورث محبة ما، وحتى أن علم الحساب والمنطق الذي ليس فيه تعرض للمذاهب بنفي ولا إثبات إذا قيل: إنه من علوم الفلاسفة الملحدين نفر طباع أهل الدين عنه، وهذا الميل والنفرة الصادران عن هذا الجنس ليسا بظن ولا علم فلا يصلح ما يثيرهما أن يجعل مقدمة لا في القطعيات ولا في الظنيات ولا في الفقهيات.
But it is an inclination due to a cause, like your belief that whoever goes out at night goes out for suspicion, for the soul's inclination towards this accusation is due to a cause. And if you repeatedly tell a group that, for example, a blue-eyed blond is only treacherous and malicious, then when they see him, their inclination to believe in his treachery is greater than their inclination to believe in his integrity, and this is without a verified cause, but rather pure imagination due to hearing. Hence it is said: whoever hears, is deluded. So between this and verified conjecture there is a difference. And close to this are the imaginative premises, which are likening a thing to something reprehensible or commendable because it shares with it a description that is not the cause of the reprehensibility or commendability, so the soul inclines due to it, and that is not part of conjecture at all. And this, although the lowest rank, moves people to most actions, and from it originate most behaviors of people, both undertaking and refraining. And they are the poetic premises we mentioned, and you do not see a rational person free from being affected by it, to the extent that when a man proposes to a woman and mentions that her name is the name of some Indian or repulsive Sudanese, nature shuns her due to the ugliness of the name, and this imagination resists beauty and produces some love. And even arithmetic and logic, which do not engage with doctrines positively or negatively, when it is said that they are among the sciences of atheist philosophers, the natures of religious people shun them. And this inclination and aversion arising from this kind are neither conjecture nor knowledge, so what they arouse cannot be made a premise in certainties, nor in conjectural matters, nor in jurisprudential matters.
Source pages p. 98

Commentary
This passage is a definitional exposition of imaginative premises (al-mukhayyilat) and their distinction from verified conjecture. The author begins by describing a type of psychological inclination that arises from a cause, such as believing that someone who goes out at night must be up to no good. This inclination is based not on verified evidence but on repeated hearsay, leading to a biased belief. The author then contrasts this with verified conjecture (al-maznun al-muhaqqaq). He introduces imaginative premises as similar: they involve likening something to something else due to a shared description that is not the actual cause of praise or blame, causing an emotional inclination. This inclination is not true conjecture; it is of the lowest rank yet it powerfully motivates human actions and behaviors. The author emphasizes that everyone, even rational people, is affected by such poetic premises. He gives examples: a woman's name can cause aversion despite her beauty, and the label 'atheist philosophy' can cause religious people to reject logic and arithmetic. The conclusion is that these imaginative effects are neither knowledge nor valid conjecture, so they cannot serve as premises in any demonstrative, probable, or jurisprudential reasoning. All examples used are from everyday human psychology, not from Islamic sciences specifically, but the author mentions arithmetic and logic as objects of aversion due to association with atheists, which is relevant to Islamic context.
