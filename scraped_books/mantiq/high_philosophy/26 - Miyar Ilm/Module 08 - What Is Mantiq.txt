What Logic Teaches: The Transition from Known to Unknown
Author defines logic's subject and begins the division of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define logic's subject according to al-Ghazali and distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq as the two divisions of knowledge.

Core Idea
Logic is the tool for moving from known concepts to unknown conclusions.

This passage from al-Ghazali's Mi'yar al-'Ilm sets the foundation for all of logic. It tells us what logic is about: not facts, but the method of correctly moving from what we already know to what we do not yet know. It also introduces a critical division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq, which frames every logical operation that follows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of logic
Find where the passage states what logic teaches. It says 'its content is teaching the manner of transition from the forms present in your mind to the matters absent from you.' This is the core definition: logic is about the process of arriving at new knowledge from existing mental forms.
The 'forms' refer to concepts already known; the 'matters absent' are unknown conclusions.
2
Understand the concept of intiqal (transition)
The passage emphasizes that this transition has a specific form and order. If followed correctly, it leads to the goal; if neglected, it fails. The correct method is very similar to incorrect ones, which is why a science of logic is needed.
Intiqal is the reasoning process itself, not the content of the premises or conclusion.
3
Recognize the division of knowledge into tasawwur and tasdiq
The author states that the goal of logic is knowledge, and knowledge is divided into knowledge of essences (tasawwur) and implicitly knowledge of judgments (tasdiq). Tasawwur is knowing what something is, e.g., knowing 'human'. Tasdiq, though not fully stated here, is knowing that a proposition is true.
This division is foundational: every logical operation deals with either concepts or judgments.
4
Connect the definition to the division
The transition (intiqal) that logic teaches moves either from a tasawwur to another tasawwur (e.g., defining terms) or from tasdiq to tasdiq (e.g., syllogisms). Understanding this prepares you for the rest of the science.
Later lessons will detail how each type of transition works.

Two Types of Knowledge: Conception and Assent
Author divides knowledge into conception and assent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) and provide an Islamic example of each.

Core Idea
Knowledge is either knowing a thing's essence or knowing a relation between essences.

After defining logic's goal as knowledge, the author divides knowledge into two fundamental types: conception and assent. This lesson will help you recognize these two cognitive acts, which form the foundation of all theoretical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Locate the division of knowledge
Find where the author says 'the sought objective is knowledge, and knowledge divides into...' and note the two categories.
2
Define conception (tasawwur)
What is 'knowledge of the essences of things'? The example given is knowing 'man' or 'tree'. Conception is mere awareness of what something is, without any judgment.
Think of it as having a mental image or understanding of a term.
3
Define assent (tasdiq)
What is 'knowledge of the relation of these conceived essences'? The example: 'Man is an animal' or 'Man is not a stone'. Assent involves affirming or negating one thing about another.
Assent can be true or false; conception is simply understanding.
4
Apply to the examples given later
Look at the two examples: 'al-'aqar is wine' (conception) and 'the world has a Creator' (assent). Identify which type of knowledge each example aims to produce.
This reinforces the distinction.

Tools of Knowledge: Definitions and Arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given Islamic text passage aims to provide conception (via a definition) or assent (via an argument), and name the corresponding logical tool.

Core Idea
Definitions give conception; arguments give assent.

Having established the two types of knowledge, the author now introduces the tools that produce them: the explicative statement (qawl sharh) for conception and the argument (hujja) for assent. This lesson shows how to match the tool to the cognitive need, using examples from the passage.

2
Identify the tool for assent

Core Idea
Words indicate meanings in three ways, but only correspondence and inclusion serve definitions.

In this passage, the author lays out the foundational classification of how words relate to their meanings. Understanding these three types of indication is crucial because definitions in logic—and in Islamic sciences like fiqh and kalam—depend on precise word-meaning relationships. We will explore each type with examples from Arabic, and see why the third type, implication, is excluded from definitions.

Commentary
This passage defines the three types of indication (dalala) by words. The author begins by stating that words indicate meanings in three distinct ways. Implication is excluded because it was not intentionally put by the language-maker, and because the implications of a thing can be infinite, leading to an absurdity where a word would indicate unlimited meanings. Thus definitions rely only on the first two types.

The Logical Definition of the Noun (Ism)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentiae in the logical definition of ism and explain how it differs from the grammatical definition.

Core Idea
The noun in logic is a conventional sound, abstracted from time, with no independent parts—a precise tool for propositional building.

In this lesson, we examine the logician's definition of the noun, which is the foundational building block of every proposition. Unlike the grammarian's definition, the logician strips the noun down to its essential logical features. This precise definition allows us to use nouns in syllogisms without ambiguity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus
Find the broad category to which the noun belongs. In the definition, the noun is first called a 'sound' (sawt). This is the material basis, common to all uttered words.
The genus is the most general category; here it is 'sawt', distinguishing nouns from non-vocal signs.
2
Extract the first differentia: mode of signification
The noun signifies 'by convention' (bi-tawatu'). This means the connection between sound and meaning is based on human agreement, not nature. This excludes natural cries and onomatopoeia.
This differentia distinguishes conventional words from non-conventional sounds.
3
Extract the second differentia: temporal abstractedness
The noun is 'abstracted from time' (mujarrad 'an al-zaman). Unlike the verb, which carries temporal meaning (past, present, future), the noun does not indicate time.
This is a key difference from the grammatical definition, where nouns can have gender and number but still lack tense.
4
Extract the third differentia: non-compositionality of parts
No part of the noun, when isolated, indicates any meaning that contributes to the whole. For example, in 'book', the letters 'b', 'o', 'o', 'k' alone do not signify. This excludes compound expressions.
This differentia ensures the noun is a simple term, not a phrase.
5
Extract the fourth differentia: determinate meaning
The noun indicates a 'determinate meaning' (ma'nan muhassal), a specific concept. This excludes particles like 'هل' (interrogative), which do not express a complete concept on their own.
This differentia distinguishes nouns from particles.

Core Idea
Analogical words have different meanings linked by resemblance and transfer.

Three Types of Words Used for Different Meanings
Author defines musta'ara, manqula, and mushtaraka with their distinguishing features.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between musta'ara, manqula, and mushtaraka words and classify given examples correctly.

Core Idea
Words can share meanings through metaphor, transfer, or original commonality.

In this passage, the author classifies words that have multiple meanings into three categories. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for avoiding confusion when interpreting Islamic texts, where a single term may carry different technical meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its meanings
Take a word that appears with multiple meanings. List all its known meanings from the text or context.
This step isolates the data needed for classification.
2
Determine if one meaning is original
Check whether the word's primary, original meaning is known (via linguistic assignment) and whether other meanings are derived from it (through metaphor or transfer) or assigned equally from the start.
Original meaning is key for musta'ara and manqula.
3
Assess the permanence of derived meanings
If a derived meaning is used only occasionally and not fixed, it is musta'ara (metaphorical). If it becomes permanent and the word is used for both meanings regularly, it is manqula (transferred).
Permanence distinguishes musta'ara from manqula.
4
Confirm homonymy (mushtarak) if no derivation
If the word was assigned to multiple meanings without any one being original or derived from another, it is mushtaraka (homonymous). The word 'eye' is an example: it refers to a water spring, a dinar, the sun, and the seeing organ, all equally assigned.
Homonyms have no transfer; they are originally common.

The Perpetual Proposition (Dā'im): Always True but Not Necessarily So
Author defines perpetual proposition and distinguishes it from necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a perpetual proposition (dā'im) from a necessary proposition (ḍarūrī) by analyzing the examples of individuals and universals provided by the author.

Core Idea
Perpetual truth can arise from accidental continuity, not essential necessity.

In this lesson, we explore a subtle but important classification in Islamic logic: the perpetual proposition (dā'im). While it is always true, its truth is not necessary—it depends on accidental or existential circumstances. The author illustrates this with clear examples from individuals and universals. Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to evaluate premises in theological and legal reasoning, where confusing perpetual with necessary can lead to faulty conclusions.

From the answer, deduce that a perpetual proposition is one that is always true, but its truth is not due to essential necessity; rather, it is due to accidental or existential continuity. Note that the author coins the term 'existential proposition' (qaḍiyya wujūdiyya) for individuals.

Analyze the individual example (black person)
The example: 'You may say he is black-skinned as long as skin exists, but blackness is not necessary for his skin; rather it happens to exist perpetually.' The perpetual truth here is accidental—the skin could have been white. This illustrates an existential proposition.
The blackness is perpetually present but not essential; it is a contingent property that happens to persist.
4

The example: 'Every celestial body is either rising or setting'—true at every hour, but not necessary for its essence (unlike rationality for humans). The perpetual truth arises from position and motion, not from the celestial body's intrinsic nature.
The contrast with 'animal for human' shows essential necessity: being an animal is part of the essence of being human.
5
Synthesize the distinction
Combine the individual and universal examples to see that perpetual truth can stem from accidental or existential circumstances, not only from essential attributes. This distinction prevents conflating constant accidental properties with necessary ones.
This distinction is crucial in evaluating arguments: a premise may be perpetually true but not necessarily true, affecting its reliability in syllogisms.

Defining Substance and Distinguishing Essential Forms from Accidents
Author defines substance and distinguishes essential forms from accidents using water/heat examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the criterion of quiddity change to distinguish essential forms from accidents in examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Substance is defined negatively; essential forms change the quiddity, accidents do not.

This lesson introduces the foundational logical definition of substance (jawhar) as 'that which exists not in a subject.' The author clarifies this definition using concrete examples from the natural world: the form of water versus heat and cold. Understanding this distinction is crucial for anyone studying the categories of existence in Islamic philosophy and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of substance
Locate the sentence where the author defines 'jawhar' as 'al-mawjud la fi mawdu'.' Note that this is a negative definition—it tells us what substance is not.
The term 'mawdu`' (subject) is defined as the proximate substrate that subsists by itself.
2
Understand the meaning of 'not in a subject'
Read the author's explanation: the subject is not constituted by the thing that inheres in it. Example: color in a body—the body's essences (as a body) does not depend on the color. Contrast this with the form of water in water, where the water's essence is constituted by that form.
3
Distinguish essential form from accident using the water/heat example
The author says: when heat or cold leave water, the water remains water (its quiddity unchanged). But when the form of water leaves (e.g., water turning to air), the quiddity changes—it is no longer water. Thus the form of water is essential, heat/cold are accidental.
Essential forms are those whose departure changes the quiddity; accidents are those whose departure does not.
4
Apply the criterion to an Islamic science example
Consider a property like 'being wet' for water. Is it essential or accidental? Being wet is inseparable from water, but does its loss change the quiddity? Actually water can be turned into vapor which is not wet, but then it is no longer water. So 'wetness' is related to the form of water? Think through similar examples from Islamic cosmology or medicine.
Use this criterion to classify properties in your own study of Falsafa or Kalam.

Commentary
This passage provides the definition of substance (jawhar) as 'that which exists not in a subject.' However, the author insists that despite terminological differences, the real distinction must be maintained: some properties (like the form of water) define the very essence of a thing, while others (like heat) are accidental and can change without altering the essence.

Author discusses terminological difference with mutakallimun regarding 'accident'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Terminology may differ, but the real distinction between essential and accidental properties is objective.

In this lesson, we explore a subtle but important terminological nuance: the mutakallimun (theologians) consider even the essential form of water to be an 'accident' because it exists in a substrate. The author, while respecting their convention, argues that one cannot deny the real difference between a form that changes quiddity and one that does not. This lesson sharpens your awareness of how logical distinctions must be kept separate from terminological disagreements.

1

This shows the author's respectful attitude toward terminological differences.
3
Identify the non-negotiable logical point
Despite the terminological freedom, the author insists: 'la yumkinu inkar al-farq' — one cannot deny the difference between heat (whose departure does not change quiddity) and the form of water (whose departure does).
The logical reality is invariant, even if words differ.
4
Apply to a debate in Islamic thought

Classification of Causes: Formal, Efficient, and Final
Classification of causes into formal, adjacent, and separate, with emphasis on efficient and final.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish formal, efficient, and final causes as defined in the passage.

Core Idea
Every effect has three causes: formal (essence), efficient (agent), and final (purpose).

In this passage, the author provides a detailed classification of causes (علل) that goes beyond the simple four-cause scheme. He distinguishes between causes that are like a part of the effect (formal cause) and those that are not. Among the latter, he further divides into adjacent and separate causes, with the separate causes including efficient and final causes. Understanding this classification is essential for analyzing causality in Islamic philosophy and theology.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the primary division
Causes are divided into those that are like a part of the effect (formal cause) and those that are not. The formal cause is the essence or structure that makes a thing what it is.
The formal cause is called 'ṣūra' in Arabic.
2
Examine the adjacent cause
The adjacent cause is in contact with the effect. It either receives a quality from the effect (like a subject bearing an accident) or imparts a quality to the effect (like the form of water imprinting on matter).
This corresponds to material causation in some sense.
3
Identify the separate cause
The separate cause is distinct from the effect and divides into efficient and final causes.
4
Understand the efficient cause
The efficient cause is that from which existence proceeds, but not for its own sake. It is the agent, like a carpenter making a bed.
Arabic term: 'fā‘iliyya'.
5
Understand the final cause
The final cause is that for the sake of which the effect exists. It is the purpose or goal, like suitability for sitting for a bed. The text emphasizes it is the 'first cause' because it motivates the efficient cause.
Arabic term: 'ghā’iyya'.

The adjacent divides into that which acquires a quality from the effect and is described by it, like the subject of an accident, for it is said: the subject is hot, cold, black, white; and into the opposite of that, that the effect acquires the attribute from the cause, so the effect is described by the cause, like the form of water for the matter common between water and air during transformation. And that common thing is sometimes called hyle, and there is no dispute over using this name and replacing it. And it is ??? cause. As for the separate, it divides into that from which existence proceeds and existence is not for its sake, which is the efficient cause, like the carpenter for the bed; and into that for which the existence of the effect exists, which is the final cause, like suitability for sitting for the sake of the grapevine and the bed. The first cause is the purpose: if not for it, the carpenter would not become a carpenter; it is a cause preceding other causes, for by it the causes become causes, and its existence is posterior to the existence of the whole; only its causality is prior. The cause is always nobler than the recipient because the agent is a bestower and the recipient is a receiver. Then the cause may be essential or accidental, potential or actual, near or far, and examples of these have preceded.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies causes (علل) as part of a broader discussion on causality. The author begins by distinguishing between causes that are like a part of the effect (i.e., formal cause) and those that are not. Within the latter, he identifies two types: mulaqi (adjacent) and mubayin (separate). The adjacent cause is one that receives a quality from the effect, analogous to a subject bearing an accident (e.g., we say 'the subject is hot'), or inversely, the effect receives a quality from the cause (e.g., the form of water imprints on shared matter during transformation). The separate cause splits into the efficient cause (فاعلية) – that from which existence emanates but not for its sake, like the carpenter making a bed – and the final cause (غائية) – that for the sake of which the effect exists, like the suitability for sitting for the bed. The author emphasizes that the final cause is the first cause, as it moves the efficient cause; without it, the carpenter would not act. He notes that the cause is nobler than the recipient. Finally, he mentions that causes can be essential/accidental, potential/actual, and near/far, with examples having been given earlier.
