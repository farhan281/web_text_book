Three Judges: Sensory, Estimative, and Rational
Author refutes the critic who claims reason needs no standard by explaining the three cognitive faculties and their errors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three cognitive faculties (sensory, estimative, rational) and explain why only the rational judge is reliable, using examples from sense perception.

Core Idea
Reason alone is not enough — the soul must be trained to overcome sensory and estimative illusions.

Have you ever questioned the need for logic when reason seems naturally correct? In this passage, al-Ghazali responds to a critic who dismisses the need for a logical standard. He reveals that the human soul is initially dominated by sensory and estimative judges, which constantly mislead us. Only through rigorous logical training can the rational judge correct these errors and lead us to truth.

2

3

4
Explain the dominance of lower judges
Why does the soul initially follow sensory/estimative judges? They precede the rational judge in early life and the soul becomes habituated to their rule, making it difficult later to accept rational correction.
5
Conclude the need for logic
This demonstrates the necessity of a standard (miyar) to correct errors. Without logical training, the soul remains captive to habitual errors; logic trains the rational judge to prevail.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of a hypothetical critic who claims that reason alone suffices, so logic as a 'standard' (miyar) is unnecessary. Al-Ghazali responds by distinguishing three cognitive faculties (judges) within the human soul: sensory (hissi), estimative (wahmi), and rational (aqli). He argues that only the rational judge is correct, but the soul initially yields more to sensory and estimative judges because they dominate from early childhood. The soul becomes habituated to their rulings, and it becomes difficult to accept the rational judge's corrections. To illustrate the errors of the sensory judge, he gives examples: the sun appears small (size of a shield) but reason knows it is larger than Earth; stars appear scattered but reason knows their true sizes; shadows appear stationary but reason knows they constantly move; a child's growth appears static but reason knows it is continuous. These sensory illusions are numerous, and he only provides a few. Finally, he mentions the estimative judge (wahm), warning against its false judgments, such as positing an existent without direction (a reference to the wahm's tendency to attribute spatial location to immaterial beings). The passage thus establishes the need for logic to correct the errors of lower faculties.

Key Terms
الحاكم الحسي (al-hakim al-hissi): The sensory judge; the faculty that makes judgments based on sense perception, which is prone to error. [Note: In later psychology, this may be refined into external and internal senses; here it is a broad category.]
الحاكم الوهمي (al-hakim al-wahmi): The estimative judge; the faculty of estimation (wahm) that makes non-rational judgments, often about meanings in physical things. [Note: In the Peripatetic tradition, wahm is a distinct internal sense; here it is grouped with the sensory as a source of error.]
الحاكم العقلي (al-hakim al-aqli): The rational judge; the faculty of intellect that apprehends necessary truths and corrects sensory and estimative errors. [Note: Consistent across the tradition as the highest epistemic authority.]
الوهم (al-wahm): Estimation; a faculty that judges meanings in material objects (e.g., a wolf is dangerous) but can misapply to immaterial things. [Note: In later logic, wahm is often criticized for lacking rational justification; here it is one of the three judges.]

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument establishing the need for a rigorous standard (miyar) in reasoning, despite the common claim that reason alone suffices. The author addresses an imagined interlocutor who belittles the need for a criterion, asserting that the intellect is itself the straight balance. The author counters by describing three internal 'rulers' (hukkam) within every person: the sensory ruler (al-hakim al-hissi), the imaginary ruler (al-hakim al-wahmi), and the rational ruler (al-hakim al-'aqli). Only the rational ruler is infallible, but the human soul at its primordial nature is initially more submissive to the sensory and imaginary rulers because they reach the soul first, before the rational ruler matures. Thus the soul becomes habituated to their judgments and finds it difficult to accept the sometimes counterintuitive verdicts of reason. The author then provides concrete examples of sensory illusions: the sun appears small, stars appear like scattered coins, shadows appear stationary, and a child's height appears constant. Reason, however, demonstrates through proofs that the sun is vastly larger than the earth, that shadows are in constant motion, and that the child grows imperceptibly. These examples show that the senses can deceive. Next, the author addresses the imaginary ruler (al-wahm), which denies existence without spatial location and rejects that something can be neither inside nor outside the world. Without reason's correction, even scholars would fall into false beliefs about God. The imagination also struggles to accept that a single locus can contain multiple distinct attributes (motion, taste, color, smell) simultaneously; it can only conceive multiplicity by spatial separation. The imagination derives from the senses, and the senses perceive multiplicity by place and time, so when these are removed, the imagination fails. The author then reinforces the argument with scriptural references: the verse 'God is the light of the heavens and the earth' (Quran 24:35) contrasts divine illumination (reason) with satanic whisperings (imagination). Abu Bakr's instruction to strike the head during punishment indicates the brain as the seat of imagination and whisperings. The hadith that Satan flows through the son of Adam like blood shows the deep embeddedness of these illusions. Thus, the passage establishes that logical training is essential to correct the natural deceptions of sense and imagination.

Perhaps you say, O you who are deceived by what you have of mental sciences and infatuated with what rational proofs lead to: What is this glorification and magnification? And what need does the rational person have for a standard and criterion? For the intellect is the straight balance and the correct criterion, so the rational person after the perfection of his intellect does not need correction and straightening. So be deliberate and be certain regarding what you take lightly of the perils of rational ways, and realize before everything that in you there is a sensory ruler, an imaginary ruler, and a rational ruler. The correct among these rulers is the rational ruler. And the soul at the beginning of the innate disposition is more intense in submission and compliance to accept from the sensory and imaginary rulers, because they preceded the soul at the beginning of the innate disposition and opened it by ruling over it, so it became familiar with their ruling and became intimate with them before the rational ruler reached it. So weaning it from its familiar and submitting to what is like a stranger to the suitability of its nature became severe. So it continues to oppose the ruler of reason and deny it, and to agree with the ruler of sense and imagination and affirm them, until it is restrained by the method that we will explain in the book. And if you want to know the truth of what you say regarding the falsehood of these two rulers and their corruption, then look at the sensory ruler: how it judges, when you look at the sun, that it is in the size of a handspan, and the stars as if they are dinars scattered on a blue carpet, and the shadow falling on the ground of upright persons as if it is stationary, and even the posture of the child at the beginning of his growth as if it is stationary. And how reason knows by proofs that sense cannot dispute: that the disc of the sun is many times larger than the sphere of the earth, and similarly the stars, and how it guided us that the shadow we see as stationary is continuously moving without ceasing, and that the size of the child during the period of growth is not stationary but is in continuous and constant growth, progressing to increase in a subtle gradual progression that exhausts the sense from perceiving it, and reason witnesses to it. And the errors of sense in this kind are numerous, so do not hope to exhaust them, and be content with this small sample of its announcements to become aware of its misguidance.

As for the imaginary ruler, do not neglect its denial of something existing with no spatial indication, and its denial of something that does not suit the bodies of the world in terms of separation and connection, and cannot be described as inside the world nor outside it. Were it not for the intellect's sufficiency against the evil of the imagination in its misguidance of this, there would have become firmly established in the souls of scholars from the corrupt beliefs concerning the Creator of the earth and sky what became established in the hearts of the common people and the stupid. And we do not need to go that far in exemplifying its misguidance and illusion, for it lies regarding what is closer to sensible things than what we mentioned. Because if you present to it a single body containing motion, taste, color, and smell, and propose to it to affirm the existence of that in a single locus collectively, it recoils from accepting it and imagines that some of that is attached to some and adjacent to it. And it imagines the adhesion of each to the other in the example of a thin veil covering another, and it is not possible in its nature to understand their multiplicity except by assuming multiplicity of place. For the imagination only takes from sense, and sense in the end perceives multiplicity and distinction by distinction of place and time; so if both are removed, it becomes difficult for it to affirm numbers differing in attribute and reality existing in a single dimension. So this and its like from the errors of imagination exceed the limits of enumeration and counting. And God Almighty is thanked for what He granted of the intellect guiding from the darkness of ignorance, the deliverer by the light of proof from the darkness of the whisperings of Satan.

If you want more reinforcement in encompassing the treachery of these two rulers, then you have the derivation and investigation of what has come in the Law of attributing these obfuscations to Satan and calling them whisperings and referring them to him, and calling the light of intellect guidance and light and attributing it to God Almighty and His angels in His saying: "God is the light of the heavens and the earth." And since the seat of imagination and fancy is the brain, and they are the source of whisperings, Abu Bakr, may God have mercy on him, said to one who was carrying out the legal punishment on some criminals: "Strike the head, for Satan is in the head." And since the imaginary and fanciful whisperings are attached to the thinking power with an attachment such that few can independently achieve deliverance from them, until that is like the mixing of blood with our flesh and organs, he said: "Indeed Satan flows through the son of Adam like the flow of blood."

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument establishing the need for a rigorous standard (miyar) in reasoning, despite the common claim that reason alone suffices. The author addresses an imagined interlocutor who belittles the need for a criterion, asserting that the intellect is itself the straight balance. The author counters by describing three internal 'rulers' (hukkam) within every person: the sensory ruler (al-hakim al-hissi), the imaginary ruler (al-hakim al-wahmi), and the rational ruler (al-hakim al-'aqli). Only the rational ruler is infallible, but the human soul at its primordial nature is initially more submissive to the sensory and imaginary rulers because they reach the soul first, before the rational ruler matures. Thus the soul becomes habituated to their judgments and finds it difficult to accept the sometimes counterintuitive verdicts of reason. The author then provides concrete examples of sensory illusions: the sun appears small, stars appear like scattered coins, shadows appear stationary, and a child's height appears constant. Reason, however, demonstrates through proofs that the sun is vastly larger than the earth, that shadows are in constant motion, and that the child grows imperceptibly. These examples show that the senses can deceive. Next, the author addresses the imaginary ruler (al-wahm), which denies existence without spatial location and rejects that something can be neither inside nor outside the world. Without reason's correction, even scholars would fall into false beliefs about God. The imagination also struggles to accept that a single locus can contain multiple distinct attributes (motion, taste, color, smell) simultaneously; it can only conceive multiplicity by spatial separation. The imagination derives from the senses, and the senses perceive multiplicity by place and time, so when these are removed, the imagination fails. The author then reinforces the argument with scriptural references: the verse 'God is the light of the heavens and the earth' (Quran 24:35) contrasts divine illumination (reason) with satanic whisperings (imagination). Abu Bakr's instruction to strike the head during punishment indicates the brain as the seat of imagination and whisperings. The hadith that Satan flows through the son of Adam like blood shows the deep embeddedness of these illusions. Thus, the passage establishes that logical training is essential to correct the natural deceptions of sense and imagination.

Imaginary Deception and the Limits of Imagination
Author explains the imaginary ruler's errors and the need for reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the imaginary ruler's errors regarding non-spatial existence and multi-attributive unity, and explain how reason overcomes them.

Core Idea
The imagination cannot conceive a being without spatial location or multiple attributes in one locus.

The author now turns to the imaginary ruler (al-wahm), which is even more deceptive than the senses. It denies the possibility of existence without spatial direction and struggles to accept that a single substance can have multiple attributes like motion, taste, color, and smell simultaneously. Without reason, these imaginative limitations would lead to false beliefs about God.

Guided Path
1
Identify what the imaginary ruler denies
What kind of existence does the imagination reject? It denies existence without spatial indication and something that is neither inside nor outside the world.
2
Understand the example of a body with multiple attributes
Why does imagination struggle to accept a single locus for motion, taste, color, and smell? It imagines they must be spatially separated.
3
Recognize the source of imagination's limitation
Where does imagination derive its data? From the senses, which perceive multiplicity by place and time.
4
Appreciate reason's role
How does reason affirm what imagination denies? By abstracting from spatial and temporal conditions.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of musta'ara (metaphorical terms) in Arabic texts and distinguish them from other types of transferred terms.

Core Idea
Musta'ara borrows animal parts for non-animal things to create vivid metaphors.

In this passage, the author answers a hypothetical question by providing a rich list of classical Arabic metaphors. These are not just poetic flourishes but are systematically classified in logic and rhetoric as musta'ara (metaphorical) terms. Understanding these examples helps you recognize how language extends meanings by analogy, which is essential for interpreting religious texts and complex arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
The passage begins by stating that the examples are 'borrowing of the limbs of animals for non-animals'. This is the definition of musta'ara in this context. Note that the borrowed terms are specifically body parts of animals (e.g., head, eye, face).
2
Examine each example
Look at each phrase: 'head of capital', 'face of the day', 'eye of water', etc. In each case, an animal body part is applied to a non-animal entity. For instance, 'eye of water' uses 'eye' (a body part) to refer to a spring. Recognize the similarity that enables the metaphor.
These are conventional metaphors, not one-off poetic inventions.
3
Observe the range
The examples vary from simple two-word phrases (رأس المال) to longer idiomatic expressions (أبدى للشر ناجذيه). This shows that musta'ara can be short or extended. The longer ones often form entire metaphorical sentences.
4
Contrast with other term types
Musta'ara is distinct from manqula (transferred terms like 'prayer' for ritual) and mushtaraka (homonyms like 'ayn for multiple things). The difference: musta'ara retains a clear analogy to the original meaning, while the others may have lost that connection.
This distinction is crucial for understanding how words carry meaning in religious texts.

Commentary
This passage presents a vivid list of metaphorical expressions (musta'ara) where terms for animal body parts are applied to non-animal things. The author answers an imagined question requesting examples. Each phrase illustrates the 'borrowing' (isti'ara) of a term from an animal part to something else, e.g., 'head of capital' (capital), 'face of the day' (daytime), 'eye of water' (spring), 'eyebrow of the sun' (sun's edge), etc. These are classical Arabic metaphors common in poetry and rhetoric. The list includes longer metaphorical phrases like 'he showed the molars of evil' (meaning evil became apparent) and 'the mill of war turned' (war intensified). The examples demonstrate that musta'ara is a rich source of expressive language, distinct from transferred (manqula) and homonymous (mushtaraka) terms.
