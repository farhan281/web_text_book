Al-Ghazali's Scholarly Output: A Bibliographical Overview
Enumerates the major works of al-Ghazali across various Islamic sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize al-Ghazali's works by discipline and identify his major contributions to each field.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali’s bibliography shows mastery across logic, theology, law, Sufism, and Quranic sciences.

Al-Ghazali is one of Islam’s most prolific scholars. His writings cover nearly every Islamic science, from logic to Sufism. This lesson provides an overview of his major works, helping you navigate his intellectual legacy and see how each work fits into a broader discipline.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the range of disciplines
Scan the list and identify works that belong to logic (e.g., القسطاس المستقيم, محك النظر), theology (قواعد العقائد, تهافت الفلاسفة), jurisprudence (الوجيز, الوسيط), Sufism (منهاج العابدين, بداية الهداية), and Quranic sciences (ياقوت التأويل, خواص القرآن).
Some works may span multiple fields.
2
Group works by Arabic title patterns
Notice that many titles begin with 'الـ' and contain key terms like 'علم' or 'كتاب'. Use these clues to infer the subject matter.
Titles often indicate the field, e.g., 'الوسيط في الفروع' clearly points to fiqh.
3
Associate works with al-Ghazali's intellectual journey
Understand that his early works focus on fiqh, middle works on theology and philosophy (like Tahafut), and later works on Sufism. This list reflects his evolving interests.
For example, 'تهافت الفلاسفة' marks his critical engagement with Greek philosophy.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical enumeration of al-Ghazali's major works, covering his contributions to logic, theology (kalam), jurisprudence (fiqh), Sufism, Quranic sciences, and polemics. The list begins with works like 'Al-Qistas al-Mustaqim' (The Right Balance), a work on logic, and 'Qawa'id al-Aqa'id' (Rules of Beliefs), a theological treatise. It includes his famous refutation of the philosophers, 'Tahafut al-Falasifa' (The Incoherence of the Philosophers), and his spiritual classic 'Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din' is not listed, but others like 'Minhaj al-'Abidin' (The Path of the Worshipers) and 'Bidayat al-Hidaya' (The Beginning of Guidance) are present. The list also contains works on usul al-fiqh, such as 'Ghayat al-Wusul' (The Utmost Attainment in the Principles), and on Quranic exegesis, like 'Yaqut al-Ta'wil' (The Ruby of Interpretation). This enumeration serves as a reference for students to recognize al-Ghazali's extensive output across various disciplines.

Navigating Scholarly Bibliographies in Islamic Texts
Lists references used in the edition of Mi'yar al-Ilm.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a scholarly bibliography in a classical Islamic text and trace the intellectual lineage of al-Ghazali's sources in Mi'yar al-Ilm.

Core Idea
A bibliography is not a list of dead books but a map of living scholarly conversations.

This passage presents the bibliography used in the edition of Mi'yar al-Ilm. While it may seem like a dry list, it reveals the vast network of knowledge that al-Ghazali engaged with. In this lesson, you will learn how to read such a bibliography critically, extracting the kinds of sources (hadith, language, theology, etc.) and understanding why each is relevant to the study of Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of sources
Scan the list and categorize each entry as hadith, poetry, biography, language, theology, etc. For example, Sahih al-Bukhari is a hadith collection, while Lisan al-'Arab is a language dictionary.
This helps you see the breadth of disciplines al-Ghazali drew upon.
2
Extract full bibliographic details
For each entry, note the author, title, editor, publisher, and edition date. For example, 'صحيح البخاري: الإمام محمد بن إسماعيل البخاري. تحقيق محمد زهر بن ناصر الناصر. دار طوق النجاة، ط1، 1422هـ.' This information is essential for verifying the reliability of the edition.
3
Connect sources to Mi'yar al-Ilm
Consider why a work on logic references hadith collections or poetry. Al-Ghazali often used examples from hadith to illustrate logical principles, and poetry to show linguistic precision. Think about how each source might have been used.
This step deepens your understanding of the interdisciplinary nature of Islamic scholarship.
4
Assess the range of eras
Note the dates of the sources (e.g., 7th century AH for Bukhari, 19th century CE for modern editions). This shows the historical depth of the scholarship and helps you evaluate which sources are primary and which are secondary.
A good edition incorporates both early and modern sources.

The list shows the breadth of sources employed by the editor, spanning from early Islamic times to later medieval scholarship. Each entry typically includes the author, editor, publisher, edition, and date. This is a typical academic bibliography, not a logical argument.
