Distinguishing Qiyas al-Illa and Qiyas al-Dalala
Defining the two types of syllogism based on the middle term's causality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given syllogism as qiyas al-illa or qiyas al-dalala by identifying whether the middle term is a cause of the major term.

Core Idea
The middle term's causal role determines whether a syllogism explains why or merely that.

This passage introduces a fundamental distinction between two types of syllogism: those that provide the cause (qiyas al-illa) and those that only indicate existence (qiyas al-dalala). Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating whether an argument offers explanation or mere evidence. The author also gives examples from physics and jurisprudence to illustrate the difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the middle term
Find the term that connects the subject (minor term) and predicate (major term) in the conclusion. For example, in 'This wood is burning because fire touched it', the middle term is 'fire'.
The middle term appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
2
Check if the middle term is a cause of the major term
Determine whether the middle term is the cause of the major term's presence in the minor. In the example, fire is the cause of burning, so it is qiyas al-illa.
If the middle term is not a cause but merely indicates the major, it is qiyas al-dalala.
3
Classify accordingly
If the middle term is a cause, the syllogism is qiyas al-illa (burhan limmi); if not, it is qiyas al-dalala (burhan inni).
4
Verify with the passage examples
Apply the test to the given examples: 'This person is full because he ate now' (illa), and 'This person is full, so he recently ate' (dalala). Confirm the classification.
The reverse examples show that the direction of inference changes the type.

And if it is not a cause, jurists call it 'syllogism of indication', and logicians call it 'demonstration of the 'that'' i.e., it is a proof that the major term exists for the minor without explaining its cause. An example of syllogism of cause from physical things is your saying: This wood is burning because fire touched it, and this person is full because he ate now. And syllogism of indication is its opposite, which is to infer from the result to the producer, so we say: This person is full, so he has recently eaten, and this woman has milk, so she has recently given birth. An example from jurisprudence is your saying: This is an intrinsically impure substance, therefore prayer is not valid with it. And syllogism of indication is its opposite, which is to say: This substance is such that prayer is not valid with it, therefore it is impure. Yet writing is not a cause of knowledge; rather, knowledge is more appropriate to be considered its cause. Likewise, if two results follow from one cause, it is permissible to infer from one result to the other, and this is a syllogism of indication. An example from our saying: Adultery does not necessitate kinship so it does not necessitate prohibition of marriage, for prohibition of marriage and permissibility of looking are concomitant, and they are two results of sexual intercourse which necessitates affinity. Subtypes of Qiyas al-Illa: Cause of Predication vs. Cause of Existence
Breaking down qiyas al-illa into two subtypes based on the nature of causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between the two subtypes of qiyas al-illa: one where the middle term is a cause of the predication and one where it is a cause of the existence of the major term.

Core Idea
Causal syllogisms vary: sometimes the middle term causes the connection, sometimes it causes the very existence of the predicate.

The passage explains that qiyas al-illa has two subtypes: the first where the middle term is a cause of the predicative connection (e.g., animal causing 'body' to be predicated of human) and the second where it is a cause of the existence of the major term itself (e.g., transgression causing punishment). This nuance prevents overgeneralization of causal relationships in logic and is critical for accurate reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sections of qiyas al-illa
Locate where the author divides qiyas al-illa into two sections. The first section is introduced with 'الأول : ما يكون الأوسط فيه علّة للنتيجة ولا يكون علة لوجود الأكبر في نفسه'.
The second section begins with 'والقسم الثاني : ما يكون علة لوجود الحدّ الأكبر على الإطلاق'.
2
Understand the first subtype: cause of predication
In this subtype, the middle term is the cause of the predication of the major term on the minor, but not the cause of the major term's existence. Example: 'Every human is an animal; every animal is a body; so every human is a body.' The animal is the cause of 'body' being predicated of human, but 'body' exists first in animal.
The middle term (animal) does not cause the existence of corporeality; rather, it causes the logical connection.
3
Understand the second subtype: cause of existence
In this subtype, the middle term is the cause of the existence of the major term absolutely or conditionally. Example: 'Transgression is a cause for punishment absolutely'; 'Killing is a cause for retaliation in a specific case'.
The second subtype can be either absolute (like transgression) or restricted (like killing with specific conditions).
4
Apply the distinction to new examples
Given a syllogism, determine which subtype applies. For instance, 'Every intoxicant is prohibited; wine is an intoxicant; so wine is prohibited' is the first subtype because 'intoxicant' causes the predication of 'prohibited' on wine, not the existence of prohibition itself.
Prohibition exists in the ruling, not caused by the intoxicant.

Distinguishing True Circular Demonstration from Apparent Causal Cycles
Author resolves doubt that causal chains appear circular, clarifying true circularity requires numerical identity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish true circular demonstration (same individual) from apparent circularity (same species but different individual) using the cloud/rain example and apply this to Islamic proofs.

Core Idea
True circular demonstration requires numerical identity, not just species similarity.

A common doubt about circular demonstration arises from natural causal chains that seem to loop back on themselves. The author of this passage anticipates this doubt and clarifies that not every causal cycle is a logical fallacy. To benefit from this distinction, you need to grasp the key difference between identity in number (ayn) and identity in type (naw). This lesson will walk you through the doubt and its resolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt about circularity
Find the objection presented in the passage: a chain of causes and effects (cloud → vapor → moist earth → rain → cloud) appears to return to the starting point, suggesting circular reasoning.
The doubt is that this looks like a circle, which would make demonstration invalid.
2
Locate the author's response
The author rejects this as true circularity. He defines true circularity as using the same individual thing to explain itself (e.g., 'this cloud' because of 'this cloud').
The Arabic phrase is 'bi-aynihi' – the very same in number.
3
Analyze the cloud example for numerical identity
In the example, the final cloud is not the same individual as the first cloud. They are two different clouds, though of the same species. The first cloud causes rain, which moistens the earth, leading to evaporation and a new cloud.
The cause and effect differ numerically (bi al-‘adad), even if they share the same nature (bi al-naw).
4
Apply the rule to other Islamic proofs
Consider a theological argument that uses the Quran as evidence for the Quran's divine origin without external support. That would be circular because the same text is used to prove itself. In contrast, using one miracle to prove the prophethood of Muhammad and then using that prophethood to confirm another miracle is not circular if the miracles are different individuals.
This distinction protects against invalid reasoning while allowing legitimate causal sequences.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical refutation of a doubt about circular demonstration. The author presents an objection: in natural causal chains, we often find what appears to be circular reasoning. For example, when asked why clouds exist, we say because of vapor; vapor because of moist earth; moist earth because of rain; rain because of clouds. In the cloud example, the initial cloud is not the same individual as the final cloud; they are different clouds that share the same species. The cause (first cloud) is numerically different from the effect (second cloud), even though they are equal in species. The author explains that one cloud can be the cause of another through intermediate steps (moistening ground, evaporation, condensation). Therefore, the chain is not a true circle because the same item is not repeated; it is a linear chain of causes and effects where the type repeats, not the individual. This clarifies that circularity is only invalid when the same thing (in number) is used to prove itself.

Distinguishing Invalid Circularity from Valid Causal Cycles
Author distinguishes between invalid circular proof and valid natural cycles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify invalid circular demonstration (where the same individual is used as cause and effect) and distinguish it from natural cycles where causes and effects are different individuals of the same type.

Core Idea
Circular proof is only invalid when the exact same individual is used to explain itself.

This passage addresses a common doubt: If natural cycles like the water cycle appear to go in circles, why is circular proof condemned? The author clarifies that the invalidity lies in the identity of the individual, not the repetition of the type. This distinction is crucial for evaluating causal arguments in Islamic sciences such as Kalam and Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt presented
The objector points to a natural cycle: cloud → rain → moisture → vapor → cloud. Recognize that this appears circular. The objector argues that if circular demonstration is invalid, then this cycle must also be invalid, which is absurd.
2
Extract the author's distinction
The author distinguishes between using the exact same individual as cause and effect (invalid) versus using a different individual of the same species (valid). In the natural cycle, the cause (cloud A) and effect (cloud B) are numerically different, even though they share the same nature (cloudness).
Key phrase: 'العلة غير المعلول بالعدد' (the cause is numerically different from the effect).
3
Apply the distinction to the example
Check if the causal chain involves the same individual. In the water cycle, each link involves a different particular: this specific cloud causes rain, which causes moisture on a specific patch of earth, which causes vapor, which condenses into another specific cloud. No single individual is both cause and effect of itself.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Consider a theological argument: 'The world is originated because it changes; change requires an originator; therefore the world has an originator.' The cause (originator) is not the same individual as the world. This is valid. But if someone argued 'The world is caused by itself' (same individual), that would be invalid circularity.
This distinction helps assess the validity of causal proofs in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Fiqhi Terminology for the Four Causes
Author explains how fuqaha rename the four causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fiqhi terms for the four causes (mahall, ahl, hukm, sigha) and map them to their logical counterparts.

Core Idea
Fuqaha rename the four causes to fit legal contexts—learn their language to analyze juristic reasoning.

In logic, the four causes are matter, agent, form, and end. But when jurists analyze rulings, they use terms like mahall (locus), ahl (agent), hukm (ruling), and sigha (formula). This lesson unpacks that renaming using the example of marriage.

The author lists them implicitly through the fiqhi terms.
2
Map each to its fiqhi counterpart
The passage says: they call madda 'mahall', fa'il 'ahl', ghaya 'hukm'. The form corresponds to the contract formula (sigha). Write these mappings.
The form is not given a new name but is associated with the contract formula.
3
Analyze the marriage example
The husband is the agent (ahl), the private part is the locus (mahall), permissibility is the end (hukm), and the contract formula is the form (sigha). Check that all four are present.
Without any one, marriage does not exist.
4
Understand the necessity of the final cause
The final cause (hukm) must exist both as a rational motive before the act and as an actual result after. If a marriage yields no permissibility, it is null.
This is why sales without ownership are invalid.

وكذا في الأحكام الفقهية، والفقهاء ربما سمّوا المادة محلاً والفاعل الذي هو كالنجار والأب أهلاً، والغاية حكماً، فإذا فرض النكاح فالزوج أهل والبضع محلّ والحلّ غاية وصيغة العقد كأنها الصورة وما لم تجتمع هذه الأمور لا يتمّ للنكاح وجود، ولذلك قيل : النكاح الذي لا يفيد الحلّ لا وجود له، وكذا البيع الذي لا يفيد الملك. فإن وجود الغاية لا بد منه وكونها معقولاً باعثاً شرط قبل الوجود، وكونها موجودة بالفعل واجب بعد الوجود ومهما قدّر الفاعل والمادة موجود موجوداً لم يلزم وجود الشيء في كل حال كالنجار والخشب والأب والنطفة والبايع والمبيع، ومهما وجدت الصورة لزم وجود الشيء كصورة السرير وصورة الإنسانية، ومهما وجدت الغاية بالفعل لزم وجود الشيء كالحلّ في النكاح والصلوح للاكتنان والجلوس في البيت، والشيء بهذه الجهات الأربع يختلف في هذا المعنى. ثم كلّ هذه العلل إما بعيدة كإسلام المرأة للزوج عند ملك الزوج نصف الصداق، إنه علّة الصداق والصداق هو العلّة القريبة للتسليم، وإما بالقوة كالإسكار للخمر قبل الشرب، وإما الفعل كما في حال الشرب، وإما خاصة كالزنا للرجم، وإما عامة كالجناية للرجم أو العقوبة، وإما بالذات وهو المُسمّى علة عند الفقهاء كالزنا للرجم وإما بالعرض كالإحصان له وهو الذي يُسمّى شرطاً، فإن الرجم لا يجب إلا بالإحصان، وهي خصال كمال ولكن يعمل عمل العلة عنده، كما لو أرسلت الدعامة من تحت السقف فنزل فيقال نزوله بعلة الثقل، ولكن عند إشالة الدعامة فإن للهوي شرطاً، وهو فراغ جهة الأسفل عن جسم صلب لا ينخرق. وأمثلة هذا في المعقولات كثيرة ، فلذلك اقتصرنا على الأمثلة الفقهية، والمقصود أن المعلل في الفقه والمعقول إذا توجهت المطالبة عليه بالعلة، ينبغي أن يذكر العلة الخاصة القريبة التي بالفعل حتى تقطع المطالبة بلم، وإلا فيكون الطلب قائماً .
Similarly in juristic rulings. The jurists sometimes call the material cause 'place' (mahall), the efficient cause which is like the carpenter and the father 'agent' (ahl), and the final cause 'ruling' (hukm). If one considers marriage, the husband is the agent, the sexual organ is the place, permissibility (hall) is the final cause, and the marriage contract formula is like the form. Unless these things come together, marriage does not exist. Hence it was said: 'Marriage that does not yield permissibility has no existence,' and likewise a sale that does not yield ownership. For the existence of the final cause is necessary; its being a rational motive is a condition before existence, and its being actually existent is obligatory after existence. Whenever the efficient cause and material cause are present as existent, the existence of the thing does not necessarily follow in every case, such as the carpenter and wood, the father and sperm, the seller and the sold item. Whenever the form exists, the existence of the thing follows, like the form of a bed and the form of humanity. Whenever the final cause exists actually, the existence of the thing follows, like permissibility in marriage and suitability for concealment and sitting in a house. A thing differs in this meaning by these four aspects. Then all these causes are either distant, like the woman's conversion to Islam for the husband when the husband owns half the dowry, for it is the cause of the dowry, and the dowry is the proximate cause for delivery; or potential, like intoxication in wine before drinking; or actual, as in the state of drinking; or specific, like adultery for stoning; or general, like criminality for stoning or punishment; or essential, which is called 'illa by the jurists, like adultery for stoning; or accidental, like ihsan (marital status) for stoning, which is called 'shart' (condition). For stoning is not obligatory except with ihsan, and these are virtues but they function like the cause in his view, just as if one removes the support from under the ceiling and it falls, it is said its fall is by the cause of weight, but upon removing the support, there is a condition for descent, namely the space below being free of a solid body that does not split. Examples of this in rational matters are many, so we limit ourselves to juristic examples. The point is that the person being asked for the cause in jurisprudence and rational matters, when demanded to give the cause, should mention the specific, proximate, actual cause in order to terminate the demand for 'why'; otherwise the demand remains.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of the four causes by examining how jurists (fuqaha) rename them in the context of fiqh rulings. The material cause is called 'mahall' (place/locus), the efficient cause is called 'ahl' (agent), the final cause is called 'hukm' (ruling), and the form corresponds to the contract formula (sigha). The author illustrates with marriage: husband (agent), private part (locus), permissibility (goal), and contract formula (form). The necessity of the final cause is emphasized: before existence, it is a rational motive (condition); after existence, it must be actual. Then the author lists various divisions of causes: distant vs. proximate, potential vs. actual, specific vs. general, essential vs. accidental. He notes that jurists call the essential cause simply 'illa' (e.g., adultery for stoning) and the accidental cause 'shart' (condition, e.g., ihsan for stoning). He uses the analogy of a falling ceiling: the weight is the cause, but the removal of support is a condition. The conclusion is that in any demand for explanation (bi-lim), one must provide the specific, proximate, actual cause to satisfy the inquiry; otherwise the question remains unanswered.
