The Four Ranks of Existence: A'yan, Adhhan, Alfadz, Kitaba
Author explains the four ranks of existence: a'yan, adhhan, alfadz, kitaba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and sequence the four ranks of existence (a'yan, adhhan, alfadz, kitaba) and explain their epistemological dependence.

Core Idea
Existence flows from external objects to minds to words to writing.

The author begins this section by presenting a fundamental epistemological hierarchy: the four ranks of existence. Understanding this order is essential for grasping how knowledge is formed and how language relates to reality. In this lesson, we will explore each rank and the dependencies between them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four ranks
Locate the four terms: a'yan (external objects), adhhan (minds), alfadz (words), kitaba (writing). The author explicitly states these as the ranks.
2
Understand the sequential dependence
Note that each rank depends on the previous: without external existence, no mental image; without mental image, no word; without word, no writing. The author says: 'فالكتابة دالة على اللفظ واللفظ دال على المعنى الذي في النفس والذي في النفس هو مثال الموجود في الأعيان'.
3
Distinguish universal vs. conventional ranks
The author states that a'yan and adhhan are universal across cultures (لا يختلف بالبلاد والأمم), while alfadz and kitaba are conventional (وضع واصطلاح).
4
Apply the example of 'dinar'
The mental image (mithal) of a dinar corresponds to all dinars, not just one. This shows that the mental concept is universal, which is key for the next lesson.

القسمة الثالثة : في بيان رتبة الألفاظ من مراتب الوجود: اعلم أن المراتب فيما نقصده أربعة واللفظ في الرتبة الثالثة، فإن للشيء وجوداً في الأعيان ثم في الأذهان، ثم في الألفاظ ثم في الكتابة، فالكتابة دالة على اللفظ واللفظ دال على المعنى الذي في النفس والذي في النفس هو مثال الموجود في الأعيان. فما لم يكن للشيء ثبوت في نفسه لم يرتسم في النفس مثاله ومهما ارتسم في النفس مثاله فهو العلم ،به إذ لا معنى للعلم إلا مثال يحصل في النفس مطابق لما هو له في الحسّ وهو المعلوم، وما لم يظهر هذا الأثر في النفس لا ينتظم لفظ يدلّ على ذلك الأثر ، وما لم ينتظم اللفظ الذي ترتب فيه الأصوات والحروف لا ترتسم كتابة للدلالة عليه والوجود في الأعيان والأذهان لا يختلف بالبلاد والأمم، بخلاف الألفاظ والكتابة فإنهما دالتان بالوضع والاصطلاح. مثال به وعند هذا نقول : من زعم أن الاسم المفرد لا يقتضي الاستغراق ظنّ أنه موضوع بإزاء الموجود في الأعيان فإنهما أشخاص معينة إذ الدينار الموجود شخص معين، فإن جمعت أشخاص سُميت دنانير، ولم يعرف أن الدينار الشخصي المعين يرتسم منه في النفس أثر هو مثاله وعلم به وتصوّر له، وذلك المثال يطابق ذلك الشخص وسائر أشخاص الدنانير الموجودة والممكن وجودها فتكون الصورة الثابتة في النفس من حيث مطابقتها لكلّ دينار يفرض صورة كلّية لا شخصية؛ فإن اعتقدت أن اسم الدينار دليل على الأثر في النفس لا على المؤثر وذلك الأثر كلّي، كان الاسم كلياً لا محالة ، وما قدمناه من الترتيب يعرفك أن الألفاظ لها دلالات على ما في النفوس، وما في النفوس مثال لما في الأعيان وسيأتي مزيد بيان للمعاني الكلية المرتسمة في النفوس مشاهدة الأشخاص الجزئية في كتاب «أحكام الوجود ولواحقه»(۲)
The third division: explaining the rank of words among the ranks of existence: know that the ranks concerning what we intend are four, and the word is in the third rank. For a thing has existence in external objects (a'yan), then in minds (adhhan), then in words (alfadz), then in writing (kitaba). Writing indicates the word, and the word indicates the meaning that is in the soul, and what is in the soul is the likeness (mithal) of what exists in external objects. So if a thing does not have subsistence in itself, its likeness is not imprinted in the soul. Whenever its likeness is imprinted in the soul, that is knowledge of it, for there is no meaning of knowledge except a likeness that occurs in the soul corresponding to what it is for in sensation, and that is the known. And as long as this impression does not appear in the soul, no word is formed that indicates that impression, and as long as the word in which sounds and letters are arranged is not formed, writing is not imprinted to indicate it. Existence in external objects and minds does not differ by lands and nations, unlike words and writing, for they are indicative by convention and agreement. Example concerning it: And at this we say: whoever claims that the singular noun does not imply istighraq thinks that it is placed in correspondence to what exists in external objects, for they are specific individuals – indeed the existing dinar is a specific individual. If you collect individuals, they are called dinars. And he does not know that from the specific individual dinar an impression is imprinted in the soul that is its likeness and knowledge of it and conception of it. That likeness corresponds to that individual and to all other existing and possible individuals of dinars. So the form established in the soul, in terms of its correspondence with every dinar that is assumed, is a universal form, not a particular one. If you believe that the name 'dinar' indicates the impression in the soul, not the impressor, and that impression is universal, then the name is necessarily universal. And what we have presented of the order informs you that words have indications of what is in souls, and what is in souls is a likeness of what is in external objects. A more detailed explanation of the universal meanings imprinted in souls through observation of particular individuals will come in the book 'Ahkam al-Wujud wa Lawahiqihi' (2).

Commentary
This passage is a classification and epistemological explanation of the four ranks of existence. The author begins by stating that there are four ranks: existence in external objects (a'yan), in minds (adhhan), in words (alfadz), and in writing (kitaba). Words occupy the third rank. The author then explains the sequential dependence: a thing must have existence in itself (a'yan) for its likeness (mithal) to be imprinted in the soul, which constitutes knowledge. Without that impression in the soul, no word can be formed to indicate it; without the word, no writing can indicate it. External and mental existence are universal across cultures, while words and writing are conventional. The author then applies this to the earlier debate about istighraq: those who deny that a singular noun implies generality mistakenly think the word directly refers to external particular individuals. In reality, the word signifies the universal mental form (mithal) that corresponds to all individuals. Thus, if the name indicates that universal impression, the name itself is universal. The example of 'dinar' illustrates that the mental form matches every possible dinar, making it a universal. The passage ends by promising further elaboration elsewhere.

Key Terms
الأعيان (al-a'yan): External, concrete existents independent of mind. [Note: In falsafah, a'yan often refers to the external world as opposed to mental existence; stable.]
الأذهان (al-adhhan): The mind or mental realm where concepts and knowledge reside. [Note: No significant shift.]
المثال (al-mithal): A likeness or mental form that corresponds to the external object and is the basis of knowledge. [Note: In some contexts (Ibn Sina, Suhrawardi) mithal can refer to imaginal forms; here it is strictly mental representation.]
العلم (al-'ilm): Knowledge defined as a mental likeness (mithal) that matches the known object in sensation. [Note: This definition aligns with the standard Peripatetic view; later scholars like Fakhr al-Din al-Razi critique it.]

The Universality of Names: How Words Refer to Universal Concepts
Author argues that singular nouns indicate universal mental concepts, using 'dinar' as an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to argue that a singular noun (like 'dinar') is universal because it indicates a mental concept that corresponds to all possible individuals.

Core Idea
Words refer to mental impressions, not external particulars, so they are necessarily universal.

In the previous lesson, we learned the four ranks of existence. Now we see a crucial application: the author uses this framework to refute the claim that singular nouns are particular. By tracing the word 'dinar' back to the mental concept, he demonstrates its universality.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four ranks and the role of mental concepts
The mental concept (mithal) is a likeness of the external object. The author says: 'والذي في النفس هو مثال الموجود في الأعيان'.
2
Examine the author's example of 'dinar'
The external dinar is a particular individual, but the mental image matches that individual and all other existing and possible dinars. The author says: 'وذلك المثال يطابق ذلك الشخص وسائر أشخاص الدنانير الموجودة والممكن وجودها'.
3
Understand the direction of indication
The word 'dinar' indicates the impression in the soul (al-athan fi al-nafs), not the external impressor. The author says: 'فإن اعتقدت أن اسم الدينار دليل على الأثر في النفس لا على المؤثر'.
4
Conclude that the name is universal
Since the mental impression is universal (كلّي), the word that indicates it is also universal. The author says: 'وذلك الأثر كلّي، كان الاسم كلياً لا محالة'.

The Three Types of Words: Homonyms, Metaphors, and Transferred Terms
Author defines and contrasts three categories of ambiguous words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between homonyms (mushtarak), metaphors (musta'ar), and transferred terms (manqul) based on their origin and usage in logic.

Core Idea
Homonyms are originally shared, metaphors are borrowed, transferred terms are shifted.

In the first art of logic, the author classifies words that can cause ambiguity into three categories. Understanding the difference is crucial for accurate reasoning, especially in demonstrative proofs and religious sciences. This lesson focuses on the initial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of homonym (mushtarak)
The author states that a homonym is placed from the beginning for multiple meanings equally, with no priority. Example: 'ayn (spring, dinar, sun, eye).
Note the equal placement contrasts with other types.
2
Contrast with metaphor (musta'ar)
The metaphor is borrowed from an original meaning, not originally placed for both. Musta'ar should be avoided in demonstrations (burhan) but used in rhetoric, sermons, and poetry.
This distinction is key for logical method.
3
Contrast with transferred term (manqul)
The transferred term originally had one meaning, later shifted for new technical needs. Example: 'jawhar' from 'precious stone' to 'substance' in theology.
Manqul is common in sciences due to necessity.
4
Apply the distinction to examples
Test each type with examples from the passage: 'ayn' as homonym, 'rā's al-māl' as metaphor, 'jawhar' as transferred.
Practice strengthens recognition.

And it is the first art: on words. And what is specified by the name 'homonym' differs in that the homonym is that which was placed in the first placement as a homonym for two meanings, not on the basis that one of the two named things deserved it and then was transferred from it to something else, since there is nothing among the spring of water, the dinar, the disk of the sun, and the seeing organ that preceded in deserving the name 'ayn; rather, it was placed for all with an equal placement, unlike the metaphorical and the transferred. And the metaphorical should be avoided in demonstrations, but not in sermons, rhetorical speeches, and poetry; rather, they are more eloquent using it. As for the transferred, it is used in all sciences due to the pressing need for it, because when the language inventor did not verify all meanings, he did not assign distinct names for them, so someone else was forced to transfer. Thus, 'jawhar' was placed by the language inventor for a stone known to the money-changer, and the theologian transferred it to a meaning he obtained in one of the divisions of existents, and this is commonly used in sciences and crafts. Commentary
This passage is a definitional and classificatory exposition of the first art of logic, which deals with words (al-alfaz). The author begins by distinguishing between three types of words that can cause ambiguity: al-mushtarak (homonym), al-musta'ar (metaphorical), and al-manqul (transferred). The homonym is a word placed from the beginning for multiple meanings without one meaning having priority; the author illustrates with the word 'ayn. The metaphorical is a word used figuratively and is to be avoided in demonstrative proofs (burhan) but is effective in sermons, rhetoric, and poetry. Then the author discusses al-mushtaraka (homonyms) in more detail, dividing them into two subcategories: those that arise from grammatical form (like 'al-mukhtar' ambiguous between active and passive meaning) and those that apply to multiple things that are superficially similar but fundamentally different (like 'al-hayy' applied to God, humans, and plants, or 'al-nur' applied to physical light and intellect). Finally, the author addresses a question about the meaning of 'majaz' (figurative language), explaining that it can refer to something that implies the literal meaning, but in its unrestricted sense it is the opposite; the Quranic example is 'ask the town' where the intended referent is the people, not the physical town. The passage concludes by warning that neglecting these verbal nuances leads to confusion and error in logic and reasoning.

Blocking Infinite Regress with Primary Concepts
Author refutes infinite regress in knowledge acquisition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the sceptical argument against knowledge acquisition and explain how the author's distinction between tasawwur and tasdiq, along with primary concepts known by observation, blocks the infinite regress.

Core Idea
The infinite regress of definitions stops at primary concepts known directly by sensory observation.

This passage from al-Miyar addresses a fundamental epistemological challenge: if every conclusion requires premises, how do we ever acquire the initial premises? The sceptic argues this leads to an infinite regress. The author responds by distinguishing between conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq), showing that definitions ultimately rest on basic concepts known through observation, not further definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sceptical argument
Read the first part of the passage (the objection). The sceptic argues: if conclusions come from premises, then the premises themselves must be known. This leads to either an infinite regress (if each premise requires another premise) or the premises being innate (which is implausible because we are unaware of them).
Look for the conditional structure: 'If from premises... then how are premises obtained?'
2
Find the author's key distinction
The author responds by dividing knowledge into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). He focuses on conception, which is achieved through definition (hadd). For definition to work, the parts of the definition must already be known.
The author uses the example of defining wine (khamr) to illustrate: you need to know 'beverage', 'intoxicating', 'grapes', and 'squeezed' before understanding the definition.

The author argues that the regress cannot continue infinitely. It ends at primary concepts (awa'il) that are known by direct observation (mushahada) through inner or outer sense, without any definition. For example, the concept of 'red' is known by seeing red, not by definition.
These primary concepts are sometimes called badihiyyat (self-evident). They form the foundation of all knowledge.

Its meaning is that we have a power that perceives singular universals with the aid of outer and inner sense, and a thinking power emergent in the soul whose function is composition and analysis, capable of relating singulars to each other, and we have a power that perceives what the thinking power has placed of the singulars and the relation between them by negation or affirmation. So it perceives the eternal and the temporal and relates one to the other. Thus the rational power rushes to judgment by negation, that the eternal is not temporal, and it relates animal to man and judges that the relation between them is affirmation, that man is an animal. And this power perceives some of these relations without a middle term, and does not perceive some of them, so it pauses to seek a middle term, as when it perceives the world and the temporal and the relation between them, it does not judge by negation as it judged between the eternal and the temporal, nor by affirmation as it judged in animal and man, but pauses to seek a middle term, which is to know that it does not precede temporal things, and that whatever does not precede temporal things is temporal.
Source pages p. 115

Commentary
This passage continues the response to the objection about primary principles. The author asserts that these primary principles (awa'il) are not actual in us at all times but are potential. With the completion of the innate faculty of reason ('aql), they become actual. He describes three powers: (1) a power that perceives singular universals with the help of senses, (2) a thinking power (quwwa mufakkira) that composes and analyzes and relates singulars, and (3) a rational power (quwwa 'aqila) that perceives the relations and makes judgments. This last power makes immediate judgments (e.g., the eternal is not temporal; man is an animal) without a middle term. For judgments that are not immediate (e.g., that the world is temporal), it seeks a middle term to demonstrate it.

The Self-Refutation of Denying Rational Inquiry
Author refutes sophists and blind taqlid advocates.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the inconsistency in the sophist's and Batiniyya's rejection of nazar, and explain how their own positions implicitly rely on rational inquiry.

Core Idea
Rejecting rational inquiry is self-defeating: even the claim that reason is unreliable requires reason to be known.

The author confronts two groups who reject rational inquiry: the sophists, who deny all knowledge, and the Batiniyya, who advocate blind taqlid to an infallible imam. Using a dialectical challenge, the author exposes the inconsistency in their positions. This lesson unpacks that refutation and shows why rejecting nazar is rationally untenable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the targets of the refutation
The author argues against sophists and anyone who defends taqlid or attacks nazar. Note how the author groups them together as essentially sophists.
The key term 'sophist' (sūfisṭā'ī) here means anyone who denies the reliability of reason.
2
Follow the dialectical challenge on prophetic knowledge
The author asks: how did you come to know your prophet is truthful? If purely by taqlid, you are like Jews and Christians who blindly follow falsehood. If you claim certainty, you must have used necessity or rational inquiry (nazar).
This challenges the opponent to either admit possible error (kufr) or affirm nazar.
3
Compare the sophist and the Batiniyya
The sophist consistently denies all universal knowledge. The Batiniyya reject nazar in general but use it to establish the infallibility of their imam. This is an inconsistency.
The Batiniyya are 'lower than sophists' because they are selective in their rejection.
4
Recognize the self-refutation
The Batiniyya employ nazar to know their imam's infallibility, yet they reject nazar for other theoretical matters. This performative contradiction is the core of the refutation.
The author calls this 'madness' because it violates the principle of non-contradiction.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of those who reject rational inquiry (nazar) and advocate blind taqlid. The author begins by identifying the sophists and their followers, noting that anyone who argues for taqlid or against nazar is essentially a sophist, even if unaware. The author then presents a dialectical challenge: if a person claims to follow taqlid in accepting the truthfulness of a prophet, then their taqlid is either like that of Jews and Christians (who blindly follow error) or they have some basis. If they admit they might be wrong, that is disbelief in their own view. If they claim certainty, they must have arrived at it through necessity or reason—but if through reason, they have affirmed nazar. Next, the author points out that the sophist consistently denies universal knowledge, and the Batiniyya sect, deceived by disagreements among scholars, also reject reason and call for taqlid to an infallible imam. However, when asked how they know the imam is infallible, they resort to speculative arguments they themselves reject in other domains, exposing their inconsistency. This, the author concludes, is akin to madness. The refutation demonstrates that the total rejection of nazar is self-defeating, especially when applied to foundational religious beliefs.
