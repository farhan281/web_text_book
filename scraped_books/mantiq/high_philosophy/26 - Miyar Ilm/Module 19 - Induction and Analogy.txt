Critique of Induction: Why It Yields Only Probability
Author defines induction and argues it cannot yield certainty, only probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to critique incomplete induction using the author's two-pronged argument and explain why it fails to produce certainty.

Core Idea
Induction can never yield certainty because the unexamined may always be the exception.

The author defines induction and then subjects it to a rigorous critique. He shows that even if you examine many particulars, you cannot be sure about those you haven't examined. This lesson unpacks his argument and its implications for using induction in Islamic sciences like kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of induction
Find the definition: induction is inferring a universal judgment from examining many particulars falling under a universal concept. Note that the author calls it 'istiqra'.
The definition is in the first sentence.
2
Analyze the example given
The example: someone claims the agent of the world is a body because all observed agents (tailor, builder, etc.) are bodies. Work out the inductive inference: from many individuals to the universal 'every agent is a body'.
The example uses 'fā'il al-ʿālam' (agent of the world) referring to God.
3
Follow the first prong of critique: completeness
The author asks: Did you examine the agent of the world itself? If yes, the conclusion is already known and induction is redundant. If no, then you only examined some agents, so you can only conclude 'some agents are bodies', not 'all'. A universal conclusion requires examining all without exception—but then the sought item is already known, making induction circular.
This prong attacks complete induction (istiqra' tamm) as circular.
4
Follow the second prong: majority
If the defender says 'I examined most but not all', the author replies: it is possible that all are bodies except the one unexamined. This possibility prevents certainty; only probability (zann) is obtained.
This prong attacks incomplete induction (istiqra' naqis).
5
Assess the conclusion and connect to Islamic sciences
The author concludes that induction is useless for certain knowledge (yaqīn). In kalam, for example, proving that God is not a body cannot rely on induction; it requires a different method, such as a categorical syllogism or revelation.
This shows why Islamic logicians preferred deductive proofs.

Commentary
This passage critiques induction (istiqra') as a method for obtaining certainty. The author defines induction as examining many particulars to infer a universal judgment. He provides an example: someone argues that the agent of the world is a body because all observed agents are bodies. The critique is twofold. First, if the agent of the world was already examined, then the conclusion is known from that particular case, making the induction redundant. If it was not examined, then examining only some agents yields only a partial conclusion (some agents are bodies), not a universal one. To get a universal conclusion, one must examine all agents without exception—but then the sought particular is part of the examined set and is already known, so the induction is circular. Second, if only most agents were examined, it remains possible that the one unexamined agent (here, the agent of the world) is an exception, so only probability (zann) is obtained, not certainty. This shows that induction does not produce certain knowledge required in demonstrative sciences.

Induction: Definition, Critique, and Juridical Role
Author defines induction, critiques its certitude, and defends its use in fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete from incomplete induction and explain why incomplete induction yields only probability (zann) sufficient for fiqh but not for theoretical certainty.

Core Idea
Incomplete induction yields probability, not certainty, yet that probability is the bedrock of fiqh reasoning.

In this passage, the author formally defines istiqra' (induction) as examining many particulars under a universal and judging the universal accordingly. He critiques the claim that such induction can produce certainty, arguing that unless all particulars are examined, only probability (zann) results. However, he defends the use of induction in fiqh precisely because juridical reasoning operates on preponderant probability, not absolute certainty. The passage also distinguishes induction from analogy (tamthil) and syllogistic deduction.

Guided Path
1
Define induction as given in the text
Identify the two key components: (1) examining many particulars that fall under a universal meaning, and (2) then judging that universal based on those particulars.
Note that the author's definition covers both complete and incomplete induction.
2
Understand the critique of induction's certainty
The author presents an intellectual example: someone claims the Agent of the world is a body because all observed agents (tailors, builders, etc.) are bodies. The critique shows that if the Agent of the world is not among the examined particulars, the induction is incomplete and cannot guarantee the universal claim.
The key point: unless all particulars are examined, the inference remains probable, not certain.
3
Recognize the three-fold classification of judgment
The author divides transmitted judgment into: (i) universal to particular (valid syllogism), (ii) one particular to another (tamthil/analogy), and (iii) many particulars to one (istiqra'/induction). Induction is stronger than analogy but weaker than syllogism.
This classification helps place induction in the hierarchy of argumentative strength.
4
Apply to fiqh examples
The author gives fiqh examples: the witr prayer (if obligatory, would not be performed on a camel; induction from other obligatory acts) and the Hanafi position on waqf (binding contracts cannot have conditions followed; induction from sale, marriage, etc.).
In each case, the jurist relies on induction from multiple instances to establish a general rule.
5
Grasp the epistemic level: zann in fiqh
The author acknowledges that incomplete induction yields only zann (probability), not yaqin (certainty). However, in juridical matters, zann is sufficient because it creates preponderance (tarjih) over the opposite probability.
This epistemological stance justifies the widespread use of induction in fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage defines induction (istiqra') and critically examines its epistemological status. The author first defines induction as examining many particulars under a universal and then judging the universal based on them. He gives an intellectual example: someone claims the Agent of the world is a body because he observed that all known agents (tailors, builders, etc.) are bodies. The author critiques this, pointing out its flaw: if the Agent of the world (God) is already known to be a body, the induction is unnecessary; if not, the induction does not cover the crucial case. He argues that unless all particulars are examined, induction only yields probability (zann), not certainty. He then classifies three types of transferred judgment: (1) universal to particular (sound syllogism), (2) one particular to another (tamthil/analogy), and (3) many particulars to one (induction, stronger than analogy). He gives fiqh examples: the witr prayer not being obligatory because if it were, it would not be performed on a riding camel (using induction to prove that obligatory acts are not performed on a camel). He also discusses Hanafi positions on waqf and the repetition of wiping in ablution, showing how induction from different particulars strengthens probability. The passage concludes with an objection: why doesn't the jurist examine the specific disputed case? The answer: incomplete induction yields probability, not certainty, but that suffices in fiqh. The jurist's induction makes one probability outweigh the other, and proving something in line with many particulars is more likely than it being a rare exception.

Induction in Fiqh: Examples and the Objection of Incompleteness
Illustrates istiqra' with fiqh examples and addresses objection about incompleteness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an inductive argument in fiqh and explain how incomplete induction yields preponderant probability (ghalabat al-dhann).

Core Idea
Incomplete induction suffices for probability in fiqh, not certainty.

In this passage, al-Ghazali illustrates how jurists use induction (istiqra') to support rulings, such as witr not being obligatory because it is performed on a riding animal, unlike other obligatory prayers. He then addresses the natural objection: induction is incomplete. The answer reveals a key principle: incomplete induction still gives preponderant probability, which is the standard for fiqh. We will unpack these examples and the dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Examine the witr prayer argument
The author uses qiyas al-khalf: if witr were obligatory, it would not be performed on a riding animal; but it is; so it is not obligatory. The major premise 'obligatory prayers are not performed on riding animals' is supported by induction over many obligatory prayers. Identify the inductive step.
Induction here is incomplete because not every obligatory prayer is checked.
2
Identify the objection
An opponent could say the induction is incomplete since the jurist did not survey all instances, especially the locus of disagreement (witr itself). Understand why this seems problematic.
3
Grasp the response
The author replies that deficiency of induction only reduces certainty but does not leave probability in equilibrium; it preponderates one side. Probability is sufficient because the judgment agreeing with many particulars is more likely than a rare exception.
This is the core epistemological point: probability in fiqh is enough.
4
Apply to other examples
Consider the waqf argument and the wiping debate. In each, the inductive base is limited but yields preponderant probability. Note how different particulars (e.g., wiping in tayammum vs. khuff) strengthen the probability.
The author emphasizes that diversity of supporting particulars increases strength.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed discussion of induction (istiqra') and its application in fiqh. The author begins by giving three examples: (1) The witr prayer is not obligatory because it can be performed on a riding animal, while obligatory prayers are not performed on riding animals. This is proven by inducting over obligatory prayers such as fajr, etc., and using qiyas al-khalf (reductio). (2) The Hanafi argument that waqf (endowment) is not binding during life because binding transactions do not allow following the condition of the contracting party; this is supported by inducting over binding transactions. (3) The debate about repetition in wiping the head in wudu: one side uses induction from the three limbs (face, hands, feet) to argue that wiping is an original function and thus repetition is recommended; the Hanafi uses induction from wiping in tayammum and khuff to argue that wiping is not repeated. The author then addresses an objection: an opponent might say that the jurist's induction is incomplete because he did not survey all instances. The answer is that incomplete induction still yields preponderant probability (ghalabat al-dhann), since the affirmative judgment is more likely than an exceptional case, and this probability is sufficient in fiqh. The author then discusses the logical conditions for valid induction: complete induction yields certainty, while incomplete induction yields only probability. He illustrates with examples: one cannot conclude that all animals move their lower jaw because the crocodile moves its upper jaw; nor that all animals mount from behind because the hedgehog does otherwise. The key rule is that if an inductive generalization is used as a premise in a syllogism to prove something about the predicate, it is acceptable; but if it is used to prove the inductive premise itself by citing the particular instances, it is circular and useless. Finally, the author applies this to the rational faculty: to prove it is not imprinted in a body, one needs the premise that all imprinted powers do not perceive themselves, which is established by induction; but using that induction to prove that each individual power (e.g., sight) does not perceive itself would be circular. The passage ends mid-sentence on page 75.

Key Terms
الاستقراء (al-istiqra'): The inference from many particulars to one particular; when incomplete, it yields preponderant probability (ghalabat al-dhann). [Note: In this passage, istiqra' is used in fiqh; the distinction between complete and incomplete induction is emphasized.]
قياس الخلف (qiyas al-khalf): A syllogism by reductio ad absurdum, used here to test the premise by showing its consequence. [Note: No significant shift.]
الظن (al-dhann): Preponderant probability, sufficient for fiqh judgments, as opposed to certainty (yaqin). [Note: In usul al-fiqh, dhann is the basis of most legal rulings; in logic, it is a level of conviction below certainty.]

The Logical Conditions of Induction and the Rule of Non-Circularity
States conditions for valid induction and introduces rule against circular use.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between using an inductive generalization as a premise in a syllogism and using it to prove its own instances, and evaluate the validity of each.

Core Idea
Using induction to prove its own instances is circular and invalid.

After establishing that incomplete induction yields probability, al-Ghazali discusses the logical conditions for valid induction. He emphasizes that complete induction yields certainty, while incomplete induction only yields probability. More importantly, he introduces a crucial rule: an inductive generalization can be used as a premise to prove a new predicate about its subject, but cannot be used to prove the same predicate about its own particular instances, as that would be circular. We will examine these conditions and the examples of the crocodile, hedgehog, and motion in time.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition of completeness
The author gives examples: observing many animals does not guarantee that all animals move their lower jaw, because the crocodile is an exception. Similarly for the hedgehog. This shows that induction must be exhaustive to yield certainty.
Incomplete induction only yields probability, not certainty.
2
Identify the rule about using induction
The rule is: if an inductive proposition establishes a ruling for its predicate to be transferred to its subject (i.e., used as a premise for a new conclusion), it is permissible. But if it is used to prove the same predicate for some of its own particular instances, it is not permissible because the conclusion is already assumed in the induction.
This is the key logical point.
3
Apply to the motion example
The inductive generalization 'every motion is in time' can be used with the premise 'everything in time is originated' to conclude 'motion is originated'. But if we try to use the same induction to prove that swimming is in time, we are proving an instance that is already part of the inductive base, leading to circularity.
The author says this is not permissible.
4
Consider the rational faculty example
To prove that the rational faculty is not imprinted in a body, the premise 'all imprinted powers do not perceive themselves' is established by induction over the five senses, imagination, etc. That premise can be used to conclude about the rational faculty. But using that induction to prove that sight does not perceive itself would be circular.
The passage ends mid-sentence, but the intended rule is clear.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed discussion of induction (istiqra') and its application in fiqh. The author then discusses the logical conditions for valid induction: complete induction yields certainty, while incomplete induction yields only probability. He illustrates with examples: one cannot conclude that all animals move their lower jaw because the crocodile moves its upper jaw; nor that all animals mount from behind because the hedgehog does otherwise. The key rule is that if an inductive generalization is used as a premise in a syllogism to prove something about the predicate, it is acceptable; but if it is used to prove the inductive premise itself by citing the particular instances, it is circular and useless. The final takeaway is that such incomplete inductions only produce probable opinion, not certainty.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological critique of inductive reasoning (istiqra'). The author begins by discussing a specific case involving endowment (waqf) to illustrate that when there is possibility of alternative rulings, one only achieves probability (zann), not certainty. The author then distinguishes complete induction (istiqra' tamm), which yields certainty ('ilm), from incomplete induction, which yields only probability (zann). He warns that incomplete induction cannot establish a universal law to be used as a premise in a syllogism; it can only be used to transfer a ruling from a predicate to its subject if the induction is complete, but not to transfer a predicate to individual subjects, as that would render the syllogism circular. The author then applies this critique to a philosophical argument: the claim that the rational soul is not imprinted in a body because it perceives itself, while imprinted forces do not. The author extends the critique to a sense-based argument: a person without hearing or sight might claim that all senses require contact with the object, based on taste, touch, and smell, but this induction is incomplete because sight and hearing, which do not require contact, were not observed. The final takeaway is that such incomplete inductions only produce probable opinion, not certainty.

Tamthil: When Analogy Becomes a Valid Syllogism
Author defines analogical reasoning and critiques its use unless reduced to categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Analogy is only logically sound when reduced to a universal syllogism with a shared middle term.

In this passage, the author defines the sixth type of argument – tamthil (analogy). He critiques the common practice of transferring a ruling from one particular instance to another based on mere similarity. The key insight is that for analogical reasoning to be logical, it must be expressible as a first-figure categorical syllogism with a properly identified middle term. We will walk through his reasoning and the example from intellectual matters.

Critique of Tamthil: From Analogy to Syllogism
Al-Ghazali critiques tamthil and shows its dependence on categorical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in analogical reasoning (tamthil) and transform it into a valid categorical syllogism in the first figure.

Core Idea
Analogical reasoning is only valid when the specific example is discarded and the common meaning becomes a universal premise.

In this passage, al-Ghazali defines tamthil — the method jurists call qiyas and theologians call 'returning the absent to the present.' He then unveils its hidden weakness: tamthil appears compelling but is logically incomplete unless it is restructured as a categorical syllogism. You will learn to spot when an analogy is merely an argument from example and when it meets the rigorous standard of demonstrative proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the analogical claim
Locate the judgment being transferred from one particular instance (the shahid) to another (the gha'ib). For example: 'The heaven is originated because it is like plants and animals, which are observed to be originated.'
The specific witness is explicitly named (e.g., plant, animal, Jew, man).
2
Extract the common meaning (al-ma'na al-jami')
Determine the attribute shared between the two instances that supposedly justifies the transfer. In the example, it is 'being a body' for origination, or 'boarding the sea as a merchant' for wealth.
This is equivalent to the 'illa in usul al-fiqh, but here it must be universalized.
3
Formulate the universal premise
Turn the common meaning into a universal categorical statement: 'Every body is originated' or 'Everyone who boards the sea as a merchant becomes wealthy.' This is the major premise.
The specific witness is no longer needed; it becomes a mere instance of the universal.
4
Construct the first-figure syllogism
Combine the universal premise with a minor premise that subsumes the new instance under it: 'The heaven is a body' or 'I board the sea as a merchant.' The conclusion follows deductively, not by analogy.
Al-Ghazali insists the middle term (e.g., 'body') must be explicitly the cause of the judgment.
5
Check for hidden attributes
Even after universalization, the opponent may deny the universal because the common meaning may not be the sole cause; hidden attributes of the specific witness may be necessary. In that case, you must specify the exact condition (e.g., 'concurrent with events in a specific manner').
This step reveals the deeper epistemological limitation of analogical reasoning.

But the matter is contrary to this: whenever the judgment exists, it indicates the existence of the common meaning.'

Commentary
This passage critiques the method of tamthil (analogical reasoning), which jurists call qiyas and theologians call 'returning the absent to the present' (radd al-gha'ib ila al-shahid). The author defines tamthil as transferring a judgment from one particular instance to another that resembles it in some respect. He immediately exposes the flaw: analogical reasoning is unsound unless the judgment is first established as a universal rule. He illustrates with the claim that the heaven is originated by analogy with plants and animals. The author argues that to be valid, one must first show that plants are originated because they are bodies, making corporeality the middle term. Then one can form a universal syllogism in the first figure: 'Every body is originated; the heaven is a body; so the heaven is originated.' The specific example (plant/animal) becomes superfluous. The author reinforces this with a story of a man who becomes wealthy by following a Jew's example. The man's reasoning is flawed because the Jew's wealth does not transfer to him unless the common cause (boarding the sea as a merchant) is universalized. Hence, the condition for tamthil is that the specific witness must be discarded once the universal is established. However, the author points out a further pitfall: the common meaning (al-ma'na al-jami') may appear suitable but may not be the true cause due to hidden attributes. Even if one drops the specific witness, the opponent may reject the universal premise. One must then explicitly state the precise condition. The author gives the theological example of God's knowledge. If one argues that God has knowledge by analogy with man, the cause might be 'being a knower' or 'being a knowing man'. The former can be universalized, but the opponent may then question the universal premise. Finally, the author rejects the notion that one can prove the common meaning is the cause by seeing the judgment rise and fall with it, because the judgment may depend on multiple conditions. The passage ends mid-argument, emphasizing that the presence of the judgment indicates the common meaning, but not vice versa.

The Sixth Type of Qiyas: Hidden Meaning Analogy
Author defines the sixth type of qiyas and gives an example from purification jurisprudence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a hidden meaning analogy and apply it to a fiqh case by evaluating whether the common attribute more strongly suggests a hidden effective cause than the differing attributes.

Core Idea
Analogy is valid even without manifest suitability when the common attribute strongly hints at a hidden effective cause.

The author has already described five types of qiyas. Now he introduces a subtle type where the common attribute is determinate but lacks obvious suitability (munasaba) and influence (ta'thir). Yet it can still produce valid conjecture if it more strongly suggests that a hidden maslahi meaning (the true effective cause in God's knowledge) is contained within it, rather than in the differing attributes. This type is essential for understanding how mujtahids extend rulings in cases where the 'illa is not explicit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common and differing attributes
In the author's description, both the common attribute (al-ma'na al-jami') and the differing attributes (ma fihi al-iftiraq) are 'determinate' (mu'ayyan). Note that the common attribute lacks manifest suitability (munasaba) and influence (ta'thir) for the ruling. But it may still serve as an indicator of a hidden meaning.
Determinate means specific and known, not ambiguous.
2
Recognize the condition for preferring the common attribute
The author states that if the common attribute makes it more probable (agh lab) that it contains the hidden beneficial meaning (al-ma'na al-maslahi al-khafi) than the differing attributes do, then the ruling based on the common attribute (i.e., that both cases share the same ruling) is preferable.
This is a probabilistic judgment, not a certain one.
3
Examine the example: wudu and tayammum
The example compares wudu (ablution with water) and tayammum (dry ablution). Both are 'ritual purification' (tahara hukmiyya) from an event (hadath) that requires intention (niyya). They share that attribute. They differ in that wudu is with water while tayammum is with earth. The author argues that the hidden requirement for intention is more likely tied to the common attribute 'ritual purification' than to the differing attribute 'with water'. Thus, wudu is analogically attached to tayammum regarding intention.
The point is not that 'with water' is irrelevant, but that 'ritual purification' is a stronger indicator of the hidden cause.
4
Understand the epistemic status: conjecture (zann)
The author emphasizes that this type of analogy yields preponderant conjecture (zann), not certainty. It is a tool for ijtihad and is subject to disagreement among mujtahids. The practice of the Companions, according to the author, only shows consideration of preponderant conjectures without precise rules; any attempt to regulate it strictly is arbitrary.
This highlights the role of ijtihad in Islamic jurisprudence.

Experimented Premises (Mujarribat) and Their Certainty
Al-Ghazali defines mujarribat as judgments arrived at through repeated sense observation with a hidden syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how al-Ghazali's mujarribat combine sense observation and a hidden syllogism to produce certain premises, and distinguish between decisive and probable experimental judgments.

Core Idea
Experimented premises are sense observations plus a hidden syllogism that eliminates coincidence.

In this passage, al-Ghazali introduces the third type of certain premises: mujarribat (experimented premises). Unlike direct sense perceptions (mahsusat), mujarribat require repeated observation and an implicit rational inference that rules out coincidence. This hidden syllogism is what transforms repeated empirical regularity into certain knowledge. Understanding this concept is crucial for evaluating how the Islamic tradition grounded empirical claims within a rational framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Find the definition of mujarribat in the Arabic: 'matters that are assented to through sense with the help of a hidden syllogism.' Note that it involves both sense and reasoning.
2
Examine the examples
List the examples given (beating is painful, scammony purgative, bread satiating, etc.). Observe that they all involve repeated observation of a regular effect.
3
Understand the hidden syllogism
Al-Ghazali explains that if the observed effect were accidental or coincidental, it would not persist consistently. The mind infers a necessary connection: the observed property is essential to the object. This inference is the hidden syllogism.
4
Distinguish decisive vs. probable experiments
Experiments sometimes yield decisive (jizmi) judgment and sometimes majority (akthari) judgment. When the effect is uniform, certainty arises; when variations occur, the mind seeks a preventing cause and considers non-occurrence rare.
5
Compare to tawatur
Al-Ghazali likens repeated observations to multiple witnesses in tawatur (concurrent testimony). Each observation is like a witness, and their consistency, combined with the syllogism, compels assent.

Al-Ghazali defines hadsiyat as judgments arising from a strong innate insight.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining characteristics of hadsiyat and explain why they are classified under mujarribat in al-Ghazali's epistemology.

Intuitive judgments are certain insights rooted in observation and a hidden syllogism, but they require personal reflection to attain.

Al-Ghazali introduces a fascinating category of knowledge called hadsiyat (intuitive judgments). These are propositions that arise from a strong innate insight (hadس) based on repeated observation. Defending Certainty of Empirical Generalizations Against Occasionalism
Response to objection that empirical generalizations are not certain because causality is not necessary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the empirical certainty of a correlation and the philosophical inquiry into its causal modality, using the dialectical exchange between the occasionalist and the defender.

Core Idea
Empirical certainty survives occasionalist doubt because the conjunction itself is undeniable.

The passage addresses a powerful objection: if causality is not necessary, how can we be certain of empirical generalizations? The author offers a sharp distinction between the observable conjunction and the metaphysical explanation. This dialectical move rescues empirical certainty from occasionalist skepticism without committing to a specific theory of causality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The theologians (mutakallimun) deny necessary causality, claiming that God directly creates effects at the time of antecedents, not by them. For example, decapitation does not cause death; God creates death when decapitation occurs.
This is the occasionalist position.
2
Locate the respondent's distinction
The author distinguishes between the conjunction (al-iqtiran) itself and the mode of conjunction (wajh al-iqtiran). The conjunction is the observed correlation; the mode is the metaphysical explanation (necessity vs. custom).
This distinction is key.
3
Apply the test case
The author asks: If the theologian is told his son's neck was cut, would he doubt his death? The answer is no. He acknowledges the death but investigates the mode.
The immediate certainty of the event remains.
4
Draw the epistemological conclusion
The certainty of the empirical proposition (mujarrabat) is about the conjunction itself, not the mode. Doubt about causal necessity does not affect certainty about the correlation.
Thus empirical knowledge is secure.
5
Recognize the fallacy caution
The author labels doubt about such certainties as 'mere delusion' (waswas). It confuses two distinct questions: 'what happens?' and 'how does it happen?'
This protects empirical science from philosophical skepticism.

Intuitive Judgments (al-Hidsiyyat): Certainty Beyond Demonstration
Defines intuitive judgments as certain premises reached by strong intuition, with the example of the moon's light

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to characterize intuitive judgments, identify their epistemological features, and explain why they cannot be transmitted through proof alone.

Core Idea
Some truths are known by intuition, not demonstration, and require personal experience.

This lesson introduces intuitive judgments (al-hidsiyyat), a category of certain premises that are reached by a strong mental intuition (hads). Unlike truths derived from sense perception or explicit syllogisms, hidsiyyat are characterized by personal certainty that cannot be fully transmitted to others through teaching. The example of the moon's light illustrates how such judgments are formed and why they resist dialectical refutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristic of hidsiyyat
Locate the phrase 'قضايا مبدأ الحكم بها حدس النفس' — note that the judgment originates from an intuition of the soul, not from external senses or explicit reasoning. This is the core differentia.
The author emphasizes clarity and strength of mind (صفاء الذهن وقوته) as prerequisites.
2
Analyze the moon example
Examine how the author argues that the moon's light comes from the sun: he cites the variation of phases relative to the sun and the regularity over time. Identify the empirical observations and the inference drawn.
The argument contains both inductive reasoning (regularity) and analogy (mirror reflection).
3
Understand the transmission limitation
Find the statement that one cannot teach these judgments to others except by guiding them on the same path. Explain why this is the case: the certainty is personal and requires the hearer to independently follow the same intuitive process.
The author contrasts this with demonstrative proof (برهان) which can be taught directly.
4
Apply the lesson to the limits of debate
Reflect on the concluding remark: 'لا ينبغي أن تطمع في القدرة على المجادلة في كلّ حقّ' — not every truth can be argued into someone. This has practical implications for jadal (dialectics).
Consider how this affects theological debate and preaching.

The passage concludes with a practical note: not every truth can be demonstrated to a disputant; some knowledge requires personal experience. This is a crucial point about the limits of dialectic and demonstration.
