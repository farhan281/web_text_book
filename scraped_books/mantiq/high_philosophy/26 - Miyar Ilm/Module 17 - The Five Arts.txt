The Two Categories of Existents: Perceptible and Inferred
Author divides existents into perceptibles and things known by inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any existent as either mahsus (perceptible) or ma‘lum bi-l-istidlal (known by inference) using al-Ghazali's criteria.

Core Idea
Not all that is real is sensed; most existents are known through their effects.

Al-Ghazali begins the second art of logic by distinguishing between meanings themselves and their linguistic expressions. He then makes a fundamental epistemological division: existents are either directly perceived by the senses or known through inference. This lesson breaks down that division and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the opening lines where al-Ghazali says 'existents are divided into perceptible and known by inference.' Notice that the perceptible are the five senses' objects, and the inferred are known through effects without direct sensory contact.
The Arabic uses 'munqasima ila mahsusa wa ila ma'luma bi-l-istidlal'.
2
Understand the criteria
For perceptibles, al-Ghazali lists colours, sounds, tastes, smells, and tactile qualities. For inferred, he gives examples like the senses themselves, power, knowledge, will, and emotional states. What makes something fall into the second category is that it is not accessible to any sense but its existence is known from its effects.
3
Apply the classification to Islamic examples
Take examples from Islamic sciences: the color of a Qur'anic calligraphy is perceptible; the knowledge of the scribe is inferred. Practice classifying other realities like the existence of God, the ruh (spirit), or the intention behind an act of worship.
Use reasoning: 'How do I know this? Through direct senses or through inference from evidence?'

The second art: on the simple meanings that exist and their relation to each other. The difference between this art and the previous one is that the former examined the word insofar as it signifies meanings, and this examines the meaning insofar as it is fixed in itself, even if it is signified by the word, since meanings cannot be defined except by mentioning words. The purpose of this art becomes clear through division into types of the perceptible and the intelligible. The first division: concerning the relation of existents to our faculties of perception. Know that our investigation into the enumeration of existents and their realities – they are divided into what is perceptible and what is known by inference, whose essence is not directly accessed by any of the senses. The perceptibles are what are perceived by the five senses, such as colours, followed by knowledge of shapes and magnitudes, and that is by the sense of sight; and sounds by hearing; tastes by taste; smells by smell; roughness, smoothness, softness, hardness, cold, heat, moisture, dryness by the sense of touch. These matters and their concomitants are directly accessed by sense, i.e., the perceiving faculty from the senses attaches to them in their essence. Among them are things whose existence is known and inferred through their effects, and the five senses – hearing, sight, smell, taste, touch – do not perceive them nor reach them. An example of this is these very senses, for the meaning of each one is the perceiving power, and the perceiving power is not sensed by any of the senses, nor does imagination perceive it. Likewise power, knowledge, will, indeed fear, shyness, love, anger, and all these attributes – we know them from others with certain knowledge by a type of inference, not by any of our senses attaching to them. For whoever writes in front of us, we know with certainty his power and his knowledge by a type of writing, and his will by inference from his action. Our certainty about the existence of these meanings is like our certainty about the movements of his hand that are perceived and the arrangement of black letters on white, even though this is visible and those meanings are not visible. Rather, most existents are known by inference from their effects and are not sensed. So you should not magnify sensation and think that verified knowledge is sensation and imagination, and that what is not imagined has no reality. For if you demand of yourself reflection on the essence of power and knowledge, you will find imagination acting upon it with shaping, colouring, and estimating, while you know that the action of imagination is false, and that the reality of power inferred from action is sanctified above shape, colour, spatial occupation, and quantity. You should not deny the indication of reason concerning matters that imagination rejects. And we now alert you to the origin of this confusion. Consider that the first perceptibles of a human at the beginning of his innate disposition are his senses, so they came to dominate him. Then the most prevalent of them is sight, which perceives colours as a primary intention and shapes as a consequence. Then imagination acts upon perceptibles, mostly upon visible things, composing from visible things various shapes whose parts are visible, the composition being from it. For you can imagine a horse with a human head and a bird with a horse's head, but you cannot imagine simple parts other than what you have ever witnessed. Even if you wanted to imagine a fruit you have not seen a likeness of, you could not. Your utmost is to take something you have witnessed and change its colour, for example, a black apple – for you have seen the shape of an apple and blackness, so you combine them – or a large fruit like a watermelon. You continually combine the parts you have witnessed, because imagination follows sight, but it is only capable of composition and analysis. Imagination is always moving in composition and analysis, dominating you in that. So whenever you obtain something known by inference, imagination springs up, directing its gaze toward it, seeking its reality according to what reality is in its view, and there is no reality in its view except for colour or shape, so it seeks shape and colour, which is what sight perceives of existents. Even if you reflect on the essence of smell with an imaginary reflection, imagination seeks for the smell a shape, colour, position, and a false quantity, acting according to its nature. It is strange that when you reflect on a coloured shape, imagination does not seek from it its taste and smell – the portions of smell and taste – but when you reflect on the essence of taste and smell, imagination seeks the portion of sight, which is colour and shape, even though imagination acts upon the perceptibles of all five senses. But because its familiarity with the perceptibles of sight is stronger and greater, its seeking the portion of sight becomes more dominant and effective. So if you present to yourself your knowledge of the Maker of the world and that He exists without direction, imagination seeks for Him a colour and estimates nearness, distance, connection to the world, separation, and other things it has witnessed in coloured shapes, and does not seek for Him taste or smell. There is no difference between taste, smell, colour, and shape – all are perceptibles of the senses. So if you know the division... and both.

Commentary
This passage opens the second art (الفن الثاني) of logic, which examines meanings in themselves as they exist in reality, as opposed to the first art which studied words as signs of meanings. The author, al-Ghazali, then presents the first division of existents in relation to our cognitive faculties: they are divided into perceptibles (محسوسات) and things known by inference (معلومة بالاستدلال). Perceptibles are those directly accessed by the five senses, like colours seen, sounds heard, tastes tasted, smells smelled, and tactile qualities. Things known by inference, in contrast, are not perceived by any sense but are known through their effects. An example is the senses themselves: the meaning of 'sight' is a power that perceives, but that power itself is not seen, heard, etc. Similarly, attributes like power, knowledge, will, fear, shame, love, anger are known with certainty through inference. For instance, from someone's writing we infer his power and knowledge, and from his action his will. Our certainty about these inferred realities is as strong as our certainty about observed movements. The author then warns against overvaluing sense perception and imagination, noting that most existents are known through inference, not sensation. He illustrates how imagination, being habituated to forms and colours, tries to impose shape and colour on realities that have none, like the essence of power. He traces the origin of this error: in early childhood, senses dominate, especially sight. Imagination then composes and analyzes sensory images, but it can only work with parts it has already perceived. It cannot create genuinely new simple images. When we reflect on an inferred reality like the Creator, imagination seeks a colour, shape, distance, etc., because it is accustomed to the perceptibles of sight. However, the passage stresses that colour, shape, taste, and smell are all sensory qualities, and the reality of immaterial things like the Creator is beyond such attributes. The passage concludes by urging the reader to recognize this division and avoid the error of equating reality with what is imaginable or sensible.

Inferring Psychological Attributes from Actions
Author gives example of inferring power and knowledge from writing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Ghazali's argument that we know others' internal attributes with certainty through inference from observable actions.

Core Idea
Our certainty about inferred realities can equal our certainty about observed ones.

Al-Ghazali provides a concrete example: from someone's writing we infer their power, knowledge, and will. He compares the certainty of this inferred knowledge to the certainty of seeing the writer's hand move. This lesson examines the inferential process and its epistemic strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the example of inference
Read the passage where al-Ghazali talks about a person writing. He says we know with certainty the writer's power, knowledge, and will through the writing itself. Note that the writing is sensed (visible), but the attributes are not.
The Arabic phrase: 'من كتب بين أيدينا عرفنا قطعاً قدرته وعلمه بنوع من الكتابة'.
2
Analyze the inference structure
The observable act (writing) serves as evidence for the unseen attributes (power, knowledge, will). This is an inductive or abductive inference: from effect to cause. Al-Ghazali claims this yields certainty, not mere probability.
He compares this certainty to the certainty of observing the hand's movements.
3
Apply the same pattern to another Islamic context
In Islamic theology, we infer God's attributes (knowledge, power, will) from His creation. In fiqh, we infer intention from external actions. Practice applying this pattern: pick an action and infer the underlying attribute.
For example, from the act of charity, we infer the attribute of generosity in the giver.

The First Type of Qiyas al-'Illa: Causality of Attribution
Author defines first type of qiyas al-'illa with causal attribution example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of qiyas al-'illa and identify whether the middle term causes attribution or existence in a given syllogism.

Core Idea
In qiyas al-'illa, the middle term may cause attribution, not existence.

The author divides qiyas al-'illa into two types. The first type occurs when the middle term is a cause for the conclusion (the attribution of the predicate to the subject) but not a cause for the existence of the predicate itself. This is illustrated with the syllogism: 'Every human is an animal, every animal is a body, therefore every human is a body.' Here, being an animal is the cause of being a body attributed to human, but not the cause of corporeality itself, because corporeality belongs to animal by essence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing causal arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the syllogism
Find the categorical syllogism in the passage that exemplifies the first type: Every human is an animal, every animal is a body, so every human is a body.
2
Pinpoint the middle term
The middle term is 'animal' (al-awsat). Note that it connects the minor term 'human' to the major term 'body'.
3
Determine what the middle term causes
The author says 'animal' is a cause for the attribution of 'body' to 'human', but not for the existence of 'body'. This means 'body' is already a property of 'animal' inherently; 'animal' does not bring 'body' into existence, but rather conveys it to 'human'.
4
Generalize the insight
The genus (e.g., animal) is a cause for attributing its predicate to the species, not for the existence of that predicate. This applies to genera, differentiae, definitions, and concomitants.
The key is to separate causal role from existential role.

So the animal is a cause for the attribution of body to human, not for the existence of corporeality, for corporeality precedes by essence in the arrangement of species and genera over animal. Know that what is established for the species of the attribution of the genus to it, and likewise the genus of the genus, and likewise the differentiae, definitions, and concomitants, are only by way of the genus. The genus is a cause in its attribution to the species, not in the existence of the essence of the predicate, I mean the predicate of the conclusion. The second type: that which is a cause for the existence of the major term absolutely, not like this example. And it may not be absolute, like something that has multiple causes, for individual causes cannot be made a cause for the major term absolutely; rather, it is a cause at a particular time and in a particular place. An example in fiqh: transgression is a cause for sinfulness absolutely; fornication is a cause for stoning absolutely; but killing is required on the basis of retaliation and otherwise, but it is a cause for killing only with respect to a specific person, and that does not remove it from being qiyas al-'illa.

Commentary
This passage defines and illustrates the two types of qiyas al-'illa (syllogism of cause). The author first notes that just as qiyas al-dalala (syllogism of indication) was divided, so too is qiyas al-'illa. The Second Type of Qiyas al-'Illa: Causality of Existence
Author defines second type of qiyas al-'illa with fiqh examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the second type of qiyas al-'illa and evaluate whether the causal relationship is absolute or conditional.

Core Idea
In the second type, the middle term is the cause of the existence of the major term, either absolutely or under conditions.

The second type of qiyas al-'illa occurs when the middle term is a cause for the existence of the major term itself, not merely its attribution. The author explains that this can be absolute, as in 'transgression is a cause for sinfulness absolutely' and 'fornication is a cause for stoning absolutely,' or conditional, as when a cause has multiple factors, e.g., killing causing capital punishment only in specific cases like retaliation. This lesson unpacks the difference between absolute and conditional causal claims in Islamic logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second type definition
The second type is where the middle term is a cause for the existence of the major term absolutely (or conditionally).
2
Examine the absolute examples

3
Analyze the conditional example
Killing is a cause for capital punishment, but not absolutely; it is so only under specific conditions (e.g., intentional murder, not self-defense). The author says a cause can be particular to a time and place and still be part of qiyas al-'illa.

Contrast with the first type
In the first type, the cause does not bring about the existence of the predicate; here it does. What distinguishes these two is the role of the middle term vis-à-vis the predicate's existence.
The absolute/conditional distinction prevents overgeneralization in causal reasoning.
