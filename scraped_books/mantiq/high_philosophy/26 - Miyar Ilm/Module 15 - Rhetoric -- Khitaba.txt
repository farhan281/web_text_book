Understanding Isti'ara through Quranic Examples
Author gives Quranic examples of isti'ara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify isti'ara (metaphor) in Quranic verses and explain the basis of suitability between the borrowed word and the borrowed-from meaning.

Core Idea
Isti'ara transfers a word from literal meaning based on a relation of suitability.

The author presents several Quranic verses where familiar words are used in a non-literal sense, such as 'garment of hunger' and 'the head becomes aflame with gray hair'. These are not arbitrary but rest on a relationship of suitability (munasaba) between the borrowed word and the thing it is borrowed for. Mastering these examples helps you recognize how figurative language operates in the Qur'an, a crucial skill for tafsir and balaghah.

Guided Path
1
Locate the borrowed word (al-musta'ar)
In each verse, identify the word that is used non-literally. For example, in 'garment of hunger', the word 'garment' is borrowed.
2
Identify the literal meaning (haqiqa) of that word
Determine what the word literally refers to. 'Garment' literally means clothing; 'hunger' is a physical state.
3
Find the basis of suitability (munasaba)
Ask why the borrowed word is suitable for the new context. Garment covers the body; hunger and fear envelop a person. Suitability can be similarity, correlation, or common effect.
4
Confirm the intended meaning needs figurative interpretation
Recognize that the verse does not refer to a literal garment but to the experience of hunger and fear enveloping them like a garment covers the body.

Defining Majaz: Figurative vs Literal Meaning
Author defines majaz using the verse 'ask the village'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish majaz (figurative speech) from haqiqa (literal truth) using the example of 'ask the village' from Surah Yusuf.

Core Idea
Majaz uses a word for a meaning other than its literal one, while the literal meaning remains contextually relevant.

After exemplifying isti'ara, the author addresses the broader concept of majaz. He clarifies that majaz can refer to what necessitates the literal truth, but in unrestricted usage it is the opposite of haqiqa. He illustrates with 'ask the village' (Surah Yusuf 12:82), where 'village' stands for its inhabitants. Understanding majaz is essential for correct interpretation of texts, especially in usul al-fiqh when deriving rulings from the Qur'an and hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word used figuratively
In 'ask the village', the word 'village' (القرية) is used figuratively.
2
Determine the literal meaning (haqiqa) of that word
'Village' literally means a settlement of houses and buildings.
3
Recognize that the intended meaning is other than literal
The intended meaning is the people of the village, not the physical structures. The verse is addressing the inhabitants.
4
Note that the figurative usage implies the literal truth
To ask the villagers, one must be at the village. So the literal village is a necessary context. The author notes majaz can mean 'what necessitates the truth'.
5
Contrast with haqiqa: literal usage would be absurd
If taken literally, it would mean asking the buildings themselves, which is impossible. This highlights the need for figurative interpretation.

Hasty Generalization as a Material Fallacy
Analysis of first material fallacy as rhetorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a hasty generalization fallacy in a syllogism composed of rhetorical premises and explain why it fails to produce certainty.

Core Idea
One instance does not make a universal; certainty requires more than a single example.

The author opens his analysis of material fallacies by examining a syllogism that uses a single instance to judge all cases. This is a classic hasty generalization, common in rhetorical arguments. In this lesson, we will dissect the logical error and learn to spot it in Islamic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise type
Locate the term 'مقدمات وعظية خطابية' (rhetorical/emotional premises) in the Arabic. Recognize that these premises appeal to emotion or common experience, not to logical necessity.
2
Find the generalization
The passage states 'أخذ فيه شيء واحد ووجد على وجه فحكم به على الجميع' – a single thing found in one aspect is used to rule on everything. Underline this phrase as the locus of the fallacy.
3
Evaluate the reasoning
Ask: Does one particular case prove a universal rule? The author says 'الحكم على الجميع بجزئيات كثيرة ممتنع فكيف الحكم بجزئي واحد' – judging the universal by many particulars is impossible, so how by one? The inference is invalid for certainty.
4
Understand the epistemic limit
Even if many particulars are used, they only yield opinion (ظن), not science (علم). This clarifies why rhetorical syllogisms are not demonstrative.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed analysis of two specific material fallacies mentioned earlier. The first fallacy (the heart/brain argument) is classified as a rhetorical syllogism (qiyas khatabi) composed of emotional premises. The error lies in reasoning from a single instance to a universal conclusion. For example, the king resides in the center of his kingdom, thus the soul must reside in the center of the body (heart). However, divine attributes like mercy and anger are interpreted metaphorically (ta'wil), similar to 'descent' and 'coming'. Taking them literally leads to a false conclusion. Therefore, the error is in accepting a non-axiomatic premise without verification.

Dialectical Premises and the Fallacy of Literal Interpretation
Analysis of second material fallacy as dialectical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of accepting dialectical premises uncritically, especially regarding divine attributes, and explain the need for metaphorical interpretation (ta'wil).

Core Idea
Widely accepted premises may still be false if taken literally; ta'wil is necessary for divine attributes.

The author's second fallacy involves dialectical premises that are accepted because their opposite is repugnant, but this does not make them true. The specific error is taking God's mercy literally as humans understand it. In this lesson, we will learn to detect such uncritical acceptance and apply the principle of ta'wil.

Guided Path
1
Identify the premise type
The term 'مقدمات مشهورة جدلية' (generally accepted dialectical premises) is used. These are premises accepted due to consensus or because denying them seems odious.
2
Find the specific premise in question
The author points to 'وصف الله بالرحمة على الوجه الظاهر الذي فهمه العامة' – describing God with mercy in the literal sense understood by common people. Highlight this as the problematic premise.
3
Understand why it is accepted
People accept it because its opposite (that God is not merciful) is considered repugnant. The author notes 'سلم بعضها من حيث استبشع نقيضها' – they accept it because its opposite is repugnant.
4
Apply the correction: ta'wil
The author states that words like mercy, anger, descent, and coming must be interpreted metaphorically (مؤَوّل) for God. The literal meaning leads to anthropomorphism. Therefore, the premise is not axiomatic and leads to false conclusions if taken literally.
