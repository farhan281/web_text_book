When Exhaustive Certainty Fails: Probability in Fiqh
Author argues that the exhaustive method of rational sciences should not be applied to fiqh; probability suffices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the epistemic standards of rational sciences from legal sciences and explain why probability suffices for legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Certainty is for rational theology; probability is for fiqh.

In this passage, al-Ghazali draws a sharp methodological line: the exhaustive pursuit of certainty (istiqsa') belongs to rational sciences like kalam, not to fiqh. He warns that mixing the two paths leads to confusion. This lesson unpacks why legal reasoning operates on probable preponderance (zann) rather than certain demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two paths
Al-Ghazali contrasts the path of certainty (yaqin) with the path of probability (zann). Where does he say each path belongs? The passage explicitly assigns the exhaustive method to intellectual matters ('aqliyyat) and probability to fiqh matters.
The 'path' is a metaphor for epistemic methodology.
2
Locate the ruling on istiqsa' in fiqh
Find the phrase 'should be left entirely in fiqh matters' (yanbaghi an yutraka fi al-fiqhiyyat ra'san). What is the author's verdict on applying exhaustive rational scrutiny to legal issues?
3
Understand the epistemological reason
Al-Ghazali gives the reason: certainty is the rarest of things in theoretical matters, while probability is the easiest to attain. Why does this justify abandoning exhaustive method in fiqh? Consider the practical nature of legal decisions.
This is a foundational principle in usul al-fiqh that distinguishes the aims of logic from law.
4
Apply the principle to a real case
The passage gives examples from trade and travel. Pick one example (e.g., holding goods for fear of price decrease) and explain how the decision is based on probable preponderance, not certainty.
This shows that even everyday decisions rely on probability, so it is natural for fiqh to do so.

Commentary
This passage marks a clear methodological boundary between the intellectual (rational) sciences ('aqliyyat) and the legal sciences (fiqhiyyat). The author, al-Ghazali, states that the exhaustive method (istiqsa')—the rigorous pursuit of certainty—should be completely abandoned in fiqh. Mixing the path of certainty (yaqin) with the path of probability (zann) is a confused approach that does justice to neither. He emphasizes that certainty in theoretical matters is the rarest thing in existence, while probability is the easiest to obtain. Therefore, in fiqh, one should rely on preponderant factors (tarjih) that guide decision when one is hesitant between two options: to proceed or to refrain. He gives practical examples from trade and travel: people decide to buy or sell, hoard or release goods, based on probable gains or fears, not certain knowledge. This shows that even in everyday life, probability suffices. The lesson: the demands of rational demonstration should not be imposed on legal reasoning, which operates on probable grounds. This is a foundational principle in Usul al-Fiqh that distinguishes the aims of mantiq (logic) from fiqh.

Certainty vs. Probability in Fiqh Reasoning
Author distinguishes certain and probable reasoning, criticizing mixing methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the method of exhaustive scrutiny (istiqsa) appropriate for theoretical sciences and the method of probability (zann) appropriate for fiqh, and evaluate the consequences of mixing them.

Core Idea
Certainty is rare; practical judgment in fiqh requires only probability based on preponderance.

This passage addresses a critical methodological issue: the misuse of rigorous logical standards in Islamic jurisprudence. The author warns against applying the exhaustive scrutiny (istiqsa) proper to theoretical sciences to fiqh, which operates on probability (zann). Through the example of distinguishing tayammum from wudu and the analogy of a hesitant trader, he shows that demanding certainty in practical matters leads to paralysis. Understanding this distinction is essential for any student of usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods contrasted
The author contrasts exhaustive scrutiny (istiqsa) seeking certainty (yaqin) with the method of probability (zann) based on preponderance (tarjih). Note which is appropriate for theoretical sciences and which for fiqh.
The term istiqsa in this passage means demanding absolute certainty.
2
Analyze the example of tard vs. munasib
The author gives the example of differentiating tayammum from wudu by number of limbs. This is tard (mere coincidence) — it doesn't indicate the ruling's cause. A proper analogy would use a munasib (fitting attribute) like 'legal purification'.
Tard is here used pejoratively as a flawed analogical attribute.
3
Recognize the criticism of mixing methods
The author criticizes those who set strict conditions for analogy in theory but then use weak coincidences in practice, calling them 'effective' (mu'aththir). He points out the inconsistency and the resulting confusion.
The target of criticism is likely the ahl al-ra'y who claimed to follow strict methodology.
4
Understand the trading analogy
The author compares exhaustive scrutiny in fiqh to a trader who demands certainty of profit before traveling — such a person never trades. Similarly, demanding certainty in fiqh destroys its purpose.
This analogy illustrates the practical futility of applying theoretical rigor to probabilistic matters.
5
Conclude with the proper approach
The author concludes that each field has its proper method: exhaustive scrutiny in theoretical sciences, probability in fiqh. Mixing them is folly.
This is an early statement of the epistemological principle that methods are context-dependent.

Commentary
This passage is primarily epistemological and methodological, not a formal logical deduction. It begins by defining two types of reasoning in fiqh: one based on 'tard' (mere coincidence) that neither fits the ruling nor suggests a hidden fitting attribute, and another where the opponent's response is also pure coincidence but the reasoner must strengthen his side. The example of differentiating tayammum from wudu by the number of limbs shows that this is insufficient because the number of limbs does not affect the legal nature of purification. The author then criticizes those who apply the rigorous methods of theoretical reasoning (which seek certainty) to fiqh, which operates on probability. He illustrates with the analogy of a cautious trader who demands absolute certainty before traveling, which leads to inaction. The central lesson is that demanding the same level of certainty in fiqh as in theoretical sciences is destructive; instead, probability based on preponderance (tarjih) suffices. The author concludes by warning against mixing the two methods, as each field has its proper approach.

The Sufficiency of Conjecture in Practical Matters
Author argues conjecture suffices in practical domains; exactitude is destructive.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the epistemic standard required in practical Islamic sciences (zann) from the certainty demanded in theoretical sciences, and identify why excessive exactitude undermines practical purposes.

Core Idea
In practical domains like fiqh, reasonable conjecture is sufficient; demanding certainty paralyses action.

This passage addresses a crucial epistemological distinction: the standards of proof differ between theoretical and practical disciplines. In Islamic jurisprudence and worldly affairs, absolute certainty is often unattainable nor required. The author argues that conjecture (zann), backed by the slightest indication, is the normal and sufficient mode of reasoning for mujtahids and rational people. Demanding exhaustive exactitude in such matters not only disrupts but invalidates the very purpose of the practice.

Commentary
This passage argues that in practical domains such as fiqh and commerce, absolute certainty is not required; reasonable conjecture (ظن) is sufficient. The author begins by stating that whenever a rational person faces two equally balanced options, he cannot choose unless one side becomes preponderant through some indication, and that preponderation is conjecture. Fiqh is entirely based on such conjectures by mujtahids in reforming societal conduct. These conjectures are captured by the slightest imagination or clue, and all rational people rely on them in risky worldly matters. The author then provides a vivid example: if someone is advised to travel for trade, he might demand certainty that he will profit. The response cites successful precedents, but he counters with failures. Demanding certainty leads to paralysis—he will never trade or profit, and is deemed obsessive or cowardly. The author concludes that excessive exactitude in practical matters is pure folly, just as negligence in intellectual matters is pure ignorance. Everything must be taken from its appropriate source.

The Four Fundamental Inquiries in Islamic Logic
Author classifies the four types of questions: existence, definition, cause, and distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any scientific question into one of the four fundamental inquiries (matlab hal, ma, lim, ayy) and give examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Every scientific question reduces to existence, definition, cause, or distinction.

This passage introduces the foundational classification of questions that drive all scientific investigation in Islamic logic. The author categorizes questions based on the Arabic interrogative particle used, assigning each a specific purpose. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for framing correct arguments and avoiding confusion in theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of matalib
List the four fundamental inquiries: 'hal' (existence), 'ma' (conceptualization), 'lim' (cause), and 'ayy' (distinction). Note that each corresponds to a different interrogative particle and serves a distinct purpose in knowledge acquisition.
The author calls them 'ummahat al-matalib' (mothers of inquiries).
2
Distinguish absolute and restricted 'hal'
The 'hal' inquiry splits into two: absolute existence (e.g., 'Is Allah existent?') and restricted existence of an attribute (e.g., 'Is Allah willing?'). Recognize that the first asks about the thing itself, the second about a quality or state.
Some later texts call these 'hal basita' and 'hal murakkaba'.
3
Differentiate the two meanings of 'ma'
The 'ma' inquiry is about conceptualization (tasawwur). It has two levels: asking about the meaning of a name (e.g., 'What is the Anqa bird?') which precedes all inquiry, and asking about the essence/definition (e.g., 'What is man?') which assumes prior existence. Note the logical order: name first, then existence, then definition.
The first is called 'ma ismi' and the second 'ma haqiqi'.
4
Separate the two types of 'lim'
The 'lim' inquiry seeks the cause for the answer to 'hal'. It divides into: cause of assent (burhan al-inn / qiyas al-dalala) which justifies why we believe something exists, and cause of existence (burhan al-lim) which explains why something exists. For example, asking 'Why is the world originated?' can seek proof (assent) or the cause of its origination (existence).
This distinction is crucial for understanding types of demonstration.
5
Recognize 'ayy' and excluded inquiries
The 'ayy' inquiry seeks the distinguishing characteristic of a thing (e.g., 'Which property makes it unique?'). The author notes that questions like 'where', 'when', and 'how' (ayn, mata, kayf) are not fundamental. Consider why they are derivative rather than primary.
Some logicians merge 'ayy' with 'ma' or treat it separately.

Ghazali's Critique: Theology and Mathematics
Ghazali critiques the claim that theology requires mathematics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ghazali's critique of the claim that theology requires mathematics and evaluate the strength of his analogy.

Core Idea
Theology does not need arithmetic; insisting it does is a rhetorical trick.

Al-Ghazali, in his Tahafut al-Falasifah, exposes a common philosophical tactic: when challenged, they claim that theology is so deep that only those trained in mathematics and logic can answer. He dismisses this as groundless, arguing that discrete mathematics (arithmetic) has no essential connection to the divine sciences. This lesson unpacks his argument and its methodological significance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Find where the philosophers assert that theology requires mathematics as a prerequisite. Ghazali quotes them: 'one cannot attain knowledge of the answer to these objections except by first presenting mathematics and logic.'
This is a claim about epistemic dependence.
2
Extract Ghazali's counter-claim
Ghazali responds by separating mathematics into discrete quantity (arithmetic) and stating that theology has 'no connection' to it. He calls the opposing view a 'flagrant mistake' (khurq).
3
Analyze the analogy
Ghazali compares the claim to absurd statements like 'medicine needs arithmetic' or 'grammar needs arithmetic'. This is a reductio ad absurdum: if the original claim were true, then analogous absurdities would also be true, which they are not.
The analogy works by showing the claimed necessity is not domain-specific.
4
Assess the logical form
The critique uses an analogy to expose a false necessity. The strength depends on whether the analogy is apt. Here, the absurdity of 'arithmetic needs medicine' is clear, so the philosophers' claim is undermined.
This is a common critique in Islamic methodology: rejecting unwarranted methodological dependencies.
