Distinguishing the Two Arts of Logic
Author introduces the second art and its subject matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the subject matter of the first art (words as indicators) from the second art (meanings in themselves).

Core Idea
The second art studies meanings as they are in reality, not just as word meanings.

The author introduces the second art of logic and contrasts it with the first. While the first art examined how words signify meanings, the second art examines the meanings themselves, their existence and relations, regardless of how they are expressed. This distinction is foundational for understanding that logic deals with both linguistic and conceptual dimensions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two arts
The author explicitly states the difference: the first art considers the word in terms of its indication of meanings (اللفظ من حيث يدل على المعاني), while the second considers the meaning in itself (المعنى من حيث هو ثابت في نفسه).
2
Note the interdependence
The author points out that meanings cannot be defined except by mentioning words (إذ لا يمكن تعريف المعاني إلا بذكر الألفاظ). So the second art still relies on words for communication, but its focus is different.
This highlights the practical impossibility of separating meaning from expression entirely.
3
Relate to the purpose of logic
Understanding the domain of each art helps in analyzing arguments: sometimes the issue is about the meaning of a term, other times about the nature of the thing itself.
For example, debates about whether 'wine' means 'intoxicating beverage' (first art) vs. whether intoxication itself is the cause of prohibition (second art).

Classifying Existents: Sensible and Known by Inference
Author divides existents into sensible and intelligible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify existents as sensible (مَحْسُوس) or known by inference (مَعْلُوم بِالِاسْتِدْلَال) using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Existents are either directly sensed or known through their effects, and the certainty of inference can equal that of sensation.

The author proceeds to clarify the purpose of the second art by dividing existents according to how we perceive them. This classification is fundamental: some things are directly accessible to our five senses, while others, like mental faculties and attributes, are known only through their observable effects. The examples provided, such as the perceiving faculty itself, illustrate the method of inference (istidlal) and its epistemic strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The author divides existents into two: sensible (محسوس) and those known by inference (معلوم بالاستدلال). The key is whether the thing itself is directly perceived by the senses or not.
2
List examples of sensible things
Sensible things include colors, shapes, sounds, tastes, smells, and tactile qualities. The author lists them under the five senses.
Note that qualities like 'roughness' are also directly sensed, not just objects.
3
List examples of things known by inference
These include the perceiving faculty itself (القوة المدركة), power, knowledge, will, fear, shyness, love, anger, etc. They are known through their effects, e.g., writing indicates power and knowledge.
The author claims that the certainty of such inference is equal to certainty of direct sensation, as in the example of knowing the writer's abilities from the writing.
4
Understand the epistemic principle
The author asserts that we can have certain knowledge (يقين) of inferred things, just as we have certain knowledge of visible movements. This establishes that inference is a valid method for attaining certainty in logic and theology.
This is important for proofs of God's existence and attributes.

The Four Relations between Meanings
Al-Ghazali presents the four types of relationship between meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of concepts into one of the four relations: more general, more specific, equivalent, or partially overlapping.

Core Idea
Every pair of meanings fits one of four relations: general, specific, equivalent, or overlapping.

In this passage, Al-Ghazali provides a foundational tool for comparing concepts. When you encounter two terms in a definition or argument, knowing their logical relation is essential. This classification—general, specific, equivalent, or partially overlapping—is the key to precise reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings
Look at the two terms being compared in the example (e.g., 'human' and 'animal' or 'animal' and 'sensitive'). Write them down.
2
Determine the extension of each
Think about what each term applies to. For 'human', it applies to all human beings. For 'animal', it applies to all animals, including humans, birds, etc.
3
Check containment
Does one term's extension completely contain the other? If yes, the containing term is more general, the other more specific. If not, move to the next step.
For 'human' and 'animal', every human is an animal, but not every animal is human. So 'animal' is more general, 'human' more specific.
4
Check coextensiveness
If the two terms have exactly the same extension, they are equivalent. Example: 'animal' and 'sensitive' (if all animals are sensitive and all sensitive things are animals).
This is a metaphysical assumption; the logical point is coextension.
5
Identify partial overlap
If neither containment nor coextensiveness applies, the terms are partially overlapping: each includes some things the other does not. Example: 'white' and 'animal'—white includes non-animals (plaster), animal includes non-whites (crows).
This is the fourth relation.

The second art: On the simple meanings of existing things and the relation of some to others.

The particular and the general.

The second division of existents with respect to the relation of some to others in terms of generality and particularity: Know that a meaning among existing meanings, and a reality among established realities, when you relate it to others among those meanings and realities, you find it in relation to it either more general, or more specific, or equivalent, or more general in one aspect and more specific in another. For if you relate 'human' to 'animal', you find it more specific than it; and if you relate 'animal' to 'sensitive', you find it equivalent to it, neither more general nor more specific; and if you relate 'white' to 'animal', you find it more general in one aspect, because it includes plaster, camphor, and a multitude of inanimates, and more specific in another aspect, because it falls short of covering crows, black people, and a multitude of animals. So all realities are related in this consideration, not exceeding these four aspects. So apply what we have not mentioned to what we have mentioned.

Commentary
This passage is a logical classification of the relations between meanings (concepts) based on generality and specificity. Al-Ghazali begins this second art (after the earlier epistemological discussion) by introducing the division of existents according to the relation of some to others. He states that when you compare two meanings, you find one of four relations: (1) A is more general than B (e.g., 'animal' is more general than 'human'), (2) A is more specific than B (the converse), (3) they are equivalent (coextensive, e.g., 'animal' and 'sensitive' – it was believed that all animals are sensitive and vice versa), or (4) they are partially overlapping: more general in one aspect and more specific in another. The example of 'white' and 'animal' illustrates the fourth: white is more general in that it covers non-animals like plaster and camphor, but more specific in that it excludes some animals (e.g., crows, black people). This fourfold classification is fundamental for later discussions of definition and proof.

Concrete Individuals vs. Universals
Al-Ghazali divides existents into concrete individuals and universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between concrete individuals (a'yan) and universals (kulliyat) using al-Ghazali's division.

Core Idea
Concrete individuals are sensed; universals are what they share.

In this passage, al-Ghazali introduces a fundamental division of existents that underpins all later discussions of definition and predication. Understanding how he distinguishes between a determinate individual like 'Zayd' and the universal 'humanity' is the key to unlocking the theory of universals in Islamic logic. This skill is essential for analyzing definitions and arguments in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Find the two overarching categories al-Ghazali names: 'determinate individual existents' (a'yan) and 'non-determinate matters' (kulliyat). Note that he uses multiple terms for each (e.g., persons, particulars for individuals; general matters for universals).
2
Notice the examples for individuals
Al-Ghazali gives examples: Zayd, 'Amr, this horse, this tree, this whiteness. They are all concrete, sense-perceptible things. Also note that accidents like 'this whiteness' are included.
Accidents become determinate when tied to a subject.
3
Understand what individuals do NOT share
He stresses that individuals do not share their concrete identity ('ayn). Zayd's 'thisness' is not 'Amr's 'thisness'. The identity is unique to each individual.
4
Extract what they do share
Despite not sharing identity, individuals resemble each other in certain respects: corporeality, animality, etc. These shared aspects are what he calls universals.
5
Define universals based on the passage
Universals are not separate entities but the common features that account for resemblance among individuals. They are called 'general matters' or 'kulliyat'.
Al-Ghazali is implicitly rejecting Platonic realism here.

The third division: of existents with respect to determinateness and lack of determinateness: Know that existents divide into determinate individual existents, called a'yan (concrete individuals), persons, and particulars; and into non-determinate matters, called universals and general matters. As for concrete individual a'yan, they are the things first perceived by the senses, like Zayd, 'Amr, this horse, this tree, this sky, this star, and the like; and similarly this whiteness and this power, for determinateness enters into both accidents and substances. Then these persons, like Zayd, this horse, this tree, this whiteness, do not share in their concrete identity, for the concrete identity of this person is not the concrete identity of that other person, except that they resemble each other in matters, like the resemblance of these three in corporeality, and the resemblance of horse and man (but not tree) in animality. Thus, what constitutes resemblance among things is called universals and general matters.

Commentary
This passage presents the third division of existents: according to determinateness (ta'ayyun) versus indeterminacy. Al-Ghazali defines two categories. The first is determinate individuals (ashkhas or a'yan), which are directly perceived by the senses, such as specific persons, animals, trees, or even particular accidents like 'this whiteness' or 'this power' (note that accidents also become determinate when associated with a particular subject). The second category is non-determinate matters (umur ghayr muta'ayyina), called universals (kulliyat) and general matters (al-umur al-'amma). He emphasizes that individuals do not share their concrete identity (ayn) but share in certain aspects, like corporeality or animality. That which is shared is the universal. This lays the groundwork for the theory of universals and their role in definition and predication.

The Four Causes in Islamic Logic
Author classifies the four types of causes with definitions and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four causes (efficient, material, formal, final) in examples from Kalam, Fiqh, and everyday reasoning.

Core Idea
Every thing that has a cause can be analysed into four aspects: what makes it, what it's made of, its essence, and its purpose.

In this passage, the author breaks down the concept of 'cause' (al-'illa) into four distinct types. This classification is foundational for understanding demonstrations (burhan) in Islamic logic, because each cause can serve as the middle term when answering 'why?' The author provides examples from both rational (ma'qulat) and legal (fiqh) domains.

Guided Path
1
Identify the efficient cause
Look for the cause that originates motion or action: the agent. Examples: carpenter for chair, father for child, zina for killing in fiqh.
In fiqh, this is often called 'sabab'.
2
Identify the material cause
Find the substrate or stuff out of which something is made. Examples: wood for chair, blood/sperm for child, composition of opposites for human body.
Material cause answers 'out of what?'.
3
Identify the formal cause
Determine the form or essence that makes something what it is. Examples: form of the bed, form of man, form of sperm becoming human.
Formal cause answers 'what is it?'.
4
Identify the final cause
Find the purpose or goal for which something exists. Examples: shelter for house, sitting for bed, grinding for molars, deterrence for killing.
Final cause answers 'for what purpose?'.

Section on the divisions of the cause. The term 'cause' is applied to four meanings: (1) That from which the motion originates essentially, which is the cause of the thing's existence, like the carpenter for the chair and the father for the child. (2) The matter, which is that without which the thing cannot exist, like wood for the chair and menstrual blood and sperm for the child. (3) The form, which is the completion (tamam) of each thing; it may be called formal cause, like the form of the bed for the bed and the form of the house for the house. (4) The goal (ghaya), which is the first motive and the last thing intended, like shelter for the house and suitability for sitting for the bed. Know that each of these can serve as a middle term in demonstrations, since each can be given in answer to 'why'. As for the origin of motion, an example from rational (intellectual) matters: why did the prince fight so-and-so? It is said: because he plundered his province, so the plundering is the origin of motion. And it is said: why did so-and-so kill so-and-so? It is said: because the sultan compelled him to. And its example from fiqh: why was this person killed? It is said: because he committed zina or apostasy, so the zina is the origin of this matter, which jurists often call 'sabab'. As for matter, its example from rational matters: why does man die? You say: because he is composed of conflicting elements—heat, moisture, cold, dryness—that are antagonistic. And its example from fiqh: why is the contract of qirad and wakala invalidated by death and unconsciousness? It is said: because it is a weak, non-binding contract; this is a material cause, since dissolution occurs upon the contract just as death occurs upon a person due to a cause that is the origin of both death and dissolution. As for form, it is that by which the thing subsists; the bed is a bed by its form, not its wood, and man is man by his form, not his body. Things differ in their configurations (hay'at) by forms, not by matter. So it is not hidden that subsistence (qawam) is by form. For example, if it is said: why did this sperm become human and this wood become a bed? It is said: by the attainment of the form of man and the form of bedness. As for the goal for the sake of which the thing exists, its example from rational matters: why are molars present? It is said: because they are intended for grinding. Why did they fight such-and-such a class? It is said: to enslave them. And in fiqh: why are the adulterer, apostate, and murderer killed? It is said: to deter from obscenities. These four causes are present in everything that has a cause.
