Definition of Conversion (ʿAks) and the Rule for Universal Negative
Definition of conversion and rule for universal negative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a correct conversion of a universal negative proposition and distinguish proper conversion from mere inversion (inqlāb).

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate, preserving quality and truth; only then is it true ʿaks.

In this lesson, we learn the precise meaning of conversion (ʿaks) of categorical propositions. The author defines it as swapping the subject and predicate while keeping the quality (affirmative/negative) unchanged and only if the truth value stays the same. He warns that if truth is lost, it is not conversion but inversion (inqlāb). We will then apply this to the first type: the universal negative, which converts to itself.

The author states: make the predicate subject and the subject predicate, while preserving quality (kayfiyya) and truth value.
Preserving quality means the affirmative/negative character remains the same.
2

Conversion of Categorical Propositions
Author defines conversion and enumerates four types of categorical propositions with their conversion rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct conversion type for each of the four categorical propositions and apply the conversion rules to validly derive immediate inferences.

Core Idea
Conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality — but only for three of the four types.

In this passage, the author defines the key logical operation of conversion (ʿaks) and distinguishes it from inversion (inqilāb). He then systematically lists the four types of categorical propositions and their conversion rules, using both ordinary language examples and ambiguous letters. Understanding conversion is essential for manipulating syllogisms and deriving immediate inferences in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of conversion
The author defines conversion as making the predicate the subject and the subject the predicate while preserving both the quality (affirmative/negative) and truth. If truth is not preserved, the operation is called 'inversion' not 'conversion'.
The definition is crucial: conversion is only valid if the truth value remains the same.
2
Verify the rule for universal negative (E)
The author states that universal negative converts to universal negative. He provides a proof by contradiction: if some bird were human, then the original 'No human is a bird' would be false. Check this reasoning step by step.
This is the only type that converts to a universal of the same quantity.
3
Distinguish the rules for universal affirmative (A) and particular affirmative (I)
Universal affirmative converts to particular affirmative (not universal), because the predicate is broader. Particular affirmative converts to like itself (particular affirmative). The author warns that external knowledge might suggest a universal conversion, but logically it remains particular.
Remember: A→I (not A→A), I→I.
4
Confirm the rule for particular negative (O)
The author shows that particular negative does not convert at all, because the converse would be false or not equivalent. For example, 'Some animal is not human' (true) converts to 'Some human is not animal' (false). Also, the universal negative is not a valid conversion.
O is the only proposition type with no valid conversion.

Commentary
This passage defines conversion (ʿaks) as swapping subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality; if truth is not preserved, it is called inversion (inqilāb). The author then classifies categorical propositions into four types with their conversion rules. (1) Universal negative (E) converts to universal negative (E): e.g., 'No human is a bird' gives 'No bird is a human'. The author provides a proof by contraposition: if some bird were human, then that human would be a bird, contradicting the original truth. (2) Universal affirmative (A) converts to particular affirmative (I): 'Every human is an animal' gives 'Some animal is human', not 'Every animal is human' because animal is broader. (3) Particular negative (O) does not convert: 'Some animal is not human' is true, but 'Some human is not animal' is false; 'Every human is not an animal' is not a valid conversion. (4) Particular affirmative (I) converts to particular affirmative (I): 'Some people are writers' gives 'Some writers are people'. The author warns that sometimes one might think it converts universally due to external knowledge (e.g., only humans are writers), but the pure logical conversion is only to particular. Finally, the author mentions that logicians use ambiguous letters (a, b) to avoid confusion from extra-logical knowledge, and notes the utility of conversion for syllogistic reasoning. The sentence about universal negative converting to particular appears to be a textual corruption; the standard rule is universal to universal.

The Rule of Cause and Effect in Logical Reasoning
Author explains the logical relationship between cause and effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the correct causal implication rule and identify the fallacy of reversing the causal arrow.

Core Idea
Cause necessitates effect; effect absent implies cause absent, but not that effect caused cause's removal.

The relationship between cause and effect is foundational in Islamic logic, especially in kalam and usul al-fiqh. This passage from the Mi'yar al-'Ilm gives a concise rule: the existence of a cause necessitates the existence of its effect, but the absence of the effect only tells us that the cause was absent before it. It warns against the common mistake of thinking the effect's removal causes the cause's removal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three statements
Read the Arabic passage and list the three claims: (1) If cause exists, effect exists. (2) If effect does not exist, cause must have been absent prior. (3) If cause is removed, effect is removed. Mark which are direct and which are contrapositive.
2
Distinguish between necessitation and implication
Notice that claim (2) is a logical consequence of (1) by contraposition, but the author clarifies that the removal of the effect does not generate a new cause; it merely indicates prior removal of the cause. This guards against the fallacy of reversing causation.
3
Apply to a kalam example
Consider the argument: 'The world is temporally originated (an effect). Therefore it must have a cause (the Creator).' Using the rule, if the world were not temporally originated, we could infer that the cause was absent, but not that the absence of temporality caused the absence of the cause. This prevents the atheist from arguing that if the world were eternal, then God would not exist.
This is often a point of confusion in arguments about God's existence.
4
Identify the fallacy in common objections
When someone says, 'If God exists, then the world would have a purpose. The world has no purpose, so God does not exist,' the passage teaches that the absence of a proposed effect (purpose) can only tell us something about the cause if we already know the effect is necessary. But if the effect is not necessary, the inference fails. More precisely, the removal of an effect does not by itself prove the removal of the cause unless the effect is inseparable.

العلة، فإذا فرض موجوداً لزم أن يتبع وجوده وجود المعلول، وإذا كان المعلول مرفوعاً لزم أن يحكم أن العلة كانت أولاً مرفوعة حتى رفع هذا، لا أن رفع المعلول أوجب رفع العلّة، وأما العلة فإذا رفعناها وجب رفع المعلول بإيجاب رفع العلّة .
The cause: if it is assumed existent, then its existence necessitates the existence of the effect. And if the effect is removed, then it must be judged that the cause was first removed such that this was removed, not that the removal of the effect necessitated the removal of the cause. As for the cause, if we remove it, the removal of the effect is necessitated by the necessitation of the removal of the cause.

Commentary
This passage is a concise logical principle about the relationship between cause ('illa) and effect (ma'lul). The author states three points: First, if a cause is present, its effect must be present. Second, if an effect is absent, it implies that its cause must have been absent beforehand, but it is not that the absence of the effect caused the absence of the cause. Third, if the cause is removed, the effect is necessarily removed as well. This is a fundamental rule in causal reasoning, often used in kalam for arguments about the temporality of the world and in usul al-fiqh for determining the effective cause of rulings. The passage guards against the misunderstanding that the removal of an effect could cause the removal of its cause.
