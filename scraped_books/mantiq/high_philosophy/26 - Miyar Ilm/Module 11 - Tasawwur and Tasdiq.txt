Defining Particular and Universal Words
Author defines particular and universal words with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an Arabic term is particular or universal based on the logical criterion that a particular term prevents sharing in its concept, while a universal term does not.

Core Idea
A universal word does not prevent sharing; a particular word does.

In this passage, the author draws a fundamental distinction between particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli) words. The key criterion is whether the very conception of the word's meaning prevents sharing in that meaning. This distinction is essential for understanding how terms function in logical propositions and in Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Find the definition of particular (juz'i)
Look for the phrase that defines particular: 'ما يمنع نفس تصور معناه عن وقوع الشركة في مفهومه'. Understand that it prevents sharing by its concept alone.
Examples: Zayd, this tree, this horse.
2
Find the definition of universal (kulli)
Look for: 'الذي لا يمنع نفس تصور معناه عن وقوع الشركة فيه'. Understand that it does not prevent sharing from its concept, even if external factors limit actual instances.
Examples: human, horse, tree.
3
Distinguish the role of external factors
Note that if sharing is prevented due to external reasons (like the uniqueness of God), the word remains universal because the prevention is not from the concept itself.
The definite article (alif lam) often indicates a universal unless context makes it particular.
4
Apply to examples from the passage
Test with 'Zayd' (particular), 'al-insan' (universal), 'al-rajul' (universal in generic use, particular in reference to a known man).
Use the text's criterion to justify each classification.

The second division of the word concerning the generality and specificity of the meaning: the word is divided into particular and universal. The particular is that whose very conception of its meaning prevents sharing in its concept, such as your saying 'Zayd' and 'this tree' and 'this horse'; for what is conceived from the word 'Zayd' is a specific person whom no other shares in being understood from the word 'Zayd'. The universal is that whose very conception of its meaning does not prevent sharing in it; if it is prevented, it is due to something external to its concept and the requirement of its word, such as your saying 'human', 'horse', 'tree' — these are names of genera, species, and universal general meanings. This usage is current in the Arabic language for every name upon which the definite article (alif lam) enters, not in the context of referring to a previously known specific entity, like 'al-rajul' (the man) which is a generic name. For you might say 'the man came' intending a particular man known to the listener beforehand, so the definite article here is for definition (the man who previously came). If there is no such context, then the name 'man' is a universal name that encompasses every individual of men. If you say: 'the spherical shape encompassing twelve constellations is a sphere (falak)', and there does not exist in existence any shape with this description except one, how is the name universal while the named is one, and the definite article that requires comprehensiveness of the genus has been used? It is said to you: this is universal because we do not require that what falls under it exists actually; rather it can exist potentially and by possibility, and if its existence were supposed, it would fall under it necessarily, and before existence it falls under it, unlike the name 'Zayd' for which sharing is impossible both actually and potentially. If you say: 'the true God (al-ilah al-haqq)' is said, how is this universal while sharing in it is impossible both actually and potentially, and likewise our saying 'the sun' according to the one who does not permit another sun to exist, for it becomes definite like the person Zayd in conception? It is said to you: the word is universal, and the prevention of sharing in it is not due to the concept of the word itself but to an external meaning, which is the impossibility of two gods for the world. And we have not required in the word being universal except that it does not prevent sharing in it from its own concept and subject matter. So from the two questions and their answers, you have learned that the universal is of three kinds: a kind in which sharing exists actually, like our saying 'human' if individuals of it exist; a kind in which sharing exists potentially, like our saying 'human' if it happens that only one individual remains in existence, and the sphere surrounding twelve constellations when only one exists; and a kind with no sharing neither actually nor potentially, like 'God', yet it is still universal because the prevention is not from the word's object and predicate, unlike the word 'Zayd'. Commentary
This passage defines the logical concepts of particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli) terms, then defends the definition against two objections, and finally classifies universals into three types. The author begins with the second division of words, which is about their scope: a particular term, like 'Zayd', refers to a specific individual and cannot be shared; a universal term, like 'human', refers to a concept that can apply to many. An important nuance: the universal is defined by the fact that its concept does not prevent sharing; even if external factors prevent multiple instances (like the oneness of God), the word remains universal because the prevention is not from the concept itself. The author then addresses two objections: first, about the term 'the sphere' which refers to a unique celestial body, and second, about 'the true God' and 'the sun' which also have unique referents. In both cases, the answer is that the word is universal because the concept allows sharing; actual uniqueness is an external fact, not a logical property of the term. This leads to a threefold classification: universal with actual multiple referents, universal with potential multiple referents (only one currently exists), and universal with no possibility of multiple referents (due to external impossibility). The passage ends with a fiqhi note, noting that Islamic legal theorists (usuliyyun) debated the effect of the definite article on a singular noun, likely addressing whether it makes the term particular or universal. This shows the practical application of these logical distinctions in Islamic jurisprudence.

The Threefold Classification of Universals
Author refutes objections and classifies universals into three kinds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify universals into three kinds (actual sharing, potential sharing, no sharing due to external impossibility) and explain why unique referents like God and the sun are still universal.

Core Idea
A universal may have no actual multiple referents and still be universal if its concept allows sharing.

After defining the universal, the author addresses two objections about entities that are unique in existence. These objections refine the definition and lead to a threefold classification: universals with actual multiple referents, universals with only potential multiple referents, and universals with no possibility of multiple referents due to external impossibility. In all cases, the universality stems from the concept, not from external facts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first objection: the sphere
Find the objection: if the spherical shape encompassing twelve constellations (falak) is unique, how can it be universal? The author answers that potential existence suffices for universality; we do not require actual multiple referents.
The key point: the concept of a sphere does not prevent sharing, even if only one such sphere exists.
2
Understand the second objection: God and the sun
Find the objection: for 'the true God' and 'the sun' (according to some), even potential sharing is impossible. The author responds that the prevention is due to external impossibility (two gods cannot exist), not from the concept.
External factors do not affect the logical status of the term.
3
Extract the three kinds of universals
After the objections, the author lists three types: (1) sharing exists actually (e.g., 'human' when many humans exist), (2) sharing exists potentially (e.g., 'human' if only one remains, or the unique sphere), and (3) no sharing possible but still universal (e.g., 'God' because impossibility is external).
All three are universal because their concepts do not prevent sharing.
4
Compare with particular terms
Contrast these with particular terms like 'Zayd', which prevent sharing both actually and potentially from the concept itself. This highlights the difference.
Particular terms are inherently exclusive; universals are inherently inclusive in concept.

The Limits of Imagination and the Role of Reason
Al-Ghazali critiques imagination and divides existence into sensible and intelligible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why imagination cannot know metaphysical realities and distinguish between sensible and intelligible existence based on al-Ghazali's argument.

Core Idea
Imagination can only recombine sensed data; reason alone grasps metaphysical truths.

Al-Ghazali warns against equating knowledge with sensory perception. He shows that imagination, though powerful, is inherently limited to the material world. This lesson unpacks his argument and the crucial split between what can be sensed and what can only be known by reason.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the problem: overvaluing sensation
Al-Ghazali begins by warning not to magnify sensation and assume that what cannot be imagined has no reality. This is the central error he wants to correct.
He uses the example of power and knowledge: imagination tries to give them shape, color, etc., but their true nature is free from such attributes.
2
Understand how imagination works
Imagination can only combine or separate elements that have been previously sensed. It cannot create truly novel images. For example, you can imagine a horse with a human head, but not a fruit you have never seen.
Imagination follows sight most closely, so it tends to seek shape and color for everything.
3
Apply this to metaphysical concepts
When you try to imagine the Creator (e.g., His existence without direction), imagination automatically seeks a shape, color, location, etc. This leads to inappropriate anthropomorphism.
Al-Ghazali says this is a mistake: the Creator is not subject to such attributes.
4
Compare sense modalities
All five senses are on equal footing—they only perceive physical attributes. If imagination seeks a shape for a smell, it is misguided. Similarly, seeking sensory attributes for the Creator is misguided.
This reinforces that imagination cannot access metaphysical realities.
5
Grasp the division of existence
Al-Ghazali concludes that existence is divided into two categories: sensible (perceived by the senses) and intelligible (known by reason alone). For the latter, we must turn away from imagination and rely on reason.
This sets the stage for logic as a tool of the intellect to infer truths about the unseen.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological critique of the over-reliance on sensory perception and imagination. He then argues that what cannot be imagined may still have reality, using the example of power and knowledge: imagination tries to shape and color them, but their true nature is free from such attributes. He then diagnoses the root of confusion: in early life, senses dominate, especially sight. Imagination can only recombine previously sensed images; it cannot create entirely new ones. When we try to imagine something like the Creator, imagination automatically seeks a shape and color, assigning Him spatial attributes, which is inappropriate. Al-Ghazali emphasizes that all sense modalities (taste, smell, sight) are on equal footing in being limited to the physical. Therefore, one must recognize the division of existence into the sensible and the intelligible (perceptible by reason alone), abandon imagination, and rely on reason for the latter. This sets the stage for logic as a tool of the intellect.

Distinguishing Particulars from Universals
Author explains the third division of existents into determinate particulars and non-determinate universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given term is a particular (juz'ī) or a universal (kullī) based on whether it applies to one individual or many, using examples from the natural world and Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Particulars are unique individuals; universals are shared meanings.

Before we can build definitions or arguments in Islamic logic, we must grasp the basic building blocks of concepts. This passage from al-Mi'yār introduces the fundamental division of existents into determinate individuals (a'yān) and non-determinate universals (kulliyyāt). Understanding this distinction is essential for everything that follows, from defining terms to analyzing legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the division
The author states that existents are divided into two categories: determinate individuals (a'yān, shakhs, juz'ī) and non-determinate universals (kullī, amr 'āmm). Read the Arabic to confirm this distinction.
The terms 'ayn and shakhs refer to concrete particulars.
2
Recognize individuals
Find the list of individuals: Zayd, 'Amr, this horse, this tree, this whiteness. These are all perceived by the senses and each is unique. Note that even accidents like 'this whiteness' are considered individuals.
Determinateness applies to both substances and accidents.
3
Recognize universals
Look for what is shared: bodyness, animality, height, whiteness. These are the aspects by which individuals resemble each other. The author calls them 'universals' or 'general matters'.
Universals are not numerically identical across individuals; they exist as concepts.
4
Apply the test
Take any term. If it refers to exactly one thing (like 'this sky'), it is a particular. If it can apply to many things (like 'sky'), it is a universal. Practise with Islamic examples like 'the Prophet Muhammad' (particular) vs 'prophet' (universal).
This test is foundational for definitions and syllogisms.

Implicit Presence of Essential Parts in Concepts
Author answers the objection that definition parts are not always consciously present when knowing a thing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the definitional parts of a quiddity are implicitly present in the mind even when not consciously attended to.

Core Idea
Definitional parts are implicitly known; bringing them to explicit awareness confirms they were always present.

When we know a simple thing, do we also know its complex definition? A skeptic says no. The author responds with a subtle point: the essential parts of the concept are present latently, and we can verify this by asking someone who knows the thing whether they acknowledge those parts. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that when we know an originated thing (hadith), we know only one simple thing, yet its definition contains many parts (existence, non-existence, time, etc.). These parts are supposedly not in the mind.
2
Extract the author's key claim
The author asserts that all essential constituents (dhatiyat muqawwima) of a quiddity must enter into the conceptualization (tasawwur) along with the quiddity, but they may not occur in detail.
3
Understand the examples
If one knows an originated thing, he implicitly knows existence, non-existence, priority, etc.; if asked, he would affirm them, proving they were known. Similarly, one who knows a human implicitly knows animal, body, etc.
The examples show that denial would be false; thus the knowledge was present.
4
Formulate the implicit knowledge principle
Knowledge can be present latently without being explicitly attended to. When brought to mind in detail, one recognizes that it was already known. This resolves the objection.
5
Test the principle with another concept
Choose a concept like 'book'. Its essential parts include 'written work', 'pages', etc. Check if someone who knows 'book' would affirm these parts when asked.
This reinforces the universality of the principle.

Definition and Characteristics of Awwaliyat (First Principles)
Al-Ghazali defines awwaliyat as self-evident truths requiring only conceptualization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the defining features of awwaliyat and distinguish them from other types of certain premises.

Core Idea
Awwaliyat are truths the mind knows without proof, needing only to understand the words.

In this passage, Al-Ghazali introduces the most foundational type of certain premises in Islamic logic: awwaliyat, or first principles. These self-evident truths are accepted immediately by the mind once the terms are conceptualized. They form the bedrock of all certain knowledge, and recognizing them is essential for building valid arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject being defined
Read the passage and find the term that Al-Ghazali is defining. Notice that he begins by describing a type of knowledge without naming it, then later refers to it as 'this type of knowledge'.
The term is awwaliyat (الأوليات), though it appears later in the commentary.
2
Extract the conditions for assent
According to Al-Ghazali, what is required for the mind to assent to these propositions? He says it only depends on conceptualizing the simples (التصور) — the definitions and individual essences. No external evidence is needed.
This means once you understand the meaning of the terms, you automatically agree.
3
Analyze the given examples
Look at each example: 'Two is more than one', 'Three plus three is six', 'One thing cannot be both old and new', 'Negation and affirmation both cannot be true of one thing'. Verify that each requires only understanding the concepts, not empirical observation or reasoning.
These are purely a priori truths.
4
Understand the mental process
Al-Ghazali describes the mind as assenting 'necessarily' and 'as if it had always known it'. The mind is not aware of any source of this knowledge. This distinguishes awwaliyat from knowledge gained by inference or experience.
This necessity is logical, not psychological—it is impossible to doubt them once understood.

Distinguishing Mashhurat from Awwaliyat in Islamic Epistemology
Author explains mashhurat, their conditional truth, and why they suit fiqh but not burhan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mashhurat from awwaliyat and explain why mashhurat are suitable for fiqh but not for burhan.

Core Idea
Commonly accepted opinions are not certain; only primary axioms yield certainty.

In this passage, al-Ghazali clarifies a crucial epistemological distinction: not all widely accepted beliefs are certain. He shows that even a statement like 'Truth is good' depends on conditions, and that our social conditioning influences what we consider obvious. Through a thought experiment, he helps us see that some propositions are genuinely indubitable, while others only seem so due to custom. This distinction determines whether a premise can be used in demonstration (burhan) demanding certainty, or only in juristic reasoning (fiqh) where probability is sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of premises
Find where al-Ghazali contrasts 'mashhurat' (commonly accepted opinions) with 'awwaliyat 'aqliyya' (primary intellectual axioms). These are the two categories being distinguished.
Mashhurat are often based on social consensus; awwaliyat are self-evident.
2
Understand the conditional nature of mashhurat
Note the example 'Truth is good'. Al-Ghazali explains that this is not absolutely true but depends on conditions. He gives a counterexample: revealing the location of a prophet to be killed is truth but ugly.
This shows that mashhurat can be false in specific contexts.
3
Perform the thought experiment
Imagine you are created as an adult with no prior social conditioning. Consider the statement 'Killing a human is ugly'. Can you doubt it? Al-Ghazali says yes. This reveals that such premises are not self-evident.
The experiment strips away social norms.
4
Compare with primary axioms
Now consider 'Negation and affirmation cannot both be true' (non-contradiction) and 'Two is more than one'. Even in the thought experiment, these cannot be doubted.
These are awwaliyat: they do not depend on custom.
5
Draw the epistemological conclusion
Since mashhurat are 'close to truth but possibly false', they cannot yield certainty. Therefore they are unsuitable for burhan (which requires yaqin) but suitable for fiqh (where probability suffices).
This is a key principle in Islamic logic: the material of premises determines the strength of the conclusion.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological defense of how universal knowledge is possible given that sense perception only provides particulars. The author begins by stating that certain assent (tasdiq) requires seeking a middle term—for instance, to know the world is originated, one needs the middle term 'does not precede events'. An objector then raises a problem: all assents are preceded by concepts, and those concepts must be known. But if the only source of concepts is sense perception, which grasps only particulars, then no universal judgment could be justified. For example, seeing one person whose whole is greater than his part does not warrant the universal judgment that every whole is greater than its part. The author replies sharply: universals are intelligible ('aqliyya), not sensible (mahsusa). The intellect, not sense, is the faculty that makes universal judgments. He illustrates by analyzing the concept 'human'. The sensible human is always perceived with accidental qualities (color, size, etc.), but the intelligible human is the essence shared by all individuals regardless of accidents. The power that perceives the sensible human loaded with accidents is sense or imagination; the power that perceives the essence stripped of accidents is the rational faculty ('aql). Thus the gap between sense perception of particulars and intellectual perception of universals is vast. Universal judgments are about the universal essence abstracted from matter and accidents. This passage is crucial for understanding the Islamic epistemological distinction between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent), and the role of the intellect in grasping universals.

Abstraction of Universals from Particulars
Author explains abstraction of universals from particulars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the step-by-step process of abstracting a universal concept (e.g., 'body' or 'human') from particular sensory images using the internal faculties of imagination, estimation, and intellect.

Core Idea
Universals are not directly sensed but abstracted by the intellect from images, with divine aid.

How does observing a single person yield universal knowledge of 'humanity'? The author answers by explaining the role of internal faculties: sense feeds images to the imagination, which then compares and abstracts common and differential features, distinguishing essential from accidental. Even animals possess a form of this through estimation (wahm). Ultimately, the intellect's grasp of universals is illuminated by God or angels. This lesson walks you through that cognitive process.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sense from imagination
Understand that sense (al-hiss) conveys the form of an external object to the imaginative faculty (al-quwwa al-khayaliyya). Once the object is gone, the image remains in the imagination. This shows that imagination holds images independently of ongoing sensation.
This step is crucial because universals are abstracted from images, not from direct sensation.
2
Understand preservation by the retentive faculty
The retentive faculty (al-quwwa al-hafiza) preserves the images imprinted in the imagination, just as wax both receives and holds an engraving (unlike water which only receives).
Reception and preservation are distinct powers; this ensures images are available for later examination.
3
Examine images and identify commonality and difference
The imaginative faculty examines stored images (e.g., tree, animal, stone) and notices they all share corporeality (jismiyya) but differ in animality (hayawaniyya). It separates the common aspect into one universal and the differential aspects into other universals.
This abstraction yields universal concepts like 'absolute body' and 'animality'.
4
Distinguish essential from accidental
The intellect then determines which attributes are essential (dhati) to the thing (e.g., corporeality for animal: without it, the animal's essence is removed) and which are accidental (e.g., whiteness). This yields principles of specific concepts.
This step is foundational for definitions (hadd) in logic.
5
Recognize the role of estimation in animals
The author notes that animals also perceive non-sensible relations (e.g., enmity, compatibility) through the estimative faculty (al-wahm). This shows that abstraction of non-sensible meanings is not unique to humans.
In humans, wahm works alongside the intellect.

Commentary
This passage answers the objection: how can universal knowledge arise from observation of a particular? The author explains the process using internal faculties. First, sense conveys images to the imaginative faculty (al-quwwa al-khayaliyya), which retains them even after the object is gone. The retentive faculty (al-quwwa al-hafiza) stores these images. The author uses an analogy of vision and light: the rational power is like the eye, the imaginative particulars are like colored bodies, and the angelic illumination is like light that enables sight. This passage emphasizes that universal knowledge is not directly sensible but abstracted by the intellect with the help of internal senses, and ultimately the source of such knowledge is divine.

As for the rest of the intellects, the philosophers mentioned them in the Book of the Soul.

Commentary
This passage lists the philosophers' eight meanings of 'aql but elaborates only the first, which corresponds to the theologians' concept of intellect. The author begins by enumerating the eight: the intellect intended by theologians, theoretical, practical, material, habitual, actual, acquired, and active. Then he focuses on the first, tracing it to Aristotle's Posterior Analytics, where it is distinguished from 'ilm (knowledge). The first meaning refers to the innate concepts and assents that the soul possesses by fitrah, whereas 'ilm is acquired. This is a terminological distinction. The author then connects this to the theologian al-Baqillani's definition: intellect is necessary knowledge about what is possible and impossible, e.g., one thing cannot be both eternal and originated, one person cannot be in two places at once. Thus the first meaning of 'aql among philosophers is essentially the set of self-evident truths.

Discourse on the division of existence into the universal and the particular. Know that 'universal' is a homonym applied to two meanings: by one it exists in external realities, and by the second meaning it exists in minds, not in external realities. As for the first, it is for a thing taken absolutely without considering the attachment of another to it, nor considering its abstraction from another, rather without differentiation as to whether it is one. For example, 'human' is conceivable as a true essence, and the most necessary thing for humanity and most attached to it is its being one or many, since it cannot be conceived except thus. But the intellect is able to consider absolute humanity without paying attention to whether it is one or more. For humanity insofar as it is human is something, and insofar as it is one or more – whether potentially or actually – is something else. For humanity is human only without any other condition at all. Then generality or particularity is an additional condition beyond what is human, and unity and multiplicity are likewise. For whoever knows ‘human’ has known one thing, and whoever knows that the known human has unity has known two things: one is human and the other is unity. Similarly, if he knows multiplicity, and likewise if he knows particularity and generality. So all that is additional to the known. And that is not only if these states are assumed actually, but it is so even if assumed potentially. For you assume potentially the absolute human without attention to unity and multiplicity, and you assume unity and multiplicity after it. So in your consideration there is humanity and an addition of humanity to unity or multiplicity, and the assumption of unity and multiplicity is additional to the origin of humanity. Yes, multiplicity and unity necessarily accompany humanity in existence, but not everything that accompanies a thing is inherent in its essence. So we know that humanity insofar as it is humanity is not one or many. So there is a difference between our saying: ‘humanity does not exist except with one of the two states’ and our saying: ‘one of the two states belongs to it insofar as it is humanity.’ And the contradictory of our saying: ‘humanity insofar as it is humanity is one’ is not ‘humanity insofar as it is humanity is many,’ rather its contradictory is ‘humanity is not, insofar as it is humanity, one.’ And if that is so, it is possible for it to exist as one or many, but not insofar as it is humanity. So ‘universal’ may refer to absolute humanity devoid of the condition of unity or multiplicity or other accidents separate from every consideration except humanity, both negatively and affirmatively. And there is a difference between our saying: ‘humanity without any other condition’ and our saying: ‘humanity with the condition that nothing else accompany it,’ because the latter includes an additional condition of negation, while the former means the absolute that is completely cut off from what is beyond humanity, whether negative or affirmative. So the universal in this sense exists in external realities, for the existence of unity or multiplicity or other accidents with humanity, even if not insofar as it is humanity, does not cause humanity to depart from them in existence. For every existent along with something else not in its essence has an existence specific to it, and the joining of another to it does not necessitate the negation of its existence in terms of its essence. So humanity, when considered, exists actually in individual humans, predicated on each one, not as being one in essence nor as being many, for that is not insofar as it is humanity. The second meaning of ‘universal’ is, for example, humanity with the condition that it be predicated in some mode of predication on many. And this does not exist in external realities, for it is impossible for one identical thing to be predicated on each individual at one specific time. That is because the human that is enveloped by the accidents particular to the person Zayd is not enveloped by the accidents of ‘Amr, such that that identical humanity exists in ‘Amr numerically identical. And perhaps they are enveloped by contrary accidents. But that which is expressed is existent in minds in the sense that if the person Zayd comes to the senses first, an effect occurs in the soul, namely the impression of the form of humanity in it – and it does not know – and this form taken from humanity stripped of attention to particularizing accidents, if compared to the humanity of ‘Amr, would correspond to it, meaning that if a horse appeared to the senses after it, another effect would occur in the soul, and if ‘Amr appeared, no new effect would occur in the soul; rather all other persons are equal in that respect, whether existing persons or those possible to exist. Because its relation to all is equal, it was called ‘universal’ in this respect, for its relation to each one is one. So this form has a relation to one individual and has a relation to all other forms impressed in the soul. So when its relation to one individual and to others is one, its example of correspondence is likewise. For this reason it is said: it is universal. And its relation to the soul and to other forms in the soul is a particular relation, for it is one among individual cognitions.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'kulli' (universal) by distinguishing two distinct meanings. It begins by noting that 'kulli' is a homonym (ism mushtarak) applied to two meanings: one exists in external realities (a'yan) and the other exists only in minds (adhhan). The first meaning is expounded at length: it refers to a thing taken absolutely (mutaq) without any condition, such as humanity considered purely as a nature without regard to unity or multiplicity. The author argues that humanity insofar as it is humanity is neither one nor many; unity and multiplicity are additional accidents that accompany it in existence but are not part of its essence. This is proven by the fact that one can conceive of humanity without conceiving of its unity or multiplicity. Therefore, this absolute nature can exist in external individuals while still being predicated on each, not as one or many, but simply as the nature. The second meaning is humanity conditioned by being predicated on many (maqula 'ala kathirin). This, the author explains, cannot exist externally because it is impossible for one identical thing to be in multiple distinct individuals at the same time with their differentiating accidents. However, it does exist in the mind as a universal concept: when we encounter one individual, a mental form is impressed that is equally applicable to all similar individuals, as demonstrated by the fact that when a second similar individual appears, no new mental impression occurs. This form is called 'universal' because its relation to each individual is identical. The passage thus clarifies a central philosophical issue: the universal is not a single entity existing in multiple external objects, but rather exists as a concept in the mind that corresponds to many external individuals.

Discourse on the division of existence into the universal and the particular. Know that 'universal' is a homonym applied to two meanings: by one it exists in external realities, and by the second meaning it exists in minds, not in external realities. As for the first, it is for a thing taken absolutely without considering the attachment of another to it, nor considering its abstraction from another, rather without differentiation as to whether it is one. For example, 'human' is conceivable as a true essence, and the most necessary thing for humanity and most attached to it is its being one or many, since it cannot be conceived except thus. But the intellect is able to consider absolute humanity without paying attention to whether it is one or more. For humanity insofar as it is human is something, and insofar as it is one or more – whether potentially or actually – is something else. For humanity is human only without any other condition at all. Then generality or particularity is an additional condition beyond what is human, and unity and multiplicity are likewise. For whoever knows ‘human’ has known one thing, and whoever knows that the known human has unity has known two things: one is human and the other is unity. Similarly, if he knows multiplicity, and likewise if he knows particularity and generality. So all that is additional to the known. And that is not only if these states are assumed actually, but it is so even if assumed potentially. For you assume potentially the absolute human without attention to unity and multiplicity, and you assume unity and multiplicity after it. So in your consideration there is humanity and an addition of humanity to unity or multiplicity, and the assumption of unity and multiplicity is additional to the origin of humanity. Yes, multiplicity and unity necessarily accompany humanity in existence, but not everything that accompanies a thing is inherent in its essence. So we know that humanity insofar as it is humanity is not one or many. So there is a difference between our saying: ‘humanity does not exist except with one of the two states’ and our saying: ‘one of the two states belongs to it insofar as it is humanity.’ And the contradictory of our saying: ‘humanity insofar as it is humanity is one’ is not ‘humanity insofar as it is humanity is many,’ rather its contradictory is ‘humanity is not, insofar as it is humanity, one.’ And if that is so, it is possible for it to exist as one or many, but not insofar as it is humanity. So ‘universal’ may refer to absolute humanity devoid of the condition of unity or multiplicity or other accidents separate from every consideration except humanity, both negatively and affirmatively. And there is a difference between our saying: ‘humanity without any other condition’ and our saying: ‘humanity with the condition that nothing else accompany it,’ because the latter includes an additional condition of negation, while the former means the absolute that is completely cut off from what is beyond humanity, whether negative or affirmative. So the universal in this sense exists in external realities, for the existence of unity or multiplicity or other accidents with humanity, even if not insofar as it is humanity, does not cause humanity to depart from them in existence. For every existent along with something else not in its essence has an existence specific to it, and the joining of another to it does not necessitate the negation of its existence in terms of its essence. So humanity, when considered, exists actually in individual humans, predicated on each one, not as being one in essence nor as being many, for that is not insofar as it is humanity. The second meaning of ‘universal’ is, for example, humanity with the condition that it be predicated in some mode of predication on many. And this does not exist in external realities, for it is impossible for one identical thing to be predicated on each individual at one specific time. That is because the human that is enveloped by the accidents particular to the person Zayd is not enveloped by the accidents of ‘Amr, such that that identical humanity exists in ‘Amr numerically identical. And perhaps they are enveloped by contrary accidents. But that which is expressed is existent in minds in the sense that if the person Zayd comes to the senses first, an effect occurs in the soul, namely the impression of the form of humanity in it – and it does not know – and this form taken from humanity stripped of attention to particularizing accidents, if compared to the humanity of ‘Amr, would correspond to it, meaning that if a horse appeared to the senses after it, another effect would occur in the soul, and if ‘Amr appeared, no new effect would occur in the soul; rather all other persons are equal in that respect, whether existing persons or those possible to exist. Because its relation to all is equal, it was called ‘universal’ in this respect, for its relation to each one is one. So this form has a relation to one individual and has a relation to all other forms impressed in the soul. So when its relation to one individual and to others is one, its example of correspondence is likewise. For this reason it is said: it is universal. And its relation to the soul and to other forms in the soul is a particular relation, for it is one among individual cognitions.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'kulli' (universal) by distinguishing two distinct meanings. It begins by noting that 'kulli' is a homonym (ism mushtarak) applied to two meanings: one exists in external realities (a'yan) and the other exists only in minds (adhhan). The first meaning is expounded at length: it refers to a thing taken absolutely (mutaq) without any condition, such as humanity considered purely as a nature without regard to unity or multiplicity. The author argues that humanity insofar as it is humanity is neither one nor many; unity and multiplicity are additional accidents that accompany it in existence but are not part of its essence. This is proven by the fact that one can conceive of humanity without conceiving of its unity or multiplicity. Therefore, this absolute nature can exist in external individuals while still being predicated on each, not as one or many, but simply as the nature. The second meaning is humanity conditioned by being predicated on many (maqula 'ala kathirin). This, the author explains, cannot exist externally because it is impossible for one identical thing to be in multiple distinct individuals at the same time with their differentiating accidents. However, it does exist in the mind as a universal concept: when we encounter one individual, a mental form is impressed that is equally applicable to all similar individuals, as demonstrated by the fact that when a second similar individual appears, no new mental impression occurs. This form is called 'universal' because its relation to each individual is identical. The passage thus clarifies a central philosophical issue: the universal is not a single entity existing in multiple external objects, but rather exists as a concept in the mind that corresponds to many external individuals.

Understanding the Universal and the Complete vs. Incomplete
Author introduces second art, defines universal, and distinguishes complete/incomplete.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the universal (kulli) as a single mental image applicable to multiple individuals and differentiate it from the complete (tamm) and incomplete (naqis) categories.

Core Idea
The universal is a mental concept, not a real thing; completeness is about having all possible attributes, not about universality.

This lesson tackles a fundamental puzzle in Islamic logic and theology: how can two different things (like black and white) share a common attribute (color) yet still be different? The author explains that the universal exists only in the mind, and then introduces a new distinction—complete versus incomplete—that is often confused with the universal-particular distinction. By grasping these concepts, you will be equipped to handle many subsequent logical and theological discussions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mutakallimun's confusion
The author says the mutakallimun were confused by 'states' (ahwal). They could not understand how black and white both share 'color' yet differ in something else. This puzzle arises because they treated universals as real external things.
2
Understand the author's resolution
The author resolves the confusion by asserting that universals exist only in the mind, not externally. The universal image (like 'humanity') is a single mental representation that corresponds to many individuals through similarity, not numerical identity.
Focus on the phrase: 'there is no existence of it being universal in this respect externally'.
3
Grasp the example of humanity
The example of 'humanity' for Zaid and 'Amr shows that the mental image of humanity is one, yet it applies equally to both. Their humanity is similar, not identical in number. This is the meaning of universality.
Contrast this with the mutakallimun's view, which would require the humanity of Zaid and 'Amr to be numerically one outside the mind.
4
Distinguish universal from complete/incomplete
The author explicitly warns that 'complete' and 'incomplete' are not the same as 'universal' and 'particular'. 'Complete' means something has all that can possibly belong to it (in existence, potential, or quantity). 'Incomplete' is its opposite.
For example, a human being is complete in terms of existence but not in terms of all potential attributes (e.g., knowledge).

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and epistemological introduction to the second art of this work, which deals with the division of existence by its essential accidents. The author begins by noting that the problem of 'states' (ahwal) confused the mutakallimun. They debated whether attributes like 'color' exist in the mind or externally. The key issue is that two things, like black and white, share in the universal 'color' but differ in their specific differences. This requires understanding universal concepts. The author then clarifies the nature of the universal: it is a single mental image that corresponds to multiple individuals. For example, the image of 'humanity' in the mind is one, yet it applies equally to Zaid and 'Amr. This universality is not a real union but a similarity in meaning. The author emphasizes that grasping the universal is crucial because all intelligible concepts (ma'qulat) depend on it. Finally, the distinction between 'complete' (tamm) and 'incomplete' (naqis) is introduced, clarifying that this is not the same as the distinction between particular and universal. 'Complete' means something that possesses all that can possibly belong to it, whether in actual existence, active potential, passive potential, or quantity. 'Incomplete' is simply its opposite. This distinction is important for understanding further divisions of existence.
