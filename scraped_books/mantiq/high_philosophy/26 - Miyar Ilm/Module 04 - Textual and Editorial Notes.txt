Al-Ghazali's Bibliography as Context for His Logic
Presents a list of al-Ghazali's major works as front matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize al-Ghazali's major works by discipline and explain the significance of this list for understanding his logical method.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali's bibliography proves he was a polymath; his logic was applied across all sciences.

This passage presents a list of works by Abu Hamid al-Ghazali, taken from the front matter of his logic treatise Mi'yar al-'Ilm. While it may seem like a simple catalogue, it offers a window into the breadth of his scholarship. By surveying the titles, you will see that al-Ghazali wrote on preaching, theology, Sufism, law, and more. This context is crucial: his logic was not an abstract exercise but a tool for every Islamic science. In this lesson, you will learn to read such a bibliography as a key to the author's intellectual project.

Guided Path
1
Survey the list for familiar titles
Scan the Arabic list and note any titles you recognize from earlier studies. Write down at least three.
2
Categorize by discipline
For each title, guess the subject based on words in the title (e.g., 'فروع' suggests fiqh, 'تفسير' suggests tafsir, 'أحوال النفس' suggests Sufism).
3
Identify the logic work's place
Notice that Mi'yar al-'Ilm is not in this list because it is the book you are reading. The list serves as an introduction to the author, not the current work.
4
Reflect on the editor's purpose
Why would an editor include a bibliography in a logic book? To establish the author's authority across disciplines, thus predisposing the reader to trust his logical method.

Using a Bibliography in Islamic Research
Author lists the sources and references used in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the scholarly scope of an Islamic work by analyzing its bibliography.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the scholarly foundations of an Islamic work.

This passage presents a bibliography of sources used in the book. Bibliographies are crucial for understanding the breadth of a scholar's research and for verifying claims. In Islamic sciences, a comprehensive bibliography indicates rigour and allows readers to trace the origins of arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of bibliography entries
Each entry typically includes author, title, editor/commentator, publisher, place, edition, and year. Note the consistent pattern.
2
Categorize sources by discipline
Group entries into categories such as hadith (e.g., Sahih al-Bukhari), philosophy (e.g., Tahafut al-Falasifa), literature (e.g., Diwan al-Mutanabbi), and biography (e.g., Al-A'lam).
This reveals the interdisciplinary nature of the work.
3
Note publication details and editions
Check for critical editions, dates, and publishers. A recent critical edition indicates reliability. 'No date' entries may be less authoritative.
4
Assess the scholarly significance of the sources
Consider why the author used these specific works. For example, citing Tahafut al-Falasifa suggests engagement with philosophical theology, while Sahih al-Bukhari indicates grounding in hadith.
This helps evaluate the author's methodology and scope.
