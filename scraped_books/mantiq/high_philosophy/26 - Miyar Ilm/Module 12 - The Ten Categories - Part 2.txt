The Rational Proof for the Ten Categories
Author argues the ten categories are established by proof, not tradition, with three claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's three-part proof for the exhaustiveness of the ten categories and distinguish it from blind acceptance.

Core Idea
The ten categories are not tradition; they are established by rational demonstration.

In this passage, the author responds to a potential objection: is the classification into ten categories merely inherited from earlier philosophers, or is there a rational proof? He emphatically rejects taqlid and outlines three claims that together constitute a burhan. Let's walk through his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The author sets up a question: Is this enumeration taken by tradition (taqlid) from predecessors, or is there a proof (burhan)? Recognize that this is a common challenge to established classifications.
2
State the Author's Response
The author strongly rejects taqlid, calling it the business of the blind. He asserts the classification is established by proof. This sets the epistemological stance: rational demonstration over blind acceptance.
3
Extract the Three Claims
The author breaks the proof into three claims: (1) these ten exist, (2) nothing exists outside them, (3) it is not possible to reduce them to nine. Write these down as the structure of the argument.
These claims correspond to existence, exhaustiveness, and irreducibility.
4
Understand the Justification for Each Claim
For claim 1: known by intellect and sense. For claim 2: everything perceived is either substance or accident, and all fall under these headings. For claim 3: each category is distinct, as shown by their differences.
The author references earlier details for full justification.
5
Assess the Completeness
Does the author provide a complete proof here? He outlines the structure but relies on prior discussion. Recognize this as a summary argument, not a full demonstration.

The author does not here enumerate the categories but assumes the reader knows them. The emphasis is on the epistemic status of the classification: it is not conventional but grounded in reality.

Distinguishing Categories: Relation vs. Place vs. Passion
Author resolves doubt about categories involving relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the additional attribute that distinguishes a category like 'where' (ayn) or 'passion' (infiʿāl) from pure relation (iḍāfa maḥḍa).

Core Idea
Categories are distinguished by an extra attribute beyond mere relation.

When learning the ten categories, it is easy to confuse those that involve a relationship, like relation (iḍāfa), place (ayn), and passion (infiʿāl). The author of the Miʿyār al-ʿIlm anticipates this confusion and provides a method to resolve it: look for what each category adds beyond pure relation. This lesson will walk you through that method using the examples given in the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three confusable categories
Note that pure relation (iḍāfa maḥḍa) involves a relation between two things without any extra qualification. Place (ayn) involves a relation plus the specific attribute of being in a location. Passion (infiʿāl) involves a relation plus being affected. Write down each category and what it adds.
2
Extract the 'extra' attribute
For each category besides relation, ask: what does this category have that pure relation does not? For ayn, it is the attribute of being located; for infiʿāl, it is the attribute of undergoing change. This extra attribute makes it a distinct category.
3
Test with a concrete example
Take a substance, e.g., a stone. Its relation to the place (being on the ground) is ayn. Its relation to another stone by juxtaposition (being next to it) belongs to ayn as well, but the author notes a potential confusion due to naming. Apply the method: both involve a relation plus the attribute of spatial location, so they belong to ayn, not pure relation.
4
Apply the method to a new case
Consider the category of 'when' (matā). It involves a relation to time plus the attribute of temporal occurrence. Use the same logic to see why it differs from pure relation.

Resolving Doubts via Linguistic Analysis: Rarefaction and Condensation
Author resolves doubt about rarefaction and condensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to resolve a categorical doubt by examining the cause of the doubt, particularly the sharing of names (ishtirāk al-ism).

Core Idea
Categorical doubts often arise from ambiguous language, not from the things themselves.

The author explicitly addresses a doubt about whether rarefaction (takakhul) and condensation (takāthuf) belong to the category of quality (kayfiyya) or position (waḍ‘). He traces the doubt to linguistic confusion: the same word can refer to both the process and the resulting state. By defining the terms clearly, the categorial membership becomes evident. This lesson will train you to apply similar analysis to other doubtful cases in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubted categories
The doubt is whether takāthuf and takhallul are quality or position. Write down what each category means: quality is an attribute like color or texture; position is the arrangement of parts (e.g., sitting, standing).
2
Trace the cause of doubt
The author says the doubt arises from 'sharing of names' (ishtirāk al-ism). The same term (e.g., 'rarafaction') might refer both to the process of thinning and the resulting state of being thin. One name, two meanings. This linguistic ambiguity leads to confusion about categorization.
3
Apply the definitions provided
The author defines takhallul as the distancing of parts due to interpenetration of foreign bodies. This is a process involving change in arrangement, which points to position (waḍ‘). Similarly, takāthuf is the approximation of parts, a change in arrangement. Thus, they belong to waḍ‘, not quality.
4
Generalize the method
When you encounter a term whose category is disputed, first check if the same name has multiple meanings. Clarify what each meaning refers to. Then decide the category based on the core attribute of the meaning in question.

Disambiguating Relational Categories
Author discusses confusions between categories and their causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the category of pure relation (idafah) and other categories that involve relation, such as 'where' (ayn), by identifying the extra element beyond mere relation.

Core Idea
Relation alone is not enough—look for the specific nature behind the name.

Al-Ghazali observes that categories like 'where' (ayn) and 'position' (wad') share a relational aspect with the category of relation (idafah), leading to confusion. The key is to recognize that in such categories, the relational aspect is only part of the concept; there is an additional specific nature. This lesson trains you to spot that extra element and correctly assign phenomena to their proper categories, a skill vital for sound reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories being confused
From the passage, find which categories are being compared. For example, pure relation (idafah) and 'where' (ayn) are mentioned as potentially confused.
2
Extract the common relational aspect
Both categories involve a connection between two things. Write down that common relational feature.
For 'where', the relation is between a substance and its place.
3
Determine the extra element beyond relation
The passage asserts that in 'where' there is an additional specific nature beyond relation. Identify what that is—here, it is the spatial dimension (being in a location).
4
Attribute confusion to naming (not essence)
Al-Ghazali explains that the confusion arises because the name for 'where' is coined from a relational description, making it appear as pure relation. Conclude that the categories are distinct in essence, and the doubt is due to a shortcoming in naming.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion from the first art of logic concerning the categories. The author begins by stating that every object of intellect is either a substance or an accident, and that every substance can be included under this general statement. He then addresses the claim that one might limit the categories to nine accidents, arguing that it is not possible because the differences among the categories necessitate their distinctness. The main body of the passage deals with potential confusions that arise when distinguishing between categories, especially those that involve relation. The author notes that pure relation (idafah) may be confused with the category of 'where' (makan) or 'passion' (infi'al) because these also involve a relation, but they have an additional component beyond mere relation. However, careful examination reveals the distinction. He gives a concrete example: the confusion between a substance's relation to its place and its relation to another substance by way of adjacency. This confusion arises because the name for the category 'where' (ayn) is derived from a relational description, leading one to think it belongs to the category of relation. Similarly, he discusses the case where a genus name is relational but its species names are not, causing confusion about which category they belong to. He attributes this to a shortcoming of the name-giver. Finally, he addresses the specific example of condensation (takaathuf) and rarefaction (takhalul): whether they fall under quality (kayfiyyah) or position (wad'). The confusion here is due to the homonymy (ishtirak) of the term, as the words can refer either to the process (which might be quality) or the resulting configuration (which might be position). He defines rarefaction as the distancing of parts due to interpenetration, and condensation as the approximation of parts by compression. The passage thus highlights epistemological challenges in applying the categories correctly, emphasizing the need for precise terminology.

Resolving Doubts via Homonymy Awareness
Author discusses confusions between categories and their causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a doubt about category membership is due to homonymy (ishtirak al-lafz) and resolve it by distinguishing the different meanings of the ambiguous term.

Core Idea
One word can hide two distinct categories—unpack the meanings.

Al-Ghazali illustrates a second source of confusion: homonymy. A single term like 'condensation' (takaathuf) can refer either to a process (a quality) or to the resulting configuration (a position). This lesson equips you to detect such equivocation and correctly assign the phenomenon to its proper category based on the intended meaning, a skill essential for precision in Islamic legal and theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous term
Look for terms in the passage that are used to describe phenomena that could belong to different categories. Example: 'condensation' (takaathuf).
2
List the possible meanings of the term
From the passage, extract the definitions: rarefaction (takhalul) as the distancing of parts due to interpenetration (a process, which is a quality), and condensation as the approximation of parts (also a process). But the passage also hints that these could be considered under 'position' (wad') which concerns the arrangement of parts.
The doubt arises because the same word can denote the process (quality) or the resulting arrangement (position).
3
Determine which meaning each category captures
Al-Ghazali notes that homonymy causes the confusion. Decide which meaning belongs to which category: the process of interpenetration/reduction is a quality (kayfiyyah), while the final configuration of parts is a position (wad').
4
Apply the principle: distinguish by the intended sense
When encountering a doubtful case, check whether the term is being used in its process sense or its configuration sense. This resolves the ambiguity.

Quwwa as Principle of Change: Passive and Active Potency
Author defines potentiality and divides it into passive and active, giving examples and three distinctions.

Core Idea
Quwwa means both 'potentiality opposite actual' and 'principle of change' – confusing them leads to error in natural philosophy.

The term quwwa (قوة) is ambiguous: it can mean potentiality (opposite of actual) or the principle of change. The author warns us not to confuse them and then provides a clear division of the second meaning into passive and active potency, with vivid examples from nature. Mastering this distinction is essential for engaging with Islamic philosophical discussions on causality, change, and divine attributes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of quwwa
Find in the Arabic where the author says 'quwwa may be applied to another meaning' (قد يطلق على معنى آخر). List the two meanings: (1) potentiality opposite actual, (2) principle of change. The passage focuses on the second.
The Arabic word quwwa covers both concepts; context determines which is meant.
2
Extract the division of the principle of change
Read where the author says 'quwwa is the principle of change, either in the patient – passive potency – or in the agent – active potency' (مبدأ التغيير إما في المنفعل وهو القوة الانفعالية وإما في الفاعل وهو القوة الفعلية). The discourse on the division into the one and the many and their concomitants.
Know that 'one' is a name for a thing that does not accept division from the aspect from which it is said to be one. But the aspects due to which division is impossible and unity is established in relation to them are many. Among them is what is not divided in genus, so it is one in genus, as we say: the horse and man are one in animality, since there is no difference between them except in number, species, and accidents. As for animality, there is no difference and division between them in it. Among them is what is not divided in species, as you say: the ignorant and the learned are one in species, i.e., in humanity. Among them is what is not divided by a common accident, as we say: the raven and the mouse are one in blackness. Among them is what is not divided by proportion, as we say: the ratio of the king to the city and the ratio of the intellect to the soul are one. Among them is what is not divided in the subject, as we say: the growing and the withering are one in the subject, and likewise the smell of the apple, its taste, and its color come together in one subject, so it is said: these things are one, i.e., in the subject, not in every respect. Among them is what is not divided in its meaning in number, or is divided into numbers sharing in something, like the head, for it is one of a person, i.e., it divides into parts that have the meaning of 'head'. Among them is what is not divided by definition, i.e., its reality is not found for another and it has no peer in the perfection of its essence, as the sun is said to be one and the most deserving of things to be called one. The one in number then divides into what has actual multiplicity and is one by composition and aggregation, like a house for example, and into what has no actual multiplicity but has potential multiplicity not actual, like the body insofar as it is a body, i.e., having a continuous corporeal form, and into what has no multiplicity either actual or potential, which is every single substance according to the philosophers, and the Essence of the First Truth likewise by agreement, and this is established for the substance that is not a body, the individual isolated unit according to the mutakallimun, for it is not divisible potentially or actually, and it is one in number. That which accepts division neither potentially nor actually is most deserving of the name 'one'. The meaning understood from 'multiplicity' in contrast to unity in every rank, and the absolute many in contrast to the absolute one, is that in which there is a one and it is not one from the aspect in which it is, i.e., there is in it a one that is not a unity in it, and it is that which is answered by calculation. The many may also be many by relation. Union in quality is called similarity, in quantity equality, in genus homogeneity, in species analogy, union in extremities is called correspondence. From this emerges the explanation of the meaning of the one in genus, the one in species, the one in number, the one by accident, and the one by equality. The sum of the relations of the one are similarity, equality, correspondence, homogeneity, and analogy, and the types of the many are their opposites.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed definitional and classificatory account of the concept of 'the one' (al-wahid). The author begins by defining 'one' as that which does not accept division from the aspect from which it is called one. He then enumerates several types of indivisibility, each giving a specific sense of unity. The first type is indivisibility in genus: for example, horse and man are one in animality (genus), because they share the essence of animality without difference in that respect. The second is indivisibility in species: the ignorant and the learned are one in humanity (species). The third is indivisibility by a common accident: the raven and the mouse share in blackness, which is an accidental property common to both. The fourth is indivisibility by proportion: the relationship of a king to his city is analogous to the relationship of the intellect to the soul; these ratios are considered one in terms of proportionality. The fifth is indivisibility in the subject: different attributes like growth and withering, or the smell, taste, and color of an apple, inhere in a single subject, making them one 'in subject'. The sixth is indivisibility in meaning concerning number, like the head which is one for a person but has parts that each share the meaning 'head'. The seventh is indivisibility by definition: the sun is unique in its essence with no peer, so it is one by definition. After listing these, the author turns to the subdivision of the 'one in number' into three categories: (1) that which has actual multiplicity but is one by composition, like a house made of many parts; (2) that which has potential multiplicity but not actual, like a continuous body that is potentially divisible; (3) that which has neither actual nor potential multiplicity, like a simple substance (e.g., a single atom or the Divine Essence). This last is the most deserving of the name 'one'. The passage then discusses the many (al-kathir) and enumerates various relations of unity: similarity (union in quality), equality (union in quantity), homogeneity (union in genus), analogy (union in species), and correspondence (union in extremities). These relations correspond to different senses of 'one'. The many is simply the opposite of each of these. This lesson requires careful parsing of each type and its example, familiarizing the student with the Aristotelian-Ibn Sinic classification of unity.

Key Terms
واحد (wāḥid): That which is not divided from the aspect from which it is called one; it has various types based on the mode of indivisibility. [Note: No significant shift; this is the standard logical/metaphysical definition from Ibn Sina onwards.]
جنس (jins): A common predicate that answers 'what is it?' and includes multiple species; here, indivisibility in genus means sharing the same genus. [Note: Stable term across logic and theology.]
عرض عام ('araḍ 'āmm): A universal accident that belongs to multiple species, like blackness belonging to both raven and mouse. [Note: In logic, 'araḍ 'āmm is distinct from 'araḍ khāṣṣ; this usage is standard.]

Distinguishing Necessary and Contingent Existence
Author defines the condition for necessary existence and distinguishes it from contingent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key condition that makes a being not necessary in itself and distinguish necessary from contingent existence based on the passage.

Core Idea
A necessary being requires no external condition; a contingent being depends on a cause.

In Islamic metaphysics, the distinction between necessary and contingent existence is foundational for proofs of God's existence. This passage from the Mi'ar al-Ilm defines the criterion: if a being's existence or non-existence depends on something external, it is contingent; if it does not, it is necessary. We will parse the text to understand these conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two states
The passage mentions existence in two states regarding an attribute: absence of the attribute and presence of the attribute. Note that the attribute is external to the essence.
The attribute is something predicated of the being, not the essence itself.
2
Find the condition for not-being-necessary
If the being's existence in either state depends on something external (negation or affirmation) such that it cannot be devoid of it, then it is not necessary in itself.
The external condition could be a negation (lack of something) or affirmation (possession of something).
3
Extract the causal conditions for contingent existence
For a contingent being: existence requires the presence of a cause; non-existence requires either absence of that cause or presence of a non-existent cause. So its essence always has a condition attached.
Non-existent cause means a cause that brings about non-existence, i.e., a hindrance.
4
Contrast with necessary being
A necessary being has no such condition; its essence necessitates existence without any external factor. Thus, it is 'necessary in itself'.
The passage defines necessary being indirectly by exclusion.
