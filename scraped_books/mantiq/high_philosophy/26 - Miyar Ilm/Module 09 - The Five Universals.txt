Particular Terms (al-Juz'i)
Author defines particular term with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define a particular term based on its inherent prevention of conceptual sharing.

Core Idea
A particular term locks meaning to one individual.

Words behave differently: some point to unique individuals, others to entire categories. The author distinguishes these two types in a fundamental division. Let us first examine the particular term, where the meaning itself forbids any partner.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of 'particular'
Find the sentence beginning with 'الجزئي ما يمنع...' and identify the key condition: prevention of sharing (sharika) by the very conception of its meaning (نفس تصوّر معناه).
2
Parse the examples
Look at 'زيد', 'هذا الشجر', 'هذا الفرس'. Recognize that each points to a specific entity: Zayd is a unique person; 'this tree' refers to one tree; 'this horse' refers to one horse. The word cannot apply to another individual without changing the reference.
Contrast with a universal like 'human' which can apply to many.
3
Formulate the condition in your own words
Restate: a particular term is one whose very concept (mafhum) blocks any other individual from being indicated by the same word in the same sense.
This is the core logical property.

Commentary
This passage defines the distinction between particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli) terms. The author states this as the second division of words regarding the generality or specificity of meaning. For example, the word 'Zayd' refers to a specific individual; no one else is understood from that word. Similarly, 'this tree' points to a particular tree. Examples include 'human,' 'horse,' 'tree,' which are names of genera and species. The author notes that these are names of genera (ajnas), species (anwa'), and meanings (ma'ani). This lays the foundation for the theory of universals in logic.

Universal Terms (al-Kulli)
Author defines universal term with examples and external restriction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define a universal term, explaining why sharing is not prevented by the concept itself.

Core Idea
A universal term leaves the door open for sharing.

After defining particular terms, the author turns to their counterpart: universal terms. Unlike the particular, a universal term does not block the possibility of multiple individuals sharing in its meaning. Let us see how the author explains this and what it implies for logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of 'universal'
Find 'والكلّي هو الذي لا يمنع نفس تصوّر معناه عن وقوع الشركة فيه'. Note the negation: it does NOT prevent sharing.
The key is the absence of inherent prevention.
2
Examine the external restriction clause
The author adds that if sharing is prevented, it is due to something outside the concept (بسبب خارج عن نفس مفهومه). This means the term itself remains universal, but external circumstances may limit its application. For instance, the word 'al-Insan' (human) is universal even if only one human exists.
This nuance prevents confusion between the term's nature and real-world application.
3
Analyze the examples
The author lists 'الإنسان', 'الفرس', 'الشجر' and says they are names of genera, species, and meanings. These words can refer to any member of their type. For example, 'human' applies to Zayd, Amr, etc.
These are the building blocks of definitions and syllogisms.

The Universal Word Implies Generality by Itself
Author refutes the claim that a singular noun with alif lam does not imply istighraq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a universal word (al-lafz al-kulli) implies istighraq without needing an external qarina, and identify the error of those who deny this.

Core Idea
A universal word inherently denotes all individuals of its kind; no external indicator is needed.

A key debate in Islamic logic is whether the definite article on a singular noun automatically implies generality (istighraq). Some argue that without a contextual indicator (qarina), a word like 'al-dinar' refers only to a single unspecified individual. The author of our passage strongly refutes this, grounding his argument in the nature of the universal concept itself. This lesson unpacks that refutation and clarifies why the universal word alone suffices for generality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponents' claim
The opponents think that a singular noun with alif lam (e.g., 'al-dinar') does not imply istighraq by itself. They claim that generality is understood only through an external qarina, such as the context of pricing or the nature of superiority.
Find the exact sentences in the source where the opponents' view is stated.
2
Analyze their examples
The opponents use examples: 'al-dinar afdal min al-dirham' (the dinar is better than the dirham) and 'al-rajul khayr min al-mar'a' (the man is better than the woman). They argue that the generality of 'al-dinar' comes from the qarina of pricing, not from the word itself.
Think about what qarina they assume is at work.
3
Understand the author's refutation
The author asserts that the error arises from ignorance of the nature of the universal word (al-lafz al-kulli). The universal word itself, by virtue of signifying a concept that applies to all individuals of that kind, necessitates istighraq without any additional qarina.
The author refers to earlier discussion on the meaning of 'kulli' – recall that a universal is what can be predicated of many.
4
Recognize the root error
The root error is confusing the individual existence (al-wujud al-'ayni) with the universal concept (al-ma'na al-kulli) in the mind. The opponents think of the word as denoting a specific individual, but in logical analysis the word 'al-dinar' refers to the universal nature of dinar-ness.
This distinction is crucial in Islamic logic for understanding terms like 'kulli' and 'juz'i'.

The Fourfold Classification of Words in Relation to Meanings
Author classifies words into four types: mushtarak, mutawati', mutaradif, mutazayil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given word-meaning relationship into one of the four categories: mushtarak, mutawati', mutaradif, or mutazayil.

Core Idea
Words relate to meanings in four ways: one-to-many, one-to-one via a common meaning, many-to-one, or many-to-many.

In this passage, the author presents a foundational classification in Islamic logic: the relationship between words and their meanings. Understanding these four types is crucial for precise reasoning, especially in defining terms and constructing valid arguments. The passage gives clear examples from Arabic, such as 'ayn for homonymy and 'insan' for univocity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word(s) and meaning(s)
Look at the word or words in question. Determine whether there is one word or multiple words, and whether there is one meaning or multiple meanings.
2
Check for one word with multiple meanings
If a single word applies to multiple distinct meanings that differ in definition and essence, it is mushtarak (common/homonymous). Example: 'ayn for eye, spring, sun-disk.
The meanings must be equally applied; no priority.
3
Check for one word with a single common meaning covering many individuals
If a single word indicates multiple individuals by virtue of a shared meaning (like 'insan' for all humans), it is mutawati' (univocal). The meaning is one, but the individuals are many.
Contrast with mushtarak: here the meaning is one, not multiple.
4
Check for multiple words with one meaning
If different words refer to the same meaning (the same definition), they are mutaradif (synonymous). Example: khamr, rah, 'uqar all mean the same type of wine.
The words are different but the meaning is identical.
5
Check for multiple words with different meanings
If different words indicate different meanings with no relation, they are mutazayil (diverse). Example: faras (horse), dhahab (gold), thiyab (clothes).
This is the default: most words are mutazayil with each other.

The sixth division: On the relation of words to meanings. Know that words with respect to meanings are on four levels: common (mushtarak), univocal (mutawati'), synonymous (mutaradif), and diverse (mutazayil). As for the common: it is a single word that is applied to existents differing in definition and essence equally, like 'ain' is applied to the seeing eye, a water spring, and the disk of the sun, and these differ in definitions and essences. As for the univocal: they are those that indicate multiple individuals by one meaning common among them, like the indication of the name 'human' on Zayd and 'Amr, and the indication of the name 'animal' on human, horse, and bird, because they share in the meaning of animality, and the name is for that common univocal meaning, unlike the seeing eye and the water spring. As for the diverse: they are the distinct names that have none of these relations among them, like horse, gold, and clothes; for they are different words indicating different meanings in definition and essence. The common should be avoided in discourses, let alone in demonstrations; the univocal are used in all, especially demonstrations.

Commentary
This passage classifies the relationship between words and their meanings into four categories. The author begins by stating that there are four levels (manazil). First, mushtarak (common/homonymous): one word applied to multiple meanings with equal priority, like 'ayn meaning eye, spring, or sun-disk—these meanings have different definitions (hudud) and essences (haqa'iq). Second, mutawati' (univocal): one word applied to multiple individuals sharing a single meaning, like 'insan' applied to Zayd and 'Amr, or 'hayawan' applied to human, horse, and bird—they share the meaning of animality, and the name corresponds to that common meaning. Third, mutaradif (synonymous): different words that indicate one meaning, like khamr, rah, and 'uqar all meaning the same kind of wine. Fourth, mutazayil (diverse): different words with different meanings, like faras (horse), dhahab (gold), and thiyab (clothes). The author then gives a practical recommendation: mushtarak should be avoided in speech, especially in demonstrations (barahin), while mutawati' are suitable for all contexts, particularly demonstrations.

Distinguishing Common (Mushtarak) from Univocal (Mutawati')
Author warns against confusing common and univocal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mushtarak and mutawati' words and identify which category a given ambiguous term belongs to.

Core Idea
Mushtarak shares only the name; mutawati' shares the full definition equally.

One of the most common errors in logical definition is confusing a word that has multiple unrelated meanings (mushtarak) with a word that applies to multiple instances equally (mutawati'). This passage clarifies the precise difference and warns against slipping into confusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its meanings
Look at the word being discussed. Does it have one meaning or many? For mushtarak, the same name labels completely different meanings. For mutawati', the same meaning applies to multiple individuals.
2
Check for shared meaning
Mushtarak has no shared meaning at all between the named things. Mutawati' has a single shared meaning that applies equally to all instances.
The passage says mushtarak is 'no agreement or resemblance in meaning at all.'
3
Apply the test of definition (hadd)
If the definition (hadd) applies identically to all instances, the word is mutawati'. If different definitions are needed for different instances, it is mushtarak.
Example: 'insan' has the same definition for Zayd and 'Amr (mutawati').
4
Analyse examples from the passage
Consider ''ayn' (eye vs. spring): it is mushtarak because the definitions differ. 'Insan' for Zayd and 'Amr is mutawati' because they share the same human nature.
Use this to solidify the distinction.

Special Property vs. Common Accidental
Author classifies accidental attributes into specific and common.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any accidental attribute as a special property (khāṣṣah) or a common accidental (‛araḍ ‛āmm) based on its relation to the subject.

Core Idea
An attribute's exclusivity to a subject defines it as a property or a common accident.

In Islamic logic, attributes that are not constitutive of a subject are called accidental (‛araḍī). But not all accidents are the same: some are specific to a single kind, others are shared across multiple kinds. Recognizing this distinction is crucial for constructing accurate definitions and descriptions. In this lesson, we will explore the first division of the accidental into special property (khāṣṣah) and common accidental (‛araḍ ‛āmm), and learn to identify them using clear examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of attribution
When you encounter an attribute, first determine which subject it is being attributed to. The passage uses examples like 'man', 'horse', 'animal', etc. The same attribute may be a property for one subject and common for another.
2
Check exclusivity of the attribute to that subject
Ask: Is this attribute found only for this subject, or does it also exist for other subjects? If it is exclusive, it is a special property (khāṣṣah); if it is shared with other subjects, it is a common accidental (‛araḍ ‛āmm).
The attribute may be necessary or separating; exclusivity is determined by the subject-attribute relationship, not by modality.
3
Apply to examples from the passage
For instance, 'laughter' attributed to 'man' is exclusive (only humans laugh), so it is a khāṣṣah of man. 'Walking' attributed to 'man' is not exclusive (animals also walk), so it is a common accidental when attributed to man. But when attributed to 'animal', walking becomes a khāṣṣah because only animals walk.
4
Avoid a common mistake: confusing accidental (‛araḍī) with the category accident (‛araḍ)
The author warns that the 'accidental' here does not mean the accident opposed to substance. For example, 'white' when attributed to man means a substance (a body) with whiteness, not the accident whiteness itself. This subtlety is essential for correct application in Islamic sciences like kalam and usul.

Commentary
This passage presents the five universals and divides the accidental (‛araḍī) twice. The author first states that the constitutive (muqawwim) is called 'essential' (dhātī) by logicians, while everything else is called 'accidental' (‛araḍī), whether separating (mutafāriq) or necessary (lāzim). This accidental is then divided in relation to the subject it qualifies: if it is specific to that subject (found only for it), it is called 'special property' (khāṣṣah); if it is common (found also for others), it is called 'common accidental' (‛araḍ ‛āmm). Examples: walking/eating are special to animal, but when attributed to man they become common. Neighing is special to horse; laughter is special to man. The author warns not to confuse this 'accidental' with the category of accident (‛araḍ) opposite substance. Learning Objective

Core Idea

After dividing the accidental into special and common, the author introduces another crucial division: some accidents are 'essential' (dhātī) in the sense that they necessarily accompany the subject due to its essence, even though they are not part of its definition. Understanding this second sense of 'essential'—non-constitutive but necessary—prevents confusion in logical analysis and is vital for sciences like kalam and usul al-fiqh, where attributes of God or legal subjects must be carefully distinguished.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two senses of 'essential' (dhātī)
The author explains that 'essential' in the first sense means constitutive (muqawwim), i.e., part of the definition. The second sense applies to attributes that are not constitutive but flow from the essence. For example, 'movement' for body is not part of the definition of body, but a body moves because it is a body.
2
Identify attributes that are essential accidents

3
Apply the test of 'by virtue of the essence'
For each potential essential accident, ask: Does this attribute belong to the subject because of the nature of that subject, or because the subject falls under a broader category? For instance, movement belongs to body because body is a body (not because it exists or is a specific body). Health belongs to animal because animal is animal (not because it is a body or a horse).
4

Species: The Two Senses of 'What Is It?'
Definition of species in two senses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two senses of species (naw') and apply them in definitions.

Core Idea
Species answers 'what is it?' for individuals differing only by number, or it is the subject of higher universals.

Species (naw') has two meanings. The first is the infima species, predicated of individuals differing only in number. The second is any universal that is the subject of a higher universal (like genus) via primary essential predication. Understanding this dual usage is vital for precise logical analysis.

Guided Path

Understand the first meaning of species

This allows intermediate species (e.g., 'bird' as a species of 'animal' and genus of 'sparrow').

Property: The Exclusive Non-Essential Universal
Definition of property as a universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a property and distinguish it from the differentia and common accident.

Core Idea
Property is non-essential but exclusive to one reality.

Property (khassa) is a universal that applies to only one quiddity but does not express its essence. For example, 'laughing' is a property of humans. It is non-essential (ghayr dhati) and exclusive. Knowing property helps differentiate between essential and accidental predicates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of property
The passage defines property as 'a universal predicated of what is under a single reality only, with a non-essential predication'. So it must be exclusive to one species (e.g., only humans laugh) and not part of the essence.
Exclusivity is key: if a predicate applies to multiple realities, it is a common accident.

Distinguish from differentia
Differentia is essential (e.g., 'rational' gives the essence of human). Property is non-essential (e.g., 'laughing' does not define human's essence but follows from it). Both are exclusive, but differentia answers 'which thing is it in essence'.
Property answers 'what is its property?' not 'what is its essence?'.

Universals: Known by Intellect, Not by Sense
Author refutes the objection that universal judgments cannot arise from particular sense perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sensible particulars and intelligible universals, and explain how the intellect forms universal judgments from particular sense perceptions.

Core Idea
Universal judgments are made by the intellect on abstracted essences, not by the senses on particulars.

This passage responds to a powerful objection: if all knowledge begins with sense perception, and sense only gives us particulars, how can we ever make universal judgments? The author clarifies that the intellect has its own power to strip away accidental attributes and grasp the essence of a thing, which allows us to judge universally. This distinction between the sensible and the intelligible is foundational for all Islamic logic and epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection against universal judgments
Find the objection stated at the beginning. It says that since sense only perceives particulars, we should only make judgments about particulars, not universals.
The objector's argument: sense cannot give universal knowledge, so universal judgments are impossible.
2
Distinguish between sensible and intelligible
The author responds by distinguishing two modes of perception: sense perceives the particular with accidental attributes (color, size, position), while the intellect perceives the essence stripped of accidents.
Sensible: 'mahsus' – particular, concrete. Intelligible: 'ma'qul' – universal, abstract.
3
Examine the example of 'man' in Zayd
The author uses the example of 'man'. When we see Zayd, sense perceives a man with specific attributes (e.g., tall, dark). But the intellect abstracts the universal 'man' that is shared by all individuals.
The sensible man is always accompanied by accidental attributes; the intelligible man is just 'man' without those attributes.
4
Understand why universal judgments are intellectual
The conclusion: the intellect makes universal judgments on the universal essence it abstracts, not on the particular sensible. So the judgment 'every whole is greater than its part' is based on the intelligible 'whole', not on any particular sensed whole.
The rational power ('quwwa 'aqila') performs this abstraction.

The Two Senses of the Universal (al-Kulli)
Al-Ghazali distinguishes two meanings of universal: as a nature in external objects and as a concept in the mind.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish al-Ghazali's two senses of the universal and apply this distinction to any essence discussed in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
The universal is both a nature in things and a concept in the mind; these are two distinct homonymous meanings.

Al-Ghazali opens his discussion of universals by warning that the term 'kulli' is a homonym (mushtarak) with two radically different meanings. One refers to the nature as it exists in external individuals—like humanity in Zayd and Amr. The other refers to the mental concept that can be predicated of many. Understanding this distinction is essential before any further logical analysis of predication and definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the homonymy
Al-Ghazali states that 'kulli' is a homonym (ism mushtarak) with two meanings: one existing in external objects (a'yan) and one existing in minds (adhhan). Locate this claim in the Arabic and note the key phrase 'اسم مشترك ينطلق على معنيين'.
The Arabic text: 'اعلم أن الكلّي اسم مشترك ينطلق على معنيين'.
2
Analyze the first sense (in re)
The first sense is the nature taken absolutely, without any additional conditions. For example, 'human' considered just as human, ignoring whether it is one or many. The mind can abstract this essence from its concrete instances.
Al-Ghazali uses the example of 'al-insan' (human) to illustrate absolute humanity.
3
Distinguish essence from concomitants
Attributes like unity, multiplicity, generality, and particularity are not part of the essence. Even if they necessarily accompany the essence in existence, they are additional. The passage shows that knowing 'human' is one thing; knowing that it is one or many is an additional cognition.
Key phrase: 'نعم الكثرة والوحدة تلزم الإنسانية في الوجود لا محالة، وليس كل ما يلزم الشيء فهو له في ذاته'.
4
Contrast with the second sense (in mente)
The second sense is the universal concept in the mind that can be predicated of many. This is the logical universal used in propositions. While the first sense is the essence itself, the second is the mental tool for reasoning about it.
The passage mainly defines the first sense; the second sense is mentioned but not elaborated here.

Commentary
This passage defines the universal (al-kulli) by distinguishing its two meanings. First, a universal can be a nature existing in external objects (a'yan) considered in itself without any further conditions—like the nature of 'humanity' as it is in individuals like Zayd and Amr. Second, it can be a universal concept in the mind (adhhan) that is predicated of many, like the mental image of 'human' that applies to all humans. Al-Ghazali emphasizes that the mind can abstract the essence (e.g., humanity) from its attendant attributes like unity, plurality, generality, or particularity. These attributes are not part of the essence but are additional; even if they are necessarily concomitant in existence, they are not intrinsic. The passage is crucial for understanding the ontology of universals: the essence is one thing, its modes of being (one/many) are another. This distinction underlies the logical theory of predication and definition.

So humanity upon consideration exists actually in individual people, predicated upon each one, not as being one in essence nor as being many, for that is not insofar as it is humanity. And the second meaning of the universal is humanity, for example, with the condition that it is predicable in some manner of predication upon many, and this does not exist in external individuals, for it is impossible for one identical thing to be predicated upon each one of the individuals at one and the same time. That is because the human being who is encompassed by the accidents particularizing the person of Zayd is not encompassed by the accidents of 'Amr, such that that identical humanity exists in 'Amr, being that very same number, and perhaps they are encompassed by contradictory accidents. But this expression exists in minds, meaning that if the person of Zayd comes to sense first, an effect occurs in the soul, which is the imprinting of the form of humanity in it, without his knowing it. And this form taken from humanity abstracted from attention to particularizing accidents, if compared to the humanity of 'Amr, would correspond to it, meaning that if a horse appeared to sense afterwards, another effect would occur in the soul, but if 'Amr appeared, no new effect would occur in the soul; rather all other persons of people are equal in that respect, whether existing persons or those possible to exist, because its relation to all is the same, so it was called universal in this regard, since its relation to each one is one. So this form has a relation to one individual, and it has a relation to the other forms imprinted in the soul. So when its relation to one individual and to others is one, its example corresponding is likewise. For this reason it is said to be universal, and its relation to the soul and to other forms in the soul is a particular relation, for it is one of the individual items of knowledge imprinted in the soul. And this is what puzzled the theologians (mutakallimun) and they expressed it as 'state' (hal) and differed about affirming and denying it. Some said it is neither existent nor non-existent, and some denied it, and they were puzzled by the differentiation and commonality among names, for black and white share in 'color-ness' and differ in something; so how can that wherein is differentiation and that wherein is commonality be one? And the source of that is the misunderstanding of some of them due to believing in something having existence in the soul but no existence from outside, when a universal form is established in the soul, while in existence it is not universal in this regard; rather it is fixed in external individuals in the first regard, and the meaning of its universality is similarity (tamathul), not union (ittihad), in the humanity existing for Zayd and the humanity existing for 'Amr in that they are humanity by number. And as for its example in the rational soul for humanity, it corresponds to it and to the humanity of Zayd and 'Amr with a single correspondence, and the form in itself is one, and despite its unity corresponds to multiplicity, as if it is, in addition to it, also one, meaning that multiplicity. So this is the verification of the meaning of the universal, and it is among the most subtle things to grasp and the most important to seek, for all intelligibles are branches of verifying these meanings, so it is necessary to clarify them.

Commentary
This passage defines the two meanings of the universal (kulli) and clarifies its mode of existence. The author begins by distinguishing two statements: 'Humanity does not exist and it has one of the two states' versus 'One of the two states belongs to it insofar as it is humanity.' He then explains that the contrary of 'humanity insofar as it is humanity is one' is not 'is many' but rather 'is not one insofar as it is humanity.' This allows that humanity can exist as one or many, but not by virtue of being humanity. The universal in the first sense is absolute humanity, free from conditions of unity, multiplicity, or any accidents. This absolute universal exists in external individuals (a'yan) because unity or multiplicity accompany man but not as part of his essence. For every existent, even when accompanied by others, retains its own particular existence. So humanity is actually present in individual people, predicated on each, not as one or many in essence. The second meaning of the universal is humanity conditioned by being predicable of many. This does not exist externally because it is impossible for one thing to be predicated on many individuals at the same time. Each individual has its own particular accidents. However, this second universal exists in the mind: when we perceive Zayd, the form of humanity is imprinted in the soul without attention to particularizing accidents. This form, when compared to another person like 'Amr, matches it exactly, so the soul does not need to imprint a new form. Thus the form's relation to all individuals is the same, making it universal. The author notes that this mental universal puzzled the theologians (mutakallimun), who debated its existence as a 'state' (hal). Some considered it neither existent nor non-existent. The confusion arises from misunderstanding that the universal has existence in the soul but not externally as universal. The underlying unity in external individuals is not literal union but similarity (tamathul). The form in the rational soul corresponds to both Zayd and 'Amr equally, and it is one in itself yet corresponds to multiplicity. This verification of the universal is crucial because all intelligible concepts depend on it.
