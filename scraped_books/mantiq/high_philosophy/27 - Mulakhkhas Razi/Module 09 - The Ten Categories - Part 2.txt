Relations Are Known Only Through Their Subjects
Author argues that the genera and species of relations cannot be indicated without mentioning their subjects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why relations cannot be defined or pointed to without mentioning their subjects, and identify the role of the subject (ma'rud) in specifying a relation.

Core Idea
Relations have no independent names; they are known only through their subjects.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi discusses a fundamental feature of relations (al-idafat): they cannot be directly named or pointed to. Instead, we must mention the entities that participate in the relation—the subjects (ma'rudat). This is not because the subject is the essence of the relation, but because language lacks terms for specific relations. Understanding this helps us avoid mistakenly treating relations as independent entities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Read the first sentence: 'It is not possible to point to the genera of relation and its species except by the genera of their subjects, their species, and their individuals.' This is the thesis of the passage.
2
Understand the reason
The author then explains why: 'because there are no names for them that indicate them.' Relations have no specific linguistic labels; we must use the subject to indicate which relation we mean.
3
Analyze the example
The author gives the example: 'Zayd is in the fire.' The relation of 'in-ness' is predicated, but 'in-ness' alone is a genus. To specify it, we mention 'Zayd' and 'fire'—the subjects.
The phrase 'عد وفاتها' may be corrupt; the meaning is 'its concomitants' or 'its necessary accompaniments.'
4
Apply the principle to another relation
The author adds: 'absolute doubleness is opposite absolute half-ness.' Doubleness cannot be understood without reference to half-ness—their subjects are numbers or magnitudes.
5
Distinguish essence from indication
Note that the author says the subjects are not the essences of the relations. They are merely indicators (a'lam) for specifying the relation.

The Category of Relation and Its Three Divisions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Relation has no independent essence; it is known only through its subject.

The category of relation is unique among the Aristotelian categories because it cannot be directly pointed to. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi explains that to understand a relation, you must first understand its subject. He then classifies relations into three levels: specific, generic, and individual. This lesson will walk you through his analysis and show how knowledge of relation depends on knowledge of its substrate.

The claim is epistemological: relation is known only through its subject.

The author clarifies that it is not because the genera of subjects are the essences of relations. Why does he make this clarification? To prevent the mistaken identification of the subject's genus with the relation's genus.

This shows how al-Razi's framework applies to Islamic categories.

The Principle: Effect Cannot Surpass Its Cause
Al-Razi argues the metaphysical principle that effect cannot exceed cause in strength.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the effect cannot surpass its cause in strength and identify how conditions and obstacles affect this metaphysical principle in Islamic philosophy.

Core Idea
The effect never surpasses its cause; apparent counterexamples are explained by external factors.

This lesson explores a fundamental metaphysical principle in Islamic philosophy: the effect cannot be stronger than its cause. Al-Razi states this principle and then addresses a potential objection from biology—the heat of animals appearing more intense than fire. Understanding this principle is crucial for causal reasoning in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The author asserts that it is impossible for an effect to be stronger than its cause. This is a categorical claim about the nature of causation.
2
Understand the allowance for weakness and equality
The cause may have surplus strength, and the effect can be weaker due to conditions (shuroot) or obstacles (mawani'). Equality occurs when conditions are fully met and obstacles are absent.
Conditions and obstacles are external factors, not part of the essence of cause or effect.
3
Analyze the objection
The author presents a counterexample: the heat of animals (e.g., fever) is more intense than the heat of fire. This seems to violate the principle.
The objection is empirical, testing a metaphysical claim.
4
Examine the author's response
Al-Razi explains that animal heat arises from a mixture of elements (e.g., thick composition, admixture with other substances) whereas fire is simple. Thus, the effect is not purely from fire but from compounded causes, so the principle remains intact.
The response shows that the apparent counterexample involves multiple causes, not a single causal chain.
5
Reflect on the principle's metaphysical foundation
The principle is grounded in the idea that a cause must possess at least as much perfection as its effect, otherwise something would come from nothing. This is a core tenet of Avicennian metaphysics.
Later critiques (e.g., Ibn Taymiyya) challenge the necessity of causal connection, but al-Razi here asserts it as evident.

Essential and Accidental Contrariety in Motion
Author classifies contrariety in motion into essential and accidental.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Motion can be opposed by nature or by accident – location matters.

This lesson refines your understanding of contrariety by showing how motion can be intrinsically or extrinsically opposed. Essential contrariety inheres in the motion itself, while accidental contrariety arises from external relations. We will parse the author's classification and apply it to new examples.

Razi argues that gradual change in quality is apparent, not real.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Apparent gradual change is really a rapid succession of distinct types.

In this passage from al-Mulakhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi tackles the nature of motion in quality, such as the change from black to white or hot to cold. He argues that what our senses perceive as gradual, continuous change is actually a series of distinct types occurring instantaneously one after another. This radical claim has important consequences for how we understand physical and psychological change. Let's dissect his reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial dilemma
Razi begins by presenting a dichotomy: change in quality is either gradual (tadarruj) or instantaneous (daf'a). He argues that gradual is impossible because it leads to an infinite regress of instants or the absurdity of a single quality persisting while changing.
This sets up a conditional: if gradual, then impossible.
2

This is the core of Razi's positive account.
3

Razi illustrates his theory with ten stages between black and white. Each stage is a distinct type (e.g., shade of gray) that exists at an instant. The senses do not perceive the intermediate differences, so the overall change appears gradual from black to white.
The example shows how sensible graduation arises without real continuity.
4
Grasp the conclusion about sensible vs. real graduation
Razi concludes that sensible graduation is an illusion; real graduation would require continuous change, which is impossible. The succession of distinct types produces the appearance of gradualness.
This is the main takeaway: the phenomenon is real, but the process is not.
5
Extend to other categories
Razi applies the same principle to other accidents such as opinions, sensations, health, and illness, and briefly discusses motion in place (al-ayn) and position (al-wadʿ).
This shows the generality of his theory across categories.

Author refutes the claim that hyle can be free from magnitude.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the categorical argument that hyle must possess magnitude, and identify the logical steps in the author's critical refinement.

Core Idea
Hyle cannot be indeterminate; magnitude is necessary for differentiation.

This passage presents a sophisticated argument from the Islamic philosophical tradition. The author is refining the claim that hyle (prime matter) can exist without the form of magnitude. Using a reduction to contradiction, he shows that if hyle were free of magnitude, it would lead to absurdity. This lesson will walk you through the logical structure of this refinement, which is a categorical argument with an embedded contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
The opponent asserts that hyle can be free of magnitude (i.e., devoid of continuous quantity). Locate this claim in the passage: the author states, 'your saying that it cannot be devoid of corporeality'.
2
Extract the key premise about difference
The author argues: if we suppose hyle for a whole body and hyle for a part, they must differ. This difference cannot be in essence or accidental concomitants, so it must be in magnitude. This is the core reductio premise.
Note that the terms 'essence' and 'concomitants' are technical: essence is what makes a thing what it is; concomitants are necessary attributes.
3
Reconstruct the categorical syllogism
Formalize the argument: Major premise: Everything that differs not in essence or concomitants differs in magnitude. Minor premise: The hyle of a body and the hyle of its part differ (and are not different in essence or concomitants). Conclusion: Therefore, they differ in magnitude. Thus, hyle cannot be free of magnitude.
This is a first-figure categorical syllogism (Barbara mood).
4
Identify the contradiction
If the opponent claims hyle is devoid of magnitude, then the same hyle (or two hyles) would have to be described by magnitude (to differ) while lacking it, which is a direct contradiction.
5
Assess the author's critique
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum: assume hyle is free of magnitude, derive a contradiction, therefore the assumption is false. This is a valid logical move within the tradition.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the claim that hyle (materia prima) can exist without the form of magnitude. The author is engaging in a dialectical rebuttal of an opponent's position. The argument proceeds as follows: The opponent asserts that hyle, before receiving the bodily form, was described by a preparatory form, but was still free of the bodily form. The opponent grants that the hyle had a disposition, but the author pushes to show that this disposition itself must involve some magnitude or differentiation. The discussion then focuses on the magnitude: if hyle is the substratum for both a whole body and its parts, and two different hyles (for the body and a part) must differ, that difference cannot be in essence or concomitants, so it must be magnitude. Therefore, hyle cannot be free of magnitude; otherwise it would be described by magnitude while lacking it—a contradiction. The author then anticipates objections: perhaps the difference is not due to magnitude but to a state of the locus. He counters by showing that such a state would either imply magnitude or undermine the original argument. Further, he critiques the claim that a separate (non-material) form is possible, arguing that anything pointed to is material, and if not pointed to, it is not a form known to us. The passage ends with a discussion of corporeality's independence in receiving forms and concludes that corporeality is from matter, thus any corporeal form requires matter, leading to a regress. All examples are rooted in the physics of the Islamic falsafah tradition.

Core Idea
The celestial sphere cannot be elemental because elemental properties would make it uniform, but it is not.

In this passage, the author refutes the claim that the celestial sphere is composed of the four elements (earth, water, air, fire). He uses a series of logical arguments to show that such composition would lead to contradictions with observed celestial phenomena. Identify the target claim

The author argues that if the sphere were composed of fire, contact from all sides would necessitate uniform weakness, but celestial bodies show specific variations, contradicting that property. The author presents multiple disproofs: from elemental composition, from composite nature, and from bodily power (leading to regress). Each is a separate conditional argument building towards the same conclusion.
5
Summarize the refutation's logical force
The author concludes that the sphere is simple and not subject to elemental change, consistent with Peripatetic doctrine. The cumulative effect of the arguments makes the refutation robust.

Proof that the Earth is Spherical
Author refutes flat and concave shapes and proves earth is spherical via astronomical evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound argument used to prove that the earth is spherical, identifying its sub-arguments and addressing a key objection.

Core Idea
The earth's sphericity is proven by eliminating alternatives and confirming convexity via astronomical data.

This passage from Mulakhkhas Razi presents a rigorous proof that the earth is spherical, using a combination of logical elimination and empirical observations. The author examines three possible shapes—flat, concave, and convex—and systematically refutes the first two using evidence from sunrise times and lunar eclipses. He then demonstrates that the earth is convex in both length and width, which necessarily implies a spherical shape, and addresses an objection about the inhabited portion being only a half or quarter sphere. This lesson will guide you through the compound structure of this argument, enabling you to see how multiple lines of evidence converge on a single conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three initial hypotheses
The author lists three possible shapes for the earth: straight (flat), concave, and convex. Recognize these as the alternatives to be evaluated.
2
Analyze the refutation of the flat earth
The author argues that on a flat earth, the sun would rise and set simultaneously for all places on the same straight line. However, observations of lunar eclipses show that the local times differ across longitudes, refuting this possibility.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: if flat, then simultaneous sunrise/sunset; but observation contradicts, so flat is false.
3
Analyze the refutation of the concave earth
The author argues that if the earth were concave, then moving north would cause the north pole and nearby stars to become more hidden. But in reality, as one goes north, the north pole's altitude increases and more northern stars appear, so concave is false.
4
Establish convexity in both length and width
Having eliminated flat and concave, the author concludes the earth must be convex. He then shows that the differences in eclipse times and star altitudes vary according to a circular pattern, indicating convexity in both longitudinal and latitudinal directions.
5
Infer sphericity from bilateral convexity
A shape convex in all directions is a sphere. The author argues that since the earth is convex in length and width, the only shape that unifies these is a sphere. He supports this by noting that the variation of celestial phenomena corresponds to parts of a circle.
The final conclusion: the earth is a sphere.

Finally, he addresses a possible objection: that the inhabited part of the earth might be only a half or quarter sphere. He notes that even if so, the observed phenomena still require the entire earth to be spherical, because a half-sphere would still be part of a sphere. The proof uses empirical data (eclipse timings, star altitudes) and logical elimination to reach certainty about the earth's shape.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Opposing qualities need a third nature to prevent mutual annihilation.

When the four elemental qualities (heat, cold, wetness, dryness) attempt to coexist, they conflict. Al-Razi shows that without a mediating 'breaking nature,' they would cancel each other out, making stable composite bodies impossible. This lesson unpacks his deductive reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem of mixture
Understand that when two opposed qualities (e.g., heat and cold) meet, they tend to corrupt each other. If both were full forms, matter would have two forms – an Aristotelian impossibility.

2
Analyze the dilemma
The author presents two options: either both qualities are forms (impossible) or only one is a form. If only one is a form, the other is an accident, but then what causes the 'breaking' of the opposite quality?

Derive the need for a third nature
Since neither option directly allows stable mixture, a separate nature (the 'breaker') must exist. This nature actively overcomes the conflict, allowing the qualities to coexist in a mixed state.
This 'breaking nature' is not itself a quality but a substrate principle.

Double vision arises when the visual cones from each eye fail to converge.

Have you ever wondered why a person with strabismus sees one object as two? In this lesson, we explore the explanation given by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi in his Mulakhkhas. He attributes double vision to the misalignment of the two optic nerves, which causes the visual cones from each eye to not meet at the same point. This understanding of optics fits within the broader Islamic natural philosophy that links physical causes to sensory perception.

The edges are sensitive, leading to two perceptions.

رؤية الواحد اثنين ما ذكروه من انحراف العصبتين لما تصور أن في حالة واحدة أحد الشيئين واحد أو الآخر أنت فانه يلزم أن يكون تركيب العصبتين باقيا بحاله وناقصا [؟] وهو مخالف للروح التعافي جسم لطيف من المتع بقا وطبقى المصلين بحيث لا يقدم عنه البتة ولا يطاق [؟]. وإذا كان التقدم والنافي جائز العلة [؟] مجند [؟] يلزم وقوع الحول في أكثر الأمر أكثر الناس لأن الروح الناصر إذا تجاوز الملقي لم تحد المتان [؟] وقال الحاجة [؟] التسبب أن النود المتد من كل عين على شكل مربوط مراسم عند المن وقاعدته عند المدى وقوة هذا النور في سهى المخروط وهم يلقيان عند المبصر وتحدان وجمع للبصر هو ابتاع سمى المخروط عليه والأحول فان ستى مخروط عينيه لا يلقيان عاشة واحد بل يرى الانشاء أبدا بطرف المخروط لا بوقوع التعمين عليها والطرفان حبابيان فلاجم ترى الواحد اتنين وإنه الانتين قسط الشافي الحكما اتفقوا عليه بتارعا المتناع الحالية والعالم فيه مات ولانه حمر أن كون ذلك الفاتي الزوي ايمانه اسم من الطرف الثاني في الجهات وفدع باحث وأن الممرات وهى الصور واللون والأطراف والحج والبعد والوضع والشكل والتورق والاتصال والعدد والحركة والسكون والملاسة والخونة والشفيف والكثافة والظل والجن والفح والتشابه والاختلافِ وهذه لمود [؟] أو راجعة إليها كالترتيب الداخل تحت الوضع والكتابة وسائر النقوش الداخليين تحت الترتيب والشكل وكالاستقامة والاعوجاج والتحدب والتقعر التي تنشأ من الشكل وكالكثرة والقلة الداخلتين تحت الشكل والعدد
The seeing of one as two: what they mentioned from the deviation of the two optic nerves, when one imagines that in one state one of the two things is one or the other, then it necessitates that the composition of the two nerves remains in its state and deficient, opposing the healthy spirit, a subtle body from the pleasures of life and the layers of the two prayers such that it never proceeds from it nor is it endured. And if progress and negation are permissible for the cause, it would necessitate the occurrence of strabismus in most matters among most people, because the victorious spirit when it exceeds the limit does not measure the firmness. And need said the cause that the extending nerve from each eye has a conical shape, its base at the brain and its apex at the eye, and the power of this light in the cone, and they both meet at the seen object and are doubled; and the gathering for sight is the following of the cone upon it. And the strabismic, if the cone of his eyes does not meet at one point, but he always sees the thing at the edge of the cone, not by the falling of the two edges, and the two edges are loved, therefore he sees one as two, and the two as one. The wise judges agreed upon that, by preventing the material and the world in it died, and because it is red that that destroyer of the spoiled one is named from the second side in directions, and he left the researcher, and that the visible things are: shapes, color, edges, stones, distance, position, shape, moisture, connection, number, motion, rest, smoothness, roughness, transparency, density, shadow, side, opening, similarity and difference. And these are enumerated or refer to them, like the arrangement falling under position, and writing and the rest of the inscriptions falling under arrangement and shape, and like straightness, crookedness, convexity and concavity which arise from shape, and like abundance and scarcity falling under shape and number.

Commentary
This passage begins by addressing the phenomenon of double vision (رؤية الواحد اثنين). The author explains that it arises from the deviation of the two optic nerves (انحراف العصبتين). When the alignment is off, the two cones (المخروطات) from each eye do not converge on a single point, leading to the perception of one object as two. The text then enumerates the objects of sight (المبصرات) which include shapes, colors, edges, distance, position, shape, moisture, connection, number, motion, rest, smoothness, roughness, transparency, density, shadow, side, opening, similarity, and difference. These are considered primary visible qualities. Then the passage notes that other qualities are derived from these, such as arrangement from position, and writing from arrangement and shape, and straightness or crookedness from shape. This classification is foundational in Islamic theories of perception, linking physical optics to the categorization of sensory data.
