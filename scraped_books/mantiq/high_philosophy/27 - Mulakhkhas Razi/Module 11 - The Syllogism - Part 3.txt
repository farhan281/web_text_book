Distinguishing Natural, Forced, and Voluntary Motion
Eliminative argument for voluntary motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish natural, forced, and voluntary motion and apply the principle that what is neither natural nor forced is voluntary.

Core Idea
If motion is not natural and not forced, it must be voluntary.

Having established that circular motion is not natural, the author eliminates forced motion to conclude it is voluntary. This lesson walks through the eliminative disjunctive syllogism, a key tool in kalam for classifying phenomena.

Guided Path
1
Identify the tripartite division
The author implicitly divides motion into natural, forced, and voluntary. Find the statement 'فالحركة المستديرة غير طبيعية ولا قسرية... فهي إذن إرادية' (so circular motion is not natural and not forced... so it is voluntary).
This division is assumed as exhaustive and mutually exclusive.
2
Extract the elimination of natural motion
Natural motion was already eliminated in the previous syllogism. The passage now builds on that result.
The author refers back to the proof that circular motion is not natural.
3
Analyze the elimination of forced motion
Forced motion is defined as contrary to nature. The author argues that circular motion does not have a natural inclination being opposed, so it cannot be forced.
The phrase 'لأن القسر خلاف الطبيعة' (because forced is contrary to nature) is key.
4
Conclude voluntary motion
With both natural and forced eliminated, the only remaining category is voluntary. Therefore, circular motion is voluntary.
This is a valid disjunctive syllogism (modus tollendo ponens).

Commentary
This passage presents a syllogistic argument to prove that circular motion is voluntary, not natural or forced. The author begins with a practical example: a person throwing a stone upward does not intend rest or possible power but only upward movement. Then he discusses the concept of inclination (mayl) as the efficient cause of motion. The key argument: natural motion is rectilinear because nature seeks the shortest path and aims at a state of rest. In circular motion, the body never reaches a state of rest; at each point it moves away from that point, so it does not seek a suitable state. Therefore, circular motion is not natural. It is also not forced (qasr) because forced motion is against nature, and if there is no natural inclination, then what exists is voluntary. The author then addresses an objection about voluntary motion being for one thing only, and refutes it by noting that a voluntary agent can choose among options. He concludes with a reference to Ptolemy that circular motion is the primary cause of generation and corruption. Throughout, the argument uses categorical syllogisms with premises about the nature of motion and inclination.

Defining natural motion

Natural motion seeks rest and takes the shortest straight path.

He then lays out the criteria for what constitutes natural motion: it is a movement that seeks a suitable state and rests upon reaching it, and it must follow the shortest path, which is straight. The Categorical Syllogism Against Circular Motion
Syllogism proving circular motion is not natural

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the categorical syllogism that proves circular motion is not natural, and identify its figure and mood.

Core Idea
All natural motion is straight; circular motion is not straight; therefore it is not natural.

The author now deploys a syllogism to formally exclude circular motion from the category of natural motions. He first states a universal affirmative: every natural motion is straight. Then he notes that circular motion is not straight. The conclusion follows by contrapositive or directly by a first-figure syllogism. This logical move is a demonstration of the power of categorical reasoning in physical science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major premise
Find the universal claim about natural motion: every natural motion is straight.
2
Identify the minor premise
Find the claim about circular motion: circular motion is not straight.
3
Identify the conclusion
What follows from these premises? Circular motion is not natural.
4
Determine figure and mood
Map the terms: major term is 'natural', minor term is 'circular', middle term is 'straight'. The premises are 'All N is S' and 'No C is S', conclusion 'No C is N'. This is Celarent (first figure).
5
Verify validity
Check the conditions for first figure: major premise must be universal, minor affirmative? Actually in Celarent, major is universal affirmative, minor universal negative. Valid.

Disjunctive Elimination: Why Circular Motion is Voluntary
Disjunctive argument for voluntary motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to argue that circular motion is voluntary by applying a disjunctive syllogism that eliminates natural and forced motion.

Core Idea
Since circular motion is not natural and not forced, it must be voluntary.

Having established that circular motion is not natural, the author now considers the remaining possibilities. Motion can be natural, forced, or voluntary. Forced motion requires an external agent opposing nature, but no such agent is present. Therefore, the only remaining option is voluntary motion. This is a classic disjunctive syllogism: a logical tool for narrowing down possibilities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
What are the possible types of motion? The author considers natural, forced (qasriyya), and voluntary.
2
Eliminate natural motion
From the previous syllogism, circular motion is not natural.
3
Eliminate forced motion
Forced motion requires an external force opposing nature. The author states there is no nature that requires it or opposes it, implying it is not forced.
4
Conclude voluntary motion
The only remaining option is voluntary motion (إرادية).
5
Check validity
If the disjunction is exhaustive and the eliminations are sound, the conclusion follows necessarily.

In this passage from his Mulakhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi employs a conditional syllogism (qiyas istithna'i) to demonstrate a key physical truth about celestial spheres. By analyzing the logical structure—antecedent, consequent, and the denial of the consequent—you will see how rigorous reasoning from premises leads to a necessary conclusion. Proving the Celestial Sphere is Neither Heavy nor Light
Author proves the celestial sphere has no rectilinear motion, so it is neither heavy nor light

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound syllogism used by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi to prove that the celestial sphere is neither heavy nor light and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
The sphere's natural circular motion excludes rectilinear motion, so it is neither heavy nor light.

In Aristotelian physics, heavy and light bodies are defined by their natural rectilinear motion toward or away from the center of the world. The celestial sphere, however, moves in a circle. How do we know it cannot move in a straight line? In this passage from al-Mulakhkhas, al-Razi presents a logical proof that the sphere's natural place prevents any rectilinear displacement. Let's walk through his argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main conclusion
The final claim is: 'The celestial sphere is neither heavy nor light' (الفلك لا ثقيل ولا خفيف). This is the conclusion of the whole argument.
2
Find the major premise linking motion and heaviness
The argument relies on the definition: heavy bodies move toward the center, light bodies away from the center. Therefore, if a body cannot move toward or away from the center, it is neither heavy nor light. This is a categorical general premise.
This premise is not explicitly stated in the passage but is implied by the definitions of heavy and light.
3
Extract the sub-argument that the sphere cannot move rectilinearly
The author argues that the sphere has a natural place (hīn tabī‘ī). He then considers three possibilities: its natural place is itself, or something inside it, or something outside it. The first means it already in its place; the second leads to absurd interpenetration of bodies; the third leads to void outside the world, which is absurd. Since the only plausible option is the first, the sphere cannot leave its place by nature, so no rectilinear motion.
This is a disjunctive syllogism: either A, B, or C; not B and not C; therefore A.
4
Combine the sub-argument with the major premise
From the sub-argument we have: 'The celestial sphere cannot move toward or away from the center.' Applying the major premise, we deduce: 'Therefore it is neither heavy nor light.' This completes the categorical syllogism.
The overall argument is a compound syllogism: a disjunctive sub-argument feeding a categorical conclusion.
5
Evaluate the validity
Check that each step respects logical form. The disjunctive elimination is valid if the disjunction is exhaustive and the eliminated options are truly impossible. The categorical step is a valid modus tollens or simply a universal instantiation. Assuming the premises are accepted, the argument is logically sound.

This lesson presents a classical argument from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's Mulakhkhas against the idea that celestial spheres possess senses like animals. The argument uses a conditional syllogism (modus tollens) to show that since the purpose of senses is to obtain benefit and avoid harm, and the celestial sphere cannot experience benefit or harm, it cannot have senses. Understanding this logical form is essential for analyzing kalam arguments about the nature of celestial beings.

Guided Path

Identify the conditional premise

The author argues that the celestial sphere cannot experience benefit or harm. This denies the consequent: 'It is not the case that the celestial sphere has benefit/harm'.

The Conditional Syllogism in the Proof of Celestial Sphericity
Mathematicians' conditional reductio for sphericity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and validate a conditional syllogism (modus tollens) used in a cosmological proof.

Core Idea
The impossibility of a vacuum proves the celestial sphere must be spherical.

The mathematicians in this passage use a powerful logical tool: a conditional syllogism (qiyās istithnā'ī muttaṣil) to prove the celestial sphere is spherical. By showing that the opposite assumption leads to an impossible consequence (a vacuum), they establish their conclusion. Let's break down the structure of this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the antecedent (al-muqaddam) in the conditional premise.
The mathematicians start with: 'If the celestial sphere were polygonal, oval, or lenticular...' This is the antecedent (al-muqaddam) – the assumption they intend to disprove.
These shapes are all non-spherical; the antecedent essentially is 'the sky is not perfectly spherical'.
2
Identify the consequent (al-tālī) in the conditional premise.
'... then a vacuum would occur upon its motion.' The occurrence of a vacuum is the consequent (al-tālī) – what would follow if the antecedent were true.
In Aristotelian physics, a vacuum (inkhilā') is impossible; this is key.
3
Locate the exception (al-istithnā') – the part that is actually affirmed or denied.
Although not explicitly stated in the passage, the exception is the denial of the consequent: 'But a vacuum does not occur (is impossible).' This is the second premise in a modus tollens argument.
The passage says 'the corruption of the consequent indicates the corruption of the antecedent' – meaning the false consequnt disproves the antecedent.
4
Identify the conclusion and verify the form.
The conclusion that follows is: 'Therefore, the celestial sphere is not polygonal/oval/lenticular' – i.e., it is spherical. This matches the modus tollens form: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
Validity: modus tollens is always valid. The soundness depends on the truth of the premises (the impossibility of vacuum).

Dialectical Structure of a Reductio Argument: Objection and Response
Dialectical exchange in the reductio proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange in a reductio argument and assess the soundness of the response.

Core Idea
A good reductio is only as strong as its background premises.

This passage does not just present a proof; it contains a mini-disputation. After the mathematicians' reductio, an objector raises a specific challenge: the vacuum only occurs on certain axes. The mathematicians respond, and the author adds a methodological point. Let's map the debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant's (muddaʿī) position.
The mathematicians claim that the celestial sphere must be spherical because any non-spherical shape leads to a vacuum, which is impossible.
This is the initial position (daʿwā).
2
Find the objector's (muʿtariḍ) challenge.
The objector says: 'That vacuum only follows from the motion of the oval on the shorter diameter and the lenticular on the longer diameter; but if they rotate on the opposite axes, no vacuum occurs.'
The objection attacks the causal link between the antecedent and the consequent.
3
Analyze the respondent's (al-mujīb) reply.
The mathematicians respond that even if the motion were on the problematic axes, the vacuum is 'weak' (i.e., unlikely). Additionally, the author notes that the reductio's antecedent falsity only shows if the other premises are true, and those premises actually indicate that the sky moves in longitude and latitude (which implies rotation on multiple axes).
The reply essentially says the objection does not completely negate the impossibility of vacuum.
4
Evaluate the outcome by the rules of dialectic (jadal).
The objector's challenge does not disprove the reductio because it only shows that under one specific axis no vacuum occurs, but the reductio only requires that under relevant (actual) motion a vacuum would occur. The mathematicians strengthen their case by noting the actual motion is both longitudinal and latitudinal, so the axes that avoid vacuum are not the actual ones.
Thus the mathematicians' position holds.

Refuting Earth's Motion Using Conditional Syllogism
Conditional syllogism refuting Earth's motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the antecedent, consequent, and exception in a conditional syllogism from a physical argument and verify its validity using modus tollens.

Core Idea
Conditional syllogism deduces falsehood of an antecedent by denying its consequent.

Al-Razi employs a conditional syllogism to disprove a claim about Earth's motion. He starts with a general principle: rapid motion drags adjacent fine objects. If Earth moved rapidly eastward, it would drag the air, and we would not see westward winds. Since we do observe westward winds, the antecedent (Earth moving) is false. This is a classic example of modus tollens in Islamic logic. Let's break down its structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the 'if-then' statement in the Arabic. Here: 'فلو كانت الأرض متحركة... لأدارت الهواء مع نفسها' (If the Earth were moving... it would rotate the air). The antecedent is 'Earth is moving eastward rapidly', the consequent is 'air rotates with Earth, so we do not see westward winds'.
The conditional is implicit in the passage structure; look for 'فلو' (if) indicating the hypothesis.
2
Identify the exception (istithna')
Determine which part of the conditional is negated. The author states 'فوجب أن لا نرى البنات ولا الرياح متحركة نحو المغرب' – but we do see them, so the consequent is false. The exception is 'not Q' (we do see westward motions).
The negation is not explicit but implied by empirical observation. The arguer assumes we do see westward winds, so the consequent is denied.
3
Determine the inference type
Since the conditional is 'If P then Q', and we have 'not Q', the valid inference is 'not P'. This is modus tollens. Check if the passage concludes accordingly: 'so the Earth is not moving'.
The conclusion is not explicitly stated but is the intended refutation: Earth does not move eastward rapidly.
4
Verify logical validity
Modus tollens is always valid: if premises are true, conclusion must be true. Here, the logical form is valid. However, check if the major premise (wind drags fine objects) is accepted as true in the context.
Validity concerns form, not truth. The argument's soundness depends on the truth of the premise about wind.
5
Apply to Islamic science
Consider a similar argument in kalam: 'If the world had two gods, there would be chaos; there is no chaos; therefore there are not two gods.' Identify the conditional and exception.
This shows how conditional syllogism is used in theological proofs.

The Equator's Heat: A Syllogistic Proof
Syllogistic proof of equatorial heat

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Logic proves the equator is hottest, not temperate.

In this passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas, he tackles a disputed geographical question: is the equator temperate or extremely hot? Using a categorical syllogism, he demonstrates that the equator never experiences prolonged cold because the intense heating from long days always overwhelms the weak cooling from short nights. By breaking down his argument, you will see how syllogistic reasoning can settle empirical debates in Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
Find the main claim the author is trying to prove. In this passage, it is that the equator is constantly experiencing intense heat, or 'the heat there is very great.' The conclusion often appears at the end of the argument.
The author repeats the conclusion in various forms: 'the heat there is very great' and earlier that the equator never lacks strong heating.
2
Extract the major and minor premises
Locate the general principle (major premise) and the specific fact about the equator (minor premise). The minor premise is that the equator has such conditions: long days with intense sun and short, mild nights.
The author compares heating from the sun at extreme declinations and argues that the net effect is always intense.
3
Check the syllogistic form
Determine if the argument fits a valid categorical syllogism. Here, the middle term is 'prolonged intense heating with weak cooling.' The major term is 'very hot,' and the minor term is 'the equator.' This gives a first-figure AAA (Barbara) syllogism, which is valid.
The author also uses a secondary argument by ratio: comparing the equator's winter to others' summer, which reinforces the conclusion.
4
Evaluate empirical grounding
Note that while the syllogism is logically valid, its soundness depends on the factual truth of the premises. Al-Razi assumes that long days at the equator produce enough heat to overcome any cooling. This is based on the scientific understanding of his time.
The argument shows how logic and empirical observation work together in Islamic science.

The author uses both empirical observations and a logical chain to establish that spontaneous generation is possible. The Circularity in the Proof for the Common Sense
Author presents a proof for the common sense and then refutes it, arguing against the necessity of a separate faculty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular reasoning (dawr) in a proof for an internal faculty and explain why it invalidates the argument.

Core Idea
The proof of the common sense falls into circular reasoning, undermining its own foundation.

We often rely on internal faculties to explain perception, but not all arguments for them are valid. In this passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas, a proof for the common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak) is presented and then critiqued using the fallacy of circular reasoning (dawr). By studying this exchange, you will learn to spot when an argument assumes what it tries to prove—a critical skill in Islamic dialectics and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being proved
The author states that the common sense is supported by three things. Find the claim: there must be a power that perceives tangible and colored objects together because we compare them.
The common sense is the supposed faculty that combines different sensory inputs.
2
Extract the premises of the proof
The proof runs: we compare two percepts (e.g., 'this is that'); such judgment requires both objects to be present to a single faculty; external senses cannot combine different types; the intellect is excluded (by a separate argument); therefore, a common sense must exist.
This is an enthymematic syllogism with an implicit major premise about the need for a unifier.
3
Locate the critique
The author points out that the judgment used to prove the common sense (the comparative act) itself relies on the common sense to be explained. Thus, the proof commits circular reasoning (dawr): the common sense is used to prove the judgment, and the judgment proves the common sense.
The passage states: 'فلو أثبت به لزم الدور' (if proven by it, circularity would follow).
4
Evaluate the force of the critique
If the critique holds, the proof is invalid. Consider whether the judgment could be explained without a separate faculty (e.g., by the soul directly). If so, the proof collapses, and the common sense is not necessary.
The author does not offer an alternative here, but the logical flaw is sufficient to reject the proof as presented.

Refuting the One-from-One Principle in Faculties
Author refutes the claim that a single faculty cannot produce multiple diverse acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute the principle 'from one only one proceeds' when applied to faculties, using a reductio ad absurdum with the example of the common sense.

Core Idea
A single faculty can produce multiple diverse acts, undermining the principle 'from one only one proceeds'.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi critiques a common philosophical principle that from a simple cause only one effect can proceed. He uses the example of the common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak) to show that a single faculty can perceive multiple different objects, which would be impossible if the principle held strictly. This lesson will walk you through his logical refutation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the opponent's claim
The opponent argues that a single faculty (quwwa) can only produce one act (fi'l). This is based on the principle 'from one only one proceeds' (al-wahid la yadurru 'anhu illa al-wahid).
This principle is often used in Neoplatonic emanationist cosmology.
2
Identify the author's concession
Al-Razi concedes that the principle applies to the truly simple (al-wahid al-haqq), i.e., God, but not to composite entities like faculties.
3
Locate the counterexample
The common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak) is a single faculty that perceives diverse objects: blackness, whiteness, shapes, etc. These objects differ in genus, yet the faculty is one.
The common sense is an internal faculty that synthesizes perceptions from the five external senses.
4
Apply the reductio
If the opponent insists that different perceptions require different faculties, then common sense would have to be multiple faculties, which is absurd. Therefore, the principle fails for faculties.
This is a logical reduction: assuming the opponent's principle leads to an impossible consequence.

The impossibility of actual infinite regress is a standard kalām position.

Since infinite regress is ruled out, the chain must end. It ends at an existence that is necessary in itself (wajib al-wujud li-dhatihi). This is the desired conclusion (al-matlab): a Necessary Being exists.
Note that the argument does not depend on temporal sequence but on ontological hierarchy.

Cast the reasoning into a categorical syllogism: Major: Every contingent being depends on a cause. Minor: The chain of contingent causes cannot be infinite (unstated but implied). Conclusion: Therefore, the chain must terminate in a non-contingent (necessary) being.

The Kalam Syllogism for Temporal Origination of Bodies
The famous kalam argument for temporal origination of bodies presented as a syllogism with a causal expansion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract and validate the categorical syllogism used in the classical kalam argument for the temporal origination of bodies.

Core Idea
The temporal origination of bodies is proven by a simple categorical syllogism grounded in the inseparability of originated accidents.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas presents the celebrated kalam proof that all bodies are temporally originated (hadith). The argument is structured as a categorical syllogism, but its force lies in the detailed defense of the minor premise—that bodies are never free from originated accidents like combination and separation. In this lesson, we will unpack the logical structure step by step, so you can see exactly how the syllogism works and why it was so influential in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Extract the categorical syllogism
Identify the two premises and conclusion. The author states the major premise: 'Every body is never free from originated incidents. Everything never free from the originated is itself originated.' The conclusion: 'Every body is originated.'
This is a classic first-figure syllogism (Barbara).
2
Analyze the proof of the minor premise
The minor premise 'Bodies are never free from originated incidents' is defended by pointing to two inseparable accidents: combination (ijtima') and separation (iftiraq). These accidents are always present in bodies, and they are temporally originated because they are subject to change and judgment by knowledge.
This relies on the observation that any two bodies are either in contact or not, showing that these accidents are unavoidable.
3
Examine the reasoning for the major premise
The argument for 'whatever is never free from the originated is itself originated' involves a deeper causal analysis. The author argues that if something were eternal, it could not be subject to non-existence. But anything that never ceases to have originated accidents must itself be originated, because its perpetuity would require an eternal substratum—which leads to absurdities.
The author divides possible causes into necessitating (mujib) and free (mukhtar).
4
Verify the validity and figure
Identify the middle term: 'never free from originated incidents'. The major term is 'originated'. The minor term is 'body'. The syllogism is in the first figure (middle term subject of major premise, predicate of minor premise) and mood Barbara (A A A), which is universally valid.
5
Reflect on the significance
This argument is foundational in kalam for proving the world's temporal beginning and thus the existence of a Creator. The syllogistic form gives it logical rigor, while the defense of premises uses empirical and metaphysical reasoning.
Understanding this syllogism equips you to evaluate similar arguments in Islamic theology.

Good Predominates: A Dialectical Defense
Author rebuts objection on separating good and evil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument for the predominance of good and refute the objection about separating good and evil using the principle of impossibility.

Core Idea
Separating good from evil is impossible, not a failure of power.

The author argues that good is predominant in all levels of existence, from separate substances to elements. When an objection is raised about why good is not completely separated from evil, he responds by showing the impossibility of such separation, using the example of fire. Understanding this dialectical move is crucial for appreciating how reason defends divine wisdom without compromising omnipotence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Find the author's central assertion. It is that good is predominant in all existents other than God. This is the mudda'i (claimant's position).

2
Survey the inductive evidence
The author provides an inductive survey of three levels of creation: separate substances (intellects/souls), celestial bodies (spheres), and the elements. For each, he gives a reason why good is predominant.
For elements, health > illness, pleasure > pain.
3
Locate the objection
The mu'tarid (objector) asks: why not create good without evil? This is a common theological objection against divine wisdom.
The objection is introduced by 'فإن قيل: فهلا خلى الخير عن الشر؟'
4
Analyze the response
The author's jawab (response) argues that such separation is impossible in itself (muhal fi nafsih). He uses an analogical example: creating fire with its benefits necessarily entails burning, which is evil. Since the separation is impossible, the inability to do it is not a defect.
The impossibility is not due to lack of power but due to the nature of the created thing.
5
Evaluate the analogical structure
The argument is analogical: just as fire cannot be divested of its potential to burn, so creation cannot be divested of all evil. The principle is that certain goods entail necessary attendant evils.
This is a standard dialectical move in kalam.
