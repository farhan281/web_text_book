The Subject of Logic: Secondary Intelligibles
Author defines logic's subject as secondary intelligibles and its purpose as laws of inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject matter and ultimate purpose of logic as defined by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi.

Core Idea
Logic studies not things themselves but the mental tools that guide correct inference.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defines the scope of logic. It is not about the external world directly; rather, it examines the 'secondary intelligibles' that attach to concepts in the mind, and its goal is to discover the laws of correct reasoning. Understanding this foundation is crucial before diving into any logical analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of investigation
The passage says logic investigates things not in their absolute essence but from a specific perspective. Find the term that describes what is studied: 'secondary intelligibles' (al-ma'qulat al-thaniya).
Secondary intelligibles are attributes like universality and particularity that only exist when we reflect mentally on primary concepts.
2
Understand the aspect under which it is studied
Logic studies these secondary intelligibles 'insofar as they are a means by which one can transition from known to unknown correctly'. This is a key qualification: logic is the tool for correct inference.
The aspect (اعتبار) is what makes logic distinct from other sciences that also deal with intelligibles.
3
Recognize the ultimate purpose
Al-Razi states that the utmost purpose (al-maqsad al-aqsa) is knowledge of the laws (qawanin) that enable capturing the unknown from the known. This includes both tasawwurat (concepts) and tasdiqat (assents).
This purpose gives logic its instrumental character.
4
Connect to secondary intelligibles
The subject (mawdu') of logic is the secondary intelligibles under the aspect of being tools for inference. This means logic does not study primary intelligibles (like 'existence' or 'thing') but rather the mental relations that make reasoning possible.
This distinction avoids confusing logic with metaphysics.

Logic is a necessary aid, not a crutch for the weak mind.

His response is nuanced: the mind may suffice in some cases but not all, therefore we need logic as a tool to ensure correct reasoning when the mind alone is insufficient. The Three Types of Signification
Author defines three types of signification and explains their conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutabaqah, tadammun, and iltizam and explain why mutabaqah is the primary signification.

Core Idea
The word's meaning is three: its whole, its part, and its necessary concomitant.

In Islamic logic, every word has a meaning it is conventionally placed for (mutabaqah). But beyond that, the word can also indicate a part of that meaning (tadammun) or something necessarily associated with it (iltizam). Understanding these three types is essential for precise interpretation of sacred texts and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conventional meaning (mutabaqah)
Take a word like 'bayt' (house). Its conventional meaning is the entire building. This is the primary signification (mutabaqah).
Mutabaqah is the foundation for the other types.
2
Check for containment of a part (tadammun)
Does 'bayt' also signify a part like the roof? Yes, if the meaning is composite. Tadammun is the signification of a part, but it depends on mutabaqah.
For tadammun to hold, the meaning must have parts.
3
Check for necessary concomitants (iltizam)
Does 'bayt' imply something like 'shelter'? Yes, because a house necessarily provides shelter. Iltizam is the signification of a necessary concomitant, and it also depends on mutabaqah.
The concomitant must be truly necessary, not accidental.
4
Prioritize mutabaqah as primary
The author emphasizes that the secondary significations (tadammun and iltizam) cannot exist without the primary mutabaqah, just as a subordinate depends on its principal.
This principle is crucial for avoiding misinterpretation.

The Four Relations Between Word and Meaning
Author classifies the four possible relations between a single word and its meaning(s).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Arabic word according to its semantic relation: univocal (mutawāṭi'), proper noun ('alam), homonym (mushtarak), or synonym (mutarādif).

Core Idea
A single word can relate to meanings in four distinct ways—mastering these prevents definitional confusion in Islamic sciences.

Words are the building blocks of Islamic discourse, but the same word can function differently depending on its relation to meaning. The great logician and usuli Fakhr al-Din al-Razi here lays out a four-fold classification that every student of the Islamic sciences must internalize. This classification will sharpen your ability to analyze definitions and arguments in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and beyond.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the four-fold schema
The author divides word-meaning relations into four aspects: word and meaning both single, word multiple and meaning single, word single and meaning multiple, and both multiple. Understand that this covers all possibilities.
The fourth relation is debated; some scholars classify it as 'manqūl' (metaphorical transfer) or simply 'different words for different meanings'.
2
Distinguish mutawāṭi' from 'alam
Both fall under one word for one meaning. If the concept itself applies equally to many individuals (e.g., 'human' applies to Zayd, 'Amr, etc.), it is mutawāṭi'. If it is a proper noun applied to a specific individual (e.g., 'Zayd'), it is 'alam.
Mutawāṭi' is also called 'univocal' and 'alam is also called 'proper name'.
3
Identify mushtarak (homonyms)
When a single word is assigned to multiple distinct meanings, it is mushtarak. For example, 'ayn can mean eye, spring, or spy. The context determines which meaning is intended.
Mushtarak words are common in the Qur'an and require careful tafsir.
4
Recognize mutarādif (synonyms)
When multiple words share the same meaning, they are mutarādif. For example, 'asd' and 'layth' both mean lion. Synonyms can come from different languages (e.g., Arabic and Persian).
Some scholars deny the existence of perfect synonyms in Arabic.

Predicates attach to subjects either by essence or through an intermediary cause.

3
Identify accidental predicates

By studying this argument, you will learn how logical reasoning is applied to foundational concepts in Islamic physics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two competing definitions of place
Find the two definitions: place as dimension (bu'd) and place as surface (sath). Notice that the author rejects the first and affirms the second.
These correspond to different physical theories: the dimension view treats place as the three-dimensional space occupied by a body; the surface view treats it as the boundary of the containing body.
2
Extract the premises of the argument against dimension
The author states: (1) Dimension exists only as an accident inhering in a body. (2) If place were a dimension, it would inhere in a volume. (3) If another body enters that place, two bodies would occupy the same dimension, which is impossible (interpenetration). Put these into a syllogistic form.
The implicit middle term is 'that which would allow interpenetration'.
3
Formalize the categorical syllogism
Construct a syllogism: Major premise: Any dimension that can be shared by two bodies leads to interpenetration (which is impossible). Minor premise: Place, if it were a dimension, would be a dimension that can be shared by two bodies. Conclusion: Therefore, place cannot be a dimension.
This is a first-figure syllogism with a universal negative conclusion (Celarent-like).
4
Understand why interpenetration is impossible
Interpenetration (tadakhul al-jismayn) would mean two bodies occupy exactly the same location, which in Aristotelian physics is impossible because bodies are impenetrable. This is a basic axiom.
In kalam, some theologians allowed tadakhul for non-physical entities, but here it is rejected for physical bodies.
5
Analyze the objection and response
The passage considers a potential objection: if place is a surface, then motion would be the striking of surfaces, implying a stationary object in moving air should not be moving. The author rejects this as contrary to intuition.
This shows how definitions must also account for motion phenomena.

The Reality of Qualities: Razi's Critique of Occasionalism
Critique of occasionalist denial of inherent qualities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Razi's critique of the mutakallimun's denial of inherent qualities and evaluate its grounds.

Core Idea
Qualities are real and causally active, not mere divine habits.

In this passage from the Mulakhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi takes on the later mutakallimun (theologians) who argued that qualities like heat and color are not real properties but only habits of God creating sensations. Razi rejects this occasionalist view and defends the Peripatetic position that qualities are inherent and have causal effects. Understanding this critique is crucial for grasping the metaphysical foundations of Islamic natural philosophy and its differences from theological occasionalism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Find the exact claim of the later mutakallimun: they denied that color, heat, tastes, etc. are inherent qualities. Instead, they said God's habit is to create sensation upon contact, using fire as an example.
The key phrase: 'منعوا ان اللون هوارة' (they denied that color is something inherent).
2
Extract Razi's refutation
Notice that Razi does not provide a direct argument here but frames his critique by contrasting the Peripatetic view. He asserts that qualities are real, citing the ancients and the Peripatetics, and lists them as heat, cold, etc. His refutation is implicit: if qualities were mere habits, the regular effects of fire would not be necessary.
The text says 'واما المتاخرون من المتكلمين فمنعوا...' then presents the Peripatetic view as the alternative.
3
Analyze the grounds of the critique
Razi's grounds are empirical and philosophical: qualities like heat are clearly perceptible ('أجل العلوم بالحس') and their effects are regular. He dismisses the occasionalist alternative as insufficient to explain the consistent behavior of fire.
Look at the phrase 'ان الاستدلال على الضرورية عد' (it is impossible to argue against necessary knowledge) – Razi considers sensory evidence as foundational.
4
Assess internal validity of the critique
Evaluate whether Razi's critique is logically sound. He assumes that regularity of effect implies causal necessity, but the occasionalist could reply that God's habit is constant. However, Razi's point is that qualities themselves are the cause, not just a divine custom.
This is a philosophical debate; Razi's critique is valid within a realist ontology.

Heat's Role in Composite Bodies: Separating Dissimilar and Gathering Similar
Explanation of heat's causal role in composites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why heat causes separation of dissimilar components and gathering of similar ones in composite bodies, using the concepts of subtlety and density.

Core Idea
Heat separates by density and gathers by homogeneity.

In this portion of the Mulakhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi explains the causal effect of heat on composite bodies. He builds on the previous affirmation of qualities as real, focusing specifically on heat. Heat, he argues, imparts upward motion to lighter (more subtle) components, separating them from heavier ones. This natural process leads to the principle that heat separates dissimilar things and gathers similar ones. Understanding this principle is essential for grasping how natural philosophers explain phenomena like evaporation, distillation, and even the formation of minerals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property of heat
Find where Razi states the specific effect of heat: 'the property of heat is to provide upward motion by means of facilitating the light, while preventing that which is heavy.' This is the key causal claim.
Look at the phrase: 'من شأن الحرارة إفادة الميل المصعد بواسطة التقيل' (the property of heat is to provide upward motion by means of the heavy).
2
Understand the mechanism: subtlety and density
Razi explains that in composite bodies, components have different degrees of subtlety (لطافة) and density (كثافة). The more subtle a component, the quicker it responds to heat and rises. For example, air is more subtle than water, and water more subtle than earth.
He lists: 'الهواء سريع القبول للحرارة ثم الماء ثم الأرض' (air accepts heat quickly, then water, then earth).
3
Trace the process: heat acting on the composite
When heat acts on a composite, the lighter (more subtle) parts rise before the heavier parts. The heavier parts remain. This results in the separation of components by density, and the gathering together of like components (since each component tends to its own kind).
The process is described: 'إذا عملت المادة المركب بادر الأقل منها للتصعيد قبل مبادرة الأبطاء' (when heat acts on the composite, the less dense tends to rise before the denser).
4
Generalize to the principle: heat separates dissimilar and gathers similar
Razi concludes with the celebrated principle: 'الحارة من شانها تعيق المخلفات وجمع المتشاكلات' (heat has the property of separating dissimilar things and gathering similar ones). This explains why, in the absence of heat, similar substances coalesce.
This principle is used widely in natural philosophy, from alchemy to medicine.

Core Idea
Moisture is not a positive quality but a receptive state; its opposition to dryness is privation.

This passage from Razi's Mulakhkhas defines moisture and dryness and delves into the nature of their opposition. The definitions given by the author and the Shaykh (Ibn Sina) reveal a deep logical distinction: is moisture a positive quality or merely the absence of an obstacle? Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to analyse how contraries and privatives are handled in the Islamic sciences, from physics to theology.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definitions of moist and dry
Locate the definitions in the Arabic: moist is 'that which has no obstacle to adhesion and separation but has an obstacle to receiving shapes'; dry is 'that which has an obstacle to adhesion and separation'. Note that the definition of moist has two parts.
The first part comes from the author's earlier statement; the second part follows the Shaykh's view.
2
Identify the two interpretations of moisture
The passage refers to two interpretations: one where moisture is a receptive state (non-positive) and another where it is a genus of sensible qualities (e.g., moisture of water, dates, saliva).
Pay attention to the phrase 'according to another interpretation' at the end.

Understand the opposition debate
The author argues that according to the Shaykh, the opposition between moisture and dryness is by privation and possession (al-'adam wa al-milka), not by contrariety. This means one is the absence of the other in a subject that naturally possesses it.
Privation opposites: e.g., sight and blindness, where blindness is privation of sight in a subject capable of seeing.

The author reasons: if moisture (or receptivity) were a positive accident, it would lead to an infinite regress; if it were essential, it would require an additional cause. Therefore, it cannot be a positive quality. This is a reductio ad absurdum.
The argument also adds that if it were positive, it would be insensible, but we do not sense it in pure air.
5

The author leaves open two senses: (1) moisture as a non-existent quality (privation) and (2) moisture as a sensible genus with species. Which one is correct? The passage does not resolve it, inviting further investigation.
This ambiguity is typical in philosophical texts where multiple interpretations coexist.

Heaviness and lightness are both absolute (earth/fire) and relative (water/air).

Al-Razi now turns to the natural forces of heaviness (thiqal) and lightness (khiffa). He explains that these qualities are inherent in bodies and direct them toward or away from the center of the world. However, he notes that heaviness and lightness can be considered either absolutely (earth is absolutely heavy, fire absolutely light) or relatively (water is heavy compared to air but light compared to earth). This lesson will help you parse these distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definitions
Find where al-Razi defines heaviness and lightness. He calls them 'the natural force by which a body is moved to where its center of gravity coincides with the center of the world.'
2
Identify the absolute cases
He states that earth is absolutely heavy—it sinks below all bodies. Fire is absolutely light—it floats above all. Mark these two elements.
These are the 'simple' elements.
3
Identify the relative cases
Water and air are relative: water is heavy compared to air but light compared to earth. Al-Razi says heaviness and lightness become 'relative' when considering motion of the true body and the surrounding.
4
Note the condition for relativity
He gives an example: water encompasses earth and is encompassed by air; in this arrangement, the terms are relative. Understand that relativity depends on context of surrounding elements.

Not all certainties are equal – Razi's taxonomy of assents reveals the epistemic foundation of every Islamic claim.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi dissects the nature of assent (tasdiq), the very act of affirming a proposition. He distinguishes between decisive judgments that correspond to reality and those that don't, further categorizing sources of knowledge. This classification is crucial for evaluating the certainty of any statement in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. By the end, you'll navigate his framework with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify decisive vs non-decisive judgments
The first fork: is the mind committed (jāzim) or not? Only decisive judgments carry certainty; non-decisive ones like doubt and conjecture have lower epistemic value.
Decisive does not mean true – compound ignorance is decisive but false.
2
For decisive judgments, test correspondence to reality
If the judgment matches the thing judged (mutābiq), it qualifies as knowledge ('ilm). If not, it is compound ignorance (jahl murakkab), a dangerous state of false certainty.
This distinction is critical: a person can be absolutely certain yet completely wrong.
3
Classify the source of correct decisive judgments
Correct judgments come from sense (empirical), intellect (primary or theoretical), or a composite (differentiated perceptions or intuitive insights). Primary propositions (awwaliyyāt) are self-evident, like 'the whole is greater than the part'; theoretical ones require proof, like 'the world is created'.
This step helps you locate a claim's ground in the Islamic sciences – e.g., a primary proposition in Kalam might be 'existence is evident'.
4
Among non-decisive judgments, distinguish doubt, conjecture, and imitation
If both sides are equally plausible, it's doubt (shakk). If one side is stronger, it's conjecture (wahm). The mere belief of an imitator (muqallid) without active reasoning is also non-decisive.
This prevents conflating probabilistic knowledge with certainty.
5
Explain the acquisition of primary knowledge (awwaliyyāt)
Razi describes how the soul moves from potential to actual knowledge when conditions are met and obstacles removed. Perceptions arise through an inherent movement, and then the mind naturally relates them in judgments. This explains why primary propositions become known without explicit proof.
This theory underpins how basic axioms are known – e.g., the principle of non-contradiction is primary.

Argument that necessary sciences do not require thought

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Thought is a search; if the sought is already present, thought is impossible.

Al-Razi argues that the reception of necessary sciences by the soul does not require thought. He defines thought as a search for the unknown, and necessary sciences are already present in the prophetic faculty. Therefore, thought would be redundant and even contradictory. This argument reinforces the immediate nature of certain types of knowledge.

2

3
Apply to necessary sciences
Necessary sciences (like the axioms of logic) are inherently present in the soul, especially in the prophetic faculty. Thus, they are already possessed, and thought would be futile.
4
Recognize the conclusion
Therefore, the soul's reception of necessary sciences does not depend on thought. Theoretical sciences, however, require thought because their conclusions are not immediately present.

بنوا هذا الباب أن الخير وجود والشر عدم. وعندي أن هذا البحث لفظي، لأنا إن عنينا بالشر عدم ما من شأنه أن يكون، كان عدميا؛ وإن عنينا به الألم وما يكون طريقا إليه وما يؤدي إليه، كان وجوديا، فلا نزاع. وإن الألم كيفية وجودية، والناس في أكثر الأمر إنما يريدون بالشر الألم وما يكون طريقا إليه. قالوا: تفريعات هذا الأصل: الشيء إما أن يكون خيرا محضا، أو شرا محضا، أو خيرا من وجه وشرا من وجه. فالخير المحض هو الذي لا يتطرق العدم عليه، وهو الموجود الواجب لذاته من جميع جهاته. وأما الشر المحض فهو محال. وأما الذي يكون خيرا من وجه وشرا من وجه فهو على ثلاثة أقسام: إما أن يكون الخير غالبا، أو الشر غالبا، أو متساويين. والذي يكون الخير غالبا فيه فيجب وجوده، لأن ترك الخير الكثير لأجل الشر القليل شر كثير.
They built this chapter on the premise that good is existence and evil is non-existence. In my view, this inquiry is verbal, because if by evil we mean the non-existence of what ought to exist, it is non-existential; and if by it we mean pain and what leads to it and what results from it, it is existential, so there is no dispute. And pain is an existential quality, and people mostly mean by evil pain and what leads to it. They said: ramifications of this principle: a thing is either pure good, or pure evil, or good in one respect and evil in another. Pure good is that which non-existence does not affect it, and it is the necessary existent in all aspects. As for pure evil, it is impossible. As for that which is good in one respect and evil in another, it is of three kinds: either good is predominant, or evil is predominant, or they are equal. That in which good is predominant, its existence is obligatory, because abandoning much good for the sake of little evil is great evil.

Commentary
This passage begins by stating the foundational premise of the discussion: good (khayr) is existence (wujud) and evil (sharr) is non-existence ('adam). The author then critiques this as merely a verbal dispute. He argues that if we mean by evil the absence of what should exist, then it is non-existential; but if we mean pain and its causes, then it is an existential quality—thus the debate is semantic. People commonly refer to evil as pain or its means. The author then transitions to a classification of things in relation to good and evil: pure good—which is God, the necessary existent; pure evil—impossible; and mixed, which is further divided into three: good predominant, evil predominant, or equal. When good is predominant, creation is obligatory, as leaving off much good for little evil is itself a great evil. This mirrors the principle in fiqh and kalam that the greater good outweighs the lesser evil.
