Necessary Properties of Simple Essences: Intrinsic and Extrinsic
Author examines whether a simple essence can have a necessary property and discusses causal relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify necessary properties of a simple essence as intrinsic or extrinsic and evaluate whether a simple can have multiple necessary properties.

Core Idea
A simple essence can have necessary properties without being both agent and patient.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi examines a key metaphysical question: can a simple essence have a necessary property? He distinguishes between intrinsic necessary properties, like an accident necessarily inhering in substance, and extrinsic ones, like the world's existence depending on its cause. He also rejects the famous 'one-from-one' principle, opening the door for a simple to produce multiple effects. This analysis is crucial for understanding causation and essential attributes in Islamic metaphysics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being investigated
Find the Arabic phrase for 'simple essence' (الماهية البسيطة) and 'necessary property' (لازم). Note the author's question: هل لها لازم أم لا؟
2
Classify the types of necessary property
The author distinguishes between intrinsic (نفسي) and extrinsic (منفصل) necessity. Identify the examples: accident for substance (intrinsic), existence for the world (extrinsic).
3
Analyze the dilemma of the simple essence
Razi presents a conditional: if the simple essence necessitates its necessary property, it becomes both receptive and active – which he implies is problematic. If it does not, then it is not a necessary property. Understand the tension.
4
Evaluate the rejection of the one-from-one principle
The author explicitly rejects the principle that a simple can produce only one effect. Find the Arabic statement (وإن القول بأن البسيط لا يصدر عنه أكثر من واحد باطل عندنا). Consider its implications for causality.
5
Distinguish bilateral vs unilateral necessity
Note the final observation: necessity can be from both sides (mutual implication) or from one side only. Think of examples: mutual necessity might be like an accident and substance; unilateral necessity like the world's existence depending on its cause.

Bilateral vs Unilateral Necessity
Author distinguishes bilateral from unilateral necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between bilateral and unilateral necessary connections and provide an example from Islamic theology.

Core Idea
Necessary connections can go one way or both ways.

When we say one thing necessarily implies another, we must ask whether the implication is mutual. This lesson unpacks the author's first distinction in classifying necessary connections, a skill essential for understanding definitions and arguments in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of necessity
The author states that necessity can be from both sides (bilateral) or from one side (unilateral). Focus on the first phrase: 'وأما أن يكون اللزوم من الجانبين أو من جانب واحد'.
Bilateral means each implies the other; unilateral means only one implies the other.
2
Define bilateral necessity
In bilateral necessity, if A exists then B necessarily exists, and if B exists then A necessarily exists. They are mutually dependent.
Example: being a human and being rational (in classical definition) are bilateral.
3
Define unilateral necessity
In unilateral necessity, A necessarily implies B, but B does not necessarily imply A. The implication is one-way.
Example: fire necessarily implies heat, but heat can come from other sources, so heat does not necessarily imply fire.
4
Apply to Islamic theology
Consider the relationship between 'the world is originated' and 'it has a creator'. The former unilaterally implies the latter, but the latter does not unilaterally imply the former (since the creator could exist without creating the world).
This distinction is crucial to avoid false equivalences in kalam arguments.

Real vs Conventional Necessary Attributes
Author divides necessary attributes into real and conventional, and lists causes of necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify necessary attributes as real (haqiqi) or conventional (i'tibari) and identify the valid causes of necessity.

Core Idea
Not all necessary properties are real; some depend on external relations.

Beyond the direction of implication, the author categorizes necessary attributes themselves into real and conventional. This lesson unpacks that distinction and the four ways a property can be necessary, with the last one rejected as false.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish real from conventional attributes
Real necessary attributes (lazim haqiqi) actually exist in the subject externally, like an accident inhering in a substance. Conventional attributes (lazim i'tibari) are relational, like 'half' which depends on another number.
The author uses the example of an accident for substance as real, and half for two as conventional.
2
Understand the four causes of necessity
A necessary property can be so due to: (1) itself, (2) that in which it inheres, (3) being a locus for something else, or (4) inhering without a locus (rejected).
The fourth is rejected because an accident cannot exist without a substance.
3
Identify which causes are valid
The author accepts causes 1-3 in general, but declares the fourth as absolutely false (bāṭil ʿalā al-iṭlāq).
This aligns with the standard Islamic metaphysical principle that accidents require a substrate.
4
Apply to usul al-fiqh
In legal theory, 'intoxication' is a real attribute of wine (it inheres), while 'prohibited' is conventional (depends on divine command).
Understanding this distinction prevents flawed analogies.

Negative vs. Modified Propositions
Author clarifies the difference between negative and modified propositions and their truth conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a negative proposition (saliba) and a modified proposition (ma'dula) by analyzing the placement of negation and the truth conditions regarding subject existence.

Core Idea
Negative denies the relation; modified affirms a negative term.

In Islamic logic, it is crucial to distinguish between two types of propositions involving negation: the saliba (negative) and the ma'dula (modified). Although they may appear similar in translation, they differ in logical structure and truth conditions. This lesson will help you identify each type and understand how they behave, especially when the subject is non-existent or mental.

Guided Path
1
Identify the placement of negation
In a saliba, the negation modifies the copula (the relation between subject and predicate). In a ma'dula, the negation modifies the predicate term itself, not the relation. For example, 'Zaid is not writing' (saliba) vs 'Zaid is not-writing' (ma'dula).
2
Check the truth condition regarding subject existence
A ma'dula can be true even if the subject does not exist in external reality, as long as the mind can conceive the subject and affirm the negated predicate. A saliba, according to the author, requires the subject to exist in reality for the judgment to be meaningful.
This is a key difference highlighted in the passage.
3
Analyze the logical form
Represent the propositions symbolically. For a saliba: S is not P (negation of the copula). For a ma'dula: S is not-P (affirmation of a negative predicate). Note that the ma'dula is a type of affirmative (mujiba) because it affirms the relation, but with a negated term.
4
Apply to Islamic science examples
Consider the proposition 'The poor person does not pay zakat' (saliba) vs 'The poor person is a non-payer of zakat' (ma'dula). In fiqh, these may have different implications for the ruling.
The distinction affects how we understand legal descriptors.

Classification of Categorical Propositions by Subject-Predicate Type and Modal Truth Conditions
Classification of propositions by subject-predicate type and modal truth conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four divisions of categorical propositions based on subject/predicate type and determine the truth value of each categorical form (universal affirmative, universal negative, particular affirmative, particular negative) under the three modal matters (necessity, impossibility, possibility).

Core Idea
The truth of a categorical proposition depends on its modal matter, not just its quantity and quality.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas classifies categorical propositions into four divisions based on whether the subject and predicate are particular or universal. It then examines the truth conditions of the four categorical forms under each modal matter: necessity (وجوب), impossibility (امتناع), and possibility (إمكان). Understanding this classification is essential for evaluating the certainty of logical arguments in Islamic sciences, where the modal strength of a proposition determines its evidentiary value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions
The author divides categorical propositions based on the type of subject and predicate: (1) subject particular, predicate universal; (2) subject universal, predicate particular; (3) both universal; (4) both indefinite or one indefinite. Note the order and how each division affects truth conditions.
2
Understand the three modal matters (mādda)
The 'matter' of a proposition is the modal nature of the relation between subject and predicate: necessity (e.g., 'Man is an animal'), impossibility (e.g., 'Man is a stone'), or possibility (e.g., 'Man is a writer'). The truth of a categorical form varies with these matters.
The term 'mādda' here refers to the modal content, not the matter as in material cause.
3
Apply truth values to each categorical form
For the second division (subject particular, predicate universal), the author states: universal affirmative is false in all matters; universal negative is false in necessity, true in impossibility, unknown in possibility; particular affirmative is true in necessity, false in impossibility, unknown in possibility; particular negative is true in all matters. Use this as a template for other divisions.
The passage gives explicit truth values only for this division; other divisions are mentioned but details are corrupted.
4
Analyze the indefinite proposition (muhmala)
The indefinite proposition does not specify quantity. Its truth is conditional upon the truth of the corresponding particular. For example, 'Man is every book' is false because it would require the reality of man to apply to every book individually.
This illustrates how indefinite propositions are treated as equivalent to particular in logical analysis.

The Three Layers of Modal Propositions and Their Negations
Author classifies three layers of modal propositions and their negations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three primary modes (wujūb, imtinā‘, imkān khāṣṣ) and list all six propositions within each modal layer.

Core Idea
Every modal proposition belongs to one of three layers, each with a primary mode and five corresponding negations.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas classifies modal propositions into three distinct layers (ṭabaqāt) based on their primary mode: necessity, impossibility, and special possibility. Each layer contains the primary mode and five related negations that form a logically interconnected system. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly evaluating modal syllogisms in Islamic logic, particularly in kalām arguments about existence and temporality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three primary modes
The passage names three modes: wujūb (necessity), imtinā‘ (impossibility), and imkān khāṣṣ (special possibility). These are the 'heads' of each layer.
Special possibility (imkān khāṣṣ) is the middle mode, neither necessary nor impossible.
2
List the six propositions of the first layer (wujūb)
The first layer includes: (1) wājib an yūjad, (2) laysa wājib an yūjad, (3) mumtani‘ an lā yūjad, (4) laysa mumtani‘ an lā yūjad, (5) al-mumkin al-‘āmm an lā yūjad, (6) laysa al-mumkin al-‘āmm an lā yūjad.
Note the symmetry: each primary mode has five negations that exhaust the other modal possibilities.
3
Examine the second and third layers similarly
The second layer (imtinā‘) mirrors the first but with existence replaced by non-existence and vice versa. The third layer (imkān khāṣṣ) has only four propositions, but the author lists six in the first two layers because 'general possibility' (al-mumkin al-‘āmm) is included as a negation within necessity/impossibility layers.
The third layer (special possibility) has exactly four propositions: possible exists, possible not exists, and their negations.
4
Understand the interrelations (talāzum and ta‘ākus)
The passage states that each layer and its negations are mutually entailing (mutalāzima) and opposite (muta‘ākisa). This means that within a layer, any proposition implies its opposite? Actually, 'mutalāzima muta‘ākisa' likely means that the propositions are interrelated such that knowing one gives information about others, but they are not all compatible; they are opposites in pairs. Also, the negations of one layer do not harmonize with another layer – so a negation from the necessity layer does not directly relate to the possibility layer.
This systematic arrangement prevents equivocation between different modal layers.

Author discusses the absolute proposition and disputes about conditions for universal truth.

Learning Objective

We now turn to a key concept: the absolute proposition (al-qadiyya al-muṭlaqa) and what makes a universal claim true. The passage presents a dispute between two groups: one insists that a universal proposition must hold at all times (dā'ima), the other that it must be necessary (ḍarūriyya). Understanding this disagreement sharpens your ability to evaluate the truth conditions of universal statements in Islamic sciences.

Four Types of Conditional and Disjunctive Propositions
Author classifies shartiyyat into four types based on connector position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of shartiyya based on connector position and distinguish between the two types of munfasila (man' al-jam' and man' al-jam' wal-khuluw).

Core Idea
The position of the connector changes the logical force of the proposition.

In this lesson, we break down the classification of conditional and disjunctive propositions according to the position of the logical connector relative to the subject. This classification is crucial for understanding how propositions are used in arguments across usul al-fiqh, kalam, and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the connector type
Determine if the proposition uses a conditional connector (harf al-ittisal like 'if'/'whenever') or a disjunctive connector (harf al-infisal like 'either...or').
Connectors indicate whether the proposition is muttasil (conditional) or munfasil (disjunctive).
2
Locate the connector relative to the subject
Check whether the connector appears after the subject (mawdu') or before it. This gives one of four positions: ittisal after, ittisal before, infisal after, infisal before.
Examples: 'Whenever the sun is rising' (after subject), 'If the sun is rising' (before subject).
3
Classify the disjunctive type
For disjunctive propositions, determine whether the disjunction applies to each individual subject (man' al-jam' wal-khuluw) or to the entire set (man' al-jam' only). The key is whether the subject is taken 'according to its nature' or 'according to its totality'.
Example: 'Every number is either even or odd' applies to each number individually, so both parts cannot be true or false together. 'Either every number is even or every number is odd' applies to the whole set, so both parts can be false.
4
Test with an Islamic example
Apply the distinction to a fiqh example: 'Every act of worship is either wajib or nafl' is man' al-jam' wal-khuluw (each act is one or the other). 'Either every act of worship is wajib or every act of worship is nafl' is man' al-jam' only (both false if some are wajib and some nafl).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Substance is independent, accident is dependent, but a state can be both relative to different substrates.

This lesson introduces the foundational categories of substance and accident in Aristotelian logic as adopted by Islamic philosophers. The passage then presents a nuanced argument that a 'state' (hal) such as a substantial form can be considered an accident relative to one substrate and a substance relative to another, demonstrating the relativity of these categories.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Divisibility is an accident of magnitude but essential to matter.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas presents a subtle logical point: just because a property appears regularly on a thing does not make it essential. Here, divisibility seems inherent to magnitude, but al-Razi argues it is actually an accident that belongs to matter. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing definitions and categories in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property under discussion
The property is 'divisibility' (al-inqisam). It is being analyzed to determine whether it is essential (dhati) or accidental ('aradi) to magnitude (al-miqdar).
2
Follow the reasoning for magnitude
The passage notes that when a single magnitude is divided, two magnitudes result, and the original magnitude ceases to exist. If divisibility were essential to magnitude, magnitude would not cease when divided; but it does. Hence divisibility cannot be essential to magnitude.
3
Identify the true subject of divisibility
Since divisibility does not inhere essentially in magnitude, it must belong to something else. The author concludes it belongs to matter (al-madda), which persists through division and change.
4
Formulate the distinction
Essential property: a property without which a thing cannot exist or be conceived. Accidental property: a property that may or may not be present. Divisibility is accidental to magnitude (magnitude can be conceived without actual division) but essential to matter (matter is the substrate of change).

Author defines continuous and discrete quantity and enumerates their subtypes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given quantity is continuous or discrete, and list the subtypes of continuous quantity (magnitude and time, with line, surface, body) according to al-Razi's classification.

Core Idea
Quantity is either continuous (parts share a boundary) or discrete (parts are separate units).

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas introduces the first of Aristotle's categories: quantity. Understanding the division of quantity into continuous and discrete is essential for later discussions in logic, physics, and theology, where the nature of time, space, and number plays a key role. Let us walk through the definition and the detailed subtypes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary division of quantity
Read the first line: quantity is divided into continuous and discrete. Note the defining criterion: whether hypothetical parts share a common boundary ('a single name').
2
Distinguish continuous from discrete
For continuous quantity, parts are connected so that they share a common limit (e.g., points on a line). For discrete, parts are separate and do not share a boundary (e.g., units of a number).
Time is continuous because moments are connected by the 'now'.
3
Trace the subdivisions of continuous quantity
Continuous divides into magnitude (receptive of parts) and time (not receptive of parts). Magnitude further divides into one-dimensional (line), two-dimensional (surface), and three-dimensional (geometrical body).
The Arabic term for geometrical body is 'jism ta'līmi', distinct from natural body.
4
Understand discrete quantity as number
Discrete quantity is solely number. The author argues that any discrete collection (e.g., a crowd) is only a quantity when considered as a number of units, not as a plurality of distinct objects.
The unit may be taken abstractly or concretely, but the quantitative aspect comes from counting.

Quantity: Divisions and Mental Abstraction
Author defines quantity's divisions and its mental separation from matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish discrete from continuous quantity and explain how quantity is mentally abstracted from matter to form the mathematical body.

Core Idea
Quantity is inseparable from matter externally but mentally separable, giving rise to pure mathematics.

In this passage, al-Razi lays the groundwork for understanding quantity (al-kamm) as a category of being. He reminds us that discrete quantity (number, speech) has already been covered, and now he turns to continuous quantity—line, surface, body, and time. The key insight is that while quantity never exists apart from matter in the external world, our minds can strip away all material properties and consider the pure extension, which is the foundation of mathematical sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of quantity
Al-Razi mentions discrete (al-munfasil) and continuous (al-muttasil) quantity. Discrete includes number and speech; continuous includes line, surface, body, and time. Notice that time is considered continuous because it relates to motion, a theme in physics.

The author states that quantity 'does not separate from matter externally.' This means that in extramental reality, you never find a pure length or surface without some physical substance possessing it.

Although quantity cannot exist apart from matter in reality, the mind can 'abstract' it. Al-Razi says we can 'imagine quantity while abstracting from all matters.' This is the process that produces the mathematical body (jism taʿlīmī).

Razi's Disjunctive Refutation of Void (Khala')
Author divides void into types and refutes contiguous void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate Razi's disjunctive syllogism against the existence of contiguous void (khala' munfasil).

Core Idea
Void is impossible because every quantity is either discrete or continuous — both lead to absurdity.

This passage from Razi's Mulakhkhas presents a crisp logical refutation of void. By classifying void as a quantity, Razi forces it into the dichotomy of discrete or continuous, then shows that neither can be consistently conceived. The argument is a model of how disjunctive syllogism is used in Islamic natural philosophy to eliminate metaphysical hypotheses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching disjunction
Find where the author states that void must be either discrete or continuous. This is the main disjunction that structures the entire argument.
The Arabic says: 'فالخلاء كم، فلا بد أن يكون منفصلاً أو متصلاً'.
2
Analyze the refutation of the discrete disjunct
Why is discrete void impossible? The author argues that discrete quantity is composed of indivisible units, yet a divisible body cannot subsist in indivisible units. Since bodies are divisible, void cannot be discrete.
This is a reductio: if void were discrete, it could not contain bodies, contradicting the very concept of place.
3
Analyze the refutation of the continuous disjunct
The author splits continuous void into two subcases: essentially continuous and accidentally continuous. In the first case, it becomes a magnitude in matter, thus a body, contradicting void. In the second, void inheres in body or vice versa, also leading to void being a body.
Both subcases end with a contradiction: void would be a body, which is precisely what void is defined not to be.
4
Recognize the logical structure as a disjunctive syllogism
The argument has the form: either p or q; not p; not q; therefore neither p nor q (and thus void is impossible). Both disjuncts are eliminated by reductio.
This is a valid disjunctive syllogism if the disjunction is exhaustive. The author assumes all quantities are either discrete or continuous.
5
Evaluate the strength of the argument
Consider whether the disjunction is truly exhaustive (are there other types of quantity?) and whether the refutations rely on contested assumptions (e.g., that all continuous quantities must be in matter).
Even if one accepts the Aristotelian framework, the argument is logically sound. This illustrates how Islamic philosophers used logical tools to critique natural philosophical concepts.

Core Idea
Every magnitude requires matter; void is supposed empty, so it cannot be a magnitude.

In this passage, al-Razi takes the definition of void as a continuous quantity and subjects it to a logical test. Using a disjunctive syllogism with sub-cases, he shows that each possible way void could be continuous leads to an absurdity. This lesson will walk you through the structure of that argument, sharpening your ability to parse complex compound reasoning in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its classification
Find the initial claim: 'al-khala' is a continuous quantity (kamm muttasil). Note the category shift: void is being treated as a candidate existent, not as non-existence.
The passage starts with a definition-like claim, though it is immediately questioned.
2
Extract the disjunction
The author splits the definition into two possible ways void could be continuous: either essentially (bi al-dhat) or accidentally (bi al-‘arad). This is the main fork of the argument.
This is a classic logical division (taqsim).
3
Analyze the first horn (essentially continuous)
If essentially continuous, void is a magnitude (miqdar) with position. But every magnitude exists in matter, so void would be a body in matter – a contradiction because void is supposed to be empty.
The author uses the axiom 'every magnitude is in matter' – a premise from Aristotelian physics.
4
Analyze the second horn (accidentally continuous) and its subcases
Under accidental continuity, there are three possibilities: void is present in the body (magnitude), the body is present in void, or both are present in the subject of magnitude. Trace each subcase: the first leads to void being present in a magnitude already present in body (infinite regress), the second makes void a body, the third collapses into the first.
Each subcase is a miniature conditional argument.
5
Conclude the refutation
The author declares: 'So it is clear that what was supposed to be void is a body.' The compound disjunction has been exhausted, and every branch ended in impossibility.
This is a reduction ad absurdum (talazum) using a complete disjunction.

Knowledge of Non-Existent Things: Simple vs. Composite
Author explains how knowledge of non-existents is possible, distinguishing simple and composite non-existents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between simple and composite non-existents and explain how each is known through its existential correlates.

Core Idea
Non-existents are known indirectly through their positive components.

How can we know something that does not exist? This seems paradoxical, but Islamic logicians like Fakhr al-Din al-Razi provided a clear answer: we know non-existents not directly, but through the positive concepts they negate. In this passage, Razi distinguishes two kinds of non-existents—simple and composite—and shows that knowledge of each depends on prior knowledge of existential elements. This is a fundamental principle in Islamic epistemology, with applications in theology (e.g., knowing that God is unlike creation) and in logic (e.g., understanding the principle of non-contradiction).

Guided Path
1

The author gives the example of knowing that God is not like a certain relation (the relation of blackness to whiteness). The positive correlate here is the concept of that relation.
3
Analyze the composite non-existent example
Knowledge that two contradictories do not coexist requires knowing the two things (e.g., blackness and whiteness) and the concept of coexistence, then negating their combination.

For any non-existent, the intellect first grasps an existing concept (or concepts) and then negates or denies a relation. This is how knowledge of non-existents is possible.
This principle is used in kalam to defend knowledge of future events or attributes of God.

Commentary
This passage discusses the category of relation (al-idafa). The author first divides relatives into two types: those that indicate a relation implicitly (like father and son) and those that only share a name. He then lists properties of relatives, particularly equivalence (al-takāfu') and reciprocity (al-inkisak). Reciprocity means that if A is related to B in a certain way, then B is related to A in the corresponding way (e.g., father-son). The author addresses objections: one about relational concepts having no external existence, answered by saying they exist mentally. Another objection from naturalists (al-jawānihiyya) is that if relation were an existing attribute, it would lead to infinite regress (tasalsul) because the relation would itself require a new relation to its locus. The author seems to respond that this regress is not necessary, but the text is unclear. The passage ends with a cryptic statement about 'the speech of the night'.

Refuting the Real Existence of Relation (al-Idafah)
Author refutes claim that relation is an existing attribute, addressing infinite regress and divine transcendence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Razi's infinite regress argument against relation as an existing attribute and explain why relation is a mental consideration rather than extra-mental.

Core Idea
Relation is a mental consideration, not an extra-mental attribute—otherwise infinite regress or God as locus of accidents.

Razi argues that if relation (like fatherhood) were a real attribute inhering in a subject, it would lead to an absurd infinite regress. The occurrence of a relation in its subject is not an additional entity; it is simply the relation itself. This argument has profound implications for how we understand categories and divine transcendence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target claim
Locate the view Razi is refuting: the claim that relation (al-idafah) is an existing attribute (sifa wujūdiyya). This is the starting point of the argument.
This claim is common among philosophers who treat categories as real accidents.
2
Follow the infinite regress argument
Razi shows that if relation were an existing attribute, its occurrence (hulūl) in a subject would be distinct from itself, requiring another relation for that occurrence, leading to an endless chain—an infinite regress.
This regress is a logical absurdity (tasalsul), used to refute the initial claim.
3
Understand the response (answer)
The occurrence of a specific relation like paternity is not a separate entity; it is identical to the relation itself. The concept 'occurrence in a subject' is just that—there is no extra entity.
This blocks the regress by denying that occurrence is a distinct reality.
4
Recognize the theological consequence
Razi adds that if relation were an existing attribute, then God would be a locus for accidents (mahall li'l-ḥawādith), which is impossible in Islamic theology.
This reinforces the conclusion from a kalam perspective.
5
Draw the final conclusion
Relation exists only in the mind (fi'l-dhihn), not in external entities (fi'l-a'yān). It is a mental consideration (amr i'tibari).
This is Razi's position and aligns with Ash'ari occasionalism.

If relation were an existing attribute, it would necessarily have a subject, and its occurrence in the subject would be other than itself, so there would be another relation for the relation, then that other relation would occur in the subject, so it would have another relation, and so on, leading to an infinite regress. The answer is that the occurrence of paternity in the subject is a quality of that subject itself, but the occurrence in that subject has no concept other than being an occurrence in that subject, so no infinite regress follows. Then we say: The occurrence of a thing in a subject is not an existing thing in that subject; rather, it is a mental consideration. So relation exists in the mind, not in external entities. If relation were an existing attribute, it would necessitate that the Exalted Creator be a locus for accidents, which is impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument refuting the view that relation (al-idafah) is an existing attribute inhering in a subject. The author presents the opponents' objection: if relation were an existing attribute, its occurrence in a subject would be distinct from itself, requiring another relation for that occurrence, leading to an infinite regress (tasalsul). The author then gives the answer: the occurrence of a relation like paternity in the father is identical to the father's being a father; it has no separate concept. The notion of 'occurrence in a subject' is not an extra entity but a mental consideration (amr i'tibari). Therefore, relation exists only in the mind, not externally. Finally, the author adds a theological argument: if relation were an external attribute, God would be a locus for accidents, which is impossible. This refutes the claim and shows relation is a conceptual category.

Key Terms
الإضافة (al-idafah): Relation or relative category; Razi argues it is a mental consideration, not an extra-mental attribute. [Note: In earlier logic, al-idafah is a category of accidental being; Razi's view aligns with Ash'ari occasionalism that relations are not real existents.]
المحل (al-mahal): The subject or locus in which an attribute inheres; here used for the putative subject of relation. [Note: Standard term in kalam for substance/substratum; no significant shift.]
التسلسل (al-tasalsul): Infinite regress; a logical absurdity invoked to refute the reality of relation as an attribute. [Note: Standard term in falsafa for infinite chain; used widely in arguments against infinite regress.]
أمر اعتباري (amr i'tibari): A mental consideration or conceptual entity with no extra-mental existence; Razi classifies relation as such. [Note: In later Sufism and usul, i'tibari can mean relative or subjective; here it contrasts with wujudi (existential).]
