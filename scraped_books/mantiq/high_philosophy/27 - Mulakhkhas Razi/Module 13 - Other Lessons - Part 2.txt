Why Celestial Motion Must Be Successive: Potential vs. Actual Positions
Author explains why celestial sphere's motions occur successively due to infinite potential positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the celestial sphere's motion is successive, using the distinction between potential and actual positions.

Core Idea
The sphere’s positions are infinite in potential, so they can only be actualized one after another.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi explains why the celestial sphere cannot actualize all its positions at once. The answer lies in the nature of potentiality and actuality: the sphere has infinitely many potential positions, but they cannot all become actual simultaneously. This forces its motion to be a succession of actualizations. Understanding this reasoning is key to grasping the metaphysical foundations of motion in Islamic cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Find the key claim
The passage argues that the sphere's motion is successive (ta'aqub). Look for the conclusion near the beginning: 'ولا يمكن حصول حميها معا' (impossible for all to occur together).
2
Identify the premise about positions
The premise is that positions (awda') are infinite in number and all in potency (bi al-quwwa). The sphere cannot bring them all to act at once.
The text says: 'فانه لا وضع يحصل الا وهناك اوضاع أو لانهاية لها وهى بأسرها بالقوة'
3
Connect to the impossibility of simultaneous actualization
Since actualization of all positions together is impossible, the sphere must bring them out one by one. This leads to successive motion.
4
Recognize the metaphysical motive
The sphere observes the intellect's perfection (having all actual) and yearns to imitate it, but because its positions are infinite, it cannot do so all at once, so it does so successively.
The text: 'ثم ان الفلك لما تصور كمال العقل ... الشتاق إلى التشبه'
5
Summarize the argument in your own words
Write a brief statement: 'Because the celestial sphere has infinitely many potential positions that cannot be actualized simultaneously, its motion occurs as a successive actualization of these positions.'
This step consolidates the logical chain.

Razi's Critique of Separate Intellects as Celestial Movers
Razi critiques the philosophers' doctrine of separate intellects and their role in celestial motion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deconstruct Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's argument that the existence of separate intellects is unnecessary to explain celestial motion.

Core Idea
Razi argues that perfection alone is sufficient to cause motion, eliminating the need for separate intellects.

In this passage, Razi critically examines the philosophers' doctrine that celestial spheres are moved by separate intellects through desire for resemblance. He presents a series of arguments to show that this doctrine is not logically necessary and that alternative explanations are possible. This lesson will guide you through the key moves in his critique and help you assess its strength.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
The philosophers claim that each celestial sphere is moved by a separate, immaterial intellect because the sphere desires to resemble that intellect. The motion is thus a result of intellectual love or aspiration.
This doctrine is central to Avicennan cosmology and is often assumed in falsafa.
2
Follow Razi's first objection
Razi argues that the proximate mover of the sphere must have particular perceptions because it causes particular motions. According to the philosophers, having particular perceptions makes the mover corporeal. But the intellects are immaterial, so the mover cannot perceive them, and thus cannot desire them.
This is a reduction ad absurdum: if the mover is corporeal, it cannot perceive the abstract intellect, so desire is impossible.
3
Examine Razi's alternative explanation
Razi proposes that perfection itself is inherently desirable and sufficient to cause motion. He states: 'Every perfection is desirable, sufficient to be a motive for motion without need for the existence of the intellectual substances.' This undercuts the need for separate intellects.
This is a simpler metaphysical picture: motion arises from the sphere's own nature seeking perfection.
4
Understand the implications for intellects
Even if one accepts intellects, Razi argues that their nature (self-intellective, universal knowledge, inability to perceive particulars) leads to problems: they cannot know God or particulars, and their number is arbitrary—there is no proof they correspond to the number of spheres.
Razi thus challenges the explanatory power and coherence of the intellect doctrine.

The author sides with the latter view, emphasizing care for lower things as the motive.

Critique of the Principle 'From One Only One'
Critique of the Neoplatonic principle of emanation applied to intellects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in applying the principle 'from one only one' to the emanation of intellects and spheres.

Core Idea
The principle 'from one only one' leads to contradiction when the cause has multiple attributes.

The Neoplatonic principle claims that from a simple cause only one effect can directly proceed. But when philosophers apply it to the First Intellect, which has both necessity and contingency, they cannot explain how these two attributes generate both an intellect and a sphere without violating their own principle. This lesson unpacks al-Razi's critique of this dilemma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
The passage states the philosophers rely on the ancient principle 'from one only one proceeds' (الواحد لا يصدر عنه إلا الواحد). Find this statement and note its basis.
2
Analyze the attributes of the First Intellect
The intellect has only two attributes: necessity (وجوب) and contingency (إمكان). Why cannot these two directly cause both the intellect and the sphere? The passage argues that if we make them the cause, there would be no aspect left to necessitate knowledge of the sphere and intellect.
The philosophers need knowledge as an intermediate step.
3
Examine the proposed solution
To avoid the contradiction, the philosophers make necessity and contingency a cause of knowledge, and then knowledge becomes the cause of the sphere and intellect. Does this actually solve the problem or merely push it back?
The multiplication of intermediate causes still conflicts with the original principle.
4
Evaluate the consequences of the one-to-one correspondence
The theory requires that each higher intellect causes exactly one lower intellect and one sphere. But the passage notes we know the number of spheres yet do not know the number of intellects. Even if we knew spheres, we still wouldn't know intellects. Point out the absurdity: the theory demands a precise correspondence that cannot be verified.
5
Conclude with al-Razi's verdict
Al-Razi concludes that the principle leads to impossibilities (محال). The multiplication of explanations beyond two things violates the very principle that is supposed to guarantee simplicity. Therefore, the principle is untenable.

God Cannot Will the Impossible: A Kālām Proof
Author proves God cannot will the impossible and refutes an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the kālām argument that God cannot will the impossible, including the objection and refutation.

Core Idea
Willing the impossible would entail preference without a preponderator, which is impossible.

In Islamic theology, God's attributes are understood rationally. One key attribute is His will. This passage presents a dialectical proof that God cannot will what is impossible by its nature (al-mumtani'). The author uses a reductio argument and then addresses an objection based on divine benevolence. By following this reasoning, you will sharpen your ability to analyze theological arguments and identify logical fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis and its logical form
The thesis is 'God cannot will the impossible.' The proof proceeds by assuming the opposite (that God could will the impossible) and showing it leads to contradiction.
2
Trace the reductio argument
If God intends the impossible, and if that intended existence were more preferable to Him than non-existence, then the intention itself would be the cause of that preference—but since the impossible cannot exist, this leads to a contradiction. Otherwise, if the intention does not bring about existence, then God's will would be ineffective, which is impossible because it would be preferring one side without a preponderator (tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ).
The principle of tarjīḥ bilā murjīḥ is a foundational rule in kālām: every preference requires a preponderating factor.
3
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
The objector argues: 'Perhaps God intends the impossible for the sake of acquiring perfection for another (iḥsān).' That is, God's intention is not for the impossible to exist, but to bestow benefit on others.
This objection tries to shift the focus from existence to benevolence.
4
Analyze the refutation (jawab)

Commentary
This passage is dialectical. The author presents a proof that God cannot will the impossible. The argument is: If God intends the impossible, and if that intended existence were more preferable to Him than non-existence, then the act of intending would be the cause of that preference—but this leads to a contradiction (khalaf), because the impossible cannot exist. Alternatively, if God intends the impossible and it does not exist, then He is not truly a willer (since His will is ineffective). Moreover, preferring one possibility over another without a preponderating factor (murajjih) is impossible. An objection is raised: perhaps God intends the impossible for the sake of acquiring perfection for another (iḥsān). The author refutes this: if obtaining perfection for the other and not obtaining it are equal in God's regard, then the same impossibility of preferring without a preponderator returns. The proper understanding of God as a free agent (mukhtār) is that He is Knower of what proceeds from Him, and that proceeding is pure good without deficiency. It is not a condition of a willer that it be possible for Him not to will; rather, it is acceptable that God does not will what He knows exists. Willing the impossible is impossible for the Knower. The discussion on reward and punishment shows that denying them leads to impossible conclusions for God. The final point is that God's knowledge of whether He will or not does not negate His being a willer.

Al-Razi's Refutation of Emanation from One
Author critiques emanationist theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's logical arguments against the emanationist principle 'from one only one' and identify the contradictions he exposes.

Core Idea
Emanation fails because contingency cannot cause existence and the First Intellect's nature undermines multiplicity.

This passage shows Fakhr al-Din al-Razi dismantling the philosophers' emanationist cosmology. He targets the claim that from a single First Intellect many intellects and spheres necessarily proceed. Using a series of logical dilemmas, al-Razi demonstrates that the philosophers' causal chains are impossible, thereby defending the Islamic theological position that God directly creates all things.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' claim
Find where al-Razi summarizes the emanationist hierarchy: each intellect gives rise to an intellect and a sphere, until the Active Intellect. Note the principle that 'from one only one' is supposed to operate.
The philosophers try to derive multiplicity by considering different aspects of the First Intellect.
2
Extract the aspects of the First Intellect
Al-Razi lists three or four aspects: contingency, necessity, existence, and knowledge of another. These are supposed to be the causes for the sphere's body, soul, and intellect.
Recognize that the philosophers are multiplying within the First Intellect to explain what emanates.
3
Analyze the first objection: contingency as cause
Al-Razi argues that contingency (imkan) is a non-existential attribute and cannot be a cause for existence. If it were a cause, every contingency would cause every possible thing, leading to contradiction.
The contingency of the sphere is necessary for its essence, so it cannot be a cause for something else.
4
Analyze the dilemma about the First Intellect's nature
If the First Intellect is purely intellectual (its essence is intellect), it cannot produce a sphere. If it is existential, it is either necessary (which would make the sphere necessary too) or contingent (which would require another cause). Both lead to contradiction.
This dilemma blocks any attempt to derive the material sphere from an intellect.
5
Examine the refutation of the 'existence from the One' defense
Philosophers might say only existence emanates, and multiplicity comes from recipients. Al-Razi calls this rhetorical (khatabi). If simplicity allows multiplicity through relations, then the First Cause could directly produce multiplicity without intermediaries.
This undermines the need for the entire emanation chain.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of the emanationist cosmological scheme proposed by the Muslim philosophers (falasifa). The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, challenges their claim that from a single First Intellect (al-'aql al-awwal) multiple intellects and spheres necessarily proceed, ultimately leading to the Active Intellect that governs the sublunar world. The key principle under attack is that 'from one only one can emanate' (al-wahid la yasdur minhu illa al-wahid). The author begins by summarizing the emanationist hierarchy: each intellect gives rise to another intellect and a sphere, until the Active Intellect. The philosophers try to derive multiplicity from the First Intellect by considering different aspects of it (its contingency, necessity, knowledge, etc.). Al-Razi argues that this is arbitrary and fails logically. For instance, contingency is a relative non-existential attribute and cannot be a cause for existence. Moreover, if the First Intellect's contingency is necessary to its essence, then its necessary effect would be the sphere itself, leading to a contradiction with the sphere's own contingency. The author then presents a dilemma regarding the First Intellect's nature: if it is intellectual (i.e., its essence is intellect), it cannot produce the sphere; if it is existential, it must be either necessary or contingent—both lead to contradictions. On page 487, the author rejects the attempt to save emanation by claiming that only a single existence emanates and that multiplicity arises from the recipients (al-musta'iddat). He calls this explanation rhetorical (khatabi) rather than demonstrative. He further objects that the intellect's nobility is not proven to be simpler, and if simplicity allows multiplicity through relations and negations, then the First Cause itself could directly account for multiplicity without intermediaries. The core argument is that the emanationist framework either violates the principle of simplicity or leads to an infinite regress, thus undermining the proof of a necessary being.
