Author refutes objection about causal priority in existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange on causal priority and identify the author's refutation strategy.

Core Idea
Causal priority is essential, not existential.

In this passage, the author responds to an opponent who claims that a cause must be prior to its effect in existence. The author counters that the priority is not in existence but in essence or quiddity. Understanding this distinction is key to avoiding confusion in causal reasoning within Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Commentary
This passage is an objection and response (naqd wa jawab) regarding the priority of cause over effect. The author addresses an opponent's claim that the cause must precede the effect in existence. He refutes this by noting that contingent essences (al-mahiyat al-mumkina) are receptive to existence, and that the receptive (qabil) is prior to the received (maqbul), but not in existence—rather in essence. Similarly, for the efficient cause (al-illa al-fa'ila), the essence is a cause for existence in the sense that when the essence exists, its existence is prior to the effect's existence. However, this priority is a judgment (hukm) that obtains before existence and is a concomitant (min 'awarid) of that judgment. Therefore, the essence's necessitation of possibility (al-imkan) is not conditioned on existence, and the priority of essence over possibility is not in existence. The author thus defends the view that causal priority is essential, not existential.

Possibility is self-subsistent; it does not need a subject to inhere in.

In this passage, Imam al-Razi defends the existence of void against an objection. The objector claims we cannot know void exists because imagining a larger universe does not prove anything external. Razi's response cuts to the core: the possibility of such dimensions is a real, self-subsistent reality. Understanding this move requires grasping Razi's novel position on possibility.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange: an objection (naqd) and a response (jawab). The objector argues that we do not know void exists, because we can imagine a larger radius without positing an external existent. The author (Imam al-Razi) responds by distinguishing the imagination of a body outside the world from the possibility of dimensions inside the world. The possibility of larger bodies occupying internal dimensions is a real, certain possibility, and that possibility does not exist for external space. The objector claims possibility requires a subject, but the author counters that possibility is self-subsistent and does not need a substrate. Then on the issue of estimation: the objector says everything capable of being estimated is a body. The author replies that 'form' (al-shakl) and 'gap' (al-furja) are not bodies even if they have dimensions; they are only bodies in imagination, not in reality. If the objector means something else by 'body', it is unknown and cannot be used. This clarifies that void's possibility does not imply it is a body.

Key Terms
الإمكان (al-imkan): Possibility, a modal concept; here it is argued to be self-subsistent without needing a subject. [Note: In earlier logic, possibility often requires a subject; Razi asserts it is intrinsic.]
الشكل (al-shakl): Form or shape, which may have dimensions but is not a body. [Note: In falsafah, shakl is a quality or accident; no shift.]
الفرجة (al-furja): Gap or void interval, considered here as having dimensions but not a body. [Note: Rarely used; more common is 'khala'.]

Objection and Refutation on Motion in a Medium
Author presents and refutes an objection about motion time and obstacle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical exchange about motion and time by identifying the conditional structure of the argument and the key distinction used in the refutation.

Core Idea
The refutation hinges on distinguishing time for motion from time due to obstacle.

In this passage, an objector challenges a theory about motion in a medium. The author responds by carefully separating two components of time: the time required for motion itself and the additional time due to resistance. This logical move resolves the apparent contradiction. You will learn to follow this dialectical strategy and reconstruct the conditional argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector begins by asserting that motion as such either requires time or does not, and then argues that it does require time. Find this claim in the Arabic text.
The claim is: 'motion as such either requires time or does not. Its need for time is apparent.'
2
Extract the objector's reasoning
The objector gives two reasons: (1) motion in the spheres has half before whole, which implies time; (2) motions of spheres have obstacles, so they occur in time. Locate these reasons.
These are examples from celestial motions to support the claim.
3
Locate the author's refutation
The author responds by distinguishing: the objector's conclusion only holds if all time is attributed to the obstacle. But in reality, part of time is for the motion itself and part for the obstacle. Find this distinction in the text.
The key phrase: 'if we place all the time opposite the obstacle... but if we place some opposite the motion and some opposite the obstacle...'
4
Recognize the conditional form
The author's refutation can be seen as a conditional: If (all time is for obstacle), then (impossibility). But (not all time is for obstacle), so (no impossibility). Reconstruct this conditional.
This is a modus tollens argument: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P. Here P='all time opposite obstacle', Q='impossibility'. The author denies Q by showing the situation is not impossible.
5
Summarize the dialectical outcome
The author concludes that the impossibility is repelled. The objection fails because it assumed a false premise (all time solely for obstacle).
This is a classic jadal move: showing the objector's argument depends on an unjustified assumption.

Refutation of Sound Persistence: Dialectical Method in Kalam Physics
Author presents two objections to the denial of sound persistence and responds to them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical structure of a kalam argument: objection and response, and evaluate its logical strength.

Core Idea
Sound either persists or is momentary—dialectical refutation shows that denying persistence leads to absurdity.

In this passage, al-Razi presents two objections against the view that sound persists, then refutes them. This dialectical exchange is typical of kalam, where a claim is tested by objections (shubuhāt) and defended by answers (ajwibah). Understanding this structure helps you analyse arguments in Islamic theology and natural philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defendant's position
The author defends the persistence of sound (baqā’ al-sawt). He is responding to two objections raised by those who deny persistence.
This is the mudda'i (claimant) position in the dialectical exchange.
2
Extract the first objection
The first objection: 'If the letters of Zayd's speech were combined, they would not be distinct.' The reasoning is that distinct letters cannot coexist, so sound must be momentary.
The objection assumes that letters (discrete sounds) cannot persist together without confusion.
3
Analyze the first response
The author responds: 'The letters do persist but become intermingled (mutadādda).' This means that although multiple letters exist simultaneously, they blend, and as one fades, the next becomes clear. This explains sequential perception without denying persistence.
The response introduces a new concept (intermingling) to save the phenomena.
4
Extract the second objection and response
The second objection is based on intuition: 'We know intuitively that the solid letters that come are [generated anew].' The author refutes by reductio ad absurdum: if momentary letters do not persist, then the whole sound cannot persist either, yet we affirm sound exists externally. This contradiction refutes the objection.
The second response uses a logical consequence that the objector would find unacceptable.
5
Evaluate the dialectical outcome
Assess whether the refutations succeed logically. The first response may be weak if intermingling is not adequately explained. The second is stronger because it shows that the objection leads to a contradiction with our experience of sound.
In kalam, a valid refutation must either show the objection is based on false premises or leads to absurdity.

Author addresses objections about why the world was created at a specific time.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the objections and responses in a kalām dialectical argument about the timing of creation.

Core Idea
Dialectical exchange tests the coherence of theological claims by exposing hidden assumptions.

In kalām, the timing of creation is a critical issue. Critics argue that if no specific time has inherent merit, the world could have been created at any time, implying eternity. The author engages with objections about benefit, circular reasoning, and divine agency. Understanding how objections are structured and answered is essential for mastering the adab al-bahth tradition.

Identify the claimant's main position

(3) If God's actions are governed by benefit, creating a disbeliever who deserves punishment is not beneficial, so it should not happen.
The text numbers some objections; the third and fourth are explicitly mentioned.

The author responds to each. For the first, he implies that the time must have a special property. For the circularity, he argues against making preservation dependent on knowledge. The responses attempt to show that the objections do not undermine the original position.
4
Assess the dialectical outcome
Evaluate whether the author successfully refutes the objections or if the problem remains. The author acknowledges the need for a specific determinant and suggests that a slight change in time would not affect the benefits for human beings, implying the time is chosen for optimal benefit.

The Necessity of a Preponderating Cause for Particularization
Dialectical refutation of cause-less particularization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the kalām argument that particularization (takhṣīṣ) among equally possible alternatives requires a preponderating cause (murajjiḥ), and refute the objection of arbitrary choice using the hungry person example.

Core Idea
No particularization without a preponderator; the absurdity of the hungry person proves divine will.

One of the most powerful objections to the kalām cosmological argument is that if the world could have been created at any time, why this specific time? Could it not have happened without a cause? The atheist uses the analogy of a hungry person choosing one side of a loaf when all sides are equal—suggesting that choice can occur without a preponderating factor. In this lesson, al-Razi masterfully turns the objection around: if there were truly no preponderator, the hungry person would never choose and would starve. The fact that he does choose demonstrates the necessity of a cause. You will learn to wield this dialectical tool to defend the need for a divine will that particularizes the universe.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The atheist (al-mulḥid) argues that a possible thing can occur without a cause, using the example of a hungry person who begins eating from one side of a loaf when all sides are equal. This challenges the need for a preponderating cause (murajjiḥ) for the world's creation at a specific time.
The objection assumes that arbitrary choice is possible without a determining factor.
2
Establish the Conditions of the Example
Al-Razi asks us to assume perfect equality among all sides of the loaf: same distance, same color, same fat content, etc. Under this condition, no side is intrinsically preferable.
This sets up a scenario where, if a choice occurs, it must be due to something external.
3
Apply the Reductio ad Absurdum
If there is absolutely no preponderating factor, the hungry person cannot choose any side—he would remain undecided until he dies of hunger. Since this conclusion is absurd (he clearly chooses), the premise that no preponderator exists is false.
The absurdity proves the necessity of a preponderator, which in the case of the world is divine will.
4
Generalize to the World's Creation
The same logic applies to the universe: before creation, the world was equally possible at every moment. Its actual occurrence at a specific time requires a preponderating cause, which is identified as the divine will (irādah).
This argument is foundational in Ash'ari and later kalām proofs for God's existence.

In Islamic logic and dialectic (jadal), it is common to find arguments that conflate accidental properties with essential ones. This passage presents a brief exchange where the respondent correctly blocks such a conflation. Guided Path
