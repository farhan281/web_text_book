The Proof from Intention (Qaṣd)
Third proof: intention and universal awareness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the disjunctive argument that animal intention implies awareness of universals.

Core Idea
Intention to move cannot be toward an absolute or completely determined particular, so it must be toward a universal type.

The third proof focuses on the nature of intention (qaṣd) in animal actions. The author argues that when an animal intends to move, it cannot intend an absolutely universal movement nor a fully determined particular movement; therefore it intends a movement that falls under a universal type, showing awareness of that universal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disjunction
The author presents two options: the animal's intention is either to bring about 'movement absolutely' (the universal) or 'this specific movement' (the particular).
The Arabic: 'إما أن يكون قصده متعلقا بإيجاد الحركة مطلقا أو بإيجاد هذه الحركة المعينة'
2
Rule out the first option
If the intention were for 'this specific movement', then after the movement occurs, the intention would already be fulfilled and cannot be directed to it again. This is absurd because the animal continues to intend similar movements.
The text says: 'الأول باطل لأن القصد المتعلق بهذه الحركة ينافي تعلق القصد بها بعد تحققها' – The intention for this movement precludes intending it after its actualization.
3
Rule out the second option
If the intention were for 'movement absolutely', then it would be impossible to have an intention before any movement, because an absolutely universal intention cannot be specified. This is also impossible.
The text: 'والثاني يلزم منه تعلق القصد بها قبل تحققها، وهو محال' – It entails intending it before its actualization, which is impossible.
4
Conclude the intention type
Therefore, the intention must be related to 'a particular movement' (شخص الحركة), not 'this movement' nor 'movement absolutely'. This particular movement is an instance of a universal type.
The conclusion: 'فثبت أن القصد متعلق بإيجاد شخص الحركة لا بإيجاد هذه الحركة'
5
Infer universal awareness
If the animal intends a particular movement (an individual action of a type), it must have a concept of that type (e.g., 'walking'). This proves it perceives the universal.

بيان الصغرى من وجوه ثلثة: فإنها مدركة للجزئيات وكل من أدرك الجزئي فقد أدرك الكلي. أما الأول: فصحة الحركة. وأما الثاني: فإن الإنسان جزء من هذا الإنسان والعلم بالمركب مسبوق بالعلم بمفرداته. فالعلم بهذا الإنسان مسبوق بالعلم بالإنسان، فالعلم بالإنسان مسبوق بالعلم بالكلي. وأما الثالث: فإن الحيوان إذا قصد فعل الحركة فإما أن يكون قصده متعلقا بإيجاد الحركة مطلقا أو بإيجاد هذه الحركة المعينة. الأول باطل لأن القصد المتعلق بهذه الحركة ينافي تعلق القصد بها بعد تحققها. والثاني يلزم منه تعلق القصد بها قبل تحققها، وهو محال. فثبت أن القصد متعلق بإيجاد شخص الحركة لا بإيجاد هذه الحركة. وإذا سلم تعلق القصد بالشخص، لزم الشعور بالكلي. فوجب أن تكون الحيوانات مدركة للكليات، إذ كل نوع من الحيوان طالب لنوع معين من الغذاء وكاره لنوع آخر. فلو لا أنها تعلم النوع المطلوب عن النوع المكروه لما أمكن ذلك. فإن قيل: الحيوان لا شعور له بالماهيات النوعية، بل إذا شاهد عشبا معينا أراده، وإذا شاهد عظما معينا كرهه. قلنا: هذا يخلاف الحس، فإنها إذا جاعت تطلب العشب وإن لم تشاهد عينا، وإذا شاهدت عظما خافت وإن لم تشاهد أسدا. فالعشب من حيث هو عشب هو المطلوب، والعظم من حيث هو عظم هو المكروه.
Explanation of the minor premise from three aspects: Indeed, it (the animal) perceives particulars, and everyone who perceives a particular has perceived a universal. As for the first: the correctness of movement. As for the second: Man is a part of this man, and knowledge of the composite is preceded by knowledge of its components. So knowledge of this man is preceded by knowledge of man, and knowledge of man is preceded by knowledge of the universal. As for the third: When an animal intends to perform a movement, either its intention is related to bringing about movement absolutely, or to bringing about this specific movement. The first is invalid because the intention related to this specific movement precludes the intention relating to it after its actualization. The second entails the intention relating to it before its actualization, which is impossible. So it is established that the intention is related to bringing about a particular movement, not this specific movement. If the intention related to the particular is granted, then awareness of the universal follows. So it is necessary that animals perceive universals, since every species of animal seeks a specific type of food and dislikes another type. Thus if it did not know the desired type from the disliked type, that would not be possible. If it is said: The animal has no awareness of specific natures; rather, when it sees a specific herb it wants it, and when it sees a specific bone it dislikes it. We say: This contradicts sense, for when it is hungry it seeks herb even if it has not seen a specific one, and when it sees a bone it fears even if it has not seen a lion. So herb qua herb is what is desired, and bone qua bone is what is disliked.

Commentary
This passage presents three proofs for the minor premise of a syllogism concluding that animals perceive universals. The author is arguing that animals must have awareness of universal concepts because their behaviors—seeking specific types of food and fearing specific predators—cannot be explained by mere perception of particulars. The first proof is from the correctness of movement: animals move towards objects they have not yet perceived, implying knowledge of the universal type. The second proof uses the example of man: knowing an individual man requires prior knowledge of the universal 'man'. The third proof is about intention: when an animal intends to move, it cannot intend an absolutely universal movement nor a fully determined particular movement; rather, it intends a specific movement undetermined as to its individual instance, which requires knowledge of the universal type. The author then addresses an objection that animals respond only to particular percepts. He refutes it by noting that hungry animals seek herb in general, and fear bone even without seeing a lion, proving they recognize the universal types.

Key Terms
قصد (qaṣd): Intention or purpose, especially related to movement; the mental act directing an action.

Key Takeaway
The intention proof shows that animal actions require universal concepts because the object of intention cannot be purely universal nor purely particular.

Refutation of the Objection: Animals and Particulars
Objection and refutation regarding animal perception of universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the author's refutation of the objection that animals only respond to particular percepts.

Core Idea
Animals seek the universal type, not just a particular individual.

An opponent objects that animals only react to particular sensible objects, not universal concepts. The author refutes this by appealing to observable behavior: hungry animals seek the type 'herb' even without seeing a specific herb, and fear the type 'bone' even without seeing a lion. This lesson examines the structure of the objection and response.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector claims: 'The animal has no awareness of specific natures (universals); rather, when it sees a specific herb it wants it, and when it sees a specific bone it dislikes it.'
The Arabic: 'فإن قيل: الحيوان لا شعور له بالماهيات النوعية، بل إذا شاهد عشبا معينا أراده، وإذا شاهد عظما معينا كرهه.'
2
Identify the author's response
The author says this contradicts the senses: when hungry, the animal seeks 'herb' even without seeing any herb; when it sees a bone, it fears even if it hasn't seen a lion. Thus, herb qua herb is desired, bone qua bone is disliked.
The response: 'قلنا: هذا يخلاف الحس، فإنها إذا جاعت تطلب العشب وإن لم تشاهد عينا...'
3
Analyze the refutation's logic
The author uses empirical counterexamples: an animal's generic seeking/avoidance shows it recognizes the universal type, not just the particular instance. This is a reductio ad absurdum of the objection.
The argument is: If the objector were right, an animal could only desire a particular herb it sees, but we observe it desiring herb in general. Hence the objector is wrong.
4
Assess the debate
Who wins? The author provides convincing observational evidence. The objector's position fails because it cannot explain why a hungry animal actively searches for herb without a present percept.
