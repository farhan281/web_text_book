Analyzing a Dialectical Refutation: The Case of Aging and Death
Author responds to an objection about the cause of death, arguing that the dissolutive powers increase in old age.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a dialectical refutation in a natural philosophical argument and distinguish between the objection and the author's response.

Core Idea
Death is not due to increased dissolution but to the prolonged effect of causes and depletion of natural moisture.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi responds to an objection about why the body inevitably dies. The objector claims that if the nutritive power balanced dissolution in youth, it should do so in old age, preventing death. Al-Razi refutes this by showing that the dissolutive factors, though similar, act over a longer duration, and a cascade of causes leads to the extinction of natural heat. This lesson will guide you through unpacking the dialectical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (mu'tarid)
Find the objector's claim in the Arabic: 'The dissolution in youth was equal to what was supplied, so why does it increase in old age?' This posits that the supply should match dissolution to avoid death.
The objector assumes equality of supply and dissolution is the norm.
2
Find the author's initial reply
Al-Razi counters by pointing out that dissolutive factors (heat, movement) may actually be less in old age, so the imbalance is not due to increased dissolution.
This is a direct refutation of the assumption.
3
Trace the causal cascade
The author explains a series: decreased natural moisture -> decreased natural heat -> weakened nutritive power -> accumulation of unnatural moistures -> further decrease in heat -> eventual extinction.
This avoids circular reasoning by pinpointing the primary cause.
4
Evaluate the dialectical outcome
Determine whose position is upheld. The objector's position is refuted; the author's explanation using duration and moisture depletion is shown to be consistent.
The author also uses an analogy of a lamp to illustrate the process.

The Soul's Consciousness of Vegetative Acts: A Conditional Argument
Author argues against soul's role in vegetative acts via consciousness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional argument (modus tollens) that denies vegetative actions to the soul, and evaluate the objection and its refutation.

Core Idea
Consciousness is the litmus test for the soul's agency over bodily acts.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi deploys a conditional argument to show that vegetative actions like growth and digestion cannot be directly caused by the soul, because we are not conscious of them. He then anticipates and refutes a subtle objection—that the soul might have unconscious awareness of these acts. This lesson walks you through the logical moves and the dialectical exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main argument structure
Recognize that the author uses a conditional (if-then) statement to link the soul's agency with consciousness. The overall structure is modus tollens: deny the consequent, conclude the antecedent is false.
2
Extract the antecedent and consequent
Antecedent: 'the soul performs vegetative acts' (these were listed as nutrition and growth). Consequent: 'the soul is conscious of them'. Write these out clearly.
The acts are internal processes like digestion and assimilation.
3
Check the denial of the consequent
The author asserts that we are not conscious of these vegetative acts—they occur without our awareness. This denies the consequent. Therefore, via modus tollens, the antecedent is false: the soul is not the agent.
This is a valid inference: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P.
4
Identify the objection
The objector suggests that the soul might have consciousness of these acts but lacks consciousness of that consciousness—unconscious awareness.
This attempts to preserve the antecedent by claiming consciousness exists but is not felt.
5
Analyze the refutation
The author rejects the objection using a reductio ad absurdum: If unconscious consciousness were allowed, one could absurdly claim to know all things without any awareness of that knowledge. This shows the objection leads to an unacceptable conclusion.
The parallel is from epistemology: knowledge implies awareness; separating them is incoherent.

The ninth proof states that corporeal powers tire with repetition, whereas the rational power strengthens. Each proof is a self-contained syllogism or argument. The overall conclusion is that the rational soul is immaterial and separate from the body. All examples used are from classical psychology and philosophy, not from fiqh or kalam, but they are within the Islamic intellectual tradition (e.g., perception of the heart/brain, infinite numbers).

Author presents a proof that the soul cannot be material because it knows opposites simultaneously.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional syllogism (modus tollens) that proves the soul cannot be material because it knows opposites simultaneously.

Core Idea
The soul's ability to hold contrary forms simultaneously proves it is not a body.

In this passage, the author presents a concise yet powerful argument: if the locus of knowledge were material, it could not hold two opposites at once. Yet we experience knowing black and white together. Therefore the soul is not material. This lesson unpacks the conditional syllogism behind the proof, a standard tool in Islamic philosophical theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional premise
Find the 'if-then' statement in the Arabic. The author says: if the locus of knowledge were a body (hypothesis), then it would be possible for the same person to know and not know the same thing from different aspects.
The conditional is implied: 'If it were a body, then it would not be impossible for it to have knowledge and ignorance regarding the same thing from different sides.'
2
Find the exception (istithna')
The exception is the denial of the consequent: but we find that a person knows black and white together, i.e., he knows both opposites simultaneously, which is not possible if the locus were material.
The consequent is negated: it is false that the same person can know and not know the same thing in the same respect; in fact, he knows both opposites.
3
Confirm the form is modus tollens
Modus tollens: If P then Q, not Q, therefore not P. Here P = 'the locus is material', Q = 'it is possible to have knowledge and ignorance from different aspects'. We deny Q (we know opposites simultaneously, which is impossible for a material substrate), so we conclude not P (the soul is not material).
This is a valid inference. The author uses the impossibility of contrary forms in a single material substrate as the key premise.
4
Verify the metaphysical principle
The argument relies on the principle that a material body cannot simultaneously possess two opposite qualities (like black and white) in the same part. Knowledge is the presence of the form of the known; black and white are contrary forms. Therefore, if the soul were material, it could not know both at once.
This principle is accepted in Peripatetic natural philosophy and carried into Islamic philosophy.

Dialectical Objection: Mental Images and the Immaterial Soul
Author presents an objection against the materiality of the rational soul based on mental images.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical objection against the materiality of the rational soul and evaluate its logical force.

Core Idea
Mental images cannot be divided — this forces the rational soul to be immaterial.

This passage presents a key dialectical argument in Islamic psychology: the objection that mental images cannot inhere in a material substance. Understanding this argument helps us grasp why many Muslim philosophers and theologians argued for the immateriality of the rational soul. Let's walk through the logical steps of the objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being attacked
The objector is attacking the position that the rational soul is material (jismānī). Find the conclusion the objector wants to establish: that the soul is a non-material substance.
2
Understand the problem with material inherence
If a mental image (e.g., a square) were in a material substance with parts, the image would be divided among the parts, becoming multiple, but the image is one. This is a contradiction.
The passage mentions 'measures' (maqādīr) that would exist in multiple loci.
3
Consider the alternative: a simple substance
If the locus is a simple non-material substance (jawhar mujarrad), then the image inheres as one. So the soul must be simple.
This is the intermediate conclusion: the soul is not material.
4
Recognize the further dilemma implied
The passage hints that even a simple substance cannot hold two distinct images simultaneously without contradiction (coexistence of two like things). This deepens the debate and invites further response.
This is a secondary objection within the same dialectical context.

Commentary

But the image is a unified mental content. If, on the other hand, the locus is a simple non-material substance (jawhar mujarrad), then the image can inhere as a single form. However, this leads to another problem: if the same simple substance contains two distinct images simultaneously, that would require two forms in one subject, which is impossible. The passage is dense and uses the example of a square to illustrate the dilemma. The underlying issue is the nature of the rational soul and its independence from the body.

Refuting the Necessary Self-Knowledge Argument
Author refutes the first objection concerning necessary self-knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between knowledge of existence (anniyya) and knowledge of essence (mahiyya) and use this distinction to refute the claim that necessary self-knowledge proves the soul is material.

Core Idea
Knowing 'that' I exist is not knowing 'what' I am.

The deniers argue that every person necessarily knows their own specific self, concluding that the self must be a bodily entity. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi refutes this by showing that necessary self-knowledge is only knowledge of one's existence and distinctness, not of one's quiddity. Thus, the argument fails to establish the soull's materiality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the deniers' claim
Find the claim: 'everyone necessarily knows his own specific self' (يعلم ذاته المخصوصة). This is presented as their strongest objection.
2
Separate existence from essence
Razi distinguishes: we know our identity (huwiyya) as existent and distinct, but not the quiddity (mahiyya) of that identity. So the knowledge is of existence only.
3
Explain why the conclusion does not follow
The deniers infer materiality from direct knowledge, but direct knowledge of existence does not reveal the substance's nature. The argument is therefore inconclusive.
4
Apply the distinction to a parallel case
In kalam debates on the soul, if someone claims 'I know my self, therefore it is an accident', use the same distinction: you know it only as existent, not as accident nor substance.

Refuting the Intellectual Pain Argument
Author refutes the argument that the soul suffers intellectual pain after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for intellectual pain and evaluate the deniers' argument that the soul suffers after death due to unfulfilled longing for knowledge.

Core Idea
Intellectual pain after death is not certain: ignorance of loss or transformation of beliefs removes the basis for pain.

The deniers claim that the soul, after separation from the body, suffers intense intellectual pain because it longs for knowledge but cannot attain it. They argue by analogy to physical pain, claiming intellectual pain is stronger. Fakhr al-Din al-Razi responds by questioning whether the soul knows it has not attained knowledge after death, and whether its beliefs persist. Since these are uncertain, the pain is not proven.

Guided Path
1
Identify the deniers' premises
They argue: (1) The soul longs for knowledge. (2) It does not attain it. (3) It is not occupied with other things. (4) Therefore it feels pain, which is the greatest misery.
2
Recognize the analogical argument
They compare intellectual and physical pleasures to argue that intellectual pain is stronger.
3
Find the author's first objection
Pain only occurs if one knows they have not attained the longed-for thing. If the soul believes it has attained, there is no pain.
4
Find the author's second objection
After death, beliefs may persist (so no loss) or transform (ignorance becomes knowledge). In either case, pain is not certain.
5
Assess the conclusion
Since the conditions for pain are uncertain, the deniers cannot be certain of the soul's misery after death.

The Conditional Syllogism Against Bodily Resurrection
Author refutes the conditional objection against bodily resurrection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct the conditional syllogism used to refute the objection that bodily resurrection requires re-creation of the non-existent.

Core Idea
The objection is refuted by denying the conditional premise: if resurrection requires i'ādat al-ma'dūm, then it's impossible; but the consequent is false, so the antecedent is false.

The author presents the philosophers' objection in the form of a conditional: if bodily resurrection involves restoring a non-existent thing, it is impossible. He then shows that this conditional is false because the consequent is contradicted by revelation, thus the antecedent (that resurrection requires i'ādat al-ma'dūm) is also false. This is a classic modus tollens argument.

Guided Path
1
Locate the conditional statement
Find where the author states 'If it is the re-creation of the non-existent, then it has already been discussed' and later 'the falsity of the consequent is based on the falsity of the antecedent.' This sets up the conditional.
2
Identify the antecedent and consequent
The antecedent is 'bodily resurrection is a re-creation of the non-existent (i'ādat al-ma'dūm).' The consequent is 'it is impossible.'
3
Recognize the denial of the consequent
The author implies that the consequent is false because revelation affirms bodily resurrection as possible. Therefore, the conditional statement is rejected, and the antecedent must be false.
This is modus tollens: if P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
4
Conclude the refutation
Thus, the objection fails: bodily resurrection does not require re-creation of the non-existent. The author proceeds to explain why (the quality that individuates perishes, so resurrection is a new creation, not a re-creation).

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of objections against bodily resurrection (al-ma'ād al-jismānī). The author begins by justifying why the Prophet (peace be upon him) addressed bodily resurrection to the common people: because spiritual resurrection is beyond their conception, so bodily terms serve as an example. Then the author addresses the main objection: if bodily resurrection requires re-creation of a non-existent thing (i'ādat al-ma'dūm), it is impossible; but if it is a new creation after dispersion, it is false for ten reasons. He proceeds to explain the first reason: the individual's identity is tied to a specific quality that distinguishes him from others. This quality is a property of the body and ceases when the body disperses. If God re-creates the body, He must also re-create that quality, which is part of the essence; thus it becomes re-creation of the non-existent. The author then refutes a counterargument that the parts persist and recombination suffices. He uses the example of a sheep burnt to ashes: the ash is not the same sheep because the parts have separated and joined their respective elements. Similarly, if one person eats another, the parts become part of the eater's body, so re-creating the original person would require re-creating those parts as belonging to both, which is impossible. The argument uses a conditional syllogism: if bodily resurrection were possible, it would require re-creating the non-existent; but that is impossible (as previously argued), so the consequent is false, therefore the antecedent (the objection's premise) is false. The author is defending the Islamic doctrine of bodily resurrection against rationalist critiques, emphasizing that God's power is not limited by such objections.

The Identity Argument: Why Resurrection Cannot Be Mere Reassembly
Author argues that personal identity requires a quality that perishes, so resurrection implies re-creation of non-existent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the argument that bodily resurrection implies re-creation of the non-existent because the quality that individuates a person ceases at death.

Core Idea
Your identity is not in your particles but in a quality that perishes, so resurrection is a new creation of that quality.

The author provides a detailed philosophical argument for why resurrection cannot be mere reassembly of the same particles. He distinguishes between the shared corporeality and the individualizing quality (al-mumayyiz). When the body disintegrates, that quality is lost, so any re-creation must bring back that quality, which is a re-creation of the non-existent. This supports the conditional syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish shared and distinguishing aspects
The author says a specific human shares corporeality (jismiyya) with others but is distinguished by individuality (shakhṣiyya). These are different.
2
Identify the individuating quality
Individuality is an added quality (ṣifa) that subsists in the body. It is what makes you you.
3
Understand that this quality ceases with dispersion
When the body's parts scatter, that quality necessarily ceases to exist. It becomes non-existent (ma'dūm).
4
Conclude the need for re-creation of the non-existent
If God wants to resurrect the same person, He must re-create that quality, which is part of the essence of that person. Thus, resurrection would be a re-creation of the non-existent (i'ādat al-ma'dūm).

Al-Razi's Dialectical Defense of Bodily Resurrection
Author refutes objections to bodily resurrection using logical arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's logical responses to the main objections against bodily resurrection and evaluate their strength using debate map analysis.

Core Idea
Bodily resurrection is rationally possible because only a few original parts need re-creation by an all-powerful God.

In this passage from the Mulakhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defends the Islamic doctrine of bodily resurrection against philosophical objections. He addresses concerns about consumed body parts, infinite regress, and the nature of the afterlife. The debate reveals how kalām uses logical reasoning to uphold revealed truths. We will dissect the objections and responses to see how al-Razi employs dialectical logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the consumption objection
Find the objection that if a body part is consumed by another human, resurrection would require it to be in two bodies simultaneously. Al-Razi counters by distinguishing original parts (al-ajza' al-asliyya) from acquired parts.
Original parts are few and remain intact at death.
2
Extract the response of original parts
Al-Razi states that the original parts that persist from birth to death are few and do not disperse, making re-creation of the same body possible for a free agent (God).
3
Analyze the infinite cycles objection
The objector argues that if past cycles are infinite, resurrecting all bodies would lead to infinite coexistent bodies, which is absurd. Al-Razi denies the premise of infinite past cycles.
This is a key metaphysical claim.
4
Examine the metempsychosis charge
If resurrection occurs in this world, it is not resurrection but metempsychosis (tanasukh). Al-Razi responds that another world is possible, avoiding the charge.
He asserts the existence of another world as a logical possibility.

In this passage, the author presents a multi-layered argument for the existence of animal souls, grounding it in the necessary knowledge of identity and the unity of perception. Commentary
This passage is a proof by elimination. The author, likely following the Islamic philosophers, aims to establish that the first emanation from God must be the intellect. The argument proceeds by ruling out all other possible candidates: accident, substance, body, matter, form, and soul. First, the meaning of 'intellect' is defined as a non-material, self-subsistent entity independent in its agency. Then the philosophers provide seven arguments (only some are detailed). The first argument shows that the first emanation cannot be an accident because that would lead to a circular dependency with substance. It cannot be a body because a body is composite (matter and form), and a composite cannot proceed directly from a single, simple cause without mediation. It cannot be matter because matter is merely receptive, but the first emanation is an agent; a single thing cannot be both agent and patient. It cannot be form because form's influence either depends on matter (making matter prior) or does not (making form independent of matter, which contradicts being a form). It cannot be soul because the first emanation would then be immanent in all bodies, which is a property of soul, but the first emanation is the cause of all bodies, so it must be independent of them. After eliminating all these, only the intellect remains, supported by the prophetic hadith. The logical method here is a disjunctive syllogism: either the first emanation is A, B, C, D, or E; all except the last are impossible; therefore it is the last.

The Four Proofs and the Contingency Syllogism
Lists four methods to prove the Necessary Being with syllogistic support.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the categorical syllogism used to prove that every body is contingent and identify the major and minor premises in the author's argument.

Core Idea
Four methods, one conclusion: contingency requires a Necessary Being.

The author presents four ways to prove the existence of the Necessary Being, all based on contingency (imkan) and origination (huduth). But before reaching the final proof, he must establish that the world is possible. He provides multiple syllogisms to prove the minor premise. This lesson will walk you through one of those syllogisms—the composition argument from hyle and form—so you can see how categorical syllogisms are used in kalam cosmology.

Guided Path
1
Locate the conclusion of the composition argument
In the passage, after the phrase 'كل جسم مركب من الهيولى والصورة', the author concludes 'فالجسم ممكن لذاته وأجزانه'. This is the conclusion that bodies are contingent in themselves.
Contingency here means possible existence.
2
Identify the premises that lead to this conclusion
The argument uses two premises: (1) Every body is composed of hyle and form, (2) Everything composed of hyle and form is in need of both its parts. Additionally, an implicit premise: Everything that is needy for its parts is possible. The author states: 'وكلما كان كذلك فهو ممكن'.
This is a chain of two categorical syllogisms.
3
Formalize the first categorical syllogism
Major Premise: Everything composed of hyle and form is needy (for its parts). Minor Premise: Every body is composed of hyle and form. Conclusion: Every body is needy.
This is a Barbara syllogism (first figure, AAA).
4

The Fourth Way: Existence Additional to Quiddity
Razi's fourth way: existence additional to quiddity proves contingency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct Razi's fourth proof that a body is possible if its existence is additional to its quiddity, and apply it to show the contingency of the world.

Core Idea
If existence is not part of a thing's essence, it is contingent.

Razi's fourth way to prove the contingency of bodies draws on the distinction between existence and quiddity. He states: every body whose existence is additional to its quiddity is possible. In this lesson, we will unpack this syllogism and see how it grounds the need for a creator.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major premise
The passage states: 'Every body, if its existence is additional to its quiddity, then it is possible in itself.' This is a universal conditional that serves as the overall rule.

2

Razi assumes that bodies have existence additional to their quiddity. This is not argued here but taken as evident from the nature of bodies as composites (to be proven elsewhere).
The minor is: 'Every body is such that its existence is additional to its quiddity.'
3

From the two premises, the conclusion follows: 'Therefore, every body is possible in itself.' The passage explicitly gives this conclusion.
This is a valid categorical syllogism in the first figure (Barbara).

The conclusion that bodies are possible then combines with the earlier premise 'every possible has an efficient cause' to prove that bodies have a creator.
This is a compound syllogism: bodies are possible → they need a cause → that cause is the necessary being.

Commentary
This passage is a series of syllogistic proofs for the contingency of bodies and the existence of a necessary creator. It begins by stating that there are three chapters, and this is the first on establishing the director of the world. The author then lists four ways to prove that every body is contingent (possible). The first way: everything possible needs an efficient cause, and bodies are possible, so they need a cause. The second way: every body originated in time has an originator. The third way: every divisible body has parts, so it depends on its parts and is therefore contingent. The fourth way: if a body's existence is additional to its quiddity, it is possible; and many bodies share a quiddity, so they are not necessary. The author then presents a detailed argument from composition: every body is composed of matter and form, each needing the other, so the composite is possible. Furthermore, every body either belongs to a species under a genus, in which case its individuals depend on separate causes, or it is itself a composite of genus and differentia, which also implies contingency. Thus every body is possible and depends on another. The text then introduces the argument from the indifference of the possible: a possible being does not exist or not exist by itself, so it needs a determining cause. This is applied to bodies, leading to the conclusion that the world has a creator who is not a body nor a spirit. Next is the argument from the possibility of attributes: bodies share corporeality but differ in attributes, so their specific attributes must come from an external cause; otherwise, there would be an infinite regress or circularity. This proves that bodies need a free agent for their attributes. Finally, the argument from temporal origination is presented: every body is temporally originated, and everything temporally originated has an originator. The minor premise (bodies are originated) is deferred, and the major premise (every originated is possible) is noted. This multi-faceted approach uses categorical syllogisms to demonstrate the contingency of the physical world and the necessity of a transcendent cause. All examples used are from the Islamic sciences: e.g., the argument that a composite of matter and form is contingent mirrors the Kalam argument that the world, being composed, is originated and requires a creator.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The contingency argument is a categorical syllogism that ends with a Creator who is neither body nor accident.

In this passage, al-Razi presents two proofs from possibility (imkan). The first is a crisp categorical syllogism: every body is contingent, every contingent requires a maker, therefore every body has a maker. This lesson walks you through extracting and validating that syllogism, which is the backbone of many kalām arguments for God's existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion (natija)
Find the sentence that states what the argument is trying to prove. It often begins with 'فثبت' or 'فكل جسم'.

2
Extract the premises (muqaddimat)
Look for two statements that, taken together, yield the conclusion. One is about bodies being contingent, the other about contingents requiring a cause.
Premises: 'Every body is contingent' (كل جسم ممكن) and 'Every contingent requires a cause' (كل ممكن له علة).
3
Identify the middle term (hadd al-awsat)
The term that appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. Here it is 'contingent' (ممكن).
Middle term: 'contingent' (ممكن).
4
Determine the figure and mood
Check the position of the middle term: is it subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor? This is the first figure (al-shakl al-awwal).
Figure: 1st (subject of major, predicate of minor). Mood: Barbara (AAA).
5
Verify validity
First figure AAA is always valid. Check that the terms are univocal and the premises are true according to kalām metaphysics.
The syllogism is valid; the only potential issue is the truth of the premises, which the author assumes from earlier discussions.

The Huduth Demonstration for a Creator
Author presents arguments from huduth for a creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the key categorical syllogism in the kalām proof from huduth and explain why the ultimate cause must be non-corporeal and voluntary.

Core Idea
The world's temporality proves its need for an eternal, non-corporeal creator.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas presents the classic kalām proof for God's existence from the temporal origination (huduth) of bodies and their attributes. The argument proceeds in two stages: first, bodies are shown to be originated; second, their attributes cannot be self-produced, leading to a voluntary external cause. Understanding the logical structure of these arguments is essential for engaging with Islamic theology at a rigorous level.

Guided Path
1
Extract the categorical syllogism for bodies
From the Arabic, identify the two premises: 'every body is originated' and 'every originated has an originator'. This forms a categorical syllogism with the conclusion 'every body has an originator'.
The middle term is 'originated' (muhdath), connecting the minor (body) and major (has an originator).
2
Verify the syllogism's validity
This is a first-figure syllogism, Barbara mood (AAA-1): all A are B, all B are C, so all A are C. It is valid by definition. Ensure that the premises are justified: the author argues the minor premise (bodies are originated) via their contingency.
3
Analyze the argument from attributes
The passage next asks: who produces the temporal attributes inhering in bodies? The body itself cannot, because it lacks awareness and would produce uniform effects. This is a conditional argument: if the body were the cause, then it must have certain properties; it does not, so it is not the cause.
The author uses examples like the sperm-drop to show the body's incapacity at its weakest state.
4
Follow the reasoning to an external voluntary cause
Since the body cannot cause its own attributes, the cause must be external. That external cause cannot be another body (to avoid regress). Therefore it must be a non-corporeal voluntary agent (a director).
5
Combine the arguments to reach the desired conclusion
Both arguments converge: bodies need a director, and the director cannot be a body. The director is therefore necessary of existence by itself. If the world itself were necessary, that would be God; if contingent, it requires a chain ending in a necessary being.
This final step relies on the impossibility of infinite regress (not detailed here).

The minor premise (bodies are originated) is postponed. The major premise is sometimes formulated as: every originated is contingent and every contingent has a cause. The minor is justified: an originated thing is receptive to existence and non-existence; since it is not eternal, it is non-existent by itself, so it needs a cause. The second argument, on attributes, argues that the temporal attributes inhering in bodies—like qualities and accidents—cannot be produced by the bodies themselves because the body lacks awareness and power; moreover, if the body were the cause, all bodies would produce the same effect. The cause must be external. It cannot be another body, as that would regress. The ultimate cause must be a non-corporeal, voluntary director. If the universe is necessary by itself, that is the required conclusion; if contingent, it needs a cause, and so on, leading to an infinite regress or a necessary being.

The Impossibility of Multiple Necessary Beings: A Reductio Proof
Author proves that there cannot be multiple necessary beings due to composition and dependence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's reductio argument against multiple necessary beings and identify the two key dilemmas (entailment and composition).

Core Idea
Multiple necessary beings would require composition and dependence, contradicting necessity itself.

This passage from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's Mulakhkhas presents a rigorous logical proof for tawhid, the oneness of God. Al-Razi shows that the very assumption of two or more necessary beings leads to absurdities, proving that the Necessary Being must be one. We will dissect his argument step by step, understanding how he uses reductio ad absurdum (khulf) to refute the possibility of plurality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial assumption
The argument assumes multiple necessary beings. They share the attribute of necessity (wujub) but differ in their essences (huwiyyat). Note that what is shared is different from what distinguishes them.
This sets up the problem: each being has both a shared aspect and a unique aspect.
2
Analyze the relationship between shared and distinguishing attributes
Al-Razi asks: what is the relationship between the shared necessity and the distinguishing determination? Are they mutually entailing (talazum) or not? This leads to the first dilemma.
The Arabic terms ishtirak (sharing) and imtiyaz (distinction) are key.
3
Follow the first dilemma: entailment or non-entailment
If the two attributes are not entailed, then conjoining them requires an external cause, making each being dependent and thus contingent—contradicting necessity. If they are entailed, then either determination causes necessity (making necessity contingent) or necessity causes determination (making all necessary beings identical). Both are absurd.
This is a classic reductio: assuming plurality leads to contradiction.
4
Follow the second argument: composition and infinite regress
If there is no entailment, each necessary being is composed of two parts: shared necessity and individual determination. This composition makes each being dependent on its parts, contradicting necessity. Moreover, this leads to an infinite regress of parts, which is impossible.
Al-Razi uses the principle that a necessary being must be simple (basit) and non-composite.
5
Consider the escape: negative attribute
Al-Razi considers the possibility that necessity is a negative attribute (like 'not needing a cause'). If so, sharing it does not introduce composition because negative attributes do not add real parts. However, even this does not rescue the claim of plurality; the overall conclusion remains that multiple necessary beings are impossible.
This shows the depth of al-Razi's analysis—anticipating and undermining a potential objection.

Author presents a dialectical exchange on the oneness of the Necessary Being

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Multiple eternal attributes entail either multiple necessary beings or a possible Necessary Being—both impossible.

This passage presents a key theological proof in Islamic philosophy and kalam: that the Necessary Being (wajib al-wujud) must be absolutely one, and that any attribution of multiple eternal attributes (sifat) leads to contradiction. Al-Razi constructs a conditional argument: if the attributes were necessary by themselves, there would be multiple necessary beings; if they were possible, the One would be both agent and receiver, which is impossible. An objector challenges the proof, and al-Razi responds using analogies from their own philosophical system. Understanding this dialectical exchange helps you see how rigorous logical reasoning is applied to defend the core Islamic creed of tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Read the opening part of the passage where al-Razi states that the Necessary Being cannot have multiple eternal attributes. Locate the conditional structure: 'if attributes are necessary by themselves, then... ; if they are possible, then...'.
The main claim is that multiple eternal attributes are impossible.
2
Unpack the conditional argument
Break down the two horns of the conditional. Horn 1: Attributes are necessary → multiple necessary beings (contradicts the oneness of wajib al-wujud). Horn 2: Attributes are possible → they are effects of the essence, making the One both receptive and agent (impossible).
This is a classic reductio ad absurdum (burhan al-tanafi).
3
Analyze the objector's challenge
Find the objector's question: 'Why prevent the attributes from being necessary because of the statement that the Necessary Being is not more than one?' The objector seems to argue that the premise that attributes are in need of the described is problematic.
The objector is using the author's own principles against him.
4
Follow the author's response
Al-Razi responds by drawing an analogy with form and matter in their own philosophy: form is an attribute of matter but is not in need of matter; rather, matter needs form. He then extends the argument to knowledge and relations, showing that if attributes exist as separate entities, they would make God a locus of accidents.
The response uses internal consistency of the opponent's system.
5
Evaluate the dialectical outcome
Determine who has the stronger position based on the exchange. Al-Razi shows that either horn of the conditional leads to absurdity, and the objector's alternative fails because it contradicts the nature of the Necessary Being.
In jadal, the burden of proof is on the objector to provide a coherent alternative.

Distinction of Knower, Knowledge, and Relation as Refutation

Critique of the Philosophers' Emanation Theory
Razi critiques the philosophers' emanation theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Razi's critique of the philosophers' claim that God cannot be a willer in the sense of seeking perfection, and evaluate his refutation.

Core Idea
God's will is not contingent; He wills what He knows will exist, and seeking perfection is impossible for Him.

Razi takes on the Greek philosophers' view that the Necessary Being cannot have intention or will in bringing about the world, because that would imply seeking perfection. He systematically refutes this by analyzing the concepts of seeking perfection, benefit, and preponderance. This lesson dissects his critique and the defensive responses.

1
Identify the philosophers' claim

Critique of Emanation: Possibility as Cause
Author critiques the causality of possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical critique against the claim that possibility (imkān) can be a cause of existent things.

Core Idea
Possibility, being non-existent, cannot cause existence — a logical blow to the emanation scheme.

The philosophers argued that the first intellect has two aspects: possibility and existence. From possibility the sphere emanates; from existence the second intellect. The author targets this causal claim head-on. By examining the ontological status of possibility, you will learn how to use logical reasoning to dismantle a metaphysical theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophers' causal claim
Find the sentence: 'أن إمكانه علة للفلك' — that its possibility is the cause of the sphere. This is the claim under scrutiny.
2
Determine the ontological status of possibility
The author asserts that 'الإمكان أمر عدمي' — possibility is a non-existent thing. Ask: why does the author consider it non-existent? Because possibility is a mere potential, not actual.
3
Apply the principle of causality
A cause must be something existent. The author implies: non-existent things cannot cause existent things. This is the major premise.
This principle is accepted by both parties in medieval Islamic debate.
4
Formulate the syllogism
Combine the premises: (1) No non-existent thing is a cause of an existent thing. (2) Possibility is non-existent. Therefore, possibility is not a cause of the sphere.
This is a valid categorical syllogism (Cesare).

Core Idea

The Neoplatonic axiom that from a single simple cause only one effect can proceed has been a cornerstone of emanationist metaphysics. Core Idea
The first intellect cannot be simple because it is composed of genus and differentia; and the number of intellects is not fixed by spheres.

Author presents the kalām cosmological argument for huduth al-ajsam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major and minor premises in the kalām argument for the origination of bodies and verify its syllogistic validity.

Core Idea
The kalām proof for huduth al-ajsam is a categorical syllogism relying on the inseparability of accidents.

This passage presents the famous proof that bodies are temporally originated using a categorical syllogism. The argument is not merely a simple syllogism; it includes a rigorous defense of both premises. In this lesson, we will parse the syllogistic structure and understand how the author defends each premise against objections.

Guided Path
1
Locate the conclusion (natija)
Find the final sentence: '...so that which is never free from temporal things must have a beginning, and that is what is sought.' The conclusion is that every body has a temporal beginning (hadith).
The conclusion is explicitly stated at the end.
2
Extract the major premise (al-kubra)
The general rule stated at the beginning: 'Everything that is never free from a temporal thing is itself temporal.' This is the major premise of the syllogism.
It is presented as a universal conditional.
3
Extract the minor premise (al-sughra)
The specific claim: 'Every body is never free from temporal things.' The author specifies that bodies are never free from the accidents of composition and separation, which are temporal.
The minor premise is defended with examples of accidents.
4

The author argues that composition and separation are necessary for bodies (they are inseparable) and that they are temporal because they necessitate knowledge, which cannot be an attribute of the eternal.
This step shows that the minor premise is not taken as axiomatic but is proven.
5
Analyze the defense of the major premise
The author uses modal reasoning to show that composition cannot be necessary or eternal. If it were necessary, it would lead to absurdities; thus it must be temporal, establishing the major premise.
The defense involves a reductio ad absurdum and modal distinctions.

الحجة المشهورة على حدوث الأجسام: إن كل جسم لا يخلو عن الحوادث، وكل ما لا يخلو عن الحادث فهو حادث. بيان الصغرى: أن الأجسام لا تخلو عن الاجتماع والافتراق، وهما حادثان. أما الأول فضروري أن كل جسمين فلا بد أن يكونا متماسين أو غير متماسين. وأما الثاني فلات الاجتماع والافتراق يحتم عليهما العلم، وكل ما يتق عليه العلم يستحيل أن يكون قديما. بيان الأكبر: إن اجتماع الجسمين لو كان واجبا لكان ذلك الوجوب إما لذات الجسمين أو لحال فيه أو لمحل أو لما ليس حالا فيه ولا حاله، ويبطل هذه الأقسام بما ذكرنا في إثبات الصانع، فيبقى أن ذلك ليس بواجب. ثم إن القديم لا يصح عليه العدم، فإن كان واجبا لذاته استحال عدمه، وإن كان ممكنا لذاته فالمؤثر فيه إما موجب أو مختار. فإن كان موجبا فإما لا يتوقف تأثيره على شرط آخر أو يتوقف لكن ذلك الشرط إن كان قديما لزم أن لا عدم إلا لعدم تلك العلة أو ذلك الشرط القدمين، وحينئذ يكون الكلام في عدمها كالكلام في الأول. وأما إن توقف تأثيره على شرط حادث فإما أن يتوقف على حادث معين وهو محال، أو على حوادث لا بداية لها وهو محال على ما سيأتي. وإن كان مختارا وكل ما هو فعل فاعل مختار فهو محدث. ثبت أن كل ما صح عليه العدم محدث. ثبت أن كل جسم فإنه لا يخلو عن الحوادث وقد[؟] استحالة حوادث لا أول لها، فما لا يخلو عن الحوادث أول يجب أن يكون له أول، وهو المطلوب.
The famous argument for the temporal origination of bodies: Every body is never free from temporal things, and everything that is never free from a temporal thing is itself temporal. Explanation of the minor premise: Bodies are never free from composition and separation, and they are temporal. As for the first, it is necessary that every two bodies must either be contiguous or not contiguous. As for the second, because composition and separation necessitate knowledge, and everything that necessitates knowledge is impossible to be eternal. Explanation of the major premise: If the composition of two bodies were necessary, that necessity would be either due to the essence of the two bodies, or a state in them, or a locus, or something that is neither a state nor a locus; and these divisions are invalidated by what we mentioned in proving the Creator, so it remains that it is not necessary. Then the eternal cannot cease to exist; if it were necessary by itself its non-existence would be impossible, and if it were possible by itself then the effective cause is either necessitating or free. If it is necessitating, then either its effect does not depend on another condition or it does, but if that condition were eternal, then non-existence would only occur due to the non-existence of that cause or that eternal condition, and then the discussion about their non-existence is like the first. And if its effect depends on a temporal condition, then either it depends on a specific temporal thing, which is impossible, or on an infinite series of temporal things, which is impossible as will come. And if it is free, and everything that is an act of a free agent is temporally originated, then it is established that everything that can cease to exist is temporally originated. It is established that every body is never free from temporal things, and it has been established the impossibility of an infinite series of temporal things, so that which is never free from temporal things must have a beginning, and that is what is sought.

Commentary
This passage presents the famous kalām argument for the temporal origination of bodies, structured as a categorical syllogism. The author first states the major premise: whatever is never free from temporal things is itself temporal. Then the minor premise: bodies are never free from the accidents of composition and separation, which are temporal. The defense of the minor premise shows that composition and separation are necessary for bodies and are known to be temporal. The defense of the major premise argues that if composition were necessary, it would lead to absurdities, and then a detailed modal analysis shows that the only coherent possibility is that bodies are temporally originated. The argument concludes that bodies must have a beginning. Students should parse the syllogistic structure and the modal reasoning used to refute the possibility of an eternal cause.

Two Positions on the Problem of Evil
Author concludes the dialectical discussion on evil with the two positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the necessitarian (al-mujib) and free-agent (al-mukhtar) models of divine action in the problem of evil.

Core Idea
God's free choice, not mechanical necessity, resolves the problem of evil.

This short conclusion from Razi's Mulakhkhas presents the two fundamental positions on the problem of evil: that God is a necessary cause (al-mujib) compelled to create, or that He is a free agent (al-mukhtar) who acts by choice. The author decisively rejects the first and affirms the second, while carefully avoiding the extremes of compulsion (jabr) and delegation (tafwid). This lesson guides you through the dialectical landscape of this classical Kalam debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Locate the two contrasting positions: al-mujib (necessitation) which 'cuts off this research', and al-mukhtar (free choice) with denial of jabr and tafwid, which is affirmed as 'the manifest truth'.
These positions represent opposite views on God's relationship to creation.
2
Compare their implications for evil
Consider: Under al-mujib, evil would be necessary and unavoidable, eliminating moral accountability. Under al-mukhtar, evil is chosen for a wise purpose, preserving divine justice and human responsibility.
The author implies that only the second view allows meaningful discourse on evil.
3
Analyse the author's rejection of jabr and tafwid
Note the two extremes rejected: jabr (compulsion) makes God directly cause human actions, and tafwid (delegation) removes God from creation entirely. The author rejects both to maintain a middle path.
This is a classic Ash'arite position.
4
Evaluate the logical force of the conclusion
Assess whether the author provides a proof or simply states a preference. The passage does not argue but declares the truth, typical of a dialectical conclusion after earlier arguments.
The missing syllogistic reasoning may have been given in earlier parts of the text.

Commentary
This passage concludes a dialectical discussion on the problem of evil. The author summarizes the two possible positions: (1) the philosophers' view that God is a necessary cause (al-mujib) who is compelled by His essence to create, which cuts off further inquiry; (2) the correct view that God is a free agent (al-mukhtar) who chooses to create, while avoiding both compulsion (jabr) and delegation (tafwid). The author affirms the latter as the manifest truth (al-haqq al-mubin). This guards against the misunderstanding that God is forced to create evil or that He delegates creation to secondary causes.
