Two Types of Division: Real and Logical
Author states the two types of division (real and logical) with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real division (whole into parts) and logical division (universal into particulars) and classify examples from Islamic sciences accordingly.

Core Idea
Division reduces composites to simples, either by splitting a whole or a universal.

Have you ever wondered how scholars break down complex concepts into their basic components? In logic, division (al-qisma) is the method used to analyze a whole or a universal into its constituents. This passage from al-Rāzī's Mulakhkhaṣ explains the two types of division: real division, which splits a physical or mental whole into its parts, and logical division, which splits a universal into its particular instances. Understanding this distinction is crucial for precise definition and classification in Islamic sciences like fiqh and uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of division
In the passage, division is divided into two types: dividing the whole into its parts (real division) and dividing the universal into its particulars (logical division). Locate these in the text.
2
Distinguish real from mental composition
Notice that the author subdivides the first type into mental composition (like the concept of blackness from genus and differentia) and external composition (natural or artificial). Understand that real division can be physical or conceptual.
3
Apply the types to Islamic science examples
Think of an example like dividing the prayer into its pillars, conditions, and sunan – is that real or logical division? Justify based on the definitions from the text.
Consider examples from fiqh, hadith, or tafsir.
4
State the purpose of division
The author says division is a path to reducing compounds to simples. This means division helps identify the basic, indivisible components of a composite, whether physical or conceptual.

Commentary
This passage discusses the method of division (qisma) used to discover genera and differentiae. The author begins by acknowledging the difficulty of precisely identifying essential and accidental attributes. When we have two things that share some aspects but differ in others, it is challenging to determine the full extent of their commonality and difference. To overcome this, division is introduced as a reliable method. Division is of two types: (1) dividing a whole into its parts (real division), where the whole is composed of parts either in the mind (like the concept of blackness composed of genus 'color' and differentia 'contraction of pupil') or externally—either natural (like an animal composed of organs) or artificial (like a bed composed of wood). Each of these can be further divided into composition of homogeneous parts (like body from humors) or heterogeneous parts (like fire). (2) Dividing a universal into its particulars (logical division), where the universal being divided may be a genus, species, differentia, property, or individual. The purpose of division is to reduce complex composites to their simple elements. For example, in fiqh, dividing the act of worship into essential pillars (arkan) and conditions (shurut) is a logical division of the universal 'worship' into its particulars.

Deriving the Six Directions from the Three Dimensions of a Body
Author argues that the six spatial directions arise from the three dimensions of a body.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the six spatial directions from the definition of a body as having three dimensions, and identify the conventional versus natural basis of that derivation.

Core Idea
The six directions are a logical consequence of three dimensions, not an independent property.

In this passage, al-Razi presents a concise argument that every body, by virtue of having three dimensions (length, width, depth), necessarily has six ends or sides. However, he immediately qualifies that this derivation depends on arbitrarily selecting one dimension as the primary axis. Let's unpack his reasoning and understand why this matters for how we think about space in Islamic physics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of jism being used
The passage begins: 'al-jism min an fihi ab'aad thalatha' — the body is such that it has three dimensions. This is a definitional claim.
In Islamic physics, a body is defined as a substance with three dimensions.
2
Recognize the implicit syllogism
The author reasons: every body has three dimensions; every dimension has two ends; therefore every body has six ends (directions). Write this as a categorical syllogism.
This is a valid first-figure (Barbara) syllogism.
3
Note the qualification about conventional assumption
The author says this result follows only if one dimension is taken as the basis (asl) and the others are set perpendicular. Why is this qualification important?
It shows the six directions are not absolute in the body but depend on our coordinate choice.
4
Distinguish between natural and conventional directions
In Islamic physics, some directions (like above/below relative to the celestial sphere) are absolute, while others (right/left, front/back) are relative to the observer. This passage lays the groundwork for that distinction.
This distinction is crucial in theological discussions about Allah's attributes.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional argument establishing the basis for the six spatial directions (jihaat sitta). The author states that a body (jism) necessarily has three dimensions (ab'aad thalatha): length, width, and depth. Each dimension has two ends (tarafaan) or extremes. Therefore, the body has six ends or sides (atraaf sitta). These correspond to the six directions: above/below, right/left, front/back. However, the author immediately qualifies this: this result only follows if we arbitrarily assume one dimension as the primary axis (imtidaad waahid) and take it as the foundation (asl), and then define the other two dimensions as perpendicular to it. This is a conventional (wad'i) or relational definition, not a natural (tabi'i) one. In other words, the six directions are not absolute in the body itself but depend on how we orient our coordinate system. This distinction is important because later, in physics, the real directions (above/below) are absolute relative to the celestial sphere, while the others are relative to the observer. The commentary uses an example from geometry: a cube has six faces, but which face is 'front' depends on the observer's standpoint. In Islamic theology, this is relevant to discussions about the direction of prayer (qibla) and the attributes of Allah, which are not confined to spatial directions.

Uniform Motions: Definition and Three Modes
Author defines uniform motion and its three modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of uniform motion (mutābiqa) and distinguish its three modes: actual, potential, and imaginary.

Core Idea
Uniform motions are those with equally proportioned parts, realized actually, potentially, or imaginatively.

This passage introduces the third division of motions: uniform versus non-uniform. The author defines uniform motion and explains that uniformity can exist in three distinct modes. Parsing this definition will sharpen your ability to classify physical phenomena in Islamic physics.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate 'mutābiqa' (uniform motions) in the Arabic. The definiendum is motion (ḥaraka) qualified by uniformity.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is motion; the differentia is the equality of its parts (tawāfuq al-ajzā'). This gives the core definition.
3
Distinguish the three modes of uniformity
The author lists actual (bi‑l‑fi‘l), potential (bi‑l‑quwwa), and imaginary (bi‑l‑tawahhum) uniformity. Understand each: actual is realised now, potential can be realised, imaginary exists only in mental comparison.
Examples from the text: actual = a straight line; potential = something divisible into equal parts; imaginary = comparing two motions in the mind.
4
Test the definition's completeness
Check if all uniform motions are captured and non-uniform ones excluded. The three modes cover all possibilities, but note that imaginary uniformity may be debated.

The True Cause of Contrariety in Motions
Author identifies the cause of contrariety in motions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the true cause of contrariety between motions and explain why other factors (mover, time, medium) are rejected.

Core Idea
Two motions are contrary only because their starting and ending points are opposite.

After classifying uniformity, the author investigates contrariety. He rejects several possible causes—mover, time, subject—before concluding that contrariety arises solely from the endpoints. This analytical elimination of alternatives is a key skill in Islamic philosophical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question being asked
The author asks: what makes two motions contrary? He rejects several candidates before giving the correct cause.
2
List the rejected candidates
The mover (al-muḥarrik), time (al-zamān), and the subject (mā fīhi al-ḥaraka) are all considered and dismissed.
3
Understand why each is rejected
For the mover: motion of air downward and fire upward are not contrary despite contrary movers. For time: times are not contrary. For the subject: ascending and descending are contrary even when the medium (air) is the same.
4
Accept the correct cause
The passage states 'فبقى ان تكون العلة تضاد ما عنه وما اليه' — the cause is the contrariety of the starting point and the ending point.

Dialectical exchange on infinite actual division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the dialectical structure of claim, objection, and response in the Peripatetic argument about infinite actual division of bodies.

Core Idea
Dialectical reasoning tests a thesis by objection and defence before accepting it.

This passage presents a dialectical exchange on whether actual division of a body can be infinite. The Peripatetics claim it can, but an objector challenges them. Understanding this structure helps you see how Islamic philosophers argued rigorously, testing each claim against objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Peripatetic claim
Find where the Peripatetics state their position on actual division. They say it reaches infinity, and they give two reasons. First, the body has two ends that are distinct, and continuity implies indefinite divisibility.
The second reason is not fully preserved but likely involves the nature of continuity.
2
Locate the objection
Look for the phrase 'someone may say'. The objector asks: why can't the two parts of a divided body differ in essence? This challenges the Peripatetic assumption that parts are similar.
Even if this seems far-fetched, the objector demands proof, shifting the burden.
3
Find the Peripatetic response
The response argues that magnitude has a limit of smallness; beyond that, the essential form is destroyed. They also bring in Ibn Sina's argument that body is composed of matter and form, and for celestial bodies, division would destroy the form.
The response uses the principle that one nature cannot have contradictory effects.
4
Map the dialectical structure
Now you have three parts: claim (Peripatetic thesis), objection (why not different essences?), response (limits of smallness and matter-form composition). This is a classic jadal (dialectical) exchange.
Recognise that the objector does not fully reject but questions the necessity.

The Two Types of Mixture (Mizaj): Primary and Secondary
Author divides mixture into primary and secondary types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between primary and secondary mixtures and identify examples of each from Islamic natural philosophy.

Core Idea
Mixture is not just one kind: primary from elements, secondary from already-mixed substances.

In Islamic natural philosophy, the concept of mixture (mizaj) is crucial for understanding how elements combine to form complex bodies. The author here divides mixture into two types: the first arises directly from the interaction of the four elements, while the second results from the interaction of qualities that are themselves already mixed. This classification helps explain the formation of metals and other compounds, such as gold from mercury and sulfur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types in the Arabic
Look for the terms 'al-mizaj al-awwal' and 'al-mizaj al-thani'. The first is primary, the second is secondary.
2
Understand the basis of division
The primary mixture comes from the direct interaction of the elements (fire, air, water, earth). The secondary mixture comes from the interaction of already mixed qualities—composite bodies acting on each other.
This is a key distinction in natural philosophy.
3
Analyze the example of gold
Gold is given as a secondary mixture formed from mercury and sulfur. Why? Because mercury and sulfur are themselves composites, so their interaction yields a secondary mixture.
Think of this as compound-level combination.
4
Apply the classification to other cases
Consider other substances in nature: can you classify their formation as primary or secondary? For example, the formation of a simple mineral from direct elemental interaction is primary; the formation of an alloy from two metals is secondary.

Author classifies internal senses into four powers: common sense, imagination, estimation, memory.

The perceiving power is either perceiving particulars or universals. The perceiver of particulars is either the external, which you have known, or the internal.
