How perception of individuals relies on universals: Razi's argument from identical bodies
Author explains how individuals are known and why two identical bodies can be confused.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Razi's argument that perceptual knowledge of individuals attaches to the universal nature, not the individuating principle.

Core Idea
When two identical bodies cause confusion, perception must be of the universal, not the individual.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi tackles a fundamental epistemological question: how do we perceive individual objects? He distinguishes between direct self-awareness (wijdan) and external perception via pointing (ishara). Then he presents a powerful argument: if two objects are completely indistinguishable, we cannot tell them apart; therefore, our perception does not grasp what makes each unique but rather their shared universal nature. This argument ties logic to ontology and has implications for how we know individuals in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two modes of perceiving individuals
Razi states that perceiving an individual is either by direct experience (al-wijdan) which is self-awareness, or by pointing (al-ishara) to an external object. Locate these terms in the Arabic.
2
State the key question about pointing
Razi asks: does the pointing refer to the individual's unique identity (its haqiqa) or to it described by the common reality (al-qadr al-mushtarak)? This frames the problem.
3
Analyze the thought experiment
Imagine two bodies identical in all sensible qualities like shape, color, size. If you see one and then it disappears, when it returns, you cannot tell if it is the same one. This shows confusion (ishtibah) occurs.
4
Trace Razi's logical conclusion
Razi argues: if perception grasped the individuating principle (what makes each body itself), confusion would be impossible because that principle is unique. Since confusion occurs, perception must be of the shared universal, not the individuator.
This is a modus tollens argument: if P then Q; not Q, therefore not P.

And the universal is more known than the particular by induction or argument, because the more general is less conditioned and restricted than the more specific, and whatever is like that occurs more frequently in the mind and is more known. And the perception of a particular individual insofar as it is that individual is either by direct experience—which is what everyone observes of his own specific self insofar as it is itself—or by pointing when we point to it. And here is an inquiry: does the pointing attach to it insofar as it is itself, or insofar as it is described by the common reality? And there is a problem: because if we suppose two bodies each equal to the other in form, color, magnitude, and all other sensible qualities, then one would be confused with the other; so if we saw one of them and then it disappeared and we saw it again, we would not know whether what we saw first is what we saw second. And it is known that the thing by which each of them is distinguished from the other is not shared, because it is impossible that what is shared be identical to what distinguishes. And if perception attached to the specific individual according to that thing for which it is itself, it would be impossible for it to be confused with another, because it is impossible for that thing to occur in another. Since confusion occurs, we know that perception attaches to it insofar as it is described by the common reality. But if the truth is that it attaches to it insofar as it is itself, but the imagination does not perceive that clearly, we will detail that in metaphysics.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument about how we perceive individual objects. Razi first states a general principle: the universal is more known than the particular because it is less restricted (less conditions) and thus occurs more frequently in the mind. This is like saying the concept 'animal' is more familiar than 'horse' or 'man' because it is broader. He then divides perception of an individual into two modes: direct experience (الوجدان), which is self-awareness, and pointing (الإشارة) to an external object. The key question is whether a pointing gesture refers to the individual's unique identity (its 'this-ness') or to it described by a universal. Razi presents a problem (شكل) using an example of two completely identical bodies (same shape, color, size, etc.). Since they are indistinguishable to the senses, we cannot tell them apart after separation. This proves that our perceptual knowledge of such objects does not grasp their unique individuating principle (which would prevent confusion); rather, we grasp them through the shared universal (the common reality). If perception directly grasped the individuator, confusion would be impossible. Since confusion happens, the content of perception must be the universal. Razi ends by noting that the alternative (that perception does grasp the individual but we fail to notice) is a metaphysical issue deferred. In Islamic law, this relates to the problem of identifying the same witness or the same object in evidentiary contexts.

The Paradox of Seeking Quiddity: Known or Unknown?
Author examines the difficulty of seeking a quiddity that is either known or unknown, and proposes a solution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the paradox of seeking quiddity and explain the two attempted solutions given in the text.

Core Idea
The paradox of seeking definition is resolved by partial knowledge, but the resolution itself faces difficulties.

This passage presents a fundamental epistemological problem: how can one seek to know a quiddity if one either already knows it (making the search redundant) or does not know it at all (making recognition impossible)? The author explores proposed solutions and their limitations, revealing the subtlety of defining quiddities in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two horns of the paradox
The passage states that if the quiddity is already conceived, seeking is impossible (tahsil al-hasil); if not conceived at all, seeking is also impossible. Locate these in the Arabic.
Look for the phrases 'تحصيل الحاصل محال' and 'استحال كونه طالبا لها'.
2
Extract the proposed resolution
The author says it is permissible (yajuz) that the quiddity be known in one respect and unknown in another. Find this statement.
The phrase 'فلذلك يجوز أن يكون معلومًا من وجه ومجهولا من وجه آخر'.
3
Analyse the first attempted answer
The passage points out that even if known in one aspect, seeking from that aspect is impossible because of tahsil al-hasil, and seeking from the unknown aspect is impossible because it is not perceived. Understand this objection.
The objection is detailed: 'فانه وإن كان في الشيء الواحد أن يكون معلوما من وجه آخر، لكنه استحال أن يكون مطلوبا من الوجه الذي هو معلوم...'.
4
Examine the second attempted answer
The distinction between incomplete knowledge (al-'ilm al-naqis) and complete knowledge (al-'ilm al-kamil) is introduced, but the problem returns. Follow this reasoning.
The text says: 'أن القصد المعلوم هو الحضور العلم الناقص، غير المطلوب؛ بل المطلوب هو العلم الكامل. وحينئذ يعود الإشكال.'
5
Assess the overall conclusion
Does the passage resolve the paradox? The author seems to leave the problem unresolved. Reflect on why this matters in Islamic epistemology.
The passage ends with 'يعود الإشكال' (the problem returns).

Explanation: Whoever seeks to know a quiddity, either that quiddity is already conceived by him or not. If the first, it cannot be sought because acquiring what is already acquired is impossible. If the second, it also cannot be sought because if it is not conceived at all, it is impossible for him to be a seeker of it, and because how would he know that what he found is what he was seeking? Thus it is not possible to answer by taking these two aspects. Therefore, it is permissible that it be known in one respect and unknown in another. By it being known, directing the request towards it is possible; by it being unknown, the seeker can be a seeker of its acquisition, by knowing it with incomplete knowledge in one respect and seeking complete knowledge. For the first answer: even if in one thing it can be known from one aspect and unknown from another, it is impossible for it to be sought from the aspect it is known, because seeking what is already acquired is impossible, and it cannot be sought from the aspect it is unknown, because that aspect is unknown and not perceived, so directing the request to it is impossible. The second answer: the intended known is the presence of incomplete knowledge, not the sought; rather the sought is complete knowledge. Then the problem returns.

Commentary
This passage addresses a classic epistemological paradox in seeking definition (or knowledge) of a quiddity. If the quiddity is already known, seeking it is redundant; if completely unknown, one cannot recognize it when found. The proposed resolution is that a quiddity can be known in one respect (e.g., generic knowledge) and unknown in another (e.g., specific differentia). Thus, one can seek to know it more fully. However, the author points out that even this resolution faces difficulties: if it is known in one aspect, one cannot seek it from that aspect; if unknown in another, one cannot direct the request to it. A second attempted answer distinguishes between incomplete knowledge (which we have) and complete knowledge (which we seek), but this also leads back to the problem. The passage reflects the nuanced discussion of the epistemology of definition in post-classical logic.

The Paradox of Seeking Concepts
Author presents the epistemological paradox of seeking concepts and distinguishes it from assents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemological problem of seeking concepts and distinguish it from seeking assents.

Core Idea
You cannot seek a concept you do not already possess, nor seek one you already have.

This passage tackles a fundamental puzzle in Mantiq: can we acquire new concepts through definitions? The author argues that seeking a concept is paradoxical. Let's parse the argument step by step to understand why definitions cannot give us entirely new knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
Find how the author distinguishes between tasawwur (concept) and tasdiq (assent). In the example of seeking whether the world is originated, we already possess the concepts of 'world' and 'origination'; the sought is the relation of one to the other.
2
Understand the paradox for concepts
Read the part: 'if it is not present to you, it is impossible to seek its acquisition' and 'if it is present, its seeking is also impossible.' Explain why each case fails: without prior concept, no recognition; with it, seeking is redundant.
3
Apply the problem to definitions
Consider the implication: definitions (hadd) are supposed to convey the essence of a thing. But if they rely on already known simple concepts, they cannot introduce fundamentally new concepts. They can only combine known elements.
4
Distinguish the case of assents
Assents are different: in seeking an assent, the needed concepts are already present, so you can recognize the truth when you find it. The paradox applies only to concepts themselves.

Commentary
This passage presents an epistemological argument about the nature of seeking knowledge. It distinguishes between seeking an assent (tasdiq) and seeking a concept (tasawwur). The author uses the example: if we want to know whether the world is originated, we already have the concepts of 'world' and 'origination', so we can recognize the result. This works for assents. But for concepts, there is a paradox: if you do not have the concept, you cannot seek it because you would not know what to look for; if you already have it, seeking it is redundant. This objection is directed at claims that definitions (hadd) can produce new concepts. The argument is that definitions cannot give us new simple concepts, only compound ones. This sets up the subsequent discussion of definitions as either hadd (essential) or rasm (descriptive). The passage is a teacher's orienting thought: the five universals are the lens through which every definition is evaluated.

Why the Proposition Cannot Be Defined: Circularity and Self-Evidence
Author critiques three definitions of proposition and declares it indefinable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular definitions and explain why qadiyya is considered self-evident in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Every attempt to define the proposition fails by circularity; it is known intuitively.

In this passage, the author reviews three attempted definitions of the proposition (qadiyya) and shows how each one falls into circular reasoning. He then argues that the concept of a proposition is so basic that it cannot be defined—every rational person recognizes it by intuition. Understanding this lesson sharpens your ability to detect when a definition merely presupposes what it tries to explain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three definitions
Find the three proposed definitions of qadiyya: (1) 'that which is said to the speaker that he is truthful or lying', (2) 'that which admits truth and falsity', (3) 'that in which a judgment is made of attribution or negation'. Write them down to see the pattern.
2
Extract the circularity in each
For each definition, note which term is used that already assumes the concept of a proposition. The first uses 'truth' (sidq), which is defined as correspondence—but correspondence requires a proposition. The second uses 'assent' (tasdiq), defined as affirming truth—circular. The third uses 'judgment' (hukm), which is close to 'affirmation' or 'negation'—those also depend on propositions.
Circularity: the definiens uses a term that itself can only be understood through the definiendum.
3
Understand the author's conclusion
The author states that the quiddity (mahiyya) of qadiyya is beyond definition because everyone intuitively knows the difference between a report (khabar) and other speech, like commands. He gives the example of knowing that you exist—a specific proposition—which presupposes the general concept of a proposition.
Self-evident concepts cannot be defined without circularity.
4
Apply the principle to other Islamic sciences
Recognize that many fundamental concepts in fiqh and kalam (like 'hukm' or 'wajib') are also often treated as self-evident. The same test of circularity can be used to evaluate any definition.

Refuting the Form Theory of Knowledge
Refutation of form theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dilemma used to refute the form theory of knowledge.

Core Idea
If knowledge were a form identical to the object, two identicals cannot coexist; if different, it fails to represent.

Another theory is that knowledge is a mental form (surah) that either mirrors the essence of the known object or is different. The author presents a devastating dilemma: if the form is identical to the known object, then two identical things (the object and its mental image) would coexist in the same subject—impossible. If it is different, then it cannot be knowledge of that object at all.

Guided Path
1
State the form theory
The view under attack: knowledge is a mental form (surah) that corresponds to the known object. Find 'كونها صورة' in the Arabic.
2
Present the dilemma
The author argues: either the form is identical (مماثلة) to the essence of the known, or it is different (مختلفة). These are the only possibilities.
A logical dilemma is a choice between two unacceptable options.
3
Analyze the first horn
If the form is identical to the known object, then two identical things (the object and the mental image) would exist in the same subject (the knower). But two identical things cannot coexist in the same relation—there's no reason one is a state (حالة) and the other a locus (محل).
This contradicts the principle of non-identity.
4
Analyze the second horn
If the form is different from the known object, then it does not represent the essence of the known, so it cannot be knowledge (تعقل) of that object.
5
Conclusion of the refutation
Both horns lead to absurdity, so the form theory is false.

Infinite Regress of Mental Forms and Critique of Third Level
Author discusses mental forms, universals, and the infinite regress objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the infinite regress objection to mental forms and evaluate the critique of the third level of knowledge.

Core Idea
The mind can abstract universals, but infinite regress is only potential; the third level of knowledge is rejected.

This passage from Ibn al-Mulakhkhas delves into the nature of mental forms (الصور العقلية) and the problem of universals. The author raises a powerful objection: if the mind can abstract one universal form from particulars, it could then abstract a second universal from the first, leading to an infinite regress. How does the author respond? He also critiques Ibn Sina's notion of a 'third level' of knowledge, arguing that it conflates potentiality with actuality. This lesson will help you navigate these nuanced epistemological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The author begins by considering the union of intelligibles. What problem arises if the mind can abstract universals from particulars? The potential for an infinite regress of mental forms.
The author uses the metaphor of a valley to illustrate the collection of intelligibles.
2
Understand the infinite regress objection
The author explains that if the mind can take a universal form from individuals, it can take a second universal form from the first, and so on ad infinitum. He notes that this regress is only potential in human thought, not actual, and that it is not impossible as long as it does not have a beginning.
The distinction between potential and actual intellection is crucial here.
3
Distinguish potential vs. actual
The author clarifies that an infinite regress is possible in separate intellects (al-'uqul al-mufariqa) where it is actual, but for humans it is only potential. The impossibility would only arise if there were no beginning.
This distinction saves the theory from absurdity.
4
Analyze the critique of the third level
The author rejects Ibn Sina's third level (knowing, forgetting, then recalling by ability). He argues that the person has actual knowledge of his ability to answer, and there is a clear distinction between the state before and after hearing the question, so it is not mere potentiality.
The critique challenges the coherence of an intermediate state between potential and actual.

Commentary
This passage is a complex epistemological discussion about the nature of mental forms (الصور العقلية) and universal concepts. The author begins by considering the problem of how the mind can abstract a universal from particular individuals. He raises the issue of infinite regress: if the mind can take a universal form from individuals, it could then take a second universal form from the first, and so on ad infinitum. The author distinguishes between potential and actual intellection, noting that the infinite regress is only potential in human thought but actual in separate intellects. He then critiques a position attributed to 'the Sheikh' (likely Ibn Sina) that posits a third level of knowledge: knowing something, forgetting it, and then recalling it upon being asked. The author argues that this is not truly potential knowledge because the person already has actual knowledge of his ability to answer, and there is a clear distinction between before and after hearing the question. He concludes that this third level is false because if the person had detailed knowledge, he would be able to distinguish each part, which he cannot. The passage is dense and technical, engaging with the Islamic philosophical tradition's debates on universals and mental existence.

The Form Theory of Knowledge: Objection and Response
Author presents an objection to the form theory of knowledge and the Sheikh's answer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the conditional objection to knowledge as form and explain the Sheikh's response using the distinction between general and specific.

Core Idea
Knowledge is not a separate form but the presence of an abstract quiddity; the objection fails to distinguish the specific from the general.

In this passage, the author presents a powerful objection to the view that knowledge is the acquisition of a mental form (sura). The objection uses a conditional argument to show the impossibility of self-knowledge if a form is other than the knower. The Sheikh (Ibn Sina) responds by refining the definition: knowledge is the presence of an abstract quiddity, not a form that duplicates the known. This lesson unpacks the logic of the objection and the philosophical subtlety of the response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's conditional structure
The objection states: IF the form obtained by the intellect is other than its essence, THEN two absurdities follow. Locate the 'if' clause and the two consequences.
The conditional is: 'إذا كان الذي حصل للعاقل غير ذاته فهو باطل من وجهين'.
2
Analyze the first absurdity
The first consequence is that the form must equal the known in its complete quiddity, leading to two identical things with no reason to assign roles of intellect and intelligible. Understand why this is problematic.
This argument relies on the principle of identity and distinction of roles.
3
Analyze the second absurdity
The second consequence is a dilemma: either the knower knows the form before having it (leading to circularity) or does not know its essence after having it (contradicting self-knowledge). Trace the two horns.
The dilemma targets the possibility of self-knowledge under the form theory.
4
Examine the Sheikh's response
The Sheikh redefines knowledge as the presence of an abstract quiddity, not a separate form. He argues that the objection's specific claim (that the form is other) is not essential; rejecting it does not reject the general principle of knowledge as presence. The composite of quiddity and the knower is a relation making self-knowledge possible.
Key move: 'لا يلزم من كذب الخاص كذب العام'.

Commentary
This passage refines the definition of knowledge as the acquisition of a form. The author presents an objection to this view, then gives the Sheikh's (Ibn Sina's) response. The objection has two parts: 1) If the form is other than the knower's essence, it must equal its complete quiddity, leading to two identical things and no reason to assign intellect and intelligible roles. 2) Either the knower knows the form is similar to its essence before having it (so knowledge is prior) or after, but if after, it would know its essence only after the form, which is not true for self-knowledge. The Sheikh answers that knowledge is not the form itself but the presence of an abstract quiddity. The objector's assumption that the quiddity must be totally other is a specific detail; rejecting that detail does not reject the general principle that knowledge is a presence. The composite of quiddity and the individual (the knower) is a relation, so self-knowledge is possible without a separate form. The argument uses a conditional objection ('if...then') and a response that distinguishes specific from general.

Classification of Assents into Primary and Theoretical
Author divides assents into primary and theoretical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between primary (awwali) and theoretical (nazari) assents based on the need for proof.

Core Idea
All knowledge rests on primary self-evident truths that require no proof.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi lays out the foundational structure of human knowledge. He begins by defining what an assent (tasdiq) is, then classifies assents into necessary and non-necessary. The necessary assents are further divided into those that are immediate (awwali) and those that require investigation (nazari). This classification is crucial because it establishes that all theoretical knowledge ultimately traces back to self-evident principles, preventing an infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of assent (tasdiq)
Razi defines tasdiq as the mind's judgment of something by affirmation or negation. Notice that it is a mental act, distinct from mere conception (tasawwur).
This definition is the starting point for classifying types of knowledge.
2
Identify the two main categories: necessary vs. non-necessary
Razi says either the judgment is necessary (daruri) or not. Necessary means the mind cannot doubt it. Non-necessary judgments require proof.
Necessary here does not mean legally obligatory; it means intellectually undeniable.
3
Further divide necessary judgments into those corresponding to reality and those not
Necessary judgments that correspond to reality are true; those that do not correspond are false. But Razi focuses on the true ones.
This step ensures we are dealing with certain knowledge.
4
Distinguish primary (awwali) from theoretical (nazari) within the true necessary
Primary judgments are known without any mediation or proof; theoretical judgments require investigation (nazar) and rely on a proof. The key is the presence or absence of a middle term.
Example: 'The whole is greater than the part' is primary; 'The world has a Creator' is theoretical.
5
Recognize the role of sense and intuition in theoretical knowledge
Razi states that judgments that are not obtained purely by intellect require mediation from outside, like sense perception or intuition. This shows that theoretical knowledge depends on empirical or intuitive inputs.
This connects to the principle that all theoretical knowledge traces back to primary knowledge without infinite regress.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion on the nature of primary propositions and the soul's capacity for knowledge. The author begins by defining primary affirmations (التصديقات الأولية) as knowledge of propositions that necessarily involve negation or affirmation. These propositions can be demonstrated, but the demonstration itself relies on primary principles; otherwise, circularity results. He then lists several primary truths: existence is not free from pre-eternity and temporal origination, not free from necessity and possibility, the law of non-contradiction (a thing cannot be both existent and non-existent), material things of the same nature are equal, and a single body cannot be in two places at once. These are known directly, not through proof. He then addresses an objection: that these principles are obscure while the derived sciences are clear, and the clear cannot come from the obscure. The author responds that these aspects, though initially obscure, are universally agreed upon by minds. He then discusses the soul's ability to understand multiple concepts despite its simplicity, attributing this to the holy soul (النفس القدسية) which has many necessary truths and quickly transfers to theoretical ones without error. He concludes by distinguishing two types of inquiry: natural (prone to no error) and artificial (may have error), and argues that the soul's reception of knowledge does not depend on thought because thought is a request for something absent, while the knowledge is already present.

Parsing the Epistemological Terms: From Sensation to Assent
Definitions of key epistemological terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish and define the key epistemological terms: idrak, shu'ur, hifz, tadhakkur, ma'rifa, fahm, 'ilm, sidq, and tasdiq as presented in this passage.

Core Idea
Knowledge begins with raw perception and culminates in the acknowledgment of truth.

This passage from al-Razi's Mulakhkhas lays the foundational vocabulary for Islamic epistemology. Each term marks a distinct stage in the cognitive process, from initial awareness to confirmed judgment. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for later discussions on the division of knowledge into conceptualization (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq).

Guided Path
1
List the terms introduced
Read the first sentence and identify the six terms: shu'ur, idrak, fahm, ma'rifa, 'ilm, and ihata. Note that the passage defines each in sequence.
Ihata (comprehension) is mentioned but not separately defined; it likely overlaps with idrak.
2
Analyze the definition of idrak
Find how idrak is defined: linguistically 'meeting and reaching,' technically the intellect's grasp of a thing's quiddity. Compare with shu'ur, which is 'perception without confirmation.' The key differentia is the presence or absence of confirmation (istithbat).
Idrak includes confirmation, shu'ur lacks it.
3
Trace the progression to memory and recognition
When full meaning is obtained, truth is accepted. If the meaning can be recalled, it becomes hifz (memory). Recognizing a previously known thing upon re-perception is ma'rifa. Note the link between perception, preservation, and recognition.
Ma'rifa requires awareness of identity with past perception.
4
Define fahm and ifham
Fahm is intending the meaning from the speaker's words; ifham is conveying meaning to the listener. These are active and passive aspects of understanding.
Fahm is receptive, ifham is communicative.
5
Distinguish 'ilm, sidq, and tasdiq
'Ilm is a judgment (hukm) affirming or denying something. Sidq is the correspondence of that judgment with reality. Tasdiq is the acknowledgment of that correspondence. Note that tasdiq is an additional mental act beyond mere judgment.
This division underlies the broader tasawwur/tasdiq dichotomy.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the divisibility of intellectual forms. The author begins by stating that if a universal concept were composed of parts, each part would either be knowledge or not, leading to a regress. He then considers things that admit division, such as the divine essence, unity, and the point, and argues that if knowledge of them were divided, two possibilities arise: the parts are either common (sharing the same nature) or different. The second possibility is false because each part would have to differ from the whole in reality or accidents; but if the difference is by accidents, the form would not be abstract, and thus not universal. The author further argues that if division is a condition for intelligibility, it leads to absurdities. He then rejects the idea that intellectual forms are corporeal, because that would imply infinite parts in a single substance, which is impossible. Finally, he considers the possibility that the locus of perception is corporeal, like a point, but rejects it because a point cannot have mixture or exist independently. The arguments rely on principles of metaphysics and psychology, using examples like the concept of 'man' shared among all humans.

Divine Foreknowledge and Determinism
Author presents objection that divine foreknowledge leads to determinism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical argument that divine foreknowledge leads to determinism and identify its premises and conclusion.

Core Idea
If God knows it, it must happen — that is the logical challenge of divine foreknowledge to human free will.

This passage from Razi's Mulakhkhas presents a formidable objection to the compatibility of divine foreknowledge and human freedom. The objection argues that God's knowledge of future events prior to their occurrence necessitates those events, leaving no room for contingency. The argument's logical structure is what we will unpack in this lesson.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
Find the text stating that God knows particulars before they occur with universal knowledge, and at their occurrence with particular knowledge. This sets up the basis for the objection.
2
Extract the first conditional premise
Locate the phrase indicating that if God knows something will exist, then its existence is necessary (واجب). Note the reasoning that change in God's knowledge is impossible.
3
Extract the second conditional premise
Find the corresponding claim that if God knows something will not exist, then its non-existence is necessary (واجب). Confirm that the same impossibility of change applies.
4
Recognize the disjunctive conclusion
The text concludes that there is no escape from the two contradictories: everything is either necessary existence or necessary non-existence. This is the deterministic conclusion.
The argument implicitly assumes that God's knowledge is exhaustive and immutable.
5
Formalize the argument
Write the argument as: (1) If God knows P will exist, then P exists necessarily. (2) If God knows P will not exist, then P does not exist necessarily. (3) God knows either P will exist or P will not exist. Therefore, P either exists necessarily or does not exist necessarily. Hence, determinism.
This is a compound conditional argument with a disjunctive premise.

The Logical Impossibility of Knowing God's Essence
Author argues that God's essence is unknowable based on logical principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate al-Razi's argument that God's essence is unknowable, using the logical principle of quiddity and its concomitants.

Core Idea
God's essence is unknowable because its definition escapes both necessary and acquired knowledge.

In this passage, al-Razi deploys a logical principle and a two-part dilemma to demonstrate that we cannot know God's essence. He draws on the fact that a quiddity's necessary concomitants do not reveal the quiddity itself. This critical argument is central to theological discussions about the limits of human knowledge of the Divine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conclusion
Read the last sentence: 'Therefore He, as such, is unknown.' This is the conclusion of the entire argument. The author aims to prove that God's reality (haqiqa) and specific essence (dhat mu'ayyana) are beyond our knowledge.
2
Trace the Logical Principle
The author starts by citing a claim from logic: 'The necessary concomitants of the quiddity are not known from the perspective of its quiddity.' This means that even if we know everything that necessarily accompanies a thing's essence, we still do not grasp the essence itself. Apply this to God: we may know His attributes, but not His essence.
Key term: 'اللوازم' (necessary concomitants).
3
Analyze the Two-Part Dilemma
The author presents a dilemma: if God's essence were known, it would be either known necessarily or acquired. He then refutes both. Necessary knowledge (idṭirār) is impossible 'by necessity'—we do not have direct intuition of God. Acquired knowledge (iktisāb) is impossible because 'the acquisition of a concept is impossible'—a reference to earlier logical arguments that simple realities cannot be defined.
This dilemma structures the core of the argument.
4
Evaluate the Final Point on Specific Essence
Even if the dilemma were bypassed, the author adds a further point: what we know of God—'a single essence described by attributes'—could apply to more than one (conceptually), but an individual essence (al-dhat al-mu'ayyana) is unique and not shareable. Therefore, we cannot know God in His individuality.
This reinforces the unknowability from another angle.

Commentary
This passage is a critical argument against the claim that God's essence can be known. The author begins by citing a logical principle: the essential concomitants of a quiddity are not known from the quiddity itself. That is, even if we know what properties necessarily accompany a quiddity, we do not grasp the quiddity in its own essence. Applying this to God, the author concludes that we do not know God's reality. He then refutes two possible ways of knowing God's essence: necessary knowledge and acquired knowledge. Necessary knowledge is impossible because we do not have direct intuition of God. Acquired knowledge is impossible because, as established earlier in logic, acquiring a concept (tasawwur) through definition is impossible for simple realities. Even if we somehow knew God's reality, we would not know His specific identity (al-dhat al-mu'ayyana) because what we know of God is a set of attributes that could apply to more than one (conceptually), whereas the individual essence is not shareable. Thus, God's essence in itself remains unknown.

Refutation of the Emanationist Principle
Refutation of the emanationist principle and defense of divine freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct and evaluate the mutakallimun's critique of the Neoplatonic principle 'from the One only one proceeds' and articulate the kalam alternative of divine free agency.

Core Idea
The mutakallimun reject the necessary emanation of a single effect from God, affirming instead that God acts by will.

The philosophers argued that from a simple First Principle only one simple effect (the First Intellect) can proceed. This principle, known as qāʿidat al-wāḥid, is foundational to the emanationist cosmology of Ibn Sīnā and others. In this passage, the author (likely Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī) subjects this principle to a rigorous critique, pointing out internal inconsistencies and defending the kalām position that God is a free agent (al-mukhtār), not a necessitating cause (al-mūjib). Although the Arabic is partly corrupt, the logical structure of the refutation can be reconstructed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target principle
What is the core claim of the philosophers that is being attacked? The principle that from a simple First Principle only one simple effect proceeds.
2
First objection: the First Intellect would be composite
The author argues that the First Intellect, if it were the first emanation, would itself be composite (genus + difference, matter + form), contradicting the claim that it is simple. Why is this a problem? Because the principle requires the first effect to be simple like its cause.
3
Regress argument
If the First Intellect necessarily produced a sphere and another intellect, that would lead to an infinite regress of spheres, which is impossible. However, the philosophers limited the number of spheres. So the necessity is not binding.
4
Alternative: a chain of intellects without fixed limit
The First Intellect could cause a chain of intellects without a fixed limit, and the correspondence of intellects to spheres is not necessary.
5
Author's conclusion: the free agent
The author concludes that the truth is the doctrine of the free agent (al-mukhtār). Therefore, the principle 'from the One only one proceeds' is not a necessary truth; God can create multiple things directly by His will.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion of the philosophical principle 'from the One only one proceeds'. The author argues that the claim that the first intellect must be simple and composite only of genus and difference is misguided. He points out that if the first intellect necessarily produced a sphere and another intellect, it would lead to an infinite regress of spheres, which is impossible. But if it is not necessary, then the first intellect need not be the cause of only one thing; it could be the cause of a single intellect, which then causes another, and so on. The author then critiques the idea that the number of intellects equals the number of spheres, discussing matter and form. He concludes that the truth according to the mutakallimun is the doctrine of the free agent (al-mukhtar), and that there is no need for these philosophical speculations. The passage is a refutation of the necessitarian emanationist view in favor of divine volition.
