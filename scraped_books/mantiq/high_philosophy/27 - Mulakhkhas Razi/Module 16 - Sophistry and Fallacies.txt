Refuting the Nature of Place: Dimension and Surface
Author refutes Platonic and Aristotelian views on place as dimension or surface.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's reductio ad absurdum arguments against defining place as a dimension or a surface, and identify the logical structure of each refutation.

Core Idea
Defining place as dimension leads to infinite regress; defining it as surface leads to absurdity.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi critiques two influential philosophical accounts of place: Plato's view that place is the apparent surface of the containing body, and the Aristotelian view that place is the inner surface of the containing body. He also targets a subgroup who treat place as a dimension (bu'd). His goal is to show that these definitions lead to logical impossibilities. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to detect flawed definitions in metaphysical arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Views
Locate where the author mentions the first view (Plato's apparent surface) and the second view (the inner surface). Recognize that he also mentions those who affirm dimension as place.
The text is OCR-damaged, but the translation indicates these two distinct positions.
2
Analyze the Argument Against Dimension
Follow the author's reasoning: if place were a dimension, motion would require that dimension to move, which would require another dimension to receive it, leading to an infinite regress of interpenetrating dimensions. This is impossible.
This is a reductio ad absurdum: the definition leads to a contradiction.
3
Analyze the Argument Against Surface
If place is a surface, motion occurring on that surface could be essential or accidental. If essential, motion would affect bodies with dimensions, which is absurd. If accidental, the same infinite regress recurs. The third possibility (neither essential nor accidental) is dismissed for two reasons: it would require dimensions to be material, and the nature of dimension precludes it.
The author systematically eliminates options.
4
Evaluate the Conclusion
The author concludes that both views are impossible. Check whether his arguments are logically valid: do they successfully show the definitions lead to absurdities?
Consider whether the premises are accepted by the opposing philosophers.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of two views on the nature of place (al-makan). Refuting Atomistic Reduction of Qualities
Author refutes ancient atomistic reduction of sensible qualities to atomic shapes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ancient atomistic theory of sensory qualities and explain Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's objection to reducing qualities to atomic shapes.

Core Idea
Sensory qualities cannot be reduced to geometric shapes; they are real accidents inhering in bodies.

Ancient Greek atomists, such as Democritus and Leucippus, argued that all sensible qualities arise from the shapes of indivisible atoms. For example, they claimed that tetrahedral atoms produce heat, cubic atoms produce cold, and jagged atoms produce sharp tastes. In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi criticizes this reductionist view, asserting that qualities like heat and cold are genuine accidents that can change independently of atomic shape. This refutation is crucial for understanding the Islamic Kalam position on the reality of accidents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ancient atomists' claim
Look for the statement that atoms have different shapes (e.g., tetrahedron, cube) and that these shapes directly cause specific sensible qualities like heat, cold, and taste.
The passage mentions 'what is surrounded by four triangles' (tetrahedron) for heat and 'what is surrounded by six squares' (cube) for cold.
2
Understand the examples given
Note how each shape is linked to a quality: tetrahedron=heat, cube=cold, jagged shapes=sharp tastes. This is the core of the atomistic theory being refuted.
The author likely argues that these associations are arbitrary and insufficient.
3
Recognize al-Razi's critique
Al-Razi objects that a body can change its temperature (e.g., water heating up) without any change in its underlying atomic shape. Therefore, qualities cannot be solely due to shapes.
The passage likely states that heat and cold are not mere geometric properties but real accidents.
4
Apply the critique to other qualities
Extend the same reasoning to color and taste: if shapes alone determined these, they would never change without molecular rearrangement, which is false.
The author emphasizes that sensible qualities are genuine attributes of bodies, not illusions caused by shape.

Refuting Democritus on Celestial Motion and God's Purpose
Author refutes Democritus' view and critiques divine purpose as implying need.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Democritus' cosmology fails because his principles (void, atoms) are false, not because his logic is flawed.

Democritus explained celestial motions as accidental results of atoms colliding in the void. The author refutes this not by attacking the inference but by invalidating the underlying principles. He then turns to a theological question: if God acts for a purpose, does that imply need? This lesson examines both critiques and their logical structure.

The author says the refutation (فساد هذه الشبهة) is by invalidating the principles (بإفساد أصولها). He does not argue against the logical connection but attacks the truth of Democritus' foundational claims.
Strategy: reject premises, not reasoning.

Analyse the theological argument

Refuting Objections in Dialectical Debate: The Example of Projectile Motion
Author refutes objections to the argument that force does not weaken naturally, using stone throw example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a dialectical refutation: the objection (mu'tarid) and the response (jawab) using the example of projectile motion.

Core Idea
A dialectical argument is strengthened by answering objections without surrendering the original claim.

In this passage from the Mulakkhkhas, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi defends the view that force does not naturally weaken against two objections. The first objection claims that a thrown stone should continue indefinitely if force persists; the second argues that the stone must stop at an equilibrium point. Al-Razi's responses illustrate the classic dialectical pattern: concede no foundational principle, but explain away the apparent counterexample by invoking external factors like the medium or accidental motion. You will learn to dissect such exchanges as part of the adab al-bahth tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original claim (mudda'i)
What position is the author defending? In this passage, al-Razi maintains that force (quwwah) does not weaken intrinsically. Identify this claim before looking at the objections.
2
Extract the first objection (mu'tarid 1)
The objector argues: if force does not weaken, why does a thrown stone eventually stop? Find this objection in the Arabic and note its underlying assumption that a continuing cause produces continuing effect unless the cause itself decays.
3
Analyse the first response (jawab 1)
Al-Razi replies that the medium (e.g., air or water) resists and separates the projectile from the original impetus, using the analogy of trying to quickly pull apart a connection in water. The force remains, but the material surroundings prevent its full expression.
4
Extract the second objection (mu'tarid 2)
The second objection asserts that the stone must reach a point of equilibrium (mu'ādalah) where it has no motion. Find this and note that it assumes moving bodies always seek their natural place.
5
Analyse the second response (jawab 2)
Al-Razi denies the necessity of equilibrium in all cases. He gives an example of the moon (or celestial sphere) which moves accidentally (bi al-ta'arruḍ) without reaching equilibrium. The stone may likewise be accidentally moving due to impressed force.

Refuting Circular Arguments: Al-Razi on Time and Motion
Author refutes the claim that motion proves time exists, showing circularity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify circular reasoning (dawr) in a theological proof and reconstruct al-Razi's critique of the argument for time from motion.

Core Idea
Al-Razi exposes the argument for time from motion as circular: it already assumes what it claims to prove.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi dismantles an attempt to prove the existence of time based on fast and slow motions. He shows that the very definition of fast/slow and the concept of simultaneity presuppose time. The argument commits the fallacy of circularity (dawr). This lesson will guide you through his logical dissection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the argument's structure
The argument claims that the existence of fast and slow motions, and their simultaneous start and end, proves time exists. Note the three premises mentioned by al-Razi.
The exact phrasing is corrupted, but the structure is clear.
2
Examine the definition of 'fast' and 'slow'
Al-Razi argues that defining 'fast' requires comparing distances covered in less or equal time. Thus, time is already assumed.
3
Analyze the notion of simultaneity
Saying two motions start together involves an 'instant', which itself depends on the concept of time.
4
Recognize the circularity
Since each premise already assumes time, the argument cannot prove time without circularity.
Al-Razi concludes: 'falaw athbattum al-zamān bi-hādhā al-dalīl, lazima al-dawr'.
5
Evaluate the refutation
Al-Razi's critique is logically sound: it reveals that the argument's premises are not independent of the conclusion.
Consider whether the argument could be rephrased to avoid circularity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence in the celestial realm.

In this passage, the author critiques an argument that because we have not observed change in celestial bodies, they must be immutable. This is a common logical fallacy that the author exposes by noting the limits of human observation. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to evaluate theological and philosophical claims based on empirical evidence.

Refuting the Objection of Shared Parts in Bodily Resurrection
Refutation of objection to bodily resurrection due to shared parts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deconstruct the philosophical objection against bodily resurrection and identify the theological principle used to refute it.

Core Idea
Divine omnipotence overrides apparent logical impossibilities in resurrection.

This passage from Razi's Mulakhkhas presents a classic objection to bodily resurrection: if body parts are shared through consumption, resurrection seems impossible because one part cannot be in two bodies. Razi refutes it by appealing to God's absolute power and the principle that what is possible in initial creation is even more possible in re-creation. We will analyze the objection and refutation step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's objection
Read the first sentence starting with 'فإن قيل' (If it is said). The opponent argues that if a body part is transferred from one person to another through consumption, then upon resurrection God would have to place that part in both bodies, which they claim is impossible.
The impossibility is based on the logical principle that one thing cannot be in two places at once.
2
Extract the logical structure of the objection
The objection is a conditional argument: If parts are shared, then resurrection would require a part to exist in two bodies; this is impossible; therefore, bodily resurrection is impossible. Identify the premises and conclusion.
The opponent assumes that physical laws of identity apply to resurrection.
3
Analyze Razi's refutation
Razi counters by asserting God's omnipotence: 'لا محذور في ذلك' (there is no impossibility in that). He gives an example from initial creation: parts are shared between parents and offspring in the sperm. If that is possible in creation, it is 'أولى' (more appropriate) in re-creation.
Razi's argument is from the greater to the lesser: if God can create, He can recreate.
4
Assess the method and implications
Razi does not deny the logical principle but argues that divine power is not bound by ordinary physical laws. He also clarifies that this does not entail two bodies in one space—it is an 'وجود عرضي' (accidental existence). Consider whether this refutation is convincing from a theological perspective.
This is a common Kalam response: prioritizing revelation and divine attributes over purely rational objections.
