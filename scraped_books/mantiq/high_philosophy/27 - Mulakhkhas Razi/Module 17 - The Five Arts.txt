Intellection as an Additional Relation: Logical Analysis
Author argues that intellection is a relational state, not identical to the form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major and minor premises in the argument about intellection and evaluate the validity of the objection based on the priority argument.

Core Idea
Intellection is not identical to the form's presence; it is a distinct relational state.

In this passage, the author engages in a critical refinement of a key epistemological question: what exactly is intellection? He first states a position that intellection is an additional relation (ḥāla iḍāfiyya) that supervenes on the form's presence. Then he raises a dialectical objection that denies the major premise and dismisses the counterargument from priority as merely persuasive. This lesson will help you dissect the logical structure of this debate, an essential skill for engaging with Islamic philosophical texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the position being stated
Read the first part of the passage. The author selects 'the first division' and asserts three things: the potential intellect intellects the form present in it; this intellection occurs with respect to the form's existence for it; and this intellection is an additional relation. These three claims form the minor premise, the clarification, and the major conclusion respectively.
2
Extract the two premises of the syllogism
The argument can be reconstructed as: Minor premise: The potential intellect intellects the form when the form is present. Major premise (contested): This intellection is an additional relation (not merely the form's presence). Conclusion: Therefore, intellection is a relational state. The objection denies the major premise.
The author grants the minor but challenges the major.
3
Analyze the objection and the priority argument
The objectors argue that if the form alone sufficed for intellection, then intellection would always occur when the form is present, which is not the case. Hence intellection requires an additional relation. The author rejects this as persuasive (rhetorical) rather than demonstrative, because it assumes that different types of intellection must have the same foundation.
The author does not accept the priority argument as a proof; he implies it is based on a false universalization.
4
Evaluate the overall logical structure
The author's own position is that the major premise is false: intellection is not a separate state but rather identical to the form's presence in some sense. The passage thus presents a dual-layer argument: a stated view and a critique of that view's supporting argument.
This is a typical critical refinement: the author refines the concept of intellection by distinguishing it from a mere relation.

Why Natural Forces Cannot Be the First Cause
Author argues that elemental and natural forces cannot be the first cause of events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's argument that neither voluntary nor natural forces can serve as the first cause of events, and identify the key logical dependencies.

Core Idea
Every candidate for first cause fails because it depends on something else.

This passage is a critical examination of the claim that natural or voluntary forces could be the ultimate source of all events. The author systematically shows that each type of force relies on external conditions—voluntary forces need a preponderator, and natural forces require an external cause for their interaction. By tracing these dependencies, you will see why only an independent first agent can be the true first cause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification of forces
First, note the author's division: voluntary forces (al-quwwa al-ikhtiyāriyya) and natural forces (al-quwwa al-tabi'iyya). Natural forces include those inherent (like fire and water) and non-inherent (like magnetism). Also, elemental forces are listed as four types (self, coercive, simple nature, acquired).
The Arabic terms for the four types are uncertain due to OCR issues, but the classification is clear.
2
Analyze voluntary forces
The author argues that voluntary forces have power over two opposite actions and thus require a preponderator (murajjih) to determine which action occurs. This dependency makes them unfit to be the first cause.
A preponderator is an external factor that tips the balance; without it, a voluntary agent would never act.
3
Analyze natural forces
Natural forces, like heat or gravity, can only act when they interact with other bodies of diverse natures. But bodies of diverse natures are naturally repellant; they cannot meet on their own. Therefore, their meeting—and hence the activation of natural forces—requires an external cause.
The author uses the example of fire and water: they naturally separate, so an external force must bring them together for any effect.
4
Draw the conclusion
Since both voluntary and natural forces rely on something outside themselves, neither can be the first cause. The first cause must be independent and self-sufficient.
This argument is a form of regress: any candidate that requires a prior cause is disqualified.

Dialectical Analysis of Matter and Form as Mutual Causes
Dialectical examination of causal priority between matter and form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze a dialectical argument about the causal relation between matter and form, identifying the main claim, objections, and responses.

Core Idea
Dialectical refinement shows that the causal link between matter and form is not trivial; each assumption must be critically examined.

In this passage, the author engages in a dialectical investigation of whether matter (hayula) or form (sura) is the cause of the other's existence. This is a classic problem in natural philosophy. By following the back-and-forth, you will learn how to dissect a dialectical exchange and evaluate the strength of each argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim and attempted elimination
Read the first part of the passage (up to 'so it remains that form is the cause'). The author argues that matter cannot be the cause because it is receptive to many forms, so its relation to each is equal. Hence form is left as the candidate. Note the reasoning: matter's receptivity to infinite forms makes it an unlikely cause.
This is a standard argument in Aristotelian tradition: the cause must be determinate, but matter is indeterminate.
2
Extract the objection against the necessity of a cause
Look for the objection beginning 'We do not grant that every two concomitants must have one be the cause of the other.' The opponent denies the presupposition that one of matter and form must cause the other. This challenges the foundational assumption of the debate.
The objection targets the principle of mutual causality in concomitant pairs.
3
Analyze the argument against form as an absolute cause
The passage then argues that form cannot be an absolute cause because it depends on matter. Find the argument: 'Hyle precedes form as a cause? Likewise form is needful in its essence of matter.' This establishes that form is not independent.
This is a crucial move: it shows that if form were the absolute cause, it would need to be prior to matter, but it is not.
4
Examine the counter-objection regarding priority
The opponent responds by questioning the notion of priority. They suggest that shape is with corporeality, so perhaps matter is prior in a different sense. Look for the discussion of 'corporeality and shape exist together' and the distinction between types of priority.
This part introduces subtle distinctions about causal and temporal priority.
5
Evaluate the final position on the abstract cause
The author concludes by suggesting that the true cause might be an abstract intellect (not God directly). Find the argument: 'it is not permissible that that abstract be God, for the One only produces one.' The dialectic ends with a compromise: the cause is a celestial intelligence.
This shows how dialectic can lead to a refined position, not a simple affirmation of either extreme.
