Identifying Manuscript Colophons vs. Logical Content
Manuscript ending with colophon, ownership notes, and Persian poetry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a manuscript colophon from the main logical text of al-Mulakhkhas and recognize the importance of manuscript studies in preserving Islamic intellectual heritage.

Core Idea
Not every page of a manuscript contains logical argument—colophons are the scribe's signature, not the author's proof.

This lesson examines a page from an actual manuscript of Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's al-Mulakhkhas. The OCR text reveals a colophon—a scribal note at the end of a copy—rather than the logical content of the book. Understanding what a colophon looks like prevents confusion between paratext and main text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the passage type
Look for phrases like 'تحرر كتاب' (completed writing) or dates. These indicate a colophon, not the author's argument.
Colophons often include the scribe's name and date.
2
Extract the date and scribe
In the text, we see 'الاثنين الخاص عشر من شهر ربع الراقي سنه سبع و سبعنی و ستمان' (Monday 12th of Rabi' al-Akhir 770 AH). This is a scribal note.
3
Recognize absence of logical content
Compare with previous lessons from al-Mulakhkhas. This passage lacks definitions or syllogisms. The logical discussion is missing.
If you were studying logic, you would skip to earlier pages.

Navigating Garbled Manuscript Passages: Genre Recognition
Identifying genre of a garbled manuscript note

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify textual genre and assess reliability of a garbled manuscript passage for logical analysis.

Core Idea
Even corrupted texts can yield genre clues through names, titles, and formulas.

In manuscript studies, not every passage is meant to teach logic. Colophons, dedications, and ownership notes are common but often garbled. Learning to recognize them saves you from misinterpreting non-logical material. This lesson uses a heavily corrupted passage from al-Mulakhkhaṣ to illustrate how to spot such notes.

Guided Path
1
Scan for names and honorifics
Look for proper names (e.g., 'Abdullah', 'Fakhri') and titles ('al-Afandi'). These often indicate a dedicatory or ownership note.
2
Identify formulaic phrases
Words like 'walad' (children) suggest biographical context. In Arabic manuscripts, colophons often use 'qad faragha min...' (finished from...) or similar phrasing.
3
Assess OCR risk
If the text contains unrecognizable words or broken continuity (e.g., 'لجندقون', 'قا بعد ا وبرج'), consider it high-risk and do not rely for logical content.
4
Decide on interpretation
When a passage is too corrupt, note it as textual notes only; do not treat it as a logical argument.
