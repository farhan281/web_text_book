Understanding Section Headings: Al-Maqal
Author marks the start of a new discourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the function of the term 'المقال' as a structural marker in classical Islamic scholarly works.

Core Idea
المقال signals the start of a new discourse — a key to navigating scholarly texts.

In classical Islamic scholarship, a single word can carry the weight of an entire section. The term 'المقال' (al-maqal) is one such word: it marks a major division in a structured text. Understanding this marker helps you orient yourself within a work and recognize the organization of the author's argument.

Guided Path
1
Locate the term in the text
Find the word 'المقال' in the source. Note its position: it is a heading, often at the start of a new part of the book.
In the Mulakhkhas, it appears on page 5 as a standalone heading.
2
Understand its lexical meaning
Lexically, 'المقال' derives from 'قول' (speech) and refers to a discourse or article. In scholarly works, it denotes a self-contained section treating a specific topic.
It is not an oral speech but a textual division.
3
Recognize its structural role
Just as a chapter divides a book, 'المقال' divides a work into major discourses. It tells you that the author is beginning a new line of inquiry or a new thematic unit.
The Mulakhkhas is divided into several maqalat, each covering a distinct logical subject.
4
Apply the knowledge to other texts
Look for 'المقال' in other classical works, such as al-Ghazālī's Maqāṣid al-Falāsifa or Ibn Sīnā's al-Shifā'. Notice how each maqala introduces a broad subject, and within it, there are further subdivisions like 'فصول' (chapters).
This skill is essential for navigating lengthy scholarly works.

Navigating the Fihrist of al-Razi's Mulakhkhas
Index listing the major sections of the book from tasawwur to kayfiyyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the structure of a classical Islamic logic text from its table of contents (fihrist).

Core Idea
The fihrist reveals the entire architecture of the science in one glance.

Before diving into the logical content of al-Razi's Mulakhkhas, it is essential to understand how the book is organized. The table of contents (fihrist) provides a map of the entire work, showing the two fundamental divisions of knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq) and how topics unfold from simple concepts to complex demonstrations, and even into physics and metaphysics. This lesson will train you to read such fihrists efficiently so you can orient yourself in any classical logic text.

Guided Path
1
Scan the opening line
The fihrist begins with 'هذا فهرست كتاب الملخص للإمام الرازي', identify the book title and author. This confirms you are looking at the right work.
Often the fihrist is placed at the beginning of the manuscript.
2
Identify the two main divisions
After the opening, you see 'في التصور والتصديق'. These are the two fundamental kinds of knowledge in logic: conceptualization (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). The entire book is structured around this bipartite division.
Every major logic text follows this division.
3
Trace sub-topics under tasawwur
Under tasawwur, the index lists: universals (kulli/juz'i), the five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, general accident), and definition (hadd/rasm/ta'rif). Observe the page numbers to see the sequence.
These topics constitute the theory of simple concepts.
4
Trace sub-topics under tasdiq
Under tasdiq, the index lists: propositions (tasdiqat), modalities, syllogism, demonstration (burhan), and various types of inference (istithna', istiqra', tamthil). This is the theory of judgment and proof.
Note that the index also includes topics like 'aks (conversion) and naqd (contradiction).
5
Note the integration of physics and metaphysics
After logic, the fihrist continues into topics like body, place, void, categories (quantity, quality), existence, and the Necessary Existent. This shows that al-Razi's text covers not only logic but also natural philosophy and metaphysics.
This is common in comprehensive works of the Islamic intellectual tradition.

The Role of the Colophon in Islamic Manuscripts
Scribal colophon with basmalah and scribe's name.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a classical Islamic manuscript colophon and explain its significance for textual transmission.

Core Idea
A colophon is the manuscript's birth certificate.

A colophon (khatimat al-kitab) is a scribal note at the end of a manuscript that records essential information about its production. Even when the text is corrupted, as in our source, the colophon tells us the scribe's identity and the completion date. This seemingly minor detail anchors the manuscript in time and place, allowing scholars to trace the chain of transmission (isnad) and verify the authority of the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basmalah
The colophon opens with 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' — the standard invocation. Note that every Islamic manuscript begins with the basmalah, whether at the start or as part of the colophon.
2
Locate the scribe's name
The scribe introduces himself as 'al-'abd al-faqir Muhammad ibn Abd al-Mumin ibn Khwaja Ali al-Khadra'. This formula of humility ('the poor servant') is typical. The name includes his lineage and a nisbah (al-Khadra, possibly a place).
The scribe's full name is crucial for identifying the copyist and linking the manuscript to a known person.
3
Find the completion date
The phrase 'yawm al-khamis' (Thursday) indicates the day of the week. The year is unfortunately lost to corruption. A complete colophon would also include the month and year, and often the place of copying.
The day alone is not enough, but it shows that scribes recorded the time of completion.
4
Interpret the colophon's function
The colophon serves as a signature and a record of the copy's authenticity. By reading it, you know who copied the text, when, and in some cases where and for whom. This allows later scholars to evaluate the manuscript's reliability and position in the transmission chain.
Without a colophon, a manuscript is anonymous and its provenance uncertain.
