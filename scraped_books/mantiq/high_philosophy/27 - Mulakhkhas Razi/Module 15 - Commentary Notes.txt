The Problem of Knowing Absolute Non-Existence
Author argues that only relative non-existence is knowable, not absolute non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between absolute and relative non-existence and explain why absolute non-existence cannot be known according to al-Razi.

Core Idea
Absolute non-existence, lacking any determination, is inherently unknowable.

In this passage, al-Razi tackles a deep epistemological puzzle: can non-existence be known? He distinguishes two kinds of non-existence and argues that only relative non-existence (the absence of a specific thing) is accessible to knowledge. We'll trace his reasoning and the difficulties that arise, including the problem of mental images.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of non-existence
Locate where the author mentions 'absolute non-existence' (al-'adam al-mutlaq) and 'relative non-existence' (al-'adam al-mudaf). What is the key difference between them?
Absolute non-existence is pure nothingness; relative non-existence is the lack of a specific existent.
2
Understand the claim about knowledge
The author asserts that absolute non-existence is not known or reported, while relative non-existence is known. Note that 'reported' (yukhbar 'anhu) implies it can be the subject of a proposition.
This sets up the initial position.
3
Examine the difficulty with absolute non-existence
Follow the objection: if absolute non-existence were known, it would have to be from the concept of relative non-existence. Also, to know relative non-existence, one must already know non-existence as such. This seems to require that absolute non-existence be known in some sense.
The author responds by noting that absolute non-existence has no determination (ta'yin), so the intellect cannot point to it.
4
Consider the mental image objection
The author anticipates the reply that we can form a mental image (surah dhihniyya) of non-existence. He argues that even if mental images are accepted, they must correspond to an external individual. Since absolute non-existence has no external individual, the mental image fails.
This shows the deep problem: knowledge requires a correspondence between mind and reality.
5
Evaluate the overall argument
Assess whether al-Razi's argument is convincing. Does he successfully show that absolute non-existence is unknowable? What difficulties remain?
The author ends with a prayer, indicating the difficulty is not fully resolved.

Mental vs External Composition in Quiddities
Author distinguishes mental from external composition using blackness and human examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mental and external composition and identify which type applies to a given quiddity based on whether its parts are separable in external reality or only in the mind.

Core Idea
Some quiddities are externally simple but mentally complex.

In logic and metaphysics, it is crucial to understand that not every conceptual analysis corresponds to a real separation in external existence. This passage explains the difference between mental composition (tarkib dhihni) and external composition (tarkib khariji) using two clear examples: the human being and blackness. By mastering this distinction, you will be better equipped to analyze definitions and avoid confusing conceptual parts with real parts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of composition
Read the first sentence carefully. The author distinguishes between mental composition (tarkib dhihni) and external composition (tarkib khariji). Note the Arabic terms: التركيب الذهني and التركيب الخارجي. Understand that mental composition refers to parts that are distinguished by the mind but not separable in reality, while external composition refers to parts that are actually distinct in external existence.
2
Analyze the first example: human
The author gives the human being as an example of external composition. The soul and body are two parts that are actually distinct in external existence. They can exist separately (e.g., soul after death). This is clear external composition.
Think of how in biology, body and soul are considered distinct substances in Islamic philosophy.
3
Analyze the second example: blackness
Blackness (sawad) is given as an example of mental composition. Externally, blackness is a single sensible quality. It cannot be split into parts in reality. However, the mind can analyze it into its genus (color) and differentia (darkening). These parts are mentally distinguishable but not externally separable.
The author explicitly says: 'blackness does not separate from its differentia in existence, but it separates in the mind.'
4
Apply the distinction to other quiddities
Now test your understanding: Consider other examples like 'whiteness' or 'knowledge'. Would they have mental or external composition? Remember the key criterion: whether the parts can be separated in external reality or only in mental analysis.
This skill is essential for evaluating definitions in Islamic sciences.

Accidents Not Divisible into Parts
Author argues that some accidents exist in the subject as a whole, not in each part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify accidents that are not pervading and explain how the author's redefinition resolves the objection.

Core Idea
Some accidents inhere in the whole without being divisible into parts.

In this passage, al-Razi confronts a challenge to the concept of 'pervading accident'. The objector cites unity, point, and relations as accidents that exist in the whole subject but not in each part. Al-Razi responds by refining the definition itself. Let's work through this logical exchange to understand how definitions can be clarified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's examples
Find the three examples given: unity (al-wahda), point (al-nuqta), and relations (al-idafat) like paternity. Notice that they exist in the subject as a whole but not in every part.
These are presented as counterexamples to the claim that all accidents are 'pervading' (sariya).
2
Understand the objector's challenge
The objector argues: Since these accidents exist in the subject but not in each part, they disprove that all accidents exist in every part. The implicit assumption is that 'pervading' means existing in every part.
The objector's reasoning relies on the usual understanding of 'pervading accident'.
3
Analyze the author's redefinition
Al-Razi redefines 'pervading accident' as one that is 'assumed to exist in the parts of its subject' (yufiradu wujuduhu fi ajza' al-mahall). This is a conditional definition: it applies only to accidents that are supposed to be in parts.
By this definition, unity, point, and relations are not intended to be in parts, so they are not counterexamples.
4
Evaluate the author's response
The author notes that the objector's examples presuppose that these accidents should be in every part, then shows they are not. But since they are not of the type that require partitive existence, the objection fails. The redefinition removes the apparent contradiction.
This is a classic move in Islamic logic: clarifying the domain of a definition to avoid false objections.

Al-Razi's Compound Refutation of Void Existence
Al-Razi refutes void using compound reductio arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Razi's compound syllogism against the existence of void.

Core Idea
Al-Razi uses multiple linked reductio arguments to show that assuming void leads to logical absurdities.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a sophisticated chain of arguments to refute the existence of void (al-khalā'). He examines the implications of void in two contexts: through pores in bodies and through the transfer of bodies. By showing that each leads to contradictions, he establishes the impossibility of void. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of these arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
Al-Razi intends to prove that void is impossible. The thesis is stated at the beginning: the refutation of void.
2
Extract the first sub-argument
If void existed through pores, bodies would be composed of scattered points, which is impossible. This is a reductio: assume void through pores → absurd consequence → assumption false.
3
Extract the second sub-argument
If void existed for body transfer, then either interpenetration or circular dependence would occur, both impossible. Another reductio with two branches.
4
Combine the sub-arguments
Both paths lead to impossibility, so the concept of void is universally refuted.

The Circularity Objection in Body Motion
Al-Razi uses conditional reasoning to show circularity in body exchange.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyze al-Razi's circularity argument against the void using conditional reasoning.

Core Idea
Al-Razi proves that if two bodies exchange places, motion becomes circularly dependent, which is impossible.

In his refutation of void, al-Razi presents a powerful argument: if a void existed for bodies to move into, then when two bodies exchange places, each motion would depend on the other, creating an infinite circular regression. This lesson focuses on this specific conditional argument and its logical structure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional statement
Find the conditional in the passage: 'if each of the two transferred to the other's place, then the motion of each would require the motion of the other.' This is the core conditional.
2
Recognize the consequence as impossible
Al-Razi states that this leads to circularity (dawr), which is impossible because it would require each motion to precede itself.
Circularity is generally rejected in kalām.
3
Apply modus tollens
Since the consequent (circularity) is impossible, the antecedent (exchange of places) must also be impossible. Therefore, void cannot allow such exchange.
This is a classic modus tollens argument.
4
Connect to the refutation of void
The impossibility of such exchange supports the overall conclusion that void is impossible, because void would permit such motion.

Razi's Refutation of Atomistic Motion
Author refutes motion composed of indivisible parts via two arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Razi's two arguments against motion being composed of indivisible parts and evaluate their logical validity.

Core Idea
Motion cannot be composed of indivisible parts because space is divisible and continuity requires no gaps.

This lesson examines Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's refutation of the claim that motion is composed of indivisible parts (atomism). He presents two arguments: the first appeals to the divisibility of the spatial distance traversed, the second to the impossibility of transitioning between discrete units without halting motion. Understanding these arguments sharpens your ability to critique philosophical theories using precise logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being refuted
Find the statement that motion is composed of successive indivisible parts (أجزاء لا تتجزأ). This is the target of Razi's critique.
The claim is a form of atomism applied to motion.
2
Extract the first argument
Look for the argument that focuses on the distance (مسافة) being divisible (منقسمة). Razi argues that if motion were composed of indivisible parts, the distance would also have to be indivisible, but it is actually divisible. This is a contradiction.
The first argument relies on the premise that spatial extension is infinitely divisible.
3
Extract the second argument
Identify the argument about continuity: if motion is composed of discrete parts, then at the moment of transitioning from one part to the next, motion would cease (انقطعت الحركة). This is impossible because motion is continuous.
The second argument appeals to the nature of change and motion as a continuous process.
4
Evaluate the logical force
Assess whether each argument validly proves the impossibility. Check if the premises (divisibility of space, continuity of motion) are accepted by the opponent. If they are, the arguments are sound.
Razi's arguments are persuasive within the Aristotelian framework where space and motion are continuous.

Critiquing the First Approach to Motion: Regress and Circularity
Author critiques two approaches to motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify infinite regress (tasalsul) and circular reasoning (dawr) in arguments about motion and explain why they invalidate the argument.

Core Idea
The first approach to motion collapses under its own logical weight—regress and circularity prove it false.

This passage examines multiple ways of understanding motion (ḥaraka). The author first discusses a definition of motion as a relation, then critiques two approaches. The first approach, which he quickly dismisses, falls into two classic logical fallacies: infinite regress (tasalsul) and circularity (dawr). This lesson focuses on identifying these fallacies in the context of defining motion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under critique
The first approach defines motion as a relational attribute. The author asserts that this leads to logical problems.

Locate the fallacies
The text states that the first approach leads to tasalsul and dawr. Tasalsul means an infinite chain of causes, and dawr means the definition presupposes what it tries to prove.
These are classic signs of an invalid argument.
3
Explain why they are problematic
In Islamic logic, an argument that leads to an infinite regress never reaches a foundation, while circular reasoning assumes the conclusion. Both make the argument unsound.
This is a standard refutation technique in kalam.

Motion as Continuous Entity: Defending External Existence
Author critiques two approaches to motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between motion as passage (qat‘) and motion as continuous entity (tawassut), and evaluate which has external existence according to the author.

Core Idea
Motion is real as a continuous entity, not just a mental construct—the author defends its external existence.

After critiquing two approaches, the author clarifies that motion in the sense of 'passage' (qat‘) exists only in the mind, but motion as a continuous transition (tawassut) has real external existence. This lesson focuses on this distinction and the author's justification using sense perception and logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two senses of motion
The author distinguishes between motion as qat‘ (traversal from point A to point B) and motion as tawassut (the continuous state of being between two points).

Understand the status of qat‘
The author concedes that qat‘ has no external existence—it is only a mental concept. Why? Because if the moving object has not yet reached the endpoint, the traversal is incomplete; if it has reached, the traversal is past. So it never exists at any moment.
This is a standard argument that passage is never present.
3
Understand the status of tawassut
The author argues that there is a continuous entity (amr muttāṣil) that endures over time, which is perceived by sight. This is motion itself as a single thing that persists.
This is the motion we actually see.
4
Examine the argument for external existence
The author appeals to sense perception (ḥiss)—we see motion as a continuous thing, not as a series of discrete points. This is a key move in Islamic epistemology.
Sensation is considered a valid source of knowledge in kalam.

Essential vs Accidental Motion
Razi refutes philosophers on essential motion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish essential motion from accidental motion by identifying whether the motion depends on another.

Core Idea
Motion dependent on another is accidental, not essential.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi critiques the philosophers' claim that a body's motion can be essential even if it depends on another body. He argues that true essential motion must be independent, and any dependence renders it accidental.

This is the core criterion.
3
Apply to motion of a body
If a body's motion depends on the motion of its part or another body, then it is not essential but accidental.
Example: motion of a part with the whole is accidental.
4
Recognize the objection
Razi uses this to refute the philosophers' view that all motion is essentially from the body itself.
He concludes that dependent motion cannot be essential.

Core Idea

This passage from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's al-Mulakhkhas fi al-Mantiq wa al-Hikma critiques Thabit ibn Qurra's claim that a stone falls due to attraction by the earth's totality. Al-Razi presents the opposing view, then systematically refutes it with four distinct arguments. By analyzing this refutation, you will learn how mutakallimun defend their theories by exposing logical and empirical flaws in alternatives.

Thabit's view is that the earth's totality attracts the stone, just as it would stay wherever placed in empty space.

The fourth argument is a fundamental attack on the explanatory mechanism itself.

How does the natural inclination theory avoid these problems? It posits an inherent nature (tabi'a) in each element to seek its natural place, which is simple, observable, and does not require an external attractive force.

Commentary
This passage critiques Thabit ibn Qurra's theory of natural motion, specifically the idea that a stone falls to the earth because it is attracted by the totality of the earth. The author first presents the common view: a stone returns to the center because it naturally seeks it; otherwise, its motion would be inexplicable. Then the opposing view is introduced: perhaps the stone seeks the totality of the earth, not a specific center. Thabit ibn Qurra argued that the earth does not seek a particular place; if you imagine empty space and place the earth anywhere, it would stay there. The stone's return is due to seeking the earth's totality. The author then refutes this with four arguments. First, if attraction from the constellations were involved, it would cause absurdities like sticking to the edge of a well. Second, if the whole earth attracts, the entire body cannot avoid that attraction. Third, it would imply that each element forms a solid sphere (the earth a solid ball, water a solid ball, etc.), which contradicts the observed state of elements intermingling. Fourth, the attribution of attraction to the totality of the earth is meaningless because attraction is not a conceivable property of the earth as a whole; only the natural inclination of each element to its proper place remains as the explanation. Thus the author defends the classical natural place theory against an alternative physical model.

Refuting the Principle that a Single Faculty Produces Only One Effect
Critique of the principle of unity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the principle 'from one only one proceeds' and explain the author's critique that a single faculty can have multiple effects through multiple instruments.

Core Idea
The principle 'from one only one proceeds' is false when instruments are multiple; a single faculty can produce varied effects depending on conditions.

The author tackles a key philosophical principle used by opponents to argue that one faculty can only have one function. He shows that this principle does not hold when instruments are multiple, using examples from nature (causing motion and rest) and the rooster's command. This lesson dissects that critique.

Guided Path

State the principle being criticized
The principle is: 'From one only one proceeds' (al-wahid la yasduru 'anh illa al-wahid). Find this phrase in the text.

Refuting the Change Objection: Knowledge as a Relational Attribute
Author responds to the objection that God's knowledge of particulars entails change in the divine essence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the dilemma regarding God's knowledge of particulars and explain how the mutakallimun resolve it by distinguishing essential from relational attributes.

Core Idea
God's knowledge of particulars is relational; relations change without affecting the essence.

This passage captures a classic objection against God's knowledge of particulars: it seems to force a choice between an eternal knowledge that must change or a temporal knowledge that would make God a locus for temporal events. The mutakallimun answer by redefining divine knowledge not as an essential attribute that changes, but as a relational state (idafa) that can be actualized without altering the divine essence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for defending divine immutability in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dilemma
The objection first sets up a dilemma: God's knowledge of particulars must be either eternal or temporal. Recognize this as a disjunctive argument intended to force an impossibility.
The dilemma is a common tool in jadal to corner an opponent.
2
Analyze the first horn
If knowledge is eternal, then the object known (which changes) would require the eternal knowledge to change – impossible. Identify why the object's change is taken to necessitate change in knowledge.
This assumes knowledge is essentially tied to its object.
3
Analyze the second horn
If knowledge is temporal, then temporal events (the knowledge) would inhere in God's essence – also impossible, because God cannot be a locus for temporal phenomena.
This relies on the Ash'ari principle that accidents do not subsist in God.
4
Examine the response
The author refutes the dilemma by redefining knowledge as a relational attribute (hala idafiyya). Relations can be actualized without the essence changing – like becoming a father after being childless does not change your essence.
The key move is to deny that knowledge is an essential attribute that must change when its object changes.
5

So if the Most High Creator were knowledgeable of particulars, that knowledge would be either eternal or temporal. If it is eternal, then the object known would change, thus necessitating change in the eternal knowledge, which is impossible. If it is temporal, then temporal events would inhere in His essence, which is also impossible. The answer: knowledge is a relational state, and change of relations is permissible with respect to Allah Most High, so change of the essence is not necessitated.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic theological objection against God's knowledge of particulars and the response from the perspective of the speculative theologians (mutakallimun). The objection is structured as a dilemma: if God knows particulars, His knowledge must be either eternal or temporal. If eternal, then since particulars change, the object of knowledge changes, which would require the eternal knowledge to change—an impossibility. If temporal, then temporal events would inhere in God's essence, which is also impossible because the divine essence cannot be a locus for temporal events. The response shifts the discussion to the nature of knowledge: knowledge is a relational attribute (al-'ilm al-idafi). Relations can change without affecting the essence. For example, if someone becomes a father after having been childless, the relation changes but the essence remains the same. Similarly, God's knowledge of particulars is a relation to known objects; as those objects come into existence, the relation is actualized without any change in the divine essence. This refutation relies on the key distinction between essential attributes (sifat dhatiyya) and relational attributes (sifat idafiyya).
