The Three Types of Signification: Mutabaqa, Tadamun, Iltizam
Author classifies types of signification for words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the three types of signification (mutabaqa, tadamun, iltizam) and give an Islamic example for each.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: whole, part, or necessary concomitant.

In Islamic logic, understanding how words convey meaning is foundational. This lesson will clarify the three distinct ways a word can indicate its meaning, using examples from Islamic sciences to solidify the concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Locate in the passage the names of the three types of signification: al-mutabaqa, al-tadamun, al-iltizam.
They are introduced immediately after the initial division.
2
Define each type
From the passage and commentary, understand that mutabaqa indicates the whole meaning, tadamun indicates a part, and iltizam indicates a necessary external concomitant.
The passage defines mutabaqa more explicitly; tadamun and iltizam are defined by contrast.
3
Apply to an Islamic term
Take the word 'masjid' (mosque): mutabaqa gives the meaning of a place for prayer, tadamun gives the building materials, iltizam gives the act of prayer.
This shows how a single word can have all three types of signification.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of lafz (word/speech) in logic. For Islamic examples: the word 'mosque' (masjid) has mutabaqa of a place of worship; tadamun of the building; iltizam of prayer. Author defines particular and universal words.

Core Idea
A term is particular if its concept prevents sharing; it is universal if it does not.

Beyond types of signification, words are also classified by their scope. A word can refer to a single individual or to a class. This lesson explains the logical distinction between particular and universal terms, essential for constructing precise definitions and arguments.

Conversion of Modal Propositions Based on Subject Conception
Author explains conversion rules for modal propositions based on subject consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the correct conversion of a modal proposition by analyzing whether its subject is taken according to essence or external existence.

Core Idea
Modal conversion changes modality; it all depends on how you consider the subject.

In non-modal logic, conversion (al-‘aks) preserves truth value. But when modalities like necessity and possibility enter, conversion becomes more nuanced. Fakhr al-Dīn al-Rāzī here teaches that the key factor is how we conceive the subject: as a mere essence or as an actually existing thing. This distinction determines what the converted proposition looks like.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modal type of the original proposition
Note its modality: necessary, possible, conditional, etc. This is the starting point for conversion.
2
Determine how the subject is taken
Is the subject considered as a universal concept (hasaba al-haqiqah) or as an existing entity (hasaba al-wujud al-khariji)? This distinction is crucial for the conversion rule.
3
Apply the conversion rule
If the subject is taken according to essence, the conversion of a particular necessary yields a universal possible. If taken according to external existence, a necessary becomes a general absolute.
4
Check consistency
Verify that the resulting modality matches the pattern given by al-Razi. For example, you should not get a necessary from a possible conversion.

Conversion of Conditioned Propositions: Subject in Essence vs External Existence
Conversion rules for conditioned and possible propositions based on subject existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish whether a proposition's subject is taken in essence (bi-ḥasab al-ḥaqīqa) or in external existence (bi-ḥasab al-wujūd al-khārijī) and apply the correct conversion method for conditioned and possible propositions.

Core Idea
The same proposition converts differently depending on how its subject is taken.

In classical Islamic logic, conversion (ʿaks) is not a mechanical process. Al-Rāzī explains that the method depends on whether we consider the subject term in its essence (its quiddity regardless of existence) or in external existence (only actually existing individuals). This distinction is crucial for converting conditioned and possible propositions correctly. In this lesson, we will unpack al-Rāzī's rules and apply them to examples from Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two ways of taking the subject
Al-Rāzī identifies two approaches: 'according to essence' (bi-ḥasab al-ḥaqīqa) considers the concept of the subject irrespective of whether it has actual instances; 'according to external existence' (bi-ḥasab al-wujūd al-khārijī) considers only those individuals that exist in the external world. This distinction changes how conversion is performed.
For example, the subject 'temporally originated thing' can be taken as a concept (any possible temporal object) or as actually existing objects (like the sun).
2
Identify the conversion rule when the subject is taken in essence
Al-Rāzī states that when the subject of a general conditioned proposition (al-mashrūṭa al-ʿāmma) is taken in essence, it does not convert using the methods of reductio (al-khalf) and supposition (al-iftirāḍ). Those methods only apply when the subject is taken in external existence.
The Arabic: 'فإن كان الأول لم تعكس بالوجوه التي قدمناها من الخلف والافتراض'
3
Identify the conversion rule when the subject is taken in external existence
If the subject is taken in external existence, the conversion uses conditional supposition (al-iftirāḍ al-mashrūṭ). This method involves positing hypothetical instances to infer the converse.
The Arabic: 'وإنما يتقربان إذا أخذنا موضوع القضية بحسب الوجود الخارجي'
4
Apply the distinction to possible propositions
For possible propositions, al-Rāzī applies the same logic. If the subject is taken in essence, the conversion of a necessary general possible yields a general possible. If taken externally, the conversion of various types (general conditioned, perpetual, absolute general, etc.) also yields a general possible.
This demonstrates that the subject's mode fundamentally determines the conversion outcome.

Direct Conversion and Contrapositive in Immediate Inference
Author discusses conditions for direct conversion and contrasts with contrapositive

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct conversion and contrapositive, and identify the condition of preserving quality in immediate inferences.

Core Idea
Direct conversion preserves truth only when quality is equal; contrapositive negates both terms.

In this passage, the author examines two types of immediate inference: direct conversion and contrapositive. He stresses a key condition for direct conversion—preserving the quality of the original proposition—and warns against common mistakes. Through a counterexample involving 'man' and 'laughing', he demonstrates why violating this condition leads to invalid inference. This lesson will help you apply these rules correctly in Islamic logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inference types
Find in the passage where the author mentions 'direct conversion' (العكس المستوى) and 'contrapositive' (عكس النقيض). Note that direct conversion swaps subject and predicate, while contrapositive also negates both terms.
The author contrasts them to highlight when each is valid.
2
Understand the condition for direct conversion
The author states: 'We have conditioned in direct conversion that it be equal to the original in quality.' This means the converted proposition must have the same affirmation/negation as the original. Without this, the inference may be invalid.
Quality refers to whether the proposition is affirmative or negative.
3
Examine the counterexample
The author considers the proposition 'Every man is actually laughing' and tests its contrapositive: 'Everything that is not actually laughing is not a man'. He notes that this fails because a dog is not laughing but is also not a man—so the conclusion is false even if the premise is true. This shows the contrapositive is not always valid for universal affirmatives without restrictions.
The passage also mentions a reductio argument and discusses temporal modifiers, but the core example illustrates the condition.
4
Contrast with contrapositive
Contrapositive (عكس النقيض) negates both the subject and predicate. Unlike direct conversion, it does not require equality in quality—it changes the quality by negation. The author implies that contrapositive must be handled with care, especially regarding universality and quantity.
In Islamic logic, contrapositive is often used in proofs by contradiction.

This passage presents a critical refutation of the view that priority and posteriority (قبلية وبعدية) are existent attributes. The author, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, argues that they are merely relational (إضافات). If they were independent existents, they would have to coexist in their substrates, leading to the impossible situation where something is both prior and posterior simultaneously. Furthermore, if priority were a real attribute, it would have to be either prior, posterior, or simultaneous relative to everything it delimits, which leads to an infinite regress. Even if we grant that this argument proves the existence of time (زمان), it only proves that time has parts that are essentially prior and posterior, which is a separate issue. The argument follows a dialectical pattern: stating a thesis (priority and posteriority are relations), showing the absurdity of the opposing view, and then clarifying the implications.

Key Takeaway
Al-Razi's reductio shows that reifying relations like priority and posteriority leads to logical contradiction; they must be understood as mere relations.
