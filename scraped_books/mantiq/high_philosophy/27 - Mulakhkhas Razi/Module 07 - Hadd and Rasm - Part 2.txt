Learning Objective

Core Idea
Is color real or composite? The answer determines how we classify the accidents of the physical world.

This passage presents two definitions of 'real color' (al-kawn al-haqiqi) that have different implications for how we understand the nature of colors. Identify the two opinions

Classify the definitions as hadd or rasm

Classification of Colors from Mixture of Black and White
Author details the production of various colors from mixing black and white.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the proportions of black and white that produce each intermediate color as described by Razi.

Core Idea
Colors arise from quantitative mixtures of black and white.

In this passage, Razi explains how various colors are generated from the two extremes of whiteness and blackness. Understanding this classification helps us see how definitions in Islamic science often depend on proportional relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extremes
Note that the author starts by stating that colors require light to be actualized and then introduces the two extreme colors: whiteness (bayad) and blackness (sawad). These serve as the foundational poles.
All other colors are mixtures of these two.
2
Spot the equal mixture
When whiteness and blackness are mixed equally, the result is dust color (ghubra). This is the midpoint between the extremes.
No color is ever pure black or white in this scheme.
3
Observe dominance effects
If blackness dominates whiteness, redness (humra) results. If the dominance intensifies, a darker color (perhaps more intense red) appears. If whiteness dominates, yellowness (sufra) results.
Dominance determines the hue family.
4
Trace further mixtures
Yellowness mixed with bright blackness produces greenness (khudra). Greenness with additional blackness yields intense kuhlness (kuhliyya shadida); with whiteness yields another color (uncertain). Further mixtures with black give indigo (niliyya), and indigo with ochre gives violet (urjuwaniyya).
The chain shows how new colors are derived stepwise.
5
Recognize the overarching division
Finally, the author notes that colors are broadly divided into bright (mushriqa) and dark (muzlima), depending on their proximity to white or black.
This binary division is a logical consequence of the two-source model.

Author rejects two definitions of form (surah) and moves to a critical debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to critically evaluate a definition by identifying its weaknesses (e.g., circularity, incompleteness) using the method shown in this passage.

Core Idea
A corrupt definition fails to capture the essence; surah is not simply 'first perfection'.

In this passage, the author rejects two common definitions of surah (form). He calls them corrupt (fasid) without giving extended refutation, because their flaws are evident on inspection. This lesson teaches you to apply the same critical eye to definitions in Islamic sciences, especially in Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions criticized
Find the first definition: surah as 'first perfection of the transparent' (al-kamal al-awwal lil-shaffaf). The second: 'the quality whose sight depends on vision' (al-kayfiyya allati yatawaqqafu ibsaruha 'ala al-ibsar).
The author says 'you have already known the corruption of such definitions' – implying they are obviously flawed.
2
Infer why these definitions are corrupt
The first definition is too broad: many things have a first perfection that are not surah (e.g., transparency itself). The second is circular: it defines form in terms of vision, but vision itself is perception of form.
The author does not elaborate but expects the reader to recognize the deficiencies.
3
Contrast with a sound characterization
Later, the author states that surah is an existential quality (kayfiyya wujudiyya) additional to color. This avoids the pitfalls: it specifies the genus (quality) and differential (additional to color).
This is a hadd haqiqi? Not explicit, but a rasm tamm if it captures the essence.

Apply the critique to a definition from fiqh
Consider the definition: 'Ijtihad is the total expenditure of effort to reach a legal ruling.' Is it complete? Does it exclude effort for non-legal matters? Does it include efforts of the Prophet? Discuss its strengths and weaknesses.
This mirrors the author's method: check if the definition is too broad, too narrow, or circular.

Is Surah an Additional Quality to Color?
Author argues that surah is an existential quality additional to color.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the argument that surah is an existential quality additional to color, using empirical and logical considerations.

Core Idea
Color can exist without shimmer; shimmer can exist without color – thus they are distinct.

The author argues against the view that surah (form/shimmer) is identical to color. Understand the opposing view

Analyze the empirical proof
The author lists examples: shiny objects appear differently in dim light vs. bright light; the same object can seem shiny in one condition and not in another. This variability indicates that surah (the quality of being shiny/radiant) is a separate attribute from color.
He uses a progression from the crown to the moon to the sun to show degrees of appearance.

Consider the role of sense perception
When the sense is weak (darkness), we may perceive a false shininess, but enhancing light removes it. Thus what we thought was surah was actually a perceptual error. Real surah is constant under proper conditions.
This shows that surah is not just brightness; it is an objective quality.

Evaluate the final claim
The author concludes that surah is an existential quality (kayfiyya wujudiyya) additional to color, because whiteness/blackness exist without surah (in darkness) and surah exists without color (e.g., light itself).
This is a key point: if two things can exist independently, they are distinct.

Author answers an objection about the movement of rays.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The objection that rays move is based on a false premise about motion; al-Razi refutes it by questioning the observer's reliability.

After distinguishing light from color, al-Razi anticipates an objection: rays (shu'a') appear to move from the sun, cause effects, and reflect. He answers that the minor premise is false – what we perceive as motion is due to weakness of the eye. This lesson will guide you through the structure of this jadal exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection

3
Analyze the refutation
How does al-Razi refute the objection? He uses the example of the sun rising: light appears all over the earth simultaneously, not traveling. This shows that the apparent movement is an illusion.
This is an empirical counter-argument.

Core Idea
No choice occurs without a cause that tilts the balance.

Distinguishing Essential and Accidental Final Causes
Author divides final cause into essential and accidental with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given effect is an essential or accidental final cause based on conditions of intention and separability.

Core Idea
The same cause can be essential or accidental depending on whether the effect is intended.

Fakhr al-Din al-Razi divides the final cause into two types: essential (dhatiyya) and accidental (ittifaqiyya). Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing causal relationships in Islamic theology and jurisprudence. This lesson will walk you through Razi's criteria and examples so you can apply the distinction to arguments about intention and causality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and effect
In any causal claim, determine what is acting as the cause (sabab) and what is the effect (musabbab). For example, in 'sitting leads to uncovering,' sitting is the cause and uncovering is the effect.
The effect may be unintended.
2
Check if the effect is intended
Ask whether the effect is the purpose for which the cause was undertaken. If yes, the effect is an essential end. If not, it is accidental.
Intention does not require consciousness; it is about inherent or defined purpose.
3
Test for separability
Razi says an accidental cause is one where the effect can be separated from the cause by an equal or greater gap (infikāk musāwin aw akthar). Ask: Could the cause occur without this effect? If easily separable, the effect is accidental.
If all conditions are fulfilled, separation is not possible, making it essential.
4
Apply to the digging example
In the digging for treasure example, notice how the same act (digging) can be accidental if the treasure is not in the digging site, but essential if the digger knows where the treasure is and uses proper tools.
Context and conditions determine the type of final cause.

Core Idea
Nature acts for purposes, but not every natural outcome is intended.

In this passage, the author engages with a key debate in Islamic natural philosophy: do natural motions have intrinsic ends (ghayat dhatiyya)? He offers a subtle argument using the simple example of a grain of wheat growing into an ear under suitable conditions. The author concludes that only motions whose outcomes are consistent (occur always or mostly) have intrinsic ends. The grain example belongs to this category; medicines, whose effects are inconsistent, do not.

Core Idea
Motion is impossible because its existence in the present leads to contradiction.

After presenting Plato's definition, the author introduces a proof attributed to Eudoxus that seeks to show motion cannot exist. The argument is a classic dilemma: either motion exists in the present or not, and both options lead to absurdity. If motion does not exist in the present, then it cannot exist in the past or future either, because those are defined relative to the present. Thus, motion does not exist at all.

4

Is the disjunction exhaustive? Does the argument commit a fallacy like false dichotomy? What assumptions about the nature of time and the present does it rely on?
The argument resembles Zeno's paradoxes and presupposes an atomistic view of time.

Qualitative Change: Gradual Perception vs. Instantaneous Reality
Author argues that qualitative change is instantaneous in reality, not gradual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the compound argument that qualitative change is instantaneous and evaluate its strength using the visual ray analogy.

Core Idea
Perception can deceive: what seems gradual may be instantaneous and vice versa.

This passage challenges our everyday experience of change. The author, building on Ibn Sina, argues that qualitative change – like heat, color, or the formation of an animal – occurs in an instant, not bit by bit. He uses a clever argument about the visual ray to show that even what appears instantaneous can have imperceptible duration, and conversely, what appears gradual can involve an instantaneous core event. Let's dissect this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
Read the opening line: 'not everything that appears instantaneous is actually so, and vice versa.' This is the thesis the author will prove. Note that it is a double claim: some apparent instant events have duration, and some apparent gradual events are instant.
This is the key logical move: perception is not a reliable guide to the nature of change.
2
Analyze the visual ray proof
The author presents Ibn Sina's argument: if the visual ray travels from the eye to objects, then the time to reach the fixed stars should be proportionally much longer than to a near object. Yet when we open our eyes, we see both instantly. The conclusion is that the time to the stars is imperceptibly short, so what appears instantaneous actually has duration.
This is a reductio ad absurdum of the assumption that perceived instantaneity implies real instantaneity.
3
Apply the reverse example
The author gives the example of an embryo developing into an animal. We see gradual growth, but the 'animal form' (the rational soul) is imparted instantaneously. Thus what appears gradual can involve an instantaneous event.
This balances the argument: the principle works both ways.
4
Draw the conclusion
The author concludes that one cannot rely on apparent gradualness to argue about the nature of qualitative change. The underlying reality may be instantaneous, even if perception suggests otherwise.
This has implications for natural philosophy and kalam debates about creation.

The third chapter on its types: motion is either one in person, or in species, or in genus. As for personal unity, it is realized when there is unity of subject and what it is in. Unity of subject and time requires unity of every part, because it is impossible for one thing to be in two places or to restore what is non-existent. And it is necessary that the motion's what-it-is-in be one, because a mover can traverse a distance and yet grow, and the beginning may be different, so it is not one motion. Thus the motion is one only if the mover and subject are one and the motion is continuous. The unity of the mover is not indicative, because if we have a mover moving a body and then another mover moves it at the same boundary, the motion is one, unless the division destroys the continuous unity, like celestial motion which has divisions due to spaces.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of unity of motion. The author identifies three: personal (الشخصية), specific (النوعية), and generic (الجنسية). For personal unity, two conditions are necessary: the subject (الموضوع) must be one, and the 'what it is in' (ما فيه الحركة) must also be one. This means that the moving body must be the same throughout, and the kind of motion (place, quality, etc.) must also be the same. The author explains that if a mover traverses a distance while simultaneously growing or changing in quality, the motion is not one in person because the beginning and end differ. Also, the moving agent can change without breaking unity, as long as the motion remains continuous. The example of celestial motion is given: although it appears continuous, it may have divisions due to the spaces involved.
