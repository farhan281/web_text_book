Classification of Attributes: Internal vs. External
Author classifies names/attributes into internal and external, with subclasses of external attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given attribute as internal or external, and further categorize external attributes into negative, relative, real, or combinations thereof.

Core Idea
Attributes are either essential (internal) or accidental (external), and external ones break into negatives, relatives, and reals.

In this passage, the author lays out a foundational classification of attributes (sifāt) that every logician must master. Whether you are analyzing a definition or discussing divine attributes, this taxonomy helps you pinpoint exactly what kind of property is being asserted. Let's walk through the categories step by step.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish internal from external
The name of a thing indicates either something internal to its essence (dākhil) or something external (khārij). Internal attributes are essential; external are accidental.
Internal attributes are discussed elsewhere; focus on external here.
2
Recognize the three basic external types
External attributes are either negative (salbiyya, e.g., 'not-corporeal'), relative (iḍāfiyya, e.g., 'father'), or real (ḥaqīqiyya, e.g., 'living').
Real attributes are positive determinations.
3
Handle combined attributes
External attributes can be simple or composite, e.g., negative+relative, relative+real, etc. The author lists all seven possibilities (3 simple + 4 composite).
Combinations are formed by conjoining the basic types.
4
Apply to the divine attributes controversy
The philosophers (ḥukamā') deny real attributes for God because of divine simplicity. This classification reveals that for God, only negative and relative attributes are accepted by them.
This distinction is central to Kalam debates on divine attributes.

Classifying Good and Evil: Pure, Mixed, and Impossible
Author classifies good and evil into pure good, pure evil, and mixed, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of good and evil according to Razi and explain why pure evil is impossible.

Core Idea
Pure evil cannot exist; every existent has some good.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi classifies good and evil into three categories: pure good, pure evil (which he argues is impossible), and mixed. He then argues that in all created things, good predominates. This classification is not merely philosophical but has theological implications for understanding divine wisdom in allowing evil.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage divides things into pure good (al-khayr al-mahd), pure evil (al-sharr al-mahd), and mixed (good in one aspect, evil in another). Locate the sentence that introduces this division.
The division is introduced after 'ثم اعم قالوا تفريعات هذا الأصل'.
2
Define pure good
Pure good is defined as that which cannot be described by non-existence. The author identifies the Necessary Existent (God) and all His attributes as pure good. Find the sentence and note the reasoning.
The text says: 'فالخير المحض فهو الذي لا يتيح العدم عليه والعلا شئ من صفاته منو الموجود الواجب لذاته'.
3
Explain why pure evil is impossible
The author states pure evil is impossible because insofar as something exists, it has some good. Find the line 'و اما السر الحق فهو محال' and restate the argument in your own words.
The argument hinges on the equivalence of existence and good; even a harmful thing has some benefit (e.g., fire burns but also provides heat and light).
4
Analyze the subcategories of mixed
Mixed good and evil is further divided into equal, good predominant, and evil predominant. The author argues that in all existents besides God, good is predominant. List the three types of existents he gives as examples.
The examples: separate substances (intellects, souls), bodies (celestial spheres), and elements (earth, water, etc.).
5
Answer the objection
The objection is: if evil is necessary, why create it? The author responds that avoiding the greater good due to a lesser evil is itself a greater evil. Find the rhetorical question and response.
The response uses the example of fire: its benefits outweigh the harm it causes to some animals.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional classification of good and evil. The author first notes that the discussion is verbal, depending on whether evil is defined as non-existence or as pain (which is existential). Then he presents a threefold division: things are either pure good (only God), pure evil (impossible), or mixed (good and evil combined). The mixed is subdivided into equal, good predominant, and evil predominant. The author argues that in all existents other than God, good predominates, giving examples: separate substances (intellects, souls), bodies (spheres), and elements. He answers an objection: if evil is necessary, it is not a defect in the Creator because the evil is inseparable from the greater good (e.g., fire burns but its benefits outweigh). The classification is systematic and uses logical division.
