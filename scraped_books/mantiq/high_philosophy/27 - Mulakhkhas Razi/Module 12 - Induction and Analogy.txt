Complete Induction vs. Analogy: Certainty in Reasoning
Author compares complete induction to divisive syllogism and critiques analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish complete induction from analogy and explain why analogy is considered weak in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Complete induction is deductive; analogy is uncertain.

In this passage, the author clarifies two non-demonstrative reasoning methods used in Islamic law: complete induction (al-istiqrā' al-'āmm) and analogy (al-tamthīl). He argues that complete induction, if exhaustive, is actually a valid syllogism. However, analogy is weak because the attribute used to transfer a ruling may not be the complete cause. This distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two reasoning types
The passage contrasts 'complete induction' (istiqra' 'amm) and 'analogy' (tamthil). Note that the author treats complete induction as a divisive syllogism, while analogy is criticized.
Divisive syllogism (qiyas muqassim) is a deductive argument that exhaustively divides a genus into its species.
2
Understand the equivalence of complete induction to divisive syllogism
Complete induction examines every individual of a class. If all individuals share a property, the conclusion is universal. For example, if every intoxicant (wine, beer, hashish) is forbidden, then 'all intoxicants are forbidden' follows deductively.
This is valid only if the enumeration is truly exhaustive.
3
Analyze the critique of analogy
The author says analogy is weak because its effect would only be realized if we already knew that wherever the attribute (e.g., intoxication) exists, the ruling (forbiddance) exists. But that requires knowing the ruling in the branch (far') beforehand, making the argument circular. Moreover, the attribute might be only part of the cause, a condition, a confirmer, or a remover of an obstacle—not the complete 'illa.
This critique is common in usul al-fiqh debates about the validity of qiyas.
4
Apply the critique to a fiqh example
Consider the analogy: 'Wine is forbidden because it intoxicates; therefore, nabidh (date wine) is forbidden because it intoxicates.' The critique asks: Is intoxication the complete cause? Perhaps it is only a condition, or the real cause is something else. Thus, the analogy is not certain.
This shows why scholars differ on the strength of analogical reasoning in Islamic law.

Commentary
This passage critiques two forms of non-demonstrative reasoning used in Islamic law: complete induction (istiqra') and analogy (tamthil/qiyas). The author states that complete induction (enumerating all individuals) is actually a divisive syllogism (qiyas muqassim) — a valid deductive argument. For example, if we examine every type of intoxicant and find all are forbidden, we can deduce 'Every intoxicant is forbidden'. That's valid only if the induction is exhaustive. On the other hand, analogy (used in fiqh) is weak (da'if) because it relies on a common attribute to transfer a ruling from a known case (asl) to a new case (far'). The critique is that for analogy to be valid, we would need to establish that wherever the attribute exists, the ruling exists; but that requires already knowing the ruling in the branch, making the analogy circular. Moreover, the attribute might be merely part of the cause, a condition, a confirmer, or remove an obstacle, but not the complete cause ('illa). The author thus questions the certainty of analogical reasoning, a common topic in usul al-fiqh debates.

Definition and Conditions of Tamthil
Definition of tamthil and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tamthil and identify its two essential conditions.

Core Idea
Tamthil transfers a ruling via a shared cause, not mere resemblance.

How do Islamic jurists extend rulings from known cases to new ones? The answer lies in tamthil, or analogical reasoning. But this is not a free-form comparison—it requires a precise causal link. In this passage, the author defines tamthil and sets its necessary conditions, grounding us in a core tool of Islamic legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic. What is the author calling 'tamthil'?
The term appears at the start: 'فهو التمثيل'.
2
Extract the definition
The author says tamthil is 'to judge on the disputed case similar to the judgment on the agreed case due to a shared meaning'. Note the key components: a disputed case, an agreed case, a shared meaning.
The shared meaning (ma'na mushtarak) is central.
3
Find the two conditions
The author then states 'its condition: that the common attribute be a cause for the ruling in the original, and that there be nothing in the branch that prevents the ruling.' Identify these as the two conditions.
First condition: causality of the attribute in the original. Second condition: no impediment in the branch.
4
Distinguish from universal induction
The author begins by contrasting tamthil with universal induction (istiqra' 'amm). Understand that tamthil is non-universal induction, while universal induction leads to qiyas muqassam (divisive syllogism).
This distinction prevents confusing tamthil with deductive syllogisms.

Proving Causality and the Infinite Regress Objection
Discussion of the method to prove causality and the infinite regress objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection against causal tamthil and evaluate the response regarding infinite regress.

Core Idea
The cause must be limited to specified categories to avoid infinite regress.

Once the conditions of tamthil are established, a deeper question arises: how do we know that the shared attribute is truly the cause? The logicians propose methods to prove causality. However, a powerful objection surfaces: if every ruling requires a cause, that cause would itself need a cause, leading to an infinite chain. The author presents a response that appeals to the finite categories of causes. Let's examine this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The passage says: 'if the ruling were caused by a cause, an infinite regress would follow'. Understand the problem: if every ruling must have a cause, then the cause itself is a ruling that needs a cause, and so on ad infinitum.
This is a classic philosophical problem (tasalsul).
2
Find the first response
The author answers: 'the cause is limited to the divisions mentioned'. This means the categories of causes (e.g., attributes like intoxication) are finite, so the regress stops at the fundamental causes.
The 'divisions mentioned' refer to earlier sections of the text (not in this chunk).
3
Refine the response
The author adds: 'even if the exclusivity is granted, it is not that the cause is the sum of them all, but it is permissible that the cause be the sum of some of them, not each one individually.' This addresses a potential misinterpretation: the cause might be a combination of attributes, not each one singly.
This nuance prevents the objection that if the cause is a single attribute, it might not be exhaustive.
4
Evaluate the argument
Assess whether the response successfully blocks the infinite regress. Does limiting causes to finite categories solve the problem? Consider whether the categories themselves might still require justification.
This step encourages critical thinking about the strength of the response.

Refuting Anaxagoras Through Empirical Transformation
Empirical refutation of Anaxagoras' mixture theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's empirical objection to Anaxagoras' mixture theory and evaluate its strength.

Core Idea
Anaxagoras is refuted by observable transformation, not mere combination.

The author tackles Anaxagoras' ancient theory that every substance contains parts of every other substance, and that similars attract to produce a new nature. This lesson shows how a simple empirical observation—food changes into something entirely different—undermines the theory and supports genuine transformation (istihala).

Guided Path
1
Identify Anaxagoras' claim
Anaxagoras held that all things contain minute parts of all substances (e.g., bone, flesh, apple, garment). These parts are extremely small and when many gather, similars attract and a nature appears.
2
Find the author's empirical objection
The author points to food turning into something else (e.g., into vinegar or other substances). If the original mixtures existed with their qualities in the food, we would perceive them; since we do not, the theory fails.
3
Understand the epistemological principle
The author relies on sensory evidence that qualities change. He also mentions colors, tastes, and odors changing, which necessitates transformation (istihala), not mere latency (kumun) and emergence.
4
Evaluate the objection's strength
The argument is inductive and empirical. It assumes that if something were present, it would be observable. Is this always valid? Consider cases where qualities might be masked. The author treats this as decisive.

Inductive Proof: Composites from Earth and Water
Inductive demonstration of elemental composition via synthesis and analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two inductive methods (analysis and synthesis) to demonstrate elemental composition.

Core Idea
Analysis and synthesis are the two empirical pillars of elemental induction.

After refuting Anaxagoras, the author provides a positive proof that all composites are ultimately composed of earth and water. Using two complementary inductive methods—synthesis (tracing from animal back to earth) and analysis (burning or distilling an organ)—he establishes the elemental basis.

Guided Path
1
Understand the synthesis argument (tarkib)
Trace the chain: animal arises from blood, blood from food, food from plants, plants from earth and water. Thus the composite (animal) ultimately derives from earth and water.
2
Understand the analysis argument (tahlil)
Take an organ (e.g., flesh) and distill or burn it. The products are earth and water. This shows the organ's constituents.
3
Recognize the inductive logic
Both methods are inductive: from specific instances (this animal, this organ) we generalize that all composites are earthy and watery. The author uses two independent lines to strengthen the conclusion.

Inductive Proof of the Four Primary Qualities
Inductive enumeration proving the four primary elements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the inductive enumeration used to prove that the four primary tangible qualities (heat, cold, moisture, dryness) are the basis of physical transformation.

Core Idea
Induction enumerates all tangible qualities to isolate the four that cause interaction.

The author uses a complete induction (istiqrā' tāmm) of tangible qualities to establish that the four primary elements—fire, air, water, earth—are the fundamental constituents of all physical compounds. This lesson unpacks how he eliminates secondary qualities and reduces the list to four.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and method
The author aims to prove that all physical compounds reduce to four elements. He uses two methods: (1) analysis/composition observations, and (2) induction of qualities. Your task is to spot the inductive step where he lists all tangible qualities and eliminates non-essential ones.
The claim is that heat, cold, moisture, dryness are the only primary qualities.
2
Identify the induction's domain
The author restricts induction to tangible qualities because only these cause physical interaction (tafā'ul). He explicitly excludes colors, tastes, smells as they do not produce transformation. Notice that this restriction is crucial for the completeness of the induction.
The domain is 'tangible qualities' (al-malmūsa).
3
Distinguish primary from secondary
The author lists secondary tangible qualities like shape, roughness, smoothness, etc., and argues they are not suitable because they are not subject to intensity (shidda/ḍa'f) and are shared. He eliminates them. Your task: confirm that these secondary qualities cannot cause transformation according to the text.
Secondary qualities are non-gradable and universal, hence cannot explain change.
4
Reduce the primary list
The author lists ten primary qualities: subtlety, density, dryness, wetness, viscosity, friability, hardness, softness, roughness, smoothness. He then reduces them: subtlety/density/wetness/dryness refer to moisture/dryness; the others are qualitative/positional. Follow the reduction to see that only four remain.
The logical reduction is based on categorization: some are qualitative, some positional.
5
Conclude that only four remain
After exhaustive induction, no other quality remains suitable for interaction. The author concludes that the four primary qualities (heat, cold, moisture, dryness) are the only ones that cause transformation, thus proving the four elements.
This induction is presented as complete and certain (yaqīnī).

Commentary
This passage presents an inductive argument to prove that the four elements (fire, air, water, earth) are the primary constituents of all physical compounds. The author begins by stating that analysis (breaking down compounds) and composition (building up) both indicate that compounds ultimately reduce to these four. He gives an example: if you take a homogeneous organ and subject it to water and air, you get earth and water (i.e., the simpler components). He also observes that colors, tastes, and smells can change into one another, which proves that transformation (istihāla) occurs, implying underlying common elements. Then he uses induction (istiqrā') to examine all perceptible qualities. He notes that only tangible qualities (al-malmūsa) are relevant for interaction (tafā'ul). Among tangible qualities, he distinguishes primary from secondary. Secondary qualities like shape (shakl) are not suitable because they are not subject to intensity and are shared; they do not cause transformation. Induction and the Four Elements: Al-Razi's Argument for Material Generation
Al-Razi argues for the necessity of the four elements using induction and rebuts the fire objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an inductive argument (istiqra') for the necessity of the four elements in generation and evaluate its completeness.

Core Idea
Induction reveals the pattern of creation—complete generables arise only from the four elements.

In this passage, Fakhr al-Din al-Razi tackles a tricky question: does the element of fire actually enter into the composition of living bodies? He uses a mix of deductive reasoning about heat and an inductive argument from observation to conclude that the four elements are necessary for generation. This lesson dissects his argument and shows how induction (istiqra') works in natural philosophy, a key tool in Islamic kalam and falsafa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Find the central thesis al-Razi is trying to prove. It is stated in the last part: 'complete generables occur only when these four are present'.
The claim is about the necessity of the four elements (earth, water, air, fire) for generation.
2
Analyze the objection
What objection does al-Razi address? Someone might argue that fire is not needed because animals and plants do not contain elemental fire, only accidental heat.
Al-Razi concedes accidental heat is of the same genus as fiery heat but argues that elemental fire cannot persist in composites.
3
Examine the rebuttal
Al-Razi gives two reasons: (1) flames extinguish when separated from fuel and become air; (2) small fiery particles cannot survive mixed with watery and earthy opposites over time.
This rebuttal uses empirical observation about fire's nature.
4
Extract the inductive inference
After the rebuttal, al-Razi states that 'complete induction indicates that some complete generables occur only when these four are present.' Here 'complete induction' (istiqra' 'amm) refers to an enumeration of observed cases.
He does not list specific cases but implies that empirical observation supports the generalization.
5
Evaluate the induction
Is this induction complete or incomplete? Since we cannot observe all generables, it is incomplete. Al-Razi seems to present it as decisive, but later logicians might question its certainty.
In Islamic logic, incomplete induction yields probability, not certainty. Al-Razi's use of 'complete' may be rhetorical.
