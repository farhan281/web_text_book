Learning Objective

Core Idea
Correct manuscript attribution is essential to studying the Islamic intellectual tradition.

This passage from a critical edition corrects a recurring error: manuscripts of Shams al-Din al-Samarqandi's Adab al-Bahth are often mislabeled as al-Mantiq al-Kabir by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. Understanding these two works and their proper attributions is crucial for anyone studying Islamic logic and dialectics, as confusion can lead to erroneous conclusions about a scholar's contributions.

2

Al-Razi's work belongs to the formal logic tradition (mantiq); al-Samarqandi's work belongs to the dialectical tradition (jadal/munazara).
3
Examine the misattribution pattern
The passage lists three manuscripts (numbers 430, 2783, 2389) that were mislabeled as al-Mantiq al-Kabir but are actually Adab al-Bahth. Notice the repeated phrase 'also' indicating this is a common error.
4
Understand the significance

Pitfalls in Manuscript Attribution: The Case of al-Mantiq al-Kabir
Critique of manuscript misattribution and its causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the reliability of a manuscript attribution by identifying common errors such as overreliance on opening phrases and secondary sources.

Core Idea
A shared incipit does not prove shared authorship.

In the Islamic scholarly tradition, the authenticity of a text depends on accurate manuscript attribution. This passage reveals a concrete example where three copies of a work on dialectical disputation (Adab al-Bahth wa al-Munazara) were mistakenly cataloged as al-Razi's al-Mantiq al-Kabir because both begin with the same phrase: 'al-Hamdu li-Wahib al-'Aql'. Understanding why this error occurred will sharpen your ability to critically assess manuscript records and avoid similar mistakes in your own research.

The opening sentence (al-jumla al-iftitahiyya) of both works is identical: 'al-Hamdu li-Wahib al-'Aql'. The passage argues that such similarity can be a factor in miscataloging.

The presence of 'al-Imam' for al-Razi in those notes indicates al-Kashi's respect for al-Razi as his teacher. Thus, the core text is likely al-Razi's own composition.

Understanding the Manuscript Basis of al-Mantiq al-Kabir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key features of the only surviving manuscript of al-Mantiq al-Kabir and explain how its editorial history affects its reliability as a source for Razi's logic.

Core Idea
A single manuscript, corrected by a student of the author, can be highly reliable.

The passage you are about to read describes the editorial methodology applied to the unique manuscript of Razi's al-Mantiq al-Kabir. Understanding this process is crucial for evaluating the text's authenticity and accuracy. You will learn how the manuscript, though alone, was corrected by experts including a student of Razi, and how it relates to Ibn Sina's foundational works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the manuscript's uniqueness
Note that the book has only one surviving copy. This is both a limitation and a focus for careful study.
A single copy means no cross-verification with other witnesses, but also fewer textual variants.
2
Recognize the editorial process
The manuscript was edited as part of a PhD dissertation, with mutual checking by a professor. Errors were corrected and readings discussed.
This shows the importance of collaborative scholarly verification.
3
Note the connection to Ibn Sina's works
The passage states that al-Mantiq al-Kabir was composed relying on Ibn Sina's al-Shifa and al-Isharat wa al-Tanbihat. This provides a textual basis for corrections.
Comparison with these sources helps identify and correct potential errors.
4
Assess the role of early readers
Zayn al-Din al-Kashi, a student of Razi, read and corrected the manuscript. This early intervention resulted in few spelling or verbal errors.
Al-Kashi's corrections add authority to the text.
5
Identify physical and editorial features
The manuscript lacks modern paragraphing and dotting (diacritics), has repeated pages (70a/71b), and uses red ink for subtitles. The editor added some marginal headings in brackets.
These features affect readability and interpretation; the editor's additions must be distinguished from the original.
