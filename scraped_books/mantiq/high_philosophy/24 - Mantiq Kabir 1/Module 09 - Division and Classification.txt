General and Specific Possibility: al-Imkan al-'Amm wa al-Khas
Author distinguishes two definitions of possibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between general possibility (al-imkan al-'amm) and specific possibility (al-imkan al-khas) and apply them to Islamic modal categories (wajib, mumkin, mumtani').

Core Idea
General possibility includes the necessary; specific possibility only the contingent.

The science of logic defines possibility in multiple ways. This lesson focuses on the first two meanings presented: the general (al-'amm) and the specific (al-khas). Understanding the difference is crucial for precise theological arguments about God's existence and attributes.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two definitions
Read the passage carefully where it says: 'إما أن يعنى به ما يلازم سلب الضرورة العدم' (general) and 'إما أن يعنى به ما يلازم سلب ضرورة العدم والوجود جميعا' (specific). These are the two definitions.
The definitions are presented as two alternatives after 'إما'.
2
Test the scope with the necessary being
Apply the first definition: is the necessary (wajib) not impossible? Yes, so it qualifies as possible in the general sense. Apply the second: is the necessary neither necessary nor impossible? No, it is necessary, so it is not possible in the specific sense.
The passage explicitly says 'وهذا الإمكان من جملة ما يحمل على الواجب'.
3
Contrast with impossibility
The first definition makes only the impossible non-possible. The second definition makes both necessary and impossible non-possible. Identify what each definition includes.
This step clarifies why there are three categories under specific possibility: necessary, possible, impossible.
4
Recognize the naming convention
The passage names the first 'general' (عام/عامي) and the second 'specific' (خاص/خاصي). Note the shift in usage: in theology, 'imkan' usually refers to the specific meaning.

Commentary
This passage defines three distinct meanings of the term 'imkan' (possibility) as understood in modal logic. The author begins by referencing a discussion in another work (al-Mantiq al-Kabir) and then explains two primary meanings. The first meaning (al-imkan al-'amm) is defined as what accompanies the negation of the necessity of non-being; that is, something is possible in this sense if it is not impossible. Under this definition, everything that is not possible is impossible, and even the necessary being (al-wajib) can be called possible in a certain sense because it is not impossible. However, the author clarifies that this general possibility actually applies to both sides of affirmation and negation: a thing can be said to be possible to exist and possible not to exist. But the necessary being does not have this duality. The second meaning (al-imkan al-khas) is what accompanies the negation of the necessity of both being and non-being; that is, a thing is possible in this specific sense if it is neither necessary nor impossible. This is the common understanding of possibility as contingency. The author notes that these two are named 'general' and 'specific' respectively. Then an objection is raised: if possibility is defined as what accompanies the negation of necessity, then it cannot be merely the negation itself because that would be a self-accompaniment. The author refutes this by explaining that general possibility is not the negation itself but that which accompanies it, i.e., a broader concept that includes necessity and specific possibility. The objection that this would make the necessary composite is dismissed because accompaniment does not imply composition. Finally, a third meaning is introduced: al-imkan al-akhass, which is the negation of necessity from every aspect, completely free from any necessity. This is the purest form of possibility because it has no admixture of necessity. The passage thus lays out the modal framework necessary for understanding discussions of existence and causation.

The Objection and the Purest Possibility: al-Imkan al-Akhas
Author refines the definition of possibility and introduces a third meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the logical objection that possibility cannot be merely the negation of necessity, and explain the third meaning of possibility (al-akhass) as the most exclusive.

Core Idea
Pure possibility negates necessity from every angle—essential, temporal, and circumstantial.

After establishing two meanings of possibility, the passage addresses a subtle objection: if possibility is defined as what accompanies the negation of necessity, then it cannot be that negation itself. The author resolves this and introduces a third, more exclusive meaning that eliminates all forms of necessity.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the objection
The passage records: 'وقد قيل في قولهم...' The objection states that if possibility is defined as what accompanies the negation of necessity, then it would be the same as the negation itself, which is impossible because a thing cannot accompany itself.
The objection is based on logical self-reference.
2
Follow the author's resolution
The author replies that general possibility is not the negation itself but that which accompanies it. Hence the objection fails because accompaniment does not require identity.
The author uses the distinction between a property and what it accompanies.
3
Address the composition worry
The objector might fear that if the necessary being is described by this accompanying notion, it would be composite. The author dismisses this because accompaniment does not entail part-whole inclusion.
This preserves divine simplicity.
4
Identify the third meaning
The passage then introduces al-imkan al-akhass: 'سلب الضرورة من كل وجه'—negation of necessity from every aspect. This meaning excludes all necessity, even accidental necessity (like the necessity of a past event).
The passage calls this 'أحق باسم الإمكان' (more deserving of the name possibility).
5
Contrast the three meanings
Under general, the necessary is possible. Under specific, only contingent. Under the most specific, nothing that has any necessity (even from time or description) is possible. This is the purest modal contingency.

Binary Successive Division in Logical Classification
Author explains that division must be binary stepwise.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of binary successive division to classify concepts in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
True division is binary and successive, not direct multiple splits.

This passage introduces a fundamental rule of logical division: you cannot split a genus directly into three or more species. Instead, you must first divide into two, then continue dividing each part. This principle ensures clarity and completeness in classification, as seen in the three types of indication which result from two binary divisions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the rule
The author states that any concept (indication or otherwise) cannot be divided into three parts all at once. Instead, it must first be divided into two parts, then each part subdivided. This is the method of binary successive division.
This prevents missing intermediate categories.
2
Apply to the given example
The three types of indication (discussed in the chapter) are not obtained by a single threefold split. They require two binary divisions. For instance, first divide indication into conventional and non-conventional, then further divide one of those.
The passage does not specify the exact binary splits, but the principle is clear.
3
Generalize the pattern
The author extends the pattern: a threefold classification needs two divisions, a fourfold needs three divisions, and so on. This is a general rule for any existential division.
Note: the parts may include negations (absence of the rest).

Fourfold Division of the Simple Word
Author divides the simple word into four classes based on unity/multiplicity of word and meaning, then subdivides the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the four types of simple words based on unity and multiplicity of word and meaning, and justify why this division is exhaustive (qisma 'aqliyya).

Core Idea
The simple word divides into exactly four types by combining one/multiple word and one/multiple meaning.

The simple word (mufrad) can be analyzed by two features: whether the word itself is one or multiple, and whether its meaning is one or multiple. This yields a rational division that covers all possibilities. Mastering this classification is essential for understanding how terms function in Islamic sciences, especially when definitions and legal rulings hinge on the precise type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two criteria
The division uses two attributes: the word's multiplicity/unity and the meaning's multiplicity/unity. Recognize that each can be either one (muttaḥid) or multiple (mutakaththir).
2
Enumerate the four combinations
Logically, there are four possibilities: (1) both one, (2) both multiple, (3) word one, meaning multiple, (4) word multiple, meaning one. Write them down and label them.
Use the Arabic markers: أ, ب, ت, ث as in the text.

Types of Words: Simple and Compound
Author classifies words as simple or compound in word and meaning, with the reverse compound word simple meaning impossible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any word into one of three logical categories based on its form and meaning, and justify why the fourth category is impossible.

Core Idea
A word can be simple or compound in form AND meaning, but never compound in form with simple meaning.

This passage establishes a foundational classification in Islamic logic: words can be simple or compound both in their vocal form and in the meaning they convey. Understanding these three possibilities and the impossibility of the fourth is essential for analyzing definitions and propositions accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word's form
Determine whether the word is a single unit (mufrad) like 'الجوهر' or composed of multiple meaningful parts (murakkab) like 'العالم حادث'.
Form is about the vocal structure, not the meaning.
2
Identify the word's meaning
Is the meaning simple (one concept) or compound (a composite of multiple concepts)? For example, 'الجوهر' has a simple meaning, while 'الأسود' when used as a name for a composite essence has a compound meaning.
Meanings can be composite even if the word form is simple.
3
Determine the combination
Map form and meaning: both simple (type 1), both compound (type 2), or simple form with compound meaning (type 3). These are the only possibilities.
The fourth combination (compound form, simple meaning) is impossible as argued.
4
Exclude the impossible
Why can a compound form never signify a simple meaning? If the word names the thing with all its parts, it is simple; if it names only some parts, the rest are neglected (muhmal) and cannot naturally combine with the used part.
This logical argument prevents a category error.

Unitary Terms: Proper Names, Common Terms, and Graded Terms
Author subdivides the first category (both word and meaning singular) into three.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish proper names (‛alam), common terms (mutawāṭi'), and graded terms (mushakkik) within the category of terms whose word and meaning are both singular.

Core Idea
Even when both word and meaning are single, a term can be exclusive, equal, or graded in its application.

After establishing the fourfold division, the author focuses on the first category: terms where both word and meaning are singular. He further subdivides these into three types based on how the meaning relates to individuals. Is the concept designed to prevent sharing, like a proper name? Or is it shared equally, like 'animal'? Or does it admit degrees, like 'existence'? This nuance is crucial for understanding how terms function in definitions and syllogisms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types within unitary terms
The author says the first category (both singular) has three possibilities: (1) the concept prevents sharing (proper names); (2) the concept is shared equally among individuals (mutawāṭi'); (3) the concept is shared but with variation (mushakkik).
These are the three subcategories.
2
Analyze the proper name ('alam)
Proper names like 'Zayd' or 'this man' have a concept that designates one specific individual. The passage argues that such a term cannot be predicated of multiple individuals – it is manā' 'an al-sharika (prevents sharing).
Think of proper names as exclusive labels.
3
Analyze the common term (mutawāṭi')
Terms like 'animal' or 'man' have a meaning that applies equally to all individuals. The author stresses equality: the mosquito is as much an animal as the elephant; the female is as human as the male.
This is univocal predication.
4
Analyze the graded term (mushakkik)
Terms like 'existence' (wujūd) or 'blackness' (sawād) admit degrees: one existence can be prior, another posterior; one black can be blacker than another. The variation comes from accidental factors of the subject.
This is analogical predication.

ثم المركب من الألفاظ حكمه حكم المفرد منها في أنه لا يدل من حيث هو هو إلا بالتواطؤ ; وقد مرّ معنى التواطؤ من قبل. ولا يقال كيف هو والتأليف بين الألفاظ المفردة على هيئة مخصوصة لا يكون بالتواطؤ بل المعنى الذي يتولد من المعاني المفردة يقتضي ذلك بعينه فإن التغير في المفرد الذي منه غيره مما يقتضي التغير في الغير بالتواطؤ أيضًا. فأما نفس التركيب فلا يمكن أن يكون بالتواطؤ لاستحالة التغير فيه وإن كانت هيئة التركيب تغيرت بحسب لغة لغةً; فإن المضاف إليه مثلا يؤخر في لغة ويقدم في لغة. وكذلك الموضوع والمحمول فلا ضرورة لترتيبها في الطبع. وما قيل في الفرق بين المركب والمؤلف فقد مرَّ في بيان المفرد. ولما كانت المركبات محتاجًا إليها لدلالتها على ما هو في النفس من المعاني. وإنها متركبة من المفردات لا محالة فكانت مختلفة تارة بحسب اللفظ لاختلاف الألفاظ المفردة تارة بحسب المعنى لاختلاف المعاني الموجودة في النفس : أما بحسب اللفظ فإن منها ما يكون تاما; أي تام الدلالة فهو الذي يدل كل جزء منه بانفراده على معنى مستقل بنفسه كقولك كاتب «زيد و زيد كاتب مثلا، ومنها ما يكون ناقصا; أي ناقص الدلالة وهو الذي لا يدل جزء منه بانفراده كما يدل في التام مثل قولك زيد كان إذا كان في شك أن يقول كان مريضًا لا أنه كان في ذاته فإن كان مما يتم دلالته حينئذ، وأما الحصر فيهما فظاهر. وأما بحسب المعنى، فإن منها ما يكون تقييديا، وهو الذي يتركب على طريق الحدود والرسوم بأن يكون بعض أجزائه مقيدًا بالبعض، فيصلح أن يورد بين أجزائه لفظة «الذي»، كقولنا الحيوان الناطق». فإنه يصلح أن يقال فيه الحيوان الذي هو الناطق»، وإنه أي التقييدي في قوة لفظ مفرد يعني يمكن أن يقوم مقامه مفرد يفهم منه ما يفهم من ذلك، نحو قولنا الإنسان فإنه
Then the compound of expressions has the same ruling as the simple among them, in that it does not indicate by itself except by convention (bi al-tawātu'); and the meaning of tawātu' has passed before. And do not say: how is that? For the combination of simple expressions in a specific form is not by convention; rather, the meaning that is generated from the simple meanings necessitates that very thing. For the change in the simple that changes it is something that necessitates change in the other by convention as well. As for the composition itself, it cannot be by convention because of the impossibility of change in it, even though the form of composition changes according to each language. For example, the genitive (al-muḍāf ilayh) is postponed in one language and advanced in another. Likewise, subject and predicate – their order is not necessary by nature. And what has been said about the difference between compound (murakkab) and composite (mu'allaf) has passed in the explanation of the simple. Since compounds are needed to indicate what is in the soul of meanings, and they are necessarily composed of simples, they differ: sometimes according to wording due to the difference of simple expressions, and sometimes according to meaning due to the difference of meanings present in the soul. As for according to wording, among them there is what is complete (tāmm), i.e., complete in indication, which is that every part of it individually indicates an independent meaning, like your saying 'Kātib Zayd' or 'Zayd Kātib' for example. And among them there is what is incomplete (nāqiṣ), i.e., incomplete in indication, which is that its part does not individually indicate as it indicates in the complete, like your saying 'Zayd kāna' when one doubts that he says 'kāna marīḍan' (he was sick) not that he was in himself; then it becomes complete in indication at that time. As for the restriction to these two (complete and incomplete), it is obvious. And as for according to meaning, among them there is what is restrictive (taqyīdī), which is composed in the manner of definitions and descriptions, such that some of its parts are restricted by others, so that the word 'alladhī' (which) can be inserted between its parts, like our saying 'al-ḥayawān al-nāṭiq'. For it is permissible to say about it 'al-ḥayawān alladhī huwa al-nāṭiq'. And this restrictive equivalent is in the power of a simple word, meaning that a simple word can take its place and one understands from it what one understands from that, like our saying 'al-insān' – for it...

Commentary
This passage is a classification and explanation of compound expressions (المركب من الألفاظ) in logic. It begins by stating that compound expressions, like simple ones, indicate only by convention (تواطؤ) – i.e., by linguistic agreement, not by nature. The author anticipates an objection: the combination of words in a specific structure might seem conventional, but he clarifies that the individual words are conventional, while the composition itself is not conventional because the arrangement (e.g., word order) can vary across languages without altering the generated meaning. For example, in some languages the genitive comes first, in others last; similarly, subject and predicate order is not fixed by nature. The author then notes that the difference between compound and composite was discussed earlier. The main body of the passage divides compounds in two ways: by wording (لفظ) and by meaning (معنى). By wording, compounds are either complete (تام) or incomplete (ناقص). A complete compound is one where every part individually indicates an independent meaning, like 'كاتب زيد' (Zayd is writing) or 'زيد كاتب' (Zayd is a writer). An incomplete compound is one where a part does not individually indicate an independent meaning, like 'زيد كان' (Zayd was) – it requires completion (e.g., 'كان مريضا' – he was sick) to become fully indicative. By meaning, compounds are divided into restrictive (تقييدي), which is the type used in definitions and descriptions where one part restricts another, such that the word 'الذي' (which) can be inserted, e.g., 'الحيوان الناطق' (the speaking animal) can be rephrased as 'الحيوان الذي هو الناطق' (the animal that is the speaker). This restrictive compound is equivalent in meaning to a simple word like 'الإنسان' (human). The passage ends mid-sentence with 'نحو قولنا الإنسان فإنه' (like our saying 'al-insān' for it...), suggesting further examples. Throughout, the author uses examples from Arabic grammar and logic to illustrate the types.
