Contrariety and Intensity in Change: Whitening and Blackening
Author discusses contrariety and intensity in processes of change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contrariety and the two types of intensity (proximity to endpoint and speed) in change processes using the examples of whitening and blackening.

Core Idea
Change processes, like blackening, admit intensity both by closeness to the endpoint and by speed.

This lesson examines how processes of change exhibit contrariety and degrees of intensity. The author argues that just as black and white are contraries, so are the processes of blackening and whitening. These processes can be more or less intense in two ways: by proximity to the final state or by speed. Understanding this analysis is crucial for later discussions in kalam about accidental change and in usul al-fiqh about the intensity of legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contraries
Black and white are contraries; similarly, the processes of blackening and whitening are contraries.
2
Note the logical relationship
Contrariety of states implies contrariety of processes that lead to those states.
3
Recognize two types of intensity
Intensity can be by proximity to the endpoint (e.g., closer to pure black) or by speed (faster change).
4
Distinguish stable state from process
The stable state (e.g., blackness) is different from the process (blackening); the process does not require conceiving of the endpoint in the same way.

Aristotle's Objection: Circular Definition of Motion and Time
Author presents Aristotle's objection and a response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the circularity in defining motion as gradual when 'gradual' requires time, and evaluate the proposed response that gradualness is intuitively understood without definition.

Core Idea
Defining motion as gradual risks circularity if 'gradual' requires time, but the response argues that gradualness is a primitive concept.

This lesson analyzes Aristotle's objection to the definition of motion as 'the gradual exit from potentiality to actuality'. The objection is that 'gradual' (tadrij) can only be defined by time, and time can only be defined by motion, creating a vicious circle. The author presents a response: the concepts of instantaneous and non-instantaneous are self-evident; no definition is needed. This exchange illuminates important issues about definitional foundations in logic and physics.

Guided Path
1
State the contested definition
Motion is defined as 'the gradual exit from potentiality to actuality'.
2
Identify Aristotle's objection
The term 'gradual' is defined by time, and time is defined by motion, so the definition is circular.
3
Understand the response
The conception of 'instantaneous' and 'non-instantaneous' is itself a gradual notion, meaning it is directly perceived and does not require definition.
4
Evaluate the response
Consider whether 'gradual' is truly primitive or still depends on time. Does the response successfully avoid circularity?

Motion as First Perfection: Aristotle's Alternative and Its Critique
Author presents Aristotle's definition of motion as first perfection and a critique.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Aristotle's alternative definition of motion as a first perfection and identify the subsequent critique that this definition is more obscure than motion itself.

Core Idea
Aristotle defined motion as a 'first perfection' of a body in potentiality, but this definition is criticized for being even more obscure.

In response to the circularity objection, Aristotle proposed an alternative definition: motion is a 'first perfection' (al-kamal al-awwal) of a body that is in potentiality insofar as it is in potentiality. This means motion is an actuality that remains partly potential. However, this definition is criticized: it is more obscure than the original, and the notion of 'first perfection' may itself imply gradualness. This lesson examines the definition and the critique, highlighting challenges in defining fundamental concepts.

Guided Path
1
Understand Aristotle's reasoning
Motion is a perfection of a body, but unlike others, it leads to something else and remains partly potential.
2
Grasp the concept of 'first perfection'
It is the actuality of a potential as potential (e.g., the ability to move is a perfection that still points to further perfection).
3
Identify the critique
The definition is 'more obscure' than motion itself, and the 'first perfection' seems to imply gradualness, which is not necessarily true for all motions.
4
Evaluate the definitions
Compare the original gradual definition and the first perfection definition, considering the objections to each.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional analysis of the decisive statement (al-qawl al-jazim), which is the proposition (qadiyya). The author begins by distinguishing between categorical (hamli) and conditional (shartiyya) propositions. He notes that in Greek, conditional propositions require existential words indicating relation and time, while in Arabic, only conditionals need such words (e.g., 'if it is', 'whenever it is', 'either it is or it is'). Categoricals in Arabic do not require a copula; rather their essential pillars are matter and form. The matter is the subject (mawdu') and predicate (mahmul), while the form is the relation (nisba) between them, which is distinct from each. These three—subject, predicate, and relation—compose any categorical proposition. The author then clarifies that merely assembling these three concepts in the mind does not suffice; the mind must actively judge the attachment of predicate to subject, whether affirmatively or negatively. This is because the thing in itself is not the same as the thing considered as subject or predicate; the thing can be known without knowing its role in a proposition. Utterance (lafz) imitates what is in the mind, so it must indicate three things: the subject, the predicate, and the relation between them. The indicator of the relation is not a necessary part of the utterance; it is often omitted in Arabic, relying on context. For example, 'al-insan katib' (the human is a writer) uses a verbal form to imply relation, while 'al-insan hayawan' (the human is an animal) has no explicit copula. Commentary
This passage defines and critiques the ways in which universality (الكلي) can be signified. It opens by mentioning three modes: correspondence (مطابقة), inclusion (تضمن), and implication (التزام). The author quickly dismisses inclusion because it would make universality part of the essence, which is impossible since particular instances like 'this man' lack universality. Then he dismisses implication because it requires the mind to necessarily move from the essence to the universal, which does not happen for all essences. However, he allows implication for clear cases. The discussion then shifts to the linguistic expression 'لا واحد من الناس' (No one of people) and compares it to 'ليس أحد من الناس' (Not one of people). He argues that 'لا واحد' expresses a real universal negation, while 'ليس أحد' is actually a particular negation despite appearances. The author refutes those who claim that 'لا واحد' necessitates universality by wording or usage, stating it is not known. The key takeaway is that true universality is not merely a linguistic form but requires that the mind can move from the essence to the universal without obstruction.

Modality: Distinguishing Darura, Dawam, and Itlaq
Defines al-jiha and distinguishes darura, dawam, and itlaq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish darura, dawam, and itlaq al-'amm in modal propositions and identify their hierarchical relations.

Core Idea
Modality in logic is graded: necessity (darura) is stricter than perpetuity (dawam), and both fall under the general absolute.

In this passage, the author introduces the concept of modality (al-jiha) and explains how judgments can be qualified. You will learn the key distinction between necessity and perpetuity, and how they relate to the unmodalized absolute. This hierarchy is foundational for modal syllogisms in Islamic logic.
