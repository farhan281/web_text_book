Absolute and Conditioned Necessity in Modal Logic
Classifies necessity into absolute and conditioned, with critique of Ibn Sina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between absolute necessity (al-mutlaqa) and conditioned necessity (al-mu'allaqa) and identify which modal propositions fall under each type.

Core Idea
Absolute necessity is eternal and unconditioned; conditioned necessity depends on the subject's existence.

In this passage, the author begins classifying necessity (darura) into two fundamental types: absolute (mutlaqa) and conditioned (mu'allaqa). Absolute necessity applies only to necessary existents like God, where the judgment holds eternally without any condition. Conditioned necessity applies to contingent beings, where the truth of the proposition depends on the existence of the subject. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating modal syllogisms in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of necessity
Find where the author introduces 'mutlaqa' and 'mu'allaqa'. Note the defining feature: unconditioned vs conditioned.
2
Examine the absolute necessity example
Read the example about God: 'Allah is living, knowing, powerful'. Why is this absolute? Because God's existence is eternal and unlimited.
3
Examine the conditioned necessity example
Read 'Man is necessarily an animal'. The author argues this is conditioned because man is not eternal; the necessity depends on the existence of man.
4
Understand the disagreement with Ibn Sina
The author notes that Ibn Sina considered the man-animal proposition as unconditioned, but the author argues it must be conditioned, otherwise contradictions arise.

Commentary
This passage expands on the classification of necessity (darura). The author first distinguishes absolute necessity (mutlaqa), where the judgment holds eternally unconditionally, as in statements about God. Then he turns to conditioned necessity (mu'allaqa), which is further divided based on the nature of the condition. The condition can be the perpetuity of the subject's essence (such as 'man is necessarily an animal'), which is actually a common type requiring the subject to exist. The author notes a disagreement: Ibn Sina and the author of al-Basa'ir considered this type as unconditioned, but the correct view is that it is conditioned, otherwise contradictions arise. Next, the condition can relate to a description of the subject. If the description is essential (like 'humanity' for man), the necessity is equivalent to absolute necessity because the description always accompanies the subject. If the description is non-essential (like 'writer' for man), we get either the general conditioned (where the predicate holds as long as the description exists) or the special conditioned (where the predicate does not hold perpetually but only when the description exists). Similarly for conditions on the predicate side. This detailed taxonomy is foundational for modal syllogistic.

Conditioned Necessity Based on Subject Description
Further subdivides conditioned necessity by condition on subject

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditioned necessity based on the subject's essence and based on a description, and identify general conditioned and special conditioned propositions.

Core Idea
Conditioned necessity can be based on essence or description; essential description yields absolute-like necessity, accidental yields general/special conditioned.

Having established conditioned necessity, the author now subdivides it according to the nature of the condition. If the condition is the essence's existence, the necessity is straightforward but still conditioned. If the condition is a description, we must consider whether the description is essential or accidental. For accidental descriptions, the proposition may be general conditioned (al-mashruta al-'amma) or special conditioned (al-mashruta al-khassa). This taxonomy is vital for analyzing modal propositions in kalam and fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first subtype: condition on essence
Find the example 'Man is necessarily an animal'. The condition is the existence of man's essence. Note: this is distinct from absolute necessity.
2
Identify the second subtype: condition on description
The author describes two possibilities: the description endures with the subject's essence (essential description) or not (accidental description). For essential description, the necessity is like absolute.
3
Understand general vs special conditioned
For accidental description, if the predicate holds only as long as the description persists, it is al-mashruta al-'amma. If it also specifies that it does not hold perpetually, it is al-mashruta al-khassa.

General Possibility (al-imkan al-‘amm)
Defines general possibility and its scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define general possibility (imkan ‘amm) and identify its scope, including its application to the Necessary Being.

Core Idea
General possibility is simply non-impossibility; even God is possible in this sense.

The author introduces two senses of possibility. The first, general possibility, is the broader concept that simply negates impossibility. This understanding is crucial because it includes the Necessary Being (God) under the term 'possible', which might seem counterintuitive. Let’s parse this concept carefully.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of general possibility
Find the phrase that defines the first type: 'that which entails the negation of the necessity of non-existence, that is, impossibility.' This is the core meaning.
2
Understand the equivalence with non-impossibility
Note the author's statement: 'everything that is not possible is impossible.' This means that possible and impossible are complementary under this definition, leaving no middle ground.
3
Identify its scope: includes the Necessary Being
Find the line: 'This possibility is among what is predicated of the Necessary Being.' This shows that God, being necessary, is still 'possible' in the general sense because it is not impossible.
4
Recognize why it is called 'general'
The author says it is attributed to 'generality' or 'commonality' because it applies widely, even to the necessary. Contrast with everyday usage that restricts possibility to contingent things.

As for possibility, it is either meant as that which entails the negation of the necessity of non-existence, that is, impossibility, according to what it is used for in the first convention; and there, everything that is not possible is impossible. This possibility is among what is predicated of the Necessary Being. Or it is meant as that which entails the negation of the necessity of both non-existence and existence, according to what it is used for by transference, such that a thing can be described by the first possibility in both negative and affirmative sides: one says 'it is possible that it is' and 'possible that it is not,' i.e., not impossible that it is and not impossible that it is not. The Logical Role of 'Inasmuch as' in Essential Predication
Author analyzes the logical role of 'inasmuch as' in predication, clarifying subject and predicate parts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In essential predication, 'inasmuch as' belongs to the predicate, not the subject.

In the study of definition (hadd), we often encounter statements like 'A inasmuch as it is A is B'. A key logical question is: where does the phrase 'inasmuch as it is' attach? Does it modify the subject or the predicate? The author of this passage clarifies that when we strip accidents and consider things purely by their essence, this phrase is part of the predicate, because the predicate expresses the definition or a part of the definition of the subject. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing countless arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence where essential predications are made.

Guided Path
1
