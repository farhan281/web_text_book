The Objection: 'The Verb Does Not Inform' Is Itself Informative
A dialectical exchange: an objector argues that saying 'a verb is not informative' is itself informative; author responds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical objection about the verb's informativeness and identify the key conceptual distinction used in the response.

Core Idea
A meta-statement about a verb's non-informativeness may itself be informative unless we distinguish the word from its referent.

This passage presents a common dialectical move in Islamic logic: an objector seizes on a claim and tries to show it is self-refuting. Here, the claim is that verbs do not inform (do not predicate). The objector argues that the very statement 'verbs do not inform' is itself informative, creating a contradiction. The author's response distinguishes between the noun 'verb' and the verb as a verb, and introduces the concept of tasawwur (conceptualization). This exchange illustrates how careful terminological distinctions resolve apparent paradoxes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
Recognize the original position: that verbs do not inform (i.e., they do not constitute informative propositions). The objector challenges this.
The claim is about the nature of verbs as carriers of information.
2
Unpack the objection
The objector says: 'the speaker's statement that it does not inform is itself information about it.' This is a meta-argument: the assertion about verbs is itself a piece of information, thus contradicting the claim that verbs are not informative.
The objection exploits the fact that the statement is a sentence containing the word 'verb'.
3
Analyze the author's response
The author considers two interpretations of 'the verb' in the statement. If 'the verb' is taken as a noun (the word itself), then the statement is about a noun, not a verb, so it does not contradict. If it is taken as a verb, then the statement is indeed about a verb, but then the verb as a verb is either conceptualized (mutasawwar) or not — implying that information can be about a concept without being a proposition.
The response relies on a distinction between the linguistic term and its referent.
4
Identify the deeper point about tasawwur
The author mentions that the verb, as a verb, is either mutasawwar (conceptualized). This hints that the statement may be about the concept of the verb rather than an actual verb in a proposition, thus avoiding self-refutation.
This connects to the broader epistemological distinction between tasawwur (concept) and tasdiq (assent).

Commentary
This passage presents a dialectical objection regarding the nature of verbs and information. The objector argues that if someone states 'the verb does not inform (does not give information)', that very statement is informative about the verb, leading to a contradiction. The objection then considers two possibilities: if the thing informed about is a noun (i.e., the word 'verb' is a noun), then the statement is false because it is about a noun, not a verb; if it is a verb, then the statement is indeed about a verb. The author then adds another point: the verb, as a verb, is either conceptualized (tasawwur) or not. The text cuts off at 'and then...' so the full response is not given. This shows the intersection of logic (propositions and information) with grammar.

Is the Differentia a Cause? A Dialectical Analysis
Author presents two views on whether differentia is a cause and refutes the second.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical argument on whether the differentia is a cause and identify the logical weakness in the opposing view.

Core Idea
The differentia causes the species' specificity; the opposing view collapses into self-contradiction.

In this passage from the Mantiq Kabir, the author stages a debate about the causal status of the differentia (fasl). Some argue that the differentia must be a cause for the species' distinctiveness within the genus; others claim it is merely an attribute and thus cannot be a cause. The author dismisses the second view as very weak. This debate is not just abstract—it mirrors discussions in usul al-fiqh about whether the 'illa in analogy is truly a cause. By working through this argument, you will sharpen your ability to evaluate dialectical exchanges and spot self-defeating positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Locate the two groups mentioned: those who affirm the differentia as a cause, and those who deny its causality. Mark their respective claims in the Arabic.
The first group says 'must be a cause'; the second says 'does not have to be a cause'.
2
Analyze the first argument
Follow the reasoning for the first position: the genus cannot become a specific species without the differentia. Thus the species' subsistence depends on it, making it a cause.
Think of an example: 'animal' becomes 'human' only by the differentia 'rational'.
3
Analyze the second argument
The second group argues that the differentia could be an attribute (sifa) that depends on the subject, and what depends on something cannot be a cause of it.
They are reasoning by analogy: if the differentia is like an attribute, it cannot cause the subject's specificity.
4
Examine the refutation
The author calls the second view 'very weak' and points out that if the differentia were not a cause, it would still be a cause of what it depends on—which is contradictory.
The author's point is that the differentia is what gives the species its specificity, so it cannot depend on the species for existence.
5
Assess the outcome
Conclude which position survives the critique. The first view remains valid because the differentia is indeed the cause of the species' specificity, and the objection is self-refuting.
This outcome reinforces the foundational role of the differentia in definition.

And it is not possible that this part which is the differentia is a cause for the specificity of the species from the genus according to some; rather, it must be a cause, for the genus cannot be a specific species nor have a specificity for that species except by the existence of the differentia, so its subsistence is by it, and then it necessarily follows that it is a cause. And according to some others, it does not have to be a cause for the specificity of the species from the genus, because the differentia may be an attribute as has passed before, and an attribute is in need of the described in existence, and that which is in need of something cannot be a cause of that thing, but this is very weak. For if it were a cause, it would be a cause of that which it needs in existence, namely the specificity.

Commentary
This passage presents a logical debate (jadal) concerning whether the differentia is a cause ('illa) for the specificity of the species within the genus. The author states that some scholars affirm that it must be a cause, because without the differentia, the genus cannot become a specific species; the differentia is what makes the genus determinate. For example, 'animal' becomes 'human' only by the differentia 'rational'. Others argue that it is not necessary, because the differentia can be an attribute (sifa) that depends on the subject (the species), and what depends on something cannot be its cause. The author dismisses this second view as very weak (da‘if jiddan), because if the differentia were not a cause, it would be a cause of that which it depends on, which is contradictory. In Islamic sciences, this could be related to the debate on whether the differentia in legal analogy ('illa) is a cause of the ruling.
