The Proper Scope of Logic
Author warns against burdening logic with non-logical sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the proper scope of logic as a tool science and distinguish it from real sciences.

Core Idea
Logic is a tool, not a catch-all science.

The author issues a crucial methodological warning: logic should not be overloaded with topics that belong to other sciences. He points out that many relations considered in logical discussions are actually 'imaginary additions' whose full explanation lies in the real sciences. This lesson helps you understand the boundaries of logic so that you use it as an instrument, not a substitute for substantive inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the warning
Find the phrase 'واعلم أن جميع أمثال هذه إضافات وهمية' – the author is stating that all such things are imaginary additions. This is a caution against treating them as real logical subject matter.
The phrase 'أمثال هذه' refers to earlier examples like opposites.
2
Understand 'imaginary additions'
Recognize that 'إضافات وهمية' are relations that exist only in the mind. They are not external realities but mental constructs. The author distinguishes them from the real relations studied in other sciences.
Examples include the relation of fatherhood when considered abstractly.
3
Grasp the scope of logic
Notice that the author says 'فلا على المنطقي أن يتكلف في المنطق علما هو غير المنطق' – the logician should not burden himself with a science that is not logic. This defines logic as a tool that deals with the form of arguments, not the content of other sciences.
Logic analyzes validity, not the truth of premises about the world.
4
Apply the principle
Consider how this principle prevents confusion. For example, a logician does not need to study the physical nature of substances to determine the validity of a syllogism about them. That is the job of physics.
This keeps logic focused and prevents it from becoming a catch-all.

Defining the Scope of Logic: What Mantiq Does and Does Not Study
Author distinguishes the proper subject of mantiq from psychology and lexicography.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the subject matter of logic from psychology and lexicography by applying the author's criteria of indication (dalāla).

Core Idea
Logic studies how words indicate meanings, not how the mind works or why a word means what it does.

Logic is not a science of the soul, nor a lexicon of terms. In this passage, the author draws sharp boundaries: the proper subject of mantiq is the state of the word in indicating simple and compound meanings, because that is what leads to knowledge of the unknown. Understanding these boundaries prevents confusion with other disciplines and clarifies the unique role of logic in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify what is excluded from logic
Read the passage and list the two inquiries the author explicitly excludes: (1) how the soul perceives images of things (psychology), and (2) which specific word or writing indicates a particular meaning (lexicography).
The author says 'ليس من هذه الصناعة' for both.
2
Identify what is included in logic
Find the phrase that states the proper subject: 'بل منها أن يعرف حال اللفظ من حيث إنه يدل على المعاني المفردة أو المركبة'. This is the core of logic: the indication of words, whether individual or compound.
The goal 'ليتوصل بذلك إلى المعاني من حيث يتألف منها ما يفيد علمًا بما هو غير معلوم' explains why this is useful.
3
Analyze the key terms 'mufrad' and 'murakkab'
Note that the passage distinguishes between simple (مفرد) and compound (مركب) meanings. Logic deals with how compound expressions are built from simples and how they produce knowledge.
This distinction is foundational for later discussions of propositions and syllogisms.
4
Apply the distinction to example inquiries
Given a list of inquiries, decide whether each belongs to logic, psychology, or lexicography according to the author's criteria. For instance: 'How does memory retain concepts?' is psychology; 'What does the Arabic word 'ribā' literally mean?' is lexicography; 'How does a universal proposition indicate its parts?' is logic.
This step solidifies the boundary.
