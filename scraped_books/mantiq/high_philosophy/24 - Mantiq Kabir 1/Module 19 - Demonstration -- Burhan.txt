Existence Is Not Univocally Predicated on the Categories
Author argues existence is not a genus via predication types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between univocal, analogical, and equivocal predication and identify why existence does not fit univocal predication over the categories.

Core Idea
Existence is predicated analogically, not univocally—substance has priority over accident.

In this passage, the author sets out to prove that existence (al-mawjud) is not a genus for the ten categories. One of his key moves is to examine the type of predication involved. He distinguishes three modes: univocal (tawatu’), analogical (tashkik), and equivocal (ittifaq). By showing that existence is predicated of substance and accident with priority and posteriority, he demonstrates that it cannot be a univocal genus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three modes of predication
The author says that if existence is predicated of the ten categories, it must be either bi al-tawatu’ (univocally), bi al-tashkik (analogically), or bi al-ittifaq al-sarf (pure equivocation). Notice the distinction: univocal means one meaning applied equally; analogical means with priority and posteriority; equivocal means completely different meanings.
2
Understand the proof for the difference in the meaning of existence
The author gives a proof: substance exists by itself, accident exists by another; therefore, the meaning of existence cannot be identical. This shows that substance has a prior claim to existence.
3
Recognize that this shows analogical predication
Since substance is prior, existence is predicated of substance with priority and of accident with posteriority. This is exactly what analogical predication (tashkik) means.
In later Islamic metaphysics, this is often used to argue that existence is a transcendental concept.

Commentary
This passage critically refutes the claim that existence (al-mawjud) is a genus for the ten categories. The author begins by noting that existence is predicated of the ten categories, but this predication is not univocal (bi al-tawatu'); rather it is by analogy (al-tashkik) or equivocation (al-ittifaq). The first argument: if existence had a single meaning for all categories, then substance (jawhar) and accident ('arad) would not differ in existence; but they do—substance exists by itself, accident exists by another. Therefore, the meaning of existence is not the same. The second argument: if existence were a genus, it would be internal to the quiddity (mahiyya) of its species, like shape (shakliyya) is internal to triangle. But we can conceive a triangle without knowing whether it actually exists; hence existence is not part of its quiddity. Thus existence is an accident, not a genus. The third argument: if existence were a genus, its differentia (fasl) would either be existent or non-existent. If existent, then the differentia would already have existence and thus the genus would apply, causing the differentia to replace the species; if non-existent, the differentia would not distinguish anything. So existence cannot be a genus. The author uses the example of triangle from Euclid to illustrate that shape is essential to triangle (internal), while existence is external. This refutation relies on standard Aristotelian logic but applies it to the metaphysics of the categories.

Existence Is External to Quiddity
Author argues existence is external to quiddity using triangle example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why existence cannot be part of the quiddity (essence) of a thing, using the triangle example.

Core Idea
Existence is not part of a thing's quiddity—we can conceive a triangle without existence, but not without shape.

The author uses a powerful example from geometry to illustrate that existence is not internal to quiddity. He contrasts shape, which is essential to triangle, with existence, which is not. This shows that existence is not a genus, because a genus must be part of the essence of its species.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between essential and accidental attributes
The author says shape (shakliyya) is internal to triangle—you cannot conceive a triangle without shape. Existence, on the other hand, is not internal because you can conceive a triangle while doubting its existence.
2
Analyze the triangle example
When you imagine a triangle, you must imagine it as having shape, but you might not know if it actually exists. Existence is proven separately, e.g., from Euclid's first figure.
3
Conclude that existence is not part of the quiddity
If existence were part of the quiddity, it could not be separated in conception. Hence existence is an accident, not a genus.
This argument is foundational for the distinction between essence and existence in Islamic philosophy.

The Differentia Argument Against Existence as a Genus
Author uses differentia dilemma to disprove existence as genus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the differentia argument against existence as a genus and evaluate its validity.

Core Idea
If existence were a genus, its differentia would either be existent or non-existent—both impossible.

The author presents a concise proof by dilemma. If existence were a genus, it must have a differentia that distinguishes its species. That differentia would either exist or not exist. If it exists, then the differentia itself falls under the genus existence, leading to a regress. If it does not exist, then the differentia is nothing, so it cannot distinguish species. Hence existence cannot be a genus.

Guided Path
1
Assume existence is a genus
Let us grant, for the sake of argument, that existence is a genus for the ten categories. Then it must have a differentia that divides it into species.
2
Consider the two cases for the differentia
The differentia itself is a concept; it either exists or does not exist. These are the only possibilities.
3
Analyze the first case: the differentia exists
If the differentia exists, then existence (the genus) is predicated of it. But then the differentia would itself be a species of the genus, so it would play the role of a species, not a differentia. This is circular.
4
Analyze the second case: the differentia does not exist
If the differentia does not exist, then it has no reality, so it cannot effect any distinction. A non-existent differentia is meaningless.
5
Conclude that existence cannot be a genus
Both possibilities lead to absurdity, so the assumption is false.
This argument is a classic reductio ad absurdum.

Commentary
This passage critically refutes the claim that existence (al-mawjud) is a genus for the ten categories. Commentary
This passage addresses a critical objection regarding the relationship between the concepts of necessary existence (wajib al-wujud) and impossible non-existence (mumtani' al-'adam). The objector claims that 'necessary to exist' does not entail 'impossible not to exist' unless the two are different in meaning, because a thing cannot be a necessary consequence of itself. The author responds by clarifying that a consequent need not differ from its antecedent in all respects—only in some respect. Here, necessity and impossibility are indeed different: necessity emphasizes existence, impossibility emphasizes non-existence. The author then presents a standard answer: necessity and impossibility are essentially different, so they are not identical. For the second objection, the author notes that if necessity is defined merely as the negation of the necessity of non-existence, then it would be identical to impossibility; but if necessity is something that entails that negation (i.e., a positive existential attribute), then they are different. The author, however, criticizes this response because, on the philosophers' view, necessity is not an existential attribute. Thus the passage engages in a nuanced logical and metaphysical discussion about the nature of necessity and impossibility.
