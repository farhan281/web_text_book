Causality and Attributes: The Relational Nature of Knowledge
Author explains why both relata are needed for causal knowledge and how attributes attach to quiddity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why knowledge of a cause alone is insufficient for affirming causality and distinguish between essential and accidental attributes in quiddity.

Core Idea
Causality is relational: you cannot know the causal link without conceiving both cause and effect.

This passage from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's commentary on Ibn Sina's Isharat unpacks a crucial epistemological principle: to know that A causes B, you must have a conception of both A and B. This principle has deep implications for how we define things and how attributes attach to quiddities. Let's walk through his argument step by step, using his own examples from natural philosophy and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about causality
Al-Razi begins by stating that knowledge of the effect requires another condition: the conception of the effect. The quiddity of the cause alone is insufficient for causality because causality is a relational (idafi) matter.
2
Understand the relational nature
Relational attributes (al-umur al-idafiyya) require both relata. For causality, you need both cause and effect conceived. If the cause's essence alone does not guarantee the attribute of causality, then knowing the cause alone does not give knowledge of causality.
This parallels the need for both relata in other relational attributes like fatherhood.
3
Apply to knowledge of the effect
Since knowledge of causality requires both relata, knowing only the cause does not necessitate knowing the effect. However, when both are conceived, the mind must assent to the causal relation between them.
Al-Razi gives the example: knowing heat (cause) and the sensation of heat (effect) together yields the judgment that heat causes the sensation.
4
Extend to attributes attaching to quiddity
Attributes (a'rad) attach to quiddity only after its realization. They cannot be constituents (muqawwimat) of quiddity, or else an infinite regress would result. For example, the triangle's attributes like having three sides are not constituents but necessary concomitants.
5
Distinguish types of attributes and the role of intermediates
Some attributes are necessary (lazim) and attach directly (without middle) or through an intermediate (wasat) that is self-evident. The intermediate must be self-evident to avoid infinite regress. Knowledge of quiddity can be either by essence (e.g., heat sensed) or by properties (e.g., the world's origination by proofs).
This distinction underlies the two types of definitions: hadd (essential) and rasm (descriptive).

Therefore, it is necessary that attributes be either with a middle, as mentioned, or without a middle. And it cannot be said that that middle is not a self-evident attribute unless knowledge of the quiddity is a cause of knowledge of its proximate attribute, and that is impossible; otherwise all attributes would be known and self-evident in attachment. For if it is proximate among the attributes, then it is not self-evident in attachment to its subject unless it is present in the mind, and that is known by reflection. Then knowledge of the quiddity, whether the quiddity is simple or composite, may be according to its essence or may not be, but rather according to what pertains to it. So it is known either by its essence or by its concomitants and attributes. For if we know heat by sense, for example, that is knowledge of the thing in terms of its specific reality and determinate quiddity, and it is the perfection of degrees of knowledge according to most [scholars]. As for if we know that the world is originated by the proofs indicating it, we do not know what the originator is, although it is known that the world is originated by its bringing into existence; and if that were the case, then the sought [answer] in the question about the simple quiddity is either the first of them, as when asked about heat and it is said 'that which a human perceives by the sense of touch when contacting the body of fire,' or the second question, as when asked about the originator and it is said 'the originator is that for which the thing transferred from non-existence to existence.' And similarly for the composite, for the sought is either the first of them or the second. And that will be fully known in the definitions by genus and differentia and by description later, God willing.

Commentary
This passage from Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's commentary on Ibn Sina's Isharat is an epistemological and definitional discussion. The author begins by stating that knowledge of the cause alone does not yield knowledge of causality; one must also conceive the effect. He explains that causality is a relational notion requiring both relata. This principle is then applied to the attributes (a'rad) that attach to quiddity (mahiya). Al-Razi argues that attributes only attach after the quiddity is realized, and they cannot be constituents (muqawwimat) because that would lead to an infinite regress. He then discusses necessary attributes (lawazim) and the role of an intermediate (wasat) in their knowledge, emphasizing that the intermediate must be self-evident (bayyin) to avoid regress. The discussion then shifts to how we know quiddities: either by their essence (e.g., heat through sensation) or by their properties (e.g., knowing the world is originated without knowing the originator's essence). This distinction is linked to the two types of definitions: haddi (essential) and rasmi (descriptive), which will be elaborated later. Throughout, al-Razi uses examples from natural philosophy (heat, fire) and kalam (the world's origination) to illustrate his points.

Resolving Priority in Definition: Al-Razi's Critique of Ibn Sina
Author critiques Ibn Sina's phrasing regarding priority in definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'animal as a material part' and 'animal as a predicate' in the definition of human, and explain how this distinction resolves an apparent logical reversal.

Core Idea
The distinction between conditional and unconditional meaning of 'animal' resolves the priority puzzle in definition.

In defining 'human' as 'rational animal', there is a subtle question: does the concept of animal precede that of human logically or existentially? Ibn Sina's phrasing seemed to imply that the act of making animal comes before making human, but al-Razi points out a potential reversal. The solution lies in distinguishing two senses of 'animal' — one as a mere component, the other as the predicate itself. This lesson will walk you through that distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key concepts
Recognize the terms 'animal with the condition of nothing' (bi-shart la shay') and 'animal without condition' (la bi-shart shay'). These are distinct logical notions.
2
Understand 'animal with condition of nothing'
This is the abstracted genus as a material part of the definition. It is not the predicate but a component whose existence precedes the composite.
Think of it as the genus considered in isolation, stripped of any differentia.
3
Understand 'animal without condition'
This is the predicate itself, which is identical in existence with human. It does not have a separate existence; its existence is the existence of human.
When we say 'human is animal', the predicate animal is not a separate substance.
4
Apply the distinction to Ibn Sina's statement
When Ibn Sina says 'that which makes it animal precedes it, so it makes it human', he refers to the making of animal as a predicate, which is ontologically simultaneous with making human. The priority only applies to the material part.
Al-Razi's correction: 'that which makes it human precedes it, so it makes it animal' would be correct for the material part.
5
Reconstruct the correct phrasing
According to al-Razi, the proper order is: the act of making human (the composite) is prior in the sense that the material part (animal) is included. The predicate animal follows from the composite.
This distinction prevents logical error in analyzing definitions.

Resolving the Paradox: Animal as Predicate vs. Animal as Part
Al-Razi resolves a paradox in Ibn Sina: animal as predicate vs. animal as part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'animal without condition' (predicate) and 'animal with the condition of not being something' (part) and explain how this distinction resolves Ibn Sina's paradox about priority.

Core Idea
Animal as part is prior; animal as predicate is not.

In his commentary on Ibn Sina's al-Isharat, al-Razi encounters a puzzling statement: 'What makes it an animal precedes what makes it a man.' This seems to reverse the expected order because animal is part of man. Al-Razi unveils a crucial distinction between two senses of 'animal' that saves the claim and clarifies the relationship between predication and composition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Paradox
Read Ibn Sina's statement: 'What makes it an animal is what precedes it in making it a man.' Notice that if 'animal' is a part of man, we expect the animal-maker to be prior. But al-Razi says this is a point of doubt. Why?
The doubt arises because the reader might interpret 'animal' as the predicate in 'man is an animal', where priority is reversed.
2
Extract Al-Razi's Two Senses of 'Animal'
Al-Razi introduces a key distinction: 'animal with the condition of not being something' (al-hayawan bi-shart la shay') is the material part of man, existing independently. 'Animal without condition' (al-hayawan la bi-shart) is the predicate, whose existence is identical to man's.
The terms 'conditioned' and 'unconditioned' are technical in Avicennan logic.
3
Determine Priority for Each Sense
As part (conditioned), animal is prior in existence to man. As predicate (unconditioned), animal has no separate existence; its existence is the existence of man, so man is prior.
4
Apply the Distinction to Ibn Sina's Statement
If Ibn Sina meant the conditioned sense (part), his statement is correct: the animal-maker precedes the man-maker. If he meant the unconditioned sense (predicate), the statement would be problematic. Al-Razi resolves that the statement is about the part, not the predicate.
This shows the importance of context in interpreting logical statements.
5
Recognize the Broader Implication
This distinction prevents confusion in definitions and predications. It shows that a term can have different logical roles (part vs. predicate) that affect its existential priority.
al-Razi refers to his larger work for further details.

Evaluating Manuscript Attribution: The Case of al-Mantiq al-Kabir
Discusses manuscript history and evidence attributing al-Mantiq al-Kabir to al-Razi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the chain of transmission of a manuscript and assess the strength of evidence for its authorship using historical clues from the text and ownership history.

Core Idea
A single surviving manuscript does not invalidate authenticity; the chain of ownership and contemporary testimony provide the evidence.

In this lesson, we explore a real case from the Islamic intellectual tradition: the debate over the authorship of al-Mantiq al-Kabir, attributed to Fakhr al-Din al-Razi. You will learn how scholars use biographical data, ownership records, and cross-references to evaluate attribution even when only one copy exists. This skill is essential for anyone working with classical Islamic manuscripts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary witness
Find the key figure who transmitted information about the manuscript. In the passage, Najm al-Din al-Qazwini reports that his students had a copy.
Al-Qazwini is a known logician and copyist.

Trace the chain of ownership
Follow the ownership sequence: Zayn al-Din al-Kashi → Najm al-Din al-Qazwini → Hasan ibn Muhammad al-Tabasi → Ottoman palace library. Note the evidence: al-Kashi's paragraphs in the work, al-Qazwini's transcription in 662 AH, and Bayezid II's seals.
The chain is unbroken and documented.

Assess the objection to authenticity
The main objection is that only one copy exists, which seems inconsistent with al-Razi's fame. Understand why this raises doubt.
Single copies often trigger suspicion of forgery.
4
Evaluate the counterargument
The passage argues that the manuscript's chain and historical context (copying in Maragha, transfer to Ottoman palace) explain its scarcity. The testimony of al-Qazwini and the presence of al-Kashi's paragraphs support authenticity.
The chain itself is strong, and the rarity is explained.
5
Draw a conclusion
Based on the evidence, the attribution to al-Razi is plausible. The single copy is not a decisive objection.

Definition of the Particle (Adah) in Logic and Grammar
Author defines the particle and explains its three types of attachment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three defining characteristics of a particle (adah) and apply them to classify any Arabic word as a particle or not.

Core Idea
A particle only has meaning by connecting other words, never alone.

In both logic and grammar, words are divided into three categories: noun, verb, and particle. The particle is the most subtle because it has no independent meaning—it functions like a tool that relates other words. This lesson unpacks the precise definition of the particle as given by the logicians, focusing on its simplicity, non-derivation, and conventional agreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The passage defines the third part of speech: 'al-adah' (the particle). Locate the Arabic term and note that it is also called 'harf' (letter/particle) in grammar.
In logic, 'adah' often refers to logical operators, but here it is grammatical particles.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The definition says: 'al-lafzatu al-mufradatu al-la mushtaqqatu al-dallatu 'ala ma'nan bi al-tawatu''. Break it down: genus = 'simple word' (mufrada), 'non-derived' (ghayr mushtaqqa). Differentia = 'indicates a meaning by conventional agreement' (dalla 'ala ma'nan bi al-tawatu').
The phrase 'al-la mushtaqqa' is a scribal variant of 'ghayr al-mushtaqqa'.
3
Check what the particle does NOT do
The passage explicitly states: the particle does NOT indicate a meaning by itself (la tadullu wahdaha 'ala ma'nan bi mithli nafsihi). Instead, it indicates the connection (irtibat) between two meanings when accompanied by other words.
This distinguishes the particle from nouns and verbs, which can stand alone as complete utterances.
4
Test the definition with an example
Take the preposition 'bi' (in 'marartu bi Zaydin'). Does it fit? It is simple, non-derived, indicates a meaning (by/locality) only by convention, and does not stand alone but connects the verb 'marartu' to 'Zaydin'. So it is a particle.

The Three Types of Particle Attachment in Speech
Author defines the particle and explains its three types of attachment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of particle attachment (prepositional, conjunctive, and sentential) and classify any Arabic particle accordingly.

Core Idea
Particles attach to words or sentences in three ways: to make verbs transitive, to conjoin, or to qualify a whole statement.

After defining the particle, the author explains how particles attach to other words. This is crucial because the function of a particle determines its logical role. The passage identifies three types of attachment: mediating between verb and noun (e.g., prepositions), joining elements under a common operator (e.g., conjunctions), and modifying an entire sentence (e.g., negation, interrogation). Understanding these helps in parsing logical propositions in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three primary attachment types in speech
The passage says speech (kalam) involves attachment (ta'alluq) of words to each other, and there are three ways: noun-to-noun, noun-to-verb, and particle to both. Focus on the third: particle attachment.
The other two are handled in grammar; here the focus is on particles.
2
Learn the first subtype: prepositional attachment
The first subtype is when the particle mediates between a verb and a noun, as in prepositions like 'bi'. The passage explains that these particles 'transit from verbs to what they do not normally reach by themselves'.
Example: 'marartu bi Zaydin' – 'bi' enables the verb 'marartu' to reach 'Zayd'.
3
Learn the second subtype: conjunctive attachment
The second subtype is when the particle attaches by conjunction ('atf). The particle (e.g., 'wa') makes the second noun enter under the same grammatical operator as the first. Example: 'ja'ani Zayd wa 'Amr' – the verb 'ja'ani' applies to both nouns.
The key is that the second element shares the same operator.
4
Learn the third subtype: sentential attachment
The third subtype is when the particle attaches to a whole sentence, such as particles of negation ('ma', 'la'), interrogation ('hal'), condition ('in'), etc. These particles restrict the meaning of the entire sentence. For example, 'ma kharaja Zayd' restricts the negation to Zayd's going out, not the act in general.
The passage gives the example of 'la rajula fi al-dar' for negation of genus, which generalizes over the noun.
5
Apply the taxonomy to a new particle
Take the particle 'hal'. Does it mediate between verb and noun? No. Conjoin? No. Attach to whole sentence? Yes – it asks a question about the entire proposition. So it is the third subtype.
This classification helps in formalizing logical propositions.

Finding the Contradictory of Modal Conditional Propositions
Author details contradictories for modal conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contradictory of any given modal conditional proposition by applying the rule of modality inversion and quantity/quality difference.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a modal conditional inverts modality and usually differs in quantity and quality.

The author systematically lists contradictories for various modal conditionals. Instead of memorizing each case, we will extract the consistent pattern: to negate a modal proposition, you must invert its modal force (necessity ↔ possibility) and often change its quantifier and quality. This skill is essential for constructing valid arguments and avoiding logical fallacies in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the modality type
Look at the original proposition: is it necessary (ضرورية), possible (ممكنة), permanent (دائمة), absolute (مطلقة), or temporal (وقتية)? For example, 'بالضرورة كلما كان أب كان ج' indicates a necessary conditional.
The modality is indicated by terms like 'بالضرورة' (necessarily) or 'بالإمكان العام' (by general possibility).
2
Invert the modality
The contradictory will have the opposite modality: necessity becomes general possibility (ممكنة عامة), permanence becomes general absolute (مطلقة عامة), and so on. The author states that the contradictory 'الممكنة العامة المخالفة كما وكيفا' is the counterpart to the necessary.
The inversion is part of 'mukhalafa' (difference) in both quantity and quality.
3
Check quantity and quality difference (mukhalafa kama wa kayfa)
The contradictory must differ in both quantity (general vs. particular) and quality (affirmative vs. negative). For a general necessary proposition, the contradictory is a general possible proposition with opposite quality (e.g., 'ليس كلما' instead of 'كلما').
In some particular cases, the contradictory may be a permanent (دائمة) proposition that agrees in quality (muwafaqa kayfa).
4
Apply to a concrete example from the text
Take the proposition: 'بالضرورة كلما كان أب كان ج ما دام ب' (necessarily, whenever A is B then C as long as B). Its contradictory is 'ليس كلما كان أب كان ج حين هو ب بالإمكان العام' (not every time A is B then C when it is B by general possibility) — differing in modality (possible) and quality (negation).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Logical rules are formal, not tied to any specific subject matter.

This lesson clarifies that logical rules are not dependent on the particular examples used. They apply to the form of reasoning, not the content. Understanding this abstraction is crucial for applying logic across different Islamic sciences.

Distinguishing Quiddity from Accident in Logic
States that logical rules are intellectual and abstracted from material content.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between judgments based on quiddity (essence) and judgments based on accidental attributes.

Core Idea
What belongs to a thing by its essence is necessary, while accidental judgments are contingent.

The author makes a crucial distinction: a thing's quiddity (māhiyya) is its essence, and judgments based on quiddity are distinct from judgments based on accidental qualities. This distinction is vital for understanding necessity in logical rules and in Islamic theological discussions.

Guided Path
1
Define quiddity (māhiyya)
Find the term 'al-mahiyya' in the Arabic. It refers to the essence of a thing—what it is in itself. For example, the quiddity of 'human' is 'rational animal'.
Quiddity answers the question 'what is it?'.
2
Differentiate from accident
Accidents (أعراض) are attributes that are not part of the essence, such as 'being a writer' or 'being tall'. The passage states that the judgment on the quiddity is different from the judgment on the thing when qualified by an accident.
3
Apply the author's point
The author says the judgment on the quiddity is distinct from the judgment when the thing is described by a certain attribute. For instance, 'human is a rational animal' is a quiddity judgment; 'human is a writer' is accidental. The logical rules that apply to quiddity judgments are necessary, not contingent on accidents.
4
Understand necessity
What belongs to a thing by its quiddity is necessary—the quiddity cannot exist without it. So, rationality is necessary for human, but being a writer is not. This is crucial for arguments about divine attributes in kalam.
