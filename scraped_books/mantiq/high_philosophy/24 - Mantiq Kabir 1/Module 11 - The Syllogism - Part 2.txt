Implications of Universal Exclusive Disjunction
Author enumerates implications of universal affirmative exclusive disjunctive

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive multiple implied disjunctives from a given universal exclusive disjunction using equivalents, contradictories, and implications.

Core Idea
From one exclusive disjunction, many others are entailed by substituting equivalents and contradictories.

The author now shows that the universal exclusive disjunction 'Every A is either B or C' does not stand alone: it logically implies a host of other disjunctive propositions. Understanding these implications will help you see the richness of logical entailment and prepare you for later uses in argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Recognize the universal affirmative exclusive disjunction 'Every A is either B or C' (man'at al-jam' wal khuluw).
2
Derive the tautological implication
From the original, the simplest implication is 'Every A is either B or not B', composed of the antecedent and the negation of B.
This is a logical tautology but demonstrates the method.
3
Substitute equivalents for the second disjunct
Replace 'not B' with any term that is equivalent to it (musawi), or replace C with its equivalent, yielding more disjunctives.
The number of such disjunctives is as many as the equivalents.
4
Use contradictories and implications
Similarly, use what contradicts B (munafi) and what is implied by B and C (muqtada) to generate further disjunctives.
5
Recognize the multiplicity
The author emphasizes that the number of such implications is vast, based on the multiplicity of equivalents and contradictories.

Implicative and Converse Disjunctions
Author defines and illustrates disjunctions that are mutually implicative and convertible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when two disjunctive propositions are mutually implicative and converse (logically equivalent) based on the pattern of their disjuncts.

Core Idea
When disjunctions share a common disjunct and differ in the other, they can become logically equivalent.

In the previous lessons, we learned about disjunctive propositions and their types. Now the author introduces a special relation between disjunctions: mutual implication and convertibility. This means that under certain conditions, two disjunctions can be logically equivalent, so that each entails the other. The passage establishes a pattern: when the disjunctions share a common disjunct (either in the first or second position) and the other disjuncts vary, they can be mutually implicative and converse. This is important for understanding how different formulations of a disjunction can be equivalent in logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common disjunct
In the first set of examples, note that the first disjunct is always 'B' across all lines: 'A is either B or C', 'A is either B or D', etc. The second disjunct varies. Identify what remains constant.
2
Observe the pattern of the negations
Each disjunction is paired with its negation in the form 'not at all not every A is either B or C'. This double negation indicates that the statement is being affirmed as true. Note that the negation uses the same disjuncts.
3
Understand mutual implication
The author says these disjunctions are 'mutalazima and muta'akisa' (mutually implicative and converse). That means each statement implies the other. For instance, 'Every A is either B or C' implies 'Every A is either B or D' (under certain conditions), and vice versa. The condition is that the disjunctions differ in their two forms (i.e., the second disjunct differs) but are still mutually implicative.
4
Check the second set of examples
Now the common disjunct is 'C' in the second position. The first disjunct varies: D, H, W, etc. The same pattern of mutual implication holds. This shows the symmetry: whether the common disjunct is first or second, the mutual implication holds.

Even for actual existence, universal and particular may behave differently in inference.

This lesson examines a historical claim about actual affirmative propositions. Some early scholars held that when these propositions are interpreted externally—referring to real extra-mental existence—the universal and particular forms follow the same logical rules. However, this view is likely contested by the author, highlighting a subtle but important distinction in propositional logic.

This tension is why the author likely critiques the claim.

The Conversion of Perpetual Negative Universals: Al-Razi's Challenge

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Perpetual negatives fail to convert when the predicate is non-essential and the subject is real.

In this passage, we encounter a major dispute in post-classical logic: whether the perpetual negative universal proposition converts to itself. The majority holds that it does, but Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi presents a powerful counterexample. We will trace his reasoning, the objections raised against him, and the responses that defend his position.

Guided Path
1
Recall the standard conversion rule
For a universal negative (E proposition), standard conversion yields another universal negative. The majority extends this to perpetual negatives.
The standard rule is that 'No S is P' converts to 'No P is S'.
2
Understand al-Razi's distinction
Al-Razi distinguishes between external (khariji) and real (haqiqi) subjects. For real subjects, where the subject is an essence, conversion may fail.
This distinction is crucial for understanding his objection.
3
Examine al-Razi's example
Consider the perpetual negative 'No human is a writer at any time'. Al-Razi claims this is false but that its truth is possible. If the conversion rule held, 'No writer is a human at any time' would be true, which is clearly false. Thus the rule fails for real subjects.
The example hinges on writing being a non-necessary attribute.
4
Analyze the first objection to al-Razi
Some object that if the original were true, it would lead to absurdities. The author shows this objection misses the mark by confusing the truth conditions of the original.
The objection attempts to prove the original cannot be true, but al-Razi only needs possibility.
5
Analyze the second objection and response
Others attack al-Razi's proof that if writing is not necessary at one time, it is never necessary. The author dismisses this as invalid reasoning, noting that falsity of a proof does not falsify the conclusion.
The author concludes that al-Razi's position remains intact.

Conversion of General Conditional and General Conventional
Conversion rules for general conditional and conventional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conversion rule for general conditional and general conventional propositions and verify it with examples from Islamic logic.

Core Idea
General conditionals and general conventionals convert exactly to themselves.

In this passage, the author clarifies that some proposition types retain their form upon conversion. We focus on the general conditional (al-mashruta al-'amma) and general conventional (al-'urfiya al-'amma), which convert to themselves due to the nature of their conditions. Let's see why.

Guided Path
1

2

The author says they 'convert to themselves,' meaning the converse proposition is identical in form and truth value. For example, 'Every A is B as long as A exists' converts to 'Every B is A as long as B exists'? No, it converts to itself, so the same subject and predicate. The text says 'تنعكس كنفسها' meaning it converts like itself, i.e., the converted proposition has the same quality and quantity as the original. For a universal affirmative general conditional, the converse would be a universal affirmative general conditional. The subject and predicate swap, but the condition remains attached to the new subject. So it's not identical in words but in form.
Be careful: 'converts to itself' means the converse has the same type, not the same subject-predicate order.
3
Apply to an Islamic example
Take the proposition: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden as long as it intoxicates' (al-mashruta al-'amma). Its converse is 'Every forbidden thing is an intoxicant as long as it is forbidden'? Actually, the author's point is that the general conditional behaves like an external proposition, where condition is tied to subject's essence. The conversion preserves the condition on the new subject. For example, if we have 'Every human is rational as long as he exists', the converse 'Every rational is human as long as it exists' is also true. So it works.
This shows the importance of essential vs. accidental predicates.

Conversion of Special Conditional and Special Conventional
Conversion rules for special conditional and conventional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to convert special conditional and special conventional propositions to their general forms and differentiate between the majority and minority views.

Core Idea
Special conditionals and conventionals convert to their general forms, but not always.

The author now turns to the 'special' versions of conditional and conventional propositions. Unlike the general types, these do not convert to themselves but to a more general form. There is disagreement on whether this conversion always holds. Let's explore the rules and the preferred view.

Guided Path
1
Define the special types
A special conditional (mashruta khassa) is a proposition where the condition applies only to some instances of the subject, e.g., 'Every human is breathing at some time'. The special conventional ('urfiya khassa) is similar but based on customary usage.
These are more specific than the general ones.
2
State the majority rule
The majority (jumhur) hold that both special types convert to their general forms in all cases. For example, a special conditional 'Every A is B at some time' converts to a general conditional 'Every B is A at some time? No, it converts to the general form meaning the converse becomes a general conditional: 'Every B is A as long as B exists'. Actually, the text says 'تنعكسان إلى عامتهما' meaning they convert to their general [counterparts].
So the converse is a general proposition of the same kind.
3
Examine the minority view
Some scholars argue that the conversion is not always valid; it holds only in some cases. The author sides with this view, calling it 'the sounder' (al-asahh). The reason is that the special condition may not hold after conversion.
This reflects caution in logical inference.
4
Apply to an Islamic example
Consider the proposition from Fiqh: 'Every traveler may shorten prayers when traveling' (special conditional: condition is traveling). Its converse would be 'Every person who may shorten prayers is traveling (as long as they shorten prayers)'. Is this always true? No, because a resident may also shorten prayers in some cases (e.g., illness). So conversion is not universal, supporting the minority view.
This shows practical relevance.

Commentary
This passage presents three objections from a hypothetical critic against the author's claim that a universal negative modal proposition converts universally. The objector argues that the conversion fails when the consequent (the predicate of the original proposition) is something that never exists in reality. The first objection (أ) states: If the original proposition is true and implies a relation, then the non-existence of the antecedent is a necessary consequence of the implication. Therefore, if the consequent never exists, the antecedent must also never exist, making the original vacuously true but its converse (which would require the antecedent to exist when the consequent does not) impossible. The second objection (ب) uses the third figure syllogism to show that if the original is true, its contradictory (i.e., the negation of the converse) must also be true, leading to a contradiction unless the entailment is not universal. The third objection (ت) similarly argues that the truth of the original entails the truth of something that contradicts the truth of the converse, again blocking universal entailment. The author then responds to the first objection. He distinguishes between the implication alone and the composite of implication without the consequent. The non-existence of the antecedent is a necessary consequence of the composite, not of the implication per se. Therefore, when the consequent is non-existent, the composite (implication without consequent) is negated, which is exactly what is needed for the converse to hold. The response is cut off, but the point is that the conversion remains valid even when the consequent is non-existent. For example, in fiqh, consider the proposition: 'If someone drinks an intoxicant, he is punished.' The consequent (punished) may not always exist due to lack of evidence, but the implication still holds. The conversion would be: 'If someone is not punished, he did not drink an intoxicant.' The objector's worry is that if 'not punished' never occurs, the original may be vacuously true, but the author clarifies that the logical structure remains sound.

Proof that True Antecedent Necessitates Consequent
Proof of necessary implication in conditionals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the proof that in a valid conditional, the truth of the antecedent necessitates the truth of the consequent and derive the contrapositive.

Core Idea
The truth of a conditional is defined by the impossibility of the antecedent without the consequent.

This passage presents a rigorous proof that in a conditional proposition, if the antecedent is true, the consequent must be true. The author uses the concept of necessary implication (luzūm) and shows that the contrapositive follows by necessity. This principle is foundational in Islamic logic and is used extensively in kalām and uṣūl al-fiqh to ensure consistency in arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author aims to prove that truth of the antecedent implies truth of the consequent. Note that he is responding to objections, so the proof is dialectically framed.
The claim is the conclusion of the proof: truth of original entails truth of converse (contrapositive).
2
Analyze the first proof via impossibility
The author argues that the combination of truth of original without truth of converse is impossible. This impossibility is either because possibility contradicts implication or because the absence of converse negates the original. Thus, the truth of the original universally entails the truth of the converse.
This is a proof by contradiction: assume the opposite leads to an impossibility.
3
Understand the second, more direct proof
The author states: 'If the original is true, then the existence of the antecedent without the consequent is impossible.' This is because the truth of a conditional is precisely this impossibility.
This directly links truth of the conditional to the impossibility of the antecedent alone.
4
Derive the contrapositive
From the impossibility of the antecedent without the consequent, the author deduces that the contradictory of the consequent necessarily implies the contradictory of the antecedent. This is the contrapositive (ʿaks al-naqīḍ).
This step is crucial: it shows that implication is logically equivalent to its contrapositive.
5
Refute the objection about possibility
An objector might say this inference holds only if the contradictory is possible. The author replies that assuming the contradictory is enough; its actual possibility does not affect the entailment.
This strengthens the proof: the contrapositive holds regardless of the contingent status of terms.
