Truth-value of isolated words
Author states that an isolated word is neither true nor false, and classifies individual words.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish truth-evaluable propositions from isolated words and classify individual words into noun, verb, and verbal noun using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
Truth and falsity belong only to complete propositions, not to isolated words.

This lesson clarifies a foundational principle in Islamic logic: truth (ṣidq) and falsity (kidhb) are properties of propositions (qaḍāyā), not of isolated terms. The author uses an analogy between conceptual meanings and linguistic expressions to drive this home. Understanding this prevents confusion when analyzing statements in the Islamic sciences, such as rulings in fiqh or theological claims in kalām.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the analogy
Notice that the author draws a parallel between 'individual meaning' (al-mufrad min al-maʿānī) and 'individual word' (al-mufrad min al-alfāẓ). Just as a single concept like 'human' is neither true nor false, a single word like 'human' is also neither true nor false. This is the core analogy.
The point is to shift attention from meaning to language without losing the logical principle.
2
Identify the condition for truth
Find the phrase 'عند اقتران أنه موجود أو ليس بموجود' (when there is coupling with existence or non-existence). This tells you that truth-value arises only when a term is combined with a judgment of existence or non-existence, forming a proposition. In the mind or in utterance, this coupling is necessary.
The term iqtirān here refers to the copula or the linking judgment that turns a term into a proposition.
3
Classify the types of individual words
The author then lists three grammatical categories: noun (ism), verb (kalima), and verbal noun (maṣdar). Acknowledge that this is a traditional Arabic grammar division. Note that all three are still isolated words; none of them alone constitutes a proposition.
This prepares for later lessons on compound expressions where words combine to form propositions.
4
Apply to a real Islamic example
Take the term 'ṣalāh' (prayer). As an isolated word, it is neither true nor false. Only when we say 'Prayer is obligatory' (al-ṣalātu wājibatun) do we have a truth-evaluable proposition. Test other Islamic terms in the same way.
This principle helps avoid category errors when discussing Islamic rulings or theological statements.

Two Types of Conceptualization and the Resolution of Circularity in Definition
Author explains that definition gives conceptualization only after existence is known, distinguishing two types of tasawwur.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of tasawwur and explain how definition avoids circularity by requiring prior conceptualization of the name's meaning only.

Core Idea
Definition yields essence-conceptualization only after existence is known; circularity is avoided by a prior name-conceptualization.

How can a definition give us new knowledge if we already need some conceptualization to understand it? This passage tackles that puzzle head-on. The author shows that the conceptualization needed before assent is merely of the name's meaning, not of the essence. Once we know the thing exists, the same definition then unveils its essence—no circularity involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of tasawwur
Find in the Arabic where the author distinguishes between conceptualization of the meaning of the name (tasawwur bi-hasb ma'na al-ism) and conceptualization of the essence (tasawwur bi-hasb al-dhat). Notice which one precedes assent.
2
Understand the role of existence (wujud)
The author states that definition benefits conceptualization of essence only for one who already knows the thing exists. For one who does not know existence, definition only gives the meaning of the name.
This is the key to resolving circularity.
3
Trace the dependency chain
Assent (tasdiq) depends on prior conceptualization of the name's meaning. Definition can then use that same meaning to lead to essence-conceptualization after assent. No circle arises because the prior conceptualization is different from the one definition yields.
4
Recognize the author's refutation of the objection
The hypothetical objection is: 'If definition needs prior conceptualization, and assent needs prior conceptualization, we have a circle.' The author refutes by showing the prior conceptualization is of the name, not the essence.
The objection is explicitly stated and answered.

Then among the necessary concomitants is that you know afterwards that definition, although it indicates the quiddity of the thing and benefits conceptualization of its essence, is only beneficial in relation to one who knows the existence of the thing. As for in relation to one who does not know, it indicates the meaning of the name of that thing. So when he obtains knowledge of the existence of that thing, the same definition becomes indicative of the thing in terms of its essence, beneficial for conceptualization of it in terms of essence for him. And the conceptualization that was mentioned earlier, which is judged to precede assent, is the conceptualization in terms of the meaning of the name, not in terms of the essence; for conceptualization in terms of essence is after knowledge of the existence of the thing and assent to it. And it should not be said: if definition does not benefit conceptualization except after knowledge of existence and assent, and assent cannot be except after conceptualization, then definition does not benefit conceptualization except after conceptualization. And that is impossible because of the impossibility of circularity. For the conceptualization that assent depends on is conceptualization of the meaning of the name when it is used, otherwise it cannot be judged that it exists or does not exist, so it is required to precede assent. Unlike conceptualization in terms of essence, for it is not required to precede assent, but rather it is after it, as passed. Then the conceptualization that precedes assent does not require that if one knew the existence of the thing, it would be the very same conceptualization of the reality of the thing and its quiddity; rather perhaps it is a conceptualization of it from the aspect of one of its necessary concomitants or accidents likewise. And mostly it is contrary to what it is; so take heed of what you know.

Commentary
This passage tackles a subtle epistemological issue: how can a definition give new conceptualization if it presupposes knowledge of existence? The author distinguishes two types of conceptualization (tasawwur). First, conceptualization of the meaning of a name (tasawwur bi-hasb ma'na al-ism) is what precedes assent (tasdiq). Second, conceptualization of the essence (tasawwur bi-hasb al-dhat) comes after knowledge of existence and assent. For example, when someone hears the term 'angel', they have a minimal conceptualization of the meaning of the name. This allows them to ask whether angels exist. After assenting to their existence, they can seek a definition that gives deeper conceptualization of the essence. So definition does not lead to circularity: the prior conceptualization is merely of the name's meaning, not of the essence. The author also notes that the prior conceptualization might be through a necessary concomitant or accident, not necessarily through the essence. This resolves the potential objection that definition would require prior conceptualization, making it impossible.

Are Modalities Positive or Negative? The Author's Refutation
Author refutes the claim that necessity, impossibility, and possibility are negative entities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical contradiction in treating necessity as a species of possibility and explain why modalities are relational (iʿtibāri) rather than negative.

Core Idea
Modalities are relational, not negative; treating them as negative leads to contradiction.

This lesson examines a critical debate in Islamic logic: are the modalities of necessity, impossibility, and possibility merely negative concepts or positive realities? The author argues they are neither; they are relational (iʿtibāri) considerations that do not exist independently. His arguments include a powerful reductio showing why necessity cannot have possibility as its genus. By understanding this, you gain insight into how logicians distinguish between essence and existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under attack
Find the claim being refuted: that the three modalities (necessity, impossibility, possibility) are negative entities (amr ʿadami). Look for the phrase 'من الأمور العدمية' in the Arabic.
The author states 'فيها من الكلام بأنها من الأمور العدمية'.
2
Extract the author's first argument
The author argues that if universal possibility were a genus for necessity, necessity would be composite, and everything composite is possible. This would make the necessary being possible—a contradiction. Trace the steps: genus assumption → composition → possibility → contradiction.
Key phrase: 'فيكون الواجب ممكنا؛ هذا خلف'.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The author considers the objection that possibility is pure negation. He counters that if special possibility were pure negation, it would be possible in itself, leading to an infinite regress of possibilities. Understand how this refutes the negative view.
Look for 'وإلا لكان ممكنا لذاته فيكون إمكانه زائدا عليه؛ ولزم التسلسل'.
4
Resolve the puzzle about special possibility
The puzzle: if something is possible, it is either existent (hence necessary) or non-existent (hence impossible). The author resolves it by saying possibility is judged per essence, not per actual existence. A thing can be necessary from one aspect and possible from another.
Key: 'الحكم عليه بالنظر إلى ذاته لا بالنظر إلى كونه موجودًا أو معدوما'.
5
Conclude the author's position
Synthesize: modalities are relational (iʿtibāriyya), not negative or positive realities. They depend on the essence of the thing and cannot exist independently in the external world.
The author concludes 'الحق أنها من الأمور الاعتبارية'.

Fifth, regarding these three—necessity, impossibility, and possibility—there is discussion: are they entirely negative things or not? The truth is that they are relational (iʿtibāri) things, having no existence in themselves in external entities. It has been said about possibility that the general one is an expression of the negation of impossibility, and impossibility is pure negation; thus general possibility is a positive matter, but it cannot be a genus for essential necessity. Special possibility is similar, even if shared between them; for if it were a genus, then the distinction of essential necessity from possibility would be by actuality, and whatever is like that is composite, and the composite is possible due to its dependence on another (its part), so the necessary would be possible—this is a contradiction. Then one may say that it is pure negation, otherwise it would not be predicated of special possibility, and special possibility is pure negation, otherwise it would be possible in itself, so its possibility would be additional to it, leading to infinite regress. As for the special [possibility], it cannot exist at all; because the possible is either existent—in which case it is necessary of existence and not possible of non-existence—or non-existent—in which case it is necessary of non-existence and not possible of existence. Whoever insists on this only recognizes special possibility with respect to future time, just as this particular thing is described as being alive now; in the future it can remain alive or not, but this does not undermine the judgment that a thing is possible in the present and future; for the judgment is with respect to its essence, not with respect to its existence or non-existence. And it is not intellectually impossible for a thing to be necessary from one aspect and possible from another.

Commentary
This passage critically examines the ontological status of the three modalities: necessity, impossibility, and possibility. The author begins by stating that some consider them purely negative (madūmiyya), but he asserts they are relational (iʿtibāriyya)—they do not exist independently in the external world. He then focuses on possibility: some claim that universal possibility (al-imkān al-ʿāmm) is simply the negation of impossibility, making it positive in a sense. However, this cannot serve as a genus for necessity (wujūb), because if it did, necessity would be composite (having possibility as a genus), and anything composite is possible (dependent on its parts), leading to the absurd conclusion that the necessary being is possible. The author then addresses a possible objection (naqd) that possibility is pure negation, otherwise it would not be predicated of special possibility (al-imkān al-khāṣṣ). He argues that if special possibility were pure negation, then it would be possible in itself, leading to an infinite regress of additional possibilities. Next, he tackles a puzzle about special possibility: the possible seems to be either existent (thus necessary) or non-existent (thus impossible). The resolution is that possibility is judged relative to the essence, not to actual existence or non-existence. A thing can be necessary from one aspect (e.g., its essence) and possible from another (e.g., its duration). The author concludes that there is no intellectual contradiction in something being both necessary and possible from different perspectives.

Key Terms
الأمور العدمية (al-umūr al-ʿadamiyya): Negative entities, things that are mere privations or negations.
الأمور الاعتبارية (al-umūr al-iʿtibāriyya): Relational or mental considerations, not real existents.
الإمكان العام (al-imkān al-ʿāmm): Universal possibility: the negation of impossibility; everything that is not impossible.
الإمكان الخاص (al-imkān al-khāṣṣ): Special possibility: the possibility of a thing to exist or not exist, i.e., contingency.
الوجوب (al-wujūb): Necessity of existence, the opposite of possibility.
الامتناع (al-imtināʿ): Impossibility, the opposite of possibility.
