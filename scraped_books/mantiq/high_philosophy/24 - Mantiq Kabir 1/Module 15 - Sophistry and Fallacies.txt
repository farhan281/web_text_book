Refuting the Objection that Logic is Unnecessary
Author refutes objections to logic's utility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute the objection that all conceptions are intuitive, using the author's dialectical strategy.

Core Idea
The objection that logic is useless self-destructs through contradictions.

A common objection against logic is that if all conceptions are intuitive, then logical inference is redundant. The author presents this objection and then systematically dismantles it. By analyzing this dialectical exchange, you will learn how to defend the utility of logic against a sophisticated critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector asserts that all conceptions are non-acquired (i.e., intuitive), so logic is wasted. Write down the exact claim: 'التصورات بأسرها غير كسبية'.
2
Understand the objector's argument
The objector reasons: if you seek a conception, you either already have awareness of it (impossible to seek what you know) or you don't (impossible to seek the unknown). This supposedly proves all conceptions are intuitive.
This is a binary dichotomy that the author will later criticize.
3
Follow the author's first rebuttal
The author shows that the objector's own reasoning leads to contradictions: if the first case (awareness) is realized, the second case cannot be, and vice versa. This implies an impossibility in seeking itself.
The author uses a reductio ad absurdum.
4
Examine the author's second rebuttal
Even if knowledge follows without acquisition (i.e., intuitively), some minds require logical rules to be prepared for that knowledge. Thus logic is still needed.
This is a pragmatic defense: logic trains the mind to receive intuitive knowledge.
5
Conclude the author's defense
The objections fail because they either lead to contradictions or ignore the pedagogical role of logic. Logic remains useful for those who need structured reasoning.
The overall strategy is to show the objection is self-defeating or incomplete.

And it may also be said that all conceptions are non-acquired, for the seeker of a conception either has awareness of that sought object or not. And it cannot be sought, and it cannot be said that he can have awareness of it from some aspect and not from another, because a single thing cannot be that way, and what is perceived is necessarily other than what is not perceived. Conceptions are obtained in a way that no one can deny, so they are intuitive. And if they were intuitive, then the implication of the first consequent from those intuitives would be intuitive, and then it would follow that the implication of every science from another science preceding it would be intuitive, thus logic would be wasted. Then someone may say that this is among what is not considered, because if the first of them is realized—which is his statement: 'If he has awareness of that sought object, then it cannot be sought at all, inevitably obtaining the obtained'—then the second of them cannot be realized, and vice versa; necessarily the second is realized between the necessities of the first and the second of them, and it is something that the second is realized between them. And if it were so, then the necessary would be among the necessities of some necessary absolutely, and vice versa. Upon this it is said: What you have mentioned is not realized unless there is realized among things what can never be realized, which is the impossibility of seeking—that is the impossibility in his statement 'if it is perceived'—and not its impossibility on the assumption that seeking is not impossible in every form—that is the non-impossibility in his statement 'everything whose seeking is impossible is unknown and not perceived.' And the necessary is only among the necessities of some necessary. And it is not said: Granted it is so, but the assumption is among the impossibilities; for the assumption is not from them unless that sought object is perceived from every aspect and also not perceived from every aspect. There is no dispute about that, but the discussion is about what is perceived in an indefinite manner, and that is among what it admits. And it may also be said: Granted that from every science another science follows without acquisition, which is thought (fikr) as defined. But it is not far that in some souls their preparedness to accept what follows from it depends on learning these logical rules. Upon this assumption, logic is not wasted.

Commentary
This passage is a dialectical exchange presenting and refuting objections against the necessity of logic. The author begins by stating an objection: If all conceptions are intuitive (badihiyya), then logical inference is unnecessary, because every implication would also be intuitive, rendering logic useless. The objector then elaborates: if a seeker already has awareness of the sought conception, seeking it is impossible (obtaining the obtained); if he has no awareness, he cannot begin to seek it. The author refutes this by showing that the objector's reasoning leads to contradictions, specifically the impossibility of seeking anything unknown. He then offers a second rebuttal: Even if knowledge follows from prior knowledge without acquisition (fikr), some minds require learning logical laws to be prepared for such inference. Thus logic is not wasted. The use of terms like 'tasawwurat' (conceptions) and 'badihiyya' (intuitive) is consistent with standard Islamic logic. An example from fiqh: If all legal rulings were intuitively known, ijtihad would be unnecessary; but the need for ijtihad in deriving rulings from evidence shows that some knowledge requires reasoning.
