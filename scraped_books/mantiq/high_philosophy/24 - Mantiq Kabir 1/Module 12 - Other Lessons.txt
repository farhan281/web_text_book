Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refute a historical claim about the origin of a logical term using textual evidence.

Core Idea
The term 'al-hayniyya' predates al-Razi; it appears in Ibn Sina's al-Isharat.

In the study of Islamic logic, accurately tracing the origin of technical terms is crucial for understanding intellectual history. This lesson examines a specific claim about the term 'al-hayniyya' and demonstrates how textual evidence can correct misconceptions. Guided Path

Evaluate the refutation
Since evidence from a earlier authoritative source is presented, the claim is refuted. The term was known before al-Razi, though its precise definition came later from al-Razi's followers.

This method of tracing a term to earlier sources can be used to verify or refute similar claims about other logical terms.

Defending Logic Against the Infinite Regress Objection
Author raises objection and gives two answers to the infinite regress problem.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection against the need for logic and evaluate the author's two responses to the infinite regress argument.

Core Idea
Logic avoids infinite regress by relying on innate knowledge and self-evident principles.

A powerful objection challenges the very enterprise of logic: if reasoning requires a law, that law itself must be justified, leading to circularity or infinite regress. In this passage, the author (likely al-Razi or a similar scholar) presents this objection and then offers two distinct answers. Understanding this dialectical exchange is crucial for appreciating how Islamic logicians defended their discipline against skeptical attacks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's structure
The objection is a conditional argument: if reasoning about unknowns requires a law (logic), then that law is either self-evident (making its pursuit wasteful) or theoretical (requiring another law, leading to infinite regress). If reasoning does not require a law, then logic is unnecessary entirely.
This is a classic argument against any foundational discipline.
2
Extract the first answer based on innate disposition
The first answer argues that the law (logic) may already be possessed by some people due to their pure natural disposition (fiṭra nīra). For them, the objection that pursuing it is wasteful does not apply because they already have it. For others with partial ability, logic facilitates perfection. Those without any ability are beyond concern.
This answer appeals to varying human capacities.
3
Extract the second answer based on self-evident principles
The second answer distinguishes between self-evident (badīhī) and theoretical (nazarī) principles. The law itself may be from primary self-evident propositions that require no further law. Some unknowns are known directly from primaries, others require theoretical reasoning, and some cannot be known from either.
This answer avoids regress by positing a bedrock of self-evident truths.
4
Evaluate whether the objection is fully resolved
Assess if the two answers together successfully block the infinite regress. The first answer shifts the burden to individual capacity; the second provides a foundationalist solution. Consider if any remaining gaps exist, such as how to guarantee that the self-evident principles are indeed self-evident.
This step requires critical thinking, not just summary.

Refuting the Objection: Logic's Necessity and Human Capacity
Author gives first response to the objection that logic is useless, discussing degrees of human capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the author uses a tripartite classification of human capacity to refute the objection that logic is unnecessary.

Core Idea
The objection that logic is useless fails because human capacities vary; for many, logic is already present or helpful.

In this passage, the author addresses a common objection: if logic is a tool for knowledge but not everyone can acquire it, then pursuing it is wasted. The author responds by showing that the objection assumes a false universal. He categorizes people based on their capacity for acquiring knowledge and demonstrates that for each category, logic is either already present, beneficial, or irrelevant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection claims that logic is useless because it is unattained for many, so seeking it is wasted effort. Note that this is a universal negative claim.
2
Recognize the author's conditional response
The author first states that the objection would hold only if everyone were dependent on logic and it were unattained for all. But reality contradicts this: it is attained for some.
The author uses a conditional to expose the flaw.
3
Trace the tripartite classification
The author divides people by capacity: those with capacity for acquisition (salahiyyat al-tahsil) and those without. For those with capacity, he distinguishes two subcases: those who can achieve fully through innate pure nature (fitra nira) and those who can only partially achieve.
This classification covers all possibilities.
4
Apply the classification to refute the objection
For those with full capacity, logic is already present; for those with partial capacity, logic helps; for those with no capacity, logic is irrelevant. In no case is the original objection valid.
The refutation is complete because every category is addressed.

Types of Laws for Knowing Unknowns: First Principles vs Theoretical
Author gives second response: knowing unknowns from knowns requires an artificial law, which may be from first principles or theoretical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between first principles (awwaliyat) and theoretical matters (nazariyat) as sources of knowledge, and identify the role of logic as an artificial law for theoretical unknowns.

Core Idea
Logic is the artificial law needed for theoretical knowledge, even though some truths are self-evident.

When faced with the objection that logic is unnecessary because some knowledge is innate or self-evident, the logician responds by unpacking how we move from known to unknown. Not all unknowns are alike: some can be known through first principles, but many require a theoretical, artificial law — and that is precisely the function of logic. This lesson clarifies that distinction and its implications for Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of law
Locate in the Arabic where the author mentions that the artificial law (qanun sina'i) may be from first principles (awwaliyat) or from theoretical matters (nazariyat). Understand that first principles are self-evident and need no further law, while theoretical matters are arrived at through reasoning.
First principles include statements like 'the whole is greater than its part' which are known immediately.
2
Classify unknowns by knowability
From the passage, extract the three categories of unknowns: those knowable from first principles, those knowable only from theoretical matters, and those knowable from neither. Note that theoretical matters include all intermediately complex truths.
An example of an unknown from first principles is the impossibility of contradiction; an unknown requiring theory is the judgement that 'the world is temporally originated'.
3
Connect to Islamic sciences
Recognize that in disciplines like kalam and usul al-fiqh, many essential truths (e.g., existence of the Creator, obligation of prayer) are not self-evident but require theoretical reasoning. This is where logic provides the artificial law to move from known premises to unknown conclusions.
For instance, the proof that the world is originated requires a syllogism, which is a product of logic.
4
Apply the classification
Take a specific Islamic science claim and determine which category it falls into. For example, 'The Qur'an is the word of God' – is it known from first principles or theoretical reasoning? Why? This will solidify your understanding of when logic is indispensable.
Use the threefold division from the passage as your framework.

The Objection That All Concepts Are Innate and the Beginning of Its Refutation
Author presents an objection that all concepts are innate and begins a counter-response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the logical structure of an objection against the usefulness of logic and identify the initial steps of its refutation.

Core Idea
If seeking concepts is either redundant or impossible, then logic is useless — but this objection collapses on its own disjunction.

In this passage, an objector challenges the entire project of logic by claiming that all conceptualizations (tasawwurat) are innate, not acquired. The argument is a classic dilemma: either you already know the concept or you don't — both options make seeking impossible. The author then begins to dismantle this objection, showing that each horn leads to an absurdity. This lesson will help you dissect such dialectical moves, a skill essential for rigorous theological and legal debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's core claim
Read the first line: 'All concepts are non-acquired.' This is the thesis being defended by the objector. Note that it undermines the possibility of learning through logic.
2
Extract the disjunctive dilemma
The objector presents a disjunction: either the seeker has awareness of the concept or does not. From each alternative, they derive an impossibility (redundancy or total ignorance). Identify these two conditional statements in the Arabic.
The dilemma is a form of conditional reasoning where each antecedent leads to the same problematic consequent.
3
Analyze the objector's conclusion
The objector concludes that since the dilemma shows concepts are innate, their implications would also be innate, making logic a wasted effort. Pinpoint the sentence: 'then it would follow that... logic would be wasted.'
4
Observe the respondent's initial move
The author begins the counter-response with 'Then someone may say...' focusing on the first horn. They argue that if awareness exists, seeking is impossible (obtaining the obtained). This already makes the second horn irrelevant, but the response goes further to show that the objector's own reasoning leads to a contradiction.
The response uses a technique called 'taking the first assumption seriously' to expose an internal flaw.

The Threefold Division of Single Words
Classification of single words by independence and time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given single word into noun, verb, or particle using the logical criteria of independence in meaning and time indication.

Core Idea
A single word is either independent (noun/verb) or dependent (particle); independent words are further split by time.

The author begins by sorting all single words into three bins: noun, verb, and particle. This is not just grammar—it's a logical move. Understanding the two criteria (independence and time) gives you a mental sorting algorithm that works for any word in any Islamic text.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the two criteria
The author uses two criteria: (1) independence in conveying meaning (is the word meaningful on its own?) and (2) indication of time (past, present, future).
Independence is about whether the word gives a complete meaning without support.
2
Apply the first criterion: independence
If the word is not independent, it is a particle (e.g., 'min'). If it is independent, move to the second criterion.
Particles need other words to complete meaning, like prepositions or conjunctions.
3
Apply the second criterion: time indication
If the independent word indicates time (e.g., 'yaktubu' = he writes, indicates present), it is a verb. If it does not indicate time (e.g., 'kitab' = book), it is a noun.
The verb is defined as indicating an event and its time; the noun is free from time.

Definition of the Particle and Its Three Attachments
Definition of particle and its three attachment modes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the particle (أداة) according to the logician's criteria and identify its three types of attachment in Arabic sentences.

Core Idea
The particle has no meaning alone; it only connects meanings.

After covering the noun and verb, we now turn to the third category of indicating words: the particle (أداة). In logic, particles are defined precisely to distinguish them from nouns and verbs. They indicate a meaning by convention but do not signify a complete concept by themselves; rather, they show the relationship between other words. Guided Path

The restrictions (قيود) were previously explained for noun and verb; here they are applied to particle.

Understand why it is called 'tool'
The author explains that the particle alone does not indicate a meaning by itself; rather, it indicates the connection between two meanings when associated with other words. The author states that the attachment of the particle to nouns and verbs is of three types: (1) mediating between verb and noun (e.g., prepositions), (2) attachment in conjunction (e.g., 'wa'), and (3) attachment to the whole sentence (e.g., negation, interrogation, condition). He then categorizes the indicating words into three: noun (اسم), verb (كلمة), and particle, and states that speech (الكلام) is the attachment of some to others. Three basic attachments exist: noun-to-noun, noun-to-verb, and particle-to-both. The author elaborates on the particle's attachment, dividing it into three subtypes: (1) mediating between verb and noun, as in prepositions that make verbs transitive; (2) attachment in conjunction, where the second element falls under the same operator as the first; (3) attachment to the whole sentence, as with negation, interrogation, condition, and consequence particles. In the last case, the scope of the particle is restricted to the subject-predicate relation, not the absolute meaning. The author provides linguistic examples, such as 'لا رجل في الدار' (no man is in the house) and contrasts it with 'ليس رجل في الدار' (there is not a man in the house) to illustrate generality vs. particularity. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid collective and individual predication and identify the conditions under which a compound predicate can be applied.

Core Idea
Predication depends on whether attributes exist absolutely or restrictively, and collective predication requires shared domain.

In this passage, the author delves into the nuanced rules of predication, specifically when we can say a compound like 'skillful physician' versus saying 'physician' and 'skillful' separately. Understanding these conditions is crucial for accurate logical analysis, especially when dealing with compound predicates in theological or juridical definitions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the absolute vs. restrictive distinction
Identify whether an attribute exists in the subject absolutely (without qualification) or restrictively (with a specific condition). For example, 'physician' can be absolute if Zayd is a doctor, but 'physician without excellence' is restrictive.
This distinction is key to determining which predications are valid.
2
Apply the rule for individual predication
If an attribute exists absolutely in the subject, it can be predicated absolutely individually. If it exists restrictively, it can be predicated restrictively individually. 3
Test collective predication
A compound predicate like 'skillful physician' is valid collectively only if both attributes are from the same domain (e.g., both in medicine). If they are from different domains (e.g., medicine and tailoring), collective predication fails, even if each is valid individually.
The compound implies a shared specialization.

بالذات عن الإمكان بالفعل، وما يكون كذلك فهو مركب والمركب ممكن لافتقاره إلى الغير وهو جزؤه فيكون الواجب ممكنا ؛ هذا خلف. .. ثم لقائل أن يقول إنه سلب محض وإلا لا يكون محمولا على الإمكان الخاص، والإمكان الخاص سلب محض وإلا لكان ممكنا لذاته فيكون إمكانه زائدا عليه؛ ولزم التسلسل . وأما الخاص فإنه لا يمكن أن يكون موجودًا البتة؛ وذلك لأنه لا يخلو من أن يكون الممكن موجودًا؛ وحينئذ يكون واجب الوجود فلا يكون ممكن العدم، أو معدوما، وحينئذ يكون واجب العدم فلا يكون ممكن الوجود. ومن أصر على هذا فإنه لا يعترف بالإمكان الخاص إلا بالنسبة إلى الزمان المستقبل كما أن هذا التخصيص متصف بصفة كونه حيا في الحال؛ ففي الاستقبال يمكن أن يبقى حيا ويمكن أن لا يبقى لكنه لا يكون واردا على الحكم يكون الشيء ممكنا في الحال والاستقبال؛ إذ الحكم عليه بالنظر إلى ذاته لا بالنظر إلى كونه موجودًا أو معدوما، ولا يمتنع في العقل أن يكون الشيء واجبًا من وجه وممكنا من وجه.
By essence from actual possibility, and whatever is like that is composite, and the composite is possible due to its need for another which is its part, so the necessary would be possible; this is a contradiction. Then one may say that it is a pure negation, otherwise it would not be predicated of the special possible, and the special possible is a pure negation, otherwise it would be possible in itself, so its possibility would be additional to it, leading to regress. As for the special, it cannot exist at all; because the possible is either existent—then it is necessary of existence, so it is not possible of non-existence—or non-existent—then it is necessary of non-existence, so it is not possible of existence. And whoever insists on this does not recognize the special possible except with respect to future time, just as this particular thing is described by the attribute of being alive now; in the future it may remain alive or not, but it does not apply to the judgment that a thing is possible in the present and future; because judgment is with respect to its essence, not with respect to its existence or non-existence, and it is not intellectually impossible for a thing to be necessary in one respect and possible in another.

Commentary
This passage engages in a critical argument about the concept of the 'special possible' (الإمكان الخاص). It begins with a reductio: if something is necessary by essence and possible in actuality, then it is composite (having both essence and accidental possibility), and whatever is composite is contingent because it depends on its parts. This would make the necessary being contingent—a contradiction. Then a hypothetical objector says the special possible is a pure negation; otherwise, if it were a positive attribute, it would be self-possible, leading to an infinite regress. The author then argues that the special possible cannot be actualized at all: if it exists, it is necessary; if it does not, it is impossible. Finally, a nuanced position is acknowledged: the special possible only applies to future contingents, and a thing can be necessary from one aspect (e.g., its essence) and possible from another (e.g., its existence with respect to a future time). This refines the earlier absolute rejection.

Key Terms
الإمكان الخاص (al-imkan al-khass): A modal notion defined as pure negation (سلب محض) that cannot be actualized; it is only relative to future time. [Note: In later logic, it may be treated as potentiality; here it is critically examined.]
الواجب (al-wajib): That which is necessary of existence; its non-existence is impossible.
الممكن (al-mumkin): That which is neither necessary nor impossible; it can exist or not exist.

Key Takeaway
The special possible is a modal notion that cannot be actualized in present existence; it only has meaning as a future possibility relative to an entity's essence.
