Contrapositive of Negative Conditionals
Author explains that negative conditional propositions do have a valid contrapositive, specifically a particular affirmative one.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the correct contrapositive of a negative conditional proposition and verify its validity using reductio ad absurdum.

Core Idea
Even negative conditionals have a valid contrapositive—a particular affirmative one.

In the previous lesson we learned about contrapositives of affirmative conditionals. Now we turn to negative conditionals, such as 'Not at all if A then B' (a universal negative conditional). You might think such a proposition has no valid contrapositive, but the author demonstrates that it does—specifically a particular affirmative conditional like 'Sometimes if not B then A'. The proof relies on reductio (al-khalf), a standard argument technique in Islamic logic. Understanding this will sharpen your skill in handling immediate inferences and deepen your grasp of the relationship between conditionals and their negations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original negative conditional
Locate the conditional proposition that is negative. For example, 'Not at all if A then B' is a universal negative conditional. It asserts that it is never the case that if A then B.
In the Arabic, the term 'ليس البتة إذا كان أب) كان جدا' represents 'not at all if A then B'.

Recall the contrapositive rule for negatives
The author states that a negative conditional (actual, universal or particular) converts into a particular affirmative contrapositive. That is, from 'Not at all if A then B' we infer 'Sometimes if not B then not A' (with absolute modality).
The clause 'فينعكس عكسا جزئيا' means it yields a particular contrapositive.

Conversion of Conditional Propositions: Implicative vs Accidental
Explains the conversion of implicative and accidental conditionals in universal and particular forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between implicative and accidental conditionals and determine when contrapositive conversion is valid.

Core Idea
Only implicative conditionals allow contrapositive conversion; accidental ones do not.

Conditional propositions appear frequently in Islamic legal reasoning, e.g., 'If the sale is valid, then ownership transfers.' But not all conditionals behave the same way when we try to convert them. This lesson clarifies the crucial distinction between necessary (implicative) and accidental conditionals, and shows when the contrapositive is a valid inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conditional
Look at the connection between the antecedent (المقدم) and consequent (التالي). If the connection is necessary (e.g., based on definition or cause), it is implicative (لزومية). If the connection is merely coincidental, it is accidental (اتفاقية).
The passage uses the terms ملزوم (entailed) and لازم (entailer) for the antecedent and consequent in implicative conditionals.
2
Apply contrapositive to implicative conditionals
For an implicative conditional, the contrapositive is valid: negate both parts and swap them. For example, from 'If the entailed exists, the entailer exists' we infer 'If the entailer does not exist, the entailed does not exist.' The passage states that this holds universally and particularly.
The passage also mentions that even a particular conditional can imply a universal one from one side.
3
Test accidental conditionals
For accidental conditionals, the contrapositive does not hold. Try it: from 'If the antecedent exists, the consequent exists' (accidental), you cannot infer 'If the consequent does not exist, the antecedent does not exist' because the connection is not necessary.
The passage explicitly says accidental conditionals do not convert by contraposition, whether universal or particular.
4
Generalize the rule
The validity of contrapositive conversion depends on the nature of the connection, not on the universality or particularity of the proposition. Always check if the conditional is implicative before applying contrapositive.

Contrapositive Conversion of Universal Negative Actual Conditionals
Discusses contrapositive conversion of negative actual conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform the contrapositive conversion of a universal negative actual conditional and verify its logical validity using proof by contradiction.

Core Idea
Contraposition transforms 'No A implies C' into 'Some not-C implies A'.

In this passage, the author explains how negative conditional propositions convert by contrapositive. He begins with actual conditionals (those concerning actual existence). Understanding this conversion is crucial for immediate inferences in Islamic logic, as it allows us to derive new propositions from given ones without additional premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposition
Locate the universal negative actual conditional: 'Not at all if A is B, then C is D' (ليس البتة إذا كان أب كان جدا). This is a universal negative proposition in the actual modality.
The original is a conditional of the form 'If P then Q' negated universally.
2
State the contrapositive target
The author claims that this converts to a particular absolute universal: 'It may not be that if not C D, then not A B' (قد لا يكون إذا لم يكن ج د لم يكن أب). Note the addition of 'it may not be' indicating particularity.
The conversion is from universal to particular, and from actual to absolute.
3
Apply proof by contradiction
Assume the contrapositive is false, leading to its contradictory: 'Always if not C D, then not A B' (دائما إذا لم يكن ج د لم يكن أب). Then derive a contradiction with the original 'Not at all if A B, then C D'.
The author uses khalaf (reductio ad absurdum) to establish the conversion.
4
Generalize the rule
The same proof works for all universal negative actual conditionals, whether necessary or not. The contrapositive becomes a particular absolute statement (جزئية مطلقة عامة).
This rule is derived from the broader theory of conversion (عكس) in categoricals.

Contrapositive Conversion of Negative Possible and Accidental Conditionals
Discusses contrapositive conversion for possible and accidental conditionals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to perform the contrapositive conversion of negative possible and accidental conditionals, distinguishing the modal differences.

Core Idea
Negative possible conditionals contrapose to particular possible conditionals; accidental conditionals follow the same pattern.

The author extends the conversion rule to possible (mumkina) and accidental (ittifaqiyya) conditionals. For possibles, the contrapositive retains a modality of possibility but shifts from specific to general possibility. Accidental conditionals, which lack a genuine implication, still obey the same conversion pattern, as shown by the example 'man exists, vacuum exists'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original modal context
For possible conditionals, note the modality: the original contains 'with specific possibility' (بالإمكان الخاص). The conversion yields 'with general possibility' (بالإمكان العام).
Specific possibility is more restricted than general possibility.
2
Apply the conversion rule for possibles
A universal negative possible conditional 'Not at all if A B is C D with specific possibility' converts to 'It may not be that if not C D then not A B with general possibility'.
The modality weakens from specific to general possibility.
3
Consider accidental conditionals
Accidental conditionals (e.g., 'If man exists, then vacuum exists') are not genuine implications but still convert. The same formula applies: 'Not at all if man exists then vacuum exists' converts to 'It may not be that if vacuum does not exist then man does not exist'.
The proof by contradiction works equally for accidental conditionals.
4
Verify the particular cases
For particular negatives (e.g., 'It may not be that if man exists then vacuum exists'), the conversion yields a particular of the same modality: 'It may not be that if vacuum does not exist then man does not exist'.
The pattern holds for both universal and particular, with appropriate modality.

Contrapositive Conversion of Conditional Propositions
Author explains the contrapositive conversion for conditional propositions with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to construct the contrapositive of any universal or particular conditional proposition and verify its validity using a proof by contradiction.

Core Idea
Contraposing a conditional swaps and negates antecedent and consequent, always yielding a particular modal proposition.

In the previous lessons, we learned about contrapositive conversion for categorical propositions. Now, the author extends this to conditional propositions. He shows that when you swap and negate the antecedent and consequent, the result is always a particular proposition with a modality of 'possible general'. This is proven by a simple proof by contradiction. Let's explore the rule and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type and quantity of the conditional
Determine if the conditional is universal (e.g., 'it is absolutely not the case that if p then q') or particular (e.g., 'it may not be that if p then q'), and whether it is implicational (luzumiyya) or conjunctive (ittifaqiyya).
The quantity and type affect the modality of the contrapositive result.
2
Perform the contrapositive operation
Swap the antecedent and consequent and negate both. For a universal conditional 'not (if p then q)', the contrapositive is 'it may not be that if not q then not p'.
The result is a particular proposition, not universal.
3
Adjust the modality
For universal conditionals, the contrapositive has modality 'possible general' (imkan 'amm). For particular conditionals in actuals, it becomes 'absolute general' (mutlaqa 'amma).
This is because the contrapositive of a universal is particular, and the modality shifts from necessity to possibility.
4
Verify via proof by contradiction
Assume the contrapositive is false, which would make its contradictory true. Show that this leads to an impossibility (contradiction) based on the original proposition.
The author uses this method to guarantee the validity of the conversion.
5
Apply to conjunctive conditionals
The same rule applies to conjunctive conditionals (e.g., 'if man exists then void exists'). The contrapositive is formed similarly, and the proof by contradiction holds.
The example 'man exists' and 'void exists' illustrates the rule for accidental connections.
