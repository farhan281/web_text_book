Objection to Learning Logic: Circularity and Infinite Regress Refuted
Author presents and refutes the objection that learning logic leads to circularity or infinite regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the objection that learning logic leads to circularity or infinite regress and explain the author's solution through ordered sciences.

Core Idea
The chain of knowledge ends in self-evident truths, not infinite regress.

A common objection to learning logic is that it seems impossible because it would require prior knowledge, leading to circularity or an endless chain. In this passage, the author identifies this objection and dismantles it by showing that sciences are not homogeneous—they include self-evident foundations and acquired knowledge arranged in a specific order. This refutation is crucial for establishing the possibility and legitimacy of logical study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Find the statement that learning logic is impossible (la yumkin al-battah). The objection is introduced with 'wa qad qila fihi...'
2
Extract the two horns of the dilemma
The objection claims that if learning logic requires acquisition (kasb), then either dawr (circularity) or tasalsul (infinite regress) follows. Both are impossible.
Dawr occurs if the same knowledge depends on itself; tasalsul if an endless chain of prior knowledge is needed.
3
Understand the definition of thought
The author defines fikr (thought) as a transition from present mental contents to absent ones, which must be acquired. This sets up the dependency that leads to the dilemma.
The phrase 'al-fikr al-muta'attib lil-'azm' may be obscure, but the key is that thought moves from known to unknown.
4
Grasp the author's refutation
The author responds by noting that sciences are divided into badihi (self-evident) and kasbi (acquired). They are not all the same type; rather, they are arranged in a specific order (murattaba tartiban khassan). Self-evident truths ground the acquired ones, stopping the regress.
The objection assumed all knowledge requires acquisition; the author shows this assumption is false.
5
Connect to the logician's task
The author concludes that the logician must examine the prior sciences and how they are composed to lead to unknowns. This makes logic possible without falling into vicious circularity or regress.
The ordered arrangement is key: the logician does not need infinite prior knowledge, only properly ordered self-evident starting points.

And it has been said about it: 'It is absolutely impossible, otherwise either circularity or infinite regress would occur, because if everything depends on acquisition' – that is thought, and thought here is the thought that intends determination, which is the transition from matters present in the mind to matters not present. Commentary
This passage is dialectical: it presents an objection to the possibility of learning logic and then refutes it. The objection runs: if all knowledge requires prior knowledge (acquisition), then learning the first principles would lead either to circularity (the same knowledge depending on itself) or infinite regress (an endless chain of prior knowledges). The author identifies 'thought' (fikr) as a transition from present to absent mental contents, and those absent contents must be acquired. The objection is that the very act of thinking would require prior acquisitions, leading to a vicious cycle. The author's response is to clarify that sciences are not all of one type; some are self-evident (badihiyya) and others are acquired (kasbiyya). Moreover, prior sciences need not be either exclusively; they must be arranged in a particular order. Therefore, the logician must examine those prior sciences and how they lead to desired unknowns, thus avoiding regression. The key point is that the logical order breaks the infinite chain by grounding the acquisition in self-evident principles.

Logical Rules Are Abstract and Rational
Author argues that logical rules are purely rational, abstracted from matter and attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between judging an essence as such and judging it as qualified, and explain why logical rules are universal despite material exceptions.

Core Idea
Logical rules apply to essences as such, not to qualified instances.

This passage opens a new chapter on rational rules. The author makes a crucial epistemological point: logical rules are abstract and independent of the specific content or subject matter of propositions. To understand why material exceptions do not invalidate a rule, we must distinguish between judging a thing's essence (mahiyya) as such and judging it as qualified by an attribute. This distinction is foundational for the universality of logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
The author states that logical rules are rational (aqliyya) and abstracted (mujarrada) from matters (mawadd) and from agreement/disagreement in substances and accidents. Note that this means the validity of a logical rule does not depend on the specific content of propositions.
Rational here means purely conceptual, not empirical.
2
Understand the essence-qualification distinction
The author distinguishes between judging the essence of a thing as such (min haythu hiya al-mahiyya) and judging it as qualified by an attribute (muttasifa bi-sifa). The former gives necessary and universal properties; the latter may give contingent or particular properties.
The qualified essence is composite, while the pure essence is simple or basic.
3
Apply to the example of conversion
The author explicitly says that if a proposition is convertible in some matters but not others, that does not mean the conversion rule is invalid in itself. The rule applies to the proposition qua proposition (its essence), not to its particular qualified instances.
This illustrates how the essence-qualification distinction protects logical rules from counterexamples.
4
Recognize the warning
The author ends by cautioning that some opposed earlier scholars because they neglected this reality about essences. Reflect on why ignoring this distinction leads to errors in logic.
This reinforces the importance of the epistemological point.

Chapter on rational rules. Know that logical rules are rational rules, and rational rules are abstracted from matters and from what pertains to them of agreement and disagreement in substances and accidents. So it does not follow that if a proposition is convertible in some matters but not others, or in some respects but not others, that it is convertible in itself insofar as it is itself. And the judgment on the essence of a thing insofar as it is the essence is different from the judgment on it insofar as it is qualified by a certain attribute in the mind or in the external world. So what belongs to a thing according to its essence is necessary for that thing in such a way that the existence of that essence cannot be without it at all. And likewise if it is according to its essence qualified by a certain attribute; for the qualified essence insofar as it is a qualified essence is a composite essence in reality. And the judgment on the essence insofar as it is the essence is not specific to some essences to the exclusion of others. And the essence may be simple, or it may be composite. And composite essence may be composed of simples, or it may be composed of composites, as was previously mentioned in what we are discussing of categoricals and conditionals. So take heed from what you have known, for among them are those who opposed the earlier scholars due to their heedlessness of the reality of what they held. And with God is success.

Commentary
This passage opens a new chapter entitled 'On Rational Rules'. The author asserts that logical rules are rational rules ('aqliyya), which are abstracted (mujarrada) from subject matters (mawadd) and from any agreement or disagreement in substances and accidents. This means that the validity of a logical rule, such as conversion, does not depend on the specific content of the propositions. If a certain proposition converts in some materials but not others, that does not imply the rule is not generally valid, because the rule applies to the proposition qua proposition, not to its material. The author then introduces a crucial epistemological distinction: judging a thing's essence (mahiyya) as such is different from judging it as qualified by an attribute (sifa). What belongs to a thing by virtue of its essence is necessary and inseparable from its existence. However, if the essence is considered together with an attribute, it becomes a composite essence (mahiyya murakkaba) in reality, and the judgment on the qualified essence is not the same as on the pure essence. This distinction explains why logical rules are universal: they apply to essences as such, not to particular qualified instances. The author warns that some have opposed earlier scholars because they neglected this reality, and concludes with a prayer.
