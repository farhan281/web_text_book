The Relativity of the Five Universals
Author explains that each universal is only so in relation to something else

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which universal a term is in a given relation and explain how removing the differentia transforms a genus into a species.

Core Idea
Genus, species, difference, property, and accident are relative terms, not fixed labels.

This passage from the logic text explains a crucial point: the five universals (al-kulliyyat al-khams) are not absolute categories. A term like 'animal' can be a genus when considered with its differentia (rational), but without that differentia it becomes a species. The author uses concrete examples to show how each universal shifts depending on what it is compared to. Understanding this relativity is essential for constructing correct definitions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the universal in its context
The passage defines each universal as only being that universal in relation to something else. For example, genus is not genus for everything, only for its species.
2
Remove the differentia from a genus
Take 'animal' without its differentia 'rational'. When considered alone, 'animal' becomes a species (a kind of animal) rather than a genus.
This is the key illustration.
3
Apply the same logic to all five universals
The author shows that 'rational' without 'animal' becomes a species; 'laughing' without 'human' becomes a species; 'white' without a subject becomes a species.
Each universal can become a species when taken absolutely.
4
Summarise the relativity principle
The five universals are not intrinsic properties of terms, but depend on the terms they are compared to. A term may be genus in one relation and species in another.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional exposition of the relative nature (munasabat) among the five universals. The author explains that each universal is only a universal with respect to something else. For example, genus is genus only in relation to its species, difference is difference only for a particular genus, species is species only in relation to its genus, and so on. He then gives an important illustration: when a universal is taken without its differentia, it can become a species. For instance, 'animal' without 'rational' is a species (of animals), not a genus. Similarly, 'rational' without 'animal' is a species (of rational beings), 'laughing' without 'human' is a species, and 'white' without a subject is a species. This shows that 'genus', 'species', 'difference', 'property', and 'common accident' are relative terms, not fixed categories.

Key Terms
الجنس (al-jins): Genus: that which is predicated in answer to 'what is it?' over multiple species; only genus in relation to its species.
النوع (al-naw'): Species: that which is under a genus; only species in relation to its genus; can become species when taken without differentia.
الفصل (al-faṣl): Difference: that which distinguishes a species within a genus; only difference for a genus; can become species when taken without genus.
الخاصة (al-khāṣṣa): Property: that which is unique to a species; only property in relation to that species; can become species when taken without the species.
العرض العام (al-'araḍ al-'āmm): Common accident: that which occurs to multiple species; only accident in relation to its subjects; can become species when taken absolutely.

Why Genus is Not a Genus for Its Difference
Author refutes the idea that genus is genus for difference, and clarifies their external relation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why genus is not predicated of difference and identify the correct external relationship between them in definition.

Core Idea
Genus and difference are not in a genus-species relation; difference is external to genus.

This passage clarifies a common confusion: one might think that because difference is part of the definition, genus applies to it. The author shows that if genus were predicated of difference, an infinite regress would follow. Instead, difference is a meaning external to genus, and when genus is said of difference, it is like a necessary accident.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rejected claim
The author states that genus is not a genus for its difference, nor is difference a species of genus. Recognize this as the core denial.
2
Follow the reductio argument
If difference were a species of genus, it would need another difference to distinguish it, leading to an infinite regress. This shows the impossibility.
The text says: 'for then it would need another difference'.
3
Examine the external relationship
Difference is said to be 'a meaning external to the nature of genus qua its nature'. Similarly, genus is external to difference. They are not in a Categorical relation of inclusion.
4
Analyze the example of 'rational' and 'animal'
'Rational' is not 'animal possessing speech' but simply 'something possessing speech', though it implies animality. The combination 'animal possessing speech' is the species 'human'. This illustrates how difference relates to genus.
5
Understand the analogy with necessary accident
When genus is predicated of difference, it is like a necessary accident accompanying its subject. The difference combines with genus to constitute the species actually, while properties attach only after the species is constituted.
This clarifies the logical role of difference vs. property.

The Relativity of the Five Universals
Author explains how the five universals are relative to their individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how each of the five universals can be considered a different universal relative to different individuals.

Core Idea
A universal's classification depends on the individual it is predicated of.

The five universals are not fixed in their roles. A genus can become a species, a differentia can become a species, depending on what we leave out or include. This lesson walks through the examples given by the author to show this relativity.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of relativity
A universal's role changes based on whether the differentia or genus is included. For instance, 'animal' is a genus for man, but without 'rational' it becomes a species relative to a particular animal. The author says: 'the things sharing in a genus may exist in such a way that the genus is only a species relative to them'. This means the same term can be genus in one context and species in another.
This is not a contradiction; it depends on the level of specificity.
2
Examine the example of 'animal'
Take 'animal' as a genus when it includes differentia like 'rational' for man. But if you consider 'animal' without any differentia, and look at a particular animal (this horse), then 'animal' is predicated of that individual and is a species relative to it, because 'animal' is predicated of many individuals without further differentiation.
The key is that the differentia is omitted.
3
Extend to other universals
Similarly, 'rational' without animality is a species relative to a particular rational being; 'laughter' without being human is a species for a particular laughter; 'white' for a particular white thing. Each universal, when considered without the higher genus, becomes a species for that individual.
This shows the relativity of all five universals.
4
Recognize the condition of mutual externality
Just as genus is not genus of its particulars without differentia, it is not genus of the differentia itself. The differentia is external to the genus's nature. The author argues that if differentia were a species of genus, it would need another differentia, leading to infinite regress.
This reinforces the distinct roles of genus and differentia.

The Logical Differentia: Concrete Predicate Not Abstract Noun
Author clarifies that the logical differentia is the concrete attribute, not the abstract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the logical differentia (concrete predicate) from the abstract noun and correctly identify the five universals as concrete predicates.

Core Idea
The five universals are the concrete predicates, not the abstract concepts.

The author emphasizes that the logical differentia of man is 'rational' (natiq) not 'rationality' (nutq). This applies to all universals: genus is 'animal' not 'animality', species is 'man' not 'humanity', etc. This lesson unpacks why the concrete predicate is used.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of universals
The five universals are predicates that are said of many individuals. For example, 'animal' is predicated of man, horse, etc. The universals are the terms that appear in propositions as predicates.
They must be concrete so they can be attributed.
2

Commentary
This passage enumerates the five types of substance: hyle (prime matter), form (sura), body (composite of hyle and form), soul (which is not a locus, resident, or composite but related to body as its administrator), and intellect (which has no such relation). Then the author presents a reductio argument from the majority ('inda al-jumhur) against substance being a genus for these five. The argument proceeds by examining the quiddity (mahiyya) of which 'substance' is predicated. If that quiddity is simple, then substance cannot be a genus because a genus always requires a differentia to complete the species; a simple would not be composed of genus and differentia. If it is composite, then the simples composing it must be either accidents or substances. If they are accidents, then substance would be constituted by accidents, which is impossible because substance is prior. If they are substances, then they themselves, being simple, are not under any genus, so substance cannot be a genus of them. Moreover, it is impossible that some simples are substances and others accidents. Substance cannot be constituted by accident; a composite of substance and accident is neither simply substance nor accident. Instead, the aggregate is both. Therefore, substance is not a genus. This argument is crucial because it shows that the five substances are not species of a single genus; they are distinct kinds. In the Islamic sciences, this relates to discussions about the nature of the soul and intellect in kalam and falsafah.

Definitions of Universal, Accident, and Substance
Author establishes that every universal is said of a subject, every accident exists in a subject, and defines substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given term as universal, accident, or substance by applying the criteria from this passage.

Core Idea
Every universal is said of a subject; every accident exists in a subject; substance is neither.

This passage establishes the core definitions that underlie the entire edifice of Islamic logic: what it means for a term to be universal, to be an accident, and to be a substance. These definitions are not just abstract; they are the foundation for distinguishing essential and accidental predication, which is crucial in every Islamic science from Kalam to Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key equivalences
The passage states: (1) Every universal is said of a subject, and everything said of a subject is universal. (2) Every accident exists in a subject, and everything existing in a subject is an accident. Notice that these are biconditional equivalences.
The term 'said of a subject' (maqul 'ala al-mawdu') means predicated of a subject, e.g., 'man' is said of Zaid.
2
Understand the reason for universality
A universal is said of a subject because it has particulars (juz'iyyat) either actually (e.g., Zaid under 'man') or potentially (e.g., species under genus). This ensures the equivalence holds.
3
Distinguish between the two relations
'Said of a subject' is about predication (a universal term applied to its particulars). 'Exists in a subject' is about inherence (an accident residing in a substance). They are different relations, but the passage shows they are coextensive with universals and accidents respectively.
Not everything said of a subject exists in it (e.g., 'man' is said of Zaid but does not exist in Zaid as an accident).
4
Define substance negatively
Substance (jawhar) is defined as that which does not exist in a subject. This is a negative definition (rasm), identifying what substance is not rather than what it is.
Example: a human being (insan) is a substance because it does not inhere in anything else.
5
Apply the definitions to new examples
Take a term like 'whiteness' (bayad). Is it said of a subject? Yes, we say 'the wall is white.' So it is universal. Does it exist in a subject? Yes, whiteness inheres in a wall. So it is an accident. Take 'human': said of many individuals, yes, so universal; does it exist in a subject? No, humans are substances. So it is not an accident.
This confirms the passage's equivalences.

Commentary

The Fourfold Classification of Things by Predication and Existence
Presents a fourfold classification of things based on predication and existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any concept into one of four categories based on whether it is predicated of a subject and/or exists in a subject.

Core Idea
Everything is either predicated of a subject, exists in a subject, both, or neither.

Building on his critique, the author presents a precise fourfold classification of all things (concepts) based on two criteria: predication (being said of a subject) and existence in a subject. This classification refines the traditional substance-accident distinction and is essential for understanding predication in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two criteria
The author uses two criteria: 'predicated of a subject' (maqūl 'alā mawḍū') and 'existing in a subject' (mawjūd fī mawḍū'). These are independent, giving four combinations.
Note: 'existing in a subject' means inhering as an accident, not just spatially.
2
Identify the first category: universal substances
Things that are predicated of a subject (e.g., 'insān' said of Zayd) but do not exist in a subject. These are universal substances, like the species 'man'.
Example: 'insān' is a universal substance.
3
Identify the second category: particular accidents
Things that exist in a subject (e.g., a particular whiteness in a body) but are not predicated of a subject (they are particular, not said of many). These are particular accidents.
Example: 'this whiteness' is a particular accident.
4
Identify the third category: universal accidents
Things that are both predicated of a subject (e.g., 'whiteness' said of a particular white thing) and exist in a subject (whiteness in a body). These are universal accidents, like the attribute 'whiteness' in general.
Example: 'al-bayāḍ al-kullī' (universal whiteness).
5
Identify the fourth category: particular substances
Things that are neither predicated of a subject (they are individuals) nor exist in a subject (they are substances). These are particular substances like Zayd, Amr, or this soul.
Example: 'Zayd' is a particular substance.

Classifying Genera and Species: High, Low, and Intermediate
Author defines high genera, low species, and intermediate genera/species with their differentiate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given genus or species is high, low, or intermediate based on its differentiate.

Core Idea
High genera lack constitutive differentiae; low species lack divisive differentiae; intermediates have both.

This passage from the logic text explains the hierarchy of genera and species on the tree of Porphyry. It clarifies which differentiae apply at each level: constitutive differentiae define a species within a genus, while divisive differentiae split a genus into its species. Understanding this classification is essential for constructing valid definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the level: high, low, or intermediate
Read the term and determine if it is the most universal (high), most specific (low), or somewhere in between (intermediate). High genera have no genus above them; low species have no species below them.
2
Check for constitutive differentiae
If the term is a species, it must have a constitutive differentia (e.g., rationality for human). High genera lack these because they are distinguished by themselves.
3
Check for divisive differentiae
If the term is a genus, it must have divisive differentiae that split it into species (e.g., rational/irrational for animal). Low species lack these because they have no species below them.
4
Verify using the passage's criteria
Confirm your classification against the author's description: high genera have only divisive differentiae, low species have only constitutive differentiae, intermediates have both.

Genus Applying to Species Differing in Priority
Author discusses how genus applies to species differing in priority and posteriority, using examples from quantity and substance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a genus applies to species that differ in priority and posteriority, and explain why this does not invalidate the genus-species relationship.

Core Idea
A genus can apply to species that are not equally primary without breaking universal logic.

This passage tackles a subtle puzzle: if a genus applies to species that are ordered before and after each other, does that mean the genus is not truly a genus? The author uses examples from quantity, number, and substance to show that priority and posteriority do not negate genus. The discussion is part of a broader inquiry into the five universals, specifically the relationship between genus and species.

Guided Path
1
Identify the puzzle
The author introduces a known objection: many genera apply to species differing in priority and posteriority. That is, one species is considered prior to another under the same genus. Understand that this is seen as a potential challenge to the definition of genus.
The objection is that if genus is truly universal, it should apply equally to all species.
2
Examine the quantity example
Quantity (al-kamm) is given as a genus that applies to continuous (e.g., lines) and discrete (e.g., numbers) species. The discrete is said to be prior to the continuous. Note the reasoning: discrete quantity (number) is conceptually simpler and foundational.
Continuous quantity depends on discrete in some sense.
3
Examine the number example
Number itself applies to two, three, and other numbers, which differ in priority and posteriority. Two is prior to three. This shows that even within a species (number), there can be priority, but number is still a single genus.
This reinforces that priority does not break genus.
4
Consider the substance example
Substance applies to primary (individuals) and secondary (species) substances, and to simple and compound. Primary substance is prior to secondary. The author mentions this as another example but postpones a full resolution.
This example is central to metaphysics.
5
Understand the author's postponement
The author says it is better to address this puzzle later, indicating that the examples are not yet a full refutation but set the stage. Recognize that logical puzzles often require deeper investigation.

Author explains that logical fusul are substances but do not entail substantiality.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Logical differentiae are substances, but their meaning does not include substance.

When logicians define a species like human, they use the differentia 'rational'. But does that differentia itself include the idea of being a substance? The author clarifies that logical differentiae are indeed substances—they exist as real properties—but their conceptual meaning does not entail substantiality. This nuance is crucial for understanding the five universals and how definitions work.

Identify the claim
The author states that logical differentiae are substances but do not entail substantiality. Find this statement in the Arabic passage.

The author uses 'natiq' (rational) as an example. Its meaning is 'a thing possessing speech', not 'a substance that possesses speech'.

Continuous and Discrete as Differentiae of Quantity
Author explains differentiate of quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a term functions as a species or a differentia within a genus, using the example of continuous and discrete quantity.

Core Idea
Differentiae share subject with species but differ in consideration.

This passage clarifies a subtle point in the logic of categories: continuous and discrete are not species of quantity but its differentiae. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the five universals to real-world classifications. We will walk through the author's reasoning using the familiar example of 'rational' derived from 'speech'.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish species from differentia
Recall that a species includes the genus plus a differentia. If continuous were a species, it would include quantity in its definition. The author says continuous and discrete are differentiae, not species, unless the genus is conjoined.
Species are predicated of individuals; differentiae are predicated of species.
2
Examine the example of natiq and nutq
The differentia 'rational' (natiq) is derived from a meaning 'speech' (nutq) that exists in the species but is not itself predicated of the species. This shows that the differentia is not a separate entity but a consideration of the species.
Nutq is not a species of human; it is the source of the differentia.
3
Apply to continuous and discrete
Just as natiq is the differentia of human, continuous and discrete are differentiae of quantity. They are not separate species because they share the same subject (quantity) but differ in consideration (continuity vs. discreteness).
The essence of the species and the logical differentia are one in subject.
4
Understand the absence of simple differentia
The author states there is no simple differentia for continuous and discrete. This means continuity is not caused by an external continuity, nor discreteness by an external discreteness. The differentia is intrinsic.
This prevents an infinite regress of differentiae.

Distinguishing Privation and Possession from Other Types of Opposition
Author differentiates privation/possession from other opposites via subject conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate privation/possession from contrariety, relation, and contradiction based on the condition of the subject and the possibility of an intermediate.

Core Idea
Privation and possession require a subject capable of the attribute, with no middle state; contraries allow a middle, relatives are interdependent, contradiction is binary truth.

In Islamic logic, four types of opposition are distinguished: privation/possession, contrariety, relation, and contradiction. This passage unpacks the unique conditions that set privation/possession apart, particularly the requirement that the subject be capable of the attribute and that one of the two opposites always applies. Understanding these distinctions is essential for accurate definition and reasoning in kalam and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of opposition
List the four opposites: privation/possession (العدم والملكة), contrariety (التضاد), relation (التضايف), and contradiction (التناقض). Note that the passage focuses on privation/possession.
2
Focus on privation/possession: subject condition
The author states that for privation/possession, the subject must be naturally capable of possessing the attribute. For example, blindness only applies to subjects that normally have sight (animals), not to inanimate objects.
This distinguishes it from other opposites where the subject condition is different.
3
Compare with contrariety: intermediate possibility
Contraries like hot and cold can have an intermediate (warm), but privation/possession has no intermediate – either the subject has the property or it does not, if it is capable.
The author gives the example of whiteness of cactus (a natural inseparable quality) as a contrary with no intermediate.
4
Compare with relation: mutual reference
Relatives (like father and son) are defined by mutual relation; each is understood only in relation to the other. In privation/possession, each term is independent of the other beyond the opposition.
5
Compare with contradiction: truth conditions
Contradictory propositions (e.g., 'Zayd is blind' and 'Zayd is not blind') are always one true and one false. In privation/possession, both can be false if the subject lacks capacity (e.g., a rock is neither blind nor seeing) or if the subject does not exist.
The passage clarifies that for a non-existent subject, both privation and possession are false.

Commentary
This passage is an extended classification and differentiation of the four types of opposition familiar in Islamic logic: privation and possession (al-ʿadam wa al-mulka), relation (al-taḍāyuf), contrariety (al-taḍādd), and contradiction (al-tanāquḍ). The author begins by clarifying the behavior of privation and possession when the subject exists or does not exist. He notes that if the subject is non-existent, both terms (e.g., blind and seeing) are false because the subject is absent. This distinguishes it from contradiction, where one must be true and the other false. He then specifies that in the 'commonly known' type of privation (al-mashhūrī), the privation applies only after the time when the possession was expected, and if the subject exists but lacks the natural capacity (like a rock cannot be blind), it is neither blind nor seeing. He then contrasts relatives (al-mutaḍāyifān) with other opposites: each relative is defined in relation to its correlate (e.g., father and son), while other opposites do not require this mutual relation. For contraries (al-mutaḍāddān), he notes that they may have an intermediary (e.g., warm between hot and cold), which is impossible for other opposites. Then he delves deeper into the unique conditions of privation and possession: the subject must be capable of possessing the attribute at some time, and the privation occurs when that possession is absent at a time when it could have been present. This condition does not apply to contraries, where the subject can transition between extremes without a specific time constraint, and where the middle state (e.g., lukewarm) can exist without either extreme. He also notes that in privation and possession, one of the two must always apply (if the subject is capable), whereas in contraries, the middle state can exist without either extreme. Finally, he clarifies a linguistic point: terms like 'blindness' (al-ʿamā) refer to the nature of the privation itself, not to the state of being blind (al-yaʿmā). Thus, we say 'Zayd becomes blind' (yaʿmā) but not 'Zayd is blindness' (ʿamā) – the former indicates a relation to the subject, the latter is the abstract concept. Throughout, the author uses intuitive examples like sight and blindness (relevant to animal subjects) and whiteness of cactus (a natural, inseparable quality) to illustrate the points.

So a thing, any thing, relative to the first is either impossible or not impossible, and it is the possible in the general sense. And relative to the second, it is of three divisions: either impossible, or necessary, or possible, and this is the possible in the special sense. It is also expressed in another phrase: it is said 'either impossible of existence, or impossible of non-existence, or neither impossible of existence nor impossible of non-existence', or 'either necessary of existence, or necessary of non-existence, or neither necessary of existence nor necessary of non-existence'. [T] And in the third, it is what is neither realized nor necessary in the future; and this is the most specific of the second, just as the second is [more specific] than the first. So the possible is of three divisions, each ordered above the other in the order of the more general above the more specific: in the first, its judgment is 'not impossible' — and by judgment we mean what is judged of affirmation or negation; in the second, 'not necessary'; in the third, 'not realized and not necessary in the future'. It is apparent that non-existence in the present is not among the necessary concomitants; this will be known in its place, God willing.

Commentary
This passage defines the three meanings of the term 'possible' (الممكن) in modal logic. The author first explains that 'possible' has three usages: (1) The first meaning: what is not impossible, regardless of whether it is necessary or not. This is the broadest sense, called 'general possibility' (الإمكان العام). (2) The second meaning: what is neither necessary nor impossible; both its affirmation and negation are equally possible. This is the 'special possibility' (الإمكان الخاص). (3) The third meaning: what is not actually present and not necessary in the future; it refers to future contingents that have not yet occurred. This is the most specific sense. The author then clarifies the relationships among these three: the first is the most general, the second is more specific than the first, and the third is the most specific. The passage also presents alternative phrasings: for example, the threefold division for the special sense is 'impossible, necessary, possible'. The author notes that the criterion for each meaning is the judgment (الحكم) of affirmation or negation: in the first it is 'not impossible', in the second 'not necessary', and in the third 'not present and not necessary in the future'. This careful classification is foundational for modal logic, especially for analyzing propositions in the Islamic sciences, such as distinguishing between necessary truths (e.g., 'God exists') and contingent ones (e.g., 'Zayd will travel tomorrow').

Exclusive Disjunction: Structure and Entailments
Author explains the structure and logical implications of exclusive disjunction and negative universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of an exclusive disjunction and determine whether a universal negative disjunction entails its positive counterpart.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction asserts exactly one disjunct true, but its entailments depend on the relation between contradiction and entailment.

In this passage, the author defines the exclusive disjunction (al-mani'at min al-khuluw dūn al-jam') and explores its logical implications. You will learn how it is constructed from the antecedent and what is more general than the contradictory of the consequent. Moreover, you will understand the crucial distinction between universal negatives that entail the universal positive and those that do not, a distinction that affects what conclusions can be drawn.

As for the exclusive disjunction (man'at al-khuluw), it is like what is composed from the very antecedent and what is more general than the contradictory of the consequent, and likewise from the very consequent and what is more general than the contradictory of the antecedent, and the multiplicity in them is according to the multiplicity in the more general. Also, accordingly, with respect to negatives, the positive among disjunctives, whether real or unreal, is not true unless a negative among disjunctives is true within it. That is because a thing is not contradictory to a thing unless it entails what contradicts it, and it does not entail that unless the negation of contradiction is valid according to what was known before. This is the discussion of the universal positive. As for the universal negative, the universal negative may be entailing the universal positive as mentioned before, or may not be entailing. As for the entailing one, just as it entails the positive, so it entails all the consequences of the positive from real and unreal disjunctives, positive and negative. As for the non-entailing one, it does not entail either positives or negatives because the negation of contradiction may separate from the negation of entailment, or may not separate; and the negation of entailment may separate from the negation of contradiction, or may not separate. If it is so, then one does not judge by negation upon negation nor by affirmation at all, so it does not entail from the positives and negatives in this case contrary to when it entails the universal positive. Commentary
This passage defines the structure of exclusive disjunction (المانعة من الخلو دون الجمع) and discusses its logical properties, particularly the relationship between positive and negative disjunctive propositions. The author begins by explaining how exclusive disjunction is composed: it involves the antecedent and something more general than the contradictory of the consequent, or vice versa. The multiplicity of possibilities depends on the degree of generality. Then the author moves to negative disjunctives: a positive disjunctive (whether real or unreal) is true only if a negative disjunctive is implicitly true within it. The reasoning is that contradiction (العناد) requires entailment (اللزوم) of the contradictory. The discussion then treats the universal negative (السالبة الكلية): it may or may not entail the universal positive. If it does entail, then it entails all consequences of the positive. If it does not entail, then nothing can be inferred about positivity or negativity because the negation of contradiction does not consistently correlate with the negation of entailment. The author concludes by linking this to earlier discussions on conditionals, showing how to determine necessary consequences in various types of conditionals.

Key Terms
المانعة من الخلو دون الجمع (al-mani'at min al-khuluw dūn al-jam'): Exclusive disjunction: a proposition that asserts that at least one of two disjuncts is true, but not both. The author defines it as composed of the antecedent and what is more general than the contradictory of the consequent. [Note: Stable term across classical logic; sometimes called 'exclusive or'.]
اللزوم (al-luzūm): Entailment or necessary consequence; the relation where one proposition logically implies another. [Note: In later works, luzūm is often contrasted with istilzām, but here it is used broadly.]
العناد (al-'inād): Contradiction or opposition between two propositions; the logical relation of being mutually exclusive. [Note: Often synonymous with tanāquḍ, but here it specifically refers to the mutual exclusivity in disjunction.]
