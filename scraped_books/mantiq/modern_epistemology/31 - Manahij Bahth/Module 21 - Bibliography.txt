Navigating Classical Arabic Bibliographies
Lists key classical works on sects, history, and biographies of scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate and identify key classical Arabic bibliographical works on Islamic intellectual history and assess their relevance for research.

Core Idea
Bibliographies are treasure maps to the classical Islamic sciences.

This lesson introduces a list of essential reference works on sects, philosophers, and physicians. These are not mere lists; they are primary sources that form the backbone of scholarly research in Islamic intellectual history. By learning to navigate them, you will equip yourself with the tools to trace the transmission of ideas, verify claims, and build your own research foundation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genre of each work
Notice how the works are categorized: 'Milal wa Nihal' (sects and creeds), 'Fihrist' (catalogue), 'Tabaqat' (biographical classes), 'Akhbar al-Hukama' (biographies of philosophers). Each genre serves a distinct purpose in accessing historical knowledge.
For example, Al-Shahrastani's work classifies theological sects.
2
Note the editions and publication details
Each entry includes a place and year (or 'no date'). These details are critical for verifying the reliability and availability of the edition. Compare editions to see if they are critical or popular.
Some editions, like that of Al-Fihrist (Leiden, 882 AH), are early scholarly prints.
3
Recognize the scope and overlap
Al-Shahrazuri's 'Rawdat al-Afrah' is noted to greatly resemble Al-Bayhaqi's 'Tatimmat Siwan al-Hikma'. Such overlaps indicate dependency or shared sources – a clue for critical research.
Cross-referencing these works can reveal chains of transmission.
4
Assess the value statement
The passage ends by asserting the 'great value' of these works for understanding Muslim history of sciences. This is not just opinion; it is a methodological signal that these are foundational sources.
Treat this list as a reading list for serious engagement with Islamic thought.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing classical Arabic references relevant to the study of Islamic intellectual history, including logic, theology, and the classification of sciences. It begins with general references, starting with al-Shahrastani's "Al-Milal wa al-Nihal" (Sects and Creeds) and Ibn Hazm's work on the same topic, both from Cairo editions. Al-Mubashshir ibn Fatik's "Mukhtar al-Hikam" collects sayings and biographies. The passage ends by emphasizing the great value of these works for understanding the history of Islamic sciences.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali fused logic with usul; Ibn Hazm rejected qiyas — two pivots in the logical-legal tradition.

This bibliography from Manahij Bahth lists key works in usul al-fiqh. Among them, al-Ghazali's al-Mustasfa marks a turning point: it was the first to integrate Aristotelian logic into Islamic jurisprudence. In contrast, Ibn Hazm's al-Ihkam represents the Zahiri school's absolute rejection of qiyas (analogical reasoning). Understanding these positions is crucial for grasping the debates around logic in Islamic sciences.

3
Contrast with Ibn Hazm
Ibn Hazm's al-Ihkam absolutely denies qiyas. This reflects the Zahiri school's rejection of analogical reasoning in law. Contrast this with al-Ghazali's embrace of logical methods.

Al-Ghazali’s Major Works on Logic and Philosophy
Author lists key books of Usul al-Fiqh and Kalam with brief notes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list al-Ghazali's key logical and philosophical works and explain the purpose of each.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali wrote both against philosophy and using logic—he is a pivotal figure in Islamic epistemology.

This bibliography lists several of al-Ghazali's works: Tahafut al-Falasifa, Maqasid al-Falasifa, Mi'yar al-Ilm, Mihakk al-Nazar, al-Qistas, Faysal al-Tafriqa, al-Risala al-Laduniyya, and al-Munqidh min al-Dalal. These span from critiques of philosophy to original works on logic and epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Separate polemical from constructive works
Tahafut al-Falasifa is polemical against philosophers; Mi'yar al-Ilm is a constructive logic text. This division helps you understand Ghazali's dual role as critic and builder.
He uses logic to refute the philosophers, but also writes independent logical works.
2
Identify the epistemological works
al-Munqidh min al-Dalal is a spiritual autobiography about his search for certainty; Faysal al-Tafriqa deals with boundaries of belief and unbelief.
These works address the foundations of knowledge and faith.
3
Note the logical treatises
Mi'yar al-Ilm, Mihakk al-Nazar, and al-Qistas are Ghazali's main logical works, each presenting aspects of Aristotelian logic with Islamic examples.
Mi'yar al-Ilm is his most comprehensive logic book.
4
Understand the relationship between them
Maqasid al-Falasifa is an objective summary of philosophy; Tahafut refutes it. Together they show Ghazali's strategy of 'know and then refute'.
This pair illustrates his method of engaging with falsafa.
