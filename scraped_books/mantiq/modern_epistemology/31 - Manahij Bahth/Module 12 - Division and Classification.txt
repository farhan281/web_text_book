The Three Types of Verbal Signification
Author defines the three kinds of verbal signification with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of verbal signification (mutabaqa, tadamun, iltizam) using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Words signify meanings in three ways: complete correspondence, partial inclusion, or necessary implication.

In Islamic logic, understanding how words convey meaning is foundational. The passage defines three types of verbal signification (dalala) that every student of logic must master. This lesson will help you recognize each type and apply the distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being analyzed
First, look at the word and its supposed meaning. For example, 'human' and its meaning 'rational animal'.
The word is the signifier, the meaning is the signified.
2
Determine if the meaning is the complete intended meaning
If the word was coined to signify that exact meaning, it is mutabaqa (correspondence). Example: 'human' for 'rational animal' exactly.
Mutabaqa is the whole meaning.
3
Check if the meaning is a part of the whole
If the word signifies only a component of its full meaning, it is tadamun (inclusion). Example: 'human' for 'animal' alone.
The part must be essential.
4
Assess if the meaning is external but necessary
If the word implies something outside its own essence but necessarily concomitant, it is iltizam (implication). Example: 'roof' implying 'wall' or 'created' implying 'creator'.
The implied meaning is separate but necessary.

First: The signification of words for meanings. This is also what appears to us in the books of Islamic commentators regarding verbal investigations. The Islamic commentators—whether Stoics or Peripatetics, early or late—place this division at the beginning of their discussions, except for a few logicians like Abu al-Salt al-Dani in his book Taqwim al-Dhihn (1) who did not investigate the signification of words for meanings. This investigation in brief consists of the following: The signification of a word for a meaning is considered from three aspects. First: signification by correspondence (dalalat al-mutabaqa)—which is the signification of the word for the meaning for which it was coined, like the signification of 'human' for 'rational animal' and the signification of 'house' for the combination of wall and roof. Second: signification by inclusion (dalalat al-tadamun)—which is signification of a part of the parts of the meaning contained in it, like the signification of 'human' for 'animal' or for 'rational' alone, and the signification of 'house' for 'wall' or 'roof'. Third: signification by implication (dalalat al-iltizam)—which is the entailment of something that is concomitant to it but at the same time external to its essence, like the signification of 'roof' for 'wall' and 'created' for 'creator'. Al-Suhrawardi calls signification by correspondence 'intentional signification' (dalalat al-qasd), inclusion 'encompassing' (al-hi'ta), and implication 'parasitic' (al-tatful). The Islamic commentators, especially the later ones, expanded on these investigations and built upon them many linguistic subdivisions. But what is the source from which the Muslims derived this division? It is established that Aristotelian logic did not know it, nor did the logic of the Greek commentators. So we have three possibilities. First: that the Muslims themselves innovated this division under linguistic influences. Second: that they derived this investigation specifically from Stoic logic, and the idea of 'signification' (dalala) or 'signified' (madlul) has reached us from Stoic thought; so it is probable that the idea of signification among the Muslims was connected to some of these investigations, although we cannot be certain of the matter to any degree of certainty. Third—and it is the most likely probability—that the Muslims derived or knew this idea, i.e., the idea of significations, from Stoicism, while also having another idea about this investigation among themselves. This is evidenced by the fact that some logicians investigate the explanation of the meaning of signification among logicians and among grammarians and its difference in each group. For logicians, signification is 'the state of a thing such that from its conceptualization the conceptualization of something else necessarily follows.' This logical signification is divided into signification of the real meaning (mutabaqa), signification of part of the meaning (tadamun), and signification of the necessary concomitant of the meaning (iltizam). The grammarians have another definition of significations that differs from the logical definition. They define signification as 'understanding the meaning from the word used for it. If the meaning is what the word is coined for, it is correspondence; if it is coined for a part of it, it is inclusion; if it is external to it, it is implication. And it is impossible among the Arabic grammarians for these significations to be unified in a single usage as is the case among the logicians (1).' From this, it becomes clear to us that the matter was discussed from two different perspectives, and that the Muslims had specific thinking about it, and it is likely that the idea of logical signification entered the Islamic commentators from Stoic logic.

Logicians and Grammarians: Two Definitions of Dalala
Author contrasts logical and grammatical definitions of dalala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast the logical and grammatical definitions of dalala and explain why logicians allow multiple simultaneous significations while grammarians do not.

Core Idea
Logicians define dalala as conceptual necessity; grammarians define it as usage-based understanding.

The passage highlights a critical difference between how logicians and grammarians understand signification, particularly whether a word can signify in multiple ways at once. This lesson unpacks those definitions and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Learn the logicians' definition
For logicians, dalala is 'the state of a thing such that from its conceptualization the conceptualization of something else necessarily follows.' This is a relationship of mental entailment.
2
Learn the grammarians' definition
For grammarians, dalala is 'understanding the meaning from the word used for it.' It is based on linguistic usage and convention.
3
Compare the consequences
Because logicians focus on conceptual necessity, they allow a word to have multiple dalala simultaneously (e.g., 'human' signifies animal and rational at once). Grammarians, based on usage, hold that a word in a single usage can only have one type of signification.
4
Apply to an example
Consider 'created' implying 'creator' (iltizam). For a logician, this is valid because the concept of created entails a creator. For a grammarian, 'created' in its usage does not directly signify 'creator'; that would be understood only in a specific context.

First: The signification of words for meanings. This is also what appears to us in the books of Islamic commentators regarding verbal investigations. The Islamic commentators—whether Stoics or Peripatetics, early or late—place this division at the beginning of their discussions, except for a few logicians like Abu al-Salt al-Dani in his book Taqwim al-Dhihn (1) who did not investigate the signification of words for meanings. This investigation in brief consists of the following: The signification of a word for a meaning is considered from three aspects. First: signification by correspondence (dalalat al-mutabaqa)—which is the signification of the word for the meaning for which it was coined, like the signification of 'human' for 'rational animal' and the signification of 'house' for the combination of wall and roof. Second: signification by inclusion (dalalat al-tadamun)—which is signification of a part of the parts of the meaning contained in it, like the signification of 'human' for 'animal' or for 'rational' alone, and the signification of 'house' for 'wall' or 'roof'. Third: signification by implication (dalalat al-iltizam)—which is the entailment of something that is concomitant to it but at the same time external to its essence, like the signification of 'roof' for 'wall' and 'created' for 'creator'. Al-Suhrawardi calls signification by correspondence 'intentional signification' (dalalat al-qasd), inclusion 'encompassing' (al-hi'ta), and implication 'parasitic' (al-tatful). The Islamic commentators, especially the later ones, expanded on these investigations and built upon them many linguistic subdivisions. But what is the source from which the Muslims derived this division? It is established that Aristotelian logic did not know it, nor did the logic of the Greek commentators. So we have three possibilities. First: that the Muslims themselves innovated this division under linguistic influences. Second: that they derived this investigation specifically from Stoic logic, and the idea of 'signification' (dalala) or 'signified' (madlul) has reached us from Stoic thought; so it is probable that the idea of signification among the Muslims was connected to some of these investigations, although we cannot be certain of the matter to any degree of certainty. Third—and it is the most likely probability—that the Muslims derived or knew this idea, i.e., the idea of significations, from Stoicism, while also having another idea about this investigation among themselves. This is evidenced by the fact that some logicians investigate the explanation of the meaning of signification among logicians and among grammarians and its difference in each group. For logicians, signification is 'the state of a thing such that from its conceptualization the conceptualization of something else necessarily follows.' This logical signification is divided into signification of the real meaning (mutabaqa), signification of part of the meaning (tadamun), and signification of the necessary concomitant of the meaning (iltizam). The grammarians have another definition of significations that differs from the logical definition. They define signification as 'understanding the meaning from the word used for it. If the meaning is what the word is coined for, it is correspondence; if it is coined for a part of it, it is inclusion; if it is external to it, it is implication. And it is impossible among the Arabic grammarians for these significations to be unified in a single usage as is the case among the logicians (1).' From this, it becomes clear to us that the matter was discussed from two different perspectives, and that the Muslims had specific thinking about it, and it is likely that the idea of logical signification entered the Islamic commentators from Stoic logic.

Divisions of Expression: Simple, Compound, and Composite
Author presents the third division of lafz: mufrad/murakkab/mu'allaf with subtypes and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mufrad, murakkab, and mu'allaf, and identify examples of each in Islamic logical definitions.

Core Idea
In Islamic logic, expressions are either simple, compound, or composite—each with a distinct relationship between parts and whole meaning.

The author explains that expressions are divided based on simplicity and composition. While Aristotle originally gave a twofold division, later Muslim logicians added a third category: mu'allaf (composite). Understanding this taxonomy is essential for analyzing definitions and propositions in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage divides expressions into mufrad, murakkab, and mu'allaf. Mufrad is simple like 'Zayd'; murakkab is compound like 'abd Allah'; mu'allaf is composite like 'hayawan natiq'.
Note that later commentators introduced mu'allaf as a third category, distinct from murakkab.
2
Understand the criterion for each
For mufrad, no part indicates any meaning. For murakkab, parts indicate a meaning that is not part of the whole's meaning. For mu'allaf, parts indicate a part of the whole's meaning.
This criterion helps in analyzing definitions: a proper definition (hadd) is mu'allaf.
3
Recognize subtypes of murakkab
Murakkab is further divided into idafi (additive, e.g., 'ghulam Zayd'), taqyidi (restrictive, e.g., 'hayawan natiq'), and isnadi (predicative, e.g., 'Zayd qa'im').
These subtypes are important for identifying different types of logical expressions.
4
Apply the distinction to Islamic examples
Take the example 'al-insanu hayawan natiq'. 'al-insanu' is mufrad (with definite article still one word). 'hayawan natiq' is mu'allaf because each part indicates part of the meaning (genus and differentia).
This shows the practical use in defining terms in fiqh and kalam.

Parts of Speech: Noun, Verb, Particle in Islamic Logic
Author presents the fourth division of lafz: the parts of speech (ism/fi'l/harf) and discusses Ibn Sina's placement of this topic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three parts of speech (ism, fi'l, harf) as used in Islamic logic and explain their role in forming propositions.

Core Idea
In Islamic logic, every proposition is composed of noun, verb, and particle—a tripartite structure derived from Aristotle but refined by Muslim logicians.

The fourth division of expressions deals with the parts of speech: noun (ism), verb (fi'l), and particle (harf). This division is intimately connected to the study of propositions, since a proposition (qadiyya) is formed by combining these elements. The passage discusses how Ibn Sina placed this topic in the section on propositions, while later logicians placed it under concepts.

Identify the three parts of speech
The passage divides mufrad into ism (noun), fi'l (verb), and harf (particle). Ism is a conventional word abstracted from time; fi'l is associated with time; harf has no independent meaning.
These categories correspond roughly to Arabic grammar, but logic focuses on their function in propositions.

Define ism as given in the text
The author defines ism as 'an expression indicating by convention, abstracted from time, and none of its parts indicates isolation.' This definition distinguishes ism from fi'l (which involves time) and harf (which does not indicate independently).

Understand the logical significance
According to Aristotle, a proposition consists only of ism, fi'l, and harf. For example, in 'Zayd qa'im', Zayd is ism, qa'im is fi'l (or predicate), and the copula is implied or a harf. This tripartite structure is fundamental to logical analysis.
Ibn Sina placed this discussion in the section on propositions (qadaya) because of its direct relevance to propositional form.

Platonic Division: Definition, Types, and Conditions
Author explains Platonic division, its types, and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of Platonic division, distinguish its two types, and list the four conditions for a valid division according to al-Zarkashi.

Core Idea
Platonic division was a path to certainty for some Islamic scholars, distinct from Aristotle's logic.

Platonic division (qisma) offers a deductive method that some Islamic scholars preferred over Aristotelian logic for achieving certainty. This lesson unpacks the core definition, the two main types—divisive and constant—and the four conditions that ensure a division is valid. Understanding this tool helps in systematically classifying concepts in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of division
The passage defines division as 'multiplication of the one in estimation' (takthir al-wahid taqdiran). This means taking a single concept and conceptually splitting it into parts or species.
This definition sees division as an analytical tool, not just a classification.
2
Identify the two types
Division is divided into divisive division (qismat tamyiz), which separates species under a genus, and constant division (qismat thawabit), which splits a concept into constant parts like substance into matter and form.
Remember: tamyiz = distinguishing species; thawabit = fixed components.
3
List the four conditions for validity
The passage mentions four conditions: no overlap (adam al-tawasul), no excess (ziyada), no deficiency (nuqsan), and no incompatibility (tanafur). These ensure that the division is exclusive, exhaustive, and consistent.
These conditions apply to both types of division.
4
Recognize the historical context
Note that Imam al-Haramayn (al-Juwayni) adopted this method as an alternative to Aristotelian logic, considering it a path to truth. Other scholars like al-Farabi, al-Sadr al-Shirazi, and al-Zarkashi also discussed it.
This shows that Islamic logicians engaged with both Platonic and Aristotelian traditions.

The Division of Sabr wa Taqsim: Munhasir and Muntashir
Author divides sabr wa taqsim into munhasir and muntashir and distinguishes their epistemic outcomes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between munhasir and muntashir types of sabr wa taqsim and determine whether the method yields certainty or probability based on the nature of the evidence.

Core Idea
Only the confined (munhasir) sabr yields certainty; the scattered (muntashir) yields only probability.

In usul al-fiqh, the method of sabr wa taqsim (survey and division) is used to identify the effective cause ('illah) for legal analogy. However, not all applications of this method produce certainty. This passage from the Manahij al-Bahth text explains the crucial division between the confined and scattered types, and what each entails for the strength of the conclusion. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating whether a juristic argument is definitive or merely probable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
The passage divides sabr wa taqsim into two main sections: al-munhasir (confined) and al-muntashir (scattered). Locate these terms in the Arabic text and note how they are introduced.
2
Understand the condition for munhasir
For munhasir, the attributes are fully inventoried, and each unsuitable attribute is eliminated by conclusive evidence. The elimination reasons include: the attribute is nullified, it is merely coextensive, or it suffers from a defect. After elimination, the remaining attribute is determined as the cause.
The text also mentions a case where suitability of a deleted attribute does not appear after investigation, but this still falls under munhasir because the elimination was based on evidence.
3
Understand the condition for muntashir
Muntashir occurs when the attributes are not confined between negation and affirmation, or even if confined, the evidence for eliminating all but the specific attribute is only probabilistic (ẓannī). The key is that the elimination does not reach certainty.
4
Distinguish epistemic outcomes
The passage explicitly states that munhasir leads to certainty (yaqīn), while muntashir leads to probability (ẓann). This means that only munhasir can serve as a definitive proof in legal reasoning.

Commentary
This passage presents the division of the method of sabr wa taqsim into two types, as agreed upon by most usulis. The first type is al-munhasir (the confined), where the investigator can inventory all possible attributes that could serve as the cause for the compared case (maqīs 'alayh). Then each attribute is tested, and those that are not suitable are eliminated by evidence. Reasons for elimination include: the attribute is nullified (mulghā), it is merely coextensive (ṭardī) without being the true cause, or it suffers from a defect such as deficiency, fracture, or obscurity. From this division, the author draws a further epistemological implication: the munhasir type leads to certainty (yaqīn), while the muntashir type leads only to probability (ẓann). This is a crucial distinction because it determines whether the method can serve as a definitive proof or only a persuasive one in legal reasoning.

Sibr and Taqsim: Division and Exclusion as a Method for Establishing Cause
Author explains the method of sibr and taqsim and its epistemological status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the method of sibr and taqsim, identify its epistemological status, and apply it to a simple usul al-fiqh problem.

Core Idea
Division and exclusion (sabr wa taqsim) narrows possibilities but does not yield certainty.

In Islamic legal and rational reasoning, establishing the cause ('illa) of a ruling is crucial for analogical extension. The method of sibr and taqsim (division and exclusion) is one of the tools used to identify that cause. This lesson explains the method, its variations, and its epistemological limitations according to later theologians.

Guided Path
1
Define sibr and taqsim
Sibr means testing/examining; taqsim means dividing. Together it is a procedure of dividing possible causes and excluding those that do not apply.
2
Identify the two views on its status
Some consider it an independent method; others see it as a way to establish the cause in analogies. The author mentions both views.
3
Understand the logical structure
The method involves negation and affirmation: eliminating impossible causes until only one remains. Example: 'If it were visible, we would see it' – obstacles like proximity, distance, veil are excluded.
4
Recognize its epistemological limitation
Later theologians hold that it does not yield certain knowledge (la yufid 'ilma); it can be refuted by showing alternative possibilities.

Al-Ghazali's Classification of Truth Seekers
Al-Ghazali's classification and critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four groups of truth seekers according to al-Ghazali and explain his critique of three of them.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali rejects rationalist and esoteric methods, endorsing only the mystical path of direct experience.

Al-Ghazali presents a famous classification of truth seekers into four groups. He critiques three of them—theologians, philosophers, and Batinites—while upholding the Sufi path. This lesson unpacks his classification and his reasons for rejecting the others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four groups

2
Understand the method of each group

Why does he reject them? They rely on borrowed premises from opponents and focus on refuting contradictions rather than building certain knowledge.
4
Analyze his critique of philosophers
Why does he initially recommend logic then reject it? He became aware that applying Aristotelian logic to Islamic sciences led to contradictions, so he demolished his earlier endorsement.
5
Analyze his critique of Batinites
What is their weakness? They depend on an infallible teacher, making knowledge inaccessible without that teacher.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological classification of the methods of seeking truth within Islamic thought. It begins by presenting al-Ghazali's famous division of truth-seekers into four groups: theologians (mutakallimun), Batinites (esoterics), philosophers, and Sufis. Al-Ghazali rejects the first three methods, criticizing the theologians for relying on borrowed premises and focusing on refuting opponents rather than building certain knowledge; the philosophers for their reliance on Aristotelian logic which, when applied to Islamic sciences, leads to contradictions; and the Batinites for their dependence on an infallible teacher.
