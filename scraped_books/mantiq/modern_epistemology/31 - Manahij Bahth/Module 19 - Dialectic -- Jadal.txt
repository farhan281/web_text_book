First Argument for Ṭard and Its Objection
Author presents the first argument for ṭard and its objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the first argument for ṭard based on emptiness of mind and evaluate the objection that this state is impossible for a mujtahid.

Core Idea
The first argument for ṭard assumes emptiness of mind; the objection shows it is impossible for a mujtahid.

In this passage, the author presents the first argument used by proponents of ṭard (coincidence) to justify their method. They claim that when a mujtahid knows a ruling must have a cause, and his mind is empty of other descriptions, he naturally presumes the observed description is the cause. Then an objection is raised: such emptiness is impossible for any reasoning person, especially a mujtahid seeking causes. Let's analyse this dialectical exchange step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants' argument
The proponents of ṭard argue that if a mujtahid knows a ruling must have a cause and his mind is empty of other descriptions, he will presume that the observed description is the cause. This is because the cause must exist and no alternative is perceived.
The argument is based on a psychological presumption (ẓann), not certainty.
2
Extract the premise of emptiness
The key premise is 'khalwat al-dhihn' (emptiness of mind from other descriptions). The proponents claim this state is possible and leads to a valid presumption.
This premise is what the objection targets.
3
Reconstruct the objection
The objector argues that emptiness of mind from all other descriptions is inconceivable (ghayr ma'qūl) for any human being who is engaged in reasoning about a specific matter, especially a mujtahid seeking to reach a valid presumption.
The objection denies the premise outright as unrealistic.
4
Assess the dialectical outcome
Based on the passage, the objector's critique seems to refute the first argument by showing its foundation is impossible. The author does not provide a response from the proponents, so the objection stands as a rebuttal.
In adab al-bahth, this would mean the claimant's argument is invalid unless they can show how emptiness is possible.

Al-Ilzāmāt: The Dialectical Method of Impositions
Author discusses ilzāmāt, its forms, and its dialectical weakness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two forms of ilzāmāt (qiyās al-ṭard and qiyās al-ʿaks) and explain why the method's strength depends on the opponent's admission.

Core Idea
Al-Ilzāmāt forces a conclusion using the opponent's own premises, but its weakness lies in the opponent's denial of the attributed cause.

This lesson examines the fifth method of establishing a cause: al-ilzāmāt. Unlike direct proof, ilzāmāt builds on what the opponent already accepts. We will explore its two syllogistic forms and the scholarly debate about its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forms
From the text, find qiyās al-ṭard (co-extensiveness syllogism) and qiyās al-ʿaks (converse syllogism). Understand that ṭard extends the ruling and ʿaks uses contraposition.
2
Understand the dialectical premise
Note that ilzāmāt relies on the opponent's admission of a cause and ruling. The method is built on what the opponent concedes.
3
Evaluate the weakness
The opponent can deny the attribution of cause or ruling, weakening the argument. The syllogisms themselves are not weak, but the attribution is contestable.
4
Compare scholarly positions
Al-Rāzī treats ilzāmāt as genuine syllogisms; al-Ījī as a method for establishing cause; the commentator of al-Mawāqif criticizes al-Ījī, asserting it is an independent method.

Commentary
This passage discusses the fifth method: al-ilzāmāt (impositions). The author notes that scholars differ about this method. He first presents al-Rāzī's view from Nihāyat al-ʿUqūl: al-ilzāmāt are genuine types of syllogism, taking two forms. One is qiyās al-ṭard (co-extensiveness syllogism), which applies the ruling of the original case (aṣl) to the subsidiary (farʿ), whether the ruling is positive (ṭard fī al-ithbāt) or negative (ṭard fī al-nafy). In modern terms, this is like affirming the antecedent. The second form is qiyās al-ʿaks (converse syllogism), which uses the contrapositive: from the non-existence of the consequent to the non-existence of the antecedent. Then the author presents the view of Ṣāḥib al-Mawāqif (al-Ījī), who treats ilzāmāt as a famous method for establishing the common cause in analogical reasoning (qiyās al-ghāʾib ʿalā al-shāhid). Al-Ījī defines it as a syllogism based on what the opponent accepts, using a differentiating cause (ʿilla fāriqa) that exists in the opponent's original but not in the subsidiary being measured. The text then explains the weakness: if the method were simply qiyās al-ṭard or qiyās al-ʿaks, those syllogisms themselves are not weak. However, the weakness arises because we attribute the determination of the cause and the ruling to the opponent. The opponent can then deny either the cause or the ruling, claiming that the particular determination is not correct. Finally, the passage notes that the commentator of al-Mawāqif (al-Sharīf al-Jurjānī) criticizes al-Ījī for considering ilzāmāt as a separate method for establishing the cause, while in al-Rāzī's division it is actually an independent third method. There is also an opinion that ilzāmāt can be considered an independent method like al-sabr wa al-taqsīm, while still including a type of proof.

Al-Sijistani's Critique of Affirming and Negating Divine Attributes
Al-Sijistani critiques both Ash'ari and Mu'tazili on divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Sijistani's critique of both Ash'ari and Mu'tazili positions on divine attributes and explain why he considers both forms of tashbih (anthropomorphism).

Core Idea
Both affirming and denying divine attributes lead to anthropomorphism—only the form differs.

This lesson examines a classical critique from Abu Sulayman al-Sijistani, a polymath of the 4th/10th century, against the two dominant theological schools of his time: the Ash'aris and the Mu'tazilis. Al-Sijistani uses logical tools—specifically the principle of non-contradiction—to argue that both schools fall into tashbih (anthropomorphism), albeit in different ways. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to detect hidden inconsistencies in theological arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrines
Note the two positions: Ash'aris affirm attributes distinct from God's essence; Mu'tazilis deny any distinct attributes and claim God is not describable by attributes.
These are the two poles of the debate on divine attributes.
2
Recognize the logical principle invoked
Al-Sijistani begins by stating that both schools departed from the principle of non-contradiction and the excluded middle (al-thālith al-marfū'). This means they assume a false dichotomy or violate logical consistency.
The excluded middle states that between contradictories, no middle exists.
3
Analyze the Ash'ari critique
Al-Sijistani argues that affirming attributes distinct from the essence leads to obvious anthropomorphism (tashbīh jālī) and multiplies eternal beings (ta'addud al-qudamā').
This is a theological and logical objection: multiplicity of eternals contradicts tawhīd.
4
Analyze the Mu'tazili critique
Al-Sijistani objects that denying attributes leads to hidden anthropomorphism (tashbīh khafī). He poses a dilemma: if attributes are identical to the described, absurdities follow (e.g., blackness is black); if they are distinct, then created attributes share in the Creator's nature. Either way, tashbih results.
The dilemma uses conditional reasoning to show incoherence.
5
Examine the names-attributes argument
Al-Sijistani further criticizes the Mu'tazilis for affirming names like 'Hearing' and 'Seeing' while denying the corresponding attributes. He argues that names logically entail attributes; otherwise, a knower without knowledge would be possible, which is absurd.
This reinforces the internal inconsistency of the Mu'tazili position.

This passage confronts a foundational claim of Greek philosophy: that every effect has a necessary natural cause. Understanding this critique is essential for grasping how Islamic theology engaged with and transformed philosophical concepts.

Chapter Seven: Critique of the Aristotelian Law of Causality. Aristotle considered the law of causality among the absolutely primary premises. It cannot be impugned in its self-evidence. Aristotle treated causality not only as a principle or a natural or metaphysical problem, but also as a rational logical law upon which all logical investigations rely. The idea of causality in Aristotle's investigations transferred to the Islamic world. It found acceptance among the Islamic Peripatetic school, and indeed the greatest of the Islamic Peripatetic philosophers, Ibn Rushd, places it in the first rank in his investigations. Commentary
This passage presents the classic debate on causality between al-Ghazali and Ibn Rushd. The author first explains al-Ghazali's position: that what we call cause and effect are merely two events that occur in succession, with no necessary rational connection between them. Al-Ghazali argues that observation only shows conjunction (occurrence alongside) not causation (occurrence by). He provides numerous examples from everyday life and the sciences to illustrate that God can create the effect without the usual cause. The burning of cotton upon contact with fire is not due to fire's action but to God's direct creation; fire is inanimate and does nothing. Al-Ghazali challenges the philosophers to provide a proof for necessary causation, asserting that there is none. The passage then quotes al-Ghazali's example of the soul and faculties in the sperm, which are not produced by natural elements, the father, or the human himself. Al-Ghazali's key idea is that existence 'alongside' a thing does not imply existence 'by' it. He also raises the possibility of hidden permanent causes that we do not observe. The conclusion is that there is no causal relation between contingent things; only the constancy of habit explains the regular succession. The second part of the passage introduces Ibn Rushd's critique: denying causality undermines all sciences and logic. Ibn Rushd labels al-Ghazali's view as rhetorical/dialectical or sophistical, warning that logic itself presupposes causes and effects, and knowledge of effects depends on knowledge of their causes.

Commentary
In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya makes a concession about the usefulness of logic. He acknowledges that hidden premises and definitions can benefit certain people, especially the stubborn and the sophists. He explains that clarity and obscurity are relative; what is clear to one person may be hidden to another. Ibn Taymiyya therefore sees that the methods of logic are beneficial for such people and for disputation. He traces the origin of Aristotelian logic to the need to combat the Sophist movement. However, he adds that not all sophists need the same logic; some need more, some need less. He concludes by comparing the sophist to someone who makes a mistake in arithmetic, which is a clear-cut science, but the erring person does not recognize his error until the correct proof is presented.

Two Dialectical Methods: Examples and Prefetching Premises
Author describes two dialectical methods: using multiple examples or prefacing premises one after another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two dialectical methods of using multiple examples and prefetching premises, and explain how they function in Islamic debate.

Core Idea
Dialectical methods are valid paths to certainty, not just Aristotelian syllogism.

In Islamic debate (jadal), when an opponent knows the truth but refuses to admit it, a dialectician must use skillful methods. This lesson introduces two such methods from Ibn Taymiyya's critique: using multiple examples and gradually presenting premises. These methods avoid coercion and lead the opponent to admit conclusions willingly.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dialectical context
Note that the arguer (mustadill) is the questioner, not the objector. The goal is to lead the opponent to truth step by step, not to coerce him.
2
Method one: Using multiple examples (tamthīl)
The dialectician strikes multiple examples for the opponent. Examples can clarify even sensory and intuitive matters, revealing the situation.
Tamthīl is distinct from qiyās; it is a separate dialectical tool.
3
Method two: Prefetching premises (istislāf al-muqaddimāt)
Premises are placed one after another. Once the opponent admits each premise, the arguer shows that they entail the required conclusion. This avoids the appearance of coercion.
This method resembles a cumulative case argument.
4
Recognize the contrast with coercion
The passage warns against coercion (ilzām), which forces admission against the opponent's will. The dialectical methods aim for voluntary acceptance through reasoned steps.
5
Appreciate Ibn Taymiyya's broader point
The passage concludes that there are many paths to certainty, not just Aristotelian syllogism. This challenges the exclusivity of formal logic.

This gives us a picture of some of the dispute that arose between the rationalists among the theologians and the people of taste among the Sufis, and the attempt of each group to impugn the path of the other. Alongside this, there was also a dispute between the Ishraqi Sufis and the Peripatetic philosophers, not regarding the conception of the path of knowledge per se, but regarding the source of each of these two paths. The source of the Ishraqi path is, as Suhrawardi says, 'the Imam of wisdom and our chief Plato,' and Plato is the true philosopher because his investigatory wisdom follows the method of the Ishraqis — 'which was established and reported by the first generation of sages who are among the elite, prophets, and saints, such as Agathodaemon, Hermes, Empedocles, Pythagoras, Socrates, Plato, and their likes.' They in their mystical experience emulate the first principles and assume the character traits of God, i.e., they realize in themselves and encompass the entire macrocosm and become disengaged from matter in all respects through knowledge as it is in the form of existence. As for the wisdom of the Aristotelians, it is a wisdom with flimsy foundations and false principles. 'Nor that of the Peripatetics, the followers of the first teacher Aristotle, due to the weakness of their foundations and the falsehood of their basic premises.' This led to their being deprived of reaching — i.e., seeing meanings and observing existents face to face. They resorted to thought and the organization of evidence in the form of syllogism and demonstration, and they labored and established definitions and descriptions, but they did not know the alternation of illuminative lights. Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition of the dispute between the Ishraqi (illuminationist) philosophers, led by Suhrawardi, and the Peripatetic philosophers (followers of Aristotle). The author first notes that the earlier debate between rationalist theologians and Sufis was about method, but the Ishraqi-Peripatetic debate is about the source of the path of knowledge. The Ishraqis trace their source back to Plato and the ancient sages (like Agathodaemon, Hermes, Empedocles, etc.) who achieved mystical insight by emulating the first principles and acquiring divine attributes. They claim that through spiritual experience they realize the entire cosmos and become disengaged from matter, attaining knowledge as it truly is. In contrast, the Peripatetic wisdom is described as having flimsy foundations and false principles. The Ishraqis accuse the Peripatetics of being deprived of direct vision (mu'ayana) of meanings and existents; instead, they rely on thought, syllogistic demonstration, and definitions, but do not know the 'alternation of illuminative lights' (tanawwub al-anwar al-ishraqiyya), i.e., the direct illumination from the divine realm. Thus the Peripatetics, for the Ishraqis, lack true insight.
