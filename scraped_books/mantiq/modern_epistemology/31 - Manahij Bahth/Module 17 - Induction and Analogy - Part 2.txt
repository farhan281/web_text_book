The Debate on Tamthil's Certainty: 'Abd al-Shakur vs. al-Tusi
Author examines the debate over whether analogical reasoning yields certainty or probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct 'Abd al-Shakur's defense of tamthil's certainty and al-Tusi's critique, and evaluate the logical force of each position.

Core Idea
'Abd al-Shakur argues tamthil reduces to qiyas and yields certainty if the cause is known; al-Tusi argues the common factor may not be a cause in the branch.

The passage presents a fierce debate: 'Abd al-Shakur claims that tamthil with certain premises yields certain conclusions, while al-Tusi counters that the common factor may not be a genuine cause in the branch. The commentator refutes al-Tusi by citing the conditions set by the imams. This lesson will walk you through the logical moves of both sides.

Guided Path
1
Identify 'Abd al-Shakur's core claim
He asserts that tamthil returns to qiyas: if premises are certain (the cause is definitively established in both original and branch), the conclusion is certain.
He accuses Ibn Sina and al-Tusi of misunderstanding.
2
Extract al-Tusi's critique
Al-Tusi argues that tamthil does not yield certainty because the common factor (jami') might not be a cause in the branch, or if it is, the inference becomes demonstration (burhan), making tamthil redundant.
His statement: 'tamthil with the original is redundant'.
3
Understand the commentator's rebuttal
The commentator (author of Sharh al-Sullam) replies that the imams conditioned that the original not be specific and that the cause be known to exist in the branch. If these are certain, the conclusion is certain.
Thus al-Tusi's critique relies on a misconception of the conditions.
4
Evaluate the epistemological status
Determine under what conditions each position holds. Does 'Abd al-Shakur's defense succeed if the cause is certain? Does al-Tusi's critique hold if the cause is uncertain?

And Imam al-Haramayn says: 'Our imams arranged the intellectual proofs in an order that we will transmit and then show its corruption'(2). And he attributes this claim to a misunderstanding of Shaykh Ibn Sina and his followers among the philosophizing logicians. Then he says: 'Indeed Nasir al-Din al-Tusi has reached in this matter the limit of dullness, and thought that nothing follows from it.' And he affirms that tamthil returns to syllogism (qiyas): if its premises are certain, it yields certainty like logical syllogism. And he holds that no human being, whoever he may be, can doubt that 'if it is established that the judgment of a particular is caused by a cause definitively, and that cause exists in another particular definitively, then that judgment is established in that particular definitively, especially if it is known definitively that the cause was only placed as a cause for the judgment to extend by it.' As for when the premises are probable, then analogical reasoning yields probability.

As for the fact that the analogies of the jurists are probable, that is not due to weakness in the form of analogical reasoning itself, 'but because most of its premises are probable'(3).

Then he cites the statement of Nasir al-Din al-Tusi criticizing tamthil: 'It does not yield certainty because the common factor (jami') is a cause for the judgment in the original (asl) because it is original. Then it may be below the branch (far'), and perhaps it divides into two parts, one of which is a cause and the other not. And the original is specific to the first. Then if it is correct that the common factor is a cause in the branch, the inference would be a demonstration (burhan). And tamthil with the original is redundant.' And the commentator discusses this statement at length and affirms that the imams who upheld analogical reasoning stipulated that the original should not be specified for the judgment. And that it should be known that the judgment is caused by the cause — the specific cause — and it exists in the branch. If these things are established with certainties, the judgment is established certainly; if established with probability, the judgment is established with probability; and if not established at all, tamthil is not valid. From here the collapse of al-Tusi's statement becomes clear, who claims that Muslims say that the judgment is specific to the original without the branch. In reality, the judgment must be transferred to the branch, as long as the cause that entailed this judgment in the original exists in the branch(1).

Indeed, this whole dispute goes back to the failure of Islamic logicians to distinguish between Aristotelian tamthil and the theological reasoning from the unseen to the seen. There is a complete difference between the two: the first leads to probability, and the second leads to certainty. Because while the second relies on the methods of cause (masalik al-'illa), the first — in its Aristotelian form — relies on some similarity between the two particulars. But the failure of the later scholars to differentiate between the two in expression led to the claim that they are in essence one thing. So the Islamized Aristotelians looked at both as tamthil, and that both lead to probability.

And Ibn Taymiyya mentions after that that a group of Muslim theorists did not accept the logicians' methods of inference, but rather 'they infer with proofs that entail their implications according to them without regard to the measure of the logicians.' But he affirms that the majority of theorists reason from the unseen to the seen if the common factor entails the judgment, as they exemplify with conjunction by definition, cause, condition, and proof. And there are those who disputed with these theorists that the establishment of the judgment in the unseen is because of its establishment in the seen. Rather they say: 'The universal proposition itself is sufficient for the purpose without need for tamthil.' And Ibn Taymiyya holds that this also occurs in the legal matters: 'For whenever one establishes the proof that the judgment is attached to the common description, one does not need the original. Commentary
This passage critically examines the debate among Muslim logicians regarding the epistemological value of analogical reasoning (tamthil) and its relation to qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid (reasoning from the unseen to the seen). The author begins by noting that some later scholars, such as 'Abd al-Shakur, defend tamthil as capable of yielding certainty if its premises are certain, attributing opposition to a misunderstanding by Ibn Sina and al-Tusi. 'Abd al-Shakur argues that tamthil reduces to qiyas, and if the cause is known to be the universal cause, certainty follows. The author then presents al-Tusi's critique: tamthil does not yield certainty because the common factor may not be a cause in the branch, or if it is, the inference becomes demonstration, making tamthil redundant. The commentator (the author of Sharh al-Sullam) rebuts by noting that the proponents stipulated that the original not be specific, and that if the causal connection is known with certainty, the judgment transfers. The core of the dispute, the author asserts, is the failure to distinguish between Aristotelian tamthil (based on mere similarity, yielding probability) and the theological qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid (based on causal reasoning, yielding certainty). Finally, Ibn Taymiyya is cited, noting that some Muslim theorists reject logical formalization altogether, while others accept causal reasoning without the need for tamthil. The author concludes that the universal proposition suffices.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: The Sufficiency of Universal Propositions
Author examines the debate over whether analogical reasoning yields certainty or probability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's critique that analogical reasoning becomes unnecessary once a universal causal proposition is established.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya argues that once you prove the universal, tamthil becomes redundant.

Ibn Taymiyya enters the debate by noting that some Muslim theorists reject logical formalization. He asserts that if a universal proposition linking a description to a judgment is established, one can directly apply it without needing to cite an original case. This lesson explores his alternative to analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's claim
He says: 'The universal proposition itself is sufficient for the purpose without need for tamthil.'
This directly challenges the need for analogical reasoning.
2
Understand his reasoning
If the universal 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' is proven, then for any particular intoxicant (e.g., nabidh), the conclusion follows directly without invoking a specific original case like wine.
The universal serves as the major premise in a syllogism, not a analogy.
3
Compare with standard tamthil
In standard tamthil, one argues: 'Wine is forbidden because of intoxication; nabidh is also intoxicating; therefore nabidh is forbidden.' Ibn Taymiyya's approach eliminates the step of citing wine.
This makes the argument a simple categorical syllogism, not an analogy.
4
Evaluate the implications
Does this mean tamthil is never needed? If the universal is universally known, yes. But if the universal is itself derived from induction, tamthil might still be useful.

Ibn Taymiyya's introduction of the three innate types of inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of inference that Ibn Taymiyya claims are innate to human nature and distinguish them from formal Aristotelian syllogism.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya grounds correct reasoning in innate human nature, not Greek logic.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the dominance of Aristotelian logic by grounding reasoning in human nature (fitra). He identifies three types of inference that people use instinctively without formal training: tadakhul (overlap), talazum (entailment), and taqseem (division). Understanding these types helps us see how Islamic epistemology offers a more natural account of how we know things.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of fitra
Ibn Taymiyya holds that humans are innately disposed to correct reasoning without instruction. This is the basis for his alternative to formal logic.
Fitra is a key concept in Islamic thought, especially in epistemology.
2
Learn the first type: tadakhul
Tadakhul (overlap) occurs when one thing is contained within another, e.g., a species under its genus. Inference moves from the containing to the contained or vice versa.
For example, if all animals are mortal and man is an animal, then man is mortal.
3
Learn the second type: talazum
Talazum (entailment) involves a necessary connection between two things, such as cause and effect. If A entails B, inferring B from A is innate.
For example, if it rains, the ground becomes wet (if the relationship is known).
4
Learn the third type: taqseem
Taqseem (division) is reasoning by exhaustively dividing possibilities. If there are only two alternatives and one is eliminated, the other follows innately.
For example, if a ruling is either wajib or mubah, and it is not mubah, it is wajib.
5
Contrast with logicians' condition
Logicians require two premises for a syllogism, but Ibn Taymiyya argues these three types are naturally used without formal structure.
This sets up his critique of the two-premise requirement.

The Critique of Two Premises and Certainty of Analogical Reasoning
Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the two-premise requirement and his defense of tamthil's certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that analogical reasoning (tamthil) can yield certainty, and explain why he rejects the logicians' requirement of two premises.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya shows that the syllogism's universal premise itself depends on analogy.

Ibn Taymiyya not only identifies innate types of inference but also critiques the formal requirement of two premises. He argues that certain knowledge can be obtained through tamthil (analogical reasoning), which logicians dismiss as conjectural. Yet, he shows that the universal premise of a categorical syllogism—which is supposed to guarantee certainty—itself relies on analogical reasoning. This critique undermines the epistemic superiority claimed by Aristotelian logic.

Guided Path
1
State the logicians' requirement
Logicians insist that a valid inference must have exactly two premises, as in a categorical syllogism. Without two premises, they claim no certainty.
This is the standard Aristotelian position.
2
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's objection
He argues that certainty can be achieved without two premises, using analogical reasoning (tamthil) as an example.
He points out that everyday reasoning often uses analogy and yields certainty.
3
Analyze his key argument
The categorical syllogism relies on a universal premise (e.g., 'All A are B'). To be certain of that universal, one must be certain that all individuals of A share the relevant property. This certainty comes from recognizing the similarity (al-mushtarak) among those individuals—which is exactly what analogical reasoning does.
Thus, analogy is more fundamental; the syllogism depends on it.
4

Logicians claim analogy is conjectural; Ibn Taymiyya argues it can be certain if the similarity is properly established. The universal premise of a syllogism is only as certain as the analogy that supports it.
This undermines the hierarchy: syllogism over analogy.
5
Conclude the critique's force
Ibn Taymiyya's argument shows that either both syllogism and analogy can produce certainty, or neither can; the logicians' distinction is unfounded.
This opens the door for Islamic qiyas (analogical reasoning in fiqh) to be considered certain.

The Quranic Alternative: Innate Analogy of Tard and 'Aks
Author criticizes Ghazali's adoption of Greek logic and proposes Quranic scales of analogy based on similarity and difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qiyas al-tard (analogy of concomitance) and qiyas al-aks (analogy of opposition) and apply them to new cases using the principle of innate recognition of similarity and difference.

Core Idea
The Quran teaches us to judge similar things alike and different things differently.

After rejecting Greek logic, Ibn Taymiyya presents a positive alternative: a simple, innate system of reasoning based on recognizing similarity (tamathul) and difference (ikhtilaf). He calls these qiyas al-tard (when similar cases share the same ruling) and qiyas al-aks (when different cases receive different rulings). These are drawn directly from the Quran and are meant to be accessible to all people through their God-given fitra.

Guided Path

Understand the innate capacity

Define qiyas al-tard
When two things are similar (e.g., water and water), the intellect naturally applies the same ruling to both. This is qiyas al-tard: analogy of concomitance.

Define qiyas al-aks
When two things are different (e.g., water and earth), the intellect distinguishes between them and applies different rulings. This is qiyas al-aks: analogy of opposition.
The example: 'وإذا رأى المختلفين كالماء والتراب، فرق بينهما'.
4
Connect to prophetic guidance
Ibn Taymiyya insists that these analogies are used in the Quran itself. The messengers came to complete and confirm the fitra, not to replace it. The Quran provides the perfect measure (al-mizan) for correct analogy.
The passage states: 'فالميزان المنزل من الله هو القياس الصحيح'.

Jabir ibn Hayyan's critique of analogical reasoning from the witnessed to the unseen

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key conditions Jabir ibn Hayyan places on analogical reasoning for certainty, and evaluate his critique of the mutakallimun's use of qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid.

Core Idea
Without quantity (al-kamm), analogical inference from the witnessed to the unseen remains only probable, not certain.

In this passage, Jabir ibn Hayyan, a pioneering experimental scientist, challenges the certainty claimed by mutakallimun for their analogical reasoning (qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid). While theologians used this method to infer divine realities from observed phenomena, Jabir argues that such inference lacks necessity unless the observed instance is proven to be a mere part of a larger whole with known quantity. His critique resonates with later usul al-fiqh positions that analogical reasoning yields only probable knowledge.

2
Extract Jabir's central objection
Jabir argues that the observed model (al-namudhaj) does not necessitate another thing of its genus. It could itself be a universal (kulli) rather than a part (juz'). Locate his reasoning about possibility.
The passage states 'it is possible that these things in the world are universals in themselves, not parts'.
3
Understand the role of quantity (al-kamm)
Jabir insists that certainty in analogical inference requires knowing the quantity of the unseen part. Without it, the inference is merely possible. Identify where he emphasizes 'kamm'.
He says 'no certainty in this inference until we reach the quantity of these fragments'.
4
Examine the part-whole condition
Jabir rejects the assumption that part and whole mutually imply each other. He argues one must first prove the observed is a part. Find his discussion of part-whole correlation.
He states that part and whole are correlatives but one must prove the thing is actually partial.
5
Evaluate implications for theological reasoning
Consider how Jabir's critique undermines the certainty of theological analogies. Also note his constructive proposal: analogical reasoning based on homogeneity is valid but only probable unless quantity is established.
The passage ends by noting that Hanafi usul al-fiqh similarly views qiyas as probable.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed critique by Jabir ibn Hayyan of the analogical reasoning method (qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid) employed by Islamic theologians (mutakallimun). The author begins by stating the theologians' claim: from a known example (al-namudhaj) of a certain genus, they infer the existence of another thing of the same genus. Jabir rejects this, arguing that such an indication is neither necessary nor constant. He notes that the theologians considered this analogy certain in theological matters, but he, as an experimental scientist, disagrees. He then discusses how the Manichaeans used the same method to argue for dualism: since the world contains light and darkness, good and evil, etc., there must be corresponding realities outside the world. Jabir counters that this inference is not obligatory unless it is proven that the observed instances are parts of a larger whole. It is possible that the observed instances are themselves universals, not parts. Therefore, the inference remains uncertain until the quantity of the parts is established. Jabir emphasizes that a single model does not provide certain knowledge. He affirms the probabilistic nature of experiment, though he contradicts himself by suggesting that repeated experiments can yield certain knowledge. Central to his critique is the concept of quantity (al-kamm). He rejects the mutual implication of part and whole, arguing that one must first prove that the observed thing is indeed a part before inferring another like it. Without such proof, the inference is merely possible, not necessary. He concludes that the analogy based on homogeneity (dilalat al-mujanasa) is valid for establishing analogical reasoning, but it does not yield certainty unless quantity is accounted for. The passage ends by noting that Jabir would have found his views anticipated by early Hanafi jurists who held that such analogies are only probable (dhanni) in usul al-fiqh. This critique is significant because it challenges the epistemological foundation of theological analogical reasoning and introduces quantitative and experimental considerations.

Key Terms
قياس الغائب على الشاهد (qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid): Analogical reasoning from the witnessed (observed) to the unseen (unobserved), used by mutakallimun in theology; Jabir critiques its certainty. [Note: In later usul al-fiqh, this term is often synonymous with qiyas, but here it specifically refers to theological analogy.]
الأنموذج (al-namudhaj): The model or sample; a particular instance used to infer the existence of another of the same genus. [Note: No significant shift; stable as a technical term.]
الكم (al-kamm): Quantity; the amount or extent that must be established for analogical inference to yield certain knowledge. [Note: In Aristotelian logic, kamm is a category; here it is used in a methodological sense.]

Custom as the Basis of Inductive Reasoning in Islamic Thought
Author explains Jabir's view on custom as inductive foundation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why custom-based inference yields only probable knowledge (ẓann) and not demonstrative certainty (yaqīn).

Core Idea
Induction in Islam relies on custom, not necessary causation.

In Islamic epistemology, knowledge is divided into certain (yaqīnī) and probable (ẓannī). Jabir ibn Hayyan, following usūlī scholars, argues that the inductive method based on observed custom (al-ʿādah) yields only probability, never certainty. This lesson unpacks that foundational claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of custom (al-ʿādah) in inference.
Read how Islamic scholars base their reasoning on the repeated observation of events without asserting a necessary connection. This is the core of custom-based induction.
2
Contrast custom-based inference with demonstrative proof (burhān).
Note that Jabir explicitly says custom-based inference contains no necessary, compelling certain knowledge. It is only persuasive (iqnāʿī) knowledge.
3
Understand the epistemic status: ẓann (conjecture).
Recognize that this type of knowledge is called ẓann, which is contrasted with ʿilm (certainty) in uṣūl al-fiqh.
This sets the stage for understanding the probabilistic nature of induction in Islamic sciences.

Islamic scholars - theologians and jurists - base their analogy on the idea of custom, the meaning of which is that when they observe an event followed by another event customarily, they judge that if they observe this event again, the other will follow or accompany it, but without verifying a necessary connection between the two; rather it is a custom based on observation and experience. The scholars of usul al-fiqh held that this course of custom is not certain. Jabir ibn Hayyan followed them and declared the probability of this path. As for the inference taken from the course of custom, it contains no necessary, compelling, demonstrative certain knowledge at all, but rather persuasive knowledge that reaches the level of 'more appropriate, more likely, more worthy' and nothing more. Therefore people frequently use it and apply it - it is more appropriate and more likely than the other two paths, because it is 'analogy (qiyas) and induction of parallels (istiqra' al-naza'ir) and citing them as evidence for what is sought.' Here he explicitly mentions the term 'induction' (al-istiqra') and uses it, and also mentions the term 'qiyas' meaning usuli analogy not logical analogy, but he rises above the concept of usuli analogy. It is based on two particular incidents, between which the path of custom leads to induction of parallels, i.e. induction in the sense of the word based on particular incidents and facts relying on this path. Jabir ibn Hayyan noted that this inductive path contrasts with the demonstrative path in the probability of the former and certainty of the latter. He also noted that its strength and weakness are 'according to the abundance or scarcity of parallels and similar examples' - to the extent that some theologians thought it might lead to 'demonstrative certain knowledge, if there is not found in all that precedes it a single thing contrary to what testifies to a matter.' Jabir ibn Hayyan emphasizes the pressing need to know the modality of this reasoning for its importance in the craft, i.e. in alchemy. Therefore he explains it and identifies its weakest and strongest degrees. Jabir ibn Hayyan holds that the weakest found in this path of reasoning is that for which there is only one example, such as our saying that a certain woman will give birth to a boy, and our evidence: because she gave birth to a boy in the first year, and that woman had given birth to only one child. As for the strongest found in this path, it is 'that for which everything in existence is an example, and there was found in the past nor in the present anything contrary to it, like our saying: This night of ours will be followed by a day that follows and succeeds it. Our evidence: we have never found a night except that it was followed by a day; so it is apparent that it can only be as we have found.' This is a picture of the weakest and strongest in this path. As for the forms in between, they are strong and weak in reasoning according to the abundance or scarcity of parallels. Jabir ibn Hayyan emphasizes that there is in this reasoning no necessary, compelling certain knowledge, but rather it is conjectural knowledge. People use it because they attach and cite the present as evidence for the absent, due to the conjectures and estimations in the soul. Matters should proceed according to system, similarity, and likeness. People always conduct their affairs on conjecture and estimation, and it almost becomes certainty to the point that if an event happens to them on a certain day, they hope for the occurrence of the same exact event on the same day of the next year. Commentary
This passage presents Jabir ibn Hayyan's critique of reasoning by customary course (al-istidlal bi-majra al-ada) and connects it to the problem of induction. It opens by describing Galen's use of customary reasoning to assert the uniformity of the celestial world based on consistent observation and transmitted reports. The materialists (Dahriyya) extended this reasoning to deny anything contrary to their experience, such as miracles. Jabir then explains that custom is merely regular succession, leading only to probable judgments, not certainty. Human knowledge is limited and cannot encompass all particulars; thus, breaking of customs is possible, as proven by miracles like the birth of a man without a woman (Adam or Jesus) and Abraham's preservation from fire. Jabir argues that the materialists rejected such proofs because they relied solely on their limited observation. He further emphasizes that our inability to observe all existents means we cannot deny the possibility of the unseen. The passage then draws a parallel to David Hume's critique of induction, noting that modern science accepts probabilistic conclusions. Jabir's deeper aim is to reject Aristotelian causality, accepting customary reasoning only as a conjectural method, but even this can be overridden by divine intervention. The commentator (the author of Manahij Bahth) adds that Jabir's critique anticipates the modern problem of induction.

Jaber's Critique of Induction
Critique of induction and inference by custom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Jaber ibn Hayyan's critique of inductive reasoning (al-istiqra') and explain why he denies it yields certainty.

Core Idea
Jaber anticipated Hume: custom gives probability, not proof.

Jaber ibn Hayyan, an 8th-century alchemist and philosopher, launched a devastating critique of inductive reasoning. Using examples like the crocodile and magnet, he argued that inference from repeated observation can never guarantee truth. In this lesson, we dissect his argument and see how he challenges the foundations of empiricism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target
Jaber attacks induction (istiqra') and inference by custom (istidlal bi-majra al-'ada). The claim that repeated patterns yield certain knowledge is his target.
2
Extract his examples
The crocodile (unseen by many) and the magnet (unseen attraction) show that non-observation does not prove non-existence, nor does observation of regularity prove necessity.
3
Note the deeper target
Jaber denies Aristotelian causality: we cannot know necessary causal relations through induction. This is the basis of his critique.
4
Understand his acceptance of custom as conjectural
He does not discard empirical inference entirely but assigns it probability (zann), not certainty.
5
Connect to modern philosophy
The author notes Jaber preceded David Hume in raising the problem of induction. This shows the Islamic tradition's sophistication.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological analysis of Jaber ibn Hayyan's methodology. The author first discusses Jaber's critique of inductive reasoning (al-istidlal bi-majra al-'ada). Jaber argues that inference by custom yields only probability, not certainty. He uses examples like the crocodile and magnet: the fact that many have not seen them does not negate their existence. Jaber anticipates David Hume's problem of induction. The passage then notes that Jaber's fundamental critique targets Aristotelian causality itself—he denies that necessary causal relations can be known through induction. He accepts custom as a conjectural path but insists that even its regularity can be broken by divine intervention (miracles). Next, the passage turns to inference by traces (al-athar). The author reconstructs Jaber's view: al-athar means transmitted evidence (testimony, hearsay, narration). Jaber critiqued Galen and the Dahriyya for relying on uncertain traditions, but he did not entirely reject transmitted evidence. To understand Jaber's view on al-athar, we must consider his general theory of certainty. He distinguishes between first principles (awa'il) and secondary matters (thawani). First principles are known with certainty and require no proof; secondary matters are derived from them. The question is how we access first principles. Jaber says they come through intuition (hads), but not ordinary human intuition—only the intuition of prophets and their successors (Imams of Ahl al-Bayt). This intuition is a form of direct vision ('ayan) that establishes irrefutable proof. Thus, certain knowledge (including certain traces) comes only through prophetic/Imami transmission. Jaber's Ismaili allegiance explains why he attributes his works to Imam Jaafar al-Sadiq, who is considered the possessor of direct vision and infallible knowledge. Therefore, al-athar is a path to certainty only when it comes from the Prophet's progeny.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Jaber ibn Hayyan's hierarchy of knowledge: first principles, secondary matters, and the role of prophetic intuition (al-hads) in providing certain knowledge, including the conditions for certain transmitted evidence (al-athar).

Core Idea
Certain knowledge of first principles is reserved for prophets and Imams via direct vision.

After dismantling induction, Jaber ibn Hayyan provides a positive epistemology. He distinguishes first principles (self-evident truths) from secondary matters derived from them. But he denies that ordinary humans can access first principles through reason alone; only prophets and Imams, through divine gift, possess direct vision ('ayan) and infallible intuition that yields certain proof. This lesson unpacks that theory and its implications for transmitted evidence.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological analysis of Jaber ibn Hayyan's methodology. The author first discusses Jaber's critique of inductive reasoning (al-istidlal bi-majra al-'ada). Jaber argues that inference by custom yields only probability, not certainty. He uses examples like the crocodile and magnet: the fact that many have not seen them does not negate their existence. Jaber anticipates David Hume's problem of induction. The passage then notes that Jaber's fundamental critique targets Aristotelian causality itself—he denies that necessary causal relations can be known through induction. He accepts custom as a conjectural path but insists that even its regularity can be broken by divine intervention (miracles). Next, the passage turns to inference by traces (al-athar). The author reconstructs Jaber's view: al-athar means transmitted evidence (testimony, hearsay, narration). Jaber critiqued Galen and the Dahriyya for relying on uncertain traditions, but he did not entirely reject transmitted evidence. To understand Jaber's view on al-athar, we must consider his general theory of certainty. He distinguishes between first principles (awa'il) and secondary matters (thawani). First principles are known with certainty and require no proof; secondary matters are derived from them. The question is how we access first principles. Jaber says they come through intuition (hads), but not ordinary human intuition—only the intuition of prophets and their successors (Imams of Ahl al-Bayt). This intuition is a form of direct vision ('ayan) that establishes irrefutable proof. Thus, certain knowledge (including certain traces) comes only through prophetic/Imami transmission. Jaber's Ismaili allegiance explains why he attributes his works to Imam Jaafar al-Sadiq, who is considered the possessor of direct vision and infallible knowledge. Therefore, al-athar is a path to certainty only when it comes from the Prophet's progeny.

Author describes Ibn al-Haytham's inductive method and his use of induction before deduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe Ibn al-Haytham's inductive method and explain why he placed induction before deduction in scientific inquiry.

Core Idea
Ibn al-Haytham's method: start with observation, then ascend to certainty through careful reasoning.

In this passage, we encounter the epistemological approach of al-Hasan ibn al-Haytham, one of the greatest scientists of the Islamic world. His method, as he describes it, begins with induction of particulars and gradually progresses to deductive reasoning and certainty. Understanding this method is crucial for appreciating how Islamic scholars integrated empirical observation with logical reasoning, a foundation for modern scientific inquiry.

Commentary
This passage is a historical-epistemological analysis of Islamic logic. It opens by discussing al-Ghazali's initial synthesis of Greek and Islamic thought, which he later rejected due to inherent contradictions. The author then highlights three key characteristics of Islamic logic as it developed in the later tradition. First, it is inductive (istiqra'i), as opposed to Aristotelian deductive logic. This inductive approach explains why later scholars selectively adopted Stoic elements: Stoic logic is not metaphysical and not tied to Greek theology, unlike Aristotelian logic. The author notes that later scholars (especially commentators on al-Sullam) distinguished between permissible unmixed logic and forbidden philosophical logic mixed with corrupt beliefs. They also avoided metaphysical topics like the categories and only touched on demonstration (burhan) incidentally. Second, Islamic logic is linguistic: it relies on language characteristics. Although Stoic logic also relied on Greek, the idea that logic connects to language appealed to later scholars, leading them to create new sections on expressions (alfaz) and lengthy discussions of verbal definitions (hudud lafziyya). Third, Islamic logic is nominalist and sensory: it denies universals, accepts only particulars, and rejects purely rational universal knowledge. These three characteristics align Islamic logic closely with Stoic logic and distinguish it from Aristotelianism. The author then draws two conclusions: (1) Islamic thinkers representing the true spirit of Islam rejected Aristotelian logic because it is deductive and ignores inductive/empirical methods; (2) Muslims developed their own inductive method, which later transmitted to Europe via Spain. The passage ends with citations from Muhammad Iqbal and Briffault to support the claim that Roger Bacon and European empirical science derived from Islamic sources.

Commentary
This passage is a historical-epistemological analysis of Islamic logic. It opens by discussing al-Ghazali's initial synthesis of Greek and Islamic thought, which he later rejected due to inherent contradictions. The author then highlights three key characteristics of Islamic logic as it developed in the later tradition. First, it is inductive (istiqra'i), as opposed to Aristotelian deductive logic. This inductive approach explains why later scholars selectively adopted Stoic elements: Stoic logic is not metaphysical and not tied to Greek theology, unlike Aristotelian logic. The author notes that later scholars (especially commentators on al-Sullam) distinguished between permissible unmixed logic and forbidden philosophical logic mixed with corrupt beliefs. They also avoided metaphysical topics like the categories and only touched on demonstration (burhan) incidentally. Second, Islamic logic is linguistic: it relies on language characteristics. Although Stoic logic also relied on Greek, the idea that logic connects to language appealed to later scholars, leading them to create new sections on expressions (alfaz) and lengthy discussions of verbal definitions (hudud lafziyya). Third, Islamic logic is nominalist and sensory: it denies universals, accepts only particulars, and rejects purely rational universal knowledge. These three characteristics align Islamic logic closely with Stoic logic and distinguish it from Aristotelianism. The author then draws two conclusions: (1) Islamic thinkers representing the true spirit of Islam rejected Aristotelian logic because it is deductive and ignores inductive/empirical methods; (2) Muslims developed their own inductive method, which later transmitted to Europe via Spain. The passage ends with citations from Muhammad Iqbal and Briffault to support the claim that Roger Bacon and European empirical science derived from Islamic sources.
