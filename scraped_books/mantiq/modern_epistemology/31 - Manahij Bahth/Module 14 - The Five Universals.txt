Particular and Universal Terms
Author defines particular and universal terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a particular term from a universal term by applying the criterion of 'prevention of sharing in conception'.

Core Idea
A term's universality is determined by whether its meaning can be shared by multiple individuals.

In this passage, the author introduces the second division of words: the distinction between particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli). This is a foundational logical tool that later became essential for usul al-fiqh scholars when discussing general and specific texts. Understanding this distinction will sharpen your ability to analyse definitions and arguments in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Read the author's definition of the division
Start by noting that the division is 'with respect to the generality and specificity of meaning.' This tells you that the classification depends on the scope of the term's application.
2
Extract the definition of 'particular' (juz'i)
The author says: 'the particular is that which, by its conceptualization, prevents the occurrence of sharing in it.' This means the very idea of the term rules out multiple instances. For example, 'this house' or 'Zayd'—you cannot conceive of another individual fitting that description.
This is a strict logical definition, not a grammatical one.
3
Extract the definition of 'universal' (kulli)
The universal is 'that which, by its conceptualization, does not prevent the occurrence of sharing in it.' So the term's meaning allows for multiple individuals. For example, 'human' can apply to many people without contradiction.
Later, the five universals (kulliyat khamsa) are built on this concept.
4
Apply the criterion to new examples
Test terms from Islamic sciences: is 'Qur'an' universal or particular? It is universal because it can apply to any copy; 'the Qur'an revealed to Muhammad' is particular. This distinction is crucial in usul al-fiqh when discussing general (al-'amm) and specific (al-khas) texts.
The author notes that later usul scholars like al-Amidi explicitly used this logical division.

Defining Particular and Universal Terms
Author defines particular and universal terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define juz'i and kulli and apply the definition to identify whether a term is particular or universal in logical contexts.

Core Idea
A universal term's concept does not block sharing; a particular term's concept does.

In logic, words are divided based on the scope of their meaning. Some terms refer to a single individual, others to many. This lesson introduces the key distinction between particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli) terms, which forms the foundation of categorical logic and definition theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the Arabic terms being defined in the passage: جزئي and كلي.
These are the terms under discussion.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
Both definitions share the genus 'prevention of sharing' (المنع من وقوع الشركة). The differentia is the presence or absence of prevention: juz'i has it, kulli lacks it.
The definitions are real definitions (hadd haqiqi).
3
Test the definition
Apply to examples: 'Zayd' is particular because its concept prevents sharing; 'insan' (man) is universal because it does not.
The criterion is conceptual, not grammatical.
4
Note the epistemological shift
Logicians focus on the concept (tasawwur). In usul al-fiqh, context (qarina) matters more. This distinction will become important later.
This prepares for the upcoming debate.

Commentary
This passage defines the second division of words: into particular (juz'i) and universal (kulli). The particular is defined as 'that whose concept prevents sharing in it' — meaning you cannot apply it to more than one individual in the same sense. The root of the disagreement is that usuliyyun view the singular noun as standing for individual existents (thus particular), while logicians view it as an abstract concept or a set of qualities (thus universal in itself). The Genericity Debate: Usuliyyun vs Logicians
Author discusses linguistic problems around genericity and the definite article.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast the usuli and logical positions on whether the definite article alone makes a term general, and explain the root cause of the disagreement.

Core Idea
The usuli sees the singular noun as particular by default; the logician sees it as universal in essence.

After defining universal and particular terms, the author highlights a key disagreement between usuliyyun (Islamic legal theorists) and logicians. Does a term like 'al-insan' become general only through a contextual indicator (qarina), or is it inherently general? This lesson unpacks that debate and its implications for understanding Quranic and legal language.

Guided Path
1
State the problem
The definite article (alif lam) attaches to a singular noun. Does it automatically make the noun general and encompassing (istighraq)?
This is a linguistic problem not explored by Aristotle.
2
Present the usuli position
Usuliyyun like al-Amidi hold that the singular noun alone (e.g., 'insan') is particular because it is set for individual existents. Generality comes from a qarina, like the context of comparison.
The example: 'al-insanu khayrun min al-hayawani' (Man is better than animal).
3
Present the logical position
Logicians argue that 'insan' is an abstract concept or set of qualities, so it is universal by itself, needing no external qarina.
For them, 'insan' is a universal term inherently.
4
Identify the root cause
The difference stems from whether you view the word as a name for individual instances (usuli view) or as a representation of a universal concept (logical view).
The author calls this 'a matter of great subtlety'.

The Division of Lafz into Juz'i and Kulli: A Debate on Generality
Author presents the Aristotelian division and the usuli-mantiqi debate on universality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the usuli and mantiqi positions on whether a universal term (kulli) implies comprehensiveness (istighraq) without a contextual indicator (qarina).

Core Idea
The universal term's generality: intrinsic for logicians, dependent on context for usuliyyun.

This lesson introduces the Aristotelian division of expressions into particular and universal, but quickly moves into a central debate between legal theorists and logicians. The bone of contention is whether a noun like 'al-insan' (man), when it has the definite article, automatically refers to all individuals of its kind. Understanding this dispute will sharpen your ability to recognise how different disciplines define and use universality.

Find in the passage what the usuliyyun claim about the simple noun with alif lam and what the logicians claim. Note the key terms: istighraq, qarina.

Commentary
This passage defines the division of lafz (expression) into juz'i (particular) and kulli (universal), a core Aristotelian classification that the Peripatetic commentators adopted. The author then highlights a linguistic problem raised by the usuliyyun (legal theorists) that Aristotle did not address: whether the definite article (alif lam) attached to a simple noun confers istighraq (comprehensiveness or generality). For example, in the statement 'al-insan khayr min al-hayawan' (man is better than animal), the usuliyyun argue that 'man' and 'animal' do not automatically imply every individual; rather, there is a qarina (contextual indicator) of preference between the species that conveys generality. The logicians, however, assert that a kulli lafz inherently demands comprehensiveness without needing an external indicator. The root of disagreement: usuliyyun consider the simple noun as a representation of particular existents, making it essentially juz'i until a qarina elevates it; logicians view the noun as an abstract mental concept or set of attributes, making it kulli by itself. This debate touches on the philosophy of language and ontology of concepts.

The Three Essential Universals: Genus, Species, and Difference
Author defines the three essential universals: genus, species, difference.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

After establishing the division of attributes into essential and accidental, the author now unpacks the essential category. He explains that the essential is divided into three: genus, species, and difference. Each has a distinctive logical function in defining the essence of something. 2

3
Find the definition of difference
Difference is that which distinguishes one species within a genus, answering 'which thing is its essence?' Example: rational for human.
4
Compare the three definitions
Note how genus is broader, species is narrower, and difference is specific.

The Six Universals of Ikhwan al-Safa: Adding Shakhs
Author explains the Muslim addition of a sixth universal 'shakhs' by Ikhwan al-Safa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six terms of Ikhwan al-Safa and explain how shakhs adds to the five Aristotelian universals.

Core Idea
Ikhwan al-Safa added the individual (shakhs) as a sixth universal to account for the particular.

In this lesson, we explore a significant Muslim contribution to logic: the addition of a sixth universal, shakhs (the individual), by the Ikhwan al-Safa. While Aristotle and Porphyry listed five universals (genus, species, differentia, property, accident), Muslim logicians critically engaged with this framework and introduced the concept of the individual as a distinct term. This lesson will examine the definitions of all six terms and the rationale behind this expansion.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Muslim reception of Isagoge
Note how Muslims thoroughly studied, translated, commented on, and critiqued Porphyry's Isagoge, leading to innovations like al-Abhari's broader logic text.
2
Identify the original five Aristotelian universals
Recall Porphyry's five terms: genus (jins), species (naw'), differentia (fasl), property (khassa), and accident ('arad). These are the standard tools for definition.
3
Observe the addition of a sixth term
Ikhwan al-Safa added 'shakhs' (individual) as a sixth universal, reclassifying the six into three 'substances' (shakhs, jins, naw') and three 'attributes' (fasl, khassa, 'arad).
4
Extract the definitions of the six terms
Read the Arabic definitions: shakhs is a term for a singular perceptible entity; species encompasses multiple individuals with one form; genus encompasses multiple forms; differentia is essential and removal destroys the thing; property is non-essential but stable; accident is transient.
5
Analyze the classification into substances and attributes
Note that substances (mawsuflat) indicate entities existing in themselves, while attributes (sifat) indicate qualities. This bipartite division is an innovation over the purely universal-focused list.

The Muslims knew Isagoge thoroughly. It was translated into Arabic multiple times and many Muslims commented on it. Moreover, al-Abhari's famous text (d. 663 AH/1164 CE) was composed under the name 'Isagoge', and it is a general presentation of logic, not only of Porphyry's introduction. The Muslims wrote long commentaries on it, and these commentaries are a vast heritage requiring special study. But the Muslims did not accept the words of Isagoge as they were; rather, they added to it interesting elements and deepened their analysis. So some Muslims added a sixth term to those five terms. This is what we see with the Ikhwan al-Safa, for they included under the heading of Isagoge a chapter on the six terms: three of them indicate substances (mawsuflat) and three indicate attributes (sifat). As for the three terms indicating substances, they are: shakhs (individual), jins (genus), and naw' (species). The three indicating attributes are: fasl (differentia), khassa (property), and 'arad (accident). We observe that 'shakhs' is the new element among these terms. The Ikhwan al-Safa define it as: 'every term by which a singular existent, distinct from other existents, perceived by one of the senses, is pointed to; examples: this man, this essence, this tree, that wall, that stone' or 'everything that points to one specific thing.' As for species, they define it as 'every term that points to a multiplicity encompassed by one form, such as human and horse' or 'a term that applies to or encompasses several individuals of agreeing forms.' The genus for them is 'every term that points to a multiplicity of different forms all encompassed by one form, such as animal, for it encompasses humans, beasts, and birds.' The attributes are three types: differentia, which is the attribute that if it is removed, the existence of the thing described is removed; the differentia is essential and substantial. The property is an attribute or attributes that if removed, the existence of the thing described is not removed, but these attributes are slow to disappear. The accident is attributes that occur to a thing and disappear without its disappearance, but they are quick to disappear. These are the six terms according to Ikhwan al-Safa. It is clear that shakhs is a brilliant analysis of Porphyry's idea of the individual.

Ikhwan al-Safa's Six Universals: A New Classification
Ikhwan al-Safa's classification of six universals into substances and attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six universals of Ikhwan al-Safa and categorize them into terms of substance and attributes.

Core Idea
Ikhwan al-Safa expanded the five universals to six by adding 'al-shakhs' (the individual).

In this lesson, we see how the Ikhwan al-Safa, an influential philosophical group, reworked Porphyry's five universals. They introduced a sixth term, al-shakhs, and classified the six into two groups: those signifying substances (the described) and those signifying attributes. This classification reflects a deeper engagement with the nature of existence and predication.

Guided Path
1
Recall the standard five universals
The five universals from Porphyry are jins (genus), naw' (species), fasl (differentia), khassa (property), and arad (accident). Keep these in mind.
2
Find the addition of a sixth term
In the passage, the author states that some Muslims added a sixth term to the five. Locate where the Ikhwan al-Safa are mentioned and identify the sixth term as al-shakhs.
3
Identify the two categories
The six terms are divided into three substance terms (al-shakhs, al-jins, al-naw') and three attribute terms (al-fasl, al-khassa, al-arad). Note that substance terms signify 'the described' and attribute terms signify 'the attributes'.
4
Recognize al-shakhs as the new element
The passage explicitly says 'the individual is the new element among these terms'. Understand that al-shakhs is the unique addition.
5
Reflect on the rationale
Consider why the Ikhwan al-Safa might have felt the need to add a term for the individual. This shows their concern for concrete particulars in logic.

Substance Universals: al-Shakhs, al-Jins, and al-Naw' in Ikhwan al-Safa
Definitions of the three substance universals: shakhs, jins, naw'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the three substance universals according to Ikhwan al-Safa and distinguish between them.

Core Idea
Al-shakhs points to a unique individual, al-jins to a wide category, and al-naw' to a specific kind.

The Ikhwan al-Safa provided detailed definitions for the three universals that signify substances (the described): the individual (shakhs), the genus (jins), and the species (naw'). In this lesson, we analyze their definitions and understand how each captures a different level of generality.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definitions in the passage
Find the part where the Ikhwan al-Safa define al-shakhs, al-naw', and al-jins. They are given sequentially.
2
Analyze the definition of al-shakhs
Al-shakhs is defined as 'every word by which is pointed to an existence isolated from other existences, perceived by one of the senses'. It points to a single specific thing, e.g., 'this man'.
3
Analyze the definition of al-naw'
Al-naw' is defined as 'every word by which is pointed to a multitude that a single form encompasses', e.g., 'man' or 'horse'. It applies to several individuals sharing a form.
4
Analyze the definition of al-jins
Al-jins is defined as 'every word by which is pointed to a multitude differing in forms that a single form encompasses all of them', e.g., 'animal' includes man, beasts, birds.
5
Compare the scopes
Notice the hierarchy: al-shakhs covers one individual, al-naw' covers a group with identical form, al-jins covers a broader group with differing forms. This shows increasing generality.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of the Universal Proposition
Ibn Taymiyya critiques the necessity of universal propositions in demonstration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's dilemma for universal propositions and explain why he argues they are not epistemically foundational for demonstration.

Core Idea
Universal propositions cannot be the foundation of certainty; particulars come first.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the core Aristotelian claim that demonstration requires a universal proposition. He argues that such universals are either self-evident (making them unnecessary) or theoretical (leading to circularity or regress). This critique strikes at the heart of syllogistic certainty and affirms the primacy of particular, experiential knowledge in Islam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim under attack
The logicians claim that certain knowledge requires demonstration, and demonstration requires a universal proposition as a major premise. Find this claim in the passage: 'العلوم اليقينية النظرية لا يحصل عليها إلا بالبرهان ولا بد في البرهان من قضية كلية.'
This is the target of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.
2
Understand the dilemma
Ibn Taymiyya presents a dilemma: the universal proposition is either intuitive (badihi) or theoretical (nazari). If it is intuitive, then its individual instances are even more intuitive, making the universal redundant. If it is theoretical, proving it requires another demonstration, leading to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). Trace this reasoning in the passage.
This dilemma is the core of his critique.
3
Apply the dilemma to concrete examples
Consider the example from the passage: knowing that 'one is half of two' does not require the universal 'every whole has a half'. Similarly, knowing that a particular thing cannot be both existent and non-existent does not require the law of non-contradiction. These are particular judgments. Verify this with the Arabic: 'فالإنسان يعلم أن هذا الواحد نصف هذين الأثنين من غير استدلال على ذلك بالقضية الكلية.'
These examples show that universals are not needed for particular knowledge.
4
Formulate Ibn Taymiyya's conclusion
The basic axioms of demonstration are particular and experiential, not universal and rational. The universal proposition is merely an enumeration of particulars; demonstration does not produce new knowledge but involves begging the question (musadara 'ala al-matlab). Extract this conclusion from the passage: 'فبديهيات البرهان الأساسية أساسها جزئي لا كلي، محسوسي تجريبي لا عقلي.'
Ibn Taymiyya reverses the priority of universal over particular.
5
Assess the force of the critique
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's critique is logically sound. He uses a valid dilemma and provides counterexamples. Later logicians like Mill and Bosanquet independently reached similar conclusions. The critique also aligns with the Islamic emphasis on tawatur (mass transmission) as a source of certainty.
The critique does not reject all universals but denies their epistemic priority.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the Aristotelian concept of the universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya) as the foundation of demonstration (burhan). The author begins by noting that the science of hadith terminology was a response to logic, defending the tawatur of revelation. Then he moves to the third aspect of critique. The logicians claim that certain knowledge requires demonstration, and demonstration requires a universal proposition. Ibn Taymiyya argues that this universal proposition is either intuitive (badihi) or theoretical (nazari). If intuitive, then knowledge of its particular instances is even more intuitive, so the universal is unnecessary. If theoretical, it requires further intuitive premises, leading to circularity (dawr) or infinite regress (tasalsul). He gives examples: knowing that one is half of two does not require the universal that 'every whole's half is such.' Similarly, knowing that a thing cannot be both existent and non-existent does not require the law of non-contradiction. These are particular judgments from which we may later derive general laws. Thus the foundations of demonstration are particular and experiential, not universal and rational. Ibn Taymiyya anticipates later nominalists like Bosanquet and Mill, who argued that the laws of thought are derived from experience and that universal propositions merely enumerate particulars. Therefore, demonstration does not produce new knowledge but involves a petition principle (musadara 'ala al-matlab). The author notes Ibn Taymiyya's affinity with Sextus Empiricus and the tradition of skepticism, yet he imbues the critique with originality. The passage is a sustained attack on the epistemological core of Aristotelian logic, asserting the primacy of particular, empirical knowledge over universal axioms.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique: Demonstration's Universals Are Mental, Not Real
Ibn Taymiyya argues demonstration yields only mental universals, not real knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that demonstrative syllogisms yield only mental universals and therefore cannot provide knowledge of specific existing entities, especially the Necessary Existent.

Core Idea
Demonstration yields only mental universals; real existence belongs to particulars.

Ibn Taymiyya goes beyond the typical charge of begging the question. He argues that the very nature of demonstrative reasoning, which relies on universal propositions, confines it to mental concepts. Since external reality consists solely of particular existents, demonstration cannot deliver knowledge of any actual thing — including the Necessary Existent. This lesson unpacks his critique step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn Taymiyya's two-part claim about demonstration
Find the sentence where he says (a) demonstration yields only universal knowledge and (b) universals exist only in minds, not externally.
Look for the phrase 'البرهان لا يفيد إلا العلم بالكليات، والكليات إنما تكون في الأذهان لا في الأعيان'
2
Extract the implication for knowledge of particulars
If only universals are known, and nothing external is universal, then no external particular is known. Identify how he concludes that demonstration teaches nothing about actual existents.
He states 'لم يعلم بالبرهان شيء من المعينات فلا يعلم به موجود أصلاً'
3
Apply the critique to the Necessary Existent
The Necessary Existent is a specific, non-universal entity (its conception precludes participation). How does Ibn Taymiyya argue that demonstration fails to prove it?
He says 'واجب الوجود يمنع تصوره من وقوع الشركة فيه' so it is not universal, hence cannot be known by demonstration.
4
Identify the alternative epistemology implied
Ibn Taymiyya suggests that existence is self-evident and that true knowledge of God comes through worship and love, not syllogistic reasoning.
He asks rhetorically 'أي كمال للنفس في مجرد تصور هذه الأمور العامة الكلية...' implying that real perfection is in knowing God through worship.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's radical critique of the Aristotelian concept of demonstration (burhan). The author begins by stating that Ibn Taymiyya goes beyond the typical charge of petitio principii (مصادرة على المطلوب) in demonstrative premises. Instead, he aims to dismantle the entire Greek logical edifice. Ibn Taymiyya argues that genuine knowledge is only the certain universal knowledge obtained through demonstration, but he then turns this against the logicians: demonstration yields only universals, but universals exist only in the mind (الأذهان), not in external reality (الأعيان). Since reality consists of particular, specific existents (أعيان معينة), demonstration cannot give us knowledge of any actual existent. He asks rhetorically what perfection there is in merely conceiving abstract universals without knowing the particular entities, especially the knowledge of God, which is the ultimate perfection. Ibn Taymiyya then targets metaphysics (الإلهيات) specifically. He asserts that the topic of existence (الوجود) and its divisions cannot be approached via universals because existence itself is self-evident (ظاهر) and does not require definition or demonstration—even logicians concede this. The goal of rational inference is not existence per se but its divisions: necessary and possible, substance and accident, etc. He then scrutinizes the two main divisions: the Necessary Existent (واجب الوجود) and the Possible Existent (ممكن الوجود). The Necessary Existent is a specific, non-universal entity (its conception precludes multiple participation, unlike universals). Since demonstration only yields universal knowledge, it cannot prove the Necessary Existent, nor the immaterial intellects, celestial spheres, or elements—all of which are particular substances. Similarly, possible existents are mutable and not universal in a necessary sense, so demonstration fails to capture their essential truths. Ibn Taymiyya concludes that when Aristotelian demonstration is applied to metaphysics, it produces nothing. Moreover, reliance on universal propositions leads philosophers to posit fictitious abstract entities like 'essences in themselves' (الماهيات المجردة من حيث هي هي), which have no real existence. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya's critique is epistemological: he challenges the foundational claim that demonstration gives certain knowledge of reality, arguing instead that it yields only mental constructs.

Ibn Taymiyya on demonstration's failure for possible existents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument that demonstration fails for possible existents (mumkin al-wujud) because they are mutable, and identify his charge that universal propositions lead to positing non-existent abstract essences.

Core Idea
Possible existents change; demonstration cannot give permanent universal truths about them.

Ibn Taymiyya extends his critique from the Necessary Existent to contingent beings. He argues that possible (mumkin) entities are subject to change, so any universal claim made about them through syllogistic demonstration cannot have necessary persistence. Consequently, demonstration cannot yield stable knowledge of the mutable world. Moreover, he charges that the logicians, by focusing on universals, end up positing fictitious abstract essences (al-mahiyat al-mujarrada) that have no real existence. This lesson focuses on that aspect of his critique.

But he linked it to his general doctrine and then extracted from it the result that we will see later. Ibn Taymiyya was not content with saying that in every demonstration that includes a universal proposition there is a begging of the question, but he attempted with all his effort to destroy the entire edifice of Greek Aristotelian science. So he said that particular knowledge is not knowledge at all and that true knowledge is the certain universal knowledge that we attain through demonstration, because the results known by demonstration are only universal. So he attempted to apply the rules of demonstration to metaphysics, and he did not arrive at anything at all. Because demonstration only yields knowledge of universals, and universals are only in minds, not in external objects, and they do not exist in reality, so we will not arrive at any existing thing. Ibn Taymiyya states that if demonstration yields only knowledge of universals, and universals exist only in minds not in external objects, and there is nothing external but a specific existence, then nothing particular is known by demonstration, so no existing thing is known by it at all. Rather, it only knows things estimated in minds, not actualized in the outside at all. Ibn Taymiyya says: And what perfection is there for the soul in merely conceiving these general universal things if it does not conceive the specific particular entities of existents? And what knowledge is this about the Lord of the worlds, for whom souls are only perfected by knowing Him and worshipping Him with love and humility? The topic of existence and its concomitants—which is the topic of the highest science—cannot be reached through universals. This becomes clear in our research or in our application of demonstration to those topics. As for existence, it is a manifest matter, so there is no need to attempt to define it or demonstrate it. Ibn Taymiyya states that the logicians themselves admit this. So it is not the goal of rational inference, but rather its divisions are the goal. And the divisions themselves are necessary and possible, substance and accident, cause and effect, eternal and temporal, which are more specific than the name existence. 'And merely dividing a general matter into divisions, without arriving at knowledge of the divisions, does not require a great universal knowledge higher than the conception of existence.' This becomes clear from the investigation of the two main divisions of existence, which are the Necessary Existent and the Possible Existent. As for the Necessary Existent: it is the most perfect of existents. Its existence is specific, not universal, because the universal does not prevent its conception from allowing multiple participation. And the Necessary Existent, its conception prevents multiple participation. Therefore, we will not attain by demonstration knowledge of the Necessary Existent, nor the ten intellects, nor the spheres, nor the elements. For all of them are specific substances, not general universal matters. In other words, Ibn Taymiyya states that the philosophers do not arrive at the totality of their existents, and so demonstration yields no true knowledge. As for things of possible existence, syllogistic demonstration does not yield universal matters 'necessary in persistence in them' because those possible things do not continue in a single state, but are subject to change and transformation. Ibn Taymiyya applied the idea that demonstration only yields knowledge of universals to three metaphysical topics, and its two divisions—the Necessary Existent and the Possible Existent—and in the three topics, demonstration produced nothing. If the demonstration itself, based on a universal proposition, does not lead to the proof of the Necessary Existent and the Possible Existent, which are specific entities existing in reality, then on the other hand it will rely on the metaphysical views of the Greeks, like the claim of absolute existences that have no existence or proof in reality. The most important of those existences are the abstract essences in themselves (al-mahiyat al-mujarrada min haythu hiya hiya). But Ibn Taymiyya maintains that these existences have no existence.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's radical critique of the Aristotelian concept of demonstration (burhan). The author begins by stating that Ibn Taymiyya goes beyond the typical charge of petitio principii (مصادرة على المطلوب) in demonstrative premises. Instead, he aims to dismantle the entire Greek logical edifice. Ibn Taymiyya argues that genuine knowledge is only the certain universal knowledge obtained through demonstration, but he then turns this against the logicians: demonstration yields only universals, but universals exist only in the mind (الأذهان), not in external reality (الأعيان). Since reality consists of particular, specific existents (أعيان معينة), demonstration cannot give us knowledge of any actual existent. He asks rhetorically what perfection there is in merely conceiving abstract universals without knowing the particular entities, especially the knowledge of God, which is the ultimate perfection. Ibn Taymiyya then targets metaphysics (الإلهيات) specifically. He asserts that the topic of existence (الوجود) and its divisions cannot be approached via universals because existence itself is self-evident (ظاهر) and does not require definition or demonstration—even logicians concede this. The goal of rational inference is not existence per se but its divisions: necessary and possible, substance and accident, etc. He then scrutinizes the two main divisions: the Necessary Existent (واجب الوجود) and the Possible Existent (ممكن الوجود). The Necessary Existent is a specific, non-universal entity (its conception precludes multiple participation, unlike universals). Since demonstration only yields universal knowledge, it cannot prove the Necessary Existent, nor the immaterial intellects, celestial spheres, or elements—all of which are particular substances. Similarly, possible existents are mutable and not universal in a necessary sense, so demonstration fails to capture their essential truths. Ibn Taymiyya concludes that when Aristotelian demonstration is applied to metaphysics, it produces nothing. Moreover, reliance on universal propositions leads philosophers to posit fictitious abstract entities like 'essences in themselves' (الماهيات المجردة من حيث هي هي), which have no real existence. Throughout, Ibn Taymiyya's critique is epistemological: he challenges the foundational claim that demonstration gives certain knowledge of reality, arguing instead that it yields only mental constructs.
