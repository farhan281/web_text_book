Begging the Question: The Fallacy of Circular Syllogism
Author defines begging the question and traces its critique history.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of begging the question (al-muṣādara ʿalā al-maṭlūb) in a syllogism and explain why it fails to produce new knowledge.

Core Idea
Begging the question makes a syllogism circular: the conclusion is already in the premises.

This passage introduces a classic fallacy in logic—begging the question—where the conclusion is identical to the major premise. It traces the critique from Sextus Empiricus through Islamic commentators to John Stuart Mill. Understanding this fallacy helps you spot circular reasoning in Islamic theological and legal arguments.

Guided Path
1
Recall the structure of a categorical syllogism.
A syllogism has two premises and a conclusion. The major premise contains the predicate of the conclusion. Identify the major premise and the conclusion in a given argument.
2
Identify the fallacy condition.
The author says the fallacy occurs when the conclusion and the major premise are one and the same thing. That means the conclusion is being assumed as a premise. Check if the major premise and conclusion are identical in subject and predicate.
3
Test with an example.
If the major premise is 'All nabidh is intoxicating' and the conclusion is 'Nabidh is intoxicating', the premise already says what the conclusion says—no new information. This is begging the question.
In a valid syllogism, the major premise should be a general rule, e.g., 'Every intoxicant is forbidden', and the conclusion should be a specific instance.
4
Understand the critique's significance.
The critique questions whether syllogisms can produce new knowledge. Islamic commentators recognized this issue, and later critics like Mill used it to challenge Aristotelian logic. Consider how this critique applies to Islamic legal reasoning.

It is that the conclusion and the major premise are one and the same thing, so the syllogism does not bring anything new at all. This issue was raised by Sextus Empiricus as a critique of Aristotelian syllogism, and Islamic commentators knew it. Commentary
This passage defines the logical fallacy known as 'begging the question' or petitio principii. The author states that it occurs when the conclusion is identical to the major premise, meaning the argument is circular and produces no new knowledge. The origin of this critique is attributed to Sextus Empiricus, who used it to attack Aristotelian syllogism. Islamic commentators recognized this problem, and later Muslim critics of logic also employed it. In modern times, John Stuart Mill made this objection the cornerstone of his critique of syllogism. The passage thus traces the historical transmission of this logical issue.

Two Forms of Anthropomorphism: Al-Sijistani's Critique
Critique of Ash'ari and Mu'tazili positions on attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how both Ash'ari and Mu'tazili positions on divine attributes lead to anthropomorphism according to al-Sijistani's critique.

Core Idea
Both affirmation and negation of attributes can lead to anthropomorphism—clear or hidden.

Al-Sijistani targets two rival schools of kalam: the Ash'aris who affirm attributes additional to God's essence, and the Mu'tazilis who negate them. He argues that both methods fail to preserve God's transcendence. Understanding his logical dissection reveals how theological positions can be evaluated for hidden inconsistencies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Locate in the passage the description of the Ash'ari method (affirming attributes additional to essence) and the Mu'tazili method (negating attributes).
The Ash'aris are said to commit 'clear anthropomorphism'; Mu'tazilis commit 'hidden anthropomorphism'.
2
Analyze the consequence of each position
Al-Sijistani says Ash'ari affirmation leads to clear tashbih (anthropomorphism) and multiplicity of pre-eternals. Mu'tazili negation leads to hidden tashbih because it treats God like abstract substances.
Hidden tashbih means that by denying all attributes, God becomes like abstract, non-describable entities.
3
Extract the principle behind the critique
Notice that al-Sijistani is applying a logical consistency test: each position, if held consistently, leads to an undesirable consequence that undermines the very transcendence both schools claim to protect.
This is a form of reductio ad absurdum used in kalam.

طريق الإثبات - إثبات الصفة لله - طريقاً خاطئاً ، لأن في الإثبات تشبيهاً لله بالأجسام ، فإن طريق المعتزلة طريق النفي - نفي الصفات عن الله - طريق خاطىء أيضاً ، لأن في النفي تشبيهاً لله بالجواهر المجردة ، وإذا كان المعتزلة أنكروا أن يكون الله صفة أو حد أو نعت ، فانهم لم ينكروا أن يكون الله لا صفة
Abu Sulayman al-Sijistani criticizes the methods of proving or negating God's attributes according to two sects of theologians, the Ash'aris and the Mu'tazilis. In this critique he departs from the principle of the excluded middle. As for the Ash'ari method—affirming attributes additional to the essence—it leads, in al-Sijistani's view (380 AH / 990 CE), only to a clear and obvious anthropomorphism of the Creator by the attributes of creatures on one hand, and to a multiplicity of pre-eternals on the other. To escape this anthropomorphism, the Mu'tazilis resorted to negating the attributes additional to the essence, saying: God is not described, not limited, not qualified, and not visible. Al-Sijistani objects to this method, saying it does not lead to affirming the existence of God as transcendent of attributes, because what then is the case of the described, the limited, and the qualified among His creatures—is it the attribute, the limit, and the qualification? Or is the described other than its attribute, the limited other than its limit, and the qualified other than its qualification? If the attribute is the described, the limit is the limited, and the qualification is the qualified, then it would necessarily follow that blackness is the black thing and whiteness is the white thing. And if the described is other than the attribute, the limited other than the limit, and the qualified other than the qualification, then all these things—which are of God's creation—would share with their Creator, who is transcendent of attribute, limit, and qualification. And if it is permissible for a creature to share with the Creator in one aspect, it is not impossible for it to share with Him in all aspects. At this point al-Sijistani arrives at the statement: 'Whoever worships God by negating attributes falls into hidden anthropomorphism—and they are the Mu'tazilis—and whoever worships Him by affirming attributes falls into clear anthropomorphism—and they are the Ash'aris.' But the Mu'tazilis nevertheless affirmed for Him 'names that are not stripped of attributes and qualifications,' or more precisely they said that His attributes are other than His essence, so He is Hearing by essence, Seeing by essence, but they negated hearing, sight, and knowledge. And in this there is error. For these names, if they adhere to any essence, the attributes due to which the names occurred adhere to it. Otherwise, monstrous impossibilities would follow. If we allowed a knower without knowledge or a powerful without power, then it would be permissible for the ignorant, with the absence of knowledge, to be a knower, and for the incapable, with the absence of power, to be capable... The Mu'tazilis respond to this by saying they were careful not to apply 'sight' to 'the Seeing One' when the name 'the Seeing One' is directed toward the essence of God. And they held that whoever has the name 'the Seeing One' necessarily, because of sight, permits blindness upon him. And whoever has the name 'the Powerful One' necessarily, because of power, permits incapability upon him... Likewise they negated from God that whose opposite would be necessitated by its removal. Al-Sijistani refutes this statement by saying that sight is not the cause of the existence of blindness, nor is power the cause of the existence of incapability... Otherwise, the existence of blindness whenever sight exists, and the existence of incapability whenever power exists, would be necessary... Rather, if knowledge is found in some possessors of knowledge without ignorance following it, and power is found in some possessors of power without incapability following it, then it becomes clear that the cause of the appearance of ignorance and incapability is not knowledge and power, but rather the possibility that defects of ignorance and incapability may befall some possessors of knowledge and power. And God is not a locus for defects, nor do defects enter upon Him. So if the Mu'tazilis hold that whoever is knower by knowledge or powerful by power permits ignorance and incapability upon him, then al-Sijistani holds that whoever is knower, knowledge precedes him; and whoever is powerful, power accompanies him... So if they transfer the judgment of the witnessed to the absent, they conclude that it is permissible that in the absent there be a knower without knowledge or a powerful without power, then others may say: if they have not seen a knower by knowledge except that incapability has been permitted upon him, then they might witness in the absent a knower by knowledge upon whom ignorance is not permitted, or a powerful by power upon whom incapability is not permitted... How then can one of the two analogies be given preponderance? Here the proofs are equal, as they say. So if the Ash'ari method—the method of affirmation—affirming an attribute for God—is a mistaken method, because in affirmation there is anthropomorphism of God with bodies, then the Mu'tazili method—the method of negation—negating attributes from God—is also mistaken, because in negation there is anthropomorphism of God with abstract substances. And if the Mu'tazilis denied that God is attribute, limit, or qualification, they did not deny that God is without attribute.

Commentary
This passage presents Abu Sulayman al-Sijistani's logical critique of two opposing theological schools on the issue of God's attributes. The author first describes al-Sijistani's criticism of the Ash'ari position (attributes are additional to essence), which leads to clear anthropomorphism (tashbih jali) and implies multiple pre-eternals. Then he turns to the Mu'tazili response (negating attributes), which al-Sijistani argues leads to hidden anthropomorphism (tashbih khafi). The core logical problem is that both positions, if examined consistently, force an undesirable consequence: either the Creator shares properties with creatures (if attributes are real) or creatures share in the Creator's transcendence (if attributes are denied). Al-Sijistani illustrates this with a dilemma: if attribute equals described, then blackness equals black thing—absurd; if attribute is other, then created things share in the Creator's attribute-transcendence—also problematic. The Mu'tazili attempt to affirm names without attributes is shown to be self-defeating: names imply the attributes from which they derive. If 'Knower' does not require knowledge, then one could be ignorant and still be knower—an impossibility. The Mu'tazili retort that affirming sight would permit blindness in God is refuted by distinguishing between cause and consequence: sight does not cause blindness, rather blindness is a defect that can affect creatures but not God. The passage concludes that both methods are flawed because they lead to anthropomorphism (bodies vs. abstract substances). The lesson thus demonstrates how logical analysis can expose inconsistencies in theological arguments, a key skill in Islamic dialectic.

The Dilemma of Attributes and Described Objects
Logical dilemma on attribute and described object

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Sijistani's dilemma about whether attributes are identical to or separate from the described object, and explain why both options are problematic.

Core Idea
If attributes equal the described, absurdity; if separate, creatures share in God's transcendence.

Al-Sijistani presents a classic logical dilemma to challenge the Mu'tazili negation of attributes. He forces a choice between two possibilities regarding the relationship of attributes to the objects they describe. Either possibility leads to an unacceptable conclusion. This lesson unpacks that dilemma in detail.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dilemma options
Find where al-Sijistani asks: 'Is the attribute the described? Or is the described other than its attribute?' The two horns of the dilemma are thus: identity or distinction.
This is a standard logical dilemma form.
2
Examine the first horn
If attribute equals described, then blackness = black thing, whiteness = white thing. This is absurd because properties would be identical to substances.
This violates common sense distinctions.
3
Examine the second horn
If attribute is separate from described, then created things (which have attributes and limits) would share with God in being transcendent of attribute/limit/qualification. This denies God's uniqueness.
This is the hidden anthropomorphism al-Sijistani warns against.
4
Apply the dilemma to the Mu'tazili position
The Mu'tazilis cannot escape: if they affirm creatures are described, they must admit either identity or distinction. Both lead to consequences that undermine their own goal of preserving tanzih (transcendence).
Thus, the Mu'tazili negation, meant to avoid tashbih, actually entails it in a hidden way.

Abu Sulayman al-Sijistani criticizes the methods of proving or negating God's attributes according to two sects of theologians, the Ash'aris and the Mu'tazilis. In this critique he departs from the principle of the excluded middle. As for the Ash'ari method—affirming attributes additional to the essence—it leads, in al-Sijistani's view (380 AH / 990 CE), only to a clear and obvious anthropomorphism of the Creator by the attributes of creatures on one hand, and to a multiplicity of pre-eternals on the other. To escape this anthropomorphism, the Mu'tazilis resorted to negating the attributes additional to the essence, saying: God is not described, not limited, not qualified, and not visible. Al-Sijistani objects to this method, saying it does not lead to affirming the existence of God as transcendent of attributes, because what then is the case of the described, the limited, and the qualified among His creatures—is it the attribute, the limit, and the qualification? Or is the described other than its attribute, the limited other than its limit, and the qualified other than its qualification? If the attribute is the described, the limit is the limited, and the qualification is the qualified, then it would necessarily follow that blackness is the black thing and whiteness is the white thing. And if the described is other than the attribute, the limited other than the limit, and the qualified other than the qualification, then all these things—which are of God's creation—would share with their Creator, who is transcendent of attribute, limit, and qualification. And if it is permissible for a creature to share with the Creator in one aspect, it is not impossible for it to share with Him in all aspects. At this point al-Sijistani arrives at the statement: 'Whoever worships God by negating attributes falls into hidden anthropomorphism—and they are the Mu'tazilis—and whoever worships Him by affirming attributes falls into clear anthropomorphism—and they are the Ash'aris.' But the Mu'tazilis nevertheless affirmed for Him 'names that are not stripped of attributes and qualifications,' or more precisely they said that His attributes are other than His essence, so He is Hearing by essence, Seeing by essence, but they negated hearing, sight, and knowledge. And in this there is error. For these names, if they adhere to any essence, the attributes due to which the names occurred adhere to it. Otherwise, monstrous impossibilities would follow. If we allowed a knower without knowledge or a powerful without power, then it would be permissible for the ignorant, with the absence of knowledge, to be a knower, and for the incapable, with the absence of power, to be capable... The Mu'tazilis respond to this by saying they were careful not to apply 'sight' to 'the Seeing One' when the name 'the Seeing One' is directed toward the essence of God. And they held that whoever has the name 'the Seeing One' necessarily, because of sight, permits blindness upon him. And whoever has the name 'the Powerful One' necessarily, because of power, permits incapability upon him... Likewise they negated from God that whose opposite would be necessitated by its removal. Al-Sijistani refutes this statement by saying that sight is not the cause of the existence of blindness, nor is power the cause of the existence of incapability... Otherwise, the existence of blindness whenever sight exists, and the existence of incapability whenever power exists, would be necessary... Rather, if knowledge is found in some possessors of knowledge without ignorance following it, and power is found in some possessors of power without incapability following it, then it becomes clear that the cause of the appearance of ignorance and incapability is not knowledge and power, but rather the possibility that defects of ignorance and incapability may befall some possessors of knowledge and power. And God is not a locus for defects, nor do defects enter upon Him. So if the Mu'tazilis hold that whoever is knower by knowledge or powerful by power permits ignorance and incapability upon him, then al-Sijistani holds that whoever is knower, knowledge precedes him; and whoever is powerful, power accompanies him... So if they transfer the judgment of the witnessed to the absent, they conclude that it is permissible that in the absent there be a knower without knowledge or a powerful without power, then others may say: if they have not seen a knower by knowledge except that incapability has been permitted upon him, then they might witness in the absent a knower by knowledge upon whom ignorance is not permitted, or a powerful by power upon whom incapability is not permitted... How then can one of the two analogies be given preponderance? Here the proofs are equal, as they say. And if the Mu'tazilis denied that God is attribute, limit, or qualification, they did not deny that God is without attribute.

Names Without Attributes: The Mu'tazili Error
Refutation of Mu'tazili distinction between names and attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why al-Sijistani argues that affirming names like 'the Seeing One' without the attribute of sight leads to logical inconsistency.

Core Idea
Names imply the attributes from which they derive; denying the attribute while keeping the name is contradictory.

The Mu'tazilis attempted a middle path: they affirmed that God has names (like al-Basir, al-Qadir) but denied that these names imply real attributes. Al-Sijistani shows this is impossible; if a name applies to something, the corresponding attribute must be present. Otherwise, absurdities follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazili claim
Find where the passage says the Mu'tazilis affirmed names (like 'Seeing') but negated the attributes (sight). They said God is 'Hearing by essence, Seeing by essence' but not with attributes.
This is the so-called 'ahwal' (states) theory.
2
Follow al-Sijistani's refutation
He argues: if a name like 'Knower' applies to an essence, the attribute of knowledge must also apply. Otherwise, you could have a knower without knowledge, which is impossible.
This is a logical principle: names derive from attributes.
3
See the absurd consequence
If we allow a knower without knowledge, then an ignorant person could also be called 'knower' (since the absence of knowledge doesn't prevent the name). This is a blatant contradiction.
Al-Sijistani uses reductio ad absurdum.

Ibn Taymiyya on the Limited Utility of Logic
Ibn Taymiyya argues that logic benefits certain stubborn individuals and sophists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the claim that logic is universally beneficial and explain his alternative view that logic is a treatment for sophistry, not a universal tool.

Core Idea
Logic is medicine for sophists, not food for everyone.

In this passage from his critique of logic, Ibn Taymiyya takes a nuanced position. He does not reject logic outright, as many assume, but instead argues that its benefit is relative. For clear-minded people, logic is unnecessary; for the obstinate and sophists, it may serve as a therapeutic tool. Ibn Taymiyya also situates the origin of Aristotelian logic historically, as a response to sophistry. This lesson will guide you through his argument and its implications for how we view logical methods in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about relativity
Read the first lines: 'النسبية وأن بعض الناس قد ينتفعون بالدليل الخفي والحد الخفي' (Relativity and that some people benefit from hidden evidence and hidden definition). Ibn Taymiyya asserts that the benefit of logical methods is not absolute but depends on the person.
The term 'النسبية' (relativity) sets the stage for his critique.
2
Distinguish two types of people
Ibn Taymiyya contrasts those who accept clear, obvious evidences with those who need hidden, contrived logical forms. The latter are the 'obstinate ones' (المعاندون) or sophists (السوفسطائية). He says their souls are accustomed only to knowledge gained through struggle.
This distinction is key to understanding his limited endorsement of logic.
3
Understand the disease analogy
Ibn Taymiyya compares sophistry to a disease of the heart that requires treatment, like a bodily illness. Just as a sick person needs medicine, sophists need logic as a therapeutic tool. But healthy people do not need it.
This analogy reinforces that logic is not for everyone.
4
Recognize the historical context
He claims that Aristotelian logic emerged as a response to the sophists' movement. Logic was developed to stop sophistry and provide a method for dispute. Thus logic's purpose is dialectical, not universal.
This historical claim challenges the idea that logic is a timeless, universal method.
5
Appreciate the pluralism of methods
Ibn Taymiyya concludes that there are many ways to certainty, not just logical syllogism. He gives examples: step-by-step premises in calculation, multiple examples in disputation, and the dialectical method of eliciting premises from the opponent.
This pluralism is a direct refutation of the monopoly claim by logicians.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis by Ibn Taymiyya of the claim that logical methods (especially definitions and syllogisms) are universally beneficial. He begins by noting the relativity of benefit: some people only accept hidden evidence and definitions, not obvious ones. These are the obstinate (mu'anidun) or those whose souls are habituated to struggling for knowledge. Ibn Taymiyya observes that many theoreticians (nuzzar) have become accustomed to only accepting knowledge after thorough investigation and debate, even if the matter is clear to others. Therefore, logical methods have a limited utility: they can benefit some obstinate individuals, whom Ibn Taymiyya identifies as sophists. He then historically contextualizes Aristotelian logic as a response to sophistry, likening the disease of sophistry to bodily illnesses that require specific treatments. However, he immediately qualifies that even sophists do not universally need this logic; some need it, others need more or less. He draws an analogy to someone who errs in calculation: for those who persist in error despite clarity, a longer path of step-by-step premises or multiple examples may be needed. In disputation (munazara), the dialectical method used by early Muslim and Greek thinkers involved the questioner eliciting premises sequentially from the opponent, forcing him to acknowledge the entailments. Ibn Taymiyya concludes that there are multiple ways to certainty, and it is absurd to claim that only logical syllogism yields certainty.

Ibn al-Qayyim's Critique of Aristotelian Logic
Author presents Ibn al-Qayyim's criticism of Aristotelian logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn al-Qayyim’s critique of Aristotelian logic and evaluate its internal consistency.

Core Idea
Ibn al-Qayyim: logic's falsehood outweighs its truth and it corrupts any science it enters.

In this passage, the author presents Ibn al-Qayyim's critique of Aristotelian logic, building on his teacher Ibn Taymiyya. Unlike a detailed technical refutation, Ibn al-Qayyim offers general reasons for its corruption and highlights its incompatibility with clear reason. This lesson will guide you through the structure of his critique and help you assess its logical force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target
Ibn al-Qayyim attacks the claim that logic is a "law that protects the mind" (qānūn ʿāṣim li-l-dhihn). He aims to refute this definition by showing logic's harmful effects rather than engaging with its detailed rules.
2
Analyze his deconstruction
He argues that if logic were valid, its benefit would be only like surveying or geometry. But its falsehood outweighs its truth, its principles are contradictory, and it actually leads the mind astray. He also notes that logic contains arbitrary distinctions and unsupported claims.
Ibn al-Qayyim relies on general observations rather than a point-by-point refutation of logical rules.
3
Examine the religious objection
Ibn al-Qayyim points out that the early imams (al-Shafi'i, Ahmad ibn Hanbal) never used logic, yet their knowledge was sound. Therefore, learning logic cannot be a religious obligation (fard kifayah or fard 'ayn). He asserts that logic corrupts any science it enters.
This is an appeal to authority and tradition, common in polemics against Greek philosophy.
4
Assess the internal validity
The critique is internally consistent: if logic is as harmful as claimed, then rejecting it is rational. However, it relies on empirical testimony and authority rather than a systematic logical argument. From a logician's perspective, the critique may be question-begging because it assumes logic's falsity without demonstrating it formally.
Consider whether Ibn al-Qayyim's own reasoning follows the standards he criticizes.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition of Ibn al-Qayyim's stance against Aristotelian logic. It begins by situating the jurists after Ibn Taymiyya into two groups: those who followed his critical approach (Ibn al-Qayyim, al-San'ani, al-Suyuti) and those who followed Ibn al-Salah's focus on prohibition (al-Subki). The author then focuses on the first group, starting with Ibn al-Qayyim. Unlike his teacher Ibn Taymiyya, Ibn al-Qayyim does not delve into detailed refutations of logic's principles but instead reiterates general reasons for its corruption. He argues that if logic were a valid science, its benefit would be akin to surveying or geometry—practical but limited. However, its falsehood far outweighs its truth, and its internal contradictions and inconsistent meanings actually lead the mind astray rather than protecting it. He directly refutes the well-known definition of logic as 'a law that protects the mind from error in thinking,' asserting that only someone who has studied logic deeply and recognized its corruption and opposition to clear reason can truly appreciate its flaws. He reports that a former advocate of logic was amazed at how its principles contradict sound reason. Ibn al-Qayyim also criticizes logic for containing unsupported claims, such as treating equivalent things differently or equating opposites unjustly. He praises the refutations of earlier Muslim thinkers like Abu Sa'id al-Sirafi and the theologians, and he highly commends Ibn Taymiyya's works on the subject. Furthermore, he rejects the notion that learning logic is a religious obligation (fard kifaya or fard 'ayn), arguing that the great imams of Islam, such as al-Shafi'i and Ahmad ibn Hanbal, never used logic and their knowledge was sound without it. He famously asserts that logic corrupts any science it enters, distorting its rules and principles. The passage concludes by noting that Ibn al-Qayyim claims Aristotelian logical inferences do not lead to certain knowledge. Each of these points builds a case against the epistemological and religious legitimacy of Aristotelian logic within the Islamic tradition.

According to Ibn al-Qayyim, what happens to any science that logic enters?
A) It becomes more precise and systematic.
B) It is corrupted and its rules are changed.
C) It becomes easier to understand.
D) It is elevated to a higher level of certainty.

Key Takeaway
Ibn al-Qayyim's critique relies on general claims of corruption and contradiction rather than detailed refutation, appealing to the authority of early imams and the harm logic causes to sciences.
