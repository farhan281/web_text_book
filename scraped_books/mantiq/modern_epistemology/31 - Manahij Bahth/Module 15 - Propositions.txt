The Structure of Tasdiqat: Propositions and Inference
Introduces the two parts of tasdiqat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the two main parts of tasdiqat (propositions and inference) and explain their relationship.

Core Idea
Tasdiqat is built on propositions and the inferences that connect them.

In this lesson, we explore the foundation of the third chapter: tasdiqat. The author divides it into two clear parts—propositions (qadaya) and inference (istidlal)—and notes that their connection is obvious. Understanding this structure is essential for grasping how Islamic logicians organize their study of judgments and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two parts
Read the Arabic passage and locate the words 'مبحث القضايا' and 'مبحث الاستدلال'. These are the two main topics of tasdiqat.
These correspond to 'propositions' and 'inference' respectively.
2
Understand their relationship
The author says the connection is 'ظاهرة لا تحتاج إلى توضيح' (obvious and needs no clarification). Think about how propositions serve as premises for inferences.
In logic, propositions are the building blocks; inference is the process of deriving new knowledge from them.
3
Connect to previous chapter
The author mentions that the previous chapter covered tasawwurat (conceptions). Tasdiqat builds directly on those concepts.
Tasawwurat provide the terms; tasdiqat form judgments and arguments.

The Third Chapter: On Tasdiqat. We presented in the previous chapter the topics of tasawwurat, and we will present in this chapter the topics of tasdiqat. They consist of two topics: the topic of propositions and the topic of inference. The connection between the two topics is obvious and needs no clarification. As for propositions, Aristotle devoted to their study a book from his logical works, namely 'De Interpretatione' (al-‘Ibarah). The Islamic logicians engaged with this book in commentary and detail, and they did not stop at Aristotle's investigations but added other investigations. The first and most important of those additions made by the Islamic logicians—following the later Greek commentators—is the division of propositions into categorical and conditional, and the division of conditional into conjunctive and disjunctive. Aristotelian logic did not know conditional propositions of both types at all. But they first appeared with Theophrastus and Eudemus. Then they emerged in their complete form among the Stoics, and through the Greek commentators they transferred to the Islamic world. The investigations of these propositions—whether categorical or conditional—are available to everyone in many books, such that we find absolutely no need to explain them again. However, we will only clarify one point: whether the Islamic logicians knew that Aristotle did not originate these propositions.

Origins of Conditional Propositions
Traces the history of conditional propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical development of conditional propositions from Theophrastus to the Islamic logicians.

Core Idea
Conditional propositions were not in Aristotle; they were added by later Greek philosophers and adopted by Muslim logicians.

This lesson focuses on the key addition mentioned by the author: conditional propositions (al-shartiyya). The passage explains that Aristotle did not include them; they first appeared with Theophrastus and Eudemus, were fully developed by the Stoics, and were transmitted to the Islamic world through Greek commentators. Understanding this history highlights the dynamic nature of logical science.

These were students of Aristotle.
3
Identify the ful development
Then find 'ثم ظهرت في صورتها الكاملة لدى الرواقيين' (then they emerged in their complete form among the Stoics).
The Stoics systematized conditionals.

Trace the transmission
Finally, note 'وعن طريق الشراح اليونانيين انتقلت الى العالم الإسلامي' (through the Greek commentators they transferred to the Islamic world).
Muslim logicians received and expanded upon them.

Non-Contradiction and Predication Types: The Mutakallimun's Critique
Author explains the principle of non-contradiction and the mutakallimun's critique via predication types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish conventional from non-conventional predication and explain how the mutakallimun used this distinction to critique the principle of non-contradiction.

Core Idea
The mutakallimun did not deny non-contradiction; they restricted it to conventional predication.

The principle of non-contradiction is foundational to rational thought, yet some mutakallimun questioned its absolute validity. Their critique hinges not on rejecting the principle outright, but on distinguishing two types of predication: conventional (ḥaml muta'ārif) and non-conventional (ḥaml ghayr muta'ārif). This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows how it was used to allow contradictory predicates in certain contexts, such as 'the non-concept is a concept' or 'the particular is universal.' Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping theological debates about divine attributes and the nature of existence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two predication types
Locate in the passage the definitions of conventional predication (judgment on the referent) and non-conventional predication (judgment on the concept). Note that the impossibility of opposites applies only to the first type.
Conventional predication is about what the subject refers to (maṣdaq); non-conventional is about the meaning (mafhūm).
2
Analyze the blackness example
The author gives the example of blackness inhering in a body. Blackness is not the body, yet the body is described as black. This leads to the body being 'possessor of non-body'—a kind of opposition that is allowed because the predication is by relation or derivation.
Derivation (ishtiqāq) means deriving an adjective from a property; relation (nisba) means attributing a property to a possessor.
3
Contrast Aristotle and the mutakallimun
Find the sentence where Aristotle's absolute prohibition is stated, then note the mutakallimun's condition: conjunction of opposites is permissible only when the judgment is on the concept (non-conventional predication).
This is the key point of the critique: the principle is not rejected but qualified.
4
Evaluate examples of non-conventional predication
Examine the examples given: 'the non-concept is a concept,' 'the particular is universal,' 'non-thing is a thing.' These are allowed under non-conventional predication because they speak about the concept itself, not the referent.
These examples illustrate how a thing can be described by its opposite if predication is on the concept.
5
Assess the author's conclusion
The author declares the critique intellectually weak and notes that the mutakallimun dismissed it from a religious perspective. Consider whether this dismissal is logically justified or merely a reflection of different priorities.
The author does not provide the mutakallimun's religious arguments; he only states they saw nothing of concern.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition of the mutakallimun's approach to the principle of non-contradiction. The author begins by stating that the impossibility of a thing being described by its opposite applies only when predication is by the thing's essence (bi-huwiyya). If the predication is by derivation (ishtiqāq) or relation (nisba), it is not impossible. For example, blackness inhering in a body is non-body relative to the body, so the body can be described as 'possessor of non-body.' The author then contrasts Aristotle's absolute prohibition of the conjunction of opposites with the mutakallimun's view, which allows it under non-conventional predication (al-ḥaml ghayr al-muta'ārif). In conventional predication, the judgment applies to the referent (maṣdaq) of the subject; in non-conventional, it applies to the concept (mafhūm) of the subject. The author provides examples like 'the non-concept is a concept' and 'the particular is universal' to illustrate non-conventional predication. He dismisses other arguments against the principle as weak, but notes that the mutakallimun rejected the principle not on rational grounds but because they saw it as irrelevant from a religious perspective.

حمل متعارف (ḥaml muta'ārif): Conventional predication where the judgment applies to what the subject refers to (maṣdaq). [Note: Standard usage in Arabic logic.]
حمل غير متعارف (ḥaml ghayr muta'ārif): Non-conventional predication where the judgment applies to the concept (mafhūm) of the subject, allowing contradictory predicates. [Note: This specific distinction is used by mutakallimun to critique the principle of non-contradiction.]

Defining Contradictory Propositions: al-Sijistani's Refinement
al-Sijistani defends his double negation from the charge of violating non-contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition for contradictory propositions and determine whether a given pair is truly contradictory.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires one affirmative and one negative — not just any two opposing statements.

In this passage, al-Sijistani defends his double negation analysis of divine attributes from the charge of logical inconsistency. He clarifies a crucial condition for contradictory propositions that is often overlooked. Understanding this condition is essential for correctly applying the law of non-contradiction in theological and logical discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that 'la mawsuf' and 'la la mawsuf' are contradictory, violating non-contradiction. Notice the assumption that any pair of opposed statements must be contradictories.
2
Extract al-Sijistani's condition
He states that for two propositions to be contradictory, one must be affirmative and the other negative. Both cannot be negative.
3
Apply the condition to the case
Check 'la mawsuf' (negative) and 'la la mawsuf' (negative) — both are negative, so the condition fails. They are not contradictories.
4
Distinguish jam' from raf'
al-Sijistani clarifies he is not combining both contradictories (jam') but rather raising both (raf'), which is permissible. This refines the law of non-contradiction: it applies only to true contradictories.

Commentary
This passage presents al-Sijistani's defense against a logical objection to his double negation approach (negating both 'described' and 'not described'). The objector claims that saying 'undescribed' (la mawsuf) and 'not undescribed' (la la mawsuf) are contradictory propositions, violating the law of non-contradiction. al-Sijistani responds by defining the condition for contradictory propositions: one must be affirmative and the other negative. In his case, both propositions are negative ('la mawsuf' is negative, and 'la la mawsuf' is also negative because it negates a negation). Therefore, they are not contradictories. True contradiction would be between 'mawsuf' (affirmative) and 'la mawsuf' (negative). He then explicitly states that he has not combined two contradictories (jam' bayn al-naqidayn) but rather raised both contradictories (raf' al-naqidayn) – i.e., negated both. This is a significant logical point: normally, one cannot simultaneously assert both a proposition and its negation (jam'), nor deny both (raf'), but al-Sijistani argues that denying both is permissible if the propositions are not true contradictories. The passage thus refines the understanding of the law of non-contradiction by clarifying that it applies only to affirmative-negative pairs.

People vary in the strength of their minds even more than they vary in the strength of their bodies. Some conceive the two extremes completely, so that by that complete conception the necessary implications that are not clear to another become clear to them, who could not achieve complete conception. People thus vary in conception in speed and strength. For some people consider a proposition to be sensory, experiential, demonstrative, or mutawatir, while others know it by reflection and inference. Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Greek Universals and the Concept of Jawami' al-Kalim
Author presents Ibn Taymiyya's acknowledgment of universal propositions as 'jawami' al-kalim'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's distinction between Greek-style universal propositions and 'jawami' al-kalim' and explain why he redefines universal propositions in religious contexts.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya redefines universals as 'comprehensive words' from prophetic speech, not Greek logical constructs.

In this passage, we see Ibn Taymiyya's nuanced position: he does not reject universal propositions outright, but he insists that in religious contexts, the universals are not Greek syllogistic premises but 'jawami' al-kalim'—comprehensive prophetic statements that encapsulate rulings. This is a key moment in his critique of Greek logic and his defense of a scriptural epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem Ibn Taymiyya addresses.
Note that Ibn Taymiyya is 'compelled' to admit the need for universal propositions when dealing with matters known through prophetic revelation. This shows he is responding to the challenge that religious knowledge requires universal statements.
The compulsion arises because universal propositions are necessary for deriving general rulings.
2
Extract the example he gives.
The hadith 'Every intoxicant is khamr and every khamr is haram' is presented as an example of a universal proposition. Observe that it is a single statement, not a syllogism.
This hadith is well-known in fiqh as a basis for prohibiting all intoxicants.
3
Understand Ibn Taymiyya's redefinition.
Ibn Taymiyya says this is not 'a construction according to the Greek method' but 'jawami' al-kalim'. He redefines it as comprehensive words, meaning it is a direct, concise ruling from the Prophet, not a product of logical deduction.
The term 'jawami' al-kalim' is borrowed from hadith terminology, where it describes the Prophet's ability to speak concisely with comprehensive meaning.
4
Contrast with Greek universal propositions.
In Greek logic, a universal proposition (e.g., 'All A are B') is a quantified statement that serves as a premise in a syllogism. For Ibn Taymiyya, the prophetic statement is a self-contained universal ruling that does not require syllogistic reasoning.
This contrast is essential: Ibn Taymiyya is not rejecting the universal scope, but rather the epistemological framework that derives it through Greek rules.
5
Reflect on the implications.
By redefining universals as 'jawami' al-kalim', Ibn Taymiyya safeguards the authority of prophetic speech and avoids the need for Greek logical frameworks in religious rulings. This preserves the primacy of revelation over human reasoning.
This is part of his broader project to critique the necessity of Greek logic for Islamic sciences.

Disjunction is not always exclusive; there are three types with different truth conditions.

Exclusive 'or' — exactly one alternative is true.

Suhrawardi's method for eliminating particular propositions via supposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Suhrawardi's supposition method to transform a particular proposition into a universal one.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi eliminates particularity by naming the part and judging it universally.

Suhrawardi's most detailed innovation is his method for eliminating particular propositions: instead of saying 'some A are B', we give a specific name to those A and then say 'every such named thing is B'. This lesson walks through his technique using his own examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify a particular proposition
Find a proposition that says 'some X are Y' or 'it may be that if A then B'. Note that it contains vagueness (ihmal) because 'some' or 'may be' is indefinite.
2
Isolate the partial subject or state
In the proposition, there is a 'part' (ba'd) that is being judged. In categoricals, it's a subset of individuals; in conditionals, it's some states of the antecedent.
Example: in 'some humans are tall', the part is the subset of tall humans. In 'if Zayd is in the sea, he drowns', the part is the condition where Zayd is in the sea and has no rescue.
3
Assign a specific name to that part
Create a name (like J) that refers exclusively to that part. This makes the reference definite and eliminates vagueness.
Suhrawardi says: 'let that part have a special name – let it be J'.
4
Make a universal judgment about that name
Now assert that 'every J is Y' (or 'whenever J, then ...'). This is a universal affirmative proposition, which Suhrawardi considers certain.
The original partial judgment becomes a universal one about the named set.

As for the Illuminationist discussion of propositions, Suhrawardi put forward innovative views. He did not agree with the divisions of propositions laid down by Aristotle. Rather, he tried to reduce all those propositions to the universal affirmative necessary proposition, which he calls the absolutely necessary (al-daruriyya al-battata) — and al-battata from al-batt meaning decisiveness and certainty. Propositions are divided according to quantity, according to quality, and according to modality. This is the well-known Aristotelian division, which formal logic has almost accepted until now. But Suhrawardi attacks or criticizes it. As for quantity, Suhrawardi eliminates the vague (ihmal) and particularity (budiyya) by supposition (iftirad). As for quality, he eliminates negation by conversion (ta'dil), so if negation becomes part of the subject or predicate, it does not cut the relation, and the propositions become affirmative. As for modality, Suhrawardi makes the modalities such as possibility, impossibility and others part of the predicate so that the propositions are always necessary. And we will see the ingenious attempt by Suhrawardi to eliminate the expansions of Aristotelian logic. As for the refutation of quantity, Suhrawardi tries to eliminate the particular proposition, saying: 'In the vague partial conditional, one says "it may be that if A is B then C is D, for example, or it may be that A is B and C is D, and the partial also has vagueness, for the parts of a thing are many. Commentary
This passage details Suhrawardi's radical reform of propositional logic. He rejects the Aristotelian division of propositions by quantity, quality, and modality. His goal is to reduce all propositions to the universal affirmative necessary proposition (al-daruriyya al-battata) for certainty. For quantity, he eliminates vagueness and particularity by using a suppositional name for the subject, turning partial judgments into universal ones. For quality, he transforms negative propositions into affirmative by incorporating the negation into the subject or predicate (ta'dil), so the proposition remains affirmative. For modality, he makes the modality part of the predicate, so the proposition is always necessary. This universalizes the antecedent. Suhrawardi's method simplifies logic to only universal affirmatives, eliminating the need for particular or negative propositions. The author notes that Suhrawardi believes only universal propositions are useful in sciences.

Suhrawardi's Method of Eliminating Partiality by Assumption
Suhrawardi eliminates indefinite and partial propositions by assumption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Suhrawardi's method of 'iftirāḍ' to convert an indefinite partial proposition into a universal one in both categorical and conditional forms.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi eliminates deceptive particularity by giving the part a name and judging all of it universally.

Suhrawardi recognized that indefinite and particular propositions are a source of ambiguity and error in syllogisms. He proposed a radical solution: assign a specific name to the particular part and then make a universal judgment about that name. This transforms the problematic indefinite proposition into a clear universal proposition. This method, called al-iftirāḍ, is a technique to purify scientific reasoning from misleading partiality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the indefinite or partial proposition
Find a proposition in a scientific context that is indefinite (e.g., 'Some A is B') or a conditional with a partial antecedent (e.g., 'If A is B then sometimes C is D'). Note the lack of specification.
Indefinite propositions are common in everyday speech but problematic for demonstration.
2
Recognize the source of indefiniteness
The problem is that the part (ba'd) is not specified; it could refer to any one of many possibilities. Suhrawardi says parts of a thing are many, so without specification the judgment is vague.
This vagueness can lead to fallacies in syllogisms.
3
Create a hypothetical name for the specific part
Invent a new term (e.g., J) that stands for the particular individuals or states that satisfy the predicate. In a categorical proposition, name the subject individuals; in a conditional, name the specific state of the antecedent.
The name is arbitrary but must be precise.
4
Reproposition as universal
Formulate a universal proposition using the new name: 'Every J is such-and-such' for categoricals, or 'Whenever the state occurs, the consequent follows' for conditionals. This eliminates the partiality.
The resulting proposition is comprehensive (muḥīṭa) and no longer misleading.
5
Verify suitability for demonstration
Check that the new universal proposition is suitable for use in burhān (scientific demonstration). Suhrawardi argues that sciences only deal with universal matters, not particulars.
Particulars (shawākhis) are not sought in sciences because there is no demonstration of them.

Commentary
This passage explains Suhrawardi's method for eliminating indefinite and particular propositions in the category of quantity. Suhrawardi's Elimination of Negative Propositions via 'Udul
Suhrawardi converts negatives to affirmative privatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to transform a negative proposition into an affirmative privative proposition using Suhrawardi's method and identify whether the copula is negated or the predicate is modified.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi eliminates negatives by moving negation after the copula, making it part of the predicate.

Suhrawardi proposes a radical simplification: all propositions can be affirmative universal. His trick is to move negation from before the copula (where it negates the connection) to after it (where it modifies the predicate). This produces an affirmative privative proposition. Understanding this helps you see how logical form can be manipulated without changing meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the negative proposition
Find a proposition with negation before the copula, e.g., 'Zayd laysa huwa kātiban' (Zayd is not a writer). The negation 'laysa' negates the copula 'huwa'.
2
Move negation after the copula
Take the negation and place it after the copula, attaching it to the predicate. The copula becomes affirmative. The new form is 'Zayd huwa lā kātib' (Zayd is not-a-writer).
The content remains the same: both assert Zayd is not a writer.
3
Recognize the new proposition type
The resulting proposition is an affirmative privative (mūjaba ma'dūla). The predicate is privative ('lā kātib'). The copula is affirmative, so it counts as affirmative in Suhrawardi's system.
Suhrawardi's goal is to have only affirmative propositions; this step completes the conversion.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi eliminates modal distinctions by turning all propositions into necessary affirmatives with modality in the predicate.

Suhrawardi proposes a radical simplification of modal logic: instead of treating modality as a separate element, he absorbs it into the predicate. This lesson unpacks his reasoning: because the modal status itself (e.g., the possibility of a possible thing) is necessary, every proposition can become a necessary affirmative. Understanding this move is crucial for grasping debates about modality in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard view of modality
In standard Aristotelian logic, a modal proposition like 'Zayd is possibly a writer' separates the modality (possibility) from the predicate (writer). Suhrawardi challenges this separation.
2

He notices that the modality itself has a necessary character: the possibility of the possible is necessary, the impossibility of the impossible is necessary, and the necessity of the necessary is necessary. This means all modal states are rooted in necessity.
This is the crux of his argument.

Since the modality is necessary, it can be folded into the predicate. For example, 'Zayd is a possibly-writer' becomes a predicate that includes possibility. The proposition then asserts that Zayd has the property of 'possibly-writer'.
Now the modality is part of the predicate term.

Because the modality itself is necessary, the whole proposition becomes necessary. Every proposition, regardless of its original modality, is reduced to a necessary affirmative (al-batāta).
Suhrawardi calls this type 'al-batāta' (the categorical/definitive).

Suhrawardi's Reduction of Negative Propositions
Author explains Suhrawardi's method of eliminating negative propositions by making negation part of the predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to transform a negative proposition into an affirmative modificatory proposition following Suhrawardi's method.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi eliminates negative propositions by absorbing negation into the predicate.

This lesson examines a bold logical innovation by Shihab al-Din al-Suhrawardi. He sought to simplify propositional logic by removing negative propositions entirely. Instead, he converted every negative statement into an affirmative statement with a negated term (ma'dula). This not only reduces the number of proposition types but also aligns with his view that scientific inquiry deals only with universal affirmations. Understanding this transformation is crucial for grasping later developments in Islamic logic that debated the necessity of negative propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the negative proposition
Look for a proposition where the negation directly negates the copula, e.g., 'Zaid is not a writer'. In Arabic, the negation precedes the copula. This is what Suhrawardi calls a 'qadiyya saliba'.
2
Locate the copula
The copula (rabita) is the linking verb 'is' (or 'huwa' in Arabic). In a negative proposition, the negation cuts off the relation between subject and predicate.
3
Attach the negation to the predicate
Move the negation element to become part of the predicate, after the copula. For example, change 'Zaid is not a writer' to 'Zaid is non-writer'. The copula remains affirmative, and the negated term becomes a single concept.
4
Identify the resulting proposition type
The result is an 'affirmative modificatory' (mujaba ma'dula) proposition. It is still affirmative in form, but the predicate is negated.
5
Apply Suhrawardi's universalization
According to Suhrawardi, in scientific contexts, propositions should be universal (comprehensive). So the final form is a universal affirmative with a modificatory predicate, e.g., 'Every human is non-stone'.

This passage presents Suhrawardi's innovative approach to simplifying the theory of propositions. Necessary Absolute Propositions in Suhrawardi's Logic
Definition of ضرورية بتاتة and Suhrawardi's view on science.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a necessary absolute proposition (ضرورية بتاتة) and explain Suhrawardi's reason for using only such propositions in the sciences.

Core Idea
All modal propositions reduce to necessary absolutes by folding modality into the predicate.

This lesson introduces a key concept in Suhrawardi's logic: the 'necessary absolute' proposition. Suhrawardi redefines modal statements so that the modality (possibility, necessity, impossibility) becomes part of the predicate, making the entire judgment necessary. He then argues that only these reduced propositions can be used in scientific reasoning, because certainty requires necessity. The passage clarifies how temporal and permanent modalities are handled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three initial modal propositions
The author gives three examples: possible (may be a writer), necessary (must be an animal), impossible (cannot be a stone). Note the modality in each.
These are standard modal propositions.
2
Understand Suhrawardi's reduction
Suhrawardi takes each modality and makes it part of the predicate. For example, 'Man is possibly a writer' becomes 'Man is necessarily [possibly a writer]' – the intellectual judgment is necessary.
The modality is no longer external; it becomes an attribute of the predicate.
3
Recognize why only absolutes are used in sciences
Suhrawardi asserts that we can only make decisive judgments when we know something is necessarily so. Even temporal occurrences (e.g., breathing) are formulated as necessary statements: 'Man necessarily breathes at some time' is always true.
This distinguishes necessary possibility from contingent occurrence.
4
Apply the concept to a new example
Compare writing (necessarily possible but not necessarily occurring) with breathing (necessarily occurring at some time). Both reduce to necessary absolutes, but one is a necessary absolute of possibility, the other of temporal occurrence.
This shows the flexibility of the reduction.

Reducing Necessary Propositions: Modality in Predicate
Suhrawardi's first methodological point on reducing propositions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decide when to include modality in the predicate when reducing a proposition to the necessary absolute.

Core Idea
In necessary propositions, context conveys necessity; adding modality in predicate is redundant.

When reducing propositions to the necessary absolute, Suhrawardi warns against over-adding modality. In propositions that are inherently necessary, like 'Every human is an animal', the necessity is already understood from the subject-predicate relationship. Repeating 'necessarily' in the predicate is unnecessary. This lesson unpacks that principle and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
Determine whether the given proposition is necessary (e.g., 'Every human is an animal') or non-necessary (possible or contingent). This classification decides the rule.
Necessary propositions have a subject that entails the predicate essentially.
2
Check for contextual necessity
In necessary propositions, the necessity is inherent; the context (e.g., the meaning of 'human' and 'animal') indicates necessity without an explicit modal word.
Suhrawardi calls this 'جهة الربط' (modality of connection) sufficing.
3
Decide on modality in predicate
If the proposition is necessary, do not add 'necessarily' or 'must' in the predicate. Instead, you may use 'absolutely' (بتة) without repeating modality. For non-necessary propositions, include the modality explicitly.
Example: 'Every human absolutely is an animal' (بتة) is acceptable for necessary ones.

Suhrawardi's Modal Conversion Rules
Suhrawardi's conversion rules for modal propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Suhrawardi's rules for converting necessary affirmatives and universal negative necessary propositions, and contrast them with the Peripatetic view.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi preserves modality in conversion, making every converse necessary and particular for affirmatives.

Suhrawardi agrees with standard simple conversion conditions but breaks with the Peripatetics on modal conversion. He insists that the exact modality of a necessary affirmative is preserved in its converse, which must be a particular necessary affirmative. For universal negatives, he provides a reductio to show the converse is also universal negative necessary. Understanding these rules is essential for evaluating modal arguments in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Recall standard simple conversion
Simple conversion swaps subject and predicate while preserving truth and quality. A condition: no term may become distributed in the converse that was undistributed in the original. Apply this to 'Every human is an animal' – why does 'Every animal is a human' violate the condition?
Distribution: 'animal' is undistributed in the original (predicate of affirmative) but distributed in the converse (subject of universal).
2
Identify Suhrawardi's thesis on necessary affirmatives
Suhrawardi claims that the conversion of any necessary affirmative (with modality of possibility, necessity, or impossibility) is also a necessary affirmative, but specifically a particular one. For example, 'Necessarily every human possibly is an animal' converts to 'Necessarily some thing that possibly is an animal is a human'.
The modality (al-jiha) is transferred unchanged. The converse is always particular (some).
3
Compare with the Peripatetic objection
The Peripatetics give a counterexample: 'Every writer is a human' is necessary, but its universal converse 'Every human is a writer' is not necessary, only possible. Suhrawardi responds that the correct converse is particular: 'Necessarily some human is a writer'. Identify why Suhrawardi insists on particularity.
The Peripatetics assumed universal conversion; Suhrawardi uses particular to avoid distribution issues and preserve necessity.
4
Analyze Suhrawardi's reductio for universal negative necessary conversion
For 'Necessarily no human is a stone', Suhrawardi argues the converse is 'Necessarily no stone is a human'. He proves this by assuming the converse false: its contradictory 'Some stone is a human' would be true. After conversion, this yields 'Some human is a stone', which contradicts the original universal negative. Hence the converse must be true.
The reductio relies on the rule that from 'not no A is B' follows 'some A is B', and conversion of that particular yields a contradiction.

Suhrawardi's Transformation of Negatives and Particulars
Author details Suhrawardi's technique to transform negative and particular propositions into universal affirmatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Suhrawardi's technique of eliminating negative and particular propositions by converting them into affirmative universal premises.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi simplifies syllogisms by rephrasing negatives as affirmative predicates and particulars as universals via dummy names.

In standard Aristotelian logic, negative and particular propositions are irreducible. Suhrawardi (d. 1191) proposed a radical simplification: transform every proposition into an affirmative universal. This lesson explains his two methods—making negation part of the predicate and using supposition (iftirad) for particulars—so you can apply them to any logical argument.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
Traditional syllogistics requires handling both negative and particular premises, complicating rules. Suhrawardi aims for only affirmative universal premises to avoid confusion.
2
Technique 1: Negative to affirmative
For a negative proposition like 'No S is P', move the negation into the predicate: 'Every S is non-P'. This preserves logical meaning but makes it affirmative.
Example: 'No intoxicant is permissible' becomes 'Every intoxicant is non-permissible'.
3
Technique 2: Particular to universal via supposition
For a particular like 'Some S is P', assign a dummy name (e.g., D) to the S that is P. Then assert 'Every D is S' and 'Every D is P', which are universal. The original conclusion can be deduced from these universal premises.
Suhrawardi's example: 'Some animal is rational' becomes 'Every D is rational' where D names that animal.
4
Verify the transformation
Check that the new universal premises yield the same conclusion as the original particular. For instance, from 'Every D is rational' and 'Every rational is laughing', we get 'Every D is laughing', equivalent to 'Some animal is laughing' since D is that animal.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider 'Some intoxicant is permissible'. Assign a name Z to that intoxicant. Then 'Every Z is an intoxicant' and 'Every Z is permissible'. Combine with other universal premises to deduce rulings.
This method forces all premises to be universal, strengthening certainty.
