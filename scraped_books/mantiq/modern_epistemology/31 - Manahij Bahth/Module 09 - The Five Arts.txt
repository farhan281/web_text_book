Formal vs Material Logic: Two Dimensions of Aristotelian Logic
Author explains the distinction between formal and material logic and the move to pure formalism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between formal and material logic and identify the five arts that constitute the material dimension.

Core Idea
Aristotle's logic had both form and matter; later logicians stripped matter away.

When we study classical logic, we often treat it as purely formal — rules that work regardless of content. But Aristotle himself did not separate logic from its subject matter entirely. In this lesson, we examine the distinction between formal logic (concerned with structure) and material logic (concerned with premises) and see how this distinction was later abandoned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects of logic in Aristotle's system
Notice that the passage states Aristotle considered logic 'both formal and material at the same time.' This means he did not separate the validity of an argument from the truth of its premises. Find the sentence that describes this duality.
2
Understand al-Banawi's terms: applied vs taught logic
Al-Banawi used 'applied logic' (al-mantiq al-musta'mal) for logic in practice (including material aspects) and 'taught logic' (al-mantiq al-mu'allim) for the theoretical study of form. Why do you think a distinction was needed?
3
List the five books (arts) that constitute the material topics
The passage mentions the 'five books' — Burhan (Demonstration), Jadal (Dialectic), Khataba (Rhetoric), Shi'r (Poetry), and Safsata (Sophistics). These are the material applications of logic. Write them down and think about how each deals with different kinds of premises.
4
Reflect on the difference between formal and material
Formal logic studies the structure of arguments independent of content. Material logic evaluates premises based on their truth and the type of certainty they yield. Can you see why some later logicians might try to drop the material side?

Connected to this issue is a second serious issue: that Aristotle did not limit logic to the formal but also addressed material topics. So he considered logic as both formal and material at the same time. This is what al-Banawi calls 'applied logic' (al-mantiq al-musta'mal) and 'taught logic' (al-mantiq al-mu'allim). But the Stoics reduced logic to pure formality, and many Muslims followed this latter trend. This is what Ibn Khaldun echoes in his important passage in the Muqaddimah, stating that the later scholars changed the reform of logic, and among this change is 'their discussion of the syllogism in terms of its production of demands in general, not according to its matter, and they omitted the consideration of matter – which are the five books: Demonstration (Burhan), Dialectic (Jadal), Rhetoric (Khataba), Poetry (Shi'r), and Sophistics (Safsata).' We see this in all the commentaries on Isagoge and in the hashya of al-'Attar on al-Khubaysi. Indeed, al-Sawi, despite his strong influence from Ibn Sina, mentions in his book that he will limit himself to presenting the two ways of acquiring genuine conception and assent, namely definition (hadd) and demonstration (burhan), and that he will not research dialectic, rhetoric, and poetry because they do not yield pure certainty.

Commentary
This passage discusses the shift from a logic that includes both formal and material aspects (as in Aristotle) to a purely formal logic. The key reference is al-Banawi's distinction between 'applied logic' and 'taught logic'. The Stoics advocated for pure formality, and later Muslim logicians followed suit. Evidence is taken from Ibn Khaldun, who notes that later scholars discussed syllogisms only in general terms, ignoring the material aspects contained in the five arts (Demonstration, Dialectic, Rhetoric, Poetry, Sophistics). Additionally, al-Sawi's work explicitly restricts logical study to definition and demonstration, excluding the other arts as they do not yield certainty.

The Shift to Pure Formalism: Stoic Influence and Muslim Responses
Author explains the distinction between formal and material logic and the move to pure formalism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the historical shift from material-formal logic to pure formalism and evaluate its impact on Islamic logical writings.

Core Idea
The Stoics pushed logic to pure form; Muslim logicians like al-Sawi narrowed the field.

The Aristotelian tradition treated logic as both a tool for correct reasoning and a study of the types of premises. The Stoic school, however, reduced logic to a purely formal system. This lesson explores how that shift entered Islamic logic and how figures like Ibn Khaldun and al-Sawi responded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Stoic reduction of logic to pure formality
The passage says 'the Stoics reduced logic to pure formality.' This means they eliminated consideration of the truth or material of premises. Find where this is stated.
2
Find Muslim logicians who followed the Stoic trend
The passage states 'many Muslims followed this latter trend.' Look for the evidence: Ibn Khaldun's description of later scholars omitting the five books, and al-Sawi's restriction to definition and demonstration.
3
Analyse Ibn Khaldun's critique as a commentary on this shift
Ibn Khaldun criticises the later logicians for only discussing the syllogism in general terms and ignoring the material dimension. This is a critique of pure formalism. How does his description match the Stoic shift?
4
Examine al-Sawi's position as an example of restriction
Al-Sawi, despite being influenced by Ibn Sina, chose to exclude dialectic, rhetoric, and poetry because they do not yield certainty. What does this tell us about the epistemological priorities of later logicians?

The Shift from Material to Formal Logic
Author describes the shift from material to formal logic by omitting the five arts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the key difference between formal and material logic in the Islamic tradition and identify the five arts that were omitted.

Core Idea
Later logicians narrowed logic to only certain knowledge, dropping persuasive arts.

In this passage, the author highlights a pivotal development in the history of Islamic logic: the transition from a comprehensive discipline that examined both the form and the matter of arguments to a purely formal science. Understanding this shift is crucial for recognizing why later texts focus exclusively on syllogistic structure and demonstrative reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of logic
The passage distinguishes between discussing the syllogism 'in terms of its producing results in general' (form) and 'according to its matter' (content). Find the Arabic phrase that marks this distinction.
The author says they spoke about qiyās 'without regard to its matter' (لا بحسب مادته).
2
List the five arts constituting material logic
The passage enumerates five books that were omitted: Burhān (Demonstration), Jadal (Dialectic), Khaṭāba (Rhetoric), Shi‘r (Poetry), and Safsata (Sophistic). These represent the material content of syllogisms.
These correspond to the five arts in Aristotle's Organon.
3
Explain the reason for omitting the material arts
According to al-Sāwī, only ḥadd (definition) and burhān (demonstration) yield pure certainty (yaqīn maḥḍ). The other arts do not produce certain knowledge, so they were excluded from formal logic.
This shift reflects a narrower conception of logic as solely concerned with apodictic reasoning.

Commentary
This passage explains a major shift in the history of Islamic logic: the transition from a comprehensive logic that included both form (sūra) and matter (māda) to a purely formal logic. The author notes that later logicians discussed the syllogism (qiyās) only in terms of its formal structure—how it yields conclusions regardless of content—and eliminated the investigation of its matter. The material part originally consisted of the five arts (al-kutub al-khamsa): Demonstration (Burhān), Dialectic (Jadal), Rhetoric (Khaṭāba), Poetry (Shi‘r), and Sophistic (Safsata). The author points to commentaries on Porphyry’s Isagoge and al-‘Attār’s gloss on al-Khabīsī as examples of this trend. Even al-Sāwī, despite his deep debt to Ibn Sīnā, explicitly states in his al-Baṣā’ir al-Naṣīriyya that he will limit himself to the two paths for acquiring genuine conception (taṣawwur) and assent (taṣdīq)—definition (ḥadd) and demonstration (burhān)—and will not discuss Dialectic, Rhetoric, and Poetry because they do not yield pure certainty (yaqīn maḥḍ). Thus, the shift is from a logic that included probabilistic and persuasive arguments to one focused only on apodictic reasoning.
