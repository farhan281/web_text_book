The Nature of Logic: Aristotle vs. Stoics
First general issue: logic as part or introduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main positions on the nature of logic (Aristotelian, Stoic, and Alexandrian) and explain how they relate to the role of logic in Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Logic is both the tool and a part of philosophy — a synthetic solution to an ancient dispute.

The very nature of logic was debated: Is it a mere instrument for philosophy, or a genuine part of it? This question, inherited from Aristotle and the Stoics, was taken up by Islamic logicians and had profound implications for how logic was taught and valued in the Islamic curriculum. In this lesson, you will untangle the three key positions and see how they were reconciled.

Guided Path
1
Locate Aristotle's implicit position
The passage states that historians infer from Aristotle's division of theoretical sciences that he considered logic not a part of philosophy but only an introduction to it. Identify this as the 'tool' view.
Aristotle did not explicitly state this position.
2
Identify the Stoic position
The Stoics considered logic a part of wisdom (ḥikma), dividing wisdom into natural science, dialectic (logic), and ethics. Recognize this as the 'part' view.
Dialectic here is synonymous with logic.
3
Describe the Alexandrian reconciliation
The Alexandrian commentators synthesized both views: they considered logic both an introduction to philosophy and a part of it at the same time. This is the 'both' view.
This synthetic approach became influential in Islamic logic.
4
Connect to Islamic sciences
Reflect on how this debate affects whether logic is studied as a prerequisite tool (e.g., for kalam or fiqh) or as a discipline in its own right.
Islamic scholars like al-Khwarizmi and al-Tahanawi later catalogued various positions.

And the first of the general issues that entered into the logic of the Islamic commentators and were not in Aristotle's heritage, and concerning which the Islamic Peripatetics and Stoics differed, is the investigation into the nature of logic: Is it a part of philosophy or an introduction preceding it? Aristotle did not give a clear idea about these issues, but historians have observed that there is no place for logic in his division of theoretical sciences. Hence they concluded that for Aristotle, logic is not a part of philosophy but only an introduction to it. As for the Stoics, they considered logic a part of wisdom. For wisdom in their view is divided into natural science, dialectic, and ethics, and dialectic is logic. The Alexandrian commentators did not stand bewildered before these two conflicting trends; rather they quickly reconciled them, in accordance with their synthetic method. They considered logic both an introduction to philosophy and a part of it at the same time. Al-Khwarizmi (d. 387 AH/977 CE) in his book Mafatih al-'Ulum depicts this dispute: 'Philosophy is derived from a Greek word "philosophia", meaning love of wisdom. When it was Arabized, it became "faylasuf", then philosophy was derived from it. The meaning of philosophy is knowledge of the realities of things and acting in accordance with what is most proper. It is divided into two parts: the theoretical part and the practical part. Some made logic a third part beyond these two; some made it a part of the theoretical sciences; some made it a tool for philosophy; and some made it both a part of and a tool for it.' Al-Tahanawi mentions this dispute in a form close to al-Khwarizmi, saying: 'Know that they differed on whether logic is a science or not. So whoever said it is not a science, then it is not wisdom in his view, since wisdom is science. And whoever said it is a science, they differed on whether it is from wisdom or not. And those who said it is from wisdom can differ on whether it is entirely from theoretical wisdom or not. But some of it is from theoretical and some from practical, since mental existence may be within our power and choice and may not be so. And those who said it is from theoretical wisdom can differ on whether it is one of its three divisions or another division. For whoever includes in its definition the restriction to concrete entities (al-a'yan), as in the definitions mentioned, does not count it as wisdom because...'

Islamic Classification of Logic: From Al-Khwarizmi to Al-Tahanawi
Islamic scholars' classifications of logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the different Islamic positions on whether logic is part of theoretical/practical philosophy or a tool, as reported by al-Khwarizmi and al-Tahanawi, and explain the underlying criteria.

Core Idea
Islamic logicians catalogued at least five distinct views on logic's disciplinary status, reflecting deep engagement with its nature.

Al-Khwarizmi and al-Tahanawi provide a snapshot of the diverse opinions among Islamic scholars on the classification of logic. From viewing it as a third part of philosophy to a mere tool, these positions reveal how the earlier debate was inherited and refined. In this lesson, you will parse these classifications and understand the criteria used to assign logic to a category.

Guided Path
1
Extract al-Khwarizmi's list of positions
From the quoted passage, identify the five views al-Khwarizmi attributes to philosophers: logic as a third part, as part of theoretical sciences, as a tool, as both part and tool, and implicitly as a part of practical philosophy.
The list is given in the middle of the quotation.
2
Extract al-Tahanawi's classification
Al-Tahanawi discusses whether logic is a science (ʿilm), and if so, whether it is part of wisdom (ḥikma). If part of wisdom, is it entirely theoretical, partly practical, or one of the three divisions? Identify these sub-questions.
Al-Tahanawi's text ends mid-sentence but the structure is clear.
3
Identify the underlying criteria
The key criterion is whether logic deals with mental existence (al-mawjud al-dhihni) and whether that existence is within our power. This determines if it is part of practical or theoretical wisdom.
Al-Tahanawi mentions 'mental existence may be within our power and choice and may not be so.'
4
Relate to Islamic sciences
Consider how these classifications affect the place of logic in the curriculum. For example, if logic is a tool, it is taught before other sciences; if it is a part, it is studied alongside them.
The passage directly informs the organization of knowledge in Islamic education.

Author surveys different opinions on whether logic is part of philosophy, a tool, or both.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three main positions on whether logic is part of philosophy, a tool, or both, and identify which scholars held each view.

Core Idea
Islamic philosophers debated whether logic is part of philosophy, a tool, or both — and many held contradictory positions.

This passage from Manahij al-Bahth surveys the historical debate over the nature of logic. The author notes that historians accurately portrayed three opinions, but even major figures like al-Farabi and Ibn Sina did not settle on a single view. You will learn to recognize these positions and their implications for how logic is taught and used.

Note what each scholar says: Tahaanawi omits the third view, Khwarizmi includes it; al-Farabi counts it as part in some works and as tool in others; Ikhwan al-Safa list it as a part but also as a tool; Ibn Sina shows confusion.
3

In this article, therefore, there is a direction [that considers] logic as a tool for philosophy and a power by which one can think correctly and avoid erroneous thinking, and this direction contradicts the first direction which considered logic a part of the parts of philosophy. Al-Farabi had a deep influence on the Islamic philosophical school after him in this respect – so much so that we see his confused idea about the nature of logic in the books of those who came after him. For the Ikhwan al-Safa divide the philosophical sciences into four types: 'First is mathematics, second is logic, third is natural sciences, and fourth is divine sciences.' And they say elsewhere: 'As for the philosophical sciences, they are four: among them mathematics, among them logic, among them natural sciences.' And despite the Ikhwan al-Safa counting logic as a part of philosophy, they devote a chapter to logic titled 'Chapter on that logic is the tool of the philosopher' in which it says: 'Know that logic is the balance of philosophy, and it has been said that it is the tool of the philosopher, because since philosophy is the noblest of human crafts after prophecy, it becomes obligatory that the balance of philosophy be the most correct of balances and the tool of the philosopher the noblest of tools.' As for Ibn Sina, we see in his writings also severe confusion. While he mentions in one of his epistles: 'The science that is a tool for man leading to the acquisition of theoretical and practical wisdom and protecting against oversight and error in research and transmission...' he mentions after that that logic is part of wisdom, and in another book he mentions that the divisions of theoretical sciences are four: 'natural science, mathematical science, divine science, and universal science.' Then Ibn Sina combines the two views in al-Shifa', considering logic a preface to philosophy and a part of it at the same time. This last view is adopted by all the Peripatetic commentators. But it is wrong to say that the Stoic idea was neglected or that it was merely presented by some historians of science as one of the opinions. Rather, there are several logical books written according to the Stoic view on this issue. And he relied on a passage from Ibn Khaldun in which he dates logic among the later scholars. He says: 'The later scholars did not look at logic as a tool for the sciences, but considered it as a craft in itself, and the first to speak about it in this manner was Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (d. 606 AH) and after him, Afdal al-Din al-Khunji' (died Wednesday, fifth of Ramadan, year 646). Unfortunately, we do not have any of the logical works of these thinkers to clarify the matter further.

Commentary
This passage presents the historical debate on the nature of logic: whether it is a tool (āla) for philosophy or a part (qism) of philosophy. The author begins by contrasting two directions: one that views logic as a tool and power for correct thinking, and another that considers it a part of philosophy. Al-Farabi's influence is noted; his unclear position caused confusion among later scholars. The Ikhwan al-Safa are cited as an example of those who classify logic as one of the four philosophical sciences, yet they also call logic the 'tool of the philosopher' in a separate chapter, showing an internal tension. Ibn Sina's writings exhibit similar confusion: in one epistle he calls logic a tool for wisdom, but elsewhere he includes it among the sciences, and in al-Shifa' he compromises by making logic both a preface and a part of philosophy – a view adopted by later Peripatetics. The author then discusses the Stoic view, which treated logic as a distinct craft, not merely a tool, and notes that later Muslim logicians like al-Razi and al-Khunji followed this approach, as recorded by Ibn Khaldun. The author laments the loss of their works, which would clarify the matter. The passage thus maps the spectrum of opinions on the status of logic in the Islamic philosophical tradition.

Ibn Sina's Synthesis: Both Preface and Part
Ibn Sina's combined view on logic

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Sina's compromise view that logic is both a preface to and a part of philosophy.

Core Idea
Ibn Sina's compromise made logic a propaedeutic to philosophy while also integral to it.

Ibn Sina's writings show apparent confusion: he calls logic a tool in one epistle but includes it as a science in his classification. In al-Shifa', he reconciles both by making logic a preface and a part simultaneously, a view that later Peripatetics adopted. This lesson unpacks his synthesis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting statements
Find Ibn Sina's statement from his epistle calling logic a 'tool for man' and his classification of theoretical sciences into four, including a 'universal science' that is logic.
2
Locate the solution in al-Shifa'
Read the sentence: 'Then Ibn Sina combines the two views in al-Shifa', considering logic a preface to philosophy and a part of it at the same time.'
3
Understand 'preface to philosophy'
Explain that a preface (muqaddima) means logic is a necessary prerequisite discipline that one must master before engaging in philosophy.
4
Recognize the legacy
Note that this synthetic view was adopted by all Peripatetic commentators and became the standard position in the Islamic philosophical tradition.

Later Scholastics: Logic as an Independent Craft
Later scholars and logic as an independent craft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe how later scholars like al-Razi and al-Khunji treated logic as a distinct craft rather than a tool.

Core Idea
Later logicians transformed logic from a philosopher's tool into an independent art.

According to Ibn Khaldun, later scholars (mutā'akhkhirūn) such as Fakhr al-Din al-Razi and Afdal al-Din al-Khunji did not consider logic merely as a tool for sciences but as a craft in itself. This represents a significant shift in the Islamic logical tradition, aligning with the earlier Stoic view.

3
Contrast with the tool view
Explain how viewing logic as a 'craft in itself' differs from seeing it as a mere tool for philosophy.
4
Consider the historical context
Reflect on why this shift might have occurred – e.g., the professionalization of logic, influence of Stoic thought, or the development of specialized logical manuals.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A word's logical complexity depends on whether its parts still carry meaning independently.

Understand the criterion

Tanqih al-Manat: Deleting and Determining the Cause
Definition of tanqih al-manat and its two operations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two operations of Tanqih al-Manat (deletion and determination) and explain how they refine the identification of the 'illa in qiyas.

Core Idea
Tanqih al-Manat is the juristic refinement that separates causal wheat from descriptive chaff.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone of Islamic legal reasoning: Tanqih al-Manat, the method by which a mujtahid isolates the true cause ('illa) behind a ruling. The passage cites two major usuli authorities—al-Zarkashi and al-Subki—to define the term and its two-step process: first deletion of irrelevant attributes, then determination of the cause. Understanding this process is essential for anyone wishing to apply qiyas with precision.

Guided Path
1
Unpack the linguistic meaning
Tanqih means refinement; al-manat originally means place of attachment, then metaphorically the cause. This background helps you see the purpose: to refine the attachment point of the ruling.
2
Identify al-Subki's technical definition
Al-Subki defines it as: when a text indicates causation by a description, either the particularity is dropped to take a more general meaning, or multiple descriptions exist and some are deleted by ijtihad. The summary is 'ijtihad fi al-hazf wa al-ta'yin'.
3
Distinguish the two operations
Deletion (hazf) removes attributes unsuitable for causality; determination (ta'yin) pinpoints the actual cause among the remaining. Both are necessary for a valid tanqih.
4
Connect to qiyas practice
In analogical reasoning, the jurist first deletes irrelevant attributes (e.g., color, shape) and then determines the operative cause (e.g., intoxication). This ensures the cause is sound.

Reducing Logic to Implication: Ibn Taymiyya's Core Argument
Ibn Taymiyya reduces logic to necessary implication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the relation of malzum and lazim in any argument and explain how it subsumes the conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
All logical inference is just necessary entailment—no need for Greek rules.

Ibn Taymiyya deconstructs the foundational claim of Aristotelian logic by showing that every valid argument is based on necessary implication (luzum). He argues that the logicians' conditional syllogism is merely one expression of this universal relation, not a distinct form. This lesson walks you through his reduction and its implications for the authority of Greek logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Locate Ibn Taymiyya's statement that every piece of evidence (dalil) is a necessary condition (malzum) for its implication (lazim). Understand that he is asserting a universal relation that holds for all correct reasoning.
This is the starting point of his critique: the fundamental logical unit is entailment, not syllogistic form.
2
Extract the four inference patterns
From the passage, note the four ways luzum is used: (1) from existence of malzum to existence of lazim, (2) from non-existence of lazim to non-existence of malzum, (3) from affirmation of malzum to affirmation of lazim, (4) from negation of lazim to negation of malzum. These cover all cases.
These correspond to affirming the antecedent and denying the consequent in conditional logic.
3
Compare with the conditional syllogism
Ibn Taymiyya explicitly says that the conjunctive conditional (sharti muttasil) is nothing but the relation of malzum and lazim. See how he reduces it: 'Whether expressed in conditional form or categorical form, the meaning is the same.'
He is denying that the logical form adds anything to the underlying entailment.
4
Apply to a sample argument
Take a simple Islamic argument: 'The world is created (malzum), therefore it has a Creator (lazim).' Check that the relation is necessary: if the world is created, a Creator must exist. This is the same logical relation used in complex syllogisms.
This demonstrates that the logic is universal and not Greek-specific.

Commentary

Definition of Evidence (Dalil) and Its Implication
Author defines evidence as that which implies its conclusion, emphasizing that form is irrelevant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of evidence (dalil) as entailment and explain why form is irrelevant to logical validity.

Core Idea
Evidence is defined by its logical entailment of the conclusion, not by its syllogistic form.

This passage presents the foundational definition of evidence according to Ibn Taymiyya, which the author expands upon. It strips away the formal constraints of Aristotelian syllogisms and focuses solely on the entailment relation between evidence and conclusion. Understanding this definition is crucial because it shifts the criterion of validity from form to meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and definiens
Find the term being defined (al-dalil) and its definition: 'كل ما يستدل به على غيره فإنه مستلزم له' (everything used as evidence for something else entails it). The definiendum is 'evidence', the definiens is 'that which entails its conclusion'.
2
Understand the entailment structure
Recognize the two implications: (1) if evidence is true, the conclusion must be true; (2) if conclusion is false, the evidence must be false (modus tollens). This is the core logical relation.
3
Apply the entailment to practical reasoning
Note the two consequences: if we know a proposition is false, we know no sound evidence supports it; if we know evidence is sound, we know the conclusion is true. This is the practical use of the definition.
4
Analyze the claim about form irrelevance
The author asserts that the specific syllogistic form (categorical, conditional, etc.) does not affect validity. Evidence can be restructured into any form as long as the entailment holds. Test this with examples: a categorical syllogism can be rewritten as a conditional syllogism preserving entailment.
This challenges the formalist view that only certain forms yield valid arguments.

He gives Quranic examples of this: God informed of those He caused to die then resurrected, or of the one struck with the cow, or of the Companions of the Cave. Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's critical analysis of the logicians' concept of possibility, distinguishing between mental possibility (al-imkan al-dhihni) and external possibility (al-imkan al-khariji). The author begins by noting that logicians, following the Aristotelian view, posit an abstract essence (mahiyya mujarrada) considered in itself, stripped of all positive and negative determinations, and they assume such essences have real existence externally. Ibn Taymiyya rejects this as a grave error, equating it with Platonic idealism and Pythagorean number theory. He argues that mental conception does not guarantee external reality; the mind can imagine impossibilities, and many mental suppositions never correspond to external existence. He then defines mental possibility as the mind's lack of knowledge of impossibility, not positive knowledge of possibility—a thing may be impossible externally yet deemed possible in the mind. External possibility, conversely, is grounded in actual knowledge of a thing's existence, or the existence of its like, or something even more remote in existence. This principle, Ibn Taymiyya claims, is derived from the Qur'anic method of proving the resurrection (al-ma'ad). The Qur'an does not rely on abstract mental possibility; it cites concrete historical events—like the resurrection of the dead in the story of the cow, the Companions of the Cave, and the analogy of first creation. Thus, external possibility is known through actual existence, not mere absence of contradiction. Ibn Taymiyya then critiques al-Amidi and some Ash'ari theologians who use the conditional 'If we assume this, no impossibility follows' to prove possibility. Ibn Taymiyya argues such a conditional is not self-evident nor proven, and he misunderstands their position that non-impossibility is equivalent to external possibility. Finally, he extends his critique to Ibn Sina and al-Razi on similar grounds. The passage demonstrates Ibn Taymiyya's insistence on grounding logical possibility in actual external reality, especially for theological proofs.

External Possibility: The Quranic Method of Proving Resurrection
Ibn Taymiyya defines external possibility and illustrates it with Quranic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define external possibility according to Ibn Taymiyya and identify how the Quran uses historical examples to prove the possibility of the resurrection.

Core Idea
External possibility is known through actual existence—of the thing, its like, or something more remote.

Having dismissed mental possibility, Ibn Taymiyya presents his positive alternative: external possibility. He argues that the Quran does not rely on abstract reasoning but on concrete historical events and analogies from creation. In this lesson, we explore how external possibility is defined and applied to the resurrection.

Guided Path
1
Define external possibility
Locate the passage where Ibn Taymiyya says: 'External possibility is knowledge of the possibility of a thing in the external.' Then note the three sources: existence of the thing itself, its like, or something more remote in existence.
The third source involves a qiyas awlawi (more fitting analogy).
2
Understand the Quranic method
Find the examples Ibn Taymiyya gives from the Quran: the resurrection of the dead in the story of the cow, the Companions of the Cave, and the first creation. Note how these are not mere mental possibilities but actual events.
He also mentions analogies like 're-creation is easier than the first creation' (al-Ankabut 29:19) and the creation of heavens and earth (Ghafir 40:57).
3
Contrast with mental possibility
How does this method differ from the logicians' approach? Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that external possibility is known by existence, not by mere absence of contradiction.
This grounds theology in empirical and historical evidence.

And Ibn Taymiyya holds that one should resort here to a proof from the Law, so he transmits from the Lawgiver who spoke these names: it has been established from the Prophet ﷺ that he said: 'Every intoxicant is wine', and what is not intoxicant is not wine by consensus, so it is established that wine is the intoxicant. Likewise for backbiting. Hence, a proof is necessary in definition, whether it is a definition of a name or a definition according to reality — not what the Greek logicians hold, that definition conveys the conception of the essence in the mind of the listener and that occurs merely by the definer's statement without any proof at all. And their division of necessary attributes of the described into three types: essential entering into the essence, external necessary to the essence without its existence, and external necessary to its existence — all these are false, as previously shown, if by essence they mean external existents. But if by essence they mean what is conceived in the mind, then the measure of that essence and its description follows the conception of the conceiver. So if he wishes to conceive the essence of man in the mind, the conception differs according to the conceiver: sometimes he conceives the essence of man as a sensitive, growing, voluntarily moving, rational body; sometimes he conceives it as a rational animal; sometimes as a laughing animal or merely laughing. All this may be correct in itself, but it yields no scientific benefit and never reaches objective truth; it only goes back to the conception of what the speaker intends, whether true or false. Therefore, Muslim theoreticians did not care about the logical rules of definition; in their view, they are a mechanical instrument that achieves nothing. For definition for them is by a single accompanying description, and a single accompanying description is sufficient, so they do not mention genera, differentiae, or common accidents, and they do not use them.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the Greek logical theory of definition (hadd). The argument begins by clarifying the relationship between concept (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). Ibn Taymiyya affirms that a complete concept entails assent, but an incomplete concept requires proof. He then asserts that Muslim theologians were correct: singular concepts are not known by definition alone. Definition, he argues, is actually a type of assent and therefore depends on proof, just like any other assent. He illustrates this with examples from Islamic law: defining wine (khamr) as intoxicant and backbiting (ghiba) as mentioning your brother with what he dislikes. For both, a proof from the Shari'a is needed — for wine, the Prophet's hadith 'Every intoxicant is wine' and the consensus that non-intoxicants are not wine. Thus, definition always requires a proof, whether it is a definition of a name or of a reality. Ibn Taymiyya then rejects the Greek logicians' division of attributes into essential (dhati) and accidental (lazim) categories. He argues that if by 'essence' they mean external existents, the division is false; if they mean mental concepts, then the conception is subjective and varies between individuals, leading to no objective knowledge. Hence, Muslim theorists abandoned the logical rules of definition, relying instead on a single accompanying description (al-wasf al-wahid al-mulazim) that is sufficient to distinguish the defined, without needing genera or differentiae.

The Illuminationist Critique and Reform of Aristotelian Logic
Author describes conflicts over the role of logic in attaining true knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemological grounds on which Illuminationists rejected Aristotelian logic and explain how they paradoxically used it as a foundation for reform.

Core Idea
Illuminationists reject syllogistic logic not as false, but as insufficient for direct vision of truth.

This passage reveals a deep epistemological conflict: the Illuminationists, following Suhrawardi, argue that Aristotelian logic is rooted in mere discursive reasoning (fikr) and demonstration (burhan), which cannot grant the direct unveiling (kashf) and taste (dhawq) that constitute true wisdom. They claim that the Peripatetics, including Ibn Sina, are trapped in weak foundations and false principles, deprived of immediate vision of meanings. Yet surprisingly, the Illuminationists did not discard logic; they studied it as a spiritual exercise and eventually reformed it into an 'Illuminationist logic.' Let us examine this nuanced stance.

The Illuminationist path is attributed to Plato and ancient sages; the Peripatetic path to Aristotle.
2

The critique is not that syllogisms are invalid, but that they cannot replace immediate experience.
3

The Illuminationists 'imitate the first principles and assume the character of God's morals' (يتشبهون بالمبادىء الأولى، ويتخلقون بأخلاق الله). This involves detaching from matter and folding the macrocosm within oneself through knowledge. Contrast this with the Peripatetic method of organizing proofs and definitions.
This is a spiritual practice, not a logical technique.
4
Analyze the paradoxical attitude toward Aristotelian logic
Despite rejecting Aristotelian logic as a path to true knowledge, the Illuminationists 'made its study a spiritual exercise' and later 'reformed' it, leading to what can be called 'Illuminationist logic.' How can rejection and adoption coexist?
The reform likely involved adapting logical forms to serve illuminative insights, not abandoning them entirely.

Commentary
This passage is a critical overview of the historical disputes between three groups: the speculative theologians (mutakallimun), the Sufis of taste (ahl al-dhawq), and the Illuminationist philosophers (ishraqiyun), with a focus on the role of logic in attaining true knowledge. The author first notes that Sufis, being physicians of souls and bodies, rarely suffer mental imbalance from their practices; any disorder is pre-existing. This sets the stage for the conflict: the mutakallimun and Sufis disparage each other's methods. Then the author shifts to the deeper conflict between Illuminationist Sufis and Peripatetic philosophers (masha'iyun). The Illuminationists trace their source to Plato and earlier sages like Hermes and Pythagoras, claiming that their wisdom is based on imitation of divine attributes and detachment from matter, leading to a direct vision of existence. In contrast, the Aristotelians (followers of Aristotle) are said to have flimsy foundations and false principles; they rely on thought, syllogistic proof, and definitions but are deprived of the direct vision of meanings. The Peripatetics, thus, according to the Illuminationists, never reach true knowledge because they lack the path of unveiling (kashf) and the illumination of lights. The author then quotes Suhrawardi stating that the Persians and ancient Greeks (except Aristotle) relied on taste and unveiling, while Aristotle and his followers relied solely on investigation and demonstration. Suhrawardi attacks Ibn Sina for his Aristotelianism and for attacking Plato. Despite this criticism, the Illuminationists surprisingly adopted the study of Aristotelian logic as a spiritual exercise, leading to a reform of that logic. The author concludes by noting that Sufis rejected logic (both Aristotelian and Islamic) as a theoretical path, never engaging in detailed criticism or proposing a new logic. In contrast, the Illuminationist Sufis used logic as mental exercise and then modified it. Suhrawardi criticized some Aristotelian topics and produced an innovative abbreviation of Aristotelian logic, which can be called 'Illuminationist logic'. The passage frames the upcoming discussion: Suhrawardi's position on Aristotelian and Greek logic and his new logical attempt.
