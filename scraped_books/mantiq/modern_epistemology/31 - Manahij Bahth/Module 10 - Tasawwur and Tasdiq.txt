The Two Pillars of Logic: Conception and Assent
Author presents the division into conception and assent, and their methods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tasawwur and tasdiq and identify their corresponding logical methods (hadd and qiyas).

Core Idea
Knowledge is either conception or assent; logic studies definition and syllogism.

This passage presents the foundational division of logic as found in Islamic commentaries. Before learning any logical tool, you must grasp that knowledge comes in two types: simple conception (like understanding the meaning of a term) and assent (judging that a proposition is true). Logic then provides the methods to achieve each: definition for conception, syllogism for assent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge
The author divides knowledge into tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). Conception is simple apprehension of a concept without judgment; assent is affirming or denying a proposition.
Tasawwur is not imagination—it is intellectual grasping of meaning.
2
Match each type with its method
The method to attain conception is hadd (definition), and the method to attain assent is qiyas (syllogism). Definition clarifies the essence of a term; syllogism derives a conclusion from premises.
3
Recognize the scope of logic
Logic is therefore the study of these two methods: definition and syllogism. All logical topics fall under one of these headings.
This division is the backbone of standard Islamic logic textbooks.

Commentary

The common assumption traces the division of logic into conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) back to the Stoics. The author, however, mounts a strong challenge: Stoic nominalism is incompatible with the Peripatetic notion of conception, and Aristotle himself explicitly states the division. Logical vs Grammatical Signification
Author defines logical signification and compares it with grammatical signification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between logical and grammatical signification and identify the three types of logical signification (mutabaqa, tadammun, iltizam) in a given definition.

Core Idea
Signification is the conceptual link between a sign and what it signifies—logically a necessary entailment, grammatically a linguistic convention.

This passage examines how logicians and grammarians define signification (dalala). While both acknowledge three types—correspondence, inclusion, and commitment—they differ fundamentally: logicians view it as a mental relation between concepts, while grammarians tie it to linguistic usage. Understanding this distinction equips you to analyse how words carry meaning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the logical definition
The passage defines logical signification as 'the state of a thing such that conceiving it necessitates conceiving another thing.' This is purely conceptual: a sign (dalil) is a concept that, when known, forces the mind to another concept (madlul).
The definition uses mental 'conception' (tasawwur), not physical or verbal indication.
2
Identify the three types of logical signification
The three types are: mutabaqa (the full meaning), tadammun (a part of the meaning), and iltizam (a necessary concomitant). For example, the concept 'human' mutabaqa signifies the whole; tadammun signifies 'animal' (a part); iltizam signifies 'capacity for speech' (a necessary concomitant).
Logicians allow all three to coincide in one use of a term.
3
Contrast with the grammatical definition
Grammarians define signification as 'understanding the meaning from the word used in it.' Their types depend on linguistic coinage (wad'). Mutabaqa is the original meaning; tadammun a part of that; iltizam an external meaning. Crucially, grammarians deny that all three can occur in a single usage.
This difference highlights the conceptual vs. linguistic emphasis.
4
Recognize the historical context
The author notes that logical signification likely entered Islamic thought via Stoic logic. This shows that Islamic logicians were not merely preserving Greek ideas but consciously adapting them, and that the debate with grammarians was a genuine intellectual exchange.

Ibn Taymiyya's Seventh Argument: The Circularity of Definitions
Ibn Taymiyya's seventh argument against the epistemic utility of definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's seventh argument against definitions and explain why definitions cannot be the ultimate source of new concepts.

Core Idea
Definitions are either redundant or incomprehensible – they cannot convey new conceptual knowledge.

Ibn Taymiyya continues his critique of the logical tradition by targeting a foundational claim: that definitions (hadd) are the primary means of acquiring concepts. In this seventh argument, he exposes a vicious circle: to understand a definition, you must already know the meanings of its words, which presupposes the very concepts the definition is supposed to deliver. This sharp epistemological challenge forces us to reconsider how we actually acquire knowledge of simple meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
The claim under attack is that definitions (hadd) are the primary tool for acquiring new concepts (tasawwur). Ibn Taymiyya argues this is impossible.
2
Extract the first premise
The listener cannot understand a definition unless he already understands the individual words and their indication of meanings. This is stated explicitly: 'لا يمكن للسامع فهم الحد، إلا إذا فهمت مفردات ألفاظه ودلالتها على معانيها.'
3
Extract the second premise
Knowing the indication of a word (dalalat al-lafz) for its meaning is preceded by a conception (tasawwur) of that meaning. The text says: 'والعلم بدلالة اللفظ على المعنى الموضوع له، مسبوق بتصور المعنى.'
4
Derive the dilemma
Two cases arise: either the listener already had the conception before hearing the definition, or not. If he had it, the definition is redundant. If he did not, he cannot understand the definition because he lacks the prior conception needed to understand the words. Therefore, definitions cannot provide new concepts.
This is a classic circularity argument.
5
State the conclusion
The argument concludes that definitions are neither necessary nor sufficient for concept acquisition; they either presuppose the concept or fail to convey it.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Essential Priority
Ibn Taymiyya rejects the idea that essential attributes are mentally prior to accidental ones.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's two objections to the priority of essential over accidental and explain why he considers it conventional and not innate.

Core Idea
Essential priority over accidental is conventional, not objective.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges a foundational assumption of classical logic: that essential attributes are mentally prior to accidental ones. He argues that this priority is an arbitrary convention, not a truth about reality or the human mind. This lesson examines his two-fold critique and its implications for logical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Locate the principle being critiqued: the idea that essential attributes (al-dhātiyyāt) are mentally prior to accidental ones (al-'aradiyyāt). Understand that this is a claim about mental ordering, not just temporal sequence.
2
Extract the first objection
Ibn Taymiyya argues that this priority is merely a report about the logicians' own convention (waḍ‘). They arbitrarily decide which attribute comes first. This does not correspond to external reality, which is independent of our conceptions.
The priority is 'khabar 'an waḍ‘ihim' – a report about their stipulation.
3
Extract the second objection
He appeals to fiṭra (innate disposition). If the division were innate, it would be universally recognized without imitation (taqlīd). But since it is based on variable conventions, it cannot be innate. Hence, the division is false both epistemologically (fiṭra) and ontologically (reality).
Fiṭra perceives innate matters; this division is not perceived innately.
4
Evaluate the critique's force
Consider whether Ibn Taymiyya's objections undermine the logical principle entirely. The critique shows that the priority is not a self-evident truth but a human construction. However, one might argue that logical conventions can still be useful even if not innate.
The critique does not propose an alternative; it only deconstructs the claim.

Ibn Taymiyya's Nominalist Critique of Universals
Ibn Taymiyya presents his nominalist view on universals and traces its origin to Stoics via mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's nominalist position on universals and trace its Stoic origin via the mutakallimun.

Core Idea
Universals exist only in the mind; the external world contains only particulars.

Ibn Taymiyya presents a radical critique of the Aristotelian view that universals have extra-mental existence. He argues that what is 'out there' is always particular and specified. And we see here that he criticizes the Jahmiyya notion that faith is only knowledge of God, and that action is not a pillar of faith. He holds that this Jahmiyya principle originated from the Greek method.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological stance. He affirms the intellect's ability to grasp universals, but only as a means to know particulars — not as an end in itself. He attacks the Aristotelian–Ibn Sinan view that God only knows universals, arguing that this leads to the false conclusion that the soul's perfection lies in abstract universal knowledge alone. Ibn Taymiyya insists on the integration of theoretical and practical knowledge: the soul has two faculties — theoretical scientific (quwwa 'ilmiyya nazariyya) and practical volitional (quwwa iradiyya 'amaliyya). True perfection requires the perfection of both through knowledge of God (marifa) and worship (ibada). He criticizes the Jahmiyya for making faith merely intellectual assent without action, tracing this error to the Greek separation of knowledge and practice. For Ibn Taymiyya, logic that prioritizes universals over particulars and knowledge over action leads to heresy (zandaqa). This demonstrates his deep insight into the connection between Greek logic and metaphysics.

Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological principle that conceivability does not imply existence.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The mind's reach exceeds reality's grasp — a key tool against unwarranted metaphysical inferences.

He provides striking examples, such as the conjunction of contradictories. This principle has powerful applications, especially in theological arguments that infer external realities from mere conceivability.

The Necessary Existent is less real than a flea because it lacks positive attributes.

Ibn Taymiyya's nominalist critique of metaphysical universals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's objection to metaphysical universals and his demand for concrete external examples.

Core Idea
For Ibn Taymiyya, only particulars exist; universals are mental constructs without external reality.

After dismantling the Necessary Existent, Ibn Taymiyya attacks the philosophers' entire project of discussing universals and pure intelligibles. He accuses them of empty verbalism, inventing terms like 'reality qua reality' without being able to point to a single external instance. This lesson explores his empiricist epistemology.

Ibn Taymiyya's Reduction of Concept to Judgment
Ibn Taymiyya argues that every concept is actually a judgment composed of subject and predicate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Taymiyya's argument that every concept is a judgment and identify its implications for definitions and syllogisms in Islamic logic.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya denies pure concepts; every 'concept' is already a judgment.

Classical logic sharply distinguishes between simple apprehension (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq). Ibn Taymiyya challenges this foundational distinction, arguing that what logicians call concepts are actually judgments in disguise. His critique anticipates modern psychological logicians and has profound implications for how we understand definitions and syllogisms in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recall the standard distinction
In classical Mantiq, tasawwur (concept) is simple apprehension of an essence without affirmation or denial, while tasdiq (judgment) involves assent to a proposition. Ibn Taymiyya rejects this separation.
Keep in mind that Ibn Taymiyya's target is the Aristotelian notion of a concept as a mental image free of propositional content.

If we do not do this, and do not connect with these 'possible judgments'—to speak in the language of contemporary psychological logicians—we do not arrive at anything at all. He immediately replies: It is the conception devoid of knowledge of that assent, and it is not the one devoid of all affirmative and negative restrictions. He gives an example: If someone doubts whether nabidh is forbidden or not. The doubter has conceived nabidh and conceived the forbidden. Commentary
This passage critically analyzes Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological challenge to the classical Aristotelian distinction between tasawwur (conception) and tasdiq (assent). The author begins by noting that discussions of the middle term in syllogistic and analogical reasoning lead to Ibn Taymiyya's radical position: the middle term can suffice for mental operations without a full syllogism, and complete conception of the middle term can replace the syllogism altogether. Ibn Taymiyya's core claim is that every concept can be turned into a judgment and every judgment can be turned into a concept—effectively collapsing the dichotomy. The author explains that Ibn Taymiyya analyzes the reduction of conception to assent, considering conception itself a judgmental process, thereby placing him alongside modern psychological logicians like Victor Cousin who discovered 'possible judgments' in concept formation. Ibn Taymiyya denies the existence of a 'naive conception' (tasawwur sadhij) free from affirmation or negation. Using the example of 'forbidden wine', he shows that what logicians call a simple concept (the concept of wine) is actually a judgment: 'wine is intoxicating'. The question and answer both involve a complete proposition. Similarly, a syllogism like 'every intoxicant is wine, every wine is forbidden' yields both the assent 'the intoxicant is forbidden' and the conceptual understanding that 'intoxicant' falls under 'wine'. Whether one aims at a definition (hadd) or a syllogism, the result is always a complete declarative sentence. Thus there is no isolated single concept; every mental content involves a propositional structure. The passage then attacks the notion of a 'pure concept' devoid of all restrictions—such a thing would be a mere fleeting thought, not knowledge. Positive concepts like 'human' require affirming existence, form, color, etc. Even imaginary concepts like 'sea of mercury' or 'mountain of ruby' involve the judgment of non-existence or impossibility. Negative concepts are also bound by judgments of absence. Ibn Taymiyya then clarifies what 'conception capable of being the condition for assent' means: it is not a concept free of all judgments, but one free of the specific judgment that the assent aims to establish. For example, one can conceive 'nabidh' and 'forbidden' through various judgments (that nabidh is a drink, intoxicating, etc.) without yet knowing whether nabidh is forbidden. This shows that conception is always judgment-laden; there is no epistemically neutral starting point. The passage thus presents Ibn Taymiyya as a precursor to modern psychological approaches to logic, emphasizing the inseparability of concept and judgment.

Ibn Taymiyya Refutes the Division of Knowledge into Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Ibn Taymiyya argues that all knowledge involves judgment, not pure concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to the logicians' division of knowledge and explain why he argues that all knowledge involves judgment.

Core Idea
Every concept is bound by a judgment; pure conception is a myth.

The classical logicians divided knowledge into tasawwur (simple conception) and tasdiq (assent). Ibn Taymiyya launches a radical critique, arguing that this distinction is untenable. He claims that any meaningful concept — including those of imaginary objects — inherently involves a judgment, either affirmative or negative. This lesson unpacks his argument and its implications for Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim being attacked
The logicians hold that knowledge is divided into tasawwur and tasdiq, and that tasawwur is a bare concept free from all positive and negative determinations. Locate this claim in the passage.
Ibn Taymiyya quotes this view to refute it.
2
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's central argument
He argues that both tasawwur and tasdiq involve the same cognitive operation: affirming a predicate for a subject. He asserts that a concept stripped of all determinations is not knowledge at all — it is a mere thought or fantasy.
Look for the statement 'كل ما عري عن كل قيد ثبوتي أو سلبي...'.
3
Analyse the examples given
Ibn Taymiyya provides three examples: (1) conceiving 'khamr' involves affirming its intoxicating property; (2) conceiving 'man' involves affirming existence, form, and color; (3) conceiving imaginary objects like a 'sea of mercury' requires a judgment of non-existence. Work through each example to see how judgment is present.
Even negative concepts involve a judgment of negation.
4
Synthesize his conclusion about the nature of knowledge
Since every concept is inherently tied to some judgment (positive or negative), the logicians' division collapses. All knowledge is fundamentally tasdiq. Sum up how this conclusion challenges the foundation of classical Islamic logic.
Compare with the standard view: logicians maintained that simple concepts are the building blocks of propositions.

Ibn Taymiyya's Condition for the Concept Preceding Assent
Ibn Taymiyya clarifies that the tasawwur required for tasdiq must be free only of that specific judgment, not all judgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's revision of the tasawwur-tasdiq relation and identify the role of incomplete conception in requiring a proof.

Core Idea
The concept needed for assent is not bare, but merely free of that specific judgment.

In this passage, Ibn Taymiyya addresses a key question from his earlier critique: what kind of conception is necessary before one can give or withhold assent? Rather than demanding a completely pristine concept, he argues that the concept only needs to be neutral regarding the specific judgment at hand. He illustrates this with a concrete legal example and draws an important distinction between complete and incomplete conception. This refinement preserves the utility of the concept-assent distinction while avoiding the unrealistic demand for a judgment-free mind.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question
Ibn Taymiyya begins by asking: 'What is the concept that is capable of the assent that is conditioned upon it?' This sets up his positive view.
2
Extract his answer
He immediately responds: it is the concept free from knowledge of that assent, not free from all positive and negative determinations. So the condition is narrow: only the specific judgment needs to be absent.
3
Analyze the nabidh example
Someone doubts whether nabidh is forbidden. The doubter already conceives nabidh as a drink, intoxicating, etc. These are judgments, but not the specific legal ruling. This shows that a concept can be rich with other judgments and still serve as a basis for assent about a new judgment.
The example comes from Islamic jurisprudence where nabidh's status is debated.
4
Understand complete vs. incomplete conception
Ibn Taymiyya states: the complete concept entails assent directly; the incomplete concept requires a proof (dalil) to affirm a judgment. This distinguishes cases where conception alone suffices from those needing additional evidence.
5
Reflect on the analogy with speech
He compares 'assent is preceded by concept' to 'speech is preceded by knowledge'. Just as one should not speak without knowledge, one should not assent without conception. This reinforces the necessity of some prior conception, but not a bare one.

Commentary
This passage refines the earlier critique by addressing a specific question: what kind of conception is necessary before one can give or withhold assent? Ibn Taymiyya argues that the concept need not be pristine and empty of all determinations; it only needs to be free from the particular judgment in question. He illustrates with the example of someone doubting whether nabidh (a type of drink) is forbidden (haram). The doubter already has a rich conception of nabidh—that it is a drink, that it exists, that it intoxicates, etc. All these are judgments, but they are not the specific judgment of its legal status. Thus, the concept is conditioned by many determinations, but it is still a valid basis for the subsequent assent about its prohibition. Then he draws an analogy: just as speech is preceded by knowledge, so assent is preceded by conception. The completeness of the conception matters: a complete concept directly entails its related assent; an incomplete concept requires additional evidence (dalil) to reach a judgment. This passage shows Ibn Taymiyya's nuanced position: he does not reject all distinction between conception and assent, but he redefines the necessary conception as one that is merely neutral regarding the specific assent, not devoid of all content.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of Absolute Concepts and the Alternative of Dalil al-Ayat
Ibn Taymiyya critiques absolute concepts and introduces dalil al-ayat as alternative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's objection to absolute concepts as a path to knowing Allah and explain how dalil al-ayat provides an alternative.

Core Idea
Absolute concepts cannot capture Allah's unique essence; only particular signs (ayat) that entail His essence can.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the tendency to use abstract, universal concepts like 'absolute necessary' to know Allah. He argues that such concepts only give knowledge of a universal, not the unique essence of Allah. Instead, he proposes that we must infer from particular created signs (ayat) that are necessarily connected to Allah's essence. This method, called dalil al-ayat, relies on mutual entailment (talazum) and is distinct from Aristotelian syllogism and analogy. In this lesson, we will dissect his critique and understand this alternative logical approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target: absolute concepts
Locate where Ibn Taymiyya rejects absolute human, absolute animal, absolute necessary, absolute agent, and absolute self-sufficient. Understand why he says knowing these does not give knowledge of the essence itself.
The passage explicitly states that knowing absolute human does not make one know the essence, and similarly for Allah.
2
Understand the critique: abstraction vs. essence
Ibn Taymiyya argues that absolute concepts are abstractions that strip away particularity. Knowing them does not entail knowledge of the specific, unique essence of Allah. This is why one cannot reach knowledge of Allah through such concepts.
The critique is epistemological: what is known through universals is universal, not particular.
3
Introduce the alternative: dalil al-ayat
Extract Ibn Taymiyya's definition: 'For His signs entail His essence... it is impossible for any possible thing to exist except with the existence of His essence.' This is the core of dalil al-ayat: inference from particular signs that are necessarily connected to Allah.
The signs (ayat) are particular possible things that cannot exist without Allah's essence.
4
Distinguish dalil al-ayat from syllogism and analogy
Ibn Taymiyya explicitly says dalil al-ayat is not Aristotelian syllogism (qiyas) nor analogical reasoning (tamthil). It is 'inference from a particular to a particular due to their mutual entailment.' This differs from universal-syllogistic deduction and from analogy based on similarity.
The key term is 'talazum' (mutual entailment) between the sign and the essence.
5
Reflect on the epistemological shift
Consider the implication: knowledge of Allah comes not through abstract universals but through concrete signs that are necessarily linked to Him. This shifts the focus from philosophical abstraction to empirical and scriptural signs.
This method is used in later Islamic theology, especially in arguments from creation (ayat kawniyya) and revelation (ayat shar'iyya).

Commentary
This passage critiques the use of absolute concepts to know Allah. It argues that knowing absolute human or absolute animal does not give knowledge of the essence in itself; similarly, knowing absolute necessary being, absolute agent, absolute self-sufficient does not give knowledge of Allah's essence.
