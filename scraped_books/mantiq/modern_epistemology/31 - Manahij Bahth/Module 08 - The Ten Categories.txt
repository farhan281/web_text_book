The Organon's Changing Boundaries: Form vs Matter in Logic
Author discusses the contents of Aristotle's Organon and different views on its composition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main views on the composition of the Organon and explain the Stoic rationale for omitting certain books.

Core Idea
The Organon's contents were debated; Stoics reduced it to formal works only.

The passage discusses a foundational issue: what exactly does 'logic' cover? The Organon, Aristotle's logical corpus, was not a fixed set. Different schools added or removed books based on their view of logic's scope. Understanding these historical debates helps you see why later Islamic logicians sometimes included topics like rhetoric and poetics in logic, and sometimes excluded them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three views
List the three positions: (1) Aristotle's original six books; (2) Greek commentators adding Poetry and Rhetoric, later Alexandrians adding Isagoge; (3) Stoics removing all books dealing with matter, leaving only form-focused works.
2
Analyze the Stoic rationale
The Stoics held a purely formal view of logic. Therefore they removed Posterior Analytics (demonstration), Topics (dialectic), Rhetoric, Poetry, and Sophistics because those deal with the content or matter of arguments.
This is the key logical distinction: form vs matter.
3
Connect to Islamic Peripatetic tradition
Islamic Peripatetics followed the Alexandrian expanded Organon, including Isagoge, Rhetoric, and Poetry. This influenced how they defined the subject matter of logic.

Commentary
This passage addresses the third general issue: the composition and divisions of the Organon (the collection of Aristotle’s logical works). The author explains that Aristotle’s own Organon included six books: Categories (al-Maqūlāt), On Interpretation (al-‘Ibāra), Prior Analytics (al-Taḥālīl al-Ulā), Posterior Analytics (al-Taḥālīl al-Thāniya), Topics (al-Jadal), and Sophistics (al-Safsata). Later Greek commentators added Rhetoric (al-Khaṭāba) and Poetry (al-Shi‘r) to the Organon. Then the later Alexandrian commentators added Porphyry’s Isagoge (Īsāghūjī) as an introduction to these eight books. The Islamic Peripatetic commentators (al-shurrāḥ al-mashshā’ūn) followed the Alexandrian arrangement. In contrast, the later Stoic logicians and commentators rejected this expansion. Consistent with their purely formal view of logic, they removed Posterior Analytics (which deals with demonstration), Topics, Rhetoric, Poetry, and Sophistics—all the books that treat the material content of arguments. The author notes that the Stoic influence is very evident in this reduction of the Organon to only the formal parts.

Ibn Sina's Logical Kalima: Definition and Contrast with Grammar
Author contrasts logical kalima with grammatical verb, emphasizing simplicity and time indication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical definition of kalima and distinguish it from the grammatical verb (fi'l).

Core Idea
A logical kalima must be simple and tensed; compound verbs are excluded.

Ibn Sina, the master of Islamic logic, redefines the term 'kalima' for logical purposes. While grammarians call any action word a verb, logicians demand simplicity and a time reference. This lesson will walk you through his precise definition and why it matters for analyzing propositions.

Guided Path
1
Locate Ibn Sina's definition of kalima
Find the sentence: 'al-kalima hiya lafz mufrad yadullu bi al-wad'' ala al-ma'na wa 'ala zaman dhalika al-ma'na.' This is the core definition.
Notice his use of 'lafz mufrad' (single word) and 'bi al-wad'' (by convention).
2
Extract the genus and differentia
Genus: lafz mufrad (single word). Differentia: yadullu 'ala al-ma'na wa zaman (indicates meaning and time). This differs from the grammatical definition which does not require a time indication for all verbs (e.g., imperative).
3
Understand why non-third person imperfects are excluded
Ibn Sina says they are murakkab (compound) because they include the pronoun. A compound is not a single word, so it fails the genus condition. Grammarians accept them as verbs, but logicians do not.
This is a key point of departure between logic and Arabic grammar.

As for the inflected (musrif), it is that which indicates one of the two times that flank the present — i.e., past and future.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional discussion of the logical terminology for parts of speech, focusing on 'kalima' (word/verb) and its subdivisions. The author begins by contrasting the logical 'kalima' with the grammatical 'fi'l'. Ibn Sina defines 'kalima' as a single word that by convention (bi al-wad') indicates both a meaning and a time. He then critiques the grammatical category 'fi'l' by noting that the non-third person imperfect (e.g., 'I walk' or 'you walk') is considered a verb by grammarians but is actually compound (murakkab) because it includes the pronoun, so it does not qualify as a 'kalima' in logic, which must be simple. Next, the passage divides both ism (noun) and kalima into two binary divisions: first, positive (muhassal) vs. non-positive (ghayr muhassal) — where non-positive refers to privative terms like 'non-human'; second, independent (qa'im) vs. inflected (musrif). Ibn Sina observes that Arabic lacks non-positive nouns and kalimas; they exist only in Greek and Persian. Abu al-Salt al-Dani confirms this, noting that non-positive terms in Arabic are only coinages. Furthermore, the independent kalima (like a tense-less verb) has no Arabic equivalent; instead, Arabs use future forms for present meaning, or even past forms for present (e.g., 'Zayd sahha' meaning 'he is cured'). The inflected kalima indicates past or future tense. This passage illustrates how Islamic logicians adapted Greek logical categories to Arabic, noting gaps and differences.

Divisions of Logical Nouns and Verbs: Positive/Privative and Independent/Inflected
Author lists and explains two binary divisions of ism and kalima, with linguistic observations about Arabic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize any logical ism or kalima as muhassal/ghayr muhassal and qa'im/musrif based on Ibn Sina's divisions.

Core Idea
Logic recognizes positive and privative terms, but Arabic has no native privatives; inflected kalimas indicate past or future.

Ibn Sina divides every ism and kalima two ways: one division concerns whether the term is positive (muhassal) or privative (ghayr muhassal), and the other concerns whether it is independent of time (qa'im) or inflected for time (musrif). He notes that Arabic lacks privative terms and independent kalimas, revealing the interaction between logical categories and the Arabic language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two divisions mentioned
Find where the passage says 'wa yanqasimu kullun min al-ism wa al-kalima ila qismayn: (1) muhassal wa ghayr muhassal, (2) wa ila ma huwa qa'im wa ma huwa musrif.' These are the two binary divisions.
2
Define muhassal and ghayr muhassal
Muhassal means positive, affirming a property (e.g., 'human'). Ghayr muhassal means privative, negating a property (e.g., 'non-human'). Ibn Sina says Arabic lacks such privative terms except as coinages.
Abu al-Salt al-Dani confirms this: non-positive terms are not native Arabic.
3
Define qa'im and musrif
Qa'im (independent) indicates a kalima that does not specify a tense, like a tenseless verb. Musrif (inflected) indicates one of the two times flanking the present: past or future. Ibn Sina notes that qa'im has no Arabic equivalent; Arabs use future forms for present meaning, or even past forms.
Example: 'Zayd sahha' uses past form for present meaning.
4
Apply the divisions to a real term
Take the word 'human' (insan). It is muhassal (positive) and not a kalima, so the kalima division doesn't apply. For a verb like 'he walked', it is musrif (past tense) and muhassal. Try a privative like 'non-human' — it is ghayr muhassal but not native Arabic.

The Fourfold Classification of Words by Meaning Relation
Author introduces the four Aristotelian divisions of words in relation to meanings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given Arabic word into one of four categories: mutawat'i, mushtarak, mutaradif, or mutazayil based on its relation to meaning.

Core Idea
Words relate to meanings in four ways: one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-one, and many-to-many (with nuance).

Islamic logicians adopted Aristotle's classification of words by their relation to meanings, which became foundational for usul al-fiqh and Arabic linguistics. This lesson teaches you to identify and apply these four categories—univocal, equivocal, synonymous, and divergent—to real examples from the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish mutawat'i from mushtarak
Check whether the word has one single meaning or multiple distinct meanings. If one meaning, it is mutawat'i (e.g., 'insan' for human). If multiple, it is mushtarak (e.g., 'ayn' for eye or spring).
Mushtarak words cause ambiguity and require context.
2
Identify mutaradif pairs
Look for two or more words that are claimed to have identical meanings. If they are interchangeable in all contexts, they are mutaradif (e.g., 'asad' and 'ghadanfar' for lion).
Many usulis deny that true synonyms exist.
3
Recognize mutazayil words
Find words that are closely related but not identical in meaning, often differing in nuance or connotation (e.g., 'salam' for peace vs. 'aman' for safety).
Mutazayil is sometimes called 'divergent'.
4
Apply the classification to a fiqh term
Take a word like 'salat' (prayer). Is it mutawat'i or mushtarak? It has multiple meanings (ritual prayer, blessing, connection), so it is mushtarak. This affects how legal texts are interpreted.

Mental vs. External Possibility and the Qur'anic Proof for Resurrection
Author critiques the reality of universal essences and distinguishes mental from external possibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
External possibility requires actual evidence, not mere conceivability.

Ibn Taymiyya introduces a crucial epistemic distinction: something can be mentally possible (conceivable) yet externally impossible. External possibility, on the other hand, is grounded in actual existence — either of the thing itself, its like, or something more remote. He then argues that this external possibility is the method of the Qur'an in proving the resurrection, using the a fortiori argument (qiyas al-awla). In this lesson, we will unpack this distinction and see how it refutes the philosophers' error.

2
Define external possibility
External possibility has three branches: (a) knowledge of the thing's own existence, (b) knowledge of the existence of its like, (c) knowledge of the existence of something more remote. If (b) or (c) exist, then the thing exists 'a fortiori' (bi al-awla).
3
Understand the a fortiori connection
Ibn Taymiyya explicitly links external possibility to qiyas al-awla. For example, if a less likely thing exists, the more likely thing exists with greater reason. This is used in the Qur'an to argue for resurrection: if God created the heavens and earth, He can certainly resurrect humans.
Qur'anic verses like 46:33 and 36:78-79 are classic examples.
4
Apply to the philosophers' error
The philosophers used mental possibility to claim universals exist externally. Ibn Taymiyya says this is invalid because external possibility requires proof, not mere non-impossibility.

ويرى ابن تيمية ـ كما سبق أن قلنا ـ أن المناطقة اختلفوا في تقسيمهم الصفات إلى لوازم وذاتيات . فالبعض يقول إن اللوازم هي ما افتقرت إلى وسط بخلاف الذاتيات والبعض الآخر يرى أن كثيراً من الصفات اللازمة لا تحتاج إلى وسط .
And Ibn Taymiyya holds - as we have said - that the logicians differed in their division of attributes into necessary (lawāzim) and essential (dhātiyyāt). For some say that the necessary attributes are those that need a middle term, unlike the essential ones, and others hold that many necessary attributes do not need a middle term.

Commentary
This passage states Ibn Taymiyya's observation that logicians disagree among themselves about the classification of attributes into necessary (lazim) and essential (dhati). Specifically, there is a dispute: some logicians claim that necessary attributes require a middle term to be known, while essential attributes are known without a middle term. Others hold that many necessary attributes do not require a middle term. Ibn Taymiyya points out this inconsistency to undermine the authority of the logicians' system. This disagreement shows that their division is not universally agreed upon, which supports Ibn Taymiyya's broader critique of Aristotelian logic.
