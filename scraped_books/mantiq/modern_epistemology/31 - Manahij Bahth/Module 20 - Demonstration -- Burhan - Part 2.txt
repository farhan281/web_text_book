The Quran needs no artificial logic; its proofs are inherently superior and accessible to all.

Jabir's Critique of Galen's Inductive Premise
Critique of Galen's inductive premise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why a premise like 'summer is followed by autumn' cannot be considered a primitive (ūlā) premise according to Jabir, and explain the hidden assumption it relies on.

Core Idea
Inductive claims about regularities require a metaphysical assumption of the uniformity of nature.

In this passage, Jabir ibn Hayyan critiques Galen's use of observational premises as primitive starting points for demonstration. Galen claimed that 'if summer is inevitably followed by autumn, then it only occurs after spring' is a self-evident premise. Jabir challenges this, showing that such a premise depends on a prior assumption about the eternal, unchanging order of time. This critique reveals the limits of induction and the necessity of metaphysical foundations for empirical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify Galen's claimed primitive premise
Find what Galen claimed was a primitive premise: that summer is inevitably followed by autumn, meaning it only occurs after spring.
This premise appears in the passage as part of Galen's book on demonstration.
2
Find Jabir's objection
Jabir says this premise cannot be true or primitive because it requires another premise to verify its correctness.
3
Extract the hidden assumption
The hidden assumption is that 'time is eternal and unchanging' – that the seasons have always and will always follow this pattern.
Jabir explicitly states: 'it must be preceded by another premise that times (the seasons) have not ceased and will not cease to be as they are.'
4
Assess the epistemological status
Consider whether Jabir thinks the premise of eternity is itself primitive. He does not assert it; he leaves the question open.
This shows Jabir's caution: even the needed assumption may not be self-evident.

Commentary
This passage begins with the modern commentator Zaki Najib Mahmoud highlighting two original aspects of Jabir's thought that anticipate modern scientific method: the innate human tendency to expect repetition, and the increase in probability with increased repetition. Then the author turns to Jabir's attempt to provide examples from Greek philosophers, specifically Galen. Jabir notes that Galen, despite being an Aristotelian, sometimes used inductive reasoning. He points out that Galen took premises from 'this door'—i.e., based on customary observation—and considered them 'primitives' or first principles. For instance, Galen claimed that it is a primitive premise that summer is inevitably followed by autumn, meaning it only occurs after spring. Jabir critiques this: such a premise cannot be primitive because it depends on a more fundamental assumption that the seasons have always and will always follow this pattern—that time is eternal and unchanging. Without that assumption, the regularity could break down. Jabir then questions whether the premise of eternity of time is itself primitive; he does not assert but leaves it open. Next, Jabir criticizes another method: inference by 'athar' (traces or effects of others), which is also a form of reasoning from the witnessed to the absent. He says this method also does not yield certainty. He accuses Galen of erring in using both inductive methods to conclude that the heavens are uncreated, based on the continuous observation of ancestors. The passage thus exposes the reliance of induction on metaphysical assumptions and its inability to produce certain knowledge.

Jabir's Critique of Inference by Traces and Galen's Error on the Heavens
Critique of inference by traces

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Jabir ibn Hayyan considers inference by traces (istidlal bi'l-athar) to be uncertain, and identify the fallacy in Galen's argument for the heavens being uncreated.

Core Idea
Inference from past observations does not yield certainty, especially when claiming the uncreatedness of the heavens.

Jabir ibn Hayyan also criticizes another method used by Galen: inference by traces (athar), which involves reasoning from observable effects to unobservable causes, a form of analogical reasoning. Jabir holds that this method does not reach certainty. He points out that Galen used it to argue that the heavens are not created, based on the fact that our ancestors have always seen them the same way. Jabir implies that such an argument is flawed because it assumes the pattern will continue without justification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method criticized
Find what 'istidlal bi'l-athar' means: inference from traces or effects of others, i.e., reasoning from the witnessed to the absent.
This method is similar to analogical reasoning (qiyas) used in fiqh and kalam.
2
Find Jabir's evaluation
Jabir states that this method does not reach certainty (yaqin). It is probabilistic at best.
3
Extract Galen's argument about the heavens
Galen concluded that the heavens are not created (i.e., eternal), based on the observation that 'our fathers and all the ancients have never ceased seeing it in the same way.'
This is an argument from continuous observation.
4
Analyze the error
The error is assuming that past continuity guarantees future or eternal continuity. Without a justification for why the pattern must hold, the argument is weak.
Jabir's critique aligns with the problem of induction: past observations do not logically compel future ones.
