Deductive Method and Muslim Critique
Classifies general methodologies into four types, with a focus on Muslim attitudes toward the deductive method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why classical Muslim scholars considered the deductive method sterile and identify its limitations according to the passage.

Core Idea
Muslims rejected pure deduction as sterile, but valued induction and revelation-based knowledge.

Andre Lalande's classification includes the deductive method. The passage notes that Muslims knew it as the syllogistic method and attacked it as unproductive. Understanding this critique helps us see how Islamic thought prioritized empirical and revelational knowledge over bare logical form.

Guided Path
1
Locate the deductive method
Find where the passage mentions 'المنهج الإستنباطي' and its alternative name 'المنهج القياسي'.
2
Identify the Muslim name for it
Note that Muslims called it 'al-manhaj al-qiyasi', linking it to Aristotelian syllogism.
3
Find the critique
Read the sentence: 'وهاجموه أشد الهجوم، ورأوا أنه عقيم لا يصل إلى علم نافع.'
4
Analyze the reason for sterility
Consider why they saw it as sterile: perhaps because it only reorganizes known premises without adding new knowledge.
This reason is not fully explained in the passage; infer from context.
5
Contrast with the inductive method
Notice that the inductive method is praised as the path of modern civilization, suggesting Muslims valued induction over deduction.

Third: Sections of the science of general methodologies. As for after, Andre Lalande, the historian of the experimental method, divided the science of general methodologies into: the deductive method, the inductive method, the reconstructive method, and the dialectical method. The second method — the inductive method — was the path of modern European civilization, its hallmark and its creator; its scientists and thinkers followed it and produced for us modern life. And the Muslims had arrived at all its elements centuries before Europe. As for the deductive method, they also knew it; they knew it by the name of the syllogistic method, and they attacked it most severely, seeing it as sterile, not attaining useful knowledge. As for if we move to the third method, which is the reconstructive method, we see that the Muslims established it on precise scientific foundations, in what is known as the science of hadith terminology, and the methods of verifying hadith in transmission and comprehension — these are exactly the method of modern historical research, as known by Langlois and Seignobos. And the Muslims had arrived at everything that the scholars of historical research methods arrived at, including internal and external criticism of texts, and they knew the methods of historical analysis and synthesis, examination of documents, and the method of comparison and classification. And the study of the methods of historical investigation among many of the scholars of classes (tabaqat), especially al-Taj al-Subki, Ibn Khaldun, and al-Sakhawi, will clarify this definitively. And Ibn Khaldun was not, as researchers imagine, a sociologist, but rather a scholar of historical methodology; he used the inductive method with rare skill to explain the accidental essences (al-dhawat al-'aradiyya) he encountered, an explanation based on analysis and synthesis, using analogical reasoning from the unseen to the witnessed (qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid) on one hand, and induction of accidental events in observation to arrive at general judgments. So his brilliant work in the realm of history equals exactly the work of Ash'ari jurists and scholars of usul al-fiqh and religion among them in fiqh and kalam. And the study of this method on a sound scientific path is still a virgin field in the Islamic world. And if we move to the fourth method — which is the dialectical method, we find its origins also in the books of the etiquette of investigation and disputation and debate (adab al-bahth wa al-munazara wa al-jadal), as a complete method resembling the modern dialectical method, as applied in the greatest scientific councils and academies. And this method is still preserved in books that have become old and are forgotten and folded away. And it suffices me today that I have directed the attention of Arab researchers to the importance of studying these last two methods in a systematic scientific manner.

Commentary
This passage presents a classification of general methodologies into four types, as defined by Andre Lalande. It then examines each method from an Islamic perspective. The inductive method, which drove modern European civilization, was already fully known to Muslims centuries earlier. The deductive method, known to Muslims as the syllogistic method (al-manhaj al-qiyasi), was criticized by them as sterile. The reconstructive method (al-istirdadi) was developed by Muslims in the science of hadith terminology and historical verification, including internal/external criticism, analysis, synthesis, and document examination. The passage highlights Ibn Khaldun as a historical methodologist, not a sociologist, who used induction and analogy. Finally, the dialectical method has its origins in Islamic adab al-bahth wa al-munazara. The author calls for renewed study of these last two methods.

Inductive Method and Muslim Contribution

Commentary
This passage is a historical and methodological critique of Aristotelian logic from an Islamic perspective. Using this inductive method, the author explains why philosophy (based on deduction) failed to thrive in Islam, and why figures like al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and Ibn Rushd are seen more as Hellenistic extensions than authentic Islamic philosophers.
