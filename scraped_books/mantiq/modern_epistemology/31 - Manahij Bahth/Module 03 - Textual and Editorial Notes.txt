From Greek to Arabic: The Translation of Aristotle's Organon
Describes the translation of Aristotle's logical works from Greek/Syriac into Arabic by various translators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the key translators in the transmission of Aristotle's Organon into Arabic and identify their contributions.

Core Idea
The Organon reached the Islamic world through a chain of Syriac and Arab Christian translators, not Persian summaries.

When later Muslim logicians like al-Ghazali wrote their works on logic, they were building on a foundation laid by translators who transmitted Aristotle's texts from Greek to Syriac to Arabic. This lesson maps that chain so you can evaluate the reliability of the logical sources used in Islamic sciences like Kalam and Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish summaries from translations
Note that Ibn al-Muqaffa' did not translate Aristotle's works, but summarized them. He adhered strictly to the original text without changing terms or order, as was common for summarizers of ancient books.
This is important because later scholars often mistook his summaries as translations.
2
Identify the first full translators of categorical syllogisms
Abu Nuh and Salm, head of Bayt al-Hikma, translated Aristotle's logical works up to the last of the categorical moods (al-ashkal al-hamliyya). Both were Syriac Christians, and Salm was contemporary with al-Ma'mun.
They translated only up to the categorical moods, not the entire Organon.
3
Trace the complete translation by Hunayn ibn Ishaq's school
Hunayn ibn Ishaq and his school translated the entire Organon from Greek to Syriac and then to Arabic, or directly to Arabic. His son Ishaq, who converted to Islam, translated most parts.
This school made the Organon fully available in Arabic.
4
Recognize later translators and their roles
Abu Bishr Matta ibn Yunus translated some parts (e.g., Second Analytics, Poetics) from Syriac to Arabic. His school influenced later logicians. Abd al-Masih ibn Na'ima al-Himsi also translated parts. Yahya ibn 'Adi, known as 'the Logician', translated and summarized logical works.
These translators completed the transmission, ensuring the Organon reached the Islamic world in multiple versions.

Commentary
This passage is a historical narrative chronicling the translation of Aristotle's logical works into Arabic. It begins by clarifying that the early translations attributed to Ibn al-Muqaffa' were not actually translations but summaries, and that he adhered strictly to the original text. The author then presents the first translators of the entire Organon up to the categorical moods: Abu Nuh and Salm, both Syriac Christians, with Salm being the head of Bayt al-Hikma under al-Ma'mun. Next, Hunayn ibn Ishaq and his school are credited with translating the entire Organon from Greek to Syriac and then Arabic, or directly. The author highlights Ishaq ibn Hunayn, who converted to Islam, and notes that Abu Bishr Matta ibn Yunus translated parts of the Organon. Evidence is provided from manuscript colophons: the Second Analytics mentions Matta as the translator from Syriac to Arabic after Ishaq's work; similarly for the Book of Poetry. Matta's influence is illustrated by a later logician who followed his school. Other translators include Abd al-Masih ibn Na'ima al-Himsi. Finally, Yahya ibn 'Adi, known as 'the Logician,' is noted for translating and summarizing logical works, and the passage concludes by stating that a complete Organon translation has survived to modern times. This passage establishes a chain of transmission from Greek to Arabic logic.

Did Muslims Know Stoic Logic? A Critical Inquiry
Author recounts translation history of Organon and questions Muslim knowledge of Stoic logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a given logical school (like Stoic logic) was actually known and transmitted to the Islamic world by analyzing historical evidence from translation lists and references in bio-bibliographical works.

Core Idea
Name recognition ≠ textual transmission; absence of translated works indicates lack of direct knowledge.

This passage examines a crucial question in the history of Islamic logic: did the Muslims know only Aristotelian logic, or did they also have access to Stoic logic? The author lays out the translation history of the Organon and then investigates references to Stoics in Islamic sources. Your task is to follow his critical method: separate the evidence for translation from mere name-dropping.

Guided Path
1
Identify the known translation chain
Locate the names of translators (Matta ibn Yunus, Abd al-Masih al-Himsi, Yahya ibn 'Adi) and note that they translated parts or all of the Organon. The passage states that a complete Organon reached modern times.
2
Locate the question about Stoic logic
Find the sentence: 'But was Aristotelian logic the only one that reached the Islamic world?… did Muslims know Stoic logic?' This marks the pivot from descriptive to critical mode.
3
Examine the evidence for Stoic knowledge
The author mentions that historians like al-Shahrastani and al-Qifti knew the name and some names of Stoics (Chrysippus, Zeno) and even referred to them as 'Ashab al-Mizalla'. However, he notes that no translations of their works appear in the lists of translated books.
Note the distinction: knowing a name is not the same as having texts.
4
Assess the conclusion
The passage concludes that Muslims knew the Stoic school only by name and secondary references; the original Stoic books were lost even in Greek. Therefore, direct knowledge was absent. This shows a critical approach to intellectual history.

Later Division of Definition: Predication-Based from Sullam al-`Ulum
Author mentions a later classification of definition from the author of Sullam al-`Ulum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the distinctive feature of the later classification of definition from Sullam al-`Ulum and contrast it with the Aristotelian genus-differentia approach.

Core Idea
A later scholar redefined definition as predication, shifting focus from essence to logical attribution.

In this passage, the author points out that some later logicians introduced a classification of definition not found in Aristotle. The key statement from Sullam al-`Ulum defines definition in terms of predication: 'ma'rif al-shay' ma yahmilu `alayhi' (the definition of a thing is what is predicated upon it). This suggests a shift from the traditional hadd (essence-based) to a more logical-linguistic approach. Let's explore what this means and how it fits into the broader Islamic logical tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of the new division
The passage attributes the new division to the author of 'Sullam al-`Ulum'. Recognize that this is a later work in the Islamic logical tradition, possibly by al-Bahrani or a similar scholar, which summarizes or critiques earlier views.
The title 'Sullam al-`Ulum' suggests a ladder of sciences, indicating a comprehensive approach.
2
Extract the key definition phrase
The phrase is 'معرف الشيء ما يحمل عليه'. Break it down: 'ma'rif al-shay'' (the definiens of the thing) and 'ma yahmilu `alayhi' (what is predicated upon it). This is a nominal definition that defines 'definition' itself in terms of predication.
The term 'ma'rif' here likely means 'that which makes known', i.e., the definition.
3
Analyze the predication relation
In traditional logic, definition is about essence (hadd) or description (rasm). Here, definition is framed as a relation of predication (haml). Think of how predicates attach to subjects in propositions. The definition of a thing is that which can be truly predicated of it – this resembles a rasm tamm (complete description) if it covers all essential and accidental predicates.
This shifts the focus from real essence to logical behavior.
4
Compare to Aristotelian hadd
The passage explicitly says this division is not in Aristotle. In the Aristotelian tradition, hadd requires genus and differentia. This predication-based approach might be broader, allowing definitions that are not strictly by genus and differentia, such as definitions by property or accident.
Consider how this affects the completeness and exclusivity of definitions.
5
Speculate on the full classification
Since the text is truncated, hypothesize what other types might exist in this classification. Perhaps there are definitions based on what is essential (dhātī) vs. accidental (`araḍī). This could parallel the division into hadd and rasm but expressed in terms of predication.
This is an open question – encourage further research into Sullam al-`Ulum.
