Ibn Taymiyya's Alternative: Quantity and Experimentation
Author summarizes Ibn Taymiyya's alternative methodology based on quantity and experimentation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe Ibn Taymiyya's alternative epistemological principles of quantity (al-kamm) and experimentation (al-tajriba) and contrast them with Aristotelian deduction.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya replaces static essences and mechanical syllogisms with dynamic quantity and experimental verification.

Having rejected Greek logic as incompatible, Ibn Taymiyya proposes a new foundation for knowledge: quantity and experimentation. In this lesson, we explore his critique of the limitations of syllogism and his advocacy for a more flexible, empirical approach. This alternative has profound implications for how Islamic sciences are conducted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criticism of syllogism
Find where the passage says that limiting reasoning to two-premise syllogisms is 'mechanical' and that restricting inference to syllogism, induction, and analogy is wrong.
He criticizes the standard three forms of inference.
2
Extract the positive proposal
Locate the sentences about 'quantity' (الكم) as the foundation, and 'experimentation' (التجربة) as the only way to knowledge.
He glorifies experimentation and emphasizes quantity over quality.
3
Understand the reason for dynamic change
The passage says human needs are in constant change and that essences are not static. Therefore, we can only know accidents and properties. This explains why experimentation is necessary.
This connects to the critique of 'static essences'.
4
Contrast with Aristotelian approach
Compare Ibn Taymiyya's method (quantity, experimentation, multiple inference forms) with the traditional syllogistic method (essences, deduction, limited forms).

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's fundamental critique of Greek logic as the author of Manahij Bahth summarizes it. The critique begins with the claim that Greek logic is the greatest tool undermining Islamic civilization because it operates under the guise of methodology but produces general rulings contrary to Islamic principles. Ibn Taymiyya asserts that Aristotle's logic is inseparable from Aristotle's metaphysics and theology, which contradict Islamic theology; therefore, applying Aristotelian logic to Islamic theological inquiries is impossible. When it comes to other sciences, Ibn Taymiyya finds Aristotelian logic artificial and restrictive, describing its methods as 'difficult long paths' and 'pretentious elusive expressions' that only waste time. Islamic methodology, by contrast, aims to fulfill dynamic human needs, which cannot be captured by static essences; we can only know accidents and properties, not essences. Furthermore, restricting reasoning to a two-premise syllogism is mechanical and limiting; we should not limit inference to syllogism, induction, and analogy but can reach more forms by analyzing intellectual operations. Ibn Taymiyya instead advocates for 'quantity' (al-kamm) as the basis of all intellectual processes, rejecting the authority of 'quality' and pure speculation in favor of experimentation (tajriba), which he considers the only path to certain or probable knowledge. The passage ends with a transition to the constructive aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique.

Ibn Taymiyya's Constructive Project in Logic
Ibn Taymiyya's constructive logic from the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the constructive dimension of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya built a Quranic logic of balance, not just destroyed Greek logic.

Ibn Taymiyya is often seen as a destructive critic of logic, but this passage reveals his constructive project: he replaces Greek syllogisms with a Quranic instrument called the mizan (balance). Understanding this constructive side is essential to see his full contribution to Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Locate the constructive intent
Read the first few lines: 'Ibn Taymiyya was not only a destroyer; he destroyed one side in order to build a side.' Identify the three topics where he builds: definition, propositions, and inference.
This sets the frame: critique is for reconstruction.
2
Identify the source of his alternative
Notice that he derives his logical forms from the Quran, which he calls 'the book of existence.' The key term is al-mizan (the balance).
Contrast with Greek reliance on abstract categories.
3
Summarise his two-step method
First, he critiques existing logical doctrines (e.g., Greek logic, Ghazali's five balances). Second, he proposes a Quranic alternative. The passage shows the alternative in outline.
This method applies across definition, propositions, and inference.

The constructive aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the investigations of inference, or the forms of Taymiyyan inference. Ibn Taymiyya was not only a destroyer; he destroyed one side in order to build a side. He did this in the topic of definition, as he did this in the topic of propositions. And he does the same thing in the topic of inference. Ibn Taymiyya holds that the Quran—which is the book of existence for Muslims—is what provides us with the forms of inference, or more precisely presents to us 'the balance' (al-mizan). And the balance according to many exegetes is justice, and it is that by which matters are weighed, and it is that by which justice is known. In another interpretation it is the striking of examples—and the intellectual analogies that bring together similars and separate differents. Or that by which the similarity of similars is known with respect to attributes and measures; and likewise that by which the difference of differents is known. And Ibn Taymiyya holds that this balance is quantitative, based on quantity. He gives clear examples that quantity is the matter of the balance, or the material upon which the balance works. That knowing that these dirhams or other heavy bodies are equal to these is known by their balances, and likewise knowing that this measure is like this is known by its balance—which is the measuring vessels. Likewise the measurement of time—that knowing that this time is like this time is known by its balances by which we estimate times and measure shadows, just as we know by it what is in the watercourses of the quantity of water and sand. And likewise we know by it lengths, widths, and depths, and we posit between every two similars a common amount by which it is known that one is like the other. So the balance is therefore a rational instrument in the pure sciences of quantity; rather it appears that according to Ibn Taymiyya it is quantitative primarily and essentially. Then if we move to applying the balance in juridical and legal matters, we know the branches measured upon their principles in legal matters by the common balances between them. And these common balances are in reality the shared common attribute which we have termed 'the middle term'. So if we know that God forbade grape wine for specific reasons including that it prevents from the remembrance of God and from prayer, and causes enmity among believers, then we see other types of nabidh (date wine) that resemble it in the common universal meaning which is the cause of prohibition, then this common amount—which is the cause—is the balance that God has placed in our minds so that we weigh this and make it like that. So we do not differentiate between similars—and we rely on this on a correct analogy. And this correct analogy is from justice—from the balance which God Almighty commanded that we weigh matters by. And likewise if we apply this balance in our rational sciences. It is necessary that if we arrive at universals, we must previously reach their particulars by weighing—that is, we must know their measure and quantity; and universals have no weight without knowledge of particulars. So one must weigh external existing matters with a just weighing, until we realize the universal shared attribute in the mind; or more precisely, if we arrive at a universal proposition, then there is no real value for this universal until we weigh it—i.e., realize it—by what comes to us of particulars, until we reach the correctness of the universal shared attribute. So there are, then, things weighed by the mind. And these weighed things are not the five balances of al-Ghazali which he presented in al-Qistas al-Mustaqim. Ibn Taymiyya holds—as we said before in our explanation of al-Ghazali's logic—that they are the logic of the Greeks, i.e., they are the known Greek syllogisms. And Ibn Taymiyya denies al-Ghazali's initial idea about logic: that rational meanings are shared among nations, and that the difference is only in verbal terminology. The matter is not like that; in these rational meanings there is much and corrupt (meaning: much that is sound and much that is corrupt). These logicians and al-Ghazali following them—they made this logic 'the balance' of rational balances, the intellectual syllogisms, and claimed that it is a legal instrument whose observance protects the mind from error. And it is not so.

Commentary
This passage presents the constructive aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic. The author argues that Ibn Taymiyya was not merely a destroyer; he also built a positive alternative. The key concept is the 'mizan' (balance), drawn from the Quran. According to Ibn Taymiyya, the Quran provides the true forms of inference, and the mizan is understood by exegetes as justice or as a tool to weigh matters. Another interpretation sees it as the striking of examples and intellectual analogies that unify similars and separate differents. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that this mizan is fundamentally quantitative: it is rooted in quantity, as seen in weighing coins, measuring volumes, timing durations, and gauging lengths. Thus, the mizan is a rational instrument for the pure sciences of quantity. Moving to legal and religious matters, the mizan becomes the common attribute shared between a foundational case and its branches. This common attribute is the 'middle term' (al-hadd al-awsat). For example, the prohibition of grape wine due to intoxication leads to the prohibition of nabidh (date wine) because it shares the same cause (intoxication). This shared cause is the mizan that God placed in our minds. The passage then extends the mizan to rational sciences, stressing that universals are only valid when weighed through particulars. Ibn Taymiyya contrasts his view with al-Ghazali's five balances in al-Qistas al-Mustaqim, which he equates with Greek logic. He denies that rational meanings are universally shared; rather, they contain both truth and falsehood. Ibn Taymiyya accuses the logicians, including al-Ghazali, of falsely claiming that their logic is an infallible instrument.

The Quantitative Basis of the Mizan
Ibn Taymiyya's quantitative basis for the mizan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn Taymiyya considers the mizan (balance) fundamentally quantitative.

Core Idea
The mizan is rooted in quantity (al-kamm) – weighing, measuring, timing.

Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that the Quranic mizan is not an abstract concept but a quantitative instrument. He gives examples from physical measurements to show that the mizan operates through quantity. This quantitative foundation then extends to legal and rational sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about quantity
Find the sentence: 'Ibn Taymiyya holds that this balance is quantitative, based on quantity.' Note that he explicitly states the mizan is quantitative primarily and essentially.
2
List the examples of quantity
The passage lists several domains: dirhams (weight), measuring vessels (volume), time (shadows), lengths/widths/depths. Each example shows how a common amount between two things is known by a balance.
These are not just physical balances but rational instruments.
3
Generalise the role of quantity
The mizan is the common amount (qadr mushtarak) placed between two similars. This amount is quantitative. Even in legal reasoning, the common attribute is described as a 'common amount' (capacity to cause intoxication).
Quantity here is not just number but any measurable quality.

The Mizan as the Middle Term in Legal Analogy
The mizan as middle term in legal qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Ibn Taymiyya's concept of mizan (as the middle term) to analyse a legal analogy (qiyas).

Core Idea
The mizan in law is the shared cause ('illa) that unifies original and branch.

Ibn Taymiyya applies the quantitative mizan to legal reasoning. The common attribute between a foundational case (asl) and a new case (far') is the middle term (al-hadd al-awsat). This shared cause is the mizan that God placed in our minds to weigh matters justly. Understanding this is key to his constructive logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the example of legal analogy
The passage gives the example of grape wine (khamr) prohibited for reasons: it prevents remembrance of God, prayer, causes enmity. Then other types of nabidh (date wine) are considered.
The common meaning is the cause of prohibition ('illa).
2
Recognise the middle term
Ibn Taymiyya calls this common meaning 'the middle term' (al-hadd al-awsat) or 'the shared common attribute' (al-wasf al-jami' al-mushtarak). It is the mizan that allows weighing the branch against the original.
In standard logic, the middle term connects premises; here it connects cases.
3
Formulate the analogy using the mizan
The mizan is the common amount (e.g., intoxication) that is present in both cases. Since both share this cause, the ruling of prohibition applies to both. This is the correct analogy (qiyas sahih) that Ibn Taymiyya regards as justice.
The mizan is not a syllogism but a tool of weighing by a common attribute.

Ibn Taymiyya's Critique of al-Ghazali's Five Balances
Ibn Taymiyya's critique of al-Ghazali's logic as mizan.

Learning Objective

The constructive aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of the investigations of inference, or the forms of Taymiyyan inference. Ibn Taymiyya was not only a destroyer; he destroyed one side in order to build a side. He did this in the topic of definition, as he did this in the topic of propositions. And he does the same thing in the topic of inference. Ibn Taymiyya holds that the Quran—which is the book of existence for Muslims—is what provides us with the forms of inference, or more precisely presents to us 'the balance' (al-mizan). And the balance according to many exegetes is justice, and it is that by which matters are weighed, and it is that by which justice is known. In another interpretation it is the striking of examples—and the intellectual analogies that bring together similars and separate differents. Or that by which the similarity of similars is known with respect to attributes and measures; and likewise that by which the difference of differents is known. And Ibn Taymiyya holds that this balance is quantitative, based on quantity. He gives clear examples that quantity is the matter of the balance, or the material upon which the balance works. That knowing that these dirhams or other heavy bodies are equal to these is known by their balances, and likewise knowing that this measure is like this is known by its balance—which is the measuring vessels. Likewise the measurement of time—that knowing that this time is like this time is known by its balances by which we estimate times and measure shadows, just as we know by it what is in the watercourses of the quantity of water and sand. And likewise we know by it lengths, widths, and depths, and we posit between every two similars a common amount by which it is known that one is like the other. So the balance is therefore a rational instrument in the pure sciences of quantity; rather it appears that according to Ibn Taymiyya it is quantitative primarily and essentially. Then if we move to applying the balance in juridical and legal matters, we know the branches measured upon their principles in legal matters by the common balances between them. And these common balances are in reality the shared common attribute which we have termed 'the middle term'. So if we know that God forbade grape wine for specific reasons including that it prevents from the remembrance of God and from prayer, and causes enmity among believers, then we see other types of nabidh (date wine) that resemble it in the common universal meaning which is the cause of prohibition, then this common amount—which is the cause—is the balance that God has placed in our minds so that we weigh this and make it like that. So we do not differentiate between similars—and we rely on this on a correct analogy. And this correct analogy is from justice—from the balance which God Almighty commanded that we weigh matters by. And likewise if we apply this balance in our rational sciences. It is necessary that if we arrive at universals, we must previously reach their particulars by weighing—that is, we must know their measure and quantity; and universals have no weight without knowledge of particulars. So one must weigh external existing matters with a just weighing, until we realize the universal shared attribute in the mind; or more precisely, if we arrive at a universal proposition, then there is no real value for this universal until we weigh it—i.e., realize it—by what comes to us of particulars, until we reach the correctness of the universal shared attribute. So there are, then, things weighed by the mind. And these weighed things are not the five balances of al-Ghazali which he presented in al-Qistas al-Mustaqim. Ibn Taymiyya holds—as we said before in our explanation of al-Ghazali's logic—that they are the logic of the Greeks, i.e., they are the known Greek syllogisms. And Ibn Taymiyya denies al-Ghazali's initial idea about logic: that rational meanings are shared among nations, and that the difference is only in verbal terminology. The matter is not like that; in these rational meanings there is much and corrupt (meaning: much that is sound and much that is corrupt). These logicians and al-Ghazali following them—they made this logic 'the balance' of rational balances, the intellectual syllogisms, and claimed that it is a legal instrument whose observance protects the mind from error. And it is not so.

Commentary
This passage presents the constructive aspect of Ibn Taymiyya's critique of logic. The author argues that Ibn Taymiyya was not merely a destroyer; he also built a positive alternative. The key concept is the 'mizan' (balance), drawn from the Quran. According to Ibn Taymiyya, the Quran provides the true forms of inference, and the mizan is understood by exegetes as justice or as a tool to weigh matters. Another interpretation sees it as the striking of examples and intellectual analogies that unify similars and separate differents. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that this mizan is fundamentally quantitative: it is rooted in quantity, as seen in weighing coins, measuring volumes, timing durations, and gauging lengths. Thus, the mizan is a rational instrument for the pure sciences of quantity. Moving to legal and religious matters, the mizan becomes the common attribute shared between a foundational case and its branches. This common attribute is the 'middle term' (al-hadd al-awsat). For example, the prohibition of grape wine due to intoxication leads to the prohibition of nabidh (date wine) because it shares the same cause (intoxication). This shared cause is the mizan that God placed in our minds. The passage then extends the mizan to rational sciences, stressing that universals are only valid when weighed through particulars. Ibn Taymiyya contrasts his view with al-Ghazali's five balances in al-Qistas al-Mustaqim, which he equates with Greek logic. He denies that rational meanings are universally shared; rather, they contain both truth and falsehood. Ibn Taymiyya accuses the logicians, including al-Ghazali, of falsely claiming that their logic is an infallible instrument.

Al-San'ani's Refutation of Logical Premises as Necessary for Knowledge of God
Al-San'ani refutes that logical premises are necessary for knowledge of God

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and analyze al-San'ani's argument against the necessity of logical premises for knowledge of God, using the Qur'anic hoopoe example as a key piece of evidence.

Core Idea
If a bird can achieve tawhid without logic, then humans certainly can.

Al-San'ani challenges the logicians' claim that formal premises are required for knowledge of God. He presents a striking example from the Qur'an: the hoopoe bird, which argued for God's oneness based on observable contingencies without any training in logic or kalam. This passage invites us to examine whether logical method is truly indispensable for certainty about God.

Guided Path
1
Identify the logicians' claim
Start by identifying the position that al-San'ani is attacking: that knowledge of God must be built on logical premises and theoretical methods. This is the target of his critique.
2
Examine the hoopoe example
Read the Qur'anic verse that al-San'ani cites (Surah al-Naml, verse 25). Notice that the hoopoe uses the temporality of rain and vegetation to argue for God's oneness. The hoopoe had no formal education in logic or kalam.
The verse is quoted as part of the argument.
3
Understand the implicit analogy
Al-San'ani draws an implicit analogy: if a bird's argument is accepted by Allah and considered good, then how can a rational human being be required to have more? The analogy shows that logical premises are not necessary for valid reasoning about God.
This is a key step in the critique.
4
Evaluate the strength of the counterexample
Consider whether the hoopoe's argument meets the logicians' criteria. It uses empirical observation and implicit syllogism (temporal things require a creator; rain and vegetation are temporal; therefore they have a creator). But it is not formalized. Al-San'ani argues that this informal reasoning suffices.
This challenges the necessity of formal ordering.

The Nature of Nazar and the Superiority of the Prophetic Method
Al-San'ani characterizes nazar as a contingent condition and defends the prophetic method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-San'ani's characterization of nazar as a contingent condition and distinguish his epistemological approach from that of the logicians.

Core Idea
Nazar is a contingent condition, not a mechanical cause of certainty.

After critiquing the necessity of logical premises, al-San'ani provides a positive account of how reflection (nazar) works. He describes it as a contingent condition (shart i'tibari) that does not guarantee certainty; rather, certainty is an act of God that may follow nazar, similar to how humility may follow prayer. He also emphasizes the role of necessary experience (tajriba daruriyya) and points to the practice of the Imams and Salaf as the superior method.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-San'ani's definition of nazar
Al-San'ani states that nazar is a contingent condition (shart i'tibari) according to the people of gnosis. This means it is not a necessary cause but a helpful context. Certainty occurs after it as a gift from God, like tenderness or humility.
Contrast this with the logicians' view that nazar necessarily produces certainty if properly ordered.
2
Understand the role of tajriba daruriyya
Al-San'ani claims that the basis of knowledge is necessary experience (tajriba daruriyya). He observes that righteous people who do not know the arrangement of premises often attain greater certainty and humility than theologians who do.
This empirical observation supports his view that formal method is not required.
3
Examine the evidence from the Imams and Salaf
Al-San'ani notes that the Imams of Ahl al-Bayt, including Imam Ali, did not use logical forms in their proofs for tawhid. Their method was simpler and more effective. The Salaf proceeded likewise.
This historical argument reinforces his epistemological claim.
4
Contrast the two styles
Al-San'ani concludes that there is a severe difference between the Muslim style (prophets, Imams, Salaf) and the Greek-influenced style of theologians. The former is preponderant because it is clear, while the latter is obscure and burdensome.
This is a value judgment based on effectiveness and accessibility.

As for the scholars of uṣūl al-dīn (the mutakallimūn), we said that the reason for their non-acceptance of Aristotelian logic is that they did not accept Aristotelian metaphysics, because it contradicts Islamic theology, and this Aristotelian logic is closely connected to metaphysics, and many of its principles are linked to its principles. For this reason, the mutakallimūn rejected it. And this idea is in fact one of the most precise ideas reached by Muslims, and it is sufficient to demolish Aristotelian logic from an Islamic point of view.

Commentary
This passage presents the second group of critics: the mutakallimūn (Islamic theologians). Their rejection of Aristotelian logic stems from its deep connection to Aristotelian metaphysics (ilāhiyyāt). Since they reject Aristotelian metaphysics for contradicting Islamic theology, they also reject its logic as inseparable. The author praises this insight as one of the most precise intellectual achievements of Muslims, sufficient to dismantle Aristotelian logic from an Islamic perspective.
