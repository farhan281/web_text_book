Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first two aspects of studying words in Islamic logic and explain their significance.

Core Idea
Words are studied from five aspects; the first two are indication and generality/specificity.

In this passage, the author sets the stage for studying words by outlining five aspects. The first two — indication (dalala) and generality/specificity (umum/khusus) — are introduced here. Understanding these aspects will help you navigate how Muslim logicians analyse language and how these concepts underpin later discussions in logic and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching topic
Find where the author states the starting point of linguistic studies. The passage begins by mentioning that these studies start with a famous topic: 'Indication of words on meanings'.

Extract the number of aspects

List the first aspect
The first aspect is 'from the perspective of the indication of the word on the meaning' (min nahiyat dalalat al-lafz 'ala al-ma'na). This is the foundational concern: how words signify concepts.
This topic is traditionally known as 'dalalat al-alfaz'.

List the second aspect
The second aspect is 'from the perspective of the division of the word into generality and specificity of meaning' (qismat al-lafz ila 'umum al-ma'na wa khususihi). This classifies words as general or particular in their application.
This is crucial for interpreting texts, especially in fiqh.

No Intermediate Between Existence and Non-Existence
Author critiques the concept of 'hal' as an intermediate between existence and non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical principle violated by the concept of 'hal' and distinguish the theologians' two explanations for the error.

Core Idea
The law of excluded middle leaves no room for a third state.

This passage from a classical logic text confronts the concept of 'hal' (state) which some theologians posited as an intermediate between existence and non-existence. The author argues that such a notion violates fundamental rational principles, specifically the principle of non-contradiction and the excluded middle. We will dissect the argument and see how the theologians resolved the issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thesis being attacked
Find the claim that there is an intermediate between existence and non-existence. The proponents of 'hal' assert a third state that is neither fully existent nor non-existent.
2
State the rational principle invoked
The author appeals to the principle that there is no intermediate between the affirmed and the negated (لا واسطة بين الثابت والمنفي). This is the law of excluded middle.
This principle is a basic intellectual axiom.
3
Examine the two explanations for the error
The theologians attributed the error to either sophistry (denial of the principle of non-contradiction) or a verbal reinterpretation that redefines 'existent' and 'non-existent' to create a pseudo-intermediate.
The verbal reinterpretation makes the dispute merely verbal.
4
Understand the defenders' response
Proponents of 'hal' deny positing an intermediate. They explain that 'hal' is not an independent existent but a rational attribute of an essence, thus not violating the principle.
They claim the 'hal' has derivative verification, not independent existence.

Ibn Taymiyya on Immediate Knowledge of Contradictories
Author applies Ibn Taymiyya's critique to the principles of non-contradiction and excluded middle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct Ibn Taymiyya's argument that knowledge of particular contradictories does not depend on a universal premise or syllogism.

Core Idea
Particular knowledge of contradictories is immediate – the universal premise is not necessary.

Ibn Taymiyya challenges the foundational claim of Aristotelian logic that demonstrative syllogisms are required for certain knowledge. In this passage, he applies his critique to the most basic logical principles: the law of non-contradiction and the principle of excluded middle for opposites. He argues that we know specific cases directly, without needing to frame them as syllogisms with universal premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principles under scrutiny
Notice that Ibn Taymiyya targets two principles: the law of non-contradiction for contradictories (al-naqidan) and the impossibility of coexistence for opposites (al-diddan). These are considered self-evident by logicians.
Contradictories cannot both be true nor both false; opposites cannot coexist but can both be absent.
2
Reconstruct the syllogism Ibn Taymiyya rejects
Write out the syllogism that would be needed to conclude that 'these two specific contradictories cannot coexist': Major premise: 'Every two contradictories cannot be together nor both absent.' Minor premise: 'These two are contradictories.' Conclusion: 'Therefore these two cannot be together nor both absent.'
Ibn Taymiyya argues we can know the conclusion without the major premise.
3
Analyze Ibn Taymiyya's claim of immediacy
He asserts that once we know the principle of non-contradiction (the law itself), we know the particular case directly. The universal premise is not needed as a separate step; the particular knowledge is more fundamental and epistemologically primary.
He does not deny the universal principle, but denies its necessity for deriving particular knowledge.
4
Generalize the argument to opposites
The same logic applies to opposites: we see that black and white cannot coexist in the same place at the same time without needing a universal premise. Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes that the particular knowledge is 'much more important' than the universal.
This reinforces his epistemological relativism: foundation is in the particular, not the universal.
5
Evaluate the broader implication
Ibn Taymiyya extends this critique to all demonstration, including proofs of God's existence and the world's contingency. His conclusion is that no demonstrative syllogism is necessary for knowledge – it can be attained directly through reason or revelation.
This is a radical challenge to the authority of Aristotelian logic in Islamic theology.

This directly challenges the Aristotelian doctrine that demonstration requires a universal premise. Ibn Taymiyya extends this critique to other philosophical arguments, such as the proof of 'states' (ahwal) in kalam and the demonstration of the world's origination and contingency. He concludes that no one actually needs the formal demonstrative syllogism to attain certain knowledge; the knowledge is possible without it. The passage thus reinforces Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological relativism and his rejection of the necessity of formal logic for certain knowledge.

Inference from Particular to Particular
Ibn Taymiyya's method of particular-to-particular inference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a particular-to-particular inference and verify its mutual entailment condition.

Core Idea
All inference starts with concrete particular instances, not abstract universals.

Ibn Taymiyya's epistemological method begins with the concrete: a specific cause and its specific effect. He argues that valid inference is between particular entities in the real world, not between mental concepts. This lesson unpacks that foundational step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particular instances
Pick a real-world example where one particular event (A) leads to another (B). For instance, the specific rising of the sun at a certain place and time leads to daytime at that same location.
Both A and B must be concrete, not abstract.
2
Check mutual entailment
Verify that from A's existence B necessarily exists, and from A's absence B is absent. That is, A and B are inseparably linked in reality.
This is not logical necessity but empirical constancy.
3
Apply to an Islamic science example
Consider the specific sighting of the crescent moon (hilal) at a given location and the start of Ramadan. This particular sighting entails that particular month start. The inference is from particular to particular.
In fiqh, each sighting has its own ruling.

The key is that universal inference derives from particular instances, since only particular individuals exist externally. This avoids abstract deduction and grounds inference in concrete reality.

Inference from Universal to Universal
Ibn Taymiyya's inclusion of universal-to-universal inference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish universal-to-universal inference from particular-to-particular and explain its derivative nature.

Core Idea
Universal inference is just the generalization of many particular entailments.

While Ibn Taymiyya emphasizes particular inference, he does not reject universal inference altogether. Instead, he treats it as a derivative form: when the same pattern of particular entailment repeats, we can state a universal rule. This lesson explores that transition and its application in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the transition from particular to universal
Identify a repeated pattern of particular-to-particular inference. For example, every time the sun rises, daytime follows. By induction, we can say 'whenever the sun rises, it is daytime' – a universal statement.
The universal inference is not a separate logical move; it is a condensation of repeated observations.
2
Match generality levels
Ensure that the universal conclusion matches the universal premise in generality. The example from the text: 'the genus of daytime' is co-extensive with 'the genus of sunrise'.
This is not inference from universal to particular; both terms are universal.
3
Apply to Islamic science
From many particular sightings of the crescent moon indicating the start of months, we can infer a universal rule: 'The sighting of the crescent moon indicates the beginning of a new lunar month.' This is a universal-to-universal inference.
In usul al-fiqh, this universal rule is used across different months and locations.

Conditional Syllogisms and the Relation of Evidence to Indicated
Author discusses conditional syllogisms and the relation of evidence to indicated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify conditional syllogisms as conjunctive or disjunctive and determine whether the relation between evidence and indicated is inclusion (ishtimal) or implication (istilzam).

Core Idea
Conditional syllogisms are not universal-to-particular but rest on inclusion or implication relations.

The commentator of al-Mawaqif raises a key question: Are conditional syllogisms a form of universal-to-particular reasoning? He argues they are not. Instead, the logical relation between the evidence (dalil) and the indicated (madlul) is either inclusion (ishtimal) as between genus and species, or implication (istilzam) as between cause and effect. In conjunctive conditionals, the relation is explicit implication; in disjunctive conditionals, it is implicit. Understanding this clarifies the epistemological foundation of these common argument forms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of conditional syllogism
Recognize the conjunctive (muttasil) and disjunctive (munfasil) forms. Conjunctive: 'If A then B'. Disjunctive: 'Either A or B'.
2
Examine the commentator's claim
The commentator denies that conditional syllogisms are reasoning from a universal to a particular. Instead, they involve a relation between the evidence (dalil) and the indicated (madlul).
3
Distinguish between ishtimal and istilzam
Ishtimal is inclusion (e.g., genus includes species). Istilzam is implication without inclusion (e.g., cause implies effect). Conjunctive conditionals involve explicit istilzam; disjunctive conditionals involve implicit istilzam.
4
Apply to an example
Take 'If the sun is rising, then daytime exists.' The relation is istilzam: sunrise implies daytime, but daytime is not included in sunrise. This is not a universal-to-particular deduction.

Suhrawardi's Redefinition of Contradiction
Suhrawardi's critique of Aristotelian contradiction conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Aristotelian and Suhrawardian definitions of contradiction and identify the logical implications of rejecting the quantity condition.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi: contradiction is negating exactly what you affirmed — no quantity or modality change.

The standard Aristotelian account of contradiction requires the two propositions to differ in quality (affirmation/negation) and, for quantified propositions, in quantity (universal vs. particular). This ninth condition prevents universal propositions from both being false (contrariety) and particular propositions from both being true (subcontrariety) in contingent matter. Suhrawardi rejects this condition, arguing that contradiction is simply the insertion of the negation particle without any other change. His view stems from his doctrine that the only proposition used in sciences is the necessary universal affirmative (al-batata). In this lesson, we examine the debate and its consequences for the logical square.

Guided Path
1
Identify the standard Aristotelian conditions
Aristotelian logicians list eight conditions for contradiction (subject, predicate, whole/part, etc.) and add a ninth for quantified propositions: the two propositions must differ in quantity (universal vs. particular). The contradictory of A is O, and of E is I.
These conditions ensure that contradictories cannot both be true and cannot both be false.
2
Understand why the ninth condition is necessary
Without the quantity condition, in contingent matter, two universal propositions can both be false (contrariety) and two particular propositions can both be true (subcontrariety). This violates the contradictory rule that one must be true and the other false.
Example: 'Every human is a writer' and 'No human is a writer' are both false in reality; they are contraries, not contradictories.
3
State Suhrawardi's alternative
Suhrawardi rejects the ninth and tenth (modality) conditions. He defines contradiction as simply inserting the negation particle without changing quantity or modality. For any proposition p, its contradictory is 'not p'.
This follows from his doctrine that scientific propositions are always necessary universal affirmatives (batata).
4
Evaluate the implications
Suhrawardi's view collapses the distinction between contradiction, contrariety, and subcontrariety. The Aristotelian square of opposition no longer holds; all immediate inferences based on the square become invalid under his system.
For example, 'Every human is a writer' (A) and 'Some human is not a writer' (O) are contradictories in the Aristotelian system. Suhrawardi would say the contradictory of A is 'Not every human is a writer', which is logically equivalent to O but does not involve a quantity change.

Why Contradiction Needs Both Quality and Quantity Difference
Author explains why quantity difference is necessary for contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish contradiction from contrariety and subcontrariety by applying the condition of quantity difference.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires difference in both quality and quantity; otherwise you get contrariety or subcontrariety.

In Islamic logic, the relationship between propositions is determined by their quality (affirmative/negative) and quantity (universal/particular). For two propositions to be true contradictories (tanāquḍ) — meaning they can never be true together and never false together — they must differ in both respects. The author explains that without the quantity condition, you may end up with contrariety (tadād), where both can be false, or subcontrariety (dukhūl taḥt al-taḍād), where both can be true. This lesson clarifies these distinctions using examples from Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the quality and quantity of each proposition
Determine if each proposition is universal affirmative (A), universal negative (E), particular affirmative (I), or particular negative (O).
2
Check if they differ in quality
One must be affirmative, the other negative. If not, they cannot be contradictory.
3
Check if they differ in quantity
One must be universal, the other particular. If not, they are either contraries (if both universal) or subcontraries (if both particular).
4
Apply the ruling
Contradictories cannot both be true nor both false; contraries cannot both be true but may both be false; subcontraries cannot both be false but may both be true.

To obtain a genuine contradictory of a particular proposition, one must reduce it to a universal proposition (e.g., by specifying the part and making it universal), which then has a contradictory differing only in quality. This passage explains Suhrawardi's unique position on the conversion of modal propositions, specifically the universal affirmative necessary. Suhrawardi's Conversion of Particular Negatives
Suhrawardi demonstrates two methods to convert a particular negative proposition despite Aristotelian rejection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Suhrawardi's two methods to convert a particular negative proposition.

Core Idea
Suhrawardi converts particular negatives by turning them into universal negatives or particular affirmatives.

In this passage, Suhrawardi challenges the Aristotelian view that particular negative propositions cannot be converted. He proposes two clever methods: one reduces the proposition to a universal negative by specifying a concrete instance, and the other reinterprets the negation as part of the predicate to yield a particular affirmative. We will walk through both methods and see how they expand the range of valid immediate inferences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Aristotelian position
The Aristotelians claim that a particular negative (Some A is not B) cannot be directly converted. Suhrawardi disagrees.
2
First method: specify the subject
Take 'Some animal is not human' and identify a specific kind of animal that is not human, e.g., horse. Then the proposition becomes 'No horse is a human,' which converts to 'No human is a horse.'
This changes the subject, so it is not a direct conversion but a reduction.
3
Second method: incorporate negation into predicate
Rewrite 'Some animal is not human' as 'Some animal is a non-human.' This is now a particular affirmative (Some A is B) with 'non-human' as predicate. Then convert to 'Some non-human is an animal.'
Suhrawardi considers this the true negation attached to the copula.
4
Compare the two methods
The first method yields a universal negative but loses the original subject. The second method preserves the quantification but changes the quality. Both achieve conversion.
