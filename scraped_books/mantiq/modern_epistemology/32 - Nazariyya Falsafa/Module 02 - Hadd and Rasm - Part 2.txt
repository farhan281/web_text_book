Recognizing Fitra Through Examples
Author illustrates fitra through instinctive behaviours

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify fitra as an innate guidance mechanism and provide two animal examples and two human examples from the passage.

Core Idea
Fitra is the innate compass guiding every creature to its purpose.

The concept of fitra can seem abstract, but this passage grounds it in vivid examples from the natural world and human experience. By observing how animals instinctively avoid eating their own kind and how an infant seeks its mother's milk, we can see a common thread: each creature is oriented toward its proper function. This lesson will help you recognize the pattern of fitra across different domains.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Statement on Fitra
Begin by locating the opening sentence that declares the principle: 'In the system of fitra, every creature is facilitated for what it was created for.' This is the thesis — all subsequent examples illustrate it.
2
List the Animal Examples
The passage mentions lions not eating their own species, cows as proverbially dull, dogs as intelligent, bees selecting specific flowers, lions eating meat while donkeys eat barley. Write down each example and what it reveals about that animal's innate programming.
3
List the Human Examples
At the human level, the author gives three: the infant's instinct to suckle, the universal recognition of water as a thirst-quencher, and food and clothing as necessities. Note how these are shared across all humans without teaching.
4
Synthesize the Common Principle
Ask: What do all these examples have in common? They show that each being has an inner guidance that directs it toward survival and purpose without external instruction. This is the essence of fitra as understood here.

Deviation and Elevation from the Laws of Fitra
Author defines deviation from fitra as misuse, not invalidation, and gives examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between misuse of fitra (inhiraf) and actual change of fitra, using examples from the text and Islamic ethical principles.

Core Idea
Deviation from fitra is misuse, not change; fitra remains intact.

The author clarifies that when humans deviate from fitra, they do not nullify or alter it. Instead, they misuse the innate faculties. This distinction is crucial for understanding human responsibility and the immutability of fitra. The passage uses vivid examples from archery, tools, and the story of the Lot people to illustrate how fitra can be misapplied but never invalidated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's main claim about deviation
Locate the sentence where the author states that deviation is not an invalidation (إبطال) but a misuse (استعمال). This is the core logical distinction.
The key Arabic phrase: 'فليس إبطالا لحكمها بل استعمالا لها في غير ما ينبغي'
2
Analyze the first example: the archer
Consider the archer missing the target. The bow and conditions are naturally for hitting, but misuse causes error. Does the bow change? No. Similarly, fitra's ruling is not changed, only its application.
3
Analyze the second example: tools set crookedly
Knives and saws set crookedly still perform their natural function (cutting, sawing) but not as intended. This shows that the natural function (fitra) remains, but the outcome is distorted by improper setup.
Note the phrase: 'يعمل عملها الذي فطرت عليه بعينه'
4
Distinguish between misuse and impossible deviation
The author contrasts 'deviation from the natural operation' (like sewing with a saw) as impossible. This highlights that fitra cannot be fundamentally reversed; only its proper application can be missed.
This reinforces that fitra is immutable.
5
Apply the distinction to the example of the Lot people
The Lot people reversed natural desire. The author says they 'opposed fitra' but did not change fitra itself. Fitra remains the standard. Identify how this fits the pattern of misuse.
This is a real Islamic historical example used to illustrate the concept.

Commentary
This passage defines deviation (inhiraf) and elevation (sumuww) in relation to fitra (innate nature). The author begins by stating that observed deviation is not a nullification of fitra's ruling but rather a misuse. Two examples are given: first, an archer missing the target is not a flaw in the bow but in its use; similarly, tools like saws and knives set crookedly still perform their natural function but incorrectly. The second example is more extreme: using a saw to sew—this would be using the tool for what it is not designed for. The text then emphasizes that fitra itself cannot be invalidated; only proper use is mistaken. The author then cites Quran 95:4-5 to show man's original creation in best form, but deviation leads to downfall. A specific example is the Lot people who inverted natural sexual desire. The key point is that violating fitra's ruling is not the same as changing fitra itself—fitra remains a self-evident proof. Other examples include incest. The author then likens fitra to a genetic gene that cannot be modified, though it may become diseased. Finally, elevation (sumuww) is introduced: humans can rise above fitra's laws by altruism or self-sacrifice, which transcend basic instincts.

Author explains the Quranic terms sabgha and mithaq as concepts related to fitra.

Core Idea
Sabgha and mithaq are Quranic metaphors for fitra, the indelible divine imprint on human nature.

The Quran uses vivid terms to describe the innate human disposition towards tawhid. Two such terms—sabgha (dye) and mithaq (covenant)—are explored in this passage. Understanding these terms deepens our grasp of fitra as an unchangeable, God-given nature.

Guided Path

Analyze the simile of sabgha
The author likens sabgha to a permanent dye that cannot be erased. This signifies that fitra is an unremovable mark from Allah that distinguishes humans from beasts.
Consider why the author uses the imagery of dye rather than paint.
3
Contrast sabgha with Christian baptism
The author mentions that Christians dip their children in baptism water and call it sabgha, then the Quran asserts that Allah's sabgha is superior. This contrast corrects a corrupted understanding and reinforces the unique Islamic concept.

Synthesize both terms as expressions of fitra
Both sabgha and mithaq illustrate that humans are born with an innate disposition towards tawhid. The author concludes that humans are raised upon the religion of sound reflection (din al-tafkir al-salim).
This synthesis shows how the Quran uses multiple metaphors to convey the same core concept.

Definition of Guidance (al-Hidaya) and its Metaphor of Light
Author defines guidance linguistically and explains Quranic use of light as metaphor for guidance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'al-hidaya' as a linguistic term and explain why the Quran uses 'light' as a metaphor for guidance.

Core Idea
Guidance is gentle indication; light is its perfect metaphor.

In this lesson, we examine the linguistic definition of 'guidance' (al-hidāya) as 'gentle indication' (dalāla bi-luṭf). The author clarifies common misconceptions about Quranic usage, such as 'guidance to Hell', and then shows how the Quran consistently uses light imagery to represent guidance. Understanding this metaphor is essential for interpreting key Quranic verses about Allah being the 'Light of the heavens and the earth'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Find the definiendum 'al-hidāya' in the Arabic. What is its root? Notice it is derived from the same root as 'hadiyya' (gift) and 'hawādī' (leaders).
The root is h-d-y.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The definition is 'gentle indication'. 'Indication' (dalāla) is the genus, and 'gentleness' (luṭf) is the differentia. What does 'gentle' add to mere indication?
Gentle indication implies leading with care, not force.
3
Analyze the linguistic derivation
See how the author links guidance to gift (hadiyya) and the leaders of wild beasts (hawādī al-waḥsh). What do these have in common? They all involve showing the way gently.
The connection reinforces the core idea of gentle leading.
4
Understand the Quranic metaphor
The author cites verses where guidance is called light. Identify the common function: both guidance and light enable reaching a desired goal. How does this metaphor help explain verses like 'Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth'?
Light is not literal but metaphorical for guidance.
5
Apply the definition to problematic verses
How can 'guidance to Hell' be consistent with gentle indication? The author says it is used mockingly, similar to 'give tidings of painful torment'. Explain the rhetorical device of mockery (tahakkum).
This shows the definition is flexible enough to include ironical usage.

You will see how the author uses Quranic verses and hadith to support this, and how the heart can be sealed as a divine punishment. Sources of Error and Al-Sadr's Inductive Solution
Author discusses types of error and proposes inductive method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between illusion (wahm) and error (khata') and explain how induction can reduce error according to al-Sadr.

Core Idea
Error arises from imbalance; induction reduces error by accumulating probabilities.

Having established the mizan as the criterion, the author now examines how errors occur. He distinguishes between intentional deviation (wahm) and unintentional error (khata'), and explores three accounts of error's origin. Finally, he presents al-Sadr's inductive method as a way to approach certainty.

Guided Path
1
Define wahm and khata'
Wahm is intentional or unintentional deviation from the path; khata' is unintentional error due to speed of the mizan or faulty data.
2
List the three proposed sources of error
(a) poor application of logic, (b) faulty premises in syllogisms (theoretical materials), (c) imbalance in the soul where certainty exceeds perception.
Each answer focuses on a different aspect.
3
Understand al-Sadr's inductive method
Instead of relying solely on Aristotelian syllogism, al-Sadr advocates induction and accumulation of probabilities. As probabilities multiply, the chance of error diminishes to near zero, producing certainty.
This is a mathematical practical method.
4
Compare induction with the mizan
Both aim to reduce error: the mizan does so by balanced judgment, induction by probabilistic accumulation. They complement each other.
The mizan is the internal criterion; induction is a methodological tool.

Defining Memory as a Faculty of the Soul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus and differentia in the author's definition of memory (al-dhakira) and distinguish it from mere retention (hifz).

Core Idea
Memory is not just storage—it is a faculty of the soul that preserves knowledge and enables understanding.

The author begins with striking everyday examples—returning home, selecting items in a store, recognizing a perfume—to illustrate that memory holds vast amounts of information. He then defines memory as a faculty (hay'a) of the soul, similar to retention (hifz), and explains that nerve cells do not divide, allowing lifelong preservation. In this lesson, you will dissect this definition to see exactly what makes memory a distinct power of the soul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined: al-dhakira (memory). Note its linguistic root and the author's shift from illustrations to formal definition.
The author asks 'What is memory?' before providing the definition.
2
Extract the genus
The genus (jins) is 'a faculty of the soul' (hay'a li'l-nafs). This places memory in the category of psychological powers, not a physical organ.
This distinguishes the Islamic psychological approach from modern neuroscience.
3
Extract the differentia
The differentia (fasl) is 'by which a person can preserve the knowledge he acquires and establish what leads to understanding.' The key functions are preservation (hifz) and establishment (tathbit) for understanding.
The author explicitly links memory to knowledge acquisition and comprehension.
4
Compare with retention (hifz)
The author says memory is 'like retention (hifz).' Note the similarity but also the subtle distinction: hifz is fixing the image against change, while memory is the broader faculty.
This shows the author is aware of technical distinctions even in a pedagogical context.
5
Test the definition's completeness
Does the definition include all instances of memory? Does it exclude non-memory faculties? Check if it covers both weak and strong memory, and whether it excludes forgetting.
The author later discusses weak/strong memory, so the definition is broad enough.

Insights (Basa'ir) as the Criterion of Truth
Focus on the fourth source: insights (basa'ir) as criterion of truth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of basa'ir as self-evident propositions inspired by God, and demonstrate how they function as the criterion for testing truth claims in Islamic epistemology.

Core Idea
Insights (basa'ir) are divinely inspired self-evident truths that serve as the ultimate touchstone for all knowledge.

The author singles out 'insights' (basa'ir) as a cornerstone of the Quranic epistemology. This lesson unpacks that concept in detail: what they are, how they are acquired, and why they are the final arbiter of truth. Understanding basa'ir helps you see how Islamic thought grounds certainty in divinely furnished axioms rather than pure reason alone.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of basa'ir
In the Arabic text, find the sentence that describes basa'ir as 'the cause of intelligibles' and 'the criterion and cornerstone'. Note that they are 'self-evident propositions' found in the depth of the intellect through inspiration.
The term 'basa'ir' is plural; singular is 'basira'. It is derived from 'basara' (to see clearly).
2
Identify the epistemological function
Basa'ir are used to test propositions: when uncertain, a person refers a claim to these self-evident truths. If the claim agrees with them, it is true; if not, false. They are not themselves proven but are assented to without proof.
This is similar to the concept of 'badāha' (self-evidence) in logic, but with a divine origin.
3
Compare with other sources
Unlike sensory knowledge (superficial) or intellectual reasoning (which uses basa'ir), the insights themselves are pre-rational and directly inspired. They are universal to all humans, not limited to prophets or scholars.
The author says 'equipped to all humans', indicating a kind of innate common sense.
4
Apply to a sample claim
Take a theological claim: 'God is just.' According to the passage, how would you test it? By comparing it with the self-evident proposition that 'justice is good', which is a basira. If the claim aligns, it is true.
This method avoids endless regress: the foundation is immediate and divinely given.
