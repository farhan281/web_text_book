Author defines Sufi theory and traces term origin.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the most probable etymology of the term 'Sufi' according to the passage and explain its connection to asceticism.

Core Idea
The term 'Sufi' stems from wool-wearing asceticism, not foreign influence.

How did the term 'Sufi' originate? This passage explores several theories and settles on the most historically grounded one: the link to coarse wool (suf) worn by early ascetics. Understanding this etymology is key to grasping the Sufi emphasis on renunciation and direct experiential knowledge (ma'rifa).

Guided Path
1
List the proposed etymologies
Identify the four theories mentioned in the passage: derivation from 'suf' (wool), from 'safa' (purity), from the Banu Sufa tribe, and from the practice of wearing wool as an ascetic sign.
2
Evaluate the evidence for each
Check which theory is supported by textual evidence: the passage notes that wearing wool was common among ascetics in the first two centuries AH. The other theories lack such textual backing.
The Banu Sufa theory is pre-Islamic; the 'safa' theory is etymologically plausible but less documented.
3
Identify the author's preferred view
The author concludes by supporting Ibn al-Sarraj: 'Sufi' is derived from 'suf' (wool), as wearing wool was a sign of zuhd (asceticism).
4
Connect to the Sufi theory of knowledge
Reflect on how this etymology relates to the goal of union with God (al-ittihad). The ascetic practice of wearing wool symbolizes purification from worldly distractions, which is a prerequisite for mystical knowledge.
The passage states the Sufi theory of knowledge is part of the Sufi creed.

Commentary
This passage defines the Sufi theory of knowledge (often called the theory of union with God) and explores the etymology of the term 'Sufi'. The author begins by citing Ibn al-Sarraj's explanation that the name comes from wearing wool (suf), which was the practice of prophets and saints. Alternative derivations are mentioned: some trace it to 'safa' (purity), others to the tribe Banu Sufa who served the Kaaba. The author favors the wool-origin view because wearing coarse wool was a common sign of asceticism in early Islamic centuries. The earliest recorded use of the term 'Sufi' is attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan al-Kufi in the late second century AH. Finally, the author states that the Sufi theory of knowledge is part of the broader Sufi worldview. This passage sets the historical and linguistic context for understanding Sufi epistemology, which emphasizes direct experiential knowledge (ma'rifa) through spiritual purification and union with the Divine. In Islamic logic and theology, this theory is contrasted with rationalist methods; the Sufi claim to knowledge through unveiling (kashf) is often critiqued by logicians and theologians who insist on demonstrative proof (burhan).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract and evaluate an author's scholarly credentials from a biographical passage in an Islamic academic work.

Core Idea
An author's biography reveals the intellectual lineage and authority behind a text.

When you pick up a book on Islamic epistemology or any Islamic science, the first thing you should examine is the author's biography. This passage from 'Epistemology between the Quran and Philosophy' introduces us to Abdul-Zahra Turki Al-Fatlawi. By learning to parse such biographies, you will be able to assess the reliability and perspective of a work before diving into its content.

Guided Path

Look for the author's name and place of birth. Here it is Abdul-Zahra Turki Al-Fatlawi, born in Najaf, Iraq. This situates him in a specific scholarly tradition (the Najaf Hawza).
2
Extract academic qualifications and memberships
Note the highest degree (Master's in Islamic Sciences) and professional affiliations (Arab Writers Union, etc.). These indicate his scholarly community and the standards he submits to.
3
Categorize the list of publications
The passage divides works into 'religious books' and 'historical books'. Pay attention to titles that hint at the author's expertise: epistemology, Quranic science, comparative studies, etc.
4
Assess the range and focus of the author's output
Notice the breadth: from Quran and philosophy to football rulings, from ancient writing tools to Iraqi tribes. This suggests a wide-ranging scholar, but also note which fields he prioritizes.
5
Identify the relevance to your study
Since this biography introduces a book on epistemology, you can see that the author has both religious and philosophical interests, making his perspective on Quranic epistemology particularly informed.
