Self-Refutation of Dialectical Theory
Author questions whether dialectical theory itself is absolute or relative, leading to a paradox.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and construct a self-refutation argument against any relativist claim, using the dialectical theory as a case study.

Core Idea
A relativist theory that denies absolute truth is self-refuting if it claims absolute truth for itself.

This passage presents a powerful logical critique: if the dialectical theory says all truths change, then that statement itself must change. But its proponents treat it as a fixed law. This is a classic self-refutation that shows the necessity of absolute truths, such as those in Islamic creed.

Guided Path
1
Extract the dialectical principle
Identify the claim that all truths are subject to change. The passage states: 'the law of dialectic necessitates that truths are always changing.'
This is the universal relativist claim.
2
Apply the principle to itself
Ask: Is the dialectical theory itself a truth? If so, it must be subject to change according to its own law.
Self-reference is key.
3
Check the consequence
The passage notes that the dialectical theory has remained constant for decades. Therefore, if it were a changing truth, it should have changed, but it hasn't.
This is a modus tollens structure: if changing then change; no change → not changing.
4
Identify the contradiction
The theory claims to be an unchanging law while its content says all truths change. This is self-refuting: it cannot consistently be both.
The dilemma: if absolute, it contradicts its own relativism; if relative, it must change but doesn't.

The Self-Refutation of Relativism
Author shows that the law 'all truths change' cannot apply to itself without contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a self-refuting argument and apply the dilemma of self-reference to critique claims of absolute relativism in Islamic thought.

Core Idea
A principle that claims all truths change cannot itself be true without contradiction.

This passage demonstrates a powerful logical tool: the self-refutation argument. The author takes the dialectical claim that 'all truths change' and applies it to itself, forcing the proponents into a contradiction. This type of critique is essential in Islamic theology and philosophy to defend the possibility of absolute truths, such as the existence of Allah and the truth of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal claim
Find the statement being criticized: 'All truths are subject to constant change and development.' This is the dialectical law.
2
Apply the claim to itself
Ask: does this claim itself change or not? That is, is it a relative truth or an absolute truth?
This is the key move in self-refutation arguments.
3
Consider both horns of the dilemma
If proponents say it is a changing truth, then it should have changed by now, but it has remained stable for decades — contradiction. If they say it is an absolute truth, then they admit absolute truths exist, contradicting their own doctrine.
Either way, their position is untenable.
4
Conclude self-refutation
Since the dialectical law cannot be consistently applied to itself, it is self-refuting. Therefore, the claim that all truths change is false.
This is a standard objection against any form of total relativism.

The Dialectical Rejection of Compulsion and Delegation
Dialectical refutation of jabr and tafwid with a Quranic middle path.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dialectical exchange on jabr vs. tafwid from the Imams' statements and identify the middle position of divine authorization.

Core Idea
The Islamic position rejects both jabr and tafwid, affirming a middle path of divine authorization.

This passage engages the classical debate on free will by first presenting the extremes of compulsion (jabr) and delegation (tafwid). The author then cites the Imams to show why both extremes fail and what the middle path looks like. Your task is to trace the logical structure of this refutation and synthesize the alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two extreme positions
In the passage, the initial question contrasts 'mujbar' (compelled) with 'mufawwad' (delegated). Locate these terms and note how the author frames the debate as a binary.
Jabr means God controls all actions; tafwid means humans control all actions independently.
2
Extract Imam Ali's argument against jabr
Imam Ali lists consequences: reward/punishment, command/prohibition, praise/blame would be invalid. If jabr were true, the moral order collapses. This is a reductio ad absurdum.
The argument shows that jabr is self-defeating because it undermines the very concepts that make religion meaningful.
3
Analyze the threefold classification of actions
Imam Ali divides deeds into obligations, virtues, and sins. Notice how God's command and will relate to each: obligations are by command and will, virtues by will only, sins by neither. This nuances the relationship between divine control and human agency.
This classification prevents attributing evil to God while maintaining His sovereignty.
4
Understand Imam al-Sadiq's response
When asked about compulsion or delegation, he denies both and says 'lutf' (grace) between them. Identify what 'lutf' implies: a middle ground where God grants freedom but within boundaries.
The term 'lutf' is key: it avoids the extremes and points to a nuanced relationship.
5
Synthesize the middle position
Combine the refutation of extremes with the positive statements. The passage concludes that divine authorization (takhwil) means God sets commands and prohibitions (the 'compulsion' of law) while leaving humans free to choose within that framework (the 'delegation' of action).
This is the Islamic via media: neither fatalism nor libertarianism.

The answer thus avoids both extremes, positing a middle path of 'lutf' (grace).

The Analogical Argument for Divine Authorization
Analogical reasoning to illustrate the middle position on free will.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the analogical structure of the parable of the servant and evaluate its strength in supporting the middle position on free will.

Core Idea
The parable of the servant models divine authorization: God gives man freedom within boundaries, just as a master tests his servant.

The author uses a concrete analogy to clarify the abstract theological middle path. By examining the parable of the servant, you will learn how analogical reasoning (tamthil) functions in Islamic theology and how it supports the concept of divine authorization (takhwil). Focus on the components of the analogy and assess its persuasiveness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases being compared
The parable compares a human master-servant relationship to God-human relationship. In the parable: a master gives money to a servant to spend freely in the market. The servant is being tested. In the divine case: God gives humans freedom to act in the world, with commands and prohibitions as boundaries.
The master's purpose is to test honesty; God's purpose is to test human obedience and faith.
2
Extract the common feature (the 'illa or ground of resemblance)
Both the master and God grant a degree of freedom while setting conditions and retaining ultimate judgment. The common feature is 'authorization with accountability': the agent is free to act but will be held responsible.
The phrase 'التخويل الالهي بشرطه' (divine authorization with its condition) captures this common feature.
3
Determine the conclusion drawn
The author concludes that human free will is real but limited: humans are not compelled (jabr) nor fully independent (tafwid), but authorized (mukhawwal) to act within the framework of divine law. This is the middle path.
The conclusion is explicitly stated: 'جبر يتمثل بمجموع الأوامر والنواهي ... وتفويض يتمثل بحرية التسوق'.
4
Evaluate the strength of the analogy
Consider differences: the human master may not know the outcome with certainty, but God knows. The parable's servant is unaware of being tested? Actually the servant knows the master's commands. Does the analogy fully capture God's omniscience? Assess whether the analogy successfully illustrates the middle path or if it breaks down at any point.
The author anticipates this: the master knows the outcome ('وانت تعلم') but does not interfere. This preserves divine knowledge while allowing free action.
5
Apply the analogy to the whole passage
Notice how the parable is placed after the dialectical refutation. It serves as a concrete illustration of the 'lutf' (grace) that Imam al-Sadiq mentioned. The analogy translates the abstract theological concept into a relatable example.
This step connects the analogical reasoning to the broader argument of the passage.

The author concludes that compulsion is the set of divine commands and prohibitions, while delegation is the freedom to act within this framework, citing Quran 53:39-40 that man receives only what he strives for. The answer thus avoids both extremes, positing a middle path of 'lutf' (grace).

Checkpoint
According to the parable of the servant, what does the master's giving of money to the servant represent in the context of divine action?
A) God's compulsion of all human actions.
B) God's delegation of all affairs without any guidance.
C) God's authorization of humans to act freely within set boundaries.
D) God's abandonment of humans to their own devices.

Key Takeaway
The analogical argument shows that divine authorization is not abdication but a test of human responsibility within a framework of commands and prohibitions, preserving both divine sovereignty and human accountability.
