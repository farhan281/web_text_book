Critique of Sense-Empiricism: Eight Arguments
Eight arguments refuting sense-empiricism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate eight distinct arguments against sense-empiricism as a comprehensive theory of knowledge.

Core Idea
Sensory knowledge is passive and superficial; reason is required for meaningful understanding.

This passage systematically dismantles the claim that all knowledge comes from the senses. The author presents eight arguments, from the mirror analogy to the memory problem, showing that emotional, rational, and metaphysical concepts cannot be reduced to sensory data. Understanding these critiques sharpens your ability to defend rational and intuitive knowledge in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the mirror and camera analogies
Examine arguments 1 and 2: sensory images are received passively like a mirror or camera, yet a mirror does not 'know'. Also, emotional concepts (love, beauty) cannot be pictorially represented.
These analogies highlight the difference between mere reflection and active understanding.
2
Compare human and animal perception
Consider argument 3: animals share and even surpass human senses, yet lack human understanding. Sensory knowledge is superficial and shared; it cannot account for distinctively human cognition.
This shows that sensory input alone is insufficient for higher knowledge.
3
Identify irreducible rational concepts
Examine arguments 4 and 5: concepts like causality, substance, and primary axioms (e.g., whole > part) are not derived from sensation. Experience itself depends on prior rational principles, like a doctor's exam requiring prior knowledge.
These a priori principles are essential for organizing sensory data.
4
Evaluate counterexamples and the memory problem
Arguments 6-8: dreams, illusions, and mental illness show sensation can be false. Memory cannot be explained by mere sensory reflection. Finally, Locke's internal contradiction is noted.
These undermine the reliability and completeness of sense-empiricism.

Commentary
This passage is a sustained critique of sense-empiricism (al-nazariyya al-hissiyya), the theory that all knowledge derives from sensory experience. The author presents eight numbered arguments against it. The first argument uses the analogy of a mirror: sensory images reflect in the mind like a mirror, but a mirror does not 'know'; it merely receives surfaces. Moreover, sensory input is raw material that requires processing, like a tree transforming water and light into fruit. The mind must do more than passively receive. The second argument asks whether emotional and moral qualities (love, hatred, generosity, etc.) can be pictorially represented in the brain. Since they cannot, sense-empiricism fails to account for them. The third argument notes that animals share our senses and often surpass them (e.g., a dog's smell, a falcon's sight), yet they lack human understanding. Hence sensory knowledge is superficial and shared, not sufficient for distinctively human cognition. The fourth argument lists concepts irreducible to sense: cause/effect, substance/accident, unity/plurality, existence/non-existence. Even simple perception of a falling pen requires non-sensory analysis to grasp causality. Likewise, rational axioms (whole > part, event needs cause) and mathematical truths (2+2=4) are not derived from sensation. The fifth argument concedes the importance of experience but insists its value depends on prior rational principles, just as a doctor's examination is useless without prior knowledge. The sixth point highlights counterexamples to sensory reliability: dreams, mental illness, and illusions show that sensation often lacks truth value. The seventh argument raises the problem of memory: how can storage and retrieval of countless images be explained by mere sensory reflection? This is a major challenge for empiricists. Finally, the eighth argument points out a contradiction in John Locke's thought: he valued contemplative and emotional knowledge while grounding all knowledge in sensation. The author thus systematically undermines the foundations of sense-empiricism, arguing for the necessity of innate rational principles and higher cognitive faculties.

Distinguishing Objective and Ideal Reality: Critique of Idealism
Author distinguishes objective reality from ideal reality and critiques idealism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish objective reality from ideal reality and explain the logical consequences of idealism using the author's arguments.

Core Idea
Idealism makes communication meaningless – a logical absurdity.

In this passage, the author sharpens a crucial epistemological distinction and then applies it to demolish idealism. Understanding this distinction is not just academic; it safeguards the very possibility of knowledge and dialogue, which are foundational in Islamic sciences like usul al-fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path

3
Understand the two issues

4

Consider the example of a person lacking sight and hearing. Their ideas would differ from a normal person's, showing that ideas depend on sensory input, undermining the idealist claim that all ideas are purely mental constructs.

Descartes on Innate Ideas vs Islamic Fitra
Author presents Descartes' theory of innate ideas and his method of doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Descartes' concept of innate ideas from the Islamic concept of fitra and evaluate the epistemological assumptions of each.

Core Idea
Descartes grounds certainty in innate ideas; Islam grounds knowledge in revelation and fitra as a disposition, not content.

Descartes famously argued that certain ideas, like the idea of God, are innate and not derived from experience. This passage presents his method of doubt and his conclusion. However, Islamic epistemology offers a different account of fitra. In this lesson, we will compare the two, drawing on classical Islamic sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sources of ideas in Descartes' account
In the passage, Descartes states that ideas come from sensation (al-ihsas) and innate disposition (al-fitrat). Note that he uses 'fitra' to mean innate ideas, not the Islamic concept of a natural disposition.
Notice the shift in meaning of fitra.
2
Extract Descartes' argument for the innateness of the idea of God
Descartes argues that the idea of God is too perfect to be produced by the finite human mind; therefore it must be innate and placed by God. The passage says: 'This mental phenomenon, with its perfection, cannot have the mind as its source; rather, its cause must be more perfect than the soul and mind.'
This is a causal argument based on the principle that the cause must be at least as perfect as the effect.
3
Contrast with the Islamic concept of fitra
In Islam, fitra is an innate disposition towards tawhid (monotheism), not specific ideas. Knowledge of God comes through revelation and rational reflection, not from pre-loaded concepts. The passage's use of 'fitra' differs from the classical Islamic usage.
For example, the Quran says: 'So direct your face toward the religion, inclining to truth. [Adhere to] the fitra of Allah upon which He has created [all] people.' (30:30) Fitra here is a disposition, not content.
4
Examine epistemological implications
Descartes uses the guarantee of a non-deceiving God to assert that innate ideas are true. In Islam, truth is established through revelation (wahy) and sound reason, not through innate ideas. The self-evident truths (badihiyyat) in Islamic logic are known by necessity, not because they are innate in the Cartesian sense.
Al-Ghazali, in his critique of the philosophers, argues that knowledge of God is acquired through the senses and reason, not innate.

13 - The Theory of Stimulus and Response, also called Behaviorism. Among the pioneers of this theory are the Russian physiologist Pavlov (1849–1936) and the American psychologist Thorndike (1874–1949). It is one of the most famous theories of physiology in analyzing or explaining knowledge. Before we explain it, we will review Gates' definition of learning. He says: 'Learning can be defined as a progressive change in behavior characterized on one hand by continuous adaptation to the situation and on the other hand by repeated efforts made by the individual to respond to this situation with a fruitful response.' In other words, knowledge is acquiring ways that satisfy motives and achieve goals. The stimulus M is connected to the response S or leads to it. The environment contains sources of energy that affect the senses of living beings, and we call these exciting sources 'stimuli'. Light waves fall on the eye and it sees, sound waves fall on the ear and it hears, chemical stimuli fall on the tongue and we taste. These responses are innate. As for learning, man approaches these stimuli and examines them, then organizes the relationships between stimuli and responses, and thus is the art of learning. 'The physiological tools we call conditioned reflexes and instincts are located in the brain centers beneath the cortex and in the spinal cord according to their degree of complexity, and they provide innate responses to environmental stimuli.' It has been established through experiments and tests that the cerebral cortex is divided into multiple brain regions that contribute to varying degrees. Through dissection laboratories and experiments, specific areas have been identified for sight, hearing, speech, movement, and neurological deficits. There are brain cells specialized for sounds, colors, smells, etc.; nerve messages reach them from various environmental influences. The physical influences are transformed into physiological ones upon reaching the brain centers, operating by way of stimulus and response.

Hearing's superiority is argued through Quranic emphasis, physiological complexity, and historical absence of deaf geniuses.

This passage tackles a central question in Islamic epistemology: which sense is most critical for acquiring knowledge? The author systematically assembles evidence from divine revelation, modern physiology, and human history to argue that hearing surpasses sight. This argument from anatomy is used to reinforce the scriptural evidence, showing harmony between revelation and empirical science.
5
Assess the historical observation
The author claims that history records no deaf scholars or geniuses but many blind ones, implying that hearing is more critical for intellectual achievement. This is an inductive generalization from historical records.
Consider potential counterexamples or whether this observation is exhaustive.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument regarding the acquisition of knowledge through the senses, specifically comparing hearing and sight. The author begins by stating that man is born ignorant and gains knowledge through external sensory organs, particularly hearing and sight. He notes that the Qur'an focuses on these two senses as primary examples, without neglecting smell and taste. He then cites numerous Qur'anic verses that mention hearing and sight, showing their importance. The key thesis is that hearing is superior to sight. The author claims that this was not understood scientifically until the late 19th century, when physiologists discovered that hearing is more complex and crucial because deafness disconnects one from the universe, while blindness only loses the form of things. Additional evidence: hearing remains active during sleep, while other senses rest. Historically, there are many blind geniuses but no deaf ones. The Qur'an's harsh treatment of the deaf versus its leniency toward the blind further supports this. The concluding verse redirects blindness to the heart, emphasizing spiritual insight. The argument uses inductive reasoning from Qur'anic texts and empirical observations, but not a formal syllogism.

Author presents al-Sadr's inductive proposal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe al-Sadr's proposal to use induction and probability accumulation as a replacement for Aristotelian syllogism to achieve certainty.

Core Idea
Induction with accumulated probabilities can yield certainty comparable to demonstrative proof.

Sayyid Muhammad Baqir al-Sadr proposed a novel solution to the problem of error: replace Aristotelian syllogistic reasoning with a method of induction and probability accumulation. This lesson unpacks his proposal and its implications for Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The author notes that Aristotelian syllogisms (al-qiyas al-ursuti) can lead to error, especially when premises are not self-evident. Al-Sadr offers an alternative.

2
Describe the method
Al-Sadr's method uses mathematical induction: as evidence accumulates, counter-probabilities diminish until negligible. The soul then cancels the probability of alternative, treating the result as certain.
This is a cumulative approach, not a single deduction.
3
Evaluate the claim of certainty
The passage asserts that this process yields 'demonstrative certainty' (yaqin burhani). Consider whether probability-based induction can truly match deductive certainty.
This has been a key discussion in Islamic epistemology.
