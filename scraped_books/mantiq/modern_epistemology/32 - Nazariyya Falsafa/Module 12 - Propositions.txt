Three Types of Certain Propositions: Awwaliyyat, Mushahadat, Tajribiyyat
Author classifies certain propositions into six types and describes the first three.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between awwaliyyat, mushahadat, and tajribiyyat, and correctly classify examples of each.

Core Idea
First principles are self-evident; sense perceptions rely on observation; experimental truths require repetition.

In Islamic logic, certain propositions are foundational because they require no proof or are derived from evident experience. This lesson unpacks the first three types: those known immediately by the intellect, those known through the senses, and those known through repeated experimentation. Understanding these categories helps you evaluate the epistemic strength of any claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of certainty
For any proposition, ask: Is it known immediately? By a single sense experience? Or only after repeated observations? This determines its type.
2
Check for self-evidence
If the proposition is like 'the whole is greater than the part', it is awwaliyya. No experience or external cause is needed.
Such propositions are called ‘first principles’ (awwaliyyat).
3
Check for sensory mediation
If the proposition involves a direct sense perception (external or internal), it is mushahada. For example, judging that the sun is bright involves external sight; feeling pain involves internal sense.
Internal sense perceptions are also called wijdaniyyat.
4
Check for repetition
If the proposition results from repeated observation that establishes a certain generalisation, it is tajribiyya. Example: ‘Iron expands by heat’ after multiple experiments.
This is not mere sense perception but a conclusion from repeated experiences.

Sections of certainties: The self-evident certain propositions that the intellect believes in without demanding proof are divided into six types: (1) First principles (awwaliyyat): They are propositions that the intellect affirms by themselves, i.e., without any external cause for themselves. The intellect affirms them immediately and judges them true, such as our saying 'The whole is greater than the part' or 'Two contradictories cannot coexist.' (2) Sense perceptions (mushahadat): They are propositions that the intellect judges by means of sense. Sense is of two types: external and internal. So the judgment that the sun is bright and fire is hot, the intellect judges by means of external sense, just as the knowledge that we have pain, pleasure, hunger, and thirst, the intellect perceives it by means of internal sense. This is called 'wijdaniyyat' in psychology. The ruler is the intellect by means of one of the two senses. Accordingly, the meaning of 'mushahada' is observation by external or internal sense. (3) Experimental truths (tajribiyyat): They are one of the certainties that the intellect judges by means of repeated observation, which causes a judgment to become established in the soul without doubt, such as our saying 'Iron expands by heat' after we have experienced this state several times.

Commentary
This passage presents the classification of certain propositions (yaqiniyyat) into six types, but only the first three are described here. The author begins by defining them as 'self-evident certain propositions that the intellect believes without demanding proof.' The first type, 'awwaliyyat' (first principles), are those that the intellect affirms immediately by themselves, with examples like 'the whole is greater than the part' and 'two contradictories cannot coexist.' The second type, 'mushahadat' (sense perceptions), are judgments made via the senses, which are subdivided into external senses (e.g., seeing that the sun is bright) and internal senses (e.g., feeling pain or hunger). The author notes that internal sense perceptions are called 'wijdaniyyat' in psychology, and emphasizes that the intellect is the judge using one of the two senses. The third type, 'tajribiyyat' (experimental truths), are certainties derived from repeated observation, such as 'iron expands with heat.' The passage thus establishes a hierarchy: immediate axioms, sensory data, and generalizations from repeated experience.

Six Types of Certain Propositions
Author defines six self-evident types of certain propositions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the six types of certain propositions and explain the source of their certainty.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge begins with six self-evident sources, each validated by the mind without external proof.

In this passage, the author lays out the foundational propositions that the mind accepts without external proof. These six types range from immediate logical truths like 'the whole is greater than the part' to knowledge gained through repeated experience, unanimous reports, strong intuition, and innate reasoning. Understanding these categories is essential for evaluating the certainty of any claim in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion for اليقينيات
The author defines them as propositions the mind believes without requiring proof. Notice the phrase 'يؤمن بها العقل من دون أن يطلب الدليل'.
All six types share this property of being certain without external evidence.
2
Examine the first three types
For الأوليات, المشاهدات, and التجريبيات: note the source of certainty. الأوليات are immediate self-evidence. المشاهدات rely on sense perception (external or internal). التجريبيات depend on repeated observation that creates a firm judgment.
مشاهدات include both external senses (e.g., sight) and internal senses (e.g., pain).
3
Examine the last three types
For المتواترات, الحدسيات, and الفطريات: المتواترات come from unanimous reports of a group that cannot collude on falsehood. الحدسيات arise from a strong intuition. الفطريات require a mediator that is always present in the mind.
الفطريات differ from الأوليات because they need a mediator, but the mediator is so obvious that the judgment is still considered self-evident.
4
Distinguish الأوليات from الفطريات
Both are self-evident, but الفطريات require a mediator (وسيط) that is always present, while الأوليات do not require any mediator. For example, 'two is a fifth of ten' requires the concept of multiplication, but that concept is immediately available.
This distinction is subtle but important in epistemology.
5
Apply the classification to new examples
Given a proposition, identify which type it falls under based on the source of certainty. For instance, 'Fire burns' is a observable (مشاهدات) because it is known through external sense. 'Iron expands with heat' is experiential (تجريبيات) because it requires repeated observation.
This skill helps in evaluating the certainty of knowledge claims in Islamic sciences.

تنقسم القضايا اليقينية البدهية التي يؤمن بها العقل من دون أن يطلب الدليل على ستة أنواع هي:
(۱) الأوليات: وهي قضايا يصدق بها العقل لذاتها أي من دون سبب خارج عن ذاتها. فالعقل يصدق بها فوراً ويجزم بصدقها مثل قولنا (الكل أعظم من الجزء) أو (النقيضان لا يجتمعان).
(۲) المشاهدات: وهي القضايا التي يحكم بها العقل بوساطة الحس. والحس على قسمين: ظاهري وباطني. فالحكم بأن الشمس مضيئة والنار حارة، يحكم بها العقل بوساطة الحس الظاهري كما أن العلم بأن لنا ألماً ولذة وجوعاً وعطشاً يدركه العقل بوساطة الحس الباطني وهذا هو المسمى بالوجدانيات في علم النفس. فالحاكم هو العقل بوساطة أحد الحسين، وعلى ذلك فالمراد من المشاهدة أي المشاهدة بالحس الظاهري أو الباطني.
(۳) التجريبيات: وهي إحدى اليقينيات التي يحكم بها العقل بوساطة تكرار المشاهدة مما يوجب أن يرسخ في النفس حكم لا شك فيه كقولنا (إن الحديد يتمدد بالحرارة) بعد أن جربنا هذه الحالة مرات عدة.
(٤) المتواترات: وهي قضايا تسكن إليها النفس سكوناً يزول معه الشك ويحصل الجزم القاطع وذلك بواسطة أخبار جماعة يمتنع تواطؤهم على الكذب كعلمنا بوجود البلدان النائية.
(٥) الحدسيات: وهي قضايا مبدأ الحكم بها حدس قوي من النفس يزول معه الشك ويذعن الذهن بمضمونها مثل حكمنا بأن نور القمر مستفاد من الشمس، ومنشأ هذا الحكم هو غياب ضوء القمر عندما تقع الأرض بين الشمس والقمر.
(٦) الفطريات: وهي القضايا التي قياساتها معها أي أن العقل لا يصدق بها بمجرد تصورها كالأوليات بل لابد لها من وسيط إلا أن هذا الوسيط ليس مما يذهب عن الذهن حتى يحتاج إلى فكر مثل حكمنا بأن الاثنين خمس العشرة فإن هذا حكم بدهي إلا أنه معلوم بوسيط.
The self-evident certain propositions that the mind believes without requiring proof are divided into six types:
(1) Primaries: propositions that the mind affirms by themselves, i.e., without any cause external to themselves. The mind affirms them immediately and judges them with certainty, such as our saying 'The whole is greater than the part' or 'Two opposites do not coincide.'
(2) Observables: propositions that the mind judges by means of sense perception. Sense perception is of two kinds: external and internal. The judgment that the sun is luminous and fire is hot is made by the mind by means of external sense, as is the knowledge that we have pain, pleasure, hunger, and thirst, which the mind perceives by means of internal sense — this is called 'found sensations' in psychology. So the judge is the mind by means of one of the two senses, and thus the meaning of observation is observation by external or internal sense.
(3) Experientials: one of the certainties that the mind judges by means of repeated observation, which causes a doubt-free judgment to become firmly rooted in the soul, such as our saying 'Iron expands with heat' after we have tried this condition several times.
(4) Recurrents: propositions towards which the soul settles with a tranquility that removes doubt and obtains absolute certainty, by means of reports from a group whose collusion on lying is impossible, such as our knowledge of the existence of remote countries.
(5) Intuitives: propositions whose judgment originates from a strong intuition of the soul that removes doubt and the mind submits to its content, such as our judgment that the light of the moon is derived from the sun, and the source of this judgment is the disappearance of moonlight when the earth lies between the sun and the moon.
(6) Innates: propositions that have their syllogisms with them, i.e., the mind does not believe them merely by conceiving them like the primaries, but they require a mediator, yet this mediator is not something that escapes the mind so as to require reflection, such as our judgment that two is a fifth of ten, for this is a self-evident judgment but it is known through a mediator.

Commentary
This passage defines the six types of certain propositions (اليقينيات) that form the foundation of knowledge. The author presents them as self-evident truths that the mind accepts without external proof. Each type is explained with an example. The first type, الأوليات (primaries), are immediately evident, like the whole being greater than the part. The second, المشاهدات (observables), rely on external or internal sense perception, such as heat from fire or feelings of pain. The third, التجريبيات (experientials), come from repeated observation, like iron expanding with heat. The fourth, المتواترات (recurrents), are known through unanimous reports from a group that cannot conspire to lie, e.g., the existence of distant lands. The fifth, الحدسيات (intuitives), arise from strong intuition, e.g., moonlight originating from the sun. The sixth, الفطريات (innates), require a mediator that is always present, such as knowing that two is a fifth of ten. These categories cover the foundational knowledge from which all certain knowledge is derived. Note that the الفطريات differ from الأوليات in requiring a mediator, but the mediator is so obvious that the judgment is still considered self-evident.
