Inspiration's Universal Role in Knowledge
Argues for the pervasive role of inspiration in knowledge and defines it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define inspiration (ilham) as presented in this passage and identify the evidence cited for its universal role in science, art, and jurisprudence.

Core Idea
Inspiration is not limited to prophets; it is a universal human capacity and a source of knowledge.

This passage expands our understanding of inspiration (ilham), arguing that it is not confined to religious revelation but operates across all fields of knowledge, including science, art, and the practical reasoning of a jurist. The author uses both the example of a jurist refining theories through inspiration and psychological research to show that everyone has moments of intuitive insight. By the end, we will have a clear definition and the evidence supporting inspiration's pervasiveness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Fields of Inspiration
The passage begins by listing fields where inspiration is acknowledged: science, art, and applied jurisprudence. Notice that the author includes poetry and inventing, showing a broad scope.
2
Analyze the Jurist's Example
The author describes a scenario where a jurist applies theories from usul al-fiqh to new cases. During application, the jurist gains new insights ('illuminated by inspiration') that modify or deepen the theories. This shows inspiration working within a rational discipline.
The term 'istinbat' (deduction) is used, but inspiration complements it.
3
Distinguish Intuition (Hads) as a Branch
The passage introduces 'hads' (intuition) as a branch of inspiration. It is defined as immediate knowledge without reasoning or premises, citing psychological research that everyone experiences such moments.
Note the shift from classical mantiq: hads here is equated with ilham, not quick inference.
4
Extract the Definition and Types
The summary defines inspiration as 'the casting of emotional/spiritual laws into the soul without mediation.' It then distinguishes two types: general (for all humans) and specific (for a particular group). This duality is important for understanding its application.

Commentary
This passage expands on the role of inspiration (ilham) in various fields of knowledge. It begins by noting that thinkers, inventors, and poets all acknowledge its role in science and art. Then it provides a concrete example from applied jurisprudence (fiqh tatbiqi): when problems arise in the process of deduction (istinbat), the principles of usul al-fiqh provide solutions. However, when applying these solutions to new contexts, the jurist often gains new insights through inspiration, which can modify or deepen the theories. This is described as the jurist being illuminated by the light of inspiration to reach the realities of rulings. The passage then cites psychological research to assert that every human has moments of intuition (hads), which is a branch of inspiration, where knowledge is grasped without reasoning or learning. This capacity varies among individuals. It concludes that a human can develop this inspirational power to the highest degree, as affirmed by both ancient and modern philosophers. Finally, the summary defines inspiration as the casting of emotional/spiritual laws into the soul without mediation, reiterating its two types: general for all humans and specific for a particular group.

Empirical Knowledge in Islamic Science
Author cites Muslim scientists to argue that experimentation is a source of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to cite historical examples of empirical methods used by Muslim scientists and explain why they constitute a source of knowledge.

Core Idea
Muslim scientists used experimentation as a tool for knowledge, as seen in Ibn al-Haytham's optics and al-Tusi's astronomy.

This passage presents evidence from Islamic history that empirical investigation was a legitimate and practiced method for acquiring knowledge. The author cites specific scientists and their works to show that experimentation was not alien to Islamic science. This lesson will help you understand how this fits into the broader Islamic epistemology, where acquired knowledge complements innate and revealed knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
The author concludes that non-innate (acquired) knowledge is obtained gradually through reflection, rational inquiry, effort, and the use of experiments. This claim is supported by historical examples.
2
Examine the historical examples
The passage mentions Ibn al-Haytham's work on optics (including experiments on the rainbow) and Nasir al-Din al-Tusi's observatory and astronomical tables. These demonstrate empirical methods in practice.
Ibn al-Haytham's Kitab al-Manazir was a standard textbook in Europe, showing the global impact of his empirical approach.
3
Understand the epistemological significance
The author argues that experimentation is a source of knowledge, aligning with the Qur'anic theory of knowledge that encourages reflection and observation of the natural world.
This expands the sources of knowledge beyond pure reason (nazar aqli) to include empirical investigation.
