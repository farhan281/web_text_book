The Three Sophist Theses and Their Refutation
Author describes sophist position and lists three core theses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three sophist theses from the passage and explain why Islamic epistemology rejects each one.

Core Idea
Sophism denies objective knowledge through three radical theses; Islamic thought refutes them by affirming existence, knowability, and transmissibility.

This lesson examines the sophist doctrine as presented in the passage. Sophism, rooted in Protagoras's claim that 'man is the measure of all things,' leads to three destructive theses: nothing exists, if it exists it cannot be known, and if known it cannot be transmitted. Islamic philosophers and theologians, such as al-Ghazali and al-Tusi, rejected this skepticism and affirmed that knowledge is possible through senses, reason, and revelation. Understanding these theses helps you recognize and refute radical skepticism in theological debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
Find Protagoras's dictum in the passage: 'man is the measure of all things.' Notice how it collapses truth into individual perception.
This principle is the root of all three theses.
2
Extract the three theses
Locate the list: (1) nothing exists, (2) if it exists it cannot be known, (3) if known it cannot be transmitted. Write them down in order.
These theses escalate from ontology to epistemology to communication.
3
Analyze the consequences for morality
Read the last part: there is no moral law; truth, loyalty, good, and evil are relative. Explain how this follows from the three theses.
If everything is relative, ethical universalism is impossible.
4
Trace the refutation in Islamic thought
Consider how an Islamic scholar would respond: the world exists because it is created (Qur'an), knowledge is possible through senses and revelation (Qur'an and Hadith), and prophecy proves transmission. Al-Ghazali's 'al-Munqidh min al-Dalal' refutes such skepticism.
Islamic philosophy upholds objective reality and shared knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents the sophist (sufistaiyya) doctrine as a denial of objective knowledge. It begins by stating that sophism does not accept the existence of philosophically valuable knowledge. The core idea is Protagoras' dictum that 'man is the measure of all things,' meaning that each individual's perception defines what is true and false for them. This leads to three fundamental theses: (1) nothing exists (a radical nihilism), (2) even if something exists, it cannot be known (epistemological skepticism), and (3) even if it could be known, such knowledge cannot be transmitted to others (solipsism). Consequently, there are no universal moral laws; each individual's judgment determines right and wrong. The passage then provides an etymological note: the word 'sufista' comes from Greek 'sufa' (wisdom) and 'ista' (sophistry), thus sophism meaning the 'science of sophistry'. It also mentions the Greek sophist Protagoras as the pioneer, who wrote a book doubting the existence of gods, and later Gorgias who articulated the three central theses. This lesson serves as a critique of the sophist position, which Islamic philosophers and theologians refuted by affirming the possibility of objective knowledge through sense, reason, and revelation.

The Three Theses of Sophistry
Author defines sophistry, lists its three central theses, and provides etymology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and recall the three fundamental theses of sophistry and explain its etymological roots.

Core Idea
Sophistry is the denial of objective truth and knowledge, summarized in three devastating theses.

Sophistry is not just a historical Greek movement; it is a persistent challenge to the very possibility of knowledge. The passage lays out its core claims with stark clarity. Understanding these theses is essential for anyone who wishes to defend the Islamic position that truth exists, can be known, and can be communicated through revelation and reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim of sophistry
Find the sentence that states 'Man is the measure of all things.' This is the foundational relativistic principle from which everything else follows.
This claim eliminates any objective standard outside human perception.
2
Extract the three theses
The passage lists three numbered propositions. Write them down exactly: nothing exists; if it exists, it cannot be known; if known, it cannot be communicated. Memorize them in order.
These are often called the three pillars of sophistry.
3
Analyze the etymology
Read the footnote from al-Muhaqqiq al-Tusi. Break down 'sufista' into 'sufa' (wisdom) and 'ista' (fallacy). Understand that sophistry is presented as the 'science of fallacy'.
This etymology reveals the deceptive nature of sophistry: it masquerades as wisdom but is actually a tool for error.
4
Connect to Islamic epistemology
Consider how each thesis contradicts the Quranic worldview. For example, Allah says He has created everything with truth (Al-Imran 3:191) and has taught man clear proofs (Al-Baqarah 2:256).
The fitra (innate disposition) recognizes objective good and evil, directly opposing the sophist claim that morality is merely a social construct.

Refuting Sophistry's Three Theses
Author critiques the three sophist theses using rational analysis and philosophical arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the author's three-fold refutation of the sophist theses concerning objectivity, knowledge, and moral values.

Core Idea
Sophistry denies objective reality, knowledge, and moral values — but rational analysis shows each thesis collapses under its own absurdity.

In this passage, the author takes sophistry to task by exposing the logical consequences of its three core claims. Using tools of rational analysis (al-taḥlīl al-ʿaqlī), he demonstrates that if facts are merely mental constructs, then objective reality vanishes; if knowledge is impossible, then science itself is undermined; and if moral values are not fixed, then universal human judgments about good and evil become inexplicable. As we examine his arguments, pay attention to how he uses reductio ad absurdum and empirical universality to refute each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sophist theses under attack
Find in the Arabic the three claims: (1) facts are products of our minds (subjectivity of reality), (2) things cannot be known (unknowability), (3) there are no fixed moral values. These are the targets of the critique.
The theses are implicit in the structure: the first is addressed by the Socratic reduction, the second by the argument about knowledge, the third by the discussion of moral values.
2
Examine the refutation of the first thesis (subjective reality)
The author uses a Socratic reduction: if there is no objective world, then there is no sun, moon, earth, or human, so who speaks and builds? This is reductio ad absurdum — showing the thesis leads to an absurd conclusion.
This type of argument is common in Islamic kalam, e.g., against the sceptics.
3
Examine the refutation of the second thesis (unknowability)
Accepting that something cannot be known would destroy all science and knowledge, because knowledge is built upon fixed principles (as Plato said). This is a self-defeat argument: the claim that knowledge is impossible undermines itself.
Notice the author refers to Plato's fixed principles — a philosophical appeal.
4
Examine the refutation of the third thesis (no fixed moral values)
The author points to the universal and eternal recognition of good and evil across all humans and time. Good is seen as good and evil as ugly by everyone, indicating that moral values are fixed essences (māhiyyāt thābita) that cannot be denied.
This argument uses universal consensus as evidence for objective morality.
5
Assess the author's overall conclusion
The author concludes that sophistry denies ethics and science, falls into absurdity, and though it may be a necessary starting point in thought, it fails in the first round. Evaluate whether the arguments are convincing and note any weaknesses.
Consider how a sophist might respond: e.g., universal consensus does not necessarily prove objective truth.

Commentary
This passage presents a critical refutation of sophistry by examining its three theses through rational analysis. The author begins with the first thesis (nothing exists or facts are subjective): if facts are merely products of our minds, then objective reality vanishes. Using a Socratic reduction, he points out that if there were no sun, moon, earth, or humans, then who speaks and builds the world? This absurdity refutes the claim. Against the second thesis (unknowability), the author argues that accepting it would destroy all science and knowledge, because knowledge is based on fixed principles—as Plato held. The third thesis (no fixed moral values) is countered by noting the universality of moral perceptions: good and evil are recognized across all human societies and time, indicating they are fixed essences (māhiyyāt thābita) that cannot be denied. The author then lists pairs of opposites (beauty/ugliness, etc.) as evidence of inherent contradictions that sophistry cannot explain. Finally, he cites Aristotle’s affirmation that sense-data and primary intellectual truths are decisive realities. The passage concludes that sophistry is a denial of ethics and science, falling into absurdity, and though it may serve as a provocative starting point, it fails dramatically. In the Islamic tradition, this critique resonates with al-Ghazali’s refutation of the sceptics in his Munqidh min al-Dalal, where he argues that even the sceptic must admit the reality of certain self-evident truths.

Key Terms
التحليل العقلي (al-taḥlīl al-ʿaqlī): Rational analysis, the method used by the author to examine and refute the sophist theses.

الماهيات الثابتة (al-māhiyyāt al-thābita): Fixed essences, eternal and unchanging realities such as the values of good and beauty that sophistry denies. Critique of Passive Reflection: Conditional Refutation
Author critiques Marxist reflection theory using analogy and conditional argument.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the author's conditional refutation of the reflection theory and evaluate its strength using a parallel argument from Islamic kalam.

Core Idea
Reflection alone cannot produce knowledge; the mind actively transforms sensory data, like a tree produces fruit from raw elements.

This passage challenges the Marxist reflection theory, which claims knowledge is a direct imprint of reality on the mind. The author uses a conditional argument (if knowledge were a property of matter, then all matter would perceive) and an analogy (mirror vs. tree) to show that passive reflection is insufficient. Understanding this critique sharpens your ability to test materialist epistemologies against Islamic insights on the soul and intellect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target theory
The target is the reflection (in'ikas) theory: that sensory objects imprint themselves directly in the mind, producing knowledge as a passive copy. Find where the author describes this process.
The author calls it 'physical reflection' becoming 'physiological'.
2
Extract the conditional argument
The author says: If knowledge were a property of matter (madda), then every matter would have perception (idrak). Since not all matter perceives, knowledge cannot be a property of matter. This is a modus tollens argument.
Premise: Knowledge is not a property of matter. Conditional: If it were, all matter would perceive.
3
Analyze the mirror analogy
The mirror reflects images but does not perceive, decide, or interpret. This shows that mere reflection lacks the essential features of knowledge: awareness, judgment, and will.
Analogies are not proofs but are used to illustrate the logical gap.
4
Examine the tree analogy as alternative
The mind is compared to a tree that transforms raw inputs (light, salts, nitrogen) into a new product (fruit). This emphasizes the active, organizing role of the knower, in contrast to passive reflection.
The child (idea) is not reducible to its chemical components.
5
Evaluate the critique's internal logic
The conditional argument is logically valid (modus tollens). The analogies are persuasive but not demonstrative. The critique forces a distinction between passive reflection and active intellectual processing.
Compare this to Islamic arguments that knowledge requires an immaterial soul (nafs) and 'aql.

The Chain of Equivalence in Prohibiting Sihr
Author quotes hadiths warning against practicing magic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the transitive chain in the hadith equating astrology with disbelief and explain its logical force.

Core Idea
Sihr is linked to kufr through a chain: astrologer to soothsayer to magician to disbeliever.

This passage strengthens the prohibition of magic by citing hadiths that draw an unbroken logical chain from astrology to disbelief. Understanding this chain helps you see how an apparently minor practice can be classified as major unbelief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate the terms 'السحر' (sihr), 'الشعوذة' (sha'wadha), 'الكاهن' (kahin), 'المنجم' (munajjim) in the Arabic. Note that sihr here is the prohibited form, not the lexical positive sense from the previous lesson.
This is crucial because the same word can shift meaning in different contexts.
2
Extract the chain from the hadith
In the second hadith, Imam Ali says: 'المنجم كالكاهن والكاهن كالساحر والساحر كالكافر والكافر في النار.' Write the chain: astrologer = soothsayer, soothsayer = magician, magician = disbeliever.
The particle 'ك' (ka) indicates comparison or equivalence.
3
Apply transitive inference
If A = B and B = C, then A = C. Here, astrologer = soothsayer (step1), soothsayer = magician (step2), so magician = astrologer (transitive). But the hadith explicitly says magician = disbeliever. So astrologer is also like disbeliever.
This logical operation allows the ruling to flow through the entire chain.
4
Determine the practical conclusion
The third hadith states that learning any magic (sihr) leads to kufr and the death penalty unless repentance occurs. The chain shows that even astrology falls under this heavy judgment.
The logical chain strengthens the warning: one should avoid all forms connected to sihr.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran affirms absolute truths; sophistry denies them entirely.

While sophistical thought denies any fixed truths, the Quran unequivocally affirms that objective and absolute truths exist—about the universe, life, creation, and knowledge.
