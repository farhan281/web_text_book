Parsing an Islamic Philosophy Bibliography
Page lists references and sources used in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify each reference in an Islamic philosophy bibliography by type (primary scripture, classical philosophical work, tafsir, hadith, modern study) and assess its relevance to the author's argument.

Core Idea
A bibliography reveals the intellectual lineage and the author's authoritative sources.

This page lists the sources used in 'Nazariyya Falsafa'. While not a logical argument, learning to parse such a list is a scholarly skill: it traces the author's evidence base and intellectual influences. In this lesson, you will practice categorizing these references by genre and evaluating their role in philosophical inquiry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the book’s author and main title
Look at the top of the bibliography: it begins with the author's name, then 'المصادر والمراجع'. This tells you whose references these are and for which work.
Author: Abd al-Zahra Turki al-Fatlawi; book: Nazariyya Falsafa.
2
Scan the list and classify each entry
Read through each line and assign it a category: primary scripture (Quran, hadith), classical philosophical text (e.g., al-Asfar al-Arba'a, al-Isharat), tafsir (e.g., al-Bayan fi Tafsir al-Qur'an), modern study (e.g., Allah Yatajalla fi 'Asr al-'Ilm), or reference work (e.g., al-A'lam).
Use the title and author to determine the genre.
3
Note publication details
Each entry includes publisher, place, and date. Observe the range: classical works from early Islamic centuries, modern works from the 20th century. This shows the historical scope of sources.
Dates are given in both Hijri and Gregorian.
4
Assess diversity of sources
Count how many entries are Quran/hadith, how many are philosophy, how many are tafsir, etc. A balanced bibliography often indicates a comprehensive approach.
For example, Mulla Sadra, Ibn Sina, al-Khu'i, and al-Khomeini represent different eras and schools.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing the sources and references used in the book 'Nazariyya Falsafa' by Abd al-Zahra Turki al-Fatlawi. It begins with the Holy Quran, then lists classical and modern works in Islamic philosophy, theology, exegesis, and related fields. The references include major works such as Sadr al-Muta'allihin's 'Al-Asfar al-Arba'a', Ibn Sina's 'Al-Isharat wa al-Tanbihat' and 'Ilahiyyat al-Shifa'', and contemporary writings. The list demonstrates the breadth of sources consulted, spanning from early Islamic thought to modern scholarship. The student should note the variety of genres: philosophical treatises, Quranic commentaries, hadith collections, and biographical dictionaries. This page serves as a key to the intellectual tradition underpinning the book's arguments.
