Author compiles definitions of knowledge from major Ash'ari theologians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify at least five different definitions of knowledge and attribute each to its proponent.

Core Idea
Knowledge has no single definition — each scholar emphasized a different aspect.

In this passage, the author presents a series of definitions of knowledge from leading Muslim theologians. Each definition reflects a different epistemological concern. By learning them, you will appreciate the richness and complexity of Islamic thought on knowledge.

Guided Path
1
List the scholars mentioned
Go through the passage and extract the names: al-Ash'ari, al-Juwayni, Ibn Furak, al-Ghazali, al-Razi, al-Baqillani, and the Mu'tazila. Note their death dates.
2
Extract each definition
For each scholar, write down the exact definition they gave for knowledge. Example: al-Ash'ari: 'perception of the known as it is'.
3
Compare the definitions
Identify common themes: perception, belief, action, presence. Notice which definitions emphasize the object vs the subject.
4
Assess the comprehensiveness
The passage ends with a critique that the philosophers' definition is not comprehensive. Reflect on why no single definition is universally accepted.

And it is famous among a large group of jurists that knowledge is the presence of the known with the knower. And we conclude from that that knowledge according to the Muslim philosophers is knowledge: so every knowledge is knowledge and every knowledge is knowledge, and every knower is a knower of God Almighty and every knower is a knower. And from the familiar definition of knowledge among the ancient philosophers is (the acquisition of the image of a thing in the intellect or the impression of its image in the mind), and this definition is criticized for not being comprehensive.

Can Knowledge Be Defined? The Debate Among Theologians
Author compiles definitions of knowledge from major Ash'ari theologians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the arguments for and against the definability of knowledge as presented by al-Juwayni, al-Ghazali, and al-Razi.

Core Idea
Some scholars argued knowledge is self-evident and needs no definition; others found it difficult to define.

The passage reports that al-Juwayni said defining knowledge is not necessary but theoretical and difficult; al-Ghazali agreed it's hard; al-Razi said it's self-evident. This represents a long-standing debate in Islamic epistemology: can the most fundamental concepts be defined? This lesson explores that debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scholars
Find al-Juwayni, al-Ghazali, and al-Razi in the passage. Note their positions on definability.
2
Extract their positions
Al-Juwayni: definition is theoretical and difficult. Al-Ghazali: difficult because genus and differentia are elusive. Al-Razi: knowledge is necessary and needs no definition.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Why do they think it's difficult? Al-Ghazali mentions the impossibility of finding a precise genus and differentia. Al-Razi thinks it's self-evident. What does that imply about the nature of definitions?
4
Evaluate the implications
If knowledge cannot be defined, how can we study it? If it can, what should a good definition include?

Definition of Knowledge in Islamic Epistemology
Author presents a definition of knowledge and its scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of Sheikh Misbah al-Yazdi's definition of knowledge and distinguish between acquired and presential knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge is the presence of a thing's essence, form, or concept to an immaterial being.

Islamic epistemology begins with a clear definition of its subject: knowledge (al-ilm). Sheikh Misbah al-Yazdi's definition is broad enough to include both knowledge by presence (direct awareness) and acquired knowledge (conceptual representation). Understanding this definition is the first step toward grasping deeper debates about the nature and limits of human understanding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined (al-ilm) in the Arabic passage. Note that it appears at the beginning of the quotation from Sheikh Misbah al-Yazdi.
The definiendum is the subject of the definition.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus (jins) is 'presence of something to an immaterial existent'. The differentia (fasl) specifies what is present: the essence, particular image, or universal concept. This distinguishes knowledge from other kinds of presence.
The definition is a complete hadd (real definition) because it gives the essential nature.
3
Test the definition's completeness
Check whether the definition includes all instances of knowledge and excludes non-knowledge. It includes both al-ilm al-huduri (presence of essence) and al-ilm al-husuli (presence of image/concept). It excludes material presence because the recipient is immaterial.
This definition covers sensory and intellectual awareness.
4
Compare hadd vs rasm
This is a hadd haqiqi (real definition) because it gives the essential constituent (presence). A rasm would merely describe properties. The author's commentary confirms that it is comprehensive.
Understanding this distinction helps evaluate definitions in Islamic sciences.

Commentary
This passage combines a definition of knowledge (al-ilm) with an outline of the three core issues in epistemology (nazariyya al-ma'rifa). It begins by quoting Sheikh Misbah al-Yazdi's definition: knowledge is the presence of the essence, particular image, or universal concept of a thing to an immaterial existent (like the soul or intellect). Commentary
This passage is a critical examination of sensory knowledge (al-ma'rifa al-hissiyya). The author begins by defining sensory knowledge as the result of sense attachment to the known, immediately noting its philosophical insufficiency due to errors. The critique is then supported by historical objections: Socrates questioned whether different senses yield different perceptions and whether the idea of an object (e.g., an orange) is reducible to individual sensations. Plato's position is invoked to argue that the soul, not the senses, forms ideas about existents and values. The author then presents three methodological objections. First, the sensory theory is likened to a mirror that reflects external appearances without understanding; sensory data are raw materials like water and light that need processing (like fruit fermentation) – the mind must analyze and deduce. Second, if the brain were a camera, it would record images, but feelings (wijdaniyyat) like love, generosity, courage, etc., cannot be imaged; they are non-physical. Third, animals share the same senses as humans, yet humans have higher knowledge; some animals exceed humans in sensory abilities, proving that sensory knowledge is superficial and shared, not the basis for distinctively human understanding. Finally, the author lists abstract concepts (cause/effect, substance/accident, etc.) that cannot be reduced to senses, implying a non-sensory foundation for knowledge. The overall argument is that sensory knowledge is a necessary but insufficient source; reason and the soul provide the true cognitive framework.

Four Objections to Epistemological Relativism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and reconstruct the four logical objections to epistemological relativism presented by the author, and evaluate their force.

Core Idea
Relativism is self-refuting: it cannot assert itself without absolute truth.

In this lesson, we examine four powerful objections to the claim that all truth is relative. The author of our text argues that epistemological relativism leads to absurd consequences: it undermines itself, destroys scientific knowledge, erases moral and logical distinctions, and reduces thought to madness. By parsing each objection, you will learn how to recognize self-refutation and why Islamic scholars insist on the reality of absolute truths in matters of faith and reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target doctrine
Recognize that the author is attacking the claim that all truth is relative (epistemological relativism). Understand that this is a theory about the nature of truth itself.
Relativism denies the existence of any absolute or universal truth.
2
Examine the first objection (self-refutation)
Follow the dilemma: if the theory is absolute truth, it contradicts its own claim; if it is relative, it only applies to its proponents and is not universally binding. This shows the theory is either false or irrelevant.

This undermines both rational judgment and ethical values.
5
Reflect on the fourth objection (self-evident axioms)
Note that propositions like 'one is half of two' or 'the whole is greater than the part' are self-evident and cannot be relativized. If they were relative, thought would become irrational.
These axioms are the bedrock of rational thought.
