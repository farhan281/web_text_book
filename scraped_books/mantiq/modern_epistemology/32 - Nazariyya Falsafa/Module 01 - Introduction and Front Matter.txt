This lesson examines the front matter of 'Theory of Knowledge between the Quran and Philosophy' by Abd al-Zahra al-Fatlawi. Understanding how to read a title page is essential for any student of Islamic sciences, as it immediately situates a work within its tradition and scholarly context. Place: النجف - المشخاب (Najaf - Al-Mishkhab). Year: 1430هـ (2012م). Najaf is a major center of Shia learning. This situates the work in a specific scholarly tradition and time period.

The subtitle 'دراسة مقارنة' means 'comparative study'. So the author compares Quranic epistemology with philosophical epistemology. This is a typical approach in Islamic thought to defend or critique philosophical influences.

The Purpose of Front Matter in Islamic Scholarship
Author opens with basmala, Quran verse, and dedication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the function of the basmala, Quranic verse, and dedication in framing a classical Islamic text.

Core Idea
Front matter aligns the seeker of knowledge with divine guidance and prophetic blessing.

This opening is not mere decoration. Every classical Islamic work begins with the basmala, a chosen Quranic verse, and often a dedication. These elements establish the author's orientation, seek divine assistance, and connect the work to the prophetic tradition. Understanding this frame helps you read the text with the right spiritual and intellectual posture.

Guided Path
1
Observe the invocation (basmala)
The basmala (بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم) is a standard opening that invokes Allah's name and attributes of mercy. It signals that the work is undertaken with divine aid and for a sacred purpose.
In Islamic scholarship, the basmala is not optional; it is a sunnah and a mark of respect.
2
Analyze the Quranic verse selection
The author quotes Surah Al-An'am 6:104, which emphasizes that insights (basa'ir) have come from Allah, and that seeing or being blind to them affects one's own soul. This verse sets a reflective tone, aligning the book's purpose with the pursuit of truth and personal responsibility.
The verse choice is intentional – it hints at the book's content about clear proofs and rational insight.
3
Note the dedication to the Prophet
The author dedicates the work to Prophet Muhammad (صلى الله عليه وسلم) using honorifics like 'al-amajj' (most glorious). This expresses reverence, seeks prophetic intercession, and places the work within the prophetic legacy.
Dedications are common in classical works to show humility and connect the author's effort to the prophetic mission.
4
Synthesize the three elements
Together, the basmala, the Quranic verse, and the dedication prepare the reader spiritually and intellectually. They assert that the work is grounded in divine revelation and prophetic guidance, and that the reader should approach it with sincerity and a desire for truth.
Ignoring these front matter elements can lead to misinterpreting the author's intent and the work's authority.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not every sentence in a logic book carries logical content — dedications are pious frames.

This passage is a typical dedication found at the beginning of many classical Islamic texts. It is not part of the logical argument but rather a traditional pious opening seeking blessing. Understanding its role helps you avoid misinterpreting the text's purpose.

Guided Path
1
Identify the addressee
Notice that the author is addressing someone other than the reader: the Prophet Muhammad.
The phrase 'إلى نبينا' clearly indicates the Prophet as recipient.
2
Recognise honorific epithets
The author uses terms like 'الأمجـ' (likely 'the praised') and 'المؤيد المسدد' (the supported and guided). These are formulaic praises, not logical predicates.
Such epithets are common in dedications to show reverence.
3
Understand the function
This is a dedication, not an argument. It seeks blessing and demonstrates the author's humility before the Prophet.
Front-matter dedications are standard in Islamic scholarly works.
4
Distinguish from logical content
The dedication does not affect the validity of the logical arguments that follow. Do not treat it as a premise or conclusion.
The text explicitly calls this a 'humble effort' dedicated to the Prophet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author selects epistemology as the pivotal arena of conflict between Quran and philosophy.

Core Idea
Epistemology is the battleground where revelation and reason vie for authority.

The author explicitly states that the chosen 'pillar' of the Quran-philosophy conflict is the theory of knowledge. He argues that knowledge is central to both heavenly books and rational inquiry, citing the first revealed word 'Iqra' and the historical preoccupation of philosophy with knowledge. This lesson unpacks why epistemology is the perfect meeting point and clash point between divine revelation and human reason.

Guided Path
1
Find the phrase 'theory of knowledge'

Contrast the two sources

They agree on the importance of knowledge but disagree on its source and method.

Methodological Foundations of Comparative Epistemology
Methodological and structural overview of the study.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author's key methodological choices for comparing Quranic and philosophical epistemology.

Core Idea
Clarity, conciseness, and focus on core concepts define sound comparative methodology.

This preface reveals how the author approaches a complex comparative project. You will learn to recognise the deliberate choices he makes: selecting representative theories, defining terms anew, and prioritising clarity over exhaustiveness. These choices shape the entire study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the study's stated goal
Find where the author states the purpose of the study. He says the goal is 'the comparison between the Quranic theory and the theories of philosophers.' This frames everything that follows.
The goal is comparative, not merely descriptive.
2
Recognise the method of presentation
The author explains he will present non-Muslim philosophical theories 'briefly, with a critique of the basic concepts' and leave details to original sources. He aims for 'benefit and clarity, not puzzles and obscurity.'
He rejects immersion in detail to stay focused on comparison.
3
Appreciate the definitional approach
The author mentions new definitions for philosophy and epistemology, and new names for philosophical theories (e.g., 'theory of atrophied instincts'). This shows he rethinks categories to better serve his comparative goal.
New definitions are a tool for clarity, not mere novelty.
4
Understand the structural division
The study is divided into introduction, three chapters, and conclusion. Chapter 1 covers philosophical theories, Chapter 2 the Quranic theory, Chapter 3 the comparison. This logical flow mirrors the comparative purpose.
Structure follows function: first establish each side, then compare.

Commentary
This passage is the preface of the book. The author, Abd al-Zahra Turki al-Fatlawi, begins by acknowledging that previous scholars, despite some obscurity and lack of methodology, have been a guiding light. He states that this study will present the main epistemological theories of non-Muslim philosophers briefly, with critique, leaving details to original sources. The ultimate goal is to compare these with the Quranic theory of knowledge. The author emphasizes that he has aimed for clarity and benefit, avoiding obscurity. He expresses gratitude to various professors and notes that serious thought transcends sectarianism—there is no Shia or Sunni in philosophy or science. The study is structured into an introduction, three chapters, and a conclusion. Chapter one covers philosophical epistemology, including new definitions for philosophy and epistemology, and introduces new names for various philosophical theories (e.g., theory of innate ideas, theory of atrophied instincts). Chapter two covers Quranic epistemology, with new titles such as inspiration, fitrah, revelation, etc. Chapter three makes a comparison, showing points of agreement and disagreement, and concludes with findings and a methodological presentation of sources. The author stresses that the terms used are common in daily life but are presented with a new, organized, and clear methodology.

The Quran's Two Faces of Debate: Rational Reflection vs. Futile Argumentation
Author introduces the Quran's stance on philosophical thinking vs. futile debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Quran's call for philosophical thinking (tafkīr falsafī) and its condemnation of futile debate (jadal ʿaqīm), using the author's criteria.

Core Idea
The Quran commands deep rational reflection but condemns argument without evidence.

The author begins by acknowledging the tension between Quranic discourse and philosophical terminology. He then defines philosophy as deep investigation into the roots of ideas, and argues that the Quran itself calls for this kind of thinking—citing multiple verses urging reasoning, observation, and reflection. Yet the Quran simultaneously rejects futile debate that lacks knowledge, guidance, or evidence. This lesson helps you identify the line between beneficial rational inquiry and sterile argumentation.

Guided Path

Reading a Table of Contents as an Epistemological Roadmap
Table of contents outlining the structure of the book on theory of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use a table of contents to identify the author's classification of sources of knowledge and distinguish between philosophical and Islamic approaches.

Core Idea
A table of contents is a logical map of an author's epistemology.

A table of contents is not just a list of pages; it is a structured outline that reveals the author's method and priorities. In this lesson, you will learn to decode the table of contents from 'Nazariyya al-Ma'rifa' to see how the author separates philosophical theories from Quranic ones. This skill will help you quickly grasp the scope of any scholarly work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions
Notice that the book is divided into two chapters: one on philosophy, one on the Quran. What does this tell you about the author's approach?
The division suggests the author sees both philosophical and Quranic perspectives as necessary for a complete theory of knowledge.
2
Examine the sequence of topics in Chapter One
The list starts with philosophy itself, then theory of knowledge, then sophistry, then specific theories. Why this order? Perhaps it shows a progression from general to specific, or from classical to modern?
3
Examine the sequence of topics in Chapter Two
Here the first entry is 'the term knowledge in Quranic perspective', then senses, intellect, inspiration, etc. This suggests the author starts with terminology, then moves to faculties, then external sources.
4
Compare the two chapters
Notice that Chapter One lists 17 theories, Chapter Two lists 20 elements. What does the difference in number and themes tell you? Chapter Two includes more diverse sources like dreams, devils, intuition, while Chapter One focuses on rational and empirical theories.
5
Infer the author's epistemological stance
By giving equal weight to philosophical and Quranic theories, the author likely sees both as valid, but the Quranic chapter is more detailed. What might this imply about the author's position?

The Quran's Sources of Knowledge: A Structural Overview
Lists the book's chapters and subheadings on Quranic epistemology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and categorize the main sources of knowledge according to the Quran as outlined in this book.

Core Idea
Quranic epistemology is multifaceted: senses, intellect, revelation, inspiration, and more.

This table of contents from Abd al-Zahra Turki al-Fatlawi's work provides a structured overview of the Quranic theory of knowledge and its comparison with philosophy. Study it to see how the Quran's epistemological framework encompasses both external and internal sources, setting it apart from purely philosophical approaches.

Guided Path
1
Scan the Chapter Two headings
Identify the numbered items from 1 to 21. These are the specific sources or aspects of knowledge discussed in the Quran according to the author.
Note that some headings have dashes and numbers; the order matters.
2
Categorize each source
Group the sources into categories: sensory (الحواس), rational (العقل), revelational (الوحي), experiential (التجربة), innate (الفطرة), etc. See how the Quran includes mystical elements like الإلهام and الرؤيا المنامية.
This categorization helps compare with philosophical epistemology.
3
Note the comparative structure of Chapter Three
Observe that the book compares Quran and philosophy on points of agreement and difference. This shows that the author is engaging in a comparative epistemological analysis.
The comparison likely highlights the unique features of Quranic knowledge sources.
4
Reflect on the overall organization
The book begins with a focus on the Quran (Chapter Two), then compares with philosophy (Chapter Three), and ends with results. This structure itself makes an argument: the Quran provides a comprehensive theory of knowledge that stands in dialogue with philosophy.

Structuring a Comparative Study in Islamic Epistemology
Author describes structure and aims of a comparative epistemological study.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to outline the key components of a comparative study on epistemology between the Qur'an and philosophical theories.

Core Idea
A comparative study's strength lies in its clear structure and faithful representation of each worldview.

This passage describes a researcher's approach to comparing Quranic and philosophical epistemologies. While not a classical logic text, it models how to organize a rigorous comparative analysis. Understanding this structure helps you evaluate and construct similar studies in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the study's main components
The passage states the study is divided into an introduction, three chapters, and a conclusion. Note the logical flow: first philosophical theories, then Quranic theory, then comparison.
This tripartite structure (thesis, antithesis, synthesis) is common in comparative Islamic studies.
2
Extract the purpose of each chapter
Chapter 1 defines philosophy and its epistemological theories, with critique. Chapter 2 presents Quranic epistemology with new categories like fitra and wahy. Chapter 3 compares and contrasts.
Each chapter serves a distinct function: exposition, presentation, comparison.
3
Recognize the methodological remarks
The researcher deliberately avoids excessive detail to stay focused on comparison. He also coins new terms for clarity. Note the claim about absence of sectarian bias in serious thought.
Methodological awareness is crucial for evaluating any comparative work.
4
Assess the source of authority
The passage concludes that the Qur'anic argument's strength comes from its miraculous nature (i'jaz), not from sectarian affiliation. This grounds the study in a distinctly Islamic epistemological foundation.
Compare this to how other Islamic sciences ground their first principles.
