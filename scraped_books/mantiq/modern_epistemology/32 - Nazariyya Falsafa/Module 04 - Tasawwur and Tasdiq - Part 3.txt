Quranic Epistemology: Stages and Types of Knowledge vs Philosophical Views
Author contrasts Quranic multiple stages of knowledge with philosophical single-stage view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Quranic and philosophical classifications of knowledge and identify examples of huduri and husuli knowledge.

Core Idea
The Quran teaches multiple stages of knowledge; philosophers claim one.

This passage contrasts Quranic and philosophical theories on the nature and stages of knowledge. It highlights three key differences: the number of stages, the division of human knowledge into fixed present (huduri) and acquired (husuli), and the role of divine outpouring versus experience. Understanding this contrast is essential for grounding Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the philosophical claim about stages of knowledge
Find in the text where the author states that most philosophical theories say the stage of knowledge is one. Note that this implies a single path or level of knowing.
Point 13 in the passage.
2
Identify the Quranic counterclaim
Locate the Quranic view that knowledge has multiple stages and that contemplation leads to certain knowledge. Recognize the implied invalidation of a single-stage theory.
The Quran calls for lengthy thinking to reach yaqin.
3
Compare the classification of human knowledge
Read point 14. Observe that philosophers claim all knowledge is acquired (husuli) for all people, while the Quran divides knowledge into fixed present (huduri) and living acquired (husuli). Notice the examples given for each type.
Huduri: insights (basa'ir); husuli: revelation, election, dreams.
4
Examine the foundation of knowledge
In point 15, see how empiricists (Locke, Marx, Hume) make experience the essential path, whereas the Quranic view makes divine outpouring (al-fayd al-ilahi) the basis that gives value to experiences.
Experience only reveals superficial phenomena according to the Quran.

Quranic vs Philosophical Epistemology
Contrast between Quranic and philosophical epistemologies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to contrast the Quranic emphasis on moral practice with the philosophical focus on perceptual decision mechanisms.

Core Idea
Quranic knowledge is inseparable from virtue.

Point 16 of our passage sets up a fundamental contrast: philosophical theories scrutinize how decisions emerge, while the Quranic view integrates knowledge with the call to good and virtue. This shift has profound implications for how we understand the purpose of knowing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objects of focus
Find the philosophical focus (mechanism of perceptual decision) and the Quranic focus (practice of good and virtue).
The author uses 'in contrast' (في حين) to highlight the difference.
2
Extract the value judgment
The author implies that the Quranic view is more complete because it includes a moral call.
3
Reflect on implications
If knowledge is tied to virtue, then knowing also entails responsibility.
This aligns with Islamic concepts of 'ilm and amal.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exposition that defines the cause of knowledge as a set of internal and external tools used by the human soul. The author begins by asserting that philosophy lacks a definitive answer and is characterized by helplessness and contradiction, whereas knowledge is a spiritual phenomenon with specific tools. He then quotes several Quranic verses to highlight the deficiency of those who rely on assumption and are in doubt, contrasting them with the honor bestowed upon man through knowledge. The passage culminates in a comprehensive list of knowledge tools: sensation, intellect, revelation, innate disposition, heart, chest, soul, memory, insights, inspiration, guidance, teaching, balance, dream visions, thinking, instincts, heavenly books, and prophets. The author warns that denying any of these tools cripples human cognition and leads to misguidance. The argument proceeds by first establishing the inadequacy of philosophy, then presenting the Quranic perspective, and finally enumerating the divinely provided tools, emphasizing that human perfection lies in their proper use.
