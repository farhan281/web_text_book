Classification of Human Knowledge: Intuitive and Contemplative
Author explains how sensations form knowledge and classifies human knowledge into two types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between intuitive and contemplative knowledge and identify the role of sensation in both types according to the passage.

Core Idea
All human knowledge springs from sensation; the mind only rearranges sensory data.

This passage presents a radical empiricist epistemology. The author argues that sensations provide all raw material for knowledge. He then divides human knowledge into two types: intuitive (direct, immediate) and contemplative (derived through inference). Both types ultimately depend on sensory input, and the author explicitly rejects any innate principles or metaphysical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of knowledge listed
Locate in the passage the division of human knowledge. The author mentions 'al-maʿrifa al-wijdāniyya' (intuitive) and 'al-maʿrifa al-taʾammuliyya' (contemplative). Notice that he claims three divisions but only enumerates two.
The missing third type may be sensory knowledge itself, but the passage focuses on these two.
2
Determine the basis of each type
For intuitive knowledge, the passage says it is 'true knowledge with complete philosophical value.' For contemplative, it is 'correct knowledge that occurs through the assistance of previous knowledge,' exemplified by geometric proof.
Contemplative knowledge relies on inference from prior knowledge, which itself traces back to sensation.
3
Explain the role of sensation for each
Sensation supplies the simple ideas that underpin both types. Intuitive knowledge is direct sensory apprehension; contemplative is built from simple sensory ideas through mental operations like repetition, comparison, and connection.
The author insists that without sensation, no knowledge is possible.
4
Note what the author rejects and why
The passage dismisses metaphysics and primary rational principles as hindrances. This rejection is consistent with his claim that all meanings come from sense; anything beyond is illusory.
This is a strong empiricist stance, opposing rationalist notions of innate ideas.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological classification of certainties (yaqiniyyat). The author begins by defining three specific types: mutawatirat (recurrent reports), hadsiyyat (intuitive propositions), and fitriyyat (innate propositions with inherent syllogisms). For mutawatirat, certainty arises from the impossibility of collusion in falsehood among a large group, like knowledge of distant lands. Hadsiyyat rely on strong intuition, e.g., knowing the moon's light comes from the sun by observing its disappearance during an eclipse. Fitriyyat are propositions that require a medium but the medium is so immediate it escapes notice, e.g., 'two is a fifth of ten'—this is known via a medium (the concept of division) but is considered self-evident after slight reflection. The author then notes that the enumeration of certainties into six is inductive (istiqra'i), not rational ('aqli), meaning it is based on empirical survey by scholars, not a logical necessity. The author further expands the classification to include sensory certainties: smells (mashmumat), tastes (madhuqat), and colors (mulawwanat). Each is subdivided into pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral, and the author argues that these are certainties because all humans agree universally on their basic qualities (e.g., no one denies that black is not red). This demonstrates that the realm of certain knowledge extends beyond the traditional six categories.

Commentary

Hadsiyyat rely on strong intuition, e.g., knowing the moon's light comes from the sun by observing its disappearance during an eclipse. The author further expands the classification to include sensory certainties: smells (mashmumat), tastes (madhuqat), and colors (mulawwanat). Each is subdivided into pleasant, unpleasant, and neutral, and the author argues that these are certainties because all humans agree universally on their basic qualities (e.g., no one denies that black is not red). Three Sequential Levels of Guidance
Author outlines three sequential levels of guidance after the two main types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three sequential levels of guidance (creative, illumination, reaching the desired) and explain their interdependence.

Core Idea
Guidance progresses in three stages: creative capacity, illumination of path, and arrival at goal.

After classifying guidance into two types based on where the goal is reached, the author introduces three sequential levels that precede or accompany those types. These levels are: creative guidance (takwīniyya), illumination of the path (inārat al-ṭarīq), and reaching the desired (al-īṣāl ilā al-maṭlūb). Understanding this sequence is essential for grasping how divine guidance operates systematically, and why human responsibility is meaningful only after the first two levels are in place.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three levels in the passage
Locate the sentence where the author lists: 'al-takwīniyya, inārat al-ṭarīq, al-īṣāl ilā al-maṭlūb'. Note that they are introduced after the discussion of the two types.
The passage says: 'And these guidances (creative, illuminating the path, reaching the desired) are sequential.'
2
Understand the first level: creative guidance (takwīniyya)
Creative guidance refers to the innate capacity and foundational ability to be guided. It is the precondition for any further guidance. Without it, a person cannot be held accountable (taklif).
The author says: 'whoever does not obtain the first, the second is not obtained for him, rather his assignment of duty is not valid.'
3
Understand the second level: illumination of the path (inārat al-ṭarīq)
This level involves showing the right way through revelation, reason, or natural signs. It builds on the first level and is necessary before one can actually reach the goal.
The author does not elaborate much here, but 'illumination of the path' is the guidance that shows what is right and wrong.
4
Understand the third level: reaching the desired (al-īṣāl ilā al-maṭlūb)
This is the actual attainment of the goal – whether honor/rebuke in this world or Paradise/Fire in the Hereafter. It depends on having the capacity and the knowledge of the path.
This level corresponds directly to the two types discussed earlier (worldly and otherworldly).
5
Analyze the interdependence
The levels are ordered: first, then second, then third. If the first is missing, the second cannot be obtained, and if the second is missing, the third cannot be obtained. Conversely, if the third is obtained, the first two are automatically fulfilled.
This logical sequence ensures that guidance is just: everyone receives the first level, and the second and third depend on human response.

And concerning knowledge, man comes to this world empty-minded of any science or knowledge, even intuitive knowledge. And knowledge exists potentially in the human soul, appearing and opening little by little with the passage of time and man's contact with external realities through the tools of knowledge. And in the words of Imam Ali (peace be upon him): 'Intellect is an instinct that increases with knowledge and experiences.' The human soul is one, and the names 'vegetative soul', 'animal soul', and other names are only a way of recording the manifestations of the soul, just as it is said that so-and-so is truthful, meaning his truthfulness appears more than his lying, or so-and-so is noble, meaning his nobility appears more than his immorality. Muslim scholars have likened the soul to the body: if the body is harmonious in its organs, it is healthy from diseases; but if an organ becomes disordered, the body loses balance and becomes ill. Hence it is said that so-and-so's soul is sick. This concept does not contradict that at the beginning of its creation, the soul is balanced (perfect in creation). The Holy Quran has expressed the soul in several meanings representing its states or manifestations, which we will touch upon in general outlines that can be called the levels of the soul: 1) The commanding soul (al-nafs al-ammara): The soul by its nature calls to its desires of evil. Man cannot absolve himself from inclination to evil, but he can refrain from commanding it to evil and calling it to evil, by the mercy of Allah Almighty. (And I do not absolve myself; indeed the soul is a persistent enjoinder of evil, except those upon which my Lord has mercy. Indeed my Lord is Forgiving and Merciful.) Imam Ali (peace be upon him) said: 'The soul is persistent in commanding evil and immorality. Whoever trusts it, it betrays him; whoever leans on it, it destroys him; whoever is pleased with it, it leads him to the worst of places.' Moral invocations came to modify that inclination and to motivate the soul to proceed on the path of happiness and to avoid excess and negligence. Materialistic theories called for yielding to the desires of pleasure, while monasticism called for curbing pleasures and desires and abstaining from life in caves and monasteries. Islam, however, called for a middle path between them: (Say: Who has forbidden the adornment of Allah which He has produced for His servants and the good things of provision?) And at the same time, it commands curbing the soul from committing sins and evil deeds that cause chaos in society and lead to moral decay. 2) The blaming soul (al-nafs al-lawwama): It is the conscience that reproaches man for the sins and offenses he has committed, especially after he awakens from their intoxication and descends into a whirlpool of regret for what he has done. This indicates that the soul is mixed with inclination to desires and at the same time has an inclination to truth and justice. Each manifestation has its circumstances. The dominance of desires prevents the appearance of the light of truth, so he commits sins and offenses. But when his lust subsides, the beauty of life becomes clear before him and the harms of pleasure are revealed, so the soul awakens and begins to blame and reproach, to the extent that it may drive its owner to suicide because he cannot bear the weight of that crime. In summary, the blaming soul is that which, when a man does evil, blames him and reprimands him.

The Blaming Soul (Al-Nafs al-Lawwama)
Author defines the blaming soul and its psychological dynamics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the blaming soul (al-nafs al-lawwama) and its role in moral self-correction after sin.

Core Idea
The blaming soul reproaches after sin, revealing a dual inclination to both desire and righteousness.

In this passage, the author introduces the blaming soul (al-nafs al-lawwama) as the conscience that reproaches the person after committing sins. We will explore its characteristics and how it represents the soul's capacity for moral awakening, as well as the danger of excessive regret.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of al-nafs al-lawwama
Find where the author says it is 'the conscience that reproaches man for sins and offenses, especially after awakening from their intoxication.'
Key phrase: 'وهي الضمير الذي يؤنب الإنسان على ما اقترفه من السيئات'
2
Analyze the dual nature
Notice that the soul has both inclination to desires and inclination to truth. The lawwama emerges when the latter prevails after a sinful act.
3
Examine the awakening process
The passage describes how after desires subside, the harms of pleasure become clear, and the soul blames itself. This is the moment of moral reflection.
Beware of the extreme consequence: the author mentions the possibility of suicide due to unbearable guilt.
4
Summarize the function
The blaming soul serves as a mechanism for repentance and self-correction, differentiating it from the commanding soul which incites evil.
