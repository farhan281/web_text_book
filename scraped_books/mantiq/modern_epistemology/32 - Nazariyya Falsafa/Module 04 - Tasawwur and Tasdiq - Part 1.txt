The Mu'tazilite Definition of Knowledge and Al-Jubba'i's Addition
Author presents Fakhr al-Razi's view and the Mu'tazilite definition with Jubba'i's addition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of the Mu'tazilite definition of knowledge as reported by Fakhr al-Razi and explain the significance of al-Jubba'i's addition of tranquility of the soul.

Core Idea
Knowledge is true belief plus tranquility of the soul.

Fakhr al-Razi begins by asserting that knowledge is self-evident and needs no definition. Nevertheless, he transmits the Mu'tazilite definition: knowledge is 'belief of the thing as it is.' This definition emphasizes correspondence between belief and reality. Al-Jubba'i then adds an important psychological condition: 'with tranquility of the soul towards it,' indicating that genuine knowledge involves certainty, not mere opinion. In this lesson, you will dissect this definition and understand why the additional element matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions presented
First, note Fakhr al-Razi's own claim that knowledge is necessary and independent of definition. Then find the Mu'tazilite definition he transmits: 'belief of the thing as it is.' Finally, locate al-Jubba'i's addition: 'with tranquility of the soul towards it.'
The Mu'tazilite definition has two parts: the core from the Mu'tazilites and the addition from al-Jubba'i.
2
Extract the genus and differentia
The genus is 'belief' (i'tiqad), which places knowledge in the category of mental assent. The differentia is 'of the thing as it is,' which adds the condition of correspondence with reality. Al-Jubba'i introduces a second differentia: 'with tranquility of the soul,' which adds the subjective certainty.
A definition typically has one genus and one differentia; here there are two differentiae, making it a compound definition.
3
Analyze the function of the addition
Consider what 'tranquility of the soul' excludes: it excludes mere conjecture, doubt, or belief that does not bring rest. This addition distinguishes knowledge from true belief that is not certain (e.g., a correct guess).
This aligns with the later Islamic distinction between tasdiq (assent) and yaqin (certainty).
4
Evaluate whether the definition is a hadd or rasm
A hadd (real definition) captures the essence; a rasm (description) captures distinctive properties. This definition focuses on the components of knowledge as a mental state plus a relation to reality. The Mu'tazilites likely considered it a hadd, but it leans toward a rasm because it describes knowledge by its conditions rather than its quiddity.
Fakhr al-Razi's assertion that knowledge is necessary suggests he might not accept any definition as a true hadd.

Core Idea
Definitions of knowledge shift from correspondence to presence, foreshadowing 'ilm huduri.

This passage introduces two influential definitions of knowledge from early Islamic thought. Al-Baqillani's definition emphasizes accurate correspondence between knowledge and its object, while the common juristic definition introduces the concept of 'presence,' which later becomes foundational in Islamic epistemology. The Ancient Definition of Knowledge and Its Exclusion of Presential Knowledge
Author gives the ancient philosophers' definition of knowledge and critiques it for excluding presential knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ancient definition of knowledge and explain its failure to include presential knowledge.

Core Idea
Not all knowledge is a mental image – the ancient definition excludes direct presential knowledge.

In this passage, the author first establishes that 'ilm and ma'rifa are synonymous in Islamic philosophy. He then presents the classical definition from ancient philosophers: knowledge is the acquisition of the form of the thing in the mind. But this representational model is criticized for missing a crucial type of knowledge – presential knowledge (al-'ilm al-ḥuḍūrī). Finally, an alternative definition is mentioned but dismissed as vague. Your task is to dissect these definitions and understand the logical force of the critique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the equivalence claim
The author asserts that for Muslim philosophers, 'ilm and ma'rifa are the same. Notice the symmetric relationships: every 'ilm is ma'rifa and vice versa. This sets the stage.
The passage equates knowledge with knowledge of God.
2
Extract the ancient definition
Find the phrase defining knowledge: 'حصول صورة الشيء لدى العقل أو انطباع صورته في الذهن'. This is the mental representation definition.
Two versions: 'occurrence' (husūl) or 'imprint' (inṭibā').
3
Identify the critique
The author says this definition is criticized for not including presential knowledge (العلم الحضوري). Ask: what type of knowledge would that be? Knowledge without mental representation, like self-awareness.
The critique is structural: the definition's conditions exclude a whole category.
4
Assess the alternate definition
The passage mentions 'knowledge after ignorance' (العلم بعد الجهالة). The author dismisses it as vague (مبهم عام). Why? Because it does not specify what knowledge itself is.
This definition is circular and too broad.

Core Idea
Definitions of knowledge are judged by whether they include all known objects, material and immaterial.

This passage presents two competing definitions of knowledge. Mulla Sadra offers an idealist definition that reduces knowledge to immaterial presence, while a modern exegete defines it as the transformation of external realities into mental facts. Knowledge is not only from the outside; presential knowledge comes from within.

In this passage, the author critiques a definition of knowledge that overemphasizes material phenomena. He argues that such a definition fails to include presential knowledge (al-'ilm al-huduri) and acquired knowledge (al-'ilm al-husuli). Sheikh Misbah's Comprehensive Definition of Knowledge
Author presents Sheikh Misbah's comprehensive definition of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three components of Sheikh Misbah's definition of knowledge and distinguish between acquired and presential knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge is the presence of the thing's essence, image, or concept to an immaterial entity.

Sheikh Misbah al-Yazdi provides a definition of knowledge that encompasses both presential (hudhuri) and acquired (husuli) knowledge. This definition is foundational in Islamic epistemology because it identifies the common element in all knowledge: the presence (hudur) of something known to a knowing subject. In this lesson, we unpack each component and see how it applies to key concepts in Islamic philosophy and theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify definiendum and definiens
The definiendum is 'al-ilm' (knowledge). The definiens is 'hudur dhat al-shay' aw suratihi al-juz'iyya aw mafhumihi al-kulli lada mawjud mujarrad'. Note that the definiens has three alternative elements.
2
Understand the three alternatives
The first is the essence itself (dhat al-shay'), covering presential knowledge. The second is the partial image (sura juz'iyya), covering sensory perception of a particular. The third is the universal concept (mafhum kulli), covering intellectual concepts.
3
Recognize the condition
The presence is to an immaterial existent (mawjud mujarrad), i.e., the soul or intellect. This excludes knowledge by inanimate objects.
4
See the comprehensiveness
This definition includes both 'ilm hudhuri (presential) and 'ilm husuli (acquired). In presential, the essence itself is present; in acquired, the image or concept is present.
The definition also covers the divisions of intelligibles (ma'qulat) and concepts (mafahim).

Three Basic Issues in Islamic Epistemology
Author outlines three basic issues in epistemology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the three fundamental problems of epistemology as outlined by the author.

Core Idea
Epistemology investigates the possibility, sources, and nature of knowledge.

After defining knowledge, the author presents the three core questions that any theory of knowledge must address: Can we know? Where does knowledge come from? What is the form of knowledge? These questions have been debated by Islamic philosophers and theologians for centuries. In this lesson, we explore each question and its relevance to Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first problem: possibility and limits of knowledge
Ask: Is knowledge possible? Can man know all things? This relates to skepticism and the extent of human cognition. In Islamic thought, al-Ghazali's skeptical phase and subsequent affirmation of knowledge is a classic example.
2
Identify the second problem: sources of knowledge
Determine whether intellect, senses, or other sources (revelation, intuition) are valid. In Islamic epistemology, the Qur'an and Sunnah are primary sources, and reason (aql) is also debated as a source.
3
Identify the third problem: nature of knowledge
Is knowledge intellectual and ideal, or empirical and real? This touches on debates between rationalism and empiricism in Islamic philosophy, e.g., Ibn Sina's rationalism vs. al-Ghazali's occasionalism.

Plato's Theory of Recollection: The Soul's Pre-Existent Knowledge
Plato's theory of recollection explained.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three key components of Plato's theory of recollection and explain their implications for epistemology.

Core Idea
Knowledge is remembrance of what the soul knew before birth.

This passage introduces Plato's theory of recollection (anāmnēsis), a cornerstone of his epistemology. You will learn how Plato argued that the soul, before entering the body, knew all truths in the world of Forms. Forgetting occurs at birth, and learning is the process of recalling these innate ideas. Understanding this theory will help you contrast it with Islamic views on the origin of knowledge, such as the concept of fitrah and the debate over innate ideas in Islamic philosophy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim about the soul's pre-existence
The passage states that the soul existed before the body in the world of intelligibles and abstracts (the Forms). It had complete knowledge of all truths there.
This is a central tenet of Plato's epistemology.
2
Extract the process of forgetting and remembering
Upon entering the body, the soul forgets its previous knowledge due to the shock of embodiment, but the knowledge is not completely erased. Learning is the gradual recollection of that knowledge.
3
Understand the role of sensation
Sensation acts as a stimulus (al-ihsās) that triggers the recollection process. It does not provide new knowledge but reminds the soul of what it already knows.
4
Evaluate the theory's implications for epistemology
This is an innatist view: true knowledge is not derived from sensory experience but is pre-existing in the soul. It contrasts with empiricist theories and raises questions about the origin of concepts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the function of the common sense (al-hiss al-mushtarak) as the integrating center for all sensory input.

Core Idea
The common sense is the meeting point where all sensations converge before reaching the brain.

After listing the five external senses, the author introduces the concept of a 'common sense' (hiss mushtarak) that acts as a central hub. This idea is crucial for understanding how disparate sensory data are unified into coherent perceptions. The passage describes it as the basis for each sense's transmission.

1
Locate the term 'common sense'
Find the phrase 'حس مشترك' in the Arabic text. It is described as 'أشبه بالمركز العام الذي تتلاقى فيه جميع الاحساسات' (similar to a central hub where all sensations meet).

Understand its role
The common sense is not a separate sense but the locus where the products of all senses gather. Each individual sense has a 'basis' (اساس) in it.

Connect to brain transmission
The passage states that 'الحس كرمز لبقية الحواس ينقل صورة من الواقع المادي الى الدماغ' (the sense, as a symbol for the rest, transmits an image from material reality to the brain). This suggests that the common sense is involved in the transmission.
The 'sense' here may refer to the common sense as representative.
4
Reflect on its epistemic importance
Without a common sense, each sensory modality would remain isolated. The unity of experience depends on this integrative function.
In Kalam, this is used to explain how we perceive objects as unified despite multiple sensory inputs.

The Four Technical Usages of al-'Aql
Linguistic definition and four technical usages.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four technical usages of 'aql (common people, mutakallimun, ethicists, philosophers) as classified by Sadr al-Muta'allihin.

Core Idea
The word 'aql has four distinct technical meanings across Islamic disciplines.

Sadr al-Muta'allihin's classification shows that the term 'aql is not a single, univocal concept. Its meaning shifts depending on whether it is used by common people, theologians, ethicists, or philosophers. Recognizing these nuances is essential for accurate reading of classical texts.

Guided Path
1
Trace the linguistic root
The passage begins with the linguistic meaning: 'aqala al-shay' means understanding and contemplating. Note that the core is 'perception through intellect'.
2
Identify common people's usage
Common people use 'aql as the power to distinguish good from evil in practical affairs. They refer to someone as 'āqil if they have sound judgment about what to do and avoid.
3
Identify the mutakallimun's usage
Mutakallimun use 'aql for the premises (muqaddimāt) from which judgments of good and evil are deduced. For them, 'aql is a source of moral knowledge, not the faculty itself.
4
Identify the ethicists' and philosophers' usages
Ethicists use 'aql for the actions themselves that are judged good or evil. Philosophers (al-hukamā') use 'aql as the knowing power (al-quwwa al-‘ālima), but context can shift it to mean mere perception.

The Second Meaning of ‘Aql: Instrument of Distinction
Author presents the second meaning of 'aql as the tool for discriminating between good and evil, with Imam Ali's sayings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the second meaning of ‘aql as an instrument for practical discrimination and explain its role in ethical decision-making using Imam Ali's criterion.

Core Idea
The true ‘āqil is not one who merely knows good from evil, but one who judges the better of two evils.

The author presents a second, practical meaning of ‘aql that goes beyond theoretical knowledge. Here, ‘aql is the tool that enables a person to discern what is beneficial from what is harmful in concrete situations. Imam Ali’s sayings illustrate that the highest level of intellect is not simply recognizing good versus evil, but making comparative judgments when only harmful options exist.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shift in meaning
Notice that the author explicitly states this is the second meaning of 'aql: an instrument for discriminating between beneficial and harmful. Contrast this with the first meaning (likely a theoretical faculty). This shift moves from abstract knowledge to practical application.
2
Analyze Imam Ali's first saying
Read: 'The ‘āqil is not one who knows good from evil; rather, the ‘āqil is one who knows the better of two evils.' Why does the Imam negate the simpler knowledge and affirm the comparative judgment? This indicates that true intellect is shown when no pure good is available.
3
Understand the practical implication
In real-life choices, we often face situations with no pure good; intellect is required to weigh harms and choose the lesser one. This is a higher-order cognitive skill, not just moral knowledge.
4
Connect to Islamic ethics
This principle is applied in fiqh when choosing between two prohibited acts – e.g., lying to save a life. The lesser harm is chosen. This shows 'aql as an instrument of practical reasoning.
5
Reflect on the second saying: abundance of correctness
The statement 'Abundance of correctness indicates abundance of intellect' supports the idea that correct judgments over time are evidence of a sound intellect. This is an empirical indicator of the instrument's functioning.

Third Meaning of 'Aql: Good Actions
Author presents the third meaning of 'aql as good actions, with Imam al-Sadiq's criteria for being truly 'āqil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three criteria of 'aql according to Imam al-Sadiq and explain how this definition links intellect to ethical action.

Core Idea
Intellect is not just cognition—it is manifested in just and forbearing conduct.

In this lesson, we explore the third meaning of 'aql, where it is equated with good action (al-'amal al-ḥasan). Imam al-Sadiq's statement provides concrete criteria for what it means to be truly 'āqil. This understanding bridges the gap between abstract intellect and lived virtue, showing that genuine rationality manifests in ethical behavior.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
The term being defined is 'aql in its third meaning, identified here as good action (al-'amal al-ḥasan). Note that this differs from the purely cognitive definitions.
This definitional shift shows 'aql as a moral faculty.
2
Extract the three criteria
Imam al-Sadiq lists three conditions for the 'āqil: (1) giving rights to others in satisfaction and anger, (2) wanting for others what one wants for oneself, (3) showing forbearance (ḥilm) when others stumble.
These criteria form the distinguishing features of this definition.
3
Classify the definition type
This is a rasm (description) because it lists essential characteristics rather than providing a genus and differentia. The genus is 'good action', and the differentia is the set of three criteria.
A hadd would require a simpler, more formal structure.
4
Assess completeness
Consider whether the three criteria exhaust what it means to be 'āqil in this sense. They cover justice, empathy, and forbearance, but other virtues like wisdom might also be implied.
Imam al-Sadiq presents these as necessary and sufficient for this third meaning.

The Fourth Meaning of 'Aql: Knowing Power as Inner Light
Author presents the fourth meaning of 'aql as the knowing power, with an Imami saying and a common definition as inner light.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourth meaning of 'aql as the knowing power and explain the metaphor of light used in its definition.

Core Idea
‘Aql is not just reasoning; it is an inner light that unveils intelligibles directly.

In this passage, the author expands on the fourth meaning of ‘aql, moving beyond mere reasoning to present it as a knowing power (al-quwwa al-‘ālima). He supports this with a saying of Imam Musa al-Kazim linking intellect to scholarly etiquette and moral restraint. Then he gives a striking definition: ‘aql is a light that shines in the soul, removing veils so that intelligible forms become clear, as sensory forms appear to the eye. This metaphor places ‘aql as an intuitive illumination rather than a purely discursive tool.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth meaning of 'aql
Locate the phrase 'al-quwwa al-‘ālima' in the Arabic. The author explicitly calls this the fourth meaning of 'aql.
This meaning is common in Islamic philosophy.
2
Examine the Imami saying
Read the quote from Imam Musa al-Kazim. It connects intellect ('aql) to the etiquette of scholars ('adab al-‘ulamā') and refraining from harm (kaf al-adhā). This suggests that intellectual growth is tied to ethical and scholarly behavior.
The saying adds a practical dimension to the definition.
3
Analyze the light definition
Break down the definition: 'aql is a light (nūr) that shines in the horizon of the soul (ufq al-nafs), from which the coverings of veils (ghawāshī al-hujub) are removed, and images of intelligibles (suwar al-ma‘qūlāt) become manifest like sensory images in the eye.
This is a metaphorical definition emphasizing direct, intuitive knowledge.
4
Compare to sensory knowledge
The definition explicitly parallels the intellect's perception of intelligibles to the eye's perception of sensibles. This underscores the idea that intellect is a direct 'seeing' of universals.
This analogy is powerful but also has limits—unlike the eye, the intellect does not have a physical organ.

Identify the two premises

Derive the conclusion
From the two premises, the mind infers: 'Therefore, the water temperature rises.' This is the third proposition.

Commentary
This passage defines fitra (innate nature) and provides extensive examples from the animal and human realms to illustrate its workings. The author begins by defining fitra as the collection of drives and controls—psychological, physical, and hormonal—that govern an individual's life. Finally, the author discusses laughter and crying as innate responses to deviations from the natural fitra system: smiling for slight deviations, laughter for sharper deviations, and crying for sorrowful deviations. The intensity of these reactions corresponds to the degree of deviation. This passage serves to establish fitra as a divinely instituted system that guides all creatures, especially humans, toward their natural ends and reactions.

Love of Absolute Perfection as an Innate Manifestation
Author discusses the innate love of absolute perfection as a manifestation of fitra.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the argument from innate love of perfection to the existence of absolute perfection.

Core Idea
The infinite human longing for perfection points to a Perfect Being beyond all worldly objects.

This lesson examines the author's claim that all humans share an innate love for absolute perfection. This love drives all human endeavors and never finds ultimate satisfaction in worldly objects, leading to the conclusion that the true beloved is absolute perfection—a key argument for God's existence in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The author states that all humans have an innate love for perfection. Locate the sentences that assert this as a universal fact.
2
Observe the evidence

3
Recognize the dissatisfaction pattern

5
Apply to theology
In Islamic kalam, this argument is used to prove the existence of a Perfect Being (God). Reflect on how the innate love for perfection leads to knowledge of the Necessary Being.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the manifestations of innate human nature (fitra), a key epistemological concept in Islamic thought. The author begins by referencing deviant sexual practices as violations of fitra, then enumerates several innate inclinations. First is the instinct for self-preservation and aversion to death and deficiency. Central is the innate love (ʿishq) for absolute perfection and pure beauty. The author argues that all humans are created with this love; it is the hidden driver behind all pursuits—wealth, knowledge, worship, etc. However, the beloved objects are never fully satisfying; as humans attain one object, they soon seek a more perfect one, demonstrating that the true beloved is absolute perfection without any defect. The second major manifestation is innate religiosity (taʿabbud): humans are naturally inclined to worship and rely on a higher power, especially in times of distress. The author cites the Qur'anic verse (29:65) as evidence that even polytheists turn to God sincerely when in danger. Third, the human soul innately distinguishes between high and low, preferring the noble and good while detesting the elevation of the base. This leads to an innate sense of justice and moral valuation. Fourth, humans naturally accept good and reject evil when known. Finally, love of comfort and freedom are mentioned as examples. The passage thus establishes that certain universal human tendencies are part of the original creation, providing a basis for epistemology and ethics.
