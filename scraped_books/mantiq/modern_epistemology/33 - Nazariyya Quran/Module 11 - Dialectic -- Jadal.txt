Refining Takfir: The Case of Bodily Resurrection
Author critiques al-Ghazali's takfir of philosophers, using Ibn Sina as an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between outright denial of a creedal principle and interpretive disagreement on its modality, and apply this distinction to evaluate whether takfir is warranted.

Core Idea
Takfir is for denying the principle, not for differing on the 'how'.

This passage addresses the important issue of takfir (declaring someone a disbeliever). Al-Ghazali declared certain philosophers kafir for denying bodily resurrection. The author argues that such a blanket ruling is too harsh because the philosophers did not deny the reality of resurrection, only its modality. This lesson will help you understand the critical distinction between rejecting a fundamental creed and interpreting its details, and why this distinction matters in Islamic theology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of critique
Find where the author refers to al-Ghazali's position: the assertion that philosophers who deny bodily resurrection deserve takfir. This is the claim being refined.
Look for the phrase 'التشنيع الذي أوصله الغزالي حد وجوب الكفر'.
2
Extract the author's core distinction
Notice how the author distinguishes between 'al-munkir al-jahid' (the denier who rejects) and 'al-muta'awwil' (the one who interprets the modality). The former deserves takfir; the latter does not.
This distinction is based on the reality (haqiqa) vs. the modality (kayfiyya).
3
Apply the distinction to Ibn Sina's case
The author cites a researcher who showed that Ibn Sina affirmed the resurrection in principle (asl) and bodily resurrection according to revelation, but his intellect could only grasp spiritual resurrection. He is not a denier, so takfir does not apply.
Ibn Sina's position is an interpretation (ta'wil), not rejection.
4
Evaluate the implications for takfir
Conclude that takfir should be reserved for those who reject a definitive creed outright, not for those who differ on how it occurs. This refines the scope of takfir in Islamic theology.
This is a critical tool for avoiding extremism in declaring disbelief.

The Dialectical Relationship Between Āfāq and Anfūs
Explores the dialectical relationship between the two divisions of the witnessed realm using Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the dialectical interdependence between the external world (āfāq) and the self (anfus) using Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
The horizons and selves are not separate but dialectically intertwined.

This passage explores how the Quran presents a relationship of mutual interaction between the external cosmos (āfāq) and the inner self (anfus). Through three verses, we see that reflection on the self leads to understanding the creation of the heavens and earth, and vice versa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two domains
Recognize that the passage discusses two fields: āfāq (horizons/external world) and anfus (selves/inner world).
2
Extract the Quranic evidence
Read each of the three verses and note how they link the two domains.
Verses: Al-Kahf 18:51, Ar-Rum 30:8, Ar-Rum 30:22.
3
Analyze the linkage
In each verse, see how the external and internal are connected—e.g., verse 30:8 calls for reflection within the self about the creation of the heavens and earth.
4
Synthesize the dialectic
Conclude that the relationship is dialectical: each domain illuminates the other, and they are not independent.

Commentary
This passage discusses the dialectical relationship (علاقة جدلية) between the two fields of afaq and anfus. It cites three Quranic verses as evidence. The first verse (Al-Kahf 18:51) emphasizes that neither the creation of the heavens and earth nor the creation of selves was witnessed by others, pointing to divine transcendence. The second verse (Ar-Rum 30:8) calls for reflection within the self (anfus) about the creation of the heavens and earth, indicating a connection between internal reflection and external observation. The third verse (Ar-Rum 30:22) highlights signs in the creation of heavens and earth and in the diversity of languages and colors, showing that both domains contain signs for the knowledgeable. The passage thus argues that the external (horizons) and internal (selves) are not disconnected but interact dialectically to reveal divine truths.

Quranic Dialectical Method and Epistemological Research Aspects
Author explains Quran's dialectical strategy and previews the epistemological study.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quran's dialectical strategy of shifting to a common ground and explain the two research directions in Islamic epistemology.

Core Idea
The Quran does not impose premises; it transforms them into conclusions acceptable to both parties.

This passage introduces the Quran's unique argumentation method, which avoids imposing premises and instead seeks common ground. It also outlines the two-fold research focus of the author: the problem of doubt and certainty, and the nature of truth. We will analyze the method using Quranic examples and understand how this approach relates to Islamic dialectic (jadal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial strategy
The author states the Quran questions and answers at once, but the opponent is forced to accept that guidance is either with the Quran's owner or his opponent. Note that the opponent has no alternative but to affirm this framework.
2
Recognize the call for detachment
The Quran sometimes calls for 'al-tajarud' (detachment) from prior assumptions so that the opponent can personally verify the truth through reason, whether in pairs or alone.
See verse Saba 46.
3
Examine the Abraham-Nimrod debate
In al-Baqara 258, Abraham shifts the argument from Nimrod's claimed ability to give life and death to a shared premise about the sun's rising. Observe how this transforms a disputed premise into an undeniable conclusion.
The shift illustrates the Quranic method.
4
Understand the transformation of musallama
The author says the Quran does not insist on a premise the opponent rejects; instead, it transforms that premise into a logical conclusion, thereby making it a methodological premise (musallama) for both parties.
This is the core dialectical move.
5
Outline the two research aspects
The author introduces two directions: first, research on doubt vs certainty in the Quran; second, research on truth and its possibility. These structure the rest of the study.

Chapter Four: The First Section - Knowledge between Doubt and Certainty. The Quran, though it questions and answers at once, the other party has nothing but to affirm what the Quran has established: that guidance is either with the Quran's owner or his opponent, and how can opposites meet? The Quran calls, sometimes, to detachment and discarding any prior determinations, so that the other party can verify for themselves the truth of the Prophet's call - but that will only be after using the intellect, whether that be in a meeting of opponents, or each being alone: 'Say: I only exhort you to one thing: that you stand up for Allah in pairs and individually, then reflect. There is in your companion no madness; he is only a warner to you before a severe punishment' (Saba: 46/34). And the Quran descends with the other without insisting on arguing him regarding his doubt, in order to make the reply in another, more eloquent and greater style: 'Have you not seen the one who argued with Abraham about his Lord because Allah had given him kingship? When Abraham said: My Lord is the One who gives life and causes death. He said: I give life and cause death. Abraham said: Indeed Allah brings the sun from the east; so bring it from the west. So the disbeliever was dumbfounded, and Allah does not guide the wrongdoing people' (al-Baqara: 258/2). We observe in all these verses the lack of insistence on positing a premise and then proceeding with it as long as the other does not acknowledge it. Rather, the Quran's method of descending with him transformed it into a logical conclusion, and thus a methodological premise for both parties. The research on the possibility of obtaining knowledge takes two directions: one is research on possibility from the side of the problem of doubt and certainty and the stance towards them in the Holy Quran, and the other side is research on truth and the possibility of obtaining it.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion explaining the Quranic method of argumentation and introducing the structure of the study. The author first states that the Quran, while engaging in dialogue, does not impose its premises; rather, the opponent is forced to acknowledge the Quran's established truth that guidance is either with the proponent of the Quran or his adversary. The Quran sometimes calls for detachment from prior assumptions so that the opponent can personally verify the Prophet's message, but only after using reason, whether in group discussion or individual reflection. Two Quranic verses are cited as examples: the first (Saba 46) urges standing for Allah in pairs or alone to reflect, emphasizing that the Prophet is only a warner; the second (al-Baqara 258) recounts Abraham's debate with Nimrod, where Abraham shifts the argument from the opponent's claimed ability to give life and death to a shared premise about the sun's rising. The author observes that the Quran does not insist on a premise that the opponent rejects; instead, it transforms that premise into a logical conclusion, thereby making it a methodological premise acceptable to both sides. Finally, the author outlines the research's two aspects: one examines the problem of doubt and certainty in the Quran, and the other examines truth and the possibility of attaining it. All examples are from the Quran itself, consistent with Islamic intellectual tradition.
