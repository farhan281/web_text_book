Defining the Theory of Knowledge in the Quran
Author defines the first key term: theory of knowledge in the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'the theory of knowledge in the Quran' as a complete integrated system and identify its five constituent components.

Core Idea
The Quran's theory of knowledge is an integrated system, not scattered verses.

The author opens by defining the central term of the study: the theory of knowledge in the Quran. This is not a single verse but a complete system that encompasses the concept, nature, sources, methods, and possibility of knowledge. Understanding this definition is the foundation for the entire course.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined: 'نظرية المعرفة في القرآن الكريم' (theory of knowledge in the Holy Quran).
2
Break down the definition into components
The author lists five components: concept of knowledge, its nature, its sources and methods, the possibility of obtaining it, and its limits. List them in order.
These components will each be defined in subsequent terms.
3
Note the integration claim
The definition says 'complete integrated system' (نسق كلي متكامل). This means the components are not independent but interrelated.
This is a key claim about the Quran's epistemological coherence.
4
Connect to educational knowledge
The definition adds that this system reflects on 'educational knowledge' (المعرفة التربوية). This is the application dimension.
This connects theory to practice.

- The nature of knowledge in the Holy Quran: determining the relationship between the knowing subject (man) and the known object (the Holy Quran) and interpreting this relationship in light of the general concept of the theory.

- Sources and methods of knowledge in the Holy Quran: the origins from which the Muslim researcher derives his knowledge and the tools with which he deals with various phenomena. - The possibility of obtaining knowledge in the Holy Quran: investigating the Quran's view of knowledge, in terms of whether it is inherited or acquired, and its position on the method of skepticism and skeptics, and building on illusion and conjecture, then the Quran's view of truth from its various angles.

- Educational implications: employing the concept of the theory of knowledge in the current reality of Islamic education, through the indication of Quranic texts related to the theory on the totality of educational knowledge associated with it.

Defining Sources and Methods of Knowledge in the Quran
Author defines the third key term: sources and methods of knowledge in the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish primary sources (revelation, sense, reason) from secondary sources (intuition, inspiration, true dreams) of knowledge in the Quran.

Core Idea
The Quran recognizes multiple sources but ranks revelation, sense, and reason as primary.

This lesson clarifies the third key term: the sources and methods of knowledge. The author provides a clear hierarchy: primary sources (revelation, sense perception, reason) and secondary sources (intuition, inspiration, true dreams). This distinction is crucial for understanding the Quranic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Locate the term and its two categories
Find 'مصادر المعرفة وطرائقها' (sources and methods). Note that the author divides them into primary (رئيسة) and secondary (ثانوية).
2
List primary sources
The primary sources are: الوحي (revelation), الحس (sense perception), العقل (reason). They are called 'origins' (أصول) and 'tools' (أدوات).
These are the foundational channels of knowledge.
3
List secondary sources
The secondary sources are: الحدس (intuition), الإلهام (inspiration), الرؤيا الصادقة (true dreams).
These are supplementary but not primary.
4
Understand the hierarchy
The author presents primary and secondary as distinct categories. Primary are the foundation; secondary are additional but not equal in authority.
This hierarchy is important for epistemological evaluation.

Defining the Possibility of Knowledge in the Quran
Author defines the fourth key term: possibility of obtaining knowledge in the Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Quran's stance on the possibility of knowledge, including its view on inherited vs. acquired knowledge, skepticism, and truth.

Core Idea
The Quran affirms that knowledge is possible and addresses its origin, the limits of skepticism, and the nature of truth.

The fourth term examines whether knowledge is possible according to the Quran. This sub-topic investigates the Quran's position on whether knowledge is inherited or acquired, its response to skepticism, and its concept of truth. This is a meta-epistemological question that underlies all other definitions.

Guided Path

Identify the term and its three sub-questions

The Quran's answer affects how education is conceptualized.

Analyze the stance on skepticism
The author mentions 'the method of skepticism and skeptics' (منهج الشك والشكاك) and 'building on illusion and conjecture' (البناء على الوهم والظن).
The Quran rejects extreme skepticism.
4
Analyze the Quran's view of truth
The author ends with 'the Quran's view of truth from its various angles' (نظرة القرآن الكريم إلى الحقيقة من زواياها المختلفة).
This indicates a multi-faceted concept of truth.

Author presents linguistic and technical definitions of 'knowledge' from al-Tahanawi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the linguistic derivation of ma'rifa and explain its relationship to 'ilm and perception.

Core Idea
Ma'rifa is rooted in recognition and extends to all forms of perception.

Before diving into technical definitions, we must understand the linguistic basis of 'ma'rifa'. Al-Tahanawi's summary begins with the root 'عرف' and its verb forms, showing that 'ma'rifa' is closely tied to the idea of knowing something by a mark or evidence. This lesson will walk you through the language and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root
Find the triliteral root of 'ma'rifa' in the passage. It is 'ع ر ف'. The noun 'urf' means 'known' and is the opposite of 'nukr' (unknown).
2
Understand the verb forms
The verb 'arrafa' (Form II) means to make someone know something, as in 'arrafa baytahu' (made him know his house) or 'arrafa al-amr' (informed him of the matter).
Form II often has a causative meaning.
3
Analyze Sibawayh's example
Sibawayh says 'arraftuhu Zaydan' – he made the verb transitive to two objects: 'him' and 'Zayd'. This shows that ma'rifa can involve direct object knowledge.
This grammatical point links to epistemology: knowledge is direct awareness.
4
Connect to 'ilm and perception
The passage states that 'irfan equals 'ilm. So linguistically, ma'rifa is synonymous with knowledge in an absolute sense, but the technical definitions will refine this.

Al-Tahanawi's Sixfold Typology of Knowledge as Perception
Author presents linguistic and technical definitions of 'knowledge' from al-Tahanawi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any instance of knowledge (ma'rifa) according to al-Tahanawi's six categories of perception (idrak).

Core Idea
Knowledge as perception covers simple/compound, particular/universal, and conceptual/judgmental modes.

After the linguistic root, al-Tahanawi presents six technical definitions that exhaustively categorize knowledge as perception. Each definition targets a different aspect: absolute vs. specified, simple vs. compound, particular vs. universal, and evidence-based. This lesson will help you navigate this typology and apply it to Islamic epistemic examples.

Guided Path
1
List the six definitions
Write down the six definitions from the passage. They are enumerated, but note that definition 3 appears to have its number missing in the OCR (shown as a dash). It is 'Perception of the compound'.
The six definitions are: 1) absolute perception, 2) perception of the simple, 3) perception of the compound, 4) perception of the particular, 5) perception of the universal, 6) perception of the particular from evidence.
2
Identify the axes of classification
The definitions use dimensions: simple vs compound, particular vs universal, and also note that each can be either conceptual (tasawwur) or judgmental (tasdiq). The first definition is absolute.
Thus, any perception can be classified along these axes.
3
Distinguish concept (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq)
Tasawwur is the mental representation of an essence without judgment, while tasdiq is affirmation or denial. Many definitions specify both modes.
Example: tasawwur of 'human' is a concept; tasdiq that 'human is rational' is a judgment.
4
Understand the sixth definition: particular from evidence
This is knowledge of a particular thing based on a sign or proof (dalil). For instance, knowing a specific person by seeing their footprints.
This connects ma'rifa to inference (istidlal), common in kalam and fiqh.

Defining al-Ghayb: Genus, Differentia, and the Role of al-Shahada
Author defines al-Ghayb as all that cannot be fully perceived by the senses, and links it to al-Shahada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the genus and differentia from the definition of al-Ghayb and evaluate its completeness.

Core Idea
The Islamic concept of al-Ghayb is defined by the limitation of human senses, not by absence of evidence.

In this passage, the author provides a precise definition of al-Ghayb that distinguishes it from both materialist and Western metaphysical conceptions. Understanding how this definition is constructed helps us appreciate the epistemological foundation of Islamic theology, where the unseen is known through its evidence in the witnessed world.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate the phrase 'عالم الغيب' in the Arabic passage. Note that the author gives a definition in the final sentence: 'إن عالم الغيب هو جميع ما لا قدرة للإنسان على الإحاطة بتفصيلاته، عن طريق الحواس، على نحو قاطع.'
The definiendum is 'عالم الغيب'.
2
Extract the genus (المقولة العامة)
The genus is the broader category: 'جميع ما لا قدرة للإنسان على الإحاطة بتفصيلاته' (everything that man cannot fully encompass). This includes both material and immaterial things that are beyond human full perception.
The genus is not simply 'non-sensible' but specifically what is beyond human capacity to encompass.
3
Extract the differentia (الفصل المميز)
The differentia specifies the type of incapacity: 'عن طريق الحواس، على نحو قاطع' (through the senses, definitively). This distinguishes al-Ghayb from things that are temporarily unknown or partially perceived.
The differentia emphasizes the role of sensory limitation.
4
Test completeness of the definition
Check whether the definition includes all instances of al-Ghayb (e.g., the six heavens and earths mentioned earlier) and excludes what is not al-Ghayb (e.g., material objects perceived by the senses). The definition covers the six heavens and earths as they are beyond full sensory perception.
The author also notes that evidence for al-Ghayb is in al-Shahada, so the definition implicitly requires evidence from the witnessed world.
5
Evaluate the type of definition
This definition is a rasm (description) rather than a hadd (essential definition) because it describes al-Ghayb through the relation to human perception, not through its essence. It is effective for demarcating the concept.
In Islamic logic, a hadd requires the true genus and differentia. Here the genus is 'what man cannot encompass' and differentia is 'through senses definitively'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intellect is two: one innate like a seed, one acquired like sunlight—both needed for knowledge and responsibility.

Having defined 'aql, the author now presents a classification found among scholars: intellect is divided into instinctive (gharizi) and acquired (mustafad). The instinctive is an innate preparedness for knowledge, while the acquired is the actual knowledge that strengthens it. The Rational and Empirical Necessity of Prophethood
Author argues prophethood is a necessity using reason and empirical evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the rational and empirical arguments the author uses to establish the necessity of prophethood.

Core Idea
Prophethood is a rational and historical necessity, not merely a religious belief.

The author begins by asserting that prophethood is a necessity (darura) affirmed by both intellect and the inductive study of human societies. This lesson examines that double argument: the rational consensus on the greatness of prophets and the empirical evidence of their monotheistic message. Understanding this lays the foundation for the epistemological framework of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of evidence
Read the first two Arabic sentences. The author mentions two grounds: 'تؤيدها العقول' (supported by intellects) and 'يثبتها الواقع الاستقرائي للمجتمعات الإنسانية' (established by the inductive reality of human societies). Recognize these as rational and empirical arguments.
This is the core structure of the proof.
2
Analyze the rational argument
The rational argument is that 'إجماع البشرية على عظمة هؤلاء الأنبياء' (consensus of humanity on the greatness of these prophets) and the monotheistic content of their message ('التوحيد') indicate that prophethood is a universal human need. Why would reason affirm this? Because humans require guidance on the Creator-creature relationship.
Consensus (ijma') is a common proof in kalam.
3
Analyze the empirical argument
The empirical argument is based on 'الواقع الاستقرائي' (inductive reality). The author claims that observing human societies across history shows that prophets have been universally revered and their teachings have shaped civilizations. This is an inductive generalization from historical data.
This is similar to the argument from general revelation.
4
Synthesize the two arguments
Combine both: reason demands a link between Creator and creature, and history shows that prophets provide this link. Thus, prophethood is a 'ضرورة' (necessity) that no rational society can ignore.
The synthesis is implicit but crucial.

Tawatur and the Testimony of Modern Science
Author explains tawatur as condition for certain knowledge and cites Bucaille's empirical testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions of tawatur to evaluate the certainty of historical reports, and analyze how Maurice Bucaille's study uses empirical evidence to affirm the Quran's divine origin.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge of revelation requires mass transmission (tawatur) and consistency with reason.

After establishing the necessity of prophethood, the author introduces an epistemic criterion: tawatur (mass transmission). This lesson unpacks that criterion and then shows how a modern researcher, Maurice Bucaille, applied empirical methods to support the Quran's authenticity. You will learn to distinguish certain historical knowledge from probable reports.

Guided Path
1
Define tawatur and its conditions
From the Arabic: 'المعرفة الخبرية تكون يقينية حينما تبلغ حد التواتر' (historical knowledge becomes certain when it reaches the level of tawatur). The two conditions are: (1) the number of transmitters makes collusion on a lie impossible, and (2) the content does not contradict rational principles ('لا تعارض مضمون هذا الخبر مع أصل من أصول العقل').
Tawatur is a key concept in usul al-fiqh for establishing the authenticity of hadith.
2
Apply tawatur to the Quran
The Quran has been transmitted by countless individuals across generations, meeting the numerical condition. Its content also aligns with reason, as argued by scholars. Thus, for Muslims, the Quran is certain (yaqini) knowledge.
This is a standard kalam argument.
3
Analyze Bucaille's inductive study
The author cites Maurice Bucaille, who studied the three scriptures and concluded that the Quran is the most reliable source. Bucaille's argument is empirical: the scientific statements in the Quran could not have been known in 7th-century Arabia, so they must be from a divine source. This is an inductive (istiqra'i) proof.
Bucaille's work is an example of using modern science to corroborate revelation.
4
Evaluate the strength of the evidence
Does Bucaille's study meet the tawatur condition? It is a single researcher's inductive conclusion, not mass transmission. However, the author uses it as supplementary evidence. Distinguish between the certain knowledge from tawatur and the probable evidence from empirical study.
The author does not claim Bucaille's study gives yaqin, but it is a 'shahada' (testimony).

Critiquing Divine Knowledge and Inspiration as Epistemic Sources
Critique of 'ilm ladunni and ilham as epistemic sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemic criteria used by Ibn al-Qayyim to reject claims of knowledge without evidence and evaluate his redefinition of 'ilm ladunni.

Core Idea
True knowledge requires proof; claims of direct divine knowledge without evidence are unreliable.

This passage tackles a critical epistemological question: can knowledge be obtained without any reasoning or evidence? The author, citing Ibn al-Qayyim, argues that all genuine knowledge must be supported by proofs. Claims of 'divine knowledge' (al-‘ilm al-ladunni) that bypass evidence are rejected, and inspiration (ilham) is deemed subjective and uncertain unless verified by revelation. Understanding this critique is essential for distinguishing valid from invalid sources of knowledge in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Target Claim
Find the claim being criticized: that knowledge can be acquired directly from God without any cause or reasoning (i.e., the common Sufi notion of 'ilm ladunni). The passage begins by noting the Quranic verse and then critiques the idea that such knowledge makes all other knowledge vanish.
The target is not the term 'ilm ladunni itself, but the claim that it can occur without evidence.
2
Analyze Ibn al-Qayyim's Argument
Ibn al-Qayyim argues that true knowledge is based on evidence (shawāhid) and proofs (adilla). He asserts that Allah has linked definitions to their causes, so no knowledge can occur without a proof. He emphasizes that even prophets were given proofs to establish their truth.
Note the epistemological principle: definitions (ta'rīfāt) are tied to causes (asbāb), just as existents (kā'ināt) are tied to their causes.
3
Examine the Redefinition of 'Ilm Ladunni
The passage redefines 'ilm ladunni as 'that for which the correct proof has established that it came from Allah on the tongue of His messengers.' This shifts it from direct, unmediated knowledge to mediated, proven knowledge via revelation.
This is a critical refinement: the new definition requires evidence and channels knowledge through prophetic transmission.
4
Apply the Same Logic to Ilhām
The author extends the critique to inspiration (ilham). Since we cannot verify its origin (God, Satan, or self), it yields only probability, not certainty. Ibn al-Sam'ani's position is that inspiration must conform to Sharia to be accepted, and even then it is not a legal proof (hujja shar'iyya) unless all other proofs are absent.
Thus, ilham is demoted from a source of knowledge to a subjective experience that can only be accepted if it aligns with revelation.

Chapter Four, Section One: Knowledge between Doubt and Certainty
Allah, Allah said: (The believers will gather on the Day of Resurrection and say: 'If only we could seek intercession from our Lord.' They will come to Adam and say: 'You are the father of mankind; Allah created you with His hand, made His angels prostrate to you, and taught you the names of everything. So intercede for us with your Lord.') 
As for an objector objecting to the reason for Allah teaching Adam all kinds of named things, and among them were some that are not rational, since He said 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahum) and did not say 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahā) – because among them were the angels, humans, and jinn, who are rational, so the more complete prevailed, according to the Arab custom of giving precedence to the more perfect over the deficient. And because the presentation is of the named things (musammayāt), not of the names, since presenting names would linguistically require saying 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahā). So the use of 'these' (hāʾulāʾi) and 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahum) indicates that what was presented are the named things. Consequently, the human being – about whom the entire hadith concerns his vicegerency on earth – was taught the things he uses on earth through the teaching of Adam as a representative type, and not as an individual or as a specific sample of the things that humans use in their lives until the Day of Resurrection, whether these things are animals, plants, or inventions, from Adam until Allah inherits the earth and those on it. But [they taught] the knowledge of their properties (khaṣāʾiṣ), attributes, elements, and modalities. As for the realities (ḥaqāʾiq), they are known through the analysis of these attributes, elements, and components. This refutes those who hold that the names referred to in the verse are specifically the beautiful names of Allah, or the names of the angels, even though the hadith is not specific to the domain of religious knowledge so that this could be said; rather, it encompasses all human knowledge. Therefore, when the angels realized that the matter did not concern them because it was not confined to the domain of religious knowledge, they ceased [their objection]. 
If we give preponderance to the view that knowledge of these names is nothing more than knowledge of properties and attributes, and not knowledge of essence (māhiyya) and quiddity (kunn), this does not contradict the methodology of Islamic research, which affirms the existence of things in the worlds of the unseen and the witnessed, and makes it possible for the knowing being to infer their existence, without that meaning the perception of quiddity, because it does not fall within the circle of human knowledge in the first place.
And if these interpretations of the meaning of the names in the noble verse are probable, and some are closer to understanding and acceptance than others, there is a contemporary understanding held by some educational researchers, which is that what is meant by teaching Adam 'all the names' is Allah's creation of the capacity for learning in the human being (Adam), as evidence by His statement: 'Who taught by the pen, taught man what he did not know' (Al-ʿAlaq 96:4-5). And some add that the knowledge of the names of everything means the ability to use symbols, and it is not merely producing specific sounds or syllables of words, but rather it means conveying opinions and what is stirred in the soul to others with complete clarity.
The reality is that this last educational interpretation, in its two branches, proceeds from a hypothesis whose import is that the individual human being is an identical image of his father Adam (the first human) in all his properties, components, and elements, since they are transmitted to him genetically from the beginning of human creation until the last creature from it that ends with the story of cosmic existence.
And if we accept this hypothesis, we will conclude that there are in the human being faculties that distinguish him from the rest of creatures, and based on them it is right for us to describe the human being as a 'knowing animal' (ḥayawān ʿārif), because he is capable of learning, bearer of the trust (amāna), charged with responsibility, and accountable for his deeds.

Chapter Four, Section One: Knowledge between Doubt and Certainty
Allah, Allah said: (The believers will gather on the Day of Resurrection and say: 'If only we could seek intercession from our Lord.' They will come to Adam and say: 'You are the father of mankind; Allah created you with His hand, made His angels prostrate to you, and taught you the names of everything. So intercede for us with your Lord.') 
As for an objector objecting to the reason for Allah teaching Adam all kinds of named things, and among them were some that are not rational, since He said 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahum) and did not say 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahā) – because among them were the angels, humans, and jinn, who are rational, so the more complete prevailed, according to the Arab custom of giving precedence to the more perfect over the deficient. And because the presentation is of the named things (musammayāt), not of the names, since presenting names would linguistically require saying 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahā). So the use of 'these' (hāʾulāʾi) and 'He presented them' (ʿaraḍahum) indicates that what was presented are the named things. Consequently, the human being – about whom the entire hadith concerns his vicegerency on earth – was taught the things he uses on earth through the teaching of Adam as a representative type, and not as an individual or as a specific sample of the things that humans use in their lives until the Day of Resurrection, whether these things are animals, plants, or inventions, from Adam until Allah inherits the earth and those on it. But [they taught] the knowledge of their properties (khaṣāʾiṣ), attributes, elements, and modalities. As for the realities (ḥaqāʾiq), they are known through the analysis of these attributes, elements, and components. This refutes those who hold that the names referred to in the verse are specifically the beautiful names of Allah, or the names of the angels, even though the hadith is not specific to the domain of religious knowledge so that this could be said; rather, it encompasses all human knowledge. Therefore, when the angels realized that the matter did not concern them because it was not confined to the domain of religious knowledge, they ceased [their objection]. 
If we give preponderance to the view that knowledge of these names is nothing more than knowledge of properties and attributes, and not knowledge of essence (māhiyya) and quiddity (kunn), this does not contradict the methodology of Islamic research, which affirms the existence of things in the worlds of the unseen and the witnessed, and makes it possible for the knowing being to infer their existence, without that meaning the perception of quiddity, because it does not fall within the circle of human knowledge in the first place.
And if these interpretations of the meaning of the names in the noble verse are probable, and some are closer to understanding and acceptance than others, there is a contemporary understanding held by some educational researchers, which is that what is meant by teaching Adam 'all the names' is Allah's creation of the capacity for learning in the human being (Adam), as evidence by His statement: 'Who taught by the pen, taught man what he did not know' (Al-ʿAlaq 96:4-5). And some add that the knowledge of the names of everything means the ability to use symbols, and it is not merely producing specific sounds or syllables of words, but rather it means conveying opinions and what is stirred in the soul to others with complete clarity.
The reality is that this last educational interpretation, in its two branches, proceeds from a hypothesis whose import is that the individual human being is an identical image of his father Adam (the first human) in all his properties, components, and elements, since they are transmitted to him genetically from the beginning of human creation until the last creature from it that ends with the story of cosmic existence.
And if we accept this hypothesis, we will conclude that there are in the human being faculties that distinguish him from the rest of creatures, and based on them it is right for us to describe the human being as a 'knowing animal' (ḥayawān ʿārif), because he is capable of learning, bearer of the trust (amāna), charged with responsibility, and accountable for his deeds.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological and definitional discussion. It begins by citing a hadith about the believers seeking intercession from Adam on the Day of Resurrection, which leads to an objection: Why does the verse use a masculine plural pronoun when presenting the named things, even though some named things are inanimate? The author explains that among the named things were rational beings (angels, humans, jinn), so the masculine plural was used by way of predominance. He further argues that the presentation was of the named things (musammayāt), not the names themselves, based on the wording 'he presented them' (ʿaraḍahum). Consequently, the teaching to Adam was not of a limited set of names but of all things humans will use on earth until the end of time—animals, plants, inventions—teaching their properties, attributes, and elements, not their essences. This refutes the interpretation that the names were exclusively the divine names or angel names; rather, they encompass all human knowledge. The author then mentions a contemporary educational interpretation that 'teaching all names' signifies Allah's creation of the capacity to learn and use symbols in humans, thus transferring this capacity to all generations genetically. If we accept this, the author concludes, we can define humans as a 'knowing animal' (ḥayawān ʿārif) characterized by learnability, bearing of the trust, responsibility, and accountability. This definition guards against the misunderstanding that Adam's knowledge was limited to religious matters or essences; instead, it establishes that human knowledge is primarily of properties and that the capacity to learn is inherent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate the Quranic principle that truth is the ultimate goal of inquiry and identify its implication for avoiding error in Islamic research.

Core Idea
After truth, there is only error — that is why loving truth is the first rule.

The author begins with the foundational rule: love and veneration of truth. He quotes verse 10:32 to emphasize that truth is the only path; after it, only error remains. This rule sets the motivational framework for all Islamic research.

Guided Path
1
Locate the Quranic evidence
Find the verse cited in the passage (Yunus 10:32). Notice how it frames the relationship between truth and error: 'And what is there after truth except error?'
2
Understand the logical implication
The verse implies that truth and error are mutually exclusive and exhaustive categories. If one does not have truth, one is necessarily in error. This motivates the seeker to pursue truth with urgency.

Apply to scholarly practice
In Islamic scholarship, this rule means that a researcher must prioritize truth over personal opinion or school affiliation. For example, a jurist should accept a stronger evidence even if it contradicts his madhhab.

Research and the Condemnation of Taqlid
Author enumerates four rules derived from the Quran for seeking truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Quranic critique of blind imitation and distinguish between acceptable following and blameworthy taqlid in Islamic scholarship.

Core Idea
The Quran condemns taqlid because it blocks the path of inquiry.

The second rule emphasizes that truth is reached through research, not blind imitation. The author notes that the Quran repeatedly criticizes those who follow without evidence. This rule sets the epistemological standard for Islamic scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condemnation of taqlid
The passage states that the Quran condemns imitation and its people in more than one verse. Recall that taqlid is following without evidence.
2
Understand the rationale
Taqlid is blameworthy because it bypasses the process of inquiry (bahth) which is the means to reach truth. Without inquiry, one may follow error unknowingly.
3
Distinguish acceptable following
Not all following is taqlid. In Islamic jurisprudence, ittiba' (following with evidence) is acceptable, while taqlid (blind following) is condemned. The rule calls for active research.
See the difference between taqlid and ittiba' in Usul al-Fiqh.

Truth is the Believer's Lost Property
Author enumerates four rules derived from the Quran for seeking truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that truth should be accepted from any source, and avoid the error of rejecting truth based on its origin.

Core Idea
Truth is like a lost item: the believer picks it up wherever it lies.

The third rule uses the metaphor of the believer's lost property (dallat al-mu'min) to indicate that truth should be taken from any vessel, regardless of the source's status. This rule guards against sectarianism in knowledge acquisition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the metaphor
The phrase 'dallat al-mu'min' (believer's lost property) implies that truth belongs to the believer wherever it is found. Just as one would pick up a lost item, a believer should accept truth from any source.
2
Consider the implication for sourcing knowledge
This rule encourages openness: truth can come from a non-Muslim, a different madhhab, or an unexpected source. The believer should not reject it due to prejudice.
3
Apply to Islamic scholarship
In hadith studies, scholars accepted narrations from non-Muslims when reliable. In usul al-fiqh, a mujtahid may benefit from insights of other schools. The rule promotes intellectual humility.
For example, Imam al-Shafi'i adopted some views from his Hanafi teachers despite differences.

Humility Before Absolute Knowledge
Author enumerates four rules derived from the Quran for seeking truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of epistemic humility when facing uncertainty, recognizing the limits of human knowledge as taught by the Quran.

Core Idea
Only Allah has absolute knowledge; humans must pause when uncertain.

The fourth rule instructs believers to stop when faced with uncertainty, acknowledging that human knowledge is limited. The verse 'You have not been given of knowledge except a little' serves as a reminder of our epistemic boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic basis
The passage cites verse Al-Isra 17:85: 'And you have not been given of knowledge except a little.' Reflect on its meaning: human knowledge is limited compared to Allah's absolute knowledge.
2
Understand the rule of pausing
The rule says 'refraining from confusion regarding knowledge of the truth' — when one is uncertain (ishtibah), one should pause rather than rush to a conclusion.
3
Apply in Islamic research
In kalam and usul, scholars often say 'Allahu a'lam' (Allah knows best) when unable to resolve a question. This rule prevents speculation and promotes honesty in scholarship.
For example, when discussing the nature of Allah's attributes, mutakallimun stop at certain points, acknowledging that full comprehension is beyond humans.

[الإسراء: ٨٥/١٧] وعلى لسان (۱) محمد يحيى من يملك الحقيقة المطلقة، ۹۳
The Holy Quran presents a set of rules for seeking cognitive truth, which some researchers have attempted to compile. They can be summarized as follows: 1- Love and veneration of truth, considering that the purpose of life is the search for it: 'And what is there after truth except error?' [Yunus 10:32]. 2- The way to reach truth is through research, and therefore we find condemnation of imitation and its people in more than one verse, as we have seen. 3- Taking truth from whatever vessel it comes out of, for it is the believer's lost item. 4- Refraining from confusion regarding knowledge of the truth, for Allah is the possessor of absolute knowledge: 'And you have not been given of knowledge except a little.' [Al-Isra 17:85] And on the tongue of (1) Muhammad Yahya, 'Who possesses absolute truth,' 93.

Commentary
This passage is an enumeration of four rules derived from the Quran for seeking truth. The author introduces it by stating that the Quran provides a set of rules that some researchers have collected. Then he lists them: First: love and veneration of truth, citing the Quranic verse that after truth there is only error. Second: the method is research and inquiry, condemning blind imitation (taqlid). Third: one should accept truth from any source, as it is the believer's lost property. Fourth: one should pause when uncertainty arises, because only Allah has absolute knowledge, and humans have only a little knowledge. The list ends with a reference to Muhammad Yahya, possibly a scholar.

Key Terms
التقليد (al-taqlīd): Blind imitation, following without evidence; criticized as an obstacle to truth.
ضالة المؤمن (ḍāllat al-mu'min): The believer's lost property; metaphor for truth that should be sought and accepted wherever found.
