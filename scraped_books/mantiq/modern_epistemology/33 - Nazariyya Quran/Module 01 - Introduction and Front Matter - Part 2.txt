Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran's knowledge framework rests on ethics, field, and unity — inseparable and foundational.

This passage opens Chapter Two by laying out the structure for a deep exploration of knowledge (ma'rifa) in the Quran. The author announces three key aspects: ethics of knowledge (akhlaq al-ma'rifa), the field of knowledge (midan al-ma'rifa) encompassing the unseen and seen, and the unity of knowledge (wahdat al-ma'rifa). Before diving into these, a preliminary conceptual discussion is promised. This lesson helps you map the architecture of this Quranic epistemology.

The author claims unity of knowledge. Ask: What does unity mean in a Quranic context? Is it source-based or goal-based?
Unity here likely refers to the integrated Islamic worldview.

Innate Ethical Values in the Quran
Author defines innate ethical values and their actualization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the characteristics of innate ethical values according to the Quran as described in the passage.

Core Idea
Ethical values are innate potentials actualized through life; violation does not erase awareness.

The passage explains the Quranic view that ethical values are not merely learned, but are inherent in human nature. Understanding this foundation helps us grasp why humans are accountable for their ethical choices even when they go against their innate disposition.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of ethical values as fitri
Find the phrase where the passage states that ethical values are 'innate rulings or principles' (أحكاما أو مبادئ فطرية). Note the term 'fitri' which indicates innateness.
2
Distinguish potential vs. actual
The passage says these principles exist 'potentially' (بالقوة) in the mind after birth and become 'actual' (بالفعل) through life. Understand that innateness does not mean fully formed at birth.
3
Identify the sign of innateness
The passage gives a key sign: even if a person violates these values, they remain aware of them and feel the violation. This awareness despite violation indicates the value is innate.
4
Connect to broader context
The passage then links this to the necessity of knowledge for ethical action and the Quran as source of ethics. See how the innate nature sets the stage for revelation's role.

Chapter One: Ethics of Knowledge and its Unity. Ethics of Knowledge: The Quran views ethical values in their entirety as innate rulings or principles in humans, which exist - like other innate principles - potentially in the human mind after birth, then become actual thereafter through the human's movement in life. This does not mean that humans always comply with them; rather, they may violate them, but they remain aware of them, feeling their violation, and this is a sign of their innateness. The importance of discussing the ethics of knowledge as an epistemological determinant of the content of ethical action emerges from the angle that among the necessary conditions for studying ethics is possessing knowledge beforehand to choose the type of ethical action. The hypothesis here is that the Holy Quran is the source of these ethics, based on the premise that the purpose of knowledge is to enable humans to populate and develop the earth, according to the methodology of Allah Almighty. In light of this, ethics become governing controls in the use of knowledge. Since knowledge - any knowledge - is a double-edged sword, it can be used for construction or destruction, as is the case in technical applications of science and mathematics, for example. They have led to the invention of means in industry, agriculture, transportation, and satellites that can greatly contribute to building and developing the universe. They have also led to the invention of weapons of mass destruction, and thus they also contribute to destroying humanity or threatening it at least. The position adopted by Islamic education is that it is a constructive, not destructive, education, committed to ethical controls in the human cognitive journey and the means and ends associated with it that do not deviate from human benefit and considered interests. Accordingly, the Holy Quran came with a set of values that the seeker of knowledge should adopt, including: 1 - Prohibition of Doctrinal Disagreement: That is, the Holy Quran has prohibited - in more than one verse - the path of some People of the Book, whose rulings and perceptions regarding the totality of religious issues differed, despite the availability of knowledge in a clear manner: {And be not like those who became divided and differed after the clear proofs had come to them. And those will have a great punishment} [Al-Imran: 3/105]. {And We had settled the Children of Israel in a secure settlement and provided them with good things. And they did not differ until after knowledge came to them} [Yunus: 10/93].

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological introduction to a chapter on the ethics of knowledge in the Quran. The author first defines the Quranic view of ethical values as innate (fitri) principles existing potentially in humans and actualized through experience. He clarifies that violation of these values does not negate their innateness, as humans remain aware of them. The author then emphasizes that knowledge is a prerequisite for ethical action, establishing the Quran as the source of these ethics, with the ultimate purpose of knowledge being the constructive development of the earth under divine guidance. He illustrates the dual nature of knowledge using modern examples from technology and weaponry, arguing that Islamic education opts for constructive use. Finally, the author presents the first Quranic value: prohibition of doctrinal disagreement, citing two verses that condemn the division of the People of the Book after receiving clear knowledge, thereby reinforcing the Quran's emphasis on unity of faith.

The Quranic Prohibition of Doctrinal Disagreement
Author presents Quranic prohibition of doctrinal disagreement with two verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Quranic ruling on doctrinal disagreement and identify its basis in the cited verses.

Core Idea
The Quran prohibits doctrinal disagreement after clear evidence has been revealed.

The author presents the first Quranic value: the prohibition of doctrinal disagreement, citing two verses that condemn the division of the People of the Book after knowledge came to them. This principle is foundational for Islamic unity and distinguishes permissible differences in interpretation from blameworthy splits in creed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the value being presented
The passage lists 'prohibition of doctrinal disagreement' as the first value. Note the phrase 'النهي عن الاختلاف العقدي'.
2
Read the first verse (Al-Imran 3:105)
The verse warns: 'Be not like those who became divided and differed after the clear proofs had come to them.' Highlight the condition: after clear proofs (البيّنات).
3
Read the second verse (Yunus 10:93)
This verse states: 'They did not differ until after knowledge came to them.' Note that knowledge (العلم) here refers to clear evidence.
4
Understand the implication
Both verses show that disagreement in matters of creed is blameworthy only when clear evidence has been revealed. Before that, difference might be excusable. This establishes that unity in fundamentals is required.

Commentary
This passage is a chapter from a book on Quranic epistemology, discussing the ethical foundations of knowledge according to the Quran. The author presents five main points. First, objectivity in issuing judgment: the Quran commands justice in all judgments, including scientific ones, devoid of bias. The author cites verses 4:135, 5:8, and 4:58 to emphasize fairness even against one's own interests or enemies. This is presented as a foundation for scientific research ethics. Second, objectivity in dealing with others: the Quran acknowledges the virtues of some People of the Book, cautioning against blanket condemnation. Verses 3:113-115 and 3:75 illustrate that among them are upright and trustworthy individuals, while some are not, and the misconduct of some is linked to a false belief that they are not accountable to others. The author calls for balanced treatment in scholarly disagreements. Third, a warning against racial arrogance: the same verse (3:75) is used to show how such arrogance leads to lying and ignorance. The author connects it to modern phenomena like Western monopolization of knowledge. Fourth, prohibition of conjectural knowledge: the Quran dismisses knowledge based on mere supposition (zann) and guesswork, citing 6:116. A footnote clarifies that the prohibited conjecture is weak conjecture (zann marjuh), as opposed to preponderant belief with possibility of its opposite. The author uses definitions from al-Jurjani and al-Maidani.

The Unity of Divine Revelation: Argument and Educational Implications
Author argues for unity of revelation and derives educational principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main premises the author uses to argue for the unity of revelation and trace how he derives educational implications from them.

Core Idea
All revelation is one in origin; the Quran confirms and supersedes, not replaces, earlier messages.

In this passage, the author builds a case for the unity of all divine revelations by citing Quranic verses and Ibn Taymiyya's interpretation. He shows that the Quran does not present Islam as a new religion but as the continuation of Abrahamic monotheism. Then he extracts two fundamental principles for Islamic education. This lesson will walk you through the logical flow of his argument and help you apply the same method of deriving educational principles from revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
Locate a sentence that states the thesis about religion being one in its foundation. The author says: 'indicating that religion is one in its foundation' and later 'Islam... only represents an extension of the Abrahamic, pristine community.'
2
Analyze the Quranic evidence
List the verses cited: 2:135-136, 42:13, 3:67-68, 16:123. For each, note how they support unity: belief in all prophets, same essential command, Abraham as a Muslim, command to follow Abraham's way.
Pay attention to how each verse contributes a different aspect: belief, legislation, identity.

Examine Ibn Taymiyya's role
Read the author's quote of Ibn Taymiyya: the ordained religion is the same command to establish and not divide. The author then adds a qualification: unity is not absolute in details. How does this nuance strengthen the argument?
Ibn Taymiyya is used to anchor the argument in classical authority.

Distinguish beliefs from laws
The author states there are fixed beliefs (constant) and variable legislation (details fitting circumstances). Identify the terms used: 'al-mu'taqadat al-thabita' and 'al-tashri' al-mutaghayyir'. Why is this distinction crucial for claiming unity despite apparent differences?
This distinction resolves the objection that different laws mean different religions.

The derivation is a move from theological truth to educational practice.

قمين بالتنويه في هذا الموضوع أن مناسبة تأجيل هذا الفصل إلى هذا الموضع من الدراسة دون الابتداء به - كما درج على ذلك بعض الباحثين حين عدوا مسألة الحصول على المعرفة مسلّمة لا تستحق التأجيل- المسايرة المؤقتة للاتجاه الفلسفي النازع إلى تأجيل دراسة عنصر إمكان الحصول على المعرفة ليظهر كنتيجة منطقية للبحث في طبيعة المعرفة ومصادرها وطرائقها. بمعنى أنه ما دام أن ثمة معرفة ذات طبيعة محدّدة، فإن لها مصادرها وطرائقها المحدّدة كذلك، وهذا دليل منطقي على أن المعرفة ممكنة، أو أن لها حدوداً ظاهرة مميزة. وخلافاً لمنحى من سبقت الإشارة إلى بعضهم آنفاً فلست أرى في هذا المنهج مخالفة لمنهج القرآن الكريم في التعامل مع معطيات المعرفة لدى الآخر، بل إن القرآن ليتنزّل معه، حتى يدفعه دفعاً نحو الوصول إلى النتيجة المنطقية منفرداً. فتارة يطرح مقدّمة واضحة النتائج إلا أنه يبادر بسؤال يبدو وكأنه محيّر ولكن سرعان ما يدلف منه المحاور إلى تقرير النتيجة الموضوعية بنفسه . وقُلْ مَنْ يَرْزُقُكُمْ مِنَ السَّماواتِ وَالْأَرْضِ قُلِ اللهُ وَإِنَّا أَوْ إِيَّاكُمْ لَعَلَى هُدَى أَوْ فِي ضَلالٍ مُبِينٍ ﴾ [سبا: ٢٤/٣٤].
Introduction
Chapter One
Knowledge between Doubt and Certainty
It is worthy of mention in this subject that the appropriateness of postponing this chapter to this position in the study, rather than beginning with it—as some researchers have done, considering the issue of obtaining knowledge as something taken for granted not deserving postponement—is the temporary accommodation with the philosophical trend inclined to postpone the study of the element of the possibility of obtaining knowledge so that it appears as a logical result of the investigation into the nature of knowledge, its sources, and its methods. That is, as long as there is knowledge of a specific nature, it has its specific sources and methods as well, and this is a logical proof that knowledge is possible, or that it has apparent distinctive boundaries. Contrary to the approach of those I referred to earlier, I do not see in this method any contradiction to the method of the Noble Quran in dealing with the data of knowledge of the other. Rather, the Quran accommodates itself with him, even pushing him to reach the logical conclusion by himself. Sometimes it presents a clear premise of results, but then it initiates a question that seems puzzling, but soon the interlocutor enters through it to determine the objective result himself. And say: "Who provides for you from the heavens and the earth?" Say: "Allah." And indeed, we or you are either upon guidance or in manifest error. [Saba: 24/34].

Commentary
This passage is an introductory methodological discussion explaining why the author chose to delay the chapter on whether knowledge is possible until after examining the nature, sources, and methods of knowledge. The author argues that this approach is not a concession to a philosophical trend but a deliberate method that mirrors the Quranic style. Rather than asserting the possibility of knowledge upfront, the Quran presents premises and leads the interlocutor to the logical conclusion independently. The example from Surah Saba (34:24) illustrates this: the Quran poses a question about provision, then provides the answer, but leaves the follow-up conclusion—that one side is on guidance and the other in error—for the listener to deduce. The author emphasizes that this method is consistent with the Quranic approach and is logically sound, as knowing the nature of knowledge implies its possibility.

The Quranic Method of Leading to Logical Conclusions
Author illustrates Quranic method using Surah Saba example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quranic method of presenting premises that guide the interlocutor to an independent logical conclusion, using Surah Saba 34:24 as an example.

Core Idea
The Quran does not force certainty but leads through premises to a self-evident conclusion.

The author illustrates the Quranic method with the verse from Surah Saba: 'Say: Who provides for you from the heavens and the earth? Say: Allah. And indeed, we or you are either upon guidance or in manifest error.' The verse presents a clear premise (Allah provides) and then a disjunction (we or you are on guidance or error). The Quran does not explicitly state the conclusion; it leaves the listener to connect the dots. This is a powerful pedagogical tool that respects the listener's intellect.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse
Surah Saba 34:24. Identify the two parts: a question about provision and an answer, followed by a disjunctive statement.
2
Extract the premises
Premise 1: Allah provides from the heavens and earth. Premise 2: The speaker and the listener are either on guidance or in error.
3
Identify the implicit conclusion
The listener is expected to realize: since Allah's guidance is true, the one who follows it is on guidance, and the one who rejects is in error. The conclusion: the Prophet and believers are on guidance, the rejecters are in error.
4
Analyze the method
The Quran does not explicitly state the conclusion; it presents premises and lets the rational mind deduce. This teaches that logical inference is a valid way to arrive at truth.

Quranic Affirmation of Human Knowledge Capacity
Author discusses Quran's affirmation of human knowledge acquisition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quranic basis for human knowledge acquisition through sensory and intellectual faculties and contrast it with innate monotheistic knowledge.

Core Idea
The Quran grounds all acquired knowledge in God-given senses and intellect, not in pre-birth content.

This passage explores how the Quran views the human capacity for knowledge. The author argues that while humans are born without knowledge, they are equipped with hearing, sight, and intellect to acquire it. This lesson will help you trace the argument through the cited verses and understand its implications for Islamic epistemology, especially the distinction between acquired knowledge and innate fitra.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's central claim
Find the sentence that states the Quran establishes inherent constitutional qualities in humans that indicate the possibility of obtaining knowledge. Note how the author contrasts the initial state of ignorance with the potential for learning.
The claim is that humans have imkan dhati (inherent potential) for knowledge.
2
Extract the tools of knowledge from the verses
From Quran 16:78, list the three faculties God gives humans: hearing, sight, and hearts (hearts often refer to intellect/cognition). These are the means by which knowledge is acquired after birth.
The author uses 'sensory and intellectual methods' to categorize these.
3
Analyze the progression from ignorance to knowledge
Trace the timeline: humans are created knowing nothing (76:1-3), then God provides eloquence and teaching (55:1-4), and finally learning by pen (96:4-5). This shows knowledge is acquired postnatally through divinely provided means.
Note the emphasis that learning is a process, not an innate endowment.
4
Examine the exception of old age and childhood
The author explains Quran 16:70, which describes returning to a state of ignorance in old age. They compare childhood (ignorance) and old age (weakness) as two forms of 'decrepitude' (aradhal). This reinforces that knowledge is tied to the functioning of faculties.
The author addresses a potential linguistic objection about the superlative form used for two different states.
5
Distinguish acquired knowledge from innate monotheistic fitra
The author concludes that the verses affirm knowledge comes only through postnatal tools, but clarifies this does not negate the innate fitra (innate knowledge of God). Understand that fitra is a general awareness, while detailed knowledge requires revelation.
This distinction is crucial for reconciling the passage with other Islamic teachings.

The Quran's Stance on Doubt
If we want to know the Quran's stance on the issue of doubt and those who advocate it, it is good for us to present the Quran's view of man in terms of his inherent potential to obtain knowledge, and in terms of the qualities that qualify him to acquire it, as follows:
First: Inherent Potential
-
The Noble Quran establishes the inherent constitutional qualities of man, which in their entirety indicate - as we will see - the possibility of his obtaining knowledge. For although he was created empty of any knowledge, he is able to obtain it after his birth through what he has been given of sensory and intellectual methods of knowledge: "And Allah has brought you out from the wombs of your mothers while you know nothing, and He has made for you hearing, sight, and hearts that perhaps you will be grateful" [An-Nahl 16:78].
And He emphasized this meaning, saying: "Has there come upon man a period of time when he was not a thing mentioned? Indeed, We created man from a drop of mixed fluids that We may test him, and We made him hearing and seeing. Indeed, We guided him to the way, whether he be grateful or ungrateful" [Al-Insan 76:1-3]. "The Most Gracious taught the Quran, created man, taught him eloquence" [Ar-Rahman 55:1-4].
And man has the capacity to learn by the pen after he did not know: "Who taught by the pen, taught man what he did not know" [Al-Alaq 96:4-5].
And man may return to this stage of not knowing: "And among you is he who is returned to the most decrepit of age so that he knows nothing after having known something" [An-Nahl 16:70]. The intended meaning of the verse is that the state of childhood and infancy represents a "most decrepit" state, just like the state of old age and advanced senescence. As for how it is valid to use the superlative form ("aradhal") on the pattern of "af'al" for two persons at the same time, the matter differs from the above; the state of old age is more decrepit (aradhal) in terms of weakness of faculties, and the state of childhood is more decrepit in terms of ignorance. And the result in the outcome is one: lack of knowledge or its weakness to the point where it almost equals non-existence. It is noted here that the sum of these preceding verses explicitly state that man does not possess any knowledge except through his tools of knowledge after birth, but this does not contradict the innate nature of monotheistic knowledge, by which we mean knowledge of God, the Creator, the Originator of the universe and its Manager in general terms, for detail is the affair of revelation (2).

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion presenting the Quran's stance on human capacity for knowledge. The author introduces a section on the Quran's view regarding doubt, then focuses on the 'inherent potential' (al-imkan al-dhati) of humans to acquire knowledge. The passage begins by stating that the Quran establishes inherent constitutional qualities in humans that indicate the possibility of obtaining knowledge. The author supports this with several Quranic verses, explaining that although humans are created without knowledge, they are equipped with sensory and intellectual faculties (hearing, sight, hearts) to gain it after birth. The verses cited (An-Nahl 16:78, Al-Insan 76:1-3, Ar-Rahman 55:1-4, Al-Alaq 96:4-5) highlight the progression from ignorance to knowledge, emphasizing that learning occurs through divinely given tools. The author then discusses the return to ignorance in old age (referencing An-Nahl 16:70), clarifying that both childhood and advanced age are states of 'decrepitude'—one due to lack of knowledge, the other due to weakness. The author addresses a potential objection about using the superlative 'aradhal' for two different states, explaining that each is considered 'most decrepit' in its own respect. Finally, the author notes that while these verses affirm that knowledge comes only through postnatal tools, this does not contradict the innate disposition (fitra) of monotheistic knowledge, which is a general awareness of God, while details come through revelation.

Ibn Khaldun's Classification of Knowledge
Author summarizes the study's structure, including classification of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given field of knowledge as natural (rational/empirical) or transmitted (revealed) according to Ibn Khaldun's scheme.

Core Idea
Ibn Khaldun divides knowledge into natural (philosophical) and transmitted (revealed) — a framework foundational for Islamic epistemology.

The passage mentions that the study adopted Ibn Khaldun's classification of knowledge into two types: natural (including rational and empirical) and transmitted (knowledge of revelation). This classification is crucial for understanding how Quranic knowledge relates to other types of knowledge. In this lesson, we will examine this classification and see examples from Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Natural knowledge ('ilm tabi'i) is acquired through reason and experience; transmitted knowledge ('ilm naqli) is based on divine revelation.
Ibn Khaldun calls natural knowledge 'philosophical' (falsafiyya) and transmitted knowledge 'religious' (shar'iyya).
2
Subdivide natural knowledge
Natural knowledge includes rational sciences (like logic, mathematics) and empirical sciences (like physics, medicine).
These are universal and can be studied by any rational being.
3
Understand transmitted knowledge
Transmitted knowledge depends on revelation and includes tafsir, hadith, fiqh, and kalam (though kalam also uses rational methods).
Its authority comes from the Quran and Sunnah.
4
Apply the classification to an example
For instance, Arabic grammar is a transmitted science because it was developed from the Quranic text, while medicine is a natural empirical science.
Some sciences, like usul al-fiqh, combine both.

Ibn Khaldun's Classification of Knowledge in Quranic Epistemology
Author adopts Ibn Khaldun's classification of knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn Khaldun's dichotomy of natural and transmitted knowledge and its application in Quranic epistemology.

Core Idea
Ibn Khaldun divides knowledge into natural (rational & empirical) and transmitted (revelation), a framework adopted to analyze Quranic epistemology.

The source chunk mentions that the study adopts Ibn Khaldun's classification of knowledge into two categories: natural and transmitted. This classification is crucial for understanding how the study approaches different types of knowledge discussed in the Quran. This lesson unpacks that classification and its relevance.

Guided Path
1
Locate the classification in the passage
Find where the author discusses 'al-tasnif al-khalduni'. The passage says knowledge is of two types: natural (philosophical: rational and empirical) and transmitted (revelational sciences).
2
Define the two categories
Natural knowledge comes from human reason and empirical observation; transmitted knowledge comes from divine revelation (wahy).
3
Understand the Quran's relationship to both
The Quran provides transmitted knowledge, but also encourages reflection on natural phenomena (e.g., Quranic verses about creation). Thus both categories are relevant.
4
Note the study's adoption
The author says the study 'adopted' (tabannat) this classification, implying it is used as a lens to organize the analysis of Quranic verses about knowledge.

Qiyas al-Awla: The Quran's A Fortiori Reasoning
Extracts the concept of qiyas al-awla from the thematic index and explains its logical structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a qiyas al-awla argument and construct one using a Quranic example.

Core Idea
Qiyas al-awla argues from lesser to greater: if a property holds for the smaller case, it holds even more for the larger.

In this thematic index, the author lists 'the Quran's reasoning by prima facie analogy (bi-tariq al-awla)' as a central method. This fortiori argument, known in Islamic logic as qiyas al-awla, is a powerful tool used to prove divine attributes and moral principles. Let us break down its logical structure and see how the Quran employs it.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the structure of qiyas al-awla
Qiyas al-awla consists of an original case (asl), a derived case (far'), and a common attribute ('illa) that is stronger in the far'. If the hukm holds for the asl, it holds even more for the far' because the 'illa is more intense.
This is stronger than ordinary qiyas because the far' is more deserving of the ruling.
2
Find the Quranic example from the passage
The passage mentions that Prophet Muhammad and Prophet Noah confirm they have no knowledge of the unseen (ghayb). The argument: if even the best of creation do not know the ghayb, then certainly ordinary humans do not. The asl is the prophets, the far' is ordinary humans, the 'illa is their humanness, which is weaker in prophets? Actually, the argument works because the prophets have higher status, yet they still deny knowledge, so the far' (ordinary humans) are even less deserving of such knowledge.
This establishes that knowledge of ghayb belongs exclusively to Allah.
3
Construct a new qiyas al-awla example
Consider the Quranic principle: 'Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it' (99:7). If a tiny good deed is rewarded, then a greater good deed is even more deserving of reward. Here the asl is a small deed, far' is a large deed, 'illa is the good itself, which is greater in the large deed.
This reasoning is common in discussions of reward and punishment.
4
Distinguish qiyas al-awla from other analogies
Unlike ordinary analogical reasoning (qiyas), where the far' is equal to the asl in the 'illa, in qiyas al-awla the far' has a stronger 'illa. This makes the conclusion more certain.
The Quranic method of 'bi-tariq al-awla' is thus a form of a fortiori argument.

Key Terms
الغيب (al-ghayb): The unseen, that which is beyond human sensory perception; knowledge of it belongs exclusively to Allah in this context.
بطريق الأولى (bi-tariq al-awla): Reasoning by prima facie analogy (a fortiori argument): if something is true for a lesser case, it is all the more true for a greater case.
السننية (al-sunaniyya): The principle of divine patterns or laws (sunan) that govern the universe, including causality.
السببية (al-sababiyya): Causality, the cause-effect relationship as a manifestation of divine sunna.
