Defining the Nature of Knowledge: Subject, Object, and Process
Author defines nature of knowledge and its subjectivity/objectivity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the nature of knowledge from its sources and methods, and identify the subjective and objective dimensions in an Islamic educational context.

Core Idea
Knowledge is not just its sources—it is the dynamic relationship between the knower and the known.

In this passage, the author clarifies a common confusion: the nature of knowledge (tabi'at al-ma'rifa) is often conflated with its sources or methods. But true epistemological inquiry focuses on the relationship between the knowing subject (al-dhat al-'arifa) and the known object (al-mawdu' al-ma'ruf), along with the cognitive process. This distinction has profound implications for Islamic education, as it requires balancing attention to both the learner and the subject matter.

The author divides external existents into al-shahada (sensible world) and al-ghayb (unseen world). This frames the scope of knowledge: both the physical and metaphysical realms are objects of knowledge.
This classification is preliminary but important for understanding the known objects in this study.
2
Distinguish nature from source
The author rejects a definition that reduces the nature of knowledge to its sources (e.g., revelation, reason) or methods (observation, deduction). The key difference: nature concerns what knowledge essentially is, while sources/methods concern how it is obtained.
The author acknowledges some interconnection but insists on conceptual separation.
3
Extract the precise definition
The nature of knowledge is defined as 'the relationship between the knowing subject and the known object, along with an explanation of the cognitive process that occurs between them.' Write this definition down.
This definition is more specific and removes confusion.
4
Apply to the study's context
Man is the knowing subject; the Quran is the known object. The outcome of the cognitive process is the extraction of the Quranic theory of knowledge and its educational implications.
This shows how the general definition applies to a specific Islamic science.
5
Analyze the educational implications
Pure subjectivism (focus only on learner) or pure objectivism (focus only on content) leads to imbalance. Islamic education requires attention to both, as explained by Phenix: knowledge involves both external reality and internal forces like feelings and goals.
This integration leads to dynamic understanding and revision.

والحق أنه يمكن صوغ تعريف أكثر تحديداً، وأوجز عبارة، يزيل الالتباس الذي قد يُشعِرُ به التعريف الأول، والذي أرجع طبيعة المعرفة إلى ما قد يشبه البحث في مصادرها وطرائقها. ومع الإقرار بوجود قدر من الترابط والتلازم حينا بين طبيعة المعرفة من جهة ومصدرها وطرائقها من جهة أخرى؛ إلا أن العودة إلى تعريف معنى الطبيعة - على نحو من التجريد - يفرض اتجاها متباينا يميل إلى التحديد أكثر، إذ إن معنى الطبيعة بوجه خاص ما يميز الشيء من غيره. وطبيعة الشيء هي سر نموه وتغيّره وحركاته المختلفة. وما يميّز الطبيعة عن المصدرية إنما هو القول بأن طبيعة المعرفة تعني تحديد العلاقة بين الذات العارفة والموضوع المعروف مع تفسير لعملية المعرفة التي تتم بينهما.

وبناءً على ذلك فإن الإنسان هو الذات العارفة، في حين أن القرآن الكريم - بالنسبة إلى هذه الدراسة - هو الموضوع المعروف، وحاصل عملية المعرفة بينهما هو استخلاص نظرية المعرفة في القرآن الكريم ومضامينها التربوية. وينبني على هذا التعريف من الزاوية التربوية تركيز الاهتمام على المتعلم والمادة معا، دَرْءاً للتطرف الذي يركز الاهتمام كله على المتعلم، حين الأخذ بالاتجاه القائل بذاتية المعرفة. وبالمقابل فإن هناك من يرى موضوعية المعرفة، وذلك يعني انصراف الاهتمام إلى المادة الدراسية. ومبدأ التوازن في التربية الإسلامية يقتضي الاهتمام بالأمرين معا. ويزيد فينكس الأمر تفصيلاً فيقول: فإذا كانت المعرفة موضوعية تماما فإن المتعلم يتضمن الامتثال لما يوجد في الخارج. والعقل على هذا الأساس يجب أن يطبع على الدوام على نمو طبيعة الأشياء الخارجية. فالتدريب والتركيز على المعلومات المعروفة، والملاحظة الدقيقة للحقائق هي بعض طرق تشكيل الفهم حتى يتفق مع ما هو موجود موضوعيًا. وإذا كانت المعرفة ذاتية تماما فإن التدريس لا يمكن أن يحدث بأي حال من الأحوال. فالنمو يجب أن يحدث من الداخل عن طريق القوانين الداخلة في تكوين الفرد. وإذا نظرنا إلى المعرفة على أن لها مكونات ذاتية وأخرى موضوعية منفصلة أو متصلة، فإن التوجيه التعليمي سيحدث مع اهتمام بطبيعة العالم الخارجي، واهتمام بالقوى الداخلية كالمشاعر والأهداف والنظرات التي تكون شروط المعرفة. فالإدراك والفهم لن يدخلا في تشكيلات مقننة لحقيقة لا يمكن تغييرها، كما يقال، ولن يسمح لها بحرية التفضيل الخاص حرية لا تناقش. ولكنها ستشتق الكشف الجالة للعالم وتراجع على أساسه. وبهذا توضّح فضل العناصر الخاصة - الفردية والجماعية – التي تتضمنها كل معرفة، ذات مغزى وارتباط إنساني.
Second chapter - Introduction. Then these external existents are divided, in relation to man, into two parts: A) existents in the world of testimony: they are the things that surround man in the natural world, such as inanimate objects, plants, animals, and man, and he perceives them with his senses. B) existents of the unseen world, such as the Preserved Tablet, Paradise, Hell, the Throne, and the like, and they also have their fixed existence in the external world. The truth is that it is possible to formulate a more specific and more concise definition that removes the confusion that the first definition might cause, which traced the nature of knowledge to something like investigating its sources and methods. While acknowledging some degree of interconnection and correlation sometimes between the nature of knowledge on one hand and its source and methods on the other, returning to defining the meaning of nature—in an abstract manner—imposes a divergent direction that tends to be more specific, since the meaning of nature in particular is what distinguishes a thing from others. And the nature of a thing is the secret of its growth, change, and various motions. What distinguishes nature from source is the statement that the nature of knowledge means determining the relationship between the knowing subject and the known object, along with an explanation of the cognitive process that occurs between them. Accordingly, man is the knowing subject, while the Noble Quran—with respect to this study—is the known object, and the outcome of the cognitive process between them is the extraction of the theory of knowledge in the Noble Quran and its educational implications. From the educational perspective, this definition entails focusing attention on both the learner and the subject matter together, to guard against extremism that focuses all attention on the learner when adopting the view of the subjectivity of knowledge. Conversely, there are those who hold the objectivity of knowledge, which means shifting attention to the subject matter. The principle of balance in Islamic education requires attention to both matters. Phenix elaborates further, saying: If knowledge is completely objective, then the learner submits to what exists externally. The mind, on this basis, must always be impressed by the growth of the nature of external things. Training, focus on known information, and precise observation of facts are some ways of shaping understanding to agree with what exists objectively. If knowledge is completely subjective, then teaching cannot occur under any circumstances. Growth must occur from within through the laws internal to the individual's constitution. If we view knowledge as having both subjective and objective components, separate or connected, then educational guidance will occur with attention to the nature of the external world and attention to internal forces such as feelings, goals, and perspectives that constitute the conditions of knowledge. Perception and understanding will not enter into codified formations of an unchangeable truth, as it is said, nor will they be allowed freedom of private preference without discussion. Rather, they will derive the revealing state of the world and revise accordingly. Thus, the special elements—individual and collective—that every meaningful and humanly relevant knowledge contains are clarified.

Commentary
This passage serves as an epistemological introduction, defining the nature of knowledge within the context of a study on the Quranic theory of knowledge. It begins by classifying external existents from the human perspective into two categories: those perceptible to the senses (al-shahada) and those belonging to the unseen (al-ghayb), such as the Preserved Tablet and Paradise. This classification sets the groundwork for discussing knowledge. The author then critiques an initial definition that reduced the nature of knowledge to its sources and methods. Instead, a more precise definition is proposed: the nature of knowledge is what distinguishes a thing from others, and specifically for knowledge, it is the relationship between the knowing subject (al-dhat al-'arifa) and the known object (al-mawdu' al-ma'ruf), including an explanation of the cognitive process. This definition is applied to the study: man is the knower, the Quran is the known, and the resulting knowledge is the theory of knowledge and its educational implications. The educational angle is highlighted: a balanced approach requires attention to both the learner (subjective) and the subject matter (objective), avoiding extremes of pure subjectivism or objectivism. The passage then quotes Phenix to elaborate on the consequences of pure objectivity (mind conforms to external reality) vs. pure subjectivity (no teaching possible) and advocates for an integrative view where subjective and objective components interact, leading to dynamic understanding. The key point is that knowledge is not a static, unchangeable truth but involves continuous discovery and revision incorporating both individual and collective elements.
