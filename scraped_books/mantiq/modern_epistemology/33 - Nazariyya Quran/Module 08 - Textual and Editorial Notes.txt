Parsing a Bibliography in Islamic Academic Works
List of references and sources cited in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a bibliographic entry in an Islamic academic work and distinguish between journal articles and books.

Core Idea
Bibliographies are the DNA of scholarship; they reveal the sources of an author's thought.

Bibliographies (al-masadir wa al-maraji') are common in Islamic scholarly works, especially contemporary ones. They show the range of sources an author consulted. In this lesson, we'll examine a real bibliography from a book on Quranic theory. You'll learn to parse entries for authors, titles, journals, dates, and publishers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heading and structure
Look for the heading 'المصادر والمراجع'. The list follows it. Notice that some entries begin with a bullet (•) and some with a dot. That indicates separate entries.
2
Deconstruct an entry
Take any entry, e.g., 'الجمع بين القراءتين: قراءة الوحي وقراءة الكون، طه العلواني، الإنسان، العدد ١٣ السنة الثالثة، ١٤١٥هـ - ۱۹۹٥م، باريس: دار أمان ، للصحافة والنشر.' Break it down: Author (طه العلواني), Title (الجمع بين القراءتين...), Journal (الإنسان), volume (العدد ١٣), year (السنة الثالثة), date (١٤١٥هـ - ۱۹۹٥م), place (باريس), publisher (دار أمان للصحافة والنشر).
Note the extra space before the comma after 'أمان' – often due to typesetting.
3
Distinguish between journal articles and books
Journal articles have issue numbers and journal names. Books have only publisher and place. For example, 'الدين والفلسفة، حمدي زقزوق، المسلم المعاصر، العدد ٦٢' is a journal article, while 'مفهوم التربية الإسلامية، عبد الرحمن صالح عبد الله، الدراسات الإسلامية، العدد ٢' is also a journal article. All entries here are from periodicals; no standalone books appear. This is common in bibliographies focusing on articles.
4
Analyze the date format
Entries often include both Hijri and Gregorian dates (e.g., '١٤١٥هـ - ۱۹۹٥م'). The Hijri year comes first, followed by the Gregorian year. Note that the numbers may use Eastern Arabic numerals or Persian/Urdu variants, but they are consistent.
