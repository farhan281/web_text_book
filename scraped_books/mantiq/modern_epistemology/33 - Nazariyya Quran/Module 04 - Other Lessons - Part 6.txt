Haqq and Haqiqa: Truth and Reality in the Quran
Author defines haqq and haqiqa, cites scholars, and argues Quran uses only haqq.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the philosophical usage of al-Haqq (truth) and al-Haqiqa (reality) and explain why the Quran uses only al-Haqq for both concepts.

Core Idea
The Quran unifies truth and reality under one term: al-Haqq.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial epistemological question: how do the terms al-Haqq and al-Haqiqa differ? The author begins with lexical and terminological definitions from al-Jurjani, then highlights the philosophical distinction between truth (attribute of judgment) and reality (attribute of things). However, a survey of the Quran reveals that the word 'haqiqa' never appears—only 'haqq' is used. This leads the author to argue that the Quranic worldview does not separate truth from reality, and that the philosophical split is not applicable to the revealed text.

Guided Path
1
Identify lexical definitions
Read the opening: al-Haqq is 'the established without doubt,' al-Haqiqa is 'the thing used in its original meaning' and also 'the thing established with certainty.' Note that initially they seem synonymous.
Both terms share the root ح-ق-ق, indicating firmness and certainty.
2
Extract al-Jurjani's terminological definitions
Al-Jurjani defines al-Haqq as 'the judgment corresponding to reality' applied to statements, beliefs, etc. He defines al-Haqiqa as 'the thing settled in its place,' the original meaning of a word (as opposed to metaphor). Compare these definitions.
Al-Jurjani's definitions are from 'al-Ta'rifat'.
3
Analyze the philosophical distinction
The author presents the logicians' view: al-Haqq is a quality of speech/judgment (truth), while al-Haqiqa is a quality of things (reality). Understand the rose example: the rose is a reality (existing thing), its redness is truth (correct judgment).
This mirrors the Western distinction between truth as property of propositions and reality as existence.
4
Survey the Quranic evidence
The author states that a survey of Quranic verses with the root ح-ق-ق shows that the word 'haqiqa' never appears; only 'haqq' is used. This is the key empirical observation.
Check this claim by recalling verses like 'Ja'a al-haqq' (17:81) and 'Inna al-din 'inda Allah al-Islam' but note the author's claim is about the noun form 'haqiqa'.
5
Evaluate the author's conclusion
The author concludes that the philosophical distinction is not authoritative over the Quran. Therefore, in this study, 'haqiqa' will be used as the primary term for reality, but when 'haqq' appears (especially in the Quran), it carries the meaning of 'haqiqa'. Assess whether this conclusion follows from the evidence.
The author is making a methodological choice based on Quranic usage, not denying the philosophical distinction in other contexts.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological and linguistic analysis of the two key terms al-Haqq and al-Haqiqa. The author begins by giving lexical definitions: al-Haqq is the established without doubt, and al-Haqiqa is the thing used in its original meaning, also meaning certainty. Then he presents the terminological distinction according to al-Jurjani, who defined al-Haqq as a judgment corresponding to reality, and al-Haqiqa as the thing settled in its place, as opposed to metaphor. The author then notes that he does not see a real difference between them, only a verbal one; both ultimately refer to the certain and established. However, he explains that logicians and philosophers distinguish them: al-Haqq (Truth) is a quality of judgment (e.g., our statement is true), while al-Haqiqa (Reality) is a quality of things (e.g., the thing exists). He gives the example: the rose is a reality (when it exists truly), and its being red is truth (if it is truly red). After this, the author makes a crucial Quranic observation: the word 'Haqiqa' never appears in the Quran, only 'Haqq' is used. Therefore, the philosophical distinction between the two is not applicable to the Quranic text. The author then declares that his study will not strictly follow philosophical or logical rules, and he will use 'Haqiqa' as the primary term in line with contemporary usage, while interpreting occurrences of 'Haqq' (especially in the Quran) in the sense of 'Haqiqa'. The passage ends with a footnote expanding on philosophical definitions of Haqiqa as correspondence between thought and reality. Throughout, the author blends lexical, terminological, and Quranic analysis to argue for a unified concept of truth/reality in the Quranic worldview.

Distinguishing al-Haqq and al-Haqiqa
Author distinguishes between truth as reality and truth as judgment, then critiques philosophical distinction from a Quranic standpoint.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-haqq and al-haqiqa as defined in this passage and explain the Quranic usage of al-haqq alone.

Core Idea
Truth in the Quran is judgment-reality: al-haqq, not a separate philosophical al-haqiqa.

In this passage, the author draws a crucial epistemological distinction between two terms often confused: al-haqiqa (the existing reality of a thing) and al-haqq (the correctness of our judgment about that thing). He then shows that the Quran exclusively uses al-haqq, challenging the philosophical separation of these concepts. As we parse this text, we will sharpen our ability to identify and apply these terms in Islamic discourse, especially when engaging with Quranic verses and philosophical claims.

Commentary
This passage defines and distinguishes between two key terms: al-haqiqa (reality/truth as existence) and al-haqq (truth as correct judgment). The author first explains that when we say 'the rose is red,' the rose itself is a reality (haqiqa) from among the realities of existence, while our judgment that it is red is the truth (haqq) if that judgment corresponds to reality. Thus, every proposition has two aspects: the judgment itself (whether it is true or false) and the subject of judgment (whether it actually exists as a reality). The author then argues that in the Quran, only the term al-haqq appears, never al-haqiqa. Therefore the philosophical and logical distinction between the two terms is not Quranic and cannot be applied to Quranic discourse. However, the author clarifies that this study will use the term al-haqiqa because it is prevalent in contemporary intellectual literature, understanding al-haqq in Quranic contexts as referring to al-haqiqa based on linguistic and terminological considerations. The footnote elaborates that philosophical dictionaries define al-haqiqa as correspondence of thought to reality (a relative concept) or as logically indubitable truth. The passage concludes that from philosophical, logical, and educational perspectives, the distinction between the two terms is merely terminological and carries no practical consequence beyond requiring consistency. This passage is epistemological in nature, parsing the meaning of fundamental truth-related terms and situating them within a Quranic framework.

The Quranic Lesson on Limits of Knowledge
Author uses Quranic story to argue limits of human knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to interpret the story of Adam (al-Baqarah 31-33) as evidence that human knowledge is limited to attributes and signs, not essences.

Core Idea
God taught Adam only names, not essences; human knowledge is confined to the apparent.

The author uses the Quranic story of Adam teaching the names to the angels to illustrate that human knowledge is limited to symbols and attributes. This passage is key to understanding the Islamic epistemological stance on the limits of reason.

Certain vs. Probable Texts in Revelation
Author distinguishes certain and probable texts in revelation with hadith and juristic commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qaṭʿī and ẓannī revelatory texts and apply the Prophet's instruction to the context of fatwa and ijtihad.

Core Idea
Certainty in revelation is limited; most texts are probable, requiring ijtihad and humility.

This passage introduces a foundational epistemological distinction in Islamic legal theory: some texts from revelation yield certain knowledge (qaṭʿī), while others only probable knowledge (ẓannī). The Prophet's instruction to army commanders underscores that even when applying God's law, human judgment can err. Ibn al-Qayyim and Ibn Taymiyya extend this principle to caution against claiming divine authority for rulings derived from ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two textual categories
Note the author's classification: certain data (معطيات قطعية) from revelation are limited, while probable texts (ظنيات) are more numerous. Understand that certainty is reserved for explicit and unambiguous statements.
2
Analyse the prophetic hadith
Read the hadith about besieging a fortress. The Prophet instructs commanders not to impose 'God's judgment' but their own, because they cannot be certain of God's specific ruling in that particular situation. This illustrates the fallibility of human ijtihad in probable matters.
The hadith text has a typo: 'الرسول - الله -' should be 'الرسول صلى الله عليه وسلم'.
3
Examine Ibn al-Qayyim's commentary
Ibn al-Qayyim applies the hadith's principle to fatwa: a mufti must not testify on behalf of God and His Messenger unless he knows the ruling from explicit text. Blind imitation (taqlid) does not justify certainty. This connects the epistemological distinction to legal practice.
4
Consider Ibn Taymiyya's anecdote
Ibn Taymiyya recounts a judgment based on a saying (قـول) rather than clear text. This anecdote reinforces the danger of attributing rulings to God without certain knowledge.

Nawawi vs Ibn al-Qayyim on Ijtihad Error
Nawawi cites a hadith to support mukhti'a; Ibn al-Qayyim explains that error is forgiven but not attributed to Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-Nawawi and Ibn al-Qayyim's interpretations of the hadith on ijtihad error, and explain how Ibn al-Qayyim's response refines the concept of istifragh al-wus'.

Core Idea
The hadith does not force the mukhti'a position; error is forgiven but not attributed to Allah.

In this passage, you will encounter a critical refinement in the debate between the mukhti'a (those who hold only one mujtahid is correct) and the musawwiba (those who hold every mujtahid is correct). Al-Nawawi cites a well-known hadith to support the mukhti'a view, but Ibn al-Qayyim offers a nuanced reinterpretation that undermines this claim. Pay close attention to how Ibn al-Qayyim distinguishes between the erring mujtahid's personal conviction and the actual divine ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith in question
Locate the hadith cited by al-Nawawi: 'If a judge judges and exerts ijtihad, then hits (the truth), he has two rewards; if he judges and exerts ijtihad then errs, he has one reward.' This is the core textual evidence.
The hadith is found in the source as: 'إذا حكم الحاكم فاجتهد، ثم أصاب فله أجران، وإذا حكم فاجتهد ثم أخطأ فله أجر واحد'.
2
Understand al-Nawawi's claim
Al-Nawawi argues that this hadith supports the mukhti'a position because it distinguishes between a correct outcome (two rewards) and an erroneous one (one reward), implying that only one outcome matches Allah's actual ruling.
For the mukhti'a, truth is singular, and error is forgivable but not rewarded equally.
3
Follow Ibn al-Qayyim's response
Ibn al-Qayyim concedes that the erring mujtahid is forgiven and rewarded, but he insists that the mujtahid must not claim his erroneous ruling is Allah's ruling. The hadith does not say the errant judge's verdict is Allah's ruling; it only promises forgiveness and a single reward.
Ibn al-Qayyim stresses the difference between being excused (forgiven) and actually having hit the truth.
4
Compare the two interpretations
Al-Nawawi sees the hadith as proof that only one answer is correct (mukhti'a). Ibn al-Qayyim sees it as supporting the idea that ijtihad effort itself earns reward, regardless of correspondence to the truth, which aligns with the musawwiba view.
The key is whether the hadith speaks about the ruling's ontological truth or the mujtahid's personal accountability.

This passage discusses a specific debate about the interpretation of a hadith. The Dialectical Refutation of Skepticism
Author refutes skeptics by exposing the contradiction in their claim that they do not know whether things have reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the dilemma used to refute skepticism and identify the self-contradiction in the skeptic's claim.

Core Idea
The skeptic's claim refutes itself: either they know they don't know, or they don't know that they don't know — both prove they know.

This passage demonstrates a powerful dialectical method used in Islamic Kalam to dismantle skepticism. The author presents a simple question that traps the skeptic in a self-contradicting position. Understanding this refutation not only defends the possibility of certain knowledge but also sharpens your skill in identifying circular arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the skeptics' claim
Find the exact statement the skeptics make. They say they do not settle on denying realities, but rather announce they do not know whether things have a reality or not.
The skeptics' position is one of agnosticism about reality.
2
Analyze the dilemma question
The author asks: 'Do you know that you do not know?' This question forces the skeptic to take a position: either yes or no.
This is the crucial turning point in the dialectic.
3
Examine the 'yes' answer
If the skeptic says 'yes,' they admit to knowing something (that they do not know), which contradicts their claim that they know nothing.
A direct contradiction.
4
Examine the 'no' answer
If the skeptic says 'no,' they do not know that they do not know, then the question can be repeated about their ignorance, leading to an infinite regress. The author argues this regress ultimately proves that they know realities but are obstinate.
The regress ends with a claim of knowledge, not ignorance.
5
Recognize the final conclusion
The author concludes that in either case, the skeptics are forced to admit knowledge, exposing their position as contradictory and their attitude as obstinacy.
The refutation is complete regardless of their answer.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological argument for the possibility of obtaining cognitive truth (الحقيقة المعرفية). The author begins by establishing that God is absolute truth, citing Quranic verses such as 'That is God, your Lord, the Truth' (10:32) and 'God is the clear Truth' (24:25). Since God is absolute truth, everything that comes from Him is truth, including His word. The author then argues that the Quran is a book of truth, presenting absolute truths because its source is God. The Quran is a reliable cognitive source for historical events (e.g., the story of Moses and Pharaoh in 28:3), definitive truths about the Last Day (31:33), and the scenes of Resurrection (6:30, 39:75). Additionally, the signs in the heavens and earth are created with truth (14:19, 44:38-39, 38:27). However, the author clarifies that these creations are not eternal but have a specified term (46:3). The passage concludes that the truth about the physical world (آيات الآفاق) is limited to time and space, aligning with the wisdom of creation. The overall argument is that cognitive truth is attainable because it is rooted in divine revelation and the created order.

Throughout, the author uses Quranic verses as evidence to establish that truth is a central, objective reality that the Quran affirms and defends.

These verses are cited to show that disbelief is not just rejection but an epistemic failure.
2
Extract the universal claim about conjecture
In Yunus 10:36, the Quran declares: 'Indeed, assumption does not avail against the truth at all.' This is a general epistemological principle: dhann can never produce truth.
This is a universal negative statement—no amount of conjecture can yield certainty.
3
Analyze the logical force of the claim
The phrase 'la yughni min al-haqq shay'an' means conjecture provides no benefit or sufficiency regarding truth. It is not that conjecture sometimes fails; it always fails in the domain of haqq.
This is strong epistemic dismissal: only knowledge (ilm) or certainty (yaqin) can attain truth.
4
Connect to the Quranic call for certainty
Contrast dhann with yaqin: the Quran demands certainty in matters of faith (e.g., belief in the Last Day). Conjecture is not a valid basis for creedal commitment.
This principle underpins Islamic theology: creed must be based on definitive evidence (dalil qat'i).

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exposition that demonstrates how the Quran establishes absolute truth (al-haqq) as a foundational concept across multiple domains. The author begins by stating that belief in the Last Day is one of the definitive truths presented by the Quran, citing verse 31:33 of Luqman. Then, the author shows that the events of the Resurrection are part of this truth, using verse 6:30 of Al-An'am to illustrate that even the disbelievers will acknowledge the truth on that Day. Next, the author moves to the cosmological realm: the creation of the heavens and the earth with truth is presented as a sign in the visible world (ayat al-afaq). Verses from Ibrahim (14:19), Ad-Dukhan (44:38-39), and Sad (38:27) are quoted to show that creation is not frivolous or in vain but purposeful and true. The author then adds an important qualification: the truth of the horizons is not eternal, as the creation has a specified term, citing Al-Ahqaf (46:3) and Ar-Rum (30:8). This underscores that truth in creation is tied to a limited duration. The passage then shifts to the human response to truth, focusing on disbelievers. The author describes their stance as denial, mockery, and reliance on conjecture, quoting Al-An'am (6:5), Yunus (10:35-36), and Al-Mu'minun (23:70). Arrogance prevents them from recognizing truth, as in Al-Qasas (28:39). Finally, despite this opposition, the Quran calls for dialogue with non-Muslims based on truth, citing Al-Ma'idah (5:77). Throughout, the author uses Quranic verses as evidence to establish that truth is a central, objective reality that the Quran affirms and defends.

Key Terms
الحق (al-haqq): Absolute truth or reality, as used in the Quran, referring to objective, divine truth that is certain and corresponds to reality. [Note: In later philosophical texts, 'haqq' may carry metaphysical connotations; here it is primarily used in a Quranic and theological sense.]
الظن (al-dhann): Conjecture or assumption, epistemically weak and insufficient for attaining truth, as opposed to certainty (yaqin). [Note: In usul al-fiqh, dhann can be a valid basis for legal rulings; here it is clearly negative.]

Dialogue on Truth: The Quran's Call to Engagement
Author concludes with the Quran's call for dialogue grounded in truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the Quranic approach to interfaith dialogue based on truth (haqq) and apply it to contemporary interfaith encounters.

Core Idea
The Quran calls for dialogue, but only within the framework of absolute truth, not relativistic pluralism.

After critiquing the disbelievers' rejection, the Quran does not abandon them; it invites them to dialogue. In this lesson, we examine the final verse cited by the author, which addresses the People of the Scripture (Christians and Jews) and calls them not to exceed limits in religion beyond the truth. This reveals that genuine dialogue must be anchored in truth, not in a 'live and let live' relativism.

Guided Path
1
Read the verse on dialogue
Al-Ma'idah 5:77: 'Say, O People of the Scripture, do not exceed limits in your religion beyond the truth.' Note the audience and the command.
'Do not exceed limits' (la taghlu) forbids extremism and deviation from truth.
2
Analyze the phrase 'beyond the truth' (ghayr al-haqq)
The verse implies there is a standard of truth (al-haqq) that should not be transgressed. Dialogue must respect this boundary.
Truth is the reference point; exceeding it means going into falsehood.
3
Contrast with modern dialogue models
Many today advocate for 'open dialogue' without absolutes. The Quranic model maintains truth as non-negotiable while engaging respectfully.
This prevents syncretism and preserves the integrity of the Islamic message.
4
Apply to a contemporary dialogue scenario
Consider a conversation with a Christian about the divinity of Jesus. The Quranic approach would invite the other to 'come to a common word' (Surah Al Imran 3:64) but not concede on truth.
Dialogue is a means to convey truth, not a platform for compromise.

Recognizing the Quranic Argument of First Priority (Qiyas Awla)
Quranic rational argument for resurrection by first priority analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qiyas awla (argument by first priority) in Quranic passages and explain its logical structure.

Core Idea
The Quran argues from greater creation to lesser resurrection using first-priority analogy.

In this passage from Surah Ya-Sin, the Quran presents a rational argument for resurrection. The disbeliever doubts that God can revive decayed bones, but the Quran points to the greater act of creating the heavens and earth. This is a classic example of qiyas awla (argument by first priority), where the stronger case implies the weaker. Understanding this rhetorical-logical device is essential for recognizing how the Quran engages reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt
The disbeliever asks: 'Who will give life to bones while they are decayed?' This is the objection based on the apparent impossibility of resurrection.
2
Locate the Quranic response
The response directs attention to two greater creations: (1) the first creation of humans, and (2) the creation of the heavens and earth. Both are acts that only God can perform.
3
Understand the logical relationship
If God can create the vast heavens and earth (the greater), then He is certainly able to recreate decayed bones (the lesser). This is the qiyas awla: the argument from the stronger to the weaker case.
In Usul al-Fiqh, qiyas awla is a form of analogical reasoning where the 'illa (cause) in the original case is more evident.
4
Apply the concept to other verses
Look for similar patterns where the Quran argues from an obvious case to a disputed one, e.g., Surah al-'Alaq 96:6-8 or Surah al-Mulk 67:23-24.
This recognition helps in tafsir and in appreciating the Qur'an's rational appeal.
