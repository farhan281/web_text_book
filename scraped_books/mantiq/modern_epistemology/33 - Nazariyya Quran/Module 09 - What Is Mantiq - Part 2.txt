Defining Possibility (al-Imkan) in Epistemology
Author defines possibility via al-Jurjani and introduces the problem of doubt and certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-Imkan according to al-Jurjani and distinguish it from necessity and impossibility.

Core Idea
Possibility is the state between necessity and impossibility — the essence does not determine existence or non-existence.

The passage opens with a fundamental epistemological question: can we attain certainty? To answer, we must first define 'possibility' (al-imkan). Al-Jurjani provides a crisp definition that situates possibility between necessity and impossibility. This definition is the key to understanding the debate between skeptics and dogmatists in Islamic thought.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of imkan in the Arabic
Find the phrase where al-Jurjani (d. 816 AH) defines al-imkan. Look for the words 'عدم اقتضاء الذات الوجود والعدم'.
This definition is a concise logical formulation.
2
Parse the definition's components
'The essence (al-dhat) does not necessitate (la taqtadi) existence (al-wujud) nor non-existence (al-'adam).' That is, the essence itself gives no reason either for being or for not being.
The term 'essence' refers to the quiddity of the thing itself.
3
Contrast with wujub and imtina'
Al-Jurjani's definition implicitly contrasts possibility with necessity (wujub: essence necessitates existence) and impossibility (imtina': essence necessitates non-existence). What falls between these two extremes is possible.
This tripartite division is foundational in Islamic logic.
4
Relate the definition to the epistemological problem
The author immediately applies this concept: if knowledge is possible, then we can seek certainty. If not, we are limited to doubt or opinion. The definition of imkan thus sets the stage for the entire inquiry into the sources and limits of knowledge.
Notice how the author links the logical definition to a real epistemic question.

The Three Quranic Grades of Certainty
Author defines the three Quranic grades of certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Quranic grades of certainty (ilm, ayn, haqq al-yaqin) based on their epistemological source and provide an example of each.

Core Idea
Certainty in Islam is not monolithic; it has three distinct levels tied to how you know.

In Islamic epistemology, certainty (yaqin) is not a single state but a hierarchy. This passage from our source defines three grades derived from the Quran and common scholarly agreement. Using the simple example of honey, the author clarifies how knowledge from report, direct sight, and personal experience yield different degrees of conviction. Understanding these grades sharpens your ability to evaluate claims in theology and spirituality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms
Find the three Arabic terms for the grades of certainty: ‘ilm al-yaqīn, ‘ayn al-yaqīn, ḥaqq al-yaqīn. Note that each builds on the previous.
2
Determine the mode of apprehension for each
For ‘ilm al-yaqīn, the mode is hearing or inference (e.g., being told about honey or deducing its existence). For ‘ayn al-yaqīn, it is direct visual observation. For ḥaqq al-yaqīn, it is direct personal experience and taste.
The author uses the honey example: being informed, seeing, tasting.
3
Rank the grades by strength
Compare the certainty each yields. Which is more certain: hearing about honey or tasting it? The author explicitly states each is higher than the previous. ḥaqq al-yaqīn is the highest.
This ranking matches the intuitive notion that direct experience is most certain.
4
Apply the classification to an Islamic example
Consider knowledge of paradise: you may have ‘ilm al-yaqīn from trusting the Quran, ‘ayn al-yaqīn if you see it in a vision (as some prophets did), and ḥaqq al-yaqīn if you taste its bliss directly in the afterlife.
This shows how the tool applies beyond the honey analogy.

Al-Ghazali's Methodological Doubt: From Skepticism to Divine Light
Author presents al-Ghazali's methodological doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace al-Ghazali's methodological doubt and explain how he ultimately grounds certainty in divine illumination.

Core Idea
Al-Ghazali doubts everything to purify knowledge, but finds certainty not in reason alone but in divine light.

Al-Ghazali describes a radical skeptical method: he even doubts the senses and reason. Yet he emerges from this crisis not through logical argument but through a light from God. This lesson explores his journey and its implications for Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Understand al-Ghazali's definition of certain knowledge
Find in the passage where al-Ghazali defines certain knowledge: a disclosure that leaves no doubt, is immune to error, and cannot be shaken even by miracles. Note his example of turning stone to gold.
This definition sets the high standard for what counts as certainty.
2
Examine the doubt of sense perception
Al-Ghazali uses two examples: the shadow that appears stationary but moves gradually, and the star that looks small but is huge. These show that the senses can be deceived, so sensory knowledge is not certain.
The shadow example illustrates motion over time; the star example shows reason correcting sense.
3
Follow the extension of doubt to reason
Al-Ghazali reasons: if reason can correct the senses, perhaps there is a higher judge beyond reason that could correct reason. He does not find such a judge but says its absence does not prove impossibility. This leads to total skepticism.
This step is crucial: it shows that reason itself is not self-certifying.
4
Recognize the role of divine illumination
Al-Ghazali reports that after two months of sophism, Allah cured him by casting a light into his chest. This light restored certainty to necessary truths, and he emphasizes it was not through rational argument.
The light is described as the 'key to most knowledge', indicating its foundational role.
5
Reflect on the limits of reason and the need for divine guidance
Consider the epistemological implication: while reason is useful, ultimate certainty requires divine illumination. This is a key principle in Islamic thought, especially in Sufi epistemology.
Al-Ghazali's position does not reject reason but places it under a higher authority.

The Two Schools of Ijtihad: Musawwiba and Mukhatti'a
Author defines two ijtihad schools and quotes Ghazali.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the musawwiba and mukhatti'a schools of ijtihad by their core claims about correctness in speculative matters.

Core Idea
Musawwiba: every mujtahid correct. Mukhatti'a: only one correct.

In usul al-fiqh, a key debate concerns the status of ijtihad in speculative matters (al-zanniyyat). Two main schools emerged: al-musawwiba and al-mukhatti'a. This lesson defines each school and clarifies their fundamental positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two named schools
Locate the terms al-musawwiba and al-mukhatti'a in the Arabic. Notice they are introduced as two 'trends' (ittijah).
2
Extract the core claim of al-musawwiba
The passage states that musawwiba consider every ijtihad based on revelation to be correct even in speculative matters. This means they hold that all mujtahids are correct in such cases.
3
Extract the core claim of al-mukhatti'a
Mukhatti'a hold that correctness is confined to one side (a person or school), and anything else is error. Thus, only one view is correct.
4
Compare the two positions on the existence of a predetermined divine ruling
Note that later in the passage, the schools are further divided on whether a specific divine ruling exists in cases without explicit text. The musawwiba deny such a ruling (according to the verifiers), while the mukhatti'a affirm it. This is a key differentia.
This step foreshadows the internal subdivisions.

This passage defines two major trends in Islamic legal theory regarding the validity of ijtihad. The first trend, al-musawwiba, holds that every mujtahid who bases his reasoning on revelation is correct, even in speculative matters. Ghazali's Taxonomy of Views within Musawwiba and Mukhatti'a
Ghazali's summary of internal divisions within ijtihad schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the internal positions within the musawwiba and mukhatti'a regarding the existence of a predetermined divine ruling in cases without an explicit text.

Core Idea
Ghazali's summary reveals that the musawwiba and mukhatti'a are not monolithic; each contains nuanced views on divine rulings.

After defining the two main schools, the author quotes al-Ghazali to show that there are internal debates within each school. Ghazali, a prominent musawwibi, distinguishes three positions among those who hold that every mujtahid is correct, and two positions among those who hold that only one is correct. This lesson unpacks these subdivisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the musawwiba positions mentioned by Ghazali
There are two: the verifiers (muhaqqiqun) who deny a predetermined ruling, and others who affirm a predetermined ruling but do not require the mujtahid to find it.
2
Identify the mukhatti'a positions
The mukhatti'a all agree that a specific divine ruling exists. But they differ on whether there is evidence for it. Some say there is no evidence (like a hidden treasure), others say there is evidence, and among those, some say it is definitive, others conjectural.
3
Locate Ghazali's own position
Ghazali states his own view: every mujtahid in speculative matters is correct, and there is no specific divine ruling. This aligns with the verifiers among the musawwiba.
4
Analyze the logic: why does the existence of a predetermined ruling matter?
The disagreement hinges on whether the mujtahid's task is to discover a pre-existing ruling or to construct a ruling based on conjecture. This affects the epistemology of ijtihad.
