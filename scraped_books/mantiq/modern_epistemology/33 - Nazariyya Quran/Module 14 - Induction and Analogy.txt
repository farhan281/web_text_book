Quranic Experimental Method and Four Usul al-Fiqh Principles
Author explains Quranic experimental method and its roots in four causal principles of Islamic legal theory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the four causal principles from Usul al-Fiqh that the author presents as the foundation of the Quranic experimental method.

Core Idea
The Quranic experimental method is rooted in Usul al-Fiqh's causal principles.

The author argues that the Quran promotes an experimental method, exemplified by Prophet Ibrahim's inquiry into idols. This method is grounded in four causal principles from Usul al-Fiqh: ittiraad al-'illa, in'ikaas al-'illa, dawaran al-'illa, and tanqih al-manat. Understanding these principles shows how Islamic legal theory provides a foundation for empirical science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic basis
Locate the verse quoted (al-Shu'ara' 26:72-73) and understand how it demonstrates a pre-test/post-test logic: Ibrahim asks whether the idols benefit or harm, testing before and after the act of calling.
This is an example of empirical inquiry in the Quran.
2
Distinguish the four principles
List the four principles: ittiraad (constant accompaniment in existence), in'ikaas (reflection in non-existence), dawaran (co-extensiveness in both), and tanqih al-manat (elimination and specification of the cause).
These are methods for establishing causality in usul al-fiqh.
3
Connect dawaran to experimentation
Note that dawaran is explicitly equated with the experiment and yields certainty when frequently observed. This is the core of empirical method.
The author states: 'هذا الدوران عين التجربة، بحيث يفيد القطع كلما كثر'.
4
See the logical structure
Recognize that these principles form a coherent method for establishing causality: cause present → ruling present (ittiraad), cause absent → ruling absent (in'ikaas), and both together (dawaran) confirm causality. Tanqih al-manat isolates the true cause from multiple attributes.
This structure parallels modern scientific methodology.

Commentary
This passage argues that the Quran promotes an experimental method, exemplified by Prophet Ibrahim's inquiry into idols. The author claims this method is rooted in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh) based on the concept of 'illa (cause). Four principles of causal inference from usul are presented as the foundations of empirical research: 1) Ittiraad al-'illa: the cause is constantly accompanied by the ruling; if the cause exists, the ruling exists. 2) In'ikaas al-'illa: if the cause is absent, the ruling is absent. 3) Dawaran al-'illa: co-extensiveness of cause and effect in both existence and non-existence; this is considered the essence of experimentation and yields certainty when frequently observed. 4) Tanqih al-manat: eliminating irrelevant attributes and isolating the true cause through elimination and specification. Thus, the author presents a distinctly Islamic origin for scientific methodology, rooted in the logical reasoning of usul al-fiqh.

Ibrahim's Inductive Method in the Quran
Author analyzes Quranic stories of Ibrahim to illustrate empirical method and inductive reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the inductive reasoning structure in the Quranic account of Ibrahim's method and map it onto the five steps of empirical scientific inquiry.

Core Idea
Ibrahim's method models inductive reasoning from particular observations to the universal truth of Tawhid.

This passage examines the Quranic stories of Prophet Ibrahim as a prototype of empirical scientific thinking. The author shows how Ibrahim's journey from sensing a problem to reaching certainty about the Creator follows the five steps of the scientific method, relying on inductive reasoning. We will break down this method to understand how induction operates in an Islamic epistemic context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial problem sensing
Ibrahim senses a problem in his society: the worship of celestial bodies as gods. This is the first step of the scientific method: sensing a problem.
2
Extract the hypotheses
Ibrahim formulates hypotheses: the star, the moon, and the sun as potential gods. These are the proposed solutions to the problem.
3
Note the empirical testing
Each hypothesis is tested through observation: the star sets, the moon sets, the sun sets. Ibrahim uses sight (ḥiss) as the instrument to falsify each hypothesis.
4
Recognize the inductive move
From the particular observations of setting, Ibrahim inductively concludes that no celestial body deserves worship. This is inductive reasoning: from specific instances to a universal judgment.
Induction here is presented as leading to certainty (yaqīn).
5
Map onto the five-step scientific method
The passage explicitly lists the five steps: 1) sensing the problem, 2) defining it, 3) formulating hypotheses, 4) testing and selecting the correct hypothesis, 5) evaluation and judgment. Confirm each step with the Ibrahim narrative.
This mapping shows the harmony between Quranic guidance and empirical science.

The Five-Step Inductive Method in the Quran: The Ibrahimic Paradigm
Author outlines a five-step inductive method and illustrates it with the story of Ibrahim's search for God.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five steps of inductive reasoning as derived from the Quran and apply them to analyze the Ibrahimic method of seeking God.

Core Idea
Induction in the Quran is not a Western import; it is the method of Ibrahim himself.

This passage presents a methodological framework for knowledge acquisition rooted in the Quran. The author enumerates five steps of an inductive method that mirror modern scientific reasoning but are anchored in revelation. He then demonstrates that this method is exactly what Ibrahim (AS) used in his search for God, as recorded in Surah Al-An'am. By studying this 'Ibrahimic paradigm,' you will see how the Quran endorses empirical observation and rational inference as paths to certainty about the divine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial problem
The method begins with 'sensation of the problem.' In Ibrahim's story, the problem is the idolatry of his father and people. He senses that their worship is based on error.
2
Define the problem precisely
The second step is defining the problem. Ibrahim explicitly states: 'Do you take idols as gods? Indeed, I see you and your people in manifest error.' This clarifies the issue.
3
Formulate hypotheses
The third step is formulating hypotheses. Ibrahim tests possible objects of worship: first a star, then the moon, then the sun. Each is a candidate for lordship.
These hypotheses are based on observable celestial phenomena.
4
Test hypotheses against a criterion
The fourth step is examining and selecting the best hypothesis. Ibrahim uses the criterion of permanence: a true Lord must not be subject to change or disappearance (setting). Each candidate fails this test.
The Arabic 'afala' (set) indicates a change in state.
5
Issue a universal judgment
The final step is evaluation and judgment. After eliminating all temporary objects, Ibrahim declares his conclusion: 'I have turned my face towards Him who created the heavens and the earth.' This is a universal judgment about the one true Creator.
The judgment is universal (kulli) and certain.

Quranic Reasoning by Priority (al-Istidlal bi Tariq al-Ula)
Describes the Quranic method of a fortiori reasoning using comparative examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a Quranic a fortiori argument and explain its role in proving the possibility of resurrection.

Core Idea
If Allah created the immense heavens and earth, recreating humans is even easier.

Islamic theologians often use Quranic arguments to establish the possibility of resurrection. One powerful method is reasoning by priority (tariq al-ula), where a stronger case establishes a weaker one. In this lesson, we explore how the Quran compares the creation of the heavens and earth to the creation of humans, concluding that resurrection is not only possible but easier for Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phenomena compared
Find the two creations being compared in the Quranic verses: the heavens and earth versus humans.
2
Determine which is claimed to be greater
Quran 40:57 explicitly states that creating the heavens and earth is greater than creating humans.
3
Extract the conclusion for the lesser case
If Allah can create the greater (heavens and earth), He can certainly recreate the lesser (humans after death). This is the a fortiori reasoning.
The conclusion is that resurrection is easier than initial creation.
4
Recognize the implicit logical structure
This is not mere analogy but a fortiori: the greater includes the lesser. The argument is: if A > B, and A is possible, then B is even more possible.
5
Apply to theological debates
This reasoning is used to refute those who deny resurrection. It shows that the same power that created the universe operates with greater ease in recreating humans.

Reasoning by Signs of the Horizons
Describes the Quranic method of reasoning from cosmic signs to prove the Creator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the steps of Quranic inductive reasoning from cosmic signs to the existence of the Creator, using the method of al-istidlal bi ayat al-afaq.

Core Idea
The Quran uses observable cosmic phenomena as inductive premises to infer the Creator's existence and attributes.

This lesson introduces a distinctive Quranic method of reasoning that does not rely on abstract syllogisms but on concrete signs in the universe. The author presents several verses where the Quran calls attention to natural processes — the separation of heavens and earth, the water cycle, the revival of barren land — and invites rational inference to the Creator. This method is a form of induction (istiqra') where multiple instances of orderly, life-giving phenomena lead to a universal conclusion about the Creator's power and wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the observable phenomena (signs)
Locate the natural events described in the Quranic verses: the splitting of heavens and earth (21:30), creation of life from water (21:30), subjection of sun and moon (29:61), and revival of dead land by rain (29:63, 41:39).
2
Notice the rhetorical structure
Each verse uses a question or condition that presupposes a clear answer: the phenomena are attributed to Allah by the very people being addressed (e.g., 'they will surely say, Allah' in 29:61, 29:63).
3
Extract the inductive inference
From multiple specific instances (creation, sustenance, revival), the Quran draws a general principle: these acts of giving life and order point to a single Creator who is all-powerful and all-knowing. This is an inference from particular cases to a universal conclusion about the Creator.
4
Evaluate the completeness and certainty
Is the induction complete (tamm) or incomplete (naqis)? The Quran presents several distinct signs; though not exhaustive, the accumulation of evidence builds certainty (yaqin). The author calls the natural world a 'vast laboratory' for this reasoning.
