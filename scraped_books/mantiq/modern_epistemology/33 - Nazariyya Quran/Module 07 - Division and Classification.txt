Ibn Khaldun's Binary Classification of Knowledge
Author surveys different classifications of knowledge from Islamic scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between natural knowledge and transmitted rational knowledge in Ibn Khaldun's classification.

Core Idea
Ibn Khaldun divides knowledge into what human reason can attain and what must be received from revelation.

Ibn Khaldun, the great historian and philosopher, offered a binary classification of knowledge that reflects the sources of human understanding: reason and revelation. This lesson unpacks his two categories and their epistemological foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Find in the passage the two types: natural knowledge (al-ma'rifa al-tabi'iyya) and transmitted rational knowledge (al-ma'rifa al-'aqliyya al-wad'iyya).
2
Examine the source of each
Natural knowledge is attained by human intellect alone. Transmitted knowledge is based on report from the Lawgiver (revelation).
3
Understand the role of reason
In natural knowledge, reason is the primary tool. In transmitted knowledge, reason only attaches derivative issues to foundations (al-furu' lil-usul).
4
Recall the modern endorsement
The passage notes that Islamic educational conferences recommended Ibn Khaldun's classification, highlighting its relevance.

Classifications of Knowledge: Researchers differ widely in classifying knowledge, some differences due to the classifier's doctrinal tendency, others due to a technical approach in classification regarding comprehensiveness and detail. Commentary
This passage from a modern work on Quranic epistemology presents a survey of various classifications of knowledge in the Islamic tradition. The author begins by noting that classifications differ based on doctrinal tendencies and technical preferences. Then, under 'First: The Binary Classification,' four main subdivisions are listed. (1) The Khaldunian classification distinguishes natural knowledge (philosophical, attainable by human reason) from transmitted rational knowledge (based on revelation, with limited rational scope). (2) Next is a classification into divine knowledge (God's eternal, non-acquired knowledge) and originated sciences (created knowledge, divided into necessary and speculative). (3) Al-Ghazali's division separates transaction (belief, action, abandonment) from unveiling (inner spiritual knowledge). (4) Finally, empirical knowledge (from physics to social sciences) is contrasted with abstract knowledge (mathematics, philosophy, literature, jurisprudence, Sufism). The author concludes by noting that recent Islamic educational conferences endorsed Ibn Khaldun's classification. The passage aims to demonstrate the richness of epistemological thought in Islam and the variety of ways scholars have categorized knowledge.

Divine Knowledge and Originated Sciences
Author surveys different classifications of knowledge from Islamic scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between divine knowledge (al-`ilm al-ilahi) and originated sciences (al-`ulum al-muhdatha) and their subdivisions.

Core Idea
God’s knowledge is eternal and unacquired; all created knowledge is originated and either necessary or speculative.

The passage presents a classification that distinguishes the knowledge of the Creator from the knowledge of creatures. This lesson explores the unique attributes of divine knowledge and the two types of originated sciences: necessary and speculative.

Guided Path
1
Define divine knowledge
Locate 'al-`ilm al-ilahi' in the passage. Note its attributes: absolutely eternal (qadim mutlaq), neither necessary nor acquired.
2
Define originated sciences
Find 'al-`ulum al-muhdatha'. These are what creatures learn, including humans, jinn, angels, even animals.
3
Subdivide originated sciences
They are divided into two: necessary knowledge (where doubt is impossible) and speculative knowledge (requiring research and reflection).
4
Contrast with divine knowledge
Divine knowledge is unique to God; originated sciences are temporal and acquired. Consider the implications for epistemology.

Al-Ghazali's Division: Transaction and Unveiling
Author surveys different classifications of knowledge from Islamic scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Ghazali's classification of religious knowledge into transaction (mu'amala) and unveiling (mukashafa).

Core Idea
For al-Ghazali, religious knowledge has an outer dimension (belief, action, abandonment) and an inner luminous reality revealed to the purified heart.

Imam al-Ghazali offered a profound epistemological distinction between the external practice of religion and the inner spiritual realization. This lesson unpacks his categories of mu'amala and mukashafa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Find 'mu'amala' and 'mukashafa' in the passage. Mu'amala concerns outward religion; mukashafa is inner knowledge.
2
Break down mu'amala
Mu'amala has three parts: belief (i'tiqad), action (fi'l), and abandonment (tark). These cover the practical aspects of faith.
3
Understand mukashafa
Mukashafa is described as a light that appears in the heart after purification from blameworthy traits. It is the science of the inner state (ilm al-batin).
4
Contrast the two
Mu'amala is accessible through external learning and practice; mukashafa is a direct spiritual insight granted to the purified soul.

Empirical and Abstract Knowledge
Author surveys different classifications of knowledge from Islamic scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between empirical knowledge and abstract knowledge as presented in the modern classification.

Core Idea
Empirical knowledge comes from experiment and observation; abstract knowledge is attained through pure reason and reflection, covering higher sciences.

The final binary classification in the passage contrasts knowledge obtained through the senses and experiment with knowledge reached by abstract thought. This lesson clarifies the scope and method of each.

Guided Path
1
Define empirical knowledge
Find 'al-ma'rifa al-tajribiyya'. It encompasses physical sciences, social sciences, and everything obtained through empirical studies.

Certainty-Based Tripartite Classification
Classification of knowledge by certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify knowledge as certain, probable, or erroneous based on correspondence to reality.

Core Idea
Certain knowledge corresponds to reality; probable knowledge is likely but unconfirmed; erroneous knowledge is false.

The third classification uses certainty as the criterion, measuring how well knowledge matches reality. This is similar to the Islamic epistemological framework of yaqīn, ẓann, and jahl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Find in the Arabic: certain knowledge (al-maʿrifa al-yaqīniyya), probable knowledge (al-maʿrifa al-ẓanniyya), and erroneous knowledge (al-maʿrifa al-khāṭiʾa).

In uṣūl al-fiqh, evidence is classified as qatʿī (certain, like the text of the Qur'an) or ẓannī (probable, like a weak hadith). Erroneous knowledge would be a mistaken ruling.
This classification is essential for ijtihād.

Why Does the Quran Always Mention 'Ghayb' Before 'Shahada'?
Author explains the consistent order of ghayb before shahada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain two reasons given by scholars for the consistent order of 'al-ghayb' before 'al-shahada' in the Quran.

Core Idea
Despite the higher honor of unseen knowledge, the Quran delays the witnessed because of its greater multiplicity or the boundlessness of the unseen.

A careful reader of the Quran will notice that whenever the paired phrase 'al-ghayb wa al-shahada' appears, the unseen is always mentioned first. This might seem to contradict the principle of balāgha (eloquence) that the more praiseworthy term should be delayed. This lesson examines the author's two explanations for this phenomenon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the observation
Notice that in all Quranic verses with 'ghayb and shahada', ghayb comes first. The author points out this pattern and uses it as a starting point.
Example: Surah Ar-Ra'd 13:19
2
State the apparent contradiction
Ordinarily in praise (al-madh), the more praiseworthy item is placed last. Since knowledge of the unseen is more honorable than knowledge of the witnessed, one might expect the witnessed to come first—but the opposite occurs.
3
Analyze the first explanation
The author suggests that witnessed matters concerning Allah are more numerous than unseen matters concerning us. Since knowledge is honored by the multiplicity of its objects, delaying the witnessed is more appropriate because it is larger in scope.
This explanation focuses on quantity.
4
Analyze the second explanation
Alternatively, unseen matters are boundless in extent while witnessed matters are limited. The verse about the soul (Quran 17:85) is cited as evidence that human knowledge is limited. This vastness of the unseen justifies its priority in mention.
This explanation focuses on infinity vs. finitude.

Triple Classification
Perhaps this classification is the most present among the other classifications, both ancient and modern. For example, Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi (d. 508 AH) says according to the method of Abu Mansur al-Maturidi: 'When realities and sciences are established, we say that the causes and paths of knowledge are three: one of them is the sound senses, which are the sense of hearing, the sense of sight, the sense of smell, the sense of taste, and the sense of touch; the second is the truthful report; and the third is reason.' Ibn Taymiyya (d. 728 AH) said according to the school of Ahmad ibn Hanbal: 'The paths of knowledge are three: sense, reason, and what is composed of them like report. Among matters are those whose knowledge cannot be obtained except through report, just as every person knows through truthful reports like the mutawatir report, and what is known through the reports of the prophets, peace be upon them all.' This classification is supported by some contemporary researchers, including some figures of the school of 'Islamization of Knowledge' – reference was made earlier to some of them adopting the binary classification – who consider them to be revelation, reason, and the universe; while others consider them to be God, the Quran, and the universe. All of this confirms that a precise crystallization of this topic has not yet been achieved, even in the circles most interested in it. Perhaps this is due – among other reasons – to the two previous problems: the lack of agreement among researchers regarding their conception of the extent of agreement or disagreement concerning the terms 'sources' and 'methods'. It is also due to the absence of a formula representing a minimum consensus on the classification of these sources and methods.
Quadruple Classification
This classification can be described as Western philosophical; we mention it here only for completeness of the classification series, not because we believe in its soundness. It is originally attributed to Plato.

Commentary
This passage deals with the epistemological classification of the sources and means of knowledge. The author presents first the 'triple classification' as the most prevalent among Islamic scholars, both classical and modern. He cites two classical authorities: Abu al-Mu'in al-Nasafi (Maturidi tradition) and Ibn Taymiyya (Hanbali tradition). The author laments that a precise formulation of this topic has not been achieved, attributing this to lack of consensus on the terms 'sources' and 'methods'. Finally, he introduces the 'quadruple classification' attributed to Plato, describing it as Western philosophical and included only for completeness, not because he considers it sound. The Five Faces of Correspondence to Truth
Author enumerates five possible relationships between a perception/expression and truth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify any statement about Islamic doctrine into one of the five faces of correspondence to truth, and explain why only the first face constitutes truth.

Core Idea
Only complete correspondence to truth is true; all other relationships are false.

In Islamic epistemology, truth is defined as correspondence (mutabaqa) between a mental conception or statement and external reality. But not all claims that aim at truth achieve it fully. The author provides a precise classification of five possible relationships any perception or expression can have with the truth. Understanding this classification is essential for distinguishing correct from incorrect beliefs in Islamic theology, especially when dealing with subtle deviations.

Guided Path
1
Understand the baseline: complete correspondence
The first face is total alignment with reality. Only this is true (haqq). Recognize that any claim of truth must be measured against this standard.
Think of correct statements about Allah's attributes, e.g., 'Allah is powerful' without exaggeration or deficiency.
2
Identify the second face: addition (excess)
Addition means including something beyond the truth. This constitutes falsehood proportional to the excess. Example: attributing a body to Allah (anthropomorphism) adds a quality not present in reality.
In 'ul al-fiqh, this relates to ghuluww (exaggeration) in religion.
3
Identify the third face: deficiency
Deficiency means omitting part of the truth while claiming full correspondence. The falsehood equals the amount omitted. Example: denying some of Allah's attributes (ta'til).
This is the opposite of excess.
4
Identify the fourth and fifth faces: deviation and total departure
Deviation combines excess and deficiency, like tahrif (distortion). Total departure means no correspondence at all; the statement is entirely false. Example: outright denial of Allah's existence.
The author merges the fourth and fifth in description but treats them as distinct.
5
Apply the classification to a theological example
Take a statement like 'Allah is powerful and has a hand'. Identify the correct part (power) and the addition (hand). Classify it as second face (excess) because of the addition, not as fourth face (deviation) because it does not combine both excess and deficiency.
Practice with several examples to internalize the classification.
