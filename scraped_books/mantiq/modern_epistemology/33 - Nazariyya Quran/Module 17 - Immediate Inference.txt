Qiyas al-Gha'ib: Shared Method, Different Epistemic Result
Defense of qiyas al-gha'ib and critique of biased labeling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the shared method of qiyas al-gha'ib in both Islamic and Aristotelian traditions and critique the logical inconsistency in labeling one as rational and the other as merely faith-based.

Core Idea
Islamic qiyas yields certainty; Aristotelian analogy yields only probability — a difference that undermines claims of Greek rationality over Islamic faith.

In this passage, the author addresses the claim that Greek thought is rational while Islamic thought is based on faith and the unseen. He shows that both traditions use the same method of analogical reasoning from the visible to the unseen. However, the epistemic rigor differs: Islamic scholars achieve certainty, while Aristotelian analogy only reaches probability. This core insight allows us to expose the flaw in the writer's biased labeling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the shared method
Find where the author states that qiyas al-gha'ib is a 'shared scientific method' used by both Muslim usuliyyun and followers of Aristotle. Understand that both traditions reason from observed phenomena to unseen realities.
This method is foundational in Islamic theology and jurisprudence, e.g., inferring the attributes of Allah from observable creation.
2
Extract the epistemic difference
Note the contrast: Islamic qiyas 'involves investigation such that it leads to certainty (yaqīn)', while Aristotelian analogy 'only achieves probability (ẓann)'. This is the essential difference.
In usul al-fiqh, yaqīn is required for foundational creedal matters; ẓann suffices for many legal rulings.
3
Analyze the writer's inconsistency
The author asks: if both use the same method, why does the writer label Greek thought as rational/objective and Islamic as faith-based/unseen? Note the logical implication: the writer's label is inconsistent and reveals a civilizational defeat.
The author is engaging in dialectical critique, using the opponent's own premise to expose a contradiction.
4
Formulate the logical question
The author poses a direct rhetorical question: 'Why then did you describe the other as rational and objective, and the self as faith and unseen?' This question forces the reader to confront the bias.
Consider how this critique applies to contemporary discussions of Islamic vs. Western science.

As for what is said about analogical reasoning from the visible to the unseen (qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid), that is a shared scientific method, followed by the usuliyyun among Muslim scholars, just as the followers of the Aristotelian method followed it, with an essential difference: that among Muslims it involves investigation such that it leads to certainty, whereas in Aristotelian analogy it only achieves probability. And this dissipates the author's description in a later instance, which reveals a real civilizational defeat of the self before the other — attributing rationality and objectivity to Greek thought in contrast to Islamic thought which has an immanentist and unseen character. The logical question here: is not qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid a shared method between the two parties? Why then did you describe the other as rational and objective, and the self as faith and unseen, implying an absence of rationality and objectivity among Muslims? And if we read the last statement of the author in which he claims victory for reason, then we — with a little reflection — will realize that his victory in reality was not a victory for reason as an abstract value, but rather a victory for political authority which inflicted various kinds of physical and psychological harm on its opponents for their opposition to it in the famous issue of the creation of the Qur'an. Is this the reason of al-Ma'mun and the Mu'tazilite trend which al-Ma'mun supported? Then why did the Mu'tazila split into about twenty factions, all claiming the authority of reason? Which reason won and which lost? And where is the position of the author's reason among those mental extravagances?

Commentary
This passage is a critical refutation of a writer who claims that Greek thought is rational and objective while Islamic thought is based on faith and the unseen. The author argues that both traditions use the method of qiyas al-gha'ib 'ala al-shahid (analogy from the visible to the unseen), which is a shared scientific method. The essential difference is that Islamic scholars apply rigorous investigation (taharrī) that leads to certainty (yaqīn), whereas Aristotelian analogy only yields probability (ẓann). Therefore, the writer's labeling of Greek thought as rational and objective and Islamic thought as immanentist is inconsistent and reflects a civilizational inferiority complex. The author then questions the writer's claim of victory for reason in the context of the Mihna (inquisition) over the creation of the Qur'an. The author argues that what happened was not a victory for abstract reason but for political authority (al-Ma'mun and the Mu'tazilites). The fact that the Mu'tazila split into many factions, all claiming reason, shows that 'reason' is not monolithic and can be misused. The author ends by asking which reason actually won and where the writer's own reason stands in light of these historical excesses. Throughout, the author uses dialectical questioning to expose the writer's inconsistency and to defend the rational credentials of Islamic thought.

The Mihna: Victory for Reason or Political Authority?
Critique of the Mihna as a victory for political power

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the author's argument that the Mihna (inquisition over the creation of the Qur'an) was a victory for political power, not for abstract reason, and evaluate the implications for claims of rationality.

Core Idea
The Mihna was a victory for political authority, not abstract reason; the fragmentation of the Mu'tazila further undermines any monolithic claim of reason.

The author continues his critique by examining the writer's appeal to the Mihna as a victory for reason. He argues that what actually triumphed was political power, wielded by al-Ma'mun and the Mu'tazilites, who persecuted opponents. Moreover, the Mu'tazila's internal fragmentation into many factions, all claiming reason, shows that 'reason' is not a singular authority. The author questions where the writer's own reason stands in light of these historical excesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim about the Mihna
Find where the author states that the writer's claimed 'victory for reason' was actually a 'victory for political authority' that inflicted harm on opponents.
The Mihna refers to the Abbasid inquisition under al-Ma'mun, testing the doctrine of khalq al-Qur'an.
2
Analyze the evidence: political authority and harm
The author notes that the political authority (al-Ma'mun and the Mu'tazila) caused physical and psychological harm to those who disagreed on the creation of the Qur'an. This, he implies, is not a victory for abstract reason but for power.
Consider whether coercion can ever be associated with genuine rationality.
3
Examine the fragmentation of the Mu'tazila
The author asks: why did the Mu'tazila split into about twenty factions, all claiming the authority of reason? This suggests that 'reason' is not a unified standard but can be used to justify diverse positions.
The diversity of Mu'tazilite schools (e.g., al-Jubbai, al-Nazzam) shows that appeals to reason alone did not produce consensus.
4
Formulate the rhetorical question
The author ends with pointed questions: 'Which reason won and which lost? Where is the author's reason among those mental extravagances?' These questions force the reader to reflect on the ambiguity of the term 'reason' in historical contexts.
This challenges any simple narrative of 'reason' versus 'faith'.
