Chapter Four: Section Two - The Possibility of Attaining Cognitive Truth
In summary, the issue of truth in this framework represents one of the absolute constants that admit no dispute, and the factor of relativity is not included in it, for it is of clarity and certainty such that claiming relativity in any part of it becomes a kind of folly. And no one can - absolutely - believe in the principle of relativity or apply it to everything, for any thought, whatever its source, must have logical or self-evident constants, just to be able to exist and stand.
And when claims of relativity and lack of stability in everything are raised, they are promoted with the aim of weakening the faith of Muslims and shaking it in their souls, while the beliefs of secularists, Crusaders, and Zionists are fixed, firm, and absolute, and on them their states and systems are based.
Rules for Seeking Truth
The Noble Qur'an presents a set of rules for seeking cognitive truth, which some researchers have attempted to compile, and they can be summarized as follows:
1 - Love of truth and magnifying it, as that is the goal of research in life. 'So what is after the truth except error?' [Yunus 10:32].
2 - The way to reach the truth is research, and therefore we find condemnation of blind imitation and its people in many verses, as has passed with us.
3 - Seizing the truth from whatever vessel it comes from, for it is the lost property of the believer.
4 - Stopping short of confusion in knowing the truth, for Allah is the possessor of absolute knowledge. 'And you have not been given of knowledge except a little' [Al-Isra 17:85]. And on the tongue of the Prophet: 'Say: I am not an unprecedented messenger, and I do not know what will be done with me or with you; I only follow what is revealed to me' [Al-Ahqaf 46:9].
5 - Knowledge itself is a value, as it is a path to acquiring information, according to specific methods and systems, and therefore there is distinction between scholars and the ignorant: 'Say: Are those who know equal to those who do not know?' [Az-Zumar 39:9].
6 - The relativity and probability of knowledge: 'And say: My Lord, increase me in knowledge' [Ta-Ha 20:114].
7 - The necessity of referring to the entire Qur'an to know a single Qur'anic truth.
8 - Ascribing revelation exclusively as the source, from the perspective of ascribing divinity and lordship exclusively to Allah Almighty.
9 - Knowledge through revelation and reason together.
10 - The necessity of consistency of the truth deduced from the Qur'an with other Qur'anic truths.
11 - Sincerity of intention and soundness of purpose.
In a general educational manner, we can benefit from truth in the process of education through the following:
1 - Providing the philosophy of education with an intellectual background about the reality of existence and the truths it contains, so that education can present a balanced definition of existence, its purpose, and the laws governing its movement through the experiences it offers to learners in educational situations, to affirm the balance between the theoretical and practical aspects. This is done while planning the desired educational programs and objectives.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion from a contemporary Islamic perspective, asserting that absolute truth exists and that the Quran provides a methodology for seeking it. The author begins by affirming that truth is an absolute constant that admits no relativity, arguing that even those who claim relativism rely on absolute logical or self-evident principles to make their claim. The author then criticizes the promotion of relativism as a means to weaken Muslim faith, pointing out that secularist, crusader, and Zionist ideologies themselves are presented as absolute. This sets the stage for presenting Quranic rules for seeking truth. The rules are enumerated: 1) Love and veneration of truth as the goal of life; 2) Research is the means, with condemnation of blind imitation; 3) Accept truth from any source, as it is the 'lost property of the believer'; 4) Acknowledge human limitation in absolute knowledge, citing verses that show even the Prophet does not know the unseen; 5) Knowledge is a value, distinguishing scholars from the ignorant; 6) Recognize the relativity of human knowledge, always seeking increase; 7) Consult the entire Quran for a single truth; 8) Revelation is the sole source of divine knowledge; 9) Combine revelation and reason; 10) Ensure consistency among deduced truths; 11) Sincerity and sound intention. The passage concludes with educational applications, emphasizing that the philosophy of education should be grounded in the truth of existence, balancing theory and practice. Throughout, the author uses Quranic verses and scholarly references to support the argument. The passage does not engage in formal logical argumentation but provides a methodological framework for epistemology rooted in Islamic sources.

العقل (al-'aql): Reason, a valid and necessary tool for knowledge, but subordinate to revelation. [Note: In classical mantiq, 'aql is the faculty of intellection; here it is paired with revelation.]

Primary Sources of Knowledge: Sense, Intellect, and Revelation
Author presents the table of contents of a book on Quranic theory of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the three primary sources of knowledge in the Quranic framework: sense, intellect, and revelation.

Core Idea
The Quran recognizes three primary sources for knowledge: sensory perception, rational reasoning, and divine revelation, each with its own characteristics and roles.

The passage presents a tripartite classification of sources and methods of knowledge: sense (hiss), intellect (aql), and infallible report (wahi). These are considered both sources and methods simultaneously. The study acknowledges each individually and emphasizes their integration. Understanding this classification is foundational for the Islamic epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sources
Look for 'al-hiss', 'al-aql', and 'al-wahy' (or 'al-khabar al-ma'sum'). The passage says the study adopts the tripartite classification.
2
Note the phrase 'sources and methods simultaneously'
Each is not just a source but also a method, meaning they are both the origin and the process of acquiring knowledge.
3
Examine the description of sense
Sense is distinguished from experimentation; types: external and internal senses; importance and relativity.
4
Examine intellect
Intellect is characterized by causality; its philosophy in the Quran; its relativity. Note the causal principle is highlighted.
5
Examine revelation
Revelation is linked to the necessity of prophethood; it has a purpose, characteristics, and integrates with other sources.
