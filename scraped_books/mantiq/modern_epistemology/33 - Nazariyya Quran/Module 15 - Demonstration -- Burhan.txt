The Quran's Demand for Demonstrative Proof
Author explains the Quranic demand for proof (burhān) to urge rational inquiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Quran uses the demand for proof (burhān) to establish the authority of reason over conjecture and imitation.

Core Idea
The Quran does not reject reason—it demands rational proof.

This passage explores a key aspect of the Quran's approach to intellect: mobilizing the demonstrative mind. The author shows that the Quran repeatedly calls for evidence (burhān) and criticizes arguments based on wishful thinking or conjecture. By understanding this method, we see that Islamic education is fundamentally grounded in rational demonstration, not blind taqlīd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic verses cited
Locate the four verses the author uses: Baqarah 111, Aal-e-Imran 65-66, Anbiya 24, and Yunus 68. Each verse involves a demand for proof or a criticism of argument without knowledge.
The verses target Jews, Christians, polytheists, and general claims about Allah.
2
Extract the common demand for proof
Notice the repeated phrase 'Bring your proof' (hātū burhānakum) and the condemnation of arguments lacking authority. The Quran insists that any claim, especially in matters of creed, must be supported by rational evidence.
This includes even the core issue of tawḥīd.
3
Understand the implication (dalālat al-iltizām)
The author argues that demanding proof implies that reason itself is a valid source of evidence. If the Quran commands us to bring proof, it implicitly affirms the intellect's capacity to know and evaluate.
This is a logical implication, similar to uṣūl al-fiqh terminology.
4
Connect to Islamic education's foundation
The passage concludes that Islamic education is based on demonstration (burhān) and evidence (ḥujja), not imitation (taqlīd) or mere conjecture (ẓann). Recognize that this principle guides how knowledge is sought and validated.
Apply this to modern debates: the Quran encourages critical inquiry.

Commentary
This passage explains the Quranic method of urging the use of demonstrative reason (burhān). The author first introduces the aspect of diverse incitements to use the intellect, distinguishing it from purely rational methods. The key formulation is 'Mobilizing the demonstrative intellect': the Quran calls people to seek proof and avoid wishful thinking and weak conjectures. The author then cites several Quranic verses: In Surah al-Baqarah, the Quran challenges Jews and Christians who claimed exclusive salvation to bring their proof. In Surah Aal-e-Imran, it criticizes arguing about Abraham without knowledge. The Quran rejects any discussion lacking the authority of demonstration, even on matters like tawḥīd. The verse from Surah al-Anbiya again demands proof from polytheists. Finally, in Surah Yunus, the Quran condemns those who attribute a son to Allah without any authority. The author concludes that addressing people with demands for evidence (ḥujja or burhān) indicates that reason itself is a proof and support, by implicit indication (dalālat al-iltizām). The educational takeaway is that Islamic education is founded on demonstration and evidence, not imitation (taqlīd) or mere conjecture (ẓann).

The Threefold Classification of Conflicting Proofs
Ibn Taymiyya classifies types of contradictory proofs to resolve alleged reason-revelation conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any pair of conflicting proofs into one of three categories: both definitive, both speculative, or one definitive and one speculative.

Core Idea
Ibn Taymiyya's three-case taxonomy resolves the alleged conflict between reason and revelation.

Ibn Taymiyya provides a systematic classification for handling apparent contradictions between proofs. By determining the certainty level of each proof, we can apply the appropriate rule and avoid false dichotomies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two proofs in conflict
Start by clearly identifying the two proofs that are claimed to contradict each other. Determine whether each is from revelation (naql) or reason (aql).
2
Determine the certainty level of each proof
For each proof, decide whether it is definitive (qat'i) or speculative (dhanni) based on Ibn Taymiyya's criteria. A definitive proof yields certain knowledge; a speculative proof yields probable knowledge.
Use the definitions from the key terms to guide your assessment.
3
Classify the pair into one of three cases
Place the pair into the relevant category: (1) both definitive, (2) both speculative, or (3) one definitive and one speculative. This classification determines the next step.
Ibn Taymiyya's three cases are exhaustive and mutually exclusive.
4
Apply the rule for the case
For case 1 (both definitive): they cannot truly conflict; re-examine or reinterpret. For case 2 (both speculative): seek tarjih (preponderance). For case 3 (one definitive, one speculative): the definitive takes precedence regardless of source.
These rules are agreed upon by rational people, according to Ibn Taymiyya.

Ibn Taymiyya is a respected imam in the Salafi school, and he uncovered the illusion of alleged contradiction in this book. Among what he said: If it is said that two proofs conflict, whether both are scriptural or rational, or one is scriptural and the other rational, then it must be said: They are either both definitive, or both speculative, or one is definitive and the other speculative. This is agreed upon by all rational people, because a definitive proof is one whose implication must be established, and its indication cannot be false. Then if two definitive proofs conflict and one contradicts the other, combining two contradictories would be necessary, which is impossible. Rather, every set of proofs that are thought to conflict while being considered definitive must be such that both or one of them is not definitive, or their implications are not contradictory. For with known contradictory implications, conflict of the two proofs is impossible. If one of the two conflicting proofs is definitive and the other is not, then it must be given precedence by consensus of rational people, whether it is scriptural or rational, because conjecture does not remove certainty. If both are speculative, then one seeks to give preponderance to one of them; whichever is preponderant is given precedence, whether scriptural or rational. There is no answer to this except to say: The scriptural proof cannot be definitive. Then it is said: This, besides being false, is of no avail, because on this basis the definitive must be preferred because it is definitive, not because reason is the foundation of revelation. These people made their basis for preference that reason is the foundation of revelation, which is false, as will be explained, God willing. And if it is assumed—the speech is still Ibn Taymiyya's—that a definitive and a speculative proof conflict, no rational person disputes giving precedence to the definitive; but that the scriptural cannot be definitive is a very difficult matter. Also, people agree that much of what the Messenger brought is known necessarily from his religion, such as the obligation of acts of worship, the prohibition of immoralities and injustice, the oneness of the Creator, the affirmation of the Hereafter, and other things.

Commentary
This passage is a critical exposition by Ibn Taymiyya addressing the alleged contradiction between reason and revelation. He begins by stating his purpose: to refute the illusion of conflict, as done in his book 'Dar' Ta'arud al-'Aql wa al-Naql'. The core argument proceeds by classifying types of conflicting evidence. Ibn Taymiyya enumerates three possibilities: both proofs are definitive (qat'iyyan), both speculative (dhanniyyan), or one definitive and the other speculative. For the first case, he asserts that two definitive proofs cannot truly conflict because a definitive proof necessarily yields its conclusion; if they contradicted, it would require combining two contradictories, which is impossible. Therefore, if conflict is perceived, at least one of the proofs must not be genuinely definitive, or the apparent contradiction is not real. This is a logical principle agreed upon by all rational people. In the second case, where one proof is definitive and the other speculative, the definitive must be given precedence, regardless of whether it is from reason or revelation. The reason given is that conjecture (zann) cannot overturn certainty (yaqin). In the third case, where both are speculative, one must seek preponderance (tarjih) based on evidence, again without bias for reason or revelation. Ibn Taymiyya then anticipates an objection: that scriptural evidence can never be definitive. He dismisses this as false and useless, because it wrongly establishes reason as the foundation of revelation. He further argues that it is absurd to claim that scriptural proofs cannot be definitive, since many core Islamic teachings (e.g., obligation of worship, prohibition of evil, tawhid, afterlife) are known by necessity (darura) from the Prophet's message. The commentary concludes by reinforcing that definitive scriptural proofs exist, and any alleged conflict with definitive rational proofs is illusory.

Two Definitive Proofs Cannot Conflict
Ibn Taymiyya argues that two definitive proofs cannot conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to prove why two definitive proofs cannot genuinely conflict using Ibn Taymiyya's logical argument.

Core Idea
Two definitive proofs cannot conflict because that would entail impossible contradictory implications.

Ibn Taymiyya's argument is grounded in the logical principle of non-contradiction. Understanding this principle is crucial for defending the harmony between reason and revelation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of a definitive proof (qat'i)
A definitive proof is one whose implication must be true. Its indication cannot be false. This means that if a proof is qat'i, its conclusion is certain.
Refer to the key term 'qat'i' for a precise definition.
2
Assume two definitive proofs conflict
Suppose we have two definitive proofs that appear to yield contradictory conclusions. According to the definition, both conclusions would have to be true simultaneously.
This is the starting point of Ibn Taymiyya's reductio ad absurdum.
3
Recognize the impossibility
If both conclusions are true and they contradict each other, then we have a logical contradiction (two opposites both true). This is impossible by the law of non-contradiction.
Ibn Taymiyya states 'combining two contradictories would be necessary, which is impossible.'
4
Conclude that conflict cannot occur
Therefore, the initial assumption must be false. Two definitive proofs cannot truly conflict. If conflict appears, at least one proof is not definitive, or the contradiction is not real.
This conclusion is agreed upon by all rational people, as Ibn Taymiyya says.

فالواجب أن يقال: لا يخلو إما أن يكونا قطعيين، أو يكونا ظنيين، وإما أن يكون أحدهما قطعياً والآخر ظنياً. فأما القطعيان فلا يجوز تعارضهما سواء كانا عقليين أو سمعيين، أو أحدهما عقلياً والآخر سمعياً، وهذا متفق عليه بين العقلاء، لأن الدليل القطعي هو الذي يجب ثبوت مدلوله، ولا يمكن أن تكون دلالته باطلة. وحينئذ فلو تعارض دليلان قطعيان وأحدهما يناقض الآخر للزم الجمع بين النقيضين وهو محال، بل كل ما يعتقد تعارضه من الدلائل التي يعتقد أنها قطعية فلابد من أن يكون الدليلان أو أحدهما غير قطعي، أو أن لا يكون مدلولاهما متناقضين، فأما مع تناقض المدلولين المعلومين فيمتنع تعارض الدليلين. وإن كان أحد الدليلين المتعارضين قطعياً دون الآخر، فإنه يجب تقديمه باتفاق العقلاء، سواء كان هو السمعي أو العقلي، فإن الظن لا يرفع اليقين. وأما إن كانا جميعاً ظنيين: فإنه يصار إلى طلب ترجيح أحدهما، فأيهما ترجح كان هو المقدم، سواء كان سمعياً أو عقلياً. ولا جواب عن هذا إلا أن يقال: الدليل السمعي لا يكون قطعياً، وحينئذ فيقال: هذا مع كونه باطلاً فإنه لا ينفع، فإنه على هذا التقرير يجب تقديم القطعي لكونه قطعياً، لا لكونه أصلاً للسمع، وهؤلاء جعلوا عمدتهم في التقديم كون العقل هو الأصل للسمع، وهذا باطل كما سيأتي بيانه - إن شاء الله . وإذا قدر أن يتعارض قطعي وظني لم ينازع عاقل في تقديم القطعي، لكن كون السمعي لا يكون قطعياً دونه خرط القتاد. وأيضاً فإن الناس متفقون على أن كثيراً مما جاء به الرسول معلوم بالاضطرار من دينه كإيجاب العبادات، وتحريم الفواحش والظلم، وتوحيد الصانع، وإثبات المعاد، وغير ذلك. وحينئذ فلو قال قائل: إذا قام الدليل العقلي القطعي على مناقضة هذا فلا بد من تقديم أحدهما. فلو قدم هذا السمعي قدح في أصله، وإن قدم العقلي لزم تكذيب الرسول فيما علم بالاضطرار أنه جاء به وهذا هو الكفر الصريح، فلا بد لهم من جواب عن هذا. والجواب عنه أنه يمتنع أن يقوم عقلي قطعي يناقض هذا. فتبين أن كل ما قام عليه دليل قطعي سمعي يمتنع أن يعارضه قطعي عقلي.
It is necessary to say: they are either both certain, or both probable, or one is certain and the other probable. As for two certain proofs, they cannot conflict—whether both are rational, both transmitted, or one rational and one transmitted. This is agreed upon by all rational people, because a certain proof is one whose implication necessarily holds and whose indication cannot be false. If two certain proofs conflicted and one contradicted the other, that would necessitate combining two contradictories, which is impossible. Rather, for any proofs believed to be certain but thought to conflict, it must be that both or one of them is not certain, or that their implications are not contradictory. With known contradictory implications, conflict of the proofs is impossible. If one of the two conflicting proofs is certain and the other not, then the certain must be given precedence by agreement of all rational people, whether it is the transmitted or the rational, because probability does not remove certainty. If both are probable, then one seeks to weigh one over the other; whichever is preponderant is given precedence, whether transmitted or rational. No response to this is possible except to say: the transmitted proof cannot be certain. Then it is said: this is false and not beneficial, because on this assumption the certain must be preferred because it is certain, not because it is a foundation for the transmitted. These people rely on the claim that reason is the foundation for revelation, which is false, as will be explained—if God wills. If it is assumed—and the discussion is still Ibn Taymiyya's—that a certain and a probable conflict, no rational person disputes giving precedence to the certain. But to claim that the transmitted cannot be certain is extremely difficult. Furthermore, people agree that much of what the Messenger brought is known necessarily from his religion, such as the obligation of acts of worship, the prohibition of indecencies and injustice, the oneness of the Creator, the affirmation of the afterlife, and so on. So if someone says: if a certain rational proof arises contradicting this, one must be preferred. If the transmitted is given precedence, it undermines its foundation; if the rational is given precedence, it necessitates disbelieving the Messenger in what is known necessarily that he brought—and this is clear disbelief. They must have an answer to this. The answer is that a certain rational proof contradicting this cannot exist. Thus it is clarified that every matter supported by a certain transmitted proof cannot be opposed by a certain rational proof.

Commentary
This passage from Ibn Taymiyya offers a systematic treatment of conflicting evidence (ta'arud) between rational and transmitted proofs. It is a critical passage because Ibn Taymiyya is refuting the claim that reason must always take precedence over revelation when they appear to conflict. He begins by laying out a classification: two pieces of evidence can be both certain (qat'i), both probable (dhanni), or one certain and the other probable. For two certain proofs, conflict is impossible regardless of their source—both rational, both transmitted, or one each. The reason is that a certain proof necessarily yields its truth; if two certain proofs contradicted each other, that would require both a proposition and its negation to be true simultaneously, which is logically impossible. If they appear to conflict, then either one or both are not actually certain, or their implications are not truly contradictory. When one proof is certain and the other probable, the certain must be preferred, because probability cannot override certainty. This is agreed upon by all rational people, whether the certain one is rational or transmitted. When both are probable, one seeks to weigh them; the preponderant one is followed. Ibn Taymiyya then anticipates an objection: the rationalists might claim that transmitted proofs can never be certain. He dismisses this as false and unhelpful, because even if true, the certain proof would still be preferred due to its certainty, not because it is foundational for revelation. The rationalists' actual basis, he says, is that reason is the foundation (asl) for revelation, which he will refute later. He also argues that to claim transmitted evidence cannot be certain is extremely difficult (khart al-qatad) because many religious doctrines—like obligatory worship, prohibition of sins, tawhid, and the afterlife—are known by necessity (darura) as coming from the Prophet. Thus, if a certain rational proof were to contradict such a known transmitted matter, preferring the rational would entail rejecting what is known necessarily about the Prophet's message, which is clear disbelief. The only coherent answer is that a certain rational proof cannot genuinely contradict a certain transmitted proof. Hence, all certain transmitted proofs are immune from conflict with any certain rational proof.

A) The rational proof must be preferred because it is rational.

Key Takeaway
Revealed truths known by necessity are certain and cannot be genuinely contradicted by any rational proof.
