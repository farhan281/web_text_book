Critique of Dividing Senses into Apparent and Hidden
Author critiques the division of senses into apparent and hidden, emphasizing Quranic integration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the critique of the division of senses into apparent and hidden and articulate the Quranic integration of sensory and intellectual faculties.

Core Idea
The Quran does not divide senses; it integrates hearing, sight, and heart as unified epistemic tools.

This passage delves into a common philosophical classification of senses and challenges its applicability to the Quranic worldview. The author argues that dividing senses into apparent and hidden not only lacks evidence but also obscures the Quran's distinctive method of integrating sensory and intellectual knowledge. As you study this critique, you will learn to identify the author's grounds, evaluate the argument, and appreciate the Quranic perspective on knowledge acquisition.

Guided Path

Locate the doctrine being critiqued: the division of senses into apparent (ẓāhira) and hidden (bāṭina). The author states this division lacks support.
This classification is common in Islamic philosophy but absent from the Quranic text.
2

Find the reasons the author gives against the division: absence of evidence from the Quran, and that the Quran mentions senses together (hearing, sight, heart) indicating integration.
The author references verses from Surah an-Nahl and al-Isra'.
3
Analyze the author's alternative
The author proposes that the Quran's method is to mention senses based on their cognitive-perceptual value and function, not their composition. The heart (fu'ad) represents intellectual powers.
This integration is a key feature of the Quranic epistemological approach.
4
Evaluate the critique's internal validity
Assess whether the author's argument is logically sound: does the absence of explicit Quranic division prove the Quran rejects it? Does the integration argument hold? Consider the educational implications mentioned.
The author concedes the division has no educational impact, focusing instead on functional use of senses.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the classification of senses into apparent (ẓāhira) and hidden (bāṭina). The author argues that this division is not supported by strong evidence and misrepresents the Quranic approach. He points out that verses such as those in Surah an-Nahl and al-Isra' mention hearing, sight, and the heart together, indicating an integration of sensory and intellectual faculties rather than a separation into categories. The Quran does not explicitly use the term 'aql (intellect) as a noun but employs verbal forms and other terms to refer to intellectual processes. The author concludes that the Quranic method evaluates senses based on their cognitive value and function, not their composition. He also notes that the educational implications of this division are neutral; what matters is how senses are utilized in education.
