Innate vs Acquired Knowledge: The Quranic Balance
Author discusses innate vs acquired knowledge and Quranic epistemology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between innate and acquired knowledge and explain their implications for Islamic education.

Core Idea
Knowledge is both subjective and objective, requiring attention to both the learner and the curriculum.

The Quranic perspective on knowledge avoids extremes: it recognizes both innate disposition (fitrah) and acquired learning from the external world. This holistic view shapes how we approach teaching Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two hypotheses
Read the first paragraph of the passage. The author contrasts two possibilities: knowledge is either innate (inherited) or acquired from the external world. Write down each hypothesis.
2
Analyze the implications
If knowledge is innate, learning is subjective and focuses on the learner. If acquired, emphasis shifts to curricula. Think about how each view affects educational methods in a madrasa.
3
Consider the Quranic synthesis
The author states that knowledge is both subjective and objective. Find this claim in the text. How does including revelation as both source and method prevent bias?
4
Apply to Islamic sciences
Reflect on a specific Islamic science (e.g., usul al-fiqh). Where do we treat knowledge as innate (like the fitrah for tawhid) and where as acquired (like learning rulings)?

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological discussion on the possibility of knowledge and the roots of skepticism. The author begins by framing the problem: knowledge can be viewed as innate (inherited) or acquired from the external world. If innate, learning is subjective; if acquired, the curriculum is emphasized. The author asserts that knowledge is both subjective and objective, so both learner and material matter. The Quranic perspective is introduced to counter external influences like that of al-Ghazali. The complexity of methodology is highlighted, as knowledge relies on senses, reason, and intuition, all vulnerable to biases. Revelation, if included as both source and method, would reduce this interference. The author then presents John Locke's view that knowledge emerges from ideas via sense, reason, or intuition, but notes Locke's omission of revelation. The passage then traces the root of doubt to sophistry (sufsata'iyya), defined by al-Jurjani as a compound syllogism from illusions meant to mislead. From sophistry sprang sects: al-'Inadiyya (deny realities), al-La'adriyya (agnostics), and al-'Andadiyya (reality follows belief). Muslim scholars refuted them, exemplified by al-Baghdadi's response to the 'Inadiyya, calling them obstinate. The passage thus connects the epistemological problem of skepticism to historical sophistical movements and offers a corrective through revelation and Islamic critique.

Key Terms
السوفسطائية (al-sufsata'iyya): A sect that uses compound syllogisms from illusions to mislead opponents, as defined by al-Jurjani in the footnote. [Note: Stable term in logic and philosophy for sophistical reasoning.]
العنادية (al-'inadiyya): Those who deny the realities of things, claiming they are illusions and fantasies. [Note: Specific to this classification; not a common term in standard logic works.]
اللاأدرية (al-la'adriyya): Agnostics who deny knowledge of existence or non-existence, perpetually doubting. [Note: Corresponds to modern agnosticism, but defined here within Islamic heresiography.]
العندية (al-'andadiyya): Those who claim that realities of things depend on beliefs, so belief determines existence. [Note: A form of relativism; similar to subjective idealism but in Islamic context.]

The Sophistical Syllogism: The Tool of Misleading Opponents
Al-Jurjani's definition of sophistry with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a sophistical syllogism as defined by al-Jurjani and analyze its use of illusory premises.

Core Idea
Sophistry uses valid logical forms with false premises to mislead opponents.

Al-Jurjani defines sophistry as a compound syllogism composed of illusions, intended to mislead and silence an opponent. We will dissect his example: substance exists in the mind; everything in the mind is an accident; therefore substance is an accident. Though formally valid, the premises are deceptive.

Guided Path
1
Extract the syllogism
From the footnote, identify the major premise, minor premise, and conclusion of the example: 'الجوهر موجود في الذهن' (Substance exists in the mind), 'كل موجود في الذهن قائم بالذهن عرض' (Everything existing in the mind is an accident), 'الجوهر عرض' (Substance is an accident).
2
Determine the figure and mood
Classify the syllogism by figure. The middle term is 'موجود في الذهن' (existing in the mind). It is the subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise, so it is first figure. The mood is AAA (Barbara). Check formal validity.
3
Assess the truth of premises
Consider whether the premises are objectively true. Is it really true that everything existing in the mind is an accident? What about concepts of substances? Are they accidents? Identify the illusion.
4
Define sophistry
Conclude that sophistry is a material fallacy: the form is valid but the premises are false or deceptive. The purpose is to mislead, not to discover truth.

Al-Nasafi defines munazara and argues sophists are outside its scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the necessary conditions for a valid munazara and explain why sophists cannot participate.

Core Idea
Debate requires shared principles; sophists deny all principles.

In Islamic dialectics, munazara is not just any argument—it is a structured inquiry between parties who accept certain basics. Al-Nasafi explains that the sophist's rejection of all realities makes him ineligible for debate, forcing a different response.

Sophists deny all realities and have no agreed-upon principles, making debate inconceivable.

4

This is not debate but discipline.

Al-Nasafi's Practical Contradiction Argument: The Sophist's Own Survival Refutes Him
Al-Nasafi argues that sophists' survival and reactions to punishment demonstrate their implicit knowledge of realities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct al-Nasafi's practical contradiction argument as a modus tollens conditional syllogism and apply it to similar skeptical claims.

Core Idea
The sophist's actions prove he knows realities, contradicting his words.

Al-Nasafi offers a powerful argument: if the sophist truly believed that nothing is real and knowledge is impossible, he would not avoid fire or seek food. Since he does, his actions prove his words false. This argument uses practical necessity to expose the sophist's inconsistency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sophist's verbal claim
The sophist asserts that there is no reality for anything and no knowledge of anything; all is mere opinion.
This is the position al-Nasafi aims to refute.
2
Observe the sophist's actual behavior
Despite his denial, the sophist eats food, wears clothes, avoids fire and snakes, i.e., he acts as if he knows what harms and benefits him.
This behavioral evidence is the basis of the contradiction.
3
Extract the implied conditional premise
If a person had no knowledge of realities (e.g., that fire burns), he would not avoid fire. Similarly, if he had no knowledge of food's benefit, he would not seek it.
This is the 'if-then' statement underlying the argument.
4
Apply modus tollens
The sophist does avoid fire and seek food. Therefore, by modus tollens, he does have knowledge of realities. This refutes his claim.
Modus tollens: If P then Q; not Q; therefore not P.
5
Generalize to the punishment scenario
If the sophist were consistent, he would not complain when beaten or starved, since he denies the reality of pain and hunger. But he does complain, further proving his own inconsistency.
Al-Nasafi uses this to justify discipline as a means of forcing admission.

B) That he knows the realities of things, such as what preserves life.
C) That he is stronger than other people.
D) That his philosophy is correct.

Key Takeaway
A consistent denial of all reality is pragmatically self-refuting; human survival depends on knowing what is real.

How This Lesson Applies — to MY Life?
