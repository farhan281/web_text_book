Ibn Taymiyya's Three Ways of Knowledge
Author presents Ibn Taymiyya's threefold classification and contemporary receptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Ibn Taymiyya's three ways of knowledge and explain how contemporary scholars have reinterpreted them.

Core Idea
The three ways of knowledge: sense, intellect, and report, are the pillars of Islamic epistemology.

Ibn Taymiyya, the influential Hanbali theologian, laid out a simple yet profound classification of the sources of knowledge. This passage states his view and then shows how contemporary scholars have adopted and modified it. Understanding this classification helps you see the foundations of Islamic epistemology and the ongoing debates about knowledge sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three ways of knowledge
Read the Arabic: طرق العلم ثلاثة: الحس والعقل والمركب بينهما كالخبر. Find the three terms: الحس (sense), العقل (intellect), and الخبر (report) as the composite of both.
2
Understand the role of report (khabar)
Ibn Taymiyya says some knowledge can only be attained by report, like mutawatir reports and prophetic reports. This highlights the importance of testimony in Islamic epistemology.
3
Compare with contemporary reformulations
Notice contemporary scholars replace 'report' with 'revelation' or use 'Allah, Quran, universe'. Identify the shifts from the original tripartite scheme.
4
Diagnose the lack of consensus
The author points to two reasons: disagreement on the terms 'sources' and 'methods', and absence of an agreed classificatory framework. Reflect on why this matters for Islamic knowledge production.

Commentary
This passage presents Ibn Taymiyya's classification of the ways of knowledge into three: sense, intellect, and the composite between them which is report. The author quotes Ibn Taymiyya directly, noting that he follows the Hanbali school. The classification is then examined in light of contemporary scholarship: some modern researchers, especially those from the 'Islamization of Knowledge' movement, adopt this tripartite scheme but with variations, substituting revelation for report, intellect for reason, and adding the universe. Others replace the triad with Allah, the Quran, and the universe. The author then observes that despite interest in such classifications, no precise formulation has been achieved. He attributes this to two underlying issues: disagreement over the terms 'sources' and 'methods', and the absence of a minimally agreed classificatory framework. The passage thus serves both as a historical presentation and a critical assessment of the state of the field.

Plato's Fourfold Classification of Knowledge Sources
Author outlines Plato's four sources of knowledge: sensation, opinion, deduction, and intellection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define Plato's four sources of knowledge—sensation, opinion, deduction, and intellection—as outlined in the passage.

Core Idea
Plato systematized four ascending sources of knowledge, from sensory perception to abstract intellection.

This passage presents Plato's fourfold classification of the sources of knowledge, which the author includes for completeness but does not fully endorse. By studying these categories, you will be able to compare them with Islamic epistemological frameworks that prioritize revelation alongside reason and sense.

Guided Path
1
Locate the four sources in the Arabic text
Find the numbered list in the Arabic: 1) الإحساس, 2) الظن, 3) الاستدلال, 4) التعقل. Read their Arabic definitions.
The terms are technical; note the root letters of each.
2
Define each source using the passage
For sensation (الإحساس): perception of accidents and images in wakefulness and dreams. For opinion (الظن): judgment about changeable sensible things. For deduction (الاستدلال): knowledge of mathematical essences in physical objects. For intellection (التعقل): apprehension of abstract essences like bigness and smallness.
The passage defines each with examples.
3
Recognize the hierarchical order
Note the progression from particular (sensation) to universal (intellection). Plato sees a hierarchy: each level is a different mode of knowing.
This is a key feature of Platonic epistemology.
4
Reflect on the author's disclaimer
The author says the classification is presented 'more for completeness than belief in its soundness.' Consider why an Islamic scholar might be cautious about a purely rationalist framework without revelation.
This sets up a critical perspective.

The Fourfold Classification
And this classification can be described as Western philosophical, which we present here for the completeness of the circles of classification, more than for our belief in its soundness. It is originally attributed to Plato (d. 347 BC), whom some researchers consider the first philosopher to investigate the question of knowledge for its own sake, and who emerged from the dualism between the Sophists inclined to consider knowledge as arising from sensation and that it is partial and changing, and Socrates (d. 399 BC) who traces true knowledge back to the intellect. But Plato investigated the sources of knowledge and found them to be four: 1- Sensation, which is the perception of accidents of bodies or their likenesses in wakefulness and their forms in sleep. 2- Opinion, which is judgment about perceptible things as relative and changing due to their connection with matter. Likewise, these judgments - 3- Deduction, which is knowledge of mathematical essences realized in perceptible things, such as arithmetic, astronomy, and geometry. 4- Intellection, which is the perception of essences abstracted from all matter, that is when one looks at bigness or smallness as meanings separate from it and applies them to it. And we find that there are those who believe in this classification with differences in some terms without a real change in its essence. And one researcher emphasizes the danger of considering one of the four sources as the sole source, for that would lead to much misuse, and therefore they must all cooperate to achieve their goals.

Commentary
This passage presents Plato's fourfold classification of the sources of knowledge. The author introduces it by noting its Western philosophical origin, stating it is included for completeness rather than endorsement. Plato is credited as the first philosopher to study knowledge systematically, transcending the dualism of Sophists (who relied on sensation) and Socrates (who relied on intellect). Plato's four sources are then enumerated: First, sensation—perception of accidents in wakefulness and dreams. Second, opinion—judgment about sensible things as relative and changeable. Third, deduction—knowledge of mathematical essences (arithmetic, astronomy, geometry) that are realized in physical objects. Fourth, intellection—apprehension of abstract essences like 'bigness' and 'smallness' as separate concepts applied to matter. The passage concludes by noting that some modern scholars accept this classification with minor terminological variations, and a researcher warns against using any single source exclusively, emphasizing the need for their combination.

The Danger of Epistemic Monism: Why No Single Source Suffices
Author warns against relying on a single source of knowledge, following Plato's fourfold classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the danger of relying exclusively on one source of knowledge, as warned by the researcher cited in the passage, and relate this to Islamic principles of multiple evidence.

Core Idea
Using only one source of knowledge leads to misuse; true understanding requires combining all available sources.

The passage concludes with a strong warning from a researcher: taking any of Plato's four sources as the sole source of knowledge leads to serious misuse. This lesson unpacks that warning and connects it to Islamic epistemology, where multiple sources—Quran, Sunnah, reason, and consensus—must work together for sound judgment.

1
Find the warning in the Arabic text

The warning comes at the end after the enumeration.
2
Analyze the consequences of epistemic monism
Consider each source alone: if only sensation, no universal knowledge; if only opinion, no certainty; if only deduction, only mathematical; if only intellection, disconnected from sensible reality. What would be missing?
The researcher says it leads to 'misuse'.
3
Connect to Islamic epistemology
In usul al-fiqh, rulings are derived from multiple sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. Relying only on textual sources without reason leads to literalism; relying only on reason without texts leads to speculation. The warning parallels the need for integration.
Islamic scholars emphasize the complementarity of evidence.
4
Reflect on the author's own caution
The author introduces the classification as 'more for completeness than belief in its soundness.' Consider how this caution reinforces the researcher's warning.
The author distances himself from Plato's framework.

Locate the author's name
In each entry, the author's name usually appears first, often after a bullet point. Note the full name and any honorifics or titles. For example, 'سيّد قطب' is the author of the first entry.
Sometimes the author may be an institution, like 'وزارة التربية والتعليم'.
2
Identify the title and subtitle
The title is in Arabic and may be followed by a subtitle or description. Look for the main work and any explanatory phrases. For instance, 'في ظلال القرآن' is the main title, and 'سيّد قطب' is the author.

3

Some entries may lack edition info, indicated by 'بدون طبعة' (no edition).

Multiple publishers may be listed with a dash or comma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract and evaluate key bibliographic information (author, title, editor, edition, place, publisher) from a classical Islamic citation.

Core Idea
A bibliography is not just a list; it is a scholarly chain of trust.

The bibliography of a scholarly work reveals the sources the author relied upon. For an Islamic text, noting the edition, publisher, and editor can indicate the reliability and authenticity of the reference. This lesson teaches you to parse and evaluate such entries critically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and title
Look for the author's name (e.g., أبو حامد الغزالي) and the title (e.g., المستصفى من علم الأصول). The title often begins with 'al-' and contains keywords like 'fi' or 'li'.
Some entries list the editor after the title in parentheses.
2
Locate editing and publication details
Find the editor (تحقيق), edition number (ط), date (both Hijri and Gregorian), place of publication, and publisher. Example: تحقيق: محمد ناصر الدين الألباني، ١٤٠٥ هـ - ١٩٨٥م، ط الثالثة، بيروت ودمشق: المكتب الإسلامي.
Dates may be given in Hijri only or both.
3
Assess completeness
Note if any information is missing: without تاريخ (date), بدون طبعة (no edition), بدون مكان للنشر (no place). Missing data can affect scholarly verifiability.
An entry like 'بدون تاریخ، بدون طبعة، بدون مكان للنشر: دار الفكر' is less reliable.
4
Recognize the scholarly field
Determine the discipline from the title: usul al-fiqh (المستصفى), hadith (مشكاة المصابيح), philosophy (معجم الفلاسفة), language (المعجم الوسيط). This shows the breadth of sources used.
Some works cover multiple fields.

Parsing an Arabic Academic Bibliography
Lists main references and periodicals used in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of academic references (author, title, edition, publisher) in Arabic bibliographic entries from both book and periodical sections.

Core Idea
A bibliography is a map of scholarly dialogue – each entry is a node in the network of Islamic knowledge.

Academic references are not just lists; they are the backbone of scholarly integrity. In Islamic scholarship, proper citation ensures that ideas are traceable and debates are grounded. This passage from a contemporary work shows how a scholar cites both classical and modern sources, following a structured format.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sections
Look at the heading structure. You will see 'المصادر والمراجع' (Sources and References) for books, and 'ثانياً - الدوريات' (Secondly - Periodicals) for journal articles. Notice the separation.
The dash and numbering indicate a clear division.
2
Extract components of a book entry
Each book entry typically includes: author (مؤلف), title (عنوان), edition (طبعة), place of publication (مكان النشر), publisher (ناشر), and year (سنة النشر). Sometimes the year is given in both Hijri and Gregorian.
Example: 'موسوعة لالاند الفلسفية' has translator and supervisor in parentheses.
3
Parse a periodical entry
For journal articles, the entry includes: article title (عنوان المقال), author (مؤلف), journal name (اسم المجلة), issue number (العدد), year (السنة), and publisher (ناشر). Often the journal name is italicized or marked.
Example: 'إسلامية المعرفة' from the journal 'المسلم المعاصر'.
4
Recognize common patterns
Many entries follow the pattern: Author – Title – Edition – Place: Publisher. Some include translation details ('ترجمة'). Notice the use of bullet points (•) and dashes.
This is a standard Arabic academic citation style.

How to Read a Bibliography in Islamic Scholarship
Author provides a bibliography of sources used in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the author, title, publication details, and context of a source from a bibliography in an Islamic scholarly work.

Core Idea
A bibliography is the scholar's intellectual footprint – it shows where he stood.

Bibliographies may seem like dry lists, but they are essential tools for verifying claims and tracing the lineage of ideas. In Islamic scholarship, knowing the sources behind a work helps you assess its authority and background. This lesson teaches you how to decode a bibliography entry from the book you are studying.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Author
Look for the author's name at the beginning of the entry. Note full name and any institutional affiliation.
Sometimes an author is also listed as editor or compiler.
2
Locate the Title and Publication Type
Find the title of the work. Is it a book, a conference paper, a lecture? This tells you the format and likely audience.
Titles may be abbreviated or include subtitles.
3
Extract Publication Details
Look for the date (Hijri and Gregorian), publisher, place, and editor if any. These details help verify the source and assess its timeliness.
Absence of a publisher might indicate a manuscript or private circulation.
