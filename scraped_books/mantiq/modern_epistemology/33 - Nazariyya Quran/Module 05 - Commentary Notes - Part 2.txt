Learning Objective

Core Idea

The author takes issue with a claim that rationalists also link education to religion, but in a different way from the traditionalists (ahl al-naql). He sees this as a dangerous conflation that masks the deviant nature of groups like the Ismailis and gnostic Sufis. This lesson walks you through his critique and its implications for defining legitimate intellectual traditions in Islam.

Understanding Bibliographical References in Islamic Scholarship
Author presents a bibliography of Islamic sources cited in the work.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract key publication details from a bibliography entry and assess the authority of cited sources.

Core Idea
A bibliography is a roadmap to the sources that support a scholar's argument.

This passage is a list of references from a modern Islamic work. It includes classical hadith collections, tafsir, linguistics, and modern Western psychology in translation. Learning to read a bibliography tells you what kind of evidence the author values and allows you to verify claims yourself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and title
Look for the name of the author and the full title of the work. Often the title comes first, followed by the author in parentheses or after a comma.
Some entries have joint authors; note all.
2
Note the editor/translator
Some entries have editors or translators in parentheses. This indicates the edition quality and whether the work is a translation.
Editors like 'Muhammad Fuad Abd al-Baqi' indicate a critical edition.
3
Check publication details
Look for publisher, place, edition number (e.g., 'third edition'), and date. Multiple editions suggest the work is widely used and updated.
Absence of date or edition may indicate a lesser-known edition.
4
Classify the type of source
Is it a primary source (e.g., hadith collection), a classical commentary, or a modern study? This tells you the role in the author's argument.
Example: 'Sahih al-Bukhari' is primary; 'Fath al-Bari' is a commentary.
5
Evaluate the reliability
Check if the edition is well-regarded (e.g., Sahih al-Bukhari with Fath al-Bari is a standard edition). Translations into Arabic may have varying accuracy.
Modern works from established presses (e.g., Dar al-Qalam) are generally reliable.
