Defining Rationally Impossible Existence
Author defines impossible existence and gives the example of the impossibility of simultaneous existence and non-existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define rationally impossible existence and distinguish it from other modal categories using the criterion of intellectual necessity.

Core Idea
The rationally impossible is what the intellect forces as non-existent; no circumstance can make it possible.

In this passage, the author defines the second modal category—al-mustahil. Understanding this definition is crucial because it sets rational boundaries for theological and logical argumentation. The example of contradictories clarifies the concept and prevents confusion with merely customary impossibilities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Find the term being defined in the Arabic: 'مستحيل الوجود عقلاً' (rationally impossible existence). What exactly is being defined?
It is the second of three modal categories (necessary, impossible, possible).
2
Extract the genus
What broader category does it belong to? It is a mode of existence (or non-existence) considered by the intellect. The genus is 'existence (or non-existence) as judged by reason'.
The genus is the domain of modal judgments.
3
Extract the differentia
What distinguishes it from the possible and necessary? The author says: 'the intellect necessitates its non-existence and does not permit the possibility of its existence in any state.' This is the differentia—intellectual necessity of non-existence across all conceivable circumstances.
Contrast with the possible: intellect does not necessitate either existence or non-existence.
4
Apply the definition to the example
Why is simultaneous existence and non-existence of a single thing, from one aspect, rationally impossible? Because existence and non-existence are complete contradictories; the intellect cannot conceive their combination.
The example illustrates the differentia: no matter how far-fetched the assumptions, the intellect rejects it.
5
Check the limits
When is it not impossible? The author clarifies: if the aspect or time differs, there is no impossibility. This shows that the impossibility is tied to the condition of unity of aspect, time, and place.
This nuance prevents overextension of the concept.

Core Idea
Rational impossibility arises from logical contradiction, not empirical experience.

In Islamic theology (kalam), distinguishing what is rationally impossible from what is merely contingent is crucial for sound reasoning about God's attributes and actions. This lesson unpacks the definition of rational impossibility and demonstrates it through three clear examples.

Core Idea
Rational necessity: what reason cannot conceive as non-existent.

In Islamic theology (kalam), the distinction between necessary and contingent being is foundational. This passage defines 'wajib al-wujud 'aqlan' as something whose existence reason necessitates and whose non-existence is unimaginable. The author provides seven examples that illustrate different cases of rational necessity, from part-whole relationships to causation and the unique Divinity.

Rational Proof for the Necessity of the Creator's Existence
Author presents a rational proof that the Creator's existence is necessary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the rational argument for the necessity of the Creator's existence and identify it as a modus tollens conditional syllogism.

Core Idea
If absolute non-existence were the origin, existence could never arise; since existence exists, a Necessary Creator must exist.

This passage presents a concise rational argument for the necessity of the Creator. The author uses a conditional reasoning structure to show that absolute non-existence cannot be the source of existence. Let's unpack the logical steps of this proof and see how it exemplifies a modus tollens argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conclusion
The central claim is that the Creator's existence is necessary (wajib) and non-existence is impossible. Find this in the Arabic.
The conclusion is stated at the beginning: 'وجود الخالق العظيم واجب'.
2
Extract the conditional premise
The author says: 'if non-existence were the origin, it would be impossible for non-existence to transform into existence.' This is the conditional (if-then) statement.
Note the key phrase: 'لو كان هو الأصل، لاستحال أن يتحول العدم بنفسه إلى وجود'.
3
Identify the exception
The argument assumes that existence does exist. This acts as the denial of the consequent (since the consequent says existence from non-existence is impossible, but existence is real).
The exception is implicit: existence is a fact, so the consequent is false.
4
Determine the logical form
This is a valid modus tollens: If P then Q. Not Q. Therefore not P. Here P = 'non-existence is the origin', Q = 'existence from non-existence is impossible'. Since existence exists (not Q), non-existence is not the origin. Hence the Creator must exist necessarily.
Modus tollens is a standard deductive form.

This passage presents a rational proof for the necessity of the Creator's existence and the impossibility of His non-existence. Therefore, existence must originate from a Necessary Existent (the Creator). This is a brief syllogism: if non-existence were the origin, change to existence is impossible; but existence exists; therefore non-existence is not the origin; therefore there is a necessary Creator.

Core Idea
Circular reasoning makes a thing depend on itself, which is impossible.

The concept of circular reasoning (al-dawr) is a fundamental logical fallacy. In this lesson, we explore its definition through vivid examples from the natural world—the hen and egg, and the water cycle. These examples demonstrate why any chain that returns to its starting point is rejected by sound reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
Read the passage's description: 'If a thing revolves upon itself after passing through another element, it is obviously impossible.' The key is that the thing's existence is made to depend on itself.
2
Examine the hen-egg example
The passage says the first egg is a cause for the first hen, and the first hen is a cause for the first egg. Trace the dependence.
3
Generalize the pattern
Understand that any circle, regardless of length, ultimately makes the first thing cause itself, which is impossible because it would require the thing to exist before it exists.
This impossibility is called muhal bil-dhat (impossible in itself).

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies circular reasoning (al-dawr) as a logical fallacy. It opens with two concrete examples: the hen-egg cycle and the water-vapor-cloud cycle. In each, the author shows that the alleged causal chain eventually makes a thing depend on itself, which is impossible because it requires a thing to exist before it exists in order to cause itself. The author then distinguishes between explicit circularity (al-dawr al-sarih), where the circle involves only one intermediary or no intermediary, and implicit circularity (al-dawr al-mudmar), where there are two or more intermediaries. This distinction warns the reader not to be fooled by a longer chain, which might disguise the circularity. Next, the text introduces a crucial division: sequential circularity (al-dawr al-sabqi) versus co-existent circularity (al-dawr al-ma'i). Sequential circularity involves temporal or causal priority and is impossible. Co-existent circularity is merely conceptual, like the mutual dependence of father and son, or greater and smaller; these are defined in relation to each other but do not involve causal priority, so they are permissible. The examples given—Abu Talib and 'Ali, and the two mosques—illustrate this. The passage thus teaches that circular reasoning is only a fallacy when it is sequential, not when it is merely a conceptual correlation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A long chain of intermediaries can hide circularity—but it's still invalid.

Circular reasoning can be obvious, like the hen-egg cycle, or hidden behind multiple steps, as in the water-vapor-cloud cycle. This lesson teaches you to see through the complexity and recognize the fallacy regardless of how many intermediaries are involved.

2
Count intermediaries
Explicit (sarih) circularity has 0 or 1 intermediary; implicit (mudmar) has 2 or more.
3
Recognize the trick
The passage warns that multiplicity of intermediaries should not fool us; the circularity is still invalid.
The type depends on the number of intermediaries between the thing and itself.

Defines circular reasoning, distinguishes explicit/implicit and sequential/co-existent types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sequential circularity (dawr sabqi), which is impossible, and co-existent circularity (dawr ma'i), which is permissible.

Core Idea
Not all circularity is fallacious: correlatives like father and son are naturally interdependent.

This lesson explores the concept of co-existent circularity (al-dawr al-ma'i) and shows why it is logically acceptable.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish causal from conceptual dependence
Sequential circularity (sabqi) involves a thing's existence depending on itself via a causal chain. Co-existent circularity (ma'i) involves two things that are defined relative to each other.
2
Analyze the father-son example
Abu Talib being a father depends on Ali being a son, and vice versa. This is simultaneous, not causal.
3
Apply to other correlatives
Greater and smaller are also co-existent; one cannot be conceived without the other.
Co-existent circularity is sometimes called 'ada i'tibari (conventional correlation).

Defining Infinite Regress and Its Impossibility
Defines infinite regress and states its impossibility in actual existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define infinite regress (al-tasalsul) and explain why it is impossible for actual existents.

Core Idea
Without a first cause, nothing would ever exist – that is why infinite regress is impossible.

This passage from Dawabit al-Ma'rifa defines the concept of infinite regress (al-tasalsul) and states its logical impossibility. Understanding this concept is crucial for arguments for the existence of a necessary being (Allah) in Islamic theology and philosophy. Let us break down the definition and the reasoning behind the impossibility claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of tasalsul
Read the Arabic text carefully. The passage defines tasalsul as a chain where the existence of a contingent being depends on an efficient cause, that cause on another, and so on without end. This is the definition you need to internalize.
The key phrase is 'دون نهاية' (without end).
2
Break down the causal chain
Notice that each link in the chain is a 'mumkin' (contingent being) that requires a cause. Each cause is itself contingent and therefore requires its own cause. This creates a dependency structure that cannot terminate.
The term 'علة مؤثرة' (efficient cause) is used, emphasizing actual production.
3
Recognize the impossibility claim
The author states that an infinite regress among actual existents is impossible by reason. Locate the phrase 'مستحيل عقلاً' (impossible by reason). This is not just physical impossibility but logical impossibility.
The impossibility applies specifically to actual existents (موجودة فعلاً), not to conceptual or mathematical series.
4
Extrapolate the reasoning
Think through why this is impossible: if every cause requires a prior cause, and there is no first cause, then the chain can never get started. No effect would ever be produced because each cause is waiting for its own cause. Therefore, the existence of any actual thing would be prevented.
This reasoning is foundational for the cosmological argument in kalam.
5

Connect this to the proof for God's existence. The impossibility of infinite regress implies that there must be a first cause that is not itself contingent – a necessary being (واجب الوجود). This is the core of the contingency argument.
In kalam texts, this is often called 'burhan al-imkan' or 'burhan al-huduth'.

Author classifies circular reasoning into explicit, implicit, sequential, and concurrent types.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all circular reasoning is invalid; concurrent circularity is permissible in correlatives.

This passage from Dawabit al-Ma'rifa teaches us to recognize circular reasoning in its various forms. Some are obviously invalid, but others, like conceptual mutual dependence, are acceptable. Understanding these distinctions helps us avoid fallacious arguments while appreciating legitimate conceptual interdependence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the warning about multiplicity of intermediaries
The author warns that even if many intermediaries are involved, the intellect must not be tricked—any form of self-dependence is rejected. This sets the stage for the classification.
This warning applies to both explicit and implicit circularity.
2

Explicit circularity occurs when a thing depends directly on itself without any intermediary, e.g., 'the universe creates itself' or the chicken-and-egg problem with a single element. Recognize it as immediate self-reference.

The multiplicity of steps can obscure the circularity, but the author warns against this deception.

The author clarifies that both explicit and implicit types are instances of sequential circularity, which is impossible by intellect. Sequential means there is a temporal or logical priority that loops back.
This is the invalid kind that is universally rejected.
5
Distinguish concurrent circularity (al-dawr al-ma‘i) as permissible
Concurrent circularity involves mutual conceptual dependence without temporal priority, like fatherhood and sonship, or greater and smaller. These concepts are understood together; existence does not depend in a sequential way. Recognize this as acceptable.
This type is often encountered in relational concepts and is not a logical fallacy.

Distinguishing Mahiyya and Huwiyya: Essences of Universal and Particular
Author defines mahiyya and huwiyya as essences of universal and particular with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify mahiyya and huwiyya in given examples and explain why mahiyya is shareable while huwiyya is unique.

Core Idea
Mahiyya is the shared essence of a universal; huwiyya is the unique essence of a particular.

We often speak of the 'essence' of a thing, but in Islamic logic this concept splits into two distinct terms: mahiyya for universals and huwiyya for particulars. The passage before you lays this out with concrete examples from genus, species, and individual. Understanding this distinction is key to precise definitions and sound reasoning in every Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Identify universals and particulars in the examples
The author gives three examples: a genus (unnamed but implied as 'animal' from context), species ('human'), and a particular individual ('Khalid'). Notice that 'human' applies to many individuals, while 'Khalid' refers uniquely.
2
Extract the constitutive elements (essence)
For 'human', the constitutive elements are 'animal' (genus) and 'rational' (differentia). For 'Khalid', his essence is being a human with a specific determination (tacayyun) that no other person shares. These elements are what make the thing what it is.
3
Separate accidental attributes
The passage lists accidents for Khalid: knowledge, anger, hair color, etc. These do not belong to his essence because he would still be Khalid without them. Contrast this with the constitutive elements: without rationality, 'human' would change its essence.
4
Apply the terminology: mahiyya vs huwiyya
The author states that the essence of a universal is called mahiyya, and the essence of a particular is called huwiyya. So 'rational animal' is the mahiyya of human, while what makes Khalid uniquely himself is his huwiyya.
5
Explain shareability
Mahiyya is shareable because many individuals fall under the same universal (e.g., all humans share the mahiyya 'rational animal'). Huwiyya is unique; each particular has its own huwiyya that distinguishes it from all others. This is why the passage says 'the distinctness of persons in external existence is by their huwiyyas.'

Author defines efficient cause and material cause.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Every contingent event needs both a maker and raw matter.

This lesson unpacks the first and third definitions from the passage: the efficient cause (al-ʿilla al-fāʿila) and the material cause (al-ʿilla al-mādiyya). Understanding these two causes is essential for analyzing how things come into existence according to Islamic rational theology. Identify the efficient cause

Identify the material cause

This example is from the Quran (32:7): 'He began the creation of man from clay.'
4
Distinguish the two causes
The efficient cause is the doer, the material cause is the stuff. Without the efficient cause, nothing happens; without the material cause, many physical things cannot exist.
In kalam, the material cause is sometimes omitted for mental or spiritual entities.

Commentary
This passage defines the four causes (efficient, final, material, formal) that are necessary for the coming into existence of any originated thing. The author begins with the efficient cause: every originated thing requires an agent that brings it from non-existence to existence. This is the 'illa fa'ila. Next, if the agent is wise and has will, it acts for a purpose; this purpose is the final cause ('illa gha'iya), which exists first as a concept in the mind and then as the realized goal. The author carefully distinguishes the two aspects of the final cause: the motivating idea before action and the actualized capability after action. Then the material cause ('illa maddiya): the originated thing needs matter or basic materials to exist. The author then provides a single example—the desire to build an air/space vehicle to shorten distances. The desire to travel is the final cause, the materials (metals, fuel, etc.) are the material cause, and the specific design and structure of the vehicle is the formal cause. The efficient cause is implied as the human intellect and industry that bring the vehicle into existence. The passage emphasizes that the final cause is both the first in thought and the last in actuality.

Understanding Hyle and Form: Matter and Shape in Islamic Philosophy
Author defines hyle and form as philosophical terms with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'hyle' and 'form' as used in Islamic philosophy and explain their relationship using the example of molten wax.

Core Idea
Hyle is the formless substratum; form is the shape that never exists without hyle.

In Islamic philosophy, the concepts of hyle (matter) and form are fundamental to understanding the composition of physical substances. This passage defines these two terms clearly and illustrates their relationship with a simple example. By mastering this distinction, you will be able to analyse how philosophers conceive of material objects.

1
Identify the two terms defined
The passage defines 'hyle' (الهيولى) and 'form' (الصورة). Note that they are always paired in philosophical discussions.

Hyle is the substance stripped of all corporeal form, but it never exists in reality without some form. It is the receptive substratum.
The author emphasizes that hyle is not found alone in the actual world.
3
Understand the definition of form
Form is the shape that inheres in hyle; it can be mentally isolated but never exists alone in reality. Form is classified as an accident (arad).
Form is not a self-subsisting entity.

Molten wax without shape is hyle; when shaped into a bird, tree, etc., that shape is the form. The wax itself is the underlying matter.
This example makes the abstract concepts concrete.
5
Reflect on mutual dependency
Hyle always has form; form always requires hyle. Together they constitute a body (jism). The philosopher isolates them mentally, but they are inseparable in reality.
This dependency is key to understanding physical objects.

The two terms 'hyle' and 'form' are used in philosophical books for several meanings, the most important of which are the following:
1. 'Hyle': the substance or essence stripped of any corporeal form, which receives different corporeal forms in imagination or mental conception, as well as in reality.

Essential Properties: From General to Specific
Author states the fifth rule about essential properties in broader and narrower categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that what is essential in the more general is essential in the more specific, and identify cases where the reverse does not hold.

Core Idea
Essential properties of the wider category always apply to the narrower, but not vice versa.

One of the most fundamental rules in definition and classification is that the essential properties (dhati) of a broader category carry over to any narrower category under it. For example, if humanity is a type of animal, then everything essential to animal—like being a living, sensing body—is also essential to human. But human has its own unique essential properties, like rationality, that do not belong to animal in general. This rule, with its extensions to pillars and conditions, is a cornerstone of valid logical reasoning in Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify broader and narrower categories
Find the pair of categories where one is more general (a'amm) and the other more specific (akhass). For example, animal (hayawan) is broader than human (insan).
2
List essential properties of the broader
Determine what is essential (dhati) to the broader category. For animal, essential properties include being a living body that moves by will, senses, and grows.
These properties constitute the essence of animal.
3
Check if those properties apply to the narrower
Since human is a type of animal, every essential property of animal must also be essential to human. Verify: humans are living bodies that move, sense, and grow—true.
This confirms the rule.
4
Test the reverse: properties of the narrower
Take an essential property of the narrower category, e.g., rationality (natiqiyya) for human. Is it essential to animal in general? No, because not all animals are rational.
This shows the reverse does not hold.

Apply corollaries to pillars and conditions
Identify a pillar (rukn) or condition (shart) for the broader category, e.g., intention (niyya) is a condition for all acts of worship. It is therefore a condition for specific acts like prayer. But specific conditions like facing the qibla are not conditions for all worship.

But scholars of adab al-bahth (the etiquette of research and debate) added another classification that considers both the nature of the definiendum and the listener's state. This lesson unpacks their fourfold division and shows how the author refines it into simpler binary categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types
Read the passage and list the four types of definition given by the adab al-bahth scholars: al-ta'rif al-haqiqi, al-ismi, al-lafzi, and al-tanbihi. Note the defining feature of each.
Focus on the Arabic terms and their English equivalents.
2
Contrast haqiqi and ismi
These two are distinguished by whether the quiddity (mahiyya) has external individuals. Haqiqi defines real essences; ismi defines imagined ones (even if they exist in reality, but no individuals are known). See the phoenix example.
This distinction is based on the thing itself, not the listener.
3
Analyze lafzi and tanbihi
Lafzi is purely verbal: replacing an unfamiliar word with a familiar synonym. Tanbihi reminds the listener of something already known but forgotten. Note that tanbihi considers the listener's state, unlike the first three.
The author says tanbihi differs from the others in taking the addressee's condition into account.

The author proposes two binary divisions: (1) ta'limi (instructive) vs tanbihi (reminder) based on whether the definition gives new knowledge or reminds; (2) haqiqi vs ismi based on existence in reality. See how these simplify the fourfold scheme.
The author claims these are in harmony with sound division (salamat al-taqsim).

Clarifying the Intended Meaning in Definitions
Author explains the first aspect of clarifying intended meaning in definitions with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how clarifying the intended meaning (taḥrīr al-murād) of the definiendum can resolve objections to a definition without changing its wording, using examples from Islamic legal usage.

Core Idea
A definition may be valid in one sense but rejected in another; clarifying intent saves the definition.

When a definition is challenged, the immediate response is often to modify it. But the Islamic logical tradition teaches a more refined strategy: clarify what you meant by the term being defined. This lesson unpacks that technique through a concrete example from the science of munazara (dialectics).

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and the objection
In the example, the definition is 'dābbah is every animal that walks on four legs'. The objection is that it excludes creeping animals, i.e., it is not comprehensive (ghayr jāmiʿ).
2
Determine the definer's intended scope
The definer clarifies that by 'dābbah' he means the common usage (ʿurf), which in his context refers only to quadrupeds, not the broader linguistic sense that includes all creeping animals.
The intended scope matters: it restricts the definition to a specific domain.
3
Assess how the clarification resolves the objection
The objection was based on a linguistic interpretation that includes all creeping animals. By restricting to common usage, the definition becomes comprehensive within that intended scope, thus repelling the objection.
This shows the objection is invalid once the intended meaning is made explicit.
4
Reflect on circumstances where this tactic is appropriate
This tactic works when the definiendum has multiple possible meanings (e.g., urfi vs. lughawi). It is not appropriate if the definition is inherently flawed regardless of intended meaning.

Core Idea
Clarify a term, not the whole definition.

When a definition is attacked, the definer's first move is not always to rewrite it. Sometimes the objection comes from a misunderstanding of a specific term. In this lesson, you will learn how to spot such objections and defend a definition by clarifying what you really meant by that term. We will use the classical example of defining 'animal' as 'a growing, sensing, thinking body'.

Identify the objector's interpretation

Clarify the intended meaning

4
Verify that the objection is resolved
Check that with the clarified meaning, the definition now covers all intended cases and the objection no longer stands.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A definition's validity is relative to its underlying scholarly school.

This lesson teaches a key dialectical move in Islamic scholarship: when a definition is challenged, the definer can rebut by specifying the doctrinal framework (madhab 'ilmi) upon which it rests. Resolving Circularity via Separation of Aspect
Author explains how to block circularity objection via separation of aspect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and construct a response to a circularity objection in a definition by distinguishing between the conventional and technical senses of a term.

Core Idea
Separation of aspect (ikh tilāf al-jiha) breaks the cycle of circularity in definitions.

A common objection to definitions is that they are circular, where the definiendum and definiens depend on each other. This passage shows how the defender can respond by showing that the same term is used in different senses (conventional vs. philosophical), so the dependence is not in the same respect. This is a classic dialectical move in Islamic logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under attack
Locate the definiendum and definiens in the passage. In the example, 'ilm' is defined as 'idrāk al-ma'lūm'.
The definiendum is what is being defined; the definiens is the expression used to define it.
2
Extract the opponent's circularity objection
The objector argues that the definiens contains a term ('ma'lūm') which itself depends on the definiendum ('ilm'), creating a cycle. Understand why this is considered logically impossible (muhāl).
Circularity of the sabqī (precedential) type is invalid because it leads to infinite regress.

Locate the defender's response: separation of aspect
The defender (al-māni') claims that 'ma'lūm' in the definiens is used in a conventional sense (every fixed mental form), not in the philosophical sense that depends on 'ilm'. This makes the aspect (jiha) different, so the cycle is broken.
The key phrase is 'al-jiha munfakka' – the aspect is separate.
4

Check that the conventional meaning of 'ma'lūm' is indeed independent of the philosophical definition of 'ilm'. In common usage, 'known' simply means whatever has a mental form, regardless of how knowledge is technically defined.
If the aspects overlap, the response fails. The defender must demonstrate real linguistic convention.

Responding to Objections Against Definitions
Author classifies four objections and gives responses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to respond to four common objections against a definition using the dialectical strategies of al-mani' (the defender).

Core Idea
A flawed wording does not always break a definition: dialectical defenses can salvage it.

This passage from Dawabit al-Marifa teaches how to defend a definition when it is attacked for not meeting 'good conditions.' The author classifies four types of wording defects and provides ready-made replies. By learning these, you will not only spot weak objections but also master the art of protecting a definition in debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection type
Listen to the objector's claim and determine which of the four categories it falls under: incorrect wording, metaphorical without qarina, ambiguous/homonymous, or strange wording.
The objector (al-mustadil) will point to a specific word in the definition.
2
Recall the defense strategies for that category

This is a boundary case; the passage signals it will be treated later.

Objections to Implicit Claims in Definitions
Explains objections targeting implicit propositions in definitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify implicit claims in a definition and construct a valid objection or response in a dialectical exchange.

Core Idea
The real debate in definitions often hides beneath explicit wording, in silent claims about completeness and essentiality.

When a Muslim scholar proposes a definition, his words carry hidden presuppositions about its completeness, essentiality, or uniqueness. This lesson unpacks how objections may target those implicit claims, and what dialectical rules govern such exchanges. Understanding this will sharpen your ability to dissect definitions in usul al-fiqh, kalam, and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit definition
Locate the definiendum and the wording of the definition given by the definer. For example, 'al-qiyas is the application of the ruling of the original case to a new case based on a shared effective cause.'
2
Extract the implicit claims
Determine what the definition silently asserts: that it is a complete real definition (hadd tamm), that its parts are essential (dhātī), that it is uniquely correct, etc. These are not stated but are logically entailed.
Common implicit claims: 'This definition covers all instances', 'It excludes all non-instances', 'Its components are genus and differentia'.
3
Construct an objection with evidence
The objector must provide evidence (dalīl) to refute one or more implicit claims. For instance, show that the definition fails to include a necessary condition, or that it includes a non-essential feature.
The objection is only accepted if accompanied by proof.
4
Evaluate the definer's response
The definer may respond by denying (manʿ) the objector's evidence, i.e., rejecting its validity without necessarily supplying counter-evidence. This is a legitimate dialectical move.
The definer does not need to prove his denial, but may choose to.

Before challenging a definition, first check its source; then check its wording.

In Islamic dialectics (munazara), debating definitions follows a strict order. This passage outlines the first two steps: verifying the transmission of the definition, and then checking for ambiguous or misleading wording. Understanding this order ensures that objections are relevant and the debate proceeds methodically.

Guided Path
1
Determine the presenter's stance
Ask: Is the presenter transmitting the definition from an authority without committing to its correctness, or is he originating it or committing to its correctness? This decides the valid grounds for objection.
If transmitted without commitment, the only valid objection is on the authenticity of the transmission.
2
Check for ambiguous or misleading wording
If the presenter originates or commits to the definition, examine the wording for ambiguous (ghāmiḍ) or misleading (mūhim) terms. Such terms allow the objector to request clarification.
The presenter must then explain the intended meaning.
3
Proceed to next step if no issues
If the wording is clear and unambiguous, the debate moves to the next stage (not detailed in this passage).

First Stage: Checking Validity Conditions of a Definition
Explains the first stage of examining a definition in disputation: checking the conditions of validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a definition meets the basic conditions of validity (shurūṭ al-ṣiḥḥa) and formulate an objection when it fails.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be inclusive and exclusive, or it fails the first test.

Before any deeper examination, a definition must pass a basic check: Does it include all instances of the definiendum and exclude everything else? If it fails here, the disputation can end immediately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under scrutiny
Locate the definition proposed in the disputation and isolate its components (genus, differentia, or property).
2
Check inclusivity (jāmiʿ)
Does the definition cover every individual of the definiendum? Try to find a counterexample that should be included but is not.
If it fails, the definition is غير جامع.
3
Check exclusivity (māniʿ)
Does the definition exclude everything outside the definiendum? Try to find something that is not part of the definiendum but satisfies the definition.
If it fails, the definition is غير مانع.
4
Check other conditions
Does the definition imply an impossibility or is it more obscure than the definiendum? These also render the definition invalid.
The passage mentions يستلزم المحال and أخفى من المعرَّف.
5
Determine objection or acceptance
If any condition is violated, the objector can raise an objection; otherwise, proceed to the next stage.
The proponent must respond with pre-established answers.

Second Stage: Examining Implicit Claims in a Definition
Explains the second stage: examining implicit claims in a definition and how to contest them.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify implicit claims in a definition (e.g., being a real definition or complete description) and challenge them in a disputation.

Core Idea
A definition often carries hidden claims about its type that can be contested with evidence.

After a definition passes the basic validity check, the next step is to examine whether it contains implicit claims about its own nature—such as being a real definition (ḥaqīqī) or a complete description (rasm tāmm). The objector can challenge these claims and must provide proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify implicit claims in the definition
Read the definition carefully. Does the proponent claim it is a real definition (حقيقي), a complete limit (حد تام), or a complete description (رسم تام)? Look for explicit or implicit assertions.
These claims are not always stated outright; they may be implied by the structure or context.
2
Formulate an objection to the claim
If the definition is claimed to be a real definition, argue that it does not express the essence (e.g., it uses a property instead of differentia). For a complete limit, check if both genus and differentia are proximate.
The objector bears the burden of proof (يقيم الدليل).
3
Provide evidence for the objection
Construct a counterexample or show that the definition fails the criteria for that type. For instance, show that the differentia is not specific or the genus is not proximate.
The disputation proceeds according to rules for assents (تصديقات).
4
Proponent's response
The proponent may defend the claim by refining the definition or providing additional justification, following the established rules of disputation.

Third Stage: Evaluating the Conditions of Goodness of a Definition
Explains the third stage: checking if the definition fulfills the conditions of goodness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify violations of the conditions of goodness (shurūṭ ḥasana) in a definition and use them to object in a disputation.

Core Idea
Even a logically valid definition can be rejected if it fails the rhetorical and stylistic conditions of goodness.

The final stage examines whether the definition meets the conditions of goodness (شروط حسنة)—secondary criteria that make a definition effective in communication and disputation. If it fails, the objector can still object, even if the definition is logically sound.

Guided Path
1
Understand the conditions of goodness
These include clarity, brevity, avoiding ambiguity, using well-known terms, and not being too technical or obscure. They are not strictly logical but enhance dialectical efficacy.
The passage does not enumerate them, but they are known from the broader tradition.
2
Check the definition against these conditions
Examine if the definition is easily understandable, concise, and free from unnecessary complexity. For instance, does it use unfamiliar jargon that hinders communication?
Example: A definition using rare Arabic terms may violate the condition of clarity.
3
Formulate an objection based on a violation
If the definition is overly long, ambiguous, or uses technical terms where simpler ones exist, the objector can point out the violation of conditions of goodness.
The objector does not need to prove it fails the earlier stages; this is a separate aspect.
4
Proponent's response
The proponent may argue that the definition's style is acceptable or that the conditions are not mandatory, but the objector's point can still weaken the definition's acceptance.
The proponent answers with pre-established responses.

B) Yes, if it violates conditions of goodness.
C) Only if the objector does not like it.
D) No, because the third stage only applies if earlier stages failed.

Key Takeaway
A definition can be logically perfect but still rejected if it fails the dialectical standards of goodness.
