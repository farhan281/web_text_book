The Three Sources of Mental Perceptions
Author enumerates the three sources of mental perceptions and their role as raw material for inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the three sources of mental perceptions as described by the author, and explain how they serve as raw material for inference.

Core Idea
Our minds start blank; all knowledge arrives through senses, feelings, or reports.

Before we can use logic to deduce new knowledge, we need to understand where our basic perceptions come from. In this lesson, the author introduces three pathways that feed the mind with raw data. Recognising these sources helps us evaluate the reliability of our starting points.

Guided Path
1
Identify the blank slate assertion
Notice the author's opening: the mind at birth is like a white page. This sets up the need for external sources of knowledge.
This concept resembles the tabula rasa found in both Islamic and Western thought.
2
List the three sources
The author enumerates exactly three: (1) external senses, (2) internal emotional feelings, (3) reports from others. Memorise them in order.
Each source has its own reliability conditions mentioned later.
3
Evaluate each source's reliability
For senses, the author notes they can be sound or defective. For feelings, they are subjective but can be objective if shared. For reports, they may be true or false.
This awareness is crucial for assessing the validity of premises in arguments.
4
Connect to inferential work
The author calls these perceptions 'raw material' for intellectual deduction. Logic will use these as premises to produce new conclusions.
Without these raw materials, deduction has no starting point.

المدركات الذهنية وأقسامها
حينما نولد تكون صفحة أذهاننا بيضاء خالية من أيّة مسجّلات علمية أو إدراكية، ومنذ ولادتنا تبدأ أشرطة أذهاننا بتسجيل ما يرد إليها من مدركات، والطرق التي تنقل المدركات إلى أذهاننا ثلاثة :
الطريق الأول : حواسنا الخمسُ الظاهرة، فهذه تنقل إلى أذهاننا ما تستطيع الإحساس به من صور العالم الخارجي عنا، وقد تكون الحواس سليمة الإحساس فتنقل نقلاً صحيحاً، وقد تكون عليلة أو مختلة فتنقل نقلاً خاطئاً أو مشوّهاً .
الطريق الثاني : مشاعرنا الوجدانية الداخلية، وهذه المشاعر تنقل إلى أذهاننا ما نحسّ به في داخلنا من وجدانيات، كاللذة والألم، والحب والكراهية، والحقد والحسد، والخوف والطمع، وما أشبه ذلك، وأحكامنا في هذا المجال أحكام ذاتية بحتة، وقد تكون موضوعية حينما تتوافق مع مشاعر الآخرين توافقاً تاماً .
الطريق الثالث : ما ينقل إلينا من أخبار تصف لنا ما توصل إليه المخبرون من معارف، أو ما أرادوا أن يصوّروه لنا سواء أكان موافقاً للواقع أو مخالفاً له .
وهذه المدركات التي ترد إلى أذهاننا من هذه الطرق تكون بمثابة المواد الخام للأعمال الفكرية الاستنتاجية، إذ يعمل الفكر على استنتاج مدركات جديدة لم ترد إليه عن أي من الطرق الثلاثة الآنفة الذكر، منها الاستنتاجات
(1) Mental perceptions and their divisions. When we are born, the page of our minds is white, devoid of any scientific or perceptual recordings. From our birth, the tapes of our minds begin recording what reaches them of perceptions. The ways that convey perceptions to our minds are three: The first way: our five external senses. These convey to our minds what they can sense of images of the external world around us. The senses may be sound in sensation, so they convey correctly, or they may be defective or impaired, so they convey incorrectly or distortedly. The second way: our internal emotional feelings. These feelings convey to our minds what we feel inside us of emotions, such as pleasure and pain, love and hatred, malice and envy, fear and greed, and the like. Our judgments in this area are purely subjective, and may be objective when they fully coincide with the feelings of others. The third way: what is conveyed to us of reports that describe to us what the reporters have arrived at of knowledge, or what they intended to depict for us, whether it conforms to reality or contradicts it. These perceptions that reach our minds through these ways serve as raw material for intellectual inferential work, as the intellect works to deduce new perceptions that have not reached it through any of the aforementioned three ways, including deductions.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological introduction, classifying the origins of mental perceptions. The author begins by asserting that the mind at birth is a blank slate (tabula rasa), a concept familiar in Islamic thought (comparable to Ibn Sina's notion of the rational soul as a clean tablet). Then the three pathways are enumerated. First, the five external senses: they convey sensory images of the external world, but can be unreliable if defective. Second, internal emotional feelings: subjective inner experiences like pleasure, pain, love, etc. These are largely subjective, though they can be objective if shared. Third, reports from others: transmitted knowledge, which may be true or false. The author concludes by stating that these perceptions become the raw material (al-mawād al-khām) for intellectual inferential work. The mind then deduces new perceptions not directly obtained from the three routes. This sets the stage for logic as the tool for valid deduction. The passage emphasizes the empirical and rational foundations of knowledge, a necessary preface to a logic textbook.

The Definition of a Proposition: Subject, Predicate, and Relation
Definition of proposition with its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three components of any logical proposition (qadiyya) and distinguish the subject from the predicate.

Core Idea
Every proposition is a triple: subject, predicate, and an affirmative or negative relation.

In logic, a proposition is not just any sentence—it must consist of two terms linked by a relation. This lesson unpacks the foundational definition given in the source passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
From the passage: 'Any two singular terms between which a positive or negative relation occurs, one of them is subject and the other is predicate, and their combination with the relation between them is a proposition.' List the components.
The relation can be positive or negative.
2
Understand the role of each component
The subject is what is spoken about; the predicate is what is said about the subject; the relation connects them and indicates affirmation or negation.
3
Apply the definition to an example
Take the proposition 'Muhammad is the Seal of the Messengers'. Identify the subject (Muhammad), predicate (Seal of the Messengers), and relation (affirmative).
Relation is indicated by inflection and structure, not necessarily a word.
4
Check the definition's completeness
Does the definition cover both affirmative and negative propositions? Yes, as it explicitly includes 'positive or negative'.

الموضوع والمحمول والنسبة بينهما
كل مفردين تجري بينهما نسبة موجبة أو سالبة فأحدهما موضوع والآخر محمول، ومجموعهما مع النسبة بينهما قضية.
والموضوع في تراكيب الجمل العربية يأتي مبتدأ أو نحوه إذا كانت الجملة جملة اسمية، ويأتي فاعلاً أو نحوه إذا كانت الجملة جملة فعلية، وأما المحمول فهو الركن الثاني من ركني الإسناد، وهو الخبر أو ما يقوم مقامه في الجملة الاسمية، والفعل أو ما يقوم مقامه في الجملة الفعلية، وأما النسبة بين الموضوع والمحمول فتدل عليها حركة الإعراب وهيئة تركيب الجملة، وقد يستعمل عند المناطقة للدلالة على النسبة ضمير الفصل وهو رابط غير زماني، أو فعل الكون وهو رابط زماني، كقولنا:
الثلج هو ماء متجمد، والإنسان كان نطفة من مني يمنى.
والأصل في الجملة أنها موجبة ولو لم يوجد فيها كلمة تدل على الإيجاب، فإذا استعمل فيها ما يدل على الإيجاب فهو تأكيد للمعنى الإيجابي الذي تحمله، فإذا أريد فيها معنى السلب فلا بد من استعمال أداة من أدوات السلب (أي النفي) للدلالة عليه، مثل: لا، ليس، غير، ما، لن، ونحوها.
أمثلة:
أ - الثلج ماء متجمد، هذا الكلام (قضية) وهي جملة اسمية، الموضوع فيها هو (الثلج)، وهو مبتدأ، والمحمول فيها (ماء متجمد) وهو خبر، والنسبة الإيجابية بينهما قد دلت عليها حركة الإعراب وهي الرفع في الخبر، وهيئة تركيب الجملة .
ب - الثلج ليس حاراً ، (قضية) وهي جملة اسمية أيضاً، كسابقتها، إلا أن النسبة فيها سالبة، والأداة التي دلت على السلب هي كلمة : (ليس).

د ـ لم تشرق الشمس، (قضية) وهي جملة فعلية، الموضوع فيها : (الشمس) وهو في الجملة فاعل، والمحمول فيها (تشرق) وهو فعل، إلا أن النسبة فيها سالبة، والأداة التي دلت على السلب هي كلمة (لم).

Subject, Predicate, and the Relation between Them.
Any two singular terms between which a positive or negative relation occurs, one of them is subject and the other is predicate, and their combination with the relation between them is a proposition.
The subject in the structures of Arabic sentences comes as a mubtada' or its equivalent if the sentence is nominal, and it comes as a fa'il or its equivalent if the sentence is verbal. As for the predicate, it is the second pillar of the two pillars of predication; it is the khabar or what takes its place in the nominal sentence, and the verb or what takes its place in the verbal sentence. As for the relation between the subject and predicate, it is indicated by the inflectional movement and the structure of the sentence. Among the logicians, to indicate the relation, they sometimes use the separate pronoun (damir al-fasl), which is a non-temporal copula, or the verb of existence (kana), which is a temporal copula, as in our saying:
'Snow is frozen water' and 'Man was a drop of emitted semen.'
The default in the sentence is that it is affirmative even if there is no word indicating affirmation; if a word indicating affirmation is used in it, it is a confirmation of the affirmative meaning it carries. If a meaning of negation is intended in it, then an instrument of negation (i.e. negation) must be used to indicate it, such as: la, laysa, ghayr, ma, lan, and the like.
Examples:
A - 'Snow is frozen water.' This speech is a proposition, and it is a nominal sentence; the subject in it is 'snow' which is a mubtada', and the predicate in it is 'frozen water' which is a khabar, and the affirmative relation between them is indicated by the inflectional movement, which is the raf' in the khabar, and the structure of the sentence.
B - 'Snow is not hot.' It is a proposition, also a nominal sentence like the previous one, except that the relation in it is negative, and the instrument that indicated negation is the word 'laysa'.
C - 'The sun rose.' It is a proposition, and it is a verbal sentence; the subject in it is 'the sun' which is in the sentence a fa'il, and the predicate in it is 'rose' which is a verb, and the affirmative relation between them is indicated by the inflectional movement, which is the raf' in the fa'il, and the structure of the sentence.
D - 'The sun did not rise.' It is a proposition, and it is a verbal sentence; the subject in it is 'the sun' which is in the sentence a fa'il, and the predicate in it is 'rose' which is a verb, except that the relation in it is negative, and the instrument that indicated negation is the word 'lam'.
And each of the two singular terms may be suitable to be the subject of the proposition or its predicate. Then the determination of subject and predicate in the structure of the proposition reverts to the mental state that requires that one of them specifically be the subject and the other be the predicate.
For example, in the following two singular terms: 'Muhammad' and 'Seal of the Messengers', each of them is suitable to be subject and to be predicate in a general way, but the determination of subject and predicate from them follows what the situation requires. So if the situation requires establishing that Muhammad is described as the Seal of the Messengers, then the first of them should be made subject - i.e. mubtada' in the sentence - and the second made predicate - i.e. khabar in the sentence - so we say: 'Muhammad is the Seal of the Messengers.' And if the situation requires establishing that the Seal of the Messengers is Muhammad, no other, then 'Seal of the Messengers' should be made subject - i.e. mubtada' - and 'Muhammad' made predicate - i.e. khabar - so we say: 'The Seal of the Messengers is Muhammad.'
The proposition in this discipline is equivalent to the complete informative sentence according to linguists.

Commentary
This passage defines the essential components of a logical proposition (qadiyya). The author begins by stating that whenever two singular terms (mufradayn) are linked by a positive or negative relation (nisba), one becomes the subject (mawdu') and the other the predicate (mahmul), and the combination of the two terms plus the relation constitutes the proposition. This is a clear foundational definition.

Next, the author maps these logical components onto Arabic grammar: in a nominal sentence, the logical subject corresponds to the mubtada' (or its equivalent), and the predicate corresponds to the khabar. In a verbal sentence, the logical subject is the fa'il (or its equivalent), and the predicate is the verb (or its equivalent). The relation between subject and predicate is indicated by the inflectional movement (harakat al-i'rab) and the sentence structure (hay'at tarkib al-jumla). Moreover, the author notes that logicians may use a 'separate pronoun' (damir al-fasl) as a non-temporal copula, as in 'al-thalju huwa ma'un mutajammid' (Snow is frozen water), or the verb 'kana' as a temporal copula, as in 'al-insanu kana nutfatan' (Man was a drop).

Then the author explains the default affirmative nature of sentences: a sentence is considered affirmative even without an explicit affirmatory word; any such word only confirms the affirmation. To express negation, a negation tool must be used, such as 'la', 'laysa', 'ghayr', 'ma', 'lan', etc.

Four examples are given to illustrate:
A) 'Al-thalju ma'un mutajammid' — affirmative nominal sentence, subject 'thalj', predicate 'ma' mutajammid', relation shown by raf' on the khabar.
B) 'Al-thalju laysa harran' — negative nominal sentence, negation by 'laysa'.
C) 'Ashraqat al-shamsu' — affirmative verbal sentence, subject 'shams' (fa'il), predicate 'ashraq' (verb), relation shown by raf' on fa'il.
D) 'Lam tashriq al-shamsu' — negative verbal sentence, negation by 'lam'.

Finally, the passage addresses a nuance: either singular term can potentially serve as subject or predicate depending on the speaker's intention. For example, with 'Muhammad' and 'Khatam al-Mursalin' (Seal of the Messengers), if the intent is to affirm that Muhammad possesses the attribute of being the Seal of the Messengers, we make 'Muhammad' the subject and 'Khatam al-Mursalin' the predicate. If the intent is to affirm that the Seal of the Messengers is Muhammad (and no one else), we reverse them. This shows that logical subject and predicate are not fixed by the words themselves but by the mental state or purpose of the speaker.

The passage concludes by equating the proposition in logic with the complete informative sentence (al-jumlatu al-mufidatu fa'idatan tammatan) in linguistics.

Mapping Logical Subject and Predicate to Arabic Grammar
Mapping logical components to Arabic grammar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical subject and predicate in both nominal and verbal Arabic sentences and explain how the relation is indicated.

Core Idea
Logical subject is mubtada' or fa'il; logical predicate is khabar or fi'l.

The passage bridges logic and Arabic grammar by showing how logical components appear in real sentences. This lesson will train you to spot the mawdu' and mahmul in any sentence.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sentence types
Nominal sentences start with a noun; verbal sentences start with a verb. Example: 'al-thalju ma'un mutajammid' (nominal) vs. 'ashraqat al-shamsu' (verbal).
2
Identify subject and predicate in nominal sentences
In a nominal sentence, the logical subject corresponds to mubtada' (e.g., 'al-thalju') and the logical predicate to khabar (e.g., 'ma'un mutajammid'). The relation is indicated by raf' (nominative case) on the predicate and sentence structure.
3
Identify subject and predicate in verbal sentences
In a verbal sentence, the logical subject is the fa'il (e.g., 'al-shams') and the logical predicate is the verb (e.g., 'ashraqat'). The relation is indicated by raf' on the fa'il and sentence structure.
4
Practice with negation examples
In negative sentences, the same mapping holds but the relation is negative. For 'al-thalju laysa harran', the subject is 'al-thalju', predicate 'harran', relation negative signaled by 'laysa'.

Types of Denotation: Implication and Concomitance
Author defines and exemplifies implication and concomitance denotation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify implicative and concomitant denotation in Arabic discourse and classify concomitant denotation as rational or customary.

Core Idea
A word denotes its full meaning, parts of it, or necessary consequences—each a distinct logical channel.

In this passage, the author unpacks two subtle but powerful ways words convey meaning beyond their literal sense: implicative (tadhamn) and concomitant (iltizam) denotation. These are not mere figures of speech; they are logical structures that underpin interpretation in the Islamic sciences, from usul al-fiqh to tafsir. Understanding them sharpens your ability to extract precise rulings and meanings from sacred texts.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three types of denotation
Recall the earlier division: مطابقة (whole), تضمن (part), التزام (concomitant). In this lesson, focus on the last two. Implicative denotes a part of the full meaning; concomitant denotes something external but necessarily entailed.
The full meaning of 'human' is 'rational animal'. Implicative isolates 'animal' or 'rational'. Concomitant adds 'capacity for writing'—not in the definition but inseparably attached.
2
Analyze implicative examples
Examine each example: the distant figure (animal or rational), the inheritance claim (part of comprehensive knowledge), the doctor's advice (vitamins are part of 'fruits and vegetables'). Notice that the implied information is not stated directly but is a subset of the word's total meaning.
The key test: can the implied meaning be said to be 'within' (ضمن) the original meaning? If yes, it's tadhamn.
3
Analyze concomitant examples
Examine 'human' for capacity for knowledge (customary necessity), 'even number' for divisibility by two (rational necessity), and the rectangle shape (rational). Notice the necessity is of two kinds: rational (عقلي) and customary (عرفي). Distinguish between them.
Customary necessity: though not logically impossible, sound humans normally possess the attribute. Rational necessity: the attribute follows from the definition by reason.
4
Apply the distinction to Islamic texts
The passage notes that concomitant denotation is abundant in the Qur'an and Sunna and is key to balaghah. Practice identifying whether a commonly understood implication in a verse or hadith is tadhamn or iltizam, and if iltizam, whether the necessity is rational or customary.
Example: 'And give the relative his due' (17:26). Does 'due' imply (tadhamn) specific amounts or necessary consequences (iltizam) such as preserving kinship?

Commentary
This passage defines two types of denotation: implicative denotation (dalaalat al-tadhmin) and concomitant denotation (dalaalat al-iltizam). The author first revisits the definition of implicative denotation: it is the denotation of a word to a part of its comprehensive meaning (the meaning that includes that part and other parts). Three examples are given. First, the word 'human' (insaan) can denote only the animal part or only the rational part, depending on context. If someone asks whether a distant figure is inanimate or animal, answering 'human' conveys animality (part of the meaning). If they ask whether it is rational or non-rational animal, the same answer conveys rationality. Second, a person claiming knowledge of inheritance laws (faraa'id) is taken to have implied knowledge of specific rulings like the grandfather with siblings, because that is part of the comprehensive meaning. Third, a doctor recommending fruits and vegetables for vitamins implicitly indicates that vitamins exist in those foods. Then the author defines concomitant denotation: a word signifying a meaning external to its literal or figurative meaning, but necessarily consequent upon that meaning either rationally or customarily. The name 'concomitant' (iltizam) reflects that the meaning is not directly indicated but is entailed by the core meaning in mind or custom. Examples: The word 'human' denotes capacity for knowledge and writing, which are not part of the definition of human but are customary concomitants. The statement 'this is an even number' necessarily implies divisibility by two without remainder (rational concomitance). A geometric shape that can be split into two identical squares from midpoints of opposite sides must be a rectangle, not a square (also rational). The author notes that this type of denotation is abundant in Arabic discourse, including the Qur'an and Sunna, and is a key element of eloquence (balaaghah). The passage ends with a citation of a poet describing his praised one (presumably an example of concomitant denotation in poetry), though the line is cut off.

Commentary
This passage defines the two fundamental categories of concepts in logic: the particular (juz'i) and the universal (kulli). It begins by stating that mental singular concepts are divided into these two types. To properly understand them, we need a clear definition of each. The author gives multiple definitions for the particular: it is a mental concept limited to one individual, or it does not admit sharing in the mind, or it is a concept for which it is impossible to suppose it applies to more than one specific individual. These definitions emphasize that a particular is conceptually restricted to a single entity. The linguistic indicator of a particular in speech is a proper name (alam), like 'Sa'id', 'Khalid', 'Salih'. The proper name is assigned exclusively to one individual; even if two people share the same name, they are considered two distinct proper names that happen to be identical in pronunciation. The universal is defined as a mental concept whose conception does not prevent sharing—meaning it can be predicated of multiple individuals in the mind, even if in external reality it only applies to one or none. Its linguistic indicator is indefinite nouns (nakirat) or definite nouns functioning like indefinites, such as nouns defined by 'al- that indicates the genus (e.g., 'al-insan' meaning humankind). The author provides a list of examples: human, animal, star, bird, sun, moon, creator from nothing, non-existent, phoenix, partner of the Creator, necessary of existence. He then comments on three observations from these examples: First, some universals like 'sun' and 'moon' have only one instance in our reality, but the mind can conceive of multiple instances; if another sun were created, the same universal term would apply. Second, some universals like 'creator from nothing' have only one instance in reality (God), and reason judges that no second instance can exist. Third, some universals like 'phoenix' have no instance at all in reality. The key point is that universality is a mental property, not dependent on external multiplicity.

Distinguishing Necessity and Possibility in Modal Propositions
Defines directed propositions and distinguishes necessity from possibility with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a proposition expresses necessity or intellectual possibility based on the role of reason.

Core Idea
Modality is not in the proposition itself—it is the intellect's judgment of necessity or possibility.

In classical logic, a proposition can be stated with or without an explicit modality. When the modality is stated, it becomes a 'directed proposition' (muwajjaha). The two fundamental modalities are necessity and possibility. This lesson will teach you to recognize them using the author's examples and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Term 'Directed Proposition'
Look at the definition: any proposition that explicitly states its modality (jiha). Find this in the Arabic passage: 'وحين نصرح في القضية بجهتها تسمى القضية موجهة'.
2
Distinguish Necessity from Possibility
Necessity (darura) means the intellect judges that the relation cannot be otherwise—either necessarily existent (e.g., 'every whole is greater than its part') or necessarily non-existent (impossible). Possibility (imkan 'aqli) means the intellect judges that the relation could be otherwise—neither necessary nor impossible, like fire burning things.
The author emphasizes that possibility is not about actuality but about rational conceivability.
3
Apply to Examples
Take the propositions given: 'Every whole is greater than its part' is necessary because denying it leads to contradiction. 'Fire burns combustible things' is possible because reason can conceive of a universe where it does not burn.
4
Note the Characterization of Possibility
The author explains that for possibility, the relation is not rationally necessitated; it is contingent on the natural order. This is key: possibility does not require actual occurrence.

Definition of Ma'rifa and 'Ilm as Perception of Reality
Author defines ma'rifa and 'ilm as perception of reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ma'rifa and 'ilm based on the author's definitions and identify the role of mutabaqa in true knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge is true perception conforming to reality.

This passage introduces two foundational terms in Islamic epistemology: ma'rifa (knowledge) and 'ilm (science). The author defines ma'rifa broadly as any perception of forms, attributes, or meanings, and then specifies that 'ilm is the perception of a thing as it is in reality. The key criterion for truth is mutabaqa—conformity between perception and reality. Understanding these definitions is essential before proceeding to the logical tools of tasawwur and tasdiq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definienda
Locate where the author defines ma'rifa and then 'ilm. Note that ma'rifa is introduced first, followed by 'ilm as a specific type of perception.
2
Extract the genus and differentia for ma'rifa
Ma'rifa's genus is 'idrak' (perception). Its differentia is the broad range of perceived objects: forms, attributes, signs, abstract meanings, regardless of extramental existence.
This makes ma'rifa a very general term.
3
Extract the genus and differentia for 'ilm
'Ilm also has genus 'idrak'. Its differentia is the condition 'as it is in reality' ('ala ma huwa 'alayhi fi al-waqi'), which implies conformity (mutabaqa).
Thus 'ilm is a narrower, more perfect type of knowledge.
4
Understand the perfection of knowledge
The perfection (kamal) of ma'rifa is said to be mutabaqa—conformity of perception to reality. This perfection is exactly what 'ilm is. So 'ilm is the perfect form of ma'rifa.
This aligns with the correspondence theory of truth.
5
Distinguish the two terms
Ma'rifa can be any perception, even if imperfect or not conforming to reality. 'Ilm specifically denotes perception that matches reality. In later tradition, 'ilm often becomes the technical term for certain knowledge.
The footnote also notes a linguistic usage of 'ilm for probable knowledge in the Quran.

Defining Istidlal: The Core Logical Operation
Author defines istidlal as mental inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the definition of istidlal and distinguish it from empirical knowledge.

Core Idea
Istidlal moves from the known to the unknown without experience.

In this passage, the author lays down the foundational definition of istidlal, the engine of all logical inference. Understanding this core operation is essential because every proof in kalam, fiqh, and usul depends on moving from known truths to unknown conclusions. Pay close attention to the condition that the conclusion should be new relative to the premises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum
Locate the term being defined (الاستدلال) and note that it appears twice at the start: the first is the section heading, the second begins the definition.
2

The author describes istidlal as a 'mental logical process' (عملية عقلية منطقية) – that is the genus. The differentia is 'deducing an unknown proposition from known ones' – i.e., the movement from known to unknown without recourse to experiment.
This is a rasm (description) rather than a hadd haqiqi, because it gives the function rather than the essence.

Analyze the condition of newness
The author states that the deduced proposition should normally be new relative to the premises; otherwise the inference is trivial. However, an exception is made when the inference is used to convince another person.
This prevents the definition from being too narrow.
4
Compare the two formulations
Two equivalent formulations are given: one uses 'proposition' (قضية), the other 'judgment of assent' (حكم تصديقي). Notice that both emphasize the logical, non-empirical nature of the process.
The second formulation is more precise by specifying the type of knowledge involved.

What is Contrariety in Propositions?
Definition and illustration of contrariety

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a pair of propositions are contraries and explain the logical conditions of contrariety.

Core Idea
Contrariety excludes joint truth but allows joint falsehood.

In logic, understanding relations between propositions is essential for sound reasoning. The author defines contrariety precisely: if one is true, the other must be false, but if one is false, the other may be true or false. This lesson unpacks that definition using vivid scales of opposites like colors and directions.

Guided Path
1
Locate the Definition
Find the sentence in the Arabic that begins with 'معنى التضاد في القضايا'. It states: truth of one entails falsity of the other, but falsity of one does not entail truth of the other.
2
Contrast with Contradiction
Note that in contradiction, falsity of one entails truth of the other. Here, both can be false. This is the key difference between تضاد and تناقض.
3

Look at the scales: colors, directions, time, etc. For each scale, if one item is true, all others in that scale are false. But if one is false, another item may also be false.
For example, if a color is not red, it could be green or blue, not necessarily green.
4
Generalize the Condition
Extract the principle: contrariety arises when the subject has multiple mutually exclusive predicates (like colors). The falsehood of one predicate does not force truth of any specific other.

The meaning of contrariety in propositions: that the truth of one of the two propositions necessarily entails the falsity of the other, but the falsity of one of the two propositions does not necessarily entail the truth of the other, because it is possible that the reality is contrary to both of them, so then they would both be false. And the meaning of contrariety arises when the opposition between the two propositions is between the judgment about a thing and the judgment about some individuals of the opposite, not about all of them, and that is when the thing has multiple opposite individuals that cannot coexist. So if this thing is false, then its falsity does not mean the truth of a specific individual among the individuals of the opposite, each individual of which is considered a contrary to another, because it is possible that this individual is also false, while the true one is neither of them together. And to bring that closer to understanding, we present scales of a group of opposites below, and we apply to them some clarifying examples: Purple, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red — the spectrum of colors. Noun, Verb, Particle — the divisions of the word. Each one in a scale from these scales is the opposite of all others in the same scale if they are combined, but it is a contrary to each individual of them. So if in the scale of primary colors the thing being red is false, that does not entail that it being green is true, because it is possible that it is contrary to both of them, so it might be blue for example or another color. And if it being larger than that is false, that does not entail that it is smaller than it, because it is possible that it is neither smaller nor larger, but rather equal to it. And if the word 'la' being a noun is false, that does not entail that it is a verb, because it is possible that it is contrary to both of them, so it is a particle, which is the reality, and so on. Commentary
This passage defines the logical relation of contrariety (تضاد) specifically as it applies to quantified propositions (قضايا محصورة). The author begins by stating the precise definition: if one proposition is true, the other must be false, but if one is false, the other may be true or false — it is not necessarily true. This distinguishes contrariety from contradiction, where falsity of one entails truth of the other. The reason falsity of one does not guarantee truth of the other is that both could be false, a scenario when the subject has multiple mutually exclusive predicates (like colors). The author then illustrates with 'scales' (سلالم) of opposites, such as the color spectrum (red, green, blue, etc.) or directions (up, down, left, right). In such a scale, each item is opposite to all others collectively but a contrary to each individually. For example, if a thing is not red, it might be green, but it could also be blue — so we cannot infer it is green. Classifying Mental Information: Existent, Non-Existent, and Secondary Intelligibles
Author divides mental information and defines secondary intelligibles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any mental concept as either having external verification or being a secondary intelligible, using the infinite regress test.

Core Idea
Secondary intelligibles are mental conventions; if affirming them as real leads to infinite regress, they are not externally existent.

In this passage, the author presents a foundational classification in Islamic logic: everything we know is either existent in the external world or non-existent. But there is also a special category of concepts — secondary intelligibles — that describe both existents and non-existents yet have no external reality themselves. Understanding this distinction is crucial for avoiding confusion between mental constructs and real entities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main divisions of mental information
The author states that mental information (al-ma'lūmāt al-dhihniyya) divides into two sections: that which has verification in the external (al-mawjūd) and that which does not (al-ma'dūm). Notice that the division is exhaustive.
2
Trace the subdivisions of the existent
The existent is split based on having a beginning: eternal (qadīm) has no beginning; originated (hādith) has a beginning. The originated further divides into substance (jawhar: essentially spatial) and accident ('arad: inhering in substance). The author mentions a rejected third category (mujarrad).
3
Trace the subdivisions of the non-existent
The non-existent is divided into impossible (mustahīl: cannot exist) and possible (mumkin: can exist).
4
Understand the nature of secondary intelligibles
The author introduces 'mental conventional concepts' (mafhūmāt i'tibāriyya dhihniyya), also called 'secondary intelligibles' (al-ma'qūlāt al-thāniyya). These are attributes that describe both existent and non-existent items, but they only exist in the mind, not externally. Examples: existence, non-existence, pre-eternity, origination.

Apply the infinite regress rule
The author gives a rule: if affirming that a concept is an existential matter leads to affirming a similar concept in an endless chain, then it is a mental conventional concept with no external existence. Test this with examples: pre-eternity, permanence, origination, existence, necessity. For each, assuming it has external reality forces an infinite regress of the same attribute, proving it is not external.

Commentary
This passage classifies the fundamental questions (أمهات المطالب) that arise in the pursuit of knowledge. The author presents four categories, each associated with a specific interrogative tool.

First (أ): Questions about existence (أصل الوجود) or description (وصف), using 'هل' (hal?). Example: 'Are jinn existent?' (existence) and 'Do jinn intermarry and reproduce?' (description).

Second (ب): Questions about definition, using 'ما' (ma?). This includes three sub-types: (1) explanation of reality via a proper definition (حد) — e.g., 'What is truth?' with the response 'a statement corresponding to reality'; (2) distinction by specific properties (رسم) — e.g., 'What is the spirit?' with the answer 'a secret from Allah's secrets by which life subsists'; (3) clarification of the intended meaning of a word (تعريف لفظي) — e.g., 'What is al-qaswara?' with the answer 'the lion, or the hunter.'

Third (ج): Questions about cause, using 'لِمَ' (lima?). This is split into efficient cause (علة مؤثرة) and final cause (علة غائية). Example of efficient cause: 'Why does strong electric current kill?' with a natural explanation. Example from fiqh: 'Why did you rule that wine is forbidden?' with the textual evidence from the Qur'an (5:90). Example of final cause: 'Why did Allah create humans and jinn?' with the purpose of testing and accountability; also 'Why did Allah forbid wine?' to protect health and society.

Fourth (د): Questions about distinction, using 'أي' (ayyu?). This applies when we already know something generally but need to differentiate its parts. Example from the Qur'an (57:20): 'Which of the two parties is in Paradise and which in the Blaze?'

Throughout, the author grounds each category with examples from Islamic theology and jurisprudence, emphasizing that logic serves the understanding of divine revelation and natural phenomena.

Core Idea
Mutual entailment: affirmation or negation of one implies the same of the other.

In the previous rule, we considered simple one-way implication. Here, the author introduces a crucial distinction: some entailments run only in one direction, while others are reciprocal. Though the passage ends mid-sentence, the definition of mutual entailment requires the converse. Reflect: if daytime exists, must the sun have risen? In normal circumstances, yes. For merely by our conception of 'one' and our conception of 'half of two', we judge that one is half of two as a primary evident judgment, without the mediation of conceiving anything else. And by our conception of 'the whole' and our conception of 'greater than the part', we judge that the whole is greater than the part as a primary evident judgment, without the mediation of conceiving anything else. And likewise for the third example and for all similar examples.
Source pages p. 415

Commentary
This passage defines the first type of evident propositions: the primary evident (al-jali al-awwali). The author gives examples such as 'one is half of two' and 'the whole is greater than the part'. The key characteristic is that the judgment is formed immediately upon conceiving the subject and predicate, without need for any intermediate concept or syllogism. For instance, understanding 'one' and 'half of two' directly yields the judgment that one is half of two. This type of proposition is foundational because it requires no reasoning process.
