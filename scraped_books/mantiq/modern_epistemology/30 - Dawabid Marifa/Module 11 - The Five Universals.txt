Defining the Particular and Its Indication by Proper Names
Author defines the particular and explains how proper names serve as indicators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a particular concept from a universal concept and identify how proper names indicate particulars in speech.

Core Idea
The particular is a mental concept that cannot be predicated of more than one individual; proper names are its linguistic indicators.

This passage introduces one of the most fundamental divisions in logic: the split between particulars and universals. Understanding the particular is essential because it forms the basis for all higher-level logical operations, from definition to syllogism. The author not only defines the particular but also shows how proper names function as its real-world signposts, a connection that bridges abstract mental concepts with concrete speech.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of the particular
The author gives three equivalent formulations: a mental concept limited to one individual, a concept that does not admit sharing, and a concept of which it is impossible to suppose its truth of more than one. Focus on the core idea: impossibility of multiple instantiation.
Note that the definition is about mental concepts, not physical objects.
2
Contrast with the universal
The particular is defined in opposition to the universal. While the particular cannot apply to many, the universal can. Keep this distinction clear as you proceed.
3
Understand how proper names indicate particulars
Proper names (ism 'alam) like Sa'id, Khalid, and Salih are assigned to specific individuals. The mind cannot apply such a name to another individual, even if identical in attributes. This shows how language mirrors logical particularity.
The text clarifies that two individuals sharing the same verbal name are actually two different proper names in terms of assignment.
4
Apply to examples
Test the definition on new examples: Is 'Zayd' particular? Is 'human' particular? Justify using the criterion of 'impossible to predicate of many.'

The Six Types of Universal Concepts
Enumerates six divisions of universal based on existence of individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal concept into one of the six types based on rational possibility and actual existence of its individuals in external reality.

Core Idea
A universal's type depends on whether its individuals can exist and how many actually exist.

The author presents a classification of universal concepts based on their relationship to external reality. This is not just a theoretical exercise—it's essential for understanding how terms like 'Allah' or 'human' function in theological and legal discourse. By learning these six types, you will be able to accurately identify the logical status of any universal you encounter in Islamic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal term
Find the universal concept being discussed. What is its name? Look for the term that can apply to multiple individuals in the mind.
e.g., 'sharik al-Bari' (partner of the Creator), 'Anqa', 'shams' (sun), 'insan' (human).
2
Determine rational possibility
Ask: Is it rationally possible for any individual of this universal to exist? This distinguishes type 1 (impossible) from the rest.
Type 1: rational impossibility (e.g., partner of the Creator). All other types are rationally possible.
3
Check actual existence count
If possible, how many individuals have actually existed? None? One? Finite? Infinite? This gives types 2-6.
Type 2: possible but none exist (Anqa). Type 3: possible but only one exists (sun, or humanity during Adam alone). Type 4: impossible to have more than one (Wajib al-Wujud). Type 5: possible infinite but only finite exist (human, star). Type 6: possible infinite and actually infinite (Allah's knowledge, number).
4
Apply the classification
Given any universal, run it through these checks and assign it to the correct division. Use the six-fold scheme as a diagnostic tool.
Practice with examples from Islamic theology and law to internalize the classification.

Definition of Genus from the Five Universals
Author defines genus as the first universal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the genus in a given definition and explain why it is a universal predicated of multiple different species in answer to 'What is it?' using examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Genus captures the shared essence of diverse species, answering the fundamental question: 'What is it?'

This lesson begins the study of the five universals (al-kulliyat al-khams), which are the building blocks of definition in Islamic logic. We start with the first: genus (al-jins). The passage explains its meaning with the example of 'animal' and provides the formal definition that genus is predicated of many things differing in essence in answer to 'What is it?'. Understanding genus is essential for precise definitions in fiqh, kalam, and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
List the five universals
From the passage, note that the author enumerates five universals: genus, species, differentia, property, and common accident. Write them down to see the whole classification.
This classification is standard in Islamic logic.
2
Locate the definition of genus
Find the sentence: 'Genus: it is a universal concept that includes the entire common essence among multiple things differing in reality.' Highlight the key terms: universal concept, common essence, differing in reality.
The definition has two parts: what genus is (concept) and what it does (includes common essence).
3
Analyse the example of 'animal'
The author gives 'animal' as an example. Notice that 'animal' applies to man, horse, gazelle, etc. These individuals differ in their complete essence (humanity vs. equinity) but share a part of essence: animality. Thus 'animal' is a genus.
This shows genus is broader than species.
4
Examine the formal definition
The formal definition states: 'predicated of many things differing in essence in answer to 'What is it?'.' The phrase 'What is it?' is the question that probes the essence. Genus answers that question for a group of diverse species.
This distinguishes genus from species, which answers 'What is it?' for individuals of the same species.
5
Apply to an Islamic legal example
Think of the genus 'intoxicant' in fiqh. It includes wine, beer, etc., which differ in their specific ingredients but share the essence of intoxication. The genus allows the ruling of prohibition to apply to all.
This demonstrates how genus functions in qiyas (analogical reasoning).

Distinguishing Essential from Accidental Attributes
Author distinguishes essential from accidental attributes of things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given attribute is essential (dhatiyya) or accidental ('aradiyya) and explain your reasoning using the criteria from the passage.

Core Idea
Essential attributes define what something is; accidental attributes describe what it has.

In logic, before we can define anything properly, we must first understand which attributes belong to its very essence (dhat) and which are merely external descriptions. This passage from the Dawabit al-Ma'rifa gives us a clear distinction: essential attributes are internal to the reality of a thing, while accidental attributes are external, whether inseparable or separable. Mastering this distinction is the first step to building correct definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and locate the two types of attributes: essential (dhatiyya) and accidental ('aradiyya). Note that the author defines each by whether it is 'internal to the reality' of the thing or not.
2
Analyze the essential examples
The author gives 'animality' and 'rationality' for a human being. Ask yourself: If you removed animality or rationality, would a human being still be a human being? The answer is no — these are constitutive of the essence.
3
Analyze the accidental examples
Walking and laughter are given as accidental. For walking, consider: an animal that never walks (like a snake) is still an animal. For laughter, a human who never laughs is still human. These attributes can be present or absent without destroying the essence.
The author notes that accidents may be inseparable (like laughter for humans? actually laughter is often considered accidental but inseparable in the sense that humans have the potential) or separable.
4
Apply the criterion
Now you can test any attribute: ask 'Is this attribute internal to the reality (haqiqa) of the thing? Would the thing cease to be what it is without it?' If yes, it's essential; if no, it's accidental.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of attributes: essential (dhatiyya) and accidental ('aradiyya). Essential attributes constitute the inner reality (haqiqa) of an entity, such as animality and rationality for a human being. Accidental attributes, in contrast, are external qualities, which may be inseparable (lazim) or separable (mufariq). Examples: walking is accidental for animal, laughter is accidental for man. The author uses classical examples to illustrate that these attributes are not part of the definition (hadd) but are external.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any universal concept as essential or accidental and further categorize it into one of the five universals.

Core Idea
The five universals are the essential and accidental categories that structure all definitions in Islamic logic.

This passage begins by defining the universal (kullī) and then organizes all universals into two fundamental types: essential and accidental. The essential includes genus, species, and differentia; the accidental includes special property and common accident. Guided Path
1
Identify the universal concept
Start with the concept you want to classify, such as 'human', 'animal', or 'rationality'.
2

3

If essential, ask: does it encompass multiple species? (genus). Is it a species under a genus? (species). Does it distinguish the species within its genus? (differentia).

Example: 'laughter' is a special property of humans; 'blackness' is a common accident.

Hierarchy of Genera and Species
Author summarizes the five universals and explains the relative hierarchy of genera and species up to the highest genus and lowest species.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether a genus is proximate or remote relative to a given species, and identify the highest genus and lowest species in any hierarchy.

Core Idea
Genus and species are relative: one term can be both a genus and a species depending on its position in the hierarchy.

After listing the five universals, the author explains how genera and species relate hierarchically. The same concept can be a genus for what is below it and a species for what is above it. This relative nature is crucial for building definitions and classifications in the Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify a species and its immediate genus
Take a species, for example 'human'. Determine its immediate genus. In Islamic logic, the immediate genus of 'human' is 'animal'.
2
Climb the hierarchy upward
Check if that genus ('animal') is itself a species of a higher genus. 'Animal' is a species of 'body' (jism). Continue ascending until you reach a genus with no higher genus – the genus of genera (jins al-ajnas).
In the passage, the highest genus is called جنس الأجناس.
3
Descend to the lowest species
From a given genus, descend to the species that cannot be a genus for anything below – the species of species (naw' al-anwa').
Example: 'human' is the lowest species in the hierarchy if no further subdivision exists.
4
Distinguish proximate and remote genera
If a genus is directly above a species, it is a proximate genus. If there are intermediate genera, it is a remote genus. Remoteness can be by one or more steps.
From 'human', 'animal' is a proximate genus; 'body' is a remote genus.

Tabaayun: Complete Difference in Meaning and Extension

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether two terms are mutabaayin by checking for complete difference in both concept (mafhum) and extension (misdaq).

Core Idea
Tabaayun is total non-overlap in both meaning and instances — two worlds apart.

In Islamic sciences, we often need to compare concepts. Is 'salat' the same as 'sawm'? Obviously not, but why exactly? The answer lies in a logical ratio called tabaayun, which the author defines as complete difference in both concept (mafhum) and extension (misdaq). This lesson unpacks that definition with clear examples from everyday language and Islamic disciplines.

Identify the two terms

Check conceptual difference

Check extensional difference
Ask: Does any individual that falls under the first term also fall under the second? For 'human' and 'horse', no individual is both. If there is no overlap, the pair is mutabaayin.
Extension (misdaq) refers to the actual individuals the concept applies to.

Practice with Islamic examples
Try pairs like 'salat' and 'sawm', or 'wudu'' and 'tayammum'. Confirm that they differ in both concept and extension.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Coextension means a universal's meaning fits all individuals equally—but not all examples are uncontroversial.

In this passage, the author introduces the fifth relation between a universal and its individuals: coextension (al-tawāṭuʾ). He explains that a coextensive universal has its meaning present equally and without gradation in every individual, with respect to the essential concept. He then provides examples to illustrate this, but also offers a critical note on one of them, showing that even established textbook examples can be challenged. The Sixth Relation: Tashkik (Analogy of Intensity)
Defines tashkik with examples and naming rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify tashkik in a term and distinguish it from both univocal (tawatu') and homonymous (mushtarak) relations.

Core Idea
Tashkik: same meaning, different intensity – the middle ground between univocity and homonymy.

In the previous lessons, we learned about univocal terms (tawatu'), where the meaning is equally present in all individuals, and homonymous terms (mushtarak), where a single word has completely different meanings. But what if the meaning is the same yet varies in intensity? This is exactly the relation of tashkik (analogy/equivocity of intensity). In this passage, the author defines tashkik, gives clear examples from the physical world, and explains why such terms are called 'mushakkik' (causing doubt).

Guided Path
1
Identify the relation being defined
The passage begins with 'al-nisba al-sādisa: al-tashkīk'. Recognize that tashkik is the sixth relation among the five universals (though here it is a relation of universals). The core idea is that a universal meaning exists in its individuals but in varying degrees.
2
Analyze the defining phrase
The key phrase is 'wujūd maʿnan kulliyy fī afrādihi mutafāwitan' (existence of a universal meaning in its individuals is varying). Note that the variation is relative to the concept for which the word was coined (bi-ḥasabi l-mafhūm alladhī wuḍiʿa lahu l-lafẓ al-kulliyy). This means the same core meaning applies, but with different intensities.
Example: the word 'nūr' (light) has a single meaning – luminosity – but its intensity differs across individuals.
3
Examine the examples
Two examples are given: 'nūr' (light) and 'bayāḍ' (whiteness). For each, the author lists individuals with varying degrees of the quality. For light: sun > moon > electric lamp > candle/match. For whiteness: snow > elephant bone. This illustrates that the universal meaning is not equally present; rather, it is 'mutafāwit' (varying).
These examples are tangible and help internalize the concept of intensity variation.
4
Understand the naming rationale
The term that exhibits tashkik is called 'al-mushakkik' (causing doubt). The reason: the observer hesitates between two classifications: is it univocal (tawatu') because the meaning is one, or is it homonymous (mushtarak) because the individuals are very different? Since it is neither fully equal nor fully different, doubt (tashakkuk) arises. Hence the name.
The Arabic root sh-k-k means doubt. So mushakkik literally means 'that which causes doubt'.
5
Differentiate from tawatu' and ishtirak
Contrast tashkik with the two extremes: In tawatu', the meaning is equally present in all individuals (e.g., 'insān' for humans). In ishtirak, the word has multiple unrelated meanings (e.g., 'ʿayn' for eye, spring, etc.). In tashkik, the meaning is one but present in different intensities. This intermediate status is what makes tashkik unique and important in Islamic logic.
Memorize: tawatu' = equal intensity, tashkik = varying intensity, ishtirak = different meanings.

Examples: 1) Like the word 'light' (nur): the existence of the meaning of this word in its individuals is varying, not consistent, for the light of the sun is stronger than the light of the moon, and both of them are stronger than the light of an electric lamp, and all of them have light stronger than the light of a candle or a match. B) Like the word 'whiteness' (bayad) or 'white' (abyad): this is a universal whose meaning in snow is more intense than it is in elephant bone, and it is observed in white things that their whiteness is varying, not consistent. And that which has this relation of tashkik is called (al-mushakkik). The reason for this name is that the relation of the existence of the meaning in the individuals makes the observer doubt between two things: whether it is consistent, so he joins it to the univocal, or it is completely different, so he joins it to the homonym (which is one word with multiple meanings). Since the observer imagines that he hesitates between the univocal and the homonym, it is fitting that it be called mushakkik, because the relation causes doubt.

Commentary
This passage defines the sixth relation: al-tashkik, where a universal term applies to individuals with varying intensity. Unlike tawatu', the meaning is not equally present in all individuals but differs in degree. Two examples are given: 'light' (nur) — the light of the sun is stronger than that of the moon, and both stronger than an electric lamp, etc.; and 'whiteness' (bayad/abyad) — e.g., snow is whiter than ivory. The passage then explains the naming: such a relation is called 'mushakkik' (causing doubt) because the observer is uncertain whether to classify it as univocal (tawatu') or homonymous (mushtarak). The meaning is not completely identical (like homonyms) nor completely equal (like univocals), but somewhere in between, hence doubt arises. This relation is crucial in Islamic philosophy and theology, e.g., the term 'existence' (wujud) is often considered mushakkik, applying to God and creatures in different degrees.

Seventh Relation: Taraduf (Synonymy)
Defines the relation of taraduf with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of taraduf (synonymy) and explain the definition based on the passage.

Core Idea
Taraduf: multiple words, one meaning — the words follow each other.

The seventh relation among word relations is taraduf, i.e., synonymy. In usul al-fiqh, recognizing when words are synonyms prevents misinterpretation of legal texts. This passage defines taraduf and provides three illustrative examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristic of taraduf
Read the definition: 'نسبة لفظ إلى لفظ من جهة دلالة كلّ منهما على معنى واحد'. The key is that the meaning is one and the words are multiple.
2
Examine the examples provided
The passage gives three sets: (1) insan / bashar, both meaning 'human'; (2) asad / layth / qaswara, all meaning 'lion'; (3) zakhar / tama / tamalla, all meaning 'overflowed' or 'became full'. Verify that each set shares exactly one meaning.
3
Understand the name mutaradifa
The passage explains the etymology: 'تترادف، أي: تتوالى ويتابع بعضها بعضاً'. This shows that synonyms 'follow one another' in pointing to the same meaning.
4
Distinguish taraduf from other relations
Contrast with ishtirak (one word, multiple meanings) and tashkik (gradation). Taraduf is about multiple words, one meaning.

The seventh relation: (al-taraduf) which is a relation of one word to another concerning each of them indicating a single meaning that they share in indicating. So in taraduf, the meaning is one and the words are multiple: i.e., there is one meaning and several words, each indicating it. Examples: A) Like the two words 'insan' and 'bashar': the meaning of each of the two words is the same for both. The reason for this name is that the words in it tataraduf, i.e., follow one after another, each succeeding the other in indicating one meaning.

One Attribute, Multiple Logical Roles
Author illustrates how one attribute can be a differentia for one type and a general accident for another.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether an attribute functions as a differentia (fasl) or a general accident ('arad 'amm) depending on the genus level under consideration.

Core Idea
One attribute can be a differentia for one species and a general accident for another.

This short passage from the Dawabit al-Ma'rifa demonstrates a critical insight: the logical status of an attribute is not fixed—it depends on the classification hierarchy. The same attribute that distinguishes one species within a genus may become a general accident when a broader genus is considered. Understanding this relativity prevents confusion in defining terms and evaluating universal relationships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attribute in question.
Find the attribute 'al-mukayyaf' (qualified) in the Arabic. It includes colored, scented, tangible, etc.
2
Determine the genus and species being contrasted.
The genus is 'jism' (body). The species are dense (al-kathif) and subtle (al-latif). The attribute 'qualified' separates dense from subtle.
3
Check if the attribute is essential for differentiating those species.
Yes, 'qualified' (specifically 'colored') is used to define the dense as 'a colored body', so it acts as a differentia for dense within the genus body.
4
Consider a broader genus that includes unrelated species.
If the broader genus is 'jism' (body) itself, 'qualified' applies to both dense and subtle, so it is not a differentia here. If an even broader genus like 'substance' (jawhar) is considered, 'qualified' becomes a general accident because not all substances are qualified.
5
Conclude that the same attribute can be a differentia for one classification and a general accident for another.
Thus, the logical role depends on the classification level. This is a key lesson in the five universals.
