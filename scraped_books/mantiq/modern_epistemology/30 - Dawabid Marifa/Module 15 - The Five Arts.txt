Demonstrative and Dialectical Proofs: Certainty and Binding Force
Author defines demonstrative and dialectical proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish demonstrative proof (burhani) from dialectical proof (jadali) by their premises, epistemic status, and binding force.

Core Idea
Burhani gives certainty; jadali binds by acceptance — both compel, but differently.

In the hierarchy of proofs, the highest two are demonstrative and dialectical. Both are binding on the listener, but for different reasons. Demonstrative proof yields certain conviction through true premises, while dialectical proof binds because its premises are accepted by the opponent — either as widely known or as part of their own doctrine. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in Islamic theology and jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the highest proof
Find in the passage the proof that yields 'al-yaqeen al-jazim' (certain conviction). What is it called?
Al-hujjah al-burhaniyyah.
2
Identify the binding condition
Look for the phrase 'mulzimah' (binding). Which proof is described as such? On what basis does it bind the opponent?
Al-hujjah al-jadaliyyah binds because its premises are either mashhurat (widely accepted) or match the opponent's doctrine.
3
Compare the epistemic status
Burhani yields certainty; jadali yields less than certainty but still binds. What does jadali not necessarily produce?
Certainty; it only binds, but the premises may not be absolutely true.
4
Check the basis of binding
For jadali, the binding force relies on the opponent's acceptance, not on the objective truth of premises. How does this differ from burhani?
Burhani binds by virtue of its truth, not merely acceptance.

Orders of Proofs
Debaters and those who present proofs employ types of proofs and evidences:
Among them is what yields certain conviction, and it is (the demonstrative proof).
And among them is what yields less than that.
If it is binding upon the other party—the debater or the one to whom the proof is presented—by virtue of his acceptance of the premises of the proof due to their widespread fame approaching the strength of certainty, or because they are his own doctrine, then it is 'the dialectical proof.'
And if it is not binding upon the other party—the debater or the addressee—but it yields a preponderant and acceptable opinion, then it is 'the rhetorical proof.'
And if it is less than that, but it plays with the emotions of the addressee, so he responds to its content and is affected by it, even if he knows of its unsoundness, then it is 'the poetic proof.'
And if it is composed of false premises, or contains what is false and unsound, then it is 'the rejected proof.' This rejected proof, if it is based on an unintentional error, then it is 'error.' And if it is based on an intentional error for the purpose of obfuscating the opponent or misleading the addressee, then it is 'sophistry,' and the sophistical proof is called a sophistical proof, named after a group of philosophers who appeared in the ages of Greek philosophy called 'the Sophists.' From it is derived the word 'safasata' meaning presenting a proof based on sophistry.

Commentary
This passage classifies proofs hierarchically. It begins by stating that debaters and reasoners use various types of proofs and evidences. The highest is demonstrative proof (burhaniyya) which yields certain conviction. Next is dialectical proof (jadaliyya) which binds the opponent because the premises are either widely accepted or match the opponent's own doctrine. Then rhetorical proof (khatabiyya) yields probable opinion without being binding. Then poetic proof (shi'riyya) moves emotions even if false. Finally, rejected proof (marfuda) is based on false premises; it splits into unintentional error (ghalat) and intentional sophistry (mughalata), the latter linked to the Greek Sophists. The passage thus provides a complete taxonomy of argument types according to their epistemological strength and purpose, reflecting the classical Islamic classification of the five arts (al-sana'i' al-khamsah). Each type is defined by its effect on the audience and the nature of its premises.

Rhetorical, Poetic, and Rejected Proofs: Persuasion, Emotion, and Fallacy
Author defines rhetorical, poetic, and rejected proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate rhetorical, poetic, and rejected proofs, and distinguish unintentional error from intentional sophistry.

Core Idea
Rhetoric persuades, poetry stirs, and falsehood deceives — each has a distinct flaw.

Below the binding proofs are those that do not bind: rhetorical proofs yield probable opinion, poetic proofs move the emotions even if false, and rejected proofs are based on false premises. The rejected proof divides into unintentional error (ghalat) and intentional sophistry (mughalata). Understanding these lower levels is essential for evaluating non-demonstrative arguments and avoiding fallacies.

Guided Path
1
Identify rhetorical proof
Find the proof that yields 'zann rajih' (preponderant opinion) but is not binding. What is it called?
Al-hujjah al-khatabiyyah.
2
Identify poetic proof
Find the proof that plays with emotions ('tatala'ab bi-masha'ir') and affects the listener even if he knows it is false. What is it called?
Al-hujjah al-shi'riyyah.
3
Identify rejected proof
Find the proof composed of false premises. What is it called? What are its two subcategories?
Al-hujjah al-marfudah; subcategories: ghalat (unintentional error) and mughalata (intentional sophistry).
4
Distinguish error from sophistry
How does the passage differentiate ghalat from mughalata? What is the key factor?
Ghalat is unintentional; mughalata is intentional for obfuscation or deception.

Author enumerates five types of arguments with their characteristics.

Guided Path

This distinction is crucial for selecting the appropriate argument in different contexts.

Rhetorical and Poetic Arguments
Author enumerates five types of arguments with their characteristics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between rhetorical and poetic arguments based on their effect on the audience.

Core Idea
Rhetorical arguments produce probable opinion; poetic arguments move emotions regardless of truth.

The author continues with arguments that yield less than certainty. The rhetorical argument (الخطابية) provides a preponderant opinion (ظن راجح) that is acceptable but not binding. The poetic argument (الشعرية) is even lower, manipulating emotions even if the audience knows it is false. These are often used in persuasion and art.

Guided Path
1
Focus on non-binding arguments
Both rhetorical and poetic arguments are non-binding (غير ملزمة). They do not force the opponent to accept the conclusion.
2
Analyze the rhetorical argument
It yields a preponderant opinion (ظن راجح مقبول). It is based on plausible premises that make the conclusion likely but not certain.
Common in sermons and political speeches.
3
Analyze the poetic argument
It plays with emotions (تتلاعب بمشاعر المخاطب). The audience is influenced even if they know the argument is false.
Poetic arguments are not about truth but about emotional impact.
4
Contrast rhetorical and poetic
Rhetorical appeals to reason (weakly), while poetic appeals to emotion. Both are below dialectical in epistemic value.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The poetic argument moves emotions without requiring truth – its power lies in imagination, not evidence.

In the Islamic classification of the five arts (sina‘at khams), the poetic argument occupies a unique place: it does not aim at certainty or probability, but at emotional engagement. This lesson unpacks its definition from Dawabid Marifa, showing how it relies on imaginary premises and false images to stir the soul. You will learn to recognize poetic arguments and understand why not all emotionally charged speech qualifies as poetic in the logical sense.

Not every emotionally stirring speech is poetic—other arguments can combine logic and emotion.

The author warns against conflating poetic arguments with other types that also stir emotions. While all can move the soul, only poetic arguments lack logical grounding. Let's see the key distinction.
