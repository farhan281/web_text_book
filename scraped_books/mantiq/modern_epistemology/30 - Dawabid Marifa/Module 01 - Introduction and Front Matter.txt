Parsing the Title Page of Dawabit al-Ma'rifah
Title page providing author name, book title, subtitle, and publisher.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the front matter of Dawabit al-Ma'rifah and explain its significance as a work of Islamic logic.

Core Idea
This title page announces a reformulation of logic from an Islamic epistemological perspective.

The title page of a scholarly work often encapsulates the author's project. In this lesson, we will dissect the front matter of Dawabit al-Ma'rifah to understand its scope and aims, and how it positions itself within the Islamic intellectual tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his background
Look at the first line: Abd al-Rahman Hasan Habankah al-Maydani. This is a modern Syrian scholar (1920–2005). His name indicates his lineage and affiliation.
Knowing the author's era helps situate the work's response to both classical and modern challenges.
2
Break down the main title 'Dawabit al-Ma'rifah'
Dawabit means 'controls' or 'criteria'; al-Ma'rifah means 'knowledge'. So the title means 'Controls of Knowledge' – referring to the standards that delineate valid knowledge.
This title signals the book's concern with epistemology (theory of knowledge) from an Islamic standpoint.
3
Examine the subtitle 'Usul al-Istidlal wa al-Munazarah'
Usul al-Istidlal means 'Principles of Inference'; wa al-Munazarah means 'and Debate'. This indicates the book covers both logical reasoning and dialectical argumentation.
The inclusion of munazarah links to the Islamic tradition of jadal (debate).
4
Understand the tagline: 'siyaghah li al-mantiq wa usul al-bahth mutamashiyah ma'a al-fikr al-Islami'
Siyaghah means 'formulation'; al-mantiq is logic; usul al-bahth is principles of research; mutamashiyah ma'a al-fikr al-Islami means 'in harmony with Islamic thought'. This explicitly states the book's goal: to present logic in an Islamic framework.
This distinguishes it from works that simply reproduce Greek logic without adaptation.
5
Note the publisher and place – what does that suggest about the intended audience?
Dar al-Qalam in Damascus is a reputable Islamic publishing house. The location in Syria suggests a readership in the Arab and Muslim world, particularly those engaged in Islamic studies.
The publisher may also indicate the scholarly network of the author.

Parsing the Title: Dawabit al-Ma'rifa
Title page announcing the scope: epistemology, logic, and debate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse the title of Dawabit al-Ma'rifa into its three components and explain the scope of each (epistemology, logic, and dialectic).

Core Idea
The title itself is a syllabus: epistemology, logic, and debate.

The title of a scholarly work often encapsulates its entire project. In this case, the three-part title 'Dawabit al-Ma'rifa wa Usul al-Istidlal wa al-Munazara' signals a comprehensive treatise covering the criteria for valid knowledge (epistemology), the rules of reasoning (logic), and the art of structured debate (dialectic). Let us unpack each component to understand the author's intended scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
Read the title and note the wa (and) connecting three phrases: dawabit al-ma'rifa, usul al-istidlal, and al-munazara. These are the three pillars of the work.
The conjunctions divide the title into distinct but related disciplines.
2
Define 'dawabit al-ma'rifa'
'Dawabit' (plural of dabt) means controls, rules, or criteria. 'Ma'rifa' is knowledge. Together, it refers to the epistemological framework for distinguishing true knowledge from false.
This term is specific to this work; it emphasizes criteria for certainty.
3
Define 'usul al-istidlal'
'Usul' means foundations or principles. 'Istidlal' is inference. This refers to the logical principles of reasoning, such as syllogistic forms.
This parallels the subject matter of classical mantiq.
4
Define 'al-munazara'
'Munazara' means dialectical discussion or debate. It indicates the rules for argumentation and disputation (jadal/adab al-bahth).
In later tradition, this becomes a technical field with its own manuals.
5
Relate the three to Islamic sciences
Each component corresponds to a discipline: epistemology (usul al-fiqh and kalam), logic (mantiq), and dialectic (jadal). Recognising this helps in understanding the text's interdisciplinary approach.
The author likely intended to integrate these fields for a robust method of seeking truth.

Using Islamic Examples for Logic
Author on using diverse Islamic examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Islamic examples make logic relevant and prevent stagnation.

The author highlights that his book includes diverse examples from the Qur'an, Sunnah, creed (ʿaqāʾid), and jurisprudence (fiqh), instead of the few traditional examples that cause stagnation. This lesson explores why Islamic examples are superior for learning logic.

The Two Main Subjects of Logic: Definition and Proof
Author introduces the two pathways of logic: definition for concepts and proof for assents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two main subjects of logic (definition and proof) and identify which one applies to concepts and which to assents.

Core Idea
Logic has two essential tasks: defining concepts and proving assents.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational division of logic into two pathways. The author reminds us that mental perceptions are either concepts (tasawwur) or assents (tasdiq). When we need to convey them or acquire new ones without sensory aids, we must use either definition (mu'arraf) for concepts or proof (hujja) for assents. This sets the stage for the entire study of logic.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two types of mental perceptions
Identify that the author states mental perceptions are confined to concepts (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat). This is the starting point for understanding the purpose of logic.
Concepts are simple apprehensions; assents involve judgment.
2
Identify the need for communication beyond sensory means
Recognize the scenarios described: when we lack sensory or material means to convey our perceptions to others or to learn unknown ones ourselves, we must resort to either definition or proof.
This highlights logic's role when direct experience is unavailable.
3
Understand that definition serves concepts
The first way is definition (mu'arraf or qawl sharih), which conveys the form of a simple term or distinguishes it from others. It is used for concepts.
Definition has its own controls to ensure it leads to a correct concept.
4
Understand that proof serves assents
The second way is proof (hujja or dalil), which presents theoretical evidence to attain or convince someone of an affirmative or negative judgment. It is used for assents.
Proof has controls to be sound, convincing, and binding.
5
Recognize the two main inquiries of logic
The author concludes that the essential purpose of this art is confined to two inquiries: definitions (for concepts) and proofs/evidences (for assents). Each has general principles that must be known beforehand.
This division structures the rest of the study of logic.

Commentary
This passage introduces the two fundamental subjects of logic: definition (mu'arraf) and proof (hujja). The author begins by recalling that mental perceptions are of two types: concepts (tasawwurat) and assents (tasdiqat). When we need to convey these perceptions to others or to learn unknown ones ourselves, and we lack direct sensory or material means, we must resort to either definition or proof. The first way, definition, is used for concepts; it is a statement that clarifies the form of a simple term or distinguishes it. The author notes that definitions have specific controls to ensure they lead to a correct concept. The second way, proof, is used for assents; it involves presenting theoretical evidence to reach or persuade someone of an affirmative or negative judgment. Proofs also have controls to be sound and binding. The author then concludes that the entire art of logic is essentially divided into these two inquiries: definitions (for conveying concepts) and proofs/evidences (for attaining theoretical assents and convincing others). Before each inquiry, general principles must be understood, some common to both and some specific to each. This passage thus sets the stage for the rest of the work, establishing that logic serves to clarify concepts and establish assents through methodical definitions and proofs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge is acquired through methodical operations, not random intuition.

This short passage marks a transition in the text from foundational concepts to the actual tools of inquiry. It tells us that knowing something (ma'rifa) is not enough—we must understand how to reach it and how to verify it using structured research methods. The author divides these methods into two pairs of operations: division and collection, which organize concepts, and analysis and synthesis, which build arguments.

Classification of Inference: Direct and Indirect
Author outlines the three chapters on inference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct and indirect inference and identify examples of each from Islamic legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Inference is either direct (one step) or indirect (requires a middle term).

This lesson introduces the foundational classification of inference in Islamic logic. It maps out the three chapters that follow, giving you a bird's-eye view of the logical tools you will learn. Understanding this classification helps you see where each inferential method fits in the broader system.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories of inference
Look at the outline: it splits inference into direct (mubashir) and indirect (ghayr mubashir). Direct inference yields a conclusion from one premise; indirect uses a middle term.
2
Note the subcategories under direct inference
Direct inference includes opposition (taqabul) and conversion ('aks). Opposition examines relations like contradiction; conversion swaps subject and predicate.
3
Note the subcategories under indirect inference
Indirect inference includes induction (istiqra'), syllogism (qiyas), and analogy (tamthil). These require multiple premises or a middle term.
Syllogism (qiyas) here is categorical syllogism, not legal analogy.
4
Understand the third chapter: ranks of arguments
The outline ends with a chapter on ranks of arguments (maratib al-hujaj), which classifies proofs by certainty. This chapter will help you evaluate the strength of different inferences.
5
Apply the classification to a concrete Islamic example
Take the proposition 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (from a hadith). Direct conversion gives 'Some forbidden things are intoxicants'. Indirect syllogism: 'Every intoxicant is forbidden, nabidh is an intoxicant, therefore nabidh is forbidden'. Contrast the two.

Direct inference includes two sub-topics: opposition (taqabul) and conversion ('aks). Navigating the Structure of Islamic Dialectic: The Three Methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main methods of munazara (al-man', al-mu'arada, al-naqd) and locate their sections in the Dawabid Marifa table of contents.

Core Idea
The three methods of munazara are the backbone of Islamic dialectic.

This lesson takes you through the table of contents of a classic munazara treatise. You will see how the author organizes the fundamental strategies of debate: denial, counter-argument, and refutation. Understanding this structure is your first step into the discipline of Islamic dialectic.

Guided Path
1
Find the three methods
Look for the numbered 'طريق' entries in the Arabic. They are marked 'الطريق الأولى' (first method), 'الطريق الثانية' (second method), and 'الطريق الثالثة' (third method).
Each method has a distinct Arabic term in parentheses: المنع, المعارضة, النقض.
2
Note the subdivisions of al-mu'arada
The second method is further divided into two: 'المعارضة في الدليل' (counter-argument in the proof) and 'المعارضة في العلة' (counter-argument in the cause). This shows the author's precision.
These subdivisions appear on page 429.
3
Observe the progression of topics
After the three methods, the index lists arrangements of debates, then prohibitions, then terminology, and finally Quranic debates. This suggests a pedagogical flow from basic tools to full application.
Note the logical sequence: methods → arrangement → ethics → terminology → examples.
4
Locate page numbers for each section
The right column gives page numbers for each entry. For example, 'المنع' is on page 427, 'المعارضة' on 428, 'النقض' on 437. Use this to navigate the book.
Page numbers are in Arabic numerals (e.g., ٤٢٧ = 427).
