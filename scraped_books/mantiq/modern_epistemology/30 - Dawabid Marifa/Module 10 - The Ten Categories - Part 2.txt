Life (al-Hayah): Definition and Implications
Author defines life as a power requiring sensation and movement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define life (al-hayah) as a power necessitating sensation and movement, and distinguish it from other attributes.

Core Idea
Life is an internal power that enables sensation and voluntary movement.

In the section on attributes specific to living beings, the author first defines life. He says life is a power (quwwa) that necessitates sensation (hiss) and movement (haraka). This definition is precise: life is not merely the presence of a body, but an active principle that enables two key functions. Understanding this definition helps separate mere living beings from inanimate objects and from higher attributes like power and will.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of life
Find the Arabic phrase: 'الحياة: وهي قوة تقتضي الحس والحركة.'
2
Break down the definition into genus and differentia
Genus: power (quwwa). Differentia: necessitating sensation and movement.
3
Examine the components: sensation and movement
Sensation is perception of stimuli; movement is self-initiated motion. Both are essential for life per this definition.
4
Contrast with non-living things
Stones do not have sensation or self-movement, so they are not alive according to this definition.
5
Consider edge cases
Plants: they have growth but not sensation? The author's definition focuses on sensation, so plants may be excluded.

Commentary
This passage is a classification and definition of attributes (a'rad). The author begins with the physical observation that everything in the universe is in motion, and apparent rest is relative. This sets the stage for discussing the metaphysical concepts of conjunction and separation of two substances: if they meet with no third substance between them, it is conjunction; otherwise separation. The author then notes that modern science shows atoms have voids, so true conjunction is rare; what we see is proximity.

Next, the author lists general accidents common to many bodies: composition, heaviness, lightness, heat, cold, dryness, softness, moisture, color, odor, energy, hardness, flexibility, resistance, etc. These are qualities that can be analyzed by reflection.

Then the author turns specifically to living beings (al-ahya'), enumerating ten categories with definitions:
1. Hayat (Life): a power requiring sensation and movement.
2. Qudra (Power): an existential attribute affecting action according to will.
3. Irada (Will): attribute by which the agent prefers to do or leave an act.
4. Karahiya (Aversion): the soul's repulsion from something deemed harmful or ugly.
5. I'tiqad (Belief): a decisive judgment that can change; correct if matching reality, corrupt otherwise.
6. Zann (Conjecture): preferring one side of a judgment when it is preponderant; it has degrees down to illusion.
7. Tafakkur (Reflection): mental process leading to knowledge, belief, or conjecture.
8. Alam (Pain): perception of what nature dislikes.
9. Ladhdha (Pleasure): perception of what is suitable.
10. A list of further attributes: health, illness, joy, sadness, shame, dread, anger, love, satisfaction, hope, fear.

The author systematically defines each term, ensuring precise conceptual boundaries for use in logic and other sciences.

Belief (al-I'tiqad): Decisive Judgment and Correspondence to Reality
Author defines belief as a changeable decisive judgment, correct if matching reality.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define belief (i'tiqad) as a changeable decisive judgment and explain the distinction between correct and corrupt belief.

Core Idea
Belief is a decisive judgment that can change; its correctness depends on correspondence to reality.

Among the specific attributes of living beings, belief holds a special place because it involves the intellect. The author defines belief (i'tiqad) as a decisive judgment (hukm jazim) that is subject to change. He further distinguishes correct belief (matching reality) from corrupt belief (not matching reality). This definition is crucial for logic and epistemology, as it links truth to correspondence with the real world.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of belief
Find the Arabic: 'الاعتقاد: وهو الحكم الجازم القابل للتغير.'
2
Identify the genus and differentia
Genus: judgment (hukm). Differentia: decisive (jazim) and changeable (qabil lil-taghyir).
3
Understand the two types
Correct (sahih) corresponds to reality; corrupt (fasid) does not.
4
Apply the definition to an example
Consider the belief that the world is created. If it matches reality, it is correct; if not, corrupt.
5
Note the changeability
Belief can change with new evidence, unlike certain knowledge (yaqin) which is unwavering.

Six Directions and Their Combination in One Object
Author enumerates the six directions and demonstrates how they can all apply to one thing from different perspectives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how six directional concepts can simultaneously apply to a single object from different relational perspectives.

Core Idea
Six distinct directions can all describe one thing through different perspectives.

This passage introduces the six spatial directions as conceptually distinct categories. But the author immediately shows that these categories are not mutually exclusive in external reality—a single object can be above, below, front, back, right, and left, depending on the relation. Understanding this is crucial for avoiding false dichotomies in logic and Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six directions
List the six categories given: فوق (above), تحت (below), أمام (front), خلف (back), يمين (right), شمال (left).
These are the standard spatial directions.
2
Understand their conceptual distinctness
The passage says the mind defines each with a different reality (حقيقة تخالف). So each direction is a distinct concept.
Conceptually, 'above' is not the same as 'below'.
3
Observe the real-world combination
The author says: 'but we observe in reality that some things... are above from one perspective and below from another...' This shows that distinct concepts can apply to the same thing.
Reality is not limited by conceptual exclusivity.
4
Analyze the Kaaba example
Break down each directional description of the Kaaba relative to different reference points: above earth, below sky, front for one facing it, back for one turning away, right for one standing at the spout facing east, left for one standing between the Yemeni corners facing east.
Each description depends on a specific relation (i'tibar).
5
Formulate the principle
Conceptual divisions (in the mind) do not force mutual exclusivity in external existence when the relation changes.
This principle applies broadly in logic and Islamic sciences.

2. The directions are divided into six divisions: above, below, front, back, right, and left. The mind defines for each division a reality different from the reality of every other division. But we observe in reality that some things that are described as 'above' from one perspective are 'below' from another, 'front' from a third, 'back' from a fourth, 'right' from a fifth, and 'left' from a sixth. For example, the Kaaba is above the earth, below the sky, front for one facing it, back for one turning away from it, to the right of one standing towards the rainwater spout facing east, and to the left of one standing between the two Yemeni corners also facing east. Thus all six divisions come together in one thing from different perspectives.

Commentary
This passage classifies the six directional concepts (above, below, front, back, right, left). It first states that each has a distinct meaning in the mind (conceptually different). Then it shows that in concrete reality, a single object can receive all six descriptions simultaneously when considered from different relations. The example of the Kaaba illustrates this: it is above the earth, below the sky, in front of someone facing it, behind someone turning away, to the right of someone facing east near the spout, and to the left of someone standing between the two Yemeni corners. This teaches that conceptual divisions can be real in the mind but may coincide in external existence.
