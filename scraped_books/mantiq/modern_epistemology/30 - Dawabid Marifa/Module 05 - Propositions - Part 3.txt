Conditions for Contradiction: When Apparent Opposites Are Not Contradictory
Author illustrates conditions where apparent contradictories are not contradictory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when two propositions that appear contradictory are not actually contradictory by checking the conditions of subject, predicate, time, place, and relation.

Core Idea
Contradiction requires identity of subject, predicate, time, place, and relation— not just opposite truth values.

You've learned that contradictory propositions cannot both be true or both false. But not every pair of affirmative and negative statements is truly contradictory. The author illustrates this with seven examples from everyday language and the Qur'an. This lesson will train you to spot the hidden differences that dissolve apparent contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions
Find the pair of statements that seem to affirm and deny the same thing. Write them down.
2
Check the subject term
Does the subject refer to the same entity in both propositions? If the word is ambiguous (homonym), the subjects differ and contradiction fails.
Example (أ): 'Tripoli' in Syria vs. 'Tripoli' in Libya.
3
Check the predicate term
Does the predicate have the same meaning in both? If it has multiple meanings (homonym) or is interpreted differently, contradiction fails.
Example (ب): 'generous' as bountiful vs. resembling a deer.
4
Check time, place, or other conditions
Are the propositions about the same time, place, relation, or aspect? If these differ, the propositions are not contradictory.
Example (ھ): drowsiness at midnight vs. after sunrise.
5
Conclude whether contradiction exists
If any of the above conditions differ, the propositions are not contradictory. They may both be true, both false, or one true and one false.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the logical conditions for contradiction (تناقض) by showing when two propositions that appear contradictory are actually not. The author begins by stating a general rule: if the two propositions differ in their conditions (شروط) or what they are related to (متعلقات), then they are not essentially opposed (متقابلتين), meaning they are not genuine contradictories. Consequently, the difference in quality (affirmative vs. negative) does not produce contradiction. Such propositions can both be true, both false, or one true and one false—just like any two unrelated propositions.

The author then provides seven examples (أ–ز) to illustrate various ways contradiction can fail:

- Example (أ): Two sentences with identical wording 'The city of Tripoli is on the Syrian coast' and its negation, but the subject refers to two different cities (Tripoli in Syria vs. Tripoli in Libya). Here the subject is equivocal; contradiction fails because the terms are not identical in meaning.

- Example (ب): The predicate 'generous' (كريم) is used homonymously: one meaning 'bountiful', the other 'resembling a deer'. Similarly, 'Masrur' as a name vs. as a psychological state. Predicate ambiguity breaks contradiction.

- Example (ج): A poetic line about a man who sleeps with one eye and is awake with the other. The two statements 'he is awake' and 'he is asleep' are true of different aspects, so no contradiction.

- Example (د): Comparing Khalid and Sa'id in body vs. age; Salih and Rabih in knowledge vs. chest width. The predicate 'bigger' or 'broader' is interpreted differently in each proposition.

- Example (هـ): Time difference: drowsiness at midnight vs. not after sunrise.

- Example (و): The Quranic verse about the Prophet's throwing; negation refutes the effect (killing), affirmation confirms the physical act. The predicate 'threw' differs in meaning: one refers to the result, the other to the action itself.

- Example (ز): Eating meat of sheep vs. lizard or pig; the predicate 'meat' is restricted by type.

In each case, the apparent contradiction dissolves because one of the seven conditions for genuine contradiction (like unity of subject, predicate, time, place, relation, etc.) is violated. The passage thus serves to sharpen the student's understanding of contradiction by showing its boundaries.

Unity Conditions for Contradiction
Author explains conditions that break subject-predicate unity and prevent contradiction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to test whether two propositions form a true contradiction by identifying any differences in attached conditions (time, place, attributes, etc.).

Core Idea
Any condition change breaks the unity needed for contradiction.

Contradiction is a powerful logical tool, but it only works when propositions are perfectly aligned. The passage explains that even a single difference in the circumstances attached to the subject or predicate—whether in time, place, attribute, or state—destroys the opposition. Let's see how to apply this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and predicate of each proposition
Write down the subject (موضوع) and predicate (محمول) for both statements. Ensure you capture the exact wording.
The subject might be a single term or a description.
2
Check if predicates are contradictory in meaning
Are the predicates exactly opposite, like 'standing' and 'not standing', or 'knowing' and 'not knowing'? If not, no contradiction is possible even if other conditions match.
3
List all attached conditions
Examine each proposition for any qualifiers: time (e.g., 'morning prayer'), place (e.g., 'in the house'), attributes (e.g., 'as a scholar'), or other restrictions.
Conditions can be explicit or implied.
4
Compare conditions between the two propositions
If any condition differs (e.g., one refers to Zaid in the house, the other to Zaid outside), the unity is broken, and no contradiction exists.
This includes partial differences in the subject or predicate.
5
Conclude about contradiction validity
If all conditions are identical and predicates are opposites, it is a true contradiction. Otherwise, it is not.

Commentary
This passage discusses the requirement of unity for contradiction. The author states that contradiction is nullified when the predicate's attachment differs, which is essentially a difference in the predicate itself. The same applies to any conditions or qualifiers that modify the subject or predicate. For example, in fiqh, "Zaid is praying" and "Zaid is not praying" are contradictory only if the time and place are the same. If one refers to the morning prayer and the other to the afternoon prayer, there is no contradiction. Thus, any alteration in the circumstances—whether attributes, states, accidents, times, or places—breaks the necessary unity, and the opposition ceases to be a true contradiction.

Finding the Contradictory of a Universal Affirmative Proposition
Author begins section on contradiction in quantified propositions, using examples to derive the rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the contradictory of a universal affirmative proposition using the method of example-based logical analysis.

Core Idea
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is the particular negative, not the universal negative.

In this passage, the author begins to explore how contradiction works for quantified propositions. Using examples, we see that even when a universal affirmative is false, its contradictory must be true. The author tests the universal negative as a candidate but implies it fails because both can be false. This lesson teaches you to derive the correct contradictory by focusing on logical form rather than content.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition type
The example given is 'Every animal is a human' – a universal affirmative proposition. Note its quantity (universal) and quality (affirmative). It is provided as false.
2
Test the candidate contradictory
Propose the universal negative 'No animal is a human' as a possible contradictory. Check if it is necessarily true when the original is false. The text implies this candidate fails because both universal affirmative and universal negative can be false together (e.g., some animals are humans, some are not).
3
Check logical structure
Recognize that for a universal affirmative (A), the contradictory must differ in both quantity and quality. The universal negative (E) differs only in quality, so it is a contrary, not a contradictory.
The correct contradictory is the particular negative (O): 'Some animal is not a human'.
4
Derive the correct contradictory
The contradictory of a universal affirmative is always a particular negative: 'Some S is not P'. This ensures that exactly one is true and one is false for any content.
This rule applies to all universal affirmative propositions regardless of subject matter.

The Contradictory of Universal Propositions with Exclusive Terms
Specifies the rule for forming contradictories of universal propositions with exclusive term relations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to form the correct contradictory of any universal categorical proposition where the subject and predicate are related as distinct, opposite, or contradictory, by changing only the quality.

Core Idea
For universal propositions with exclusive or opposite terms, the contradictory is simply the universal with the opposite quality.

In this passage, the author explains that understanding contradiction is crucial for demonstration. Sometimes a demonstration proves something indirectly by refuting its contradictory. The author then gives a specific rule: when a universal proposition has a subject and predicate that are distinct, opposite, or contradictory, its contradictory is formed by only changing the quality without altering the quantity. This rule ensures a perfect contradiction between the two propositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the universal proposition and its quality
Determine whether the proposition is affirmative or negative. For example, 'Every angel is a human' is universal affirmative.
2
Check the term relation
Examine if the subject and predicate are distinct (e.g., angel and human), opposite (e.g., white and black), or contradictory (e.g., existent and non-existent). If yes, proceed.
If not, the rule may differ.
3
Change only the quality
If affirmative, make it negative; if negative, make it affirmative. Keep the quantity universal.
Do not change quantity.
4
Verify the contradiction
The original and result should be opposite in truth value. For example, 'Every angel is a human' is false, 'No angel is a human' is true.

Subalternation: The Relationship Between Universal and Particular Propositions
Author defines subalternation between quantified propositions and lists its logical implications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define subalternation, state the four logical implications between a universal and its corresponding particular proposition, and apply these rules to any pair of affirmative or negative propositions in Islamic reasoning.

Core Idea
Universal truth guarantees particular truth; particular falsehood guarantees universal falsehood.

This passage introduces the concept of subalternation (al-tadākhul), which describes the logical relationship between a universal proposition and its corresponding particular proposition when they share the same quality. Understanding subalternation is crucial for correct reasoning and avoiding fallacies in Islamic legal and theological arguments, such as when inferring rulings from general principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two propositions in subalternation
Subalternation involves two propositions that differ only in quantity (universal vs particular) but agree in quality (both affirmative or both negative). For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (universal affirmative) and 'Some intoxicant is forbidden' (particular affirmative).
2
Check the truth direction from universal to particular
If the universal is true, the particular must be true. Because the universal asserts about all individuals, so it includes the particular. For instance, if 'Every act of worship is intended for Allah' is true, then 'Some act of worship is intended for Allah' is necessarily true.
3
Check the truth direction from particular to universal (only false upward)
If the particular is false, the universal must be false. If even some individuals are false, the universal cannot be true. For example, if 'Some prayer is invalid' is false, then 'No prayer is invalid' (universal negative) must also be false because if some prayer is invalid the universal negative cannot hold.
4
Note the non-implications
The truth of the particular does NOT imply truth of the universal; and the falsehood of the universal does NOT imply falsehood of the particular. For example, 'Some animals are human' is true but 'Every animal is human' is false. And 'Every human is an animal' being false does not make 'Some human is an animal' false (it is true).
This is a common pitfall in reasoning.

The Four Logical Relations of Categorical Propositions
Author defines the four logical relations between categorical propositions with truth conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradiction, contrariety, subcontrariety, and subalternation between categorical propositions and apply their truth conditions to Islamic legal and theological examples.

Core Idea
Four relations govern truth transfer between opposites: contradiction flips, contrariety blocks both true, subcontrariety blocks both false, subalternation flows downward.

This passage lays out the four fundamental relations among categorical propositions that form the square of opposition. Understanding these is essential for valid inference in Islamic logic, especially when evaluating arguments in fiqh and kalam. For example, knowing that a universal affirmative implies the particular affirmative allows you to derive rulings from general principles.

Guided Path
1
Parse contradiction (tanāquḍ)
Identify that two contradictory propositions always have opposite truth values: if one is true the other is false, and vice versa. Note the pairs: personal affirmative vs personal negative, universal affirmative (A) vs particular negative (O), and universal negative (E) vs particular affirmative (I).
Contradiction is the strongest opposition; it requires difference in both quality and quantity.
2
Parse contrariety (taḍādd)
Two universal propositions of opposite quality (A and E) cannot both be true, but they can both be false. For example, 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (A) and 'No intoxicant is forbidden' (E) cannot both be true, but both could be false if only some intoxicants are forbidden.
Contrariety applies only to universals.
3
Parse subcontrariety (dukhūl taḥt al-taḍādd)
Two particular propositions of opposite quality (I and O) cannot both be false, but they can both be true. For instance, 'Some intoxicant is forbidden' (I) and 'Some intoxicant is not forbidden' (O) can both be true (if some are forbidden and some are not), but cannot both be false.
This relation is sometimes called 'subcontrariety' or 'inverse of contrariety'.
4
Parse subalternation (tadākhul)
The truth of a universal proposition implies the truth of its corresponding particular (A→I, E→O), but not vice versa. Falsity of the particular implies falsity of the universal (¬I→¬A, ¬O→¬E). For example, if 'Every intoxicant is forbidden' (A) is true, then 'Some intoxicant is forbidden' (I) must be true; if I is false, A must be false.
Subalternation is a one-directional entailment from universal to particular.

The Seventh Badihi: Al-Khafi al-Mutawatir
Author defines the seventh type of badihi with Islamic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a khafi mutawatir proposition and distinguish it from other types of badihi.

Core Idea
Recurrent testimony from a group unable to collude yields certain knowledge, making propositions self-evident for those in the tradition.

In this lesson, we explore the seventh type of badihi: al-khafi al-mutawatir (the hidden recurrent). This type of proposition is grounded in the concept of tawatur (recurrent testimony) from Islamic epistemology. The author provides a clear definition and Islamic examples, showing how certain historical and religious facts become self-evident for Muslims through widespread transmission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition
What is the judgment being made? For example, 'The Quran is a book transmitted by Muslims from Muhammad.'
It should be a claim about a historical or factual matter.
2
Check the basis of the judgment
Is the judgment based on testimony from a group that it is habitually impossible to collude on a lie? If so, it may be khafi mutawatir.
The testimony must be from a sufficiently large and independent group.
3
Verify the condition of tawatur
Ensure that the report has been transmitted continuously (mutawatir) without interruption, and that the transmitters are reliable and cannot have conspired.
This is a standard condition in hadith and usul al-fiqh.
4
Consider the awareness of the individual
For whom is this proposition self-evident? The author specifies that it is self-evident for one who is aware of the transmission or who lives in an Islamic society.
The certainty depends on the recipient's exposure to the mutawatir report.
