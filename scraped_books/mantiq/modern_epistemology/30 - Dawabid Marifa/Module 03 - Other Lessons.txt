The Three Paths of Perception
Author identifies the three ways perceptions reach the mind

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and categorize the three paths of perception as described in the passage and apply them to examples from Islamic sciences.

Core Idea
Knowledge enters the mind through three channels: senses, inner feelings, and reports.

How do we come to know anything? This passage lays the epistemological foundation by identifying the three routes through which raw perceptions reach the mind. Understanding these paths is crucial for evaluating the reliability of knowledge claims in any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Locate the three paths
Find the three ways listed by the author. They are senses, inner feelings, and reports. Notice the order and any qualifying descriptions.
2
Analyze the first path: external senses
The external senses convey information about the external world. The author notes they can be sound or impaired. Ask: In Islamic law, how does sense perception function in witnessing or determining the start of Ramadan?
3
Analyze the second path: inner feelings
These are subjective emotions and states. The author says judgments here are subjective unless they agree with others. Think of inner certainty (yaqin) in faith: is it purely subjective or can it be shared?
4
Analyze the third path: reports
Reports convey knowledge from others, whether true or false. In hadith studies, reports are classified by reliability (mutawatir vs. ahad). Consider how the authenticity of a report affects its epistemic value.
The author does not discuss degrees of reliability here, but you can anticipate that later.
5
Synthesize the three paths
Recognize that these three paths are sources of raw data, not processed knowledge. They provide the material for reasoning (which will be covered in later lessons). Check your understanding by classifying given knowledge claims into one of the three paths.

When we are born, the page of our minds is white, devoid of any scientific or perceptual recordings. From our birth, the tapes of our minds begin recording what comes to them of perceptions. The ways that convey perceptions to our minds are three: The first way: our five external senses; they convey to our minds what they can sense of the forms of the external world. Commentary
This passage introduces the sources of human knowledge, classifying them into three distinct channels. The author begins by describing the mind as a blank slate at birth, which then receives perceptions through three routes. First, the five external senses, which may be accurate or faulty. Second, internal emotional states, which are subjective but can sometimes align with others' experiences. Third, reports from others, which may be true or false. These three categories form the raw material for all intellectual operations.

The Three Types of Signification: Conformity, Implication, and Compliance
Author defines three types of denotation with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalālat al-muṭābaqa, al-taḍammun, and al-iltizām, and identify which type is at work in a given linguistic expression from Islamic texts.

Core Idea
Words signify in three ways: full meaning, part of meaning, or necessary implication.

In this passage, the author classifies how words convey meaning—a crucial skill for interpreting Qur'an, Hadith, and legal texts correctly. Understanding these three types of signification will sharpen your analytical reading of any Islamic source. Let's explore each type with concrete examples from the tradition.

Guided Path
1
Understand dalaalat al-mutabaqa (conformity)
Focus on the definition: the word indicates the entirety of its literal or figurative meaning. The author gives the example of 'insan' signifying the complete concept of 'rational animal'. Notice the correspondence between the word and the full understanding derived from it.
Think of it as the default, straightforward use of a word.
2
Understand dalaalat al-tadammun (implication)
Here the word indicates only a part of its meaning. The author uses the same word 'insan' but now to convey just 'animal' or just 'rational' in context. The part is understood within the whole, but the whole is not the intended meaning.
This is like synecdoche in rhetoric, but logically distinct.
3
Understand dalaalat al-iltizam (compliance)
The word indicates a meaning external to its own definition but necessarily connected intellectually or conventionally. The definition is given but no examples in this passage; it is entailed by the word's meaning.
For example, 'roof' entails 'wall' by necessary coexistence.
4
Practice distinguishing the three types
Given a phrase like 'I saw a lion in the battle'—where 'lion' means 'courageous man'—determine which type of signification is used. Then do the same with a Qur'anic example: 'slaughter a cow' where the genus name suffices for any cow.

And here is the explanation of these three sections with clarification by examples:
Signification of Conformity:
We know that a word's signification of the entirety of its literal or figurative meaning is called signification of conformity, and it was named conformity due to the correspondence between the meaning of the word and the understanding derived from it.
Examples of signification of conformity:
Like the signification of the word 'human' for the rational animal, for the word 'human' is assigned to a being possessing animality and rationality, and when from this word the entirety of this meaning is derived, then correspondence has occurred between its meaning and the understanding derived from it.
And like the word 'human', all names of genera when they denote in usage the entirety of their literal or figurative meanings, such as the saying of Allah the Exalted: 'Indeed Allah commands you to slaughter a cow' — the word 'cow' is a name of a genus brought to denote the entirety of what it was assigned to literally, which is the known animal, so any cow would have sufficed to execute the command had the Children of Israel slaughtered it, but they imposed hardship on themselves by requesting specification, so Allah imposed hardship on them. And like your saying: 'I saw a lion fighting in the ranks with the fighting of one desperate for death' — the word 'lion' in this sentence was brought to denote figuratively the entirety of the figurative meaning, which is the courageous human.
And like the signification of proper names for their referents, and like the signification of verbs for the entirety of their literal or figurative meanings, and like the signification of sentences for the entirety of their meanings as well.
Signification of Implication:
And we know that signification of implication is the word's signification of a part of its literal or figurative meaning, and it was called implication because part of the meaning is understood within the understanding of the entire meaning, except that understanding the entire meaning was not intended, but rather the intended thing is the understanding of this part, and when the word indicates it and other things, it is possible to pick out the intended part found within the meaning that includes it and other things.
Examples:
Like the signification of the word 'human' for 'animal' only or for 'rational' only. This can be depicted by a questioner asking about a distant figure: 'Is it inanimate or animal?' and he has no purpose to know more than that, so we say to him: 'It is human', and from this word he derives that it is an animal not an inanimate, and this meaning is a part of the meaning for which the word 'human' was assigned. Or he asks: 'Is it rational or a non-rational animal?' so we say to him: 'It is human', and from this word he derives by way of signification of implication that it is rational; as for it being an animal, that is something already known to him, and what he derived is its being rational, and this is a part of the meaning of 'human'.
And also among the examples: A person says: 'I am knowledgeable about the obligatory shares and the division of inheritances', so we say to him: 'Then explain to us the rulings of the grandfather with the siblings', and he says: 'I did not tell you that I know these rulings', so we say to him: 'Your claim of knowledge of the obligatory shares and the division of inheritances implied that you are knowledgeable about the rulings of the grandfather with the siblings, and we understood this from your speech by way of signification of implication.'
And the doctor says to the patient: 'You need vitamins, so eat more fruits and uncooked vegetables.' If he says that to him, he has implicitly guided him to the fact that vitamins are naturally present in fruits and uncooked vegetables, and he has indicated them to him by way of signification of implication.

And we also know that signification of compliance is the word's signification of a meaning external to its literal or figurative meaning, but it is necessary for it intellectually or conventionally, and it was called signification of compliance because the derived meaning was not directly indicated by the word, but its meaning entails in the intellect or convention this derived meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of semantic signification (dalala). The author presents three types: conformity (mutabaqa), implication (tadammun), and compliance (iltizam). For each, he provides a definition and examples rooted in Arabic usage and Islamic contexts.

First, signification of conformity: the word indicates its full meaning, whether literal or figurative. The example of 'insan' (human) for 'hayawan natiq' (rational animal) illustrates that when the word is used, the entire concept is understood. The Qur'anic example of 'baqara' (cow) in the verse about slaughtering a cow shows that the genus name 'cow' denotes any cow; the full literal meaning is intended. The figurative example of 'asad' (lion) for a courageous man shows that the word denotes the entire figurative meaning. The author also notes that proper names, verbs, and sentences all signify by conformity when they convey their full meanings.

Second, signification of implication: the word indicates only a part of its meaning. The classic example is using 'insan' to convey only 'animal' or only 'rational' depending on context. The author illustrates with a scenario: a person asks whether a distant figure is animate or inanimate; the answer 'it is human' conveys the part 'animal' without the full definition. Another scenario shows understanding 'rational' from 'human' when the animal aspect is already known. The fiqh example of claiming knowledge of inheritance law implies knowledge of specific rulings like the grandfather with siblings, which is a part of that knowledge. The medical example of a doctor advising vitamins implies that vitamins exist in the recommended foods.

Third, signification of compliance: the word indicates something external to its meaning but necessarily connected intellectually or conventionally. The definition is given but no examples in this passage; the text likely continues with examples on the next page. The name 'compliance' comes from the idea that the derived meaning is not directly mentioned but is entailed by the word's meaning in the mind or custom.

Throughout, the author emphasizes that these distinctions help us understand how words convey meaning in different ways, which is foundational for logical analysis in Islamic discourse.

Two Ways to Classify Necessary Concomitants
Classification of necessary concomitants via two approaches.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two main approaches to classifying necessary concomitants (al-lazim) and identify examples of each type.

Core Idea
Necessary concomitants are classified either by existential realm or by evidentness of the relation.

This passage presents two systematic ways of dividing al-lazim: one by realm of existence (mental, external, or both) and another by whether the relation is self-evident or requires proof. Understanding these classifications is essential for analysing how logicians establish necessary relationships between concepts in Islamic disciplines.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two approaches
Find where the author introduces each approach using 'الطريق الأول' and 'الطريق الثاني'. Recognize that these are two alternative schemas.
The first approach uses three categories; the second uses two with a subdivision.
2
Analyse the first approach
List the three types: (1) in mind and external together, (2) in mind only, (3) in external only. Note the examples for each.
Example for type 2: conception of sight when imagining blindness.
3
Analyse the second approach
Distinguish evident (bayyin) from non-evident (ghayr bayyin). The evident is further split into mental and non-mental.
The mental evident is when conceiving the accompanied suffices to conceive the concomitant.
4
Compare the two approaches
Notice that the first approach focuses on where the relation exists (ontology), while the second focuses on how it is known (epistemology).
The same relation can be classified differently under each approach.

Commentary
This passage classifies necessary concomitants (al-lāzim) into two different schemes. First, the author notes that the rational necessary concomitance is called 'evident' (bayyin). The footnote expands this into two types: evident in the specific sense (al-lāzim al-bayyin bi al-ma‘nā al-akhass) and evident in the general sense (al-bayyin bi al-ma‘nā al-a‘amm). The specific sense is when merely conceiving the accompanied (malzūm) suffices to conceive the concomitant. The general sense requires conceiving both to conceive the concomitance. Then the author presents two methods of dividing the necessary concomitant. The first method divides it into three: (1) both mental and external, (2) mental only, (3) external only. Examples are given: the need of an originated thing for an originator is both mental and external; the concept of sight when imagining blindness is mental only; the blackness of the crow is external only. The second method divides it into evident and non-evident. The evident is further divided into mental and non-mental. The mental evident is when conceiving the accompanied suffices; the non-mental requires both. The non-evident requires proof. This classification helps understand how logicians analyze necessary relations.

The Universal: Definition and Linguistic Indicators
Defines the universal and gives linguistic indicators and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of the universal and recognize its linguistic indicators in Arabic.

Core Idea
The universal is a mental concept that can be shared by many, even if only one exists.

This lesson begins by defining the universal (al-kullī), a foundational concept in Islamic logic. You will learn how the universal is a mental concept that allows for participation, and how it is signaled in Arabic speech through indefinite nouns and definite nouns indicating genus. Examples like 'sun' and 'moon' illustrate that universality is conceptual, not dependent on external multiplicity.

Guided Path
1
Examine the definition
Read the definition of the universal: 'every mental concept whose conception does not prevent participation in it.' Note that it is a mental concept (mafhūm dhihnī) and the key condition is that the concept can be shared by multiple individuals in the mind, even if in reality there is only one or none.
2
Identify linguistic indicators
The text states that indefinite nouns (nakirāt) and definite nouns that function like indefinites (e.g., nouns with 'al' for the genus) signal universality. Think of examples in Arabic like 'rajul' (a man) or 'al-insān' (mankind).
3
Analyze the examples

This shows universality is about the mental concept, not actual plurality.
4
Distinguish the universal from the particular
Contrast the universal with the particular (juz'ī), which prevents participation. The universal does not prevent sharing; the particular does. Confirm that the definition excludes concepts that can only be applied to one individual (like 'this specific sun').
The passage does not define particular explicitly, but the contrast is implied.

The Six Divisions of the Universal
Classifies the universal into six divisions based on external existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given universal concept into one of the six divisions based on the existence and possibility of its individuals.

Core Idea
The six divisions map the relationship between a universal concept and the external existence of its individuals.

This lesson presents the classification of the universal into six divisions, based on whether its individuals can exist, do exist, and how many. The divisions range from rationally impossible individuals (e.g., partner of God) to actually infinite individuals (e.g., God's knowledge). Understanding these divisions helps in analyzing the ontological status of concepts used in theology and other Islamic sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of universal
Remember that a universal is a concept that can apply to multiple individuals in the mind. The six divisions depend on the external existence and possibility of those individuals.
2
Examine the basis of division
The author divides the universal with respect to the existence of its individuals in the external world and with respect to quantity and quality. The key factors: (a) whether existence of any individual is possible or impossible; (b) whether individuals actually exist; (c) how many exist (finite or infinite).
3
Work through the six divisions
List the six divisions: 1) impossible existence of any individual (sharik al-Bari); 2) possible but no individual exists ('anqa'); 3) possible but only one exists (sun, early human); 4) impossible to have more than one (Wajib al-Wujud); 5) possible unlimited but only finite exist (human, star); 6) possible and actually infinite (God's knowledge, number).
4
Apply to new concepts
Take a concept like 'angel' (malak). Determine: is its existence possible? Yes. Are there individuals? Yes. How many? Possibly many but finite? Or infinite? In Islamic belief, angels are created beings, so finite. Thus it falls under the fifth division. Practice with other concepts from Islamic theology.

External Senses and the Process of Knowledge Acquisition
External senses as sources of empirical knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the process by which external sensory experiences lead to knowledge, including the roles of memory and thought, and identify the limitations of the external senses.

Core Idea
Senses are windows; experience and memory are the gatekeepers of certain knowledge.

This lesson explores how the five external senses (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch) form the foundation of empirical knowledge. The author explains that sensory experiences, when repeated, are recorded by memory and then processed by thought. However, he also warns that the senses are limited—both in range and in the conditions required for accurate perception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five external senses
From the passage, list the five external senses mentioned: touch (lams), taste (dhawq), sight (basar), hearing (sam‘), and smell (shamm). For each, note what kind of attributes it perceives.
The author gives specific examples for each sense: touch perceives heat/cold, etc.; taste perceives sweetness; sight perceives colors, shapes; hearing perceives sounds; smell perceives odors.
2
Trace the knowledge acquisition process
Follow the author's description: sensation → repeated experience (khibra) → confirmation by repetition → recording in memory (dhakira) → processing by thought (fikr). Understand that memory only records what is confirmed, and thought works on stored images.
The author says: 'memory records what the senses confirm through repetition of experience, then thought begins its work.'
3
Identify the limitations of external senses
The author notes two types of limitations: quantitative (cannot perceive beyond a certain distance) and qualitative (each sense requires specific conditions, e.g., light for sight, magnification for tiny objects). People also vary in sensory strength.
The passage refers to senses as 'short-range windows limited quantitatively and qualitatively.'
4
Summarize the epistemological claim
The overall claim is that external sensory knowledge is foundational but imperfect. Certainty (yaqin) comes from repeated experience confirming the data, not from a single sensation.
This aligns with the Islamic epistemological distinction between necessary (daruri) and acquired (muktasab) knowledge.

Internal Senses and the Limits of Empirical Knowledge
Internal senses and limitations of sensory knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between external and internal sensory knowledge and explain why both are limited according to the author.

Core Idea
Our inner experiences are like outer senses: limited and dependent on memory and thought.

This lesson examines the internal senses (al-hawas al-batina), which perceive inner experiences such as love, hatred, and emotional states. The author parallels the process for internal and external senses: both involve experience, memory, and thought. He concludes that internal senses, like external ones, are limited. This highlights the overall partiality of human empirical knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand what internal senses are
The author refers to al-hawas al-batina as the faculty that perceives internal experiences: love, hatred, constriction/expansion of the chest (i.e., anxiety/relief), spite, envy, etc. These are felt introspectively.
These are not the five internal senses of classical philosophy but a broader notion of inner feelings.
2
Compare the process with external senses
For internal senses, the same sequence occurs: experience (e.g., love) → thought perceives it → memory records it → thought works on the recorded material. This mirrors the external sense process.
The author says: 'thought perceives these experiences, and memory records them, then thought begins its work on what is recorded.'
3
Note the limitation of internal senses
The author states clearly: 'And the internal senses are limited, just as the external senses are limited.' Though he does not elaborate further on the nature of their limitation, the comparison implies they have similar constraints.
One can infer that internal senses cannot perceive all inner states (e.g., one cannot feel another's love directly) and may require conditions (e.g., self-awareness).
4
Summarize the overall epistemological picture
The author presents a unified view: all empirical knowledge—whether from external or internal sources—is acquired through the same tripartite process (sensation/experience, memory, thought) and is inherently limited.
This supports the Islamic emphasis on revelation as a necessary supplement to empirical knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is an epistemological exposition on the sources of human knowledge, focusing on sensory experience (both external and internal) and the role of memory and thought. The author begins by distinguishing types of knowledge: acquired knowledge (al-ma'arif al-muktasaba) includes what one personally learns, what one receives from others, and what is inspired by God to prophets (revelation). The focus then shifts to ordinary empirical knowledge gained through the five external senses (al-hawas al-zahira). The author provides examples: touch (al-lams) perceives heat, cold, roughness, etc.; taste (al-dhawq) perceives sweetness; sight (al-basar) perceives colors, shapes, dimensions; hearing (al-sam') perceives sounds; smell (al-sham) perceives odors. Each sense yields experiences (khibra) that accumulate and form knowledge. The author then describes the process: senses are like windows (manafidh) through which thought (al-fikr) views the world; sensory data is transmitted to intellectual perception; memory (al-dhakira) records what the senses confirm through repeated experience; then thought works on the stored images. Next, the author notes the limitations of the senses: they are limited quantitatively (cannot perceive beyond a certain distance) and qualitatively (require specific conditions, e.g., sight needs light, tiny objects need magnifiers). People also vary in sensory strength. Finally, the author discusses internal senses (al-hawas al-batina), which perceive internal experiences such as love, hatred, constriction or expansion of the chest, spite, envy. These are also recorded by memory and processed by thought. The passage concludes that internal senses are limited like external ones. The overall argument is that empirical knowledge, though foundational, is limited and dependent on both external and internal perception.

The intellect is limited but not useless; it operates within its domain.

All examples used are from general epistemological concepts, not specific Islamic sciences, but the discussion of necessary/impossible/possible is rooted in kalam, and the reference to al-Ghazali ties it to Islamic thought.

These include acknowledging impossibility, possibility, and the need for a cause for originated things. He then mentions that the intellect recognizes the possibility of numerical series but stops at infinity; it also recognizes the impossibility of circularity and infinite regress of events. 

Next, the author discusses the power of imagination (takhayyul), which can combine known parts to form new compounds but cannot invent entirely new parts not derived from sense perception. The imagination works through analysis, synthesis, addition, and subtraction. 

The author then turns to the limits of the intellect. Like the senses, the intellect has boundaries: it is bound by time and space, always asking 'when?' and 'where?'. It cannot conceive of realities that transcend time and space. A specific example is the question of whether the universe is finite or infinite. The intellect cannot decide between these two possibilities, because both lead to contradictions: if finite, there is something beyond; if infinite, it defies sensory logic. The intellect remains stuck in oscillation. 

Finally, the author addresses the unseen world (ghayb). The intellect cannot independently judge the unseen except through its self-evident laws (a priori principles) such as non-contradiction, the three modes of existence (necessary, impossible, possible), causality, etc. The author quotes al-Ghazali to emphasize the difference between what the intellect cannot perceive (it neither affirms nor denies) and what it can judge with certainty (either impossible or necessary). The intellect is limited but not useless; it operates within its domain.

All examples used are from general epistemological concepts, not specific Islamic sciences, but the discussion of necessary/impossible/possible is rooted in kalam, and the reference to al-Ghazali ties it to Islamic thought.

Distinguishing Rational Impossibility from Non-Perception
Author discusses the mind's inability to conceive infinity/finitude and distinguishes rational impossibility from non-perception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between what the mind can decisively judge as impossible and what lies beyond its perception, using the self-evident laws of reason.

Core Idea
Reason has limits, but within its limits it delivers certain judgments.

The human mind faces a dilemma when contemplating the universe: it cannot fully conceive infinite extension nor absolute finitude. Yet reason has self-evident laws that allow it to judge the unseen with certainty. This lesson clarifies how to distinguish between matters that are rationally impossible (contradictions) and matters that are simply beyond the mind's perception—a critical skill for sound theological reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mind's dilemma
Notice that the mind cannot accept the universe as finite (because 'what after the end?') nor as infinite (because that seems inconceivable to the senses). This hesitation arises from the mind's inherent limitation—it is 'limited' (محدود).
This is not a logical contradiction but a perceptual limitation.
2
Extract the self-evident laws of reason
The author lists several laws that the mind applies to the unseen: non-contradiction, impossibility, the trichotomy of necessary/impossible/possible, causality, possibility of infinite regression in conception but impossibility in actual past events, etc. List them as the mind's tools for certain judgment.
3
Apply the distinction: rational impossibility vs. non-perception
The author, citing al-Ghazali, states that what is beyond the mind may be far from conception, but the mind can never judge as impossible something that is merely beyond its perception. Only things that contradict the self-evident laws (e.g., a square circle) are truly impossible.
Example: The mind cannot perceive Allah's essence, but it knows that attributing imperfection to Him is impossible due to the law of non-contradiction.
4
Test the distinction with a new example
Given a claim about the unseen, ask: (1) Does it violate a self-evident law of reason? If yes, it is rationally impossible. (2) Is it merely beyond sensory or intellectual perception? If yes, it cannot be affirmed or denied by reason alone—it requires revelation.
This protects against overextending reason or abandoning it in the unseen.

Verifiability: The Fourth Condition of a Scientific Hypothesis
Fourth condition and value of scientific hypothesis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition of verifiability to distinguish between hypotheses that are provable and those that are not, using Islamic examples.

Core Idea
A hypothesis must be capable of proof; otherwise it is valueless, no matter how plausible.

The fourth condition stresses that a scientific hypothesis must be verifiable. The author gives Darwin's theory and the nebular hypothesis as examples of unverifiable claims. In Islamic thought, this mirrors the requirement that claims in kalam and fiqh must be demonstrable through reason or revelation.

Guided Path
1
Read the fourth condition carefully
The condition states: 'The hypothesis must be among things capable of being proven. If it is not capable of that, it is rejected from the start.' Note the phrase 'qabila li-an yubrahan 'alayha' (capable of being proven).
The author uses the term 'burhan' (demonstration) which connects to classical mantiq.
2
Analyze the examples provided
The author cites Darwin's hypothesis on human evolution from animals and the nebular hypothesis on the universe's origin. Both are considered unverifiable and also contradictory to rational principles.
In Islamic context, such hypotheses might be rejected because they cannot be proven empirically or rationally, or because they contradict revelation.
3
Translate to Islamic sciences
Consider hypotheses in kalam, such as 'the world is eternal' vs. 'the world is created'. The first is not verifiable by empirical means and contradicts rational proofs for huduth (temporal origination), so it is rejected. The second is verifiable through the argument from contingency.
This mirrors the condition: if a hypothesis cannot be proven and contradicts established rational principles, it is invalid.
4
Understand the consequence of ignoring this condition
Without verifiability, anyone could propose any mental possibility, leading to endless speculation and harm to knowledge.
This reinforces the need for rigorous criteria in both science and Islamic theology.

The Method of Co-extensiveness and Co-exclusiveness (Dawaran)
Explains the third method: dawaran by co-extensiveness and co-exclusiveness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of the dawaran method and evaluate its strength as an inductive argument.

Core Idea
Dawaran combines co-presence and co-absence to yield strong presumption, not certainty.

The method of dawaran is a powerful inductive tool used to infer causality by observing that when a phenomenon is present, a particular factor is also present, and when the phenomenon is absent, that factor is also absent. This lesson breaks down its logical structure using the crime example from the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phenomenon and candidate cause
The phenomenon is crime prevalence; the candidate cause is weak religious restraint. The passage compares countries with high and low crime rates.
Ensure the candidate cause is defined clearly before proceeding.
2
Apply co-presence (method of agreement)
Observe that in countries with many crimes, the same factor (weak religious restraint) is present. This eliminates other differing factors as potential causes.
The passage states: 'most individuals... agree on nothing except weakness of religious restraint'.
3
Apply co-absence (method of difference)
Observe that in countries with few crimes, the only differing factor is strong religious restraint. The absence of the factor correlates with absence of the phenomenon.
The passage: 'differ in nothing... except in strength of religious restraint'.
4
Conclude causality by co-extensiveness
Since the cause and effect are present and absent together, the factor is identified as the cause (here: weak religious restraint causes crime).
The author explicitly calls this 'the existence of the phenomenon when the cause exists, and the absence when the cause is absent'.

Commentary
This passage defines the third method of extracting a cause ('illa) in inductive reasoning, known as the method of co-extensiveness and co-exclusiveness (dawaran). The author begins by illustrating the method with an example: comparing countries with high and low crime rates shows that the only common factor in high-crime countries is weak religious restraint, and in low-crime countries it is strong religious restraint. Observing both cases together reveals that the phenomenon (crime) exists when the supposed cause (weak religious restraint) exists and is absent when the cause is absent. This is the classic method of difference. The author then evaluates the method: it combines the earlier methods (presumably the method of agreement and method of difference) and therefore is stronger than either alone, analogous to two witnesses being stronger than one. However, it does not yield certainty but only a strongly probable opinion (zan mu'akkad). The same defects as the individual methods apply, but their probability is reduced. Next, the passage connects this method to Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh), where it is known as 'dawaran al-'illa ma'a al-ma'lul' (co-extensiveness of cause and effect). In fiqh, the existence of a ruling (hukm) with a description (wasf) and its absence without the description indicates that the description is the cause. This method is used for 'takhrij manat al-hukm' (deriving the effective cause). The text gives a classic fiqh example: the prohibition of wine is present when intoxicating strength exists and absent when it does not, proving that the intoxicating strength is the cause. The author notes that some scholars argue this proof works even more strongly in intellectual matters (where causality is necessary) than in legal matters (where the cause is an indicator, not a necessitating factor). Finally, the author comments that this proof yields predominant presumption in natural causes, sufficient for practical theories unless contradicted by stronger evidence.

Applies dawaran to extract the 'illa in usul al-fiqh.

In usul al-fiqh, the method of dawaran is a primary tool for 'takhrij manat al-hukm' (deriving the effective cause). Two Types of Entailment: Mutual and One-Way

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all entailments are reversible: know the difference between talazum and one-way luzum.

Logical entailment (luzum) governs how one proposition necessarily follows from another. In Islamic reasoning—from fiqh to kalam—understanding whether an implication runs in both directions or only one is crucial. This lesson unpacks the second rule: two types of entailment, illustrated with concrete examples from everyday and legal contexts.

Guided Path

2
Analyze mutual entailment (talazum)
Note the key property: affirming one entails affirming the other, and negating one entails negating the other. The example of sunrise and daylight illustrates this. Also note the case of existence/non-existence with opposites.
Mutual entailment is like logical equivalence.

Analyze one-way entailment
Notice that the antecedent (malzum) necessitates the consequent (lazim), but the absence of the antecedent does not necessitate absence of the consequent. The lamp/light example shows this clearly.

He first explains mutual entailment: if two things are mutual entailments, then affirming one entails affirming the other, and negating one entails negating the other. The examples given are sunrise and daylight, and fatherhood and sonship. The author notes that mutual entailment also exists between the existence of something and the non-existence of its opposite (e.g., daylight and absence of night, or a thing and the negation of its contradictory). He then explains one-way entailment: the existence of the antecedent (malzum) necessitates the existence of the consequent (lazim), but the non-existence of the antecedent does not necessitate the non-existence of the consequent, because the consequent might also be entailed by another antecedent. The examples include: an electric lamp being lit entails light, but turning it off does not entail absence of light; cutting an animal's neck entails death, but death does not entail cutting the neck; rain entails wet ground, but wet ground does not entail rain. Finally, he gives an example of one-way entailment between existence and non-existence: whiteness entails not being black, but not being black does not entail whiteness (it could be another color). The footnote clarifies that 'sunrise' means the sun's presence in the sky opposite that location, even if hidden by clouds. The passage is a clear, example-driven explanation of these fundamental logical relations.

Distinguishing Logical and Usuli General/Specific
Distinguishes logical and usuli general/specific

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the logical terms 'aamm wa akhass and the usuli terms 'aam wa khaas, and apply the entailment rule in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Logical 'aamm/akhass is not usuli 'aam/khaas; confusing them leads to fallacious reasoning.

In this lesson, we clarify a common confusion: the logicians' 'aamm wa akhass (more general/more specific) is distinct from the usuli jurists' 'aam wa khaas (general/specific). The author defines the usuli terms and explains the logical relationship between a general ruling and a specific instance. Understanding this distinction is vital for accurate reasoning in both logic and Islamic legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Recall the logical 'aamm/akhass
In mantiq, 'aamm and akhass refer to the inclusivity relationship between universals, e.g., genus (a'amm) and species (akhass). This is a purely conceptual relation, not tied to linguistic expressions.
Already covered in earlier lessons.
2
Define the usuli 'aam and khaas
Al-'aam is a lafz (expression) covering all individuals suitable for it in one context, like 'men'; al-khaas is a lafz denoting a single entity, like 'Zayd'. Notice that 'aam here is linguistic, not conceptual.
The usuli definition depends on the word's scope, not the meaning's hierarchy.
3
Apply the entailment rule for usuli general and specific
If a ruling (hukm) applies to the general (all individuals of the lafz), it necessarily applies to any specific individual falling under that general term. The reverse is not true: a ruling on a specific does not transfer to the general.
This is because other individuals may differ; the form of generality (sigha al-umum) guarantees coverage.
4
Identify the potential fallacy
Assuming that a ruling on a specific applies to the general is a fallacy (hasty generalization). The example: ruling on some people as greedy does not imply all people are greedy.
In usul al-fiqh, this ensures that a ruling stated in general terms covers all specific cases, but a specific case's ruling does not extend beyond its evidence.

Eight Essential Terms for Munazara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define eight key terms of munazara and distinguish between their meanings, especially mafhum vs masadiq and ilzaam vs ifhaam.

Core Idea
In munazara, precise terminology separates sound argument from confusion: knowing mafhum, masadiq, ilzaam, ifhaam is foundational.

This lesson introduces eight technical terms that form the backbone of Islamic debate (munazara). The first four—istifsār, 'unwān, mafhūm, and maṣādiq—deal with meaning and reference. The remaining four—ilzām, ifhām, mu'allil, and sā'il—define the roles and outcomes in a debate exchange. Mastering these terms will help you follow advanced discussions in logic and usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Group the terms by domain

Distinguish المفهوم from الماصدق
المفهوم is the conceptual meaning of a word (e.g., the concept of 'knowledge'). الماصدق are the actual individuals in the external world that the concept applies to (e.g., knowing persons). Note that the concept and its referents are not the same.
In usul al-fiqh, this distinction is used when discussing the scope of a legal term.

Differentiate الإلزام from الإفحام
Both denote 'inability', but الإلزام is the inability of the questioner (السائل) to continue objecting, while الإفحام is the inability of the defender (المعلِّل) to respond. Knowing which party is incapacitated tells you who is winning the debate.
These terms are specific to the dynamics of munazara.
4
Understand the roles المعلِّل and السائل
المعلِّل is the one who initiates the speech (the respondent/defender). السائل is the one who speaks after to criticize. The passage notes that roles may reverse during the defense, meaning the initial questioner might become the defender later.
This flexibility is important in real debates where the burden of proof shifts.
